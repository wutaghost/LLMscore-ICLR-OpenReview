009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            As large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable performance,
012            parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) has emerged as an important paradigm.
013            As a solution, low-rank adaptation (LoRA) freezes the pre-trained weights and in-
014            troduces small learnable adapters instead of fine-tuning the full set of parameters.
015            However, LoRA suffers from catastrophic forgetting, where pre-trained knowl-
016            edge is overwhlemed and forgotten as new information is learned. One cause of
017            this issue is implicit regularization, where deep learning models tend to favor more
018
               generalized solutions. This tendency leads to a significant increase in the largest
               singular values of the weights, which correspond to low-frequency components.
019
               To address this problem, we propose an advanced LoRA that balances the reten-
020
               tion of pre-trained knowledge with the learning of new information. Since fine-
021            tuning involves learning fine-grained details, which correspond to high-frequency
022            information, we designed HiLoRA, a method that injects learnable high-frequency
023            components into the pre-trained model. By leveraging the parameterized SVD
024            and constraining singular values to appropriate levels, HiLoRA adapts to new
025            tasks by focusing on the high-frequency domain with minimal change from the
026            pre-trained weights. To evaluate the effectiveness of HiLoRA, we conduct exten-
027            sive experiments on natural language understanding and question answering tasks.
028            The results show that HiLoRA not only improves performance but also effectively
029
               retains pre-trained knowledge compared to baseline models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Pre-trained language models (PLMs) have achieved remarkable performance in various natural lan-
034
      guage processing tasks (Devlin et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2019; Lan et al., 2019; Radford et al., 2019;
035
      He et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2023). The common way to
036   adapt pre-trained language models to downstream tasks is fine-tuning. However, fine-tuning all pa-
037   rameters of the model requires substantial resources. Especially, as the size of language models has
038   grown to billions of parameters, storing copies of the large model for each downstream task results
039   in significant memory consumption. To address this issue, recent studies suggest parameter-efficient
040   fine-tuning (PEFT) methods (Hu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024;
041   Meng et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024), fine-tuning with only a small number of trainable parameters.
042   Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021), which updates parameters using low-rank matrices,
043   has shown promising performance over other methods such as prompt tuning (Lester et al., 2021) or
044   prefix tuning (Li & Liang, 2021). LoRA keeps the pre-trained weights frozen and updates only small
045   number of parameters, which makes LoRA both storage- and compute-efficient. LoRA is designed
046   based on the assumption that pre-trained language models are inherently low-dimensional and can
047   learn efficiently even with random projections into smaller subspaces. The low-rank matrices serve
048   as adapters, amplifying features that were learned but not emphasized during pre-training.
049   However, the LoRA-based fine-tuning methods have limitations. In general, deep learning-based
050   models exhibit implicit regularization, a tendency for optimization algorithms and neural networks
051   to favor more generalized solutions without overfitting (Arora et al., 2019; Cao et al., 2022; Zhao,
052   2022; Li et al., 2024). As a result, the larger singular values of the learnable weights tend to increase
053   more significantly as training progresses (see Proposition 3.1). In Figure 1 (a), while the largest
      singular value increases during training, the test accuracy on pre-trained tasks decreases inversely.

       This suggests that implicit regularization is also observed in LoRA, where the largest singular value
079    increases during the fine-tuning process of the pre-trained model. Consequently, the low-frequency
080    components corresponding to large singular values of the introduced modules exert a significant
081    influence on the new task, overshadowing the pre-trained knowledge. The model gradually adapts
082    to the new task as their dominance grows, leading to catastrophic forgetting where the pre-trained
083    knowledge is overwhelmed and forgotten as the model learns new information.
084
       To overcome this limitation, we propose a High-frequency augmented Low-Rank Adaptation
085    method, called HiLoRA, which effectively learns new knowledge while retaining the pre-trained
086    knowledge. It is known that the low-frequency components are associated with large singular val-
087    ues and handle global information while high-frequency components correspond to smaller singular
088    values and capture fine-grained details (Cooley et al., 1969; Deng & Cahill, 1993; Pan et al., 2022).
089    The pre-trained model has already learned high-frequency components during its pre-training phase,
090    and the high-frequency components contain valuable information, rather than simply representing
091    noise. In Figure 1 (b), we compare the results of fitting an exponential decay function to the singular
092    values in the pre-trained weights and those of an ideal low-rank matrix. The decay rate α of the
093
       weights of model is significantly smaller than that of the ideal low-rank matrix, indicating that the
       lower singular values retain relatively large magnitudes. Therefore, the high-frequency components
094
       in the pre-trained weights play a crucial role in retaining fine-grained information.
095
096    Fine-tuning, literally, is the process of injecting new knowledge on top of the pre-trained informa-
097    tion, allowing the model to handle task-specific fine-grained details based on the major pre-trained
098    information. Therefore, to efficiently fine-tune the pre-trained models, we propose to augment an
099
       appropriate level of high-frequency components into the pre-trained model through learnable mod-
       ules. At this point, by limiting the singular values of the augmented components from becoming
100
       excessively large, the introduced modules can maintain its focus on the high-frequency domain.
101
       Through this process, the information is augmented in the high-frequency domain, allowing the
102    model to effectively learn the new task while retaining its pre-trained knowledge with only minimal
103    deviation. We conduct extensive experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of HiLoRA, demonstrat-
104    ing that it consistently outperforms LoRA and its variants across various tasks. Additionally, we
105    assess catastrophic forgetting across multiple baseline models, showing that HiLoRA significantly
106    mitigates the forgetting of pre-trained knowledge. Moreover, we achieved the outstanding results
107    with introducing at most 12 new high-frequency components, which is negligible w.r.t. the original
       model size. Our key contributions can be summarized as follows:

      LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) suggests the low-rank update of the pre-trained weights by the product of
138
      two low-rank matrices. For h = w0 x, the modified forward pass becomes:
139
140                                   h = W0 x + ∆W x = W0 x + BAx,                                     (3)

      where W0 , ∆W ∈ Rd1 ×d2 , A ∈ Rr×d2 and B ∈ Rd1 ×r with r ≪ {d1 , d2 }. A is initialized with a
142   random Gaussian initialization and B with zero, so ∆W = BA is zero at the beginning of training.
143   After fine-tuning, the learnable adapter ∆W can be integrated into the pre-trained weight W without
144   modifying the original model architecture or adding any additional inference overhead.
145
146   Directly modifying the components of ∆W . Recent studies have used SVD to analyze the com-
147   ponents of pre-trained weights. PiSSA (Meng et al., 2024) assumes that the principal components
148   have important information and enables faster convergence by updating only the top r principal
149   components while keeping the residual parts fixed. However, PiSSA directly modifies the principal
150   components of the original model weights W0 , altering the major information previously learned.
151
      This modification leads to catastrophic forgetting, where the pre-trained knowledge is forgotten dur-
      ing fine-tuning. Conversely, MiLoRA (Wang et al., 2024) proposes directly modifying the r minor
152
      components, assuming that they are noisy and less important, in order to better preserve the pre-
153
      trained knowledge. However, existing models lack of consideration for changes in the frequency of
154   introduced modules allows their influence to grow during fine-tuning, potentially overwhelming and
155   forgetting the pre-trained knowledge.
156
157   Adaptively adjusting the rank r. To adaptively adjust the rank r in each layer, AdaLoRA (Zhang
158   et al., 2023) parameterizes SVD and allocates the rank for each LoRA layer based on a sensitivity-
159   driven importance score. SoRA (Ding et al., 2023) reduces the rank of each layer by introducing
160   a sparsifying scheduler. These studies focus on pruning the number of ranks to meet a predefined
161   budget using heuristic importance scores. However, these methods are designed for dynamically
      adjusting the rank of the weight, and does not consider its frequency structure.

165   without significantly increasing the number of learnable parameters. The number of eigenvalues in

205   3.2   HIGH - FREQUENCY AUGMENTED L OW-R ANK A DAPTATION
206
207   Our goal is to enable the model to effectively learn new tasks while retaining its pre-trained knowl-
208   edge. We have identified that one of the critical causes of catastrophic forgetting in LoRA-based
209   methods is the increase in the singular values of ∆W during fine-tuning for new tasks, which am-
210   plifies the influence of low-frequency components. Therefore, we aim to address this issue by effec-
211   tively managing the frequency spectrum of the learned model.
212   In general, deep learning models including LoRA-based models, are biased towards the spectrum,
213   called spectral bias (Cao et al., 2019; Rahaman et al., 2019), meaning that the original model W0
214   tends to learn low-frequency information first and high-frequency information during the later stages
215   of pre-training. Specifically, certain patterns with high-frequency information are learned based on
      the global patterns with low-frequency information during in later stages. Fine-tuning is the process

295   3.3     C OMPARISON WITH L O RA- BASED METHODS
296
      In this section, we highlight the distinctions between our approach and other LoRA-based methods.
297
298
      Directly modifying the components of ∆W . PiSSA (Meng et al., 2024) and MiLoRA (Wang
299
      et al., 2024) assume that the principal components contain the major information, while the minor
300   components hold long-tail information or noise. They directly modify r principal/minor components
301   of W0 to learn new information. However, after initializing ∆W with these r components, no further
302   constraints are applied during training. Due to implicit regularization, the principal components of
303   ∆W grow larger, exerting greater influence over the original information and causing the model to
304   forget pre-trained knowledge. In contrast, HiLoRA preserves the entire pre-trained model and injects
305   new knowledge with generated high-frequency components through parameterized SVD, effectively
306   retaining the pre-trained knowledge by regulating the influence of ∆W from becoming too large.
307
308   Adaptively adjusting the rank r & High rank update of ∆W . AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023)
309   and SoRA (Ding et al., 2023) are designed to dynamically prune the number of ranks in each layer
310
      using SVD for stable training. On the other hand, some approaches, such as MoRA (Jiang et al.,
      2024) and ReLoRA (Lialin et al., 2023), aim to increase the rank of ∆W to enhance model capacity
311
      and improve performance. While these methods primarily focus on the rank r itself, our proposed
312
      method focuses on regulating the frequency of ∆W for a given predefined rank r, allowing to
313   efficiently adapts to new tasks without altering the overall rank.

458
       Unlike existing methods, we constrain the information on the new task to prevent it from over-
459
       whelming the pre-trained knowledge. To do, the injected frequency of ∆W has the upper bound
460    of appropriate frequency value. In this section, we investigate how HiLoRA mitigates the catas-
461    trophic forgetting during fine-tuning. The magnitude of the change in weights is used to measure
462    the change from pre-trained knowledge to new knowledge . Figure 4 (a) shows the evolution of
463    the Frobenius norm with respect to the difference between original and learned weights during fine-
464    tuning of RoBERTa on the STS-B dataset of GLUE task. LoRA and its variants show a rapid
465    increase in change as the epochs increase. In contrast, HiLoRA maintains a constant level of change
466    even with increasing epochs. Furthermore, Fig 4 (b) and (c) show the accuracy and evaluation loss
467    on pre-trained knowledge from the BookCorpus dataset, which is the source dataset for the pre-
468
       trained RoBERTa model. As the number of epochs increases, LoRA and its variants rapidly degrade
       the accuracy on the pre-trained knowledge, dropping from the original performance of 0.6 to below
469
       0.1, and the loss function increases by about 5 times. As mentioned earlier, without restrictions on
470
       the frequency domain during training, the model undergoes significant changes, leading to catas-
471    trophic forgetting of the pre-trained knowledge. On the other hand, HiLoRA effectively mitigates
472    this phenomenon, minimizing the performance degradation on the pre-trained task.

503
504    To analyze the influence of the injected components in HiLoRA on the performance of both pre-
505    trained and fine-tuned knowledge, we conduct an ablation study on the following variants: i) LoRA
506    refers to the traditional LoRA method; ii) LoRAU V ⊺ applies orthogonal regularization to the singu-
507    lar vectors without considering the singular values; iii) LoRASVD initializes the singular values as
508    ones, allowing them to be learnable from LoRAU V ⊺ ; and iv) HiLoRA refers to the proposed method.
509    We measure the accuracy on both the fine-tuned tasks, using the MRPC and SST-2 datasets from
510    the GLUE benchmark, and the pre-trained task, using the BookCorpus dataset on RoBERTabase . As
511    reported in Table 4, LoRA significantly sacrifices pre-training performance to improve performance
512
       on fine-tuned tasks. For the MRPC dataset, accuracy on the pre-trained task drops from 61.64 to
       3.77, while it achieves comparable accuracy on the fine-tuned task. LoRAU V ⊺ has limited expres-
513
       siveness because its singular values are fixed at one. As a result, it may sacrifice either pre-trained
514
       or fine-tuned knowledge depending on the task. For the MRPC dataset, it outperforms LoRA but
515    has lower accuracy on the pre-trained task, while for SST-2, it shows lower performance on the
516    fine-tuned task but better accuracy on the pre-trained task compared to LoRA. LoRASVD performs
517    better due to its learnable singular values, enabling it to retain more pre-trained information than the
518    original LoRA. Notably, HiLoRA constraints the singular values to learn in the high-frequency do-
519    main, ensuring both superior expressiveness and efficient retention of pre-trained information. For
520    both datasets, HiLoRA achieves the best performance on both fine-tuned and pre-trained tasks.

522    7                 C ONCLUSION
523
524    We propose a simple yet effective low-rank adaptation method called HiLoRA, to address the prob-
525    lem of catastrophic forgetting in LoRA, where pre-trained knowledge is overwhelmed and forgotten
526    as the model learns new information. Since fine-tuning incorporates fine-grained knowledge on top
527
       of the pre-trained information, we augment the pre-trained model with new high-frequency compo-
       nents, minimizing the impact on the pre-trained knowledge. HiLoRA achieves this by employing
528
       parameterized SVD and maintaining the augmented frequency components at appropriate levels.
529
       Our experimental results demonstrate that HiLoRA achieves promising performance on new tasks.
530    Unlike traditional LoRA-based models, the learned models effectively capture high-frequency com-
531    ponents and adapt to new information, rather than relying solely on pre-trained knowledge. With
532    minimal changes, HiLoRA successfully integrates new information into the pre-trained weights,
533    balancing between retaining pre-trained knowledge and adapting to new tasks.
534
535    Limitations. Despite the advantages of HiLoRA, there are a few limitations. First, while HiLoRA
536    focuses on effectively capturing high-frequency components, in certain scenarios, it may under-
537    represent some low-frequency information. Additionally, the optimal level of high-frequency com-
538    ponents may vary across different datasets, requiring further tuning in some cases.

740
      for infimum. When using the Frobenius norm (or spectral norm) with matrices, the Kolmogorov
741   n-width is computed by the singular values of W as follows:
742

743
744   where σn+1 is the (n + 1)-th largest singular value of the matrix W . The Kolmogorov n-width
745
      measures how well a set W can be approximated by an n-dimensional subspace. In other words, it
      represents the minimal maximum error when approximating with an n-dimensional subspace. Then
746
      we can determine the optimal dimensionality needed to achieve a desired approximation accuracy.
747
748   If the singular values decrease rapidly, W can be well approximated even for small n, and the
749   Kolmogorov n-width also decreases quickly. Therefore, the singular value decay rate α, which
750   plays a pivotal role in determining how effectively a matrix can be approximated, is commonly
751
      modeled by an exponential decay function as follows:
752                                                 σn′ = Ce−αn ,                                      (11)
               ′
753   where σn represents the n-th modeled singular values, C > 0 is a constant, and α > 0 is the decay
754   rate. When the decay rate α is low, the singular values decrease gradually, resulting in large errors
755   when approximating with the same n dimensions. To minimize the approximation errors, a larger n
      is required, indicating that significant information is contained in the lower singular values.

807   We describe the benchmark datasets of GLUE (Wang et al., 2018a) below.
808
809           • CoLA. The Corpus of Linguistic Acceptability (Warstadt et al., 2019) provides a dataset of
                English sentences, where each sentence is judged for grammatical acceptability based on


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
                data from books and journal articles. The objective is a binary classification to determine
811             whether a sentence is grammatically correct or incorrect. The dataset consists of 8.5k
812             samples for training, 1k samples for validation, and 1k samples for test.
813
              • SST-2. The Stanford Sentiment Treebank (Socher et al., 2013) includes sentences from
814
                movie reviews, along with human-provided sentiment annotations. The goal is to classify
815             the sentiment of each sentence as either positive or negative. The dataset consists of 67k
816             samples for training, 872 samples for validation, and 1.8k samples for test.
817
              • MRPC. The Microsoft Research Paraphrase Corpus (Dolan & Brockett, 2005) contains
818
                pairs of sentences automatically extracted from online news sources. Human annotators
819             label each pair, and the task is to identify whether the two sentences in a pair convey
820             the same meaning. The dataset consists of 3.7k samples for training, 408 samples for
821             validation, and 1.7k samples for test.
822
              • QQP. The Quora Question Pairs dataset (Chen et al., 2018) consists of question pairs taken
823             from Quora, a community-driven question-and-answer platform. The task is to determine
824             if two given questions are semantically identical. The dataset consists of 364k samples for
825             training, 40k samples for validation, and 391k samples for test.
826           • MNLI. The Multi-Genre Natural Language Inference Corpus (Williams et al., 2017) in-
827             cludes sentence pairs with textual entailment annotations collected through crowdsourcing.
828             Given a premise and a hypothesis, the task is to predict whether the premise entails the hy-
829             pothesis, contradicts it, or is neutral. The dataset includes both in-domain and cross-domain
830             evaluations using a hidden test set. The dataset consists of 393k samples for training, 20k
831             samples for validation, and 20k samples for test.
832           • QNLI. The Question-Answering Natural Language Inference dataset (Wang et al., 2018b)
833             consists of question-paragraph pairs from which an answer must be found. The task in-
834             volves determining whether a specific sentence from the paragraph answers the correspond-
835             ing question. The dataset consists of 108k samples for training, 5.7k samples for validation,
836             and 5.7k samples for test.
837           • RTE. The Recognizing Textual Entailment dataset (Bentivogli et al., 2009) comes from
838             a series of annual challenges focusing on textual entailment. The task is to classify sen-
839             tence pairs as either entailment or non-entailment. The dataset consists of 2.5k samples for
840             training, 276 samples for validation, and 3k samples for test.
841           • STS-B. The Semantic Textual Similarity Benchmark (Cer et al., 2017) features sentence
842             pairs drawn from various sources, including news headlines and image captions, with
843             human-assigned similarity scores. The task is a regression problem where the model must
844             predict a similarity score ranging from 0 to 5. The dataset consists of 7k samples for train-
845             ing, 1.5k samples for validation, and 1.4k samples for test.
