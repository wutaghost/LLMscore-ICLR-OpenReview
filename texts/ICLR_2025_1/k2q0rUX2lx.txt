                                                 A BSTRACT
011
012
013
               Reinforcement Learning (RL) plays a crucial role in aligning large language mod-
               els (LLMs) with human preferences and improving their ability to perform com-
014
               plex tasks. However, current approaches either require significant computational
015
               resources due to the use of multiple models and extensive online sampling for
016            training (e.g., PPO) or are framed as bandit problems (e.g., DPO, DRO), which
017            often struggle with multi-step reasoning tasks, such as math problem-solving and
018            complex reasoning that involve long chains of thought. To overcome these lim-
019            itations, we introduce Direct Q-function Optimization (DQO), which formulates
020            the response generation process as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) and utilizes
021            the soft actor-critic (SAC) framework to optimize a Q-function directly parame-
022            terized by the language model. The MDP formulation of DQO offers structural
023            advantages over bandit-based methods, enabling more effective process supervi-
024
               sion. Experimental results on two math problem-solving datasets, GSM8K and
               MATH, demonstrate that DQO outperforms previous methods, establishing it as a
025
               promising offline reinforcement learning approach for aligning language models.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large language models (LLMs) have shown remarkable performances and potentials on a wide
031
      range of tasks including dialog generation (Han et al., 2024), general question answering (Alawwad
      et al., 2024), code generation (Jimenez et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b), agents (Wang et al., 2024)
032
      and math problem solving (Yu et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024). To ensure good performance, one
033
      of the key procedures is to align the language models with human preferences or task-specific re-
034   quirements by reinforcement learning (RL) (Bai et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023). Canonically, the
035   alignment training pipeline consists of two stages (Ouyang et al., 2022). In the first stage, a reward
036   model under the Bradley-Terry model (Bradley & Terry, 1952) is trained on human or environment-
037   labeled preference data. Then the language model is trained by online RL algorithms like Proximal
038   Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017) with the reward signals provided by the reward
039   model trained in stage one.
040   Despite the good performance achieved, the online RL methods usually involve sampling during
041   training, which is both costly and unstable compared to offline methods (Choshen et al., 2020).
042   These issues are overcome by offline preference learning methods, of which the representative is
043
      Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024). DPO and its follow-ups (e.g., Zhao
      et al. (2023); Azar et al. (2024)) treat the language model as the policy model and reward model
044
      simultaneously and train the model on offline pairwise preference data directly, therefore eliminating
045
      the need for a separate reward model. Though simple, direct preference learning has been shown
046   effective and efficient in LLM alignment (Tunstall et al., 2023).
047
      However, in practice, sometimes it is hard to acquire pairwise data required by the above meth-
048   ods. This issue becomes particularly severe under scenarios like math problem solving or code
049   generation when generating high-quality data requires domain-specific expertise (Saunders et al.,
050   2022; Bowman et al., 2022). This drawback of DPO has recently been circumvented by Direct Re-
051   ward Optimization (DRO) (Richemond et al., 2024). DRO formulates the LLM generation task as
052   a single-step MDP (i.e., bandit) and adopts the framework soft actor-critic (SAC) (Haarnoja et al.,
053   2018), where the advantage is directly parameterized by the language model. Consequently, DRO
      inherits the advantage of offline policy gradient and gets rid of the dependency on pairwise data.

065   Nevertheless, DRO treats the process as a bandit problem, which neglects the intrinsic long-horizon
066   nature of a wide spectrum of tasks that require complex multi-step reasoning like math problem
067   solving and code generation (Kang et al., 2024; Miao et al., 2024), where an erroneous reasoning is
068   almost fatal. Previous RL research found that if rewards are only provided at the end of the episode,
069
      discovering this sparse reward signal is a hard exploration problem and sparse reward functions may
      not be able to meaningfully distinguish between a wide range of different policies, which makes
070
      the training inefficient (Riedmiller et al., 2018; Wilcox et al., 2022). In the meanwhile, recent
071
      studies show that signals from process reward models (PRMs) can further boost the performance
072   of language model (Zhang et al., 2024a; Lightman et al., 2023). The positional information of
073   PRM scores usually implies the critical mistakes in the reasoning and therefore provides stronger
074   supervision signals. However, if the whole generation process is simplified as a single action, the
075   process rewards will be aggregated and the positional information will be lost, implying that DRO
076   cannot efficiently utilize process supervision.
077   In order to overcome the aforementioned issues, in this paper, we propose Direct Q-function op-
078   timization (DQO), an offline RL algorithm for LLMs. In DQO, the responding procedure is for-
079   mulated as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) and our goal is to learn an optimal policy under
080   KL-regularization. Our algorithm adopts the framework of soft Q-learning, where the Q-function is
081   directly parameterized by the language model. Then both the Q-function network and the value net-
082
      work are updated according to Soft Bellman Equation on offline data. The MDP formulation makes
      DQO a multi-step learning algorithm, and can therefore exploit process reward signals. A holistic
083
      comparison of our method and other alignment methods is shown in Table 1. Unlike DPO or DRO,
084
      where all tokens (actions) within a response (trajectory) are evenly incentivized or punished, DQO
085   can discover the correct reasoning steps in a incorrect response, see the case in Table 2. Specifically,
086   our contributions are summarized as follows
087
      • We propose Direct Q-function optimization, or DQO, an offline RL algorithm for LLMs. Com-
088
        pared to previous methods, DQO can learn from offline and negative or unbalanced samples.
089
        Moreover, DQO is featured by step-wise learning, which is favorable for long-horizon tasks and
090     able to exploit process rewards.
091
092   • We introduce a practical instantiation of DQO, which equips DQO with λ-return and importance
093
        sampling. These techniques stabilize the training process and ensure a good performance.
094   • We empirically compare DQO with a wide range of widely used alignment algorithms on math
095     problem-solving tasks. Experiment results show that DQO outperforms these baselines on both
096     GSM8K and MATH datasets. Moreover, as shown by our experiment, when process rewards are
097     available, the performance of DQO can be further boosted, indicating that DQO can benefit from
098
        process rewards.

492   5   R ELATED W ORK
493   Reinforcement Learning for Language Model Alignment Aligning language models with human
494   preferences, or reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF), dates back to the work of
495   Wirth et al. (2017) and Christiano et al. (2017). It has been widely applied to a bunch of recent
496   models including GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), Gemini (Team et al., 2023), and Llama (Touvron
497   et al., 2023), leading to the surprising performance of these models. The alignment procedure usu-
498   ally takes place after supervised finetuning (SFT). In the canonical approaches of RLHF (Ouyang
499   et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Munos et al., 2024), a reward model is first trained with preference data
500   and then the model is updated with Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO). Another line of works,
501
      initiating from Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024), include SLiC (Zhao
      et al., 2023), IPO (Azar et al., 2024), KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024) and so on. These approaches are
502
      featured by directly parameterizing the reward models with the language model and then training on
503
      offline preference data. Following DPO, one branch of works, including GSHF (Xiong et al., 2024a),
504   SPPO (Wu et al., 2024) and INPO (Zhang et al., 2024b), adapts DPO or its variant to online samples
505   and iterative training and resulted to state-of-the-art models. On the other hand, Richemond et al.
506   (2024) adapted offline reinforcement learning algorithm to direct preference learning and proposed
507   Direct Reward Optimization (DRO), which combined offline policy learning with a value function
508   learning and updated policy network and value network iteratively. Our work has a similar structure
509   to DRO, but models the language generation as an MDP rather than a bandit and can utilize process
510   supervision to facilitate training.
511   Multi-step and Long Horizon RL for LLM alignment Many tasks for LLMs require LLMs to
512   reason step by step or interact with the environment turn by turn. However, the rewards are usually
513
      sparse since they are only provided at the end of a long horizon of reasoning or interactions. In
      traditional RL literature, one approach towards breaking the curse of lone horizon and sparse reward
514
      is to train or estimate an intermediate value function or process reward (Park et al., 2024) and use
515
      the process reward to guided searching (Torne et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a) and RL training.
516   The utilization of process reward has also led to better performance for LLM reasoning (Zhang
517   et al., 2024a; Lightman et al., 2023). Most straightforwardly, Snell et al. (2023) proposed ILQL,
518   which employed implicit Q-learning to train a Q-function network and V-function network. Then,
519   at inference time, ILQL uses learned value functions to perturb the log probabilities of the initial
520   policy towards utility-maximizing behavior. The success of direct preference learning also stimu-
521   lates a lot of work adapting DPO to multi-turn scenarios. To estimate process reward and utilize
522   the information provided, Chen et al. (2024a); Lai et al. (2024); Xie et al. (2024) leverages process
523   reward signals or AI feedback to construct preference pairs for intermediate steps and update the
524
      model with original DPO. On the other hand, Xiong et al. (2024b); Shani et al. (2024) extends the
      vanilla DPO to accommodate the multi-turn structure. However, these approaches require pairwise
525
      data, which might not be available or easy to obtain on some specific occasions. Our work, while
526
      following the approach of direct preference learning, eliminates the need for pairwise data and can
527   be boosted by process rewards.
528
529   6   C ONCLUSION
530
      In this work, we propose DQO, an offline reinforcement learning algorithm for enhancing the lan-
531
      guage model’s ability in multi-step reasoning. Compared to previous online methods like PPO, the
532
      offline nature of DQO bypasses the requirement of an extra reward model and online sampling dur-
533   ing training. Previous offline methods usually formulate the LLMs’ responding process as a bandit
534   problem, which usually fails to capture the implicit long-horizon and multi-step nature of those tasks
535   requiring a long chain of thought. In contrast, DQO frames the tasks as a Markov decision process
536   and further employs a soft actor-critic framework to learn the V -function and the Q-function, which
537   is directly parameterized by the language model. To verify the effectiveness of DQO, we conduct
538   extensive experiments on two math-problem-solving datasets, GSM8K and MATH, and empirical
539   results show that DQO outperforms all our baseline. Currently, our experiment results are limited to
      two base models due to time constraints and we leave it as future work.
