                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               In safety-critical domains, such as industrial systems, the lack of explainability
010
               in predictive ‘black-box’ machine learning models can hinder trust and adoption.
011            Standard explainability techniques, while powerful, often require deep expertise
012            in data analytics and machine learning and fail to align with the sequential, dy-
013            namic nature of data in these environments. In this paper, we propose a novel
014            explainability framework that leverages reinforcement learning (RL) to support
015            model predictions with visual explanations based on dynamical system simula-
016            tion. By training RL agents to simulate events that require prediction, we use
017            these agents’ critics to make classifications. Next, we employ the actors of the
018            RL agents to simulate the potential future trajectories underlying these classifi-
019
               cations, providing visual explanations that are more intuitive and align with the
               expertise of industrial domain experts. We demonstrate the applicability of this
020
               method through a case study involving monitoring a small industrial system for
021
               cyberattacks, showing how our framework generates actionable predictions that
022            are supported with visual explanations. This approach aims to bridge the gap
023            between advanced machine learning models and their real-world deployment in
024            safety-critical environments.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   With the increasing volume of data generated in industrial environments and the growing complex-
030   ity of managing these systems, deep learning has become a promising tool to automate or support
031   decision-making for industrial domain experts (Wang et al., 2022). A key function of deep learning
032   in these settings is predictive analytics—making classifications to forecast future events, which can
033
      assist operators in planning for critical decisions. Despite the potential, the adoption of deep learn-
      ing in industrial environments faces a major challenge: explainability. While deep learning models
034
      have demonstrated high reliability and accuracy, their ‘black-box’ nature has put into question their
035
      trustworthiness and limited their adoption in industries that must adhere to stringent regulatory stan-
036   dards (Ahmed et al., 2022). In safety-critical industries, transparency in decision-making is often
037   mandated by regulations.
038
039
      Several methods, notably model-agnostic techniques like Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Ex-
      planations (LIME) (Ribeiro et al., 2016) and SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) (Lundberg,
040
      2017), have been utilized to provide a degree of explainability for deep learning models. However,
041
      these techniques require a level of expertise in data analytics that most domain experts lack, making
042   them difficult for industrial practitioners to interpret and apply effectively. Instead, many of these
043   domain experts rely on time-series analysis, which involves observing data sequences over time to
044   understand system behavior, predict future trends, and make informed decisions (Fatima & Rahimi,
045   2024). As the volume of data grows, this manual analysis becomes increasingly challenging, cre-
046   ating a greater need for deep learning systems that can support decision-making while remaining
047   interpretable.
048   In this paper, we propose a novel approach that generates and explains deep learning-based classi-
049   fications for industrial dynamic systems by providing time-series explanations that align with the
050   workflows and practices of domain experts. Our approach leverages reinforcement learning (RL)
051   agents to model the sequences of events that lead to key events that domain experts need to predict.
052   Next, we demonstrate the use of the RL agents’ state-action values or state values—generated by the
053   agents’ deep neural network-based critics—to make predictive classifications, and the learned action
      policies to simulate the sequence of events that lead up to those outcomes and underlie the classifi-

      2    R ELATED W ORK
061
      Explainability: Post-hoc explainable AI (XAI) methods, designed to explain the predictions of
062
      trained black-box models, can be broadly categorized into model-specific and model-agnostic ap-
063   proaches (Minh et al., 2022). Model-specific methods are tailored to a particular class of models,
064   utilizing their internal structure to generate explanations. For instance, methods such as Layer-
065   wise Relevance Propagation (LRP) (Montavon et al., 2019), DeepLIFT (Shrikumar et al., 2017),
066   heatmaps (Payer et al., 2019), saliency maps (Adebayo et al., 2018), GradCAM (Selvaraju et al.,
067   2020) backpropagate gradients from the output to the input layers in neural networks to visually
068   emphasize regions in the input that have the greatest contribution to the model’s decision. Model-
069   agnostic methods, on the other hand, do not rely on the internals of a specific model. Instead, they
070   aim to provide explanations based purely on input-output behavior. SHAP (Lundberg, 2017) is one
071
      such method, which explains the contribution of each input feature to the prediction using game-
      theoretic principles. LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016) approximates the black-box model locally using a
072
      simpler, interpretable model and uses the coefficients of this simpler model to explain the relative im-
073
      portance of each feature to the prediction. More recently, textual justification methods have emerged
074   as an explainability approach (Shi et al., 2018; Sabol et al., 2020; Musto et al., 2021; Aminimehr
075   et al., 2024; Hartmann et al., 2022). These methods generate natural language explanations of model
076   predictions, aiming to make the reasoning accessible to general users. Musto et al. (2021) showed
077   that text justification can generate user-friendly explanations that improve transparency and enhance
078   user trust and satisfaction in the context of recommendation systems. A notable trend is that tex-
079   tual justification methods have been primarily proposed for applications such as recommendation
080   systems, medical diagnosis, and stock prediction, highlighting the effectiveness of this explanation
081   approach for domain experts in specialized fields. The motivation for our work aligns with that
082
      of textual justification, but offers a different medium of explanation tailored to domain experts in
      industrial domains: time-series plots.
083
084   Explainable RL: While sharing the same tools, the field of explainable RL (Wells & Bednarz, 2021;
085   Heuillet et al., 2021) typically focuses on interpreting the reasoning, actions, and decision-making
086   processes of RL agents after they have learned a task. In contrast, our approach does not aim to
087   explain the internal decision-making of the agents themselves. Instead, we leverage the agents’
088
      learned behaviors to explain how specific predictions could unfold in the system. The goal is not to
      understand the agent’s reasoning but to use its actions to simulate potential future events, thereby
089
      providing a clearer explanation of the predicted outcomes.
090
091   RL in Industry: RL research is pervasive across industrial sectors, including power systems (Zhang
092   et al., 2019), autonomous driving (Aradi, 2020), smart cities (Ullah et al., 2020), and manufactur-
093   ing (Wang et al., 2021).

109
110   Through training, the critic learns to map the data, collected as observations, of the environment to
111   a numerical value that predicts the event the RL agent is trained to achieve. For instance, if an RL
112
      agent is trained to inject malicious disturbances or manipulate machinery in an industrial setting, the
      critic learns to map system data to a state value that predicts when these manipulations could lead to
113
      equipment damage or system failure. Alternatively, in an autonomous driving simulation, where an
114
      RL agents affect the external vehicle’s motion, the critic learns to anticipate risky driving situations
115   based on the car’s external environment data.
116
117   In our research, we extend the critic’s state value to generate both numerical and categorical values
118
      that provide predictive classifications based on system data.
119   The actor, on the other hand, dictates the actions taken by the agent. Since the actor learns to simulate
120   the sequence of actions that lead to the predicted event, we leverage the actor to provide explain-
121   ability. Specifically, the actor is used to simulate the sequence of events leading to a classification,
122   providing supporting visual explanation.
123   Figure 1 illustrates our conceptual framework. We train multiple RL agents, each designed to cause
124   a different event that requires prediction. Each agent’s critic processes observations from the pro-
125   duction system and feeds its state-action (or state) value to a function that maps it to a numerical
126   score representing the event’s impact. These mappings may include the probability of the event,
127   which in negative impact cases, translates to a measure of risk—commonly defined as the product
128   of impact and likelihood. The outcome with the highest impact (or risk) determines the classifica-
129   tion. When prompted for an explanation, the actor corresponding to the highest impact generates the
130
      action sequence that leads to the predicted outcome. Next, the system’s simulated behavior under
      the actor’s actions is visualized as a time-series plot, allowing the user to understand the system’s
131
      future trajectory underlying the classification.
132
133   To demonstrate the advantages of our approach, particularly in safety-critical systems, we consider
134   the scenario of a small, localized electric microgrid targeted by cyberattackers. In this scenario, a
135   predictive analytics system must monitor the microgrid’s data and output a classification that reflects
136
      the potential impact of cyberattacks manipulating a generator’s load-frequency control. Maintaining
      a stable power frequency—at 60 Hz in North America—is critical for the proper functioning of
137
      the grid. Deviations cause power flicker (experienced as lights rapidly dimming and brightening),
138
      which can damage sensitive equipment by forcing it to operate outside of its designed-frequency
139   range. Power system equipment is expensive, and significant frequency anomalies are typically
140   detected by protection devices, which isolate the equipment to prevent their damage. Consequently,
141   by strategically compromising control, cyberattackers could potentially isolate generators and cause
142   power blackouts. The dangers posed by such cyberattacks have been the topic of numerous research
143   studies, surveyed in (Mohan et al., 2020).
144
      We choose this scenario for several important reasons. First, power systems are increasingly the tar-
145   get of sophisticated cyberattacks, with substantial evidence pointing to nation-state actors involved
146   in cyber espionage (Hjortdal, 2011) and attacks on electric grids, as demonstrated in the attacks on
147   Ukraine’s grid in 2015 (Case, 2016) and 2016. Power systems are also massive in scale in complex-
148   ity, and stand to significantly benefit from incorporating deep learning-based methods for monitoring
149   and risk assessment. Additionally, power systems domain experts cannot be expected to have ex-
150   tensive data analytics expertise. Second, the lack of data on cyberattacks makes it challenging to
151   predict threats from historical data. RL becomes a viable tool here, as it can generate simulated data
152   to anticipate the impact of unknown cyberattacks and help detect them when they occur. Finally, cy-
153
      berattacks often involve subtle and strategic manipulations of the system. The critic in RL can help
      uncover these complex failure modes, making it an effective approach for predicting and explaining
154
      potential risks in critical infrastructure systems.

224   4.1   E XPERIMENT
225
226   We present a case study of a small microgrid, adapted from Mohamed & Kundur (2024). The
227   microgrid is equipped with a security system that monitors the frequency and its rate-of-change
228   (derivative), providing a classification that represents the vulnerability of the system to potential
229
      cyberattacks. When a high-urgency classification is detected, it signals the likelihood of an ongoing
      cyberattack that may be compromising the system. In such a scenario, a critical decision—such as
230
      isolating compromised communication channels—must be made to mitigate the attack. However,
231
      communication between system components is vital for the safety and stability of the microgrid, so
232   this decision must be based on a highly trustworthy classification, triggered only under real threat to
233   prevent further system damage.
234
235
      In this experiment, we train RL agents to simulate cyberattacks aimed at compromising the system
      and forcing it into blackout conditions. The RL agents are tasked with learning strategies that satisfy
236
      several key objectives:
237
238         1. The cyberattacks must introduce disturbances to the generator’s control system, manipulat-
239            ing its power output. An increase in power generation will lead to a rise in the microgrid’s
240            frequency, while a reduction in power generation will decrease the frequency.
241         2. Both the frequency and its rate-of-change are measured in per-unit (pu), with frequency
242            normalized by the nominal 60 Hz. Similarly, the rate-of-change is divided by 60 Hz for a
243            consistent measurement unit. Protective devices within the microgrid are programmed to
244            activate when the frequency or its rate-of-change deviates from safe thresholds, typically
245            3% pu for frequency deviation and 5% pu for the rate-of-change.
246         3. It is desirable for the attack to keep the frequency deviations minimal to avoid triggering
247            countermeasures. The more subtle the deviations, the harder it is to detect the attack.
248            Therefore, the RL agents should aim to manipulate the rate-of-change of the frequency
249            rather than the absolute frequency itself to ensure stealthier attacks.
250   To meet these objectives, we design a reward function that guides the RL agent to force large devia-
251   tion in the rate-of-change of frequency while keeping the frequency deviation minimal. The reward
252   function is defined as follows:

257   change of the frequency. The agents observations are S = (s1 , s2 ). The first term in equation 13

259

260
      frequency deviations. Episodes terminate when the conditions in the second and third terms are
261   satisfied.

368   6       D ISCUSSION
369
370   Accessibility of explanation through simulation: One of the key strengths of this approach is its
371   ability to offer explanations for classifications and predictions in an intuitive and accessible format,
372   particularly for industrial domain experts. By leveraging simulations, this method aligns well with
373   the existing expertise of these professionals, who are accustomed to monitoring time-series plots,
374
      events, and sequences. The simulations create a visual narrative that makes understanding the un-
      derlying dynamics more straightforward, thereby facilitating the integration of machine learning
375
      insights into industrial systems.
376
377   Data generation in the absence of historical data: Another advantage of this method is its capa-
      bility to generate synthetic data for events that lack historical precedent. By employing multiple RL

      agents, the system explores and predicts a diverse range of events, including those that are subtle
379   or previously unknown. Moreover, this approach enables domain experts to iteratively interact with
380   the RL environment, allowing them to introduce new test cases or expand the number of events re-
381   quiring prediction. This adaptability is key to refining the predictive model over time, as experts can
382   augment RL agents and environments with additional details as necessary.

384   6.1   C HALLENGES
385
386   Expertise required for RL design: Although our approach alleviates the need for deep techni-
387   cal expertise in explainability methods, we acknowledge the complexity involved in designing and
388   training RL agents. This presents a potential barrier, as creating effective RL models demands spe-
389   cialized knowledge. However, the trade-off is that this complexity is primarily concentrated in the
390   RL development phase, which occurs infrequently. In contrast, the resulting system, once in place,
391
      simplifies interpretation and understanding, a task that will be required far more often by a broader
      audience of users.
392
393   The importance of historical data: While our focus has been on the use of RL in generating
394   predictions through simulation, we recognize the value of historical data in classification algorithms.
395   Historical data provides critical insights into patterns that can inform decision-making. Although our
396   method primarily emphasizes online RL learning in simulated environments, there is potential to use
397
      offline RL (Levine et al., 2020), which leverages previously collected datasets. A hybrid approach,
      combining both historical data and simulated environments, could further enhance the predictive
398
      models.
399
400   Simulation environments: A key limitation of relying on online RL is that it necessitates the avail-
401   ability of accurate dynamical models that can be incorporated into the RL environment. For many
402   industrial systems, models form the foundation of system design and are readily available. Further-
403
      more, with the rapid growth of digital twins and simulation software, domain experts will increas-
      ingly have access to models that closely mimic real-world environments. This progression will help
404
      bridge the gap between virtual simulations and real-world applications, making the adoption of our
405
      method more feasible.

408   7     C ONCLUSION
409
410   In this paper, we proposed a novel direction for explaining deep learning model classifications
411   through time-series simulations. We developed a real-time predictive system for dynamical envi-
412   ronments, where multiple reinforcement learning (RL) agents are employed to make predictions
413
      and support these predictions with simulations that visualize the forecasted system behavior under-
      lying the classification. The motivation behind this approach is to offer an alternative method for
414
      explainability in contexts where domain experts are more accustomed to analyzing time-series plots
415
      to understand system behavior and anticipate future outcomes.
