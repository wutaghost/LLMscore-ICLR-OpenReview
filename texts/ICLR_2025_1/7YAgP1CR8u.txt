009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) face significant challenges in few-shot scenarios,
012            particularly due to overfitting and long training times for high-fidelity rendering.
013            While current approaches like FreeNeRF and SparseNeRF use frequency regular-
014            ization or pre-trained priors, they can be limited by complex scheduling or potential
015            biases. We introduce FrugalNeRF, a novel few-shot NeRF framework that leverages
016            weight-sharing voxels across multiple scales to efficiently represent scene details.
017            Our key contribution is a cross-scale geometric adaptation training scheme that
018            selects pseudo ground truth depth based on reprojection error from both training
019            and novel views across scales. This guides training without relying on externally
020
               learned priors, allowing FrugalNeRF to fully utilize available data. While not
               dependent on pre-trained priors, FrugalNeRF can optionally integrate them for
021
               enhanced quality without affecting convergence speed. Our method generalizes
022
               effectively across diverse scenes and converges more rapidly than state-of-the-
023            art approaches. Our experiments on standard LLFF, DTU, and RealEstate-10K
024            datasets demonstrate that FrugalNeRF outperforms existing few-shot NeRF models,
025            including those using pre-trained priors, while significantly reducing training time,
026            making it a practical solution for efficient and accurate 3D scene reconstruction.

      Traditional NeRF methods, despite producing high-quality outputs, suffer from long training time and
071
      rely on frequency regularization (Yang et al., 2023) via multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) and positional
072   encoding, slowing convergence (Fig. 2 (a)). Alternatives like voxel upsampling (Fig. 2 (b)) attempt
073   to overcome these challenges but struggle with generalizing to varied scenes (Chen et al., 2022a;
074   Sun et al., 2022; 2023). Furthermore, using pre-trained models (Fig. 2 (c)) creates dependencies on
075   external priors, which might not be readily available or could introduce biases from their training
076   datasets (Niemeyer et al., 2022; Roessle et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023).
077
      FrugalNeRF differs from these approaches by incorporating a cross-scale, geometric adaptation
078   mechanism, facilitating rapid training while preserving high-quality view synthesis (Fig. 2 (d)). Our
079   method efficiently utilizes weight-sharing voxels across various scales to encapsulate the scene’s
080   frequency components. Our proposed adaptation scheme projects rendered depths and colors from
081   different voxel scales onto the closest training view to compute reprojection errors. The most accurate
082   scale becomes the pseudo-ground truth and guides the training across scales, thus eliminating the
083   need for complex voxel upsampling schedules and enhancing generalizability across diverse scenes.
084   FrugalNeRF significantly reduces computational demands and accelerates training through self-
085   adaptive mechanisms that exploit the multi-scale voxel structure, ensuring quick convergence without
086   compromising the synthesis quality. By fully leveraging the training data and eliminating reliance on
087   externally learned priors and their inherent limitations, FrugalNeRF provides a pathway toward more
088   scalable and efficient few-shot novel view synthesis. In conclusion, FrugalNeRF efficiently bypasses
089   the need for external pre-trained prior and complex scheduling for voxel.
090   We evaluate the FrugalNeRF’s effectiveness on three prominent datasets: LLFF (Mildenhall et al.,
091   2019b), DTU (Jensen et al., 2014), and RealEstate-10K Zhou et al. (2018) dataset to assess both the
092   rendering quality and convergence speed. Our results show that FrugalNeRF is not only faster but
093   also achieves superior quality in comparison to existing methods (Fig. 1), showcasing FrugalNeRF’s
094   proficiency in generating perceptually high-quality images. The main contributions of our work are:
095
096                   • We introduce a novel weight-sharing voxel representation that encodes multiple frequency
097                     components of the scene, significantly enhancing the efficiency and quality of few-shot
098                     novel view synthesis.
099
100                   • Our geometric adaptation selects accurate rendered depth across different scales by reprojec-
101                     tion errors to create pseudo geometric ground truth that guides the training process, enabling
102
                        a robust learning mechanism that is less reliant on complex scheduling and more adaptable
                        to various scenes.
103
104
                      • FrugalNeRF’s training scheme relies solely on available data, eliminating the need for
105
                        external priors or pre-trained models and ensuring fast convergence without sacrificing
106                     quality. It remains flexible, allowing the integration of learned priors to further enhance
107                     quality without affecting training speed.

110   Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF)(Mildenhall et al., 2020) has advanced novel view synthesis(Chen

114
      synthesis (Oechsle et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021; Jensen et al., 2014; Yariv et al., 2020; Wang et al.,
      2021a), single-view synthesis (Gao et al., 2020; Tucker & Snavely, 2020; Han et al., 2022; Wiles
115
      et al., 2020; Wimbauer et al., 2023), 3D generation (Chan et al., 2021; Wang & Torr, 2022; Chan
116
      et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2023; Li et al., 2021), and dynamic scenes (Pumarola et al., 2021; Mildenhall
117   et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023). Few-shot NeRFs (Chibane et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2023a;b; Chen et al.,
118   2022b; Zhang et al., 2021; Jain et al., 2022; Zhou & Tulsiani, 2023; Kim et al., 2022; Bortolon et al.,
119   2022; Lee et al., 2023; Seo et al., 2023b; Kwak et al., 2023) aim to reconstruct from sparse inputs but
120   face overfitting and generalization issues. Some approaches use pre-trained models (Yu et al., 2021;
121   Jain et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023; Niemeyer et al., 2022; Johari et al., 2022; Deng et al., 2023; Chen
122   et al., 2016; Uy et al., 2023), while others introduce regularizations (Yang et al., 2023; Niemeyer
123   et al., 2022; Somraj & Soundararajan, 2023; Deng et al., 2022) to improve performance.
124
      Depth regularizations. Recent works emphasize depth constraints during training. DS-
125   NeRF (Deng et al., 2022) uses sparse SfM-estimated depth, while DDP-NeRF (Roessle et al.,
126   2022) completes it with pretrained priors. SparseNeRF (Wang et al., 2023) uses prediction trans-
127   formers (Ranftl et al., 2021a; 2020) for depth priors. DäRF (Song et al., 2023) jointly optimizes
128   NeRF and MDE, and ReVoRF (Xu et al., 2024) improves geometry without heavy reliance on priors.
129   FSGS (Zhu et al., 2023) uses monocular depth priors and geometric regularization. These methods
130   may be affected by data bias and require substantial data. ViP-NeRF (Somraj & Soundararajan,
131   2023) uses visibility maps but demands significant computation time. In contrast, FrugalNeRF uses
132   geometrically adapted pseudo-GT depth, avoiding pre-trained models and extensive computation.
133
      Novel pose regularization. Novel pose regularization addresses floaters in synthesized views from
134   sparse inputs. RegNeRF (Niemeyer et al., 2022) uses pose sampling with a normalizing flow model.
135   PixelNeRF (Yu et al., 2021) extracts image features with CNNs (Krizhevsky et al., 2012) for scene
136   priors. DietNeRF (Jain et al., 2021) uses CLIP-based Transformers (Radford et al., 2021; Caron
137   et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023) for color consistency. FlipNeRF (Seo et al., 2023a)
138   samples flipped reflection rays but relies on geometry estimation. These methods often depend
139   on pre-trained models, potentially introducing bias and inference time. Our FrugalNeRF applies
140   geometric adaptation on pose rendering, avoiding pre-trained models while suppressing floaters.
141
      Frequency regularization. Positional encoding (Sitzmann et al., 2020; Tancik et al., 2020; Wang
142   et al., 2022) enables NeRF to capture high-frequency details but can lead to overfitting in few-shot
143   scenarios. FreeNeRF (Yang et al., 2023) uses scheduling for increasing input frequency. VGOS (Sun
144   et al., 2023) adopts incremental voxel training to prevent overfitting. Both methods require complex
145   scheduling and may not generalize well. SimpleNeRF (Somraj et al., 2023) introduces augmented
146   models focusing on low-frequency, leading to resource wastage. Our FrugalNeRF leverages weight-
147   sharing voxels across scales for various frequency representations, avoiding complex scheduling.
148
      Fast convergence. NeRF’s time-consuming training due to MLP queries is a common challenge.
149
      Methods like (Sun et al., 2023; 2022; Chen et al., 2022a; Sitzmann et al., 2019) replace MLPs with
150
      faster-converging representations. Instant-NGP (Müller et al., 2022) uses voxels with hash encoding
151   and density bitfield. DVGO (Sun et al., 2022) employs voxel grids with shallow MLP. TensoRF (Chen
152   et al., 2022a) decomposes radiance fields into low-rank tensors. ZeroRF (Shi et al., 2024) adapts
153   TensoRF for few-shot settings but is limited to the object level. Our FrugalNeRF uses TensoRF for
154   fast training and introduces a cross-scale geometric adaptation weight-sharing voxel framework.
155
156
      Self-supervised consistency. Consistency modeling between sparse images and warped counter-
      parts is crucial for Few-shot NeRFs. SinNeRF (Xu et al., 2022) and PANeRF (Ahn et al., 2022)
157
      use warping results as pseudo labels but require RGB-D input. SE-NeRF (Jung et al., 2023) and
158
      Self-NeRF (Bai et al., 2023) use teacher NeRF rendering results as labels, requiring effective initial-
159   ization. GeCoNeRF (Kwak et al., 2023) uses render depth for warping but needs a pre-trained feature
160   extractor. FrugalNeRF combines frequency regularization with cross-scale geometric adaptation,
161   using the best render depth at different scales as a pseudo label to ensure geometric consistency
      without relying on learned priors.

      Voxel-based NeRFs. Voxel-based NeRFs (Sun et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022a; Müller et al., 2022)

      Few-shot NeRFs. Recent methods propose various strategies to address the challenge of under-
210   constrained optimization with limited images. These include regularizing visible frequencies in
211   positional encoding (Yang et al., 2023) (Fig. 2 (a)), expanding voxel ranges incrementally (Sun et al.,

      3.2 OVERVIEW OF F RUGAL N E RF
217
218
      FrugalNeRF introduces an efficient architecture for novel view synthesis from sparse inputs without
      external priors, leveraging voxel-based NeRFs (Chen et al., 2022a; Müller et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2022)
219
      to estimate 3D geometry and reduce training time. Key features include hierarchical subsampling
220
      with weight-sharing multi-scale voxels for diverse geometric details (Sec. 3.3), a geometric adaptation
221   training strategy for few-shot scenarios (Sec. 3.4), novel view sampling with additional regularization
222   losses to minimize artifacts (Sec. 3.5), and integration of data from both training and sampled novel
223   views for robust scene representation (Sec. 3.6).
224
225   3.3 W EIGHT-S HARING M ULTI -S CALE VOXELS
226   Addressing data sparsity in few-shot scenarios, we introduce FrugalNeRF’s weight-sharing multi-
227   scale voxels, which are crucial for balancing frequency characteristics. Inspired by FreeNeRF (Yang
228   et al., 2023), which highlights the overfitting challenges with high-frequency inputs, our system adopts
229   a voxel-based representation to manage frequency components. We employ varied resolution voxels
230
      similar to NeRF’s positional encoding (Mildenhall et al., 2020), with lower resolutions capturing
      broad scene outlines and higher resolutions modeling finer details.
231
232   Unlike methods such as VGOS (Sun et al., 2023), which starts with a coarse geometry and progres-
233   sively refines details, our approach maintains generalization without intricate tuning. We construct
234   multi-scale voxels by downsampling from a single density and appearance voxel, ensuring consistent
235   scene representation(Fig. 3 (c)). This technique effectively balances different frequency bands in the
236
      training pipeline without increasing model size or memory demands.
237   With multi-scale voxels, we can further utilize multi-scale voxel color loss to guide the training (Fig. 3
238   (d)), which is crucial for few-shot scenarios in ensuring a balanced representation of geometry and
239   detail. The multi-scale voxel color loss is defined as:

248   3.4 C ROSS - SCALE GEOMETRIC ADAPTATION
249   Our cross-scale geometric adaptation approach effectively addresses the challenges of few-shot
250   scenarios by supervising geometry without ground truth depth data. Recognizing the diverse frequency
251   representation by different voxel scales in a scene, it is essential to identify the optimal frequency
252   band for each region of the scene.
253   For each ray from a training view i, we compute depth values at multiple scales through volume
254   rendering and then warp (Luo et al., 2020; Kopf et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021) view i’s input RGB to the

256   the most suitable scale for each scene area. The depth of this scale serves as a pseudo-ground truth,
257   guiding the model in maintaining geometric accuracy across frequencies (Fig. 3 (e)).

402   Implementation details. We implement FrugalNeRF based on the TensoRF (Chen et al., 2022a) and
403   utilize the official PyTorch framework. The learning process is driven by an Adam optimizer (Kingma
404   & Ba, 2014), with an initial learning rate of 0.08, which decays to 0.002 throughout the training. We
405   sample 120 novel poses along a spiraling trajectory around the training view and set the batch size for
406   both training and novel view rays to 4,096. We utilize the pre-trained Dense Prediction Transformer
407
      (DPT) (Ranftl et al., 2021b) to generate monocular depth maps from training views. Each scene in
      our model is trained for 5,000 iterations. For different datasets, we use specific voxel resolutions:
408
      6403 for LLFF and RealEstate-10K, and 3003 for the DTU dataset. Additionally, our model employs
409
      a voxel downsample ratio with s = 4, L = 2 (three levels of scale in total) to accommodate varying
410   levels of scene detail. More details can be found in the supplementary materials.

466   Weight-sharing voxels. We compared the performance and memory usage of weight-sharing
467   voxels against three independent voxels. Tab. 4 indicates that weight-sharing not only enhances
468   performance but also reduces the model size.
469
      Multi-scale voxel color loss. We demonstrate the effectiveness of multi-scale voxel color loss
470
      Lms-color by comparing it to using color loss only on the largest scale (Tab. 4, Fig. 9(Left)). Multi-
471
      scale loss improves rendering and geometry by capturing various levels of scene detail. Without

473   for gradually increasing input frequency. Our voxel grid representation offers faster training than
474   MLPs but sacrifices some continuity. The discrete nature of multi-scale voxel grids initially limits
475   our quality compared to FreeNeRF. However, integrating geometric adaptation significantly enhances
476   coherence across scales, effectively overcoming this limitation.

      presses floaters. Fig. 6 (Left) shows that during the training, low-frequency components from the

481
      components gradually increase their proportion of serving as pseudo-ground truth. Therefore, our

      FrugalNeRF could generalize better to diverse scenes without complex training scheduling. Fig. 6

      5     C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we propose FrugalNeRF, a framework that synthesizes novel views with extremely few
531   input views. To speed up and regularize the training, we propose weight-sharing voxel representation
532   across different scales, representing varying frequencies in the scene. To prevent overfitting, we
533   propose a geometric adaptation scheme, utilizing reprojection errors to guide the geometry across
534   different scales both in training and sampled novel views. FrugalNeRF performs on par with existing
535
      state-of-the-art methods on multiple datasets with shorter training time and does not rely on any
      externally learned priors.
536
537   Limitations. Few-shot NeRF relies on accurate camera poses for training. In scenarios with signifi-
538   cant changes in viewpoint or sparse training views, the model may face challenges in generalization.
539   Although our method introduces novel-view losses to deal with those unseen regions in training
      views, it is still an issue for few-shot NeRF.

      A.2   D ISCUSSIONS ON C OMPETING M ODELS
811
812   GeCoNeRF. GeCoNeRF (Kwak et al., 2023) is a few-shot NeRF that uses warped features as
813   pseudo labels, which is sufficiently different from our method. Our method primarily focuses on
814   cross-scale geometric adaptation, selecting render depths with minimal reprojection error across
815   different scales as pseudo labels to adaptively learn the most suitable geometry for each scale. In
816   contrast, GeCoNeRF, besides requiring a pre-trained feature extractor, directly optimizes warped
817   features, making it highly sensitive to geometric noise and resulting in many floaters in its rendering
818
      result as shown in our supplementary videos. Our approach, on the other hand, is more robust due
      to our proposed multi-scale voxels. Low-resolution voxels represent coarse geometry, which is less
819
      likely to produce floaters. Using this as supervision effectively suppresses the generation of floaters.
820
821
      ZeroRF. ZeroRF (Shi et al., 2024) is a concurrent work to ours, also aimed at training NeRF
822
      with sparse input views and achieving fast training times. Unlike TensoRF (Chen et al., 2022a),
823
      which directly optimizes the decomposed feature grid, ZeroRF parameterizes the feature grids with
824   a randomly initialized deep neural network (generator). This decision is based on the belief in the
825   higher resilience to noise and artifacts ability of deep neural networks. Although ZeroRF claims
826   to achieve fast convergence stemming from its voxel representation, the need to train the generator
827   results in slower training speeds compared to ours (refer to the main paper Table 2). Our method
828   directly optimizes the feature grid and utilizes cross-scale geometry adaptation to avoid overfitting
829   under sparse views, without requiring a generator that slows down convergence to form decomposed
830   tensorial feature volumes. Additionally, we found that ZeroRF is not suitable for scenes with a
831   background (e.g., LLFF (Mildenhall et al., 2019a)) or datasets like the DTU (Jensen et al., 2014)
832
      Dataset, where ZeroRF must extensively use object masks for training. These object masks are not
      provided directly in these two datasets. Otherwise, ZeroRF may produce many artifacts and floaters,
833
      or the feature volume may be filled up to fit the background, leading to severe memory consumption
834
      issues causing training failures due to out-of-memory errors.
835
836
      SparseNeRF. SparseNeRF (Wang et al., 2023) proposes a spatial continuity regularization that
837
      distills depth continuity priors, but it requires a pre-trained depth prior and is extremely slow by
838   using MLP representation. Additionally, because monocular depth prediction results lack detail,
839   SparseNeRF’s rendered results tend to be blurry and lack detail. In contrast, our proposed cross-
840   scale geometric adaptation does not rely on pre-trained priors and ensures the generation of overall
841   geometry while paying attention to details.
842
843   SimpleNeRF. SimpleNeRF (Somraj et al., 2023) introduces a data augmentation method for few-
844   shot NeRF, employing an MLP with fewer positional encoding frequencies for augmentation, but this
845   simultaneously increases the training time. In contrast, we propose an efficient cross-scale geometric
846   adaptation that achieves multi-scale representation through shared-weight voxels, eliminating the
847   need for an additional model to reconstruct the same scene. This approach yields better results with
848   lower costs.
849
850   FreeNeRF. FreeNeRF (Yang et al., 2023) is an MLP-based few-shot NeRF model. FreeNeRF
851   proposes using a scheduling mechanism to gradually increase input frequency, allowing the model
852
      to learn low-frequency geometry during the early stages of training and then ramp up positional
      encoding to enable the model to learn more detailed geometry later on. However, our approach
853
      takes advantage of the explicit voxel representation, which converges faster and allows for direct
854
      cross-scaled geometry operations. Additionally, because we employ cross-scale geometry adaptation,
855   our model dynamically determines which frequency of geometry to learn at different training stages.
856   We do not require the complex frequency scheduling of FreeNeRF, nor are we limited to learning only
857   high-frequency components in the later stages of training like FreeNeRF. This makes our method
858   simpler, more general, and more robust.
859
860   VGOS. VGOS (Sun et al., 2023) introduces an incremental voxel training strategy and a voxel
861   smoothing method for Few-shot NeRF, aimed at reducing training time. It employs a complex
862   scheduling strategy to freeze the outer part of the voxel, leading to a leaky reconstruction of the
863   background scene. Additionally, VGOS requires ground truth poses for novel pose sampling, which
      results in a quality drop when using random sampling. However, while VGOS’s training time is


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      shorter than ours, its performance significantly lags behind. Our cross-scale geometric adaptation
865   strategy eliminates the need for complex scheduling and ground truth pose sampling.
866
867   FSGS. FSGS (Zhu et al., 2023) addresses the challenge of limited 3D Gaussian splatting (3DGS)
868   by introducing Proximity-guided Gaussian Unpooling, which adaptively densifies the Gaussians
869   between existing points. Although this method mitigates the issue of insufficient GS, it still relies on
870   a sufficient initial set of Gaussians to perform effectively. In few-shot scenarios, the initial number of
871   GS can be extremely sparse, leading to suboptimal results. Furthermore, FSGS frequently requires
872   novel view inference using monocular depth models during training, which significantly increases the
873   training time. In contrast, our cross-scale geometric adaptation approach ensures rapid convergence
874   without relying on novel view inference or monocular depth models, providing efficient and robust
875
      performance even with minimal initial data.
