009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Single image face relighting is the challenging problem of estimating the illumi-
012            nation cast on images by a point light source varying in position, intensity and
013            possibly colour. Learning the relationship between the light source properties
014            and the face location is critical to the photo-realism of the estimated relit im-
015            age. Prior works do not explicitly model this relationship which adversely affects
016            the accuracy and photo-realism of the estimated relit image. We present a novel
017            framework that explicitly models this relationship by integrating a novel light fea-
018            ture embedding with self-attention and cross attention layers in a custom image
019
               relighting network. Our proposed method estimates more photo-realistic relit im-
               ages with accurate shadows and outperforms prior works despite being trained
020
               only on synthetic data. Our method is able to generalize to out-of-training light
021
               source positions and also achieves unsupervised adaptation from synthetic to real
022            images.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Single image face relighting is the complex problem of changing the illumination in the source
028
      image according to a given target light direction. It is an active area of research in computer vision
029
      and has applications in various domains such as face recognition (Le & Kakadiaris, 2019; Huang
030   et al., 2020; Qing et al., 2004), background lighting transfer (Nestmeyer et al., 2020; Pandey et al.,
031   2021) and image editing Li et al. (2018); Luan et al. (2017); Shih et al. (2014).
032
033
      Two crucial aspects of face relighting are accurate rendering of shadows and preserving of facial
      details. A popular approach for single image face relighting is to rely on the estimated image intrin-
034
      sics such as albedo and surface normal for relighting the image (Zhou et al., 2019; Hou et al., 2021;
035
      2022). Additional information such as shadow masks (Hou et al., 2021) or face geometry (Hou
036   et al., 2022) can be used to improve accuracy of estimated shadows. While these methods work
037   well, the estimated relit images are not photo-realistic since the models have been trained on the
038   inexact ground truth images in the DPR dataset (Zhou et al., 2019), and they estimate only the lu-
039   minance channel & append colour channels from the input image. Some have tried to address these
040   limitations by training on a new relighting dataset in a two-stage pipeline (Pidaparthy et al., 2024).
041   While the shadows are sharp, they are often not photo-realistic.
042   None of the prior works explicitly addressed the fundamental challenge of image relighting, which is
043   learning the complex relationship between light source information and face location & orientation.
044   Ponglertnapakorn et al. (2023) tried to implicitly learn this relationship using the prior knowledge
045   of large diffusion models, however their approach significantly increased the computation cost and
046   inference time. Further, prior works only explore relighting with white colour light and lack a
047   method to incorporate light colour.
048   To address these limitations, we designed a novel lighting embedding that allows joint modelling of
049   the light source properties, which are the position, colour and intensity. We propose a novel lighting
050   network that enables the model to learn the relationship between light source position, intensity and
051   colour. This representative light feature is then combined with image features in a residual convo-
052   lutional autoencoder. By integrating self-attention and cross-attention layers at multiple resolutions,
053   the network allows for learning the relationship between light features and image features. These
      combined features are passed to a residual decoder which estimates the relit image. The pipeline

      of our proposed image relighting network is shown in Fig 1. Through extensive experimental vali-
055   dation, we show that our method outperforms other SOTA methods and that our model generalizes
056   very well to out-of-training light source properties. Additionally, we are able to achieve unsuper-
057   vised adaptation from synthetic to real images.

090   2   P RIOR W ORK
091
092   There are broadly four types of approaches explored for face relighting: 1) intrinsic image de-
093   composition and rendering (Barron & Malik, 2014; Egger et al., 2018; Genova et al., 2018; Le &
094   Kakadiaris, 2019; Lee & Lee, 2020; Lee et al., 2005; Li et al., 2014; Lin et al., 2020; Nestmeyer
095   et al., 2020; Sengupta et al., 2018; Shahlaei & Blanz, 2015; Shu et al., 2017; Tewari et al., 2017;
096   Tran et al., 2019; Tran & Liu, 2018; 2019; Wang et al., 2008; Yamaguchi et al., 2018), 2) image-to-
097   image translation (Atoum et al., 2020; Tewari et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2019), 3) style
098
      transfer (Li et al., 2018; Luan et al., 2017; Shih et al., 2014; Shu et al., 2017; Pandey et al., 2021;
      Ponglertnapakorn et al., 2023; Yeh et al., 2022) and 4) ratio image estimation (Peers et al., 2007;
099
      Shashua & Riklin-Raviv, 2001; Wen et al., 2003; Stoschek, 2000).
100
101   Given an input image and light source position, the intrinsic image decomposition methods aim to
102   estimate components such as albedo, surface normal, reflectance and lighting. These components
103   are then used to render a relit image based on the light source position. The effectiveness of these
104
      methods heavily depends on the accuracy of each intrinsic map and a cascading of the estimation
      errors results in relit images that lack high frequency details and may contain artefacts.
105
106   To address the issue of cascading errors, some approaches have framed relighting as an image-to-
107   image translation task. Although these methods can estimate relit images with varying accuracy (Sun
      et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2019), they struggle with inaccurate shadow predictions and adapting to


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      different light intensities. Another approach to relighting is image style transfer (Li et al., 2018;
109   Luan et al., 2017), where both source and references images are provided as inputs and the lighting
110   of the reference image is transferred to the source image. Some have explored using environment
111   lighting maps of a reference image to transfer lighting to a source image (Pandey et al., 2021;
112   Ponglertnapakorn et al., 2023; Yeh et al., 2022). However, these approaches require high-quality
113   non-occluded source and reference image pairs with diverse lighting variations, and ground truth
114   environment lighting maps which can be expensive to obtain. In contrast, our method does not
115   require any reference images or environment maps and can relight an image for any given light
116   source position.
117   Some approaches have focused on learning a per-pixel multiplier map by estimating the ratio be-
118   tween the source and target images. However, these approaches require multiple input images (Peers
119   et al., 2007; Shashua & Riklin-Raviv, 2001) or both source and target images (Stoschek, 2000) at
120   inference, which limits their suitability for real-world applications.
121   Accurate estimation of shadows is critical to face relighting. Towards this end, some methods learn
122   a weightage function on the estimated shadow mask (Hou et al., 2021), while some others have used
123   ray-tracing to estimate the shadows approaches have explored the utility of a shadow pixels (Hou
124   et al., 2022). While these methods estimate shadows with reasonable accuracy, the relit images
125   often lack photo-realism due to hard boundaries in the shadow regions, whereas real shadows are
126   softer and have diffused edges. Some have tried to overcome these limitations by improving the
127   quality of the training dataset and combing attention features at multiple scales (Pidaparthy et al.,
128   2024), however the estimated relit images has colour artefact issues and the performance suffers on
129
      input images with existing shadows. Another approach tried to leveraged the knowledge of large
      diffusion models (Ponglertnapakorn et al., 2023), but this method comes at a significant increase
130
      in computational costs and inference time. Also, several pre-processing blocks further increase the
131
      inference time.
132
133   We address several limitations of the prior work and propose a lightweight network architecture for
134   fast inference on edge devices. We believe that incorporating light colour can improve the model per-
135
      formance. Hence, we design a novel light embedding that allows the model to learn the correlation
      between light source position, intensity and colour. Next, we design a novel network architecture
136
      that explicitly learns the relationship between the light source information and face location & ori-
137
      entation. This enables the model to accurately learn the face illumination and shadow strength, and
138   thus, estimate a photo-realistic relit image.

143   We obtained training and test datasets from Pidaparthy et al. (2024). We made a few important mod-
144   ifications to their dataset preparation strategy that significantly improved the quality of the ground
145   truth dataset.
146   We believe that incorporating light colour information during training can improve the illumination
147   on the face and reduce the colour artefacts in estimated relit images. Since there are no publicly
148   available relighting datasets with coloured lighting, we generated our own dataset using the syn-
149   thetic OLAT lighting rig in Blender software (Pidaparthy et al., 2024). Our dataset consisted of 7
150   maximally separated light colours: White (255, 255, 255), Red (255, 0, 0), Green (0, 255, 0), Blue
151   (0, 0, 255), Yellow (255, 255, 0), Magenta (255, 0, 255) and Cyan (0, 255, 255). Sample images
152
      from the training dataset are seen in Fig 2.
153   Next, we manually corrected the offset of the 3D models and aligned the face location all of models
154   to be the same in the synthetic lighting rig. This ensured uniformity when augmenting the training
155   dataset through rotation or displacement of the subject positions (3D models), which made it easier
156   for the model to learn the relationship between light source information and face location & orienta-
157   tion. Additionally, we also modified the 3D models to have less reflective surfaces and cast sharper
158
      shadows. This significantly improved the photo-realism of the ground truth relit images.
159   The light source properties are represented as a 7D tuple of (x, y, z, i, r, g, b) where (x, y, z), i and
160   (r, g, b) refers to the light position, intensity and colour, respectively. Using the same strategy as that
161   in Pidaparthy et al. (2024), we sample the light position from a unit volume such that x ∈ [−1, +1],

188   4     I MAGE R ELIGHTING N ETWORK
189
190   A key challenge in face relighting is precisely estimating the illumination and shadows cast on the
191   face by a light source. This involves learning the complex relationship between the face location &
192   orientation and the light source properties. In this section, we describe our novel lightweight image
193   relighting network (Fig1) that achieves two goals: 1) accurate estimation of shadows and illumina-
194   tion on the face and 2) efficient inference on edge devices. To achieve this, we propose a novel light
195   embedding that jointly models the light source properties. We train a lighting network that learns the
196
      correlation between the light source position, intensity and colour. These embeddings are combined
      with image features, learnt using a residual convolutional autoencoder, in cross-attention blocks that
197
      capture the relationship between light and face information. The combined features are then passed
198
      to a decoder to generate a photo-realistic relit image.

      4.1   L IGHTING N ETWORK
202
      Most prior work encoded only the light source position using a 9-dimensional Spherical Harmonics
203
      (SH) vector, which captures the maximum variance of the light source position moving along a unit
204   sphere (Zhou et al., 2019; Hou et al., 2021; 2022; Ponglertnapakorn et al., 2023). More recent works
205   have appended light source intensity to obtain a 10-D SH vector (Pidaparthy et al., 2024). The SH
206   vector is passed to a lighting network to obtain the light features.
207
      Although SH vectors are helpful for handling variations in the light positions across a sphere, they
208
      have some limitations. The SH vector models only the light source position. Even when intensity
209
      is appended, they fail to capture the correlation between the light source position and intensity. The
210   illumination on an image depends on both position and intensity since a high-intensity light farther
211   from the face and low-intensity light closer to the face might cast similar illuminations. Our initial
212   experiments showed that the SH vector might not be adequate to model the wider range of variations
213   in light source positions and intensities. Additionally, SH vector does not account for light colour,
214   which we believe is crucial for improving the accuracy of illumination on the face and reducing the
215   colour artefacts. This necessitates a more comprehensive light embedding that jointly models the
      light source properties and provides a rich representation of the correlation between them.

219   formers (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Vaswani et al., 2017).

      4.4   T RAINING D ETAILS
361
362   Unlike prior works, we do not estimate any image instrinsic map nor do we use any additional
363   networks during training. Our single-stage pipeline is able to estimate photo-realistic relit images
364   with high accuracy and achieve unsupervised domain adaptation from synthetic to real images. We
365   achieve this using specific data augmentation techniques while preparing the training dataset.
366   We generating the training dataset, we randomly apply left-right rotation on the 3D model of
367   r ∈ [−60, +60] degrees. We also vary the position of the 3D model w.r.t the camera by a dis-
368   placement of dx ∈ [−0.3, +0.3] and dz ∈ [−0.3, +0.3] along X and Z directions, respectively.
369   Further, we used two different types of data augmentation during training: 1) image flipping and 2)
370   brightness and contrast jitter. The image is flipped horizontally (left-right mirroring) and the light
371   source position is appropriately updated. The brightness and contrast of the RGB image are adjusted
372   by an additive factor of b and multiplicative factor of c, where b ∈ [−20, +20] and c ∈ [0.8, 1.2].
373   These augmentations result in a training dataset that captures multiple different variations of the
374
      input images and thus, improves the generalization of the model to real images.
375   The image relighting network was trained on a dataset of 21,000 images and validated on 3,000
376   images. The input data to the residual convolutional autoencoder consisted of 4-channels - RGB
377   image (IRGB ) and luminance channel (IL ), which were resized to 512 × 512 pixels. The input to
      the lighting network was the light features embedding computed on the target light source 4D tuple.


                                                          7

      The training loss was optimized using Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014) with L2 regularization
379   of 0.01. The initial learning rate of 1e − 5 was decayed by a factor of 0.9 after each epoch and the
380   network was trained for 30 epochs with a batch size of 8. In a manner similar to all prior works, we
381   also work with the segmented foreground region. We used the segmentation masks obtained using
382   the pre-trained Mask R-CNN (He et al., 2017) model in PyTorch.

513   We have shown the effectiveness of our methods for accurately modelling shadows and generating
514   photo-realistic relit images. However, there are some limitations of the model. Currently, a seg-
515
      mentation mask is used at inference to segment the foreground object (human) which is passed as
      input to the model. One possible future extension of this work is to automatically localize the face
516
      in the input image and relight the image without using any foreground segmentation mask. Another
517
      possible extension is to capture the effect of multiple light sources by modelling their effect as a
518   single composite light source.

521   7    C ONCLUSION
522
523   We proposed a novel approach for face relighting given a single image and a light source position.
524   We used a novel light embedding that jointly modelled the light source position, intensity and colour.
525
      The network enables learning the correlation between these parameters. We used cross-attention
      layers in the convolutional autoencoder to explicitly learn the relationship between the light source
526
      properties and the face location. Qualitative results show the benefit of various design choices and
527
      our model generates photo-realistic relit images. Our model is also able to easily generalize across
528   multiple different coloured lighting. Quantitative analysis showed that our model outperforms SOTA
529   methods on two challenging real image datasets. Our model is lightweight and has only 9.4M
530   parameters.
