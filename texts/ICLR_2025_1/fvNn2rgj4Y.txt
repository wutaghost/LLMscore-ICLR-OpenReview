011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We propose a noise schedule that ensures a constant rate of change in the prob-
014            ability distribution of diffused data throughout the diffusion process. To obtain
015
               this noise schedule, we measure the rate of change in the probability distribution
               of the forward process and use it to determine the noise schedule before train-
016
               ing diffusion models. The functional form of the noise schedule is automatically
017
               determined and tailored to each dataset and type of diffusion model. We evalu-
018            ate the effectiveness of our noise schedule on unconditional and class-conditional
019            image generation tasks using the LSUN (bedroom/church/cat/horse), ImageNet,
020            and FFHQ datasets. Through extensive experiments, we confirmed that our noise
021            schedule broadly improves the performance of the diffusion models regardless of
022            the dataset, sampler, number of function evaluations, or type of diffusion model.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Image generation is one of the most challenging tasks in computer vision, and a variety of deep gen-
      erative models have been proposed. Generative adversarial networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al.,
028
      2014) have long been the leading models for high-quality image generation. These generative mod-
029
      els achieved success across a wide range of fields beyond image generation, such as audio (van den
030   Oord et al., 2016; Kong et al., 2021) and 3D-point cloud generation (Yang et al., 2019).
031
032   The performance of generative models is measured using three metrics: sampling speed, sample
033
      quality, and mode coverage (Xiao et al., 2022). Despite the extensive research conducted, satisfying
      these requirements simultaneously is challenging. For example, GANs have strengths in terms of
034
      sampling speed and sample quality, but exhibit a weakness in mode coverage.
035
036   Denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs) (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020) are
037   new generative models, that achieve high sample quality and mode coverage. DDPMs are latent
038   variable models that use a Markov chain to gradually add Gaussian noise to the data, called the
039
      forward process. At the end of the forward process, the data are completely destroyed to pure noise.
      The reverse process is introduced to restore the data from the pure noise by tracing the forward
040
      process in the reverse direction. The reverse process uses a Markov chain as well, and a deep neural
041
      network is trained to sequentially remove the added noise in latent variables. Although the annealing
042   nature of DDPMs contributes to the high mode coverage, the iterative denoising steps result in slow
043   sampling speed. The number of steps in the reverse process is set to as much as one thousand, as
044   described in Ho et al. (2020).
045
      In this work, to reduce the number of steps in the reverse process, we propose a noise schedule
046
      that ensures a constant rate of change in the probability distribution of diffused data throughout
047
      the diffusion process, which we refer to as CRS. As previously stated, the reverse process needs to
048   accurately trace the forward process to restore the data from pure noise. This motivates us to search
049   for noise schedules to enhance the traceability of the forward process. Figure 1 depicts a toy example
050   of diffused data distributions with three data points in one-dimensional data space (see Appendix A
051   for details). In this example, the diffusion process can be divided into three regions. The first is an
052   area where the probability distributions hardly change, and the noise level can be changed rapidly
053   (α ≲ 0.6). The second is an area where three modes corresponding to the data points emerge, and
      careful adjustment of the noise level is necessary to enhance mode coverage (0.6 ≲ α ≲ 0.97).

      The third is an area where the peaks of each mode become distinct, and the noise level should be
077   changed slowly for sample fidelity (α ≳ 0.97). As evident from this example, the diffusion process
078   exhibits regions with rapid and slow changes in the probability distributions. To obtain CRS, we
079   measure the rate of change in the probability distribution of the forward process, which we assume
080   represents the traceability. We then determine the noise schedule so that the rate of change remains
081   constant throughout the forward process before training diffusion models. The functional form of
082   CRS is automatically determined for ensuring the constancy of probability-distributional change.
083   Therefore, it is not necessary to pre-define the noise-schedule function, such as linear (Ho et al.,
084   2020) and cosine (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021). CRS is considered a generalized version of Song &
085
      Ermon (2020), which determines the noise schedule so that a constant overlap between consecutive
      probability distributions in the forward process is achieved. Through extensive experiments, we
086
      conclude that CRS broadly improves the performance of image generation in diffusion models.
087
088   We make the following contributions:
089
090        1. We propose an efficient noise schedule for training and sampling, which we refer to as
091           CRS, to boost the performance of diffusion models. CRS enables us to automatically derive
092           a noise schedule tailored to the target dataset and type of diffusion model.
093
094
           2. CRS holds significant value as a general framework for customizing noise schedules. Once
              an appropriate distance metric for measuring the probability-distributional change is de-
095
              fined, the corresponding noise schedule is systematically obtained.
096
097
           3. We find that the efficient noise schedule depends on both the target dataset and the type of
098           diffusion model. In particular, the efficient noise schedule in latent-space diffusion models
099           is very different from conventionally used ones.
100
101        4. Through extensive experiments, we confirm that CRS broadly improves the performance of
102           image generation regardless of the dataset, sampler, number of function evaluations (NFE),
103           or type of diffusion model.
104
105   The rest of this paper is organized as follows: Section 2 explains related work on reducing the
106   number of steps in image generation. Section 3 briefly introduces diffusion models. Section 4
107   presents CRS. Section 5 presents the experimental results to confirm the superiority of CRS. Section
      6 presents a discussion, and Section 7 concludes this paper.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Various methods have been proposed to reduce the number of steps in the reverse process. Most
111   methods can be categorized into four strategies: conditional generation, data dimensionality reduc-
112   tion, samplers, and noise schedules.
113
114
      Conditional generation: This strategy reduces the number of steps by eliminating unnecessary
      sample diversity. The attributes of the samples to be generated are specified through conditioning,
115
      such as class and text (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Ho & Salimans, 2021; Rombach et al., 2022;
116
      Saharia et al., 2022; Nichol et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2023).
117
118   Dimensionality reduction of data: The latent diffusion model (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2022;
119   Podell et al., 2023) utilizes a pre-trained autoencoder to reduce the dimensionality of the data, and
120
      diffusion models are applied to the reduced data space. This strategy not only reduces the number
      of necessary steps but also decreases the processing time per step.
121
122   Samplers: This strategy devises update rules for latent variables in the reverse process. The De-
123   noising Diffusion Implicit Model (DDIM) (Song et al., 2021a) introduces a non-Markov chain to
124   generalize DDPMs and proposes a deterministic sampler. From the perspective of score-based
125   matching, Song & Ermon (2019; 2020); Song et al. (2021b) developed continuous-time diffusion
126
      models. In one of these studies (Song et al., 2021b), the time evolution of latent variables was for-
      mulated using a stochastic differential equation (SDE) and an ordinary differential equation (ODE).
127
      They also proposed using general solvers of the SDE and ODE for sampling in diffusion models.
128
      Subsequent studies have actively investigated customizing these general solvers for diffusion models
129   (Jolicoeur-Martineau et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2022a;b).
130
131   Noise schedule: Noise schedules significantly affect the performance of diffusion models (Chen,
132
      2023). Many studies have proposed noise schedules (Ho et al., 2020; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021;
      Chen, 2023), and Hoogeboom et al. (2023) proposed modifying noise schedules depending on the
133
      image resolution to be generated. However, the functional form in conventional studies, such as
134
      linear (Song et al., 2021a) and cosine (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021), is somewhat arbitrary, and it re-
135   mains unclear whether they are appropriately set. The most relevant work to ours is AYS (Sabour
136   et al., 2024), which determines a noise schedule to minimize the KL divergence between the dis-
137   tributions of real data and generated samples. Although AYS enables us to obtain noise schedules
138   tailored to the target dataset, sampler, NFE, and trained diffusion model, there are two shortcom-
139   ings. First, AYS cannot be used as a noise schedule for training because a trained diffusion model
140   is required to optimize noise schedules. Second, AYS is not applicable to several samplers, such as
141   the deterministic sampler proposed in EDM (Karras et al., 2022).
142   In our work, we propose an efficient noise schedule for both training and sampling, which is appli-
143   cable to any sampler.

191   4     P ROPOSED N OISE S CHEDULE
192
193
      In this section, we explain our motivation and present our noise schedule.
194
195   4.1   M OTIVATION
196
      As stated in Section 3, it is a good approximation to assume pθ (xt−1 |xt ) as a Gaussian distribution
197
      if T is sufficiently large. Note that ∆αt ≡ αt − αt−1 is sufficiently small when T ≫ 1. In principle,
198   when refining the noise schedule, one should consider maximizing ∆αt as much as possible without
199   breaking the aforementioned approximation. It is also conjectured that the threshold of ∆αt , where
200   the above approximation begins to break down, will vary depending on αt . (Otherwise, the noise
201   schedule that linearly decreases α would always be the most efficient, but experimental results thus
202   far do not support this.) Therefore, we need to appropriately control ∆αt when reducing the number
203   of timesteps.
204   Song & Ermon (2020) proposed an alternative approach from the perspective of score matching.
205   They considered an extremely simple case in which the dataset contains only one data point. The
206   noise schedule is then determined so that consecutive probability distributions in the forward process
207   have consistent overlap. Although this simple setting enables the analytical derivation of the noise
208   schedule, the probability distribution of the target dataset is not considered.
209
      We extend Song & Ermon (2020)’s idea and develop a generalized noise schedule. Similar to the
210   score-based generative model (Song & Ermon, 2019) inspired by simulated annealing (Kirkpatrick
211   et al., 1983), our formulation draws inspiration from the adiabatic theorem (Morita & Nishimori,
212   2008) in quantum annealing (Kadowaki & Nishimori, 1998). The adiabatic theorem formulates
213   the traceability of the instantaneous probability distribution (ground state), and a noise schedule is
214   devised on the basis of this traceability (Roland & Cerf, 2002). In quantum annealing, it is well
215   known that the noise level must be changed slowly near phase-transition points, where macroscopic
      statistical quantities undergo significant changes.

253   4.3   D ISTANCE M ETRICS
254
255   We can use arbitrary distance metrics to obtain CRS. However, due to the high-dimensional nature of
256   images, it is empirically known that accurately evaluating the probability distributions in pixel space
257
      is challenging (Lopez-Paz & Oquab, 2017). In the field of image generation, one of the most widely
      used metrics is the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel et al., 2017). The FID calculates the
258
      Fréchet distance under a normality assumption in the vision-relevant feature space embedded by the
259
      Inception-V3 model, where the dimension of the feature space is much smaller than that of pixel
260   space. In this study, as an initial attempt, we adopt the FID as a distance metric for computing
261   D̃(α, α′ ). We should note that the FID is not necessarily the optimal distance metric for CRS, and
262   it remains as future work to identify the most suitable one.

500   6   D ISCUSSION
501
502   Through extensive experiments, we confirmed that CRS broadly improves the performance of dif-
503   fusion models regardless of the dataset, sampler, NFE, or type of diffusion model. However, CRS
504
      has room for improvement in the selection of the distance metric. In this work, we adopt FID as
      the distance metric, which requires a feature model. We acknowledge that the feature models used
505
      in this work have the following two issues. The first is that, since the feature models are trained on
506
      ImageNet classification, there are concerns about whether they can accurately capture the features
507   of the target datasets. The second is that, as the feature models have not been exposed to diffused
508   images during training, they cannot correctly capture the changes in the probability distributions in
509   the forward process. We expect that self-supervised learning is one of the candidate approaches for
510   obtaining better feature models. Exploration of feature models and other distance metrics remains
511   as future work. By using a more appropriate distance metric, CRS might also be applicable as a
512   training schedule in pixel-space diffusion models.
513   CRS in the latent-space diffusion model significantly differs from conventional noise schedules. The
514   most remarkable difference is the rate of change in α(t) within low-noise regions. In pixel-space
515   diffusion models, a significant number of timesteps are consumed in regions with low noise. It is
516   presumed that, in the pixel-space diffusion models, perceptual expressions are generated in these
517   low-noise regions. On the other hand, in latent-space diffusion models, the autoencoder is respon-
518   sible for generating perceptual expressions, indicating that there is no need to allocate excessive
519   resources to regions with low-noise levels.
520
521   7   C ONCLUSION
522
523   We proposed CRS, which ensures a constant rate of change in the probability distribution through-
524   out the diffusion process. CRS does not require a pre-defined functional form of the noise schedule;
525   instead, the functional form tailored to the target dataset and type of diffusion model is systemat-
526   ically derived. In addition, CRS can be applied not only to the sampling schedule combined with
527   arbitrary samplers but also to the training schedule. Through extensive experiments, we confirmed
528   that CRS broadly improves the performance of diffusion models. The most significant challenge
529   with CRS is establishing a method for measuring the distance between probability distributions in
530
      the forward process. By identifying a more appropriate distance metric, greater improvements may
      be achieved. Furthermore, CRS is applicable beyond image generation, such as audio and 3D-point
531
      cloud generation, if we can appropriately define the distance. We aim to address this in future work.
