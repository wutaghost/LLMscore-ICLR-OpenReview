009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this past year, large language models (LLMs) have had remarkable success in
012            domains outside the traditional natural language processing, and people are start-
013            ing to explore the usage of LLMs in more general and close to application do-
014            mains like code generation, travel planning, and robot controls. Connecting these
015            LLMs with great capacity and external tools, people are building the so-called
016            LLM agents, which are supposed to help people do all kinds of work in everyday
017            life. In all these domains, the prompt to the LLMs has been shown to make a
018
               big difference in what the LLM would generate and thus affect the performance
               of the LLM agents. Therefore, automatic prompt engineering (APE) has become
019
               an important question for many researchers and users of LLMs. However, previ-
020
               ous works in APE all rely on a final checker to evaluate the performance of the
021            given prompt, which is hard to meet in the case of LLM agents where intermediate
022            feedback is easier to get, and the final evaluation could be expensive, inaccurate,
023            or even missing. In this paper, we propose a novel method, R E P ROMPT, which
024            does a “gradient descent”-like approach to optimize the step-by-step instructions
025            in the prompts given to LLM agents, based on the chat history obtained from in-
026            teractions and reflections with LLM agents. By leveraging intermediate feedback,
027            R E P ROMPT can optimize the prompt without the need for a final solution checker.
028            We have used experiments in PDDL generation and travel planning to show that
029
               our method could generally improve the performance for different reasoning tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Large language models (LLMs) have won significant success since the release of ChatGPT (OpenAI,
034
      2022). In addition to traditional natural language tasks like summarization and sentiment analysis,
035
      LLMs have been shown to be effective in many domains that are closer to applications like code
036   generation (Chen et al., 2023; Roziere et al., 2023), human-computer interaction (Li et al., 2023)
037   and math problem solving (Wei et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2024). While pure LLMs are limited in their
038   reasoning capability (Sun et al., 2023; Valmeekam et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024a), researchers
039   have introduced tool-use to LLMs and built integrated systems, namely LLM agents, to enable the
040   possibility of using LLM in even more general domains like robot controls (Wang et al., 2023a) and
041   autonomous driving (Mao* et al., 2023).
042   Behind all of these successes, prompts are playing an important role. It has been shown that differ-
043   ent prompts could lead to completely different success rates (Wei et al., 2022), and hence prompt
044   engineering is often needed for each specific task. Because prompt engineering is difficult and
045   time-consuming, automatic prompt engineering (APE) has emerged as a strategy, where the LLMs
046   themselves write in the clear language they prefer (Zhou et al., 2023). With a limited number of
047   trials, APE can efficiently converge to a robust prompt that performs better than a simple prompt on
048   traditional natural language processing tasks (Zhou et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023).
049   However, in complex reasoning tasks, APE is still under-studied and most users still use primitive
050   prompts or carefully hand-crafted prompts in their LLM. In complex reasoning tasks, also known as
051   LLM agents, the performance of a prompt—measured by the solution’s success rate—can be much
052   more costly compared to question-answering (QA) tasks, which previous APE methods mainly focus
053   on. The exact evaluation of the task could either require human experts to spend a long-time to
      evaluate, or when collecting chat history online, users will try to provide feedback in the middle

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
109
110
      Our work lies in the intersection of prompt optimization and LLM for reasoning.
111
112   In prompt optimization, many works proposed to optimize the prompt using differentiable tuning on
113   soft prompts (Lester et al., 2021; Qin & Eisner, 2021), train auxiliary models as the optimizer (Hao
114   et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023), or directly train the prompter themselves (Wang
115
      et al., 2023b). This line of work requires access to the model weights of the language models and is
      not generally applicable in the current era of using LLMs like GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023a) and Claude-3
116
      (Anthropic, 2024) through APIs. Another line of work chooses to use machine learning models to
117
      provide the approximated guidance on what prompt is better, either by using reinforcement learning
118   (Shin et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b) or doing discrete manipulation with LLM
119   feedbacks (Guo et al., 2023). There are also some other works in prompt optimization that propose
120   a relatively general solution, like using beam search or Monte Carlo tree search as the ”gradient
121   descent” optimizer (Pryzant et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). Our work is very
122   close to the second group of work, and can be seen as a generalization from the current methods
123   to domains that are more on reasoning domains. However, in our settings, we do not necessarily
124   require ground-truth feedback that checks the general performance of the current prompt, which is
125   more aligned with the potential applications of prompting for LLM agents in scenarios like GPTs
126
      OpenAI (2023b).
127   In LLM for reasoning, a key challenge identified by researchers is how to correctly use prompts
128   to guide the LLM to generate useful auxiliary output that leads to a good final solution. Chain-
129   of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022) is the most commonly used prompt that can improve the per-
130   formance of LLMs that consists of simply adding a fixed sentence of ”Let’s think step by step.”.
131   Later on, Tree-of-thought (Yao et al., 2023a) and Graph of Thoughts (Besta et al., 2024) are also
132
      proposed as an extension from the simple line-based architecture of auxiliary output to tree and
      graph-structured output. Orthogonally, researchers have also found that utilizing the interaction ca-
133
      pability of LLMs could also improve their performance. Yao et al. (2023b) proposed R E ACT by
134
      letting the LLMs list some thoughts before proposing the actual actions. R EFLEXION (Shinn et al.,
135   2023) prompts an LLM agent to reflect on the actions, and save the reflections in the memory to
136   improve the efficiency. There are extensive amounts of works like self-refine (Madaan et al., 2023),
137   RCI (Kim et al., 2023), and self-debugging (Chen et al., 2023) that use similar ideas of providing
138   feedback as guidance in the reasoning-related task and strategically adapt the idea to fit the needs of
139   specific domains. Our work considers this popular and useful workflow of iterating with feedback
140   before finalizing the answer and optimizes the prompt based on the interaction history provided.
141   This approach helps to avoid early-stage errors and, more importantly, reduces the randomness ob-
142   served in methods like ReAct and Reflexion, where specific scenarios may not receive adequate
143
      feedback. By summarizing common issues, such as budget balancing, REPROMPT ensures these
      are addressed in the prompt, allowing ReAct and Reflexion to focus on case-specific issues, like
144
      unexpected price spikes for hotels in certain cities on particular days.

149   3.1   P RELIMINARY ON LLM AGENTS
150
151   In this paper, we consider the problem of LLM agents for reasoning tasks. Because LLM might not
152   be correct in the first shot, in certain scenarios where feedback is still possible, prior approaches have
153
      proposed allowing a few interactions before the final answer is given by the LLM (Yao et al., 2023b;
      Shinn et al., 2023). In these cases, users provide the LLMs with some error information and let the
154
      LLMs try again with the additional information. This information does not necessarily provide any
155
      hint about the final solution but is an error message about why the current solution is not correct.
156   For example, this information could be a typical Python runtime error message in code generation
157   tasks. How to use specific prompts with error messages would mainly depend on different tasks. For
158   example, in the widely used R E ACT (Yao et al., 2023b), LLMs are required to provide thoughts on
159   the current results before doing the next round, and these thoughts are often not a concrete error but
160   may also include a short analysis that reaches a conclusion that the current action is good. However,
161   due to the difficulty of LLM-agents tasks, checking how good a given prompt is could be expensive
      and not able to be used regularly in the optimization process. In the closest application available


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      right now, the GPTs, users normally leave without telling LLM whether the current solution is good
163   enough or it is too far away, and the user does not want to spend any extra effort.
164
165   For LLM agents to focus on reasoning, the Chain-of-Thoughts (CoT) Wei et al. (2022) is one of
166
      the most popular methods used in LLMs. By adding a simple sentence of ”Let’s do it step-by-step,”
      LLMs will automatically consider outputting auxiliary steps before generating the final answer. Sim-
167
      ilar to how writing down the calculation procedure helps a high-school student solve math problems,
168
      the auxiliary steps are shown to be effectively helping the LLMs get a higher success rate in solving
169   reasoning questions. From task to task, the auxiliary steps could differ significantly, and the exact
170   steps needed in a task are called planning in LLM agents. A common practice is to let the LLM first
171   generate these steps and then further choose whether each step should be executed by another LLM
172   or external tools.
173
174   3.2     R E P ROMPT
175
176   3.2.1     OVERVIEW
177
178
      Specifically, in LLM agents for reasoning tasks, we consider the task planning part of LLM agents
      with prompt optimization, where the tasks of the agents are known ahead of time, and the final
179
      solution checker is not available due to its cost. A typical example of this scenario is the different
180
      GPTs in OpenAI (OpenAI, 2023b).
181
182   As shown in Fig. 1, our method, R E P ROMPT, is a prompt optimizer that is based on the interaction-
183   based action generate process. Our method is similar to a machine learning training loop, which
184
      iterates between getting the output based on the current parameter, calculating the loss based on the
      output, and optimizing the parameter based on the loss. But in our case, the parameters to be trained
185
      are the prompts going to be fed into the model, the model forward pass is replaced by the complete
186
      interaction-based action generate process, which include the feedback information generator, the
187   loss and optimizer are both LLMs instead of numerical calculation for the distance and the gradients.
188
189   Given a specific small dataset of reasoning tasks used for training, we first let the LLMs generate
190
      their responses using the current prompt. This process needs to include some interaction schemes
      with some kind of feedback provider like R E ACT or R EFLEXION , but we do not put any constraint
191
      on how this part should be done, and how accurate the feedback is. We call this process the act loop.
192
193   We then wait until a complete batch of chat histories has been collected, at which point we input the
194   entire batch into a large language model, which we call the summarizer, to summarize the primary
195   focus point. This focus point might be a recurring issue that frequently prolongs iterations or specific
196
      suggestions (or “thoughts” in the case of R E ACT) that have proven effective in producing high-
      quality responses. Typically, the essential information is already present in the chat history and does
197
      not require further analysis or summarization. Our summarizer is designed to capture key insights
198
      across various scenarios, omitting scenario-specific details and recommendations while avoiding
199   overly broad summaries that would demand additional reasoning steps. Due to space constraints,
200   we provide this prompt for this summarizer in the appendix.
201
202
      Then, with this summarized typical error, we use another LLM , the Prompt Optimizer, to update
      the actual prompt. We ask this optimizer LLM to follow the following update rules:
203
204
              1. The improvement should focus on the common prompt part rather than scenario-specific
205              prompts that change from data point to data point. For example, in the task of formula-
206              tion generation for PDDL, one of the most commonly appearing suggestions is to provide
207              more information on what specific domain the LLM is trying to generate and more detailed
208              background knowledge on what the domain is about. However, because we want to get
209              a general enough prompt to solve all the PDDL formulation generation, such an update
210              should not happen.
211           2. The improvement should prefer to identify whether the specific problem does occur in the
212              given scenario. For example, suppose there is a certain budget one wants the solution
213              provided by LLMs to satisfy, and the previous history shows that this budget constraint
214              could be one of the main problems that lead to a wrong solution. In that case, it should first
215              approximate the cost for a typical plan. If it breaks the constraint, it could prioritize the
                 budget constraint when getting a solution; otherwise, it should ignore this problem.

216
      Based on the above rules, we ask the LLM to the following steps:
217
218         1. Propose a few potential solutions to the problem.
219
            2. Analyze the solutions one by one to see which one meets the rules better.
220
221         3. Choose the single solution that is the best. Unlike APE and some of the following work
222
               (Zhou et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2022), we do not ask the LLM to give a concrete number as
               the value of the sentence.
223
224         4. Analyze the original steps in the original prompts, check whether the chosen solution
225            should be inserted before the current step or the solution is a more concrete detail on the
226
               step, and the prompt on the current step should be replaced by the solution. If it is the step,
               add the prompt here.
227
228         5. Output the final prompt that combines the original prompt and the updated prompt.
229
      To initialize the training process, we start with the original prompt. Although not all original prompts
230
      include detailed step-by-step instructions that our optimizer can leverage, we introduce an auxiliary
231
      checker (excluded from the main workflow for simplicity) to convert prompts into a structured,
232   step-by-step format when needed. Specifically, we first use a language model to assess whether the
233   current prompt already contains such instructions. If it does not, we manually append a standardized
234   sequence of steps to the prompt, placing it just before any examples. This sequence comprises two
235   primary steps: a brief problem analysis followed by the solution. This structure is functionally simi-
236   lar to a single ”Chain of Thought” (CoT) prompt (Wei et al., 2022), “let’s think step-by-step” in most
237   reasoning tasks by encouraging essential analysis without introducing domain-specific knowledge,
238   this approach maintains generality across tasks and domains.
239   We integrate the specific rules and procedural steps mentioned above into our optimizer prompt,
240   detailed in the appendix due to page limit. To assist the optimizer in handling common challenges,
241   we have pre-encoded several frequently used solutions directly within the optimizer prompt. While
242   these solutions could be discovered over multiple iterations, providing them upfront minimizes the
243   number of iterations required for prompt refinement, accelerating the optimization process.
244   After the steps, we will get an updated prompt, and we can continue to do more iterations, which can
245   also be seen as more epochs as an analogy to training ML models, until the prompt has converged
246   and does not change with more iterations. This converged prompt will help improve the generated
247   result in the first round , and also help assure common problems that can be fixed by the feedback
248   generator to be resolved as early as possible. During test time, we only need to use the converged
249   updated prompt and test it on the new test set. During the test, we do not require the exact same
250   process to generate the response, e.g., the feedback generator can be removed completely from the
251   Act procedure if it is quite expensive.
252   Note that in the optimization process, R E P ROMPT only changes the step-by-step instruction phase
253   rather than any other problem description or format requirement. This brings us to two possible
254   formats of prompts that the algorithm will end up with:
255
256         1. If the current prompt is ReAct-like, which already includes a step-by-step instruction that
257
               gives a specific step, like Thought in R E ACT, to include all the potential analysis, our
               prompt will converge to always update this thinking step by adding more and more hints
258
               on what to do to it. Our method becomes an algorithm that gives a more specific hint on
259
               what part the analysis should focus on compared to other prompt engineering work that
260            introduces hints dynamically.
261
            2. If the prompt is step-by-step, like solving a math or logical problem, then our algorithm is
262
               very likely to add more and more steps to the procedure generated by the planning. This
263
               will lead to a more concrete direction to try and focus on in the planning process, will
264            guide the LLM in getting to the correct final answer, and will serve as the planner that
265            breaks down the high-level task.
266
267   While we understand that in-context learning is very important for reasoning, we found it extremely
268   challenging to update the examples to follow our instructions step-by-step perfectly all the time.
269   And therefore, we choose not to change the examples at all. In most cases, the examples serve as
      a hint to the LLM on the output format and the related capabilities rather than a concrete guide on

      4.1     E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS
289
290   In the experiments, the stopping criteria and other hyperparameter settings for each domain will be
291   the same as in the original environments. Without specifications, we use a temperature = 0, and a
292   seed of 42. All results are tested on GPT-4-turbo-1106-preview, given its strong general capability
293   (Chiang et al., 2024). To help reproducibility, we provide all the optimized prompts generated by
294   R E P ROMPT in the appendix.
295   To test the capability of our algorithm in different scenarios, we choose two environments, PDDL
296   generation (Guan et al., 2023), and Travel Planner (Xie et al., 2024). The two tasks are selected
297   because their feedback generator is already included in the paper, and the reality is that both datasets
298   are hard to conduct iterations with feedback generators. The PDDL generation task provides accu-
299   rate but expensive feedback and challenges the exact translation capability of the LLMs, which is
300   necessary for LLMs to be further able to write correct code. The TravelPlanner environment, on the
301   other hand, provides cheap but not accurate feedback through Reflexion without knowing ground-
302   truth information. Travel Planner also provides tools to be used to query the cost information in
303
      the database and challenges the reasoning capability by asking for direct generating solutions. Fur-
      thermore, in TravelPlanner, we are testing R E P ROMPT with R EFLEXION, which further includes the
304
      thought-action-observation steps rather than a standard step-by-step instruction in the PDDL where
305
      each step is an intermediate step that could be helpful for guiding the generation for final results.
306   Given the different types of feedback, the purpose of our R E P ROMPT also change: in PDDL, our
307   R E P ROMPT serves to improve the generation performance without any iteration between the LLM
308   actor and the feedback generator that is used to reduce the cost of generating feedback, while in
309   TravelPlanner, R E P ROMPT is used to help guide the LLM to take all the important steps of concern
310   in all scenarios and reduce the potential failures.

410   easily forgotten after the interactions with feedback providers. Thus, this is one of the common
411   sense constraints that LLMs can detect themselves in the process, and it is sometimes, but not often,
412   covered in the thoughts provided in the feedback loop. Our R E P ROMPT has caught and included

419   answers due to information confusion.”, and our agents still output the wrong flight number that is
420   used for the wrong leg of the flight, and output the same flight number for both departure and arrival
421
      flight. While this theoretically could be addressed by the feedback provider, it seems that such
      feedback is never provided and addressed in the loop, and our R E P ROMPT training loop cannot catch
422
      such an error either. We are looking forward to further investigating how our R E P ROMPT framework
423
      can serve as a further level of checker on simple hallucination errors. In which, one possibility is to
424   combine scenario-specific information with chat history when we calculate the “loss”.

      In some domains, the output format can be similar to a more commonly used domain, and LLMs

482   experiment, we found that even though our prompt has explicitly required the LLM not to change any

485   in smaller cases. To solve this problem, we leverage the feedback of the syntax checker. While

629   A    L IMITATIONS
630
631   It is important to discuss the limitations of our proposed method R E P ROMPT. First of all, as the
632   prompt is optimized similarly to fine-tuning, our generated prompts are also limited to the training
633   data and harm the generalizability of LLMs to a certain degree, i.e., if the training data demonstrate
634   unique challenges to LLMs that only occur in those training scenarios but not general cases, our
635   optimized prompts may even be less efficient compared to the original prompts.
636   Next, our prompts rely on comprehensive tools available to the LLM agents. Because the optimized
637   methods are provided directly from LLMs rather than processed with a search-based method, R E -
638   P ROMPT can propose to use some statistic tools that are not available in the actual given settings.
639   We leave the possibility of letting LLM code the extra, not-available, but commonly used tools
640   themselves to future works.
641
      Furthermore, sometimes, the feedback generator, which we do not have and put any control on, can
642   generate useless or even wrong and misleading results. While R E P ROMPT is based on summariza-
643   tion, R E P ROMPT will take such feedback into the prompt if such a mistake is often made. And
644   because we do not consider removing useless steps in the prompt in this paper, such a mistake will
645   only increase the total number of tokens used, not contributing to better results. Future work could
646   add a search-based mechanism to identify such a mistake and potentially fix it, but this will poten-
647   tially require more ground-truth feedback from the environment and can lead to a more constraint in
      applicable domains.


                                                       12
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


648
      Last but not least, our proposed method is doing the planning in the prompt phase, and thus, if
649   the LLM agents are proposed for very general domains that need completely different procedures in
650   different scenarios, e.g., LLM agents for solving math problems, our proposed method will not work
651   at all. However, if the LLM agents are proposed for specific tasks, like using LLM agents to solve
652   high-school geometry problems, our proposed method could help learn the planning very efficiently,
653   as shown above in the experiments.

659   PromptAgent Wang et al. (2024) was initially proposed as an APE algorithm for question-answering
660
      tasks, which made its design mostly use the fact of ground-truth feedback provider which tells at
      least whether the final prediction is correct or not. In our TravelPlanner settings, even this boolean
661
      checker is not provided by the environment during the process of APE.
662
663   To adapt the PromptAgent framework to our settings, we created an LLM-based checker, which
664   provided exactly the same input as the plan generator together with the plan just generated. The
665   checker is called after the reflexion finished and submitted a plan, i.e., each node in the MCTS
666
      tree of PromptAgent is now a complete reflexion run. We provide the prompt of the checker in
      Fig. 3. During testing, we compared the performance of the LLM-based checker with a ground-
667
      truth checker, which is only available under specific dataset test conditions. The accuracy of the
668
      LLM-based checker is 89%. Although the 89% accuracy might seem reasonable, it’s important to
669   note that the success rate of a plan is less than 5%. This means that even a checker that consistently
670   marks plans as incorrect would achieve better performance overall. We observe the same type of
671   hallucinations as the ones in the plan generation, which is one of the main reasons that there are
672   many false negatives in this checker. Meanwhile, we have briefly tested 5 of the false negatives with
673   the latest GPT-4o, and we found that, surprisingly, they are all correct. However, to make a fair
674   comparison and for the consistent of models used in the experiment, we are still using GPT-4-turbo
675   as the model for the checker.
676   Additionally, we have made some necessary changes to the gradient descent prompt template to
677   remove the requirement of labels, also known as the ground-truth answers, which in our case is also
678   not provided.
679
      To make the comparison between R EPROMPT and PromptAgent fair, we use the Lite version of
680
      PromptAgent to limit the number of iterations. However, even with PromptAgent-Lite, it is still
681   about twice as expensive compared to R E P ROMPT, which shows another advantage of our algorithm.

829     description. The solution should not be too general and should bring in actual insights.
830     3. Analyze each steps in the original prompt, and see whether the new solution should be
831     inserted before or after the current step, or it is a superset of the current step and thus the
