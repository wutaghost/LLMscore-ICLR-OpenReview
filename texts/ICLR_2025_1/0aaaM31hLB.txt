                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Incorporating equivariance as an inductive bias into deep learning architectures
010
               to take advantage of the data symmetry has been successful in multiple appli-
011            cations, such as chemistry and dynamical systems. In particular, roto-translations
012            are crucial for effectively modeling geometric graphs and molecules, where under-
013            standing the 3D structures enhances generalization. However, equivariant models
014            often pose challenges due to their high computational complexity. In this paper,
015            we introduce REMUL, a training procedure for approximating equivariance with
016            multitask learning. We show that unconstrained models (which do not build equiv-
017            ariance into the architecture) can learn approximate symmetries by minimizing an
018            additional simple equivariance loss. By formulating equivariance as a new learn-
019
               ing objective, we can control the level of approximate equivariance in the model.
               Our method achieves competitive performance compared to equivariant baselines
020
               while being 10× faster at inference and 2.5× at training.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Equivariant machine learning models have achieved notable success across various domains, such
026   as computer vision (Weiler et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2022), dynamical systems (Han et al., 2022;
027   Xu et al., 2024), chemistry (Satorras et al., 2021; Brandstetter et al., 2022), and structural biology
028   (Jumper et al., 2021). For example, incorporating equivariance w.r.t. translations and rotations
029   ensures the correct handling of complex structures like graphs and molecules (Schütt et al., 2021;
030   Bronstein et al., 2021; Thölke & Fabritiis, 2022; Liao et al., 2024). Equivariant machine learning
031   models benefit from this inductive bias by explicitly leveraging symmetries of the data during the
032   architecture design. Typically, such architectures have highly constrained layers with restrictions
033
      on the form and action of weight matrices and nonlinear activations (Batzner et al., 2022; Batatia
      et al., 2022). This may come at the expense of higher computational cost, making it sometimes
034
      challenging to scale equivariant architectures, particularly those relying on spherical harmonics and
035
      irreducible representations (Thomas et al., 2018; Fuchs et al., 2020; Liao & Smidt, 2023; Luo et al.,
036   2024). On the other hand, equivariance constraints might limit the expressive power of the network,
037   restricting its ability to act as a universal architecture (Dym & Maron, 2021; Joshi et al., 2023).
038
039
      Equivariant layers are not the only way to incorporate symmetries into deep neural networks. Sev-
      eral approaches have been proposed to either offload the equivariance restrictions to faster networks
040
      (Kaba et al., 2022; Mondal et al., 2023; Baker et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024; Panigrahi & Mondal,
041
      2024) or simplify the constraints by introducing averaging operations (Puny et al., 2022; Duval
042   et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024). Nonetheless, while these approaches leverage un-
043   constrained architectures, they often require additional networks or averaging techniques to achieve
044   equivariance and may not rely solely on adjustments to the training protocol. To this aim, a widely
045   adopted strategy to replace ‘hard’ equivariance (i.e., built into the architecture itself) with a ‘soft’
046   one, is data augmentation (Quiroga et al., 2019; Bai et al., 2021; Gerken et al., 2022; Iglesias et al.,
047   2023; Xu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024), whereby the training protocol of an arbitrary (uncon-
048   strained) network is augmented by assigning the same label to group orbits (e.g., rotated and trans-
049   lated versions of the input). In fact, recent works have shown that unconstrained architectures may
050
      offer a valid alternative provided that enough data are available (Wang et al., 2024; Abramson et al.,
      2024).
051
052   Besides the challenges in computational cost and design, there are also tasks that do not exhibit
053   full equivariance, such as dynamical phase transitions (Baek et al., 2017; Weidinger et al., 2017),
      polar fluids (Gibb et al., 2024), molecular nanocrystals (Yannouleas & Landman, 2000), and cellular

      In this work, we present REMUL: Relaxed Equivariance via Multitask Learning. REMUL is a
      training procedure that aims to learn approximate equivariance during training for unconstrained net-
074
      works using a multitask approach with adaptive weights. We conduct a comprehensive evaluation of
075
      unconstrained models trained with REMUL, comparing their performance and computational effi-
076   ciency against equivariant models. We consider Transformers and Graph Neural Networks (GNNs),
077   as well as their E(3) equivariant versions, as our main baselines, focusing on roto-translation group.

      Our contributions are as follows:
080
               • We formulate equivariance as a weighted multitask learning objective for unconstrained
081              models, aiming to simultaneously learn the objective function and approximate the required
082              equivariance associated with the data and the task.
083
               • We demonstrate that by adjusting the weighting of the equivariance loss, we can modulate
084
                 the extent to which a model exhibits equivariance, depending on the requirements of the
085
                 task. Specifically, tasks that demand full equivariance require a higher weight on the equiv-
086              ariance component, whereas tasks that require less strict equivariance can be managed with
087              lower weights.
088
               • Empirically, we show that Transformers and Graph Neural Networks trained with our mul-
089
                 titask learning approach compete or outperform their equivariant counterparts.

101   2.1     S YMMETRY G ROUPS AND E QUIVARIANT M ODELS
102
103   Symmetry groups, a fundamental concept in abstract algebra and geometry, are a mathematical
104
      description of the properties of an object remaining unchanged (invariant) under a set of transforma-
      tions. Formally, a symmetry group G of a set X is a group of bijective functions from X to itself,
105
      where the group operation is function composition.
106
107   Equivariant machine learning models are designed to preserve the symmetries associated with the
      data and the task. In geometric deep learning (GDL), the data is typically assumed to live on some


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      geometric domain (e.g., a graph or a grid) that has an appropriate symmetry group (e.g., permutation
109   or translation) associated with it. Equivariant models implement functions f : X → Y from input
110   domain X to output domain Y that ensure the actions of a symmetry group G on data from X
111   correspond systematically to its actions on Y , through the respective group representations ϕ and ρ.
112   Formally, we say that:
113   Definition 2.1. A function f is equivariant w.r.t. the group G if for any transformation g ∈ G and
114   any input x ∈ X,
115                                         f (ϕ(g)(x)) = ρ(g)(f (x))                                   (1)
116
117   The group representations ϕ and ρ allow us to apply abstract objects (elements of the group G)
118
      on concrete input and output data, in the form of appropriately defined linear transformations. For
      example, if G = Sn (a permutation group of n elements, arising in learning on graphs with n
119
      nodes), its action on n-dimensional vectors (e.g., graph node features or labels) can be represented
120
      as an n × n permutation matrix.
121
122   A special case of equivariance is obtained for a trivial output representation ρ = id:
123   Definition 2.2. A function f is invariant w.r.t. the group G if for all g ∈ G and x ∈ X,
124                                            f (ϕ(g)(x)) = f (x)                                       (2)

      2.2   E QUIVARIANCE AS A C ONSTRAINED O PTIMIZATION P ROBLEM
127
128   Consider a class of parametric functions fθ , typically implemented as neural networks, whose pa-
129   rameters θ are estimated via a general training objective based on data pairs (x, y) ∼ q:
130                                   minimize E(x,y)∼q [L(fθ (x), y)]
                                          θ
                                                                                                         (3)
131
132   Here, L represents the loss function that quantifies the discrepancy between the model’s predictions
133   fθ (x) and the true labels y. The class of models is considered equivariant with respect to a group G
134   if it satisfies the constraint in Equation 1 for any input x ∈ X and for any action g ∈ G.
135   Equivariance is typically achieved by design, by imposing constraints on the form of fθ . Since fθ
136   is usually composed of multiple layers, ensuring equivariance implies restrictions on the operations
137   performed in each layer, a canonical example being message-passing graph neural networks whose
138   local aggregations need to be permutation-equivariant to respect the overall invariance to the action
139   of the symmetric group Sn . As such, finding an equivariant solution to the minimization problem in
140   Equation 3 corresponds to solving the following constrained optimization:
141                        minimize     E(x,y)∼q [L(fθ (x), y)]
                                θ                                                                        (4)
142
143                        subject to fθ (ϕ(g)(x)) = ρ(g)fθ (x), ∀g ∈ G, ∀x ∈ X
144   In general, such optimization is challenging, leading to complex design choices to enforce equivari-
145   ance that could ultimately restrict the class of minimizers and make the training harder. Additionally,
146   for relevant tasks, the optimal solution only needs to be approximate equivariant (Wang et al., 2022;
147   Petrache & Trivedi, 2023; Kufel et al., 2024; Ashman et al., 2024) meaning that the extent to which
148   a model needs to exhibit equivariance can vary significantly based on the specific characteristics of
149   the data and the requirements of the downstream application. In light of these reasons, we neces-
150
      sitate a flexible approach to incorporating equivariance into the learning process. To address this,
      we propose REMUL, a training procedure that replaces the hard optimization problem with a soft
151
      constraint, by using a multitask learning approach with adaptive weights.

218   Data augmentation is a widely recognized technique that enhances the performance of machine
219   learning models by including different transformations in the training process. It involved creating a
220   transformed input and measuring the original loss between the model prediction and the transformed
221   target. In contrast, our method utilizes an additional controlled equivariance loss to incorporate sym-

223   data augmentation techniques can be interpreted as special cases of Equation 7 where α = 0 and
224   β = 1.

      5   R ELATED W ORK
271
272
      Equivariant ML Models. In the vision domain, group convolutions have proven to be a powerful
273
      tool for handling rotation equivariance for images and enhanced model generalization (Cohen &
274   Welling, 2016; Cohen et al., 2019; Weiler & Cesa, 2019; Qiao et al., 2023). Similarly, the devel-
275   opment of equivariant architectures with respect to roto-translations for geometric data has been an
276   active area of research (Chen et al., 2021a; Satorras et al., 2021; Han et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2024).
277   Techniques that use spherical harmonics and irreducible representations have shown a large success
278   in modeling graph-structured data, such as SE(3)-Transformers (Fuchs et al., 2020), Tensor Field
279   Networks (Thomas et al., 2018), and DimeNet (Gasteiger et al., 2020). More recently, Brehmer
280   et al. (2023) introduced an E(3) equivariant Transformer that employs geometric algebra for pro-
281   cessing 3D point clouds.
282
      Data Augmentation and Unconstrained Models. Alternatively, integrating transformations
      through data augmentation is a widely used strategy across multiple vision tasks, enhancing per-
283
      formance in image classification (Perez & Wang, 2017; Inoue, 2018; Rahat et al., 2024), object
284
      detection (Zoph et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019; Kisantal et al., 2019), and segmentation (Negassi
285   et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021b; Yu et al., 2023). For geometric data, Hu et al. (2021) has adapted a
286   Graph Neural Network architecture with data augmentation to process 3D molecular structures. In
287   parallel, Dosovitskiy et al. (2021) introduced that Vision Transformers (ViTs) with a large amount
288   of training data can achieve comparable performance with Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs),
289   obviating the need for explicit translation equivariance within the architecture. Recently, this has
290   shown to be effective for handling geometric data (Wang et al., 2024; Abramson et al., 2024).
291   Learning Symmetries and Approximate Equivariance. Several studies have shown that the
292   layers of CNN architectures can be approximated for a soft constraint (Wang et al., 2022; van der
293
      Ouderaa et al., 2022; Romero & Lohit, 2022; Veefkind & Cesa, 2024; Wu et al., 2024; McNeela,
      2023). Conversely, van der Ouderaa et al. (2023) extends the Bayesian model selection approach to
294
      learning symmetries in image datasets. Yeh et al. (2022) introduced a parameter-sharing scheme to
295
      achieve permutations and shifts equivariances in Gaussian distributions. Recent works have relaxed
296   the hard constrained models to a soft constraint by adding unconstrained layers in the architecture
297   design (Finzi et al., 2021a; Pertigkiozoglou et al., 2024), canonicalization network (Lawrence et al.,
298   2024). , or explicit relaxation Kaba & Ravanbakhsh (2023). Additionally, Lin et al. (2019) modified
299   the loss of CNN for segmentation task. Shakerinava et al. (2022) introduced a method to learn equiv-
300   ariant representation using the group invariants, while Bhardwaj et al. (2023) defined a regularizer
301   that injects the equivariance in the latent space of the network by explicitly modeling transforma-
302   tions with additional learnable maps. In contrast, several works have started from pre-trained models
303   (Basu et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023b). Furthermore, the EGNN framework (Satorras et al., 2021)
304
      has been modified using an invariant function (Zheng et al., 2024) or adversarial training procedure
      (Yang et al., 2023). However, in our work, we start completely from unconstrained models without
305
      assuming any equivariance over the space of functions in the architecture design. Moreover, we
306
      didn’t assume a specific class of models or introduce additional parameters, which increases the
307   applicability of our method to various domains and makes it computationally efficient.

463   We also present a comparative analysis between constrained equivariant models and unconstrained
464   models focusing on molecular dynamics, specifically predicting 3D molecule structures. We utilize
465   the MD17 dataset (Chmiela et al., 2017), which comprises trajectories of eight small molecules.
466   We use the same dataset split in Huang et al. (2022); Xu et al. (2024), allocating 500 samples
467   for train, 2000 for validation, and 2000 for test. For this task, we selected the Equivariant Graph
468   Neural Network (EGNN) architecture and its non-equivariant GNN counterpart, as presented in
469   Satorras et al. (2021). We then apply REMUL procedure as well as data augmentation to the GNN
470   architecture. Both architectures have the same hyperparameters. More information is indicated in
471
      Appendix B.3.
472   Our results are provided in Table 2. We illustrate how the performance and equivariance error of a
473   GNN trained with REMUL vary across different molecules as a function of β, as shown in Figure 4
474   & Figure 9. From the results presented in Table 2, GNN trained with REMUL outperforms EGNN
475   in six out of eight molecules. Interestingly, a standard GNN, without data augmentation or REMUL,
476   surpasses the performance of EGNN for two molecules: Aspirin and Toluene. In Figure 4 & Figure
477
      9, we observe that the optimal performance of each molecule is attained at different values of the
      penalty parameter β. For instance, Malonaldehyde exhibits a direct correlation between model
478
      performance and equivariance, where a higher β yields better performance. Conversely, for most
479
      other molecules, there appears to be a pronounced trade-off where the best performance is achieved
480   at a lower value of β. This is particularly evident with molecules like Aspirin, where a standard GNN
481   architecture outperforms EGNN. We also plot the 3D structures of the eight molecules in Figure 11.
482   Molecules such as Malonaldehyde, characterized by their symmetric components, might be ideally
483   suited for equivariant design. However, this advantage does not apply to all molecules. Aspirin on
484   the other side, might have an asymmetric structure and exhibit a range of interactions and dynamic
485   states that equivariant models might simplify. Consequently, for such molecules, less equivariant
      models could potentially offer more accurate predictions.

      In this section, we analyze the relative ease of training equivariant models compared to non-

512   We explore how each architecture influences the loss landscape when trained on the same task.
513   However, due to the high dimensionality of parameter spaces in neural networks, visualizing their
514   loss functions in three dimensions might be a significant challenge. We use the filter normalization
515   method introduced by Li et al. (2018), which calculates the loss function along two randomly se-
516   lected Gaussian directions in the parameters space, starting from the optimal parameters θ∗ achieved
517   at the end of training.
518   We visualize the loss surface of the Geometric Algebra Transformer (GATr) and Transformer in
519   Figure 1, trained on the N-body dynamical system. We observe that the Transformer architecture
520
      exhibits a more favorable loss shape around its local minima, characterized by a convex structure.
      This might suggest that the optimization path for the Transformer is smoother and potentially eas-
521
      ier to navigate during training, leading to more stable convergence. In contrast, the loss surface of
522
      GATr appears more erratic and rugged. This complexity in the loss landscape can indicate multiple
523   local minima and a higher sensitivity to initial conditions or parameter settings. Such characteristics
524   might complicate the training process, requiring more careful tuning of hyperparameters. We leave
525   this for future work to analyze how the optimization path for each model behaves during training.

      7     C ONCLUSION
528
529   We introduced a novel, simple method for learning approximate equivariance in a non-constrained
530   setting through optimization. We formulated equivariance as a new weighted loss that is simultane-
531   ously optimized with the objective loss during the training process. We demonstrated that we can
532   control the level of approximate equivariance based on the specific requirements of the task. Our
533   method competes with or outperforms constrained equivariant baselines, achieving up to 10 times
534   faster inference speed and 2.5 times faster training speed.
535   Limitations and Future Directions. While we showed that unconstrained models exhibit a more
536   convex loss landscape near the local minima compared to equivariant models, this observation is
537   subject to certain limitations. Specifically, we did not account for the trajectories that these models
538   traverse to reach their respective minima. Understanding the optimization paths and how different
539   initialization settings influence these paths remains unexplored. In future work, we aim to analyze
      the optimization process of each model and how it behaves during training.
