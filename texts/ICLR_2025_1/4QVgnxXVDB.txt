011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Imitation learning (IL) aims to recover an expert’s strategy by performing super-
014            vised learning on the demonstration datasets. Incorporating IL in safety-crucial
015            tasks like autonomous driving is promising as it requires less interaction with the
016
               actual environment than reinforcement learning approaches. However, the ro-
               bustness of IL methods is often questioned, as phenomena like causal confusion
017
               occur frequently and hinder it from practical use. In this paper, we conduct causal
018
               reasoning to investigate the crucial requirements for the ideal imitation general-
019            ization performance. With insights derived from modeled causalities, we propose
020            causality-inspired contrastive conditional imitation learning (3CIL), a conditional
021            imitation learning method equipped with contrastive learning and action residual
022            prediction tasks, regularizing the imitator in causal and anti-causal directions. To
023            mitigate the divergence with experts in unfamiliar scenarios, 3CIL introduces a
024            sample-weighting term that transforms the prediction error into an emphasis on
025            critical samples. Extensive experiments in the CARLA simulator show the pro-
026            posed method significantly improves the driving capabilities of models.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Known as learning from demonstrations, imitation learning (IL) has attracted attention for its ca-
032   pability of replicating the behavior of experts in some tasks given only experts’ demonstrations
033
      available. In many real-world applications, IL is widely used, because the desired behavior patterns
      are hard to construct, and the requirements for a competent model are difficult to summarize into
034
      a reward function or optimize objective. IL approaches can be divided into two categories, namely
035
      behavior cloning (BC) and inverse reinforcement learning (IRL). BC is one of the most prominent IL
036   algorithms, which transforms the procedure of imitating experts’ strategy into a simple supervised
037   learning problem. For those tasks with strict safety concerns or expensive trial costs like autonomous
038   driving, BC is preferred as provides an effective solution with no requirement on interactions with
039   the real environment during training.
040
      However, the performance of BC methods is often questionable, especially in complex environ-
041
      ments. Many approaches (de Haan et al., 2019; Wen et al., 2020; Codevilla et al., 2019; Wen et al.,
042   2022; Ortega et al., 2021) have investigated the factors that led to the problematic decision-making
043   pattern of the imitator. What is common in these analyses is that they attribute the train-test per-
044   formance gap to causal confusion: the reliance of the imitator on spurious correlations or shortcuts,
045   instead of causal relations. Due to the lack of causal principles, models are prone to use features
046   that are spurious correlated to the expert’s actions, as relying on these correlations only needs fewer
047   parameter updates to obtain a stable and low loss in the training phase. Moreover, the potential per-
048   ception mismatch between the expert and the imitator may further impose the imitator’s reliance on
049   spurious correlations. Nevertheless, these correlations or shortcuts may hold only when the target
050
      environment has the same distribution as the distribution demonstrations sampled from. Therefore,
      imitators that are tempted by these shortcuts, perform poorly when testing them in new environ-
051
      ments.
052
053   To alleviate the above problems of BC in visually complex tasks like autonomous driving, we incor-
      porate the idea of causal reasoning to assist imitation. While typical techniques of causal reasoning


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      can not directly be deployed in these tasks with high-dimensional observations and partial visibil-
055   ity, modeling causal relations among concepts in the driving task can still provide indications for
056   learning a robust imitator. By investigating the correspondence between causal relations and the
057   imitator’s behaviors, we identify crucial traits that a robust imitator must have. With supervisions
058   from both causal direction and anti-causal direction, the latent state inferred by the imitator is urged
059   to produce stable causal effects on its descendant node.
060
      In this paper, we consider imitating experts’ behavior to achieve autonomous driving, under the
061   setting of conditional imitation learning (CIL) (Codevilla et al., 2018). Equipped with causality, we
062   propose Causality-inspired Contrastive Conditional Imitation Learning (3CIL), an imitation learning
063   method that incorporates contrastive learning and residual prediction tasks for better generalization.
064   Our contributions are:
065
066          • Based on causal reasoning about the behavior cloning process in the driving task, we iden-
067            tify crucial traits that a robust imitator must have. By incorporating contrastive learning
068            and action residual prediction objectives into imitation learning, we enhance the imitator’s
069
               representation’s robustness through influence from causal and anti-causal directions.
070          • We propose a sample-weighting process to emphasize scenarios that cause high divergences
071            between the expert and imitator, guide the imitator to adapt to diverse situations.
072          • We conduct extensive experiments on the CARLA simulator (Dosovitskiy et al., 2017) to
073            demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed 3CIL approach and the relations between
074            causal insights and actual performance.

079   2.1   S PURIOUS CORRELATIONS IN BEHAVIOR CLONING
080
081
      Compared to online reinforcement learning (RL) or IRL methods, agents trained with BC are more
      vulnerable to spurious correlations in data. As the imitator cannot interact with the target environ-
082
      ment in the training phase, it can not test or validate its learned pattern but only count on offline eval-
083
      uation metrics (e.g. frame-wise Mean Squared Error in steer angle prediction). Such phenomenon
084   is known in the literature as causal confusion (de Haan et al., 2019): BC agents lack explicit causal
085   understanding of their tasks.
086
087
      Causal confusion becomes more evident in complex tasks like autonomous driving. As special cases
      of causal confusion, inertia problem (Codevilla et al., 2019) and copycat problem (Wen et al., 2020)
088
      are proposed to describe the strong reliance of an imitator’s policy on the expert’s previous actions,
089
      even when such actions are no explicitly provided as input.
090
091   Figure 1a illustrates the decision process of imitators suffering from inertia and copycat problems.
092   ot−3∶t and oj∶j+1 denote the observations recorded in successive frames, aacc,i is the expert’s ac-
093
      celeration command in current frame i (i.e., aacc,i > 0 means speed up, aacc,i < 0 represents slow
      down), vspeed,i is the speed in current frame. As discussed by (Codevilla et al., 2019), low speed
094
      often comes with negative acceleration in demonstrations, such a strong correlation tempts the in-
095
      ertia imitator to build a pattern: low speed causes braking. However, such a correlation exists only
096   because the expert braked in previous frames. Moving a step forward, a copycat imitator seized
097   that the variation in speed in previous frames provides clues of the expert’s former action, and it
098   turns to replicate the previous action to achieve low prediction error. However, this shortcut is also
099   misleading, as in deployment time, the copycat imitator repeats its previous prediction.
100
      Researchers (Cultrera et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024; Seo et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2021; Samsami et al.,
101
      2021; Tien et al., 2022) have attributed such reliance on spurious correlations as (1) The complexity
102   of the task itself: tasks like driving have sophisticated kinematics, diverse scenarios, continuous ac-
103   tion space and numerous environmental parameters. (2) The partial observability of state: typical
104   IL approaches to achieve driving (Hu et al., 2022a; Chuang et al., 2022) often design the expert to
105   have access to the ground truth state (i.e., pre-trained RL agent with BEV observation or scripted
106   expert with ground truth information), while the imitator can only receive the visual observation and
107   measurement vector of ego vehicle. (3) The lack of explicit causal model: an imitator can not
      distinguish the causal and non-causal policies with similar offline evaluation performance, without

150   2.2      D EFINITIONS
151
152
      In this paper, we consider the imitation driving task under the partially observable Markov deci-
      sion process (POMDP) setting. POMDP is commonly used to model decision-making problems in
153
      nondeterministic and partially observable scenarios.
154
155   Similar to (Kurniawati, 2022), the POMDP model is defined as a 5-tuple < S, A, O, T , F >, where
156   S is the state space, A the action space, O the imitator’s observation space, T (st+1 ∣st , at ) denotes
157   the state transition function, and F(ot+1 ∣st , at ) denotes the observation function. Here, variables’
158
      subscripts represent the timesteps they are in. We use boldface with lowercase letters to denote
      instances in corresponding spaces, such as st ∼ S, ∼ denotes the sample process.
159
160   The partial observability in the driving task is represented by the mismatch between the expert’s
161   and the imitator’s observation form. While the expert receives and processes the ground truth in-
      formation (e.g. bird-eye view images or vectored description of the whole scenario) as state s, the


                                                                  3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      imitator can only observe an image o as the profile of the current expert state, and o only carries
163   partial information about s.
164
165   Additionally, we consider the driving task under the conditional imitation learning (CIL) setting
166
      proposed by (Codevilla et al., 2018), and introduce a measurement vector v that come with o. v
      describes the ego vehicle state and navigation information, and is also provided to the imitator as
167
      conditions. As human drivers often drive under the indication of navigation information, we also
168
      design our method to express the effect of v more comprehensively to the imitator. In the imple-
169   mentation, the navigation information can be derived from a simple route planner which requires no
170   parameter optimization, and the incorporation of v in CIL does not violate the general end-to-end
171   driving setting.
172
      At each timestep t the expert observes the state st , selects an action at ∼ πe (at ∣st ) based on its
173
      policy πe , and then observe the next state st+1 ∼ T (st+1 ∣st , at ) sampled from the state transition
174
      function. During the above interaction with environment, image observation ot and measurements
175   vt that obtained from the observation function (ot , vt ) ∼ F(ot , vt ∣st−1 , at−1 ) are recorded as the
176   proxy of the state st observed by expert. The dataset De is organized as a combination of N expert
177   demonstrations (oi , vi , ai )N
                                    i=1 . The goal of typical BC approaches is to learn a policy π(at ∣ot , vt )
178   with the supervision of the expert demonstrations such that the distribution of hidden reward gener-
179   ated by the policy π is the same as the one generated by expert policy πe .
180
      Following the setting of typical Behavioral Cloning from Observation Histories (BCOH) approaches
181   (Chuang et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2022a; Seo et al., 2023), we assume that observations in history can
182   provide useful information about st , as a snapshot typically cannot tell the whole story. Therefore,
183   we extend the temporal input range of π to encourage it to extract more information from the past
184   and have a better understanding of the current scenario. By setting a history perception window
185   length l, we use ot−l∶t to denote the observed images in the period [t − l, t], vt−l∶t is defined in a
186   similar way. In a certain timestep t, the observation history under the perception window length l of
187   an imitator is defined as ht = (ot−l∶t , vt−l∶t ), the imitator’s policy is rewrote π as π(at ∣ht ), and the
188   training dataset is organized as De = (hi , ai )N   i=1 .
189   While the incorporation of observations in the history provides vast information and helps imitators
190   learn the dynamics of the environment, it also introduces shortcuts in imitating and prevents imitators
191   from faithfully recovering πe , as showcased by Figure 1a.

      3.1   I NSIGHTS FROM C AUSALITY
197
      We use the causal diagram to give a description of the driving tasks, as shown in Figure 1b. In
198   the modeled causal graph, it’s clear to see that the observed variable tuple (ot , vt ) has not directed
199   edges that point at the current state st and action at . We design this based on the fact that (ot , vt )
200   is just a profile of the state observed by the expert, derived as (ot , vt ) ∼ F(ot , vt ∣st−1 , at−1 ).
201   Therefore, directly building a policy that maps (ot , vt ) to at is inappropriate, as the state st that
202   the expert used for the decision has not been inferred, and (ot , vt ) cannot cover enough information
203   for replicating πe (at ∣st ). On the other hand, building an imitator policy π(at ∣ht ) that considers
204   historical information can also foster the negative effects of previous actions on identifying the
205   expert’s decision process.
206   In this paper, we propose Causality-inspired Contrastive Conditional Imitation Learning (3CIL), an
207   BC approach that incorporates causal insights into its design. In the training stage, 3CL decomposes
208   the task of IL into two sub-tasks: representation learning and policy learning, corresponding to
209   learning a representation model G(ŝ∣h), and a predictor model J(â∣ŝ). Here, we add hats ‘ ˆ ’
210   to the imitator’s predictions to distinguish them from the expert’s states and actions. Based on the
211   causal graph and analysis we made above, we conclude the important traits (T1,T2, T3) that a robust
212
      imitator must have, and introduce the targeted treatments in our proposed method 3CIL, as shown
      in following paragraphs.
213
214   (T1) Ability to extract enough information from observation history, bridging a mapping
215   ht → sˆt matches (st−1 , at−1 ) → st : a robust imitator must have its clues about the current sce-
      nario. To achieve T1, 3CIL imposes a future image reconstruction task on its representation learning

216
      phase. With emphasis on the temporal and navigation conditions, we propose to model the feature
217   extraction module with a conditional Variational Auto-Encoder (VAE) and recurrent process. Based
218   on supervision from the causal direction ŝt → ôt+1 , 3CIL transforms the history concluding process
219   into a simulation of observation function F(ot+1 ∣st , at ) in the modeled POMDP. With this modifi-
220   cation, the representation model G(ŝ∣h) is urged to conclude enough information from observation
221   history ht , so that it can match with the actual expert state st in the metric of quality of inferred
222   future image observation ôt .
223   (T2) Minor reliance on spurious correlations, learning influence of previous actions through
224   at−1 → st → at instead of ht ← at−1 → at : to guarantee its performance in non-i.i.d deployment
225   time. To accomplish T2, 3CIL incorporates an action residual prediction task in the representation
226   learning phase to encourage the model to capture changes in the expert’s decisions. The proposed
227   action residual prediction task enforces the imitator to capture the variations within consecutive ac-
228   tions ∆at = at −at−1 without explicitly accessing at and at−1 , which bypasses spurious correlation
229   by the effect estimation in causal direction ŝt → ∆ât (i.e., require st to reflect clues about changes
230   between actions, instead of serve as proxy of at−1 ). We also add a contrastive learning objective to
231
      help shape a regression-aware representation space that provides clues about current action. The su-
      pervised contrastive learning guides the representation model with the anti-causal direction ŝt ← at
232
      hindsight, to enhance the consistency of causal effect from ŝt to predicting action at .
233
234   (T3) Ability to investigate the difference between the expert’s policy and the imitator’s policy,
235   identifying scenarios that caused high divergence between at and π(at ∣ht ): a robust imitator
236   must not be satisfied with its great average offline evaluation performance, but to focus on the factors
237
      that caused its inconsistency with expert behaviors. For T3, 3CIL proposes a sample weight term to
      guide the imitator focusing on samples that cause their predictions to contradict the expert.

      navigation information organized as route commands that indicate what high-level action the agent

271   Here, we move a step forward: instead of appending route commands into the feature vector for
272   downstream predictor, 3CIL processes the route command as factors that affect the state transition.

274
      Inspired by approaches in causal inference (Nie et al., 2021; Schwab et al., 2020) that impose the
      influence of treatment variables into networks’ parameters for more accurate estimations, we seek
275
      to integrate the effect of navigation condition in the whole process of the representation model. By
276
      embedding the measurement vector v (including ego vehicle speed, and route commands) into a
277   feed-forward network that decides the parameters of the hidden state’s posterior distribution, we
278   merge the influence of navigation information and current speed into the state abstraction process.
279   Therefore, by using these navigation conditions as proxy variables of actual states observed by only
280   experts, 3CIL guides the representation model to have a more reliable estimation of the current state.
281
      Concretely, we design our G(ŝ∣h) as a conditional VAE, with a recurrent state sequence module
282
      (RSSM) proposed by (Hafner et al., 2019), to simulate the state transition function T (st+1 ∣st , at ).
283   We first extract the dense features (xt−l∶t , mt−l∶t ) from non-structured historical images ot−l∶t and
284   raw measurement vectors vt−l∶t , with a pre-trained image encoder Eo and a measurement vector
285   encoder Ev in G(ŝ∣h), produced as (xt−l∶t , mt−l∶t ) = [Eo (xt−l∶t ∣ot−l∶t ), Ev (mt−l∶t ∣vt−l∶t )]. After
286   that, we model the latent representation ŝt as conditioned on these features.
287

      While embedding the influence of observation history and action residual into the representation
325   learning enhances the model’s ability to infer the current state, it inevitably introduces more promi-
326   nent spurious correlations, as discussed in former sections. Therefore, to alleviate such effects, we
327   further introduce a contrastive learning objective to help shape a robust representation model.
328
      As we aim to achieve driving with expert demonstrations, we resort to the supervised contrastive
329
      learning (Khosla et al., 2020) methods to enhance robustness, as the expert’s actions naturally reflect
330
      differences among samples. With this intuition, we introduce the Rank-N-Contrast (RNC) loss from
331   (Zha et al., 2023) to the optimization objective of representation learning in 3CIL. The idea of RNC
332   is to align distances in the representation space ordered by distances in their labels, which by design
333   meets the requirement of alleviating the effect of spurious correlation left in the features: samples
334   that carry similar historical information may end up poles apart in the representation space when
335   their corresponding action labels are different.
336

337   to infer a suitable state corresponding to the actual expert action at . In this view, the performed su-
338   pervised contrastive learning is equivalent to conducting a hindsight investigation on the consistency
339   of constructed causal effect ŝt → at .

468   In Table 1, we present the evaluation results in the CARLA simulator. A detailed ablation study is
469   provided in Appendix A.4. We analyze the performance of methods from several aspects.
470   Effect of spurious correlation. Our proposed method 3CIL is one of the most cautious drivers
471   with the lowest collision rate in half settings (3 of 6). Interestingly, another method with the lowest
472   collision rate is the earliest approach CIL Codevilla et al. (2018) which did not consider the spurious
473   correlations problem. This phenomenon can be explained when we consider the accumulated reward
474   and average speed: the quantized results of CIL in these two metrics are significantly lower than
475   most of its competitors, suggesting that CIL has built doubtful decision patterns that showed overly
476
      cautiousness. Indeed, in our observation, the agent trained with CIL often got stuck or even failed
      to launch. In contrast, the rest of the methods generally have no such issues, demonstrating the
477
      necessity of alleviating the effects of spurious correlations.
478
479   On the contrary, PALR effectively removes the effect from the previous action by regularizing condi-
480   tional dependence (ŝt ; at−1 ∣at ). However, such regularization may not be suitable for driving tasks,
481   as shown in Figure 1b, investigation of effects from previous action is still required for recovering
482
      information about the expert’s state. Indeed, although PALR achieves relatively stable rewards, it
      obtains the highest collision rates in most settings.
483
484   Sample-weighting strategies and contrastive learning help imitating. Both Keyframe and 3CIL
485   translate errors in prediction into assigned weights on corresponding samples. Experimental results
      demonstrate that sample-weighting strategies indicate the imitator to focus on crucial changepoints


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      in a simple yet efficient way. Moreover, the proposed weighting design in 3CIL utilizes errors in
487   action residual prediction in the representation learning stage instead of the copycat policy action
488   prediction errors in (Wen et al., 2021). Such a strategy enables 3CIL to identify abnormal scenes
489   that the representation model fails to cover and empowers it to cope with more general problems
490   than the copycat phenomenon.
491
      On the other hand, contrastive learning also contributes the imitating performance. Both Premier-
492
      TACO (temporal action-driven contrastive loss) (Zheng et al., 2024) and RNC (supervised con-
493   trastive loss) (Zha et al., 2023) can assist the representation model to capture the intrinsic character-
494   istics from observation history, which is demonstrated by their relatively good performance in both
495   seen and unseen scenarios. Our 3CIL approach utilizes supervised contrastive learning to help infer
496   states, as the actions made by experts can provide clear clues for constructing representation space,
497   without the need to tune hyper-parameters for sampling temporal positive/negative pairs.
498
      While methods like DIGIC that introduce causal discovery can also investigate the essential re-
499   lationships related to experts’ decisions, the masking or filtering operation will inevitably cause
500   information loss, which may lead to safety concerns in applications like driving, and sub-optimal
501   performance in evaluation.
502
      Robustness requires effort. Although variations in experimental settings and novel map layouts
503
      pose challenges to imitators’ capabilities, 3CIL still maintains a robust driving strategy. As shown
504
      in Table 1, 3CIL obtains the highest accumulated rewards in most settings (5 of 6). Recall that the
505   reward function in evaluation measures the ability of an imitator in executing general driving tasks
506   given navigation conditions, the outstanding performance in accumulated rewards indicates that the
507   pursuit for T1,T2,T3 does improve the robustness of the imitator.
508
      Moreover, when we analyze the performance of RNC and RAP that can be seen as parts of the abla-
509
      tion study of 3CIL, the effectiveness of interactions we introduced in Figure 2 and Figure 3 become
510
      evident: the shared reconstruction task Lf o , coupled with hindsight from supervised contrastive
511   learning task LRNC or action variation capturing task Lar , both alleviate the spurious correlations,
512   but fail to maintain steady performance in all settings. Concretely, when we incorporate the anti-
513   causal direction ŝ ← at influence to shape the imitator’s representation space, G(ŝ∣h) is enforced
514   arrange inferred states to match their corresponding potential actions’ propensity, but not enforced
515   to capture the effect that previous actions imposed on the current state, while RAP approach is
516   equivalent to do the opposite. Either way, the absence of essential information will result in an in-
517   adequate estimation of the expert state, hindering models from robust generalization performance.
518   Also, the sample-weighting process proposed in 3CIL does improve the imitator’s ability to handle
519
      rare scenarios, as the agent trained with 3CIL shows more caution and obtains lower collision rates.

      5   C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this work, we investigate the factors hindering imitation learning methods from generalizing
525   training performance into unfamiliar testing environments in autonomous driving tasks. Based on
526   causal reasoning about the expert’s decision process, we identify crucial traits an imitator must have
527   for robust performance. After that, we introduce Causality-inspired Contrastive Conditional Imi-
528
      tation Learning (3CIL), an imitation learning method that imposes regularization on the imitator’s
      representation by supervised contrastive learning and action residual prediction, corresponding to
529
      assigning supervisions on representation model from both causal direction and anti-causal direction
530
      to guarantee quality of the inferred state. Moreover, 3CIL introduces a sample-weighting term to
531   transform the high divergences between the expert and imitator, into the emphasis on rare scenar-
532   ios, enabling the imitator to adapt to diverse situations. We perform experiments in the CARLA
533   simulator to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed 3CIL.

738   A.1     R ELATED WORK
739
740   A.1.1    I MITATION L EARNING FOR AUTONOMOUS D RIVING
741
      Imitation Learning (IL) is widely used in autonomous driving (Le Mero et al., 2022; Bansal et al.,
742
      2018), as it requires few or zero actual interactions with the target environment. Classical literatures
743
      have divided IL into behavior cloning (BC) and inverse reinforcement learning (IRL). The idea of
744   adversarial learning also introduces adversarial imitation learning (AIL) to the IL family. End-to-end
745   autonomous driving approaches typically use BC in training, as BC does not need actual interactions
746   with the environment, but seeks to learn driving patterns from numerous offline demonstrations(Chib
747   & Singh, 2023).
748
      However, the performance of BC is often problematic, which arises from the contradiction between
749
      the i.i.d assumption made by BC and the out-of-distribution (OOD) nature of driving tasks (Sridhar
750   et al., 2023). Such a conflict leads to the compounding error that a BC imitator behaves unreliably
751   when observing unfamiliar scenarios. Moreover, BC often suffers from causal confusion (de Haan
752   et al., 2019), as its lack of the explicit causal model makes the imitator cannot tell the difference
753   between spurious correlations and causal relations. This phenomenon becomes more severe when
754   the BC imitator interacts with the environment in a sequential manner. Commonly the learned
755   shortcuts (Wen et al., 2022) fail to apply in the test stage, or the BC imitator is even stuck in delusions
      caused by itself(Ortega et al., 2021).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      A.1.2    R EMEDIES FOR C AUSAL C ONFUSION
757
758   Previous approaches have proposed several remedies for handling the phenomenon of causal confu-
759   sion to obtain a robust imitator, including: randomly masking encoded discrete features (Park et al.,
760   2021), incorporating additional supervisions or regularization on the encoder or predictor (Hu et al.,
761   2022a; Kumar et al., 2023; Seo et al., 2023), querying experts about certain scenarios (de Haan et al.,
762   2019) and performing interventions on the environment state or policy input (Pfrommer et al., 2023;
763
      Ruan & Di, 2022), filtering input features based on causal discovery (Chen et al., 2024; Samsami
      et al., 2021), maximizing certain bounds or mutual information to achieve deconfounding (Swamy
764
      et al., 2022a; Wan et al., 2023).
765
766   However, masking and regularizing encourage the imitator to be indifferent about spurious corre-
767   lated features but may cause the loss of useful information. Adding extra supervision during training
768   requires modifying the data-collecting process, and querying experts or intervening in the environ-
769
      ment seems unfeasible in the driving task. While approaches with causal discovery match humans’
      instinct, they typically only work with low-dimensional data form (i.e., vectored observation), and
770
      the choice of causal discovery algorithm impacts the imitator’s performance. Approaches aimed to
771
      deconfound may need more clues about the expert’s policy and the dynamic of target environments
772   or mainly contribute to the theoretical analysis. Therefore, we aim to develop a method that requires
773   no extra information beyond the training dataset and has a robust policy that can drive in unseen
774   environments without reliance on spurious correlations.
775
776   A.1.3    C AUSAL R EASONING
777
778   Causal reasoning (Pearl, 2009) approaches can be generally divided into two genres: causal dis-
779   covery and causal inference. Causal discovery aims to recover the underlying causal relations
780
      among variables in the target system, to help researchers learn the mechanisms of a system and
      aid downstream tasks. On the other side, causal inference is designed to learn the effect of modify-
781
      ing one/multiple variables’ value (i.e., intervening on treatment variables) on the outcome variables,
782
      while considering the mechanisms between variables.
783
784   While typical studies have demonstrated the effectiveness and necessity of causality, incorporating
785   causal reasoning into visually complex and partially observable tasks is still challenging. While
786
      efforts have been made to investigate causality in high-dimensional and confounded data (Günther
      et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2022; Wang & Zhou, 2021; Wang et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2021; Cheng et al.,
787
      2022; Yang et al., 2021), the full identification of causalities in tasks like autonomous driving is still
788
      intractable without further assumptions (Zheng et al., 2018) or specifications (Huang et al., 2020).
789   Moreover, using the observational samples alone generally cannot provide sufficient indications
790   for recovering all causal relations or estimating precise causal influence. Performing interventions
791   (Pfrommer et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2021a) or querying experts about certain scenarios (de Haan
792   et al., 2019; Zhang & Cho, 2017) are unfeasible in tasks with safety concerns and high interaction
793   frequency like robotics and autonomous driving.

      A.2.3    T RAINING
928
929   For 3CIL and baselines we used for comparison that can conduct representation learning and policy
930   learning in separate stages (i.e., DIGIC, PALR, Premier-TACO, RNC, and RAP in Section 4.1),
931   we first conduct their corresponding representation learning with expert demonstrations to obtain
932   stable representation models. For these methods, we use an Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2014) with a
933   learning rate set to 5e−6 to optimize their representation models. An early-stopping monitor module
934
      is also added to prevent models from overfitting the training set, with an evaluation set Dv divided
      from the training set De in a dividing factor 10%. After training an epoch on the training set, the
935
      representation model is required to run on Dv . If the performance increment in Dv is lower than
936
      the optimization threshold 1e − 3, this optimization epoch is marked as a potential invalid update.
937   When the consecutive invalid updates that a representation model encountered have reached the
938   early-stopping threshold (set to 10 in this phase), the optimization process of the representation is
939   finished.
940
      When the training process for representation models was finished, we fixed these models’ weights
941
      and deployed them in the following policy training phase. Similar to the former stage, an Adam
942
      optimizer with a learning rate set to 1e − 6, and an early-stopping monitor with an optimization

953   A.3.1    BASELINES
954
955   As described in Section 4.1, we have picked and implemented baselines including CIL, Keyframe
956   (Wen et al., 2021), DIGIC (Chen et al., 2024), PALR (Seo et al., 2023), Premier-TACO (Zheng
957   et al., 2024), RNC (Zha et al., 2023), and RAP (Chuang et al., 2022) in our experiments. We now
958   introduce each baseline and our implementations.
959   The Conditional Imitation Learning (CIL) approach (Codevilla et al., 2018) eases the complex
960   vision-based driving task by introducing conditions (i.e., the expert’s intention, often expressed as
961   route commands) to the model’s input. The vanilla CIL pipeline operates in a supervised learning
962   manner by directly minimizing the prediction difference between the policy and expert demon-
963   strations, as: LJ = N1 ∑N    i=1 La (at , J(ot , vt )). In our implementation, we replace the input tuple
964   (ot , vt ) with ŝt ∼ G(ŝt ∣ht ), allowing the representation to capture more information from the tem-
965   poral aspect. The overall model is still optimized by only the prediction loss.
966   The Keyframe-Focused Visual Imitation Learning (Keyframe) approach (Wen et al., 2021) allevi-
967   ates the copycat problem by introducing sample-weighting strategies, based on precomputed “action
968   prediction error” (APE) between a copycat policy and the expert demonstrations. Concretely, a copy-
969   cat policy πc (â∣at−l∶t−1 ) is trained to use only previous actions at−l∶t−1 to predict current action â.
970   The policy πc is then used to locate frames that are more likely to be changepoints, by identifying
971   the samples with high APEs. Higher weights are then assigned to these identified samples, regular-
      izing the imitator to focus on changepoints. In our implementation, we train a copycat policy whose


                                                          18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       structure and input data are set similar to Wen et al., and use their step(⋅) function to map samples’
973    APEs into weights on samples. The samples’ weights are then plugged into the learning process of
974    CIL.
975
976
       The Domain Generalizable Imitation Learning by Causal Discovery (DIGIC) (Chen et al., 2024)
       framework, may stand as a representative for approaches that combine causal discovery and IL.
977
       Specifically, they picked the covariates that directly contribute to expert action at as the input of the
978
       downstream predictor. In our implementation, the causal discovery is operated upon the extracted
979    features from the representation model G(ŝi ∣hi ). The representation model is first trained with the
980    image reconstruction and posterior regularization losses, to ensure it captures rich information from
981    raw history observations while achieving feature compression. The causal discovery task is then
982    conducted with the mutual information regression test which is provided by the causal discovery
983    toolbox (Kalainathan et al., 2020). Only features that exhibit test statistics that are higher than a
984    threshold(0.20) are picked as input for the downstream BC predictor.
985    The Past Action Leakage Regularization (PALR) method (Seo et al., 2023) bypasses the causal con-
986    fusion problem with a regularization on the conditional dependence between extracted representa-
987    tion ŝt and expert’s previous action at−1 , given current expert’s action at , as Lreg (ŝt ; at−1 , at ). Fol-
988    lowing their work, we adopt the Hilbert-Schmidt conditional independence criterion (HSCIC) from
989    (Park & Muandet, 2020) to perform past action leakage regularization in a non-parametric manner,
990    as: Lreg-HSCIC (ŝt ; at−1 , at ) = HSCIC(st , at−1 ∣at ). Such regularization term is incorporated in the
991    representation learning stage in our implementation, with parameters set as ridge lambda = 1e − 3
992    and reg coef = 0.1.
993    The Premier-TACO framework (Zheng et al., 2024) employs a temporal action-driven contrastive
994    loss function for visual representation pretraining, with a new negative example selecting strat-
995    egy. For a state ŝt , its corresponding positive example is ŝt+k , while its negative examples are
996    then selected based from a window with size w centered at state ŝt+k within the same episode, as
997    ŝt,neg ∼ (ŝt+k−w , ..., ŝt+k−1 , ŝt+k+1 , ..., ŝt+k+w ). We incorporate this framework in our representa-
998    tion learning stage with positive stride k = 4 and window size w = 5. The action encoder utilized in
999
       Premier-TACO is implemented with a three-layer MLP with 256 hidden units.
1000   Different from Premier-TACO that constructs positive pairs and negative pairs based on temporal
1001   indexes, the Rank-N-Contrast framework (RNC) (Zha et al., 2023) conducts supervised contrastive
1002   learning to shape a robust representation space with guidance from samples’ continuous labels. As
1003   part of optimization target in our 3CIL method, the LRNC in Eq 3 help aligning the distances of
1004   samples in the representation space with distances in their labels. To evaluate the benefit from LRNC
1005
       solely, we set RNC as one of our baseline, with implementation as adding it alongside with the
       image reconstruction and posterior regularization losses.
1006
1007   The Residual Action Prediction (RAP) method (Chuang et al., 2022) aims to resolve the copycat
1008   problem by designing the residual action prediction objective Eq 2. This approach is also introduced
1009   as a baseline, without the sample-weighting strategy in Eq 4.
1010   To alleviate the performance bias incurred by different model capacities, all baselines in our exper-
1011   iments share same architecture design in Appendix A.2.2 and training strategy in A.2.3. Therefore,
1012   the major differences in metrics will come from methods’ designs.
