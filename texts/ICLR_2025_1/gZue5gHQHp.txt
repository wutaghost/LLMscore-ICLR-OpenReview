011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Radiology report generation (R2Gen) has recently leveraged large language mod-
014            els (LLMs), achieving improved results. However, the generated reports still fall
015            short in both language accuracy and clinical relevance. A key challenge is learning
016
               a visual representation of radiology images that an LLM can effectively interpret.
               To address this, we propose that for a visual representation to be interpretable by
017
               an LLM, it shall also be generatable by the LLM. Building on this idea, we intro-
018
               duce a novel bidirectional learning framework for R2Gen, integrating both vision-
019            to-text and text-to-vision information to enhance visual representation learning.
020            First, we require that the visual representation aid the LLM in generating reports
021            that closely match the ground truth. Second, we require that the visual represen-
022            tation be maximally generated by the LLM when provided with the ground truth
023            report. To enable the frozen LLM to perform text-to-vision generation, we jointly
024            train a new text encoder for reports. Additionally, through an image reconstruction
025            task, we encourage the visual representation to capture the core features of input
026            radiology images. This bidirectional learning framework is realized using a frozen
027
               LLM and incurs no extra computational cost at the inference stage. Experimen-
               tal results demonstrate better alignment between the learned visual representation
028
               and the LLM’s word embedding space, along with state-of-the-art performance in
029
               both language accuracy and clinical efficacy. Our code will be publicly released.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Automated radiology report generation (R2Gen) has emerged as a promising solution to alleviate
035
      the heavy workload of radiologists while ensuring the consistency and accuracy of medical reports.
036   Significant progress has been made in this area, with numerous methods proposed in the literature
037   Chen et al. (2020; 2022); Liu et al. (2021a); Yang et al. (2022). The recent development of large
038   language models (LLMs) has further advanced this area, enabling the generation of more accu-
039   rate reports thanks to their exceptional natural language generation (NLG) capabilities Wang et al.
040   (2023b); Yang et al. (2023); Lee et al. (2023). However, despite their state-of-the-art performance,
041   current LLM-based R2Gen methods continue to face key challenges in achieving the level of lin-
042   guistic accuracy and clinical efficacy necessary for real-world medical diagnosis.
043   Among the various challenges, a key issue in LLM-based R2Gen methods is improving the effec-
044   tiveness of the visual representation that is fed into the LLM to generate reports. Since this repre-
045   sentation provides all the information that the LLM can have about the input radiology image (e.g.,
046   chest X-ray images), its effectiveness is crucial. Compared with existing approaches that incorporate
047   additional or external information (e.g., lesion-aware augmentations Hou et al. (2024), structured
048   clinical information Dalla Serra et al. (2022), or knowledge bases Huang et al. (2023); Ranjit et al.
049   (2023); Jin et al. (2024a); Bu et al. (2024)) to address this issue, this work takes a different approach
050
      that fully exploits the available image-report training pairs to enhance visual representation learning,
      without introducing any supplementary information.
051
052   In this work, we focus on enhancing the compatibility of the visual representation with the LLM, en-
053   suring it is more easily “understood” by the LLM. This challenge, commonly noted in the literature,
      arises from the well-known modality gap between vision (captured by the visual representation) and


                                                         1

054
      text (the modality on which the LLM is trained). A typical remedy to this case is to use a visual
055   mapper to project visual features onto the space where the LLM’s word embedding space resides.
056   However, this remedy alone often falls short in fully bridging the gap between visual and textual
057   modalities, limiting the LLM’s ability to fully understand the visual input. To further address this
058   gap, CLIP-based contrastive learning is a natural extension to align visual embeddings with text
059   embeddings more closely. Differently, in this work, we propose a more radical approach.
060
      Our work is inspired by Richard Feynman’s famous quote: “What I cannot create, I do not under-
061   stand.” We propose that for a visual representation to be truly “understood” by an LLM, it shall
062   be generatable by the LLM. This requirement is used to achieve a stronger compatibility between
063   the visual representation and the LLM’s word embedding space. Furthermore, the generation of
064   the visual representation by the LLM should not be done in a random or arbitrary manner. Instead,
065   considering the correspondence between each image and its ground truth report in the training data,
066   we require that the visual representation be generated when the report is used as input to the LLM.
067   Building on this idea, we propose a novel bidirectional learning framework to learn visual repre-
068   sentations for radiology report generation. It is fully built around a frozen LLM, ensuring computa-
069   tional efficiency and avoiding performance degradation from improper fine-tuning. This framework
070   involves both vision-to-text and text-to-vision tasks. For the vision-to-text task, we require the vi-
071   sual representation to enable the LLM to generate a report that closely matches the ground truth.
072   In an innovative twist, the text-to-vision task requires the visual representation to be maximally
073   generatable by the LLM when provided with the ground truth report. Considering that the LLM,
074   having been trained primarily on textual data, may not effectively handle a text-to-vision task with
075
      its built-in word embedding, we jointly learn a new text encoder between the ground truth report
      and the frozen LLM. Lastly, to enhance the visual representation further, we require it to support
076
      the reconstruction of the input radiology image, enabling it to capture the core characteristics of the
077
      underlying distribution of these radiology images.
078
079   To further understand the core principles of this visual representation learning, we analyze it through
080   the lens of model regularization. From this perspective, the text-to-vision task functions as a regu-
081
      larization constraint, preventing the visual encoder from becoming overly complex and enhancing
      its generalization capabilities. Once trained, the framework can generate a report when a radiology
082
      image is provided. Importantly, at the inference stage, only the vision-to-text branch is needed, op-
083
      erating in the same way as current LLM-based R2Gen methods. This ensures that our method does
084   not introduce any additional computational overhead when deployed.
085
086   Extensive experiments on the IU-Xray and MIMIC-CXR datasets demonstrate that the proposed
087
      framework consistently outperforms existing methods, achieving the state-of-the-art results in both
      language accuracy and clinical efficacy. Additionally, ablation studies validate the contribution of
088
      the proposed bidirectional learning process, confirming the improved compatibility between the
089
      learned visual representation and the LLM’s word embedding space.

      Radiology Report Generation. Radiology report generation (R2Gen) has traditionally focused on
095
      vision-to-text architectures, where a vision encoder extracts features from CXR scans and a text
096   decoder produces the corresponding report. Early work in this domain used CNNs and LSTMs
097   Jing et al. (2017); Wang et al. (2018); Xue et al. (2018), followed by transformer-based models that
098   significantly improved report quality Chen et al. (2020; 2022); Nicolson et al. (2023); Wang et al.
099   (2023b); Lee et al. (2023). Despite these advancements, achieving language accuracy and clinical
100   efficacy remains a persistent challenge.
101
      To improve report generation quality, several approaches have been explored. Some methods focus
102   on detecting and describing key anatomical regions Tanida et al. (2023); Dalla Serra et al. (2023),
103   while others emphasize feature alignment between visual and textual modalities Wang et al. (2022;
104   2023a); Li et al. (2023). Additionally, external knowledge sources, such as lesion-aware augmenta-
105   tions Hou et al. (2024), structured clinical information Dalla Serra et al. (2022), symptom graphs,
106   or knowledge distiller Liu et al. (2021b); Huang et al. (2023); Ranjit et al. (2023); Jin et al. (2024a);
107   Bu et al. (2024), have shown promise. However, constructing specialized knowledge bases for such
      integration remains resource-intensive and demands domain expertise.

200   Text-to-Vision Branch. The text-to-vision branch works with the vision-to-text branch to form the
201   proposed bidirectional learning process. It further tightens the connection between the extracted
202   visual embeddings Ev and the ground truth reports. This process is powered by the LLM, which
203   plays a central role in extracting the rich semantic information in the reports and converting it into
204   visual representations. Nevertheless, two subtle issues needs to be addressed beforehand.
205   One issue is that radiology reports often omit whether the CXR image is frontal or lateral, leading to
206   potential mismatches when generating visual embeddings. To avoid this, we train a classifier using
207   only CXR images to identify the view (frontal or lateral) and append this information to the report.
208   This ensures that the LLM generates visual embeddings aligned with the correct view. The classifier
209   is trained strictly on the training set images and serves as a preprocessing step, without influencing
210   the model’s training or testing.
211   The other issue, which is more important, is that we need to jointly learn a new text encoder between
212   the ground truth report and the frozen LLM, instead of directly using the LLM’s built-in word
213   embedding layer. This is due to several considerations: 1) the LLM, having been trained primarily
214   on textual data, may not effectively handle a text-to-vision task with its built-in word embedding
215   layer; 2) the text encoder can be optimized to best support the LLM to generate the targeted visual
      embeddings; and 3) this design ensures the LLM remains frozen, preserving its original capabilities.

      In addition to the above regularization perspective, we could also interpret LTVC in Eq.(5) as another
271   type of alignment between an image, represented by Ev (θv ), and its ground truth report, whose
272   semantic information is conveyed by Eg (θt ). Such alignment takes place in the space where the
273   LLM’s word embedding space resides and the alignment degree is measured by the distance be-
274   tween them. In sum, the introduction of LVTC and LREC , either interpreted from the perspective of
275   regularization or alignment, further constrains the feasible domain of the vision encoder’s parameter
276   θv . According to regularization theory Haykin (2007) (Chapter 7), this helps to reduce the model
277   complexity of the vision encoder and improve its generalization capability.

282
283   IU-Xray. Indiana University Chest X-ray Collection (IU-Xray) Demner-Fushman et al. (2016) con-
284   tains 3,955 de-identified radiology reports, each of which is associated with frontal and/or lateral
285   chest X-ray images, and 7,470 chest X-ray images in total. Each report is comprised of several sec-
286   tions: Impression, Findings, Indication, etc. In this work, we adopt the same data set partitioning as
287
      in the literature Chen et al. (2020) for a fair comparison, with a training/validation/test split set by
      7:1:2 of the entire dataset. All evaluations are done on the test set.
288
289   MIMIC-CXR. This dataset includes 377,110 chest X-ray images from 65,379 patients, each accom-
290   panied by a radiology report. We adhere to the training/validation/test split in the literature Chen
291   et al. (2020) to facilitate comparison with related methods. This split results in 270,790 images for
292   training, 2,130 for validation, and 3,858 for testing, all paired with their respective reports. Keeping
293
      the aspect ratio, all images are resized with the shorter edge to be 256 pixels.
294   Evaluation Metrics. We utilize standard natural language generation (NLG) metrics for assessment,
295   including BLEU scores Papineni et al. (2002), ROUGE-L Chin-Yew (2004), and METEOR Banerjee
296   & Lavie (2005). Specifically, to evaluate the model, we use the BLEU-4 score on the validation set
297   to select the optimal model checkpoint. In addition to the NLG metrics, we employ several clinical
298   efficacy (CE) metrics to assess the model’s ability to generate clinically accurate reports. These
299
      include the RadCliQ metric Yu et al. (2023), which combines multiple individual metrics to align
      with radiologist evaluations. We also utilize the Bert Score Zhang et al. (2019), which measures the
300
      semantic similarity between generated reports and ground-truth reports by comparing contextualized
301
      embeddings, and the RadGraph F1 score Jain et al. (2021), which evaluates the model’s ability to
302   correctly extract and describe clinical entities and their relationships. These CE metrics provide a
303   comprehensive assessment of clinical accuracy.

      Implementation Details. The Llama2-7B model 1 is utilised as text decoder, and the text encoder of
      the base version of the BLIP 2 is used as the new text encoder. The large version of the MAE 3 forms
306
      the visual part, i.e., visual encoder and decoder. Between the LLM and the visual text encoders, there
307
      is a fully connected layer that can be used to project visual text features to match the dimensions
308   of the LLM’s embedding space. Training is conducted on four NVIDIA A100 80GB GPUs for 5
309   epochs on MIMIC-CXR and 2 epochs on IU-Xray, with a mini-batch size of 4, a learning rate of
310   1e-4, AdamW optimizer Loshchilov & Hutter (2018), and a cosine annealing scheduler. Testing
311   involves beam search with a size of 3. Images are randomly cropped to 224 × 224 during training
312   and inference. Both λ1 and λ2 in equation 3 are set as 1 without fine-tuning.

      5   C ONCLUSION , LIMITATION , AND FUTURE WORK
512
513
      As we explore leveraging the powerful capabilities of LLMs to enhance radiology report generation,
      a key challenge lies in improving the compatibility between the visual representation produced   5 by
514
      the vision encoder and the operational scope of the LLM—essentially making the visual data more
515
      easily “understood” by the LLM. This work addresses this challenge by integrating both vision-
516   to-text and text-to-vision tasks, resulting in a bidirectional learning framework for radiology report
517   generation. The framework is intentionally built on a frozen LLM, preserving computational effi-
518   ciency and performance. As demonstrated through experiments, this bidirectional learning approach
519   improves report quality and strengthens the desired compatibility. In a broader sense, by fostering
520   mutual reinforcement of visual and textual embeddings, our work underscores the potential of LLMs
521   as a foundation for cross-modal generation. This paradigm not only advances radiology report gen-
522   eration but also opens new pathways for bridging modality gaps in future research.
523   Meanwhile, we also observe in our investigation that while LLMs excel at generating coherent
524   and accurate text, the visual embeddings generated by LLMs are insufficient for synthesizing high-
525   fidelity images (e.g., the input chest X-ray scans). This difficulty arises from the modality gap,
526   as LLMs are inherently designed for text generation, complicating the translation of embeddings
527   between textual and visual domains. Directly generating high-quality images from frozen LLMs
528   remains challenging. We anticipate that by fine-tuning LLMs or utilizing truly multimodal large
529   models, we can extend our work to achieve fully bidirectional generation, producing both high-
530
      quality reports and high-fidelity images. This will be an intriguing direction for our future work.
531   Ethical considerations are essential when developing radiology report generation techniques. Cur-
532   rent methods are still not sufficiently reliable for practical medical diagnosis. Even as these tech-
533   niques mature, issues of fairness, transparency, and explainability must be thoroughly addressed.
534   Furthermore, the development of radiology report generation techniques requires large amounts of
535   medical data, making it crucial to fully respect and protect patient privacy during the collection,
536
      curation, and use of benchmark datasets.
537   Our work builds on publicly available models, techniques, benchmark datasets, software packages,
538   and programming languages. The framework, method, and implementation settings are elaborated
539   in the sections of methodology and experimental study. Upon publication, we will release the source
      code and model checkpoints, along with detailed instructions to ensure full reproducibility.
