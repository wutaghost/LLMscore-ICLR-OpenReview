                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            This paper focuses on Implicit Neural Representation (INR) generalization, where
011
               models need to efficiently adapt to new signals with few observations. Specifi-
               cally, for radiance field generalization, we propose Geometric Neural Processes
012
               (GeomNP) for probabilistic neural radiance fields to explicitly capture uncertainty.
013
               We formulate INR generalization in a probabilistic manner, which incorporates
014            uncertainty and directly infers the INR function distributions on limited context
015            observations. To alleviate the information misalignment between the 2D context
016            image and 3D discrete points in INR generalization, we introduce a set of geometric
017            bases. The geometric bases learn to provide 3D structure information for inferring
018            the INR function distributions. Based on the geometric bases, we model GeomNP
019            with hierarchical latent variables. The latent variables integrate 3D information
020            and modulate INR functions in different spatial levels, leading to better general-
021            ization of new scenes. Despite being designed for 3D tasks, the proposed method
022
               can seamlessly apply to 2D INR generalization problems. Experiments on novel
               view synthesis of 3D ShapeNet and DTU scenes, as well as 2D image regression,
023
               demonstrate the effectiveness of our method.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Implicit Neural Representations (INRs) (Sitzmann et al., 2020b; Tancik et al., 2020) have recently
030   gained popularity for their ability to learn continuous, compact, and efficient representations of
031   continuous signals, especially for 3D settings (Park et al., 2019; Mildenhall et al., 2021; Mescheder
032   et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2022). Building on INRs, neural radiance fields (NeRFs) (Mildenhall et al.,
033
      2021; Barron et al., 2021) model 3D scene representation as a mapping from 3D coordinates and
      view directions to color and density values. By integrating these values along camera rays, NeRFs
034
      can render photorealistic images of scenes from novel viewpoints. Although NeRFs achieve good
035
      reconstruction performance, they must be overfitted to each 3D object or scene, resulting in poor
036   generalization to new 3D scenes with few context images.
037
038   In this paper, we focus on radiance field generalization and fast adaptation of the INR function for
039
      novel 3D scenes using only a few context image views. Previous works on INR generalization have
      approached the problem by gradient-based meta-learning (Tancik et al., 2021) to adapt to new scenes
040
      with a few optimization steps (Tancik et al., 2021; Papa et al., 2024), modulating shared MLPs
041
      through HyperNets (Chen & Wang, 2022; Mehta et al., 2021; Dupont et al., 2022a; Kim et al., 2023),
042   or directly predicting the parameters of scene-specific MLPs (Dupont et al., 2021; Erkoç et al., 2023).
043   However, the deterministic nature of these methods cannot account for the uncertainty of scenes or
044   INR functions when only few partial observations are available. This is unrealistic since there can be
045   different interpretations of limited observations.
046
      To account for uncertainty induced by few available context images, probabilistic INR functions for
047
      NeRF (Gu et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023; Kosiorek et al., 2021) have also been recently explored.
048   VNP (Guo et al., 2023) and PONP (Gu et al., 2023) infer the INR function using Neural Processes
049   (NPs) (Bruinsma et al., 2023; Garnelo et al., 2018b; Wang & Van Hoof, 2020; Shen et al., 2024), a
050   probabilistic meta-learning method that models functional distributions conditioned on partial signal
051   observations. These probabilistic methods, however, only approximate the INR functions in 3D space,
052   neglecting the interaction between 3D functions and 2D observations. Since the radiance fields model
053   relationships in 3D space, while the only available context observations are 2D images, there is an
      information misalignment between contexts and functions in radiance field generalization.

054
      To efficiently adapt to new signals with few observations, we propose probabilistic radiance field
055   generalization with Geometric Neural Processes (GeomNP). Our contributions can be summarized as
056   follows: 1) Probabilistic NeRF generalization framework. We cast radiance field generalization as a
057   probabilistic modeling problem. By doing so, we can amortize the probabilistic model over multiple
058   objects with few views, facilitating the learning and generalization of NeRF functions. 2) Geometric
059   bases. To eliminate the potential information misalignment, we design geometric bases by encoding
060   observations in 2D space with 3D prior structures. Thus, the geometric bases can aggregate locality
061   information to each 3D point, improving the exploration of high-frequency details. 3) Geometric
062   neural processes with hierarchical latent variables. Based on the geometric bases, we develop
063
      geometric neural processes to capture the uncertainty in the latent NeRF function space. Specifically,
      we introduce hierarchical latent variables to modulate the INR function at multiple spatial levels,
064
      yielding better generalization on new scenes and new views. Experiments on novel view synthesis of
065
      ShapeNet objects and real-world DTU scenes demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method
066   on 3D radiance field generalization. Nevertheless, the proposed method can seamlessly apply to INR
067   generalization in 2D signals (images).

070   2    R ELATED W ORK
071
072   Implicit Neural Representations. Implicit neural representations (INRs) parameterize a continuous
073   function from the coordinate space to arbitrary signals, offering a flexible and compact continuous
074   data representation (Sitzmann et al., 2020b; Tancik et al., 2020). Due to their continuous nature, INRs
075   have been widely used to represent 3D objects and scenes (Chen & Zhang, 2019; Park et al., 2019;
076
      Mescheder et al., 2019; Genova et al., 2020; Niemeyer & Geiger, 2021). NeRF (Mildenhall et al.,
      2021) utilizes neural radiance fields for view synthesis, mapping spatial coordinates to corresponding
077
      colors and densities, and optimizing scene representation from 2D view images using differentiable
078
      volumetric rendering. Mip-NeRF (Barron et al., 2021) incorporates multiscale representation. Ten-
079   soRF (Chen et al., 2022) enhances NeRF by factorizing the 4D scene tensor into multiple compact
080   low-rank tensor components based on matrix decompositions. NeuRBF (Chen et al., 2023b) employs
081   radial basis functions (RBF) to aggregate local neural features in the space. FactorField (Chen
082   et al., 2023a) decomposes a signal into a product of factors. These methods aggregate local neural
083   information using various pre-defined structured information, while we infer geometric bases spanned
084   in space to encode the structure information.
085   INR Generalization. Many previous methods attempt to use meta-learning to achieve INR gen-
086   eralization. Specifically, gradient-based meta-learning algorithms such as Model-Agnostic Meta
087   Learning (MAML) (Finn et al., 2017) and Reptile (Nichol et al., 2018) have been used to adapt
088   INRs to unseen data samples in a few gradient steps (Lee et al., 2021; Sitzmann et al., 2020a; Tancik
089   et al., 2021). Another line of work uses HyperNet (Ha et al., 2016) to predict modulation vectors
090   for each data instance, scaling and shifting the activations in all layers of the shared MLP (Mehta
091   et al., 2021; Dupont et al., 2022a;b). Some methods use HyperNet to predict the weight matrix of
092   INR functions (Dupont et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023). Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) have
093
      also been used as hypernetworks to predict column vectors in the weight matrix of MLP layers (Chen
      & Wang, 2022; Dupont et al., 2022b). In addition, Reizenstein et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022) use
094
      transformers specifically for NeRF. Such methods are deterministic and do not consider the uncer-
095
      tainty of a scene when only partially observed. Other approaches model NeRF from a probabilistic
096   perspective (Kosiorek et al., 2021; Hoffman et al., 2023; Dupont et al., 2021; Moreno et al., 2023;
097   Erkoç et al., 2023). For instance, NeRF-VAE (Kosiorek et al., 2021) learns a distribution over radiance
098   fields using latent scene representations based on VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2013) with amortized
099   inference. Normalizing flow (Winkler et al., 2019) has also been used with variational inference
100   to quantify uncertainty in NeRF representations (Shen et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2023). However,
101   these methods do not consider structural information and the information misalignment between 2D
102   observations and 3D NeRF functions, which our approach explicitly models.
103   Neural Processes. Neural Processes (NPs) (Garnelo et al., 2018b) is a meta-learning framework
104   that characterizes distributions over functions, enabling probabilistic inference, rapid adaptation
105   to novel observations, and the capability to estimate uncertainties. This framework is divided
106   into two classes of research. The first one concentrates on the marginal distribution of latent
107   variables (Garnelo et al., 2018b), whereas the second targets the conditional distributions of functions
      given a set of observations (Garnelo et al., 2018a; Gordon et al., 2019). Typically, MLP is employed

108
      in Neural Processes methods. To improve this, Attentive Neural Processes (ANP) (Kim et al.,
109   2019) integrate the attention mechanism to improve the representation of individual context points.
110   Similarly, Transformer Neural Processes (TNP) (Nguyen & Grover, 2022) view each context point
111   as a token and utilize transformer architecture to effectively approximate functions. Additionally,
112   the Versatile Neural Process (VNP) (Guo et al., 2023) employs attentive neural processes for neural
113   field generalization but does not consider the information misalignment between the 2D context set
114   and the 3D target points. The hierarchical structure in VNP is more sequential than global-to-local.
115   Conversely, PONP (Gu et al., 2023) is agnostic to neural-field specifics and concentrates on the neural
116   process perspective. In this work, we consider a hierarchical neural process to model the structure
117
      information of the scene.

      ShapeNet Setup. We perform the 3D novel view synthesis task on ShapeNet (Chang et al., 2015)
360   objects. Following previous works’ setup (Tancik et al., 2021), the dataset consists of objects from
361   three ShapeNet categories: chairs, cars, and lamps. For each 3D object, 25 views of size 128 × 128
362   images are generated from viewpoints randomly selected on a sphere. The objects in each category
363   are divided into training and testing sets, with each training object consisting of 25 views with known
364   camera poses. At test time, a random input view is sampled to evaluate the performance of the novel
365   view synthesis. Following the setting of previous methods (Chen & Wang, 2022), we focus on the
366   single-view (1-shot) and 2-view (2-shot) versions of the task, where one or two images with their
367   corresponding camera rays are provided as the context.
368   Implementation Details. Our context input is the concatenation of a set of camera rays and the
369   corresponding image pixels from one or two views, which are then split into different visual tokens.
370   We use the same patch size 8 × 8 as TransINR (Chen & Wang, 2022) and VNP (Guo et al., 2023),
371   resulting in 256 tokens. A linear layer and a self-attention module project each token into a 512-
372   dimensional vector. Based on the 256 tokens, we predict 256 geometric bases using two MLP
373   modules: one for 3D Gaussian distribution parameters and the other for the latent representation (32
374
      dimensions). More details are given in Appendix B.1. We obtain the object-specific and ray-specific
      modulating vectors (both are 512 dimensions) based on the geometric base. Our NeRF function
375
      consists of four layers, including two modulated layers and two shared layers.

530   5   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we addressed the challenge of INR generalization, enabling models to quickly adapt
533
      to new signals with limited observations. For radiance field generalization, we proposed Geometric
534   Neural Processes (GeomNP), a probabilistic neural radiance field that explicitly captures uncertainty.
535   By formulating INR generalization probabilistically, GeomNP incorporates uncertainty and directly
536   infers INR function distributions from limited context images. To mitigate the information alignment
537   between 2D context images and 3D discrete points, we introduce geometric bases, which learn to
538   provide structured geometric information of the 3D scene. Moreover, our hierarchical neural process
539   modeling enables both object-specific and ray-specific modulation of the INR function. In practice,
      the proposed method also seamlessly applies to 2D INR generalization problems.

      In this section, we outline the rendering function of NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2021). A 5D neural
813
      radiance field represents a scene by specifying the volume density and the directional radiance emitted
814   at every point in space. NeRF calculates the color of any ray traversing the scene based on principles
815   from classical volume rendering (Kajiya & Von Herzen, 1984). The volume density σ(x) quantifies
816   the differential likelihood of a ray terminating at an infinitesimal particle located at x. The anticipated
817   color C(r) of a camera ray r(t) = o + td, within the bounds tn and tf , is determined as follows:
818                      Z tf                                                       Z t
819
               C(r) =         T (t)σ(r(t))c(r(t), d)dt, where T (t) = exp −                σ(r(s))ds .       (11)
820                      tn                                                            tn
821
822   Here, the function T (t) represents the accumulated transmittance along the ray from tn to t, which is
823   the probability that the ray travels from tn to t without encountering any other particles. To render a
824   view from our continuous neural radiance field, we need to compute this integral C(r) for a camera
825   ray traced through each pixel of the desired virtual camera.

      Generalizable Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) Advancements in neural radiance fields have
949   focused on improving generalization across diverse scenes and objects. Wang et al. (2022) propose an
950   attention-based NeRF architecture, demonstrating enhanced capabilities in capturing complex scene
951   geometries by focusing on informative regions. Suhail et al. (2022) introduce a generalizable patch-
952   based neural rendering approach, enabling models to adapt to new scenes without retraining. Xu et al.
953   (2022) present Point-NeRF, leveraging point-based representations for efficient scene modeling and
954   scalability. Wang et al. (2024) further enhance point-based methods by incorporating visibility and
955   feature augmentation to improve robustness and generalization. Liu et al. (2024) propose a geometry-
956   aware reconstruction with fusion-refined rendering for generalizable NeRFs, improving geometric
957
      consistency and visual fidelity. Recently, the Large Reconstruction Model (LRM) (Hong et al., 2023)
      has drawn attention. It aims for single-image to 3D reconstruction, emphasizing scalability and
958
      handling of large datasets.
959
960
961
962   Gaussian Splatting-based Methods Gaussian splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023) has emerged as an
963   effective technique for efficient 3D reconstruction from sparse views. Szymanowicz et al. (2024)
964   propose Splatter Image for ultra-fast single-view 3D reconstruction. Charatan et al. (2024) introduce
965
      pixelsplat, utilizing 3D Gaussian splats from image pairs for scalable generalizable reconstruction.
      Chen et al. (2025) present MVSplat, focusing on efficient Gaussian splatting from sparse multi-
966
      view images. Our approach can be a complementary module for these methods by introducing a
967
      probabilistic neural processing scheme to fully leverage the observation.
968
969
970
971   Diffusion-based 3D Reconstruction Integrating diffusion models into 3D reconstruction has
      shown promise in handling uncertainty and generating high-quality results. Müller et al. (2023)


                                                             18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       introduce DiffRF, a rendering-guided diffusion model for 3D radiance fields. Tewari et al. (2023)
973    explore solving stochastic inverse problems without direct supervision using diffusion with forward
974    models. Liu et al. (2023) propose Zero-1-to-3, a zero-shot method for generating 3D objects from a
975    single image without training on 3D data, utilizing diffusion models. Shi et al. (2023a) introduce
976    Zero123++, generating consistent multi-view images from a single input image using diffusion-based
977    techniques. Shi et al. (2023b) present MVDream, which uses multi-view diffusion for 3D generation,
978    enhancing the consistency and quality of reconstructed models.

       Integration with PixelNeRF To integrate our method into PixelNeRF, we utilize the same feature
       extractor and NeRF architecture. Specifically, we employ a pre-trained ResNet to extract features
1002
       from the observed images. From the latent space of the feature encoder, we predict geometric bases,
1003
       which are used to re-represent each 3D point in a higher-dimensional space. These re-represented
1004   point features are aggregated into latent variables, which are then used to modulate the first two input
1005   MLP layers of PixelNeRF’s NeRF network. During training, we align the latent variables derived
1006   from the context images with those from the target views to ensure consistency.
