009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Speculative decoding (SD) has emerged as a promising approach to accelerate
012            inference in large language models (LLMs). This method drafts potential future
013            tokens by leveraging a smaller model, while these tokens are concurrently verified
014            by the target LLM, ensuring only outputs aligned with the target LLM’s predic-
015            tions are accepted. However, the inherent limitations of individual drafters, es-
016            pecially when trained on specific tasks or domains, can hinder their effectiveness
017            across diverse applications. In this paper, we introduce a simple yet efficient uni-
018
               fied framework, termed MetaSD, that incorporates multiple drafters into the spec-
               ulative decoding process to address this limitation. Our approach employs multi-
019
               armed bandit sampling to dynamically allocate computational resources across
020
               various drafters, thereby improving overall generation performance. Through ex-
021            tensive experiments, we demonstrate that our unified framework achieves superior
022            results compared to traditional single-drafter approaches.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), Gemini (Google et al., 2023),
028   and Llama (Touvron et al., 2023) have revolutionized real-world applications such as search engine
029   (Reid et al., 2024), coding assistance, and virtual assistants. However, the token-by-token generation
030   process inherent to LLMs often leads to substantial inference times, primarily due to its memory
031   bandwidth bound nature (Patterson, 2004; Shazeer, 2019). Speculative decoding (SD) has emerged
032   as a promising avenue to address this challenge (Leviathan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023). Precisely,
033
      SD employs a smaller draft model (i.e., drafter) to predict potential future tokens. These tokens are
      verified concurrently by the target LLM, ensuring only outputs aligned with the LLM’s predictions
034
      are accepted. This parallel process significantly accelerates the generation process, enabling faster
035
      and more efficient text generation.
036
037   Recent advancements in SD have primarily focused on architectural and training improvements to
038   enhance the acceptance rate of drafted tokens (Liu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2024;
039
      Miao et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023). Notably, techniques such as batched inference and tree verifi-
      cation (Sun et al., 2023; Miao et al., 2024; Cai et al., 2024) aim to increase the number of accepted
040
      tokens by exploring more decoding paths at one step, while training recipes with knowledge distilla-
041
      tion (Zhou et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) seek to better align the drafter’s distribution with that of the
042   target model. However, despite their efficacy in certain tasks, these methods often lack the versatil-
043   ity required to comprehensively cover a wide range of tasks (Liu et al., 2023; Yi et al., 2024). The
044   inherent limitations of relying on a single drafter, with its specific architectural biases and training
045   data, can hinder performance in scenarios with held-out tasks (Detailed motivation is in Section 2.1).
046
      To mitigate the limitations of single-drafter SD, we propose a novel framework that integrates mul-
047
      tiple drafters into the process. Our high level idea is to meta-draft the optimal drafter among multi-
048   ple drafters at test-time utilizing the concept of the exploration-exploitation tradeoff (Gittins et al.,
049   2011). Effectively utilizing multiple drafters in a real-world serving system presents several chal-
050   lenges. For instance, imagine a scenario where you have several drafters, each specialized for a
051   different task like translation, summarization, or question answering. Determining which drafter
052   will perform best for a given user query is not always straightforward, especially when the query
053   involves multiple tasks or when the topic evolves during the conversation. Furthermore, the sys-
      tem needs to be efficient and adaptable to varying user loads and traffic patterns, without requiring

      To address these challenges, we draw inspiration from the field of multi-armed bandits (MAB). In
143   the MAB framework, an agent repeatedly chooses an action among different choices (arms), each
144   with an unknown reward distribution, aiming to maximize its cumulative reward over time. This
145   closely parallels our problem, where each drafter can be seen as an arm, and the reward is related
146   to the number of accepted tokens or the overall speedup achieved (Algorithm 1). MAB’s inherent
147   efficiency and online learning capabilities align well with the requirements of a robust and adaptive
148   multi-drafter SD system. MAB algorithms offer a principled way to balance exploration (trying out
149   different drafters) and exploitation (using the seemingly best drafter) to identify the optimal drafter
150   for each generation step, adapting to the changing context with minimal additional compute costs.

      MetaSD: SD with multiple drafters as a MAB problem We formalize the integration of multi-
      ple drafters into SD as a MAB problem, termed as MetaSD framework. Each SD process, consisting
177
      of drafting, verifying, and accepting tokens, corresponds to one round in the MAB setting (Algo-
178
      rithm 1). At round t, a drafter at is selected from a pool of heterogeneous drafters [K]. The round
179   concludes when all B tokens have been generated. While inspired by classical bandit problems,
180   our MetaSD framework exhibits key distinctions. Unlike classical bandits with a fixed number of
181   rounds, MetaSD operates under a fixed target sequence length B and the number of total rounds
182   T is stochastic which depends on the policy. Although switching between drafters may incur costs
183   such as prefill cost for tokens and KV cache I/O, we empirically observe that it is negligible in the
184   most of our experiments. Furthermore, for the large scale scenario where switching cost might not
185   be negligible anymore, we provide a detailed discussion with a practical algorithm in Section H.2.
186   While the generated tokens can follow a non-stationary distribution, we assume stationarity within
187
      a single turn between the user and the LLM for theoretical analysis. This assumption is reasonable
      as it allows our framework to be applied with re-intialization for each new query, even in a multi-
188
      turn conversation, effectively handling the potential non-stationarity across different queries. In the
189
      experiments, MetaSD is implemented with re-initialization for every query.

194   bandit algorithm towards optimal speedup. One straightforward and readily available choice is the
195   block efficiency (BE), which quantifies the number of mean accepted tokens until a given round
196   (Sun et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024). Formally, we define the BE reward for drafter

      3.3   E XTENSIONS OF M ETA SD FRAMEWORK
328
329   Switching costs In practical implementations, switching between drafters at each round incurs a
330   computational cost due to the need to recalculate previous KV-cache values for the new drafter. This
331   aligns with the concept of bandits with switching costs (Banks & Sundaram, 1994). However, unlike
332   traditional settings where a fixed cost is incurred per switch, the cost in MetaSD is proportional to the
333   number of unprocessed tokens in the current block. To address this, we propose Algorithm 4 with
334   Sequential Halving (SH) (Karnin et al., 2013), designed specifically for this scenario. A detailed
335
      analysis along with theoretical guarantees on its performance is provided in Section H.1.
336
      Non-stationary environment Our prior analysis assumes stationary reward distributions, where
337
      the reward feedback for each drafter follows a fixed distribution. However, in certain scenarios,
338
      the reward distribution can be non-stationary. For instance, in long-context generation, the optimal
339   drafter might change as the topic or style of the generated text evolves. Despite this challenge, our
340   MetaSD framework remains applicable by leveraging non-stationary bandit algorithms. These algo-
341   rithms are designed to adapt to changing reward distributions, enabling the system to continuously
342   learn and adjust its drafter selection strategy. Detailed discussions for non-stationary algorithms
343   within the context of MetaSD are in Section H.2.

349   Models We adopt Vicuna 7B (Chiang et al., 2023) as our target LLM for both black-box and
350   white-box SD. The distinction between two paradigms lies in the drafter’s access to the target LLM’s
351   internal representations. Black-box drafters operate independently, with access only to the final logit
352   of the target LLM. In contrast, white-box drafters can leverage intermediate activations and hidden
353   states within the target LLM. For black-box SD, we utilize Vicuna 68M (Yang et al., 2024) as the
354   base architecture for our independent drafters. Each drafter is trained on a distinct task-specific
355
      dataset to ensure heterogeneity. Following established practices (Kim & Rush, 2016; Zhou et al.,
      2023; Cai et al., 2024; Yi et al., 2024), the training data for these drafters is generated via self-
356
      distillation from the target LLM. For white-box SD, we integrate Eagle (Li et al., 2024) into the
357
      target Vicuna 7B to enable white-box SD. Similar to the black-box setting, multiple Eagle drafters
358   share the same underlying architecture but are fine-tuned on distinct task-specific datasets generated
359   via self-distillation from the target LLM. To ensure a fair comparison for the baseline, we introduce
360   the One-size Fits All (OFA) drafter, which is trained on a mixed dataset spanning all tasks. Further
361   details on the training procedures and datasets used for both black-box and white-box drafters are
362   provided in Appendix F.
363
364   Number of drafts Nmax For black-box SD, we employ speculative sampling (SpS) (Chen et al.,
365   2023), generating one draft candidate per drafter, termed as MetaSpS. For multi-draft methods like
366   Medusa (Cai et al., 2024) and Eagle (Li et al., 2024), we adhere to their original settings with a
367
      tree-attention mechanism. We employ the same tree structure for multiple Eagle drafters described
      in Li et al. (2024), termed as MetaEagle. Unless explicitly stated otherwise, all approaches utilize a
368
      maximum of 5 drafts (Nmax = 5).

509     5                D ISCUSSION
510
511     Regret upper bound for MetaSD-UCB Theorem 2 provides a regret upper bound for MetaSD-
512     UCB, demonstrating that the number of rounds required to identify the optimal drafter is inversely
513     proportional to the predefined draft length Nmax . This aligns with the intuition that longer drafts
514     provide more information about the relative performance of each drafter, leading to faster conver-
515
        gence towards the optimal choice. The logarithmic dependence on the target sequence length B
        further highlights the efficiency of MetaSD-UCB in minimizing regret. These theoretical guarantees
516
        are supported by our empirical observations, where MetaSD-UCB consistently demonstrates strong
517
        performance and rapid convergence towards the best-performing drafter.
518
519
        Memory bandwidth bound A potential concern with our MetaSD framework is the increased
520
        memory bandwidth requirement due to loading multiple drafter models. However, our approach
521
        incurs minimal memory overhead. By storing all drafter weights in GPU DRAM, we avoid frequent
522     accesses to slower system memory, which are a primary bottleneck for LLMs. For instance, with
523     a 7B target LLM and float16 precision, our MetaEagle framework utilizes at most 19GB of GPU
524     DRAM during generation, compared to 17GB for a single Eagle drafter. This represents only a small
525     increase in memory usage, and importantly, it does not increase the memory bandwidth requirement
526     during inference since only one drafter is active at a time.

530
531
        In this paper, we introduce a unified framework for incorporating multiple drafters into speculative
        decoding, addressing the limitations of single-drafter approaches. We formalize this problem as a
532
        multi-armed bandit problem, termed as MetaSD, and proposed MetaSD-UCB, a novel algorithm that
533
        leverages the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) principle to dynamically select the optimal drafter at
534     each generation step. We also provide theoretical guarantees on the performance of MetaSD-UCB,
535     establishing its effectiveness in achieving near-optimal speedup even with a stochastic number of
536     rounds. Through extensive experiments on diverse and multilingual tasks, we demonstrate the su-
537     perior performance of MetaSpS and MetaEagle compared to both specialized drafters and other
538     state-of-the-art methods. Our work opens up new avenues for further research in speculative de-
539     coding, including exploring more sophisticated reward designs, incorporating switching costs, and
        addressing non-stationary environments.

944   D     P RELIMINARY: SPECULATIVE SAMPLING
945
946   Speculative decoding accelerates LLM inference by employing a smaller draft model to predict
947   future tokens, which are then verified by the target LLM. This parallel token generation can signifi-
948   cantly reduce latency, especially when the draft model’s predictions align well with the target LLM’s
949   output distribution.
950
      Algorithm 3 outlines the speculative sampling procedure (Leviathan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023).
951
      Given an initial prompt sequence, the draft model generates E potential future tokens. Concurrently,
952   the target LLM computes the probabilities of these tokens, as well as the probability of its own pre-
953   diction for each subsequent token position. A drafted token is accepted if its probability, according
954   to the target LLM, exceeds a certain threshold. This threshold is determined by comparing the target
955   LLM’s probability for the drafted token to both the draft model’s prediction and a random sample,
956   ensuring only high-confidence drafts are accepted. If a drafted token is rejected, the target LLM
957   samples a token from the residual distribution, which represents the difference between its own
958   prediction and the draft model’s. This process iterates until the desired sequence length is reached.
959   Speculative sampling allows the target LLM to process multiple tokens in parallel by drafting them
960   in advance, reducing the overall generation time. When the draft model’s predictions are accurate,
961   a significant portion of the generated tokens are accepted, leading to substantial speedup. The veri-
962   fication step and residual sampling ensure that the final generated sequence remains consistent with
963   the target LLM’s distribution, preserving generation quality. Speculative sampling provides a foun-
964   dation for our proposed framework, where we extend this approach to incorporate multiple drafters
965   and dynamically select the optimal one using MAB algorithms.

1007   E.2    BANDIT ALGORITHMS
1008
1009   Multi-armed bandit Multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem has been extensively studied for
1010   decades with various settings. For stochastic MAB setting, Lai & Robbins (1985) and Agrawal
1011   (1995) provided asymptotic optimal regret bounds that is logarithmic to the total round T and Auer
1012   (2002); Audibert et al. (2007) and Honda & Takemura (2010) proved this result also holds when T
1013   is finite. For another variant, EXP3 algorithm (Auer et al., 2002) proves the optimal regret bound
1014
       in adversarial environment where reward distribution of each arm can change by adversary in every
       round.
1015
1016
       Budgeted bandit The budgeted MAB problem address a bandit scenario where each arm pull
1017
       yields both a reward and a cost drawn from individual distributions. Here, the goal is to maximize
1018   the cumulative reward until sum of the cost reaches the budget. Then, the optimal arm would be the
1019   one with the highest reward-to-cost ratio. ϵ-First policies (Tran-Thanh et al., 2010) and KUBE (Tran-
1020   Thanh et al., 2012) assumed a non-stochastic fixed cost for each arm pull. Ding et al. (2013) provided
1021   UCB-BV algorithm where cost for each arm is assumed to be a bounded discrete random variable.
1022
1023   Bandits with switching costs In real-world scenarios, a cost may be incurred whenever switching
1024   arms. This is related to the MAB problem with switching costs. (Dekel et al., 2014; Gao et al.,
1025   2019; Rouyer et al., 2021; Esfandiari et al., 2021; Amir et al., 2022). For stochastic MAB, Gao et al.
       (2019) and Esfandiari et al. (2021) assume a fixed cost is incurred whenever switching arms. They


                                                                 19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       proved an instance-dependent regret bound O(log T ) which does not depend on the unit switching
1027   cost value.
1028
1029   Pure exploration Pure exploration or best arm identification (BAI) problems (Even-Dar et al.,
1030   2002; 2006; Audibert & Bubeck, 2010) aim to explore as much as possible throughout the round to
1031   obtain the best arm at the end of the round. This contrasts with the traditional MAB objective which
1032   is maximizing cumulative reward. Even-Dar et al. (2002); Mannor & Tsitsiklis (2004) and Even-
1033   Dar et al. (2006) investigated pure exploration in MAB under the PAC learning framework. BAI
1034   problems are primarily categorized into two settings. First, in the fixed budget setting (Audibert
1035   & Bubeck, 2010; Karnin et al., 2013; Carpentier & Locatelli, 2016), the goal is to minimize the
1036   chance of selecting sub-optimal arms within a fixed number of rounds. The other problem targets
1037
       fixed confidence setting (Karnin et al., 2013; Jamieson et al., 2014; Garivier & Kaufmann, 2016;
       Chen et al., 2017) whose objective is to minimize number of rounds required to achieve a desired
1038
       confidence level.
1039
1040
       Non-stationary bandit Non-stationary bandit problems assume that reward distribution of each
1041
       arm changes over time. The goal in non-stationary bandit problems is to find a balance between
1042   exploration and exploitation while carefully managing past information to adapt to the dynamic
1043   environment. Among the earliest works, Gittins (1974) assumed that only the best arm changes over
1044   time. This assumption was later relaxed in Whittle (1988), where the authors allow the mean reward
1045   for each arm to change at every round. Slivkins & Upfal (2008) assumed reward distribution follows
1046   a Brownian motion and established a regret upper bound that grows linear in rounds. Another
1047   line of works quantifies the degree of non-stationarity in the bandit instance by assuming a fixed
1048   value of L which represents a number of times reward distributions change. Auer et al. (2002)
1049   suggested EXP3.S algorithm and proved regret upper bound with given L but slightly worse when
1050
       L is not given. Kocsis & Szepesvári (2006) suggested Discounted-UCB, where they obtain reward
       estimates with discounting factor over time. Garivier & Moulines (2011) introduced Sliding-window
1051
       UCB, where they used fixed-size window to retain information of the rounds within the window for
1052
       estimating mean reward. ADSWITCH in Auer et al. (2019) is proven to be nearly minimax optimal,
1053   achieving the state-of-the art regret bound without any prior knowledge of L.
1054
1055
       E.3   L ARGE LANGUAGE MODELS AND BANDITS
1056
1057   Recently, several works have made connections between LLMs with bandits using the emergent
1058   abilities of LLMs. One side of works utilize LLM as an agent to solve decision making problems
1059   combining with bandit framework (Baheri & Alm, 2023; Felicioni et al., 2024; Xia et al., 2024a;
1060   Park et al., 2024). On the otherside, some of the works use bandit algorithms for improve the
1061   performance guarantee of LLMs with certain tasks such as for efficient prompt optimization (Shi
1062   et al., 2024) and online model selection (Xia et al., 2024c).
1063   Most relevant to ours, several concurrent works investigate how bandit framework can be incorpo-
1064   rated into SD. Liu et al. (2024) used Thomson sampling algorithm (which is one of the most popular
1065   bandit algorithm) to adaptively choose maximum candidate length Nmax combining with early-exit
1066   framework. Huang et al. (2024) assumed existence of multiple drafters and formulate SD as a con-
1067   textual bandit problem. However, they rely on collecting offline samples for the policy learning
1068   which can be costly. Furthermore, their approach is regarded as a classification problem that the
1069
       selected drafter is fixed in a single query. To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first to
       use MAB framework within every speculation round and provide its theoretical guarantees.

2032
2033   Our MetaSD framework is universal as it can incorporate various bandit algorithms tailored for dif-
2034   ferent scenarios. However, establishing optimality guarantees for existing algorithms in this frame-
2035
       work requires careful analysis or one should look for the different algorithm designs. This is due to
       two key distinctions in our problem formulation: (i) stochastic stopping time, and (ii) a new regret
2036
       objective defined in terms of this stopping time (Definition 2).
2037
2038   This section explores two distinct scenarios and introduces possible algorithms for each. First, we
2039   address a scenario when switching costs is not negligible anymore. In MetaSD framework, this
2040   happens when substantial computational or memory overhead is incurred when changing drafters.
2041
       Second, we consider non-stationary environment where the characteristics of the context change
       within a one generation. Finally, we briefly discuss on other possible extensions of our framework.

2129   prompt: ‘Please summarize and reason about the following article on climate change...’. Initially, a

       I.1      I S SCALING UP DRAFTER SIZE ALWAYS BETTER ?
2239
       While increasing the drafter size might seem like a straightforward path to improved performance,
2240   it can be less efficient than our MetaSD approach, especially considering memory bandwidth con-
2241   straints. Larger models demand more memory for storing weights and activations, increasing data
2242   movement between memory and processing units. This can become a bottleneck, particularly in
2243   high-performance computing where memory bandwidth is often a limiting factor. It is also dis-
2244   cussed in Yi et al. (2024) in SD scenarios. Moreover, this phenomenon is well-illustrated by the
2245   roofline model, which highlights the trade-off between computational intensity and memory band-
2246   width (Cai et al., 2024). As model size increases, computational intensity might improve, but the
2247   memory bandwidth demands can quickly limit overall speedup.
2248   In contrast, MetaSD utilizes multiple smaller drafters with lower individual memory requirements.
2249   By efficiently switching between these drafters, MetaSD can achieve comparable or superior perfor-
2250   mance to a single large drafter while mitigating the memory bandwidth bottleneck. This is because,
2251   despite having multiple drafters, MetaSD only utilizes one drafter for computation at any given time.
2252   Thus, the memory bandwidth requirement does not scale with the combined size of all drafters, but
2253   rather with the size of the individual drafter being used. Provided sufficient GPU DRAM, this ap-
2254
       proach does not have any bottleneck compared to the single drafter SD. Furthermore, MetaSD offers
       the flexibility to incorporate diverse drafters with specialized capabilities. This specialization can
2255
       be more effective than simply increasing the size of a single general-purpose drafter, particularly for
2256
       tasks demanding domain-specific knowledge.

       I.2      C OMPUTATIONAL OVERHEAD ANALYSIS
2260
       Training overhead While specialization may require additional training efforts compared to an
2261
       OFA (One-size-Fits-All) drafter, we emphasize that our approach is designed to handle real-world
2262   scenarios where heterogeneous drafters already exist in public repositories. MetaSD focuses on
2263   optimizing the utilization of such heterogeneous drafters, dynamically selecting the most suitable
2264   drafter during inference. This shifts the problem from retraining models to developing an effective
2265   strategy for utilizing pre-existing resources. Therefore, while training specialized drafters may in-
2266   volve additional costs in certain cases, the broader applicability and versatility of MetaSD provide
2267   substantial practical value. Additionally, the cost of training drafters is a general challenge shared
       across the speculative decoding research domain, not limited to our work.


                                                          42
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2268
       Inference memory-bandwidth efficiency The inference memory-bandwidth efficiency of
2269   MetaSD remains comparable to single-drafter methods. Although MetaSD employs multiple
2270   drafters, the additional memory requirements are minimal. Specifically, MetaSD increases DRAM
2271   usage by only 2 GB (from 17 GB to 19 GB), as the drafters’ weights are preloaded into DRAM.
2272   However, this does not affect VRAM bandwidth, as only the active drafter interacts with VRAM
2273   during inference. As a result, the VRAM bandwidth demands remain identical to those of single-
2274   drafter methods. This efficient memory management ensures that MetaSD maintains competitive
2275   performance without introducing significant overhead.
2276   By ensuring that only the active drafter interacts with the VRAM, MetaSD maintains parity with
2277   single-drafter approaches in terms of VRAM bandwidth demands.
2278
2279   Serving complexity Using multiple drafters in MetaSD does not inherently increase serving com-
2280   plexity. Modern distributed systems already employ model parallelism techniques to allocate work-
2281   loads across multiple GPUs effectively. In MetaSD, drafters are evenly distributed across GPUs,
2282   with each GPU independently handling its assigned drafter without added coordination costs. This
2283   design ensures the following:
2284
             • Load balancing: Drafters are distributed across GPUs based on their assigned tasks, main-
2285
               taining equivalent complexity to single-drafter systems.
2286
2287         • Minimal communication overhead: MetaSD requires no additional inter-GPU communica-
2288
               tion beyond standard model parallelism setups.
2289
2290
       Justification of overhead The modest increase in DRAM memory usage (+2 GB) and marginal
       training cost for specialized drafters is justified by the significant performance gains achieved
2291
       through adaptive optimization. MetaSD dynamically selects the most suitable drafter for each task,
2292
       consistently outperforming single-drafter methods across diverse scenarios, as highlighted in our
2293   experimental results. Furthermore, MetaSD addresses an important real-world challenge: effec-
2294   tively utilizing publicly available, pre-trained heterogeneous drafters. By providing a generalizable
2295   strategy for optimizing these resources, MetaSD adds practical value beyond specialized retraining,
2296   supporting diverse and evolving task requirements.
