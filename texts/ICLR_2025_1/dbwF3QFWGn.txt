009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Conformal prediction has emerged as an effective strategy for uncertainty quantifi-
012            cation by modifying a model to output sets of labels instead of a single label. These
013            prediction sets come with the guarantee that they contain the true label with high
014            probability. However, conformal prediction typically requires a large calibration
015            dataset of i.i.d. examples. We consider the online learning setting, where examples
016            arrive over time, and the goal is to construct prediction sets dynamically. Departing
017
               from existing work, we assume semi-bandit feedback, where we only observe the
               true label if it is contained in the prediction set. For instance, consider calibrating
018
               a document retrieval model to a new domain; in this setting, a user would only
019
               be able to provide the true label if the target document is in the prediction set of
020            retrieved documents. We propose a novel conformal prediction algorithm targeted
021            at this setting, and prove that it obtains sublinear regret compared to the optimal
022            conformal predictor. We evaluate our algorithm on a retrieval task, an image classi-
023            fication task, and an auction price-setting task, and demonstrate that it empirically
024            achieves good performance compared to several baselines.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Uncertainty quantification is an effective strategy for improving trustworthiness of machine learning
030   models by providing users with a measure of confidence of each prediction to better inform their
031
      decisions. Conformal prediction (Vovk et al., 2005; Tibshirani et al., 2019; Park et al., 2019; Bates
      et al., 2021) has emerged as a promising strategy for uncertainty quantification due to its ability to
032
      provide theoretical guarantees for arbitrary blackbox models. They modify a given blackbox model
033
      f : X → Y to a conformal predictor C : X → 2Y that predicts sets of labels. In the batch setting,
034   it does so by using a held-out calibration dataset to assess the accuracy of f ; it constructs smaller
035   prediction sets if f is more accurate and larger ones otherwise. Then, conformal prediction guarantees
036   that the true label is contained in the prediction set with high probability—i.e.,
037
                                                P[y ∗ ∈ C(x)] ≥ α,
038
039   where α is the desired coverage rate, and the probability is taken over the random sample (x, y ∗ ) ∼ D
040   and the calibration dataset Z = {(xi , yi )}ni=1 ∼ Dn .
041
      Traditional conformal prediction require the calibration set to consist of i.i.d. or exchangeable samples
042   from the target distribution; furthermore, the calibration set may need to be large to obtain good
043   performance. In many settings, such labeled data may not be easy to obtain. As a motivating setting,
044   we consider a document retrieval problem, where the input x might be a question and the goal is
045   to retrieve a passage y from a knowledge base such as Wikipedia that can be used to answer x;
046   this strategy is known as retrieval-augmented question answering, and can mitigate issues such as
047   hallucinations (Lewis et al., 2020; Shuster et al., 2021; Ji et al., 2023). A common practice is to use a
048   retrieval model trained on a large dataset in a different domain in a zero-shot manner; for instance, a
049   user might use the dense passage retrieval (DPR) model (Karpukhin et al., 2020) directly on their
050
      own dataset without finetuning. Thus, labeled data from the target domain may not be available.
051   In this paper, we propose an online conformal prediction algorithm that sequentially constructs
052   prediction sets Ct as inputs xt are given. We consider semi-bandit feedback, where we only observe
053   the true label yt∗ if it is included in our prediction set (i.e., yt∗ ∈ Ct ). Otherwise, we observe an
      indicator that yt∗ ̸∈ Ct , but do not observe yt∗ . In the document retrieval example, users might

      sequentially provide queries xt to the DPR model, and our algorithm responds with a prediction
055   set Ct of potentially relevant documents. Then, the user selects the ground truth document yt∗ if it
056   appears in this set, and indicates that their query was unsuccessful otherwise.

058
      As in conformal prediction, we aim to ensure that we achieve the desired coverage rate α with high
      probability. Letting Ct∗ denote the optimal prediction sets (i.e., the prediction sets given infinite
059
      calibration data), our algorithm ensures that with high probability, Ct∗ ⊆ Ct on all time steps t. Since
060
      Ct∗ achieves the desired coverage rate with high probability, this property ensures Ct does so as well.
061
062   A trivial solution is to always include all documents in the prediction set. However, this would be
063   unhelpful for the user, who needs to manually examine all documents to identify the ground truth
064
      one. Thus, an additional goal is to minimize the prediction set size. Formally, we consider the
      optimal prediction set Ct∗ in the limit of infinite data, and consider a loss function encoding how
065
      much worse our prediction set Ct is compared to Ct∗ . Then, our algorithm ensures this loss goes
066
      to zero as sufficiently many samples become available—i.e., the prediction sets Ct converge
                                                                                              √     to the

      optimal ones Ct∗ over time. Formally, our algorithm guarantees sublinear regret of Õ( T ).

069   Finally, we empirically evaluate our algorithm on three tasks: image classification, document
070   retrieval, and setting reservation prices in auctions. Our experiments demonstrate that our algorithm
071   generates prediction sets that converge to the optimal ones while maintaining the desired coverage
072
      rate. Moreover, our algorithm significantly outperforms three natural baselines; each baseline either
      achieves worse cumulative expected regret or does not satisfy the desired coverage rate.

074   Contributions. We formalize and solve the problem of online conformal prediction with semi-bandit
075   feedback. Our algorithm constructs compact prediction sets, ensuring high coverage probability. The
076   algorithm also provides an efficient method for collecting large datasets that are expensive to label.
077   Instead of asking the user to select the ground truth label from the set of all candidate labels, our
078
      approach only requires the user to select from a subset. This efficiency gain can be substantial when
      the label space is large. We assess the algorithm’s performance on image classification, document
079
      retrieval and reservation price-setting in auctions, showcasing its effectiveness in a wide variety of
080
      real world applications, which highlights the practical utility of our approach.

082   Related work. Recent work has studied conformal prediction in the online setting (Gibbs and Candes,
083   2021; Bastani et al., 2022; Gibbs and Candès, 2022; Angelopoulos et al., 2024a;b). These approaches
084
      are motivated by conformal prediction for time series data, where the labels can shift in complex and
      potentially adversarial ways. As a consequence, they make very different assumptions. In particular,
085
      they allow for adversarial rather than i.i.d. assumptions; however, almost all of them assume that the
086
      ground truth label is observed at every step, regardless of whether it is contained in the prediction
087   set. For example, the ACI algorithm proposed in Gibbs and Candes (2021) requires observing yt∗ at
088
      every step since it is needed to update the quantile function Q̂t (·), which is needed to compute the
089
      prediction set Ĉt (αt ) as well as the loss and gradient update. As the authors point out, they do not
090
      need to update the quantile function at every step. However, the steps where they update Q̂t cannot
091
      be chosen in a way that depends on yt∗ , since doing so would lead to a biased estimate of the quantile
092
      function. In our experiments, we demonstrate that in the semi-bandit feedback setting, updating Q̂t
093
      when yt∗ is in the prediction set can create such a bias that leads ACI to fail to achieve coverage.
094
095   Similarly, the SAOCP algorithm proposed by Bhatnagar et al. (2023) requires observing yt∗ at every
096   step to construct the prediction set St = inf{s ∈ R : yt∗ ∈ Ĉt (Xt , s)}, which is then used to compute
097   the loss and gradient, and the Conformal PID control algorithm proposed by Angelopoulos et al.
098   (2024a) uses yt∗ to compute the score st = st (xt , yt∗ ), which is needed to compute the gradient. In
099
      contrast, we are motivated by the active learning setting, where users are interested in constructing
      conformal predictors on-the-fly rather than providing a calibration set ahead-of-time. Thus, in our
100
      setting, it is reasonable to assume that the data arrives i.i.d.; however, we need to handle semi-bandit
101
      feedback since the user may be unable to provide the true label yt∗ if it is not in the prediction set.
102
103   Recently, Angelopoulos et al. (2024b) has pointed out that ACI still works if we take the “quantile
104   function” to be the identity function—i.e., Q̂t (α) = α. This strategy achieves the desired coverage
105   rate without requiring observing the ground truth labels yt∗ ; instead, they only need to observe whether
106   yt∗ is contained in the prediction set—i.e., 1(yt∗ ∈ Ĉt ). However, because they forego estimating the
107   quantile function, the resulting algorithm is extremely sensitive to the structure of the scoring function
      as well as their choice of hyperparameters (in particular, the learning rate). In our experiments, we

      show that a standard variation the scoring function (in particular, using logits instead of prediction
109   probabilities) causes their performance to significantly degrade.

111   Angelopoulos et al. (2024b) also point out an additional shortcoming of ACI, which is that it does
112
      not guarantee that prediction sets converge to the “optimal” ones in the i.i.d. setting. As noted
      by Bastani et al. (2022), this issue is reflected in the fact that achieving α coverage (the guarantee
113
114
      satisfied by the ACI algorithm) can be achieved by the “cheating” strategy that outputs Ĉt = Y with
115
      probability 1 − α and Ĉt = ∅ with probability α. To remedy this issue, Angelopoulos et al. (2024b)
      proposes a modification to their algorithm that is guaranteed to converge to the optimal threshold in
116
      the setting of i.i.d. observations. However, their result is asymptotic (in addition to suffering from the
117
      sensitivity of the scoring function discussed above). Under certain assumptions, we prove that our
118   algorithm satisfies a much stronger regret guarantee; to the best of our knowledge, this is the first
119   regret guarantee on the performance of the prediction sets in terms of convergence of τt to τ ∗ .
120
121
      An additional advantage of our algorithm is that it guarantees that τt ≤ τ ∗ with high probability.
      This guarantee mirrors the distinction between marginal guarantees (Vovk et al., 2005) and probably
122
      approximately correct (PAC) (or training conditional) guarantees in the batch setting (Vovk, 2012;
123
      Park et al., 2019). Marginal guarantees have the form PZ∼Dn ,(x,y∗ )∼D [y ∗ ∈ CZ (x)] ≥ α, i.e., α
124
      coverage over randomness in both the calibration set Z and the new example (x, y ∗ ). They have
125   the additional advantage that coverage converges to α with the number of calibration examples. In
126   contrast, PAC guarantees disentangle these two sources of randomness:
127
                                  PZ∼Dn [P(x,y∗ )∼D [y ∗ ∈ CZ (x)] ≥ α] ≥ 1 − δ,
128
129   for a given δ ∈ R>0 . In other words, coverage holds with high probability over Z. Our guarantee that
130   τt ≤ τ ∗ is equivalent to providing the coverage guarantee P(x,y∗ )∼D [y ∗ ∈ Cτt (x)] ≥ α for every t
131
      with probability at least 1 − δ over the whole time horizon. To the best of our knowledge, existing
      online conformal prediction algorithms all provide a marginal coverage guarantee (it is not even
132
      clear how a PAC guarantee would look in the adversarial setting since the calibration examples are
133
      not random). For the active learning setting, a PAC guarantee makes sense since it ensures that our
134   algorithm satisfies the desired coverage rate for every user-provided input. Finally, while we do not
135   provide an explicit guarantee that the coverage rate converges to α, our regret bound ensures that
136   τt → τ ∗ , which effectively ensures convergence to an α coverage rate.

274   Image classification task. We use the Vision Transformer (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) model on the
275   ImageNet dataset (Deng et al., 2009).1 Each image from the dataset belongs to exactly one class
276   out of the 1,000 candidate classes. Consequently, the cardinality of the label domain is 1,000 (i.e.,
277   |Y| = 1000). In the experiments, images arrive sequentially, and our algorithm aims to construct the
278   smallest candidate label set that achieves the desired coverage. We use the logits score returned by

      a ViT model (pretrained on the ImageNet training set) as our scoring function f ; we use the logits
      instead of the softmax function to evaluate sensitivity to the choice of scoring function.

420   undercover until τt gets significantly closer to the true τ ∗ .

427
428   We have proposed a novel conformal prediction algorithm for constructing online prediction sets under
429   stochastic semi-bandit feedback. We have shown our our algorithm can be applied to learn optimal
430   prediction sets in image classification, document retrieval, and second-price auction reservation
431   price prediction. Our experiments demonstrate that we maintain the desired α coverage level while
      achieving prediction set sizes that achieve sublinear regret and zero undercoverage count.
