                                                       A BSTRACT
012
013            Accurate traffic prediction faces significant challenges, necessitating a deep understand-
014            ing of both temporal and spatial cues and their complex interactions across multiple vari-
015
               ables. Recent advancements in traffic prediction systems are primarily due to the devel-
               opment of complex sequence-centric models. However, existing approaches often embed
016
               multiple variables and spatial relationships at each time step, which may hinder effec-
017
               tive variable-centric learning, ultimately leading to performance degradation in traditional
018            traffic prediction tasks. To overcome these limitations, we introduce variable-centric and
019            prior knowledge-centric modeling techniques. Specifically, we propose a Heterogeneous
020            Mixture of Experts (TITAN) model for traffic flow prediction. TITAN initially consists
021            of three experts focused on sequence-centric modeling. Then, designed a low-rank adap-
022            tive method, TITAN simultaneously enables variable-centric modeling. Furthermore, we
023            supervise the gating process using a prior knowledge-centric modeling strategy to ensure
024            accurate routing. Experiments on two public traffic network datasets, METR-LA and
025            PEMS-BAY, demonstrate that TITAN effectively captures variable-centric dependencies
026
               while ensuring accurate routing. Consequently, it achieves improvements in all evaluation
               metrics, ranging from approximately 4.37% to 11.53%, compared to previous state-of-the-
027
               art (SOTA) models. The code will be released upon acceptance.

      1    INTRODUCTION
032
033
      Traffic prediction involves forecasting future traffic conditions based on historical data collected from sen-
      sors(Jiang et al., 2021), a task that has garnered significant attention in recent years(Jin et al., 2024). Highly
034
      accurate traffic predictions can provide valuable guidance to decision-makers, enhance safety and conve-
035
      nience for citizens, and reduce environmental impact(Jin et al., 2022; Cai et al., 2020). Moreover, with the
036   rapid advancement of artificial intelligence, autonomous driving technologies will also benefit from precise
037   and timely traffic flow forecasts(Guo & Jia, 2022).
038
039
      Traffic data is predominantly spatio-temporal(Yuan & Li, 2021), indicating that it is intrinsically linked to
      the spatial location of sensors while also exhibiting temporal variations, thereby demonstrating considerable
040
      spatio-temporal heterogeneity. This characteristic makes certain methods that excel in conventional time se-
041
      ries forecasting, such as Support Vector Regression (SVR)(Awad et al., 2015), Random Forest (RF)(Rigatti,
042   2017), and Gradient Boosting Decision Trees (GBDT)(Ke et al., 2017), less effective in spatio-temporal
043   prediction tasks. In recent years, the latest advancements in spatio-temporal forecasting have witnessed the
044   rise of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) as powerful tools for modeling non-Euclidean spaces(Wu et al.,
045   2020a; Zhou et al., 2020). Following this direction, these methods can generally be categorized based on
046   how the graph is defined: models based on predefined spatio-temporal graphs(Yu et al., 2018), models uti-


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


047
      lizing learnable graph embeddings(Wu et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2023), and models with time-varying graph
048
      structures(Hong et al.; Lee et al., 2022).
049
050   Early spatio-temporal models utilized predefined graphs based on Tobler’s First Law of Geography (Miller,
051   2004) or on similarities in temporal proximity and spatial attributes (e.g., Points of Interest, POI) (Horozov
052
      et al., 2006; Liu et al., 2020) to model the relationships between nodes. While these graphs provide valuable
      prior knowledge, they often fail to capture the true dependencies between nodes, and incomplete data links
053
      can lead to omitting critical relationships. GraphWaveNet (Wu et al., 2019) addressed these limitations by
054
      introducing learnable graph embeddings, inspiring models such as RGDAN (Fan et al., 2024) and MTGNN
055   (Wu et al., 2020b). MegaCRN (Hong et al.) further improved performance by leveraging memory networks
056   for graph learning, building on the concept of learnable graph embeddings. However, these models remain
057   largely sequence-centric, which limits their ability to effectively learn variable-centric representations, ulti-
058   mately affecting their performance in traffic prediction. Recent studies have recognized this limitation and
059   highlighted the importance of variable-centric representations. For instance, Gao et al. (2024) attempted to
060   balance sequence- and variable-centric modeling through pretraining. While this approach has proven effec-
061   tive, simply combining sequence-centric and variable-centric modeling with weighted averages struggles to
062   adapt to the complexities of real-world scenarios.
063   The concept of Mixture of Experts (MoE) was first proposed by Jacobs et al. (1991) and has since undergone
064   extensive exploration and advancementsShazeer et al. (2017b); Yuksel et al. (2012). In the classical MoE
065   model, multiple experts with identical architectures are employed, and sparse gating is utilized for coarse
066   routing. Applying MoE to spatio-temporal prediction tasks allows the simultaneous use of numerous spatial
067   modeling techniques. Lee & Ko (2024) undertake a preliminary endeavor to apply the MoE model to spatio-
068   temporal sequence prediction, combining the strengths of various experts to model both repetitive and non-
069
      repetitive spatio-temporal patterns jointly. The MoE routing process can be viewed as a memory query,
      which introduces a challenge: the memory is not properly initialized at the early stages of training. This
070
      hinders MoE from learning meaningful relationships between inputs and outputs, particularly for models
071
      with significantly different structures, making it harder to train effectively.
072
073   This paper proposes a MoE model named TITAN, specifically designed for traffic flow prediction. TITAN
074   comprises three different types of experts and a routing mechanism: 1) three sequence-centric prediction
075
      experts, 2) a variable-centric prediction expert, and 3) a prior knowledge-centric leader expert. Sequence-
      centric experts focus on learning temporal dependencies and capturing patterns over time, while variable-
076
      centric experts emphasize cross-variable relationships, ensuring a more comprehensive understanding of
077
      the data. We employ a low-rank matrix to align the knowledge across experts to address the challenge
078   of integrating these heterogeneous experts. Additionally, to mitigate suboptimal routing decisions early in
079   training, we introduce a leader expert who supervises the routing process, ensuring more informed decisions
080   during uncertain situations. Through this adaptive routing mechanism, TITAN is capable of effectively
081   modeling spatio-temporal data. Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
082
083          • We propose TITAN, a novel heterogeneous mixture of experts model that utilizes both sequence-
084            centric and variable-centric experts for spatio-temporal prediction, with a leader expert supervising
085
               the routing process to improve the modeling of complex dependencies.
086          • We integrate models with different backbone networks into the MoE framework using low-rank
087            adaptive matrices, effectively reducing the inductive bias inherent in traditional MoE models. This
088            approach provides a flexible foundation for designing more complex MoE architectures.
089          • We design an expert annealing strategy for MoE’s memory query process, gradually reducing the
090            supervision from the leader expert, allowing TITAN to avoid suboptimal routing decisions early in
091            training and enhance adaptability.
092          • Our model is evaluated on two real-world datasets, achieving improvements of 4.37% to 11.53%
093            (average 9%) compared to state-of-the-art models.

      2.1   T RAFFIC F LOW P REDICTION
097
098   Traffic Flow Prediction tasks exhibit significant spatio-temporal heterogeneity and complex variable interac-
099   tion patterns. Traditional machine learning approaches like Support Vector Regression (SVR)(Awad et al.,
100   2015), Random Forest (RF)(Rigatti, 2017), and Gradient Boosting Decision Trees (GBDT)(Ke et al., 2017),
101   which rely heavily on feature engineering, struggle to capture these intricate interactions. Early spatio-
102   temporal prediction models primarily focused on incorporating spatial information into models through
103   graph structures that could effectively handle non-Euclidean spaces. For example, in 2018, DCRNN(Li
104   et al., 2018) was introduced, injecting graph convolutions into recurrent units, while Yu et al. (2018)com-
105   bined graph convolutions with Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) to model spatial and temporal fea-
106
      tures, achieving better performance than traditional methods like ARIMA(Shumway et al., 2017). Although
      these approaches were effective, they depended significantly on predefined graphs based on Euclidean dis-
107
      tance and heuristic rules (such as Tobler’s First Law of Geography(Miller, 2004)), overlooking the dynamic
108
      nature of traffic (e.g., peak times and accidents). Later work, such as GraphWaveNet(Wu et al., 2019),
109   addressed this limitation by constructing learnable adjacency matrices using node embeddings for spatial
110   modeling. Despite achieving improved results, its ability to capture anomalies remained limited. More
111   recent models like MegaCRN(Jiang et al., 2023b) improved the model’s adaptability to anomalies by inte-
112   grating meta-graph learners supported by meta-node libraries into GCRN encoder-decoder structures. While
113   these models enhanced robustness, they were constrained by independent modeling techniques. This lim-
114   itation has led to growing interest in spatio-temporal prediction models based on MoE structures(Jawahar
115   et al., 2022; Zhou et al.). For instance, TESTAM(Lee & Ko, 2024) integrated three experts with different
116   spatial capturing modules to improve spatio-temporal forecasting performance. However, these studies re-
117
      main sequence-centric, limiting their ability to capture inter-variable relationships effectively. In this paper,
      we aim to address this challenge by jointly modeling both sequence-centric and variable-centric dependen-
118
      cies, allowing for adaptive consideration of both temporal and cross-variable interactions. This approach
119
      provides a more comprehensive view of the data and enhances the ability to model complex spatio-temporal
120   dynamics.

      2.2   M IXTURE OF E XPERTS
124
      The Mixture of Experts (MoE) model, originally proposed by Jacobs et al. (1991), allows individual experts
125   to independently learn from subsets of the dataset before being integrated into a unified system. Build-
126   ing on this concept, Shazeer et al. (2017a) introduced the sparse gated Mixture of Experts (SMoE), which
127   utilizes a gating network for expert selection and implements a top-K routing strategy, selecting a fixed
128   number of experts for each input. Lepikhin et al. (2020) advanced this with Gshard, and Wang et al. (2024)
129   further demonstrated that not all experts contribute equally within MoE models, discarding less important
130   experts to maintain optimal performance. Despite these advancements, MoE models face challenges in
131   spatio-temporal tasks. The early training phase often leads to suboptimal routing, especially when encoun-
132   tering unpredictable events. In such cases, MoE struggles to query and retrieve the appropriate information
133
      from memory, resulting in ineffective routing decisions. While SMoE introduces inductive bias through
      fine-grained, location-dependent routing, it primarily focuses on avoiding incorrect routing and neglects to
134
      optimize for the best paths. Similarly, TESEAM(Lee & Ko, 2024) improves routing by utilizing two loss
135
      functions one to avoid poor paths and another to optimize the best paths for expert specialization but still
136   fails to address the fundamental issue in spatio-temporal predictions. In tasks with high spatio-temporal
137   heterogeneity, such as traffic flow prediction, the MoE model’s reliance on independent expert structures
138   increases inductive bias and reduces overall model performance. Experts with identical structures within
139   MoE introduce a strong inductive bias(Dryden & Hoefler, 2022), further limiting the model’s flexibility
140   and adaptability. Additionally, when models with entirely different structures are involved, MoE struggles

177   3.2      M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
178
179   Although sequence-centric models have shown success in spatio-temporal forecasting, they often struggle
180
      to capture complex variable interactions and are challenging to train, leading to reduced accuracy. Ad-
      ditionally, in MoE models, improper memory initialization during pre-training can result in suboptimal
181
      routing.TITAN overcomes these limitations by incorporating variable-centric and prior knowledge-centric
182
      approaches alongside the traditional sequence-centric method. As shown in Figure 1, TITAN integrates
183   these five experts: 1) three sequence-centric experts (handling sequence-center dependencies in 3.2.1), 2)
184   a variable-centric expert (focusing on variable-center interactions in 3.2.2), and 3) a prior knowledge ex-
185   pert (guiding early-stage routing in 3.2.3). The experts, except for the prior knowledge expert, are based
186   on a lightly modified Transformer architecture, reducing training complexity. The final output is managed
187   through a routing mechanism, ensuring the adaptive selection of experts.

242   3.2.2   VARIABLE - CENTERED
243
244   In sequence-centric modeling approaches, each time step embedding integrates multiple variables represent-
245   ing latent delayed events or different physical measurements. However, this may fail to learn variable-centric
246   representations and could lead to meaningless attention maps. Additionally, all sequence-centric models
247
      share the same inductive bias, which limits the performance of the MOE model. To address these issues,
      we propose a variable-centric modeling approach. Specifically, time points in each time series are embed-
248
      ded into variable-specific tokens, and the attention mechanism leverages these tokens to capture correlations
249
      between different variables. Simultaneously, a feed-forward network is applied to each variable token to
250   learn its nonlinear representation. Unlike sequence-centric models, which generate hidden states based on
251   time steps, the variable-centric model does not share the same hidden state structure, making it difficult to
252   be controlled by the MOE routing mechanism. To resolve this issue, we inject trainable rank decomposition
253   matrices into each layer of the variable-centric model to generate hidden states similar to those in sequence-
254   centric models, thereby reducing inductive bias. For input data Xdata ∈ RT ×N ×F over T historical time
255   steps, we first map the time steps to a higher-dimensional space through a linear embedding. Specifically,
256   the time steps T are transposed and embedded into the high-dimensional space:

417   The ablation study has two goals: to evaluate the actual improvements achieved by each method and to

420

421   w/o Semantics: This variant removes the heterogeneous semantics expert. In this case, TITAN only retains
422   a sequence-centric modeling approach, which can be seen as similar to the TESTAM structure.

451   5   C ONCLUSION
452
453   In this paper, we propose a heterogeneous mixture of expert model (TITAN) that can simultaneously perform
454   sequence-centric, variable-centric, and prior knowledge-centric modeling, incorporating cosine annealing of
455   prior knowledge during the training process. TITAN achieves state-of-the-art performance on two real-
456   world datasets, delivering an average improvement of approximately 9% compared to the previous best
457   baseline. Through ablation experiments, we demonstrated the effectiveness of each module within TITAN.
458   Additionally, we conducted parameter and efficiency analyses to further assess TITAN’s performance. For
459   future work, we aim to introduce more intelligent algorithms, such as heuristic methods, to optimize the
460
      routing process in TITAN and extend its application to multivariate time series forecasting tasks.

      The results of the ablation study, presented in Table 4, strongly support our hypotheses. Below, we provide
728   a more detailed explanation of each ablation variant’s outcomes:
729
730   w/o Semantics: The performance drops when the heterogeneous Semantics expert is removed, indicating
731
      that the use of heterogeneous experts significantly improves predictive accuracy. Without the semantics
      expert, the model behaves similarly to TESTAM, demonstrating that relying solely on sequence-centric
732
      modeling limits the model’s ability to capture complex relationships between variables.
733
734   Ensemble: The results for this variant reveal that the MoE approach, which dynamically selects experts,
735   outperforms a simple ensemble approach where expert outputs are combined through a weighted sum. The
736   lack of routine supervision in the ensemble setup prevents optimal expert specialization, leading to lower
737
      performance. Furthermore, the absence of the a priori expert in this setup shows the importance of including
      prior knowledge in the routing process.
738
739   w/o a priori expert: The performance decrease in this variant highlights the importance of the a priori expert
740   in guiding the routing process. Without prior knowledge to supervise the routing, the model is more prone to
741   suboptimal routing decisions, potentially leading to local minima during training. This supports the idea that
742   incorporating prior knowledge in MoE helps avoid these pitfalls and enhances overall model performance.
743   Replaced a priori expert: Replacing the a priori expert with a sequence-centric prediction module results in
744   lower performance, demonstrating that the mere inclusion of an additional expert is not sufficient to capture
745   prior knowledge effectively. This variant confirms that the MoE model struggles to leverage the added
746   temporal module and underscores the importance of properly utilizing prior knowledge to guide the routing
747   process. Simply adding another expert without a clear distinction in function does not provide the desired
748   performance gains.
749   By analyzing these detailed ablation results, we can conclude that TITAN is an integrated and cohesive
750   model where each component plays a crucial role. Both the low-rank adaptive organization and supervised
751   routing are essential for achieving state-of-the-art performance in traffic prediction.
