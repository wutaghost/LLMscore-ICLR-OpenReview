                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011
                Machine unlearning refers to removing the influence of a specified subset of training
012
                data from a machine learning model, efficiently, after it has already been trained.
013             This is important for key applications, including making the model more accurate by
014             removing outdated, mislabeled, or poisoned data. In this work, we study localized
015             unlearning, where the unlearning algorithm operates on a (small) identified subset
016             of parameters. Drawing inspiration from the memorization literature, we propose
017             an improved localization strategy that yields strong results when paired with
018             existing unlearning algorithms. We also propose a new unlearning algorithm,
019             Deletion by Example Localization (DEL), that resets the parameters deemed-to-be
020             most critical according to our localization strategy, and then finetunes them. Our
021
                extensive experiments on different datasets, forget sets and metrics reveal that DEL
                sets a new state-of-the-art for unlearning metrics, against both localized and full-
022
                parameter methods, while modifying a small subset of parameters, and outperforms
023
                the state-of-the-art localized unlearning in terms of test accuracy too.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Machine unlearning, coined by Cao & Yang (2015), is the problem of removing from a trained
029   model (the influence of) a subset of its original training dataset. While unlearning is a young area of
030   research, it has recently attracted a lot of attention (Triantafillou et al., 2024). Example applications
031   of unlearning include keeping models up-to-date or improving their quality by deleting training data
032   that is identified post-training as being outdated, mislabeled or poisoned.
033
      Unlearning is a challenging problem in deep neural networks since they are highly non-convex,
034   preventing us from easily quantifying the influence of different training examples on the trained
035   weights. As a straightforward solution to unlearning a given “forget set”, one can simply retrain the
036   model from scratch on an adjusted training dataset that excludes that set. This approach implements
037   exact unlearning, guaranteeing that the resulting model has no influence from the forget set. However,
038   this approach can be prohibitively computationally expensive. Instead, a burgeoning area of research
039   has emerged that designs methods to post-process the trained model to attempt to approximately erase
040   the influence of the forget set, efficiently. This post-processing introduces a challenging balancing act,
041   as imperfect attempts at removing some training examples after-the-fact may accidentally damage
042
      the model and overly reduce its utility (e.g. accuracy on the remainder of the training data or
      generalization ability). Therefore, designing successful approximate unlearning methods involves
043
      navigating trade-offs between i) forgetting as well as possible, ii) utility, and iii) efficiency.
044
045   We hypothesize that localized unlearning, where the unlearning algorithm operates on only a (small)
046   subset of the parameters, is a promising avenue for striking a good balance in the above trade-offs.
047   Specifically, modifying a (appropriately chosen) small fraction of the weights may be intuitively less
048
      likely to overly damage the network’s utility (e.g. generalization capabilities) and more likely to be
      efficient, since fewer parameters are subject to modification. However, the success of such a localized
049
      approach hinges on the ability to identify the right subset of parameters to perform unlearning on. In
050
      this work, we take a deep dive into different localization strategies, drawing inspiration specifically
051   from hypotheses formulated about where in the network training data is “memorized” 1 .

079   2     BACKGROUND
080
081   We begin by introducing the notation we will use in this paper and defining key concepts.
082
      Let Dtrain denote a training dataset and A a (possibly randomized) training algorithm. Then, we
083   denote by θo = A(Dtrain ) the parameters obtained by training on Dtrain using A. We will refer to θo
084   as the “original model”, i.e. the model before any unlearning takes place. We will study algorithms
085   for “unlearning” a subset S ⊂ Dtrain , referred to as the forget set. We refer to Dtrain \ S, the remainder
086   of the training data, as the retain set. While different variations are possible, we assume for simplicity
087   that the unlearning algorithm U has access to both the forget set and the retain set.
088
089   2.1   U NLEARNING
090
091   In this section, we define unlearning intuitively in a way that faithfully reflects the standard metrics
092   used in the community that we also adopt for evaluation; see Section A.1 for an alternative definition.
093   Definition 2.1. Unlearning. For a given algorithm A and dataset Dtrain , an algorithm U is said
094   to unlearn a forget set S if the unlearned model U(θo , S, Dtrain \ S) and the “retrained model”
095   A(Dtrain \ S) have the same distribution of outputs on S.
096
097
      The above compares the (distribution of) outputs of the models obtained by two different recipes.
      The first is A(Dtrain \ S), retraining “from scratch” on only the retain set, which is prohibitively
098
      expensive but ideal from the standpoint of eliminating the influence of S on the model. The second is
099
      U(θo , S, Dtrain \ S), applying U to post-process the original model θo in order to unlearn S.
100
101   In the above, we refer to “distributions” of outputs since re-running either of the two recipes with a
102   different random seed, that controls the initialization or the order of mini-batches, for example, would
103
      yield slightly different model weights, thus possibly slightly different outputs too. In our experiments
      considering classification tasks, the “outputs” are the vector of softmax probabilities, and different
104
      metrics consider different elements of that vector, e.g. the accuracy metric requires the “argmax” of
105
      that vector, whereas more sophisticated metrics consider the correct class probability (“confidence”).
106
107   model’s ability to accurately label input data. Such models do not "contain" bit-wise or code-wise copies of their
      training data.

133
      algorithm would have an M IAefficacy score matching that of the retrained-from-scratch model; see
134   Section A.3 for details. In addition, a comprehensive evaluation of unlearning also requires measuring
135   utility, which we measure via test accuracy (and retain accuracy, in Section A.8), and efficiency.

137
      Localized unlearning. In this work, we focus on “localized unlearning” algorithms U that modify
      only a (preferably small) subset of the parameters of θo , leaving the rest unchanged (see Figure 1).
138
      We view localized unlearning as a promising direction as we hypothesize that it can yield better
139
      trade-offs between unlearning efficacy, utility and efficiency, due to modifying fewer parameters.
140
141   A localized unlearning algorithm has two components. The first is a localization strategy L that
142   produces a mask m determining which subset of the parameters should be modified to carry out the
143
      unlearning request: m = L(θo , S) where m is a binary vector specifying whether each parameter
      will be updated. The second component is a unlearning strategy. This, in principle, can be any
144
      unlearning algorithm that, in this case, will operate on only the subset of the parameters indexed by
145
      m. Overall, a localized unlearning algorithm is instantiated by selecting a particular (L, U) pair.

147   2.2     M EMORIZATION
148
149   An intriguing phenomenon is that, despite models exhibiting strong generalization properties, they
150
      still tend to “memorize” some of their training data (Arpit et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2021). In fact,
      recent theories argue that some forms of memorization are in fact necessary for optimal generalization
151
      (Feldman, 2020; Brown et al., 2021; Attias et al., 2024). In the below, we first present a definition of
152
      memorization borrowed from (Feldman, 2020), and then discuss the connections with unlearning.
153
      Definition 2.2. Label memorization. Assume a dataset Dtrain = {(xi , yi )}N      i=1 , where xi and yi
154
      denote the input and label for the i’th training example, and assume a training algorithm A and a
155
      model f (x; θ) parameterized by θ mapping inputs to labels. Then, the memorization score for an
156   example (xi , yi ) ∈ Dtrain (with respect to Dtrain , A and f ) is
157

160   Intuitively, an example is highly memorized by a model if the model can only predict its label correctly
161   when that example is in the training set. This will be primarily the case for atypical, ambiguous or
      mislabeled examples that would not be otherwise correctly predicted (Feldman & Zhang, 2020).

162
      Connections with unlearning. Some forms of memorization and unlearning are intimately con-
163   nected: at the extreme where an example isn’t memorized at all, it can be thought of as being trivially
164   “unlearned” according to some unlearning metrics of interest, because the model’s predictions on
165   that example aren’t different from what they would have been had that example not been included in
166   the training set (“retrain from scratch”). Empirically, considering varying degrees of memorization,
167   Zhao et al. (2024) showed that most approximate unlearning methods are more successful on forget
168   sets which include examples that have lower memorization scores compared to those with higher
169   memorization scores. Relatedly, Jagielski et al. (2022) study catastrophic forgetting during training;
170   a phenomenon that can be characterized as reduced memorization of an example in later stages of
171
      training, that can be interpreted as a passive form of unlearning. They find that, when training on
      large datasets, examples that were only seen early in training may be less memorized, which they
172
      quantify via the failure rates of privacy attacks aiming to extract examples or infer whether they were
173
      used for training. Toneva et al. (2018) find that examples with noisy labels witness a larger number
174   of “forgetting events” during training, defined as an event where an example that was previously
175   correctly predicted becomes incorrectly predicted later in training. Based on these insights on the
176   strong connection of memorization and unlearning, we ask: does knowledge (or assumptions) of
177   where in the network a forget set is memorized give rise to improved unlearning for that forget set?
178
      Localizing memorization. Investigating the above question is a challenging undertaking, as pin-
179
      pointing where memorization occurs is in and of itself a research problem. Baldock et al. (2021)
180   define the “prediction depth” for an example to be the earliest layer in the network after which the
181   example is correctly predicted. They find that mislabeled examples are only predicted correctly in the
182   final few layers of the model, and conclude that “early layers generalize while later layers memorize”.
183   Stephenson et al. (2021) draw the same conclusion through a study of manifold complexity and
184   shattering capability. However, Maini et al. (2023) found that the parameters that memorize specific
185   examples are actually scattered throughout the network and not concentrated in any individual layer.
186

      3    R ELATED W ORK
188
189   Unlearning. Cao & Yang (2015) coined the term unlearning and proposed exact algorithms for
190
      statistical query learning. Bourtoule et al. (2021); Yan et al. (2022) propose frameworks that
      support exact unlearning in deep networks more efficiently by considering architectures with many
191
      components, where one only needs to retrain the affected parts of the model for each unlearning
192
      request. However, in the worst case, efficiency can be as poor as in naive retraining and, furthermore,
193   these specialized architectures may have lower accuracy compared to state-of-the-art ones. Instead, a
194   plethora of approximate unlearning methods (Ginart et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2019; Golatkar et al.,
195   2020a;b; Thudi et al., 2022) were developed that operate on an already-trained model to remove
196   the influence of the forget set. Simple commonly-used baselines include finetuning the model on
197   only the retain set (“Finetune”), or on only the forget set using the negated gradient (“NegGrad”),
198   or combining these two in a joint optimization “NegGrad+” (Kurmanji et al., 2024), performing
199   gradient descent on the retain set and ascent on the forget set, simultaneously. One could also apply a
200   joint optimization using gradient descent on both the retain and forget sets, after having first randomly
201
      relabelled the examples in the forget set (“Random Label”) (Graves et al., 2021; Fan et al., 2023).
      SCRUB (Kurmanji et al., 2024) builds on NegGrad+ by casting unlearning as a teacher-student
202
      problem and using distillation. Liu et al. (2024) show that sparsity aids unlearning and that adding
203
      an L1-penalty to the Finetune baseline improves its performance (“L1-sparse”). One could also
204   utilize influence function analysis (Koh & Liang, 2017) to mitigate the impact of the forget data by
205   estimating the importance of the model weights (“IU”) (Izzo et al., 2021).
206
207
      Localized unlearning. Goel et al. (2022) propose baselines that perform unlearning on only the k
      deepest (closest to the output) layers and either simply finetune them (“CF-k”) or reinitialize them
208
      and then finetune them (“EU-k”). The contemporaneous work of Foster et al. (2024) (“SSD”) uses
209
      the Fisher information matrix to identify parameters that are disproportionately important to the
210   forget set and apply unlearning on those. Fan et al. (2023) proposes Saliency Unlearning (“SalUn”),
211   which selects a subset of critical parameters by considering the gradients of the forget set and applies
212   unlearning (by default using Random Label) on the identified parameters. We will later compare
213   this state-of-the-art method to other strategies inspired by the memorization literature, and propose a
214   novel strategy that outperforms previous work. Related ideas have been proposed in the context of
215   unlearning in LLMs (Hase et al., 2024; Guo et al.), though we note that the problem formulation of
      LLM unlearning as well as the architectures and algorithms used there are substantially different and

220   4   D ERIVING AND INVESTIGATING LOCALIZED UNLEARNING BASED ON
221       MEMORIZATION HYPOTHESES
222
223   In this section, we derive localization strategies from hypotheses in the memorization literature and
224   investigate their performance in the context of localized unlearning compared to existing approaches.
225   Based on the discussion in Section 2.2, we consider two hypotheses for where memorization occurs,
226   and we then turn each one of them into a localization strategy L(θo , S). The two hypotheses are: i)
227   memorization happens in the “deepest layers”, i.e. closest to the output, consistent with (Baldock
228   et al., 2021; Stephenson et al., 2021), and ii) memorization of an example is confined to a small set of
229   channels, scattered across the network, whose location depends on the example (Maini et al., 2023).
230   We compare the unlearning performance of four localization strategies: i) Deepest and ii) CritMem,
231   derived based on the above two hypotheses, iii) selecting the shallowest rather than deepest layers
232   (“Shallowest”), as a control experiment, and iv) the localization strategy of SalUn (Fan et al., 2023)
233   (“SalLoc”) that is not motivated through the lens of memorization but is regarded as state-of-the-art
234   in the unlearning literature. Note that we reserve the name “SalUn” for the pairing of SalLoc with the
235   Random Label unlearning algorithm, which is the choice that Fan et al. (2023) found worked best
236
      (we mix-and-match localization strategies with unlearning algorithms in Section 5).
237   Deepest returns a mask that allows only the k deepest (closest to output) layers to be updated during
238   unlearning, based on the first hypothesis. If paired with an unlearning algorithm that resets the chosen
239   parameters and then finetunes them, this corresponds to the EU-k method of (Goel et al., 2022).
240   CritMem is based on the second hypothesis and implemented via the algorithm of Maini et al. (2023).
241   Given an example, it iteratively finds the deemed-to-be most critical channel, resets all its associated
242   parameters and repeats, until the prediction on that example flips to an incorrect one. The criterion
243   used to identify the next most critical channel at each iteration is obtained by multiplying the weights
244   by their respective gradients on that example, and choosing the channel for which the (sum, over
245   the channel parameters) of the magnitude of this quantity is the highest. We run this algorithm for
246   each example in S independently, record the critical channels identified for each and then consider
247   the union of those as the critical channels for S. The mask is then set to allow to only modify the
248
      parameters involved in those critical channels.
249   Shallowest returns a mask that allows only the k shallowest (closest to the input) layers to be updated
250   during unlearning, as a control experiment for Deepest.
251
      SalLoc sorts all parameters based on the magnitude of gradients over S, in descending order and then,
252   given a threshold α for the percentage of the parameters that might be updated during unlearning, it
253   returns a mask that turns on only the first α percent of elements of that sorted list (Fan et al., 2023).
254
255
      The above strategies differ along several axes. Firstly, Deepest and Shallowest are data-agnostic
      in that they do not use S to inform which parameters to choose, whereas CritMem and SalLoc are
256
      data-dependent and tailor the mask to S. Secondly, there are differences in granularity: Deepest
257
      and Shallowest consider a layer as the unit (i.e. each layer is either included or excluded as a
258   whole). CritMem’s unit is a channel while SalLoc has the finest granularity, considering each
259   individual parameter as a unit. Finally, out of the data-dependent ones, CritMem is substantially more
260   computationally expensive than SalLoc. This is because for each example in S, it applies an iterative
261   approach that resets the next most critical channel, one at a time, until the termination criterion.
262   On the other hand, SalLoc chooses the critical parameters in “one-shot” rather than iteratively and,
263   additionally, operates on batches of S. Both of these aspects make it significantly more efficient.
264   Experimental setup We conduct this investigation on CIFAR-10 using ResNet-18, and a forget set
265   comprised of 10% of the training samples, randomly selected from two classes (2 and 5). We use
266   a simple unlearning algorithm that resets the identified critical parameters (obtained based on each
267   localization strategy), and then finetunes only the identified critical parameters and the classifier
268   layer using only the retain set (“Reset + Finetune”). Since each of the above localization strategies
269   chooses a different set and number of parameters, to ensure fairness, we compare them to one another
      for different “budgets” of how many parameters are updated. For example, for Deepest, we select the

326   As discussed in Section 4, SalLoc has the finest granularity, making a decision for whether or not
327   to select individual parameters, whereas CritMem operates on the level of output channels, thus
328   deciding whether the mask should include or exclude all parameters associated with a channel, as
329   a unit. We hypothesize that, because attempts to locate where memorization occurs are based on
330   heuristics and are imperfect, the easier task of making coarser-grained decisions, as in CritMem,
331   is a more appropriate choice compared to the harder, and thus more error-prone, task of making
332   parameter-level assessments of criticality. Specifically, marking all parameters of a channel as critical,
333   if a subset of them are deemed to be critical, can be seen as useful “smoothing”, at the potential
334
      expense of leading to resetting some more parameters than may have been strictly necessary.
335   Further, we also hypothesize that CritMem’s criticality criterion that uses weighted gradients rather
336   than simply considering the magnitude of gradients themselves (SalLoc) is also more appropriate: it
337   can be seen as a more conservative choice, which is more suitable in light of the heuristic nature of
338   determining criticality. Intuitively, a weight with a small value can be seen as less critical “overall”
339   (for the training set) which is an important signal to consider in addition the gradients on specific
340
      forget set examples, as it may also serve as a useful “regularizer” when making heuristic assessments.

      Given a forget set S and an original model θo with p parameters, for j ∈ {1, . . . , p}, let θjo and
364   gj (θo , S) represent the weight and gradient values on the forget set, respectively. We define the
365   criticality score sj of the j th parameter as the magnitude of the weighted gradient over the forget set:
366
      sj = |θjo · gj (θo , S)|; this is the same criticality criterion used in CritMem, whereas SalLoc simply
367   considers the magnitude of the gradient for each parameter |gj (θo , S)| for assessing its criticality.
368   As discussed above, we choose to determine criticality in a coarser-grained way compared to individ-
369   ual parameters. To that end, for an output “channel” oi (or “neuron” more broadly, encompassing
370   non-convolutional architectures), we describe how to obtain its criticality score coi based on the
371   criticality score of its constituent parameters. Let s̃i be a list of the criticality scores for the parameters
372   belonging to neuron oi , sorted in descending order. We set the neuron criticality coi to be the average
373   of the top h scores of its associated parameters: coi = h1 Σhj=1 s̃i [j].
374   Finally, having obtained the neuron criticality scores, we put together the mask mα for parameter
375   budget α, represented as a binary vector of size p, where a 1 indicates the corresponding parameter
376   will be updated by the unlearning algorithm, whereas an entry of 0 indicates it will be kept unchanged.
377   To this end, we form another sorted list c̃, that sorts the neurons in descending order of their criticality

      So, does memorization inform unlearning? Hase et al. (2024) find that, for model editing in LLMs,
523   the “causal tracing” method (Meng et al., 2022) for knowledge localization, surprisingly, does not
524   indicate which layer to modify in order to most successfully rewrite a stored fact with a new one.
525   That is, they find that success in editing tasks is generally unrelated to localization results based on
526   causal tracing. Guo et al. study whether mechanistic interpretability insights improve unlearning
527   of “factual associations” in LLMs. They also find that localization techniques based on preserving
528   outputs (such as causal tracing) yield performance that is no better, or even worse, than non-localized
529   unlearning. However, they come up with a mechanistic unlearning method that does outperform both
530   output-based localization and non-localized unlearning, showing that some form of localization is
531
      useful. Our results, in the very different context of unlearning a subset of data in vision classifiers,
      offer an important data point in this ongoing discussion. In line with Hase et al. (2024), we find that
532
      directly translating memorization hypotheses into localization strategies does not help unlearning:
533
      Deepest led to very poor unlearning results (demonstrating either its weakness as a memorization
534   locator, or the disconnect between memorization localization and unlearning performance), and
535   CritMem, while showing more promise, did not perform better than simple baselines, while being
536   significantly more expensive than them. However, insights from (Maini et al., 2023), in particular
537   regarding the granularity and criticality criterion used during localization led us to improve upon
538   the state-of-the-art localized and full-parameter unlearning methods, renewing hopes that, while
539   memorization localization and unlearning may be separate research questions, progress in the former
      may guide progress in the latter.

      A.1    U NLEARNING DEFINITION
705
706   In this section, we discuss an alternative formal definition of unlearning, proposed in in Ginart et al.
707   (2019); Neel et al. (2021), using a notion closely related to Differential Privacy (Dwork, 2006).
708
      Definition A.1. Unlearning-2. For a training algorithm A, an algorithm U is an ( , δ)-unlearner if,
709   for any training dataset Dtrain and forget set S, the distributions of A(Dtrain \S) and U(θo , S, Dtrain \S)
710   are ( , δ)-close, where we say two distributions µ, ν are ( , δ)-close if µ(B) ≤ e  ν(B) + δ and
711   ν(B) ≤ e  µ(B) + δ for all measurable events B.
712
713   Intuitively, the above compares (the distribution of) models that are obtained by two different recipes
714   to one another:
715
716
             • A(Dtrain \ S), retraining “from scratch” on only the retain set, which is prohibitively
               expensive but ideal from the standpoint of eliminating the influence of S on the model, and
717
718          • U(θo , S, Dtrain \ S), applying U to post-process the original model θo in order to unlearn S.
719
720
      We desire unlearning algorithms U that cause these two recipes to yield similar models, with the
      second recipe being substantially more computationally-efficient compared to the first, in order to
721
      justify paying the cost of approximate unlearning rather than simply using the first recipe directly.
722
723   Note that we refer to distributions here since re-running either of the two recipes with a different
724   random seed, that controls the initialization or the order of mini-batches, for example, would yield
725   slightly different model weights in each case. The above definition therefore measures unlearning
726
      quality based on the notion of ( , δ)-closeness between the two distributions. The smaller   and δ are,
      indicating increased closeness, the better the unlearning algorithm.
727
728   Relationship and differences to our definition This definition compares distributions in weight
729   space, whereas our Definition 2.1 compares distributions of outputs of models on the forget set. We
730   opted for the latter in the main paper as it more closely reflects the metrics we use for evaluation (which
731   are the standard metrics used in unlearning papers). Note that, even works that adopt definitions in
732
      weight-space end up operationalizing them using outputs of models (Triantafillou et al., 2024) instead
      of performing weight-space comparisons. This is for several reasons: comparing weights of models
733
      directly may be inappropriate since neural networks are permutation-invariant. Weight space is also
734
      much higher dimensional, posing challenges in creating the right metrics, and, finally, ultimately
735   what we may care about for various applications of interest is the “behaviours” (e.g. predictions) of
736   models, rather than their weights. Definition 2.1 captures this more directly.

739
740   Datasets The CIFAR-10 dataset (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) consists of 50, 000 train and 10, 000 test
741   images of shape 32×32 from 10 classes. The SVHN dataset (Netzer et al., 2011) includes 73, 257
742   train and 26, 032 test samples. The samples are of shape 32×32 pixel, and from 10 classes.The
743   ImageNet-100 (Hugging Face version) dataset is a subset of ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), containing
744   126, 689 train and 5, 000 test samples from 100 classes, randomly selected from the original ImageNet
745   classes. The resolution of the images on the shortest side is 160 pixels.
746   We perform no preprocessing or augmentation on the images of CIFAR-10 and SVHN, except
747   dividing the feature values by 255. For ImageNet-100, on the other hand, we randomly crop the
748   train images to size 128×128, and horizontally flip them. Moreover, we first resize the test images to
749   160×160, and then center-crop them to 128×128. We normalize the features of both train and test
750   images with the mean and variance of ImageNet.
751   For the unlearning experiments, we employ two different forget sets: (1) IID, where we uniformly
752   select approximately 10 percent of the images from the train set, and (2) NonIID, in which we
753   randomly select half of the samples from two classes (2 and 5 in CIFAR-10, and 3 and 6 in SVHN)
754   so that the size of the forget set is almost 10 percent of the train set. Note that in the former, the
755   distributions of the samples in the forget and retain sets are highly similar, whereas in the latter, the
      sample distribution of the forget set is very different from that of the retain set.

      Models We capitalize on the original implementation of ResNet-18 and ResNet-50 (He et al.,

767   parameters) than shallower layers.

769   layers as its main building blocks. It first divides the input images into square patches (e.g. of shape
770   8×8) and give them as tokens (after some preprocessing including positional encoding) to the encoder
771   blocks. No downsampling is performed on the input tensors by the encoder blocks. Moreover, all
772   (i.e. both deeper and shallower) encoder blocks have identical number of trainable parameters. The
773   normalization layer of ViT architectures is layer normalization (Ba et al., 2016).

804   A.3    M ETRICS
805
806
      Following (Fan et al., 2023), we employ accuracy and membership inference attack (MIA) efficacy to
      evaluate the effectiveness of different unlearning algorithms.
807
808   To compute MIA efficacy, a support vector classifier (SVC) is trained on top of outputs coming
809   from the unlearned model for the task of predicting whether an example was used in training or
      not. This is performed through supervised learning where the test set is used as “unseen data” and a


                                                              15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      subset of retain set (with the same size as and similar label distribution to the test set) as “seen data”.
811   Specifically, the SVC is trained on the “prediction” outputs (i.e. the integers representing the index
812   of the predicted class), aiming to distinguish predictions from seen versus unseen data. Then, the
813   trained classifier is utilized to predict if each sample in the forget set belongs to the seen or unseen
814   data on the unlearned model. Given that, MIA efficacy is computed as follows:
815
                                                                 TN
816                                             M IAefficacy =       ,
817
                                                                 |S|
818   where TN is the number of true negatives, i.e. forget samples that the classifier recognizes as likely
819   unseen data for the unlearned model, and |S| is the size of the forget set.
820
      Intuitively, a higher value of M IAefficacy means that the unlearned model has been more successful in
821   “fooling” the SVC classifier (i.e. the “membership inference attacker”) into thinking that the forget set
822   was not used in training. However, to interpret how high we expect M IAefficacy to be for an unlearned
823   model, we must consult the reference point of how high this quantity would be for a model retrained
824   from scratch without the forget set. Note that, even in that case of “ideal unlearning”, M IAefficacy is
825   not necessarily 100%, and in fact it can be much lower than this. This is because, some examples in
826   the forget set might be so “easy” that, even without ever seeing them, the retrained model can still be
827   equally accurate on those examples as it would have been if they were actually included in training.
828   This would lead to its M IAefficacy being lower, since some forget set examples would be classified as
829
      “seen” by the SVC. For this reason, in our experiments, we use the reference point as the M IAefficacy
      obtained from retrain-from-scratch as the optimal value for this metric. An ideal unlearning algorithm
830
      would therefore match that value.
