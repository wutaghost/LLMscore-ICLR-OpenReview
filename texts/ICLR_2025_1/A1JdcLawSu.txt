009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural networks face catastrophic forgetting of previously learned knowledge
012            when training on new task data. While the field of continual learning has made
013            promising progress in reducing this forgetting, recent work has uncovered an
014            interesting phenomenon: existing techniques often exhibit a sharp performance
015            drop on prior tasks during the initial stages of new task training, a phenomenon
016            known as the “stability gap.” This phenomenon not only raises safety concerns
017            but also challenges the current understanding of neural network behavior in con-
018
               tinual learning scenarios. Motivated by this discovery, we revisit two fundamental
               questions in continual learning: 1) Is the past learned knowledge within deep
019
               networks lost abruptly or gradually? and 2) Is past learned knowledge ever com-
020
               pletely erased? Our analysis reveals that abrupt forgetting occurs not only in the
021            final fully connected layer but also permeates the feature space and most layers,
022            sparing only the earliest layers. Alarmingly, a single gradient update can severely
023            disrupt the learned class structure. We identify degenerate solutions in the softmax
024            cross-entropy loss as a major contributing factor, with memory samples exhibiting
025            higher feature norms compared to new samples. To address these issues, we pro-
026            pose Adaptive Angular Replay (AAR), a simple yet effective approach that learns
027            features in hyperspherical space using feature and weight normalization. Angular
028            ER demonstrates a strong ability to preserve class structure during task transi-
029
               tions. Additionally, we introduce an adaptive scaling strategy to further mitigate
               the stability gap and improve overall accuracy.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Machine learning has increasingly relied on training large models on static datasets to achieve im-
035
      pressive results, often surpassing human capabilities in a wide range of tasks. However, these tasks
036   are typically confined and static after deployment, reflecting a key limitation of neural network op-
037   timization: the assumption of independent and identically distributed (iid) training and testing data.
038   In real-world scenarios, data is dynamic, continuously evolving with new information arriving at
039   an unprecedented rate, often violating the iid assumption. As a result, neural networks are prone
040   to “catastrophic forgetting” (CF) (French, 1999; Delange et al., 2021), where models abruptly lose
041   previously learned knowledge when exposed to new, non-iid data. In such cases, learning agents
042   face the challenge of absorbing new information efficiently. To address these challenges, fields like
043   continual learning (CL) and lifelong learning have gained significant attention, focusing on reducing
044
      the impact of catastrophic forgetting while adapting to changing data distributions.
045   Research efforts to mitigate catastrophic forgetting have led to various promising solutions, with
046   replay-based methods achieving state-of-the-art performance (Hadsell et al., 2020; Wang et al.,
047   2021). Despite their success, recent studies have revealed an unexpected phenomenon: while
048   rehearsal-based continual learning techniques reduce forgetting, they still exhibit significant perfor-
049   mance drops during the initial phase of training on new tasks. This temporary performance decline,
050
      followed by recovery, is termed the “stability gap”(De Lange et al.). Though transient, the stability
      gap introduces potential risks to continual learning systems and underscores the need for a deeper
051
      understanding of how neural networks behave in CL settings.
052
053   Fundamental Open Questions In this paper, we explore a critical yet unresolved question in the
      literature: Does the knowledge embedded in a neural network degrade abruptly or gradually during


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      continual learning? Empirical studies generally suggest that as more tasks are introduced (Delange
055   et al., 2021; Mai et al., 2022), performance on prior tasks deteriorates, leading to the assumption
056   of gradual knowledge loss. However, these evaluations are typically conducted after each task is
057   fully trained. When performance is monitored at every gradient step, recent findings on stability
058   gap suggest a different dynamic: performance on previous tasks drops sharply in the early stages
059   of training on a new task and only gradually recovers afterward. This abrupt drop raises the possi-
060   bility of sudden knowledge loss during training. Nevertheless, a drop in task performance does not
061   necessarily indicate a complete loss of knowledge across the entire network. Previous works have
062   identified a task-recency bias in the final fully connected (FC) layer (Wu et al., 2019; Mai et al.,
063
      2022; Zhao et al., 2020), which can significantly affect the final performance. One hypothesis is
      that the stability gap is driven by this bias in the FC layer. Some supporting evidence for this is that
064
      when visualizing the training trajectories in the loss landscape, the network parameters drift slowly
065
      from low-loss regions to higher-loss areas as training progresses (Verwimp et al., 2021; Zhang et al.,
066   2022). In summary, although it has been demonstrated there is abrupt loss of task’s performance,
067   whether the core network experiences abrupt or gradual knowledge loss—and to what extent of
068   knowledge retention or loss within the deeper network layers—remains unclear.
069
      Key findings. In this work, we investigate the network’s change dynamics during task transitions to
070
      answer these questions. Our findings reveal that the stability gap extends beyond the final FC layer,
071
      affecting the network’s internal representations. Notably, we show that the stability gap persists
072   even when using a Near-Class-Mean classifier instead of cross-entropy classifier. Centered Kernel
073   Alignment (CKA) analysis reveals abrupt changes in representations in later network layers, while
074   earlier layers experience more gradual and subtle shifts. Crucially, we observe that the class structure
075   in the feature space can be entirely disrupted by just a single gradient step, underscoring the intensity
076   of knowledge loss in the network’s deeper layers.
077
      Proposed Solutions. To mitigate this abrupt loss of class structure and address the stability gap, we
078   identify degenerate solutions in the softmax cross-entropy loss as a key factor. This degeneration
079   leads to much higher feature norms for memory samples compared to the new samples. To address
080   this issue, we propose a simple but effective solution called Adaptive Angular Replay (AAR), which
081   promotes learning in hyper-spherical space using feature and weight normalization. Angular ER
082   preserves the class structure more effectively than prior methods. Additionally, to further reduce the
083   stability gap and improve overall accuracy, we introduce an adaptive scaling strategy that comple-
084   ments Angular replay. Together, these methods significantly enhance the performance of continual
085   learning systems by preserving knowledge more effectively throughout training.
086   Contributions Our contributions are as follows:
087
088          • We provide several insights into knowledge retention and loss in non-stationary data set-
089            tings: 1) The stability gap extends beyond the final FC layer and affects the entire network
090            and feature space. 2) There is a complete loss of class structure in the feature space during
091            task transitions. 3) Knowledge loss in later layers is abrupt, whereas in early layers, it is
092            more gradual.
093
             • We identify degenerate solutions in the cross-entropy loss that result in higher feature
094
               norms, contributing to the loss of class structure.
095
096          • We propose Adaptive Angular Replay, a simple and effective solution to mitigate the stabil-
097            ity gap by learning features in hyperspherical space, complemented by an adaptive scaling
098            factor to further enhance performance.

      3     A NALYSIS : THE NETWORK BEHAVIOR AT TASK TRANSITION
137
138   Understanding how and why the stability gap occurs is crucial not only for practical applications but
139
      also as a scientifically intriguing phenomenon that can deepen our understanding of network learn-
      ing behaviors in the context of non-stationary data distributions. We aim to use this phenomenon as
140
      a lens to explore the processes of information loss and retention during the learning of new informa-
141
      tion. To this end, we present a series of analyses focused on the behavior of the network during task
142   transitions.

      3.1   R EVISIT A SIMPLE BASELINE : N EAREST C LASS M EAN CLASSIFIER
146
      The cross-entropy classifier is the most widely used option in continual learning. In a CE classifier,
147
      the model’s final layer is a fully connected layer with weight matrix W ∈ RD×N , where N is the
148   number of classes. A well-known phenomenon in continual learning, referred to as task-recency
149   bias or biased fully connected layer (Wu et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2020), occurs when the logits
150   output and norm of the weights corresponding to new classes becomes significantly higher than that
151   of old classes. This bias is believed to contribute to catastrophic forgetting. A common explanation
152   attributes this bias to class imbalance, as the exemplar set storing past data is often small, meaning
153   the number of samples for new classes typically exceeds that for old classes.
154   To explore whether the final FC layer causing the stability gap, we revisit the simple baseline of the
155   Nearest-Class-Mean (NCM) classifier, which has been employed in several continual learning stud-

157   classifier, NCM does not rely on a fully connected layer for predictions but instead uses learned
158   features to compute class prototypes from memory samples. Inference is then performed based on
159   the distance between the input and the nearest class prototype. More specifically,   it computes a pro-

      The effect of scaling factor. The scaling factor plays a crucial role in controlling the ”sharpness”

      6     C ONCLUSION
462
463
      Rehearsal-based methods play a central role in fighting catastrophic forgetting when learning from
464
      non-stationary data streams. The phenomenon of stability gap raise question on current understand-
465   ing of how and why rehearsal mitigates forgetting. Our analysis on the internal workings of network
466   knowledge retention and loss reveals that 1) the stability gap is not confined to the final fully con-
467   nected layer but affects the entire network and feature space and 2) there is a complete disruption
468   of class structure in the feature space during task transitions, which can occur after just a single
469   gradient step. To address the stability challenge in continual learning, we have developed Adap-
470   tive Angular Experience Replay (AAR), a novel approach that promotes learning in hyperspherical
471   space. By using feature and weight normalization, Angular ER effectively mitigates the stability
472   gap and preserves class structure more efficiently than existing methods. Furthermore, our proposed
473
