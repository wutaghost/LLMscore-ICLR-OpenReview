011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Image quality plays an important role in the performance of deep neural networks
014            (DNNs) and DNNs have been widely shown to exhibit sensitivity to changes
015
               in imaging conditions. Large-scale datasets often contain images under a wide
               range of conditions prompting a need to quantify and understand their underlying
016
               quality distribution in order to better characterize DNN performance and robustness.
017
               Aligning the sensitivities of image quality metrics and DNNs ensures that estimates
018            of quality can act as priors for image/dataset difficulty independent task models
019            trained/evaluated on the data. Conventional image quality assessment (IQA) seeks
020            to measure and align quality relative to human perceptual judgements, but here
021            we seek a quality measure that is not only sensitive to imaging conditions but
022            also well-aligned with DNN sensitivities. We first ask whether conventional IQA
023            metrics are also informative of DNN performance. In order to answer this question,
024            we reframe IQA from a causal perspective and examine conditions under which
025            quality metrics are predictive of DNN performance. We show theoretically and
026
               empirically that current IQA metrics are weak predictors of DNN performance
               in the context of classification. We then use our causal framework to provide
027
               an alternative formulation and a new image quality metric that is more strongly
028
               correlated with DNN performance and can act as a prior on performance without
029            training new task models. Our approach provides a means to directly estimate
030            the quality distribution of large-scale image datasets towards characterizing the
031            relationship between dataset composition and DNN performance.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Ensuring the robustness of deep neural networks (DNNs) to real-world imaging conditions is crucial
037   for safety- and cost-critical applications. Extensive research has shown that DNNs remain sensitive
038   to natural distortions (Taori et al., 2020; Djolonga et al., 2021; Ibrahim et al., 2022; Geirhos et al.,
039
      2021) despite efforts to close the gap between performance on clean and naturally-distorted images.
      While much effort has focused primarily on the design and optimization of robust DNNs, there is
040
      now growing interest in developing a deeper understanding of how the properties of the image data
041
      itself influence robustness during training and evaluation (Ilyas et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2022; Pavlak
042   et al., 2023).
043
044   As image datasets grow in size, the cost and feasibility of using human annotators to assess and
045
      annotate properties of each data point becomes intractable. With pre-training datasets for founda-
      tion models and other large-scale vision models approaching hundreds of millions to billions of
046
      images (Radford et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2017; Schuhmann et al., 2022), new automated methods
047
      are needed for quantitatively assessing dataset composition. In particular, since image quality (IQ)
048   is known to impact DNN performance (Taori et al., 2020; Djolonga et al., 2021; Hendrycks &
049   Dietterich, 2019; Ibrahim et al., 2022), this motivates the need for methods that can estimate the
050   underlying quality distribution of large-scale image datasets prior to training or evaluating down-
051   stream DNNs. Here quality describes the absence of distortion but more generally relates to the
052   ability to extract task-relevant information from the image. Image quality and difficulty are closely
053   related where quality measures properties of the imaging conditions while difficulty involves content
      and composition in addition to the conditions. Effective measures of image quality should provide

054
      insight into image/dataset difficulty independent of knowledge or assumptions about the particular
055   downstream task models that will consume the data. For instance, when training data is skewed in
056   favor of high-quality images, analysis of the quality distribution using IQ metrics may help justify
057   and identify more aggressive data augmentation strategies during training to ensure task model
058   robustness. Similarly, analyzing the quality distribution of evaluation datasets may find that the data
059   is not sufficiently challenging/diverse which could lead to a false sense of task model robustness in
060   downstream evaluations. Our goal in this work is to provide a framework to identify and develop
061   quality measures that can act as priors on DNN performance towards characterizing the relationship
062   between dataset composition and DNN robustness.
063   In this work, we focus specifically on natural robustness which considers how images are distorted
064   due to real-world factors such as lighting, weather, sensor settings, and/or motion. Image quality
065   assessment (IQA) metrics have been developed over several decades of research (Wang & Bovik;
066   Xu et al., 2017; Wang, 2004; Agnolucci et al., 2024) and provide quantitative measures of quality
067   calibrated with respect to human perceptual judgements. To the best of our knowledge, little work
068   has been done to understand how these IQA metrics can help relate image difficulty and DNN
069
      performance. To make this connection explicit, we state our primary research question: What is the
      extent of the relationship between IQ and DNN performance metrics?
070
071   Contributions To answer this question, our work makes the following contributions:
072
073
      • Our primary contribution is a causal framework for analyzing the relationship between image
        quality and DNN performance in a range of IQA settings
074
075
      • We use the framework to establish theoretically and empirically the independence of image quality
        and DNN performance under general conditions
076
077
      • We identify specific conditions under which IQA metrics can be predictive of DNN performance
078   • We use the framework in the context of image classification tasks to develop a new task-guided IQA
079
        metric that enables quantitative assessments of image quality that are also predictive of downstream
        DNN task performance

082   2   R ELATED WORK
083
084   Image Quality Assessment Image quality assessment has been long-studied in the computer
085   vision and image processing literature. Full Reference IQA (FR-IQA) (Wang, 2004; Zhang et al.,
086   2011; 2018) assume the availability of a reference or “clean” image against which the test image is
087
      compared and the quality is measured. In contrast, the No Reference IQA (NR-IQA) (Mittal et al.,
      2012; Wang et al., 2022; Agnolucci et al., 2024) setting (aka Blind IQA) uses only features of the
088
      test image to estimate a quality score. In both settings, conventional IQA methods are calibrated and
089
      compared against human perceptual judgements of quality such as Mean Opinion Scores (MOS).
090   These measures are task-agnostic and humans are not required to make judgements about the content
091   of the image but only to measure subjective “quality” (typically on a scale of 1-5, Poor-High). This
092   motivates our investigation into whether these metrics can also provide task-relevant assessments of
093   image quality.
094
      Relationship of DNNs and human perception A key question of this work centers on whether
095
      IQA metrics calibrated against human MOS are sensitive to any of the same image features that
096   DNNs use for downstream tasks. Outside of the IQA literature, prior works have shown differences
097   in humans and DNNs in the context of shape/texture bias (Geirhos et al., 2018; Hermann et al., 2019),
098   shortcut learning (Geirhos et al., 2020; Zech et al., 2018; Brown et al., 2023; Ong Ly et al., 2024),
099   and error consistency (Geirhos et al., 2021; Wichmann & Geirhos, 2023), but the question remains
100   open whether IQA metrics aligned with human MOS correlate with DNN performance.
101
      Dataset difficulty/pruning Our work is strongly motivated by the growing interest in automated
102   methods for dataset analysis. In particular, new methods focus on dataset pruning (Tan et al., 2023;
103   He et al., 2023; Abbas et al., 2024), identifying difficult/important examples (Kwok et al., 2024;
104   Ilyas et al., 2022) or data slices (Eyuboglu et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2019; Sohoni et al., 2020; Chen
105   et al., 2019), and dataset auditing for shortcuts (Pavlak et al., 2023). Other results have shown that
106   understanding dataset composition matters for analyzing model robustness (Ibrahim et al., 2022;
107   Drenkow & Unberath, 2023). Our work takes a positive step towards automated methods for analyzing
      the quality distribution of image datasets and establishing priors on DNN performance.

      3     C AUSAL FRAMEWORK FOR IQA
109
110   We first provide a causal inference perspective on the IQA problem. We use causal directed acyclic
111   graphs (DAGs) to illustrate our assumptions about the imaging generating process, quality metric,
112   and performance metric as well as the interactions between all associated variables. This causal
113   framework provides a means for identifying the specific conditions under which quality metrics are
114   predictive of DNN performance.
115
      Preliminaries We specify a causal DAG G via a set of nodes V and directed edges E. To obtain the
116   causal interpretation, directed edges imply a causal relationship such that for a variable/node V 2 V,
117   V is a function of its parents (V = fV (pa(V ), U ) where U is an exogenous noise term).
118
119
      For defining causal models in the IQA context, we start from a set of factors A 2 A that capture the
      key variables in the data generating process affecting the image conditions (e.g., lighting, focal length,
120
      aperture, exposure, weather). Let X 2 X be the resulting images, and for a task T , let Y 2 Y be the
121
      label associated with X for the task. For this work, we focus on classification tasks where Y consists
122   of a discrete set of K classes (Y = {1, . . . , K}). Our quality metric Q : X ! R maps images to real
123   number scores (typically in [0, 1] where 1 is the highest quality). We also assume a downstream task
124   DNN f✓ : X ! Y that maps images to class probabilities and is parameterized by ✓. We write the
125   predicted probabilities Ŷ = f✓ (X) where Ŷ 2 RK . Given one-hot encoded labels Y and predictions
126   Ŷ , we can compute a performance metric (e.g., accuracy) M : Y ⇥ Y ! R. In the general case and
127
      without loss of generality, we assume that Ŷ is the prediction from a deterministic DNN. Similarly,
128   we also assume that Q, M are both deterministic functions of their parents in the causal DAG.
129
130   3.1   IQ METRIC DESIDERATA
131
132   Our primary motivation is to identify image quality metrics that allow us to assess the distribution
133   of image quality in large-scale datasets and establish quality-driven priors for DNN performance
134   independent of any specific trained task models. We propose the following desiderata for IQ metrics
135   towards achieving these objectives.
136
      • D1 - Sensitive: IQ metrics should be sufficiently sensitive to changes in image conditions
137
138   • D2 - Blind: IQ metrics should work in No Reference IQA (NR-IQA) settings where images are
139
        assessed without knowledge of a reference image captured under “clean” settings
140   • D3 - Predictive: IQ metrics should be correlated with DNN task performance
141   • D4 - Task Model Agnostic: IQ metrics should be designed/trained/calibrated without a priori
142     knowledge of the downstream DNN models/architectures to be trained or evaluated on the data
143     under consideration
144
145
      The first criterion (D1) is a baseline condition requiring that the metric is actually sensitive to the
      natural conditions likely in the imaging domain. D2 operates under the assumption that real-world
146
      datasets will not consist of pairs of clean/distorted images and will instead contain images collected
147
      in diverse conditions. D3 stems from the idea that Q should measure general properties of the data
148   that influence M (e.g., if Q decreases, then M should also decrease, although not necessarily at
149   the same rate). Lastly, D4 comes from the desire to use Q to assess the composition of the dataset
150   independent of any task-specific model training and without making assumptions about the type of
151   DNN to be trained downstream. In other words, we want to avoid IQ metrics that are biased towards
152   specific task models and/or require pre-training on each dataset to be analyzed.

212
      of visual features Z derived from X (Fig. 2), then we should observe that Q is correlated with M

475   6.3   E XPERIMENT - P REDICTABILITY OF DNN PERFORMANCE FOR MILDLY CORRUPTED
476         DATASETS
477
478   In the previous experiments, the use of IN-C allowed us to investigate the large-scale effect of image
479   corruptions on the predictability of performance by using multiple corrupted versions of the validation
480   set with multiple levels of severity. In real-world datasets, we expect that only a small fraction of
481   images will be corrupted. We next examine the extent to which IQA metrics can be used to show
482
      differences between the quality distributions of datasets containing varying levels of corruption while
      still satisfying D1-D4 in these more realistic settings.
483
484   To answer this question, we generate new variants of IN-val consisting of mixtures of clean and
485   corrupted images. For each variant, we specify a set of valid corruptions C, severities S, and a
      corruption probability pc . We choose a fraction 1 pc of the original IN-val image IDs to remain as


                                                                                  9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      clean images and a fraction pc to be corrupted. The corrupted images are sampled uniformly amongst
487
      the corruptions c 2 C and severities s 2 S. The resulting variant consists of the original 50k image
488   IDs with a mixture of clean and corrupted images.
489
490   We choose C to consist of all 15 corruptions in the IN-C dataset and limit severity to S = {1, 2, 3} in
491
      order to further test the sensitivity of the IQA metrics (D1). We create variants of the IN-val dataset
      for pc = N/100 for N 2 [1, . . . , 20] . We evaluate the DNNs on these dataset variants and estimate
492
      predictability using logistic regression as in previous experiments. We compute mAU C over all pc
493
      variants and find ZSCLIP-IQA (Ql ) outperforms all other NR-IQA metrics with mAU C = 0.64 with
494   the next best (CLIP-IQA) achieving only mAU C = 0.57. The results show that while all metrics can
495   distinguish between differences in the quality distributions of the dataset variants, only ZSCLIP-IQA
496   achieves high predictability over all variants. Conventional IQA metrics improve only as the number
497   of distorted images in the dataset increases (where it becomes easier to separate clean and corrupted
498   images). The full results are found in Appendix H and show that predictability with ZSCLIP-IQA
499   is stable with respect to changes in the proportion of clean/corrupted images in the dataset whereas
500   more traditional NR-IQA metrics remain near random chance AU C and exhibit higher variance as
501   pc changes.

      7    D ISCUSSION
503
504   In this work, we were motivated to identify measures of image quality that allow us to produce
505   IQ-driven priors on DNN performance. We presented a causal inference framework for this problem
506   and proposed a set of IQ metric desiderata to guide our analysis. Using our causal framework, we
507
      show conditions where image quality measures can be predictive of DNN performance. We then
      provide a detailed examination of the relationship between conventional NR-IQA metrics and DNN
508
      performance. We use our causal framework and extensive empirical evaluations in the context of
509
      image classification to demonstrate that common NR-IQA do not satisfy our desired IQ criteria.
510   We then use the causal approach to develop the task-guided ZSCLIP-IQA metric that provides a
511   causality-driven proof-of-concept metric that satisfies all IQ desiderata and paves the way for future
512   research to improve the alignment between IQA metrics and DNN performance.
513
      Potential negative societal impacts As a tool for analysis, the proposed causal framework poses
514
      minimal societal risks. While causal models require assumptions about the data generating process,
515
      these assumptions are made explicitly in the causal graph and improve the overall transparency of the
516   analysis. Of greater concern is the possibility that using quality metrics to prune/resample datasets
517   may lead to unintended consequences such as removing poor-quality images in a way that disparately
518   affects protected groups. While our work does not address the question of dataset pruning/resampling,
519   we mention this to help ensure that future researchers consider these possibilities in their own work.
520
      Limitations and future work We first recognize that image quality alone is insufficient to predict
521
      task model performance as both image content and composition play a role in task difficulty and the
522   relationship between IQA and performance may be confounded by other factors. Nonetheless, we
523   show that our causal framework still allows us to analyze the conditions where quality properties
524   of our dataset may be correlated with DNN performance. Our ZSCLIP-IQA method provides one
525   solution that satisfies the proposed IQ desiderata but we believe there are many opportunities for
526   improving on this approach in future research.
527
      We also acknowledge that our experiments only addressed image classification tasks. We focused
528   initially on classification since it is well-studied and clearly defined, with many public benchmarks
529   available for evaluation. Even in this context, we are the first to show that the notion of quality
530   is task-dependent (i.e., perceptual judgement vs. classification). Our primary contribution in this
531   work is the causal framework and we believe this provides a strong foundation for supporting future
532   research that examines similar questions for a wider range of vision tasks.
