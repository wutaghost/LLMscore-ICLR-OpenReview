011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Speech comprehension involves complex, nonlinear, and multimodal processes
014            within the brain, integrating auditory signals with linguistic and semantic infor-
015
               mation across widespread brain networks. Traditional brain encoding models, of-
               ten relying on linear mappings from unimodal features, fall short in representing
016
               these intricate mechanisms. In this study, we introduce a nonlinear, multimodal
017
               encoding model that combines audio and linguistic features extracted from pre-
018            trained deep learning models (e.g., LLAMA and Whisper). These nonlinear ar-
019            chitectures and early fusion mechanisms significantly enhance cross-modal inte-
020            gration, achieving a 14.4% increase in average normalized correlation coefficient
021            and 7.7% increase in average single-story correlation compared to the previous
022            state-of-the-art model relying on weighted averaging of linear unimodal predic-
023            tions. Moreover, this improved performance reveals novel insights into the brain’s
024            functional organization, demonstrating how auditory and semantic information
025            are nonlinearly fused within regions linked to motor control, somatosensory pro-
026
               cessing, and higher-level semantic representation. Our findings provide empiri-
               cal support for foundational neurolinguistic theories, including the Motor Theory
027
               of Speech Perception, embodied semantic memory, and the Convergence Zone
028
               model, revealing novel insights into neural mechanisms otherwise impossible with
029            simpler encoder models. By emphasizing the critical role of nonlinearity and mul-
030            timodality in brain encoding models, our work bridges neural mechanisms and
031            computational modeling, paving the way for the development of more biologi-
032            cally inspired, brain-aligned artificial intelligence systems.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   The human brain effortlessly deciphers the complex symphony of spoken language, seamlessly inte-
038   grating auditory signals with linguistic and semantic meaning. This remarkable feat of speech com-
039
      prehension involves a dynamic interplay of multiple brain regions. The brain relies heavily on the
      integration of information from different modalities (McGettigan et al., 2012; Calvert, 2001; Ghaz-
040
      anfar & Schroeder, 2006). Furthermore, this process is inherently nonlinear, with complex transfor-
041
      mations and interactions occurring across various levels of neural representation (Tuller et al., 2011).
042   Understanding these intricate neural mechanisms is not only crucial for advancing our knowledge
043   of human cognition but also holds immense potential for developing more human-like artificial in-
044   telligence and creating more effective therapies for individuals with language disorders.
045
      Language encoding models, which predict brain activity from speech stimuli, provide a powerful
046
      tool for unraveling the neural processes underlying speech comprehension (Naselaris et al., 2011;
047
      Tang et al., 2023; Vaidya et al., 2022; de Heer et al., 2017; LeBel et al., 2021; Jain & Huth, 2018;
048   Toneva & Wehbe, 2019; Goldstein et al., 2022; Schrimpf et al., 2021). Unlike traditional contrast-
049   based paradigms that rely on carefully controlled experiments, encoding models inherently capital-
050   ize on naturalistic stimuli, such as spoken language, to better capture the brain’s real-world pro-
051   cessing. This allows for a more comprehensive understanding of the brain’s activity in response
052   to complex and ecologically valid tasks, offering greater generalizability compared to simplified,
053   contrast-based tasks (Jain et al., 2024). Moreover, they are increasingly being utilized for in-silico
      experiments: computational simulations of neural activity that enable researchers to test hypotheses


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      about brain function without the need for new data collection (Jain et al., 2024; Yamins & DiCarlo,
055   2016; Ratan Murty et al., 2021; Bashivan et al., 2019; Wehbe et al., 2016).
056
057   Initially, encoding models mapped simple acoustic features (e.g., spectograms) onto brain activity
058
      (de Heer et al., 2017). The introduction of word embeddings (Mikolov, 2013) enabled incorporation
      of semantic information, revealing how meaning is represented across the brain (Huth et al., 2016).
059
      Building on these foundations, recent advances in large language models (LLMs) and sophisticated
060
      audio models have enabled even richer and more context-sensitive representations of speech. These
061   innovations have led to substantial improvements in prediction accuracy, as demonstrated by studies
062   utilizing these advanced feature extractors (Antonello et al., 2024; Vaidya et al., 2022).
063
064
      Despite significant progress, most prior work has been limited to unimodal features and linear map-
      pings, which fail to address two critical aspects of neural processing. First, the brain relies on nonlin-
065
      ear computations (Friston et al., 2000; Deco et al., 2008; Beniaguev et al., 2021; Tuller et al., 2011),
066
      enabling complex interactions across neural systems. Second, speech comprehension is a multi-
067   modal process, requiring the integration of diverse information sources (voice, gesture, linguistic)
068   across distributed neural networks (McGettigan et al., 2012). These principles, along with the Motor
069   Theory of Speech Perception (Liberman et al., 1967) and the Convergence Divergence Zone model
070   (Damasio, 1989), highlight the importance of encoding models capable of capturing both nonlinear
071   dynamics and multimodal integration to reveal the brain’s functional organization. Therefore, ad-
072   dressing these limitations is critical for building encoding models that more accurately reflect the
073   brain’s complexity and provide deeper insights into its multimodal and nonlinear processes.
074   While some studies explored multimodal models that combine linguistic features with visual in-
075   formation (Oota et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Scotti et al., 2024), the integration of auditory
076   features into language encoding remains largely unexplored. This gap in research is particularly
077   significant, as auditory features are central to speech comprehension. Recent work by Oota et al.
078   (2023) demonstrates that speech models, unlike text-based language models, capture brain activity
079   patterns in auditory regions that cannot be explained by low-level stimulus features, underscoring
080   the complementary nature of auditory and linguistic representations. Investigating how auditory and
081
      linguistic information interact in the brain could provide key insights into neural mechanisms un-
      derlying speech processing and advance the development of more brain aligned encoding models.
082
083   In this study, we address these limitations by introducing a nonlinear and multimodal encoding mod-
084   els that leverage both audio and linguistic features extracted from advanced deep learning models
085   like LLAMA and Whisper. Our work makes the following key contributions:
086          • We demonstrate a substantial improvement in prediction accuracy. Compared to the
087            state-of-the-art stacked linear regression model, we achieved a 14.4% increase in
088            mean normalized correlation coefficient (Appendix A.3). We also saw a 17.2% in-
089            crease in mean correlation across the cortex compared to widely used linear encoding
090            models (Antonello et al., 2024). This significant performance boost highlights the impor-
091            tance of incorporating nonlinearity and multimodality together for capturing the full capac-
092            ity of the brain’s language processing mechanisms, showing that these complex interactions
093
               are crucial for accurately modeling brain activity during speech comprehension.
             • We provide novel evidence for nonlinear multimodal fusion in motor, sensory, and
094
               visual brain regions, supporting existing theories in neurolinguistics while revealing
095
               new insights into the neural basis of speech comprehension. Our findings demonstrate
096            enhanced prediction accuracy in motor regions when incorporating both auditory and se-
097            mantic features, supporting the long-lasting Motor Theory of Speech Perception (Liber-
098            man et al., 1967; 1952; Poeppel & Assaneo, 2020), suggesting the involvement of motor
099            regions during speech comprehension in simulating articulatory movements. Also, we ob-
100            serve improved predictions in visual areas bordering the visual cortex when adding seman-
101            tic information, supporting the Convergence Zone model (Damasio, 1989; Damasio et al.,
102            1996; 2004) and providing evidence for a distributed, multimodal representation of seman-
103            tic concepts. Our results reveal that incorporating semantic features enhances predictions
104
               in somatosensory regions, suggesting a broader involvement of these areas in processing
               language, consistent with the concept of embodied semantics (Davis & Yee, 2021).
105
             • We establish a foundation for future research by demonstrating the potential of deep
106
               learning techniques for modeling the complexity of speech comprehension in the
107            brain. Our work paves the way for developing more sophisticated encoding models that in-

138   2.1    MRI DATA
139
140   We used a publicly available fMRI dataset (LeBel et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023) of three subjects
141   listening to approximately 20 hours of English language podcasts. The training data comprised 95
142   stories across 20 scanning sessions (approximately 33,000 time points). For testing, we used three
143   held-out stories: one averaged across ten repetitions and two averaged across five repetitions each,
144   with no session containing repeated test stimuli. Each voxel was normalized to zero mean and unit
145
      variance across time, ensuring consistent training and testing data (Antonello et al., 2024).
146
147   2.2    F EATURE EXTRACTION
148
      We extracted the features from the stimuli by taking the intermediate hidden layer representations of
149
      various large language models (LLMs) and audio models exposed to the same stimuli as the subject.
150   For semantic feature extraction, we utilized LLAMA-1 (Touvron et al., 2023a) models with 7B, 13B,
151   33B, and 65B parameters, LLAMA-2 7B (Touvron et al., 2023b), and LLAMA-3 8B (Dubey et al.,
152   2024). For audio feature extraction, we employed Whisper (Radford et al., 2023) models, including
153   Tiny, Base, Small, Large, and Large v2 and v3. All models were obtained from Hugging Face (Wolf,
154   2019) and computed using half-precision (float16) for efficiency.
155
      For LLAMA models, the stimuli were presented using a dynamically sized context window strategy
156   (Antonello et al., 2024) to balance computational efficiency and contextual coherence. Initially, the
157   context window grew incrementally as tokens were added, up to a maximum of 512 tokens, after
158   which the window was reset to a new context of 256 tokens. This approach avoided memory over-
159   heads associated with processing the entire tokenized text while maintaining sufficient contextual
160   information for accurate semantic representation. For Whisper models, the audio stimuli (waveform)
161   were processed using a sliding-window approach with a fixed window size of 16 seconds and a stride
      of 0.1 seconds. Features were extracted exclusively from the encoder portion of the encoder-decoder


                                                       3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      architecture, as it processes only the raw audio waveform input. This ensured that the extracted fea-
163   tures accurately captured audio-specific representations relevant to the stimuli. Refer to Antonello
164   et al. (2024) for further details on the process and the handling of model contexts.
165
166
      The following process adheres to Antonello et al. (2024) for fair comparison. We temporally aligned
      the hidden states from the lth layer of the language or audio models with fMRI acquisition times us-
167
      ing Lanczos interpolation. To account for temporal delays between stimulus presentation and neural
168
      responses, we concatenated representations from the four preceding timepoints (2, 4, 6, and 8 sec-
169   onds prior) for each fMRI acquisition timepoint, yielding a feature vector for each TR (see Appendix
170   A.2.1). Unless stated otherwise, we extracted semantic features from the 12th layer of LLAMA-7B
171   and audio features from the final encoder layer of Whisper Large V1. The layers were selected based
172   on our findings that performance scaling with increasing LLM size, as reported in Antonello et al.
173   (2024), does not hold for LLAMA models of size ≥7B (see Appendix A.5).

176   2.3   R EPRESENTATIONS FOR F MRI DATA
177
178
      We predicted PCA-reduced fMRI representations, rather than the full voxel space, motivated by
      three benefits. First, PCA is a common dimensionality reduction method in fMRI analysis that helps
179
      prevent overfitting and has been widely applied in neuroimaging studies (Wang et al., 2010; Mourao-
180
      Miranda et al., 2005; López et al., 2011; Koutsouleris et al., 2009). This was crucial for our study,
181   as speech comprehension engages the whole cortex and hence a vast number of voxels 80 – 90k
182   (LeBel et al., 2023), far more than in tasks like vision encoding ≈ 15k (Allen et al., 2022). In
183   fact, linearly mapping the semantic stimulus (4 × 4096) to subject S1 require 1.3 billion parameters,
184   whereas utilizing PCA (512 components) reduced this to 8.4 million, preventing overfitting. Second,
185   PCA is effective at untangling the redundancy in brain data, as fMRI voxels are highly correlated.
186   Studies such as Jabakhanji et al. (2022); Lin et al. (2022) have shown that masking up to 90% of
187   voxels does not significantly impact fMRI decoding or reconstruction performance, suggesting that
188   information is distributed and redundant. Lin et al. (2022) further showed that when training a three-
189
      layer autoencoder on fMRI data, 99% of the variance in latent features and reconstructed voxels
      could be attributed to their top 100 PCA components, further highlighting the brain’s redundancy.
190
      Lastly, PCA allows for reverse transformation to recover the original voxel space from predicted
191
      PCA embeddings, maintaining the interpretability of the model’s predictions.
192
193   In detail, we applied Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to the aggregate fMRI response matrix
194   Yorg ∈ RNTR ×Nvoxels , reducing its dimensionality to YPCA ∈ RNTR ×NPCA . NTR , Nvoxels each refers
195
      to the number of time points (TRs) and voxels respectively, and NPCA was set to 512. The encod-
      ing model was trained to predict these PCA-reduced representations, and during evaluation, the
196                test
197
      predicted ŶPCA   was reconstructed back to the original voxel space using inverse projection. This
198
      reconstructed output was then compared to the ground truth fMRI responses, Y test ∈ RNTR-test ×Nvoxels .
      More implementation details are provided in Appendix A.2.2.

201   2.4   E NCODING M ODEL
202
203   Previous research primarily employed linear regression to predict voxel responses from unimodal
204   features (audio or semantic) (Tang et al., 2023; Huth et al., 2016; de Heer et al., 2017; LeBel et al.,
205   2021; Jain & Huth, 2018; Toneva & Wehbe, 2019; Goldstein et al., 2022; Schrimpf et al., 2021).
206   Our study expands upon this approach by systematically investigating a range of encoding models
207   varying in complexity and input modality to capture more nuanced relationships between stimulus
208   representations and brain responses. We explored combinations of different stimulus representations,
209   encoding model architectures, and response representations (Details are in Appendix A.2.3) :
210   Stimulus representations (model input) included audio and semantic features extracted from vari-
211   ous versions of LLAMA and Whisper models, as explained in Section 2.2. For multimodal encoders,
212   we concatenated audio and semantic features to capture the combined effect of both modalities.
213
      Encoding model architectures assessed the impact of complexity and nonlinearity:
214
215          • Linear Regression (Linear): Following Antonello et al. (2024), we used ridge regression.
             • Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP): MLP with a single hidden layer of 256 units.


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
              • Multi-Layer Linear (MLLinear): MLP but without dropout, batch normalization, and with
217             the identity activation function. This model serves as a reduced-rank linear regression, help-
218             ing to isolate the effects of dimensionality reduction from nonlinearity.
219           • Delayed Interaction MLP (DIMLP): Used for multimodal cases, this MLP variant pro-
220             cesses each modality through separate 256-unit hidden layers before concatenation and fi-
221             nal linear projection. This allows nonlinear processing within each modality while limiting
222             cross-modal interaction to be linear, revealing the effects of nonlinear fusion of modalities.
223   Response representations (model output) included two types: the full voxel space and a 512-
224   dimensional PCA-reduced representation of the voxel responses, as explained in Section 2.3.

      2.5     N OISE CEILING AND NORMALIZED CORRELATION COEFFIICNET
227
228   Due to the inherent noise in fMRI data, there is a theoretical upper limit to the amount of explainable
229   variance an ideal encoding model can achieve, known as the noise ceiling. The noise ceiling for each
230   voxel was estimated by applying an existing method (Schoppe et al., 2016) on ten responses of the
231
       q test story. For each voxel, the maximum correlation coefficient is estimated as CCmax =
      same
232   ( 1 + SPN×N P
                     )−1 , where N is the number of repeats (10 in our case), N P is the noise power
233
      or unexplainable variance, and SP is the amount of variance that could be explained by an ideal
234
      predictive model. Afterwards, by dividing CCabs , the correlation coefficient of the encoding model’s
235   prediction with the ground truth fMRI signals (estimated using the average of test responses to the
236   same stimuli), with CCmax , we obtain CCnorm , the normalized correlation coefficient (CCnorm =
237   CCabs /CCmax ). Due to the large number of voxels (≈ 80,000), random noise can cause certain
238   voxels to have CCabs > CCmax , resulting in CCnorm > 1. To prevent this, we regularized for noisy
239   voxels by setting those with CCmax values smaller than 0.25 to 0.25 when computing CCnorm .

244   Incorporating nonlinearity into the encoding models improved performance across all modalities.
245
      As demonstrated in Table 1, utilizing a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) resulted in a notable per-
      formance increase: 3.6% for semantic models, 8.7% for audio models, and 4.1% for multimodal
246
      models, compared to traditional linear models. Furthermore, multimodality (combining audio and
247
      semantic features) yielded a substantial 12.0% gain over unimodal models, highlighting the impor-
248   tance of capturing multimodal interactions. Combining both nonlinearity and multimodality yielded
249   the greatest improvement, with a 17.2% increase in mean correlation across the cortex. These results
250   not only confirm that the brain employs nonlinear, multimodal processing for speech comprehension
251   but also provide a powerful tool for uncovering previously hidden neural mechanisms, as we will
252   demonstrate in the following sections. (correlation was computed as r2 = |r| ∗ r)

256   3.1.1    N ONLINEAR ENCODERS IMPROVE PREDICTION PERFORMANCE
257
258
      To examine the benefits of nonlinearity across feature hierarchies, we compared MLP and linear
      encoders across different layers of LLAMA and Whisper models (Figure 24 (Appendix)). MLP
259
      consistently outperformed linear encoding at every layer depth for both modalities, suggesting that
260
      nonlinear processing is advantageous regardless of feature complexity and supporting the brain’s
261   use of nonlinear computations throughout the speech processing hierarchy.

      3.1.2    N ONLINEARITY, NOT REDUCED DIMENSION , IMPROVES THE ENCODING
264
               PERFORMANCE
265
266   To confirm that MLP performance gains stem from nonlinear processing rather than reduced dimen-
267   sionality, we compared the MLP encoder with two linear control models: “Linear,” which uses lin-
268   ear regression on PCA-reduced data, and “MLLinear,” which mirrors the MLP architecture without
269   nonlinear activation functions. As shown in Table 1, both Linear and MLLinear models performed
      similarly to or worse than linear regression on the full voxel space (baseline model), particularly for

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      semantic and multimodal cases. While these models slightly outperformed the baseline for audio-
271   only data, their accuracy remained below that of the MLP encoder. These findings highlight the
272   MLP’s ability to capture nonlinear relationships drives its superior performance, not merely di-
273   mensionality reduction. Additionally, PCA proves essential for leveraging the benefits of nonlinear
274   models. MLP models predicting all voxels directly performed poorly, likely due to overfitting from
275   the large voxel space (80–90k voxels compared to 512 PCA components).

277   3.1.3    N ONLINEARITY IMPROVES BRAIN - WIDE PREDICTIONS AND SPATIO - TEMPORAL
278            CLUSTERING
279
280   Nonlinear models, such as MLPs, provide a crucial advantage over linear models by effectively
281   capturing the complex and nonlinear relationships inherent in brain activity during speech compre-
282
      hension. Comparative brain maps in Figure 25, 26, and 27 (Appendix) illustrate the superior perfor-
      mance of MLP encoders over linear encoders, with improvements in prediction accuracy distributed
283
      broadly across the cortex. The MLP model shows significant gains in regions associated with se-
284
      mantic and auditory processing, such as the medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC), precuneus (PrCu),
285   and lateral temporal cortex (LTC). These gains highlight the role of nonlinear interactions in ac-
286   curately modeling brain activity, particularly in areas involved in higher-order language processing.
287   Furthermore, our hierarchical clustering and modularity analyses (Appendix A.9.4) based on the rel-
288   ative error difference (RED) between Whisper and LLAMA encoding models reveal that nonlinear
289   (MLP) encoders outperform linear encoders in functionally grouping the brain regions according to
290   their spatial and temporal response profiles. This result suggests that our nonlinear encoding model
291   can reveal a more refined picture of the brain’s functional compartmentalization.
292   Our results of the widespread enhancement of prediction accuracy across the cortex and of the MLP
293   encoder’s ability to effectively compartmentalize brain regions based on their spatio-temporal dy-
294   namics together point towards a fundamental characteristic of brain function: the distributed nature
295   of speech comprehension, which involves coordinated activity across multiple (likely parallel) brain
296   networks. This coordinated activity of the distributed brain networks is not only crucial for language
297   processing but also plays a key role in other high-level cognitive functions (Bassett & Sporns, 2017;
298   Medaglia et al., 2015; Da Rocha et al., 2011). Our findings suggest that nonlinear models are well-
299
      suited for capturing this complex interplay of distributed neural activity, providing a more accurate
      and insightful representation of the brain’s functional organization compared to linear models.

      ulating the articulatory movements necessary for speech production, thereby aiding comprehension.

      In particular, improvements seen with the addition of auditory features align with research that dis-
384
      covered a tight coupling between auditory and motor-sensory processing (Skipper et al., 2005; Wu
385   et al., 2014; Wilson et al., 2004).
386
387   Furthermore, the improvement of somatosensory prediction from the addition of either modalities
388
      also suggests that semantic information plays a critical role in shaping activity within somatosen-
      sory regions. This suggests a broader involvement of these areas in speech comprehension than
389
      previously recognized. This aligns with the concept of embodiment of semantic memory, where the
390
      understanding of concepts is grounded in sensory and motor experiences and their memory in the
391   neocortex (Binder & Desai, 2011). Our results align with Nagata et al. (2022), who showed that the
392   sensorimotor cortex is engaged in processing both concrete and abstract word semantics.
393
394
      These enhancements in motor and sensory areas are more pronounced with MLP models, underscor-
      ing the importance of nonlinear interactions between auditory and semantic information. We explore
395
      this in more details in Section 3.3. See Appendix A.10 for subject-wise plots.

398   3.2.3   F USION OF AUDITORY AND SEMANTIC INFORMATION ACROSS BRAIN REGIONS
399
      Our analysis reveals that fusing auditory and semantic information significantly enhances fMRI pre-
400
      diction accuracy, even in regions traditionally associated with high-level visual processing. Specifi-
401
      cally, incorporating semantic features leads to improved predictions in areas situated at the border of
402   the visual cortex, including the Occipital Face Area (OFA) (Pitcher et al., 2011), Extrastriate Body
403   Area (EBA) (Downing et al., 2001), Fusiform Face Area (FFA) (Kanwisher et al., 1997), Posterior
404   Parietal Area (PPA) (Epstein & Kanwisher, 1998), and Retrosplenial Cortex (RSC) (Vann et al.,
405   2009) (Figure 1(e)). This suggests that these regions, while primarily involved in processing visual
406   stimuli, also play a role in representing linguistic semantics.
407
      This finding aligns with prior studies demonstrating that visual and linguistic stimuli with similar
408   semantic content elicit similar brain responses (Tang et al., 2024; Deniz et al., 2019; Devereux
409   et al., 2013; Fairhall & Caramazza, 2013; Popham et al., 2021; Huth et al., 2012; 2016). These
410   studies, along with our results, support the convergence-divergence-zone theory (Popham et al.,
411   2021; Damasio et al., 1996; 2004; Damasio, 1989), which posits semantic information from multiple
412   modalities is integrated at points across the cortex, leading to a unified representation of semantic
413   meaning. This model suggests that the brain constructs a modality-independent representation of
414   semantics, drawing on information from vision, language, and other senses (Binder & Desai, 2011;
415   Tang et al., 2023; 2024; Devereux et al., 2013; Fairhall & Caramazza, 2013; Martin, 2016). Our
416
      variance partitioning analysis in Appendix A.7 further supports this.
417   Crucially, our study provides novel evidence for the auditory modality’s contribution to this unified
418   semantic representation. We found that adding audio features to our multimodal models resulted in
419   a small, but statistically significant performance increase in those visual border ROIs (yellow). This
420   finding suggests that auditory information, such as tone of voice and environmental sounds, provides
421   unique semantic context not fully captured by visual or linguistic features alone.
422   Furthermore, our analysis of Broca’s area and the Inferior Frontal Sulcus Face Patch (IFSFP) re-
423   inforces the notion that these regions are involved in multimodal processing. We found that incor-
424   porating audio or semantic information significantly improved prediction accuracy in these areas,
425   particularly when using the MLP encoder. This is consistent with the growing evidence that Broca’s
426   area and IFS play a role in integrating phonological, semantic, and motor information during speech
427   comprehension (Gough et al., 2005; Nixon et al., 2004; Papoutsi et al., 2009; Bohland & Guenther,
428
      2006; Papoutsi et al., 2009; Bohland & Guenther, 2006; Guenther et al., 2015).
429   The consistent observation that multimodal fusion, particularly with nonlinear models, enhances
430   prediction accuracy emphasizes the brain’s use of complex, nonlinear computations to combine
431   information from different modalities for a holistic understanding of language. For a visualization
      of subject-wise differences in ROI prediction performance, see Figure 36 (Appendix).

      3.3     N ONLINEAR INTERACTIONS BETWEEN MODALITIES ENHANCE F MRI PREDICTIONS
433
434   3.3.1    N ONLINEAR INTERACTIONS BETWEEN MODALITIES ARE IMPORTANT FOR MOTOR AND
435            VISUAL AREAS
436
437   To investigate the role of nonlinear interactions between modalities, we developed a novel model
438
      called the Delayed Interaction MLP (DIMLP). While a standard MLP allows complex nonlinear
      interactions between all features, DIMLP processes each modality (audio and semantic) indepen-
439
      dently through separate nonlinear layers before a final linear fusion stage. This design enables us
440
      to directly compare the contribution of within-modality nonlinearity (captured by DIMLP) to the
441   contribution of cross-modal nonlinear interactions (captured by the standard MLP).
442
443   We find that both DIMLP and MLP outperform linear models (Table 1). DIMLP, incorporating only
444
      within-modality nonlinearity, yields a 2.0% average r² improvement over the linear model (from
      4.10% average r² to 4.18%). But the standard MLP, allowing full nonlinear interactions, achieves a
445
      further 2.6% gain (from 4.18% to 4.29%). This suggests that both types of nonlinearity contribute
446
      to prediction accuracy, but cross-modal nonlinear interactions are particularly crucial.
447
448   Voxelwise analysis (Figure 37 and 38 (in Appendix)) further supports this finding. While DIMLP
449   improves prediction accuracy across various brain regions compared to the linear model, the tran-
450
      sition to a standard MLP leads to further, cortex-wide enhancements. This pattern suggests that
      nonlinear interactions between audio and semantic features are essential for modeling the complex,
451
      distributed neural representations underlying speech comprehension (see Appendix A.12).
452
453   ROI-wise analysis (Figure 2) provides a nuanced view. While the benefits of nonlinearity vary across
454   ROIs, multimodal MLP consistently outperforms or matches DIMLP, and often improves over linear
455   models. Notably, motor regions (e.g., M1M, FEF) and somatosensory regions (e.g., S1M) consis-
456
      tently show gains with statistical significance when allowing nonlinear cross-modal interactions.
      This suggests that nonlinear fusion of modalities is particularly critical for these regions, supporting
457
      their role in complex multimodal processing during speech comprehension.

483   4     D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
484
485   This study underscores the transformative potential of nonlinear, multimodal encoding models for
      advancing our understanding of speech comprehension in the brain. By introducing a nonlinear


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Multi-layer Perceptron (MLP) and integrating audio and linguistic features, we achieved a 14.4%
487   increase in mean normalized correlation across the cortex compared to previous state-of-the-art (An-
488   tonello et al., 2024), predicting 34.3% of the brain’s explainable variance.
489
490
      A significant finding is the enhanced prediction accuracy in motor, sensory, and semantic brain
      regions, demonstrating the brain’s capacity for multimodal integration, in line with neurolinguistic
491
      theories. Notably, motor regions align with the Motor Theory of Speech Perception (Liberman et al.,
492
      1967; 1952; Poeppel & Assaneo, 2020), suggesting active articulatory simulation during comprehen-
493   sion. Performance gains in visual border areas, likely incorporating semantic information, provide
494   evidence for the convergence-divergence-zone theory (Damasio, 1989), suggesting distributed, mul-
495   timodal representation of semantic concepts. Variance partitioning analysis (Appendix A.7) supports
496   this, showing that the majority of explained variance comes from joint audio-semantic processing
497   rather than from either modality alone. Also, semantic feature integration improved accuracy in so-
498   matosensory regions, supporting embodied semantics (Davis & Yee, 2021). These findings show
499   how nonlinear models effectively capture complex spatial interactions essential for multimodal lan-
500   guage processing, revealing deeper insights into the brain’s mechanisms for speech comprehension.
501   A key limitation in current language fMRI research is the lack of sufficiently large datasets. Our
502   experiments revealed that increasing model complexity through additional hidden layers or sophis-
503   ticated architectures like RNNs and Transformers led to overfitting (Appendix A.4). This highlights
504   the urgent need for larger, richer language fMRI datasets to fully harness the potential of deep learn-
505   ing and drive further advancements. Similar to how large-scale datasets like the Natural Scenes
506   Dataset (NSD) (Allen et al., 2022) catalyzed breakthroughs in vision encoding and decoding (Adeli
507
      et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Scotti et al., 2024), comparable efforts in language
      fMRI could enable robust model training, improve generalization, and foster significant advance-
508
      ments in understanding the neural basis of language.
509
510   Developing inherently multimodal LLMs is also essential. Our findings suggest current multimodal
511   LLM architectures may need rethinking. Models like LLAMA 3.1 405B (Dubey et al., 2024) adopt
512   a text-centric approach: pretraining a text model alongside modality-specific encoders, then finetun-
513
      ing by freezing the text model and training only the encoders and adapters. This contrasts with our
      observations of brain processing in two ways. First, current models force other modalities to align
514
      with text representations, whereas our results show auditory information provides unique seman-
515
      tic contributions. Second, sensory inputs in these architectures are processed bottom-up, while our
516   findings suggest rich, bidirectional interactions between modalities across multiple levels of pro-
517   cessing. Future architectures could benefit from treating modalities as equal contributors to meaning
518   construction, enabling bidirectional interactions throughout the processing hierarchy. Such brain-
519   aligned multimodal LLMs may also help uncover the brain’s multimodal integration. Recent re-
520   search using multimodal transformers (Tang et al., 2024) support this potential, demonstrating their
521   capacity to capture shared semantic representations across language and vision in the brain.
522   While nonlinear encoders offer significant benefits, they also pose challenges for precisely attribut-
523   ing predictive power to individual features or modalities. Although we conduct variance partitioning
524   analysis A.7, further work is still required. Addressing this will require a multi-faceted approach to
525   interpretability. First, at the multimodal level, feature attribution techniques like SHAP (Lundberg,
526   2017) and LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016) could provide insights into how the model fuses audio and lin-
527   guistic representations. Second, at the unimodal level, leveraging methods like those demonstrated
528   by Oota et al. (2023) can help identify the unique contributions of the specific features within the
529   audio and language model representations. Third, traditional neuroimaging methods such as rep-
530
      resentational similarity analysis (Kriegeskorte et al., 2008) remain valuable for determining which
      components of language and speech representations are most predictive of brain activity across re-
531
      gions of interest. Nonlinear models also open new avenues for interpretation that are inaccessible to
532
      linear approaches as Yang et al. (2023) showed with vision encoding. For example, exploring what
533   the hidden layers encode could uncover brain-relevant multimodal features that current LLMs are
534   missing. Together, these interpretability strategies could help elucidate the ”black box” nature of
535   nonlinear encoding models, offering deeper insights into their alignment with brain processes.
536
      In conclusion, our study demonstrates the value of nonlinear, multimodal encoding models in un-
537
      derstanding the brain’s speech comprehension mechanisms. Addressing limitations in dataset size
538
      and model interpretability will be pivotal for future advancements, paving the way for biologically
539   aligned AI systems with transformative potential in research and real-world applications.

       A.5   S CALING LLM AND AUDIO MODELS DOES NOT NECESSARILY LEAD TO BETTER
1006
             ENCODERS
1007
1008   Previous research by Antonello et al. (2024) found that increasing the size of large language models
1009   (LLMs) and audio models, such as scaling OPT from 125M to 175B parameters or Whisper from
1010   8M to 637M parameters, enhanced encoding performance. However, performance gains plateaued
1011   for larger models like LLAMA-33B and OPT-175B, which they attributed to overfitting from larger
1012   hidden sizes.
1013
       Building on these findings, our study delves deeper into the scaling trends and offers a refined per-
1014   spective on their implications for brain encoding models. For audio models, we confirm a positive
1015   correlation between model size and performance, as shown in Figure 3 (d). However, this scal-
1016   ing effect does not hold for language models. Specifically, LLAMA-7B, LLAMA-13B, LLAMA-
1017   33B, and LLAMA-65B exhibit comparable encoding performance, as shown in Figure 3 (b). This
1018   suggests diminishing returns beyond 7 billion parameters, a finding consistent with prior work by
1019   Bonnasse-Gahot & Pallier (2024), which reported performance plateaus for LLMs larger than 3
1020   billion parameters.
1021   We also evaluated the impact of scaling training data by examining newer versions of LLAMA and
1022   Whisper (e.g., LLAMA-1, LLAMA-2, LLAMA-3; Whisper v1, v2, v3). Despite larger datasets,
1023   newer versions did not yield significant performance improvements for either audio or semantic en-
1024   coding models. This indicates that advancements in self-supervised learning (SSL) tasks, such as
1025   better next-token prediction, do not necessarily translate to more effective features for brain encod-
       ing. In essence, SSL improvements do not directly enhance brain-aligned representations.

       A.7     VARIANCE PARTITIONING ANALYSIS
1104
1105
       To quantify the unique contributions of different feature spaces in our nonlinear multimodal en-
1106   coding models, we employed a variance partitioning analysis similar to de Heer et al. (2017). This
1107   approach allowed us to determine how much variance could be uniquely explained by each feature
1108   versus that explained by a multiple features. We estimated both the fraction of variance explained
1109   by each feature space individually and the fraction that might be equally well explained by combi-
1110   nations of feature spaces.
1111
       We show our variance partitioning analysis results in three complementary ways: 1) voxel-wise vari-
1112   ance partition results (Appendix A.7.2), 2) voxel-wise plots showing the largest variance partition
1113   for each voxel (Appendix A.7.3), and 3) ROI-wise Venn diagrams illustrating the distribution of
1114   variance explained across different brain regions (Appendix A.7.4).
1115
       For this analysis, we fit models with all possible combinations of feature spaces: two single-feature
1116
       models (audio and semantic), one model combining both features (semantic-audio), and examined
1117
       the distribution of variance explained within brain regions. This allowed us to decompose the total
1118   explained variance into three components: variance uniquely explained by audio features, variance
1119   uniquely explained by semantic features, and variance jointly explained by both feature spaces.
1120
1121
       A.7.1    S UMMARY OF VARIANCE PARTITIONING RESULTS
1122
1123   Looking at the results of Appendix A.7.2, we observe that joint variance dominates across most
1124   cortical regions, contrasting with de Heer et al. (2017) where semantic only features showed greater
1125   dominance. This difference likely stems from our feature choices - whereas de Heer et al. (2017)
1126   used spectral and articulatory features that primarily contained information relevant mostly only to
1127   auditory cortex, our use of Whisper features provides richer auditory representations that enable
1128   better predictions beyond traditional auditory regions. This finding aligns with our earlier argument
1129   (Section 3.2.3) that multiple modalities jointly contribute to neural computations across the cortex
1130
       rather than having one modality dominate.
1131   The dominance pattern of joint variance is consistent both within and near AC, with a notable ex-
1132   ception in early auditory regions where audio features show unique contributions. This hierarchi-
1133   cal organization suggests that while early AC predominantly processes pure acoustic information,
       later AC regions integrate both semantic and auditory features for higher-level speech processing.


                                                        21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       The unique contribution of audio features in early AC is noteworthy as it suggests preservation of
1135   modality-specific processing at early sensory stages despite using rich Whisper features.
1136

1142   suggesting nonlinear encoding may better capture modality-specific processing in these regions.

       tical representation, accounting for approximately 65% of significantly predicted voxels across the
1145
       entire cortex. Core language-processing regions (AC, Broca’s area, sPMv) show particularly strong
1146
       joint representation (around 80 to 90%), supporting our hypothesis that speech comprehension re-
1147   lies on integrated multimodal processing. This integration is consistently observed across subjects,

1149   interpretation. The transition from linear to MLP encoders increases the total number of significantly

2241   To compare the performance between Whisper and LLAMA models, we define the Relative Error
2242   Difference (RED) for each voxel v at time t as:
2243
2244                     RED(v, t) = |fsemantic (v, t) − y(v, t)| − |faudio (v, t) − y(v, t)|
2245
2246   where fsemantic (v, t) is the prediction from the semantic encoding model for voxel v at time t,
2247   faudio (v, t) is the prediction from the audio encoding model for voxel v at time t, and y(v, t) rep-
2248   resents the true value at voxel v and time t. A positive RED value indicates that the audio model
2249   outperforms the semantic model at that specific voxel and time, while a negative value indicates that
2250   the semantic model performs better.
2251   In this analysis, we computed the RED between Whisper and LLAMA models for each voxel v at a
2252   given time t. For each region of interest (ROI), the average RED is calculated as:
2253
2254                                                    1 X
2255                                    REDROI (t) =        RED(v, t)
                                                        N
2256                                                       v∈ROI
2257
2258
       Where N is the number of voxels in the ROI. The correlation matrices were then computed over
       these ROI time series for both linear and nonlinear (MLP) encoders (Figure 31 (b, c)). A high
2259
       correlation between two ROIs indicates that their semantic/audio processing temporal dynamics are
2260
       similar over time.
2261

2263   each voxel (Figure 31 a). Hierarchical clustering was then performed on the correlation matrices,
2264

2266   the ROIs are not functionally clustered based on FC. However, the correlation matrices derived

       This study presents a novel approach, as it is the first to use fMRI language encoding models to
2275   group ROIs based not only on spatial dynamics but also on their temporal processing dynamics.
2276   Traditionally, voxel-wise functional classification or grouping has been the norm in fMRI analysis,
2277   focusing solely on spatial relationships. However, here with the help of fMRI encoders, we incor-
2278   porate both spatial and temporal information, allowing for a more comprehensive view of brain
2279   function, especially in the context of semantic and auditory encoding.
2280

2282   nonlinear encoders (c) scored 0.155, highlighting the improved functional clustering achieved with
