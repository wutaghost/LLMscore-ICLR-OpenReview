009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The ability to generate diverse solutions to a given problem is a hallmark of
012            human creativity. This divergent reasoning is also crucial for machines, enhancing
013            their robustness and enabling them to assist humans in many applications such as
014            scientific discovery. However, existing approaches to multi-step reasoning with
015            large language models (LLMs) have mostly focused only on reasoning accuracy,
016            without further discovering more diverse valid solutions. For example, supervised
017            fine-tuning can improve LLM reasoning quality, but requires extensive supervised
018            data to capture the full range of possible solutions. Reinforcement learning aims to
019
               find limited highest-reward solutions while neglecting the solution diversity. To
               fill this gap, we propose Flow of Reasoning (F O R), an efficient diversity-seeking
020
               LLM finetuning method aimed at improving reasoning quality and diversity with
021
               minimal data. F O R formulates multi-step LLM reasoning as a Markovian flow on
022            a DAG-structured reasoning graph. This formulation allows us to incorporate and
023            adapt principled GFlowNet approaches, for finetuning LLMs to sample diverse
024            reasoning paths with probabilities proportional to the (unnormalized) reward of
025            target problems. Extensive experiments show that, with limited training examples
026            (e.g., 15 examples), F O R enables the discovery of diverse, creative, high-quality
027            solutions, greatly outperforming a wide range of existing inference and training
028            methods across five challenging puzzle-solving tasks, including BlocksWorld
029            (embodied reasoning), Game24 (math puzzle solving), Rubik’s Cube (spatial
030
               reasoning), 1D-ARC (abstraction reasoning), and PrOntoQA (logical reasoning).

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Divergent problem solving is the ability to generate multiple diverse solutions to a given prob-
036   lem (Runco, 1991; Runco & Acar, 2012). As a hallmark of human intelligence, this ability drives
037   creativity by uncovering novel ways to accomplish a task, providing more possibilities and adaptivity
038   in different complex situations. Similarly, by encouraging machines to explore diverse solutions
039
      rather than confining to one reasoning path, we not only enhance machines’ robustness (e.g., by
      ranking or aggregating different solutions) (Wang et al., 2022), but also empower automated systems
040
      that assist humans in generating ideas and thinking out-of-the-box, thereby potentially facilitating
041
      task completion (Shinn et al., 2024), education (Li et al., 2023a), and scientific discovery (Jain et al.,
042   2023a).
043
044   State-of-the-art reasoning with large language models (LLMs), however, has largely focused on
045
      improving only the problem-solving accuracy with the topmost solution, without moving a step
      further to discover more diverse valid solutions. Specifically, inference methods, such as CoT (chain
046
      of thought, Wei et al., 2022), ToT (Yao et al., 2024), RAP (Hao et al., 2023), and others (Chen et al.,
047
      2024b; Besta et al., 2024), rely heavily on the underlying pretrained LLM’s capability and decoding
048   algorithms to obtain diverse reasoning solutions. Moreover, the search-based inference (Yao et al.,
049   2024; Hao et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b; Besta et al., 2024) can be computationally costly when
050   searching for multiple reasoning paths. On the other hand, finetuning methods improve the inherent
051   abilities of the underlying LLMs. However, the popular supervised finetuning (SFT) (Yue et al.,
052   2023; Yu et al., 2023c) often demands extensive supervision data to capture the full diversity of
053   solutions, which can be costly to label in many applications. Alternatively, reinforcement learning
      (RL), such as proximal policy optimization (PPO, Schulman et al., 2017), trains LLMs to generate

080   To overcome the limitations, we introduce Flow of Reasoning (F O R), a data-efficient approach
081   that finetunes LLMs for diverse reasoning with only minimal data. F O R draws inspirations from
082
      generative flow networks (GFlowNets) for amortized diverse sampling (Bengio et al., 2021) that
      have been studied in different domains like molecule synthesis (Koziarski et al., 2024; Kim et al.,
083
      2024a) and operation scheduling (Zhang et al., 2023a). In particular, F O R enables diversity-seeking
084
      finetuning of multi-step LLM reasoning, to sample high-quality reasoning paths with probabilities
085   proportional to the reward of target problems (as opposed to reward maximization in conventional RL).
086   To this end, we formulate multi-step LLM reasoning from a Markovian flow perspective (Figure 2),
087   where each reasoning step corresponds to an edge (action) that leads to the next node (state) in a flow
088   graph. The reasoning process thus forms a flow that travels step-by-step from an initial state to the
089   terminal states of the target problem. Based on this new formulation, we introduce the trajectory
090   balance objective and adapt efficient exploration methods from the recent GFlowNet studies, enabling
091   effective finetuning of LLMs to align with the task reward using only 15 input examples.
092   F O R differs crucially from the recent GFlowNet applications on autoregressive sequence generation
093   with or without LLMs (Hu et al., 2023a; Malkin et al., 2022a). In particular, contrary to the token-level
094   modeling in the previous work, F O R introduces higher-level modeling at the granularity of reasoning
095   steps. This combines the best of the GFlowNet sequence generation (Hu et al., 2023a; Malkin et al.,
096   2022a) and the aforementioned search-based LLM reasoning (Yao et al., 2024; Hao et al., 2023)
097   while overcoming their limitations, by enabling more flexible DAG-structured reasoning graphs, more
098   efficient handling of complex multi-step reasoning problems, and thereby greatly improved reasoning
099
      quality and diversity as shown in §4. We evaluate the divergent problem-solving capability of the
      proposed approach on five puzzle-solving problems that have proven challenging for LLM reasoning,
100
      including BlocksWorld that involves embodied reasoning (Kambhampati et al., 2024), Game24
101
      involving math puzzle solving (Yao et al., 2024), Rubik’s Cube involving spatial reasoning (Ding
102   et al., 2023), 1D-ARC involving abstraction reasoning (Xu et al., 2023b), and PrOntoQA involving
103   logical reasoning (Saparov & He, 2022). Empirical results show that F O R, with limited (e.g. about
104   15) training examples, generates diverse, high-quality solutions, greatly outperforming a wide range
105   of baselines with 20% - 85% improvements, including supervised training methods like SFT, reward-
106   maximizing reinforcement learning like PPO, diversity-seeking approaches GFN-CoT and various
107   decoding methods, and advanced inference methods like CoT, ToT, GoT, and RAP. Ablation studies
      further validate the key designs in F O R that lead to robustness and effectiveness.

124   2 R ELATED W ORK
125   LLM reasoning. Recent LLMs (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Chowdhery et al., 2023)
126   have shown strong potential in tackling complex reasoning tasks (Hu et al., 2023c; Zhang et al., 2023d;
127   Yu et al., 2023b). (1) Fine-tuning LLMs, including supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and reinforcement
128   learning (RL), is a key method for improving LLM reasoning abilities. SFT, leveraging large and
129   high-quality datasets of reasoning chains, has proven highly effective (Yu et al., 2023c; Yue et al.,
130   2023; Yuan et al., 2024a). RL techniques like PPO are widely used for optimizing reward-driven
131   behavior in LLMs (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Havrilla et al., 2024). However, both
132   approaches tend to limit solution diversity. (2) Prompting-based methods engages LLMs in a step-
133
      by-step thinking process. Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022) enhances LLM performance
      by guiding them through intermediate steps to reach the final answer. Building on CoT, methods
134
      like ToT (Yao et al., 2024) and GoT (Besta et al., 2024) model reasoning as tree and graph searches,
135
      enabling exploration of multiple paths. Other methods, like RAP (Hao et al., 2023) and XoT (Ding
136   et al., 2023) use planning approaches such as MCTS to refine reasoning trajectories.
137
138   GFlowNets. GFlowNets (Bengio et al., 2021) were developed to generate diverse, high-reward
139
      samples from unnormalized distributions (Shen et al., 2023b; Roy et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023c;
      Ma et al., 2024; Pan et al., 2023b), making them particularly effective in domains like molecule
140
      synthesis (Koziarski et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024a; Lu et al., 2024) and biological sequence design
141
      (Ghari et al., 2023; Jain et al., 2022), where diversity is essential. Unlike traditional reinforcement
142   learning (e.g., PPO), which focuses on maximizing reward, GFlowNets sample complete trajectories
143   with probabilities proportional to their rewards, promoting exploration of the solution space. Recently,
144   GFlowNets with LLMs have been applied to autoregressive tasks like token-level text generation (Hu
145   et al., 2023a), but these approaches are limited to token-level sampling, making them less suited for
146   complex reasoning. F O R extends GFlowNet principles to higher-level multi-step reasoning, modeling
147   it as a Markovian flow through a DAG, enabling the exploration of diverse reasoning paths.

151   3.1   M ULTI - STEP LLM R EASONING AS G ENERATIVE F LOW
152
      We start by formulating step-by-step LLM reasoning from the Markovian flow perspective. As we will
153   show later, the new flow-based formulation allows us to connect LLM reasoning with the GFlowNet
154   approaches for diversity-seeking finetuning. Meanwhile, the unique setting of multi-step LLM
155   reasoning also inspires generalizations to the standard GFlowNets formalism (e.g., parameterization
156   and exploration mechanisms) for enhanced efficiency. Figure 2 illustrates our approach. We refer to
157   Appendix B for preliminaries and backgrounds of GFlowNets.
158
159   The Multi-Step Reasoning Problem. Consider a multi-step reasoning problem that gives an
160   initial state s0 and a goal g. For example, in BlocksWorld (Figure 2), an initial state is the starting
161   configuration of the block stack, and a goal describes the desired configuration of blocks. Reasoning
      aims to find complete paths (or trajectories) that lead from the initial state to the states that satisfy

162
      the goal. Given a current state s, applying an action on it leads to the transition to the next state
163   s1 , denoted as s Ñ s1 . For example, in Figure 2, state s0 transits to s1 after an action "pickup
164   blue". A complete trajectory is thus a sequence of transitions τ “ ps0 Ñ s1 Ñ ¨ ¨ ¨ Ñ sn q P T ,
165   where sn is the terminal state and T is the set of all complete trajectories. Given a current state st ,
166   there could be multiple alternative next actions, resulting in different branches of the reasoning. Also,
167   different sequences of actions can lead to the same intermediate/terminal states, as shown in Figure 1.
168   As a result, the multi-step reasoning has the structure of a directed acyclic graph (DAG).
169   The reasoning graph consists of diverse trajectories that lead to different terminal states. A crucial
170   component often provided in reasoning tasks is the reward Rpsn q P Rě0 , which assigns a numerical
171   value to any terminal state sn . For instance, a terminal state meeting the goal g receives a high reward.
172   As discussed in §1, to generate diverse high-quality reasoning trajectories for solving a task, we want
173   to sample the trajectories with probabilities proportional to the reward. This significantly differs from
174   popular reinforcement learning methods (e.g., PPO) and prompting-based planning algorithms (e.g.,
175   RAP, ToT), which focus on optimizing for only the maximum-reward trajectory.
176

178   reward is particularly challenging (LeCun et al., 2006; Qin et al., 2022). To overcome the difficulty, we
179

      (Bengio et al., 2021) and has been studied in other machine learning settings like molecule generation
180
      (Pan et al., 2022; Malkin et al., 2022a; Shen et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2023c; Lahlou et al., 2023; Li
181
      et al., 2024a; He et al., 2024). Specifically, we define a trajectory flow function F : T Ñ Rě0 .

260   3.2.2    E FFICIENT E XPLORATION
261
262   The trajectory space is combinatorially large. We want to set up a πpτ, g; θq distribution in Eq.(7) that
263   enables efficient exploration of the trajectory space and produces effective samples for training the
264   parameters θ of the policy PF . Drawing inspirations from the recent GFlowNet literature (Vemgal
265   et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023a; Hu et al., 2023a), we combine both on-policy and off-policy strategies.
266
      Moreover, we adapt the local search strategy from (Kim et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022; Sendera
      et al., 2024) to further enhance the exploration and yield stronger performance.
267
268   More specifically, for on-policy explorations, we use the online policy PF pst |st´1 ; θ, gq itself and its
269   tempered version to create training trajectories τ given the goal g and initial state s0 in a reasoning
      problem. For off-policy explorations, we use standard options from previous work (Vemgal et al.,


                                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      2023; Shen et al., 2023a; Hu et al., 2023a), including a replay buffer that prioritizes past high-reward
271   trajectories, ϵ-sampling, and offline trajectory data (for Game24 in §4.3). To further explore high-
272   reward regions, we incorporate and modify a local search method (Figure 2) with higher efficiency. In
273   particular, we select the trajectory with the highest reward in each trajectory batch, truncate the latter
274   portion of the trajectory, and reconstruct it using a random policy PU . This random policy avoids the
275   computationally intensive forward process of LLMs, leading to enhanced efficiency. This approach
276   reconstructs trajectories with a high probability of receiving higher rewards, as partially destroyed
277   trajectories with high rewards are more likely to select the correct actions at the initial steps, while
278   potentially making mistakes in the subsequent steps. Further details on the exploration strategies and
279
      local search process are provided in Appendices D and E, respectively.

282   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
283
284   Baselines. We compare our approach with several prompting-based methods, including k-shot
285   CoT (Wei et al., 2022) (with k = 1, 5, 15), ToT (Yao et al., 2024) (using BFS and DFS), GoT (Besta
286   et al., 2024), XoT (Ding et al., 2023), and RAP (Hao et al., 2023). For fine-tuning-based methods,
287   we evaluate SFT with diversity-enhancing decoding strategies like Temperature Sampling (Shi et al.,
288   2024) (α “ 1.0, 0.5, 0.1, where α is a temperature used to adjust the probability distribution over
289
      the vocabulary for the next token), Nucleus Sampling (Holtzman et al., 2019) (η “ 0.95, selecting
      from tokens that together make up η of the probability mass), Typical Sampling (Meister et al., 2023)
290
      (τ “ 0.95, where the sampling distribution is restricted to words with negative log probabilities
291
      near the conditional entropy, and then η of the distribution mass is truncated), and diverse beam
292   search (Vijayakumar et al., 2016) (DBS, beam width k “ 20). Additionally, we apply fine-tuning with
293   PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) and GFN-CoT (Hu et al., 2023a). We also compare against OpenAI-O1,
294   the latest and strongest reasoning model. All finetuning methods are trained on the same dataset as
295   F O R. Except for Game24, which uses Llama-2-13B, Llama-3-8B is the base model for all other tasks.
296   In the BlocksWorld task, we evaluated most baselines for a broad comparison, but some methods
297   (e.g., GFN-CoT) showed suboptimal performance, which informed our decision to selectively apply
298   baselines in the subsequent tasks. For the remaining tasks, we focused on high-performing methods
299   from the initial evaluation.
300   Evaluation. As mentioned in §1, an effective reasoning method should not only produce correct
301   solutions but also maximize the number of correct solutions found. Unlike previous approaches that
302   evaluate a single solution per problem, we propose generating n solutions per problem and assess
303   methods based on four criteria: (1) Accuracy (Acc): Success is defined if at least one of the n
304   solutions is correct. (2) Diversity: For solved problems, we report the average number of unique
305   correct solutions among the n—higher is better (see Appendix C.1 for details). (3) Creativity: For
306
      each method, we report the proportion of unique successful trajectories found in all solutions that are
      not discovered by other methods. (See Appendix C.2 for details.) (4) Runtime: The average time
307
      taken by a method to produce one solution is used as an efficiency metric. For all the datasets, we
308
      recorded the average results and standard deviation of our performance from 3 repetitions, except for
309   tree- and graph-structured methods and O1-series, which require significant time or expense to find a
310   single solution. Creativity is calculated based on the result of 1 repetition due to the small standard
311   deviation observed in accuracy and diversity metrics.

974
975    Reasoning with LLM. Recent LLMs (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Bai et al.,
976
       2022; Chowdhery et al., 2023) have demonstrated great potentials in tackling complex reasoning
       tasks (Cobbe et al., 2021; Mishra et al., 2022; Hendrycks et al., 2021; Rein et al., 2023; Mialon
977
       et al., 2023). (1) Fine-tuning LLMs is a primary way to enhance their reasoning abilities, including
978
       SFT and reinforcement learning (RL) approaches. SFT with large-scale and high-quality datasets of
979    reasoning chains has proven very effective (Yu et al., 2023c; Yue et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024a).
980    Various methods for constructing training samples have been proposed when ground truth reasoning
981    chains are not available. For example, STaR (Zelikman et al., 2022) uses online sampling with
982    self-correction to find positive samples. ReSTEM (Singh et al., 2023) and V-STaR(Hosseini et al.,
983    2024) filter samples with external verifiers. RL techniques, particularly reward-maximizing policy
984    optimization methods like PPO, are widely employed in LLMs (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al.,
985    2022; Havrilla et al., 2024; Luong et al., 2024). However, both maximum likelihood training (i.e.
986    SFT) and reward-maximizing policy optimization (e.g., PPO) do not encourage models to generate
987
       diverse solutions. (2) prompting-based reasoning algorithms aim to better elicit the knowledge
       inside LLMs without tuning their parameters. Techniques such as CoT (Wei et al., 2022) and its
988
       variants (Chen et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2023e; Zhou et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022)
989
       have improved LLM performance by enabling them to generate intermediate steps before arriving at
990    a final answer. To provide reasoning more guidance , self-evaluation (Xie et al., 2024b; Shinn et al.,
991    2024; Madaan et al., 2024) and reward models are introduced to enhance reasoning process (Uesato
992    et al., 2022; Lightman et al., 2023) Besides, a more relevant series of works combine LLM reasoning
993    capabilities with planning and search algorithms such as MCTS (Hao et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023;
994    Zhao et al., 2024), tree and graph search (Jung et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2024; Besta
995    et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2023). Moreover, recent studies turn to amortizing computation for reasoning
996    paths (Yuan et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Bansal et al., 2024), such as self-correct (Kumar et al., 2024;
997    Saunders et al., 2022), self-improvement (Tian et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024b).
998
       GFlowNets. GFlowNets (Bengio et al., 2021) were originally proposed to learn policies for sam-
999    pling from unnormalized distributions, with a primary motivation from scientific discovery (Jain et al.,
1000   2023a), which requires generating diverse high-reward samples (Shen et al., 2023b; Roy et al., 2023;
1001   Zhang et al., 2023c; Ma et al., 2024; Pan et al., 2023b), such as molecular generation (Koziarski et al.,
1002   2024; Kim et al., 2024a; Lu et al., 2024) and biological sequence generation (Ghari et al., 2023; Jain
1003   et al., 2022). Beyond the science domain, GFlowNets have also been applied in various downstream
1004   applications such as recommendation systems (Liu et al., 2023b), domain adaptation (Zhu et al.,
1005   2023), combinatorial optimization (Zhang et al., 2023b; Kim et al., 2024b) and explainability of
1006   deep neural networks (Li et al., 2023b). Additionally, GFlowNets have proven to be suitable for
1007
       sampling from posterior distributions (Hu et al., 2023b; Deleu et al., 2022; 2024; Zhang et al., 2022).
       As a reinforcement learning method, prior works have incorporated intermediate feedback with
1008
       GFlowNets to address sparse reward issues (Pan et al., 2023a; Jang et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2022) and
1009
       multi-objective rewards (Jain et al., 2023b; Hernandez-Garcia et al., 2023; Chen & Mauch, 2023).
1010   There are also theoretical analyses treating GFlowNets as recurrent MCMC (Deleu & Bengio, 2023)
1011   and variational inference (Malkin et al., 2022b; Zimmermann et al., 2022) that are used to model the
1012   distribution over trajectories.
1013
1014   Lateral and vertical thinking. Vertical and lateral thinking (Waks, 1997; Ismayilzada et al., 2024)
1015   are two distinct approaches that differ significantly in their focus and methodology. Vertical thinking
1016   emphasizes logical, structured, and sequential reasoning, often following a step-by-step approach to
1017
       solve problems. Our work aligns with this paradigm to generate multiple correct, structured reasoning
       processes to achieve specific goals. In contrast, lateral thinking prioritizes creativity and innovation,
1018
       encouraging the exploration of unconventional perspectives and challenging established assumptions.
1019
       Multiple benchmarks are proposed to evaluate the lateral thinking ability of LLMs (Huang et al.,
1020   2023; Chen et al., 2024a; Kraaijveld et al., 2024; Todd et al., 2024). To further improve the ability of
1021   lateral and divergent thinking, (Zhong et al., 2024) designs a fine-tuning and inference framework to
1022   generate unexpectable but reasonable answers, and (Summers-Stay et al., 2023) proposes a prompting
1023   framework to enhance such ability. In addition, riddle-solving QA tasks that require reasoning about
1024   unexpected or unconventional answers such as BrainTeaser(Jiang et al., 2023) and RiddleSense (Lin
1025   et al., 2021). Future work should investigate formalizing these tasks and developing quantitative
       approaches to effectively guide LLMs in tackling them.

1168

1169   likelihood of the LLM predicting the action and (b) self-evaluation, where the LLM is prompted with
1170   the question, "Is this action good?" and the answer is mapped to a reward value. We implement ToT
1171   with both breadth-first search (BFS) and depth-first search (DFS), terminating after generating 10
1172   solutions.

1174

1176   matches the goal, a high reward will be assigned.

1472      distribution over action space PU pat |st´1 q with p1 ´ ϵq probability. (3) To further explore the

1592   the exploratory nature of FoR’s training objective, which encourages the model to sample diverse
1593   successful trajectories. By expanding the search space through high-reward exploration, FoR increases
1594   the chance of finding successful outcomes. This capability not only improves the robustness of the
1595
       model but also enhances its generalization to new scenarios, showcasing the effectiveness of FoR in
       addressing complex reasoning tasks.

       G     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
1645
1646
       Due to resource constraints, our experiments use language models with up to 13B parameters.
1647   However, we expect F O R to hold for larger models as well, and may potentially benefit larger models
1648   even more. Recent works (Xi et al., 2024; AlKhamissi et al., 2023) that finetune larger models to
1649   improve their reasoning ability with maximizing objectives usually need a large amount of data, and
1650   our data-efficient F O R may improve this process. Future work should further address two limitations
1651   in F O R.
1652
       The first is aquisation of a large amount of trajectories efficiently. Online sampling with LLMs
1653   is computationally expensive, leading to more efficient and effective strategies for exploring more
1654   complex settings such as real-world settings AlfWorld (Shridhar et al., 2020) and TravelPlanner (Xie
1655   et al., 2024a) to be further studied.
1656
       The second is faciliating F O R long-range steps reasoning. LLMs fall short in long-range planning
1657
       and reasoning, thus methods like MCTS (Feng et al., 2023) or automatic reasoning system (Trinh
1658
       et al., 2024) can be combined with LLMs for long-horizon diverse reasoning.
