009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Early exits are an important efficiency mechanism integrated into deep neural net-
012            works enabling the network’s forward pass to terminate early. These methods add
013            trainable internal classifiers to the backbone network, which, however, changes
014            the training dynamics. Most early exit methods either train the backbone network
015            and exit heads simultaneously, or train the heads independently. However, the
016            impact of this design choice on the overall network performance remains largely
017            unexplored, as most studies simply select one approach without discussing its im-
018
               plications. In this paper, we analyze the effects of these training strategies on
               multi-exit networks, showing that joint training leads to impaired performance at
019
               higher computational budgets, while disjoint training results in suboptimal perfor-
020
               mance at lower budgets. To address these limitations, we propose a mixed training
021            strategy where the backbone is trained first, followed by the training of the entire
022            multi-exit network. Our results show that this alternative training regime arrives at
023            solutions similar to standard static neural networks, yet does not share the disad-
024            vantages of disjoint training. We further analyze the differences between training
025            regimes in terms of numerical rank, gradient dominance of each exit, and mutual
026            information. Comprehensive evaluations across various architectures, datasets,
027            and early-exit methods show consistent improvements in performance and effi-
028            ciency using the proposed mixed strategy.

077   2   T RAINING REGIMES
078
079   Early exit methods fundamentally alter the organization of neural networks. It is widely believed
080   that neural networks develop a hierarchical representation of features, where earlier layers learn
081   basic shapes and patterns, while later layers progressively capture more complex abstractions (35).
082   In other words, the earlier layers are characterized by higher frequency features while later layers
083   learn low frequency elements. This regularity is disrupted in the case of early exit architectures as
084   the backbone network is given additional classifiers that are placed in earlier parts of the network.
085   These changes in architecture require a different approach for training and more nuanced analysis
086
      how the training should proceed.
087   In early-exit setting, the parameters can be divided into backbone parameters and internal classifier
088   (IC) parameters. Each of these two groups of parameters can be trained separately, or jointly. In
089   this paper, we frame the training process of any early-exit method as consisting of three following
090   phases:
091   Phase 1: Train the backbone network parameters θb by minimizing the loss at the final output layer
092   (could be the last IC or an added final classifier).

      In practical applications one can also use a set of pre-trained weights instead of training the model
109   from scratch. Correspondingly, we generalize the early exit training regimes into three types based
110   on which of the phases are performed:
111
112
      Disjoint training (Phase 1+3, ”+-+”). The model parameters undergo training during the first and
      third phases, that is the backbone architecture is trained first, and then the ICs are trained separately
113
      with the backbone parameters being frozen.
114
115   Joint Training (Phase 2, ”-+-”). The training consists only of the second phase in which the entire
116   model including the IC is trained from scratch. It is currently the most common way of training
117   early-exit methods (18).
118   Mixed training (Phase 1+2, ”++-”). The training consists of two phases. The backbone is trained
119   in isolation first, and then the entire network, including the ICs, is trained jointly. The regime
120   emphasizes the importance of backbone pre-training as a better way to initialize the architecture for
121   further training. This is our proposed way to improve early-exit training.

      3.2   N UMERICAL RANK
163
164   While different regimes may fall into different loss basins, the question arises how that affects the
165   learning outcome. To build a better understanding of multi-exit models, we look at how the choice of
166   early-exit training regime influences intermediate representations of the backbone architecture. We
167   analyze the expressiveness of early-exit architectures under different regimes by means of numerical
168   ranks of activation maps (17). Mathematically, the rank is evaluated as:
169
                                            r = Rank(A),       A ∈ Rn×m                                        (4)
170
171
      where A is the activation matrix of dimensions n (number of samples) and m (number of features).
172
173   The rank of the internal representations associated with different layers can provide insight into
174   the “expressiveness”, or capacity of the network. A higher rank (closer to the maximum possible
175
      for a given layer’s matrix dimensions) indicates that the layer can capture more complex patterns
      or features in the data, as it implies a greater degree of linear independence among the feature
176
      detectors in that layer. High-rank activations matrices in a network suggest that the network is
177
      utilizing its capacity to learn diverse, high-frequency features, whereas a low rank might indicate
178   that the network is not fully exploiting its potential.
179
180   In this framework, the numerical rank of the backbone architecture is analyzed under different early-
181
      exit regimes. A regular neural network is characterized by higher rank in earlier layers and lower
      rank in deeper layers as shown in Fig. 3a. Note that training only the backbone corresponds to
182
      the model obtained in the disjoint regime, as the backbone is not modified in this approach. In
183
      Fig. 3a we can see the change in network expressiveness after adding intermediate classifiers and
184   training the entire architecture jointly. The numerical rank rises across the layers and becomes more
185   uniform as consequence. This result indicates that optimal multi-exit models necessitates higher
186   expressiveness of layers, and training with the disjoint regime prevents this from occurring.
187
      Fig. 3b shows the difference between the network trained with mixed and joint regime. The mixed
188
      regime has a flatter structure with relatively lower ranks earlier and higher ranks later. We hypoth-
189
      esize this since early-exit architecture consists of a set of classifiers placed across the network, the
190   flatter architecture is desired for better performance across all the classifiers. In contrast, a steeper
191   curve as in the case of joint regime may resemble more a regular architecture and be less suitable
192   for early-exit task.

251   evaluation. The fact that the gradient of the last IC dominates the total gradient during almost the
252   entire training period in the mixed regime explains why it never leaves the basin that models trained
253   with the disjoint regime occupy.

256
257   In the context of neural networks, mutual information between X and Z represents how much
258
      information the input X provides about the internal representation Z after passing through a neu-
      ral network. For random variables X and Z, the mutual information is defined as: I(X; Z) =
259   R     R                  p(x,z)
260     x∈X z∈Z
                  p(x, z) log p(x)p(z) dx dz where p(x, z) is the joint probability distribution of X and Z,
261   and p(x) and p(z) are the marginal distributions of X and Z, respectively. In practical terms, for
262   neural networks, we use Monte Carlo sampling to estimate I(X; Z) due to the high dimensionality
263
      of feature spaces.
264   In their work, (10) utilize the concept of mutual information between X and Z (I(X; Z)) in the
265   framework of the information bottleneck (IB) principle. The IB principle aims to find a balance
266   between the informativeness of the representation Z for predicting the target variable Y and the
267   complexity of Z in terms of its mutual information with the input X. Specifically, minimizing
268   I(X; Z) reduces the complexity and overfitting by ensuring Z retains only the essential information
269
      from X, and maximizing I(Y ; Z) ensures that the representation Z is informative enough to predict
      the target variable Y effectively.

483   5   R ELATED W ORK
484
485   Early exiting is a notable application of the conditional computation paradigm (3). While concep-
      tually similar to earlier classifier cascades (32; 27), it differs in that all classifiers are integrated


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      within a single model, enabling end-to-end training. The first multi-exit model was introduced by
487   Teerapittayanon et al. (26), and the field has expanded considerably since its inception.
488
489   Joint training is the most widely used and well-established strategy for early-exit models (18). This
490
      approach has been successfully applied to dynamic inference under various constraints, such as
      energy or time limitations (28), and extended to diverse early-exit applications, including low-
491
      resolution classification (31), quality enhancement (33), and Question-Answering systems (25).
492
      While joint training has proven effective, several studies have demonstrated significant improve-
493   ments through modifications to the training process. For instance, knowledge distillation from the
494   final classifier to earlier internal classifiers has been shown to enhance their performance (20; 14; 16).
495   Similarly, ensembling multiple intermediate classifiers can improve prediction accuracy (21; 23).
496   The Global Past-Future (GPF) method (15) incorporates information from both earlier predictions
497   and surrogate later predictions to improve inference. Additionally, recent works (7; 34; 4) identify
498   a train-test mismatch in conventional multi-exit approaches and propose strategies to address this
499   issue, further enhancing the robustness of early-exit models.
500   SDN (11) was one of the first to explore the training of early-exit models through the pre-training
501   of the architecture’s backbone followed by separate training of the classifiers. Multiple subsequent
502   works have focused on optimizing early-exit models based only on this setup (29; 12; 16), potentially
503   limiting the general applicability of their findings. For instance, Wołczyk et al. (30) employ an
504   ensembling technique that combines predictions from earlier internal classifiers, weights of which
505   are trained in a separate, third training phase. Lahiany et al. (12) propose PTEENet, which augments
506   pre-trained networks with confidence heads that dynamically adjust based on available resources and
507
      unlabeled data.
508   Kaya et al. (11) were the first to explore both joint and disjoint training approaches for early-exit
509   models. These approaches are also briefly reviewed in surveys such as (24; 18). Furthermore,
510   techniques like weighting the losses at each exit head (36; 11; 7) or scaling gradients (14) can be
511   regarded as variations of the joint training paradigm. To the best of our knowledge, this work is the
512   first to directly compare models trained under different regimes and to provide a detailed analysis of
513
      the training dynamics of multi-exit models.
514
515   6    C ONCLUSION , D ISCUSSION AND TAKEAWAYS
516
517   This study contributes insights into the training of early-exit models, providing a foundation for
518   developing more efficient dynamic deep learning systems. The work presents a comprehensive
519   analysis and evaluation of different training regimes for early-exit models in deep neural networks.
520   By categorizing training approaches into disjoint, joint, and mixed regimes, we have demonstrated
521   that the way the backbone and internal classifiers in early-exit architectures are trained influences its
522
      performance and efficiency. Below we summarize some practical takeaways when training early-exit
      architectures.
523
524   Mixed. Mixed regime demonstrates substantial robustness across various factors, including different
525   data modalities and early-exit approaches with varying exit criteria. Therefore, the mixed regime is
526   generally preferred, combining the benefits of both disjoint and joint training. The mixed regime
527   ensures that the backbone network is well-optimized before integrating internal classifiers, leading
528
      to improved computational efficiency and accuracy. It is particularly recommended for cases where
      the performance on medium and higher computational budgets is the most important requirement.
529
530   Joint. Joint regime may be preferable as it is relatively simple to implement, and performs well for
531   small computational budgets. However, it underperforms when the backbone is initialized from a
532   pre-trained model.
533   Disjoint. This regime is generally inferior compared to the others in multiple setups, but performs
534   well for some language datasets with a low number of classes. It may be preferred when the back-
535   bone is shared, or the lack of resources prevents us from training the backbone network.
536
      Future research should investigate more sophisticated optimization techniques, such as adaptive
537
      learning rates or meta-learning strategies, specifically designed for early-exit models. Moreover,
538   other training strategies may be proposed where training is tailored to particular sub-networks within
539   the entire early-exit architecture.

742
743   The size of an internal classifier in early exit architectures refers to the number of layers and neurons
744   that make up the classifier inserted at intermediate layers of a neural network. The internal classifier
745   typically consists of a linear layer, such as a fully connected (dense) layer or a small convolutional
746
