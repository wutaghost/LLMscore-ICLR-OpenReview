009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose accelerating the simulation of Lagrangian dynamics, such as fluid flows,
012            granular flows, and elastoplasticity, with neural-operator-based reduced-order mod-
013            eling. While full-order approaches simulate the physics of every particle within the
014            system, incurring high computation time for dense inputs, we propose to simulate
015            the physics on sparse graphs constructed by sampling from the spatially discretized
016            system. Our discretization-invariant reduced-order framework trains on any spatial
017
               discretizations and computes temporal dynamics on any sparse sampling of these
               discretizations through neural operators. Our proposed approach is termed Graph
018
               Informed Optimized Reduced-Order Modeling or GIOROM. Through reduced
019
               order modeling, we ensure lower computation time by sparsifying the system by
020            6.6-32.0×, while ensuring high-fidelity full-order inference via neural fields. We
021            show that our model generalizes to a range of initial conditions, resolutions, and
022            materials.

027   Simulating the dynamics of physical systems is crucial in fields like computational fluid mechanics,
028   digital twins, graphics, and robotics. These spatio-temporal dynamics are often described by partial
029   differential equations (PDEs) in the following form:
030                    J (ϕ, ∇ϕ, ∇2 ϕ, . . . , ϕ̇, ϕ̈, . . .) = 0,   ϕ(X, t) : Ω × T → Rd ,              (1)

033
      ∇ and (·) ˙ signify the spatial gradient and time derivative, respectively. Here, Ω ⊂ Rd and T ⊂ R
034   denote the spatial and temporal domains, respectively. In this work, we focus on the deformation map
035   arising from continuum mechanics (Gonzalez & Stuart, 2008), i.e., the position field x = ϕ(X, t). Its
036   first and second temporal derivatives denote “velocity” v = ϕ̇ and “acceleration” a = ϕ̈ respectively.

044   Deep-learning-based methods have emerged as an efficient tool for solving these spatio-temporal
045
      systems (Azizzadenesheli et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; Cuomo et al., 2022). Instead of solving the
      PDE explicitly at every time-step, these deep-learning methods implicitly time-step the system via
046
      neural network evaluations. Graph Neural Network-based models, such as GNS (Sanchez-Gonzalez
047
      et al., 2020), have shown promise in simulating a diverse range of physical systems. A salient feature
048   of GNS is that after training on particular spatial discretizations of the system, it can generalize to
049   other discretizations. However, scaling GNS to large-scale systems can be challenging due to the
050   message-passing operations, which aggregate information between all the neighbors. Furthermore,
051   using a large number of message passing layers leads to over-smoothing. Neural operators Li
052   et al. (2020a); Lu et al. (2021); Kovachki et al. (2023), which learn the mappings between infinite-
053   dimensional function spaces, offer a principled way of dealing with input and output data at arbitrary
      resolution. In particular, graph-based neural operators Li et al. (2020b;c; 2024) can be applied to

082   Reduced-order methods aim to address this computational challenge by operating on reduced-order
083   systems (e.g., a subset of the full-order point clouds) (Benner et al., 2015). In particular, continuous

087   solve the PDEs explicitly at every time step, thereby preventing them from being applied to problems
088   where the PDEs are unknown (Lusch et al., 2018).

      While, reduced-order methods such as CROM Chen et al. (2023) address computational costs by
      operating in reduced-order spaces, their flexibility and speed for solving spatio-temporal dynamics is
091
      still limited by their reliance on classical numerical PDE solvers. To overcome these limitations, we
092
      use the data-driven capabilities of neural operators, which have shown significant accelerations for
093   solving PDEs on different geometries. Specifically, we leverage the graph neural operator as in Li
094   et al. (2024) to handle irregular point clouds. However, the latter model is designed to learn Eulerian
095   formulations of computational fluid dynamics problems without temporal dynamics. Lagrangian
096   dynamics, on the other hand, is influenced by inter-particle interactions, such as collisions. Thus, we
097   propose a discretization-agnostic Interaction Operator, which allows for local interactions within the
098   graph neural operator (see Section 4). Moreover, we propose a transformer neural operator in latent
099   space to efficiently model global interactions, see also Figure 1. In summary, we introduce a novel
100   method for learning Lagragian dynamics that reduces the cost for both spatial (via neural fields) and
101
      temporal (via neural operators) modeling in a fully discretization-agnostic manner.

118   Neural physics solvers Neural network-based solvers have shown great success in accelerating
119
      physical simulations, including problems in fluid dynamics Sanchez-Gonzalez et al. (2020); Kochkov
      et al. (2021); Vinuesa & Brunton (2021); Mao et al. (2020); Shukla et al. (2024); Hao et al. (2024),
120
      solid mechanics Geist & Trimpe (2021); Capuano & Rimoli (2019); Jin et al. (2023), climate modeling
121
      Pathak et al. (2022), and robotics Ni & Qureshi (2022); Kaczmarski et al. (2023). Such approaches
122   can be purely data-driven or physics-informed, i.e., leveraging an underlying PDE Raissi et al. (2019);
123   Sirignano & Spiliopoulos (2018); Richter & Berner (2022); Nam et al. (2024). Moreover, different
124   architectures have been proposed for the neural networks. Approaches based on convolutional neural
125   networks can be used to numerically solve systems of PDE on fixed regular grids Lee & Carlberg
126   (2020a); Maulik et al. (2021); Stoffel et al. (2020); Bamer et al. (2021). For more general meshes,
127   graph neural networks (GNNs) have been proposed, e.g., in the context of mesh-based physics Cao
128   et al. (2022); Pfaff et al. (2020); Han et al. (2022); Fortunato et al. (2022), Lagrangian dynamics
129   Sanchez-Gonzalez et al. (2020), parametric PDEs Pichi et al. (2024), and rigid body physics Kneifl
130
      et al. (2024). GNNs can efficiently capture spatial interactions between particles. However, their
      time complexity scales with the size of the graph since they require message-passing operations on
131
      every node. For finer resolutions, this can be computationally prohibitive. Moreover, in their standard
132
      formulation, they do not generalize to graphs that have significantly different sizes than the ones seen
133   during training.
134
135   Neural operator models Neural operators are a class of discretization agnostic neural network
136   architectures that can generalize to arbitrary discretization of input data. These architectures have
137
      been used in solving parametric PDEs Lu et al. (2021); Li et al. (2020c; 2023); Azizzadenesheli
      et al. (2024); Rahman et al. (2024); Liu-Schiaffini et al. (2024); Kovachki et al. (2023); Rahman et al.
138
      (2022a); Liu et al. (2022); Viswanath et al. (2023); Shih et al. (2024); Goswami et al. (2023), fluid
139
      dynamics Di Leoni et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024); Peyvan et al. (2024), protein interactions Liu
140   et al. (2024b;a); Dharuman et al. (2023), 3D physics Xu et al. (2024); White et al. (2023); Bonev et al.
141   (2023); Rahman et al. (2022b); He et al. (2024); Rahman et al. (2022b), weather modeling Bire et al.
142   (2023); Pathak et al. (2022), robotics Bhaskara et al. (2023); Peng et al. (2023), and computer vision
143   Guibas et al. (2021); Rahman & Yeh (2024); Viswanath et al. (2022). In our work, we propose a new
144   parameterization for the graph neural operator to efficiently capture spatial dynamics in Lagrangian
145   systems and generalize to different discretizations of reduced-order inputs.
146
      Reduced-order model Reduced-order models (ROMs) simplify high-dimensional dynamic sys-
147   tems by projecting them onto a lower-dimensional manifold, resulting in faster and less expensive
148   computations (Berkooz et al., 1993; Holmes et al., 2012; Lee & Carlberg, 2020a; Peherstorfer, 2022).
149   These methods gain computational efficiency by simulating a subset of the original spatial samples
150   (An et al., 2008). Recent neural field-based ROMs (Pan et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023; Wen et al.,
151   2023; Chen et al., 2023) demonstrate the ability to train a discretization-agnostic low-dimensional
152   representation, allowing the trained model to generalize over various geometric discretizations. We
153   extend these ROMs by using a neural operator to compute the dynamics of the spatial samples, such
154   that the model is a non-intrusive and discretization-agnostic ROM system. This combination creates
155
      a machine-learning model that generalizes across geometries while being significantly faster than
      traditional models.

      is represented in Figure 1. as the encoder-processor-decoder. In this section, we discuss how the

218
219   To learn the temporal dynamics, i.e., computing ϕ̈jtn in Equation (3), we use a discretization-invariant
220   neural operator architecture that follows the encode-process-decode setup. We propose a graph-based
221   neural operator architecture called Interaction Operator as the encoder and the decoder, while we use
222
      a neural operator transformer (NOT) as the processor.
223   Neural operators are a class of machine learning models that learn to map functions in infinite-
224   dimensional function spaces using a finite collection of discretized input-output pairs. In particular,
225   we want to learn the mapping from the current and past velocity fields (ϕ̇ti )ni=n−w to the current
226   acceleration field ϕ̈tn . The hyperparameter w defines the time window given by the past w time steps.
227   In practice, we use a finite collection of spatially discretized input-output pairs, as, e.g., provided by
228   the Q-point discretization. However, importantly, the output of the neural operator will be consistent
229   across different discretizations.

466
467   Architecture Choice The architecture contains 3 elements - the Interaction Operator, the Neural
468   Operator Transformer and the Neural Field. To underscore the importance of each of these com-
469   ponents, we perform several ablations. In the absence of the Interaction Operator, the model fails
470   to capture local spatial interactions effectively. The Neural Operator Transformer ensures that the
471   model can generalize to longer trajectories, without requiring the velocity to be injected with noise,
472   unless the system is highly dynamic, as in the case of 3D water and sand simulations. We present
473
      the ablations in Table 4. As shown in tables 2 and 9, the use of neural fields speeds up the inference,
      however, it doesn’t significantly improve the accuracy.
474
475
      Compatibility With Various Neural Physics solvers The core aspect of our framework is that
476
      it enables learning physics in a reduced order setting, allowing for inference at any spatial point
477
      with arbitrary resolution or discretization. Besides the Interaction Operator and Neural Operator
478   Transformer integrated into our model, other discretization-agnostic methods for learning temporal
479   dynamics are also applicable. As illustrated in Table 5, our setup achieves strong performance when
480   substituting the Neural Operator Transformer with a Fourier Neural Operator, though the inclusion of
481   noise during training is necessary for all physical systems. Additionally, GNS Sanchez-Gonzalez
482   et al. (2020) can be utilized as a time stepper, but the computational speed decreases due to the ten
483   message-passing blocks.

522   Handling self-contact in Materials The training data for the model is generated using MPM

      7   C ONCLUSION
529
530   In conclusion, our proposed GIOROM, can implicitly learn PDEs over several physical systems.
531   Utilizing a reduced-order modeling approach on sparse graphs, GIOROM is faster than previous
532   neural network-based physics solvers while achieving high fidelity simulations. Moreover, our neural-
533   operator-based model generalizes well across different initial conditions, velocities, discretizations,
534   and geometries.
535   Despite its promising performance, GIOROM has limitations that warrant further exploration. While
536   GIOROM is capable of generalizing across different settings, like many machine learning and
537   reduced-order methods, it struggles with extreme out-of-distribution scenarios (Li et al., 2020b; Chen
538   et al., 2023). Moreover, while GIOROM is primarily designed for continuous systems, future research
539   might explore mechanisms to explicitly handle discontinuities (Belhe et al., 2023; Goswami et al.,
      2022).

881   Here, we summarize the important ingredients of neural operators. For more details, please refer to
882   Li et al. (2020a). Operator learning is a machine learning paradigm where a neural network is trained
883
      to map between infinite-dimensional function spaces. Let G : V → A be a nonlinear map between
      the two function spaces V and A. A neural operator is an operator parameterized by a neural network
884
      given by
885
                                              Gθ : V → A, θ ∈ RP ,                                     (12)

      that approximates this function mapping in the finite-dimensional space. The learning problem can
      be formulated as

                                                                                                       (13)
889                                     θ∈RP
890   where ∥·∥V is a norm on V and D is a probability distribution on V. In practice, the above optimization
891   is posed as an empirical risk-minimization problem, defined as

896   and integral operators, defined as

903   C     I NTERACTION NETWORK
904
905   The interaction network proposed in Battaglia et al. (2016) learns a relation-centric function f that
906   encodes spatial interactions between the interacting nodes within a system as a function of their
907   interaction attributes r . This can be represented as
908                                            et+1 = fR (x1,t , x2,t , r)                               (16)
909
      A node-centered function predicts the temporal dynamics of the node as a function of the spatial
910
      interactions as follows
911                                           x1,t+1 = fo (et+1 , x1,t )                              (17)
912
      In a system of m nodes, the spatial interactions are represented as a graph, where the neighborhood
913   is defined by a ball of radius r. This graph is represented as G(O, R), where O is the collection of
914   objects and R is the relationships between them. The interaction between them is defined as
915
                                     I(G) = fo (a(G, X, fR (⟨xi , xj , rij ⟩)))                          (18)
916
917   Where a is an aggregation function that combines all the interactions, X is the set of external effects,
      not part of the system, such as gravitational acceleration, etc.

      C.1   H YPERPARAMETERS
919
920   The models were implemented using Pytorch library and trained on CUDA. The graphs were built
921   using Pytorch Geometric module. All models were trained on NVIDIA RTX 3060 GPUs
922   for 5e6 steps.
923
      The input to the model is a state vector matrix corresponding to w = 6 previous time steps of each
924   particle, along with features that represent the material of each particle. A radius graph is constructed
925   for the set of particles within the input space, such that edges are added between particles that are
926   within the radius r. The nodes of the graph are the velocity sequences for all the particles within the
927   sparse graph.
928
      The graph is constructed using radius graph defined in Pytorch Geometric. The node
929
      features and the edge features, which include the distance from the boundary points, are encoded
930   into latent vectors of size 128 using 2 MLPs. The encoder uses two layers of interaction operator.
931   The latents are then processed by two layers of Neural Operator Transformer. The decoder layers are
932   symmetric to the encoder layers. However, the decoder uses an additional projection layer with 16
933   channels that lifts the output to 128 channels, which is then projected back to the physical dimensions
934   of the input graph (2D or 3D). All MLPs within the GNO and FNO framework use gelu activation
935   function.
936
937   Training noise In more dynamic systems such as Water-3D and Sand-3D, to prevent noise
938   accumulation during long rollouts, the velocity sequence is corrupted with random walk noise during
939   training. The noise is sampled from a normal distribution N (0, σ 2 ). Systems like Plasticine or
940   Elasticity did not require any training noise.
941
942   Normalization All velocity sequences are standardized to zero mean and unit standard deviation.
943
      The dataset statistics are computed during training. Global mean and variance values from the training
      dataset are used to compute statistics.
944
945
946
      Optimizers Optimization is done with Adamax optimizer, with an initial learning rate of 1e-4,
      weight decay of 1e-6 and a batch size of 4. The learning rate was decayed exponentially from 10−4
947
      to 10−6 using a scheduler, with a gamma of 0.11/5e6
