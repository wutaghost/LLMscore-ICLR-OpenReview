011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In recent years, online reinforcement learning(RL) training methods like PPO
014            have shone in important works such as Instruct GPT. However, unlike the success
015            achieved in the language domain, online RL methods often struggle to general-
016            ize to untrained tasks in open-world environments like Minecraft, due to issues
017            like overfitting. This has become a significant obstacle in using online methods to
018            build a generalist agent. In this work, we notice the modality differences between
019            natural language environments and embodied environments such as the Minecraft
020
               environment, which inspired us to use video instructions instead of text instruc-
               tions to enhance the model’s understanding of the relationship between the en-
021
               vironment and instructions. We also introduce a new attention layer in the base
022
               model’s encoder-decoder architecture to establish a semantic and visual dual-path
023            information interaction channel, further strengthening this generalization capabil-
024            ity. After training our model on a small set of tasks, it demonstrated excellent
025            zero-shot generalization on new tasks, outperforming almost all other models in
026            the Minecraft environment on our benchmark. Our approach takes a solid and
027            important step toward unleashing the potential of online RL in building generalist
028            agents.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   In recent years, with the rising prominence of methods like Reinforcement Learning with Human
035   Feedback (RLHFOuyang et al. (2022b)) and InstructGPTOuyang et al. (2022a), online reinforce-
036   ment learning(RL) methods such as Proximal Policy Optimization (PPOSchulman et al. (2017))
037   have attracted increased attention from researchers by its strong performance. However, traditional
038   online RL is often used only for a single task and often suffers from overfitting. With the rise of the
039
      concept of generalist agents, applying online RL to instruction-following agents and achieving gen-
      eralization abilities in open-ended tasks has become a challenge. To seek a solution to this problem,
040
      we try to combine online RL with multi-task training to harness their potential in building general
041
      intelligence. In this procedure, we noticed that the ability to generalize from training on a few tasks
042   to solving open-ended tasks is the key. Existing work primarily focuses on text-based instructions,
043   which limits the model’s ability to generalize to new tasks. On the other hand, language-visual
044   alignment in traditional text instruction methods requires collecting a large amount of text and tra-
045   jectory samples during the text-based pre-training and the online tasks defining, where the text is
046   significantly more expensive than the trajectories.
047
      In this work. We draw inspiration from recent works on video instructionsCai et al. (2023) and ex-
048   plore the way to leverage video instructions and jointly online finetunes an encoder-decoder struc-
049   ture. This approach, compared to using traditional text instructions, ensures that the agent’s input
050   instructions and the actual running environment are in the same modality, naturally eliminating
051   the complex and challenging multimodal alignment process inherent in traditional text instruction
052   methods, enabling the agent to more intuitively and easily connect instructions to policies. Our
053   work demonstrates the zero-shot generalization capability of online RL with video instruction and
      highlights the potential of online RL in building open-ended generalist agents.

      2.2   M ULTI - TASK R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING IN M INECRAFT ENVIRONMENT
109
110
      Multi-task reinforcement learning (multi-task RL) involves training a single agent to perform mul-
111   tiple tasks simultaneously, with the goal of leveraging shared knowledge across tasks to improve
112   learning efficiency and performance. Instead of training separate models for each task, multi-task
113   RL aims to develop a unified policy that can generalize across different environments or objectives.
114   Moreover, in environments with open-ended tasks, such as Minecraft, multi-task RL can only train
115   on a limited set of tasks. Therefore, the agent must also possess the ability to generalize in a zero-
116   shot manner to new, unseen tasks based on the knowledge gained from existing tasks.
117   Overall, multi-task online reinforcement learning in Minecraft faces several main challenges:
118
119   Task Interference: Learning multiple tasks can lead to negative transfer, where optimizing for
120
      one task may hinder performance on others. Also, different tasks may require different strategies,
      making it difficult to balance exploration and exploitation across tasks.
121
122   Generalization Difficulties: Online fine-tuning on a small set of tasks can lead to overfitting on
123   easy tasks that provide relatively higher rewards, which may in turn compromise the generalization
124   capability of the base model. Even if the model performs well on most tasks in the training set, it
125   can still easily encounter out-of-domain situations in open-ended environments, leading to failure in
126
      test tasks.
127   Complexity of Environment: Due to the difficulties like task variety, sparce reward, and dynamic
128   environments, Minecraft becomes an extremely complex and challenging environment for multi-
129   tasking RL. Therefore we choose Minecraft as our test environment to highlight the effectiveness of
130   our approach. More details of this part will be covered in the appendix.

      Several base agents in the Minecraft environment are worth noticing: VPT(Baker et al. (2022)) lever-
138
      aged large-scale human-annotated data and model-generated labels to perform offline training on the
139   transformer architecture, achieving strong capabilities. Through subsequent online fine-tuning, it be-
140   came the first agent to mine diamonds using basic keyboard and mouse operations. However, VPT
141   could not follow human instructions. Attracted by VPT’s strong potential for instruction tuning,
142   STEVE1(Lifshitz et al. (2023)), which can follow text instructions, and GROOT(Cai et al. (2023)),
143   which can follow video instructions, were trained, and becoming models in the Minecraft environ-
144   ment that possess considerable instruction-following abilities.
145   Previous Minecraft works, such as STEVE-1 and MineCLIP agent, have focused on more traditional
146   instruction-following agents, which often use textual instructions as input. The typical approach
147   involves fine-tuning models based on the CLIP architecture in the Minecraft environment, using
148   the model to encode the textual instructions. These encoded instructions are then integrated into
149   the network’s processing of observations to generate the output actions. However, In the Minecraft
150   environment, there are often a large number of visual and semantic features. Many visual details,
151   such as item thumbnails in the inventory, objects partially visible on the screen, and items with only
152   subtle visual differences, require the model to have a thorough understanding of the fine details in
153
      the images. Additionally, encoding the 3D environment of Minecraft through video requires the
      visual encoding model to have the capability to associate the same object from different angles of
154
      view and embed it in a way that aligns with the corresponding text in a similar embedding space.
155
      These factors pose significant challenges for video-text aligning models like CLIP, as they must
156   accurately capture and link multi-view visual representations with their textual counterparts in a
157   complex and dynamic environment. Additionally, most of the training data for aligning models
158   like CLIP is collected from the internet, where semantic ambiguity is prevalent. As a result, when a
159   player is holding an axe among a herd of cows and sheep, CLIP struggles to differentiate whether the
160   scene represents ’killing sheep,’ ’killing cows,’ or simply ’wandering around,’ despite the significant
161   semantic differences between these texts. Meanwhile, the videos may appear highly similar. These
      factors make cross-modal alignment even more challenging.

      These difficulties directly lead to models heavily relying on prior experiences and memory to asso-
163   ciate familiar objects from different viewpoints with their corresponding text descriptions, as well
164   as linking specific behavior patterns and visual features—like tools in hand—with common action
165   texts. When presented with rare or entirely new objects, actions, or unseen video-text combinations,
166   visual-language multimodal alignment models like CLIP often face serious generalization issues.
167   This reliance on familiar patterns weakens their ability to handle novel inputs effectively.
168
      With an understanding of the alignment challenges faced by text instructions, we opted to explore us-
169   ing video instructions to enhance the model’s generalization ability, which became a natural choice.
170   The advantage of video instructions over text instructions is that the model can directly associate
171   visual features without requiring a complex cross-modal alignment process which may hurt diver-
172   sity. This allows for a more intuitive connection such as the common visual features between the
173   instructions and the visual environment. Therefore it may improve the model’s ability to generalize
174   to new tasks and scenarios.
175   Given GROOT’s outstanding ability to follow video instructions, we chose it as our base policy.
176   The main idea behind GROOT is to use an encoder-decoder structure. The encoder encodes the
177   input video instructions into a latent representation, which is then combined with the environment’s
178   observation. The decoder uses this latent representation along with the observation to generate the
179   corresponding actions. More detail about GROOT as our base policy is covered in section 3.3.

      During the rollout process, video instructions and the current observation are passed through a freez-
264   ing ViT encoder to obtain image feature patches. The latent z obtained from encoding the observa-
265   tion is also processed through a linear layer to produce a patch. The patches from the observation
266   are used as the query key, and after performing cross-attention with the keys and values derived from
267   the other patches through a linear layer, we obtain the embeddings for each patch in the observation.
268   These embeddings are then added to the corresponding patches in the original observation. The
269   resulting patches are subsequently sent to the encoder for encoding and decoding operations in the
      original workflow.

      4.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
308
309   We select the open-ended video game Minecraft as the testbed (Fan et al., 2022; Guss et al., 2019).
310   We primarily designed experiments for three categories of tasks to test the model’s generalization
311   ability from various perspectives: MineBlock, CraftItem and KillEntity, which are the
312   basic skills in an open-ended Minecraft environment. The agent is required to follow instructions
313   to mine the correct blocks, craft the accurate items, and kill the corresponding types of animals
314   respectively in these three categories of tasks. We recorded each task’s instruction videos manually
315
      and generated them using the model, showcasing the player successfully performing the correct
      actions on the target objects. To align with the actual instructional scenarios used during the design
316
      of the agent, the instruction videos do not include any distractors which we will mention later.
317
      We aim for the agent to perform the corresponding action on the corresponding items shown in
318   the instruction video. For example, if the instruct video shows the player crafting a clock, then
319   the agent’s task is to craft a clock as well. For each task, when the agent performs the correct
320   and complete action on the appropriate item—such as mining, killing, or crafting—it receives the
321   corresponding reward. In addition to the correct items, we introduce other items as distractors. When
322   the agent performs actions on these distractor items, we correspondingly reduce the reward as the
323   punishment. For example, in task mine obsidian in the train dataset of MineBlock category,
      We will use commands to spawn the agent on a plain, then randomly select three positions on the

325   filled with an obsidian block, and the other two positions will be filled with distractor blocks from
326   the training set, such as white wool and oak log. In this setting, if the model successfully
327   mines the obsidian, it will receive a reward; if it mines the distractor blocks white wool or oak

331   To accurately measure the agent’s performance and generalization ability from multiple dimen-

415   this issue: although there is still a gap in the agent’s capabilities between in-distribution and out-of-
416   distribution tasks, it can still achieve excellent performance on out-of-distribution tasks.

477
478
      Multi-task Reinforcement Learning Multi-task Reinforcement Learning (MTRL) is a reinforce-
479
      ment learning method that aims to enable an agent to learn and optimize across multiple tasks.
480   Unlike single-task reinforcement learning, MTRL requires the model to handle multiple tasks si-
481   multaneously, to allow the model to share knowledge between tasks, thereby improving learning ef-
482   ficiency and generalization capabilities. Most of the previous works mainly focus on in-distribution
483   tasks Guo et al. (2020). MTRL struggles with many problems such as overfitting and gradient con-
484   flict. which are similar to the challenges faced when attempting to share features between the actor
485   and critic in actor-critic methodsCobbe et al. (2021). To solve this issue, many works focus on
      optimization was proposed. Some other works focusing on RL generalization have been proposed.


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      The capability of RL to perform few-shot generalization across environments has been validated on
487   Atari by Taiga et al. (2023).Works like Zhou et al. (2024) use the images as the instruction, which is
488   similar to our work, however, it lacks semantic feature and losing the ability of base agent.
489
490   Developing Agents in Minecraft Minecraft is an open-ended, voxel-based game that provides a
491   complex environment for a wide range of tasks, making it both a challenging and inspiring plat-
492   form for developing and benchmarking decision-making agents. In recent years, it has attracted
493   increasing attention from researchers. Platforms like MineRL (Guss et al., 2019) and MineDojo
494   (Fan et al., 2022) offer customizable environments and large-scale datasets, facilitating the training
495   and evaluation of agents. One stream of work (Wang et al., 2023a;c;b) focuses on developing plan-
496   ners that utilize large language models to generate high-level actions, and rely on separately trained
497
      controllers or leveraging game mechanics to carry out these actions. In contrast, another stream
      of work, more closely related to our approach, focuses on developing low-level controllers that di-
498
      rectly interact with the environment. VPT (Baker et al., 2022) learns an inverse dynamics model to
499
      infer mouse and keyboard actions from YouTube videos and trains a behavior cloning model on a
500   web-scale dataset. They further fine-tuned the model with KL-constrained reinforcement learning,
501   achieving impressive results on the diamond acquisition task. However, their method is limited to
502   solving only a single task. In this work, we apply the same KL-constrained reinforcement learning
503   technique to fine-tune a model pre-trained on a large dataset, but with a focus on the multi-task
504   setting. Fan et al. (2022) finetunes a pre-trained contrastive video-language model for Minecraft,
505   which can be used to embed text instruction and generate reward signals. While it trains a language-
506   conditioned policy, its reinforcement learning approach isn’t built on large-scale pre-training, and
507   its generalization ability is not tested. Lifshitz et al. (2023) combines MineCLIP and VPT to train
508
      a text-conditioned policy, while Cai et al. (2023) proposes a self-supervised approach for training a
      video-conditioned policy. However, both approaches remain fully offline without interaction with
509
      the environment, which limits their performance.

512   6   C ONCLUSIONS
513
514   In conclusion, we recognize that text instruction requires an expensive and complex alignment pro-
515   cess. Therefore, we propose that video instruction has the potential to enhance the generalization
516   capability of online algorithms. During this process, we discovered that when the number of tasks is
517
      limited, the latent features encoded by video instruction are prone to overfitting to the training task
      space, making it difficult to generalize to unseen tasks. To address this, we introduce a dual-stream
518
      encoder-decoder architecture:
519
520         1. It leverages latent features pre-trained on unlabeled data to provide high-level behavioral
521            information, thereby improving the initial exploration process of reinforcement learning.
522
            2. It utilizes an attention-based visual communication module to provide more fine-grained
523
               visual concepts for specific tasks, allowing the policy to learn how to combine the environ-
524
               ment with the instruction, thus enhancing generalization.
525
526   Our method outperforms nearly all other common agents in Minecraft environments on the gener-
527   alization benchmark we designed for three types of tasks. This ability to train on small sets and
528   zero-shot generalize to open-ended tasks lays the foundation for applying online algorithms in de-
529   veloping generalist agents in open-world environments.

      To obtain the instruction videos, we manually recorded two types of tasks: crafting items and killing

631

632
      environment. In the videos recorded by human players, the success rate of achieving the goal was
633   100%. For the MineBlock task, we used Groot to follow a fixed trajectory recorded by a human
634   in the recording environment, then generated 10 additional trajectories in bulk within the same
635   environment. The success rate was not 100%, but the model’s performance was sufficient to convey
636   both action and visual information. Under this recording setup, we recorded 5-10 instruction videos
637   for each task within each category. During actual training, the agent randomly selects one of these
638   trajectories corresponding to the task as the instruction video.
