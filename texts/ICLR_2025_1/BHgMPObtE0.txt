011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Irregularly sampled multivariate time series (ISMTS) are prevalent in reality. Most
014            existing methods treat ISMTS as synchronized regularly sampled time series with
015
               missing values, neglecting that the irregularities are primarily attributed to vari-
               ations in sampling rates. In this paper, we introduce a novel perspective that
016
               irregularity is essentially relative in some senses. With sampling rates artificially
017
               determined from low to high, an irregularly sampled time series can be transformed
018            into a hierarchical set of relatively regular time series from coarse to fine. We
019            observe that additional coarse-grained relatively regular series not only mitigate
020            the irregularly sampled challenges but also incorporate broad-view temporal infor-
021            mation, thereby serving as a valuable asset for representation learning. Therefore,
022            following the philosophy of learning that Seeing the big picture first, then delv-
023            ing into the details, we present the Multi-Scale and Multi-Correlation Attention
024            Network (MuSiCNet) combining multiple scales to iteratively refine the ISMTS
025            representation. Specifically, within each scale, we explore time attention and
026
               frequency correlation matrices to aggregate intra- and inter-series information,
               naturally enhancing the representation quality with richer and more intrinsic details.
027
               While across adjacent scales, we employ a representation rectification method
028
               containing contrastive learning and reconstruction results adjustment to further
029            improve representation consistency. MuSiCNet is an ISMTS analysis framework
030            that competitive with SOTA in three mainstream tasks consistently, including
031            classification, interpolation, and forecasting.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Irregularly sampled multivariate time series (ISMTS) are ubiquitous in realistic scenarios, ranging
037   from scientific explorations to societal interactions (Che et al., 2018; Shukla & Marlin, 2021; Sun
038   et al., 2021; Agarwal et al., 2023; Yalavarthi et al., 2024). The causes of irregularities in time
039
      series collection are diverse, including sensor malfunctions, transmission distortions, cost-reduction
      strategies, and various external forces or interventions, etc. Such ISMTS data exhibit distinctive
040
      features including intra-series irregularity, characterized by inconsistent intervals between consecutive
041
      data points, and inter-series irregularity, marked by a lack of synchronization across multiple variables.
042   The above characteristics typically result in the lack of alignment and uneven count of observations
043   (Shukla & Marlin, 2020), invalidating the assumption of coherent fixed-dimensional feature space for
044   most traditional time series analysis models.
045
      Recent studies have attempted to address these challenges by treating ISMTS as synchronized,
046
      regularly sampled Normal Multivariate Time Series (NMTS) data with missing values, focusing on
047
      imputation strategies (Che et al., 2018; Yoon et al., 2018; Camino et al., 2019; Tashiro et al., 2021;
048   Zhang et al., 2021c; Chen et al., 2022; Fan, 2022; Du et al., 2023). However, direct imputation is
049   difficult, especially when sampling is sparse. Inaccurate imputation results can distort underlying
050   relationships and introduce significant noise, which can greatly reduce the accuracy of analysis tasks
051   (Zhang et al., 2021b; Wu et al., 2021; Agarwal et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024). Latest developments
052   circumvent imputation and aim to address these challenges by embracing the inherent continuity
053   of time, thus preserving the continuous temporal dynamics dependencies within the ISMTS data.
      Despite these innovations, most methods above are merely solutions for intra-series irregularities,

083   To bridge this gap, we propose MuSiCNet—a Multi-Scale and Multi-Correlation Attention Net-
084   work—to iteratively optimize ISMTS representations from coarse to fine. Our approach begins by
085   establishing a hierarchical set of coarse- to fine-grained series with sampling rates from low to high.
086
      At each scale, we employ a custom-designed encoder-decoder framework called multi-correlation
      attention network (CorrNet), for representation learning. Different from most existing methods that
087
      focus mainly on intra-series relationships, our CorrNet encoder (CorrE) captures embeddings of
088
      continuous time values by using an attention mechanism containing correlation matrices to aggregate
089   both intra- and inter-series information. This approach is crucial not only because every observation
090   in ISMTS, given the sparse sampling, is valuable for representation learning, but also due to the fact
091   that correlated variables provide deeper insights for a given query. Therefore, we designed frequency
092   correlation matrices using Lomb–Scargle Periodogram-based Dynamic Time Warping (LSP-DTW).
093   This approach addresses the challenges of calculating correlations in ISMTS and re-weights the
094   inter-series attention scores to better capture cross-series information. Across scales, we employ a
095   representation rectification operation from coarse to fine to iteratively refine the learned representa-
096   tions with contrastive learning and reconstruction results adjustment methods. This ensures accurate
097
      and consistent representation and minimizes error propagation throughout the model.
098   Benefiting from the aforementioned designs, MuSiCNet explicitly learns multi-scale information,
099   enabling good performance on widely used ISMTS datasets, thereby demonstrating its ability to
100   capture relevant features for ISMTS analysis. Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
101
102
      • We find that irregularities in ISMTS are essentially relative in some senses and multi-scale learning
        helps balance coarse- and fine-grained information in ISMTS representation learning.
103
104   • We introduce CorrNet, an encoder-decoder framework designed to learn fixed-length representations
105     for ISMTS. Notably, our proposed LSP-DTW can mitigate spurious correlations induced by
106
        irregularities in the frequency domain and effectively re-weight attention across sequences.
107   • We are not limited to a specific analysis task and attempt to propose a task-general model for
        ISMTS analysis, including classification, interpolation, and forecasting.

137   2    R ELATED W ORK
138
139   Irregularly Sampled Multivariate Time Series Analysis. An effective approach for analyzing
140   ISMTS hinges on the understanding of their unique properties. Most existing methods treat ISMTS
141   as NMTS with missing values, such as Che et al. (2018); Yoon et al. (2018); Camino et al. (2019);
142   Tashiro et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2022); Fan (2022); Du et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024). However,
143
      most imputation-based methods may distort the underlying relationships, introducing unsuitable
      inductive biases and substantial noise due to incorrect imputation (Zhang et al., 2021b; Wu et al.,
144
      2021; Agarwal et al., 2023), ultimately compromising the accuracy of downstream tasks. Some
145
      other methods treat ISMTS as time series with discrete timestamps, aggregating all sample points
146   of a single variable to extract a unified feature for each variable (Zhang et al., 2021b; Horn et al.,
147   2020; Li et al., 2023). These methods can directly accept raw ISMTS data as input but often struggle
148   to handle the underlying relationships within the time series. Recent progress seeks to overcome
149   these challenges by recognizing and utilizing the inherent continuity of time, thereby maintaining the
150   ongoing temporal dynamics present in ISMTS data (De Brouwer et al., 2019; Rubanova et al., 2019;
151   Kidger et al., 2020; Schirmer et al., 2022; Jhin et al., 2022; Chowdhury et al., 2023).
152   Despite these advancements, existing methods mainly suffer from two main drawbacks, they primarily
153   address intra-series irregularity while overlooking the alignment issues stemming from inter-series
154   irregularity, and 2) they rely on assumptions tailored to specific downstream tasks (Yalavarthi et al.,
155   2024; Wang et al., 2024), hindering their ability to consistently perform well across various ISMTS
156   tasks.
157   Multi-scale Modeling. Multi-scale and hierarchical approaches have demonstrated their utility
158   across various fields, including computer vision (CV) (Fan et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021a), natural
159   language processing (NLP) (Nawrot et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2021), and time series analysis (Chen
160   et al., 2021; Shabani et al., 2022; Cai et al., 2024). Most recent innovations in the time series
161   analysis domain have seen the integration of multi-scale modules into the Transformer architecture
      to enhance analysis capabilities Shabani et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2021) and are designed for NMTS.


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Nevertheless, the application of multi-scale modeling specifically designed for ISMTS data, and
163   the exploitation of information across scales, remain less unexplored. As far as we know, Singh
164   et al. (2019) and Zhang et al. (2023) are among the earlier works on multi-level ISMTS learning.
165   Singh et al. (2019) addresses multi-resolution signal issues by distributing signals across specialized
166   branches with different resolutions, where each branch employs a Flexible Irregular Time Series
167   Network (FIT) to process high- and low-frequency data separately. Zhang et al. (2023), on the other
168   hand, is a transformer-based model that stacks multiple Warpformer layers to produce multi-scale
169   representations, combining them via residual connections to support downstream tasks. These works
170   typically focus on either specific tasks or particular model architectures. In contrast, our design
171
      philosophy originates from ISMTS characteristics rather than being tied to a specific feature extraction
      network structure. Warpformer emphasizes designing a specific network architecture but involves
172
      high computational costs and requires manually balancing the trade-off between the number of scales
173
      and the dataset. These are challenges that our MuSiCNet avoids entirely.

177
178   As previously mentioned, our work aims to learn ISMTS representation for further analysis tasks by
179   introducing MuSiCNet, a novel framework designed to balance coarse- and fine-grained information
180   across different scales. The overall model architecture illustrated in Fig.2(a) indicates the effectiveness
181
      of MuSiCNet can be guaranteed to a great extent by 1) Hierarchical Structure. 2) Representation
      Learning Using CorrNet Within Scale. 3) Rectification Across Adjacent Scales. We will
182
      first introduce problem formulation and notations of MuSiCNet and then discuss key points in the
183
      following subsections.
184
185
      3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
186
187   Our goal is to learn a nonlinear embedding function fθ , such that the set of ISMTS data X =
188   {X1 , · · · , XN } can map to the best-described representations for further ISMTS analysis including
189   both supervised and unsupervised tasks. We denote Xn ∈ RTn ×D as a D-dimensional instance with
190   the length of observation Tn . Specifically, the d-th dimension in instance n can be treated as a tuple
191   Xdn = (xdn , tdn ) where the length of observations is Tdn . xdn = [x1dn , · · · xTdn dn ] is the list of
192   observations and the list of corresponding observed timestamps is tdn = [t1dn , · · · tTdn dn ]. We drop
193   the data case index n for brevity when the context is clear.

195   3.2   C ORR N ET A RCHITECTURE W ITHIN S CALE
196
197
      Multi-Correlation Attention Module. In this subsection, we elaborate on the Multi-Correlation
      Attention module. Time attention has proven effective for ISMTS learning (Shukla & Marlin, 2021;
198
      Horn et al., 2020; Chowdhury et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024). Many existing methods mainly focus on
199
      capturing interactions between observation values and their corresponding sampling times within
200   a single variable. However, due to the potential sparse sampling in ISMTS, observations from all
201   variables are valuable and need to be considered.
202
203
      To address this, we use irregularly sampled time points and corresponding observations from all
      variables within a sample as keys and values to produce fixed-dimensional representations at the query
204
      time points. The importance of each variable cannot be uniform for a given query and variables that
205
      provide more valuable information should receive more attention. Therefore, we designed frequency
206   correlation matrices to re-weight the inter-series attention scores, enhancing the representation
207   learning process.
208

226   Correlation Extraction. The correlation matrix is essential for deriving reliable and consistent
227
      correlations within ISMTS, which must be robust to the inherent challenges of variable sampling
      rates and inconsistent observation counts at each timestamp in ISMTS. Most existing distance
228
      measures, such as Euclidean distance, Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) (Berndt & Clifford, 1994), and
229
      Optimal Transport / Wasserstein Distance (Villani et al., 2009), risk generating spurious correlations
230   in the context of irregularly sampled time series. This is due to their dependence on the presence
231   of both data points for the similarity measurement, and the potential for imputation to introduce
232   unreliable information before calculating similarity which will be explored further in Section 4.4 of
233   our experiments.
234
      At an impasse, the Lomb-Scargle Periodogram (LSP) (Lomb, 1976; Scargle, 1982) provides en-
235
      lightenment to address this issue. LSP is a well-known algorithm for generating a power spectrum
236   and detecting the periodic component in irregularly sampled time series. It extends the Fourier
237   periodogram approach to accommodate irregularly sampled scenarios (VanderPlas, 2018) eliminat-
238   ing the need for interpolation or imputation. This makes LSP a great tool for simplifying ISMTS
239   analysis. Compared to existing methods, measuring the similarity between discrete raw observations,
240   LSP-DTW, an implicit continuous method, utilizes inherent periodic characteristics and provides
241   global information to measure the similarity.
242   As demonstrated in Fig.2(c), we first convert ISMTS into the frequency domain using LSP and then
243   apply DTW to evaluate the distance between variables. The correlation between Xdi and Xdj is:

      4.4    C ORRELATION R ESULTS
466
467
468   In this section, we focus on validating the necessity, effectiveness, and efficiency of the correlation
469   matrix in the classification task as an example.
470   First, we verify the necessity of the correlation matrix using results from all classification datasets in
471   Table 4. Removing the correlation matrix (line 4) led to performance drops across all datasets, with
472   P19 showing the largest decline due to its 94.9% missing rate. This highlights the importance of
473   capturing inter-series relationships in irregularly sampled time series, making the correlation matrix
474   essential. Replacing the designed correlation matrix with a learnable one (line 5) also worsened
475   performance, indicating that learning inter-series relationships purely from the network remains
476
      highly challenging and specialized correlation designs are needed.
477   Second, we evaluate LSP-DTW against other correlation calculation methods (I-GAK (Cuturi, 2011),
478   I-DTW (Berndt & Clifford, 1994)) on the P12 dataset to verify the effectiveness. Interpolation-based
479   methods (I-GAK, I-DTW) distort correlations, leading to unreliable results as seen in Fig.3. I-GAK
480   shows fictitious correlations based on observation rates, while I-DTW presents uniformly positive
481   correlations, neither of which captures true data characteristics. In contrast, LSP-DTW accurately
482
      identifies correlations, verified by Table 5, where it outperforms all baselines, demonstrating the
      importance of appropriate correlation modeling.
483
484   Lastly, we report the computation time for the correlation matrix. LSP-DTW based correlation matrix
485   is computed per instance in parallel, with acceptable runtimes (0.137s for P12, 0.127s for P19, 0.049s
      for PAM). It is calculated once, making it efficient for the entire learning process.

501
502   Taking P12 in the classification task with a batch size of 50 as an example, we conduct the ablation
503   study to assess the necessity of two fundamental components of MuSiCNet: correlation matrix and
504   multi-scale learning reflected in reconstruction results adjustment and contrastive learning. As shown
505   in Table 6, the complete MuSiCNet framework, incorporating all components, achieves the best
506   performance. The absence of any component leads to varying degrees of performance degradation, as
507
      evidenced in layers two to five. The second layer, which retains the multi-scale learning, exhibits
      the second-best performance, underscoring the critical role of multi-scale learning in capturing
508
      varied temporal dependencies and enhancing feature extraction. Conversely, the version lacking all
509
      components shows a significant performance drop of 1.9%, indicating that each component is crucial
510   to the overall effectiveness of the framework.
511
512   Under the same setting, our model MuSiCNet achieves a time cost of 0.240s per batch with 4.2GB of
513
      memory usage. In comparison, ViTST requires 2.196s and 40.2GB, Raindrop uses 0.124s and 4.8GB,
      MTGNN takes 0.1967s and 4.2GB, and DGM2 -O needs 0.313s and 9.1GB. MuSiCNet demonstrates
514
      lower time complexity than most other methods and significantly lower memory usage, particularly
515
      compared to ViTST, which also performs well on classification tasks.

518   5      C ONCLUSION
519
520   In this study, we introduce MuSiCNet, an innovative framework designed for analyzing ISMTS
521   datasets. MuSiCNet addresses the challenges arising from data irregularities and shows superior
522   performance in both supervised and unsupervised tasks. We recognize that irregularities in ISMTS are
523   inherently relative and accordingly implement multi-scale learning, a vital element of our framework.
524   In this multi-scale approach, the contribution of extra coarse-grained relatively regular series is
525   important, providing comprehensive temporal insights that facilitate the analysis of finer-grained
526   series. As another key component of MuSiCNet, CorrNet is engineered to aggregate temporal
527
      information effectively, employing time embeddings and correlation matrix calculating from both
      intra- and inter-series perspectives, in which we employ LSP-DTW to develop frequency correlation
528
      matrices that not only reduce the burden for similarity calculation for ISMT, but also significantly
529
      enhance inter-series information extraction.
530
531   Nevertheless, our MuSiCNet still has some limitations. Firstly, the interaction between scales could
532   potentially be further simplified. Secondly, the exploration of ISMTS for anomaly detection tasks is
533
      currently insufficient. As a task-agnostic model, our MuSiCNet should be further investigated for its
      potential in anomaly detection.

      After modification, PAM includes 5, 333 sensory signal segments, each with 600 observations from

893   F.1   M U S I CN ET PARAMETERS
894
895   We present the training hyperparameters and model parameters here. The maximum epoch is set
896   to 300, and AdamW optimizer is selected as our optimizer without weight decay. By default, the
897
      learning rate is set to 1e-3, and the learning rate schedule is cosine decay for each epoch. Batch size
      for all datasets is set to 50, the dimension of the encoder output is set to 256, and the dimension of
898
      the hidden representations in GRU is typically set to 50. The random masking ratio r for each scale
899
      is set to 0.1.
900
901   Due to inconsistent series lengths, we set the maximum reference point number, K, to 128 for long
902   series, such as P12, PAM, PhysioNet and USHCN, to 96 for Physionet12, and to 48 for short series,
903
      such as PAM and MIMIC-III.
904   Initially, the window size is set to 1/4 of the time series length and then halved iteratively until the
905   majority of the windows contain at least one observation.
906
      According to the observed timestamps on each dataset, the number of scale layers L is set to 6, 5, 7,
907   6, 8, 4, and 5 for P12, P19, PAM, Physionet, USHCN, MIMIC-III, and Physionet12, respectively. For
908   example, in classification, for P12, the scales are 4, 8, 16, 32, 64 and raw length. For P19, the scales
909   are 4, 8, 16, 32 and raw length. And for PAM, the scales are 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128 and raw length. In
910   all mainstream tasks involved, the hyperparametes λ1 , λ2 , λ3 are selected in [1e -3, 1e-2, . . . , 1e2].
911   All the models were experimented using the PyTorch library on a GeForce RTX-2080 Ti GPU.

938   F.3   PARAMETER A NALYSIS
939
940   To analyze the hyper-parameters sensitivity, we conducted the experiments for λ1 , λ2 , and λ3 with
941   grid search. Due to the closer relationship between the hyper-parameters of the adjustment term
942   and the contrastive learning term, i.e., λ1 and λ2 , we jointly analyzed λ1 and λ2 while separately
943   analyzing the hyper-parameter of the downstream task λ3 , as illustrated in Fig.4 and Fig.5.
944   From Fig.4, we can find that the adjustment term plays a greater role compared to the contrastive
945   learning term. This phenomenon matches our motivation, where the coarse-to-fine strategy can
946   effectively alleviate the difficulty of representation learning on ISMTS caused by inconsistent
947   sampling rates. In addition, when lg λ1 and lg λ2 take values around 2 and -2, respectively, our
948   MuSiCNet can perform well.
949   From Fig.5, we can find that our MuSiCNet becomes effective with large λ3 . This indicates that more
950   effective representations will be captured when utilizing downstream tasks, matching the general
951   insight. We also noticed that it becomes less sensitive when lg λ3 ≥ −1. Its suitable range may be
952   located at [1e1, 1e2].
