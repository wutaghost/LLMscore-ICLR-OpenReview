009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) have become a core ingredient in the reverse engi-
012                neering of large-language models (LLMs). For LLMs, they have been shown to
013                decompose intermediate representations that often are not interpretable directly
014                into sparse sums of interpretable features, facilitating better control and subse-
015                quent analysis. However, similar analyses and approaches were lacking for text-
016                to-image models. We investigated the possibility of using SAEs to learn inter-
017                pretable features for a few-step text-to-image diffusion models, such as SDXL
018
                   Turbo. To this end, we train SAEs on the updates performed by transformer blocks
                   within SDXL Turbo’s denoising U-net. We find that their learned features are in-
019
                   terpretable, causally influence the generation process, and reveal specialization
020
                   among the blocks. In particular, we find one block mainly dealing with image
021                composition, mainly responsible for adding local details, and, one for color, illu-
022                mination, and style. Therefore, our work is an important first step towards better
023                understanding the internals of generative text-to-image models like SDXL Turbo
024                and showcases the potential of features learned by SAEs for the visual domain.

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Text-to-image generation is a rapidly evolving field. The DALL-E model first captured public inter-
030   est (Ramesh et al., 2021), combining learned visual vocabularies with sequence modeling to produce
031   high-quality images based on user input prompts. Today’s best text-to-image models are largely
032   based on text-conditioned diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022b; Podell
033   et al., 2023; Sauer et al., 2023b; Betker et al., 2023; Pernias et al., 2023). This can be partially
034
      attributed to diffusion models’ stable training dynamics, which makes them easier to scale than
      previous approaches like generative adversarial neural networks (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021). As a
035
      result, they can be trained on internet scale image-text datasets (Schuhmann et al., 2022a) and learn
036
      to generate photorealistic images from text.
037
038   However, the underlying logic of the neural networks enabling the text-to-image pipelines we have
039   today, due to their black box nature, is not well understood. Unfortunately, this lack of interpretabil-
040
      ity is typical in the deep learning field. For example, advances in image recognition (Krizhevsky
      et al., 2012) and language modeling (Devlin, 2018; Brown, 2020) come mainly from scaling mod-
041
      els (Hoffmann et al., 2022), rather than from an improved understanding of their internals. Recently,
042
      the emerging field of mechanistic interpretability has sought to alleviate this limitation by reverse
043   engineering visual models (Olah et al., 2020) and transformer-based LLMs (Rai et al., 2024). At the
044   same time, diffusion models have remained under-explored.
045
046
      This work focuses on SDXL Turbo, a recent open-source few-step text-to-image diffusion model.
      We leverage methodologies originally developed for language models, which allow inspection of the
047
      intermediate results of the forward pass (Chen et al., 2024; Ghandeharioun et al., 2024; Cunningham
048
      et al., 2023; Bricken et al., 2023). Moreover, some even enable reverse engineering the entire task-
049   specific subnets (Marks et al., 2024). In particular, sparse autoencoders (SAEs) (Yun et al., 2021;
050   Cunningham et al., 2023; Bricken et al., 2023) are considered a breakthrough in interpretability
051   for LLMs. They have been shown to decompose intermediate representations of the LLM forward
052   pass – often difficult to interpret due to polysemanticity1 – into sparse sums of interpretable and

061   To facilitate our analysis, we developed a library called SDLens that allows us to cache and manip-
062   ulate intermediate results of SDXL Turbo’s forward pass. We use our library to create a dataset of
063   SDXL Turbo’s intermediate feature maps of several transformer blocks inside SDXL Turbo’s U-net
064
      on 1.5M LAION-COCO prompts (Schuhmann et al., 2022a;b). We then use these feature maps to

065

066
067         1. We empirically show the potential of SAEs to learn highly interpretable features in
068            diffusion-based text-to-image models.
069         2. We developed visualization techniques to analyze the interpretability and causal effects of
070
               the learned features.
            3. We perform two case studies in which we visualize and interpret the active features in
071
               different transformer blocks, finding evidence that certain transformer blocks of SDXL
072
               Turbo’s pipeline specialize in image composition, adding details, and style.
073         4. We follow up our qualitative case studies by designing multiple quantitative experiments
074            that show that our hypotheses hold up also on larger sample sizes.
075         5. As a part of our quantitative analysis, we create an automatic feature annotation pipeline
076            for the transformer block which appears responsible for image compositions.

120   Diffusion models. Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ramesh et al., 2022; Rombach
121   et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022a) sample from an unknown distribution p by learning to iteratively

123   samples from p. Mathematically, the images are corrupted with Gaussian noise and are distributed

135   defines p✓ (xt 1 |xt ). The denoising distribution p✓ (xt 1 |xt ) is parameterized to be Gaussian.

144   Latent diffusion. Originally, diffusion models operated directly on pixels (Ho et al., 2020; Song &
145   Ermon, 2020). However, training a denoising network in pixel space is computationally expensive
146   (Hoogeboom et al., 2023). Thus, Rombach et al. (2022) uses a pre-trained autoencoder to first
147   compress images and define a diffusion process in the latent space instead. To make this difference
148   clear they write p✓ (zt 1 |zt ), in which now zt refers to a noisy latent instead of a noisy image.
149
      SDXL Turbo. To speed-up inference of latent diffusion models, Sauer et al. (2023b) distills a pre-
150   trained Stable Diffusion XL (SDXL) model (Podell et al., 2023). The distilled model is referred to
151   as SDXL Turbo as it allows high-quality sampling in as little as 1-4 steps. In comparison, the original
152   original SDXL model is trained with a noise schedule of 1000 steps, but in practice, sampling with
153   20 to 50 steps still generates high-quality images.
154
      Neural network architecture. The denoising network of SDXL Turbo estimating p✓ (zt 1 |zt ) is
155
      implemented using a U-net similar to Rombach et al. (2022). The U-net is composed of a down-
156   sampling path, a bottleneck, and an up-sampling path. Both the down-sampling and up-sampling
157   paths are composed of 3 individual blocks. The individual block structure differs slightly but both
158   down- and up-sampling blocks consist of residual layers with some blocks including cross-attention
159   transformer layers while others do not. Finally, the bottleneck layer is also composed of attention
160   and residual layers. Importantly, the text conditioning is achieved via cross-attention to text embed-
161   dings performed by in total 11 transformer blocks embedded in the down-, up-sampling path and
      bottleneck. An architecture diagram displaying the relevant blocks can be found in App. B Fig. 2.

416

417

420   Cherti et al., 2023). This value reflects how semantically similar the contexts are in which the feature
421   is most active. We display our results in the first column of Table 1 (a), which shows that the CLIP
422   similarity between images with the feature active is significantly higher then the random baseline

475   6       R ELATED WORK
476
477   Analyzing the latent space of diffusion models. Kwon et al. (2023) show that diffusion models
478   naturally have a semantically meaningful latent space. Park et al. (2023) analyzes the latent space
479   of DMs using Riemannian geometry. Li et al. (2024); Dalva & Yanardag (2024) presents self-
480   supervised methods for finding semantic directions in the latent space. Similarly, Gandikota et al.
481   (2023) show that the attribute variations lie in a low-rank space by learning LoRA adapters (Hu et al.,
482
      2021) on top of pre-trained DMs. Brack et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023) demonstrate effective
      semantic vector algebraic operations in the latent space of DMs, as observed by Mikolov et al.
483
      (2013). However, none of those works train SAEs to interpret and control the latent space explicitly.
484
485

486
      Mechanistic interpretability using SAEs. Sparse autoencoders have only recently been popular-
487   ized by Bricken et al. (2023), in which they show that it is possible to learn interpretable features by
488   decomposing neurons in MLPs in 2-layer transformer language models. At the same time, a parallel
489   work decomposed the elements of the residual stream (Cunningham et al., 2023), which followed
490   up on (Sharkey et al., 2022). To our knowledge, the first work that applied sparse autoencoders
491   to transformer-based LLM was (Yun et al., 2021), which learned a joint dictionary for patterns of
492   all layers. Recently, sparse autoencoders have gained a lot of traction and many have been trained
493   even on state-of-the-art LLMs (Gao et al., 2024; Templeton & et al., 2024; Lieberum et al., 2024).
494   In addition, great tools are available for inspection (Lin & Bloom, 2023) and automatic interpreta-
495
      tion Caden et al. (2024) of learned characteristics. Marks et al. (2024) have shown how to use SAE
      features to facilitate automatic circuit discovery.
496
497   The works most closely related to our work are (Ismail et al., 2023) and (Daujotas, 2024). Ismail
498   et al. (2023) apply concept bottleneck methods (Koh et al., 2020) that decompose latent concepts
499   into vectors of interpretable concepts to generative image models, including diffusion models. Un-
500   like the SAEs that we train, this method requires labeled concept data. Daujotas (2024) decomposes
501
      CLIP (Radford et al., 2021; Cherti et al., 2023) vision embeddings using SAEs and use them for
      conditional image generation with a diffusion model called Kandinsky (Razzhigaev et al., 2023).
502
      Importantly, using SAE features they are able to manipulate the image generation process in inter-
503
      pretable ways. In contrast, in our work we train SAEs on intermediate representations of the forward
504   pass of SDXL Turbo. Consequently, we can interpret and manipulate SDXL Turbo’s forward pass
505   on a finer granularity, e.g., by intervening on specific transformer blocks and spatial positions.

509   7   C ONCLUSION
510
511
512   We trained SAEs on SDXL Turbo’s (by default) opaque intermediate representations. We highlight
513
      that this study is one of the first in the academic literature to mechanistically interpret the inter-
      mediate representations of the modern text-to-image model. Our findings demonstrate that SAEs
514
      are capable of extracting interpretable features and that they have a significant causal effect on the
515
      generated images. Importantly, the learned features shed light on the forward pass of SDXL Turbo.
516   In particular, they enabled us to observe that transformer blocks play a specific and varying role in
517   the generation process. Our findings show a clear picture for the functions of down.2.1, up.0.0,
518   and, up.0.1. For mid.0, the picture is less clear; as we observed from its learned features, it
519   seems to encode more abstract information, and interventions are less effective. These observa-
520   tions lead us to the rough hypothesis of how SDXL Turbo generates images: down.2.1 decides
521   top-level composition, mid.0 assigns low-level semantics, up.0.0 adds details based on the two
522   above, and up.0.1 fills in color, texture, and style.
523   Although our work provides important insights into the mechanisms of SDXL Turbo, we studied
524   its transformer blocks in isolation. More research is required to understand how the features of
525   SDXL Turbo interact between layers and how this affects the overall functionality of the model. A
526   promising direction would be the application of advanced interpretability techniques such as (Marks
527   et al., 2024). Marks et al. (2024) computes circuits that show how different layers and attention
528   heads wire together and, therefore, would provide insight into our hypothesis stated above.
529   In addition, the complex nature of some of the learned visual features deserves special attention. Al-
530   though some features (for example, learning on down.2.1 and up.0.1) exhibit their effect even
531   when turning them on during empty-prompt generations, other features (typically, the ones learned
532   on mid.0 and up.0.0) require an appropriate context to show their effect. This complexity poses
533   additional challenges for the automatic annotation of features. Our preliminary results in this direc-
534   tion suggest that current visual language models do not seem to pick up the features, often subtle
535   roles. Furthermore, we question whether captioning the visual features with few short sentences can
536
      adequately capture most features’ roles.
537   We believe that our work highlights the potential of SAEs in revealing the internal structure of
538   diffusion models like SDXL Turbo, and it could help future researchers answer more sophisticated
539   questions about image generation. For example, how does SDXL Turbo add illumination effects,
      render wool, hair, or reflections of objects in the water?

847   abstraction. The closest feature vector can change when varying a neuron, resulting in the neuron
848   seemingly reacting to or steering semantically unrelated things.

1064   orientations and framing. The effects of ablating other blocks such as mid.0 and up.0.0 are more
1065   subtle. For mid.0 it is difficult to describe in words and up.0.0 seems to add local details to the
1066   image while leaving the overall composition mostly intact.
