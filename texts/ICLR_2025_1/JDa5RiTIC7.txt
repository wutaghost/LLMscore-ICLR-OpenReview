009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Language agents have shown promising performance in automating web-based
012                tasks, but the complexity and vast search spaces of real-world websites challenge
013                reactive agents in identifying optimal solutions. While tree search agents offer
014                enhanced exploration by interacting with actual websites, they often incur high
015                costs, potential risks, and are challenging to implement for real-world websites.
016                This paper explores a novel paradigm leveraging large language models’ (LLMs)
017                internal world models for planning in complex environments, presenting a middle
018                ground between reactive agents and tree search agents. Results on two representa-
019
                   tive benchmarks, VisualWebArena and Mind2Web-live, demonstrate that our ap-
                   proach largely closes the gap between reactive agents and tree search agents, while
020
                   maintaining efficiency and safety advantages. Notably, tree search can be consid-
021
                   ered as approaching an upper bound for our method, as it explores actual websites
022                rather than simulations. This work opens new avenues for research into more
023                effective and secure strategies for autonomous agents in complex, dynamic envi-
024                ronments. It represents a step forward in improving upon reactive agents while
025                approaching the performance of tree search methods, without incurring their im-
026                plementation challenges and costs.1

029   1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Planning, a cornerstone of artificial intelligence since its inception, continues to drive remarkable ad-
032   vancements in the field. From AlphaGo’s (Silver et al., 2016) groundbreaking performance to recent
033   investigations into scaling inference-time compute with large language models (LLMs) (Wang et al.,
034   2024; Feng et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2024), these developments underscore planning’s pivotal role
035   in propelling AI capabilities to unprecedented heights. Notably, recent research demonstrates that
036   augmenting LLMs with advanced inference-time algorithms, such as tree search (Yao et al., 2023;
037   Hao et al., 2023), effectively improves performance on complex reasoning tasks compared to stan-
038
      dard chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning (Wei et al., 2022). These methods of scaling inference-time
      compute through planning algorithms enables LLMs to explore multiple potential solution paths,
039
      yielding more robust and accurate outputs.
040
041   However, translating these successes to complex real-world environments presents formidable chal-
042   lenges. This difficulty has contributed to a notable lag in research progress on planning in real-world
043   scenarios. Specifically, the underlying dynamics or transitions of complex environments, such as
044
      the Web (Deng et al., 2023; Koh et al., 2024a; Pan et al., 2024b) or physical environments (Li et al.,
      2024; Shridhar et al., 2020), are often unknown or incomputable. This complexity prevents the
045
      direct use of search algorithms to find the best plans ahead of time. Consequently, most existing
046
      works adopt the reactive agent paradigm, where an action is directly executed based on the current
047   observation at each step, without engaging in planning (Zheng et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Cheng
048   et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024; Lai et al., 2024). However, this approach often leads to suboptimal
049   outcomes due to insufficient exploration of the environment. To address the limitations of reactive
050   agents, one might seek to conduct online exploration with the environment to implement tee search
051   algorithms effectively. Yet, conducting online planning through real-time environmental exploration
052   poses significant challenges in terms of efficiency and raises potential safety concerns (Koh et al.,

079   In this paper, we strive to find a middle ground between planning using fully online tree search
080   and purely reactive agents based on CoT reasoning. Specifically, we build upon the hypothesis that
081   LLMs may inherently encode an internal world model of the environment, as suggested by previous
082   research (Hao et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024). While these studies indicate LLMs’ potential for world
083   modeling, they have primarily focused on simple, constrained environments (e.g., blocksworld). Our
084   work pioneers the investigation of LLM-based world models in complex, real-world scenarios, par-
085   ticularly web navigation. We leverage LLMs as simulators to predict state transitions after executing
086   actions, enabling model-based planning without actual environmental exploration (see Figure 1).
087
      To realize this, we devise a multi-stage framework for our model-based planning, comprising four
      stages: action proposal, self-refinement, simulation, and scoring. The latter two stages correspond
088
      to the transition model and reward model commonly used in world modeling. With the transition
089
      model, we simulate task execution within an imagined environment; with the reward model, we eval-
090   uate and score each simulated playout to guide the planning process. This synergy enables efficient
091   exploration of potential action sequences without real-world interaction. Our planning algorithm’s
092   concept resembles model predictive control (MPC; Garcia et al. (1989); Kouvaritakis & Cannon
093   (2016)), which effectively manages error accumulation in simulations: only the first action of the
094   top-ranked simulated trajectory is executed, with new simulations generated from the resulting state.
095   This process iterates until the model decides to terminate.
096   To validate the effectiveness of our model-based planning paradigm, we test it on open-ended web
097   environments, where an agent is expected to automate diverse tasks over real-world websites that
098   entail a tremendous search space (e.g., booking a flight or looking for a specific product). We
099   demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed method on two representative benchmarks that sup-
100   port online interaction: VisualWebArena (Koh et al., 2024a) and Mind2Web-live (Pan et al., 2024b).
101   Our model-based planning approach significantly outperforms the reactive agent on both datasets.
102   Although its performance still falls short of tree search with actual interactions, it’s important to
103   note that the tree search agent can be viewed as an upper bound for our simulation-based method.
104
      Moreover, model-based planning offers superior flexibility compared to tree search, which is often
      inefficient and impractical to implement on real-world websites.
105
106   In summary, our paper presents a pioneering study on model-based planning utilizing the internal
107   world models of LLMs in complex environments. As an initial exploration in this domain, we
      prioritize establishing the viability and potential of this paradigm over performance optimization.

      2.1   W EB AGENTS
117
118   Driven by the goal of automating tedious and repetitive web-based tasks, web agents powered by
119   (multimodal) language models have made substantial progress in various aspects. Benchmarks
120   have evolved from MiniWoB++ (Shi et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2018) to WebShop (Yao et al., 2022)
121   and WebArena (Zhou et al., 2023), offering increasingly realistic website simulations. VisualWe-
122   bArena (Koh et al., 2024a) and Mind2Web (Deng et al., 2023) challenge models’ ability to handle
123   visual information and generalize across diverse tasks, websites, and domains.
124
125   Reactive Agent. Significant progress has been made to enhance the fundamental capabilities of
126
      web agents through both prompting closed-source models (Zheng et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Deng
      et al., 2023) and training models using HTML and webpage screenshots (Lee et al., 2023; Gur et al.,
127
      2023; Furuta et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024; Baechler et al., 2024). Additionally, models’ abilities to
128
      ground web agent actions to elements have been improved through training on action-coordinate pair
129   data (You et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024). Further advancements have been achieved by training on
130   web agent trajectories, utilizing both human-annotated trajectories (Shaw et al., 2023; Hong et al.,
131   2024; Deng et al., 2023; Lai et al., 2024) and synthesized exploration trajectories (Furuta et al.,
132   2023; Song et al., 2024; Patel et al., 2024).
133
134   Tree Search Agent. Pan et al. (2024a) introduces a reward model based on GPT-4V, designed
135   to provide both step-wise and trajectory-level rewards to guide inference-time search. Search
136   Agent (Koh et al., 2024b) investigates inference-time search algorithms in interactive web envi-
137   ronments, enabling explicit exploration and multi-step planning. In contrast to Search Agent, which
138
      employs a variant of best-first tree search, AgentQ (Putta et al., 2024) and WebPilot (Zhang et al.,
      2024) utilize Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) as their primary search strategy.
139
140   While tree search on websites has demonstrated significant improvements, it still presents several
141   limitations. First, the search process substantially increases inference time due to the need for ex-
142   tensive exploration, which is difficult to parallelize given its inherently sequential nature. Secondly,
143   search necessitate backtracking to previous states. Although it is possible to implement in sandbox
144
      environments with heavy overhead by resetting the environment and storing the action sequence
      leading to a specific state (Koh et al., 2024b), is not feasible for real-world websites. Finally, search
145
      heightens the risk of destructive actions that may irreversibly alter the website’s state, potentially
146
      causing harmful side effects.

      2.2   W ORLD M ODELS
149
150   World models, a cornerstone of model-based reinforcement learning (Luo et al., 2024) since the
151   introduction of Dyna by Sutton (1991), are typically trained on observed state transitions to predict
152   future states and rewards. In addition to learned models, simulators with physical engines (Kolve
153   et al., 2017; Puig et al., 2018) can also serve as world models. These world models enable efficient
154   training through simulated experiences, reducing environmental interactions and improving sample
155   efficiency (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018). Beyond their role in training, researchers have explored
156   the use of world models to facilitate planning (Pascanu et al., 2017; Schrittwieser et al., 2020).
157   Fundamentally, world models in reinforcement learning often involve task-specific training, with a
158
      primary focus on enhancing data efficiency in the agent learning process.
159   In contrast to traditional world models in reinforcement learning, LLMs employed as world models
160   primarily focus on facilitating decision-making in planning rather than training. This distinction
161   leads LLM-based models to prioritize key task abstractions over the high-fidelity simulations typi-
      cally required in reinforcement learning. Recent research has demonstrated the potential of LLMs

      Web agents automating tasks in open-ended online environments face vast, complex search spaces
203
      where reactive or greedy strategies often fall short (Koh et al., 2024a). Conversely, tree search
204   methods based on online interaction frequently incur high costs and raise safety concerns (Koh
205   et al., 2024b; Putta et al., 2024). This paper pioneers a novel paradigm: harnessing LLMs’ internal
206   world models for virtual exploration through simulation. We posit that this new paradigm could
207   establish a middle ground between reactive agents and tree search agents, potentially striking an
208   optimal balance between accuracy and efficiency.
209
      However, achieving good performance with model-based planning is not trivial, primarily due to the
210   high complexity of real-world websites, which poses critical challenges for accurate simulation. In-
211   accurate simulations may diverge from the actual environment, potentially proposing actions within
212   the multi-step simulation that are not actually available in the real environment. Poor simulation
213   quality can also negatively impact the accurate assessment of action selection. To address these
214   issues, we employ a Model Predictive Control (MPC)-based planning framework with LLMs’ sim-
215   ulation, representing an initial effort to unlock the potential of model-based planning with LLMs for
      web automation.

247   3.1   MPC-BASED P LANNING
248
249   MPC (Garcia et al., 1989; Kouvaritakis & Cannon, 2016) is a classic control method that addresses
250   model inaccuracies. It computes an optimal trajectory in simulation, but only implements the first
251   action before re-planning with new observations. This step-wise planning approach is particularly
252   well-suited to complex web environments, where obtaining a perfect world model is extremely chal-
253
      lenging, if not impossible. Formally, given a natural language task instruction I, the planning al-
      gorithm seeks to find a trajectory of actions a0 , a1 , . . . , aT that completes the task in the target
254
      environment. This environment is governed by a deterministic transition function T : S × A → S,
255
      where S and A represent the state and action spaces, respectively. However, due to the complexity
256   of real-world websites, T is typically unknown. Moreover, the agent does not have direct access to
257   the state st ∈ S of the environment. Instead, it must determine its actions based on an observation
258   ot ∈ O of the current state st . At each time step t, given a set of candidate actions At proposed
259   from the environment, our MPC-based planning solves:

      3.2   S TATE R EPRESENTATION
291
292
      A crucial aspect of our model-based planning lies in the design choice of O′ , i.e., the state repre-
293
      sentation within simulation.2 Ideally, one would aim to align the simulated observation space O′
294   with the actual observation space O. However, actual observations typically involve multimodal per-
295   ception, including screenshots with Set-of-Marks annotations (Yang et al., 2023). This multimodal
296   nature presents a challenge for current LLMs, which are limited to uni-modal generation, making it
297   infeasible to use identical representations in simulations.
298
      In designing O′ , we face unique constraints. Generating or processing visual elements is beyond the
299
      capability of text-based LLMs. Moreover, decoding complete HTML or accessibility trees within
300   the simulation is computationally intensive and prone to errors, potentially introducing noise that
301   could compromise the planning process. To address these challenges, we opt for a natural language
302   description of the predicted state change as the new observation in simulation. This approach offers
303   several advantages:
304
305   Compatibility: It aligns with the text-based nature of LLMs, enabling seamless integration within
306   the simulation process.
307
308
      Flexibility: Natural language can capture a wide range of state changes, from simple UI updates to
309   complex interactions.
310
311
312
      Efficiency: Textual descriptions are computationally less demanding than generating or processing
      complex structural representations.
313
314
315   Relevance: We can focus on describing the most pertinent changes, filtering out irrelevant details
316   that might distract from the planning task.
317
318   Concrete examples of state change description simulated by LLMs can be found in Figure 2. We
319   provide further insights in our design choice for O′ in Section 5.1.

      L IMITATIONS
504
505
      Our study, as a pioneering exploration of MPC-based planning with LLMs for web navigation,
      naturally comes with certain limitations that pave the way for exciting future research directions:
506
507
      Simplicity of Planning Algorithm. In this preliminary work, we deliberately employed a straight-
508   forward planning algorithm to demonstrate the core potential of our approach. While effective, this
509   simplicity leaves ample room for future enhancements. More sophisticated planning techniques,
510   such as Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), could be integrated to further improve performance. As
511   a foundational study, our focus was on establishing the viability of the concept rather than opti-
512   mizing every aspect of the system. This strategic choice allows future research to build upon our
513   findings and explore more advanced planning strategies within the framework we’ve established.
514
515   Computational Cost. Our current implementation, utilizing state-of-the-art models like GPT-4o,
516   incurs significant API costs (approximately $1 per task on VWA). This cost reflects our prioritization
517
      of exploring the full potential of LLM-based planning without immediate constraints. For practical
      applications, future work could investigate cost-effective alternatives such as fine-tuning specialized
518
      models for simulation tasks. Our approach of using the most advanced available model serves as
519
      an upper bound, demonstrating the maximum potential of this paradigm. This sets a benchmark for
520   future optimizations that balance performance and efficiency.
521
522   These limitations underscore the nature of our work as a proof-of-concept, opening up numerous
523   avenues for future research and optimization. By establishing the foundational potential of MPC-
524   based planning with LLMs, we have laid the groundwork for a new paradigm in web navigation,
525   inviting further innovations that can refine and extend our approach.

759      Self-Refinement
760      You are assiting a web navigation agent to help a human user navigate a website to complete a task.
761      Given the user’s intent, the action history, and the current state of the webpage, the agent has proposed
762      a set of candidate actions to take at the current step.
763      Your role is not to determine a best action for the agent at this step, but to filter out the actions that are
764      very likely not relevant or helpful for the agent to accomplish the task.
765
         Please select all actions that you think that could possibly lead the agent to accomplish the task. It’s
766      important to note that to accomplish a task, the agent will execute a sequence of actions. So the action
767      to take at this step does not have to immediately lead to the completion of the task. You should select
768      any action that could be relevant for the agent to take in the current state of the webpage. Try to be as
769      thoughtful and comprehensive as you can! Don’t miss any possible action. If there is one action that
         is clearly the best, and all other actions are clearly not very relevant, you can only select one action.
770
         Please do this sparely, since some actions may be helpful in a longer horizon.
771
772      A action should be included as long as it could be relevant to the task, even if it may not be the most
         direct action to take at this step!! Some relevant actions might seem indirect at the first glance, but
773
         could be helpful in a longer horizon. Please also include those actions.
774
775      Please at least select one action.
776
