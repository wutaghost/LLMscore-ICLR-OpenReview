011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Fatigue modeling is essential for material-related applications, including design,
014            engineering, manufacturing, and maintenance. Central to fatigue modeling is the
015
               computation and analysis of stress intensity factors (SIFs), which model the crack-
               driving force and are influenced by factors such as geometry, load, crack shape,
016
               and crack size. Traditional methods are based on finite element analysis, which
017
               is computationally expensive. A common engineering practice is manually con-
018            structing handbook (surrogate) solutions, though these are limited when dealing
019            with complex scenarios, such as intricate geometries. In this work, we reformulate
020            SIF computation as an operator learning problem, leveraging recent advancements
021            in data-driven operator networks to enable efficient and accurate predictions. Our
022            results show that, when trained on a relatively small finite element dataset, opera-
023            tor networks — such as Deep Operator Networks (DeepONet) and Fourier Neural
024            Operators (FNO) — achieve less than 5% relative error, significantly outperform-
025            ing popular handbook solutions. We further demonstrate how these predictions
026
               can be integrated into crack growth simulations and used to calculate the proba-
               bility of failure in small aircraft applications.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In the mid-19th century, engineers noticed failures in bridges and railway components due to re-
032   peated loading. It quickly became clear that these failures were linked to the cyclic nature of the
033   stress, often occurring without any prior warning. This phenomenon was identified as metal fatigue.
034   A significant advancement in understanding metal fatigue is recognizing that structures often contain
035   crack-like defects introduced during manufacturing. The central question in fatigue crack growth
036   research is determining how long it takes for a crack to expand from an initial size to the maximum
037
      allowable size just before failure.
038   Understanding the period where the crack size grows requires the knowledge of a fatigue crack
039   growth curve, as shown in Figure 1. The vertical axis is the crack growth rate, and the horizontal
040   axis is the difference between the maximum and minimum stress intensity factor (SIF) during cyclic
041   loading (Głuchowski & Sas, 2020), where SIF is denoted as K. SIF is a fundamental concept
042   in fracture mechanics that describes the stress state near the tip of a crack and is the function of
043
      geometry and loading. It is used to predict the stress state near a crack tip and provide a failure
      criterion for materials (Tudose & Popa, 2007). In our work, stage 2 is of interest where crack
044
      growth rate da/dN is of some power function of ∆K, leading to a linear relation between the log
045
      of two quantities. Several attempts have been made to describe the crack growth rate curve using
046   semi or wholly empirical formulae fitted to a data set. The most widely known is the Paris equation
047   (Pugno et al., 2006):
048                                             da

050   To obtain the constants C and m, we need the SIF values K. Finite element (FE) analysis can effec-
051   tively compute displacement fields from which SIFs can be calculated using M-integral (Banks-Sills
052   et al., 2007). When converged, FE analysis can provide accurate solutions, but it requires substan-
053   tial computational resources, especially within design iterations. As a result, researchers frequently
      turn to handbook solutions (Toribio et al., 2022), which act as efficient surrogate models and offer

072   a convenient way to estimate SIFs with reasonable accuracy. One most widely-used example is the
073   manually-created Raju-Newman equations (Newman Jr & Raju, 1981) (Andersson) (Raju & New-
074   man, 1979), which consists of high-order polynomial fits. These equations were developed from 3D
075   FE analyses of cracks in finite elastic plates subjected to tension or bending loads. Raju-Newman
076   equations can provide accurate SIF solutions for a wide range of crack geometries, including semi-
077
      elliptical surface cracks, quarter-elliptical corner cracks, and cracks near holes. However, these
      equations have limited application when it comes to complex geometry and crack shapes.
078
079   Machine learning (ML) offers a means to create surrogate models in a more flexible and accuracy
080   way (Zhang et al., 2023). Such models can therefore be generalized to more complex geometries
081   and boundary conditions due to their ability to express complex data. Merrell et al. (2024) used
082   genetic programming based symbolic regression to learn equations that can predict SIF and improve
083
      the accuracy by 15-50% compared to Raju-Newman equations. Xia et al. (2022) introduce a SIF
      model for mode-I cracks in coal rock by training a convolutional neural network (CNN), that pa-
084
      rameterizes the coal images and accurately predicts SIFs. Xu et al. (2022) focus on probabilistic
085
      failure risk assessment for an aero-engine disk. They conduct studies with Gaussian process (GP)
086   regression, tree-structure models, and artificial neural networks (ANN). They show that the accuracy
087   of SIFs can be improved by 5%–35%. Zhang et al. (2023) use ANN to predict mixed-mode SIFs of
088   composites. The algorithm is trained on a dataset generated by combining the interaction integral
089   and the extended FE method.
090
      In this work, we introduce neural operators (Azizzadenesheli et al., 2024), a powerful new machine
091
      learning tool, for SIFs prediction with very high accuracy and generalizable to a wide range of
092   geometries and crack shapes. SIFs are the function of geometry, crack shape, and the loading.
093   The FE model defines the material geometry, crack shape, stresses, and displacement fields for
094   loading. The displacement field from FE is then used by the M-integral method to evaluate SIFs
095   along the crack front. This whole procedure can be seen as an operator learning problem where the
096   combination of the FE model and M-integral act as an operator. In our dataset, material properties
097   and loading conditions are fixed. The input to the operator is the function describing different
098   geometries/crack shapes, and the output is the SIF along the crack front.
099   Our contributions are as follows:
100
101        1. Introducing FE SIF datasets for two different crack scenarios — a surface crack in a plate
102           and a single corner crack at a shank hole in a plate — that provide different levels of
103           problem complexity.
104        2. Applying three popular neural operators, including Deep Operator Network (DeepONet)
105           (Lu et al., 2019), Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) (Li et al., 2020), and Proper Orthogonal
106           Decomposition DeepONet (POD-DeepONet), (Lu et al., 2022) on the datasets, such that
107           they act as surrogate models for predicting SIFs. We provide a comprehensive analysis of
              the model accuracy using the FE results as the benchmark.

137   2.1   S URFACE CRACK IN A PLATE
138
139   To generate the SIF dataset related to a surface crack in a plate, we employ high-fidelity FE models,
140   representing semi-elliptical surface cracks under Mode I tension (Merrell et al., 2024). This dataset
141
      comprises several simulated results of K, each characterized by distinct plate and crack geometries
      shown in Figure 2. The FE models generate 2,956 different plate geometries and crack shapes
142
      represented using features a/c, a/t, c/b, and SIF values along the crack front represented using the
143
      parametric angle ϕ. The resolution in a/c, a/t, c/b, and ϕ is 0.05, 0.05, 0.1, and 0.023 radians. The
144   corresponding range of values in the features a/c, a/t, c/b, and ϕ is 0.2 to 2, 0.2 to 0.85, 0.01 to 0.3,
145   and 0.057 to 3.088 radians, respectively. Out of 2956 different geometries and crack shapes, 2518
146   are used for training, and 438 test the accuracy of operator networks.
147

149   2.2   C ORNER CRACK IN A PLATE
150
151   Quarter elliptic corner crack is another type of crack, and we discuss the SIF dataset for a corner
152   crack at a shank hole in a plate. This scenario is generally considered more complex than a surface
153
      crack in a plain plate as it involves the intersection of multiple surfaces (the plate face, edge, and
      hole surface), creating a more complex crack front geometry. The shank hole introduces stress con-
154
      centrations and alters the stress field around the crack, making the stress distribution more intricate,
155
      which affects the SIF distribution. FE results for this scenario were obtained from the Center for
156   Aircraft Structural Life Extension (CASTLE) at the US Air Force Academy. The geometry used
157   is shown in Figure 3. The FE models generate 24,519 different plate and crack geometries repre-
158   sented using features W/r, a/c, a/t, r/t, and SIF values along the crack front represented using the
159   parametric angle ϕ (with a resolution of 0.023 radians). The corresponding range of values in the
160   features W/r, a/c, a/t, r/t, and ϕ is 1.6 to 200, 0.1 to 10, 0.1 to 0.95, 0.5 to 1.5, and 0.052 to 1.52
161   radians, respectively. Out of the 24,519 different geometries and crack shapes, 18,389 are used for
      training, and 6,130 test the accuracy of operator networks.

      4   A PPLICATIONS TO FATIGUE M ODELING

      SIF is a fundamental concept in fracture mechanics that characterizes the stress state near the tip of
      a crack. It plays a crucial role in analyzing crack behavior and predicting material failure. In this
384
      section, we show how the operator networks can be used to simulate the crack growth and perform
385
      damage tolerance modeling (Millwater et al., 2019). Crack growth simulation using SIFs involves a
386   few steps. SIF is calculated for the crack geometry and loading conditions, which characterize the
387   stress state near the crack tip. This is where the operator networks are used. Empirical crack growth
388   laws like Paris’ law (shown in Equation 1) relate the crack growth rate to the SIF range. The crack
389   is grown incrementally based on the calculated growth rate, and the crack geometry is updated after
390   each increment. As the crack grows, the SIFs need to be recalculated for the new crack geometry.
391
      For fatigue crack growth, the process is repeated several times throughout the load cycles. Growth
392   is simulated until a critical crack size is reached, indicating failure. The whole process starts with
393   the SIF values, and if the predictions are not accurate, the resulting crack growth simulation will
394   accumulate large errors. Fast inference from operator networks is another key factor that speeds
395   up the simulation. Throughout the crack growth simulation, SIF values are required hundreds of
396   times. This makes using FE methods prohibitive as they can take significantly longer to calculate
397   SIF values.
398   A corner crack in a plate is the more complicated case, and we are using this to demonstrate the
399   crack growth simulation. For example, consider an initial crack with lengths 0.0125 and 0.0125.
400   This crack is present in the plate with full width (2W ) 3.75, thickness (t) 0.125, and hole diameter
401   (2r) 0.25. Figure 12 shows the geometry for this example alongside the SIF predictions from the
402   operator networks against the FE model for this initial crack. We can see that the predictions from all
403   three models are close to the FE results. Operator networks can then be used in hypergrow (Ocampo
404   et al., 2020) to simulate the crack growth. As the predictions from the three operator networks are
405   very similar, the resulting crack growth results will also be very similar. Figure 13 shows the crack
406
      progression for DeepONet and POD-DeepONet with the number of load cycles. We can see that the
      results from both these models are very similar. As loading cycles accumulate, crack lengths grow
407
      in both directions and around 70000 cycles, the crack reaches the thickness of the plate. This is
408
      where it changes from a corner crack to a through crack (Taylor et al., 2005). Simulating the results
409   further, we can see the crack growing and it will eventually lead to failure. As FE takes a really long
410   time to calculate SIFs, it is impractical to get results for every cycle. The only thing feasible in this
411   case is to get FE runs for a few discrete cases and compare the SIF predictions from the operator
412   networks against the FE SIFs. Figures 11 shows different crack geometries and the SIF predictions
413   from operator networks alongside FE results. We can see that for all the cases, the predictions are
414   very close to the FE results with an absolute error of less than 1%.
415   SIFs from the operator networks can also be used in SMART-DT (Small Aircraft Risk Technology -
416   Damage Tolerance), which is a probabilistic damage tolerance analysis (PDTA) software developed
417   with application to small aircraft risk assessment (Millwater et al., 2019). Damage tolerance analysis
418   involves generating a deterministic crack growth analysis and finding the time at which the crack
419   can be found by inspection with 95% confidence given a non-destructive inspection technique and
420   the time at which the crack grows to critical size. The time to first inspection and inspection interval
421   is set as the time when the crack is detectable and half of the time between when the crack becomes
422   detectable and the time to failure. PDTA calculates the single flight probability of failure — the
423
      probability that a component with existing damage fractures on a flight has survived all prior flights.
      PDTA allows for uncertainties in usage, material properties, crack growth rate, and initial damage
424
      size to be incorporated into the risk analysis, but the computational expense increases considerably
425
      when uncertainty in crack growth rate is included, as this requires performing many crack growth
426   analyses. The probability of failure (PoF) in Figure 10 is evaluated from 3900 crack growth analyses
427   using DeepONet SIF model in 31.962 seconds. The PoF calculated using PDTA provides a forecast
428   of when fatigue cracks will begin to grow large enough to fracture. Planning maintenance actions
429   to inspect and repair such that PoF remains below an acceptably low risk threshold, 10−7 as noted
430   by Lincoln (1985), ensures that failures due to fatigue cracking will be extremely rare events. In
431   addition to being used as a tool for scheduling maintenance proactively, PDTA can also be used to
      estimate the economic life of aircraft and model the effects of changing the aircraft usage.

509   5   C ONCLUSIONS
510
511   For complex crack growth scenarios, the operator networks can predict SIF values accurately with
512
      errors less than 5%. This is several orders of magnitude better than the widely used handbook
      solutions like Raju-Newman equations, and at par with the industry standard FE models. Operator
513
      networks can be trained effectively on a relatively small dataset size and once trained, the predictions
514
      can be made very quickly. For fatigue crack growth simulation, SIF values are required several
515   times. FE methods are not suitable as they take a significant amount of time to calculate SIFs given
516   geometry and crack shape. Operator networks, can be used to make predictions quickly, and in this
517   work, we were able to simulate crack growth over 100000 load cycles within 0.5 seconds with an
518   accuracy of around 99%. Probability of failure can also be computed using multiple crack growth
519   analysis and using operator networks this can be done in a few seconds.

      A.1   D EEP ON ET
613
614   DeepONet is based on the universal approximation theorem for operators, which states that for any
615   continuous nonlinear operator G and any ϵ > 0, there exists a DeepONet that can approximate G
616   with an error less than ϵ. DeepONet, shown in Figure 14 is a neural network architecture designed to
617   learn nonlinear operators, based on the universal approximation theorem for operators. It consists of
618   two sub-networks — a branch network B(u) that encodes the input function u at m fixed locations
619   xi m−1
         i=0 , and a trunk network T (y) that encodes the output locations y. The DeepONet output is
620   given by:
621
622                                                     p−1
                                                        X
623                                       G(u)(y) =            bi (u) · ti (y)                           (5)
624                                                      i=0
625
626   where bi (u) and ti (y) are the outputs of the branch and trunk networks, respectively, and p is the
627   width of the last layer in both networks. This architecture allows DeepONet to efficiently learn
628   complex operator mappings G : U → V between function spaces, with applications in solving
629   differential equations and modeling dynamical systems. DeepONet is a high-level framework that
630   does not restrict branch and trunk networks to any specific architecture. y is usually low dimensional
631   which makes standard fully connected neural nets a good choice for trunk net, but the choice of
632   branch net depends on the type of input functions u.

658   A.3   FNO
659
660   Fourier Neural Operators (FNOs), shown in Figure 15 are designed to learn mappings between
661
      infinite-dimensional function spaces, typically of the form G : A → ⊓ where A and U are Banach
      spaces. The key innovation of FNOs is the Fourier layer, which performs convolutions in the spectral
662
      domain. Given an input function a(x), with aim to predict u(x), the FNO architecture has the
663
      following key features:
664
665         1. Lifting Layer: A standard neural net P lifts the input to a high dimensional space:
666            v0 (x) = P (x, a(x)).
667
            2. Fourier Layers: A series of Fourier and inverse Fourier transforms are then applied (Li
668            et al., 2020): vl (x) = σ(W vl−1 (x) + (F −1 (Rl F(vl−1 )))(x)).
669
            3. Projection Layer: Finally, another neural net Q projects the output from fourier layers
670
               back to the low dimensional space: u(x) = Q(x, vL (x)).
671
672   This formulation allows FNOs to handle a wide range of problems in scientific computing, offering
673   a powerful alternative to traditional numerical methods, especially for complex, high-dimensional
674   systems.
