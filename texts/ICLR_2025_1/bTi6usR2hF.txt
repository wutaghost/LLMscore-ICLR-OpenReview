                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009            Visual representation models leveraging attention mechanisms are challenged by
010            significant computational overhead, particularly when pursuing large receptive
011            fields. In this study, we aim to mitigate this challenge by introducing the Heat
012            Conduction Operator (HCO) built upon the physical heat conduction principle.
013            HCO conceptualizes image patches as heat sources and models their correlations
014            through adaptive thermal energy diffusion, enabling robust visual representations.
015            HCO enjoys a computational complexity of O(N 1.5 ), as it can be implemented
016            using discrete cosine transformation (DCT) operations. HCO is plug-and-play,
017
               combining with deep learning backbones produces visual representation models
               (termed vHeat) with global receptive fields. Experiments across vision tasks
018
               demonstrate that, beyond the stronger performance, vHeat achieves up to a 3×
019
               throughput, 80% less GPU memory allocation and 35% fewer computational
020            FLOPs compared to the Swin-Transformer.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; He et al., 2016) have been the
026   cornerstone of visual representation since the advent of deep learning, exhibiting remarkable perfor-
027
      mance across vision tasks. However, the reliance on local receptive fields and fixed convolutional
      operators imposes constraints, particularly in capturing long-range and complex dependencies within
028
      images (Luo et al., 2016). These limitations have motivated significant interest in developing alterna-
029
      tive visual representation models, including architectures based on ViTs (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Liu
030   et al., 2021) and State Space Models (Zhu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). Despite their effectiveness,
031   these models continue to face challenges, including relatively high computational complexity and a
032   lack of interpretability.
033
      When addressing these limitations, we draw inspiration from the field of heat conduction (Widder,
034
      1976), where spatial locality is crucial for the transfer of thermal energy due to the collision of
035
      neighboring particles. Notably, analogies can be drawn between the principles of heat conduction
036   and the propagation of visual semantics within the spatial domain, as adjacent image regions in a
037   certain scale tend to contain related information or share similar characteristics. Leveraging these
038   connections, we introduce vHeat, a physics-inspired vision representation model that conceptualizes
039   image patches as heat sources and models the calculation of their correlations as the diffusion of
040   thermal energy.
041
      To integrate the principle of heat conduction into deep networks, we first derive the general solution
042   of heat conduction in 2D space and extend it to multiple dimensions, corresponding to various
043   feature channels. Based on this general solution, we design the Heat Conduction Operator (HCO),
044   which simulates the propagation of visual semantics across image patches along multiple dimensions.
045   Notably, we demonstrate that HCO can be approximated through 2D (inverse) discrete cosine
046   transformation (DCT/IDCT), effectively reducing the computational complexity to O(N 1.5 ), Fig. 1.
047   This improvement boosts both training and testing efficiency due to the high parallelizability of DCT
048   and IDCT operations. Furthermore, as each element in the frequency domain obtained by DCT
049   incorporates information from all patches in the image space, vHeat can establish long-range feature
050
      dependencies and achieve global receptive fields. To enhance the representation adaptability of vHeat,
      we propose learnable frequency value embeddings (FVEs) to characterize the frequency information
051
      and predict the thermal diffusivity of visual heat conduction.
052
053   We develop a family of vHeat models (i.e., vHeat-Tiny/Small/Base), and extensive experiments are
      conducted to demonstrate their effectiveness in diverse visual tasks. Compared to benchmark vision

099
100
      Convolution Neural Networks. CNNs have been landmark models in the history of visual perception
101
      (LeCun et al., 1998; Krizhevsky et al., 2012). The distinctive characteristics of CNNs are encapsulated
102   in the convolution kernels, which enjoy high computational efficiency given specifically designed
103   GPUs. With the aid of powerful GPUs and large-scale datasets (Deng et al., 2009), increasingly
104   deeper (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014; Szegedy et al., 2015; He et al., 2016; Huang et al., 2017)
105   and efficient models (Howard et al., 2017; Tan & Le, 2019; Yang et al., 2021; Radosavovic et al.,
106   2020) have been proposed for higher performance across a spectrum of vision tasks. Numerous
107   modifications have been made to the convolution operators to improve its capacity (Chollet, 2017),
      efficiency (Hua et al., 2018; Yu & Koltun, 2015) and adaptability (Dai et al., 2017; Wang et al.,


                                                            2

108
      2023b). Nevertheless, the born limitation of local receptive fields remains. Recently developed large
109   convolution kernels (Ding et al., 2022b) took a step towards large receptive fields, but experienced
110   difficulty in handling high-resolution images.
111
112
      Vision Transformers. Built upon the self-attention operator (Vaswani et al., 2017), ViTs have
      the born advantage of building global feature dependency. Based on the learning capacity of self-
113
      attention across all image patches, ViTs has been the most powerful vision model ever, given a
114
      large dataset for pre-training (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Touvron et al., 2021; Peng et al., 2022). The
115   introduction of hierarchical architectures (Liu et al., 2021; Dong et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021; Lu
116   et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2021; Ding et al., 2022a; Zhao et al., 2022)
117   further improves the performance of ViTs. The Achilles’ Heel of ViTs is the O(N 2 ) computational
118   complexity, which implies substantial computational overhead given high-resolution images. Great
119   efforts have been made to improve model efficiency by introducing window attention, linear attention
120   and cross-covariance attention operators (Wang et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021; Ali
121   et al., 2021), at the cost of reducing receptive fields or non-linearity capacity. Other studies proposed
122   hybrid networks by introducing convolution operations to ViTs (Wang et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2021;
123
      Vaswani et al., 2021), designing hybrid architectures to combine CNN with ViT modules (Dai et al.,
      2021; Srinivas et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2021).
124
125   State Space Models and RNNs. State space models (SSMs) (Gu et al., 2022; Nguyen et al., 2022;
126   Wang et al., 2023a), which have the long-sequence modeling capacity with linear complexity, are
127   also migrated from the natural language area (Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023)). Visual SSMs were also
128   designed by adapting the selective scan mechanism to 2-D images (Zhu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024).
129
      Nevertheless, SSMs based on the selective scan mechanism suffer from limited parallelism, restricting
      their overall potential. Recent receptance weighted key value (RWKV) and RetNet models (Peng et al.,
130
      2023; Sun et al., 2023) improved the parallelism while retaining the linear complexity. They combine
131
      the efficient parallelizable training of transformers with the efficient inference of RNNs, leveraging
132   a linear attention mechanism and allowing formulation of the model as either a Transformer or an
133   RNN, thus parallelizing computations during training and maintaining constant computational and
134   memory complexity during inference. Despite the advantages, modeling a 2-D image as a sequence
135   impairs interpretability.
136
      Biology and Physics Inspired Models. Biology and physics principles have long been the fountain-
137
      head of creating vision models. Diffusion models (Song et al., 2020; Ho et al., 2020; Saharia et al.,
138   2022), motivated by Nonequilibrium thermodynamics (De Groot & Mazur, 2013), are endowed with
139   the ability to generate images by defining a Markov chain for the diffusion step. QB-Heat (Chen
140   et al., 2022) utilizes physical heat equation as supervision signal for masked image modeling task.
141   Spiking Neural Network (SNNs) (Ghosh-Dastidar & Adeli, 2009; Tavanaei et al., 2019; Lee et al.,
142   2016) claims better simulation on the information transmission of biological neurons, formulating
143   models for simple visual tasks (Bawane et al., 2018). The success of these models encourages us
144   to explore the principle of physical heat conduction for the development of vision representation
145   models.

      3.2     V H EAT: V ISUAL H EAT C ONDUCTION

203
      Drawing inspiration from the analogies between the principles of physical heat conduction and the
204   propagation of visual semantics within the spatial domain (i.e., ‘visual heat conduction’), we propose
205   vHeat, a physics-inspired deep architecture for visual representation learning. The vHeat model is
206   built upon the Heat Conduction Operator (HCO), which is designed to integrate the principle of heat
207   conduction into handling the discrete feature of vision data. We also leverage the thermal diffusivity
208   in the classic physical heat equation (Eq equation 1) to improve the adaptability of vHeat to vision
209   data.
210

             3.2.2   A DAPTIVE T HERMAL D IFFUSIVITY
      258
      259    In physical heat conduction, thermal diffusivity represents the rate of heat transfer within a material.
      260    While in visual heat conduction, we hypothesize that more representative image contents contain
      261    more energy, resulting in higher temperatures in the corresponding image features within U (x, y, c, t).
      262    Therefore, it is suggested that the thermal diffusivity parameter k should be learnable and adaptive to
      263    image content, which facilitates the adaptability of heat condution to visual representation learning.
      264    Given that the output of DCT (i.e., DCT2D (U 0 ) in Eq. equation 10) lies in the frequency domain,
      265    we also determine k based on frequency values (k := k(ωx , ωy )). Since different positions in the
      266    frequency domain correspond to different frequency values, we propose to represent these values
      267    using learnable Frequency Value Embeddings (FVEs), which function similarly to the widely used
      268    absolute position embeddings in ViTsDosovitskiy et al. (2021) (despite in the frequency domain). As
      269    shown in Figure 2 (a), FVEs are fed to a linear layer to predict the thermal diffusivity k, allowing it
             to be non-uniform and adaptable to visual representations.

306   3.3     D ISCUSSION
307                                                                                                    2     2
308   • What is role of the thermal diffusivity coefficient e−k(ωx +ωy )t ? When multiplying with
                            2   2
309   DCT2D (U 0 ), e−k(ωx +ωy )t acts as an adaptive filter in the frequency domain to perform visual
310   heat conduction. Different frequency values correspond to distinct image patterns, i.e., high fre-
311   quency corresponds to edges and textures while low frequency corresponds to flat regions. With
312   adaptive thermal diffusivity, HCO can enhance/depress these patterns within each feature channel.
313   Aggregating the filtered features from all channels, vHeat achieves a robust feature representation.
314   • Why does temperature U (x, y, c, t) correspond to visual features? Visual features are essentially
315   the outcome of the feature extraction process, characterized by pixel propagation within the feature
316   map. This process aligns with the properties of existing convolution, self-attention, and selective
317   scan operators, exemplifying a form of information conduction. Similarly, visual heat conduction
318   embodies this concept of information conduction through temperature, denoted as U (x, y, c, t).
319   • What is the relationship/difference between HCO and self-attention? HCO dynamically
320   propagates energy via heat conduction, enabling the perception of global information within the input
321   image. This positions HCO as a distinctive form of attention mechanism. The distinction lies in its
322   reliance on interpretable physical heat conduction, in contrast to self-attention, which is formulated
323   through token similarity. Furthermore, HCO works in the frequency domain, implying its potential to
      affect all image patches through frequency filtering. Consequently, HCO exhibits greater efficiency

      compared to self-attention, which necessitates computing the relevance of all pairs across image

534
535   We introduce vHeat, a visual representation model that combines the benefits of global receptive
536   fields, computational efficiency, and enhanced interpretability. The effectiveness of the vHeat model
537   family, including vHeat-T/S/B models, has been demonstrated through extensive experiments and
538   ablation studies, significantly outperforming popular CNNs and ViTs. The results highlight the
539   potential of vHeat as a new paradigm for vision representation learning, offering fresh insights for
      the development of physics-inspired vision models.

770   A    M OTIVATION
771
772   Modern visual representation models are built upon the attention mechanism inspired by biological
773   vision systems. One drawback of it is the lack of a clear definition of the relationship between
774   biological electrical signals and brain activity (energy). This drives us to break through the attention
775   mechanism and attempt other physical laws. Heat conduction is a physical phenomenon in nature,
776   characterized by the propagation of energy. The heat conduction process combines implicit attention
777   computation with energy computation and has the potential to be a new mechanism for visual
778
      representation models.

905   D.4    P REDICTING k BY FVE S vs. TREATING k AS A LEARNABLE PARAMETER
906
907
      After performing DCT, the features lack explicit frequency value, while FVEs provide the model
      with prior knowledge of frequency values. Similar to how the introduction of positional encoding
908
      can enhance performance even in models that include positional information (Gehring et al., 2017),
909
      predicting k by FVEs, rather than treating k as a learnable parameter, reinforces prior frequency
910   information and more clearly represents the relationship between frequency and thermal diffusivity.
