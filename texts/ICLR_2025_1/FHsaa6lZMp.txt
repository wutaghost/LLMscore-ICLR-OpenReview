009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Machine-Generated Text (MGT) detection identifies whether a given text is
012            human-written or machine-generated. However, this can result in detectors that
013            would flag paraphrased or translated text as machine-generated. Fine-grained clas-
014            sification that separates the different types of machine text is valuable in real-world
015            applications, as different types of MGT convey distinct implications. For example,
016            machine-generated articles are more likely to contain misinformation, whereas
017            paraphrased and translated texts may improve understanding of human-written
018
               text. Despite this benefit, existing studies consider this a binary classification
               task, either overlooking machine-paraphrased and machine-translated text entirely
019
               or simply grouping all machine-processed text into one category. To address this
020
               shortcoming, this paper provides an in-depth study of fine-grained MGT detec-
021            tion, categorizing input text into four classes: human-written, machine-generated,
022            machine-paraphrased, and machine-translated. A key challenge is the perfor-
023            mance drop on out-of-domain texts due to the variability in text generators, espe-
024            cially for translated or paraphrased text. We introduce a RoBERTa-based Mixture
025            of Detectors (RoBERTa-MoD), which leverages multiple domain-optimized de-
026            tectors for more robust and generalized performance. We offer theoretical proof
027            that our method outperforms a single detector, and experimental findings demon-
028            strate a 5–9% improvement in mean Average Precision (mAP) over prior work on
029
               six diverse datasets: GoodNews, VisualNews, WikiText, Essay, WP, and Reuters.
               Our code and data will be publicly released upon acceptance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      As Large Language Models (LLMs) have made significant progress in fields like conversational sys-
035
      tems (Ouyang et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023), image understanding (OpenAI,
036
      2023; Nori et al., 2023; Ni et al., 2024), and text-to-image generation (Saharia et al., 2022; Rombach
037   et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024b; 2023b), concern about the hallucination (Lin et al., 2022) and eth-
038   ical (Zellers et al., 2019) issues they may raise have increased. To mitigate such misuse, researchers
039   have introduced Machine-Generated Text (MGT) detection to distinguish between human-written
040   and machine-generated text, defending against misinformation. As shown in Figure 1 (A), previ-
041   ous work (Mitchell et al., 2023; Verma et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a) typically
042   defines this task as a binary classification problem: detecting whether the input text is machine-
043   generated or human-written. However, this binary approach often ignores fine-grained categories
044   of MGT, such as machine-paraphrased or machine-translated text. In practical applications, these
045
      fine-grained categories are critical for defending against misinformation and understanding the user
      intentions of applying LLMs. As illustrated in Figure 1 (B), articles generated by machines based on
046
      basic prompts are more likely to contain misinformation (highlighted in pink) or be used for specific
047
      purposes (e.g., propaganda or monetization Zellers et al., 2019). In contrast, machine-translated
048   and paraphrased articles modify content based on human-written sources. Users may use LLMs
049   simply to correct grammatical errors in articles. Additionally, providing human-written articles as
050   input increases the cost for bad actors attempting to spread misinformation. While some recent stud-
051   ies (Krishna et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024) have attempted to detect machine-paraphrased text, most
052   still categorize these types as a single class, overlooking the fine-grained differences among these
053   MGT categories. A concurrent study, Abassy et al. (2024), attempts fine-grained MGT detection
      but addresses solely paraphrased text, ignoring machine-translated text.

082   This paper provides an in-depth study of fine-grained MGT detection. Our task classifies a given
083   article into four categories: human-written, machine-generated, machine-paraphrased, and machine-
084   translated. A straightforward method for this task is to modify the classification heads of existing
085   detectors (Solaiman et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2024) from binary classification to
086   multi-class classification. While this strategy allows us to adapt existing approaches with minimal
087   overhead, these detectors perform poorly on out-of-domain evaluation. As shown in prior work (He
088   et al., 2023; Mitchell et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a), these detectors experience a performance
089
      drop on out-of-domain data for binary classification, making fine-grained MGT detection even more
      challenging.
090
091   To solve the aforementioned problems, we propose RoBERTa-based Mixture of Detectors
092   (RoBERTa-MoD) to achieve more robust and generalized MGT detection. Our method employs
093   M detectors, each optimized for a different domain. A gating network is then applied to assign the
094   input article to the most appropriate detectors. With this approach, RoBERTa-MoD can effectively
095
      achieve fine-grained MGT detection across various text domains. Experimental results demonstrate
      that our method outperforms individual RoBERTa-based frameworks, model-averaging ensemble
096
      models, and traditional mixture-of-experts ensemble models.
097
098   In summary, our contributions are:
099   • We conduct an in-depth study on fine-grained MGT detection, which is important for identifying
100     misinformation in machine-generated content and understanding the purposes behind users’ use
101     of LLMs.
102   • We introduce a data preparation process to generate articles across different fine-grained cate-
103     gories, enabling the automatic creation of training and evaluation data for our task.
104   • We identify a key challenge in fine-grained MGT detection: performance degradation in out-of-
105     domain evaluation. To address this, we propose RoBERTa-MoD, combining detectors optimized
106     for different domains to develop a more robust and generalized detection system.
107
      • Our method is validated on six different datasets (GoodNews, VisualNews, WikiText, Essay, WP,
        and Reuters), achieving a 5∼9 average mAP improvement.

111   Since LLM-generated articles may contain misinformation (Lin et al., 2022; Zellers et al., 2019)
112   or be used for economic or propaganda purposes (Zhang et al., 2023a), detecting MGT has be-
113   come increasingly important. Existing methods (Solaiman et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2023; Tian et al.,
114
      2024; Mitchell et al., 2023; Hans et al., 2024) typically approach this as a binary classification task,
      determining whether a segment of text is human-written or machine-generated. Metric-based meth-
115
      ods (Mitchell et al., 2023; Su et al., 2023; Bao et al., 2024; Hans et al., 2024) extract distinguishable
116
      features from the text using the target language models. E.g., Solaiman et al. (2019) apply log
117   probability, and Gehrmann et al. (2019) use the absolute rank of each token. More recently, meth-
118   ods (Mitchell et al., 2023; Su et al., 2023; Bao et al., 2024) have demonstrated that small changes
119   to MGT typically lower its log probability under the language model, a pattern not seen in human-
120   written text. Therefore, these methods introduce perturbations to the input text and measure the
121   resulting discrepancies. To improve the generalization ability of these detectors, Verma et al. (2024)
122   extract features from text using a series of language models and train a classifier to categorize these
123   features. Although these methods do not require additional databases for training, they cannot be
124   easily adapted to fine-grained MGT detection. Since fine-grained categories in MGT are also gener-
125
      ated by LLMs, theoretically, machine-translated and machine-paraphrased text would be classified
      as machine-generated text based on the statistical features extracted by these methods.
126
127   Model-based detectors (Solaiman et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2023; Bhattacharjee et al., 2023; Tian et al.,
128   2024; Zhang et al., 2024a) train classifiers on annotated corpora to directly classify input text, mak-
129   ing them effective for detecting text generated by black-box or unknown models. E.g., Solaiman
130   et al. (2019) finetuned the RoBERTa model (Liu et al., 2019) using outputs from the GPT series.
131
      Guo et al. (2023) developed a method to identify ChatGPT-generated text with the HC dataset (Guo
      et al., 2023). Tian et al. (2024) trained a detector on different scales of text, enhancing the detec-
132
      tor’s performance on shorter texts. Recently, some studies (Krishna et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024;
133
      Nguyen-Son et al., 2021) have recognized the importance of detecting other categories of MGT,
134   including machine-paraphrased and machine-translated text. For example, Krishna et al. (2024) en-
135   hanced machine-paraphrased text detection using retrieval methods, and Li et al. (2024) identified
136   paraphrased sentences through the content information in articles. Nguyen-Son et al. (2021) applied
137   round-trip translation to detect Google-translated text. A concurrent study (Abassy et al., 2024) at-
138   tempted to achieve fine-grained MGT detection. However, it mainly addressed machine-paraphrased
139   text and completely ignored machine-translated text. In our work, we manage to distinguish both
140   machine-paraphrased and machine-translated from MGT. We first modify the classification head
141   of RoBERTa, achieving fine-grained classification on human-written, machine-generated, machine-
142
      paraphrased, and machine-translated texts. We further introduce the mixture of detectors to enhance
      model performance in out-of-domain evaluations, where previous methods have struggled.

146   3   RO BERTA -M O D: RO BERTA - BASED M IXTURE OF D ETECTORS
147
148
      Given an article x, MGT detection uses a binary label y ∈ {±1} to classify x as either human-
149
      written or machine-generated. To provide a more precise indication of the article’s source, our
150   task further divides MGT into machine-generated, machine-paraphrased, and machine-translated
151   categories, extending MGT detection into a four-class problem where y ∈ {−2, −1, 1, 2}.
152
153
      A straightforward approach is to adapt existing model-based methods (Mitchell et al., 2023; Guo
      et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2024) by modifying their classification heads from binary to multi-class la-
154
      bels. The primary challenge here is that these models are often trained on specific datasets, leading to
155
      decreased performance in out-of-domain evaluations, especially for the more complex fine-grained
156   MGT classification. On the other hand, while metric-based methods (Mitchell et al., 2023; Su et al.,
157   2023; Hans et al., 2024) do not require training on specific data, they typically rely on extracting
158   features from the target LLM and classifying based on predefined thresholds. This approach is
159   not applicable to fine-grained MGT detection since machine-paraphrased and machine-translated
160   texts also contain the statistical characteristics of the target LLM. To address the challenge of out-
161   of-domain evaluation, we propose using mixture models to achieve more generalized and robust
      performance.

169   3.1      DATA P REPARATION : A RTICLE G ENERATION
170
171   As discussed in the Introduction, LLM-generated articles can either be directly produced from basic
172   prompts or be paraphrased or translated based on human-written content. To prepare such data,
173   we generate different MGT categories using article datasets. For the machine-generated category,
174
      we provide only the title as the prompt to LLMs, for example: “Write an article on the following
      title, ensuring that the article consists of approximately z sentences,” where z represents the number
175
      of sentences in the original article. This ensures that articles of different categories are of similar
176
      length, preventing the detector from using length as a classification feature.
177
178   For machine-paraphrased and machine-translated articles, we input the entire human-written article
179   as the prompt: “Paraphrase/Translate the following article: x.” For the translation task, we employed
180
      a round-trip translation strategy involving four languages: Chinese, Spanish, Russian, and French.
      We provide a specific example in Appendix C. The language models used include Llama-3 (Touvron
181
      et al., 2023), Qwen-1.5 (Bai et al., 2023), StableLM-2 (Bellagente et al., 2024), ChatGLM-3 (Du
182
      et al., 2022), and Qwen-2.5 (Yang et al., 2024)1 . Llama-3 and Qwen-1.5 are in-domain generators
183   for training the detector, and StableLM-2, ChatGLM-3, and Qwen-2.5 are out-of-domain generators
184   to evaluate the model’s generalization ability.
185
186
      To prevent the model from leaking information about the article’s category (e.g., Llama-3 often
      responds with “Here is the polished version:”), we use the text starting from the second paragraph
187
      as input to the detector.

258   In particular, each labeled data is represented by (x, y), where input x = (x(1) , x(2) , . . . , x(P ) ) ∈

262   other.

268                      we assume there exists absolute constants C1 , C2 such that almost surely 0 < C1 ≤ α, γ ≤
269                      C2 .
                       • Generate x as a collection of P patches: x = (x(1) , x(2) , . . . , x(P ) ) ∈ Rd×P , where

304   4.1   DATASETS & M ETRICS
305
306   News Datasets. The news datasets in our study include GoodNews (Biten et al., 2019) and Visual-
307   News (Liu et al., 2021). GoodNews(Biten et al., 2019) provides URLs of New York Times articles
308
      from 2010 to 2018. After filtering out broken links and non-English articles, we randomly selected
      10,000 articles for training, with 2,000 articles each for validation and testing. VisualNews(Liu
309
      et al., 2021) comprises articles from four media sources: Guardian, BBC, USA Today, and Washing-
310
      ton Post. Similar to GoodNews, 2,000 articles were randomly chosen for evaluation sets.
311
312   WikiText (Stephen et al., 2017) collected 600 training articles, 60 validation articles, and 60 test
313   articles from Wikipedia. We utilize the test set for our evaluation.
314   GhostBuster (Verma et al., 2024) collected corpora for MGT detection from student essays (Es-
315   say), creative writing (WP), and news articles (Reuters). In our experiments, we adopt the training,
316   validation, and test sets provided by MGTBench (He et al., 2023), and detect texts generated by var-
317   ious LLMs, including ChatGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022), ChatGLM (Du et al., 2022), GPT4all (Anand
318   et al., 2023), Claude (Anthropic, 2024), and StableLM (Bellagente et al., 2024).
319   Metrics. Following DetectGPT (Mitchell et al., 2023), we use the Area Under the Receiver Op-
320   erating Characteristic curve (AUROC) to measure performance. We also employ mean Average

      4.2   BASELINES
345
346   OpenAI-D (Solaiman et al., 2019) is a detector trained on outputs from GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019)
347   series. OpenAI provides two versions: RoBERTa-base and RoBERTa-large. With fine-tuning and
348   early stopping, OpenAI-D can also be used to detect text generated by other LLMs.
349
      ChatGPT-D (Guo et al., 2023) is designed to identify text produced by ChatGPT-3.5 (Ouyang et al.,
350
      2022). It is trained using the HC3 (Guo et al., 2023) dataset, which includes 40,000 questions along
351
      with both human-written and ChatGPT-generated answers.
352
353   RoBERTa-MPU (Tian et al., 2024) builds upon RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019) by incorporating a
354   length-sensitive loss and a multi-scale text module, addressing the challenges of detecting short
355
      texts. Compared to OpenAI-D and ChatGPT-D, RoBERTa-MPU improves the detection for shorter
      texts without compromising performance on longer texts.
356
357   LLM-DetectAIve (Abassy et al., 2024) distinguishes between machine-generated, machine-
358   paraphrased, and human-written text by fine-tuning RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019) and DeBERTa (He
359   et al., 2021) models. For consistency with other baselines, we apply the RoBERTa backbone of
360   LLM-DetectAIve in our experiments.
361   Binoculars (Hans et al., 2024) identifies MGT by comparing the perplexity scores of two pre-trained
362   language models (cross-perplexity), enabling zero-shot detection. Since metrics-based methods
363   classify input text by extracting distinguishable features (e.g., perplexity, absolute rank) from pre-
364   defined LLMs, they are not directly applicable to fine-grained MGT detection. This is because
365   machine-paraphrased and machine-translated texts would still be categorized as machine-generated
366   based on the features in LLMs. Therefore, we only apply this baseline to the traditional MGT
367
      detection task.

      5    L IMITATIONS
512
513
      In this paper, we highlight the importance of fine-grained MGT classification and identify out-of-
514
      domain evaluations (e.g., out-of-domain generators and zero-shot articles) as a primary challenge for
515   this task. We introduced RoBERTa-MoD to improve the performance of existing detectors. Despite
516   improvements across various datasets, our method still has several limitations.
517
518
      First, out-of-domain evaluations remain a challenge for further improvement. As shown in our
      experiments, the detectors’ performance on out-of-domain generators (StableLM-2, ChatGLM-3,
519
      Qwen-2.5) is still lower than that on in-domain generators (Llama-3, Qwen-1.5). Performance in
520
      zero-shot experiments (VisualNews and WikiText) is also lower compared to GoodNews.
521
522   Short text detection is another issue that could be addressed in future work. In this paper, we set the
523   maximum token length to 128 and achieved reasonable results. However, in our experiments, when
524
      the maximum token length is reduced to 32 or lower, almost all models, including RoBERTa-MPU,
      which is specifically designed for short text detection, perform close to random guessing. Therefore,
525
      addressing the short text detection problem in fine-grained MGT detection is a potential direction
526
      for future research.

529   6    C ONCLUSION
530
531   We conduct an in-depth study of fine-grained MGT detection, aiming to further distinguish be-
532   tween machine-paraphrased and machine-translated text from MGT. We identify a key challenge
533
      in fine-grained MGT detection as improving the model’s generalization ability. I.e., model-based
      detectors typically perform well on in-domain data, however, their performance declines when deal-
534
      ing with different domains, especially out-of-domain data. To address this challenge, we introduce
535
      RoBERTa-MoD, which consists of multiple detectors optimized for different domains, achieving
536   more robust and generalized results in multi-domain evaluations. Our method is evaluated on six
537   datasets (GoodNews, VisualNews, WikiText, Essay, WP, and Reuters), achieving a 5–9% improve-
538   ment in average mAP compared to baselines. The improvements across various datasets and gener-
539   ators demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach in fine-grained MGT detection.

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      In our study, we introduce RoBERTa-MoD to enable fine-grained classification of MGT, which can
543
      help prevent the spread of misinformation and identifying the intent behind users’ use of LLMs.
544   However, like other methods designed for MGT detection, our system cannot guarantee 100% ac-
545   curacy, especially in the more challenging fine-grained detection task. While the proposed MoD
546   strategy improves performance in out-of-domain generators and zero-shot evaluations, challenges
547   remain in identifying specific fine-grained categories (as discussed in Section 4.3). Therefore, we
548   strongly discourage the use of our methods without human supervision (e.g., in plagiarism detection
549   or similar scenarios). A more appropriate application of RoBERTa-MoD would be in defending
550   against LLM-generated misinformation under human supervision. Through this paper, we aim to
551   highlight the importance of fine-grained MGT detectors for better distinguishing articles containing
552
      misinformation and fact-based articles polished by LLMs.
