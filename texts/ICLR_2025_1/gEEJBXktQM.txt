009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Time series data, crucial for decision-making in fields like finance and healthcare,
012            often presents challenges due to its inherent complexity, exacerbating the bias-
013            variance tradeoff and leading to overfitting and underfitting in conventional fore-
014            casting models. While promising, state-of-the-art models like PatchTST, iTrans-
015            former, and DLinear are hindered by this tradeoff, limiting their ability to separate
016            predictable patterns from noise. To resolve this, we propose the IDEAS frame-
017            work, which reduces the bias-variance tradeoff to help models achieve optimal
018
               performance. IDEAS combines iterative residual decomposition, which reduces
               bias by extracting predictable patterns, and separable training, which reduces vari-
019
               ance by independently optimizing each component. We provide theoretical proof
020
               and demonstrate through experiments that IDEAS significantly improves perfor-
021            mance across four state-of-the-art models on nine complex benchmark datasets,
022            offering a more robust solution for complex time series forecasting.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Time series data plays an essential role in various fields such as weather forecasting, medical di-
028   agnosis, and traffic prediction. It is widely used to solve numerous real-world problems and has a
029   significant impact on our daily lives (Esling & Agon, 2012; Shumway et al., 2000). Due to the im-
030   portance and necessity of time series research, numerous studies have been proposed in recent years
031   for time series forecasting. In particular, unlike conventional prediction-focused studies, recent re-
032   search has aimed to analyze and predict time series data from various perspectives. For instance,
033
      PatchTST (Nie et al., 2022) and iTransformer (Liu et al., 2023) focus on segmenting time series
      data along the temporal axis or emphasize variable-centered learning. On the other hand, models
034
      like DLinear (Zeng et al., 2023) and TimeMixer (Wang et al., 2024) aim to enhance learning by
035
      decomposing time series data into individual components.
036
037   Despite these advancements, time series data remains particularly challenging due to its inherent
038   complexity, which is distinct from other types of sequential data like language or video. Time series
039
      data consists of multiple intertwined components, including long-term trends, seasonal patterns, and
      cycles, as well as unpredictable noise that often complicates the learning process. Unlike other types
040
      of data, these components must be separated to accurately model the underlying patterns. This mix-
041
      ture of predictable and unpredictable patterns often leads to a bias-variance tradeoff, where models
042   either overfit to noise or underfit by failing to capture meaningful patterns (Ramasubramanian, 2007;
043   Chen et al., 2014). Conventional forecasting models attempt to learn all components of time series
044   data—both meaningful patterns and random noise—simultaneously. This approach often leads to
045   overfitting, where models capture noise as if it were signal, or underfitting, missing important pat-
046   terns (cf.Figure. 1). As a result, the models fail to generalize well on new data. Noise embedded
047   within the data exacerbates the problem, causing overfitting when models fail to effectively dis-
048   tinguish between noise and meaningful patterns (Ying, 2019). Even when noise is excluded, the
049   simultaneous learning of multiple components can still result in sub-optimal learning due to the
050
      model’s inability to focus on each component’s unique characteristics. These limitations empha-
      size the need for a more fundamental solution that directly addresses the complexity and inherent
051
      bias-variance tradeoff in time series data, rather than just optimizing model performance.
052
053   To address the challenges of the bias-variance tradeoff in time series forecasting, we propose a novel
      and effective framework called Iterative residual DEcomposition And Seperable training (IDEAS).

      The first component, iterative residual decomposition, is specifically designed as an unbiased esti-

082   forecasting. The second component, separable training, directly addresses variance by training each
083   decomposed component independently and distinctly. This independent training strategy effectively
084   minimizes the overall impact of residual noise during the training process, further reducing the risk
085   of potential overfitting and significantly enhancing the model’s overall generalization ability.
086   By combining these two components, our approach achieves an optimal balance of the bias-variance
087   tradeoff, resulting in more accurate and reliable predictions, even with complex and noisy datasets.
088   Moreover, the IDEAS framework is highly adaptable and can be integrated with various existing
089   time series decomposition methods and forecasting models, making it applicable across a wide
090   range of fields, including finance, meteorology, and healthcare. Our main contributions of IDEAS
091   are as follows:

111   2.1   B IAS - VARIANCE TRADEOFF IN TIME SERIES FORECASTING MODEL
112
113   In time series forecasting, the bias-variance tradeoff is a fundamental challenge, especially with the
114   increasing complexity of datasets. Models with high bias tend to underfit, missing critical patterns
115   in the data, while models with high variance overfit, capturing noise along with the true signal. This
116   tradeoff becomes even more pronounced in time series data due to temporal dependencies, noise,
117
      and dynamic patterns (Geman et al., 1992; Baek & Kim, 2018). As the data’s complexity grows,
      the difficulty of finding a model that appropriately balances bias and variance increases, making it
118
      crucial to develop advanced techniques to handle these challenges.
119
120   Although recent approaches such as Transformer-based models have reduced overfitting to some ex-
121   tent, they often fail to balance the bias-variance tradeoff in more complex or noisy datasets. To miti-
122   gate this, our proposed method integrates the iterative residual decomposition and separable training.
123
      By isolating noise and training components separately, we aim to minimize overfitting while ensur-
      ing the model captures essential time-dependent patterns, thus addressing the bias-variance tradeoff
124
      more effectively. This balanced approach not only enhances model performance but also ensures
125
      that predictions are more reliable, even in the presence of intricate temporal patterns.
126

      2.2   T IME SERIES DECOMPOSITION
129
130
      Conventional time series decomposition methods, like STL or STR (Wen et al., 2019), break down
131
      data into trend, seasonality, and residual components to simplify analysis and forecasting. While ef-
132   fective in capturing patterns, these methods struggle with the growing complexity and non-linearity
133   of modern datasets. As the scale and diversity of data increase, traditional decompositions may not
134   adapt well, leading to loss of critical information in the residuals. Residuals, often treated as noise,
135   can still contain valuable and predictive information. Research shows that residuals with autocorre-
136   lation or non-zero mean suggest models have missed capturing important underlying patterns(Dama
137   & Sinoquet, 2021; Mauricio, 2008), underscoring the urgent need for improved and more advanced
138   decomposition techniques. Therefore, enhancing decomposition methods is essential to extract more
139   meaningful and actionable insights from increasingly complex time series data.
140   To address these issues, we propose the iterative residual decomposition method, which iteratively
141   applies decomposition to residuals. This helps separate predictable patterns from noise, reducing
142   data complexity and improving forecasting accuracy where conventional methods fall short.
143
144
145   2.3   T IME SERIES FORECASTING MODELS
146
147   Recent models such as Autoformer, DLinear (Zeng et al., 2023), and TimeMixer (Wang et al., 2024)
148   employ decomposition techniques to capture underlying patterns in complex time series data. At the
149   same time, models like PatchTST (Nie et al., 2022) and iTransformer (Liu et al., 2023) emphasize
150   variable-centered learning and temporal segmentation to handle multivariate time series. These
151   approaches have led to significant improvements in managing different types of time series data,
152
      highlighting the importance of decomposition and segmentation in improving forecasting accuracy.
153   However, a persistent challenge is overfitting. Although these models successfully capture intri-
154   cate patterns, they often struggle to fully differentiate between meaningful signals and noise, which
155   diminishes their ability to generalize effectively in real-world scenarios. This issue is particularly
156   prevalent in datasets with high variability and noise, where models inadvertently learn irrelevant
157   fluctuations alongside actual patterns, ultimately degrading their performance.
158   To address these challenges, we propose a novel approach that combines iterative residual decompo-
159   sition with separable learning. Iterative residual decomposition progressively separates predictable
160   patterns from residual noise, systematically reducing bias in the bias-variance tradeoff. The combi-
161   nation of these techniques not only resolves the overfitting problem but also offers a scalable solution
      to manage the increased complexity and variability inherent in modern time series data.

233       3.2   I TERATIVE RESIDUAL DECOMPOSITION
234
235
          To effectively address the bias-variance tradeoff in time series forecasting, we propose an itera-
          tive residual decomposition method that iteratively extracts predictable patterns while reducing the
236
          impact of noise throughout the training process. This approach enhances the model’s ability to
237
          generalize by separating meaningful patterns from noise at each step, thereby reducing the risk of
238       overfitting or underfitting as demonstrated in Theorem 1 and Theorem 2.
239
240       Traditional decomposition methods, such as STL or STR (Dokumentov et al., 2015; Hyndman &
241
          Athanasopoulos, 2018), often leave residuals containing overlooked predictable patterns, which can
          still contribute to the bias-variance tradeoff. Our iterative residual decomposition method addresses
242
          this by repeatedly applying a decomposition process to extract these overlooked patterns, refining the
243
          residuals over multiple iterations. This systematic extraction ensures that residuals converge toward
244       white noise, functioning as an unbiased estimator in the limit of infinite iterations. Consequently,
245       our approach effectively separates predictable patterns from unpredictable patterns and mitigates the
246       inherent bias introduced when models fail to capture all relevant patterns during training, resulting
247       in improved forecasting accuracy.
248
          Figure 3 clearly demonstrates how the iterative decomposition progressively refines the residuals
249
          towards a Gaussian distribution, validating the effectiveness of our method. In the iterative residual
250       decomposition, the initial decomposition of the input time series data XIn follows standard methods
251       like STL or STR, which separate the time series into predictable components P (1) (e.g., trend,
252       seasonality) and unpredictable residuals U (1) (e.g., noise):
253
254                                                U (1) = XIn − P (1) ,                                          (1)
255
256       At each subsequent step, the residual U (N −1) is further decomposed to extract any remaining pre-
257       dictable patterns P (N ) , progressively refining the residuals:
258
259                                             U (N ) = U (N −1) − P (N ) ,                                      (2)
260       where P (N ) represents the N -th set of predictable patterns extracted during the decomposition pro-
261       cess. For instance, in the first decomposition step, P (1) may capture large-scale patterns like trends,
262
          while in later steps, P (N ) captures more subtle predictable patterns, leaving the residuals U (N ) to
263       approach random noise as more predictable patterns are removed. As observed in Figure 3, the resid-
264       uals (blue bars) progressively become more similar to a normal distribution (red dashed line) over
265       successive iterations, confirming the iterative refinement of the residuals toward a more Gaussian-
266       like distribution. As the number of iterations N increases and approaches infinity (N → ∞), the
267       residuals U (N ) are expected to converge to pure white noise, characterized by zero mean, constant
268       variance, and no autocorrelation (cf. Figure 4). This convergence demonstrates that the iterative
269       residual decomposition method ultimately yields an unbiased estimation of the predictable compo-
          nents, effectively separating the signal from the noise in the time series data.

290   3.2.1      U NBIASEDNESS OF ITERATIVE RESIDUAL DECOMPOSITION
291
      We assume that residuals will eventually converge to noise if each iteration successfully removes
292
      predictable patterns, even in complex datasets with nonlinearity or non-stationarity. This assump-
293   tion is consistent with traditional methods like STR and STL, which are designed to handle non-
294   stationary time series, making it a mild assumption.
295
296
      To empirically validate this assumption, we propose using statistical tests such as the Shapiro-Wilk
      test for normality and autocorrelation tests (e.g., ACF or Durbin-Watson) to assess whether the
297
      residuals exhibit characteristics of noise after each iteration, as shown in Figure 4.
298
299   Theorem 1 (Unbiasedness of iterative residual decomposition). Let XIn be a non-stationary time
300   series. Assume Xin can be iteratively decomposed into predictable components P (i) and residual
301   components U (i) . As the number of iterations N → ∞, the expected value of the final residuals
302
      U (N ) will converge to white noise, indicating the iterative residual decomposition process yields an
      asymptotically unbiased estimator of the predictable components of XIn .
303
304
      Remark: In the limit as N → ∞, the iterative residual decomposition process is an unbiased
305   estimator of the predictable components of XIn . Practically, we observe that with a finite number
306   of iterations, U (N ) closely approximates white noise, demonstrating consistent evidence of near-
307   unbiasedness across diverse real-world applications and datasets.
308
309
      The iterative residual decomposition not only theoretically proves unbiasedness but also empirically
      validates this through real-world testing. The theoretical proof of Theorem 1 is provided in Appendix
310
      A. To complement this theoretical analysis, we empirically validate Theorem 1 by conducting ACF
311
      and Durbin-Watson tests across multiple iterations of the iterative residual decomposition. As shown
312   in Figure 4, the Durbin-Watson statistic (blue line) approaches the ideal value of 2.0 (red dashed
313   line), and the ACF statistic (orange line) converges towards 0.0 (pink dashed line) as the number
314   of iterations increases. This convergence indicates that the residuals become progressively closer to
315   white noise, supporting our theorem. More visualization of Figure 4 are in Appendix H.2.

      3.3.1    B ETTER BIAS - VARIANCE TRADEOFF
379
380   A significant advantage of separable training is its ability to effectively manage the bias-variance
381   tradeoff, a crucial factor in balancing model complexity and generalization. By training each com-
382   ponent independently, separable training reduces variance while maintaining low bias, resulting in
383   more accurate and generalized forecasting.
384   In conventional unified training, all components of the time series—trend, seasonality, and
385   noise—are optimized together. This introduces significant variance, as the model attempts to fit all
386   components at once, often leading to overfitting to noisy patterns and underfitting of subtle trends.
387
      By separating the training process, separable training eliminates the covariance between unpre-
388
      dictable and predictable patterns, leading to a reduced overall variance. This independent training
389   process ensures that each component is optimized more effectively, contributing to a more balanced
390   and accurate forecasting model. Mathematically, the variance in a conventional model can be ex-

      In this paper, we introduce IDEAS, a novel framework that enhances time series forecasting by ad-
534   dressing the bias-variance tradeoff through iterative residual decomposition and separable learning.
535   Our experiments demonstrate significant improvements on various benchmark datasets, particularly
536   on non-stationary data where existing methods struggle with overfitting or underfitting. By sep-
537   arating predictable patterns from noise to reduce bias and minimizing variance, IDEAS achieves
538   more accurate and robust forecasts, making it a promising solution for complex, noisy time series
539   data. Future work could explore integrating IDEAS with other techniques and applying it to broader
      domains where accurate forecasting is essential.

956   E    C OMPUTATIONAL TIME AND MODEL USAGE
957
958   This section compares the time per epoch and memory usage between the original model and the
959   proposed IDEAS framework during the training process (cf. Table 15 and Table E). The proposed
960   IDEAS applies separable training, where multiple stages of training are conducted. For instance, if
961
      the model undergoes 3 iterations, it learns 3 distinct predictable patterns separately. In this process,
      the time taken per epoch is similar to or shorter than that of the original model because the training
962
      data is processed in a more compact form than the raw data. Furthermore, since each predictable
963
      pattern becomes simpler for the model to learn, fewer epochs are required for convergence at each
964   stage. For example, if a model originally requires a total of 50 epochs for training, in separable
965   training, each stage may converge within 10 to 15 epochs. Thus, even with training across 3 stages,
966   only about 45 epochs in total are required, leading to a reduction in overall computation time.
