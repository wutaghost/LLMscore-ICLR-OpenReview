009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Machine learning holds immense promise in biology, particularly for the chal-
012            lenging task of identifying causal variants for Mendelian and complex traits. Two
013            primary approaches have emerged for this task: supervised sequence-to-function
014            models trained on functional genomics experimental data and self-supervised
015            DNA language models that learn evolutionary constraints on sequences. How-
016
               ever, the field currently lacks consistently curated datasets with accurate labels,
               especially for non-coding variants, that are necessary to comprehensively bench-
017
               mark these models and advance the field. In this work, we present TraitGym, a
018
               curated dataset of genetic variants that are either known to be causal or are strong
019            candidates across 113 Mendelian and 83 complex traits, along with carefully con-
020            structed control variants. We frame the causal variant prediction task as a bi-
021            nary classification problem and benchmark various models, including functional-
022            genomics-supervised models, self-supervised models, models that combine ma-
023            chine learning predictions with curated annotation features, and ensembles of
024            these. Our results provide insights into the capabilities and limitations of dif-
025            ferent approaches for predicting the functional consequences of genetic variants.
026            We find that alignment-based models CADD and GPN-MSA compare favor-
027
               ably for Mendelian traits and complex disease traits, while functional-genomics-
               supervised models Enformer and Borzoi perform better for complex non-disease
028
               traits. All curated benchmark data, together with training and benchmarking
029
               scripts, will be made publicly available upon publication.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Machine learning is increasingly transforming the fields of genomics, human genetics, and health-
035   care by offering new avenues for predicting the impact of genetic variants on phenotypes and by po-
036   tentially improving the accuracy of trait or disease risk predictions from individual human genomes.
037   A major challenge in these domains is determining which among millions of intercorrelated genetic
038   variants are causal for Mendelian and complex traits, including diseases. Tackling this challenge,
039
      which has profound implications for human health, requires robust and scalable methods that can
      decode the biological syntax of the human genome and how it drives molecular functions across
040
      different cells and tissues.
041
042   Three major classes of approaches have been developed to model DNA sequences and predict the ef-
043   fects of genetic variants. The first approach utilizes supervised machine learning models, commonly
044   referred to as sequence-to-function models, which are trained to predict genome-wide functional
045
      genomics experimental data from DNA sequences (Eraslan et al., 2019); we refer to these models
      as functional-genomics-supervised. These models predict the functional effects of specific variants
046
      by assessing how changes in the DNA sequence influence experimental outcomes. The second ap-
047
      proach involves self-supervised genomic language models (gLMs), such as masked or autoregressive
048   language models, which are trained only on DNA sequences from one or multiple species without
049   relying on experimental data (Benegas et al., 2024). Models that utilize sequences from multiple
050   species take advantage of evolutionary conservation to gain functional insights. Variant effects in
051   such models are assessed by comparing the log-likelihood between the alternative and reference al-
052   leles of the variant, as well as by quantifying changes in the latent representations. Another class of
053   methods includes integrative approaches, which combine machine learning predictions with curated
      annotation features to improve the accuracy of variant effect prediction (Schubach et al., 2024).

074   Despite its importance, the field currently lacks consistently processed and comprehensively curated
075   datasets of putative causal genetic variants with reliable labels. Furthermore, there is a pressing
076   need for establishing a common ground for systematically benchmarking state-of-the-art models
077   based on functional-genomics-supervised, self-supervised and integrative approaches, in order to
078
      help advance the field.
079   In this article, we present TraitGym, a curation of two benchmark datasets from human genetics:
080   one comprising causal variants for 113 Mendelian traits, and another consisting of strong causal
081   variant candidates across 83 complex traits, along with carefully constructed control sets matching
082   relevant summary statistics (such as minor allele frequencies, variant types, distances from transcrip-
083   tion start sites, and linkage disequilibrium scores) of putative causal variants. We frame the task as
084
      binary classification between putatively causal and non-causal variants, allowing to evaluate several
      state-of-the-art functional-genomics-supervised and self-supervised models, alongside integrative
085
      methods and their ensembles. We find that alignment-based integrative and self-supervised mod-
086
      els compare favorably for Mendelian traits and complex disease traits, while functional-genomics-
087   supervised models do better on complex non-disease traits. The classification of variants is substan-
088   tially harder for complex traits, but consistent improvement is observed by ensembling input and
089   predicted features from different models. Additionally, we introduce a new gLM trained specifi-
090   cally on regulatory regions and demonstrate that it compares favorably with other alignment-free
091   self-supervised language models.

      2   BACKGROUND
095
096
      One of the essential quests in biology is to understand the genotype-to-phenotype relationship (Fig-
      ure 1). The genotype is the genetic makeup of an organism, i.e., the set of DNA sequences com-
097
      posing each genome. The phenotype is the collection of observable traits of an individual, such as
098
      height or cholesterol levels. Phenotypic variance can be decomposed into components attributed
099   to genetic and environmental factors. The influence of non-coding genetic variants on phenotype
100   is mediated via the expression of genes in different tissues and cell types. Functional-genomics-
101   supervised models attempt to learn the relationship between DNA sequence and gene expression,
102   leveraging genome-wide experimental data (Eraslan et al., 2019). Natural selection closes the loop
103   by impacting which genotypes are favored over time, based on the fitness of the phenotype on a given
104   environment. Therefore, the space of observed DNA sequences contains rich information about the
105   underlying biology; this is precisely the signal leveraged by self-supervised DNA language models
106   (Benegas et al., 2024).
107   The are two classes of phenotypic traits: Mendelian and complex (Figure 2). Mendelian traits, such
      as hemophilia, can be strongly affected by a single mutation in a single gene. On the other hand,

122   3   R ELATED WORK
123
124   GeneticsGym (Finucane et al., 2024) evaluates the prediction of causal variants for human com-
125   plex traits, but limited to protein-coding variants. Dey et al. (2020) evaluate the prediction of non-
126   coding causal variants for human complex traits, but limited to a previous generation of functional-
127   genomics-supervised models. Concurrent work (Fabiha et al., 2024) also evaluates the prediction of
128   causal variants for complex traits, but does not cover self-supervised models nor Mendelian traits.
129
      Benegas et al. (2023a) evaluate the prediction of non-coding causal variants for human Mendelian
      traits, but with a much larger, non-subsampled negative set of 2.6 million variants, which makes it
130
      less practical to evaluate some of the latest, computationally expensive models.
131
132   Tang et al. (2024) benchmark the ability of functional-genomics-supervised and self-supervised
133   models to predict non-coding variant effects on gene expression, but they cover neither Mendelian
134   nor complex traits. BEND (Marin et al., 2024) and GV-Rep (Li et al., 2024) evaluate self-supervised
135
      models for the prediction of disease-associated variants from ClinVar (Landrum et al., 2020). While
      not documented, it is likely that these variants mostly cover Mendelian rather than complex diseases.
136
      Furthermore, expert-reviewed pathogenic variants in ClinVar are highly skewed towards coding and
137
      splice region variants, containing only a single promoter variant and no intergenic variants (Ta-
138   ble A.7). Neither of these benchmarks establishes adequate baselines for this task. BEND includes
139   a single early-generation functional-genomics-supervised model (Zhou & Troyanskaya, 2015), but
140   does not include any conservation-based model, which are usually strong for this task (Benegas
141   et al., 2023a). GV-Rep does not include any baseline.
142
      Thus, TraitGym is the only benchmark of causal non-coding variant prediction for both Mendelian
143
      and complex human traits. Furthermore, it is the only available framework to evaluate both the latest
144   functional-genomics-supervised and self-supervised models, as well as strong non-neural baselines.

149   TraitGym consists of two curated datasets of non-coding genetic variants affecting Mendelian and
150   complex traits (Table 1). We focus on non-coding variants since understanding their impact is a
151   particularly important use case for DNA sequence models, compared to coding variants which are
152   more commonly interpreted using protein sequence models. Further, we focus on single-nucleotide
153
      variants, the most common form of genetic variation, which is still challenging to interpret. Our data
      curation process is outlined in Figure 3 and additional details are provided in Appendix A.1.

      Mendelian traits. Curated causal non-coding variants for 113 Mendelian diseases were collected
184
      from Online Mendelian Inheritance in Man, OMIM (Smedley et al., 2016). For additional strin-
185   gency, we filtered out a small percentage of variants with minor allele frequency (MAF) greater
186   than 0.1% in the Genome Aggregation Database, gnomAD (Chen et al., 2024). We used gnomAD
187   common variants (MAF > 5%) as controls.
188
189   Complex traits. Putative causal and control non-coding variants for 83 complex traits were obtained
190   by processing statistical fine-mapping results (Kanai et al., 2021) from association studies in the
191   UK BioBank data (Bycroft et al., 2018). Specifically, we used variants with posterior inclusion
192   probability (PIP) in the credible set greater than 0.9 in any trait as positives and variants with PIP
193
      < 0.01 in all traits as controls. We additionally filtered the positive set to genome-wide significant
      variants (p < 5 × 10−8 ).
194
195
      Variant type (or consequence) annotation. We annotated the consequence (e.g., intergenic, in-
196
      tronic, 5′ UTR, 3′ UTR, etc.) of each variant using Ensembl (McLaren et al., 2016), and refined this
197   annotation by overlapping with candidate cis-regulatory elements from ENCODE (Epstein et al.,
198   2020). Distal non-exonic variants (potential enhancers) comprise a small proportion (10%) in the
199   Mendelian traits dataset but the vast majority (76%) in the complex traits dataset (Figure 4).
200
201   Matching positives and negatives. For each putative causal non-coding variant, we sampled 9 non-
202   coding variants from the control set, matching chromosome, consequence, and distance to transcrip-
203   tion start site (TSS). For complex traits, we additionally matched MAF and linkage disequilibrium
204   (LD) score (Bulik-Sullivan et al., 2015) in the UK BioBank. We sampled only 9 controls per positive
205   variant in order to be able to evaluate even the most computationally demanding models. However,
206
      we also provide a larger version of the dataset with millions of negative controls per positive variant,
      for which we evaluate a subset of the models. This expanded version of the dataset for Mendelian
207
      traits does not require any subsampling of negatives, but for complex traits we do subsample to
208
      match the MAF distribution (Finucane et al., 2024), while still keeping millions of variants.
209
210   Task definition. The task is to classify whether a variant is putatively causal for any trait or not.
211   The input data consist of the reference and alternate allele together with the DNA sequence context.
212   As evaluation metric, we calculate the area under the precision recall curve (AUPRC) for each
213   chromosome (for a model trained on the remaining chromosomes), and then compute a weighted
214   average across chromosomes based on sample size, together with a standard error estimated via
215   bootstrapping (described in Appendix A.2.4). The baseline AUPRC is 0.1, which is the proportion
      of positives.

250   We benchmark functional-genomics-supervised models, self-supervised gLMs and integrative mod-
251   els (Table 2). We introduce a new gLM, called gLM-Promoter, trained using the genomes of 434
252   animal species, following the training objective of GPN (Benegas et al., 2023b) and the ByteNet
253   convolutional architecture (Kalchbrenner et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2024). It is only trained on pro-
254   moters as an attempt to focus on regulatory regions (we would have liked to train on enhancers as
255
      well but no annotation exists for non-model organisms). Additional details on models are provided
      in Appendix A.2.
256
257   We evaluate zero-shot model scores as well as ridge logistic regression classifiers (linear probing)
258   trained using extracted features (Table 3). We use a number of folds equal to the number of chro-
259   mosomes. In each fold, we test on a single chromosome using a model trained on the remaining
260   chromosomes, and the regularization hyperparameter is chosen based on cross-validation on the
261
      training chromosomes (detailed in Appendix A.2.4).
262
      Functional-genomics-supervised models. Sequence-to-function models predict activity in thou-
263
      sands of different functional genomic tracks, covering different assays, such as gene expression or
264   chromatin accessibility, in different tissues and cell types. As variant effect prediction features, we
265   calculate the norm (across spatial positions) of the predicted log-fold-change in activity between the
266   reference and the alternate sequence, for each separate track (referred to as “ℓ2 score” in Linder et al.
267   (2023)). As zero-shot score, we aggregate the ℓ2 scores of different tracks by taking their ℓ2 norm
268   (“ℓ2 of ℓ2 scores”). Sei (Chen et al., 2022) adopts a different variant scoring approach; it maps each
269   sequence into discrete classes, such as promoters or brain-specific enhancers, and scores a variant
      according to how much it impacts the relative scores of different classes.

270
      Self-supervised models. For self-supervised gLMs, a popular zero-shot score is the log-likelihood
271   ratio (LLR) between the alternate and reference allele1 , which has been shown to reflect learned
272   functional constraints, such as transcription factor binding sites (Benegas et al., 2023b). Good re-
273   sults have also been obtained comparing the embeddings of the alternate and reference alleles (Dalla-
274   Torre et al., 2023; Mendoza-Revilla et al., 2024). We evaluate these different scoring approaches for
275   each model (Table A.8) and choose the best performing one when benchmarking against other mod-
276   els (Table A.9). We additionally obtain a high-dimensional featurization of a variant by calculating
277   the inner product (across genomic positions in a given window) between contextual embeddings of
278   the alternate and reference sequences, for each hidden dimension separately.
279
      Integrative models. CADD (Schubach et al., 2024) is built on top of a broad set of curated an-
280
      notations, including conservation, biochemical activity, population-level data as well as predictions
281   from several machine learning models. Utilizing this rich set of input features, CADD is a logistic
282   regression model trained to distinguish proxy-deleterious from proxy-neutral variants. The output
283   of the model is called the CADD score, which we use as zero-shot score. In this paper, we also train
284   our own models using the broad set of CADD input features, which we refer to as CADD features
285   even though they are the input, not the output, of CADD.

367   Model ensembling. Given the good performance obtained by different classes of models, poten-
368   tially leveraging different signals, we evaluated linear probing of combined features extracted from
369
      representative models from each class: Borzoi (predicted tracks), GPN-MSA (latent embeddings
      and LLR) and CADD (input features to the model together with the single output score). The results
370
      are summarized in Figure 6. On the complex traits dataset, ensembling the three models achieves
371
      the best performance, with a particularly high jump when combining Borzoi with either of CADD
372   or GPN-MSA. On the Mendelian traits dataset, on the other hand, ensembling the full features from
373   different models does not improve upon CADD input features. We attribute this to the fact that (i)
374   the room for improvement is relatively small and (ii) the dataset is small, making it easier to overfit
375   when using high-dimensional features. We refer to the last approach as “full” feature ensembling.
376   However, we do see small improvements when ensembling CADD with a reduced number of fea-
377   tures from other models (LLR for GPN-MSA and “ℓ2 of ℓ2 scores” for Borzoi), which we refer to
      as “lightweight” feature ensembling (Figure 7).

      7   D ISCUSSION
517
518
519
      Conclusion. TraitGym allows to benchmark DNA sequence models on the challenging task of pre-
      dicting causal variants in human genetics. Alignment-based, conservation-aware models compare
520
      favorably on Mendelian traits and complex disease traits, while functional-genomics-supervised
521
      models achieve the best performance on complex non-disease traits. A reason for hope in the par-
522   ticularly challenging complex traits dataset is that ensembling predictions and input features from
523   different models yields consistent improvements. We find that alignment-free gLMs are not com-
524   petitive on causal variant prediction. The best performing model among them—gLM-Promoter,
525   developed in this work—is not the largest gLM, nor does it have a long context. However, one of its
526   defining characteristics is that it was trained only on functional regions; this suggests that, as previ-
527   ously proposed (Tang et al., 2024; Benegas et al., 2024), data curation may warrant more research
528   than architectures. We leave this as promising future work.
529
      Limitations and future extensions. The major limitation for benchmarking causal variant predic-
530
      tion for human traits is that the number of known causal variants is small, especially for non-coding
531   regions. In the long term, we expect the number of known causal variants to increase as experimen-
532   tal and statistical techniques improve, together with larger and more diverse patient cohorts. In the
533   short term, we hope to expand the dataset to include variants from other cohorts such as FinnGen
534   (Kurki et al., 2023) and BioBank Japan (Nagai et al., 2017). One of the challenges is that, while
535   many fine-mapping results are publicly available, it is still hard to get access to other quantities such
536   as LD scores, which are important for constructing a rigorous control set.

      A.2.4   L INEAR PROBING
956
957   We train a ridge logistic regression classifier pipeline using scikit-learn (Pedregosa et al., 2011),
958   using default arguments as much as possible (Listing 1). The pipeline starts with imputation (only
959   relevant for CADD input features) and standardization. To choose the regularization hyperparam-
960   eter, we do a grid search using group K-fold cross-validation, with the groups consisting of the
961   training chromosomes. We use the default number (10)of grid points, but shift the range to allow for
962
      heavier regularization given that our regression setting is very high-dimensional.
963   We repeat the entire pipeline training on all but one chromosome and predicting on the held-out
964   chromosome. At the end we obtain predictions for all chromosomes, but each from a separate logis-
965   tic regression model. Therefore, instead of calculating a global AUPRC, we calculate the AUPRC
966   within each chromosome, and then perform a weighted average based on sample size. To obtain a
967   standard error, we calculate the standard deviation of the distribution of weighted means performed
968
      on 1000 bootstrap samples of chromosomes. To allow easy comparison, we also use the weighted
      average AUPRC to evaluate zero-shot scores, even though it is not strictly necessary.
969
970   We only evaluate zero-shot scores on the full version of the datasets. We obtain standard errors from
971   100 bootstrap samples within the positive and negative sets, in order to maintain the proportion of
      positives.
