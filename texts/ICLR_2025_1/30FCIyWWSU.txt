011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013             This paper presents Deformable Neural Vessel Representations (DeNVeR), an
014             unsupervised approach for vessel segmentation in X-ray angiography videos with-
015             out annotated ground truth. DeNVeR utilizes optical flow and layer separation
016
                techniques, enhancing segmentation accuracy and adaptability through test-time
                training. Key contributions include a novel layer separation bootstrapping tech-
017
                nique, a parallel vessel motion loss, and the integration of Eulerian motion fields
018
                for modeling complex vessel dynamics. A significant component of this research
019             is the introduction of the XACV dataset, the first X-ray angiography coronary
020             video dataset with high-quality, manually labeled segmentation ground truth. Ex-
021             tensive evaluations on both XACV and CADICA datasets demonstrate that DeN-
022             VeR outperforms current state-of-the-art methods in vessel segmentation accuracy
023             and generalization capability while maintaining temporal coherency. This work
024             advances medical imaging by providing a robust, data-efficient tool for vessel
025             segmentation. It sets a new standard for video-based vessel segmentation research,
026             offering greater flexibility and potential for clinical applications.

      visually inspecting X-ray angiography (XRA) images. By injecting contrast agents into the coronary
055   vessels and capturing the flow in the vessel structure on video, X-ray coronary angiography (XCA) is
056   a common medical imaging method that exposes patients to less ionizing radiation and provides clear
057   boundaries of the coronary arteries.
058
      Accurate vessel segmentation remains challenging due to XCA’s inherent limitations, which can
059
      obscure the severity of stenosis (Toth et al., 2014). These limitations include low signal-to-noise
060
      ratios, minimal radiation contrast (Felfelian et al., 2016), and interference from surrounding structures
061   like catheters and bones (Maglaveras et al., 2001). The complexity of interpreting 2D projections
062   of 3D vessels further complicates the task. Existing automatic angiographic vessel segmentation
063   algorithms have several drawbacks. They often require professional user input and supervision to
064   identify corresponding vessels or features in all input images (Iyer et al., 2023). The time-consuming
065   annotation and knowledge-based training processes make it challenging to adopt these methods in
066   practical settings. Models that take a single image as input discard critical information from the
067   original XCA video and show reduced adaptability and compatibility when the imaging system
068   changes or is unknown. In addition to the characteristics of X-ray angiography, involuntary organ
069
      motions and overlapping structures contribute to an increased ratio of ghosting artifacts (Liu et al.,
      2020a; Lin & Ching, 2005). These supervision, generalization, and dynamics issues significantly
070
      limit the application of automatic angiographic segmentation algorithms (Figure 1).
071
072   To address these challenges, we introduce DeNVeR (Deformable Neural Vessel Representations),
073   an unsupervised approach for segmenting cardiac vessels in X-ray videos. Inspired by Deformable
074   Sprites and using optical flow, DeNVeR starts with traditional Hessian-based filters to establish initial
075
      vessel masks as priors. It then uses a layered separation process to decompose the foreground vessel
      and background layers. Acknowledging the limitations of frame-by-frame processing, we enhance
076
      foreground-background segmentation through test-time optimization. This optimization incorporates
077
      neural representations of the Eulerian motion field (Holynski et al., 2021) and introduces a novel
078   parallel vessel motion loss, thereby improving segmentation fidelity. Our approach emphasizes
079   dynamic adaptation to cardiac movements and vessel flow, ensuring detailed, temporally consistent,
080   and unsupervised vessel segmentation in X-ray videos. Our experimental results show significant
081   performance improvements in predicting vessel regions compared to state-of-the-art models. The
082   main contributions are:
083
084          • DeNVeR uses unsupervised learning on X-ray video data, leveraging the full temporal
085            information of the videos and eliminating the need for annotated training datasets.
086          • Using optical flow and a unique layer separation strategy, DeNVeR enhances segmentation
087            accuracy and adjusts during test time, improving adaptability and ensuring consistent results
088            across cardiac conditions.
089          • We collect the first X-ray angiography coronary video dataset (XACV) with high-quality,
090            manually labeled segmentation ground truth, serving as a new standard for training and
091            evaluating video vessel segmentation models, making full use of video temporal information.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
095
096
      Traditional segmentation methods. Traditional object segmentation (Khan et al., 2020; Memari
      et al., 2019) requires heuristic human design rules or filters. Several methods are proposed, one
097
      of which designs the Hessian-based filter (Frangi et al., 1998) to enhance vessel filtering. Khan et
098
      al. (Khan et al., 2020) design retinal image denoising and enhancement of B-COSFIRE filters to
099   perform segmentation. Memari et al. (Memari et al., 2019) used contrast-limited adaptive histogram
100   equalization and designed filters to achieve the task. Another line of work proposed optimally
101   oriented flux (OOF) (Wang & Chung, 2020; Law & Chung, 2008), which performs better in adjacent
102   curvilinear object segmentation. These human strategy design methods do not need any training,
103   which gives them the advantage of fast segmentation of new data. However, these methods are often
104   confined to certain datasets and loss of generalize ability.
105
106   Supervised and self-supervised segmentation. In the domain of the supervised segmentation
107   method (Soomro et al., 2019), Esfahani et al. (Nasr-Esfahani et al., 2016) design a Top-Hat transfor-
      mation and Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for segmentation. Khowaja et al. (Khowaja et al.,


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2019) applied bidirectional histogram equalization. Another work (Yang et al., 2018; Revaud et al.,
109   2016) utilizes image masking to reduce artifacts, which relies on paired mask datasets. The most
110   popular vessel segmentation backbone recently is U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015). These methods
111   (Fan et al., 2019; Soomro et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2019a) also require extensive and time-consuming
112   human annotation, further limiting the application. Consequently, self-supervised learning methods
113   are designed to elevate performance with large-scale unsupervised data. Some self-supervised learn-
114   ing research focuses on image painting (Pathak et al., 2016), image colorization (Larsson et al., 2017),
115   and others (Doersch et al., 2015; Noroozi & Favaro, 2016; Ledig et al., 2017; Ren & Lee, 2018; Misra
116   et al., 2016; Xu et al., 2019; Benaim et al., 2020; Doersch et al., 2015; Pathak et al., 2016; Gidaris
117
      et al., 2018; Misra & Maaten, 2020; Ma et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021; Bar et al., 2022; Park et al.,
      2020; Wu et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Alonso et al., 2021; Zhong et al., 2021). Ma et al. (Ma
118
      et al., 2021) and Kim et al. (Kim et al., 2023) proposed vessel segmentation methods with adversarial
119
      learning. Unlike these supervised and self-supervised methods requiring extensive annotations, our
120   DeNVeR uses an unsupervised approach, training directly on test videos. It leverages optical flow
121   and layer separation, enhancing accuracy and adaptability through test-time training. DeNVeR also
122   utilizes temporal information, producing more coherent results than single-frame methods.
123
124   Unsupervised segmentation methods. Unsupervised segmentation methods fall into two cate-
125   gories: clustering-based and adversarial. Clustering-based approaches (Ji et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021;
126   Do et al., 2021) like Invariant Information Clustering (IIC) by Xu et al. (Ji et al., 2019) inputs into
127   clusters but struggle with curvilinear objects. Adversarial methods (Chen et al., 2019; Abdal et al.,
128   2021), exemplified by Redo (Abdal et al., 2021), generate object masks by guiding generators with
129   inputs to redraw objects in new colors.
130
131
      Video segmentation methods. Coronary artery segmentation based on sequential images such as
      SVS-Net (Hao et al., 2020) use an encoder-decoder deep network architecture that utilizes multiple
132
      contextual frames of 2D and sequential images to segment 2D vessel masks. However, these
133
      supervised methods often suffer from domain gaps between datasets and cannot generalize well. Our
134   work advances video decomposition into layers, originally proposed by Wang & Adelson (Wang
135   & Adelson, 1994) in the 1990s, by incorporating neural network techniques (Liu et al., 2020b;
136   2021). Unlike traditional methods (Black & Anandan, 1991; Jojic & Frey, 2001; Ost et al., 2021;
137   Shi & Malik, 1998; Brox & Malik, 2010), we operate unsupervised, learning deformable canonical
138   layers to model vessel motion more effectively. Additionally, while previous research such as Yang
139   et al. (2019b) and Ye et al. (2022) focused on general unsupervised video segmentation, we extend
140   these concepts to the specific domain of vessel segmentation. We address motion segmentation by
141   associating pixels with Eulerian motion (Holynski et al., 2021) clusters, adapting and extending
142
      this approach to unsupervised video vessel segmentation. Our method, DeNVeR, separates X-ray
      video into canonical foreground and background, per-frame masks, and dynamic transformations for
143
      realistic vessel motion representation optimized through specific loss functions.

148   We propose an unsupervised algorithm for Cardiac Vessel Segmentation in X-ray videos using
149   optical flow and test-time optimization. Our approach involves: coarse vascular region extraction
150   (Section 3.1), layer separation using implicit neural representation (Section 3.2), background flow es-
151
      timation and foreground optimization (Section 3.2), and application of specific loss and regularization
      terms (Section 3.4). This method addresses temporal consistency issues and enables segmentation
152
      without training data.

      3.3        T EST-T IME T RAINING FOR V ESSEL D ECOMPOSITION
209
210   Our work introduces test-time training as a key feature of our unsupervised segmentation method,
211   DeNVeR. This approach adapts the model directly to test data during inference without labeled
212   training data. Using the test video’s inherent structure and patterns, the model refines its parameters,
213   allowing it to tailor its learning to each video’s unique characteristics. This capability is particularly
214   valuable in medical imaging, where patient variability is high and personalized diagnostics are crucial.
215   Test-time training allows DeNVeR to adjust dynamically to new, unseen cases, significantly improving
      diagnostic accuracy and effectiveness.

237

242   and temporal B-spline to model the entire motion trajectory.

316

363   4.3   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
364
      In this paper, we implement the entire deep learning architecture using PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019)
365
      and train it with Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2015) on a single NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090
366   GPU. The entire testing process, including model training and inference, takes approximately 20
367   minutes and utilizes 18GB of RAM. In the preprocessing stage, we compute the optical flow using
368   RAFT (Teed & Deng, 2020).
369
      Masks obtained from preprocessing are typically discontinuous and noisy. Therefore, we utilize deep
370
      learning methods for training. To simplify the task of vessel segmentation, we divide it into two
371
      stages. In stage 1, we use MLPs to acquire the background canonical image, with λlimit = 0.02 and
372   λsmooth = 0.02. In stage 2, we employ U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) to predict masks, B-spline
373   models, and foreground canonical images. Initially, we use a warm start U-Net (Ronneberger et al.,
374   2015) network with Lprior to generate a coarse mask, with Lprior weight set to 0.5. Then, we gradually
375   incorporate Lparallel (λparallel = 0.05), Lwarp (λwarp = 0.1), Lmask (λmask = 0.1), and Lrec (λrec = 0.5)
376   to optimize DeNVeR. Specifically, our model requires 20 minutes of runtime to process a video
377   sequence of 80 frames. However, our method provides fully automatic segmentation without manual
      annotations, potentially saving significant time and resources in the long term.

406
407   Since supervised training and testing data are from the same dataset (in-domain setting), its perfor-
408   mance will be better than that of self-supervised or unsupervised methods. However, it is worth
409   noting that in this scenario, our method does not require any labels and can still outperform existing
410   self-supervised methods. Also, we test the CADICA dataset to compare the generalization ability
411
      of supervised training and our proposed unsupervised training in Figure 8. We find that supervised
      methods are limited by the domain of their training data and thus struggle to generalize well. Our
412
      method, while requiring test-time training, can adapt to various datasets in an unsupervised manner.
413
      This allows for greater flexibility and generalization across different types of vascular video data.
414

416   in the Jaccard Index and a 14.9% increase in the Dice Score, indicating a significant enhancement
417
      in performance while utilizing it as a prior. While our method is more complex than supervised

418
      training phase, though computationally intensive, is a one-time process per video. For self-supervised

      methods, we follow their tutorials to augment the training dataset and generate synthetic masks for

503
504   Layer separation bootstrapping. To validate the effectiveness of the layer separation bootstrap-
505   ping, we train foreground and background canonical images using the same representation. The
506   results are shown in Table 2, where optimizing both foreground and background canonical images
507   simultaneously leads to a decrease in the Dice score by 0.0877. The comparison is shown in Figure
508   9 (a), where the orange area indicates the difference between without and with Layer separation
509
      bootstrapping. The bottom-right corner shows a zoom-in patch, highlighting the significant effect of
      the bootstrapping step.
510
511
      Hessian prior loss Lprior . To test the effect of the Hessian prior loss, we remove the Hessian prior
512
      loss. As a result, the segmentation performance, as shown in Table 2, also decreases the Dice score
513
      by 0.1006. The comparison between the without and with Lprior is shown in Figure 9 (b), where the
514   orange area indicates the difference between them. The zoom-in patch shows that our model predicts
515   less noticeable vascular regions incorporating the Hessian prior loss Lprior .
516
517   Parallel vessel motion loss Lparallel . We conduct an experiment to assess the effect of the parallel
518   vessel motion loss by removing it from the training pipeline. As shown in Table 2, the segmentation
519   performance decreases the Dice score by 0.0368. Without this loss to enforce parallelism between
520   blood and vessels, the segmentation results are negatively affected. In addition, the comparison
521   between without and with Lparallel is shown in Figure 9 (c). The zoom-in patch shows that the image
522   with Lparallel has clearer segmented vascular regions.
523   The improvements from individual components may appear marginal. However, their cumulative
524   effect leads to overall superior performance compared to baselines. In Figure 9, we provide visual
525   comparisons of ablation studies, demonstrating that these components are essential for clear and
526   complete vessel segmentations. These components help connect disconnected or over-segmented
527   vessels in specific cases.
528
529   5     C ONCLUSIONS
530
531   This paper introduces DeNVeR, an unsupervised test-time training framework for vessel segmentation
532   in X-ray video data. DeNVeR utilizes optical flow and layer separation techniques to accurately
533   segment vessels without requiring annotated datasets. Quantitative and qualitative evaluations on the
534   XACV and CADICA datasets show that DeNVeR outperforms existing image-based self-supervised
535   methods, offering precise delineation of vessel boundaries critical for medical diagnosis and treatment.
536
537   Limitations. Our unsupervised method is sensitive to preprocessing filters, potentially misiden-
538   tifying non-vascular structures as vessels. DeNVeR’s runtime (20 minutes for 80 frames) and
539   computational requirements are also limiting factors. Additionally, our motion-based approach does
      not apply to datasets without contrast agent flow, such as retinal vessel images.
