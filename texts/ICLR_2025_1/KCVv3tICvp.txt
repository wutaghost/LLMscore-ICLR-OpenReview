009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural codec language models (or codec LMs) are emerging as a powerful frame-
012            work for text-to-speech (TTS) and other audio generation tasks. These mod-
013            els leverage advancements in language modeling and high-fidelity residual vec-
014            tor quantization (RVQ)-based audio codecs, which compress continuous wave-
015            forms into discrete codes for LMs to process. Despite the close interdependence
016            of codecs and LMs in these systems, research on codecs and LMs has largely
017            remained siloed. In this work, we bridge this gap by proposing several codec-
018
               LM co-design strategies, analyzing their effects on end-to-end TTS performance
               and efficiency. Specifically, we introduce three complementary techniques: (i) a
019
               frame-wise codec encoder that improves both LM log-likelihood and end-to-end
020
               TTS metrics, (ii) LM codebook level dropout, a method to efficiently navigate a
021            portion of the codec-LM design space by training a single LM, and (iii) increased
022            codec frame duration, which we show can accelerate inference while maintaining
023            end-to-end performance. Our experiments demonstrate that combining all three
024            co-design techniques results in doubled inference speed, and improvements in in-
025            telligibility, audio quality, and speaker control in TTS relative to a siloed baseline.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Neural codec language models (or codec LMs) (van den Oord et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2024) have
031   recently emerged as a prominent framework for text-to-speech (TTS) (Tan et al., 2021; Wang et al.,
032   2023; Yang et al., 2024) and general audio generation tasks (van den Oord et al., 2016; Copet et al.,
033
      2023; Borsos et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024), replacing autoregressive methods that model continu-
      ous raw waveforms (van den Oord et al., 2016; Kalchbrenner et al., 2018; Goel et al., 2022). The
034
      success of codec LMs can be attributed to improvements in the architecture, scaling, and efficiency
035
      of language models (LMs) (Vaswani et al., 2017; Brown et al., 2020; Dao et al., 2022; Gu & Dao,
036   2023), as well as increasingly high-fidelity convolutional audio codecs that employ the residual vec-
037   tor quantization (RVQ) technique (Zeghidour et al., 2021; Défossez et al., 2023; Kumar et al., 2023),
038   bridging continuous-domain audio generation tasks with LM methods that model discrete tokens.
039
      A codec LM-based TTS or audio generation system consists of two separately trained components
040
      working together: (i) a neural codec that is trained as an autoencoder to encode raw audio waveforms
041
      into discrete code sequences and to decode (reconstruct) the waveform from those codes, and (ii) an
042   LM that models the code sequences autoregressively. Although these two components are closely
043   coupled, they represent relatively isolated research areas. Research on codecs (Zeghidour et al.,
044   2021; Défossez et al., 2023; Kumar et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2024) primarily focuses on achieving
045   higher compression rates (i.e., lower bandwidths) while maintaining reconstruction quality, rather
046   than optimizing for downstream language modeling. Conversely, research on codec-based LMs typ-
047   ically treats the codec as a fixed module and explores how to best model the codec tokens, enhancing
048   aspects such as conditioning (Borsos et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024), RVQ code patterning (Copet
049   et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024), or non-autoregression (Wang et al., 2023). While the design space
050
      of codecs and LMs combined is too large to explore exhaustively, considering each in isolation may
      be suboptimal when the goal is to improve the end-to-end performance.
051
052   In this work, we aim to break the isolation and uncover co-design principles between the codec and
053   the LM. We identify several aspects that play a key role in the interactions between the two, and
      substantially impact the end-to-end generation quality and/or efficiency. Leveraging these co-design

075   First, we consider the receptive fields of neighboring RVQ code frames. In the context of RVQ audio
076
      codecs, we define a frame as a chunk of waveform samples (e.g., 512 samples) that correspond in
      time to one or more discrete codes (collectively, a code frame). A code frame contains multiple RVQ
077
      levels capturing increasingly fine-grained information. While typically, the convolutional receptive
078
      fields of neighboring code frames overlap both in the codec’s encoder and decoder, the overlapping
079   has different implications on the two seemingly symmetrical sides. In the decoder, it widens the in-
080   formation bottleneck enabling higher-fidelity reconstruction. In the encoder however, it may muddy
081   the relationship between time in the waveform and time in the code frames, which we show can be
082   detrimental to the downstream LM. To address this, we propose a framewise codec encoder, i.e., one
083   which encodes each waveform frame independently. Our experiments show that using a framewise
084   codec encoder (i) improves the downstream LM’s log-likelihood (>8% higher), and (ii) leads to
085   consistently better end-to-end metrics on both TTS and unconditional music generation tasks.
086   Second, given a trained RVQ codec, we propose a method for efficiently determining the subset of
087   RVQ levels to use in LM training to improve end-to-end metrics. The residual structure of RVQ
088   enables the LM to be trained on subsets of RVQ levels, raising the question of what number of lev-
089   els to model for optimal end-to-end performance. Modeling more levels might presumably improve
090   end-to-end audio generation performance by improving audio reconstruction quality. However, we
091   observe in pilot experiments that increasing the number of levels past a certain point actually de-
092   grades end-to-end performance, possibly because the LM cannot effectively model such fine-grained
093
      information. Training multiple LMs to determine the optimal level account is a computationally ex-
      pensive endeavor. Accordingly, we present LM codebook level dropout, a technique that trains a
094
      single LM on all possible level counts. We demonstrate that end-to-end performance results from
095
      a single LM training run using our technique on 12-level RVQ codes track the results of 12 LMs
096   trained on each possible level count for the same number of gradient steps. Accordingly, our pro-
097   posed technique dramatically improves the efficiency by which researchers can tune this important
098   hyperparameter.
099
      Finally, in addition to the number of levels, there exist two other salient codec hyperparameters, i.e.,
100
      the frame duration and codebook size. In siloed codec design, these hyperparameters can be freely
101
      traded off with one another without affecting the codec’s bitrate or audio reconstruction quality.
102   However, in a co-design setting, these individual hyperparameters have unique interactions with
103   the downstream LM. Crucially, LM sequence length is inversely proportional to frame duration,
104   providing a strong motivation for using longer frames to increase the efficiency of the LM. We
105   explore the end-to-end effects of increasing the frame duration while suitably adjusting the other
106   two hyperparameters to maintain codec reconstruction quality. We show that doubling the frame
107   duration of the default DAC codec (Kumar et al., 2023) to 22 milliseconds can result in doubled
      end-to-end inference speeds and comparable end-to-end metrics.

      Neural audio codecs. Compressing and quantizing long, continuous audio waveforms into shorter
130   discrete codes using a convolutional autoencoder was first proposed by van den Oord et al. (2017).
131   Their proposed VQ-VAE method involves online K-Means for quantizing latent representations and
132   a reconstruction objective on the decoder’s output. Later, SoundStream (Zeghidour et al., 2021)
133   introduced the 2D-structured Residual Vector Quantization (RVQ) to such codecs. This work also
134   integrated a mixture of discriminators, a technique adoped from GAN-based audio synthesis (Good-
135   fellow et al., 2014; Donahue et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2019; Kong et al., 2020), on top of the decoder
136   to enhance the perceptual quality of reconstructed waveforms—this RVQ-GAN setup has since been
137   a norm for neural audio codecs. EnCodec (Défossez et al., 2023) and DAC (Kumar et al., 2023) fur-
138
      ther advanced the RVQ-GAN architecture with optimized discriminator setup, activation function,
      and (low) latent dimensionality. HILCodec (Ahn et al., 2024) showed that layer-wise variance con-
139
      straining helps with the depth scaling of lightweight RVQ-GAN codecs. Overall, research in neural
140
      audio codecs has focused on achieving higher compression (i.e., lower bitrates) while maintaining
141   audio reconstruction quality, rather than downstream audio generation, and often involved detailed
142   architectural designs and tuning. In contrast, our work approaches codec design from an end-to-end
143   audio generation practitioners’ perspective, exploring codec hyperparameters that are both easily
144   configurable and influential to the end-to-end system.
145
146   LM-based end-to-end audio generation. Autoregressive modeling of compressed discrete
147   codes for audio waveforms was first proposed alongside VQ-VAE (van den Oord et al., 2017).
148   AudioLM (Borsos et al., 2023) introduced a hierarchical LM approach that first generates seman-
149   tic tokens (Hsu et al., 2021; Chung et al., 2021), derived from BERT-like pretraining (Devlin et al.,
150   2019) on audio data, followed by RVQ codes (or acoustic tokens), resulting in better long-term
151
      coherence in generated audios. To navigate the efficiency-quality tradeoff given an RVQ codec,
      VALL-E (Wang et al., 2023) proposed non-autoregressive modeling for all RVQ levels except the
152
      coarsest one, and MusicGen (Copet et al., 2023) introduced the Delay pattern, dramatically shorten-
153
      ing the sequence length while preserving key autoregressive dependencies. UniAudio (Yang et al.,
154   2024) unified tokenization schemes for text, phonemes, audio, and symbolic music to build an LM
155   for a wide range of audio generation tasks. Despite these advancements, all aforementioned work
156   treated the audio codec, which is upstream from the LM, as a fixed component, leaving out the
157   potential gains from a co-design between the codec and the LM.
158
159   Co-design of audio codecs and LMs. Compared to the two previously discussed areas, design-
160   ing codecs with the goal of improving end-to-end audio generations is a relatively nascent direc-
161   tion. SpeechTokenizer (Zhang et al., 2024) proposed to distill information in semantic tokens (Hsu
      et al., 2021) into the first (coarsest) level of the RVQ codec, alleviating the need of using two


                                                         3

      LMs (Borsos et al., 2023; Agostinelli et al., 2023) in tandem for semantic and acoustic RVQ to-
163   kens. Moshi (Défossez et al., 2024), a work conducted concurrently with ours, adopted this tech-
164   nique and used a causal codec setup to enable low-latency, streamable real-time voice conversa-
165   tions. Language-Codec (Ji et al., 2024) proposed to arrange the RVQ levels in a first-parallel, then-

170   for end-to-end system practitioners.

240   4.1     C ODES WITH NON - OVERLAPPING RECEPTIVE FIELDS (F RAMEWISE CODEC ENCODER )
241
242   Most common RVQ audio codecs (Zeghidour et al., 2021; Défossez et al., 2023; Kumar et al.,
243   2023) set the stride size of each 1D convolutional layer to be smaller than the filter size. This way
244   the neighboring outputs (along the time dimension) have overlapping receptive fields, promoting the
245   smoothness of transitions. When we consider the entire codec encoder, where multiple convolutional
246
      layers are stacked and gradually increase the receptive field of each intermediate representations,
      this overlapping property at each layer causes the receptive field of each code frame xt to overlap
247
      with those of preceding code frames [xt−k , . . . , xt−1 ].1 2 A similar property also holds in the codec
248
      decoder, i.e., each sample in the reconstructed waveform ŵ is influenced by multiple code frames.
249
250   If we reason about the frame-level overlaps, it is intuitive that they benefit the decoder, as the mutual
251   information between multiple code frames can be leveraged for improved reconstruction. On the
252
      other hand, whether these overlaps are advantageous on the encoder side is less clear. They may
      provide the opportunity for the codec to pack information in high-complexity waveform segments
253
      (e.g., fast speech with frequent intonation changes) into neighboring code frames corresponding
254
      to low-information segments (e.g., silence), hence improving audio reconstruction. However, this
255   could be detrimental for the downstream LM as each code frame may hold varying amounts of
256   (confounding) information from preceding frames.
257
258
      Therefore, we propose a setup where the codes are encoded framewise, i.e., each code frame xt has
      a receptive field covering only fs /fx waveform samples, without overlapping with other code frames.
259
      Operationally, this is achieved by reshaping the waveform (i.e., the inital input to the codec encoder)
260
      from (B, Tfs , 1), where the dimensions represent (batch, sequence, channels), to (BTfx , fs /fx , 1).
261   Since the downsampling rate of the entire encoder is precisely fs /fx , the final encoder output is of
262   shape (BTfx , 1, D), which we then reshape back to (B, Tfx , D) before quantization as in normal
263   codecs with frame-level overlaps. Note that no architectural changes are required.3
264
      This setup with encoder-framewise and decoder-overlapping receptive fields retains desirable prop-
265
      erties such as leveraging mutual information between code frames for reconstruction, and smooth

      4.3   NAVIGATING OTHER KEY CODEC HYPERPARAMETERS
325
326   In addition to the number of RVQ levels (Q), there are two additional hyperparameters that affect
327   the compression factor of the codec: (i) the codec’s frame duration (1/fx ), and (ii) the codebook size
328   (|V|). The bitrate of the codec, equal to Qfx log2 (|V|) bits per second, is a function of these three
329   factors and directly impacts the reconstruction quality. In siloed codec design, these three factors
330   can be traded off freely to optimize for higher reconstruction quality at some fixed bitrate. However,
331   in a co-design context, the LM’s behavior can be impacted by different tradeoffs even when the
332   codec’s bitrate is kept fixed.
333   Here we make several observations about frame duration and codebook size respectively in the
334   context of codec-LM co-design. Starting with frame duration, from Eqn. (3), we can observe that
335   the Delay LM training sequence length is inversely proportional to frame duration. Accordingly,
336   increasing it by a factor of two can roughly halve sequence length, resulting in efficiency gains and
337   reduced inference latency. However, codec bitrate is also inversely proportional to frame duration.
338   Therefore, to preserve audio quality with an increased frame duration, we need to also increase
339
      either the codebook size |V| and/or the number of RVQ levels Q.
340   On the other hand, increasing the codebook size |V| may have mixed impacts on the LM. On the
341   positive side, assuming the frame duration and bitrate are controlled, using a larger codebook (and
342   hence fewer RVQ levels) reduces the extent of packing information from multiple (i.e., Q′ ) code
343   frames into one Delay LM timestep x(delay)
                                              t    . However, increasing only |V| while holding Q constant
344   leads to an exponential growth in the LM’s vocabulary size (and embedding parameters) relative to a
345   linear increase in bitrate. This growth can inflate the LM’s memory footprint and introduce potential
346   modeling challenges. Thus, while our CL drop technique can help determine the best Q′ efficiently
347
      given a fixed |V|, finding the optimal |V| still requires trial and error.
348   All three of the aforementioned hyperparameters—Q, |V|, and fx —are relatively straightforward
349   to adjust in open-source codec implementations (Défossez et al., 2023; Kumar et al., 2023). Only
350   changing fx is achieved indirectly through specifying stride sizes in each strided convolutional layers
                     QS
351   since fs /fx = i=1 si , where fs is the audio sampling rate, S is the number of strided convolution
352   layers in the codec encoder, and si ’s are the individual stride sizes.

522   7       C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
523
524   In this work, we presented several co-design techniques for neural codec language models to ad-
525   dress the isolation between codec and language modeling research fronts. Specifically, we proposed
526   framewise encoder for the codec and codebook level dropout for the LM, and explored the effects of
527   longer frame durations and wider codebooks. We illustrated the individual benefits of each co-design
528   technique with end-to-end TTS experiments, and demonstrated that applying all of them improves
529   both the performance and efficiency of the codec-LM system.
530   Future endeavors may (i) study the theory of why framewise compressed representations improve
531   language modeling, (ii) develop RVQ codecs that have flexibility not only in the number of levels,
532   but also in codebook size and frame duration such that our LM codebook level dropout can be ap-
533   plied to all three key hyperparameters altogether, and (iii) uncover the scaling properties (Hoffmann
534   et al., 2022) of the optimal codec settings w.r.t. larger models and more training data.

      For LM codebook level dropout (i.e., CL drop) to be effective in determining the optimal level count,
718
      its performance profile w.r.t. the level count should trend as closely as possible to that resulting from
719
      training LMs without CL drop at every possible number of levels. Here, we find that the choice
720   of dropout distribution P(q), which determines the fraction of training steps allocated to each level
721   count, to be critical. We experiment with a total of 5 different P(q)’s detailed below:
722
