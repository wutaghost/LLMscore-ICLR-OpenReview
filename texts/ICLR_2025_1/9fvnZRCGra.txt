009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Existing handwritten text generation methods typically focus on isolated words.
012            However, realistic handwritten texts require attention not only to individual words
013            but also to the relationships between them, such as vertical alignment and hori-
014            zontal spacing. Therefore, generating entire text line is a more promising task.
015            However, this task poses significant challenges, such as accurately capturing com-
016            plex style patterns including both intra-word and inter-word patterns, and main-
017            taining content structure across numerous characters. To address these challenges,
018
               inspired by human writing priors, we focus on both the vertical style (e.g., word
               alignment) and horizontal style (e.g., word spacing and letter connections) of in-
019
               dividual writing samples. Additionally, we decompose text-line content preserva-
020
               tion across numerous characters into global context supervision between charac-
021            ters and local supervision of individual character structures. In light of this, we
022            propose DiffBrush, a new diffusion model for text-line generation. DiffBrush em-
023            ploys two complementary proxy objectives to handle vertical and horizontal writ-
024            ing styles, and introduces two-level discriminators to provide content supervision
025            at both the text-line and word levels. Extensive experiments show that DiffBrush
026            excels in generating high-quality text-lines, particularly in style reproduction and
027            content preservation. Our source code will be made publicly available.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Handwritten text, as a remarkable symbol of human civilization, has recorded the history of human
033
      society from ancient times to the present. Even today, handwriting is considered a distinctly human
      skill. In the digital age, handwritten text generation merges the personalization of traditional writing
034
      with the efficiency of automation, garnering considerable interest. This task aims to automatically
035
      synthesize realistic handwritten text images that visually convey the user’s unique writing style while
036   ensuring the content readability. This can assist individuals facing writing difficulties, accelerate
037   handwritten font design, and generate sufficient data to train more robust text recognizers.
038
039
      Current dominant methods for this task generate handwriting images at word levels. For instance,
      some GAN-based methods (Bhunia et al., 2021; Gan et al., 2022; Pippi et al., 2023a) and diffusion-
040
      based method (Dai et al., 2024) utilize reference images provided by writers as style inputs and
041
      condition on character-wise labels or images for content inputs, achieving the synthesis of handwrit-
042   ten words with controllable styles and specified contents. However, as shown in Figure 1, we observe
043   that handwritten text generation at word levels does not truly reflect the human writing process: 1)
044   Humans generally maintain vertical alignment between words, while synthesized words often have
045   arbitrary positions in the vertical aspect. 2) Different writers exhibit distinct horizontal word spac-
046   ing, but this information is lost in the generated words. To address these issues, an intuitive solution
047   is to generate entire text-lines rather than isolated words, known as handwritten text-line generation.
048   Our goal is to achieve high-quality handwritten text-line generation with desired styles and contents.
049   The generation on text-line level, nevertheless, is very challenging due to several reasons: 1) It is
050   non-trivial to accurately capture writing styles from text-lines with multiple words, as it involves
051   not only intra-word style patterns like letter connections and slant but also inter-word spacing and
052   vertical alignment. 2) Ensuring the readability of generated text-lines with numerous characters is
053   difficult; for example, in the widely used IAM dataset (Marti & Bunke, 2002), a text-line averages
      42 characters, roughly 6 times the length of a typical word.

074   Previously, several GAN-based methods targeting text-line generation have been developed. TS-
075   GAN (Davis et al., 2020) enhances the style vector by concatenating global and character-wise style
076   features. However, the character-wise feature relies heavily on an independent character recognizer,
077   making it difficult to capture all character styles accurately when the text-line contains many charac-
078   ter categories. Moreover, TS-GAN naively uses text recognizers with CTC loss (Graves et al., 2006)
079   for content supervision, which inadvertently hinders their style mimicry abilities. More specifically,
080   to minimize the CTC loss, the model is pushed to generate easily recognizable samples with simple
081
      styles (e.g., regular fonts with standard strokes). CSA-GAN (Kang et al., 2021) achieves handwritten
      text-line generation by introducing new data preprocessing and training strategies into GANWrit-
082
      ing (Kang et al., 2020), which focuses on handwritten word generation. Nonetheless, CSA-GAN
083
      exhibits poor style learning capability since it directly uses a vanilla CNN as its style encoder.
084
085   Different from them, our solution is inspired by human writing priors and is built around two key
086   principles: 1) People naturally pay attention to both vertical and horizontal styles of handwriting,
087
      as illustrated in Figure 1. The vertical style refers to the alignment of words along the vertical axis,
      while the horizontal style includes spacing between words, joins between letters, etc. 2) To ensure
088
      the content accuracy of handwritten text, at a global level, people maintain the correct character
089
      order within a text line, preserving global contextual relationships between characters. At a local
090   level, they ensure the structural correctness of each individual word.
091
092   Guided by the above human writing priors, we propose DiffBrush, a diffusion model for handwrit-
093
      ten text-line generation, featuring a dual-head style module and two-level content discriminators.
      Specifically, we employ the proxy loss (Movshovitz-Attias et al., 2017; Kim et al., 2020) to guide
094
      each head to focus on horizontal and vertical styles, respectively. For the vertical style, we randomly
095
      sample style references by column, preserving vertical alignment while disrupting word spacing and
096   cursive connections, as shown in (a) of Figure 2. We then pull together column-wise sampling results
097   from the same writer and push apart those from different writers, allowing the encoder to capture
098   discriminative vertical style features. Similarly, for the horizontal style, we sample by row, retain-
099   ing word spacing and cursive connections, as illustrated in (b) of Figure 2, and aggregate row-wise
100   sampling results from the same writer to encourage the encoder to learn horizontal style patterns.
101
      Furthermore, the proposed two-level content discriminators supervise textual content at both the line
102   and word levels (cf. Figure 4). The line-level content discriminator segments the text-line image into
103   non-overlapping parts, which are fed into a 3D CNN (Tran et al., 2015) to extract global contextual
104   relationships. By assessing the realism of these relationships, the diffusion generator is encouraged
105   to produce text-lines with correct character order. The word-level discriminator uses an attention
106   mechanism to isolate individual words from the whole text-line and verify their content authenticity,
107   guiding the generator to focus on text content at the local level. Our findings show that the two-level
      content discriminators improve content accuracy without reducing style imitation performance.

123   We summarize our contributions in three key areas: 1) We propose DiffBrush, a new diffusion model
124   targeting handwritten text-line generation. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to explore
125   how to design a diffusion model for handwritten text-line generation. 2) Inspired by two human
126   writing priors, we propose a dual-head style module, which captures both vertical and horizontal
127   writing styles, and two-level content discriminators that supervise textual content at both line and
128   word levels while preserving style imitation performance. 3) Extensive experiments on two popular
129   handwritten datasets demonstrate our DiffBrush significantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods.

132   2   R ELATED W ORK
133
134   Handwritten Text Generation. Handwritten text generation methods are generally divided into
135   online and offline: the former synthesizes dynamic stroke sequences, while the latter generates
136   static text images. Benefiting from the rapid advancement of deep learning, Recurrent Neural Net-
137   works (Kotani et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2020; Tolosana et al., 2021), Transformer decoders (Dai
138   et al., 2023), and diffusion models (Luhman & Luhman, 2020; Ren et al., 2023) have been widely
139   used for synthesizing online handwritten text. However, online methods cannot synthesize stroke
140   width, ink color, or paper background like offline methods.
141   The advent of Generative Adversarial Networks has accelerated the development of offline hand-
142   written text generation. Early works (Alonso et al., 2019; Fogel et al., 2020) use character labels as
143   content inputs and random noise as style inputs to synthesize handwritten words with controllable
144   content and random styles. To enhance style control, SLOGAN (Luo et al., 2022) conditions style
145   inputs on fixed writer IDs but fails to mimic unseen styles. Unlike them, GANwriting (Kang et al.,
146   2020) and HWT (Bhunia et al., 2021) employ CNN or transformer encoder to extract style fea-
147
      tures from style references and are thus capable of imitating any styles. Further, VATr (Pippi et al.,
      2023a) utilizes symbol images as content representations, enabling character generation beyond the
148
      training charset. In contrast to the above word-focused methods, TS-GAN (Davis et al., 2020) and
149
      CSA-GAN (Kang et al., 2021) are developed to synthesize handwritten text-lines. However, they
150   struggle to produce satisfactory results due to design drawbacks in style learning and content super-
151   vision.
152
153
      Image Diffusion. Diffusion models such as Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM) (Ho
      et al., 2020) and Latent Diffusion Model (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2022) have shown great success
154
      in image generation. For example, guided diffusion (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) and classifier-free
155
      diffusion (Ho & Salimans, 2022) condition the image synthesis on class labels. Some text-to-image
156   diffusion methods like Stable-diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) and DALL-E3 (Betker et al., 2023)
157   further employ CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to convert text descriptions into comprehensive repre-
158   sentations, thereby producing impressive results. Very recently, some methods (Wang et al., 2023;
159   Xu et al., 2024) combine adversarial learning with diffusion using a discriminator to enhance gen-
160   eration quality. Unlike these GAN-diffusion approaches that simply distinguish between real and
161   generated images, our two-level content discriminators are specifically designed to provide content
      supervision at both the line and word levels.

193   Problem formulation. We consider handwritten text-line image generation that is conditioned on
194   both content and style. Given a text string A and a style reference si randomly sampled from an
195   exemplar writer wi ∈ W, we aim to synthesize a handwritten text-line image x that captures the
196   unique calligraphic style of wi while accurately preserving the content of A. Here, A = {ai }L     i=1
197
      represents a sequence of length L, where each ai is a Unicode character, including lowercase and
      uppercase letters, digits, punctuation, etc. The key challenges lie in accurately capturing individual
198
      handwriting styles, including both intra-word and inter-word patterns from the style reference, while
199
      ensuring the readability of text lines that typically contain numerous characters.
200
201   To address this task, drawing inspiration from human writing principles related to style and content,
202   we propose to capture both vertical and horizontal styles (cf. Figure 1) from individual handwritten
203
      examples while focusing on textual content at both the line and word levels. To achieve this, we
      introduce a novel DiffBrush method that innovates a dual-head style module with its distinct proxy
204
      losses, and the two-level content discriminators. Our DiffBrush can effectively imitate style patterns
205
      from the style reference, ensuring that the generated text-lines remain human-readable.

      3.1      OVERALL S CHEME
208
209
      The proposed DiffBrush (cf. Figure 3) comprises a conditional diffusion generator and two-level
210   content discriminators. Within the conditional diffusion generator, the dual-head style module aims
211   to emulate the vertical and horizontal styles of exemplar writers. To achieve this, we first employ
212   a CNN-Transformer style encoder to extract rich calligraphic attributes from the provided style
213   reference si . The vertical and horizontal heads then capture the respective styles from the extracted
214   patterns. To guide this process, we introduce two proxy losses, VerticalPA Lver and HorizontalPA
215   Lhor , which enforce each head to focus on its corresponding style. Specifically, Lver brings closer
      the column-wise sampling results (cf. Figure 2) from the same writer, while Lhor aggregates the row-

      wise sampling results (cf. Figure 2) belonging to the same writer. Through Lver and Lhor , the two
217   heads obtain discriminative vertical and horizontal style features, i.e., Sver and Shor , respectively.
218
219   Considering the textual content, following VATr (Pippi et al., 2023a) and One-DM (Dai et al., 2024),
220
      we render the text string A into Unifont images. The strength of Unifont is its ability to represent
      all Unicode characters, allowing our method to accept any user-provided string input. We then
221
      input the rendered images into a content encoder with a CNN-Transformer architecture to obtain

      an informative content feature Q = {qi }L i=1 ∈ R

223   channel dimension. After obtaining Q and the two style representations Sver and Shor , motivated by
224   One-DM (Dai et al., 2024), we feed them into a blender with multi-head attention layers (Vaswani
225   et al., 2017) for seamless fusion to obtain the conditional information c ∈ RL×c . Specifically, we
226   use Sver and Shor as key/value vectors and Q as the query vector, successively attending Sver and
227   Shor to aggregate the style information adaptively.
228
      Guided by the fused condition c, the denoising network pθ initiates the denoising process, where
229   θ denotes the learnable parameters. Built on a U-Net architecture (Ronneberger et al., 2015), pθ
230   progressively synthesizes the desired handwritten text-line image x0 , starting from pure Gaussian
231   noise xT ∼ N (0, I). The denoising process is supervised by a diffusion loss Ldif f that minimizes
232   the mean square error (MSE) between the generated x0 and real xreal . However, relying solely
233   on Ldif f is insufficient to ensure the readability of the generated content. Therefore, two-level
234   discriminators (i.e., Dline and Dword ) are introduced to provide content feedback.
235   Specifically, the conditional diffusion generator G and the two-level discriminators D engage in
236   an adversarial learning process: G seeks to synthesize realistic images that D cannot distinguish
237   from real ones based on content, while D assess the content at both the line and word levels. The
238   readability of the generated images improves through two adversarial losses, Lline and Lword , which
239   further enhances generation quality in terms of content accuracy.
240
      In summary, the overall training objectives for the conditional diffusion generator and the two-level
241   discriminators are defined as follows:

244                                        LD = −(Lline + Lword ),                                       (2)

246   where λ serves as a trade-off factor. We alternately optimize G and D, and experimentally set λ to
247   0.05 in the training phase.

249   3.2   D UAL - HEAD S TYLE M ODULE
250
251   To capture complex style patterns within text-lines (cf. Figure 1), such as vertical alignment between
252   words and horizontal word spacing, we propose a dual-head style module to extract both vertical and
253   horizontal styles from individual reference samples. As illustrated in Figure 3, the style samples are
254   first fed into a style encoder, which combines a CNN and a transformer encoder, to obtain an initial
255   style feature sequence S ∈ Rd×c , where d is the sequence length. Subsequently, we employ two
256
      separate heads, termed vertical head and horizontal head, each containing a standard self-attention
      layer, to extract vertical style Sver ∈ Rd×c guided by Lver and horizontal style Shor ∈ Rd×c guided
257
      by Lhor from S, respectively. The details of Lver and Lhor are detailed below.
258
259   Vertical Style Learning. The goal of the proposed Lver is to guide the vertical head in extracting
260   the discriminative vertical style Sver . However, accurately learning the vertical style is challenging
261   because the samples inherently contain both vertical and horizontal style patterns. To address this
262
      issue, we propose to draw together column-wise sampling results of reference samples from the
      same writer, thereby enforcing the vertical head to learn Sver . The intuition is that the column-wise
263
      sampling process maintains vertical alignment between characters while disrupting horizontal style
264
      patterns such as word spacing and cursive connections (cf. Figure 2).
265
266   To implement this, we divide the style image into several columns and then randomly select a subset
267   following a uniform distribution. More specifically, we perform the sampling process on Sver by first
268   reshaping the sequential feature Sver back into spatial feature Ŝver ∈ Rh×w×c , and then sampling
269   columns of Ŝver to obtain scol ∈ Rh×n×c , where n = w · ρ and ρ is the sampling ratio. Next, Lver
      assigns a proxy to each writer, treating each proxy as an anchor and associating it with all column-

      3.3   T WO - LEVEL C ONTENT D ISCRIMINATORS
303
      Unlike existing methods (Davis et al., 2020; Gan et al., 2022; Pippi et al., 2023a; Dai et al., 2024) that
304   simply employ recognizers with CTC loss to improve the content readability of generated images,
305   we propose two-level discriminators focused on providing effective content feedback. The advan-
306   tage of our discriminators is that they improve content accuracy without disrupting style learning,
307   while CTC-based methods tend to hinder it. Our discriminators address two key challenges: (1)
308   How to ensure that the discriminator focuses on textual content rather than style, and (2) Consid-
309   ering that a text line typically contains numerous characters, it is challenging to provide effective
310   supervision to ensure the accurate generation of each character.
311   To address the challenge (1), inspired by pix2pix (Isola et al., 2017), we introduce textual content
312   as a conditional input, feeding it into the discriminator alongside the generated image. This ensures
313   that the discriminator focuses solely on content evaluation. For challenge (2), we break it down into
314   two more simpler subtasks: assessing the correctness of the overall character order and verifying the
315   correctness of the local text content. The proposed two-level discriminators consist of a text-level
316   discriminator and a word-level discriminator Figure 4. We detail each component below.
317   Line-level Content Discriminator. Given the generated image x0 and the content guidance Iline
318   without style information, the line-level discriminator Dline aims to determine whether the character
319   order in x0 aligns with that in Iline . Specifically, we concatenate x0 and Iline along the channel
320   dimension, and then slice the concatenated result into n non-overlapping segments {ci }ni=1 from
321   left to right. {ci }ni=1 are processed by a 3D CNN to integrate context information, outputting n
322   patches. Dline then determines whether each patch is real or fake, providing fine-grained feedback
323   on character order. The line-level discriminator loss Lline is formulated as:
                            Lline = log(Dline (Iline , xreal )) + log(1 − Dline (Iline , x0 )).         (5)

      Evaluation Dataset. To evaluate our DiffBrush in generating handwritten text-line, we use the
358
      widely adopted handwriting dataset IAM (Marti & Bunke, 2002) and CVL (Kleber et al., 2013).
359
      IAM contains 13,353 English text-line images belonging to 657 unique writers. Following the pro-
360   tocol of CSA-GAN (Kang et al., 2021), we use text-lines from 496 writers for training and the
361   remaining 161 writers for testing. CVL dataset consists of handwritten text-lines from 310 writers
362   in both English and German. For our experiments, we use the English portion, consisting of 11,007
363   text-lines, and follow the standard CVL split, with 283 writers for training and 27 for testing. In all
364   experiments, we resize the images to a height of 64 pixels while preserving their aspect ratio, as done
365   in previous works(Davis et al., 2020; Kang et al., 2021; Dai et al., 2024). To manage varying widths,
366   images with a width smaller than 1024 pixels are padded, whereas those exceeding 1024 pixels are
367   resized to a fixed size of 64 × 1024. We also conduct user studies to quantify the subjective quality
368
      of the generated handwritten text-line images in Appendix A.2.
369   Evaluation Metrics. 1) We use the newly proposed Handwriting Distance (HWD) (Pippi et al.,
370   2023b), specifically designed for handwriting style evaluation. HWD computes the Euclidean dis-
371   tance between features extracted by a VGG16 network pre-trained on a large corpus of handwritten
372   text images. 2) We evaluate content accuracy using an OCR system, following CSA-GAN (Kang
373   et al., 2021) and One-DM (Dai et al., 2024). 3) We use Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel
374
      et al., 2017), Inception Score (IS) (Salimans et al., 2016), and Geometry Score (GS) (Khrulkov &
      Oseledets, 2018) to measure the visual quality of generated images.

534   In this paper, we introduce DiffBrush, a novel diffusion model tailored for handwritten text-line
535   generation. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first exploration of diffusion models for this
536   task. Drawing inspiration from two human writing priors, we propose a dual-head style module that
537   captures both vertical and horizontal writing styles, and two-level content discriminators that super-

      A.1   M ORE I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
705
706   In our conditional diffusion generator, each Transformer layer contains the multi-head attention with
707   c = 512 dimensional states and 8 attention heads. We apply sinusoidal positional encoding (Vaswani
708   et al., 2017) to input tokens before feeding them to the Transformer encoder layer. We pre-train
709   the blender on handwritten text-line recognition task with cross-entropy loss and fix its parameter
710   during the training of the whole DiffBrush. To conserve GPU memory and accelerate the training
711   time, following Wordstylist (Nikolaidou et al., 2023) and One-DM (Dai et al., 2024), we streamline
712
      the U-Net by reducing the number of ResNet blocks and attention heads and takes the diffusion
      process into the latent space. Specifically, we adopt a powerful, pre-trained Variational Autoencoder
713
      (VAE) of Stable Diffusion (1.5) to convert the image into latent space. During the training phase,
714
      we freeze the parameters of VAE and we set T = 1000 steps, and forward process variances are set
715   to constants increasing linearly from β1 = 10−4 to βT = 0.02.
