010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            Text-Attributed Graphs (TAGs), where each node is associated with text attributes,
013            are ubiquitous and have been widely applied in the real world. The Out-Of-
014            Distribution (OOD) issue, i.e., the training data and the test data not from the same
015            distribution, is quite common in learning on real-world TAGs, posing significant
016            challenges to the effectiveness of graph learning models. Recently, Large Language
017            Models (LLMs) have shown extraordinary capability in processing text data, and
018            have demonstrated tremendous potential in handling TAGs. However, there is no
019
               benchmark work that systematically and comprehensively investigates the effect
               of these LLM-based methods on alleviating the OOD issue on TAGs. To bridge
020
               this gap, we first develop OOD-TAG, a comprehensive OOD benchmark dataset in
021
               TAGs which consists of diverse distributions. Meanwhile, we conduct a systematic
022            and comprehensive investigation on OOD-TAG with different LLM pipelines for
023            graphs. In addition, we provide original observations and novel insights based
024            on the empirical study, which can suggest promising directions for the research
025            of LLMs in addressing the OOD challenges on TAGs. Our code and dataset are
026            available in https://anonymous.4open.science/r/GraphOOD-benchmark-5FCF/.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Graph typically consists of a bunch of nodes denoting different entities and numerous edges describing
032   the connections among these nodes. In many real-world graphs, nodes are often associated with
033
      textural attributes, such as paper citation networks, where each paper node is described with its title
      and abstract (McCallum et al., 2000; Sen et al., 2008), and recommendation graphs, where both the
034
      user and item nodes are associated with characteristic descriptions in text (Zhu et al., 2021). These
035
      graphs along with textual node attributes are usually denoted as text-attributed graphs (TAGs), and
036   they are ubiquitous in various domains. Graph neural networks (GNNs) are effective extensions of
037   deep neural networks on graphs, and have shown impressive ability in graph representation learning.
038   Despite great success achieved in diverse graph-related tasks, most GNNs assume that the training
039   and test data are from the same distribution. However, such assumption is often violated in real-
040   world graph applications. For examples, the paper citation connections can shift with the change of
041   academic hot spots, and items in recommendation graphs can update with the process of science and
042   technology. These shifts in graphs have been empirically and theoretically proven to be harmful for
043   the performance of GNNs in graph-related tasks (Zhu et al., 2021) and many research efforts are
044
      made to address this so-called OOD (Out of Distribution) challenge (Gui et al., 2022; Yang et al.,
      2021) in graphs.
045
046   Recently, large language models(LLMs) have shown extraordinary context-aware knowledge and
047   semantic understanding ability (Brown, 2020). Pioneers such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) and
048   Claude-3 (Anthropic, 2024) have achieved revolutionary success in various applications from
049   different domains. Given the huge progress LLMs have made in handling the texture-related tasks
050
      and great potential of LLMs in dealing with TAGs (Zhu et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2022; He et al.,
      2023a; Chien et al., 2021), a natural question arises: Can LLMs bring a new dawn for the OOD
051
      challenge in the graph domain?
052
053   To answer this question, we embark upon a preliminary study by systematically conducting a series
      of empirical investigations. Specifically, we benchmark three widely-received LLM-compatible


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      pipelines (Chen et al., 2024; 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; He et al., 2023b;a). in graph OOD challenge,
055   including (1) LLMs-as-Enhancers: LLMs are utilized to enhance the input node features, either at the
056   text level or the embedding level; (2) LLMs-as-Annotators: LLMs are adopted to make predictions
057   for some nodes; (3) LLMs-as-Predictors: LLMs are used to make direct predictions for all the
058   nodes. In the above pipelines, the first two are GNN-integrated pipelines where GNNs are taken
059   to make the final predictions with assistance of LLMs, while the last one is GNN-excluded, where
060   we directly leverage LLMs to make the final predictions. In addition, we develop an OOD dataset,
061   OOD-TAG, including a series of widely-received protocols for distribution shift, which includes four
062   OOD types on five TAG datasets. 16 methods from three pipelines(9 from LLMs-as-Enhancers, 2
063
      from LLMs-as-Annotators and 5 from LLMs-as-predictors) are examined on OOD-TAG based on
      several aspects, including performance on both the in-distribution (ID) test set and the OOD test
064
      set; (2) Performance under various OOD types; (3) Expenditure for LLM usage in different pipeline
065
      implementations. We have conducted comparisons among the empirical results and these methods
066   with in-depth analysis and discussion. Our contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
067
068   An OOD dataset on TAGs. We develop OOD-TAG, which consists of four OOD types among five
069
      TAG datasets, and is flexible to include more OOD types on additional TAGs.
070   Comprehensive and reproducible study. We conduct a systematical study on 16 methods with
071   three LLM pipelines on OOD-TAG, and compare their performance from different perspectives. Both
072   the baseline codes and OOD-TAG are available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/GraphOOD-
073   benchmark-5FCF/.
074   Insights. Through a comprehensive study, we have concluded several findings: (1) Using LLMs as
075   feature-level enhancer or annotators can help significantly alleviate the OOD challenge in TAGs with
076   reasonable expenses; (2) When serving as a predictor, LLMs show more promising generalization
077   performance after fine-tuning; (3) Well-designed prompts that can efficiently capture the graph
078   information are essential for the LLMs’ performance.

      2    BACKGROUND
081   In this section, some preliminaries of our work will be provided. First, we will give a brief introduction
082   about Text-Attributed-Graphs (TAGs) and the node classification problem on TAGs. Next, the Out-
083   Of-Distribution (OOD) challenge on TAGs will be illustrated. In addition, we will discuss some basic
084   concepts and recent advances on Large Language Models(LLMs) on graphs, separately.
085   Text-Attributed-Graphs(TAGs). A text-attributed graph can be formulated as G = (V, E), where
086   V = {v1 , ..., vN } constitutes the set of N nodes and E = {e1 , ...ek } is the edge set describing the
087   connection among these nodes. Specifically, for each node, there is an associated text description
088   si . TAGs are prevalent in real-world applications. To take OGBN-ARXIV (Hu et al., 2021b) as
089   an illustrative example, each node in this citation graph represents an academic paper with a text
090   description consisting of its title and abstract, and each edge denotes a citation relationship between
091   two papers.
092   Node Classification on TAGs. In this study, we mainly focus on the node classification problem on
093   TAGs. Specifically, given a TAG G = (V, E) with a set of note attributes S = {s1 , ..., sN }, where
094   the node set V can be divided into a labeled node set VL and an unlabeled node set VU , the aim of
095   node classification is to predict the labels of nodes from VU . Let’s continue with the example of
096   OGBN-ARXIV. The label of each paper node is the domain category it belongs to, such as computer
097   vision and natural language processing.
098   Out-Of-Distribution(OOD) challenges on TAGs. Out-of-distribution challenges typically refer
099   to scenarios where the distributions of training set and test set are different. It is very common in a
100   variety of applications and has been widely recognized as a significant reason for the degradation of
101   model performance (Hand, 2006; Tzeng et al., 2017; LEARNING). The OOD issue is prevalent and
102   intricate in TAGs, which can manifest in diverse perspectives. For example, in OGBN-ARXIV, the
103   distribution shift between the training set and the test set can happen in the aspect of the publication
104
      periods or the research directions of these papers.
105   Large Language Models (LLMs) on Graphs. Large Language Models (LLMs) have exhibited
106   superior performance in various applications (Zhu et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2022; Bubeck et al.,
107   2023; Chien et al., 2021) and have demonstrated extraordinary potential in Text-Attributed Graphs
      (Liu et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2020b; Yasunaga et al., 2022). There are three popular


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      pipelines for applying LLMs to TAGs, including LLMs as enhancers, LLMs as predictors and LLM
109   as annotators. For LLMs as enhancers, LLMs are leveraged as enhancer for node representations.
110   Specifically, we can either take advantages of LLMs to enrich the text descriptions of nodes, or
111   generate LLM-based embeddings given the original text attributes. In addition, it is also common to
112   directly leverage LLMs as a predictor via prompting. Although being effective and straightforward,
113   this method comes at a huge cost. To ameliorate the issue, a compromise solution has been proposed
114   to use LLMs as annotators. In other words, LLMs are used to make predictions for a specific bunch of
115   samples, and then such information is leveraged to enhance some small-scale models, such as GNNs,
116   which are used for the final prediction. For the clear description of LLMs in different pipelines, we
117
      also roughly divide LLMs into two categories based on whether users have access to their embeddings
      following Chen et al. (2024). The embedding-visible LLMs, denoted as LLM ∗ in this paper, ref to
118
      LLMs that enable users to get embeddings for specific text inputs, such as BERT (Devlin et al., 2019)
119
      and Deberta (He et al., 2021b). The embedding-invisible LLMs only offer text-based user interface,
120   and make both embeddings and parameters invisible to users, such as ChatGPT , which is deployed
121   as a web service.

199   In the pipeline of LLMs as enhancers, LLMs are used to enhance the node features. There are two
200   mainstream ways to leverage LLMs to enrich the text attributes, i.e., feature-level enhancement
201
      and text-level enhancement (Chen et al., 2023). The feature-level enhancement utilizes LLMs’
      knowledge by encoding text attributes in to features. The text-level enhancement injects the LLMs’
202
      knowledge by augmenting the text attributes via extended text information. Specifically, for the
203
      feature-level enhancement, the LLMs work as feature embedding models whose outputs are then fed
204   into small-scale backbone models, such as GNNs. As illustrated in Figure 3 in Appendix B, only
205   embedding-visible LLMs are taken into account to encode the text information. For the text-level
206   enhancement, embedding-invisible LLMs are used to enrich the text attributes via various prompting
207   strategies. Recently, several papers (He et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024) have explored the text-level
208   enhancement in different manners. Here, we take TAPE (He et al., 2023b) and KEA (Chen et al.,
209   2024) as baseline methods and the illustrative examples of these two augmentations are shown in
210   Figure 4 in Appendix B.2. More details about KEA and TAPE are included in Appendix B.2.
211   While LLMs are effective predictors, their usage usually comes with high costs and slow rate. To
212   address these challenges , some research (Chen et al., 2024; 2023) propose to leverage the zero-
213   shot inference capacity of LLMs for training smaller models, such as GNNs. To provide a more
214   comprehensive survey of various LLM pipelines, we examine two approaches that utilise LLMs as
215   annotators and evaluate their performance on the OOD-TAG dataset. These two approaches are
      further illustrated in Figure 5 in Appendix C. First, we conduct an evaluation of label-free node


                                                                                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      classification on graphs using LLMs (LLMGNN (Chen et al., 2024)). Specifically, LLMs are used to
217   annotate a selected set of nodes, following which GNNs are trained using the annotations provided
218   by LLMs to predict for the remaining numerous nodes. We have also conducted an evaluation of
219   the test-time training pipeline, LLMTTT (Zhang et al., 2024), which performs test-time adaptation
220   based on annotations provided by LLMs for a carefully-selected node set. In particular, LLMTTT
221   incorporates a hybrid active node selection strategy that takes into account not only node diversity
222   but also prediction signals from the pre-trained model. With annotations from LLMs, a two-stage
223   training strategy is devised to fine-tune the test-time model using the limited and noisy labels.
224   In the LLMs-as-Predictors pipeline, LLMs are directly leveraged to solve the graph-related tasks.
225   There are two ways to utilize LLMs on graph tasks. One is to directly use the pre-trained LLMs,
226   while the other one is to first fine-tune the pre-trained LLMs on the specified graph datasets, and then
227   to use the fine-tuned LLMs to make predictions. In order to comprehensively assess the impact of
228   using LLMs as predictors on the OOD challenge in TAGs, both pre-trained and fine-tuned LLMs are
229   evaluated. These two approaches are further illustrated in Figure 6 in Appendix D. For the online
230   LLMs, we employ ChatGPT (Bahrini et al., 2023) for our experiments. Specifically, we leverage
231
      different prompts to interact with LLMs, including the prompts only consisting of node attributes, and
      the prompts containing both the node attributes and the structure information. For the offline LLMs,
232
      we choose InstructGLM (Ye et al., 2024), which employs LLama (Radford et al., 2018; Hu et al.,
233
      2021a) as its backbone and has undergone fine-tuning on a number of graph benchmark datasets.
234   InstructGLM is designed to generate highly scalable prompts based on natural language instructions.
235   It utilises natural language to describe the geometric structure and node features of graphs, thereby
236   enabling the tuning of an LLM for learning and inference tasks on graphs in a generative manner.

239   4     E XPERIMENTS
240
241   In this section, we systematically investigate the potential of LLMs in alleviating the Out-Of-
242   Distribution challenge on TAGs. Specifically, we aim at answering the following questions, where the
243   first three are to use LLMs as assistance of GNNs, while the last one is to use them as final predictors:
244   Q1: Can LLMs help alleviate the OOD challenge on TAGs via enhancing the node embeddings?
245   Q2: Can LLMs help alleviate the OOD challenge on TAGs via enhancing the node text attributes?
246   Q3: Can LLMs help alleviate the OOD challenge on TAGs via annotating some test nodes?
      Q4: Can LLMs help alleviate the OOD challenge on TAGs via directly working as predictors?

      4.1     LLM S - AS -E NHANCERS
250   4.1.1    F EATURE - LEVEL ENHANCEMENT
251
252   Experiment Implementation. To answer Q1, we implement four typies of LLMs as feature
253   enhancers, including (1) Fixed PLMs and LLMs: DeBERTa (He et al., 2021a) and LLaMA
254   (Touvron et al., 2023), both of which are embedding models that encode each word as a fixed-length
255
      vector. (2) Local Sentence Embedding Models: Sentence-BERT (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) and
      e5-large (Wang et al., 2022), which are designed to encode sentences into fixed-length vectors, with
256
      the overall aim of making the embeddings of similar sentences close to each other in the latent space.
257
      (3) Online Sentence Embedding Models: The text-ada-embedding-002 model, which operates as
258   an online sentence embedding model. Meanwhile, (4) the non-contextualized shallow embeddings
259   models: TF-IDF and Word2Vec (Mikolov et al., 2013). TF-IDF is a statistical language model that
260   trains the encoder on the entire dataset. Word2Vec generates shallow embeddings using a relatively
261   shallow neural network that learns from contextual information. Regarding the small-scale backbone
262   models, graph convolutional networks (GCN) (Kipf & Welling, 2017), and multi-layer perceptrons
263   (MLP) (Tolstikhin et al., 2021) are employed to deal with the TAGs enhanced by the LLMs.
264   Results. As shown in Table 1 and 2, the gap between in-distribution (ID) and out-of-distribution
265   (OOD) performance is notably larger for concept degree, ranging from 4% to 12%, while for concept
266   word, the difference remains below 3%. This demonstrates that the distribution shift in terms of graph
267   structure can cause a severer impact on the performance of GCN than MLP, while the performance
268   gap of MLP is more significant for the distribution shift concerning word than that concerning
269   degree, as illustrated in Table 11, 12 and Figure 7 in Appendix E, which reveals the message passing
      mechanism in GCN can help mitigate the influence caused by feature distribution.

      In this paper, we embark on a systematic study over the effect of LLMs towards OOD challenge on
532
      TAGs. We first develop an OOD dataset on five popular TAG datasets, and then examine three LLMs
533
      pipelines on the dataset from various perspectives. Our study reveals that LLMs can help alleviate
534   OOD problem on TAGs if used properly. Specifically, to use LLMs as annotators or to leverage LLMs
535   as predictors after fine-tuning are both promising predictions with affordable economic cost. Also,
536   the design of prompts especially for graphs are essential for the effect of LLMs on TAGs. In addition,
537   TF-IDF, a traditional node embedding method, show surprisingly good generalization performance.
538   In the future, we plan to include more OOD types on a broad scope of TAGs into the developed

742   Distribution shift in machine learning is generally divided into two types: covariate shift and concept
743   shift (LEARNING; Moreno-Torres et al., 2012). Machine learning models typically predict an output
744   Y ∈ Y based on an input X ∈ X . The joint distribution P (X, Y ) between inputs and outputs can be
745   written as P (X, Y ) = P (Y |X)P (X), which constitutes of two important distribution components.
746   Covariate Shifts. The assumption is that the covariate distribution P (X) shifts while the concept
747   distribution P (Y |X) remains unchanged. The variable X is related with multiple factors, and
748   covariate shifts occur only in those factors that are unrelated to Y . Formally, a dataset can be viewed
749   as a collection of |D| domain, where the distribution of each domain can be represented as Pdi (X).
750   When a domain changes, for example, transitioning from domain d1 to domain d2 , P (Y |X) remains
751   constant while P (X) changes. For instance, in the ColoredMNIST dataset (Chen et al., 2022), the
752
      shape of the digit is the true determining factor for the digit category, whereas the color of the digit
      is an unrelated factor. Covariate shift refers to the phenomenon in which the digit’s color changes
753
      without affecting the digit’s category.
754
755   Concept Shifts. In contrast to covariate shift, concept shift takes into account the variation of the
      concept distribution P (Y |X) across splits. The variable X is composed of multiple factors, including

835
836   For the text-level enhancement, embedding-invisible LLMs are used to enrich the text attributes
837   via various prompting strategies. Recently, several papers (He et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024)
838   have explored the text-level enhancement in different manners. Here, we take TAPE (He et al.,
839   2023b) and KEA (Chen et al., 2024) as baseline methods and the illustrative examples of these two
840   augmentations are shown in Figure 4.
841   (1) TAPE (He et al., 2023b): Besides titles and abstracts, the TAPE extracts LLMs’ knowledges by
842   asking for predictions and their explanations. Given a piece of raw text, TAPE uses LLMs to directly
843   make its prediction and asks the LLM to give corresponding explanation. By generating explana-
844   tion, the TAPE edifies the LLMs to exploit the logical relationship between the text features and
845   corresponding labels. For example, as shown in Figure 4, after making the predictions “Probabilistic
846   Methods, Rule Learning”, LLMs also provide the reasons for these predictions respectively. The
847   prediction texts and the explanation texts are then encoded into embeddings, separately.
848   (2) KEA (Chen et al., 2024): The knowledge-enhanced augmentation (KEA) aims to explicitly
849   incorporate external knowledge by prompting the LLMs to generate a list of knowledge entities
850   along with their text descriptions. As illustrated in Figure 4, given one piece of raw text, the LLMs
851   are asked to extract and explain the technical terms related to artificial intelligence and machine
852   learning, instead of making direct prediction. There were two manners to use the descriptions of
853   these knowledge entities. The KEA-I make the descriptions follow the corresponding technical terms
854
      in the raw texts, while the KEA-S simply piled the descriptions of these knowledge entities. The
      enhanced texts are then encoded into input features via embedding-visible LLMs, as illustrated in
855
      Figure 3. For each kind of input features, a GNN is trained specifically. During the test phase, we can
856
      choose any GNN or ensemble some of them to make predictions.

      Observation 5: The selection of the annotated node set presents a significant challenge.
891
892   Given the high cost of prediction with LLMs, it is necessary to generate pseudo labels for only a
893   subset of nodes, which requires the prior identification of a candidate node set. The selection of
894   this set should be based on confidence levels, which can be estimated from the amount of additional
895   information available; this constitutes a critical technical challenge when LLMs are utilized as
896
      annotators. Directly using LLM outputs to assess confidence levels is not feasible, as LLMs often
      provide outputs that lack meaningful interpretation. Instead, LLMs that support prediction logits can
897
      be employed to evaluate node confidence based on the output probabilities.
898
899   Moreover, nodes with the same annotation confidence may have varying impacts on other nodes
900   within the graph. Annotating certain nodes can lead to greater performance improvements, making
901   the identification of key nodes within the dataset essential. LLMGNN integrates traditional active
902
      learning with post-filtering strategies for node selection and demonstrates the effectiveness of the
      post-filtering approach. Additionally, LLMTTT leverages the predictive signals provided by the
903
      pre-trained model to enhance node selection, with experimental results indicating that incorporating a
904
      greater amount of information indeed yields significant performance gains.
905
906   Observation 6: The accuracy of annotations by LLMs determines the ceiling of model performance.
907   Intuitively, the classification performance of the model is closely related to the precision of LLM
908   annotations. We validate this hypothesis in LLMTTT. After establishing a fixed candidate node
909   set, the accuracy of the selected nodes’ labels was deliberately controlled. It was observed that
910   following the fixation of the candidate node set, the accuracy of pseudo-labels determined the ceiling
911   of the model’s classification performance. Both LLMTTT and LLMGNN utilized a confidence-based
912   node annotation method when employing LLMs for node annotation. However, experimental results
913   indicated that the accuracy of the pseudo-labels generated by our prompts did not meet expectations,
914
      impacting the subsequent classification performance of the model. Therefore, future research should
      focus on developing a method for selecting more reliable nodes for LLMs.
915
916   Given that subsequent model fine-tuning relies on annotations from LLMs, further exploration of the
917   factors significantly influencing model fine-tuning performance is warranted. Using LLMTTT as
      an example, both LLM label accuracy (LLM acc) and GCN accuracy (GCN acc) were controlled


                                                                       17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      for the selected nodes to assess whether the impact of TTT results was solely determined by label
919   accuracy. Pseudo-labels assigned to the selected nodes were treated as true labels, incorporating
920   varying degrees of perturbation. Results presented in the Table 4 indicate that, although the pseudo-
921   label accuracy of nodes used for fine-tuning was identical, the final performance (TTT acc) varied
922   due to differences in performance gaps between GCN and LLM. A larger gap corresponded to higher

926   should be directed toward nodes with high predictive entropy from the pre-trained GNN model.
927   Observation 7: The accuracy of LLM annotations and the selection of node sets mutually influence
928   each other, necessitating a balanced approach based on practical considerations.
929
      Utilizing LLMs as annotators presents two challenges that collectively impact overall performance.
930
      Taking LLMGNN as an example, although the C-Density active node selection strategy can yield
931   higher annotation quality, relying solely on this metric may lead to suboptimal model training
932   performance. This is due to the candidate node set determined by this active selection strategy,
933   which introduces issues of label imbalance. Therefore, careful consideration must be given to both
934   annotation accuracy and the candidate node set in practical situations, as balancing these factors can
935   lead to improved model performance.
