011                                                                A BSTRACT
012
013               Exploring useful behavior is a keystone of reinforcement learning (RL). Intrinsic
014               motivation attempts to decouple exploration from external, task-based rewards.
015
                  However, existing approaches to intrinsic motivation that follow general principles
                  such as information gain, mostly uncover low-level interactions. In contrast,
016
                  children’s play suggests that they engage in meaningful high-level behavior by
017
                  imitating or interacting with their caregivers. Recent work has focused on using
018               foundation models to inject these semantic biases into exploration. However,
019               these methods often rely on unrealistic assumptions, such as environments already
020               embedded in language or access to high-level actions. To bridge this gap, we
021               propose SEmaNtically Sensible ExploratIon (S ENSEI), a framework to equip model-
022               based RL agents with intrinsic motivation for semantically meaningful behavior.
023               To do so, we distill an intrinsic reward signal of interestingness from Vision
024               Language Model (VLM) annotations. The agent learns to predict and maximize
025               these intrinsic rewards using a world model learned directly from intrinsic rewards,
026
                  image observations, and low-level actions. We show that in both robotic and
                  video game-like simulations S ENSEI manages to discover a variety of meaningful
027
                  behaviors. We believe S ENSEI provides a general tool for integrating feedback
028
                  from foundation models into autonomous agents, a crucial research direction, as
029               openly available VLMs become more powerful.1

      Thus, the goal in intrinsically-motivated reinforcement learning (RL) is for agents to explore their
055   environment efficiently and autonomously, constituting a free play phase akin to children’s curious
056   play. Various intrinsic reward definitions have been proposed in the literature, such as aiming for state
057   space coverage (Bellemare et al., 2016; Tang et al., 2017; Burda et al., 2019), novelty or retrospective
058   surprise (Pathak et al., 2017; Schmidhuber, 1991), and information gain of a world model (Pathak
059   et al., 2019; Sekar et al., 2020; Sancaktar et al., 2022). However, when an agent starts learning from
060   scratch, there is one fundamental problem: just because something is novel does not necessarily mean
061   that it contains useful or generalizable information for any sensible task (Dubey & Griffiths, 2017).
062   Imagine a robot facing a desk with several objects. The robot could explore by trying to move through
063   the entire manipulable space or hitting the desk at various speeds. In contrast, human common
064   sense would likely focus on interacting with the objects or drawer of the desk since potential task
065   distributions likely revolve around those entities.
066
      Agents exploring their environment from scratch with intrinsic motivations suffer from a chicken-or-
067   egg problem: how do you know something is interesting before you have tried it and experienced
068   interesting consequences? This is a bottleneck for the types of behavior that an agent can unlock
069   during free play. We argue that incorporating human priors into exploration could alleviate this
070   roadblock. Similar points have been raised for children’s play. During the first years of life, children
071   are surrounded by their caregivers who ideally encourage and reinforce them while they explore their
072   environment. Philosopher and psychologist Karl Groos has stipulated that there is “a strong drive in
073   children to observe the activities of their elders and incorporate those activities into their play" (Gray,
074   2017; Groos & Baldwin, 1901).
075   A potential solution in the age of Large Language Models (LLMs), is to utilize language as a
076   cultural-transmitter to inject “human notions of interestingness" (Zhang et al., 2023a) into RL agents’
077   exploration. LLMs are trained on an immense amount of data produced mostly by humans. Thus,
078   their responses are likely to mirror priors or preferences about what humans find interesting. However,
079   the most prominent works in this domain assume (1) a semantically-grounded environment (Zhang
080   et al., 2023b; Du et al., 2023), (2) in the case of M OTIF (Klissarov et al., 2023), the availability of an
081
      offline dataset with exhaustive state-space coverage and messages labeling the unfolding events or (3)
      access to high-level actions in the embodied environments considered in Zhang et al. (2023a); Du
082
      et al. (2023). These assumptions are still detached from the current reality of embodied agents, e.g. in
083
      robotics, which don’t come with perfect state or event captioners, pre-existing offline datasets nor
084   with robust, abstracted away actions. Furthermore, none of these approaches learn an internal model
085   of “interestingness.” Thus, they rely on the LLM, or a distilled module, to continuously guide their
086   exploration and fail to transfer this knowledge to novel states when LLM feedback is not available.
087
      In this work, we propose SEmaNtically Sensible ExploratIon (S ENSEI), a framework for Vision
088
      Language Models (VLM) guided exploration for model-based RL agents, illustrated in Fig. 1.
089
      S ENSEI starts with a short description of the environment and a dataset of observations (e.g. images)
090   collected through self-supervised exploration. A VLM is prompted to compare the observations
091   pairwise with respect to their interestingness and the resulting ranking is distilled into a reward
092   function. When the agent explores its environment, it receives semantically-grounded exploration
093   rewards from S ENSEI. The agent learns to predict this exploration signal through its learned world
094   model, corresponding to an internal model of “interestingness”, and improves its exploration strategy
095   based on these model-based predictions.
096   Our main contributions are as follows:
097
      • We propose S ENSEI, a framework for foundation model-guided exploration with world models.
098
099
      • We show that S ENSEI can explore rich, semantically meaningful behaviors with few prerequisites.
100   • We demonstrate that the versatile world models learned through S ENSEI enable fast learning of
101
        downstream tasks.

138   2.1      R EWARD FUNCTION DISTILLATION : MOTIF ATE YOUR SENSEI
139
140
      Prior to task-free exploration, S ENSEI needs to distill a semantically grounded intrinsic reward
      function Rψ with learnable parameters ψ based on the preferences of a pretrained VLM. While
141
      the overall framework of S ENSEI is agnostic to the exact distillation method, we chose to use a
142
      vision-based extension of M OTIF (Klissarov et al., 2023; illustrated in Fig. 2a), which we refer to as
143   VLM-M OTIF.2
144
145   M OTIF consists of two phases. In the first phase of dataset annotation, the pretrained foundation
146
      model is used to compare pairs of observations, creating a dataset of preferences. For this, we prompt
      the VLM with an environment description and provide pairs of observations from Dinit , asking
147
      the VLM which image it considers to be more interesting. The annotation function is given by the
148
      VLM : O × O → Y, where O is the space of observations, and Y = {1, 2, ∅} is a space of choices
149   for the first, second or none of the observations. In the reward training phase, a reward function
150   is derived from the VLM preferences using standard techniques from preference-based RL (Wirth
151   et al., 2017). A cross-entropy loss function is minimized on the dataset of preference pairs to learn
152   a semantically grounded reward model Rψ : O → R. We use the final semantic reward function
153   Rψ whenever the agent interacts with its environment: the agent not only receives an observation
154   ot and reward rt after executing an action at , but also receives a semantically-grounded exploration
155   reward rtsem ← Rψ (ot ) (see Fig. 1, center).

      2.2   W ORLD MODEL : L ET YOUR SENSEI DREAM
163
164   We assume a model-based setting, i.e., the agent learns a world model from its interactions. Following
165   DreamerV3 (Hafner et al., 2023), we implement the world model as a Recurrent State Space Model
166   (R SSM) (Hafner et al., 2019b). The R SSM with learnable parameters ϕ is computed by
167                                     Posterior: zt ∼ qϕ (zt | ht , ot )                                      (1)
168
                                       Dynamics: ht+1 = fϕ (at , ht , zt )                                      (2)
169
                                            Prior: ẑt+1 ∼ pϕ (ẑt+1 | ht+1 )                                   (3)
170
171   In short, the R SSM encodes all interactions through two latent states, a stochastic state zt and a
172   deterministic memory ht . At each time t, the R SSM samples a new stochastic state zt from a posterior
173
      distribution qϕ computed from the current deterministic state ht and new observation ot (Eq. 1).
      The R SSM updates its deterministic memory ht+1 based on the action at and previous latent states
174
      (Eq. 2). Next, the model predicts the next stochastic state ẑt+1 (Eq. 3). Once the new observation
175
      ot+1 is received, the next posterior qϕ is computed and the process is repeated.
176
177   Besides encoding dynamics within its latent state, the R SSM is also trained to reconstruct external
178   quantities yt from its latent state via output heads oϕ :
179                    Output heads: ŷt ∼ oϕ (ŷt | ht , zt ) with yt ∈ {ot , ct , rt , rtsem }                (4)
180
181   The R SSM of DreamerV3 (Hafner et al., 2023) reconstructs observations ot , episode continuations
182
      ct , and rewards rt . For S ENSEI, we additionally predict the semantic exploration reward rtsem . The
      world model is trained end-to-end to jointly optimize the evidence lower bound.
183
184   Thus, our world model learns to predict semantic interestingness r̂tsem of states (see Fig. 1, right).
185   We could base exploration exclusively on this signal. However, we expect to face many local optima
186   when optimizing for this signal and we do not want to only explore a fixed set of behaviors, but ensure
187   that the agent goes for interesting and yet novel states. To overcome this limitation, Klissarov et al.
188
      (2023) post-process rtsem and normalize it by episodic event message counts. As we do not assume
      ground-truth countable event captions, we instead combine our new reward signal with epistemic
189
      uncertainty, a quantity that was shown to be an effective objective for model-based exploration (Sekar
190
      et al., 2020; Pathak et al., 2017; Sancaktar et al., 2022). Following Plan2Explore (Sekar et al., 2020),
191   we train an ensemble of N models with weights {θ1 , . . . , θN } to predict the next stochastic latent
192   states with
193                               Ensemble predictor: ẑtn ∼ gθn (ẑtn | ht , zt , at ).                   (5)
194   We quantify epistemic uncertainty as ensemble disagreement by computing the variance over the
195   ensemble predictions averaged over latent state dimensions J and use it as the new reward term rtdis :

202   2.3   E XPLORATION POLICY: G O AND E XPLORE WITH SENSEI
203
      We could use a weighted sum of the two intrinsic reward signals, e.g. rtsem + βrtdis , as the overall
204
      reward rtexpl for optimizing an exploration policy. However, ideally the weighting of the two signals
205
      should dynamically depend on the situation. In uninteresting states we want the agent to mostly focus
206   on optimizing interestingness (via rtsem ). However, once the agent has found an interesting state, we
207   would like the agent to branch out and discover new behavior (via rtdis ). This follows the principle
208   of Go-Explore (Ecoffet et al., 2021), where the agent should first go to an important subgoal and
209   explore from there. We implement this using an adaptive threshold parameter β ∈ {β go , β explore },
210   where β explore > β go , whose value depends on the following switching criteria:
211                                                explore dis
                                                  β        rt , if r̂tsem ≥ Qk (r̂sem );
212                            rtexpl = r̂tsem +    go dis                                                (7)
                                                  β rt ,        otherwise.
213
214   Here Qk denotes the k th quantile of r̂sem , which we estimate through an exponential moving average.
215   Thus, until a certain level of r̂sem is reached, the exploration reward mainly aims at maximizing inter-
      estingness. After exceeding this threshold, exploration more strongly favors uncertainty-maximizing

      behaviors. As soon as the agent enters a less interesting state with r̂sem < Qk , S ENSEI switches
217   back to mainly optimizing for semantic interestingness. The two trade-off factors β go and β explore ,
218   as well as the quantile k are hyperparameters. More details on this adaptation and hyperparameters
219   can be found in Suppl. B. We then learn an exploration policy based on rtexpl using the DreamerV3
220   algorithm (Hafner et al., 2023).

223   3    R ELATED WORK
224
225   Intrinsic rewards are applied either to facilitate exploration in tasks where direct rewards are
226   sparse or in a task-agnostic setting where they help collect diverse data. There are many different
227   reward signals that could be useful for efficient exploration of the environment (Baldassarre &
228
      Mirolli, 2013), such as prediction error (Schmidhuber, 1991; Pathak et al., 2017; Kim et al., 2020),
      novelty and Bayesian surprise (Storck et al., 1995; Blaes et al., 2019; Paolo et al., 2021), learning
229
      progress (Schmidhuber, 1991; Colas et al., 2019; Blaes et al., 2019), empowerment (Klyubin et al.,
230
      2005; Mohamed & Jimenez Rezende, 2015), metrics for state-space coverage (Bellemare et al., 2016;
231   Tang et al., 2017; Burda et al., 2019) and regularity (Sancaktar et al., 2024). While effective for
232   low-dimensional observations, such objectives could be more challenging to apply in the case of
233   high-dimensional image observations. An approach for exploration in image-based environments
234   is to employ low-dimensional goal spaces (Colas et al., 2019; OpenAI et al., 2021; Nair et al., 2018;
235   Pong et al., 2019; Zadaianchuk et al., 2021; Mendonca et al., 2021). An alternative, more sample
236   efficient, direction, is to learn latent world models (Hafner et al., 2019a; 2023; Gumbsch et al., 2024)
237   from visual observations and use these world models for model-based exploration (Pathak et al.,
238   2019; Sekar et al., 2020). In particular, Plan2Explore (Sekar et al., 2020) uses ensemble disagreement
239
      of latent space dynamics predictions as an intrinsic reward. While this is a very general strategy for
      exploration, this could be limited in more challenging environments where semantically meaningful
240
      or goal-directed behavior (Spelke, 1990) is needed for efficient exploration.
241
242   Exploration with foundation models: Recent improvements of in-context learning of LLMs open ad-
243   ditional ways to explore using human bias of interestingness during exploration (Klissarov et al., 2023;
244   Du et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a) and skill learning (Colas et al., 2020; 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b).
245
      M OTIF (Klissarov et al., 2023) leverages LLMs to generate intrinsic rewards by evaluating pairs of
      event captions to derive rewards, demonstrating its efficacy in the complex game of NetHack (Küttler
246
      et al., 2020). This approach has shown that intrinsic rewards can sometimes outperform direct reward
247
      maximization strategies. Similarly, ELLM (Du et al., 2023) uses LLMs to guide RL agents towards
248   goals that are meaningful and useful, based on the agent’s current state (represented by text), showing
249   improved task coverage in the Crafter environment (Hafner, 2021). Furthermore, OMNI (Zhang et al.,
250   2023a) introduces a novel method to prioritize tasks by modeling human notions of interestingness
251   using LLMs. Thereby, OMNI enhances the open-ended learning process by focusing on tasks that
252   are not only learnable but also generally interesting. LAMP (Adeniji et al., 2023) proposes to use
253   VLMs for reward modulation in an RL setup. First, a set of potential tasks are generated with an
254   LLM and then LAMP uses VLMs to generate rewards for these tasks to learn a language-conditioned
255   policy in the pretraining phase. This policy is later finetuned with actual task rewards.
256   Reward-shaping through VLMs: Most works that rely on VLMs as reward sources try to solve
257   the reward specification problem in RL. In these works, often a task is assumed to be described as a
258   language caption (Cui et al., 2022; Rocamonde et al., 2023; Baumli et al., 2023; Adeniji et al., 2023),
259   as a goal image (either in-distribution or out-of-distribution) (Cui et al., 2022), or as a demonstration
260   video of the task (Sontakke et al., 2023). In particular, RL-VLM-F (Wang et al., 2024) uses a
261   very similar setup to ours to generate reward functions. Images of initial rollouts are compared
262
      pairwise using a VLM to distill a reward function via M OTIF (Klissarov et al., 2023). However,
      unlike in our work, the VLM is explicitly prompted with the task, whereas we attempt to distill an
263
      environment-specific but general exploration reward. Furthermore, S ENSEI assumes a model-based
264
      setup to learn a world model instead of optimizing a policy based on the distilled reward function.

322   the art in model-based exploration with pixel-based observations. We collect the initial exploration
323   dataset Dinit of S ENSEI with 500k steps of Plan2Explore exploration for MiniHack and 1M steps for
      Robodesk. For data annotation, we use GPT-4 (details in Suppl. C.3).

464   4.3        FAST DOWNSTREAM TASK LEARNING
465
466   We hypothesize that world models learned from richer exploration would enable model-based RL
467   agents to quickly learn to solve new downstream tasks. We investigate this in Minihack by running
468   DreamerV3 (Hafner et al., 2023) using the previously explored world models to learn a novel task-
469   based policy. To this end, we jump-start policy training by initializing DreamerV3 with the pre-trained
470   world models from our initial 500K steps of exploration (see Sec. 4.2). We compare world models
471
      from task-free exploration with either S ENSEI or Plan2Explore. Additionally, we compare running
      DreamerV3 and training Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO, Schulman et al. 2017), a state-of-the-art
472
      model-free baseline, from scratch.
473
474   Figure 6 shows the performance of task-based policies over training. A previously explored world
475   model from S ENSEI allows the agent to learn to solve the task faster than all other baselines. As
476   S ENSEI allocates more resources to explore the relevant dynamics in the environment compared to
477
      Plan2Explore, e.g. opening the chest more frequently instead of just being near the chest, a more useful
      world model is learned that later aids policy optimization. In addition, the world models explored
478
      with Plan2Explore do not capture the task-relevant dynamics of the environment as accurately, which
479
      slows down policy learning compared to S ENSEI. Compared to learning a task policy from scratch
480   with DreamerV3, we do not observe a consistent trend of improvement from task-free exploration
481   with Plan2Explore. The model-free baseline PPO shows the first successes in the tasks early during
482   training but on average takes longer to learn to reliably solve the task. In KeyRoom, for example,
483   PPO takes more than 20M steps to consistently solve the task across all random seeds (full PPO
484   curves in Supp. Fig. 10). Thus, in this task S ENSEI outperforms PPO by roughly two orders of
485   magnitude. This shows the improved sample efficiency of our approach, combining foundation
      model-guided exploration and model-based RL.

      5   D ISCUSSION
487
488
489   We have introduced S ENSEI, a framework for guiding the intrinsically motivated exploration of
490
      model-based agents through foundation models without assuming access to expert data, high-level
      actions, or perfect environment captions. S ENSEI bootstraps its model of interestingsness from
491
      previously generated play data with e.g. information gain. On this dataset, S ENSEI prompts a VLM
492
      to compare images with respect to their interestingness and distills a reward function for semantically
493   grounded exploration. S ENSEI learns an exploration policy via model-based RL using two sources of
494   intrinsic rewards: (1) trying to reach states with high semantic interestingness and (2) branching out
495   from these states to maximize epistemic uncertainty. We show that in a simulated robotic environment,
496   this strategy leads to more object manipulations than pure information gain-oriented exploration.
497   Similarly, we demonstrate that S ENSEI discovers more semantically meaningful interactions, such as
498   applying a key to open a chest, when exploring the video game-like environments of MiniHack. In
499   both environments, S ENSEI accumulates more rewards than the state-of-the-art exploration method
500   Plan2Explore (Sekar et al., 2020), by “accidentally” solving human-designed sparse reward tasks
501
      already during exploration.
502
503   Internal model of interestingness In contrast to other methods of foundation model-guided
504   exploration (Klissarov et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024), S ENSEI combines semantic exploration with
505   world model learning, thereby learning an internal model of “interestingness.” This is a sensible
506   design choice when working with world models (as detailed in Suppl. A.3). Previous variants of
507   M OTIF (Klissarov et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024) require the complete observation in order to
508   compute the foundation model-based reward. After training, S ENSEI can predict semantic rewards
509   from its latent states, thus, also evaluating imagined and hypothetical states. As demonstrated by our
510   experiments, this can lead to significantly faster policy learning, since both VLM guidance as well as
511
      model-based RL improve sample efficiency.
512
513   Future work We hypothesize that S ENSEI learns a versatile world model from exploration, as it is
514   trained on a more interaction-rich and interesting dataset which could enable the agent to quickly
515   learn to solve downstream tasks. We showcase this in the MiniHack tasks KeyRoom-S15 and
516   KeyChest. In future work, we plan to examine this for zero-shot model-based planning (Sancaktar
517   et al., 2022). Furthermore, in Suppl. D.2, by comparing two sources for the initial play dataset
518   Dinit , we observe that S ENSEI reinforces the trends existing in the initial exploration round, while
519   still seeing improvements across different types of interactions. To further reinforce more complex
520
      types of interactions, our reward function could be refined in a new round of VLM annotations
      from a S ENSEI run. As a result, S ENSEI could potentially unlock increasingly complex sequences
521
      of behavior with each generation.We believe S ENSEI has the potential to be applied to realistic
522
      simulations or real-world tasks. Photorealism of observations are likely to help VLM annotations
523   because a large portion of their training data comes from real world photos or videos. Thus, we think
524   that S ENSEI could scale well to more realistic applications.
525
526
527   Limitations S ENSEI benefits from fully-observable observations, e.g. images that capture all
528   relevant aspects of the environment. The VLM annotations, and as a result the distilled reward
529
      function, degrade when dealing with occlusions and the lack of depth information from a single
      image in visually-complex environments such as Robodesk. In this work, we used a second camera
530
      angle for VLM annotations in Robodesk to reduce noise. We believe this can be remedied further in
531
      future work by using videos for VLM annotations to better convey temporal or partially observable
532   information.

763   A.1    W ORLD MODEL
764
765   RSSM We base our R SSM implementation on DreamerV3 (Hafner et al., 2023). For MiniHack we
766   use the small model size setting with roughly 18M parameters (ht dimensions: 512, CNN multiplier:
767   32, dense hidden units: 512, MLP layers: 2). For the more complicated Robodesk environment, we
768   use the medium model size with around 37M parameters (ht dimensions: 1024, CNN multiplier:
769   48, dense hidden units: 640, MLP layers: 3). By default, when the input observation ot is only an
770
      image, it is en- and decoded through CNNs. For MiniHack, we have an additional inventory flag that
      is processed by a separate MLP, as is customary for the Dreamer line of work when dealing with
771
      multimodal inputs (Wu et al., 2023). The MLP decoder outputs a Bernoulli distribution from which
772
      we sample the decoded inventory flag.
773
774   Reward predictors To handle rewards of widely varying magnitudes, DreamerV3 uses twohot
775   codes predicted in symlog space when predicting rewards (Hafner et al., 2023). We use the same setup
776   for all reward prediction heads, i.e., for extrinsic rewards rti for task i or the semantic exploration
777   reward rtsem . During task-free exploration, the gradients from reward predictions are stopped to not
778   further affect world model training. We do this to keep the world model somewhat task-agnostic to
779   later reuse it for multiple tasks. Similarly, to avoid overfitting to the exploration regime, we also stop
780   the gradients from the semantic reward prediction heads.
781
782   Plan2Explore Both our Plan2Explore baseline as well as our ensemble predictors (Eq. 5) are based
783
      on the re-implementation on top of DreamerV3. The most notable difference is that in original
      Plan2Explore the ensemble is trained to predict image encodings (Sekar et al., 2020), whereas the
784
      new version is trained to predict stochastic states zt . Recent re-implementations (Hafner, 2021;
785
      Hafner et al., 2022; Gumbsch et al., 2024) also used Plan2Explore with ensemble disagreement over
786   zt as a baseline and verified a strong exploration performance.
787
788   Quantile estimation We update our estimate of the quantile Qk (r̂sem ) whenever we train the
789   exploration policy. For this, we compute the k-th quantile of r̂tsem in each training batch (16 × 16).
790   We keep an exponential moving average over these estimates with a smoothing factor of α = 0.99.
791
792   Reward weighting       In practice, we compute exploration rewards (Eq. 7) using two reward factors
793   for each loss term

800   A.2    S EMANTIC R EWARD D ISTILLATION : VLM-M OTIF
801
802   For the semantic reward function Rψ : O → R, we use a 2D-convolutional neural network to encode
803   the images. We use 3 convolutional layers, where we progressively increase the number of channels
804   to num_channels_max = 64. The output then gets downsampled via max pooling before going
805   into a two-layer MLP with hidden dimensions 256 & 512 and outputting the scalar reward value.
806
      Additionally, in MiniHack we include inventory information via a separate multi-layer perceptron
      (MLP) head, consisting of 2 layers with 512 hidden units. The extracted features are concatenated
807
      with the image features and get further processed by the output MLP. The training hyperparameters
808
      for all Rψ can be found in Suppl. B.

      A.3    D ESIGN C HOICE : S EMANTIC R EWARD P REDICTIONS
811
812
      World models typically encode and predict dynamics fully in a self-learned latent state (Ha &
813   Schmidhuber, 2018; Hafner et al., 2023; Hansen et al., 2024). Thus, for a world model to predict
814   rtsem at any point in time t, we need a mapping from latent states to semantic rewards. We chose
815   to directly predict r̂tsem using a reward prediction head of the R SSM. Another option would be to
816   decode the latent state to images and use those as inputs for M OTIF. However, we believe this has
817   several disadvantages: 1) Decoding latent states to images is a computationally costly step that would
818   significantly decrease our S ENSEI’s computational efficiency. 2) We would use an indirect target (the
819   image) instead of the direct target (r̂tsem ) for training the semantic reward predictions. There would
820   be no gradient to correct somewhat reasonable image predictions that lead to inconsistent reward
821
      predictions at a given state. 3) The image predictions of the R SSM can contain artifacts, blurriness
      or hallucinations. Since M OTIF is only trained on real images from the simulation, we will likely
822
      encounter out-of-distribution errors.

952   Observations In MiniHack multiple observation and action spaces are possible. We use egocentric,
953   pixel-based observations centered on the agent (±2 grids, example in Fig. 8c). In addition to that,
954   we provide the agent’s inventory. By default, in MiniHack the inventory is given as an array of
955
      strings (UTF8 encoded), and different player characters have different starting equipment based on
      the character classes of NetHack. We simplify this by providing only a binary flag that indicates if the
956
      agent has picked up a new item. This is sufficient for the problems we consider, in which maximally
957
      one new item can be collected and starting equipment cannot be used.

959
960   Environments Here we detail the environments we tackle:
961   In the benchmark KeyRoom-S15 problem (Fig. 8a), the agent needs to fetch a key in a large room
962   (15 × 15 grids) to enter a smaller room and find a staircase to exit the dungeon. We use the default
963   action space but enable autopickup and therefore remove the PICKUP action. We use an episode
964   length of 600 time steps, which is 1.5 times longer than the default episode length.
965
      KeyChest is a novel environment designed by us, based on KeyCorridorS4R3 from MiniGrid
966   (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2024) (see Fig. 8b). The agent starts in a corridor randomly connected to
967   different rooms. A key is hidden in one room and a chest in another room. The goal is to open the
968   chest with the key in the inventory. Object positions are randomized. The action space for this task
969   contains 5 discrete actions for moving the agent in 4 cardinal directions (UP, RIGHT, DOWN, LEFT)
970   and an OPEN-action to open a chest when standing next to it with a key in the inventory. We enable
971   auto-pickup, so no additional action is needed to pick up the key when stepping on it. We use an
      episode length of 800 time steps.
