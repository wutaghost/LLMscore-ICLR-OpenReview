011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            A major challenge in causal inference from observational data is the absence of
014            perfect interventions, making it difficult to distinguish causal features from spu-
015
               rious ones. We propose an innovative approach, Feature Matching Intervention
               (FMI), which uses a matching procedure to mimic perfect interventions. We
016
               define causal latent graphs, extending structural causal models to latent feature
017
               space, providing a framework that connects FMI with causal graph learning. Our
018            feature matching procedure emulates perfect interventions within these causal
019            latent graphs. Theoretical results demonstrate that FMI exhibits strong out-of-
020            distribution (OOD) generalizability. Experiments further highlight FMI’s superior
021            performance in effectively identifying causal features solely from observational
022            data.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Causal representation learning (Schölkopf et al., 2021) aims to uncover causal features from obser-
      vations of high-dimensional data, and is emerging as a prominent field at the intersection of deep
028
      learning and causal inference. Unlike traditional causal effect of a specific treatment variable, causal
029
      representation learning does not treat any observed variable as a potential causal parent. Instead, it
030   focuses on transforming the observational space into a low-dimensional space to identify causal
031   parents.
032
033
      However, despite its promise, recent years have witnessed notable shortcomings in effectively cap-
      turing genuine causal features, particularly evident in tasks such as image classification. Numerous
034
      experiments over the past decade (Geirhos et al., 2020; Pezeshki et al., 2021; Beery et al., 2018;
035
      Nagarajan et al., 2020) have highlighted the failure of models to discern essential features, resulting
036   in a phenomenon where models optimized on training data exhibit catastrophic performance when
037   tested on unseen environments. This failure stems from the reliance of models on spurious features
038   within the data, such as background color in images, rather than the genuine features essential for ac-
039   curate classification, such as the inherent properties of objects depicted in the images. Consequently,
040   models are susceptible to errors, particularly when faced with adversarial examples.
041
      The phenomenon described above is commonly known as out-of-distribution (OOD), with efforts
042   to mitigate it termed as out-of-distribution generalization or domain generalization. To tackle this
043   challenge, numerous approaches have been proposed. Among the most significant concepts is the
044   invariance principle from causality (Peters et al., 2016; Pearl, 1995), which forms the basis of in-
045   variant risk minimization (IRM) (Arjovsky et al., 2019). IRM was the first to operationalize this
046   principle, aiming to identify invariant features through data from multiple environments. The in-
047   variance principle dictates the optimal predictor based on invariant features, ensuring minimal risk
048   across any given environment (Rojas-Carulla et al., 2018; Koyama & Yamaguchi, 2020; Ahuja et al.,
049   2020). Additionally, several works have extended IRM by imposing extra constraints on the invari-
050
      ance principle (e.g., Krueger et al. (2021); Ahuja et al. (2021); Chevalley et al. (2022)).
051   Despite the promise of IRM and the invariance principle under certain assumptions, subsequent
052   research has revealed their limitations (Rosenfeld et al., 2020). Moreover, invariance does not nec-
053   essarily imply causality universally. All state-of-the-art methods struggle to distinguish between
      spurious and true features without a perfect intervention. In other words, the absence of perfect

054
      interventions is the primary challenge for all current approaches, as it makes it difficult to reliably
055   distinguish between spurious and true features.
056
057   In this paper, we propose a very simple alternative approach to learning causal representations
058
      through covariate matching. This approach attempts to emulate perfect interventions, which is
      known to be a difficult problem. Our method eliminates the need for multiple environmental datasets
059
      and does not rely on the use of an invariance algorithm. We make only the verifiable assumption
060
      that spurious features are present in the training data, a scenario commonly encountered in practice.
061   While covariate matching is a traditional method in the statistics literature, it has been less explored
062   in causal representation learning. Leveraging the causal latent graph introduced later in the paper,
063   our matching algorithm offers an explicit interpretation of emulating perfect intervention on the
064   spurious feature. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach through unit tests (Aubin et al.,
065   2021) and experiments on image datasets. Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
066
067
      Contributions. (1) We propose an innovative and straightforward algorithm – FMI based on co-
068
      variate matching in the presence of spurious feature in the training data. By emulating perfect
069   intervention on the spurious feature, we are able to learn underlying causal feature (2) We propose
070   an approach to test the assumption of spurious feature being learned in the training environment. (3)
071   We validate our matching algorithm using a causal latent graph. (4) Our experiments on unit tests,
072   Colored MNIST, and WaterBirds datasets demonstrate the superior performance of our algorithm
073   compared to state-of-the-art methods.

      3   R ELATED W ORK
163
164
165   We summarize below some relevant works from previous literature.
166
167
      Invariance-based Domain Generalization Numerous works in the literature have studied do-
168   main generalization, a problem closely related to causal inference. Many of these works aim to
169   discover the invariant predictor, as demonstrated by studies such as Arjovsky et al. (2019); Ahuja
170   et al. (2021); Chevalley et al. (2022); Yuan et al. (2023). The concept of invariance originates from
171   causal inference as a necessary condition of causal variables (Peters et al., 2016; Bühlmann, 2020).
172   The fundamental idea behind these methods is the utilization of data from multiple environments
173   (domains), either through deliberate design or collection. For instance, Arjovsky et al. (2019) ne-
174   cessitates the environments to be in linear general position. However, these approaches exhibit a
175   voracious appetite for environments, and the invariance principle reduces the objective function to
176
      that of the standard empirical risk minimization (ERM) (Vapnik, 1991) when only one environment
      is available.
177
178
179   Causal Representation Learning Ahuja et al. (2023) provided identifiability results of latent
180   causal factors using interventional data. Buchholz et al. (2024) demonstrated identifiability results
181   under the assumption of a linear latent graphical model. Additionally, Jiang & Aragam (2024)
182   established conditions under which latent causal graphs are nonparametrically identifiable and can
183   be reconstructed from unknown interventions in the latent space. These results motivated us to
184   emulate intervention with observational data and therefore achieve causal representation learning.
185   The most relevant works to ours are MatchDG (Mahajan et al., 2021) and Chevalley et al. (2022).
186   In MatchDG, the authors employ a matching function to pair corresponding objects across domains
187   and seek features with zero distance on these matched objects while minimizing the loss on the
188   training data. However, there are at least two distinctions between our approach and MatchDG: (1)
189   We do not require data from multiple domains; (2) Our approach matches training data based on
190   their classification results of the spurious features extracted by the subnetwork, eliminating the need
191
      for any matching function.
192   In Chevalley et al. (2022), the authors randomly partition each minibatch into two groups and pe-
193   nalize the distance between the latent features learned from each group. In contrast, our method
194   does not necessitate random separation; Instead, it emulates perfect intervention, a guarantee not
195   provided in the previous work.

198   4   S INGLE T RAINING E NVIRONMENT: F EATURE M ATCHING I NTERVENTION
199       (FMI)
200

      In this section, we introduce a novel algorithm called Feature Matching Intervention (FMI) for causal
202   representation learning that solves problem (2). The idea behind FMI is that, since ERM builds the
203   model with the spurious feature, why not exploit this additional information and use the result of
204   ERM to control this spurious feature through a matching procedure? Matching has been a well-
205   known method in the causal inference literature for estimating treatment effects from observational
206   data (Stuart, 2010). Leveraging this concept, we aim to develop a method for matching the spurious
207   feature in the training environment and then training the model after this matching process. With
208   this matching procedure, we can emulate perfect intervention on the spurious feature. Example 1
209   demostrates this matching idea.
210   Example 1. Consider the task of classifying images containing the digits 0 and 1. Suppose a large
211   proportion of images with the digit 0 happen to be colored red, and a large proportion of images
212   with the digit 1 happen to be colored green. Then the Bayes classifier in the training environment
213   would be based on color. Figure 3 visualizes the classification result in the training environment
214   as well as the matching process. Clearly, after the matching process, the label and the color be-
215   come uncorrelated. Therefore, the matching corresponds to an intervention on the spurious feature
      (color).

      7                 D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
527
528   The success of FMI leads us to contemplate the root cause of poor generalizability in domain gen-
529   eralization. From our observations, the generalizability issue primarily arises from the bias of the
530   training data itself. While modern AI models can easily fit any complex functional relationship, they
531   can be ’misled’ by the training data. Whenever there is a strong spurious signal in the training data,
532   the model tends to rely on it. However, the intervention suggested by FMI can help the model elim-
533   inate the influence of such spurious features. Under these circumstances, FMI manifests superior
534   performance and there may be no need to collect data from multiple domains. It is worth noting that
535   the generalizability issues induced by covariate shift are not due to spurious features. Therefore, it
536
      is doubtful whether FMI can be applied to cases where covariate shift is present. As future work, we
      will explore scenarios where there are multiple spurious features, which could be challenging since
537
      emulating perfect interventions on multiple spurious features is not straightforward.

1030   Invariance principle was defined in Arjovsky et al. (2019, Definition 3).
1031   Definition 3. We say that a data representation Φ : X → H elicits an invariant predictor w ◦ Φ
1032   across environments E if there is a classifier w : H → Y simultaneously optimal for all environ-
1033   ments, that is, w ∈ arg minw̄:H→Y Re (w̄ ◦ Φ) for all e ∈ E.

1086   Definition 4 (Structural causal models). A structural causal model (SCM) C := (S, PN ) consists of
1087   a collection S of d (structural) assignments

1092   product distribution.

1094   from each parent in PAj to Xj , that is, from each variable Xk occurring on the right-hand side of
