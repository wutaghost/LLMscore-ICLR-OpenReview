009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep neural networks can obtain impressive performance on various tasks under
012            the assumption that their training domain is identical to their target domain. Perfor-
013            mance can drop dramatically when this assumption does not hold. One explanation
014            for this discrepancy is the presence of spurious domain-specific correlations in the
015            training data that the network exploits. Causal mechanisms, in the other hand, can
016            be made invariant under distribution changes as they allow disentangling the factors
017            of distribution underlying the data generation. Yet, learning causal mechanisms
018
               to improve out-of-distribution generalisation remains an under-explored area. We
               propose a Bayesian neural architecture that disentangles the learning of the the
019
               data distribution from the inference process mechanisms. We show theoretically
020
               and experimentally that our model approximates reasoning under causal inter-
021            ventions. We demonstrate the performance of our method, outperforming point
022            estimate-counterparts, on out-of-distribution image recognition tasks where the
023            data distribution acts as strong adversarial confounders.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The training of deep neural networks commonly relies on the assumption that the distribution of
029   the training data is representative of the distribution at inference. Despite being widely adopted,
030   this assumption has been heavily criticised as it is often challenged in practice (Langford, 2005;
031   Ben-David et al., 2006; Albuquerque et al., 2019; Arjovsky et al., 2019; Jalaldoust & Bareinboim,
032   2024). Indeed, despite tremendous progress on many vision tasks over recent years, deep neural
033
      networks face limitations and reduced performance on tasks requiring the model to shift from its
      training distribution at test time (Mao et al., 2022).
034
035   Causality theory under the do-calculus framework (Huang & Valtorta, 2006; Pearl, 2009) provides
036   tools to explain these limitations. Neural networks extract correlation patterns but do not possess
037   knowledge on the cause-effect relationships from the data; causal links and potentially spurious
038   correlations are learned alike. However, the two relationships fundamentally differ as correlations can
039
      be non-causal: e.g. a correlation between X and Y can be explained by a causal link X ← Z → Y .
      This type of correlation can be specific to the distribution if Y depends on the domain, e.g. has tusks
040
      and a prehensile trunk ← African bush elephant → is in a savannah environment. A system only
041
      learning the correlation between X and Y may fail to recognise an elephant in a different environment.
042   However, direct causal links make these relationships explicit and are therefore more robust to changes
043   in the distribution (Schölkopf et al., 2021). In particular, the Independent Causal Mechanisms
044   principle states that causal relationships are only sparsely connected and altering one should not
045   modify the others (Peters et al., 2017; Schölkopf et al., 2021), allowing some learned mechanisms to be
046   transported to new environments. Indeed, changes in the distribution can be modelled via the notion
047   of transportability of causal mechanisms (Bareinboim & Pearl, 2013; Jalaldoust & Bareinboim, 2024).
048   As such, transportable causal effects are equivalent to domain-invariant features. These principles
049   motivate the search for factorised neural models composed of independent modules representing
050
      either domain-specific or domain-invariant conditional distributions.
051   Bayesian neural networks (BNNs) (MacKay, 1992; Blundell et al., 2015b; Magris & Iosifidis, 2023)
052   are another line of work attempting to model robust representations of mechanisms by learning the
053   distribution W of a parametric function y = fw (x), w ∼ W instead of a single point estimate w.
      BNNs are particular suited to express uncertainty in their response and reduce overconfidence. By

088   We propose a Causal-Invariant Bayesian (CIB) neural network taking advantage of the causal
089
      structure of the supervised learning process to differentiate domain-specific and domain-invariant
      mechanisms. Compared to previous work, we integrate a more realistic causal graph that takes into
090
      account the Bayesian model update during learning. We use variational inference and partially-
091
      stochastic Bayesian neural networks to model the causal paths in an interventional setting. We verify
092   theoretically and experimentally the applicability of our method. In particular, we perform tests on
093   standard out-of-distribution (o.o.d) image recognition tasks. Our contributions are as follows:
094
095          • We reformulate the Bayesian inference problem to include causal interventions and the
096            supervised learning mechanism and propose a factorised model explicitly modelling domain-
097            invariant mechanisms in an unsupervised fashion,
098
             • We propose an architecture wrapped around a neural network inspired by this factorised
099            model and test it on challenging tasks requiring domain shifts,
100
101          • We show that adding our model can increase the i.i.d and o.o.d performance of the underlying
102            neural network and reduce overfitting,
103          • Additionally, we find with our model that adding unsupervised contextual information with
104            a Bayesian classifier to a standard ResNet improves its robustness and training stability,
105            even with a small number of context and Bayesian weight samples.

      Causal Inference and Transportability Causal inference methods aim to estimate the result of
      causal queries. Answering these queries require an inference model to have a partial knowledge of
112
      the causal mechanisms underlying the studied system. The causal knowledge is typically divided into
113
      three layers: observational (l1), interventional (l2) and counterfactual (l3) (Pearl, 2009). Bareinboim
114   et al. (2022) showed that these layers form a non-collapsing hierarchy, i.e. information at level i
115   is needed to answer a question at level i. Richens & Everitt (2024) further showed that an agent
116   must learn a causal model of the world (at level l2) to robustly solve a task, e.g. subject to changes
117   of domain. The problem of formally representing causal quantities is addressed by the do-calculus
118   framework, which introduces the do(·) operator corresponding to an intervention on a causal variable
119   (Pearl, 2009). For example, given a treatment T and background covariates X, the probability
120   of getting cured given by C can be expressed with the query P (C|do(T ), X). The do-operation
121   implies that the treatment is received in an unbiased manner (i.e. as in a double-blind study). An
122
      interventional query as described in the example can be reduced to observations using the rules of do-
      calculus. We describe them in Appendix A.1. Transportability theory furthermore allows representing
123
      causal mechanisms across multiple domains (Bareinboim & Pearl, 2013) by differentiating domain-
124
      specific and domain-invariant variables in a causal graph. Assuming the domains can be represented
125   with a unified causal graph, domain-specific mechanisms can be modelled by a selection variable
126   whose value depends on the current domain. The S-admissibility criterion (Jalaldoust & Bareinboim,
127   2024) states that the value of a causal variable A is invariant when shifting from a domain Mi to
128   a domain Mj if it can be d-separated from all selection variables Sij (given observations Z), i.e.
129   A⊥ ⊥ Sij |Z =⇒ P i (A|Z) = P j (A|Z). Mao et al. (2022) also integrate causal transportability for
130   vision tasks but do not include the Bayesian learning process. They combine input and contextual
131   information differently and only select context with the same label during training. Conversely, we
132   make the assumption that the context should be diverse and representative of the label distribution.
133   We use label mixup to this end. We also include new regularisation techniques.
134
135   Disentanglement A large body of work attempting to disentangle domain-specific and domain-
136   invariant information rely on an autoencoding paradigm and reconstruct the input image as part of
137   their training process (Gabbay et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022). For instance, Zhang
138   et al. (2022) build a latent space regularised to divide domain-invariant semantic features from a
139   domain-specific variation space. Similarly to our work, the authors add contextual information by
140
      feeding different samples to a variation space encoder to improve domain generalisation. However,
      they mainly rely on a reconstruction loss whereas we argue that this component is not needed
141
      for building robust representations. van Steenkiste et al. (2019) argued that having disentangled
142
      representations improved systematic generalisation in abstract visual reasoning tasks. The authors
143   also found that a low reconstruction error was not necessary for performance on downstream tasks.
144   Our work differs from the disentanglement literature by detaching from the autoencoding paradigm
145   and adopting a causal Bayesian approach.

148

149

152   optimised but sampled from a learned posterior distribution P (w|D). This distribution can be

      Izmailov et al. (2021) showed that the posterior distribution of BNNs have a high functional diversity
163   and can outperform their point-estimate counterparts on downstream tasks. However, they do not
164   necessarily show a high diversity in the parameters space and strictly following Bayesian posteriors

167   recently showed that inducing BNNs to assign similar posterior densities to reparametrisations of the
168   same functions (i.e. different weights realising the same function) could improve their ability to fit
169   the data. These findings motivate our work on integrating BNNs as a part of a larger causal neural
170   network.
171
172   Mixup Input Mixup (Zhang et al., 2018) and Manifold Mixup (Verma et al., 2019) are regularisation
173
      techniques for improving robustness in image classification tasks by interpolating inputs, respectively
      latent representations. The main principle of mixup consists of combining two inputs (xi , xj ) and
174
      labels (yi , yj ) together to form a new virtual training example x with label y: x = α · xi + (1 − α) · xj
175
      and y = α · yi + (1 − α) · yj (with α ∈ [0, 1] constant). Manifold Mixup performs a similar operation
176   but interpolates latent representations instead of the inputs. Gendron et al. (2023) argued that mixing
177   could be used to include interventional information into the network’s training. Following these
178   observations, we integrate mixup strategies as part of our training scheme.

      We now propose our Causal-Invariant Bayesian (CIB) architecture estimating the posterior probability
308   defined above. The architecture is summarised in Figure 2. It can be decomposed into three
309   components:
310
311          • A variational encoder (in blue) learning domain-invariant information R by jointly optimis-
312            ing the inference loss with an ELBO loss,
313          • An inference module (in orange) combining domain-specific and domain-invariant informa-
314            tion to solve the task,
315
             • A weight distribution W (in green) learned by optimising the inference objective with an
316            ELBO loss and from which are sampled the parameters of the inference module.
317
318   Variational Encoder The variational encoder is used to learn the intermediate domain-invariant
319   representations R and {Ri′ }Ni=1 . The network weights are learned by optimising an estimate lower
320   bound (ELBO) loss. Existing work attempting to disentangle domain-specific and domain-invariant
321   features typically use a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) and add a reconstruction loss (Gabbay et al.,
322   2021; Yang et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022). The input and the contextual information are fed
323   to two different encoders, learning to specialise to invariant features and domain-specific features,
      respectively. However, we find that a single encoder can yield better performance. By jointly giving


                                                                       6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      the input and the context to the encoder, we teach it to discard irrelevant domain-specific features
325   at once. We use a pre-trained ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016) without the final classification layer as
326   the encoder. For comparison, we perform additional experiments with the Causal Transportability
327   architecture that integrates a VAE and a reconstruction loss (Mao et al., 2022). Following the
328   methodology of partially-stochastic neural networks (Sharma et al., 2023), we use a point-estimate
329   network for the encoder and only use a Bayesian network for the inference network. This choice
330   allows to maintain the expressivity on the level of uncertainty inherent to Bayesian neural networks
331   while having a limited impact on the optimisation efficiency.

336
      Ri′ . We select the mean of the results with respect to N to estimate the marginalisation over the
      context. We use a reparametrisation trick (Blundell et al., 2015a) to maintain the full differentiability
337
      of the network. Following Elfwing et al. (2018), we change the activation layers from ReLU to SiLU.

394
      We compare our CIBResNet-18 against ResNet-18 and ResNet-18-CT in i.i.d and o.o.d settings. The
395
      second baseline is the causal-Transportability (CT) algorithm of Mao et al. (2022). Following their
396   methodology, we use a VAE for the P (R|X) encoder. Their proposed inference network is a three-
397   layers convolution network followed by a two-layers dense classifier. For a fair comparison, we use a
398   modified ResNet-18 (the same size as our model) that takes input and contextual information. More
399   details are given in Appendix B. We conduct our experiments on a single GPU Nvidia A100 with the
400   AdamW optimiser (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019). We initialise the Bayesian classifier parameters from
401   the Normal distribution N (0, 1). On CIFAR-10, we train our model and the baselines for 20 epochs.
402   We find that all models usually do not show improvements after 10 epochs. On OFFICEHOME,
403   we train the models for 80 epochs. The training sets contain less samples than CIFAR-10 and more
404
      epochs are required for convergence. ResNet-18 stops improving after 40 epochs while CIBResNet-
      18 keep improving until around 60 epochs. We use hyperparameter grid search on the validation set
405
      of CIFAR-10 to set the hyperparameters. We use the default learning rate of 0.005 for the baselines
406
      and 0.01 for CIBResNet-18. We use a batch size of 64. By default, we use N = 16, M = 16,
407   α = 0.4, β = 0.01, γ = 10−6 , ν = 10−6 and ϵ = 10−6 . The remaining hyperparameters are set to
408   their default values. When evaluating the models in o.o.d settings, we use the batch statistics to build
409   the mean and variance in the batch normalisation layers instead of the values learned on the training
410   set that follow a different distribution.

420
      learning. We further compare the training of our model with the baseline on CIFAR-10 and the first

478
479
      This study focuses on developing a theory of interventional queries for o.o.d tasks and implementing
480   an architecture suitable for answering such queries. We do not focus on the optimisation and the
481   efficiency of such a system. Bayesian neural networks are notoriously difficult to optimise (Magris &
482   Iosifidis, 2023). As discussed in the experimental section, the sampling step can hurt the efficiency of
483   the model. As argued by Hooker (2021), the available hardware has a great impact on the success of
484   a machine learning method. The current hardware landscape is well suited for point estimate neural
485   networks but not for Bayesian and variational methods requiring many sampling steps. We argue that
      developing better and usable Bayesian causal models will require, not only improving the theory, but

      7                              C ONCLUSION
518
519   We propose a Causal-Invariant Bayesian neural network architecture to improve domain generalisation
520
      on visual recognition tasks. We build a theoretical analysis for domain generalisation with deep
      learning based on causality theory and taking into account the parameter learning process. We show
521
      experimentally that following causal principles can improve robustness to distribution shifts and
522
      reduce overfitting. These problems are particularly prominent on strong reasoning tasks that require
523   neural systems to use abstract domain-invariant mechanisms and ignore spurious domain-specific
524   information (Chollet, 2019; Zhang et al., 2021). In our future work, we will extend our framework
525   to solve these challenging reasoning tasks. This investigation also provides elements to motivate
526   the development of a causality-based theory of deep learning following the Independent Causal
527   Mechanisms principle (Peters et al., 2017; Schölkopf et al., 2021). Such theory could improve
528   robustness and generalisation in neural networks and provide additional benefits such as improved
529   interpretability and modularity.

      A.1        RULES OF DO - CALCULUS
705
706
      We first describe the three rules of do-calculus (Pearl, 2009) as they are required for the proofs in
707
      Sections A.2 and A.3. The first rule states that an observation z can be ignored if it does not affect
708   the outcome y of the query given the current causal graph. The second rule states that an intervention
709   on a variable do(z) can be considered an observation z if there are no backdoor paths linking it to
710   the outcome y, i.e. if the variables Z and Y do not share any common ancestors not captured by the
711   observation z. The third rule states that an intervention do(z) can be ignored if there are no direct
712   paths between Z and Y or backdoor paths between Y and observed descendants W of Z.
713
      The three rules are formally defined as follows. For each rule, the equality holds if the independence
714   test between the variable of interest Z and the outcome Y is verified in the given causal graph. The
715   graph GX corresponds to the initial graph with the incoming links to X removed, corresponding to
716   an intervention on X. The graph GX furthermore removes the outgoing links of Z. The term Z(W )
717   removes the incmoing links of Z if Z is not an ancestor of the observed variable W .
