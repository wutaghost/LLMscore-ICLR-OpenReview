                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
               Multimodal data fusion is essential for applications requiring the integration of
010
               diverse data sources, especially in the presence of incomplete or sparsely avail-
011
               able modalities. This paper presents a comparative study of three multimodal
012            embedding techniques, Modal Channel Attention (MCA), Zorro, and Everything
013            at Once (EAO), to evaluate their performance on sparsely multimodal data. MCA
014            introduces fusion embeddings for all combinations of input modalities and uses
015            attention masking to create distinct attention channels, enabling flexible and effi-
016            cient data fusion. Experiments on two datasets with four modalities each, CMU-
017            MOSEI and TCGA, demonstrate that MCA outperforms Zorro across ranking,
018            recall, regression, and classification tasks and outperforms EAO across regression
019            and classification tasks. MCA achieves superior performance by maintaining ro-
020
               bust uniformity across unimodal and fusion embeddings. While EAO performs
               best in ranking metrics due to its approach of forming fusion embeddings post-
021
               inference, it underperforms in downstream tasks requiring multimodal interac-
022
               tions. These results highlight the importance of contrasting all modality combi-
023            nations in constructing embedding spaces and offers insights into the design of
024            multimodal architectures for real-world applications with incomplete data.

      Multimodal data is becoming the norm for deep learning applications.(Xu et al., 2023; Han et al.,
048   2023; Liang et al., 2022a) Many models are trained on data with two aligned modalities(Alayrac
049   et al., 2020; Fei et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2024; 2021; Hager et al., 2023), including incorporating
050   images into large language models.(Alayrac et al., 2022; Rahman et al., 2020) Models with more
051   than two aligned data modalities have also become well studied,(Mizrahi et al., 2024; Srivastava
052   & Sharma, 2024; Akbari et al., 2021), and recent examples have explored learning from multiple
053   unaligned or partially aligned data modalities(Yang et al., 2021; Tran et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023;
      Nakada et al., 2023).

054
      Most of these examples use a combination of two modalities of text, audio, image or video. However,
055   applications can use data other than these traditional media formats. For example, multisensor fusion
056   in home monitoring systems and robotics includes tabular sensor data and time series data from
057   different types of sensors.(Tonkin et al., 2023). Bioinformatics and biomedical applications use data
058   that consists of tabular, image, and sequence data. In these fields, each media format in the data can
059   also be comprised of different modalities having the same data type but different data source, for
060   example as in two tables of data from different types of experiments.(Cui et al., 2023; Lynch et al.,
061   2022) In examples like these, datasets with 3 or more modalities are able to be constructed.
062   As the number of modalities used for training a model increases, samples with missing modalities
063   are more likely to occur, which are called modal-incomplete samples in this study. Multimodal
064   fusion models which cannot use modal-incomplete samples may not be well suited to be used for
065   applications with datasets that have many modalities because the likelihood of missing modalities
066   increases. When a significant fraction of samples are modal-incomplete, a dataset is here referred
067   to as sparsely multimodal. This contribution explores the extent to which the amount of modal
068   sparsity, defined in M ETHODS, affects multimodal fusion models based on contrastive representation
069
      learning.
070   Constrastive learning with multimodal data describes an important class of multimodal model.(Liang
071   et al., 2022b; Shvetsova et al., 2022; Singh et al., 2022; Akbari et al., 2021; Noriy et al., 2023;
072   Radford et al., 2021) In the well-known CLIP model, it generates joint embeddings for text and
073   images which can be used for classification, regression or for conditioning of diffusion probablistic
074   models. Embeddings generated from contrastive learning can be used for multimodal retrieval, such
075
      as in the Everthing At Once model (EAO)(Shvetsova et al., 2022) where video retrieval can be
      performed via text or audio via a fused embedding space. Recent studies have shown that using
076
      contrastive learning between unimodal representations and a learnable fusion representation can
077
      generate useful fusion embeddings. Originally presented as the Zorro model, this type of model
078   uses block attention masking to effectively perform self-attention on unimodal representations and
079   attention to a fused representation, all in a single attention block.(Shi et al., 2023; Recasens et al.,
080   2023)
081
      In this study, an attention masking and contrastive representation learning strategy called Modal
082
      Channel Attention (MCA) was devised which combines aspects of EAO and Zorro. MCA improves
083
      on retrieval metrics compared with Zorro and improves on downstream task performance when
084   compared with both Zorro and EAO. This is demonstrated using two well-known datasets, each with
085   four modalities. The effect of sparsely multimodal data on each model’s performance is compared
086   throughout.

089   2   C OMPARISON OF R ELATED W ORK
090
091   In this section, existing approaches to train models on datasets with modal-incomplete samples are
092   described. First, relevant models which do not use contrastive loss, then those which include con-
093   trastive learning as a component, followed by those using only contrastive loss. Then, justification
094   for the use of a contrastive learning for the purposes of this study is given and a comparison of
095   existing contrastive models to MCA.
096   Without contrastive loss, interleaved data can include incomplete modalities due to the treatment
097   of samples as a sequence.(Alayrac et al., 2022) Similarly, masking of representations in a late stage
098   fusion block can be used naturally with modal-incomplete data(Zhang et al., 2022; Tran et al., 2023).
099   These model architectures require an autoregressive or a masked language objective in order to train
100   on the interleaved data or predict missing data. We do not explore these classes of models here and
101   instead constrain the approach to explore those using a contrastive learning objective.
102   FLAVA(Singh et al., 2022) uses multiple loss models for data which is missing modalities. For text,
103   they use a masked language objective, while for image-text pairs, they use a contrastive loss. Their
104   approach allows for unimodal and multimodal inference, but does not generate a multimodal fusion
105   embedding space. Zhang et al. (2023) developed a model which can be trained with missing modal-
106   ity combinations by projecting unimodal encodings into a modality-aligned feature space. They then
107   perform weight-shared dual attention prediction of two sets of outputs. The first output is trained
      with supervision to class labels, while the second prediction is trained with supervision to unimodal


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      predictions across epochs. This is shown to improve predictions for unseen modalities. LORRETA
109   uses an objective of predicting a third modality given two other modalities. This approach requires
110   bimodal pairs for each forward pass and can not directly embed higher order modality combina-
111   tions.(Tran et al., 2023) A similar objective of predicting missing modalities was taken in Wei et al.
112   (2023).
113
      None of these aforementioned models generate a fusion embedding space, which is required for tasks
114
      based on embeddings like retrieval and linear probing for regression and classification. There is no
115   clear extension to the aforementioned models where a fusion embedding is close to it’s unimodal
116   embeddings and to embeddings generated from modal-incomplete samples. Since the goal of this
117   study is to explore fused embedding spaces for data with more than 2 modalities when the datasets
118   are sparsely multimodal, we now turn to models designed for related purposes.
119
      The Everything At Once (Shvetsova et al., 2022) model uses a transformer encoder that creates
120
      embeddings for one or two modalities at a time with contrastive loss. The encoder is applied multiple
121   times per minibatch in order to formulate embeddings for each modality and each pair of modalities.
122   These embeddings are applied in a combinatorial contrastive loss function, such that the loss is
123   applied to each possible pair of generated embeddings. At inference time, the generated embeddings
124   are averaged to create a fusion embedding. This method requires a number of forward passes that
125   has unfavorable scaling with the number of modalities. Importantly, it does not attend jointly from
126   all embeddings at once to form a unified fusion representation.
127   Zorro is a transformer encoder model which produces both unimodal embeddings and a fusion
128   embedding which are then trained with contrastive loss.(Recasens et al., 2023) Zorro uses block
129   attention masking to prevent attention between the internal representations of different modalities,
130   but allows for unimodal self-attention and attention from unimodal representations to a fusion rep-
131   resentation. A similar architecture was recently applied by Shi et al. in order to fuse 3 modalities for
132   image segmentation in a biomedical application.(Shi et al., 2023) In this latter study, it was noted
133   that the model architecture seemed to function well even with missing modalities, but it did not
134   directly explore the performance of this type of modal fusion with sparsely multimodal data.
135   In publications presenting the Zorro and EAO models, three common data modalities (text, audio,
136   and video) were used. However, the model principles can be applied to any type and number of
137   modalities. An overview of how these models are related for 2 and 3 modality datasets is shown in
138   Figure 2. When applied to 2 modality datasets, EAO, Zorro, and MCA are conceptually similar.
139   All models separately contrast each of 2 generated unimodal embeddings with a fusion embedding.
140   The most important difference between EAO and the other models in the case of 2 modalities is that
141
      the unimodal embedding is created in the same forward pass as the fusion embedding in Zorro and
      MCA, while in EAO it is not.1
142
143   When using a 3 modality dataset, the difference between EAO and Zorro models is more significant.
144   EAO contrasts all possible pairs of unimodal and 2 modality embeddings, each generated from a
145   separate forward pass of the same transformer block, while Zorro separately contrasts each of 3
146   unimodal embeddings with a single fusion embedding, all calculated in a single forward pass. MCA
147
      combines the single forward pass structure of Zorro and the 2 modality fusion representations from
      EAO, while also creating and contrasting fusion embeddings for all other possible modality combi-
148
      nations. In the next section, MCA, along with implementations of Zorro and EAO, are presented in
149
      further detail.

      embeddings in this Zorro implementation, as these are also present in both EAO and MCA. To
217   implement EAO, the attention mask was removed2 and a separate forward pass through the same
218   transformer encoder layers was performed for each unimodal or bimodal combination of inputs.

225

      other model implementation details.
226
227   To pretrain the implemented models with sparsely multimodal data, a sample and loss masking strat-
228   egy was used, described graphically in Figure 3a. Padding tokens are used for any missing modal-
229   ities which are then masked from attention in the transformer encoders. This prevents the padding
230   tokens from attending to any other tokens. While it would be possible to adjust the MCA mask
231
      (and equivalently Zorro attention mask) to remove the tokens of a missing modality from the model
      forward pass altogether, samples must have the same number of tokens for efficient batching and
232
      thus a padding mask strategy was chosen to allow flexibility in mixing samples with heterogeneity
233
      of modality combinations.
234
235   The resultant loss function for modal-incomplete samples was chosen to include any fusion tokens
236   for which at least one modality is present. For example, if modality 1 exists in a sample and modality
237
      2 is absent, a fusion token (1, 2) will be attended to by only modality 1, but a loss will still be
      calculated between embeddings for 1 and (1, 2). If both modality 1 and modality 2 are absent, the
238
      fusion token (1, 2) will not be attended to by any tokens and no losses will be calculated using it.
239
      This choice of loss masking strategy was chosen such that the Zorro model’s contrastive loss with a
240   single fusion channel was preserved even with sparsely multimodal data. A variety of other possible
241   designs for loss and token masking are conceivable, but are beyond the scope of the present study to
242   explore.
243
      A set of consistent hyperparameters were chosen across all models to train efficiently and fall within
244
      standard ranges of parameters for transformer encoders. This allows for focus on comparisons of
245
      multimodal fusion methods and modal sparsity. The transformer encoders use a hidden size of 512
246   and 8 attention heads. The feed-forward layers use a feed-forward multiplier of 4 and the GeGLU
247   activation function. There are a total of 88 fusion tokens used in both Zorro and MCA. In Zorro, all
248   88 fusion tokens are pooled in the pooling layer as a single channel, while in MCA, each channel
249   uses 8 tokens and 11 channels are present as depicted in Figure 3b where each channel of 8 tokens
250   is shown as a single square. All models are very close in parameter count.

266
      and layer normalization, resulting in a token embedding size of Nemb . This normalized, transformed
267   vector is then added to a standard sinusoidal positional embedding vector to encode it’s position in
268   the sequence of vectors for a given modality. No other learnable token embedding is used for these

      4.1.2    TCGA
274
275   The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA)(Weinstein et al., 2013) provides a multi-omics dataset that con-
276   sisting of tabular data for gene expression, reverse phase protein arrays (RPPA), DNA methylation,
277   and miRNA measurements. This data was downloaded from the supporting information of Wein-
278   stein et al. (2013). To reduce the number of gene expression and DNA methylation columns in the
279   dataset, the top 800 genes and methylation sites with the highest variance were used to create a sig-
280
      nature of the gene expression and methylation data. For RPPA data and miRNA tables, there are 198
      protein columns and 662 MiRNA columns. To align unimodal samples into a multimodal dataset,
281
      we use provided identification numbers for patient and sample, resulting in an intersection of 7017
282
      samples that have all modalities. A test split is randomly chosen with 707 samples.
283
284   To encode TCGA tabular data, values are passed token-wise through a trainable 2 layer
285   MLP(1,Nemb ,Nemb ) with ReLU activation function. This allows a continuous representation of the
286
      tabular value into a vector with the same size as the transformer encoder embedding space Nemb .
      The tabular column index is encoded with a standard learnable embedding vector of size Nemb for
287
      each index. The value and column index encodings are added together to form the input token
288
      embedding vectors for the transformer encoders.

347   5.1        U NIFORMITY AND A LIGNMENT
348
      This section aims to analyze the characteristics of the embeddings produced by the trained mod-
349
      els. Embedding spaces trained with contrastive learning are characterized by alignment (La ) and
350   uniformity (Lu ).(Wang & Isola, 2020) These are defined by
351
                                                       La = Ex,y [||f (x) − f (y)||22 ]                                                                 (2)
352
                                                                                       −2||f (x)−f (y)||22
353                                      Lu = Ex,y [e                ]                                   (3)
354   where Ex,y is the expectation value over variables x and y. For La , x, y are positive pairs from two
355   different embedding types (e.g. a fusion and unimodal embeddings), while for Lu , x, y are pairs of
356   embeddings from a single embedding type.
357
      To compare the Lu and La of generated embedding spaces across models and varied modal sparsity
358
      we calculate these metrics using the test splits of both TCGA and CMU-MOSEI datasets. While
359
      there are multiple fusion embeddings in MCA, to compare with Zorro and EAO, we use the fusion
360   embedding that includes attention from all modalities in the following analyses. When necessary,
361   we take the mean of the metric calculated for each unimodal embedding. For example, the mean
362   unimodal Lu shown in Figure 4b is the mean of the Lu after calculating it separately for each
363   unimodal embedding type.
364
      If a model is trained to generate an embedding space with lower Lu (i.e. better, indicating a more
365
      uniformly distributed embedding space), the La of positive (matching) embeddings will tend to be
366   increased (i.e. worsened, indicating less alignment).(Wang & Isola, 2020) The Lu and La of MCA
367   and Zorro have no clear trend up to a modal sparsity of 0.4, while EAO demonstrates an monotonic
368   increase in Lu and decrease in La . As the modal sparsity is increased beyond 0.4, both EAO and
369   Zorro models have worsened Lu , even though La is not as strongly affected. For the smaller dataset
370   (TCGA), Lu increases in MCA with increasing modal sparsity.
371
      EAO has better La than MCA and Zorro. This is likely because the fusion embedding of EAO is
372   constructed directly from the average of unimodal embeddings and it’s effect is apparent in ranking
373   and recall metrics. Correspondingly, the Lu of EAO is significantly worse than MCA and Zorro.
374   This could be because EAO does not have a mechanism that fuses all unimodal representations at
375   once, where attention is calculated with, at most, only 2 modalities at a time. MCA demonstrates
376   better Lu and worse La of both unimodal and fusion embeddings than Zorro. Regardless, this
377   increase in Lu tends to outweigh the effect of La on ranking and recall metrics when comparing
      these two models, as described in the next section.

420   R1 , R5 , and R10 ). These recall metrics demonstrate the ability to use a unimodal embedding to recall
421   it’s matching fusion embedding. Ideally, any matching pair of unimodal and fusion embeddings has
422   a greater similarity than all non-matching pairs. The cosine similarities of a unimodal embedding
423   to each fusion embedding is used to calculate the rank. The median rank is the median value of
424   these ranks (Figures 5a and 5b). The recall (Rx ) is equivalent to the probability that the correct
425   fusion embedding is in the top x most similar fusion embeddings (Figures 5c and 5d. Note that as
426   modal sparsity is increased, the size of the dataset is reduced by samples which have all modalities
427   dropped, which may affect the results.
428   EAO has the best performance in ranking methods, which is not surprising given that the fusion
429   embeddings are calculated as the average of unimodal and 2 modality embeddings. In the TCGA,
430   MCA performs nearly as well as EAO, even with a modal sparsity of 0.2. The median rank is
431   improved in MCA over Zorro in most cases. In the larger CMU-MOSEI dataset the difference is
      greatest at high modal sparsity, while in the smaller TCGA dataset it is greatest at low modal sparsity.

512   6   C ONCLUSION
513
514   This study investigates the performance of multimodal embedding techniques in scenarios with
515   varying degrees of modal sparsity. By examining MCA, Zorro, and EAO embeddings on both
516   ranking/recall metrics and downstream regression/classification tasks, advantages and trade-offs as-
517   sociated with each method are demonstrated. MCA consistently outperformed Zorro across most
518   experiments. Its ability to contrast all combinations of modalities enables it to generate embed-
519
      dings that maintain improved uniformity, leading to improved results in both ranking-based re-
      trieval tasks and downstream linear probing evaluations. While EAO excelled in ranking tasks due
520
      to its post-inference calculation of fusion embeddings, it performed worse than MCA in regres-
521
      sion/classification tasks where complex multimodal interactions are required.
522
523   Overall, the results show the potential of MCA as a method for generating robust multimodal fu-
524   sion embeddings, particularly in sparsely multimodal datasets. Its demonstrated improvements over
525
      Zorro and EAO suggest that incorporating fine-grained contrastive strategies into embedding models
      can significantly improve model performance. These findings pave the way for future research into
526
      advanced multimodal data fusion techniques with applications in multimodal retrieval and down-
527
      stream tasks.
