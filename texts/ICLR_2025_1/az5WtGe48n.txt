009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The ability to adapt to new, unseen tasks with only a handful of training examples
012            is a key factor behind the unprecedented success of language models. However, in
013            computer vision, few-shot adaption has largely focused on adapting to new semantic
014            categories or answering new visual questions. Adapting a model to dense vision
015            tasks—depth estimation, surface normal estimation, semantic segmentation—has
016            only been possible with large amounts of training data and with custom decoder
017            heads, since the output spaces and the required information for different tasks vary
018            widely. For instance, depth estimation requires understanding geometry, while
019
               semantic segmentation relies on semantic information. In this paper, we found that
               the diffusion prior can effectively adapt to various dense tasks, and based on this,
020
               we introduce an adaptation mechanism that exploits a pretrained diffusion model
021
               for 12 different dense vision tasks using only a few training examples. We found
022            that even modifying a small number of input variables is sufficient to effectively
023            adapt to new unseen tasks. Building on this, we further improved the performance
024            on few-shot dense tasks. Our key insight is to reframe all dense prediction tasks
025            into a codebook-conditioned classification problem, even for continuous outputs.
026            Specifically, we learn two set of parameters: (1) concept embeddings that condition
027            the diffusion model to encode task-specific representations in their attention masks;
028            and (2) codebook embeddings that recombine discrete outputs to continuous ones.
029            With this novel design, we achieve state-of-the-art results across 12 datasets for
030
               few shot learning.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Language models are few-shot learners (Brown et al., 2020). To adapt to new unseen tasks, they
036   require only a handful of training examples. This capability has enabled the related subfield of
037   natural language processing to transition from designing task-specific objectives and architectures to
038   task-agnostic ones. In computer vision, many semantic tasks, including categorization (Tseng et al.,
039
      2020), detection (Kang et al., 2019), and visual question answering (Alayrac et al., 2022), can be
      similarly reformulated such that their output spaces use language. With this reformulation, they can
040
      use language models as decoder heads and inherit their few-shot adaptation capabilities.
041
042   Developing few-shot adaptation for dense vision tasks—depth estimation, surface normal estimation,
043   semantic segmentation, etc.—remains challenging. Multi-task generalists (Wang et al., 2023a; Yang
044   et al., 2024; Ji et al., 2023) can integrate multiple dense tasks within one framework, but they cannot
045
      adapt to unseen tasks in a few-shot setting. Past research on few-shot settings for dense vision tasks
      has mainly focused on semantic segmentation (Johnander et al., 2022; Min et al., 2021; Hong et al.,
046
      2022). However, these methods are typically effective only for tasks with discrete semantic outputs
047
      and are not suitable for tasks like depth estimation, which have continuous outputs. Furthermore,
048   dense tasks like depth estimation require the model to learn knowledge that is fundamentally different
049   from that required for semantic segmentation, making it even more challenging for previous methods,
050   which focus on semantic segmentation, to adapt to tasks like depth estimation. VTM (Kim et al.,
051   2023) is the only known work dedicated to addressing general dense tasks in a few-shot setting.
052   However, it relies on extensive pixel-level annotations for multitask learning during the pre-training
053   phase, which not only hinders its scalability but also limits its ability to generalize effectively during
      the few-shot adaptation phase. Recent works like VPD (Zhao et al., 2023) have shown that pre-trained


                                                          1
054
      diffusion models encode prior knowledge useful for various dense tasks. However, leveraging this
055   prior typically requires a large amount of task-specific training data to adapt to a new task.
056
057   In this paper, we find that a pre-trained diffusion model can better adapt to different downstream dense
058
      tasks using few-shot samples, compared to VTM or other pre-trained models, even without optimizing
      internal model parameters but instead optimizing the input. Building on this, we further combine
059
      CLIP with the diffusion prior to achieve even better adaptation across 12 downstream dense vision
060
      tasks. First, we demonstrate that pre-trained diffusion models can adapt to different downstream tasks
061   with just a few examples. To achieve this, we learn task-specific concept embeddings that query
062   the diffusion model for meaningful information. Similar concept embeddings have been utilized by
063   DETR (Carion et al., 2020) and MaskFormer (Cheng et al., 2021) to represent objectness. We extend
064   this idea to show that continuous measurements can also be effectively represented using discrete
065   concept embeddings. These concept embeddings consist of only a few hundred parameters and
066   require very few examples to train. While similar approaches have been applied to depth estimation
067   (e.g., Adabins (Bhat et al., 2021)), our work is distinct in that we demonstrate how this idea can
068   unleash the potential of pre-trained diffusion models to adapt to a wide variety of unseen dense tasks.
069
      More importantly, unlike Adabins, which incorporates concept embeddings as part of the final output
      head, we optimize these concept embeddings as part of the model’s input. This allows different dense
070
      vision tasks to share the same backbone architecture. This is akin to prompt tuning in NLP (Li &
071
      Liang, 2021), where a unified backbone can represent entirely new tasks through different inputs,
072   eliminating the need for task-specific heads to adapt to new tasks. This approach has the potential to
073   unlock in-context adaptation for dense vision tasks. We find that optimizing only these input variables
074   allows pre-trained diffusion models to slightly outperform state-of-the-art methods.
075
      Second, building on the above findings, we further reformulate all continuous dense prediction tasks
076
      into a codebook-conditioned classification problem to better leverage the diffusion prior and further
077
      improve performance on these tasks. Simply converting each task into a classification problem is
078   insufficient. For instance, in depth estimation, the distribution of depth values in an indoor scene
079   differs significantly from that of an outdoor scene. To address the varying per-scene distributions of
080   potential output values, we propose a codebook-conditioned classification approach. Building on
081   the strong baseline provided by the diffusion prior, we additionally utilize CLIP features from the
082   image to attend over a learnable codebook, where the codebook values represent multiple possible
083   distributions. These attended codebook elements are then used to recompose the classification output
084   into a continuous value. This approach enables us to better leverage the diffusion prior for few-shot
085   adaptation across diverse dense tasks.
086   Experiments across 12 dense vision tasks reveal that our mechanism can adapt diffusion models
087   to new dense tasks with as few as 10 training examples per task. We compare our method against
088   other recently proposed methods applicable to few-shot general dense prediction tasks, including
089   VPD (Zhao et al., 2023) and VTM (Kim et al., 2023). These results are consistent across 10
090   Taskonomy (Zamir et al., 2018) and 2 NYUv2 (Silberman et al., 2012) datasets. Moreover, our
091   representations outperform those from popular vision encoders, including CLIP (Radford et al.,
092
      2021) and DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023). These experimental results demonstrate that our proposed
      adaptation mechanism better unleashes the potential of diffusion models in few-shot dense tasks.

097   Few-shot learning. Few-shot learning traditionally focuses on learning new categories in tasks such
098   as classification, detection, and segmentation. Siamese networks aim to improve matching by learning
099   better embeddings, while MAML treats few-shot adaptation as an optimization objective (Finn et al.,
100   2017). Subsequently, evidence suggests that fine-tuning well-pretrained representations yields better
101   results than meta-learning methods (Tian et al., 2020). Our work investigates how to fine-tune
102   pretrained diffusion representations for downstream dense prediction tasks. However, prior research
103   on few-shot dense prediction has primarily targeted segmentation and focused on learning new
104
      categories. To the best of our knowledge, VTM is the only work addressing few-shot learning for
      universal dense prediction. However, it relies on meta-learning, which requires significant dense
105
      annotations for multiple tasks.

143   Generalist models. Recent advancements in computer vision have introduced "generalist models,"
144   capable of handling multiple tasks (Wang et al., 2023a;b; Kirillov et al., 2023). Unlike traditional
145   multi-task learning, which typically shares only encoder parameters, generalist models share all
146   parameters across tasks. During training, they jointly learn from multiple tasks, and at inference, they
147
      adapt to different tasks by altering the input prompt without modifying model parameters. Prompts
      can take various forms, such as geometric inputs (e.g., points or boxes), text, or pairs of images
148
      and targets. However, these prompt-based models are limited to handling tasks encountered during
149
      training. Even when provided with a few annotated examples as prompts, adapting to entirely unseen
150   tasks remains challenging. At best, they can quickly adjust to new classes within known tasks,
151   akin to how a painter adapts to variations within a familiar style. In contrast, we argue that a truly
152   foundational vision model should adapt easily to a wide range of unseen tasks, leveraging extensive
153   pretraining. This distinction underpins the primary difference between our approach and generalist
154   models.
155
      Generative models for perception. There is a growing trend to use generative models for inherently
156   discriminative tasks. Examples include Dataset GAN (Zhang et al., 2021), SemanticGAN (Li et al.,
157   2021), ODISE (Xu et al., 2023), and VPD (Zhao et al., 2023), among others (Bhattad et al., 2024;
158   Du et al., 2023). These methods enhance discriminative tasks by generating images, modifying
159   the generative model for novel tasks, or introducing specialized layers and decoders for specific
160   scene properties. Similarly, we leverage a pretrained diffusion model for dense perception tasks.
161   Unlike prior methods, our approach introduces a unified architecture for multiple dense tasks and
      demonstrates the ability to adapt to new tasks with only a few examples.

      3     M ETHOD
163
164
      Our method demonstrates that diffusion models can be adapted for dense vision tasks with a few
165
      training examples. Before we detail our method, we first formulate the few-shot adaptation problem
166   and revisit latent diffusion objective and architecture.
167
168   Problem formulation. Given an input image I, a pretrained latent diffusion model M, and a dense
169
      prediction task T , our goal is to generate an output J . J has the same height H and width W
      dimensions as the original image I but its values can be continuous or discrete. If T is a semantic
170
      segmentation task across K categories, the output is a binary tensor J ∈ RH×W ×K . If T is a depth
171
      estimation task, the output is a continuous tensor J ∈ RH×W .
172
173   Background on diffusion. Text-to-image diffusion models are trained to produce photorealistic
174   images conditioned on text inputs. They are trained using large scale image-text datasets scraped from
175
      the internet (Schuhmann et al., 2022) They generate images from random Gaussian noise through a
      sequence of T denoising steps (Ho et al., 2020). The denoising process is defined as a sequence of
176
      timesteps T, T − 1, ..., 0. At timestep T , a H × W × 3-dimensional random noise is sampled from a
177
      multivariate normal distribution, denoted as the initial noise xT . DDPM applies a denoising neural
178   network to iteratively de-noise latents xt+1 → xt until x0 .
179
180   Denoising in pixel space is computationally expensive as each denoising step produces a H × W × 3-
181
      dimensional noise estimation. Latent Diffusion Models (Rombach et al., 2022), M, instead
      encode the sample image x into a much smaller latent space z0 using a pretrained variational
182
      autoencoder (VAE) (Esser et al., 2020) with a h × w × c-dimensional hidden representation, such that
183
      h × w × c ≪ H × W × 3. Latent diffusion models sample zT from a random Gaussian distribution
184   and iteratively denoise in the latent space until z0 . Finally, z0 is decoded using the pretrained VAE’s
185   decoder into the pixel space.

      3.1   R EFRAMING DENSE VISION TASKS AS CLASSIFICATION
188
189   We reformulate all dense prediction tasks into classification problems. Some dense prediction tasks,
190   such as segmentation, inherently have outputs that are suitable for discrete classification. However,
191   tasks like depth estimation, whose outputs are continuous values, cannot be directly optimized through
192   classification loss. We discretize a continuous output space of size H × W × D. First, we split
193   the possible range of continuous values in the original D-dimensional output space into N buckets.
194   We assign a unique ID from {0, . . . , N − 1} to each bucket. Each ground truth continuous value is
195   discretized into the bucket with its corresponding range; the ID of this bucket is used as the category
196
      label for the classification reformulation.
197   Intuitively, for depth estimation, we divide the physical measurement of the continuous depth values
198   into B buckets. Semantically, categories represent concepts like “very close” or “a little close”, “very
199   far”, etc. We adapt Stable Diffusion with a few training examples to understand these new categories.
200
201   3.2   R EFRAMING DENSE VISION TASKS AS CLASSIFICATION
202
203   We reformulate all dense prediction tasks into classification problems. Some dense prediction tasks,
204   such as segmentation, inherently have outputs that are suitable for discrete classification. We label
205   their ground truth as GTlabel . However, tasks like depth estimation, whose outputs are continuous
206
      values, cannot be directly optimized through classification loss. For such tasks, we label their original
      ground truth as GTvalue . For these continuous-output tasks, we discretize their ground truth GTvalue
207
      of size H × W × D into GTlabel . This gives them both GTvalue and GTlabel as ground truth targets,
208
      enabling the use of both regression loss and classification loss during training.
209
210   Specifically, we split the possible range of continuous values in the original D -dimensional output
211   space into N buckets. Then, we assign a unique ID from {0, . . . , N − 1} to each bucket. Each ground
212
      truth continuous value is discretized into the bucket corresponding to its range, and the ID of this
      bucket is used as the ground truth category label for the classification reformulation.
213
214   Intuitively, for depth estimation, we divide the physical measurement of the continuous depth values
215   into N buckets. Semantically, these categories represent concepts like “very close,” “a little close,” or
      “very far.” We adapt Stable Diffusion with a few training examples to understand these new categories.

      3.3   C ONCEPT EMBEDDINGS FOR EXTRACTING S TABLE D IFFUSION REPRESENTATIONS
217
218   Although diffusion models are trained with the objective of denoising photorealistic images, their
219   learned internal representations encode sufficient semantic and geometry information. To extract
220   meaningful representations, we learn N task-specific concept tokens {T1 , . . . , TN } to replace stable
221   diffusion’s text tokens. For an input image I and task T , we load its corresponding concept embed-
222   dings as inputs to M. Our goal is to design a model that generates a probability distribution over
223   each pixel of the input image I across the N categories. Therefore, we need to output a H × W × N
224
      output.

248   3.4   L EARNABLE CODEBOOK - CONDITIONED CLASSIFICATION
249
250   Although we formulate continuous dense tasks as classification problems, their final output still needs
251   to be continuous. Therefore, for these tasks, we need to project the category distribution Mlabel
252   to the continuous output Mvalue ∈ RH×W ×D . However, mapping from discrete class labels to
253   continuous values in such tasks is an ill-posed one-to-many transformation compared to the mapping
254
      from continuous values to categories.
255   To address this, we represent the label-to-value mapping as a learnable random variable. The range of
256   these random variables needs to be conditioned on the input image. For instance, when representing
257   depth, the values should be smaller for indoor scenes but larger for outdoor images. We model
258   this random variable as a learnable codebook C, which contains K sets of mappings from labels to
259   values. Each label-to-value mapping of size N × D represents how N discrete labels correspond to
260
      D-dimensional continuous output values. Thus, C ∈ RK×N ×D .
261   For an input image I, we first calculate its distribution over the K associated mappings. Specifically,
262   we use a frozen pre-trained CLIP visual encoder to extract a D′ -dimensional feature from the input
263   image. Then, through a learnable linear mapping L of size D′ × K, this extracted feature is mapped
264   to a K-dimensional vector. After applying softmax, we obtain a distribution F of the mapping
265   relationship conditioned on the input. Using F as weights, we combine the K sets of mapping
266
      relationships C through a weighted sum to obtain C ′ ∈ RN ×D . This represents how the N discrete
      labels map to continuous output values for the input image.
267
268   Finally, based on the obtained C ′ , the sample-dependent label-to-value mapping, we use the previously
269   obtained per-pixel N -class probability distribution Mlabel to calculate the expected value at each
      pixel position. This process converts the probability distribution over discrete labels into continuous


                                                            5
270
      values. This produces Mvalue , which lies in the original output space for tasks with continuous
271   values, such as depth estimation.

      4.1   S ETUP
295
296   Data. To effectively evaluate our model, we consider two datasets: Taskonomy (Zamir et al., 2018)
297   and NYUv2 (Silberman et al., 2012). Taskonomy (Zamir et al., 2018) comprises 10 dense vision
298   tasks, including Euclidean distance, Z-buffer depth, texture edge, occlusion edge, 2D keypoints, 3D
299   keypoints, reshading, principal curvature, surface normal, and semantic segmentation. It allows us to
300   validate the model’s capability of adapting to a diverse range of tasks, each requiring a different form
301   of understanding. To further study the model’s geometric understanding capability, we additionally
302
      include two tasks from the NYUv2 dataset (Silberman et al., 2012): depth estimation and surface
      normal prediction.
303
304   Metrics. We employ different metrics for each task. Following previous work (Kim et al., 2023), we
305   utilize root mean square error (RMSE) to evaluate Euclidean distance, Z-buffer depth, texture edge,
306   occlusion edge, 2D keypoints, 3D keypoints, reshading, principal curvature, and NYU depth. We
307   adopt mean intersection over union (mIoU) for semantic segmentation. For surface normal, we use
308
      mean error (mErr).
309   Evaluation protocol. Few-shot adaptation outcomes can vary widely depending on the training
310   examples used. To address this variability, we conduct each few-shot experiment 100 times with
311   different randomly selected training data points and compute the average. We note that this setup,
312   while necessary, is absent in previous work (Kim et al., 2023).
313   Baselines. We compare our model against VTM (Kim et al., 2023) and VPD (Zhao et al., 2023).
314   VTM (Kim et al., 2023) is a state-of-the-art few-shot dense predictor, while VPD (Zhao et al., 2023)
315   is a few-shot model that also utilizes diffusion priors. To understand the potential upper limits of
316   these methods, we also conduct experiments under fully supervised conditions. For more details,
317   please refer to the supplementary material.
318   Implementation details. We use Stable Diffusion 2.1 as the pre-trained backbone to extract features
319   for both our method and the VPD baseline. We extract cross-attention and self-attention attention
320   masks from the 8th to the 12th layers, and the last three layers of the UNet. We adopt different
321   numbers of discretization buckets for different tasks. Specifically, we set the bucket size to 20 for
322   texture edge, occlusion edge, 2D keypoints, 3D keypoints, and reshading. We adopt 40 buckets for
323   principal curvature, Euclidean distance, Z-buffer depth, and surface normal estimation. For NYUv2,
      we use 50 buckets.

      4.2   D IFFUSION P RIOR AS A S TRONG BASELINE FOR F EW-S HOT D ENSE TASKS
346
347   We first demonstrate that the pre-trained diffusion model alone already serves as a strong baseline for
348   few-shot dense tasks. To isolate the impact of the diffusion model from that of the CLIP model, we
349   set the codebook size to 0 or 1. A codebook size of 0 represents a pre-defined fixed label-to-value
350   mapping, while a size of 1 represents a learnable label-to-value mapping without requiring the CLIP
351   encoder to determine how to combine different label-to-value results.
352   In addition, we compare our approach to CLIP and DINOv2. Similar to diffusion models, both were
353   pre-trained on large-scale datasets. However, unlike our model, they were trained using contrastive
354   objectives. We use a pre-trained ViT-L (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) as the encoder and attach a trainable
355   linear projection layer. We transform the continuous outputs of depth and surface normals into discrete
356   classification problems as well. For ease of comparison, we used a fixed pre-defined class-to-value
357   mapping instead of a learnable label codebook.
358   We compare the impact of using different pre-trained models as backbones on the NYU depth
359   estimation and surface normal prediction tasks, with only 20 examples provided. We use RMSE to
360   evaluate depth estimation and mErr to evaluate surface normal estimation.

499   5   D ISCUSSION
500
501   Limitations. Although we demonstrate that the diffusion prior can quickly adapt to new tasks
502   by training with few-shot examples, we identify several limitations of the current methods. First,
503   when converting continuous output tasks into classification tasks, we divide the output into varying
504   numbers of categories depending on the task. Consequently, we learn different numbers of concept
505
      embeddings for different tasks. Furthermore, for tasks with different output dimensions, such as
      depth and normal prediction, we adopt distinct methods to partition the output space. However, these
506
      strategies essentially rely on human prior knowledge about the tasks to facilitate fast adaptation.
507
      Ideally, we aim to rely solely on the model’s inherent priors, without depending on human task-
508   specific knowledge. This would enable the model to quickly adapt to new tasks using only a few
509   examples.
510
511
      Future Direction. Our goal is to enable visual models to learn new tasks quickly from a small number
      of samples, akin to human learning. Ideally, this would be achieved through in-context learning,
512
      where the model grasps the definition of unseen tasks without requiring task-specific fine-tuning,
513
      relying solely on a few annotated examples. While LLMs achieve this for many language tasks,
514   few-shot in-context learning for unseen dense vision tasks remains challenging due to the diversity in
515   output formats across dense tasks, as well as the vastly different knowledge requirements for tasks
516   like semantic segmentation (semantic understanding) and depth estimation (geometric reasoning).
517
      This work takes a step toward this goal by relaxing the no-fine-tuning restriction, exploring how
518
      diffusion models can adapt to new tasks using only a few examples with minimal parameter changes.
519
      We focus on modifying input parameters rather than internal ones, aligning with in-context adaptation.
520   Unlike task-specific heads, this approach enables task differences to be defined through inputs, which
521   is closer to in-context learning. Our findings show that diffusion models can adapt to unseen dense
522   tasks by optimizing only input parameters, demonstrating their potential as general decoders for task
523   definitions via inputs.
524
      Future directions include learning task definitions from few-shot samples through feedforward
525
      mechanisms, such as using autoregressive models to learn concept embeddings (task vectors) that
526   prompt the diffusion decoder. Another promising direction is studying relationships between tasks
527   represented by these task vectors. We aim to develop a promptable interface for downstream task
528   adaptation without gradient-based backpropagation, leveraging the diffusion prior as a foundation.
529
      Conclusion. In this work, we repurpose stable diffusion into a backbone that can adapt to various
530
      dense prediction tasks with only a few training samples. The key lies in transforming tasks with
531
      different output spaces into classification tasks, as this simplifies the optimization process. By
532   utilizing the different levels of attention masks within stable diffusion, we have transformed the pre-
533   trained generation model into a mask proposal generator. Furthermore, we have learned a codebook
534   to map the category labels obtained from classification back to the original numerical output space.
535   Extensive experiments have demonstrated that our method successfully adapts the priors learned by
536   the pre-trained diffusion model to various perception tasks through few-shot learning.
