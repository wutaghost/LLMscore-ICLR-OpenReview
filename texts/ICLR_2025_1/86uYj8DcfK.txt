009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The image Difference Captioning (IDC) task is to describe the distinctions between
012            two images. However, existing datasets do not offer comprehensive coverage
013            across all image-difference categories. In this work, we introduce a more extensive
014            dataset, DiffTell, which encompasses various types of differences between images,
015            including global image alterations, object-level changes, and text manipulations.
016            DiffTell includes both newly collected data and filtered data used in previous
017            studies. Additionally, to scale up the data collection without prohibitive human
018            labor costs, we explore the possibility of automatically filtering for quality control.
019
               We prove that both traditional methods and recent multimodal large language
               models (MLLMs) show improved performance on the IDC task after training
020
               on the DiffTell dataset. We conducted extensive ablation studies to provide a
021
               thorough analysis of the performance gain from DiffTell. Experiments show DiffTell
022            significantly enhances the availability of resources for IDC research, offering a
023            more comprehensive foundation and benchmark for future investigations.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Given the great progress in image generation (Ramesh et al., 2021; Rombach et al., 2022a), dissemi-
029
      nating AI-modified fake images can lead to widespread misinformation, erosion of public trust, and
030
      manipulation of public opinion on critical issues. Emerging open standards, such as C2PA (Coali-
031   tion for Content Provenance and Authenticity, 2023), outline provenance frameworks that utilize
032   perceptual hashing techniques to link images found in the public domain with a federated database
033   of original content (Black et al., 2021; Pizzi et al., 2022). Upon retrieving the source image, image
034   difference captioning (IDC) models can describe the discrepancies between the circulated image and
035   its original, enabling individuals to make more informed and nuanced trust assessments. IDC has
036   been researched with various algorithms (Tan et al., 2019; Qiu et al., 2021; Tu et al., 2021; Guo et al.,
037   2022b; Yao et al., 2022b; Tu et al., 2023e;a). However, the image domain and the difference types of
038   the current IDC dataset are either limited or small-scaled, as summarized in Table 1. This makes the
039
      generalization ability of the current model unsatisfactory; thus, a comprehensive IDC dataset on a
      large scale is needed.
040
041   The IDC dataset consists of the data triplet, including one image pair (the original and the manipulated)
042   and one language caption describing the difference between them. The formal definition is given in
043   Section 3.1. As shown in Table 1, existing datasets focus either on domain-specific images, such
044   as Spot-the-diff (Jhamtani & Berg-Kirkpatrick, 2018a) with frames of the surveillance videos, or
045
      3D-rendered scenes with limited objects and change types (color, texture, add, drop, remove) in
      CLEVR (Park et al., 2019b). Even though image editing request (IER) has various types of editing
046
      on the real natural images, it is limited in volume (∼ 4K) since manual human editing is costly
047
      and time-consuming, making it harder to scale up (Tan et al., 2019). Given the development of
048   generative AI and image editing technologies, language-guided AI-manipulated image data have
049   been created with data triplet: before-edited image, after-edited image, language editing request.
050   InstructPix2Pix (Brooks et al., 2023) leverages GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) to scale up possible editing
051   commands and resort to prompt2prompt (Hertz et al., 2022) for automatic editing. However, we
052   find that it includes a high error rate, which is over 60%. MagicBrush (Zhang et al., 2023) provides
053   10K manually annotated real image editing triplets with careful quality control but only contains
      local edits. It has showcased the importance of high-quality data for language-guided image editing.

      Therefore, we identify a need for an IDC dataset that is varied in manipulation types and maintains
055   high quality at a large scale.
056
057   To better support research in image difference captioning, we introduce the DiffTell dataset, specifi-
058
      cally created to encompass a broader range of editing types, including both real and synthesized image
      pairs, while maintaining careful quality control. We include four categories of image difference:
059
      background change, local object change, text manipulation, and image style change from various
060
      data sources. Examples of the DiffTell dataset are illustrated in Fig. 1. We first include two accessible
061   language-guided image editing datasets InstructPix2Pix (Brooks et al., 2023) and MagicBrush (Zhang
062   et al., 2023). We manually filtered out the noisy, low-quality data in InstructPix2Pix. As text manipu-
063   lation is critical in creating fake news, we enriched the text addition and removal data by inpainting
064   the text in MARIO-10M images (Chen et al., 2023a). In addition, we extended the object addition
065   and removal by inpainting the COCO (Lin et al., 2014) dataset. All AI-generated editing outcomes
066   have passed the quality filtering process. Moreover, since the labor cost of manual quality filtering
067   could be expensive when scaled up, we further learn an automatic data filtering model to reduce
068   the cost and observed the benefit of such an auto filtering process according to model captioning
069
      performance.
070   Multimodal large language model (MLLMs) have become increasingly popular in the research
071   community due to their strong general-purpose capability. By linking large language models (LLMs)
072   with visual conditioning (Liu et al., 2023e; Zhu et al., 2023), MLLMs have shown impressive results
073   in natural instruction-following and visual reasoning capabilities. Meanwhile, the DiffTell dataset
074   can serve as a visual instruct finetuning (Liu et al., 2023e) step upon the multiple MLLM models.
075
      We demonstrate the general improvement of IDC performance using the DiffTell dataset on various
      baselines, indicating its value and benefits. In summary, our contributions are
076
077    • Proposing the DiffTell dataset that includes various kinds of changes with high-quality samples on
078       a larger scale than previous datasets;
079    • Proving that DiffTell can boost the IDC on various baselines on both IER and PSBattle datasets;
080
       • A detailed analysis of how the DiffTell dataset enhances IDC in different editing categories;
081
082    • Probing the model-based data filtering given the fixed amount of human-filtered data, allowing
083
          potential data scale-up.

099   2.1    M ULTIMODAL L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS

      With the development of visual encoder and its combination to large language models (LLMs), multi-
101
      modal large language models (MLLMs) (Liu et al., 2024; 2023c;d; Zhu et al., 2023) show promising
102   capability to understand images, accept text inputs, and generate natural-language responses. Increas-
103   ing the model capacity and dataset size can generally improve the capability of MLLMs (Zhang et al.,
104   2022; Bai et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023c). Visual encoders (Radford et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; 2023)
105   are applied to encode visual information into visual tokens, providing input for the LLMs. Other
106   strategies like expanding the instruction-tuning dataset (Liu et al., 2023a) and increasing the visual
107   resolution (Wang et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b) can also improve the performance of
      the MLLMs. Recently, MLLMs have been used to understand fine-grained images, such as in local

108
      region understanding (Chen et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023f). Image difference captioning is closely
109   related to fine-grained image understanding with multiple-image input.

111
      2.2   I MAGE D IFFERENCE C APTIONING
112
113   As mentioned above, MLLMs are used to understand the local region. Image difference captioning
114   (IDC) is more challenging because the model needs to not only understand each image correctly but
115   also capture and identify the difference between two images correctly and express it precisely in
116   language. In IDC, the caption aims to describe the differences between the images while ignoring
117   their commonalities. The first work on IDC, Spot-the-Diff (Jhamtani & Berg-Kirkpatrick, 2018b),
118   categorizes different types of changes and uses an LSTM-based network to model them. DUDA (Park
119
      et al., 2019a) improves the robustness against slight global changes by analyzing image differences at
      a CNN semantic level instead. Viewpoint invariant encoders have been proposed in M-VAM (Shi
120
      et al., 2020b), VACC (Kim et al., 2021), and VARD (Tu et al., 2023c) to mitigate potential viewpoint
121
      differences, while (Sun et al., 2022) uses bidirectional encoding to improve change localization
122   and NCT (Tu et al., 2023d) aggregates neighboring features with a transformer. IDC-PCL (Yao
123   et al., 2022a) and CLIP4IDC (Guo et al., 2022a) adopt BERT-like training strategies to model the
124   difference-captioning language. SCORER (Tu et al., 2023f) applies a self-supervised cross-view
125   representation reconstruction technique for difference captioning. Recently, with the advancement
126   of MLLMs, more datasets have integrated the existing IDC dataset to train powerful MLLMs with
127   diverse capabilities. For instance, LLaVA-OneVision (Li et al., 2024) includes the CLEVR dataset,
128   while Mantis-Instruct (Jiang et al., 2024) incorporates the Spot-the-Diff dataset.
129
130   2.3   I MAGE E DITING
131
132
      One of the biggest challenges in IDC is the shortage of high-quality, comprehensive datasets of
      paired images. The development of diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020) significantly improves the
133
      quality and controllability of the generated images. By controlling the cross-attention, diffusion
134
      models can transform the image globally (Rombach et al., 2022a; Saharia et al., 2022). Local editing
135   depends on the fine-grained predicted or user-provided mask, such as inpainting (Lugmayr et al.,
136   2022; Nichol et al., 2021; Avrahami et al., 2022). Different from the image transformation and local
137   editing, the input of the instruction-guided image editing is in the command format rather than the
138   detailed description and mask (Brooks et al., 2023). DiffTell significantly benefits from the progress
139   in image generation models (Rombach et al., 2022b), especially the local editing model, leveraging
140   their capabilities to enhance the quality and diversity of the dataset.

144   3.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
145
146   For IDC problem, when presented with two similar images, denoted as I1 and I2 , our objective is to
147   employ a vision-language (VL) model, fθ , to articulate the distinctions between I1 and I2 in natural
148   language. This can be represented as: TI1 ,I2 = fθ (I1 , I2 ), where TI1 ,I2 represents the descriptive
149   caption text provided by the model regarding the dissimilarities between the images, and θ signifies
150   the model parameters within the VL model. The elements I1 , I2 , and TI1 ,I2 collectively form the
151
      constituents of each sample within the IDC dataset.
152

287   Quality Check Statistics We use LabelBox2 as our crowdsourcing platform. Each sample added to
288   DiffTell is initially labeled by an annotator and then reviewed by a high-performing annotator selected
289   by us. To identify high-performing annotators, we have each annotator label 500 images to assess
290   their understanding of the task, and we manually evaluate their accuracy. The top 30% of annotators
291
      are selected as high-performing and assist with the review process on a larger scale. On average, the
      labeling time is 56.73 seconds, while the reviewing time averages 72.44 seconds.

296   human Photoshopped. And we can control the type of difference in the dataset based on the editing
297
      we applied, allowing future balancing and debias of various IDC categories.

395   4.2            M AIN R ESULT
396
397   Quantitative Result We report the experiment results on the IER testing set and PSBattle dataset with
398   and without DiffTell in Table 3. Results demonstrate DiffTell’s ability to enhance performance across
399   nearly all evaluation metrics and for all baseline methods, underscoring the contribution of the DiffTell
400
      dataset on IDC. Notice that OpenFlamingo-3B with LLM backbone is less capable than CLIP4IDC
      with a much smaller model size. We suspect that the Flamingo model does not have direct modeling
401
      of the interaction between the two images because each image feature is cross-attentioned by language
402
      token, then the language tokens will interact via causal attention. In contrast, in CLIP4IDC, the two
403   image patch features extracted by CLIP are fused using a transformer, which is a direct information
404   interaction among image tokens, serving as a strong condition to guide the transformer decoder to
405   generate the language that describes the visual difference. There is no image encoder in the Fuyu
406   model, and the image is patched linearly to the transformer. Thus, Fuyu can accept an image of
407   the arbitrary size, improving its capability to detect tiny differences and small objects. This can be
408   the reason why Fuyu improves greatly after fine-tuning. For Llava-Interleave-8B, the pre-trained
409   interleaved dataset provides a good knowledge base for the model to understand the context with
410   multiple image inputs. Thus, it outperforms the IDC-specific model without DiffTell and can perform
411
      best among all the baselines. In addition, the performance on the PSBattle dataset is generally lower
      than IER, which is as expected since PSBattle is used for zero-shot tests without the training set.
412
413   Qualitative Study We compare the prediction for the OpenFlamingo-3B and CLIP4IDC models
414   trained with and without DiffTell. The visualization examples of IER and PSBattle testing set are
415   shown in Figs. 4 and 5, respectively.
416   As depicted in Fig. 4, the model demonstrates enhanced proficiency in describing local object
417   changes, text detection and recognition, background alterations, and image style changes. In the
418   text manipulation example, the model exhibits OCR capabilities without relying on existing OCR
419   techniques. Notably, in the local object change example, the model accurately identifies the addition
420   of a tattoo on the girl’s back, showcasing its capability to recognize modified objects and discern
421   correct object relationships. Furthermore, in the third example depicting a background change, the
422   model with DiffTell uses around rather than from, underscoring its spatial recognition capability.
423   In the zero-shot testing scenario of PSBattle, anticipating imperfect predictions is reasonable. How-
424   ever, it is crucial to observe the conceptual similarity between predictions and ground truth. Similar
425   to the earlier findings, the model acquires the capability of object change perception and OCR even
426   without an LLM backbone.

507   4.4   AUTOMATIC DATA F ILTERING
508
509   The cost of manual data filtering can become a bottleneck when scaling up this dataset. To address
510   this, we propose an alternative automatic data filtering pipeline, as shown in Fig. 6. Using a dataset
511   previously reviewed by humans, we compile both accepted and rejected samples as the training set
512
      for a binary classifier. The classifier’s input consists of features extracted by the OpenFlamingo-3B
      model, which has been fine-tuned on the IDC task. This classifier can assist annotators in more
513
      efficiently filtering the data.
514
515   To validate the effectiveness of our pipeline, we train a quality classifier on an annotator-validated
516   subset of MARIO-10M, comprising 10K accepted and 10K rejected samples. We use an SVM as the
517   classifier, splitting 16K samples for training and 4K for testing, achieving an accuracy of 85.22%.
518
      The classifier is then applied to unseen data from MARIO-10M, filtering 10K accepted samples.
      This unseen data is newly in-painted using FireFly Generative Fill, as explained in Section 3.3, and
519
      generation stops once 10K accepted samples are collected through the classifier. We compare the
520
      performance on IER dataset of the IDC model (OpenFlamingo-3B) trained on three subsets from
521   MARIO-10M: 10K auto-filtered samples, 10K randomly selected samples, and 10K manually filtered
522   samples. The randomly selected data is taken directly from the in-painted model without quality
523   control, while the manually filtered data is a subset of MARIO-10M used in DiffTell. The results in
524   Table 5 demonstrate that the auto-filtered training data can achieve much better performance than
525   unfiltered data (random data), and be comparable to human filtered training data. Such a result shows
526   the necessity of the filtering step in our designed pipeline and highlights the classifier’s effectiveness
527   and the potential for scaling data collection using this auto-filtering pipeline.
528
529   5     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
530
531   In this study, we introduce DiffTell, an extensive and high-quality dataset for image difference
532   captioning (IDC). This dataset addresses the gaps in diversity and scale that were previously present
533   in the IDC task. Through comprehensive experiments conducted on diverse testing sets and employing
534   various baseline methods, we demonstrate the efficacy of our dataset in enhancing performance.
535   Additionally, we analyze to understand the improvement contributed by each component of DiffTell to
536   different image difference categories. We aspire that DiffTell will play a significant role in advancing
537
      the development of more sophisticated multi-modality models for IDC and language-guided image
      editing in the future. As for future work, at this time, we only use human-filtered data for supervised
538
      fine-tuning. We hope to utilize the human-filtered data (acceptance and rejection) for preference
539
      optimization (Rafailov et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024) to boost the performance.

766   A       E XISTING DATASETS
767
768   The most commonly used datasets in the IDC task are CLEVR change (Park et al., 2019b), Spot-
769   the-Diff (Jhamtani & Berg-Kirkpatrick, 2018b) and Image Editing Request (IER) (Tan et al., 2019).
770   CLEVR change constitutes a sizable synthetic dataset characterized by moderate viewpoint variations.
771   Spot-the-difference is composed of pairs of frames extracted from video surveillance footage and the
772   corresponding textual descriptions of visual changes. IER is crawled from the practical image editing
773   requests from the Reddit channel, consisting of 3,939 pairs of real images, accompanied by 5,695
774
      editing instructions. Each image pair in the training set is associated with one instruction. In contrast,
      each image pair is linked to three instructions for a more objective evaluation in the validation and
775
      testing sets. Because IER is collected from a real-world scenario, it covers more image difference
776
      categories such as background change, text manipulation, and local object change. The definition of
777   the image difference categories can be found in Section 3.2. Due to the single domain in CLEVR
778   and Spot the Difference datasets, we mainly use IER in this work as the testing set, which aligns our
779   scope to have a comprehensive, diverse, and practical dataset.
