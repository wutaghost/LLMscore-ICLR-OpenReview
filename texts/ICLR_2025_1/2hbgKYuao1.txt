009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011       We propose an expressive and efficient approach that combines the strengths of two prominent
012       extensions of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs): Subgraph GNNs and Structural Encodings (SEs).
013       Our approach leverages walk-based centrality measures, both as a powerful form of SE and also
014       as a subgraph selection strategy for Subgraph GNNs. By drawing a connection to perturbation
015       analysis, we highlight the effectiveness of centrality-based sampling, and show it significantly
016       reduces the computational burden associated with Subgraph GNNs. Further, we combine our
017       efficient Subgraph GNN with SEs derived from the calculated centrality and demonstrate this
018
          hybrid approach, dubbed HyMN, gains in discriminative power. HyMN effectively addresses
          the expressiveness limitations of Message Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs) while mitigating
019
          the computational costs of Subgraph GNNs. Through a series of experiments on synthetic and
020
          real-world tasks, we show it outperforms other subgraph sampling approaches while being
021       competitive with full-bag Subgraph GNNs and other state-of-the-art approaches with a notably
022       reduced runtime.

025   1     I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Scarselli et al., 2009; Gori et al., 2005; Micheli, 2009) have
028   achieved great success in learning tasks with graph-structured data. Typically, GNNs are based on
029   the message passing paradigm (Gilmer et al., 2017), in which node features are aggregated over
030   their local neighborhood recursively, resulting in architectures known as Message Passing Neural
031   Networks (MPNNs). MPNNs have been shown to suffer from limited expressive power: they are
032   bounded by 1-WL (Xu et al., 2018; Geerts & Reutter, 2023; Weisfeiler & Leman, 1968; Morris et al.,
033
      2019; 2023) cannot count certain substructures (Chen et al., 2020; Bouritsas et al., 2022; Tahmasebi
      et al., 2020) or solve graph bi-connectivity tasks (Zhang et al., 2023b).
034
035   In order to improve expressive power, some works augment the node features with Structural
036   Encodings (SE) or Positional Encodings (PE) (Bouritsas et al., 2022; Dwivedi et al., 2021; Fesser
037   & Weber, 2024). These predefined features provide additional information on the graph topology,
038   leading to an improvement on benchmarks compared to using the raw features (Rampášek et al., 2022).
039
      Recently, Subgraph GNNs (Bevilacqua et al., 2021; Frasca et al., 2022; Zhang & Li, 2021; Cotta
      et al., 2021) have been proposed to overcome some of the expressivity limitations of MPNNs. This
040
      approach preserves equivariance while relying less on feature engineering. First, a Subgraph GNN
041
      transforms a graph into a “bag of subgraphs” based on a specific selection policy. These subgraphs are
042   then processed by an equivariant architecture and aggregated to make graph- or node-level predictions.
043   One common approach to generate subgraphs, known as node-marking, is to mark a single node in
044   the graph (Papp & Wattenhofer, 2022). In this case, each subgraph is then “tied” to a specific node in
045   the original graph, and a shared MPNN generates a representation for each subgraph.
046
      In general, Subgraph GNNs show good empirical performance but come with a high computational
047
      cost. For a graph of N nodes and a maximum degree dmax , then Subgraph GNNs with node-marking
048   based policies have a computational complexity O(N 2 · dmax ). To reduce computational complexity,
049   it was suggested sampling subgraphs randomly (Bevilacqua et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2022) or, more
050   recently, learning sampling policies (Qian et al., 2022; Bevilacqua et al., 2024; Kong et al., 2024).
051   However, while random approaches have been shown to be suboptimal (Bevilacqua et al., 2024;
052   Bar-Shalom et al., 2024b), learnable policies are difficult to train in practice and become impractical
053   when sampling a larger number of subgraphs, due to the use of discrete sampling and RL-based
      objectives.

054
      In this work, we present an approach that drastically reduces the complexity of Subgraph GNNs while
055   maintaining their competitive performance. We achieve this by identifying easy-to-compute struc-
056   tural features that unlock a parsimonious yet effective subgraph selection strategy, while providing
057   complementary information enhancing the model’s expressiveness.
058
      We first identify a family of node centrality measures (Estrada & Rodriguez-Velazquez, 2005; Benzi
059
      & Klymko, 2013; 2014) as easy-to-compute scores that: (i) recapitulate well the extent to which a
060
      subgraph alters the graph representation; (ii) correlate with relevant substructure counts. In light of
061   this, we propose to leverage these measures to efficiently and effectively reduce the size of bags of
062   subgraphs without requiring learning of additional components. Specifically, we prioritize selecting
063   marked subgraphs associated with the top-ranking nodes according to these centrality measures and,
064   in particular, to the Subgraph Centrality by Estrada & Rodriguez-Velazquez (2005).
065
      Then, we propose to additionally interpret Centrality scores as Structural Encodings (CSEs), and to
066
      utilize them to augment the node features of the selected subgraphs. This combination of approaches
067   is justified from an expressiveness perspective, as we show neither of the two subsume the other.
068   We demonstrate node marking of (selected) subgraphs separates graphs that are not separable by
069   CSE-based feature augmentations and, vice-versa, that CSEs allow to separate pairs indistinguishable
070   to our subsampled Subgraph GNNs.
071
      The resulting method, dubbed HyMN (Hybrid Marking Network), is an approach whereby Subgraph
072
      GNNs and Structural Encodings work in tandem to effectively overcome the expressiveness limitations
073   of MPNNs and unlock the application of Subgraph GNNs to larger graphs previously out of reach.
074   Our approach is provably expressive, does not require feature engineering or learnable sampling
075   components and, importantly, maintains a low computational cost. The practical value of our approach
076   is confirmed by the positive results achieved over a series of experimental analyses conducted
077   over synthetic and real-world benchmarks. We show HyMN outperforms other subgraph selection
078   strategies, and performs on par or better than full-bag Subgraph GNNs by only sampling one or two
079   subgraphs. Additionally, HyMN is competitive to (and sometimes better than) Graph Transformers
080   and other state-of-the-art GNNs, while, as we show through extensive wall-clock timing analyses,
081
      featuring a more contained computational run-time.
082   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
083
      1. We show that walk-based node centrality measures and, in particular, the Subgraph Centrality
084
         of Estrada & Rodriguez-Velazquez (2005), represent a simple and effective indicator of subgraph
085      importance for subsampling bags in Subgraph GNNs.
086   2. We demonstrate that centrality-based structural features can be employed as Structural Encodings
087      to enhance the discriminative power of Subgraph GNNs with subsampled bags of subgraphs.
088   3. We provide strong experimental evidence showcasing the effectiveness of our sampling strategy
089      and the efficacy of additionally incorporating centrality-based SEs.
090
      Overall, our results validate our method as a simple, expressive and efficient approach that competes
091   with state-of-the-art GNNs with reduced empirirical run-times.

098   Expressive power of MPNNs. The expressive power of MPNNs has become a central research topic
099
      since they were shown to be bounded by the 1-WL isomorphism test (Morris et al., 2019; Xu et al.,
      2018; Morris et al., 2023). This has led to approaches which aim to obtain different representations
100
      for non-isomorphic but 1-WL equivalent graphs. These include using random features (Sato et al.,
101
      2021; Abboud et al., 2020), higher-order message passing schemes (Bodnar et al., 2021b;a; Morris
102   et al., 2019; 2020b) and equivariant models (Maron et al., 2018; Vignac et al., 2020). One of the most
103   common approaches is to inject positional and structural encodings into the input layer (Bouritsas
104   et al., 2022; Fesser & Weber, 2024; Dwivedi et al., 2021; Kreuzer et al., 2021) using Laplacian
105   PEs (Dwivedi & Bresson, 2020; Kreuzer et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Lim et al., 2023; 2024) or
106   distance information (Ying et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020). A random-walk based encoding (RWSE)
107   was proposed in (Dwivedi et al., 2021) and was shown to be able to distinguish regular graphs such
      as the 1-WL-equivalent CSL graphs (Murphy et al., 2019).

      Subgraph GNNs. A recent line of work has proposed representing a graph as a collection of
109   subgraphs obtained by a specific selection policy (Zhang & Li, 2021; Cotta et al., 2021; Papp &
110   Wattenhofer, 2022; Bevilacqua et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2022; Papp et al., 2021; Frasca et al., 2022;
111   Qian et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a). These approaches, jointly referred to
112   as Subgraph GNNs, allow to overcome the expressivity limitations of MPNNs without introducing
113   predefined encodings. A powerful, common selection policy termed node marking involves generating
114   a subgraph per node by marking that node in the original graph, with no connectivity alterations.
115   Although it can increase expressive power beyond 1-WL (Frasca et al., 2022; You et al., 2021),
116   this approach has a high computational complexity as we need to consider and process N different
117
      subgraphs, where N is the number of nodes in the original graph.
118   Several recent papers have focused on scaling these methods to larger graphs. Beyond random
119   sampling (Bevilacqua et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2022), Qian et al. (2022) first proposed gradient-based
120   techniques to learn how to subsample the bag of subgraphs. Bevilacqua et al. (2024) introduced
121   Policy-Learn (PL), which iteratively predicts a distribution over nodes in the graph and samples
122   subgraphs from the full-bag accordingly. A similar approach, called MAG-GNN has also proposed to
123
      sample subgraphs using Reinforcement Learning (RL) (Kong et al., 2024). Both of these approaches
      involve discrete sampling which can complicate the training process, often requiring 1, 000 − 4, 000
124
      training epochs. Another recent approach leverages the connection between Subgraph GNNs and
125
      graph products (Bar-Shalom et al., 2024a) to run message passing on the product of the original
126   graph and a coarsened version thereof (Bar-Shalom et al., 2024b). The control over the computational
127   complexity generally comes from the existence of cluster-like structures in the graph which, however,
128   may not be aligned with the preset number of subgraphs. Additionally, the locality bias afforded by
129   the coarsening may not generally be effective across tasks. In such cases, coarsening approaches that
130   are not learnable and based, e.g., on spectral clustering could lead to suboptimal results.
131
      Node Centrality. One common way to characterize nodes in a graph is by using the concept of node
132   centrality. A node centrality measure defines a real-valued function on the nodes, c : V → R, which
133   can be used to rank nodes within a network by their “importance”. Different concepts of importance
134   have led to a myriad of measures in the Network Science community. They range from the simple
135   Degree Centrality (central nodes have the highest degrees) to path-based methods (Freeman, 1977)
136   like the Betweenness Centrality (central nodes fall on the shortest paths between many node-pairs).
137   An important family of centrality measures quantifies the importance of nodes based on walk counts.
138   As noted in (Benzi & Klymko, 2014), most of these measures take the form of a power series,
139   where numbers of walks for any lengths are aggregated with an appropriate discounting scheme.
140
      Prominent examples include the Katz Index (Katz, 1953) (KI) and the Subgraph Centrality (Estrada
      & Rodriguez-Velazquez, 2005) (SC):

      We focus on Subgraph GNNs with a node-marking selection policy (Papp & Wattenhofer, 2022; You
155
      et al., 2021). Given an N -node graph G = (A, X), a node-marking Subgraph GNN processes a bag of
156
      subgraphs obtained from G, viz. BG = {{S1 , S2 , . . . , SN }}. Here, Si = (A, Xi ) and Xi = X ⊕ xvi ,
157   where ⊕ denotes channel-wise concatenation and xvi is a one-hot indicator vector for node vi .

159   Goal. In order to reduce the computational complexity of a Subgraph GNN, we aim to reduce the
160   size of the bag by efficiently and effectively sampling k < N subgraphs.

      The sampling procedure must be efficient in that it should avoid computationally complex operations
163   or the use of learnable components requiring more involved training protocols (Qian et al., 2022;
164   Bevilacqua et al., 2024; Kong et al., 2024). Ideally, it should consist of a simple and lightweight
165   preprocessing step prior to running a chosen Subgraph GNN. The sampling procedure must addition-
166   ally be effective, which means it should closely approach the performance of a full-bag Subgraph
167   GNN with as few subgraphs as possible. Put differently, it should prioritize marking those nodes
168   that most quickly lead to performance improvements. As an example, to ground the discussion:
169   randomly selecting subgraphs (Bevilacqua et al., 2021; Papp et al., 2021) is an efficient but not
170   effective strategy; learning which subgraph to mark via RL (Kong et al., 2024) is a more effective
171
      approach, but it may not be efficient enough.
172   We start by presenting considerations on effectiveness which will naturally lead us to focus on walk-
173   based centrality measures for our purposes. Upon them, we show we can build an effective strategy
174   that is also efficiently executed as a simple preprocessing step.
175
176   3.2   E FFECTIVE N ODE M ARKING
177
178   What makes a node a good marking candidate? We propose approaching this question by considering
179
      node marking as a graph perturbation. Marking a node changes the initial node features: this
      alteration in the input will ultimately be reflected in the output graph representation, and understanding
180
      how node marking impacts the output graph representation is instrumental in designing effective
181
      sampling strategies. Beyond binary graph separation, we claim that an effective marking should be
182   able to (i) alter graph representations sufficiently; (ii) induce perturbations that correlate with graph
183   targets. Ideally, when marking nodes jointly optimizes (i) and (ii), a Subgraph GNN with a small
184   bag can then improve on a standard MPNN by sufficiently separating more graphs and in a way that
185   assists the training objective.
186

      graphs representations, we analyze the simplest case: marking a single node. In particular, we ask

189   MPNN, we consider an L-layer GIN (Xu et al., 2018; Chuang & Jegelka, 2022):

      High-centrality marking induces the largest perturbations. As a first experiment, we examine
222
      the extent to which node centralities recapitulate the amount of perturbation induced by marking
223   their corresponding nodes. We consider the same setting discussed above: marking one node to
224   transition from G to Sv , where Sv is obtained from G by marking node v. For a centrality measure c,
225   we consider three cases: v attains the minimum of c (i), the maximum of c (ii), is randomly picked
226   (iii). In each of these cases, we measured the distance ∥f (Sv ) − f (G)∥ on 100 graphs from two
227   different real-world datasets from the popular TU suite: MUTAG and NCI1 (Morris et al., 2020a).
228   Here, f is an untrained 3-layer GIN (Xu et al., 2018). Figure 1 shows results for the walk-based
229   Subgraph Centrality (Estrada & Rodriguez-Velazquez, 2005), where horizontal lines indicate the
230   average representation distance, and (i), (ii), (iii) are color-coded, resp., in green, blue, red.
231   From the plots, it is clearly visible how marking nodes with the lowest centrality leads to the smallest
232   change in graph representation. This result gives direct empirical validation to the upper-bound
233   analysis and the consequential observation discussed above. In accordance with our claim, subgraphs
234   associated with low centrality nodes can be interpreted as “redundant” w.r.t. the original input graph,
235   or simply as poor marking candidates. Second, we note that marking nodes with the highest centrality
236   induces the highest average perturbations, above random marking. This result is particularly relevant
237
      as it complements the above theoretical analysis: the walk-based upper-bound (Equation (3)) only
      suggests, but does not necessarily entail, that high-centrality marking associates with the largest
238
      perturbations. Figure 1 shows that this occurs in practice on these datasets, further motivating our
239
      proposed sampling strategy. Results for other centrality measures are found in Appendix D; they
240   indicate that high-centrality marking leads, in all cases, to larger perturbations than random marking,
241   with the highest values attained by walk-based centrality measures (see Section 2).
242
243
      High-centrality marking aligns with substructure counting. We have shown how marking nodes
      with higher centrality can lead to larger graph perturbations. However, this may not be sufficient. As
244
      an example, consider, two (non-isomorphic but) 1-WL-equivalent graphs. Node marking can alter
245
      their message-passing-based representations, but not necessarily in a way to induce separation: ideally,
246   if the two graphs are associated with different targets we aim to induce dissimilar perturbations so to
247   assist the training goal. In effect, we want our sampling strategy to alter the graph representations in
248   a way that is consistent with the target space. Motivated by the observation that the presence and
249   number of structural “motifs” are often related to graph-level tasks (Kanatsoulis & Ribeiro, 2024),
250   we empirically study how marking-induced perturbations correlate with counting small substructures,
251   as a general, yet relevant, predictor for graph-level targets.
252   We randomly generate 100 Erdös-Renyi (ER) graphs, each with N = 20 nodes and wiring probability
253   p = 0.3. Similarly as above, we experiment with various centrality measures, by marking a single
254   node v which attains either the maximum or minimum centrality value, or is randomly picked. Again,
255   we record the perturbation ∥f (Sv ) − f (G)∥ given by the same architecture described above. On the
256   same graphs, we count the number of various substructures, and evaluate the Pearson correlation
257   between this value and the recorded perturbations. Results are reported in Table 1.
258   The top section of the table compares the correlations obtained by marking randomly or based on the
259   Subgraph Centrality. The perturbations induced by high-centrality marking correlate significantly
260   more with the considered substructures than those induced by low-centrality-based or random marking.
261   The bottom section of the table presents results for other centralities not based on walks. We note how
262   they all deliver better correlations w.r.t. random marking, but not as high as those attained by the SC.
263
      Discussion. Overall our experiments indicate the following. First, high-centrality sampling appears
264   to be a better approach than random sampling, especially when walk-based centrality measures are
265   employed: on average, it selects marking candidates inducing the largest amount of perturbations
266   over the original graph representations, and in a way that correlates with counts of relevant graph
267   substructures. Second, the walk-based Subgraph Centrality stands out as a particularly promising
268   candidate: it is efficient to precalculate this measure and sample subgraphs based on that, while, on
269   average, it performed as the best one in the experiments discussed above.

412   Our experiments aim to validate arguments in the previous sections and to empirically answer the
413
      following questions:
414   (Q1) Can Subgraph Centrality be used to effectively subsample subgraphs for Subgraph GNNs?
415   (Q2) Can HyMN efficiently scale to graphs out of reach for Subgraph GNNs?
416   (Q3) How does HyMN perform on real-world datasets w.r.t. strong GNN baselines?
417   (Q4) What is the impact of incorporating CSEs?
418   Synthetic experiment for counting substructures. The ability of a model to count local substruc-
419   tures is an acknowledged way of evaluating its expressive power (Bouritsas et al., 2022; Arvind et al.,
420   2020; Tahmasebi et al., 2020). In order to answer (Q1) and test the efficacy of subgraph sampling with
421   node centrality, we explored the ability of a Subgraph GNN to count different small substructures as
422   we increase the number of subgraphs in our bag. We closely followed the experimental procedure of
423   (Chen et al., 2020), but modified the data generation process to render the task more challenging and
424   informative4 . We compared the performance of sampling subgraphs based on different approaches:
425
      random sampling and sampling based on the highest values of different centrality measures. No
      CSEs are employed in this setting. Section 5 reports results for triangle and 4-cycle counting. These
426
      results demonstrate the significant improvement afforded by high-centrality sampling over random
427
      sampling with fewer subgraphs (Q1). Additionally, we show that SC-based sampling generally outper-
428   formed other centrality measures, demonstrating the benefits of focusing on walk-based centralities,
429   in alignment with our analysis in Section 3. Appendix D reports results for additional substructures,

       F.1        T HE ARCHITECTURAL FAMILY OF S UBGRAPH GNN S
1596
       The term “Subgraph GNN” refers to a broad family of recent Graph Neural Networks sharing a
1597   common architectural pattern: that of modeling graphs as sets (bags) of subgraphs. Subgraphs are pro-
1598   cessed by a backbone GNN, possibly flanked by additional information sharing modules (Bevilacqua
1599   et al., 2021). Bags of subgraphs are formed by selection policies, which typically extract subgraphs
1600   by applying topological perturbations such as node- (Cotta et al., 2021; Papp et al., 2021) or edge-
1601   deletions (Bevilacqua et al., 2021), or by marking nodes (You et al., 2021; Papp & Wattenhofer,
1602   2022).
1603   In formulae, a Subgraph GNN f can be described as (Frasca et al., 2022):
1604
1605                                   f : G 7→ µ ◦ ρ ◦ S ◦ π (G),                                           (43)
1606   where π is the selection policy; S applies the backbone GNN – with, potentially, information sharing
1607   components; ρ, µ are pooling and prediction modules.
1608
       Various choices for the above terms give rise to different Subgraph GNN variants (Frasca et al.,
1609
       2022). For non-trivial selection policies and sufficiently expressive backbones, these exceed 1-WL
1610   discriminative power (Bevilacqua et al., 2021), thus surpassing standard message-passing networks.
1611
1612   The most popular selection policies are node-based: selected subgraphs are in a bijection with nodes
1613
       in the original input graph. Prominent policies in this class include node-deletion, ego-networks
       and node-marking. From an expressiveness perspective, node-marking subsumes the first two
1614
       policies (Papp & Wattenhofer, 2022; Zhang et al., 2023a). This policy constructs bags of subgraphs
1615
       as:
1616                         πNM : G = (A, X) 7→ {(A, X ⊕ e1 ), . . . , (A, X ⊕ en )},                 (44)
1617
       where ei ∈ {0, 1}n×1 is i-th element of the canonical basis 10 and ⊕ denotes concatenation across
1618
       the channel dimension.

1620
       Node-based Subgraph GNNs encompass several architectures, including ID-GNNs (You et al., 2021),
1621   (n−1)-Reconstruction GNNs (Cotta et al., 2021), Nested GNNs (Zhang & Li, 2021), GNN-AK (Zhao
1622   et al., 2022), SUN (Frasca et al., 2022) and the maximally expressive GNN-SSWL (Zhang et al.,
1623   2023a). For 1-WL-expressive backbones, Subgraph GNNs with node-based policies are bounded in
1624   their expressive power by 3-WL (Frasca et al., 2022). Detailed, structured charting of their design
1625   space, along with fine-grained expressiveness results, are found in (Frasca et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
1626   2023a).
