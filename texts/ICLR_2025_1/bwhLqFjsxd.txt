009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            As humans seek to collaborate with, learn from, and better understand artificial
012            intelligence systems, developing AI agents that can accurately emulate individual
013            decision-making becomes increasingly important. Chess, with its long-standing
014            role as a benchmark for AI research and its precise measurement of skill through
015            chess ratings, provides an ideal environment for studying human-AI alignment.
016            However, existing approaches to modeling human behavior require large amounts of
017            data from each individual, making them impractical for new or sparsely represented
018
               users. In this work, we introduce Maia4All, a model designed to learn and adapt
               to individual decision-making styles efficiently, even with limited data. Maia4All
019
               achieves this by leveraging a two-stage fine-tuning method to bridge population
020
               and individual-level models and uses a meta-network to initialize and refine these
021            embeddings with minimal data. Our experimental results show that Maia4All
022            can accurately predict individual moves and profile behavioral patterns with high
023            fidelity, establishing a new standard for personalized human-like AI behavior
024            modeling in chess. Our work provides an example of how population AI systems
025            can flexibly adapt to individual users using a prototype model as a bridge, which
026            could lead to better and more accessible human-AI collaboration in other fields like
027            education, healthcare, and strategic decision-making. Maia4All implementation is
028            available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/UChess-3103.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The rise of artificial intelligence (AI) systems that rival or surpass human ability in domains where
      humans remain active has introduced the possibility of people collaborating with and learning from
034
      AI agents. A line of research has pursued this vision in the model system of chess, where AI became
035
      superhuman 20 years ago, people vary widely in their ability, and vast detailed datasets of action traces
036   abound. Since capturing human decision-making style is a prerequisite to algorithmically-informed
037   teaching and collaboration, previous work has focused on creating AI agents that mimic human
038   play (McIlroy-Young et al., 2020; 2022; Jacob et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2024). Further, since capturing
039   individual decision-making style is a prerequisite to personally tailored algorithmic instruction,
040   researchers have developed models of how specific people play chess, surpassing population models
041   in their accuracy rates on their target individual’s decisions (McIlroy-Young et al., 2022).
042   However, these fine-tuning-based models require extraordinary amounts of data per person to function.
043   When Maia, a human-like chess engine, was fine-tuned to play like specific individuals, gains in
044   accuracy over base Maia were only achieved when the player had 5,000 games worth of data (McIlroy-
045   Young et al., 2022). This is an immense amount of game-playing; a typical person would take around
046   1,000 hours to play this many games, which is equivalent to almost 25 weeks of full-time work at 40
047   hours per week. To put this in perspective, less than 1% of players on Lichess, a popular online chess
048   platform, have played at least 5,000 games. Therefore, the fine-tuning-based approaches explored in
049   previous work are proofs of concept that individual-level modeling is possible in chess, but it isn’t a
050
      full solution to the problem because it doesn’t work for the vast majority of people who would stand
      to benefit from algorithmically-informed teaching, learning, and collaboration.
051
052   How could we go about modeling individual-level decision-making behavior for people with much
053   more modest amounts of data available? This is a difficult task for two reasons. First, existing
      models for modeling human decision-making, such as Maia and Maia-2 (McIlroy-Young et al., 2022;


                                                         1
054
      Tang et al., 2024), are population models. This makes direct fine-tuning difficult, especially for
055   low-resource players, as previously discussed. Second, human action prediction is formulated as a
056   generative task to predict the next move that requires a model with strong generalization capabilities,
057   which is particularly hard to achieve in a low-resource environment.
058
      In this work, we propose Maia4All, a model that overcomes both of these challenges and can
059
      successfully predict chess moves at the individual level. Strikingly, Maia4All can model individual-
060
      level play with only 20 games of data. While Maia-2 shows virtually no progress when given 20
061   games of data played by a specific individual, and Maia fine-tuned with 1,000 games even gets worse,
062   Maia4All significantly rises in accuracy from a baseline of 51.4% to 53.2%—a comparable rise to
063   the accuracy gains reported in previous work using 5,000 games per player (McIlroy-Young et al.,
064   2022).
065
      We achieve data-efficient modeling of individual behavior in chess with two methodological con-
066
      tributions. First, we design a two-stage fine-tuning approach, where we first fine-tune Maia-2 to a
067   diverse set of prototype players with rich game histories in order to adapt the model parameters from
068   population-level modeling to individual-level modeling. Empirically, this makes it easier for the
069   model to further adapt to low-resource players. In the second stage, this prototype-infused model with
070   individual-level modeling capabilities is used as a bridge to be further fine-tuned with low-resource
071   player data. Our second contribution is to start with a discriminative task instead of attempting the
072   difficult generative task directly; we first find the most similar prototype player to the target player we
073   want to model with a prototype-matching meta-network. Once we’ve identified a suitable prototype
074   player, we initialize the target player’s embedding with the prototype’s embedding, and fine-tune on
075
      their limited data with this much better start.
076   Our framework not only provides state-of-the-art human modeling in chess, which can open the
077   door to personalized AI teaching, learning, and collaboration, but it also holds potential for broader
078   applications in domains where human-AI collaboration and algorithmically-informed education are
079   possible.

082   2    R ELATED W ORK
083
084
085   Human Behavior Modeling in Chess. The challenge of creating a chess engine that can outplay any
086   human was solved over 20 years ago. The research focus shifts towards extracting useful knowledge
087
      from these superhuman systems for humans. A direct way of doing this is to probe an AI chess
      engine in a human representation space. Evidence of human chess concepts learned by AlphaZero
088
      is found and measured by linear probes (McGrath et al., 2022). Going further, AlphaZero also
089
      encodes knowledge that extends beyond existing human knowledge but is ultimately learnable by
090   humans (Schut et al., 2023). Another direction was the creation of a ‘behavioral stylometry’ model
091   that can identify chess players from the moves they play (McIlroy-Young et al., 2021). Moreover,
092   efforts have been made towards creating systems that can act as guides to humans (McIlroy-Young
093   et al., 2020; Jacob et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2024), in which a model is trained to predict the next
094   move a human will play, instead of optimizing for winning the game. In addition to predicting human
095   actions at the population level, the models have been fine-tuned for individual-level human behavior
096   modeling under data-rich settings (McIlroy-Young et al., 2022).
097
098   Few-shot Learning and Meta Learning. Few-shot learning focuses on the ability of models to
099
      learn and generalize from a very limited amount of labeled training data (Fei-Fei et al., 2006; Fink,
      2004; Wang et al., 2020). Modeling unseen players follows the few-shot learning paradigm, where
100
      players’ behavioral patterns are revealed by a limited collection of historical behaviors. Meta learning
101
      is a main approach to few-shot learning, aiming to improve novel tasks’ performance by training
102   on similar tasks. Meta learning can be categorized into metric-based methods (Vinyals et al., 2016;
103   Snell et al., 2017; Koch et al., 2015; Sung et al., 2018) that aim to learn a similarity or distance
104   function over objects and represent the relationship between inputs and the task space, model-
105   based methods (Santoro et al., 2016; Munkhdalai & Yu, 2017), which focus on designing models
106   with internal mechanisms to quickly adapt to new tasks, and optimization-based methods (Ravi &
107   Larochelle, 2016; Finn et al., 2017; Nichol et al., 2018; Raghu et al., 2019) that aim to learn an
      initialization such that the model can adapt faster with few examples from there. Maia4All can be


                                                          2
108
      regarded as a meta learning framework in that we learn a prototype matching meta network for player
109   embedding initialization.
110
111   Imitation Learning. We can also frame this work as part of the imitation learning tradition, where a
112   model is trained to perform some task after observing expert (human) demonstration(s) (Schaal, 1999;
113   Zare et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2019). In the imitation learning context the model is usually attempting
114   to learn a value function (inverse RL) (Ng et al., 2000), or to quickly learn an optimal solution to a
115   given optimization problem (Schaal, 1999). In this paper we attempt to learn a flawed value function
116   using non-expert demonstrations. Additionally, many imitation learning methods require the model
117   to be in the same, or similar, state to the demonstrated one (Ho & Ermon, 2016; Zare et al., 2024)
118
      which is a condition that is impossible to guarantee in chess outside of the early game.

      3.1   OVERVIEW
124
      As shown in Figure 1. (a) and (b), either the well-established human-like chess engine Maia (McIlroy-
125
      Young et al., 2020; 2022) and the state-of-the-art model Maia-2 (Tang et al., 2024) can be used as
126   the population-level pre-trained foundation model to be fine-tuned towards individual players. We
127   use Maia-2 as the base population model as it enables us to adjust its predictions by only varying the
128   population embeddings. This property is particularly desirable for individual behavior modeling
129   because the problem of adapting to individual players can be reduced to finding representative player
130   embeddings. Thus the variant player embeddings can guide the personalized adjustments of human
131   move predictions over a frozen model.
132   We face two challenges in fine-tuning Maia-2 for players with rare game histories. On the one hand,
133   Maia-2 is a population model, which means its parameters are trained toward modeling common
134   behavioral patterns among groups of players. This makes direct fine-tuning difficult, especially
135   for low-resource players. Therefore, as shown in Figure1. (c), we design a two-stage fine-tuning
136   approach to bridge population-oriented and individual-oriented parameters. To achieve this, we first
137   fine-tune Maia-2 to a diverse set of prototype players with rich game histories (Maia-2-Prototype). In
138   the second stage, the Maia-2-Prototype model with individual-level modeling capabilities is used as a
139   bridge to be further fine-tuned to model low-resource players.
140   On the other hand, human action prediction is formulated as a generative task to predict the next move
141   that requires a model with strong generalization capabilities, which is particularly hard to achieve
142   in a low-resource environment. Therefore, we start with a discriminative task instead of attempting
143   the difficult generative task directly. We first find the most similar prototype player to the target
144   player we want to model with a prototype-matching meta-network, as shown in Figure 2. Once we’ve
145   identified a suitable prototype player, we initialize the target player’s embedding with the prototype’s
146
      embedding, and fine-tune on their limited data with this much better start.

      3.3   M AIA -2-P ROTOTYPE
200
201   We build on Maia-2 to develop the individual model:
202
203                                                                        Maia-2-Prototype(B, i|θ′ , EI ),                                                                                                     (2)
204   where EI denote the embedding matrix of individual players. The shared parameters θ′ are initialized
205   with θ. Since θ is already trained towards modeling diverse groups of players, it is easier to update to
206   a universal set of parameters that models the more fine-grained individual-level move predictions.
207
      While Maia-2-Prototype can be directly used to model the behaviors of individual players in I, it
208
      is unrealistic to include all players (e.g., 11 million on Lichess) in Maia-2-Prototype. On the one
209
      hand, learning a separate embedding for huge amounts of players can be challenging for computation
210   and model learnability. On the other hand, most players haven’t played enough games to support an
211   accurately learned embedding for them. Furthermore, there are always new chess players, making
212   it infeasible to have their embedding pre-trained. Therefore, we regard Maia-2-Prototype as a base
213   model that can facilitate further adaptation to all individual players, in particular players with rare
214   histories. This extensibility requirement guided us to select the player set I in Maia-2-Prototype under
215   two criteria: First, players should have sufficient historical games to ensure that their decision-making
      styles can be well learned in EI and so θ′ is not being interfered by under-trained embeddings.


                                                                                                             4
216
      Second, the number of players should be balanced for each strength level. This design not only helps
217   the model to learn a universal set of parameters θ′ for an unbiased distribution of players but also
218   facilitates prototype-informed initialization.
219
220
      Following previous work (McIlroy-Young et al., 2020), besides the main policy head to predict the
      human move, we include a value head to predict the game outcome as a regression task, where
221
      the labels 1, 0, -1 denote winning, drawing, and losing, respectively. To enhance the model’s
222
      understanding of the game state, we inject auxiliary information as labels, including legal moves
223   represented by multi-hot vectors and human move information: one-hot vectors of which piece is
224   moved, which piece is captured (if any), the move’s originating square, the move’s destination square,
225   and whether or not the move will deliver a check. It enriches the model’s understanding of human
226   chess moves by providing contextual insights beyond mere move indices and integrating essential
227   objective knowledge of the game’s mechanics, which has been proven to be helpful in human move
228   prediction (Tang et al., 2024). This head is trained using bit-wise binary cross-entropy loss with
229   multi-hot labels. The training objectives of these heads are balanced to contribute equally to model
230   optimization.

237   3.4.1    M AIA 4A LL F INE - TUNING
238
239   Ideally, player strengths and styles can be well-learned by directly fine-tuning Maia4All with the new
240   player embedding e:
241                                                       Full fine-tuning
242
                      Maia-2-Prototype(B, u|θ′ , eu ) −−−−−−−−→ Maia4All(B, u|θ′′ , e′u )                  (3)
243   where the full parameter set is fine-tuned. However, since modeling unseen players follows the
244   few-shot learning paradigm, severe overfitting is expected when limited historical behaviors are
245   provided. Moreover, full fine-tuning results in a shifted player embedding space for each player.
246   As a result, the player embeddings learned are not transferable or directly comparable with other
247   (pre-trained or future) player embeddings, which hinders its applications in downstream tasks such as
248   behavioral stylometry (McIlroy-Young et al., 2021).
249   Since the shared parameters θ′ in Maia4All are specifically learned towards modeling a wide range
250   of individual players, this enables player embedding fine-tuning without updating θ′ :
251                                                       Emb fine-tuning
252                   Maia-2-Prototype(B, u|θ′ , eu ) −−−−−−−−→ Maia4All(B, u|θ′ , e′u )                   (4)
253   Note that the universal set of parameters θ′ are shared across pre-trained or unseen players. The
254   number of trainable parameters is drastically reduced to the embedding dimension d, preventing
255   overfitting and enhancing knowledge sharing on the general understanding of individual behavior
256   modeling.
257
      Nevertheless, fine-tuning under low-resource settings is still challenging. Therefore, we propose to
258
      initialize unseen player embeddings with prior knowledge for data-efficient parameter updates. Such
259   prior knowledge can be player strengths, as reflected by their ratings, and player styles, as derived
260   from their historical behaviors.
261
262   3.4.2    S TRENGTH I NFORMED I NITIALIZATION
263
264   In chess, decision-making style is informed by one’s strength: e.g., a novice will typically not employ
265   deep stratagems that indicative of grandmaster-level insight. Therefore, the player strength levels are
266
      ideal starting points to learn player styles:
267                                               Initialize
                                              eu ←−−−− E′P [r(u)],                                         (5)
268
269   where E′P denote the learned population embedding matrix in Maia-2-Prototype and r(u) represent
      the strength level of player u.


                                                           5
270
      However, player strengths are not accurately measured by the ratings until many games with different
271   opponents are played and recorded. In real-world applications, player strength is very likely to be
272   unknown and may be inaccurate. For example, when a grandmaster player joins an online chess
273   platform as a new member, the rating will start to update from a preset rating that is close to the
274   average ratings of the crowd. In this case, until many games are played on this platform, the player’s
275   rating will be far from the grandmaster level. Therefore, we need a procedure to initialize the player
276   embedding that does not rely on the long-term strength measurements provided by ratings.
277
278   3.4.3    P ROTOTYPE I NFORMED I NITIALIZATION
279
      We aim to initialize unseen player embeddings with similar player embeddings. We denote the
280
      pre-trained players as prototypes to be matched. Since we balanced the number of players within each
281
      strength level during Maia-2-Prototype training, prototypical players should ideally cover the player
282   styles within each level. We train a transformer-based meta-network for prototype matching, i.e.,
283   finding the most similar prototypical players. In particular, as shown in Figure 2, given a collection
284   of historical behaviors of a prototype player, we use ResNet-based towers pre-trained by Maia-2 to
285   extract positions before and after actions, and employ stacked Transformer layers to aggregate action
286   embeddings. We use the frozen prototypical player embeddings EI to measure the similarities and
287   use cross-entropy loss for model training. The goal is to recognize the player given the historical
288   actions. In the inference stage, we input the historical moves of unseen players to the meta-network
289   and take the matched prototype embedding as the initialization for eu .
290
291   3.4.4    D ISCUSSION
292   Note that both prototype matching and Maia4All exploit the same set of historical behaviors. However,
293   prototype matching is a much easier task than human move prediction. This is because prototype
294   matching is essentially a discriminative task against a fixed set of classes (prototypes), and human
295   move prediction is a next-move generative task that requires a deeper understanding of the player’s
296   decision-making style. Therefore, we initialize the player embedding with the easier prototype
297   matching task to get a rough understanding of how similar players behave and further calibrate the
298   player embedding with human move prediction loss.

      Datasets. Online chess platforms feature a variety of game types, including blitz, rapid, and classical,
305
      each representing games played at different time controls (amount of time given to each player for the
306   whole game). We use data from Lichess, a well-known large open-source chess platform, and its open
307   database. In Lichess, since each game type is given a separate rating, ratings across different game
308   types are not comparable (e.g. a rating of 1800 in “Rapid” is significantly weaker than a rating of
309   1800 in “Blitz” on Lichess). We focus on Blitz games because it’s data-rich and we do not mix with
310   other game types to ensure the ratings are meaningfully comparable. For individual model training
311   and fine-tuning, we use the full game history in 2023 to compromise between the changing player
312   strengths and styles over time and the availability of rich historical behaviors. We divide players
313   into 11 bins: under 1100, over 2000, and nine 100-point wide strength bins from 1100 to 2000, i.e.,
314
      |EP | = 11. During Maia-2-Prototype training, we use the game history of the N most frequent
      players in each strength level, i.e., |EI | = 11N . We use 10 pre-trained and unseen players in each
315
      strength level for testing. We simulate unseen players with limited game history by limiting the
316
      training positions to the first M positions, and we test them with the last 2048 positions in 2023. This
317   yields the testing datasets for the testing datasets of prototypical and unseen players with 225,280
318   positions each.

351   an extensive human study: we observe what humans would play in natural situations recorded by
352   the Lichess Database, and see if it matches the predicted move of our system. We also measure

355   it corresponds to a higher likelihood (lower log-likelihood) of the correct human move. We report

      Baselines. Maia (McIlroy-Young et al., 2020) is a set of 9 separate models, each trained on a different
360   set of players at different skill levels from 1100 to 1900. Maia-1100 models the weaker players,
361   Maia-1500 the intermediate players, and Maia-1900 the higher-skill players. We choose one of the
362   Maia models for each population such that it is the nearest to their strength level for fair comparison.
363   Since Maia-Individual (McIlroy-Young et al., 2022) is designed for data-rich settings, the published
364   results of Maia-Individual indicate that it requires 5,000 games per player to show improvement over

454   Prototype Matching. The distribution of the prototypes to be matched is a hyperparameter. As
455   shown in Figure 4, if we only include the prototypes from a biased distribution of the population, i.e,
456   only select from low/medium/high-level players, it will result in lowered move prediction accuracy
457   and raised perplexity compared to uniformly select N prototypes from each strength level. Such
458
      results support our design choices of selecting the prototypes uniformly to cover the population space.
459   The number of prototypes N per strength level is also a hyperparameter. Choosing an appropriate
460   N needs to compromise between the representativeness of prototypes for each range, i.e., more
461   prototypes can better cover the player embedding space, and the difficulty in the prototype matching,
462   i.e., more prototypes means more candidates to be classified against. This is evidenced by the results
463   shown in Figure 4. We evaluate the top 1 matching accuracy of prototypical players under low-
464
      resource settings (800 positions). Increasing N from 10 to 150 yields gradually lowered performance
      in prototype matching, while the best-performing Maia4All is achieved with a tradeoff between
465
      prototype matching accuracy and player embedding space coverage.

468   Young et al., 2021), i.e., identifying players given their historical behaviors. Since we freeze the
469   shared parameters θ′ and only finetune player embeddings for unseen players, the player embeddings
470
      are directly comparable. Therefore, our design supports behavioral stylometry off the shelf. With
      only 800 positions (around 20 games), our model can identify the player with 89% accuracy with 1

475
476   We introduce Maia4All, a model designed to learn and adapt to individual decision-making styles
477   efficiently, even with limited data. Maia4All achieves this by leveraging a two-stage fine-tuning
478   paradigm and using a meta-network to initialize and refine these embeddings with minimal data.
479
      Our experimental results show that Maia4All can accurately predict individual moves and profile
      behavioral patterns with high fidelity, establishing a new standard for personalized human-like AI
480
      behavior modeling in chess. Our work provides an example of how population AI systems can
481
      flexibly adapt to individual users using a prototype model as a bridge, which could lead to better
482   and more accessible human-AI collaboration in other fields like education, healthcare, and strategic
483   decision-making.

      A.1   P OSITION R EPRESENTATION AND E NCODING
616
617   We follow the well-established prior works McIlroy-Young et al. (2020); Silver et al. (2017) to
618   represent chess positions as multi-channel 8 × 8 matrices, including:
619
620          • Piece Representation: The first 12 channels categorize the board’s pieces by type and color,
621            with one channel each for white and black Pawns, Knights, Bishops, Rooks, Queens, and
622            Kings. A cell is marked 1 to denote the presence of a piece in the corresponding location,
623
               and 0 otherwise.
624          • Player’s Turn: A single channel (the 13th) indicates the current player’s turn, filled entirely
625            with 1s for white and 0s for black, providing the model with context on whose move is being
626            evaluated.
627          • Castling Rights: Four channels (14th to 17th) encode the castling rights for both players,
628            with the entire channel set to 1 if the right is available or 0 otherwise.
629          • En Passant Target: The final channel (18th) marks the square available for en passant capture,
630            if any, with 1 and 0s elsewhere.
631
632   One important departure from previous work is that we only use the current chess position, and not
633   the last few chess positions that occurred in the game (models have typically incorporated the six
634
      most recent positions in the game). Many games with perfect information, including chess, can
      be modeled as alternating Markov games Littman (1994); Silver et al. (2016), where future states
635
      are independent of past states given the current game state. Therefore, the current chess position
636
      theoretically encapsulates all the information necessary to make future decisions. Although human
637   decision-making in chess may sometimes subtly depend on the historical lead-up to the current
638   position, these effects are anecdotally small.
639
640
      In exchange, we gain two large practical benefits. First, modeling AI-human move matching
      in a Markovian way vastly improves training efficiency by reducing the computational load via
641
      significantly smaller data usage for each decision. Second, it also enhances flexibility, enabling our
642
      resulting model to make predictions even without historical data, which is particularly advantageous
643   in situations where only the current position is available, like chess training puzzles or any position
644   that didn’t necessarily occur in a full game.
