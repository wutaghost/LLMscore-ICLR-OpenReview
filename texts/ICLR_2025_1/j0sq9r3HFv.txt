011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013           In computational neuroscience, extracting parameters for constructing biologi-
014           cally realistic neural models is a resource-intensive task that requires continuous
015           updates as new research emerges. This paper explores utilizing large language
016
              models (LLMs) in automating parameter extraction from scientific literature for
              biologically realistic neural models. We utilized open-source LLMs via Ollama
017
              to construct KGs, capturing parameters such as neuron morphology, synapse dy-
018
              namics, and receptor properties. SNNBuilder Gutierrez et al. (2022), a framework
019           for building spiking neural network (SNN) models, serves as a key validation ex-
020           ample for our framework. However, the methodology we outline here can extend
021           beyond SNNs and could applied to systematic modelling of the brain.By experi-
022           menting with different prompting strategies—general extraction, in-context hints,
023           and masked prompting—we evaluated the ability of LLMs to autonomously ex-
024           tract relevant data and organize it within an expert-base or data-driven ontology, as
025           well as to infer missing information for neural model construction. Additionally,
026           we implemented retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) via LangChain to further
027
              improve the accuracy of parameter extraction through leveraging external knowl-
              edge sources. Analysis of the the generated KGs, demonstrated that LLMs, when
028
              guided by targeted prompts, can enhance the data-to-model process, paving the
029
              way for more efficient parameter extraction and model construction in computa-
030           tional neuroscience.

              1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034           In computational neuroscience, parameterization of brain models is a time-
035           consuming task. As it requires identifying parameters related to the structure and
036           function of the brain region being modeled. Data-to-model frameworks, such as
037           SNNbuilder (Spiking Neural Network builder) Gutierrez et al. (2022), can assist
038           in model building; however, since these parameters primarily come from scien-
039
              tific publications, their extraction requires continuous updates as new research
              emerges, making the process both time and resource intensive.
040
041
              LLMs (Large Language Models) can enhance the data-to-model process in com-
              putational neuroscience and have shown promise in certain generalization tasks
042
              out of their domain Yang et al. (2024). LLMs provide a valuable metholdogy
043
              for exploring vast amounts of information structred and unstructed information
044           through their large paramter counts and pretrained weights.
045
              However, their full potential in research remains largely untapped without agu-
046           menting external data. By augmenting LLMs with external data in a process
047           known as retrieval augmented generation (RAG) Lewis et al. (2020), model perfor-
048           mance in NLP tasks can be greatly improved. In neuroscience researchers could
049           have access to a more intuative approach to identify paramters by utilizing scien-
050           tific publications and interacting via natural language with the model and database.
051           Furthermore, extracted brain data can be structured to mirror the brain’s architec-
052           ture. Experts could develop a brain ontology to organize this information intu-
053           itively and in an easily understandable format. State-of-the-art research for cre-
              ating graph structures based on scientific text, such as Graph RAG Edge et al.

                 (2024), can often generate structures based on their probabilistic reasoning rather
055              than expert-driven design, which results in less interpretable outcomes.

057              tologies remains largely unexplored. In this work, we carried out experiments with
058              different promoting strategies to guide the graph construction process towards a
059              more comprehensive model.

061              2   R ELATED W ORK
062
063              Recent work on ontology-guided knowledge graph (KG) construction Cauter &
064              Yakovets (2024) has demonstrated the effectiveness of LLMs such as Llama-2
065              and Llama-3 in extracting domain-specific facts. Using a few semantically simi-
066
                 lar examples, the researchers could compare their performance to state-of-the-art
                 fine-tuning methods on the Llama-3-70B-Instruct model. This approach aligns
067
                 with this research, with LLMs used to extract SNN parameters from scientific
068
                 literature and relying on similar prompting techniques.
069
                 Our work aligns with the broader trends we have seen in AI in healthcare, where
070
                 LLMs can aid in parsing large amounts of unstructured medical data Kather et al.
071              (2024). Recent work with LLMs in clinical health extraction contexts has shown
072              improvement when using in-context learning approaches and external knowledge
073              bases Li et al. (2024). Such progress highlights the trend in AI in health domains
074              toward identifying valuable data points from large volumes of unstructured texts,
075              thus reducing the human need to adhere to strictly structured formats, which is
076              typical for electronic health records Nashwan & AbuJaber (2023).

                 When provided with this detailed, hierarchical structure, the potential of the Graph

333           Table 2 contains the results for experiments 4.2.1, 4.2.2, and 4.2.3. For these ex-
334           periments, we used Leiden Modularity Traag et al. (2019) to assess the community
335           structure within the KGs. A higher modularity score indicates a more well-defined
336           cluster of entities.
337           Despite producing the fewest nodes, 144, and edges, 141, the base prompt
338           achieved the highest Leiden Modularity score of 0.63. This suggests that while
339           fewer nodes and entities were created with this method, they were clustered into
340           more well-defined communities. It also indicates that there are more intra-cluster
341
              relationships.
342           The in-context prompt extracted a significantly larger number of nodes at 325 and
343
              edges at 422 but saw a slight reduction in Leiden Modularity. While the LLM
              did extract more entries and relationships, the lower score suggests that additional
344
              noise may have been introduced through these new connections. However, we
345
              still see a well-defined community structure via a modality of 0.60, suggesting
346           that the graph’s coherence was not severely compromised when compared to the
347           base graph.
348           The masked prompt did produce the largest graph with 326 nodes but had slightly
349           fewer edges at 360. It also had the lowest modularity score of 0.55. This sug-
350           gests that while it did capture a comparable amount of entities to the in-context
351           approach, they were not as well connected and formed and had more diffuse clus-
352           ters. This could entail that this approach suggests inferred relationships that may
353           not strongly correlate, resulting in a lower modularity score of 0.55.

              7   C ONCLUSION
382
383           As a first step in exploring the use of Graph RAG for modeling brain-related
384           knowledge graphs, we generated graphs based on a limited corpus of neuroscience
385           papers. These initial experiments helped understand how KGs were by using
386           LLMs and the impact of prompting approaches on their structures. However, sev-
387           eral significant challenges remain to ensure its alignment with real-world brain
388
              modeling, and currently, the results of our experiments do not fully capture the
              complexities of modeling the brain; the current graphs may not fully capture the
389
              structure of the brain and various organizations.
390
              Future work can focus on two main areas to address the aforementioned limita-
391
              tions. The first is to use a larger corpus of papers to represent more of the com-
392
              plexity of neuroscience and brain modeling. Second, we can use a node validation
393           step to ensure that only relevant entities based on ontology are present in the graph.
394           Techniques such as prompt tuning and finetuned models can aid in achieving this.
395           Additionally, the creation of a neuroscience-based QA dataset to validate neuronal
396           parameters can help evaluate our generated KGs.
