009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have highlighted the risk of
012            misusing them, raising the need for accurate detection of LLM-generated content.
013            In response, a viable solution is to inject imperceptible identifiers into LLMs,
014            known as watermarks. Previous work demonstrates that unbiased watermarks
015            ensure unforgeability and preserve text quality by maintaining the expectation of
016            the LLM output probability distribution. However, previous unbiased watermarking
017            methods suffer from one or more of the following issues: (1) requiring access to
018
               white-box LLMs during detection, (2) incurring long detection time, (3) being
               not robust against simple watermarking attacks, (4) failing to provide statistical
019
               guarantees for the type II error of watermark detection, and (5) being not statistically
020
               unbiased for low-entropy scenarios, which hinder their deployment in practice. This
021            study proposes the Sampling One Then Accepting (STA-1) method, a watermark
022            that can address all of these issues. Moreover, we discuss the tradeoff between
023            watermark strength and text quality for unbiased watermarks. We show that in
024            low-entropy scenarios, unbiased watermarks face a tradeoff between watermark
025            strength and the risk of unsatisfactory outputs. Experimental results on both low-
026            entropy and high-entropy datasets demonstrate that STA-1 achieves text quality
027            and watermark strength comparable to existing unbiased watermarks, with a low
028            risk of unsatisfactory outputs. Implementation codes for this study are available
029
               online (hidden for peer review).

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Large language models (LLMs) are large-scale deep learning models that can understand and generate
034
      natural languages by learning from a large amount of textual data. Typical generative LLMs, such as
035
      ChatGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022) and LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023), can answer questions, translate
036   languages, and create codes with qualities comparable to humans. As LLMs can generate contents
037   more efficiently at a lower cost compared to humans, the risk of LLMs being employed to generate
038   biased, fake, or malicious contents is also increasing (Mirsky et al., 2023). For example, LLMs may
039   exhibit biased information against underrepresented groups of people (Abid et al., 2021; Fang et al.,
040   2024), create misinformation (Pan et al., 2023), and harm academic integrity (Zhao et al., 2023).
041   To reduce the harm caused by LLMs, identifying LLM-generated content precisely and efficiently
042   becomes a crucial issue (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023a).
043   Watermarks are identifiers imperceptible to humans but detectable by certain models (Liu et al.,
044   2023b). In the era of LLMs, a watermarking method is a strategy to control the randomness of
045   token generation by LLMs (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023a; Fernandez et al., 2023), with the random-
046   ness preserved confidentially by LLM owners. In practice, watermarks should have unforgeability
047   against deciphering watermarking generation attacks (Liu et al., 2023b). A watermarking method
048   demonstrates the ability against forgeries if it can hide the distinguishability between the original
049   unwatermarked text and its watermarked counterpart (Christ et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b). Thus,
050
      it is required that a watermarking method adjusts the probability distribution while maintaining the
      same expectation as the unwatermarked distribution (Hu et al., 2024; Kuditipudi et al., 2023), defined
051
      as unbiased watermarks.
052
053   Existing unbiased watermarks can be categorized according to the stage where watermarks are
      injected: distribution reweighting and controlled sampling (Liu et al., 2023b). For distribution

054
      reweighting, Hu et al. (2024) proposes γ-reweight, which uses the log-likelihood ratio (LLR) test
055   by comparing the likelihood of the text produced by watermarked and unwatermarked white-box
056   LLMs. It requires the prompt as input and a white-box LLM in watermark detection (Fernandez
057   et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024). Also, the watermark is unstable because changing the first token of the
058   generated text can lead to huge deviations from the original likelihood value (Fernandez et al., 2023).
059   In response, Wu et al. (2024) avoid the LLR test and propose Dipmark, an extension of γ-reweight
060   with more general parameter settings. However, although both γ-reweight and Dipmark ensure the
061   type I error of watermark detection, they fail to provide statistical guarantees for the type II error (Hu
062   et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024). For controlled sampling, Christ et al. (2023) introduce a watermarking
063
      method that uses a sequence of random values to guide the token sampling process. However, their
      method is not robust enough against simple removal attacks (Liu et al., 2023b). Kuditipudi et al.
064
      (2023) also use random values to control the sampling and introduce a permutation test on detection
065
      that does not require white-box access to LLMs. However, this permutation test is time-consuming
066   theoretically and empirically. Fairoze et al. (2023) propose to sample the token sequence generation
067   until its hash matches a key value. According to their distortion-free definition, the upper bound
068   of the difference between probabilities before and after watermarking is exp(−a), where a is the
069   minimal entropy. The difference is not negligible in low-entropy scenarios. Note that using random
070   values to control sampling can be treated as a special case of distribution reweighting where only
071   the probability of the sampled token is reweighted to 1 (Kuditipudi et al., 2023). Thus, we build our
072   analysis framework in Section 2 solely based on distribution reweighting.
073   We conclude the research gaps in Table 5 in Appendix A. In response to the challenges, we propose
074   the Sampling One Then Accepting (STA-1) method that can simultaneously overcome the above
075   gaps. STA-1 traces back to the original watermarking method (denoted as KGW) (Kirchenbauer
076   et al., 2023a) where the token set is divided into a green and a red list at each generation step. Instead
077   of raising logits in the green list, STA-1 samples a token from the original probability distribution
078   and accepts it if it is in the green list. If the sampled token is in the red list, it resamples another
079   token and accept it. By counting the number of green list tokens, it employs the z-test for watermark
080
      detection, which naturally addresses the issues encountered with the LLR test and eliminates the need
      for prompts and white-box LLMs in detection. Simultaneously, the detection of STA-1 is efficient
081
      and only requires O(m) time complexity, where m is the number of tokens. The STA-1 method is
082
      also robust against simple insertion and removal attacks because changing tokens can only affect
083   the detection score around these tokens. Meanwhile, we prove that STA-1 is unbiased and provides
084   statistical guarantees for the type II error. More interestingly, the bounds are linked to the Gini
085   index of the probability distribution, which is a common metric in machine learning (Breiman, 2017)
086   compared to the proposed Spike entropy in previous work (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023a).
087
      In this study, we also clarify the watermark strength and text quality tradeoff in unbiased watermarks.
088
      The KGW method faces a tradeoff between watermark strength and text quality, which means a higher
089   detection power results in a lower text quality (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023a). Previous work claims
090   that unbiased watermarks can avoid this tradeoff given the preserved text quality by maintaining the
091   expectation of probability distribution (Hu et al., 2024). We challenge this claim by considering a
092   simple low-entropy scenario, where we show that unbiased watermarks still face a tradeoff between
093   watermark strength and text quality. However, under the same expectation constraint, the text quality
094   is related to the risk of unsatisfactory outputs. Specifically, unsatisfactory outputs in low-entropy
095   scenarios represent that the watermarking method alters probability distribution too much such
096   that high-probability tokens cannot be sampled at risk. We discuss the risk via the variance of the
097
      probability after altering, which is a common practice of risk-return analysis (Sharpe, 1998). We
      prove that STA-1 is less risky than previous unbiased watermarks. Moreover, we propose STA-M,
098
      an extension of STA-1, by setting up a threshold for entropy in generation (Lee et al., 2023; Wang
099
      et al., 2023) and sampling more times for high-entropy steps. Although STA-M is not unbiased
100   theoretically, it allows higher watermark strength with small performance shifts empirically. Also,
101   STA-M is robust against various watermarking attacks. We summarize our contributions as follows:
102
103
      1. We propose STA-1, an unbiased watermarking method that is practical and has statistical guarantees
      on type II error of watermark detection. Moreover, we introduce STA-M, an extension of STA-1 that
104
      enhances watermark strength with low text quality shifts.
105
106   2. We clarify the watermark strength and text quality tradeoff in unbiased watermarks. In low-entropy
107   scenarios, the text quality is related to the risk of unsatisfactory outputs. We show that STA-1 has a
      lower risk theoretically compared to other unbiased watermarks.

111   dataset and robustness against different watermarking attacks.

158   scheme. Given a uniformly distributed key k, RDW first shuffles all probability masses within the
159   interval [0, 1], then it randomly samples a value u ∼ U(0, 1). Here, u is viewed as the cumulative
160   distribution function value of PM (xt |x−Np :(t−1) ) with respect to the permutation; it is subsequently
161   inverse transformed to generate a token. The probability of the sampled token is reweighted to 1,
      while the probabilities of all other tokens are reweighted to 0.

      3     A S IMPLE P ROTOCOL FOR A L OW- ENTROPY S CENARIO
163
164
      The low-entropy text refers to a relatively deterministic sequence in natural language. The entropy
165
      measures the uncertainty of the probability distribution PM (xt |x−Np :(t−1) ) at a single generation
166   step among the token set V, where low entropy means low uncertainty. For example, in code writing,
167   the structure of a code sequence is regularized where few changes can be made (Lee et al., 2023).
168   More explicitly, for a typical English pangram such as ‘The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy
169   dog’ (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023a), both humans and machines should generate similar if not identical
170   output. For example, when provided with the prompt ‘The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy’, the
171   trained LLaMA-2-7B (Touvron et al., 2023) outputs an empirical probability above 0.8 for the next
172   word ‘dog’.
173   Problem modeling. Low-entropy scenarios exist in text generation tasks of LLMs. We aim to model
174   a simple problem protocol for the low-entropy generation scenario. For simplicity, we consider the
175   low-entropy scenario where only one token probability is significantly large. Specifically, denote
176   pmax as the largest probability of a token in the probability distribution PM (·|x−Np :(t−1) ). We make
177   an intuitive assumption that except pmax , other |V| − 1 probabilities are small enough to uniformly
178   fill in the remaining 1 − pmax probability value.

      4.2   S AMPLING M T HEN ACCEPTING
338
      A low-entropy scenario indicates a low Gini index which weakens the watermark strength based
339   on Theorem 2. To enhance the watermark strength, we propose the Sampling M Then Accepting
340   (STA-M) method, an extension of STA-1. STA-M employs a heuristic threshold τ for entropy at each
341   generation step. In detail, at generation step t, we first calculate the entropy τ t of the probability
342   distribution PM (·|x−Np :(t−1) ). If it shows low entropy τ t ≤ τ , we apply STA-1 at this generation
343   step; if it shows high entropy τ t > τ , we repeat sampling if the previously sampled token is in the
344   red list, and the procedure repeats at most M times.
345
      The detailed algorithm and analysis of STA-M can be found in Appendix E. According to Remark 3
346   in Appendix E, STA-M is biased. In low-entropy steps where probabilities are concentrated on a few
347   tokens, actively using STA-M by repeated sampling can skew these probabilities, thereby reducing
348   text quality. On the contrary, in high-entropy steps, since there are more acceptable tokens, the
349   impact of repeated sampling on text quality is weakened. Therefore, STA-M only repeats sampling in
350   high-entropy steps, which could increase watermark strength and largely maintain text quality.

      6    C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
518
519
520   In this work, we propose a new unbiased watermarking method named STA-1. We clarify the text
521   quality (regarding the risk of unsatisfactory outputs under the same expectation) and watermark
522
      strength tradeoff of unbiased watermarks in low-entropy scenarios. We also extend STA-1 to STA-
      M which can enhance watermark strength with small text quality shifts. Experimental results
523
      on low-entropy datasets prove that STA-1 is comparable to other unbiased watermarks and has
524
      a low risk. Moreover, results from the high-entropy dataset demonstrate the efficiency of STA-1
525   and the robustness of STA-M. Future work of our study can be conducted in several ways. First,
526   watermarking low-entropy tasks is still challenging and future work can devise better watermarking
527   methods. Second, future work could incorporate more datasets and generative LLMs for evaluation of
528   our method. Third, it is also possible to consider context code history to extend the unbiased results
529   from the token level to the sequence level.

722   Distribution reweighting refers to methods that adjust the output distribution PM (xt |x−Np :(t−1) ) at
723   each step t by artificially increasing probabilities for certain tokens while reducing those for others.
724   The direction and magnitude (increasing or decreasing) of change in probability mass for a token are
725   determined by the private key k.
726   KGW (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023a) first randomly splits the vocabulary set V into two non-overlapping
727   lists based on a uniformly distributed key k: a ‘green’ list and a ‘red’ list. This method has two
728   versions: the ‘hard’ version completely ignores the red list tokens and only samples tokens from the
729   green list; The ‘soft’ version adds a predefined constant δ to logits of green list tokens while keeping
730   logits of red list tokens fixed. The soft KGW reweights distribution as

1025   St. Petersburg paradox (Wikipedia, 2024). Assume that one must choose either one lottery from
       the following two lotteries. (1) Lottery 1 (L1) has a 0.8 probability of earning nothing and the other


                                                        19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       0.2 probability of losing 1,000 dollars. (2) Lottery 2 (L2) has a 0.5 probability of losing 100 dollars
1027   and the other 0.5 probability of losing 300 dollars.
1028
1029   It is easy to show that L1 and L2 have the same expected outcome that 0.8 × 0 − 0.2 × 1000 =
1030
       −0.5 × 100 − 0.5 × 300 = −200. However, risk-averse people will choose L2 as they do not want
       to take the risk of losing 1,000 dollars.
1031
1032   Computationally, assume the person has 1,001 dollars in total and the utility function is ln(Y ) (Debreu
1033   et al., 1954), where Y is the wealth. The utility function measures happiness. It is a concave function
1034   (such as ln(Y )) because people are happier if they are wealthier (ln′ (Y ) > 0) but the increment of
1035   happiness decreases as the wealth increases (ln′′ (Y ) < 0).
1036   The weighted utility of L1 and L2 are as follows
1037
1038

1039                             U (L2) = 0.5 × ln(901) + 0.5 × ln(701) ≈ 6.68.
1040
       Based on the weighted utility, risk-averse people will choose L2.

1045
       case, the two unbiased watermarks in Example 2 can be viewed as L1 and L2 in the lottery example.
       Sampling B may not be a big issue in high-entropy scenarios because it should not significantly harm

       text quality as much as 1,000.

       Existing white-box watermarking techniques fall into two categories: watermarking during logits and
1277
       probabilities generation, and watermarking by controlling sampling strategies.
1278
1279   Watermarking during logits and probabilities generation. This category of watermarking methods
1280   inserts watermarks into LLMs by artificially adjusting the raw logits or probabilities generated by the
1281
       LLM. Among this category, Kirchenbauer et al. (2023a) propose the first watermarking method based
       on logits adjustment. Their approach randomly partitions the vocabulary set into a green and a red list
1282
       at each generation step, increasing the logits of green list tokens while keeping red list tokens’ logits
1283
       fixed. Lee et al. (2023) extend the green and red list-based watermarking method to low-entropy
1284   scenarios. They adjust the logits only during high-entropy generation steps, leaving the raw logits
1285   unchanged for low-entropy steps. Ren et al. (2023) improve the vocabulary set partition process by
1286   determining the green and red lists based on semantic embeddings of preceding tokens rather than
1287   their hash values. Fernandez et al. (2023) propose a multi-bit watermarking method that generates
1288   a multi-dimensional vector at each generation step, which is utilized to modify logits produced by
1289   the original LLM. Their approach allows embedding any bit of watermarking information, up to the
1290   dimension of the vector used in the logits adjustment. Yoo et al. (2023) develop a multi-bit method by
1291   extending the two-list partition idea to multi-list partitions. At each generation step, the vocabulary
1292
       set is divided into multiple lists. Based on the message to be inserted, the logits for tokens in a
       selected list are increased, while the token logits in all other lists remain unchanged.
1293
1294   Instead of splitting the vocabulary set into different lists, Hu et al. (2024) introduce a method that
1295   randomly shuffles the order of all token probabilities within the interval [0, 1], setting the probabilities
       in the first half of the interval to 0 and doubling those in the second half. During the detection phase,


                                                                                 24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       a likelihood ratio test examines the significance of the likelihood that the given text is generated with
1297   the adjusted probability distribution. Wu et al. (2024) further generalizes this method by introducing
1298   a hyperparameter α ∈ [0, 0.5], which controls the two cutoff points α and 1 − α within the interval
1299   [0, 1]. The probability masses for the three resulting sub-intervals are adjusted accordingly.
1300
       Watermarking by controlling sampling strategies. This category of watermarking methods inserts
1301
       watermarks into the token sampling process by using watermark information to control the sampling
1302
       of candidate tokens. For example, Christ et al. (2023) introduce a watermarking method that represents
1303   each token in the vocabulary set as a binary string of 0s and 1s. Next, a sequence of values from 0 to 1
1304   is sampled uniformly. These values guide the token sampling process: if the predicted probability for
1305   a position in the binary string is larger than the corresponding pseudo-random value, that position is
1306   assigned a 1; otherwise, it is assigned a 0. Once all positions are determined, the token corresponding
1307   to the resulting binary string is sampled. Additionally, previous work (Kuditipudi et al., 2023) use
1308   a sequence of values randomly sampled from a uniform distribution between 0 and 1. The value
1309   controls the token sampling process through a decoder function, where the decoder function varies
1310   based on the sampling strategy. Hou et al. (2023) sample new sentences according to the original
1311
       LLM until a sentence’s semantic value falls into the acceptance region. The acceptance region is
       predefined by randomly splitting the space of semantic embedding according to the context and the
1312
       key.
