011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013           This study seeks to understand and address a phenomenon observed in Large Vision
014           Language Models (LVLMs) related to their attention mechanism. Interestingly,
015           LVLMs tend to disproportionately focus on a few image tokens that lack meaning-
016
              ful, query-related semantics, leading to sharp outlier values in the attention maps
              — tokens we refer to as blind tokens. In well-designed attention mechanisms, the
017
              principle is to assign higher weights to the most relevant tokens. However, in this
018
              case, the attention imbalance leads to overemphasis on uninformative tokens, which
019           is far from ideal. Our analysis shows that tokens receiving lower attention weights
020           often hold critical information necessary for capturing subtle visual details. We hy-
021           pothesize that over-reliance on blind tokens contributes to hallucinations in LVLMs.
022           To address this, we introduce a novel decoding technique called Attentional Vision
023           Calibration (AVIS C). During the decoding phase, AVIS C identifies blind tokens by
024           examining the image-wise attention distribution and dynamically adjusts the logits
025           for the prediction. Specifically, it contrasts the logits conditioned on the original
026           visual tokens with those conditioned on the blind tokens, thereby reducing the
027
              model’s dependency on blind tokens and encouraging a more balanced considera-
              tion of all visual tokens. We validate AVIS C on standard hallucination benchmarks,
028
              including POPE, MME, and AMBER, where it consistently outperforms existing
029
              decoding techniques.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
      Large Vision Language Models (LVLMs) (Dai et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c;b; Bai
035
      et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024a) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in generating coherent
036   and contextually relevant descriptions from visual inputs. This success largely hinges on the models’
037   ability to interpret and integrate complex visual information with textual data. However, a persistent
038   challenge with these models is their tendency towards "hallucinations" — producing inaccurate or
039   fabricated descriptions that do not accurately reflect the visual data. The phenomenon of hallucination
040   in LVLMs can significantly impede their reliability, especially in applications requiring precise and
041   trustworthy visual descriptions.
042   Modern LVLMs (Dai et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023c) are predominantly based on transformer ar-
043   chitecture (Vaswani et al., 2017), where the most critical component is the attention mechanism.
044   In this framework, the highest attention weights are assigned to tokens that the model considers
045   most important for generating the output. This concept implies that tokens with higher attention are
046   essential ingredients in the generation process, directly guiding the model’s output choices.
047
      The intuitive alignment between attention weights and key tokens is a well-established principle.
048   For instance, the DINO (Caron et al., 2021) or OpenCLIP Ilharco et al. (2021) produce attention
049   maps that naturally concentrate on semantically meaningful regions of an image. Textual attention
050   mechanisms (Vaswani et al., 2017) were initially developed with a similar intuitive basis. It is worth
051   to question whether these attention mechanisms in LVLMs truly align with their intended purpose.
052   Our investigation reveals that LVLMs (Liu et al., 2023c; Dai et al., 2024) exhibit biased attention
053   toward specific image tokens, which we refer to as "blind tokens”. These tokens, despite receiving
      high attention, are not crucial for prediction or semantic understanding.

079   This phenomenon aligns with the findings in (Darcet et al., 2023). They identified artifacts in
080   the feature maps of vision transformers (Touvron et al., 2022; Caron et al., 2021; Oquab et al.,
081   2023). These artifacts primarily appear in low-informative background areas of images during
082   inference, where such regions receive disproportionately high attention despite containing minimal
083   local information. (Darcet et al., 2023) suggest that, during attention operations, these tokens are
084   repurposed to aggregate global image information while discarding spatial details, likely due to their
085   association with repetitive or less informative image patches.
086   We are curious whether these counterintuitive attentional biases may be inherent to attention mecha-
087   nisms themselves. Our work seeks to investigate whether this phenomenon extends beyond Vision
088   Transformers to LVLMs and how differences in architecture, task, and domain contribute to atten-
089   tional behavior. Building on (Darcet et al., 2023), we aim to explore how these attentional patterns
090   manifest in LVLMs.
091   This issue becomes particularly apparent in image with uniform pixel values, where no specific region
092   of the image warrants special attention. As illustrated in Fig. 1 (Top), even when an image contains no
093   objects and consists only of a uniform yellow background, some regions still receive disproportionate
094   focus. These blind tokens highlight potential flaws in how the model interprets visual data during the
095   decoding process, as LVLMs (Dai et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023c) often focus on irrelevant tokens.
096   We analyze the correlation between actual object regions and attention weights in LVLMs using the
097   COCO2014 dataset Lin et al. (2014). We ask LVLMs to describe the images and analyzed attention
098   distribution across 24 × 24 patches, comparing bounding boxes to attention weights on these patches.
099   An example is shown in Fig. 1 (Bottom). Specifically, we assess the proportion of blind tokens within
100   the bounding boxes. The results show that, on average, only 3.7% of actual object regions overlap
101   with blind tokens, and only 23.3% of attention weights are assigned to the object regions, while the
102   rest focused on other areas. This suggests that, the model’s actual focus does not well align with
103   object regions, which are crucial for accurate image descriptions.

      Such skewed attention, which disproportionately favors blind tokens while overlooking non-blind
140
      tokens containing crucial visual details, can lead to misclassifications or entirely incorrect predictions.
141
      We hypothesize that this over-reliance on blind tokens contributes to hallucinations in LVLMs.
142
143   In response to this challenge, we propose a novel method termed Attentional Vision Calibration
144   (AVIS C), which recalibrates the model’s attention during the decoding phase. Unlike existing ap-
145
      proaches that typically require extensive training (Jiang et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Zhou et al.,
      2023; Yu et al., 2023b) or auxiliary models (Zhao et al., 2024; Wan et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024;
146
      Yang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b), AVIS C operates without these prerequisites.
147
148   AVIS C dynamically modifies the decoding process in three steps: (i) Based on our findings that
149   different LVLMs and across layers exhibit different attentional patterns (see Fig. 4), we first select
150   relevant layers that allocate a higher proportion of attention to image tokens. (ii) Next, we identify
151
      blind tokens, which disproportionately dominate attention. These tokens are isolated, while all other
      image tokens are zeroed out, creating a biased visual input. (iii) Finally, we employ a contrastive
152
      decoding (Leng et al., 2023; Favero et al., 2024). This technique contrasts the logits derived from the
153
      original visual input with those derived solely from the blind tokens. By doing so, it amplifies the
154   influence of tokens that exhibit significant differences between the two distributions.
155
156   Notably, AVIS C does not directly manipulate attention weights. Instead, it adjusts the influence of
157
      blind and non-blind tokens at the prediction level using contrastive decoding, reducing the impact of
      blind tokens while enhancing the influence of non-blind ones.
158
159   Through a series of experiments on hallucination benchmarks like POPE (Rohrbach et al., 2018),
160   MME (Fu et al., 2024), and AMBER (Wang et al., 2023b), we demonstrate that AVIS C significantly
161   mitigates hallucination while simultaneously improving the models’ ability to capture and describe
      detailed image attributes more accurately.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
163
164   LVLMs (Li et al., 2023a; Zhu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a) are prone to generating hallucinations,
165   i.e., misalignment between visual inputs and textual outputs. These hallucinations manifest across
166   various semantic dimensions such as incorrect object presence, attributes, or relations.
167
      To mitigate these, researchers have developed strategies across three levels:
168
      Input-level. Efforts here focus on data quality improvement to reduce hallucinations (Gunjal et al.,
169
      2023; Liu et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023a; Lu et al., 2024), including the introduction of negative
170
      data (Liu et al., 2023a), counterfactual data (Yu et al., 2023a) to challenge the model’s assumptions,
171   dataset cleansing to minimize noise and errors (Wang et al., 2024; Yue et al., 2024).
172
      Model-level. This includes increasing the resolution at which models process visual data (Chen et al.,
173
      2023b; Liu et al., 2023b; Zhai et al., 2023), or enhancing perception abilities through advanced vision
174   encoders (He et al., 2024; Jain et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024b). These are usually training-based (Jiang
175   et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2024), and often involve auxiliary supervision from external datasets (Chen
176   et al., 2023c) and reinforcement learning techniques (Zhao et al., 2023; Gunjal et al., 2024; Sun et al.,
177   2023; Yu et al., 2023b) to better align model outputs with accurate visual representations.
178   Output-level. Techniques like contrastive decoding (Leng et al., 2023; Favero et al., 2024) directly
179   contrast incorrect predictions during decoding, helping models distinguish between accurate and
180   inaccurate descriptions. Guided decoding (Zhao et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024)
181   leverages external models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) or DETR (Carion et al., 2020) to enhance
182   accuracy. Other approaches include training-free methods (Wan et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024;
183   Huang et al., 2023) and post-hoc corrections via self-feedback (Lee et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024).
184   Among these, we focus on contrastive decoding methods: (1) VCD (Leng et al., 2023) mitigates
185   statistical biases and language priors by contrasting output distributions from original and distorted
186   visual inputs, moderating decoding probabilities. (2) M3ID (Favero et al., 2024) uses a similar
187   approach where the reference image amplifies its influence over the language prior, thereby enhancing
188   the generation of tokens with higher mutual information with the visual prompt.
189
      Our approach belongs to the output-level category. AVIS C analyzes attention patterns to identify
190   blind tokens during decoding steps. It then utilizes a contrastive decoding technique to enhance token
191   prediction. Our method does not require additional training, external data or models, and costly
192   self-feedback mechanisms.

521   5      C ONCLUSION
522
523   This study highlights the phenomenon of blind tokens in LVLMs, where the attention mechanism
524   disproportionately focuses on uninformative image tokens, leading to skewed attention distributions
525   and contributing to hallucinatory outputs. To mitigate this issue, we introduced a novel decoding
526   technique, termed Attentional Vision Calibration (AVIS C), which dynamically adjusts the logits by
527   identifying blind tokens through an analysis of image-wise attention distribution. AVIS C recalibrates
528   the model’s attention, without requiring additional retraining, external datasets, or self-feedback
529   mechanisms, thereby significantly reduceing the model’s reliance on these blind tokens. Through
530
      extensive experiments on hallucination benchmarks such as POPE, MME, and AMBER, AVIS C
      consistently outperforms existing decoding techniques, improving the model’s ability to capture
531
      subtle visual details by redirecting attention toward more informative tokens.
532
533   Limitation. The discriminative capabilities of LVLMs using AVIS C diminish in tasks that involve
534   counting objects within an image. This suggests that blind tokens may sometimes contain essential
535   information, particularly in object-counting scenarios, leading to reduced performance in the “Count”
536
      category of MME and the “Numbers” category of AMBER.
537   Future Work. Building upon insights from (Darcet et al., 2023), the blind token phenomenon may
538   extend beyond LVLMs and could be a general characteristic of transformer-based architectures. This
539   motivates us to reasonably hypothesize that such counterintuitive attentional biases might be inherent
      to attention mechanisms themselves. We aim to further explore this in future research.

      A    C OMPARISON OF O UR W ORK WITH (DARCET ET AL ., 2023)
811
812
      Short summary of (Darcet et al., 2023).
813
814   Darcet et al. (2023) identify artifacts in feature maps of various vision transformers, particularly when
815   comparing DINOv1 (Caron et al., 2021) and DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023). Darcet et al. observe that
816
      the image token attention weights are not evenly distributed across informative regions of the image;
      instead, they are concentrated in seemingly unnecessary regions, such as redundant background areas.
817
      These regions correspond to artifact patches, referred to as "high-norm outlier tokens", which receive
818
      high attention.
819
820   Darcet et al. demonstrate that high-norm outlier tokens contain minimal local information while
821   retaining significant global information. Specifically, in position prediction and pixel reconstruction
822
      tasks – where locality is crucial – these outlier tokens perform significantly worse than normal image
      tokens. However, when Darcet et al. conduct linear probing classification experiments using the class
823
      token, high-norm outlier tokens, and normal image tokens, the high-norm outlier tokens outperform
824
      the normal image tokens. This indicates that while these outlier tokens lose local information, they
825   effectively encode global information about the image. Darcet et al. suggests that during the attention
826   mechanism’s internal operations, these tokens are repurposed to capture global information, likely due
827   to their association with repetitive or less informative image patches. Moreover, the paper presents
828   experimental results demonstrating that when additional memory space (i.e., register tokens) is added
829   to store this information, the artifacts disappear.
830
      How different it is from Blind tokens?
831
832   "Blind tokens" in our work and the "high-norm outlier tokens" described in (Darcet et al., 2023)
833   show similar findings. Both tokens are identified by their significantly high attention weights and are
834
      associated with regions in the image that appear irrelevant to the target task. However, despite these
      conceptual similarities, there are several key differences between the two:
835
836
             • Source of Attention Weights: In (Darcet et al., 2023), the high-norm outlier tokens are
837            derived from the attention weights computed within the vision transformer layers, whereas
838            our blind tokens are based on attention weights calculated by the LLMs within the Large
839            Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) (e.g. Vicuna-7B in LLaVA-v1.5-7B). Furthermore, each
840            transformer-based architecture has different mask designs.
841
             • Task & Architectural Differences: The presence and pattern of high-norm tokens in vision
842            transformers appear to be highly sensitive to the training schemes and specific architectures
843            employed. For example, while DINOv1 (Caron et al., 2021) does not exhibit high-norm
844            tokens, they suddenly emerge in DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023), which has been refined
845            for dense prediction tasks. This suggests that the patterns of high-norm tokens can vary
846            significantly depending on the trained target task and the model architecture. Given that
847            these models are all confined to visual tasks, this variation is likely influenced by the specific
848            task the model is trained on. LVLMs are essentially a combination of LLM, a visual encoder,
849            and a vision-to-text projector, primarily designed for image-related question-answering
850
               tasks. These models are trained using an auto-regressive token prediction scheme, which
               significantly differs from the architecture and training methods of vision transformers.
851
               Additionally, Darcet et al. shows that high-norm tokens in vision transformers encapsulate
852
               global image information, as evidenced by their strong performance in linear probing
853            classification tasks. However, it remains unclear whether these tokens play a similarly
854            critical role in the language token prediction tasks of LVLMs.
855
             • Domain Differences: While both our blind tokens and the high-norm tokens (Darcet et al.,
856
               2023) originate from image patch tokens, LVLMs project image tokens into a LLM space
857            for further attention computations. This contrasts with vision transformers, where all tokens
858            remain within the image patch domain during attention operations. It is uncertain whether
859            the high-norm tokens defined in (Darcet et al., 2023) can be similarly defined in LVLMs. To
860            our knowledge, we first observe and define a phenomenon akin to this in LVLMs.
861
862   To explore the correlation between these concepts further, we conducted additional experiments.
863   Specifically, when examining the vision transformer used as the visual encoder in LLaVA-1.5-7B
      (e.g., CLIP-L-336px), we observed the emergence of high attention weight regions, corresponding


                                                         16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      to the high-norm tokens described in (Darcet et al., 2023). Furthermore, we discovered a notable
865   correlation between these high-norm tokens and the blind tokens selected based on LLM attention
866   weights. According to our analysis on the POPE-COCO-Random benchmark, we found the following
867   statistics (The criteria for determining high-norm tokens were the same as for blind tokens):
868
869            • 𝑃(blind token|high-norm token) = 40.38%
870            • 𝑃(high-norm token|blind token) = 31.27%
871
872   While high-norm tokens do not completely overlap with blind tokens, the relatively small number of
873   blind and high-norm tokens (on average, 12.95 out of 576 total tokens) suggests a strong correlation.
874   Therefore, these tokens may share certain underlying properties. However, many blind tokens are
875   distributed at the beginning and end of the image token sequence. Whether high-norm tokens share
876   this characteristic is unclear, but this feature appears to be unique to blind tokens in LVLMs, rather
877
      than high-norm tokens in the ViT-based architecture discussed in (Darcet et al., 2023). Based on this
      evidence, we conclude that while blind tokens and the artifacts described in (Darcet et al., 2023) are
878
      not identical, they may share certain properties. Despite these differences, we believe that (Darcet
879
      et al., 2023) complements our findings and could further stimulate exploration of erroneous focus
880   within attention mechanisms across modern architectures.
881
882   Is it really a good idea to reduce the dependency on blind tokens?
883   In our work, we observed that, similar to (Darcet et al., 2023), blind tokens tend to emerge in
884   non-important patches, such as backgrounds. However, a key difference is that while they describe
885   high-norm tokens as containing global information at the expense of local information, we found that
886   blind tokens contain information irrelevant to generating a response to the textual query. While Darcet
887   et al. found that high-norm tokens carry global information, they try to mitigate their influence, as
888   their presence negatively impacts dense prediction tasks that require spatial locality. For instance, in
889
      attention-based object discovery tasks like LOST (Siméoni et al., 2021), high-norm tokens can directly
      cause errors. Similarly, we argue that the presence of blind tokens is not a desirable phenomenon,
890
      especially for image-related response prediction tasks. Our approach stems from the observation that
891
      blind tokens do not contain question-relevant information. If a small number of blind tokens carry
892   global information and are key sources for token prediction (with these tokens, as expected, having
893   high attention weights), their performance should be reasonably strong. However, the results shown
894   in Figs. 1 and 2 challenge this assumption. As seen in Fig. 2, the truly important information is often
895   found in non-blind tokens. Therefore, our contrastive decoding scheme, which reduces the influence
896   of blind tokens and strengthens the influence of non-blind tokens, can be understood as a method to
897   mitigate hallucination.
