011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Action Models (LAMs) for AI agents have significant potential, but their
014            development is often constrained by the reliance on supervised learning and man-
015            ual data curation, which are both time-consuming and costly. To address these
016
               limitations, we present the LAM Simulator, a comprehensive framework designed
               for online exploration of agentic tasks with high-quality feedback. This frame-
017
               work includes a curated set of high-quality agentic tasks, a diverse collection of
018
               tools, and an interactive environment where agent models can call tools, receive
019            execution responses, and obtain action feedback. Our findings indicate that the
020            LAM Simulator significantly enhances model performance and effectively iden-
021            tifies and addresses potential issues. Specifically, our model, LAM-Sim-8x7B,
022            demonstrates an 18.54% improvement over its base LAM and significantly out-
023            performs other state-of-the-art alternatives on ToolEval benchmark. Furthermore,
024            we have demonstrated that LLMs lacking in agentic capability can greatly ben-
025            efit from the implementation of LAM Simulator. Our experiments with a model
026            trained on Mixtral-8x7B-Instruct-v0.1 have yielded a doubling to tripling of per-
027
               formance. Remarkably, the data construction process for training these models
               requires minimal human intervention, making the LAM Simulator a robust frame-
028
               work for accelerating the development of AI agents.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large Action Models (LAMs) (Zeng et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Zhang et al.,
034
      2024b) are an advanced type of Large Language Model, specifically optimized for tool usage, rea-
035
      soning, and function calling. Recent advancements have propelled their capabilities, making them
036   integral to applications such as smart assistants, AI agents, and task automation. While strong
037   closed-world commercial models like Claude-3 (Anthropic, 2024) and GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023)
038   can also perform complex agent tasks, LAMs benefit from specialized training for enhanced perfor-
039   mance in agent applications and offer more open-source options and developments for the commu-
040   nity. As use cases grow, the demand for more accurate models will continue to increase.
041
      Current approaches for creating open-sourced LAMs include prompt engineering, incorporating ad-
042   ditional contextual information into agent prompts, Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT), Reinforcement
043   Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al. (2022)), among others. However, most of
044   these methods rely heavily on supervised learning and manual data curation, a process that is both
045   time-consuming and expensive.
046
      The concept of agent self-exploration has emerged as a promising avenue for reducing human la-
047
      beling and annotation effort in the development of agent models. Recent studies, including those
048   by ToolTalk (Farn & Shin, 2023), WebArena (Zhou et al., 2023), and APIGen (Liu et al., 2024b),
049   have demonstrated the ability to generate high-quality data for agent learning and evaluation through
050   automated means. However, these still have some limitations. ToolTalk is limited to tasks that are
051   curated or filtered by humans. WebArena is constrained by a specific set of tasks and very limited
052   action spaces within the web environment domain. APIGen, while showing considerable potential
053   in generating function-calling data, is limited to single-turn function calling and primarily focused
      on ensuring the correctness of function names and corresponding parameters.

054
      On the other hand, existing open-source agent models such as Lemur (Xu et al., 2024),
055   AgentLM (Zeng et al., 2023), and the latest xLAM (Zhang et al., 2024b) still rely on rule-based
056   methods and strong LLMs for collecting and filtering agent trajectories. Moreover, without a care-
057   ful process of providing feedback or crafting the dataset, it is difficult to resolve the agent’s issue
058   of handling specific errors such as JSON parsing errors, tool hallucinations, and argument inaccura-
059   cies. This approach also limits the agent model’s ability to explore a broader range of states based
060   on past agent trajectories and to identify potential improvements.
061   In light of these limitations, we present the LAM Simulator, an all-encompassing framework for AI
062   agent learning. Our framework enables AI agent models to interact with environments and tools
063   in real-time, facilitating problem-solving and providing feedback useful for model learning at any
064   point in the process, including both action-wise feedback and task-wise feedback. This enables
065   a wide ranges of applications, from generating pairwise data for Direct Preference Optimization
066   (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) training, to generating high-quality data for supervised training. Figure
067   1 illustrates our framework.

134   3     LAM S IMULATOR F RAMEWORK
135
136   This section outlines the technical details and design principles of the LAM Simulator. The frame-
137   work is crafted to enable agent models to autonomously explore and enhance their problem-solving
138   abilities. It offers a variety of tasks, access to an extensive set of tools, interactive elements, and
139
      automated, high-quality feedback throughout the exploration process. The detail examples of core
      components are included in Appendix A.2.
140
141
      3.1   F RAMEWORK OVERVIEW FOR DATA G ENERATION THROUGH ONLINE EXPLORATION
142
            AND HIGH - QUALITY FEEDBACK
143
144   In the LAM Simulator framework, an User Command is a natural language query given by a user
145   to instruct the Agent. The Agent can use multiple tools in sequence to solve tasks. During this
146   process, the Agent assesses the current state to make the decision for next action, makes tool calls,
147   and observes the environment’s feedback. This cycle continues until the Agent solves the task or
148   reaches the step limit.
149   Generating User Command: We begin the data generation process by creating User Command
150   data using a Template Matching technique, allowing us to extract key components from the user’s
151   command for task evaluation.
152
      First, the Task Manager receives “Content Dataset” that contains data entries as shown at the leftmost
153
      block in Figure 1. Each data entry, referred to as “Content data”, includes three critical components:
154
155    • task: The name of the Abstract Task for template matching.
156    • user command parameters: Parameters used to generate the User Command with avail-
157      able templates inside Abstract Task.
158    • task available tools: Optional tools available for the task. If this component is left
159      blank, the default tools created inside the Abstract Task will be used.
160
161   Subsequently, the Task Manager extracts the task from the “Content data” to identify the perti-
      nent Abstract Task. Within the designated Abstract Task, the Task Manager searches for the “User


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Command Template” that precisely matches the given user command parameters. For in-
163   stance, if the “Content data” includes {"num apps":5} and {"domain":"gaming"} as its
164   user command parameters, the Task Manager will locate an “User Command Template”
165   within the Abstract Task that includes these parameters and no others. Assuming the template found
166   is “Show me num apps apps from the app store in domain,” the Task Manager will generate the
167   User Command “Show me 5 apps from the app store in gaming” to send to the Agent. An error is
168   triggered if no matching User Command Template is found, and consequently, no User Command
169   is created.
170   If task available tools are provided, the Task Manager uses this specific set; otherwise, it
171   defaults to the tools in the Abstract Task. The Task Manager queries the Tools Database to extract
172   metadata such as descriptions and parameters. With this information, it generates a User Command
173   that includes the specified tools and their metadata.
174
      Converting Data and Prepare for generation: The Task Manager sends the User Command, Task
175   Available Tools, and Final Task Evaluators to create a “Conversation Data” object, which includes
176   conversation history, available tools, and evaluators. The “Conversation Data” is then being sent to
177   the Agent LLM for generating actions.
178
      Generating data with only interaction and feedback: Once the “Conversation Data” reaches the
179
      Agent LLM, the interaction process begins. The Agent LLM utilizes this data to generate actions
180
      and interact with the environment by repeating the following steps until the task is completed or the
181   maximum number of steps is reached:
182
183    • Action Generation and Interaction: The Agent LLM produces actions based on its understanding
184      of the task, the available tools, and the current context from the conversation history. It formulates
185      a plan for the current step, specifying tool calls and the corresponding parameters. If the Agent
186      determines it has enough information to provide the final answer to the User Command, it is
187      expected to use a special tool, Finish, to wrap up the answer.
188    • Agent’s Action Syntax verification: The Syntax Verification Engine checks that the action com-
189      mands generated by the Agent are syntactically correct. If they pass the syntax check, the actions
190      are forwarded to the Request Execution Engine. If they fail, feedback is sent back to the Agent
191      for correction. Feedback mechanisms will be discussed in the Evaluators component under 3.2.1.
192    • Request Execution: The Request Execution Engine, consisting of multiple environments (Env 1,
193      Env 2, etc.), executes the actions given by the Agent LLM.
194    • Evaluation: The Evaluation Engine evaluates the Agent’s responses. It assesses the Agent’s
195      action and, if the Agent is at the final step, it evaluates the overall success of the task. Section
196      3.2.1 will give futher explanation.
197
       • Feedback loop: The feedback from the Evaluation Engine is integrated into the Conversation
198      Data. Additionally, the newly generated actions from the Agent are appended to the conversation
199      history for the next steps in the interaction process.
200
201   In summary, the LAM Simulator framework is designed to generate data with high-quality feedback
202   for training agents. It structures interactions using template-based user commands, employs a com-
203   prehensive task manager, and offers detailed automated feedback mechanisms. This ensures that
204   the Agent receives continuous and constructive feedback and is thus able to perform its exploration
205   progress to improve agent-task-solving capability without any human intervention. This makes the
206
      LAM Simulator a powerful and efficient framework for developing and refining Agentic LLMs.

208   3.2     E VALUATORS
209
      The core functionalities of the LAM Simulator focus on generating timely feedback for agent ac-
210   tions. This subsection discusses the evaluators currently supported by the LAM Simulator.
211
212   3.2.1    I NTERMEDIATE ACTION E VALUATOR
213
214   The Intermediate Action Evaluator assesses each action generated by the Agent LLM during in-
215   termediate steps of resolving the user request. It takes the Agent’s generated text (as a string) and
      evaluates it through the following steps:


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      1 Verify that the string is correctly parsed and contains the required components: (1) thought, (2)
217     tool calls, and (3) tool arguments. If this passes, proceed to the next step. Otherwise, a Structure
218     Error is raised.
219
220
      2 Ensure that the tool call is valid by checking if the tool name is included in the provided list of
        tools. If this passes, proceed to the next step. Otherwise, a Tool Name error is raised.
221
222   3 Validate that the tool arguments are correct for the selected tool calls. This includes ensuring all
223     required arguments are present, no unknown arguments are included, and all arguments are of
224     the correct type. If this passes, the action is marked as successful. Otherwise, a Tool Arguments
225     Error is raised.
226
      The Intermediate Action Evaluator is instrumental in ensuring the Agent operates within the cor-
227
      rect parameters throughout its task-solving process. By breaking down the evaluation into distinct
228
      checks, LAM Simulator is able to provide precise and actionable feedback at each step.
229
230
      3.2.2    F INAL TASK E VALUATOR
231
232   Another key component of the LAM Simulator is the Final Task Evaluator. Our framework auto-
233   mates the evaluation of the Agent’s final response to the User Command, eliminating the need for
234   human intervention or powerful Large Language Models. This ensures consistency, efficiency, and
235   scalability.
236
      Each of our Final Task Evaluators takes the user command parameters and an Agent final
237   response in string format. From here, the Final Task Evaluation is performed as follows:
238
239   1 Retrieve Gold Label: The Final Task Evaluator has an internal solution trajectory for each Ab-
240     stract Task that is hidden from the Agent. Using the user command parameters as the
241     initial input, the evaluator executes each tool in this solution trajectory sequentially to gather the
242     necessary information to solve the task, known as the gold label. In the rare event that any
243     execution fails on the Final Task Evaluator’s side, a special message is returned indicating that
244     this evaluation run is invalid.
245   2 Comparison with Gold Label: The evaluator compares the gold label with the Agent’s fi-
246     nal response. Each task has a unique method for this comparison, which can include exact
247     match, structural match, or key information inclusion. If the Agent’s response matches the gold
248     label using the defined method, the Task response is marked as “Passed”. Otherwise, it is
249     marked as “Failed”.
250
      The Final Task Evaluator provides objective and systematic evaluation of the Agent’s performance.
251
      Utilizing an internal solution trajectory that leverages user command parameters to generate
252
      gold labels and predefined comparison methods, the LAM Simulator delivers consistent and
253   fair assessments of the Agent’s final responses. This automation reduces dependency on manual
254   evaluations, thereby streamlining the training process.

338   4.2     G ENERATING P REFERENCE DATA PAIRS FOR DPO T RAINING
339
340   One notable application is utilizing the action rewards to generate preference data for DPO training.
341
342
      4.2.1    E XPERIMENT S ETUP
343   Base models We chose two models xLAM-7B-r and xLAM-8x7B-r from the xLAM series (Zhang
344   et al. (2024b)) as our baselines. Despite their strengths in tool usage and function calls, these models
345   sometimes fail. We aimed to use the LAM Simulator to help these models identify and rectify their
346   weaknesses through DPO.
347
      Generating preference data for DPO For single-turn use cases, it is straightforward that we can
348   use the same context to generate several different variances of responses and use those variances
349   to create pair-wise data. However, multi-turn conversations present a significant challenge. At an
350   intermediate step Si , an agent may generate several action variations Ai1 , Ai2 , . . . , Ain , making a
351   complete traversal of all paths computationally infeasible due to exponential growth.
352
      To address this, we developed a Multi-Turn preference data generation method. At each conversa-
353
      tion step, instead of always choosing the highest-reward response, we randomly select one response
354   to add to the conversation history. This prevents the agent from encountering only high-reward sce-
355   narios, exposing it to a mix of easy and difficult situations. Consequently, the agent learns to manage
356   diverse challenges more effectively. This chosen action then guides the next steps, simplifying the
357   process and reducing computational costs. This method helps agents improve their multi-turn inter-
358   action performance by learning from diverse feedback.
359
      Training data construction Using 4 H100 GPUs for 8 hours and following our methodologies to
360   generate multi-turn preference data mentioned above, the candidate base models can successfully
361   generate between 300 to 500 pair-wise data points per run.
362
363
      Evaluation Metrics: Following the methodology of ToolEval, we used pass rate as the metric,
      where the final agent responses are evaluated by GPT-4-0125-preview to determine task successes.

      In this paper, we presented LAM Simula-
      tor, a comprehensive framework designed to advance the development of Large Action Models
526
      (LAMs) by enabling self-learning through online exploration and automated feedback. Our system
527
      effectively addresses the limitations of traditional supervised learning and manual data curation,
528   offering a scalable solution that enhances both agentic performance and training efficiency. LAM
529   Simulator provides real-time interactions, multi-turn task processing, and high-quality feedback,
530   contributing to significant improvements in model training performance across various benchmarks,
531   such as ToolEval, where models trained via our simulator outperformed other leading models. Our
532   framework accelerates the learning and adaptation process of LAMs with minimal human interven-
533   tion, demonstrating its potential as a pivotal tool for future research and development in AI agents.
534   However, the LAM Simulator still has some limitations. The current implementation focuses on
535   predefined tasks and tools, which may limit its adaptability in more dynamic or unstructured envi-
536   ronments. In future work, we aim to expand the framework’s generalization capabilities by incorpo-
537   rating a wider range of tasks and tool integrations, as well as exploring methods for better handling
538   ambiguous or incomplete task specifications. Furthermore, we plan to investigate the scalability of
539   the system in environments with more complex action spaces and interdependencies, pushing the
      boundaries of autonomous agent learning.

635
636   To ensure the quality of our dataset, we implemented a filtering process to exclude data points that
637   involve one or more non-working tools. This section outlines the high-level procedures for this data
638   filtering method.
639
      We defined a set of keywords associated with non-working tools, such as ”API doesn’t exist” and
640
      other related error messages. These keywords help identify data points with errors in the intermedi-
641   ate steps of the models. This set of keywords is being constructed by manually analyzing the tools
642   usage logs of the tools collection in the evaluation datasets.
643
644
      Then, we performed traversing each test set and For each test set, we identify data points containing
      any of the predefined error keywords, which allows us to filter out the data points effectively.
645
646   By implementing this filtering procedure, we maintain a high standard of data quality, removing
647   instances with non-functional tools that could otherwise skew the evaluation results of our models.
      This step is crucial in ensuring the robustness and reliability of our LAM Simulator framework.

1030
1031   A.3.1    H UMAN - CRAFTED TASKS & T OOLS
1032   We started our tasks coverage by brainstorming the common requested can be received by Agents,
1033   allowing us to come up with four domains:
1034
1035           • Data: Involves tasks such as data retrieval, processing, and analysis.
1036           • Tools: Focuses on utilizing tools to perform actions or solve tasks.
1037
               • Entertainment: Covers tasks related to finding information on entertainment options such
1038
                 as movies, or executing entertainment-related actions, like playing games.
1039
1040
               • Sciences: Involves specialized topics about sciences.
1041
       With the domain being determined, we started composing detail tasks for each domain. An example
1042   of an Abstract Task about search movie details is provided in Appendix A.2.1.
1043
1044   With the list of tasks we can support, we then constructing at least one hidden solution path for each
1045
       of our crafted tasks. We came up with the necessary tools to solve it. During this phase, we are
       also collecting tools related to each task to broaden our tools collection. We stored each tool and
1046
       its execution logic, and register the document about that Tools for the Agents to refer to into our
1047
       system. An example of a tool documentation is provided in Appendix A.2.2.
1048
1049
       A.3.2    T OOL B ENCH EXTRACTED TASKS & T OOLS
1050
1051   In addition to the tools we crafted ourselves, we also obtained many of our tools from ToolBench.
1052   ToolBench offers a great collections of tools, with over 16,000 instances from RapidAPI Hub, a
1053   marketplace for developers to share APIs in different domains. However, during our quality as-
1054   sessment, we found several tools from this batch to be of poor quality or completely unusable. To
1055
       ensure only the best tools were used, we implemented a stringent evaluation process, including test
       runs and detailed reviews of each tool’s performance and documentation. Ultimately, this helped us
1056
       narrow down the selection to 3,420 reliable tools from ToolBench that met our standards for quality
1057
       and documentation, making them suitable for our needs.
1058
1059   The documents for each of the tools we extracted from ToolBench are then collected and registered
1060   in our system. We used the same tool documentation format in Appendix A.2.2 to ensure unification.
