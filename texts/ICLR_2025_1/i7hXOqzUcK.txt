028                                                     A BSTRACT
029
030              The integration of language and 3D perception is crucial for developing embodied
031              agents and robots that comprehend and interact with the physical world. While
032              large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive language understand-
033
                 ing and generation capabilities, their adaptation to 3D environments (3D-LLMs)
                 remains in its early stages. A primary challenge is the absence of large-scale
034
                 datasets that provide dense grounding between language and 3D scenes. In this
035
                 paper, we introduce 3D-GRAND, a pioneering large-scale dataset comprising
036              40,087 household scenes paired with 6.2 million densely-grounded scene-language
037              instructions. Our results show that instruction tuning with 3D-GRAND signifi-
038              cantly enhances grounding capabilities and reduces hallucinations in 3D-LLMs.
039              As part of our contributions, we propose a comprehensive benchmark 3D-POPE
040              to systematically evaluate hallucination in 3D-LLMs, enabling fair comparisons
041              among future models. Our experiments highlight a scaling effect between dataset
042              size and 3D-LLM performance, emphasizing the critical role of large-scale 3D-text
043              datasets in advancing embodied AI research. Notably, our results demonstrate early
044
                 signals for effective sim-to-real transfer, indicating that models trained on large
                 synthetic data can perform well on real-world 3D scans. Through 3D-GRAND and
045
                 3D-POPE, we aim to equip the embodied AI community with essential resources
046
                 and insights, setting the stage for more reliable and better-grounded 3D-LLMs.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
050
051   Embodied Artificial Intelligence (EAI) represents a frontier in robotics and machine learning. In EAI,
052   the integration of perception, language, and action within physical spaces is crucial for developing
053   intelligent systems capable of meaningfully navigating and interacting with their environments.
      Central to this vision is the concept of grounding language in the physical world (Bisk et al., 2020;


                                                                   1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Chandu et al., 2021). Grounding connects abstract linguistic constructs to concrete objects in three-
055   dimensional space, thereby enabling robots and intelligent agents to effectively understand and
056   manipulate their surroundings.
057
058
      Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have greatly benefited Embodied Artificial
      Intelligence (EAI). LLMs demonstrate exceptional capabilities in understanding language instructions
059
      (OpenAI, 2024b; Touvron et al., 2023), perceiving the environment (Liu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a;
060
      Alayrac et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023a; Yang et al., 2024a), and planning detailed actions (Brohan
061   et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023d). The primary inputs to LLMs, other than pure language, have been
062   the combination of language and 2D images, categorizing these models as 2D-LLMs. The significant
063   advancements in 2D-LLMs can be largely attributed to their training on extensive vision-language
064   datasets. These datasets (Schuhmann et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023b), comprising billions of image and
065   text pairs, have been instrumental in enhancing the models’ understanding of visual content and its
066   contextual relevance to textual information. These large datasets have provided the foundational data
067   necessary for training models that excel at integrating visual perception with language processing.
068   Despite some progress in equipping LLMs to understand 3D scenes (3D-LLMs) (Hong et al., 2023b;
069
      Huang et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023c; Chen et al., 2023;
      Qi et al., 2023), these models remain in their early stages due to the scarcity of 3D scene and text
070
      pairs. In this work, we introduce 3D-GRAND, a pioneering million-scale dataset designed for
071
      densely-grounded 3D Instruction Tuning.
072
073   Recently, SceneVerse (Jia et al., 2024) concurrently introduced a large-scale 3D vision-language
074   dataset. However, a significant limitation of this dataset is the absence of object grounding in language,
075
      which is crucial for enhancing model usability in robotics tasks and reducing hallucination. Research
      on 2D-LLMs indicates that grounding language to 2D contexts notably mitigates hallucination in
076
      language models (You et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023; Lai et al., 2023; Rasheed et al.,
077
      2023; Zhang et al., 2024), thereby enhancing the reliability and interpretability of generated responses.
078   While 2D grounding has been extensively explored, extending these principles to 3D environments
079   is still underdeveloped. This situation raises two critical questions: (1) Is there any hallucination
080   in 3D-LLMs and if so, how severe is it? (2) Can densely-grounded data mitigate hallucination
081   for 3D-LLMs? These questions underscore a critical need within the research community for the
082   development of an evaluation benchmark specifically designed for 3D-LLMs and the construction of
083   a large-scale, 3D-grounded dataset.
084   To quantify hallucination in 3D LLMs, this work introduces 3D-POPE (3D Polling-based Object
085   Probing Evaluation). 3D-POPE provides a comprehensive and standardized protocol for evaluating
086   hallucination that enables systematic assessment and facilitates fair comparisons across 3D-LLMs,
087   enhancing our understanding of model capabilities in object hallucination. Specifically, we pose
088   existence questions to 3D-LLMs and evaluate their responses, as illustrated in Fig 1.
089
      To evaluate the role of densely-grounded dataset, we introduce a pioneering million-scale dataset, 3D-
090   GRAND, for densely grounded 3D instruction tuning. 3D-GRAND includes 40,087 household scenes
091   paired with 6.2 million scene-language instructions, featuring dense phrase-to-object grounding. We
092   conduct rigorous human evaluations to ensure the dataset’s quality. Our results trained with 3D-
093   GRAND highlight the dataset’s effectiveness in enhancing grounding and reducing hallucination for
094   3D-LLMs. We highlight the effectiveness of incorporating 3D-GRAND in Fig 1 and introduce each
095   category of 3D-GRAND and provide examples in Fig 2.
096   To sum up, our contributions include:
097
      • 3D-GRAND, the first million-scale, densely-grounded 3D-text dataset for grounded 3D Instruction
098     Tuning. 3D-GRAND includes 40K household scenes paired with 6.2M densely-grounded scene-
099     language instructions.
100
101
      • 3D-POPE, a suite of benchmarks and metrics that systematically evaluate hallucination, enabling
        fair comparisons of future 3D-LLM models in terms of object hallucination.
102
103   • Quantitative research findings regarding hallucination, grounding, and scaling that provide guidance
104     to future research: (1). training 3D-LLMs with 3D-GRAND significantly reduces hallucinations,
105     particularly when the data is densely grounded; (2). densely grounded instruction tuning sig-
106     nificantly enhances the grounding capabilities of 3D-LLMs; (3). scaling densely grounded data
107
        consistently improves grounding accuracy and reduces hallucination; and (4). models can success-
        fully transfer from sim-to-real, providing an early signal for a low-cost and sustainable future of
        scaling synthetic 3D data to help on real tasks.

      Injecting 3D into LLMs. Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have inspired
127
      research into extending their capabilities to 3D environments, leading to the development of 3D-
128
      LLMs (Chen et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024a; Zhu et al., 2023c). Notable works
129   in this field include 3D-LLM (Hong et al., 2023b), which integrates 3D point clouds and features
130   into LLMs to enable tasks such as captioning, question answering, and navigation. LEO (Huang
131   et al., 2024) excels as an embodied multi-modal generalist agent in perception, grounding, reasoning,
132   planning, and action in 3D environments, highlighting the potential of 3D-LLMs in understanding
133   and interacting with the physical world. The most relevant work to our model is Chat-3Dv2 (Wang
134   et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2023a), which grounds generated scene captions to objects in 3D scenes.
135   However, Chat-3Dv2’s dataset is limited to one type of 3D-text task (scene captioning) and only
136   includes 705 captions from a subset of ScanNet scenes. In 3D-GRAND, we expand this concept by
137
      diversifying 3D-text tasks and increasing the dataset size to a million-scale. Our results demonstrate
      promising data scaling effects and sim-to-real transfer, paving the way for future large-scale training
138
      of 3D-LLMs.
139
      Object Hallucination of VLMs. While 2D VLMs have achieved impressive performance, they are
140
      prone to hallucinating objects that do not exist in the provided images, a problem known as object hal-
141
      lucination (Dai et al., 2023; Rohrbach et al., 2018). Several methods have been suggested to mitigate
142   the object hallucination issue, such as integrating an external object detector zhai2023halle, applying
143   visually grounded visual instruction tuning you2023ferret,zhang2024groundhog or reinforcement
144   learning sun2023aligning,gunjal2024detecting, performing iterative refinement zhou2023analyzing,
145   and adapting the decoding strategies huang2023opera. To quantify and mitigate this issue, several
146   benchmarks have been proposed. CHAIR (Caption Hallucination Assessment with Image Rele-
147   vance) (Rohrbach et al., 2018) measures the frequency of hallucinated objects in image captions by
148   comparing the objects mentioned to the ground truth annotations. POPE (Probing Object Hallucina-
149   tion Evaluation) (Li et al., 2023c) assesses a VLM’s ability to identify the presence or absence of
150
      objects through yes/no probing questions. However, these studies primarily focus on 2D image-text
      datasets like COCO (Lin et al., 2014). In contrast, object hallucination in 3D-LLMs remains largely
151
      unexplored. Our work addresses this gap by introducing 3D-POPE, a comprehensive benchmark for
152
      evaluating object hallucination in 3D-LLMs. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first object
153   hallucination benchmark for 3D-LLMs.
154
      Grounding Datasets for 3D-LLMs. In the 2D domain, large-scale datasets with grounding infor-
155
      mation have been instrumental in advancing vision-language research. Notable examples include
156   RefCOCO (Yu et al., 2016), which provides referring expressions for objects in COCO images (Lin
157   et al., 2014). Additionally, 2D LLMs (Peng et al., 2023; Rasheed et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Lai
158   et al., 2023; You et al., 2023) have been trained with densely-grounded web-crawled image-text
159   pairs. In the 3D domain, there is a growing interest in creating datasets that pair 3D scenes with
160   textual annotations (Yuan et al., 2022; Abdelreheem et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023c; Chen et al.,
161   2021). ScanRefer (Chen et al., 2020) pioneered this effort by contributing a dataset of ScanNet (Dai
      et al., 2017) scenes with referring expressions. Table 1 introduces the efforts made by the community.

      However, these datasets have limited grounding annotations and often focus on a single task, such as
179
      referring expression comprehension or visual question answering. In contrast, our proposed dataset,
180   3D-GRAND, stands out by providing 6.2 million densely-grounded scene-language instructions
181   across a diverse set of 3D-text tasks and 40,087 household scenes. This enables a wide range of
182   grounding tasks and facilitates the development of more reliable and better-grounded 3D-LLMs.
183
      Among recent datasets, 3D-GRAND is most similar to SceneVerse (Huang et al., 2023c). They are
184
      both million-scale grounding datasets for 3D-LLMs. However, there are a few key differences: (1)
185
      SceneVerse (Huang et al., 2023c) provides only sparse grounding, while 3D-GRAND is densely
186   grounded. In 3D-GRAND, every noun phrase in the text—whether it’s in captions, QAs, or object
187   references—is explicitly grounded to a corresponding object in the 3D scene, whereas SceneVerse
188   does not offer this level of grounding granularity. To elucidate this difference, we present Table 2 and
189   3 that compares SceneVerse and 3D-GRAND; (2) the language annotations of 3D-GRANDare more
190   trustworthy and have higher quality. Hallucination is known as one of the most common mistakes
191   of LLMs (Huang et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2023c; Rohrbach et al., 2018) In 3D-GRAND, we employ
192   a hallucination filter to check and delete any annotations with hallucinated object IDs. This is not
193   possible for SceneVerse since they have pure language output. 3D-GRAND is also quality-checked
194
      by humans to ensure the quality.

      3    3D-GRAND: T HE 3D G ROUND A NYTHING D ATASET
217
218   In this section, we introduce 3D-GRAND, a large-scale, densely-grounded 3D-text dataset designed
219   for grounded 3D instruction tuning. We describe the data collection process, dataset statistics, and
220   the unique features that make 3D-GRAND a valuable resource for advancing research in 3D-LLMs.
221   3D scene collection. The majority of 3D-text research is currently based on ScanNet scenes collected
222   from real camera scans, which are limited in scale. However, recent advancements have led to the
223   development of numerous synthetic data generation pipelines (Mittal et al., 2023; Deitke et al., 2020;
224   Ehsani et al., 2021; Deitke et al., 2022; Kolve et al., 2017; Puig et al., 2023; Szot et al., 2021; Manolis
225   Savva* et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2024b; Höllein et al., 2023; Schult et al., 2023b; Juliani et al., 2020;
226   Epic Games). Given the scalability of these synthetic data generation pipelines, we explore the
227   potential of using synthetic 3D scenes to enhance 3D-text understanding.
228   Synthetic data offers significant advantages, such as lower costs and greater accessibility, making
229   it an attractive alternative. If models trained on simulated 3D-text data can effectively transfer to
230   real-world 3D scenes, the research community stands to benefit immensely.
231
      To this end, we curate a diverse collection of 40,087 high-quality 3D indoor scenes from the 3D-
232   FRONT (Fu et al., 2021) and Structured3D (Zheng et al., 2020) datasets. These datasets are chosen for
233   their large quantities of synthetic indoor scenes with professionally designed layouts. The collection
234   includes a variety of room types, such as living rooms, bedrooms, kitchens, office spaces, and
235   conference rooms. We further process these 3D scenes to generate per-room 3D point clouds. Details
236   on point cloud rendering and cleaning are provided in the Appendix.
237
      Densely-grounded text annotation. The definition of densely-grounded text is that every noun
238
      phrase of object mentioned in the text should be associated with an 3D object in the 3D scene. This
239
      is illustrated in Figure 2. This is a difficult type of data to get annotations on. Early work such as
240   ScanEnts3D (Abdelreheem et al., 2024) relies on hiring professional human annotators to obtain such
241   annotations. The authors report that crowd-sourcing annotators (Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT)
242   (Crowston, 2012)) were not able to reliably complete this task and they had to hire professional
243   annotators (error rate AMT: 16%, professional: <5%). Yet our human quality check shows that
244   LLMs (GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2024b)) can achieve <8.2-5.6% densely-grounding error rate (see Appendix
245   for detail). This finding is in accordance with recent studies (Ding et al., 2023; Tan et al., 2024)
246   reporting LLMs can be human-level annotators. The accuracy of LLM-annotation provides one
247   motivation for considering LLMs as densely grounding annotation tool.
248   The second, and perhaps more critical, motivation is the scalability of annotation. While we can
249   potentially scale up 3D scenes using synthetic data generation pipelines, annotating these scenes
250   with human effort is both costly and time-consuming, especially for complex tasks like densely
251   grounding annotation. To put the cost of money and time in perspective, for the data we annotated
252   in this paper, we estimate that obtaining the same annotations with human annotator would cost at
253   least $539,000 and require 5.76 years (no eat, no sleep) worth of work from a professional annotator
254
      (earning minimum wage of $10.67 per hour). In contrast, using LLMs (GPT4 (OpenAI, 2024b)), we
      achieve the same results for $3,030 within 2 days, representing a 178x reduction in cost and a 1051x
255
      reduction in time. At the time of writing, the cost and time further decreases by 50% to $1,500 and 1
256
      day, with the introduction of GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024a).
257
258   As previously discussed, using humans to annotate 3D scenes can be an exhaustive process. Mean-
259   while, 2D-LLMs demonstrate remarkable capabilities in understanding visual inputs and generating
260
      language, making them well-suited for creating high-quality, grounded language annotations. How-
      ever, due to the hallucination issues and data issues in 2D-LLMs, aggregating information across
261
      images, even those originating from the same scene, is not feasible yet.
262
263   In contrast, Large Language Models (LLMs) excel at understanding structural data and generating di-
264   verse and fluent language (OpenAI, 2024b). They have demonstrated capabilities in spatial reasoning
265   (Bubeck et al., 2023), solving both elementary and sophisticated math problems (Wu et al., 2023;
266
      Imani et al., 2023). To address the limitations of 2D-LLMs when annotate 3D scenes, we leverage
      the strengths of LLMs. By integrating detailed, accurate information into a reliable scene graph, we
267
      provide LLMs with the necessary data to reason effectively and generate precise annotations.
268

      • 3D Model to 2D Image. In the 3D-Front dataset, each object is sourced from 3D-Future (Fu
      et al., 2021), which provides a ground truth 2D image for each object. For the Structured3D dataset,
286
      individual images for each object are not available. Therefore, we utilize the set-of-mark prompting
287
      technique (Yang et al., 2023), where each object to be annotated is circled in red in the images.
288   • 2D Image to Attributes. We use GPT-4V to generate detailed language annotations for each 2D
289
      object image, including attributes like name, color, finish, and texture. The naming is now open-
290   vocabulary, contrary to being class-agnostic.
291   • List of Attributes to Scene Graph. We structure each individual objects’ annotations into a JSON-
292   based scene graph that captures the relationships and attributes of objects within the scene. Note that
293   we obtain this scene graph from synthetic data which we can guarantee the correctness.
294   • Scene Graph to Generated Annotations. Based on the given scene graph, we will be able to
295   produce 3D-Grounded Object Reference, 3D-Grounded Scene Description, and 3D-Grounded QA
296   using GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2024b) with various prompts, which we will show in the appendix.
297   • Generated Annotations to Processed Annotations. After we acquire raw annotations, we will apply
298   hallucination filters and template augmentation for the phrase tag to remove low-quality annotations
299   and augment generated annotations.
300   With this pipeline, we generate a diverse range of 3D vision-language understanding tasks as shown
301   in Figure 2. On a high level, these tasks can be categorized into:
302
303   • 3D-Grounded Object Reference: Given a 3D scene and an object of interest, 3D-LLM is required
304
      to generate a description that uniquely identifies the target object. The description includes text and
      grounding information, not only for the target object but also for any landmark objects mentioned
305
      in the description. This task is conceptually similar to Visual Grounding, Scene-aware Object
306
      Captioning, and Dense Captioning in 2D vision-language research.
307   • 3D-Grounded Scene Description: Given a 3D scene, the 3D-LLM generates a description that
308
      captures the salient aspects of the environment. The description includes both text and grounding
309   information, linking the language to specific objects or regions in the scene.
310   • 3D-Grounded QA: Given a 3D scene and a question about the environment, the 3D-LLM generates
311   an answer that is grounded in the scene. Both the question and answer include text and grounding
312   information, ensuring that the 3D-LLM’s responses are contextually relevant and accurate.

      Dataset highlights. 3D-GRAND possesses several unique features that distinguish it from existing
342   3D-language datasets: (1). Large-scale: With 40,087 scenes and 6.2 million annotations, 3D-
343   GRAND is the largest 3D-language dataset to date, providing ample data for training and evaluating
344   3D-LLMs. (2). Dense grounding: Unlike recent million-scale datasets like SceneVerse, which lack
345   grounded language annotations, each language annotation in 3D-GRAND is densely grounded to
346   specific objects or regions within the 3D scenes, facilitating fine-grained language understanding
347   and generation. (3). Diverse language tasks: 3D-GRAND supports a broad array of grounded
348   language tasks, including object reference , spatial reasoning, and scene understanding, making it
349   a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating 3D-LLMs. (4). High-quality annotations: We utilize a
350
      hallucination filter to mitigate hallucination of the language annotations in 3D-GRAND. They are
      also human-evaluated to ensure the quality.
351
352   These unique features establish 3D-GRAND as a valuable resource for advancing research in 3D-
353   LLMs and embodied AI. By providing a large-scale, densely-grounded 3D-text dataset, 3D-GRAND
354   enables the development and evaluation of more capable and reliable 3D-LLMs that can effectively
355   understand and interact with the physical world.
356   4      3D-POPE: A B ENCHMARK FOR E VALUATING H ALLUCINATION IN
357
             3D-LLM S
358
359
      To systematically evaluate the hallucination behavior of 3D-LLMs, we introduce the 3D Polling-based
360   Object Probing Evaluation (3D-POPE) benchmark. 3D-POPE is designed to assess a model’s ability
361   to accurately identify the presence or absence of objects in a given 3D scene.
362   Dataset. To facilitate the 3D-POPE benchmark, we curate a dedicated dataset from the ScanNet
363   (Dai et al., 2017) dataset, utilizing the semantic classes from ScanNet200 (Rozenberszki et al., 2022).
364   Specifically, we use the ScanNet validation set as the foundation for evaluating 3D-LLMs on the
365   3D-POPE benchmark.
366   Benchmark design. 3D-POPE consists of a set of triples, each comprising a 3D scene, a posed
367   question, and a binary answer (“Yes” or “No”) indicating the presence or absence of an object (Fig. 1
368   middle). To ensure a balanced dataset, we maintain a 1:1 ratio of existent to nonexistent objects when
369   constructing these triples. For the selection of negative samples (i.e., nonexistent objects), we employ
370   three distinct sampling strategies:
371   • Random Sampling: Nonexistent objects are randomly selected from the set of objects not present
372     in the 3D scene.
373   • Popular Sampling: We select the top-k most frequent objects not present in the 3D scene, where
374     k equals the number of objects currently in the scene.
375   • Adversarial Sampling: For each positively identified object in the scene, we rank objects that are
376     not present and have not been used as negative samples based on their frequency of co-occurrence
377     with the positive object in the training dataset. The highest-ranking co-occurring object is then
        selected as the adversarial sample. This approach differs from the original POPE (Li et al., 2023c)


                                                                  7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
        to avoid adversarial samples mirroring popular samples, as indoor scenes often contain similar
379     objects.
380
      These sampling strategies are designed to challenge the model’s robustness and assess its susceptibility
381   to different levels of object hallucination.
382
      Metrics. To evaluate the model’s performance on the 3D-POPE benchmark, we use key metrics
383   including Precision, Recall, F1 Score, Accuracy, and Yes (%). Precision and Recall assess the model’s
384   ability to correctly affirm the presence of objects and identify the absence of objects, respectively.
385   Precision is particularly important as it indicates the proportion of non-existing objects generated by
386   the 3D-LLMs. The F1 Score, combining Precision and Recall, offers a balanced view of performance
387   and serves as the primary evaluation metric. Accuracy measures the proportion of correctly answered
388   questions, encompassing both “Yes” and “No” responses. Additionally, the Yes (%) metric reports the
389   ratio of incorrect “Yes” responses to understand the model’s tendencies regarding object hallucination.
390
      Leaderboard. We establish a public leaderboard for the 3D-POPE benchmark, allowing researchers
391   to submit their 3D-LLM results and compare their performance against other state-of-the-art models.
392   The leaderboard reports the evaluation metrics for each model under the three sampling strategies,
393   providing a transparent and standardized way to assess the hallucination performance of 3D-LLMs.

402   5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
403
      Model. Our proposed model is based on Llama-2 (Touvron et al., 2023). The input is object-centric
404   context, including a scene graph with each object’s category, centroid (x, y, z), and extent (width,
405   height, depth), along with the text instruction and user query. During training, we utilized ground-
406   truth centroids and extents. For inference, we used bounding boxes predicted by Mask3D (Schult
407   et al., 2023a). Examples of input/output and details of the model can be found in the supplementary
408   material.
409
      Baselines. We compare our 3D-GRAND against the following baselines: 3D-LLM (Hong et al.,
410
      2023b), LEO (Huang et al., 2024), and 3D-Vista (Zhu et al., 2023c). Each model, along with the
411
      specific checkpoint used to obtain the results, is documented in the appendix.
412
413   Datasets. We evaluate our model 3D-GRAND on two datasets: 3D-POPE and ScanRefer. 3D-POPE
414   is our newly introduced benchmark dataset for evaluating object hallucination in 3D-LLMs, as
415   described in Section 4. For ScanRefer, We utilized the validation split which contains 9,508 natural
416
      language descriptions of 2,068 objects in 141 ScanNet (Dai et al., 2017) scenes.
417   Metrics. For the ScanRefer benchmark, we use the official evaluation metrics, including Accu-
418   racy@0.25IoU and Accuracy@0.5IoU. For the 3D-POPE benchmark, we report accuracy, precision,
419   recall, F1 score, and “Yes” rate under the three sampling strategies described in Section 4.
420
      Implementation Details. The 3D-GRAND model is LoRA-finetuned (Hu et al., 2022) based off
421
      Llama-2. We use DeepSpeed ZeRO-2 (Rasley et al., 2020) and FlashAttention (Dao, 2024) to save
422
      GPU memory and speed up training. The model is trained in BF16 precision on 12 NVIDIA A40
423   GPUs with a combined batch size of 96 and a learning rate of 2e-4. We use the AdamW (Loshchilov
424   & Hutter, 2019) optimizer with a weight decay of 0.01 and a cosine learning rate scheduler. We train
425   the mode for 10k steps, which takes approximately 48 hours.

      6               C ONCLUSION
527   In this paper, we introduced 3D-GRAND, a large-scale, densely-grounded 3D-text dataset designed
528   for grounded 3D instruction tuning, and 3D-POPE, a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating object
529   hallucination in 3D-LLMs. Through extensive experiments, we demonstrated the effectiveness of our
530
      dataset on 3D-LLMs in improving grounding and reducing hallucination, achieving state-of-the-art
      performance on the ScanRefer and 3D-POPE benchmarks. Our ablation study and qualitative analysis
531
      highlighted the importance of densely-grounded instruction tuning, the data scaling law, and effective
532
      sim-to-real transfer in developing high-performing 3D-LLMs. We hope our contributions and findings
533   can spark further research and innovation in this field, ultimately leading to the development of more
534   advanced and capable 3D-LLMs for a wide range of applications.

      A.2    T RAINING DATA

898
      There are two flavors of models that we fine-tuned: one grounded object reference model, and one
899   grounded QA model. The grounded object reference model was trained using the grounded object
900   reference data on 3D-FRONT train split, which consist of 234,791 3D-text pairs, each of which are
901   densely grounded. This model was used to generate the ScanRefer results presented in Table 6, 7,
902   and Figure 4 The grounded QA model was trained using a subset of 200k grounded QA: object
903   existence data from the 3D-FRONT train split. The reason that we select a subset of 200k QAs is
904   simply because the entire grounded QA dataset is too large and we do not have enough resource
905   to train on all data. However, as shown in Table 5 and Figure 4, we find even such a subset is very
906   effective in reducing hallucinations in 3D-LLMs.
907   We provide official data splits of train, val and test (90%, 5%, 5%) in our dataset release. The val and
908   test proportion might seem small, but given our dataset’s million-scale, they should be sufficiently
909   large for any development and evaluation purposes.

912
913   The two flavors of model mentioned above are LoRA-finetuned (Hu et al., 2022) based off Llama-2.
914   We use DeepSpeed ZeRO-2 (Rasley et al., 2020) and FlashAttention (Dao, 2024) to save GPU
915   memory and speed up training. The model is trained in BF16 precision on 12 NVIDIA A40 GPUs

1003   D     H UMAN VALIDATION
1004
1005   Because our dataset generation process involves GPT-4V, there is a potential for hallucinations.
1006
       We identify three types of possible hallucinations that could impact our dataset: the text might
       inaccurately describe an object’s property, such as color or size (termed incorrect object attribute);
1007
       it might incorrectly depict the spatial relationship between two objects (termed incorrect spatial
1008
       relation); or it might describe an object that does not exist in the referenced scene at all (termed
1009   incorrect object existence). Additionally, inaccuracies in our dataset may also arise from incorrectly
1010   grounding the wrong object.
1011
1012
       To validate our dataset against these potential failures, we plan to verify a subset of our data through
       crowdsourcing to ascertain the frequency of these failure cases.

1059   D.1.2             C ROWD - SOURCING VALIDITY
1060
1061   Validating a dataset necessitates a high level of attention from annotators. We curate sets of instruc-
1062   tions, qualifying tasks, and honeypot tasks to ensure that the annotations obtained from crowdsourcing
1063   are reliable. The crowdsourcing process is illustrated in Figure 10.
1064   Before presenting any tasks to the workers, we present them with a set of instruction tasks that show
1065   an example annotation, the correct response (as determined by us), and the reason why that response
1066   is correct. They are paired with an incorrect example and an explanation of why it is incorrect in
1067   order to ensure unbiased annotations. Examples of qualifying instructions are shown in 11. These
1068   instructions are intentionally brief, as we found through trial-and-error that longer, paragraph-based
1069   instructions were largely ignored by annotators.
1070   Qualifying tasks are presented to the annotators before they are shown any real tasks in order to train
1071   them to complete the real task with a high accuracy. Annotators are both shown the correct answer
1072   and a reasoning as to why it is correct for every qualifier. We set the minimum qualifier accuracy
1073   to 0.75 to ensure that annotators must achieve a minimum competency before annotating real tasks.
1074   Every dataset is given between 12 and 30 specially crafted qualifying tasks that demonstrate the
1075   possible inaccuracies that could appear in the data. These qualifiers are divided equally between true
1076
       and false examples so as not to bias workers towards any one answer.

1078   ing a high quality of annotations throughout the entire job. Because we annotate the honeypot tasks
1079   before showing them to annotators, we are able to evaluate any given worker’s accuracy on honeypot
       tasks. We set the minimum honeypot accuracy to 0.89 to ensure that annotators are maintaining

1172
1173   We perform validation on 10,200 room-annotation pairs. From each of the three data types, 1,700
1174   pairs are sampled for validation of both text truthfulness and grounding accuracy. A subset of 800
1175   rooms is uniformly chosen, with 400 designated for text truthfulness and another 400 for grounding
1176   accuracy. The text data is uniformly sampled from these rooms. We report accuracies for both text
1177   truthfulness and grounding accuracy in Table 10.
1178   We report comprehensive statistics from the annotation process in Table 11. We observe a very low
1179   qualifier pass rate ranging from 11 - 20 % across the different tasks in our data, suggesting that our
1180   qualifiers were effective in allowing only the most attentive annotators qualify to annotate real tasks.
1181   In addition, none of these annotators were banned due to honeypots. This increases our confidence
1182   that our qualification process is effective in training annotators and filtering out those who were not
1183   attentive. We also observe that workers spend roughly the same time on real tasks and honeypot tasks,
1184
       suggesting that the honeypots are indistinguishable from real tasks for the annotators. This further
       supports the validity of our annotations.
