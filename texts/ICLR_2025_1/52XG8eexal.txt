009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep state-space models (Deep SSMs) have shown capabilities for in-context
012            learning on autoregressive tasks, similar to transformers. However, the architec-
013            tural requirements and mechanisms enabling this in recurrent networks remain
014            unclear. This study demonstrates that state-space model architectures can per-
015            form gradient-based learning and use it for in-context learning. We prove that a
016            single structured state-space model layer, augmented with local sliding window
017            attention, can reproduce the outputs of an implicit linear model with least squares
018            loss after one step of gradient descent. Our key insight is that the diagonal lin-
019
               ear recurrent layer can act as a gradient accumulator, which can be ‘applied’ to
               the parameters of the implicit regression model. We validate our construction by
020
               training randomly initialized augmented SSMs on simple linear regression tasks.
021
               The empirically optimized parameters match the theoretical ones, obtained ana-
022            lytically from the implicit model construction. Extensions to multi-step linear and
023            non-linear regression yield consistent results. The constructed SSM encompasses
024            features of modern deep state-space models, with the potential for scalable train-
025            ing and effectiveness even in general tasks. The theoretical construction elucidates
026            the role of sliding window attention and multiplicative interactions in recurrent
027            architectures as the key ingredients for enabling the expressive power typical of
028            foundation models.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The current generation of Large Language Models (LLMs) and foundation models are extremely
034   capable and have started proliferating in several real-world applications. These models are based
035   on the transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017), and a big part of their capability has been at-
036   tributed to in-context learning (Wei et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023). In-context learning in transformers
037   is relatively well studied (Wies et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023; Garg et al., 2023). A
038   prominent explanation for the mechanism used by transformers to do in-context learning is that the
039
      model performs in-context learning by gradient descent (von Oswald et al., 2023; Akyürek et al.,
      2024). But, the quadratic dependence of transformers on the input length makes them computa-
040
      tionally expensive. To mitigate this, there has been much work on alternatives based on recurrent
041
      networks, such as state-space models (Gu et al., 2021; Gu & Dao, 2023) and linear recurrent net-
042   works (Orvieto et al., 2023). These recurrent models can perform inference efficiently since the
043   computational complexity of recurrent networks is linear in the sequence length. At the same time,
044   linear recurrent networks allow parallelization across the sequence during training using associative
045   scan. The latest versions of these models are competitive with transformers at scale (Dao & Gu,
046   2024; De et al., 2024), and also capable of in-context learning (Grazzi et al., 2024). However, the
047   mechanism they use for in-context learning remains unclear.
048   This work focuses on the in-context learning mechanism of State-Space Models (SSMs). Multiple
049   variations of state-space models have recently shown competitive performance at scale (Gu & Dao,
050   2023; De et al., 2024), while earlier generations struggled with scaling (Smith et al., 2022; Poli
051   et al., 2023). All SSMs, linear attention models, and other linear recurrent networks share a common
052   formalism of being linear recurrent networks interleaved with non-linear layers. On the other hand,
053   the ability to do in-context learning seems to be a hallmark of most recent scalable variants (Grazzi
      et al., 2024). Which features of these successful models contribute to in-context learning, as opposed


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      to earlier variants? Using a constructive approach, we pinpoint input-dependent input and output
055   processing, as the key features required for in-context learning.
056
057   We show that SSMs with local sliding window attention, a form of input-dependent input processing,
058
      can perform in-context learning analogously to transformers, i.e. through gradient descent steps on
      an implicit linear regression problem. The key insight we use is that the state of the recurrent network
059
      can be used to aggregate gradients of the parameters of the implicit linear model, which can later be
060
      ‘applied’ to the initial parameters of the implicit model. Using the general SSM formalism, we show
061   how to design the recurrent and output equations that enable them to do in-context learning. Our
062   construction, which we call GD-SSM, results in a state-space model that is potentially applicable to
063   general-purpose tasks as much as in-context learning tasks.
064
      In summary, our contributions are to show that:
065
066          • one-layer SSMs with diagonal recurrence, two-dimensional state, and input-dependent in-
067            put and output processing can perform one step of minibatch gradient descent on an implicit
068            least-squares loss function;
069
             • multi-step (minibatch) gradient descent can be achieved by stacking the 1-layer model;
070
071
             • gradient descent for an implicit non-linear regression problem can be achieved by augment-
               ing the SSMs with non-linearities;
072
073          • a randomly initialized model trained on regression tasks learns parameters that match our
074            construction for in-context learning tasks based on regression.

128   3     SSM S CAN EMULATE GRADIENT DESCENT ON LINEAR REGRESSION TASKS
129
130   We will now show that an SSM as described in Section 2 can perform gradient descent on an implicit
131   linear model to minimize a least squares loss (for particular choices of parameters). Extensions to
132   non-linear regression models are considered in Section 3.3.
133   Consider a linear regression problem. The goal is to minimize the corresponding least squares loss
134   using gradient descent. The linear model will be the implicit model on which the SSM performs
135   gradient descent. Performing mini-batch (batch size > 1) gradient descent on the parameters of
136   this implicit model involves two steps: (i) to accumulate gradients of the loss with respect to the
137   parameters, and (ii) apply the accumulated gradient to the initial value of the parameters of the linear
138   model to calculate the updated parameters. Predictions can be made with the updated parameters by
139
      combining them linearly with the input.
140   Assume the training samples for the linear regression problem are provided as a sequence of inputs
141   and targets. A large enough SSM can then accumulate the gradients of the loss function in its state
142   if a sliding window attention-like layer processes the sequence inputs before the recurrence1 .
143   Given the accumulated gradients, the next-step emission of the SSM is equivalent to i) updating
144   the parameters of the implicit model gradient and ii) computing the model output with the updated
145   parameters. Multiple steps of gradient descent can be achieved by stacking multiple layers, while
146   nonlinearity in the implicit model can be handled by adding nonlinear input-output embedding lay-
147   ers. We argue that the architecture that allows a single layer to perform gradient descent provides
148   the inductive bias for the model to do in-context learning.

      3.3    G ENERALISING TO ANY REGRESSION PROBLEM
305
306   Multi-step gradient descent: The proposed construction can be extended to multi-step gradient
307   descent. Since each layer of a GD-SSM produces the parameters of the implicit linear model updated
308   by one step of GD, this is equivalent to stacking together multiple layers. In our derivations above,
309   we assumed the initial parameter of the implicit linear model is 0. In the multi-step GD, all layers
310   other than the first will correspond to a non-zero initialised implicit linear model. Technically,
311   extra gradient steps in the implicit model introduce one additional term in the gradient accumulation
312   equation. Each of the two terms requires a separate (parallel) recurrence and is performed by a
313
      dedicated layer. At the end, the states are combined to obtain the multiple-step GD update, with
      minor extra computational burdens. See Appendix A.2 for a detailed construction of multi-step GD.
314
315
316   Non-linear regression: Non-linear regression can be handled by adding MLP layers to the GD-
317   SSM. In the previous sections, we let GD-SSM accumulate the gradients of a linear regressor. Addi-
318   tional MLP layers can learn to transform the state of these linear layers into quantities corresponding
319
      to the gradient of the implicit non-linear model. See Appendix A.3 for a detailed explanation.
320
321   Regularisation terms in the loss: As the recurrent layers only accumulate the gradients, we can
322   separate the gradient calculation and accumulation from its application. This has a practical advan-
323   tage. Any input-independent regularisation term, e.g. L2 norms, can be added to the model without
      changing the recurrence structure. See Appendix A.4 for details.

472   5     R ELATED WORK
473
474   In-context learning in transformers has been studied extensively, and various mechanisms have been
475   proposed to explain it (Hendel et al., 2023; von Oswald et al., 2023; Akyürek et al., 2022). Of
476   those, the most prominent is that the self-attention mechanism performs gradient descent on a linear
477
      loss. A construction with linear self-attention was demonstrated by von Oswald et al. (2023). While
      linear-self attention can be written as an RNN (Katharopoulos et al., 2020b), the results of von
478
      Oswald et al. (2023) depends on the tokens being constructed in a specific way, which limits the
479
      generality of their construction. Moreover, the most common type of self-attention used is softmax
480   scaled dot-product self-attention rather than linear self-attention, and the basic linear self-attention
481   mechanism used in von Oswald et al. (2023) is not competitive with softmax self-attention. Whereas,
482   we show a construction that uses local-self attention with state-space models, which has been shown
483   to be competitive with softmax self-attention transformers (De et al., 2024). Zucchet et al. (2023)
484   show a correspondence between gated linear models (which include many commonly used forms
485   of SSMs) and linear self-attention, which is known to be able to do ICL. But compared to both
      von Oswald et al. (2023) and Zucchet et al. (2023), our model is more parsimonious because of


                                                                                                  9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      the following: (1) In general, a linear self-attention model requires 3f 2 parameters (f 2 each for
487   query, key and value) for input dimension f 5 and without including the output projection, whereas
488   our model equivalently only requires f 2 parameters, one each for each of the f 2 recurrent units.
489   (2) linear self-attention as constructed in von Oswald et al. (2023) requires 12f 2 parameters to do
490   GD on a f × f linear regression problem, whereas ours still only requires f 2 parameters. (3) to
491   construct a gated linear recurrent model from linear self-attention as done in Zucchet et al. (2023)
492   requires O3(d4 ) parameters for a linear self-attention with 3d2 parameters. This blows up the size
493   of the model very significantly. On the other hand, since ours is a explicit and direct construction,
494   our model size remain proportional to the size of the linear regression problem.
495   Liu et al. (2024) formulate the SSM layer as an online optimization objective with exact solution.
496   Similarly, Sun et al. (2024) use the state of the SSM to perform GD. But both these papers consider
497   online updates, that is minibatch size 1 updates. One layer of their model is not capable of perform-
498   ing larger minibatch updates. Moreover, without sliding window attention, their online optimization
499   objective is limited to using single sequence tokens with a single-layer. And finally their goal is
500   not to explain how in-context learning happens with existing architectures, but rather to develop
501
      entirely new SSM variants using the idea that an optimization process can be used for compressing
      information as well, which is the key property required of a recurrent network.
502
503   The combination of local-self attention with Mamba has been used in for improving performance in
504   multiple instances, including most recently De et al. (2024); Ren et al. (2024). But these papers do
505   not specifically construct their model for ICL, nor do they attempt to distill inductive biases required
506   for ICL in state-space models.
507
508   6       D ISCUSSION
509
510   This work establishes a clear connection between state-space models (SSMs) and gradient-based in-
511   context learning. We have demonstrated, both theoretically and empirically, that SSMs can emulate
512   gradient descent on implicit regression models, providing a mechanistic explanation for their in-
513   context learning capabilities. Our construction, GD-SSM, reveals the crucial role of architectural
514
      features such as sliding window attention and multiplicative output interactions in enabling this
      behavior. These findings not only explain the success of recent SSM variants in in-context learning
515
      tasks but also provide valuable insights for the design of future sequence models.
516
517   The alignment between our theoretical construction and the behavior of trained models suggests
518   that the gradient descent mechanism may be a natural inductive bias in these architectures. This
519   understanding opens new avenues for analyzing and improving sequence models, potentially leading
520
      to more efficient and effective architectures for a wide range of tasks.
521   Future research could explore the implications of this mechanism in larger-scale models, more com-
522   plex tasks, and real-world applications. Additionally, investigating how this understanding can be
523   leveraged to enhance the design and training of state-space models could yield significant advance-
524   ments in the field of sequence modeling.

                                                                                                         N

800

801   To evaluate the proposed model, we conducted experiments using a constructed token dataset with

804   No activation function was used during training.
805   The training process spanned a maximum of 300,000 epochs with a batch size of 64. A cosine
806   annealing schedule and linear warmup were utilized for the learning rate, beginning with an initial
807   SSM learning rate of 1 × 10−4 for optimizing the SSM parameters using the AdamW optimizer.

809   were also optimized using AdamW. A weight decay of 0.05 was applied to regularize the model and
