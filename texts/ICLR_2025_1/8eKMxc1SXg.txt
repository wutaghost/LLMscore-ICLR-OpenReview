                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have significantly advanced generative AI in terms of creating
012            and editing naturalistic images. However, improving the image quality of gener-
013            ated images is still of paramount interest. In this context, we propose a generic
014            kurtosis concentration (KC) loss, which can be readily applied to any standard
015            diffusion model pipeline to improve image quality. Our motivation stems from
016            the projected kurtosis concentration property of natural images, which states that
017            natural images have nearly constant kurtosis values across different band-pass
018
               versions of the image. To improve the image quality of generated images, we
               reduce the gap between the highest and lowest kurtosis values across the band-pass
019
               versions (e.g., Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT)) of images. In addition, we also
020
               propose a novel condition-agnostic perceptual guidance strategy during inference
021            to further improve the image quality. We validate the proposed approach for three
022            diverse tasks, viz., (1) personalized few-shot finetuning using text guidance, (2)
023            unconditional image generation, and (3) image super-resolution. Integrating the
024            proposed KC loss and perceptual guidance has improved the perceptual quality
025            across all these tasks in terms of FID, MUSIQ score, and user evaluation. Code
026            and README are provided in the supplementary material.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Multi-modal generative AI has advanced by leaps and bounds with the advent of the diffusion
031   model. Large-scale text-to-image diffusion models, e.g., DALLE Ramesh et al. (2022), Stable-
032   diffusion Rombach et al. (2022) synthesize high-quality images in diverse scenes, views, and lighting
033
      conditions from text prompts. These models generate high-quality and diverse images since they
      have been trained on a large collection of image-text pairs and can capture the visual-semantic
034
      correspondence effectively. While diffusion models generate images that appear highly realistic,
035
      recent studies have demonstrated that these images can still be distinguished from natural ones
036   using advanced image forensic tools Corvi et al. (2023). This suggests that although state-of-the-art
037   generative models excel at tasks like image editing, they often leave behind subtle, unnatural artifacts.
038   Ensuring high image quality is therefore critical for various generative tasks, such as personalized
039   few-shot finetuning Ruiz et al. (2022); Kumari et al. (2022), super-resolution Karras et al. (2022);
040   Dhariwal & Nichol (2021), image restoration, and unconditional image generation.
041
      Our goal is to improve the image quality using natural image statistics by exploiting the well-known
042   kurtosis concentration property of natural images Zhang & Lyu (2014); Zoran & Weiss (2009);
043   Wainwright & Simoncelli (1999). This property states that natural images have nearly constant
044   kurtosis (fourth order moment) values across different band-pass (e.g., Discrete Cosine Transform
045   (DCT), Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT)) versions of the images Zhang & Lyu (2014). Inspired by
046   this property, we propose a novel kurtosis concentration (KC) loss, which is generic and applicable to
047   any diffusion-based pipeline. More specifically, this loss minimizes the gap in the kurtosis of an image
048   across band-pass filtered versions and improves the quality of the generated images. We also propose
049   a novel perceptual guidance (PG) strategy during inference which is agnostic to conditioning (e.g.,
050
      text/class) and further improves image quality. Both KC loss and PG strategies are general-purpose
      and do not require any labels. It can be adapted to various generative tasks with minimal effort. In
051
      this work, we experiment with diverse tasks: (1) personalized few-shot finetuning of text-to-image
052
      diffusion model, (2) unconditional image generation, and (3) image super-resolution.
053
      Our major contributions are as follows:

      3.2       K URTOSIS C ONCENTRATION (KC) LOSS
237
      In this work, we introduce a novel KC loss function for training deep generative models, leveraging
238   the KC property of natural images to improve perceptual quality. Unlike previous approaches Zhang
239   & Lyu (2014) that used the KC property for tasks like noise estimation and source separation, our KC
240   loss can be integrated into any diffusion pipeline, and we validate its effectiveness with state-of-the-art
241   diffusion models.
242
      Suppose, we need to train or finetune a diffusion model fθ using input training images (x) with or
243
      without a conditioning vector c. The conditioning vector could be text, image, or none (in the case of
244   the unconditional diffusion model). Given an initial noise map ϵ ∼ N (0, I), and a conditioning vector
245   c, the generated images obtained from fθ is given by xgen = fθ (x, ϵ, c). Typically, the diffusion
246   model is trained to minimize the l2 distance between the ground truth image (x) and the noisy image
247   (xgen ) Dhariwal & Nichol (2021) or their corresponding latent in case of Latent Diffusion Model
248   (LDM) Rombach et al. (2022). Without loss of generality, we are referring to that as reconstruction
249   loss (Lrecon ) between the ground-truth image (x) and the generated image (xgen ), denoted by,
250
                                                    Lrecon = Ex,c,ϵ [ ||xgen − x||22 ]                               (7)
251
252   Note that, for diffusion models trained to predict the added noise, we could deterministically obtain
253   the intermediate clean image from the predicted noise and apply the loss to that. Next, we will
254   describe the KC loss. Note, that the KC property holds across different bandpass transformed domains
255   (DCT, DWT, fastICA) and we choose DWT because it is widely used due to its hierarchical structure
256   and energy compaction properties E Woods & C Gonzalez (2008). Typically, DWT transforms
257
      images into LL (low-low), LH (low-high), HL (high-low), HH (high-high) frequency bands and each

258

259
      image xgen is then transformed using Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT) with kernels k1 , k2 , .., kn

283   3.3   P ERCEPTUAL GUIDANCE (PG) DURING INFERENCE
284
285   Directly applying the KC loss to diffusion training improves sample quality over vanilla diffusion.
286   Additionally, we introduce a novel PG mechanism during inference to further enhance perceptual
287   quality. Intuitively, this is similar to classifier-free guidance, where the diffusion model is run both
288   conditionally and unconditionally at every step, and the difference in the output is considered as the
289
      gradient direction towards the condition. While classifier-free guidance can improve the perceptual
      quality as a by-product, it has several potential drawbacks, such as: (1) It works with conditional
290
      models only. (2) The goal of classifier-free guidance is to generate images aligned with the condition,
291
      which may not explicitly align with the perceptual quality. Instead, we propose to produce two
292   samples of varying perceptual quality (with two different models with and w/o KC loss) and amplify
293   the intermediate output at each step toward better perceptual quality. The key advantages of such a
294   design are: (1) it is independent of condition, (2) can operate in parallel to traditional classifier-free
295   guidance.
296
      Suppose a baseline diffusion model (fB ) is trained with Ltask + Lrecon . We also train another model
297
      (fP ) with the proposed KC loss, i.e., Ltask + Lrecon + LKC . During inference, let ϵt be the noisy
298   latent at time step t. The output of the two diffusion model fB and fP would be ϵ∗t,B = fB (ϵt , t, c),
299
      and ϵ∗t,P = fP (ϵt , t, c), respectively, where c is the conditional vector, which can be null as well. We
300
      calculate the latent for the next time step t − 1 as ϵt−1 = ϵ∗t,B + λ(ϵ∗t,P − ϵ∗t,B ). This will be iterated
301   T times to generate the final sample.
302
303   After a simple algebric manipulation, the equation becomes, ϵt−1 = λ ∗ ϵ∗t,P − (λ − 1)ϵ∗t,B . Thus,
304   if λ is greater than 1, we essentially substract the part corresponding to lower perceptual quality
305
      (baseline, no KC loss) from a higher quality one (perceptual model). Intuitively, this is similar to the
      vector direction from point B to point A, where B is baseline model and A is perceptual model, i.e.,
306
      direction towards a better perceptual quality in the diffusion noisy latent space.
307
308   To prevent numerical issues like overflowing when linearly combining scaled output of two different
309   models, we use the standard clipping strategy used in guided diffusion Dhariwal & Nichol (2021),
310   stable diffusion Rombach et al. (2022) etc. For example, we keep the variance of the model fixed
311
      during training and inference. We only calculate the mean during inference using the output of the
      pre-trained diffusion UNets fB and fP . After performing a linear combination, we clamp the final
312
      value between [-1, +1] to preserve the scale of the distribution during reverse diffusion.
313
314   Note that, the PG steers the generated images to gradually move towards better perceptual quality, with
315   or without any conditional guidance. The PG can also be interpreted as a special form of conditional
316   guidance, where the same input is conditioned on two models, one trained vanilla, and another trained
317
      with perceptual KC loss, and the difference between their outputs guides the denoising process during
      inference, leading to improved perceptual quality. We experimentally verify the efficacy of perceptual
318
      guidance.

322   Why diffusion generated images have higher kurtosis values? Natural images typically exhibit
323   smooth transitions and structured patterns, leading to a pixel intensity distribution with fewer outliers
      and, consequently, lower kurtosis Zoran & Weiss (2009); Lyu et al. (2014); Wainwright & Simoncelli

341   (1999); Bethge (2006). In contrast, diffusion models generate images through iterative refinement of
342   pure noise into coherent structures. Due to imperfections in the trained UNet and finite denoising steps,
343   residual high-frequency noise may persist in the final output, leading to more extreme pixel intensity
344   values and contributing to heavier tails in the distribution Zhang et al. (2023). This phenomenon
345   is more effectively characterized in the frequency domain (wavelet transform), which generally
346   correspond to higher kurtosis values Zhang & Lyu (2014) (Fig. 8).
347   How does KC loss improve image quality? Intuitively, minimizing the KC loss can be seen as a
348   locality-aware smoothing of the data distribution to enhance perceptual quality. For instance, in Fig. 6,
349   the output of GD without KC loss exhibits undesirable abrupt changes near the eye region. Ideally,
350   we should first detect such regions and then apply suitable operations to improve perceptual quality.
351   However, performing a spatially varying refinement is challenging, and globally applying such filters
352   might be sub-optimal for other regions. It has been observed that the wavelet coefficients of natural
353
      images follow a Generalized Gaussian density Moulin & Liu (1999); Sharifi & Leon-Garcia (1995);
      Mallat (1989), and Kurtosis quantifies the heaviness of the tails and peakedness of a distribution
354
      compared to the Gaussian distribution Zhang & Lyu (2014); Maier (2021). Therefore, kurtosis
355
      across bandpass wavelet filtered versions of the image automatically provides locality of the abrupt
356   changes and minimizing KC loss performs locality-aware smoothing of the data distribution. In
357   Fig. 6, incorporating KC loss enhances the generated eye region.

419   4.2    TASK 2: U NCONDITIONAL IMAGE GENERATION
420   Unconditional image generation operates without the need for text or image guidance. It aims to learn
421   the training data distribution through a generative model (in this case, a diffusion model) and produce
422   samples that resemble the training data distribution. We opted for the well-known unconditional
423   image generation pipeline, the DDPM Ho et al. (2020), to test the efficacy of KC loss. PG is especially
424   effective here because classifier-free guidance cannot be applied.
425
      In DDPM, we directly integrate the KC loss into the image space, demonstrating the flexibility
426   of our proposed loss. We experimented with the Oxford-flowers Nilsback & Zisserman (2006),
427   CelebA-faces Zhang et al. (2020), CelebAHQ Karras et al. (2017), Stanford-Dogs Khosla et al. (2011)
428   and Stanford-Cars Krause et al. (2013) datasets, achieving consistent improvements in image quality,
429   as shown in Table 2 and Figure 5. Additionally, PG further enhances image quality, as indicated
430   in Table 2. Human evaluation is not feasible for unconditional image generation due to the lack of
431   one-to-one correspondence between training and generated images, but quantitative and qualitative
      analyses demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach.

888   D     KC LOSS ADDED AS A REGULARIZER
889
890   We would like to highlight that in our work, the underlying theoretical framework behind the forward
891   and reverse diffusion processes remains unchanged; rather, we focus on improving the performance
892   of the denoising neural network used to approximate the reverse diffusion trajectory.
893   Suppose, we have the input training images (x) and conditioning vector c. The conditioning vector
894   could be text (text-to-image model), image (image-to-image model), or none (in case of the uncon-
895   ditional diffusion model). In the forward process, the noisy versions of image x at timestep t is
896   generated as xt = αt x + σt ϵ, where ϵ ∼ N (0, I).
897
      In the reverse process, a denoised autoencoder (fθ ) is trained to predict the denoised version of the
898
      image (xt,gen ) at each timestep t from the noisy images xt , i.e., xt,gen = fθ (xt , c, t). Typically, the
899   denoised autoencoder (fθ ) is trained by minimzing the Mean Squared Error between the real image
900   (x) and the generated denoised version of the image at time step t (xt,gen ) averaged over timesteps
901   and noise variances as denoted by,
902
903
                                           Lrecon = Ex,c,ϵ,t [ ||xt,gen − x||22 ]                           (21)
904
905   The kurtosis concentration loss is applied on the generated images (xt,gen ), and therefore can be
906   considered as a function (f ′ ) of xgen as follows:
907
908                                            LKC = Ex,c,ϵ,t [f ′ (xt,gen )]                               (22)
909
                            ′
910   Note the function f is difference between the maximum and minimum values of the DWT filtered
911   version of input xt,gen .
912   Therefore, the total loss function can be written as,
913
                             Ltotal = Ex,c,ϵ,t [ ||xt,gen − x||22 ] + Ex,c,ϵ,t [f ′ (xt,gen )]              (23)
914
915   In our work, the above-mentioned framework remains the same. Instead, the proposed KC loss acts
916   as an additional regularizer to the training of the denoising neural network, which helps it to denoise
917   xt better (Lemma 2, main paper), ultimately improving the approximation of x, i.e., xt,gen at each
      time step t.

1484   L.2   V IDEO EDITING
1485
       In text-based video editing Wu et al. (2023), input videos (e.g., goldfish swimming) need to be edited
1486   through text prompt (i.e., replace goldfish by shark, Fig. 28). Tune-a-video Wu et al. (2023) is a
1487   baseline method which perform this task in a one-shot finetuning way. We add our plug-and-play
1488   KC loss to the standard diffusion loss in Wu et al. (2023) pipeline, and observe that adding KC loss
1489   improves the editing quality. This is verified quantitatively, where we evaluate on the test set provided
1490   in Tune-A-video, i.e., 42 videos from DAVIS dataset wit 140 prompts. We measure the average CLIP
1491   frame similarity, CLIP text similarity and pick-score of the edited videos and report it in Tab. 16.
