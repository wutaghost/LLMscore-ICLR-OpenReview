009                                                           A BSTRACT
010
011             The motif scaffolding problem, crucial in drug and enzyme design, involves creating
012             a scaffold to support one or more specified motifs. Existing methods employ the
013             paradigm of inpainting, thereby limiting their applicability to scenarios where
014             only a single motif is present or the positions between multiple motifs are known.
015             However, in many significant scenarios, the positions between motifs are unknown,
016             leaving the multi-motif scaffolding problem open. To tackle this challenge, we
017             introduce a protein diffusion model called MoDiff. During the diffusion process,
018             MoDiff implicitly assigns motifs to the protein backbone, thereby achieving the
019
                automatic design of relative positions among motifs. Our experiments demonstrate
                that MoDiff can: 1) solve the multi-motif scaffolding problem even when the
020
                positions between motifs are unknown, and 2) generate diverse scaffolds based on
021
                multiple given motifs. This indicates that MoDiff is a potential general solution to
022             the multi-motif scaffolding problem.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Protein design plays a crucial role in drug development and enzyme design, often abstracted into the
028   motif-scaffolding problem (Procko et al., 2014; Correia et al., 2014; Jiang et al., 2008; Siegel et al.,
029   2010). Here, ‘motif’ refers to specific functional segments of protein structures. The objective of
030   this problem is to devise a scaffold that supports designated motifs, thereby imparting the desired
031   functionality to the designed protein. Recently, protein design approaches based on diffusion
032   models (Song et al., 2021) have made significant strides in generating scaffolds for single motif. For
033   instance, RFdiffusion (Watson et al., 2023), employing the inpainting paradigm, treats the given motif
034   as the known part of the protein, with the remaining amino acids treated as inpainting content to
035   generate the corresponding scaffold.
036   However, existing methods face significant limitations when applying the inpainting paradigm to
037   the design of scaffolds for multiple motifs (introduced later). At the same time, the multi-motif
038   scaffolding problem holds paramount importance in the design of antibodies and enzymes (Sec.2.1),
039   as proteins frequently contain more than one motif. Consequently, identifying a general solution for
040   the multi-motif scaffolding problem holds great significance.

      The limitations of existing methods in addressing the multi-motif scaffolding problem can be primarily
055   attributed to two aspects, as shown in Fig. 1. On one hand, the inpainting paradigm (Watson et al.,
056   2023) necessitates a defined and rational relative positioning of motifs, enabling the generation of a
057   scaffold by treating multiple motifs as a “single entire motif”. On the other hand, in many multi-motif
058   scaffolding problems (Sec.2.1), the positions between motifs are unknown and need to be designed,
059   posing a challenge for existing methods. This challenge persists even in the presence of alternative
060   motif-scaffolding approaches like Chroma (Ingraham et al., 2023) and SMCDiff (Trippe et al., 2022).
061   As a result, addressing the multi-motif scaffolding problem still demands extensive experimentation
062   and expert knowledge.
063   In this paper, we introduce a motif-conditioned generation model called MoDiff. MoDiff automati-
064   cally establishes the sequential and spatial relative positions between motifs, addressing the challenge
065   of unknown motif positions and providing the capability to generate diverse and designable scaffolds.
066   The core concept of MoDiff differs from inpainting. Through learning from data distributions, the
067   MoDiff network guides motifs to rational positions on the protein backbone during the diffusion
068   process, achieving the automatic design of relative positions among motifs.
069   However, we observed that traditional cross-attention methods are ineffective in guiding motif gener-
070   ation. To address this issue, we propose an implicit matching module. Specifically, during training, as
071   we utilize randomly cropped segments from the protein as virtual motifs, the correspondence between
072   motifs and the protein backbone is known. Consequently, within this module, this correspondence is
073   leveraged to provide matching supervision between motifs and the protein backbone, guiding the
074   model in placing motifs at reasonable positions along the backbone. Additionally, we introduce a
075
      motif reconstruction loss to further guide motif generation, ultimately enhancing the success rate of
      scaffold design.
076
077   In silico experiments demonstrate that MoDiff, when confronted with diverse motifs, exhibits the
078   capability to 1) effectively design the positions between motifs and 2) generate diverse and designable
079   scaffolds. Interestingly, we observe a significant increase in the novelty of proteins generated by
080   MoDiff compared to the unconditioned baseline, VFN-Diff (Mao et al., 2023). This phenomenon
081
      arises because, in cases where specified motifs are sourced from different proteins, there may not be
      any known protein containing the given motifs simultaneously. Consequently, the proteins designed
082
      by MoDiff with a higher probability differ from all known proteins. Next, ablation experiments
083
      indicate the effectiveness of the motif reconstruction loss and implicit matching module, significantly
084   enhancing the success rate of design. Those suggest that MoDiff represents a potential general
085   solution to the multi-motif scaffolding problem. The contributions of this study can be summarized
086   as follows:
087
088          • We highlight the limitations of existing methods in addressing the multi-motif scaffolding
089            problem and showcase the promising prospects of deep learning approaches in this domain.
090          • We introduce MoDiff, a method capable of efficiently designing proteins with multiple
091            motifs. Experimental results support MoDiff as a potential general solution to the multi-
092            motif scaffolding problem.
093          • The proposed implicit matching module and motif reconstruction loss effectively enhance
094            the success rate of design.

099   2.1   M ULTI - MOTIF S CAFFOLDING P ROBLEM
100
      Many protein design problems are formulated as the multi-motif scaffolding problem (Cao et al., 2022;
101
      Wang et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2021; Sesterhenn et al., 2020; Linsky et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022).
102   For instance, 1) a protein (Roy et al., 2023) with high selectivity is designed by incorporating multiple
103   known binding motifs, and 2) through expert knowledge, two EF-hand motifs are successfully
104   integrated into a protein (Roel-Touris et al., 2023) to enhance enzyme activity. Notably, in many
105   cases (Roy et al., 2023; Roel-Touris et al., 2023; Davila-Hernandez et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023), at
106   least one position, either in terms of spatial or sequential relative positions between motifs, remains
107   uncertain. While some problems under this circumstance can be addressed, it still requires extensive
      trial and error, human effort, and expert knowledge. Moreover, these methods exhibit significant

      limitations (Gelman & Gruebele, 2014; Tischer et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021; Tischer et al., 2020;
109   Huang et al., 2022a), especially when faced with situations lacking suitable templates and references
110   in the Protein Data Bank (PDB). In contrast, MoDiff presents a potential general solution that does

113   2.2   D IFFUSION M ODELS FOR M OTIF S CAFFOLDING
114
115   With the emergence of numerous protein diffusion models (Wu et al., 2022; Yim et al., 2023;
116   De Bortoli et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022b; Anand & Achim, 2022; Lee et al., 2023; Lisanza
117   et al., 2023; Martinkus et al., 2023), protein design has witnessed a revolutionary paradigm shift.
118   Motif-scaffolding stands as a central task in protein design, with various diffusion model methods
119
      addressing this challenge over time. In SMCDiff (Trippe et al., 2022), the authors initially train an
      unconditionally generated protein backbone diffusion model, ProtDiff. Subsequently, they introduce
120
      guidance for motif generation based on ProtDiff. Chroma (Ingraham et al., 2023) adopts a similar
121
      paradigm, further incorporating various types of guidance. RFdiffusion (Watson et al., 2023), on the
122   other hand, employs a different inpainting paradigm, treating motifs as given content and generating
123   scaffolds as inpainting components. However, these methods face a common limitation when
124   designing scaffolds to support multiple motifs: the inability to design the positions between motifs,
125   requiring predetermined rational positions for motifs. Nevertheless, as mentioned, determining the
126   positions between motifs is often challenging, restricting the applicability of these methods.

      3.1   PARAMETERIZED R EPRESENTATION
131
132   Proteins typically consist of N amino acids, and the backbone of each amino acid is composed of four
133   atoms: C, Cα , O, N. Following the approach of AlphaFold 2 (Jumper et al., 2021), a deterministic
134   frame T ∈ SE(3) can be established for the atoms C, Cα and N through the Gram–Schmidt process.
135   Consequently, i-th amino acid can be represented by a backbone frame Ti ∈ SE(3), and the structure
136   of the protein backbone is parameterized as N frames T = [T1 , T2 , ..., TN ] ∈ SE(3)N . In previous
137   protein studies, specific segments with distinct biological functions were identified. Any of these
138   segments can be referred to as a motif, denoted as TM = [T1M , T2M , ..., TMM
                                                                                     ] ∈ SE(3)M . Here, M
139   represents the number of amino acids in a motif. As the motif is a segment within the protein T, an
140
      alignment permutation π of the protein T exists such that TM = Tπ . Starting from this point, π
      represents a specific index permutation of amino acids.
141

143   3.2   M ULTI - MOTIF S CAFFOLDING P ROBLEM
144
      Given the structures of multiple motifs, the objective of multi-motif scaffolding is to sample a protein
145   of length N that incorporates the given motifs. For the sake of brevity, we will describe the case of
146   having two motifs from this point onward, but these concepts can be straightforwardly extended to
147   scenarios with more motifs.
148
      Definition 1. (Multi-motif Scaffolding Problem). Given two motifs TM1 ∈ SE(3)M1 , TM2 ∈
149
      SE(3)M2 , the task is to sample a protein structure T̂ = [Tˆ1 , Tˆ2 , ..., TˆN ] ∈ SE(3)N from the condi-
150
      tional probability distribution pθ (T̂|TM1 , TM2 ), where T̂ contains the motifs within the protein
151
      structures. Here, M1 and M2 represent the numbers of amino acids in the two motifs, respectively.
152
153   Intractability. The structure of proteins must satisfy the natural constraints, meaning not all protein
154   structures are physically viable. This necessitates the proper arrangement between motifs TM1
155   and TM2 ; otherwise, the corresponding protein T̂ may not exist. However, 1) TM1 and TM2 are
156   respectively defined with respect to two distinct global frames T g1 , T g2 ∈ SE(3), meaning that the
157   reasonable spatial relationship between TM1 and TM2 is unknown and must be systematically
158   designed. 2) The amino acids correspondence π between the motif and the protein is also unknown,
159   requiring a method to establish this correspondence. For instance, the design of a rational slice
160   index π1 is necessary to ensure that motif TM1 aligns appropriately with a segment of protein T̂,
161   simultaneously ensuring the existence of the protein. Similarly, a corresponding π2 also needs to be
      designed for motif TM2 .

253   4.2   F RAMEWORK OF M O D IFF
254
255   MoDiff consists of two branches: the motif branch and the protein backbone branch. These branches
256   are employed to encode the geometric information of motifs, TM1 , TM2 , and the protein backbone
257   T(t) , respectively. Furthermore, within the protein backbone branch, the representation of motifs is
258   aggregated into the protein backbone representation and utilized to predict the denoised structure
259   T̂(0) .
260   The motif branch. The two motifs, TM1 , TM2 , are separately fed into a same geometric encoder to
261   obtain corresponding d-dimensional geometric features HM1 ∈ RM1 ×d and HM2 ∈ RM2 ×d . Here,
262   we employ a standard VFN network, VFNmotif , as the geometric encoder, treating each amino acid
263   as a node for encoding. This can be written as:
264
                                             HMi = VFNmotif (TMi )                                           (6)
265
266   The protein backbone branch. During training, the protein T is randomly noised at time step t and
267   then used as the input T(t) for the protein backbone branch. This backbone T(t) is similarly encoded
268   using a standard VFN network VFNbb to obtain corresponding geometric features H ∈ RN ×d ,
269   written as:

306   The goal of the Implicit Matching Module is to aggregate the motif features of HM1 , HM2 into

308

309

310

      5.1   T RAINING D ETAILS

392   To construct the training set, we employ the approach used in FrameDiff to select proteins meeting
393   specific criteria from the Protein Data Bank (Berman et al., 2000). The criteria requires protein
394   lengths to fall within the range of 60 to 512 amino acids and have a resolution lower than 5Å. This
395   selection process results in a total of 21,399 proteins. Throughout the training procedure, pairs of
396   virtual motifs, each with lengths ranging from 10 to 20 amino acids, are randomly extracted from the
397   protein backbones (Eq.5) and subsequently input into the model. To speed up the training, we utilize
398   VFN-Diff (Mao et al., 2023) as pretraining weights and conducted training for 250,000 iterations.

660   A.1   L IMITATIONS
661
      The problem of assembling motifs to achieve specific functionalities remains unresolved. In proteins
662
      such as enzymes, motifs need to be assembled in a specific manner to realize particular biological
663   functions. In this study, while MoDiff successfully automates the assembly of different motifs, the
664   functionality resulting from the assembly of these motifs has not been explored. This aspect involves
665   wet-lab experimental testing and is left for future work.
666
      Generating long proteins is challenging. Incorporating more motifs results in longer protein lengths.
667
      However, both VFN-Diff and RFdiffusion exhibit noticeable limitations in generating proteins
668
      exceeding 500 amino acids, with a significant decrease in designability. This limitation hinders the
669   success rate of MoDiff in assembling more motifs, such as five motifs, leading to lower success rates.
670   Addressing the unresolved issue of enhancing the diffusion-based model to generate long proteins
671   remains a critical area for future research. Further exploration is needed in future work to tackle this
672   challenge.
673
674   A.2   I MPLEMENT DETAILS
675
676   The model described in our study features a backbone architecture equivalent to the VFN-Diff model
677   for consistent performance, coupled with a motif module designed to enrich the representation
678   of protein features. The motif module comprises a VFN module with 4 blocks, a self-attention
679   mechanism with 2 blocks and 8 heads, and a cross-attention component with 2 blocks to integrate
680
      motif and backbone information. Additionally, the motif node embedding size is set to 128.
681
682   A.3   T RAINING DETAILS
683
      Hyper parameters. In our training protocol, we employ the Adam optimizer with a learning rate set
684
      to 0.0001, and momentum hyperparameters β1 and β2 at 0.9 and 0.999, respectively. We maintain a
685
      batch size of 64. For the loss function, we weight the motif fape loss LMR at 3 and the matching loss
686   Lmatching at 10. All other settings are consistent with those outlined in the VFN-Diff.

      Motif RMSD. As defined in Eq. 1, RMSDmotif assesses whether the given motif exists in the protein
789   structure.
790
791   scTM. scTM represents the protein’s designability, i.e.whether the generated protein structure is
792
      feasible. Specifically, the generated structures are fed into ProteinMPNN (Dauparas et al., 2022)
      to predict the corresponding sequences, which are then utilized by ESMFold (Hie et al., 2022) for
793
      structure prediction. If the structure predicted by ESMFold is similar to the generated structure (high
794
      scTM), it implies the generated structure is feasible.
795
796   Success rate. Success rate signifies the proportion of successful designs, where motifs are present
797   (RMSDmotif < 1Å), and the protein is designable (scTM > 0.5).
798   Diversity: For assessing the diversity of the generated protein sequences, we follow the methodology
799   outlined in (Yim et al., 2023) and utilized MaxCluster (Herbert & Sternberg, 2008) for hierarchical
800   clustering of protein backbones. A selection criterion, scTM > 0.5, was impose for cluster considera-
801   tion to minimize the influence of proteins with low designability on the diversity metric. We select a
802   single protein with the highest scTM score from each diffusion sample, generated by ESMFold, for
803   inclusion. The diversity metric is then calculated as the ratio of the number of clusters to the total
804   diffusion samples, Ndif f , ensuring a more stringent and relevant evaluation.
805   pdbTM: Novelty in protein design is quantified by comparing the highest-scoring proteins from
806   ESMFold-generated PDB files against the PDB database using Foldseek (van Kempen et al., 2023).
807   Proteins are required to have an scTM score greater than 0.5 to be included in this analysis. The
808   pdbTM score is used to measure structural novelty, with proteins considered novel at a threshold of
809   pdbTM < 0.7, referred to as pdbTM0.7 . This cutoff, less stringent than the pdbTM < 0.6 criterion
      used in (Yim et al., 2023), allows for the recognition of a wider range of novel protein structures.

811
812   Analysis of motif features’ influence. In Sec. 5.3, we investigate the relationship between the
813   success rate and various motif features: the number of hydrogen bonds per residue, the number of
814   contacts per residue, and the solvent-accessible surface area (SASA). Our findings reveal a positive
815   correlation between both the number of hydrogen bonds per residue and the number of contacts per
816   residue with the success rate, while the solvent-accessible surface area exhibits a negative correlation
817   with the success rate.
818   Building on this analysis, we further explore the impact of these motif features on scTM and
819   RMSDmotif >1 ratio in Fig. 4. Interestingly, we observe no significant correlation between the motif
820   features and scTM. However, there is a positive correlation between both the number of hydrogen
821   bonds per residue and the number of contacts per residue with RMSDmotif >1 ratio, and a negative
822   correlation between solvent-accessible surface area and RMSDmotif >1 ratio.
823   These results suggest that the difficulty of a motif, as reflected by RMSDmotif >1 ratio, predominantly
824   influences the success rate. This also implies that the bottleneck of the MoDiff model lies in encoding
825   complex motifs and generating them with high precision under specified conditions.
