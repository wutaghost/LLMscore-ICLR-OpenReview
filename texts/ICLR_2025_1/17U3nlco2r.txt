011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Traditional deep neural networks (DNNs) predominantly adhere to a similar de-
014            sign paradigm. Even with the incorporation of additive shortcuts, they lack ex-
015            plicit modeling of relationships between non-adjacent layers. Consequently, this
016            paradigm constrains the fitting capabilities of existing DNNs. To address this
017            issue, we propose ChebyNet, a novel network paradigm to build Chebyshev poly-
018            nomial connections between general network layers. Specifically, we establish re-
019
               cursive relationship among adjacent layers and polynomial relationship between
               non-adjacent layers to construct ChebyNet, which improves representation capa-
020
               bilities of the network. Experimentally, we comprehensively evaluate ChebyNet
021
               on diverse tasks, including function approximation, semantic segmentation, and
022            visual recognition. Across all these tasks, ChebyNet consistently outperforms
023            traditional neural networks under identical training conditions, demonstrating su-
024            perior efficiency and fitting properties. Our findings underscore the potential of
025            polynomial-based layer connections to significantly enhance neural network per-
026            formance, offering a promising direction for future deep learning architectures.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) have achieved remarkable progress across diverse areas (LeCun
033
      et al., 2015), including computer vision (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; He et al., 2016; Huang et al., 2017),
      natural language processing (Sutskever et al., 2014; Vaswani et al., 2017), reinforcement learning
034
      (Mnih et al., 2013), speech recognition (Hinton et al., 2012) and other fields (Dong et al., 2021;
035
      Wainberg et al., 2018). Despite these advancements, the underlying design paradigms often rely
036   on a fixed layer structure where non-adjacent layers have limited interactions, typically restricted
037   to additive shortcuts as seen in ResNets(He et al., 2016). Although considerable efforts have led
038   to innovations such as dense connections(Huang et al., 2017), attention mechanisms(Vaswani et al.,
039   2017), etc, the design paradigms constraint inherently restricts the expressive power and fitting ca-
040   pabilities of the network, thereby capping its potential performance.
041
      The limited inter-layer relationships in neural networks constrain their learning and representational
042   capabilities. Despite the introduction of additive shortcuts, these simplistic additive inter-layer con-
043   nections still fail to provide the complex layer-wise interactions required(Bengio et al., 2013; Oye-
044   dotun et al., 2023). This observation raises a critical question: how can we enhance the interaction
045   between layers to improve both fitting capability and computational efficiency?
046
      In this paper, we propose ChebyNet, a novel architecture that leverages Chebyshev polynomial layer
047
      connections to enhance the representational capacity and efficiency of neural networks, motivated by
048   the best uniform approximation properties and numerical stability of Chebyshev polynomials(Mason
049   & Handscomb, 2002). Specifically, inspired by the approach of approximating numerical functions
050   using a family of basis functions, we establish Chebyshev polynomial connections between layers
051   in ChebyNet. The process involves two key steps: (1) generating Chebyshev basis functions of the
052   layer features within the neural network, and (2) performing element-wise multiplication (Hadamard
053   product) between these basis functions and the network’s output at the current layer. The former
      establishes recursive relationship among adjacent layers while the latter constructs polynomial rela-

105   2   R ELATED W ORK
106
107   The success of DNNs relies on extensive research and thorough exploration of their architectures.
      Vast categories of DNN layers, such as the fully-connected layer (LeCun et al., 1998), convolutional


                                                       2

108
      layer (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), pooling layer (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), batch normalization layer
109   (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015), etc, renders countless models like ResNet (He et al., 2016), gated recur-
110   rent unit (Cho et al., 2014), generative adversarial network (Goodfellow et al., 2014), Transformer
111   (Vaswani et al., 2017), etc.
112
      Despite the substantial progress in DNN design, the prevailing architectural paradigm often imposes
113
      limitations on the interaction between non-adjacent layers. Most conventional architectures employ
114
      fixed pathways for information flow, where connections between layers are primarily additive or
115   sequential, restricting the model’s ability to capture complex relationships within the data (He et al.,
116   2016). Such designs restrict the approximation and representation capabilities of DNNs, making it
117   difficult to learn intricate patterns that extend beyond local interactions. Consequently, there is a
118   critical need for novel architectures that facilitate richer inter-layer connections, thereby enhancing
119   the expressiveness and overall potential of neural networks.
120
      Despite several studies, polynomial functions have been significantly underestimated in the con-
121   struction of DNNs. According to Weierstrass’s approximation theorem (Weierstrass, 1885; Stone,
122   1932), polynomial functions can approximate any continuous function with arbitrary precision, mak-
123   ing them ideal candidates for activation functions in DNNs. Recent research has explored the in-
124   tegration of polynomial functions into DNNs, focusing on two primary aspects: (1) polynomial
125   activation functions (PAC) and (2) polynomial relationships between layers. We elaborate on these
126   approaches below.
127   Numerous attempts have been made to incorporate polynomial activation functions into neural net-
128   works. Following the ReLU construction methodology, (López-Rubio et al., 2019) introduced seg-
129   mented polynomial activation functions, while (Loverich, 2015) demonstrated the superiority of
130   these functions over segmented linear activation functions. However, the piecewise functions used
131   in these experiments are non-differentiable, leading to an increased risk of overfitting during train-
132   ing. Additionally, the use of low-order polynomials reduces the network’s nonlinearity, limiting its
133   ability to learn and represent complex features. Optimization challenges also persist, with (Goyal
134   et al., 2020) addressing these issues through the introduction of a new normalizing transformation.
135   Learnable parametric polynomial activation functions have also been proposed (Feng & Yang, 2023;
136   Wu et al., 2018; Agostinelli et al., 2014; Piazza et al., 1993; Guarnieri et al., 1999), wherein the ac-
137   tivation function parameters are learned during training or tuned via heuristic algorithms. However,
138   these networks introduce higher computational complexity and pose difficulties in maintaining sta-
139   ble training conditions.
140   Orthogonal polynomials have been explored in activation function design due to their favorable
141   mathematical properties. For instance, (Venkatappareddy et al., 2021) utilized Legendre polyno-
142   mials for constructing activation functions, but (Deepthi et al., 2023) highlighted that Legendre
143   polynomials may struggle to adapt to moderate and highly non-linear features. Similarly, Hermite
144   polynomials have been employed in activation function design (Ma & Khorasani, 2005), but their
145   effectiveness has been demonstrated only in networks with a single hidden layer. Chebyshev poly-
146   nomials have also been employed as activation functions in several studies (Deepthi et al., 2023;
147
      Wang et al., 2022; Carini & Sicuranza, 2016; Sornam & Vanitha, 2018; Zhiqi, 2016; Lee & Jeng,
      1998; Li et al., 2019), but unrestricted inputs may lead to unstable network training, necessitating
148
      normalization techniques (Wang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2019). Moreover, current research has solely
149
      validated Chebyshev polynomial activation functions in networks with a single hidden layer (Lee &
150   Jeng, 1998).
151
152   Another promising direction involves establishing polynomial relationships between layers within
153
      the network. Previous work has primarily focused on low-order (typically second-order) polyno-
      mial connections, enabling quadratic interactions between layers (Chrysos et al., 2022). While this
154
      method has demonstrated some advantages in terms of improved representational capabilities, it re-
155
      mains limited in scope. Higher-order polynomial connections, particularly those with advantageous
156   mathematical properties, have the potential to further enhance the network’s expressiveness. How-
157   ever, these have not been extensively explored due to the significant computational and optimization
158   challenges involved.

      In this section, we introduce ChebyNet, which is founded on two fundamental principles: the supe-

168   3.1     C HEBYSHEV POLYNOMIALS
169
170   Chebyshev polynomials play a crucial role in approximation theory. The roots of Chebyshev poly-
171   nomials of the first kind are employed in polynomial interpolation, producing polynomials that ef-
172   fectively mitigate the Runge phenomenon and offer optimal uniform approximation for continuous
173   functions (Mason & Handscomb, 2002).
174   Chebyshev polynomials possess multiple definition formulations, such as trigonometric definition
175   (as shown in equation 1), commuting polynomials definition, Pell equation definition, etc.

191   complex patterns in data and enhance the network’s representational power. These favorable math-

195   3.2     C HEBY N ET: E NHANCING LAYER INTERACTIONS WITH C HEBYSHEV POLYNOMIALS
196
197   Inspired by the approximation of numerical functions using a family of basis functions, ChebyNet
198   incorporates Chebyshev polynomial connections to enhance interactions between network layers.
199   The construction of ChebyNet centers around two key components: the recursive relationship among
200
      adjacent layers and the polynomial relationship between non-adjacent layers.
201
202
      3.2.1    R ECURSIVE RELATIONSHIP AMONG ADJACENT LAYERS
203   The first aspect of ChebyNet’s architecture is grounded in the recursive equation of Chebyshev poly-
204   nomials, establishing a connection among three adjacent layers, as shown in equation 2. Specifically,
205   given three consecutive layers, Li−1 , Li , Li+1 , the output of layer Li+1 is defined in terms of the
206   outputs of the two preceding layers as follows:
207
                                       Li+1 (x) = 2x ◦ Li (x) − Li−1 (x),                                (3)
208
209   where x represents the input features of the Chebyshev layer (also referred to as the intermediate
210   representations within a DNN) and ◦ denotes the Hadamard product (element-wise multiplication).
211   This recursive relationship facilitates stable and efficient propagation of information across the net-
212
      work layers.
213   It can be observed that this recursive structure among the three adjacent layers is, in fact, equiva-
214   lent to constructing Chebyshev basis functions derived from the input features. By embedding the
215   Chebyshev polynomial structure into the network topology, the model is able to learn complex rela-
      tionships between layer outputs, significantly enhancing its representational and fitting capabilities.

217
218   The second aspect involves establishing polynomial relationships between non-adjacent layers. This
219   is achieved by performing element-wise multiplication between basis functions derived from the
220   input features (or the output of the last layer) and the network’s output at the current layer.
221

222   layer, and g(·) denotes a down-sampling operation to align the dimensions of x with the output f (x)
223   of the current layer. The aggregated features can then be expressed as follows in equation 4:

      3.3     I MPLIMENTATION DETAILS
237
238   Although several closed-form expressions exist for calculating n-order Chebyshev polynomials, in-
239   cluding trigonometric definitions (see equation 1), commuting polynomials, and Pell equation-based
240   formulations, our extensive empirical studies demonstrate that the recursive formulation (see equa-
241   tion 2) provides superior numerical stability, which is crucial for ensuring consistent performance
242   and facilitating reliable gradient propagation during the training process. This recursive approach
243   effectively preserves the integrity of both the parameters and their corresponding gradients, signifi-
244   cantly reducing the risk of overflow during computation. Considering the generally low-degree poly-
245   nomials employed in practice, the additional computational complexity introduced by the recursive
246
      formulation is negligible and does not substantially affect the model’s overall efficiency. Further-
      more, we propose an optimized implementation leveraging dynamic programming principles, which
247
      strikes an efficient balance between time complexity (O(n)) and space complexity ((O(1))).
248
249   By definition, Chebyshev polynomials require their arguments to lie within the interval [-1, 1] by
250   definition. Consequently, to effectively integrate Chebyshev polynomial-based layers into neural
251   network architectures, it is essential to transform input vectors to meet this constraint. Our com-
252
      prehensive empirical study reveals that applying non-linear transformations, such as Sigmoid and
      Softmax, to the input components substantially outperforms traditional linear normalization tech-
253
      niques.

518   5   C ONCLUSION
519
520   In this paper, we propose ChebyNet, a novel neural network architecture that utilizes Chebyshev
521   polynomial connections to enhance representational capacity and computational efficiency. By in-
522   troducing polynomial transformations between layers, ChebyNet offers a more expressive and flex-
523   ible framework with robust approximation capabilities, enabling superior performance in various
524   tasks. Our empirical results demonstrate that ChebyNet consistently outperforms traditional neural
525   networks in terms of both accuracy and resource efficiency, making it a promising approach for
526   advancing deep learning models. These findings suggest that polynomial-based layer connections
527
      could play a key role in future neural network developments.
