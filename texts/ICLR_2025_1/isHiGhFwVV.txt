                                                        A BSTRACT
008
009
010                Anomaly detection focuses on identifying samples that deviate from the norm.
011
                   When working with high-dimensional data such as images, a crucial requirement
                   for detecting anomalous patterns is learning lower-dimensional representations
012
                   that capture concepts of normality. Recent advances in self-supervised learn-
013
                   ing have shown great promise in this regard. However, many successful self-
014                supervised anomaly detection methods assume prior knowledge about anomalies
015                to create synthetic outliers during training. Yet, in real-world applications, we
016                often do not know what to expect from unseen data, and we can solely leverage
017                knowledge about normal data. In this work, we propose C ON2 , which learns rep-
018                resentations through context augmentations that allow us to observe samples from
019                two distinct perspectives while keeping the invariances of normal data. C ON2
020                learns rich representations of context-augmented samples by clustering them ac-
021                cording to their context while simultaneously aligning their positions across clus-
022
                   ters. At test time, representations of anomalies that do not adhere to the invari-
                   ances of normal data then deviate from their respective context cluster. Learning
023
                   representations in such a way thus allows us to detect anomalies without making
024
                   assumptions about anomalous data.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Reliably detecting anomalies is essential in many safety-critical fields such as healthcare (Schlegl
      et al., 2017; Ryser et al., 2022), finance (Golmohammadi & Zaiane, 2015), industrial fault detection
031
      (Atha & Jahanshahi, 2018; Zhao et al., 2019), or cyber-security (Xin et al., 2018). A common real-
032
      world example of anomaly detection is the standard screening scenario, where doctors regularly
033   examine the general population for anomalies that would indicate a health risk. Standard screening
034   datasets thus predominantly comprise samples from healthy people, as most screened individuals do
035   not exhibit any diseases. Detecting anomalies in this setting is challenging, as anomalies can arise
036   from an arbitrary set of potentially rare diseases or measurement errors, while we predominantly
037   encounter normal samples from healthy people in the dataset. The field of anomaly detection tackles
038   such problems by learning representations that reflect normality during training and, at test time,
039   detecting anomalies as deviations from the learned normal structure (Ruff et al., 2021).
040   Recent works have demonstrated that learning a representation space containing features that tightly
041   represent normality is essential for anomaly detection (Ruff et al., 2018; Oza & Patel, 2018;
042   Sabokrou et al., 2020). Current state-of-the-art methods carefully design synthetic anomalies and
043   explicitly encourage anomalous representations to be different from normal ones (Tack et al., 2020;
044   Wang et al., 2023). However, anomalies can be diverse and unexpected, making it difficult to simu-
045   late them in real-world settings.
046   This work presents a novel anomaly detection objective, C ON2 1 , which learns informative, tightly
047   clustered representations of normal samples. We illustrate an overview of the algorithm in Figure 1.
048   Unlike previous works, which focus on prior knowledge about anomalies, the proposed C ON2 mod-
049   els properties of normal samples, which is particularly useful in more specialized data, such as in the
050   medical domain, which we demonstrate in our experiments. C ON2 leverages context augmentations
051   that let us observe samples in different contexts while preserving their normal content. Our new
052   C ON2 objective clusters representations according to these contexts while encouraging similar rep-

090   2   R ELATED W ORK
091
092   Learning useful normal representations of high-dimensional data to perform anomaly detection has
093   recently become a popular line of research. Prior work has tackled the problem from various angles,
094   for instance, using hypersphere compression (Ruff et al., 2018). Other popular methods define
095   pretext tasks such as learning reconstruction models (Chen et al., 2017; Zong et al., 2018; You
096   et al., 2019) or predicting data transformations (Golan & El-Yaniv, 2018; Hendrycks et al., 2019b;
097   Bergman & Hoshen, 2019). While these approaches had some success in the past, the learned
098
      representations are not very informative. On the other hand, methods learning more informative
      representations through self-supervised learning have recently been shown to improve over prior
099
      work (Sun et al., 2022; Sehwag et al., 2021).
100
101   Another line of work focuses on estimating the training density with the help of generative models,
102   detecting anomalies as samples from low probability regions (An & Cho, 2015; Schlegl et al., 2019;
103   Nachman & Shih, 2020; Mirzaei et al., 2022). However, these methods tend to generalize better to
104
      unseen distributions than to the observed training distribution (Nalisnick et al., 2018), which proves
      problematic for anomaly detection.
105
106   In addition to the traditional setting, where we assume training data without any labels, some recent
107   works weaken this restriction and assume access to a limited number of labeled samples. This
      setting is called anomaly detection with Outlier Exposure (OE) (Hendrycks et al., 2019a), and it has

131   specialized domains.
132
      Another setting that has recently gained popularity is out-of-distribution (OOD) detection. In OOD
133
      detection, we have additional information about our dataset in the form of labels. Anomaly detection
134
      is a special case of OOD detection with only a single label. While the problem is similar, most
135   approaches that tackle OOD detection make specific use of a classifier trained on the dataset labels
136   (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017; Lee et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022), which cannot directly be applied
137   in the anomaly detection setting, as training a classifier on a single class is not straightforward.
138
      In comparison, our method operates in the traditional anomaly detection setting and can be applied
139
      to datasets without knowledge about anomalies. Further, while we assume access to a dataset con-
140
      taining normal samples, our method does not rely on additional labels, as they can be difficult and
141   expensive to obtain, particularly in more specialized settings.

146   In the following, we introduce the notion of context augmentations and then present our C ON2 ob-
147   jective, which leverages these augmentations to learn tightly clustered, informative representations.
148   We then explain how to use these representations to detect anomalies at test time.
149
150   3.1   C ONTEXT AUGMENTATION
151
152   The intuition behind context augmentation comes from the observation that certain transformations
153   can augment a sample into another context, creating a distinct new view without altering its infor-
154   mation content. For example, inverting an image, i.e., exchanging every pixel value x with the value
155   1−x, neither adds nor destroys any information (alignment) but instead allows us to observe the same
156   sample from a different perspective. In the following, we want to learn these invariances while still
157   being able to distinguish between the two transformations to learn symmetric representation clusters
158
      for both the original and the augmented sample space. In the previous example, this only works if
      the inverted version of an image does not naturally appear in the dataset already (distinctiveness).
159
      Otherwise, it is impossible to distinguish between original and transformed instances properly.
160
161   We define two requirements that let us determine whether a transformation is suitable as a context
      augmentation for a given dataset. Let X ⊂ X be our dataset, let tC ∶ X → X be a data augmentation,

186

189   samples of such a dataset.

      the input space. Then, we require that d(x, x′ ) ≈ d(tC (x), tC (x′ )), i.e., originally similar normal
192
      samples should stay just as similar in the new context, meaning that the original and the context-
193
      augmented normal distributions should align. For instance, masking part of a torso x-ray image
194   would violate alignment, as we could potentially remove important regions, such as the lungs, from
195   the image altogether. On the other hand, two vertically flipped x-rays are as similar to each other as
196   their original counterparts.
197
      While it may be dataset-dependent whether a transformation, such as histogram equalization (Equal-
198
      ize), fulfills these conditions, there are transformations, such as vertical flipping (Flip) or color in-
199
      version (Invert), that seem to fulfill distinctiveness and alignment across a broad range of datasets.

      3.3   A NOMALY D ETECTION
271
272   In the anomaly detection setting, we typically assume an unlabeled training set containing predom-
273   inantly normal samples, whereas we want to discriminate between normal and anomalous samples
274   at test time (Ruff et al., 2021). To detect anomalies, we typically define an anomaly score function
275   S that maps a given sample’s representation onto a scalar, determining its anomalousness. We can
276   then define a threshold on this anomaly score, predicting anomaly for samples above the threshold
277   and normal for samples below. We provide additional background about the anomaly detection
278
      setting in Appendix A.2.
279   To detect anomalies using the representations of C ON2 , we define two anomaly score functions
280   that measure how well a test sample adheres to the context representation clusters. One of the
281   most popular and straightforward approaches to achieve this is a non-parametric nearest neighbor
282   approach (Bergman et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2022). Our first score adopts a similar procedure using
283   the cosine similarity. Specifically, let us define the cosine distance between the training set Xtrain
284
      and a given test sample x with transformation t as

320   4     E XPERIMENTS
321
322   In the following, we present how C ON2 allows us to learn highly informative representations of
323   normality by incorporating prior knowledge about invariances of normal data. After briefly intro-
      ducing our baselines, we demonstrate how we can leverage this knowledge in a realistic medical


                                                         6
324
      setting, showcasing the applicability of our method to a specialized domain where prior knowledge
325   about anomalies is typically hard to obtain. Further, we present the generality of our method by
326   comparing it to baselines on popular natural image datasets in the one-class classification setting,
327   where anomaly detection with C ON2 consistently exhibits strong performance across various
328   settings. We refer to Appendices C and D for more details regarding the choice of hyperparameters
329   and our datasets.
330
331   Baselines We compare our work to various recent contrastive anomaly detection baselines, in-
332   cluding SSD (Sehwag et al., 2021), CSI (Tack et al., 2020), and UniCon-HA (Wang et al., 2023).
333   SSD works by learning representations using SimCLR and detecting anomalies with a Mahalanobis
334   distance-based anomaly score. Similarly, CSI and UniCon-HA learn representations with SimCLR
335   but additionally design synthetic anomalies using rotation transformations. CSI leverages these
336   synthetic anomalies using an additional classifier to discriminate between normal and synthetic
337
      anomaly samples and detects anomalies at test time with a score that combines nearest neighbor
      distance, sample norm, and classifier confidence. UniCon-HA does not require an additional clas-
338
      sifier but instead clusters all normal samples close to each other while minimizing the similarity
339
      of synthetic anomaly representations and normal training samples. UniCon-HA also modifies the
340   instance discrimination loss to weight positive and negative pairs according to the distance between
341   representations. It further introduces a hierarchical augmentation scheme that lets them apply their
342   loss on different layers of their neural network architecture using layer-specific augmentation strate-
343   gies. We also compare against a baseline that learns SimCLR embeddings and detects samples in
344   nearest neighbor fashion similar to KNN+ (Sun et al., 2022), which was originally developed for
345   out-of-distribution detection. Finally, we also compare to anomaly detection using CLIP (Radford
346   et al., 2021; Liznerski et al., 2022), which detects anomalies by using a pretrained CLIP model
347   and comparing image embeddings with text embeddings describing the normal class. Apart from
348
      CLIP-AD, we conduct all experiments with the ResNet18 architecture (He et al., 2016) to ensure
      comparability between methods.

      et al., 2015; Hendrycks et al., 2019b), Dogs vs. Cats (Cukierski, 2013), and Muffin vs. Chihuahua

412
      classification setting, we typically work on multi-class classification datasets where we consider one
413
      of the classes as the normal class and the rest as anomalies. In particular, we train our model on
414   the training samples of the normal class and want to differentiate between unseen samples of this
415   normal class and all other classes at test time. Here, we train each model across three seeds for each
416   class of each dataset, reporting the mean and standard deviation of the resulting AUROCs.

      On natural images, the Equalize context augmentation does not satisfy distinctiveness, as this trans-
418
      formation often results in scenes that seem slightly differently illuminated (see Figure 2 for some
419
      examples). We thus only present results of C ON2 with Flip and Invert context augmentations. In
420   Section 4.1, we saw that the more efficient SLH anomaly score exhibits relatively strong performance,
421   however, SNND typically performs slightly better and we thus only report SNND in this section. Fur-
422   ther, we note that CLIP-AD exhibits a strong performance on natural image datasets as, during
423   training, CLIP has been exposed to samples that are similar to anomalies of this one-class classi-
424   fication setting. It is thus hard to compare CLIP-AD to our method and baselines, which were all
425   trained without outlier exposure, on natural image datasets and we thus do not compare to CLIP-AD
426   in this section. For completeness, we report the full results including including both scores, C ON2
427   with Equalize, and CLIP-AD results for all datasets in Appendix E.2.

      may not always fully satisfy the distinctiveness assumption.

471   5   C ONCLUSION
472
473   In this work, we presented a novel approach to anomaly detection, focusing on learning represen-
474   tations of normality by leveraging prior knowledge about invariances in the normal data rather than
475   simulating anomalous data as in previous works. Employing knowledge about the invariances of nor-
476
      mal data is more realistic and provides a stronger foundation for anomaly detection, particularly in
      specialized domains such as healthcare, where anomalous data is rare or hard to simulate accurately.
477
478   C ON2 learns dense, highly informative context clusters that capture the properties of normal data.
479   These clusters provide rich representations and ensure that a sample’s relative positioning is consis-
480   tent across clusters, strengthening the model’s ability to differentiate between normal and anomalous
481   data. This results in a more structured representation space, making our approach well-suited for
482
      anomaly detection tasks with our anomaly score functions.
483   We demonstrated the efficacy of our approach on two real-world medical imaging datasets, where
484   our method achieved impressive results. This highlights the applicability of C ON2 in safety-
485   critical applications where robust anomaly detection is essential. Additionally, our approach exhib-
      ited strong performance on natural imaging datasets, consistently outperforming baseline methods,


                                                        9
486
      demonstrating its versatility across different domains. Further, our method highlights the impor-
487   tance of domain-specific approaches in specialized fields like healthcare, where tailored models can
488   outperform foundation model-based approaches such as CLIP-AD, despite their success in more
489   general settings.
490
      In conclusion, C ON2 represents a significant advancement in anomaly detection by learning struc-
491
      tured representations of normal data without relying on anomalous data. This approach is partic-
492
      ularly valuable in specialized, high-stakes settings, offering robust and effective solutions across
493   various application domains.

761   A.1     C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING
762
763   Recently, contrastive learning has emerged as a popular approach for representation learning
764   (van den Oord et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020). By design, contrastive learning has the capability to
765   learn representations that are agnostic to certain invariances (von Kügelgen et al., 2021; Daunhawer
766   et al., 2023), which makes contrastive learning a particularly interesting choice to learn informative
767   representations of normal samples (Tack et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2023), as it allows us to incor-
768
      porate prior knowledge about our data into the representing learning process in the form of data
      augmentations. More specifically, invariances are learned by forming positive and negative pairs
769
      over the training dataset by applying data augmentations that should retain the relevant content of a
770
      sample.
771
772   The goal of contrastive learning is to learn an encoding function gθ (x) , where representations of
773   positive pairs of samples are close and negative pairs are far from each other. For a given pair of
774
      samples x, x′ ∈ X, we can define the instance discrimination loss as (Sohn, 2016; Wu et al., 2018;
      van den Oord et al., 2019)

800   A.2     A NOMALY D ETECTION
801
      In the anomaly detection setting, we are given an unlabeled dataset {x1 , . . . , xn } = X ⊂ X , while
802
      assuming that most samples are normal, i.e., the dataset is practically free of outliers (Ruff et al.,
803   2021). The goal is to learn a model from the given dataset that discriminates between normal and
804   anomalous data at test time.
805
806
      In this work, we assume the challenging case where our dataset is completely free of anomalies.
      Hence, we aim to discriminate between the normal class and a completely unobserved set of anoma-
807
      lies at test time. This setting is sometimes called one-class classification or novelty detection.
808
809   To achieve this goal, one straightforward approach is to approximate the distribution pX (x) directly
      using generative models (An & Cho, 2015; Schlegl et al., 2019). Because we assume normal data to

821   lie in high-density regions of pX , we can discriminate between normal and anomalous samples by
822   applying a threshold function pX (x) ≤ τ , where τ ∈ R is an often task-specific threshold (Bishop,
823   1994). As density-based approaches are often difficult to apply to high-dimensional data directly
824   (Nalisnick et al., 2018), we follow a slightly different line of work.
825   In this paper, we focus on learning a function gθ ∶ X → Z that provides us with representations that
826   capture the normal attributes of samples in the dataset (Sehwag et al., 2021; Tack et al., 2020; Wang
827   et al., 2023), by mapping normal samples close to each other in representation space. On the other
828   hand, anomalies that lack the learned normal structure should be mapped to a different part of the
829   representation space.
830
      Given gθ (x), a popular approach to detect anomalies is by defining a scoring function S ∶ Z → R
831   (Breunig et al., 2000; Schölkopf et al., 2001; Tax & Duin, 2004; Liu et al., 2008). The score function
832   maps a representation onto a metric that estimates the anomalousness of a sample. To identify
833   anomalies at test time, we can use S similarly to the density pX , i.e., we consider a new sample x
834   to be normal if S(gθ (x)) ≤ τ , whereas S(gθ (x)) > τ means x is an anomaly.

930   D     E XPERIMENTAL D ETAILS
931
932   Setting We evaluate our method in the so-called one-class classification setting (Ruff et al., 2021).
933   More specifically, during training we assume to have access to only the normal (healthy) class. At
934   test time, the goal is to detect whether a new sample stems from the normal class seen during training
935   or whether it seems anomalous, i.e., deviates from the training distribution.
936
937   Metrics Typically, there is a high-class imbalance between normal and anomalous samples in the
938   one-class classification setting. Further, setting an appropriate threshold for the anomaly score is
939   often task-dependent. Therefore, a popular approach to evaluating the performance of anomaly
940
      detection methods is to use the area under the receiver operator characteristic curve (AUROC) (Ruff
      et al., 2021). This metric is threshold agnostic and robust to class imbalance.
941
942   For all our experiments, we report mean and standard deviation over three seeds per class of the
943   dataset. Note that the average results of a dataset are aggregated over different one-class classifica-
944   tion settings, one per class of the dataset.
945
946   Hyperparameters Similar to our method, all baselines make use of test-time augmentations. By
947
      default, both CSI and UniCon-HA use 40 test time augmentations, which we adopt for all baselines.
      In our experiments, we set the augmentation class T to the set of augmentations introduced by Chen
948
      et al. (2020). For the context augmentation, we experiment with vertical flips (Flip), inverting the
949
      pixels of an image (Invert), i.e., tInvert (xij ) = 1 − xij , and histogram equalization (Equalize), see
950   Figure 2 for an illustration.
951
952   We choose hyperparameters for C ON2 based on their performance on the CIFAR10 dataset and keep
953
      them constant across all experiments. We linearly anneal the hyperparameter α in LC ON2 from 0 to 1
      over the course of training to encourage the model to first learn the context-specific cluster structure
954
      while gradually aligning representations over the course of training. We optimize our loss using the
955
      AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999, weight decay λ = 0.001,
956   and using a learning rate of 10−3 with a cosine annealing (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) schedule. We
957   run all experiments for 2048 epochs.
