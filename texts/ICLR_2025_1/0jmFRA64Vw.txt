011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Federated Learning (FL) has garnered increasing attention due to its unique char-
014            acteristic of allowing heterogeneous clients to process their private data locally
015
               and interact with a central server, while being respectful of privacy. A critical
               bottleneck in FL is the communication cost. A pivotal strategy to mitigate this
016
               burden is Local Training, which involves running multiple local stochastic gradi-
017
               ent descent iterations between communication phases. Our work is inspired by
018            the innovative Scaffnew algorithm of Mishchenko et al. (2022), which has con-
019            siderably advanced the reduction of communication complexity in FL. We intro-
020            duce FedComLoc (Federated Compressed and Local Training), integrating prac-
021            tical and effective compression into Scaffnew to further enhance communication
022            efficiency. Extensive experiments, using the popular TopK compressor and quan-
023            tization, demonstrate its prowess in substantially reducing communication over-
024            heads in heterogeneous settings.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Privacy concerns and limited computing resources on edge devices often make centralized train-
029   ing impractical, where all data is gathered in a data center for processing. In response to these
030   challenges, Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as an increasingly popular framework (McMahan
031   et al., 2016; Kairouz et al., 2019). In FL, multiple clients perform computations locally on their
032   private data and exchange information with a central server. This process is typically framed as an
033   empirical risk minimization problem (Shalev-Shwartz & Ben-David, 2014):

037   where fi represents the local objective for client i, n is the total number of clients, and x is the model
038   to be optimized. Our primary objective is to solve the problem (ERM) and deploy the optimized
039   global model to all clients. For instance, x might be a neural network trained in an FL setting.
040   However, a considerable bottleneck in FL are communication cost, particularly with large models.
041
      To mitigate these costs, FL often employs Local Training (LT), a strategy where local parameters
042   are updated multiple times before aggregation (Povey et al., 2014; Moritz et al., 2016; McMahan
043   et al., 2017; Li et al., 2020; Haddadpour & Mahdavi, 2019; Khaled et al., 2019; 2020; Karimireddy
044   et al., 2020; Gorbunov et al., 2020; Mitra et al., 2021). However, there is a lack of theoretical
045   understanding regarding the effectiveness of LT methods. The recent introduction of Scaffnew by
046   Mishchenko et al. (2022) marked a substantial advancement, as this algorithm converges to the exact
047   solution with accelerated complexity, in convex settings.
048   Another approach to reducing communication costs is through compression (Haddadpour et al.,
049   2021; Condat et al., 2022; Yi et al., 2024). In centralized training, one often aims to learn a sparsified
050   model for faster training and communication efficiency (Dettmers & Zettlemoyer, 2019; Kuznedelev
051   et al., 2023). Dynamic pruning strategies like gradual magnitude pruning (Gale et al., 2019) and
052   RigL (Evci et al., 2020) are common. But in FL, the effectiveness of these model sparsification
053   methods based on thresholds remains unclear. The work by Babakniya et al. (2023) considers FL
      sparsity mask concepts, showing promising results.

054
      Quantization is another efficient model compression technique (Han et al., 2021; Bhalgat et al.,
055   2020; Shin et al., 2023), though its application in heterogeneous settings is limited. Gupta et al.
056   (2022) introduced FedAvg with Kurtosis regularization (Chmiel et al., 2020) in FL.
057
058
      Furthermore, studies such as Haddadpour et al. (2021); Condat et al. (2022) have theoretical con-
      vergence guarantees for unbiased estimators with restrictive assumptions. As this work employs the
059
      biased TopK compressor these are unsuitable in this case.
060
061   Thus, we tackle the following question:
062   Is it possible to design an efficient algorithm combining accelerated local training with compression
063   techniques, such as quantization and Top-K, and validate its efficiency empirically on popular FL
064   datasets?
065
      Our method for investigating this question consists of two steps. Firstly, we design an algorithm,
066
      termed FedComLoc, which integrates general compression into ScaffNew, an accelerated local train-
067
      ing algorithm. Secondly, we empirically validate FedComLoc for popular compression techniques
068   (TopK and quantization) on popular FL datasets (FedMNIST and FedCIFAR10).
069
070   We were able to answer this question affirmatively with the following contributions:
071
                • We have developed a communication-efficient method FedComLoc for distributed train-
072
                  ing, specifically designed for heterogeneous environments. This method integrates general
073               compression techniques and is motivated by previous theoretical insights.
074
075
                • We proposed three variants of our algorithm addressing several key bottlenecks in FL:
                  FedComLoc-Com addresses communication costs from client to server, FedComLoc-Global
076
                  addresses communication costs from server to client and FedComLoc-Local addresses lim-
077
                  ited computational resources on edge devices.
078
079
                • We conducted detailed comparisons and ablation studies, validating the effectiveness of our
                  approach. These reveal a considerable reduction in communication and, in certain cases,
080
                  an enhancement in training speed in number of communication rounds. Furthermore, we
081
                  demonstrated that our method outperforms well-established baselines in terms of training
082               speed and communication costs.

087   2.1       L OCAL T RAINING
088
      The evolution of LT in FL has been profound and continuous, transitioning through five distinct
089
      generations, each marked by considerable advancements from empirical discoveries to reductions in
090
      communication complexity. The pioneering FedAvg algorithm (McMahan et al., 2017) represents
091   the first generation of LT techniques, primarily focusing on empirical evidence and practical appli-
092   cations (Povey et al., 2014; Moritz et al., 2016; McMahan et al., 2017). The second generation of LT
093   methods consists in solving (ERM) based on homogeneity assumptions such as bounded gradients1
094   (Li et al., 2020) or limited gradient diversity2 (Haddadpour & Mahdavi, 2019). However, the practi-
095   cality of such assumptions in real-world FL scenarios is debatable and often not viable (Kairouz et
096   al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021).
097   Third-generation methods made fewer assumptions, demonstrating sublinear (Khaled et al., 2019;
098   2020) or linear convergence up to a neighborhood (Malinovsky et al., 2020) with convex and smooth
099   functions. More recently, fourth-generation algorithms like Scaffold (Karimireddy et al., 2020), S-
100   Local-GD (Gorbunov et al., 2020), and FedLin (Mitra et al., 2021) have gained popularity. These
101   algorithms effectively counteract client drift and achieve linear convergence to the exact solution
102   under standard assumptions. Despite these advances, their communication complexity mirrors that
103   of GD, i.e. O(κ log ϵ−1 ), where κ := L/µ denotes the condition number.
104   The most recent Scaffnew algorithm, proposed by Mishchenko et al. (2022), revolutionizes the field
                                                       √

      2.2   M ODEL C OMPRESSION IN F EDERATED L EARNING
147
      Model compression in the context of FL is a burgeoning field with diverse research avenues, par-
148
      ticularly focusing on the balance between model efficiency and performance. Jiang et al. (2022)
149
      innovated in global pruning by engaging a single, powerful client to initiate the pruning process.
150   This strategy transitions into a collaborative local pruning phase, where all clients contribute to an
151   adaptive pruning mechanism. This involves not just parameter elimination, but also their reintro-
152   duction, integrated with the standard FedAvg framework (McMahan et al., 2016). However, this
153   approach demands substantial local memory for tracking the relevance of each parameter, a con-
154   straint not always feasible in FL settings.
155
      Addressing some of these challenges, Huang et al. (2022) introduced an adaptive batch normaliza-
156   tion coupled with progressive pruning modules, enhancing sparse local computations. These ad-
157   vancements, however, often do not fully address the constraints related to computational resources
158   and communication bandwidth on the client side. Our research primarily focuses on magnitude-
159   based sparsity pruning. Techniques like gradual magnitude pruning (Gale et al., 2019) and RigL
160   (Evci et al., 2020) have been instrumental in dynamic pruning strategies. However, their applica-
161   tion in FL contexts remains relatively unexplored. The pioneering work of Babakniya et al. (2023)
      extends the concept of sparsity masks in FL, demonstrating noteworthy outcomes.


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Quantization is another vital avenue in model compression. Seminal works in this area include Han
163   et al. (2021), Bhalgat et al. (2020), and Shin et al. (2023). A major advance has been made by Gupta
164   et al. (2022), who combined FedAvg with Kurtosis regularization (Chmiel et al., 2020). We are
165   looking to go even further by integrating accelerated LT with quantization techniques.
166
      However, a gap exists in the theoretical underpinnings of these compression methods. Research
167
      by Haddadpour et al. (2021) and Condat et al. (2022) offers theoretical convergence guarantees for
168
      unbiased estimators, but these frameworks are not readily applicable to common compressors like
169   Top-K sparsifiers. In particular, CompressedScaffnew (Condat et al., 2022) integrates an unbiased
170   compression mechanism in Scaffnew, that is based on random permutations. But due to requiring
171   shared randomness it is not practical. Linear convergence has been proved when all functions fi are
172   strongly convex.
173
      To the best of our knowledge, no other compression mechanism has been studied in Scaffnew, ei-
174
      ther theoretically or empirically, and even the mere convergence of Scaffnew in nonconvex settings
175   has not been investigated either. Our goal is to go beyond the convex setting and simplistic logistic
176   regression experiments and to study compression in Scaffnew in realistic nonconvex settings with
177   large datasets such as Federated CIFAR and MNIST. Our integration of compression in Scaffnew is
178   heuristic but backed by the findings and theoretical guarantees of CompressedScaffnew in the con-
179   vex setting, which shows a twofold acceleration with respect to the conditioning κ and the dimension
180   d, thanks to LT and compression, respectively.

      4                        E XPERIMENTS
251
252
      Baselines. Our evaluation comprises three distinct aspects. Firstly, we conduct experiments to
      assess the impact of compression on communication costs. FedComLoc is assessed for varying
253
      sparsity and quantization ratios. Secondly, we compare FedComLoc-Com with FedComLoc-Local
254
      and FedComLoc-Global. Thirdly, we explore the efficacy of FedComLoc against non-accelerated
255   local training methods, including FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2016), its Top-K sparsified counterpart
256   sparseFedAvg, and Scaffold (Karimireddy et al., 2020).
257
258
      Datasets. Our experiments are conducted on FedMNIST (LeCun, 1998) and FedCI-
259   FAR10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) with the data processing framework FedLab (Zeng et al., 2023).
260   For FedMNIST, we employ MLPs with three fully-connected layers, each coupled with a ReLU
261   activation function. For FedCIFAR10, we utilize CNNs with two convolutional layers and three
262   fully-connected layers. Comprehensive statistics for each dataset, details on network architecture
263   and training specifics can be found in Appendix A.
264
265   Heterogeneous Setting. We explore different heterogeneous settings. Similar to (Zhang et al.,
266   2023; Yi et al., 2024), we create heterogeneity in data by using a Dirichlet distribution, which assigns
267   each client a vector indicating class preferences. This vector guides the unique selection of labels
268   and images for each client until all data is distributed. The Dirichlet parameter α indicates the level
269   of non-identical distribution. We also include a visualization of this distribution for the CIFAR10
      dataset in Appendix B.1.1.

      4.6   F ED C OM L OC VARIANTS
433
434   In this section, we compare FedComLoc-Local, FedComLoc-Com, and FedComLoc-Global on Fed-
435   CIFAR10. The findings are illustrated in Figure 9. Observe that at high levels of sparsity (indicated
436   by a small K in TopK), FedComLoc-Com underperforms the other algorithms. This could be at-
437   tributed to the heterogeneous setting of our experiment: each client’s model output is inherently
438   skewed towards its local dataset. When this is coupled with extreme TopK sparsification, more bias
439   is introduced, which adversely affects performance. Conversely, at low sparsity (e.g. K = 90%),
      FedComLoc-Com surpasses FedComLoc-Global. In addition, we observe that sparsity during local
440
      training (i.e. FedComLoc-Local) tends to yield better results. One possible explanation is that due
441
      to the local data bias the communication bandwidth between client and server might be crucial. Re-
442
      member that FedComLoc-Local had no communication compression while both FedComLoc-Com
443   and FedComLoc-Global do. Further FedMNIST results are shown in the Appendix.

      This paper advances the field of FL by tackling one of its main challenges, namely its high com-
      munication cost. Building on the accelerated Scaffnew algorithm (Mishchenko et al., 2022), we
461
      introduced FedComLoc. This novel approach blends the practical compression techniques of model
462
      sparsity and quantization into the efficient local training framework. Our extensive experimental
463   validation shows that FedComLoc preserves computational integrity while notably cutting commu-
464   nication costs.
465
466
      Future research could explore the reduction of internal variance in stochastic gradient estimation,
      akin to the approach described in Malinovsky et al. (2022). The FedComLoc-Global algorithm we

      propose offers potential for obtaining a sparsified model suitable for deployment. Additionally,
468
      investigating the development of an efficiently sparsified deployed model extensively presents an
469   intriguing avenue for further study.

650   A.1                     DATASETS AND M ODELS
651
652   Our research primarily focuses on evaluating the effectiveness of our proposed methods and var-
653   ious baselines on widely recognized FL datasets. These include Federated MNIST (FedMNIST)
654   and Federated CIFAR10 (FedCIFAR10), which are benchmarks in the field. The use of the terms
655   FedMNIST and FedCIFAR10 is intentional to distinguish our federated training approach from the
656   centralized training methods typically used with MNIST and CIFAR10. The MNIST dataset consists
657
      of 60,000 samples distributed across 100 clients using a Dirichlet distribution. For this dataset, we
      employ a three-layer Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) as our default model. CIFAR10, also compris-
658
      ing 60,000 samples, is utilized in our experiments to conduct various ablation studies. The default
659
      setting for our FedCIFAR10 experiments is set with 10 clients. The model chosen for CIFAR10 is
660   a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) consisting of 2 convolutional layers and 3 fully connected
661   layers (FCs). The network architecture is chosen in alignment with (Zeng et al., 2023).
662
663   A.2                     T RAINING D ETAILS
664
665   Our experimental setup involved the use of NVIDIA A100 or V100 GPUs, allocated based on their
666   availability within our computing cluster. We developed our framework using PyTorch version
667   1.4.0 and torchvision version 0.5.0, operating within a Python 3.8 environment. The FedLab frame-
668
      work (Zeng et al., 2023) was employed for the implementation of our code. For the FedMNIST
      dataset, we established the default number of global iterations at 500, whereas for the FedCIFAR10
669
      dataset, this number was set at 2500. We conducted a comprehensive grid search for the optimal
670
      learning rate, exploring values within the range of [0.005, 0.01, 0.05, 0.1]. Our intention is to make
671   the code publicly available upon the acceptance of our work.

700   The Dirichlet non-iid model serves as our primary means to simulate realistic FL scenarios.
701   Throughout this paper, we extensively explore the effects of varying the Dirichlet factor α and ex-
      amine how our algorithms perform under different degrees of data heterogeneity. In Figure 10, we

      present a visualization of the class distribution in the FedCIFAR10 dataset. We visualize the first 10
703   clients. This illustration clearly demonstrates that a smaller α results in greater data heterogeneity,
704   with α = 1000 approaching near-homogeneity. To further our investigation, we conduct thorough
705   ablation studies using values of α in the range of [0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9, 1.0]. It is important to note
706   that an α value of 1.0, while on the higher end of our test spectrum, still represents a heterogeneous
