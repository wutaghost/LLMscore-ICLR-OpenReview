011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Video Highlight Detection and Moment Retrieval (HD/MR) are essential in video
014            analysis. Recent joint prediction transformer models often overlook cross-task dy-
015            namics and video-text alignment. We propose VideoLights, a novel HD/MR
016            framework addressing these limitations through: (i) Convolutional Projection
017            and Feature Refinement modules with an intermodal alignment loss for better
018            video-text feature alignment. (ii) Bi-Directional Cross-Modal Fusion network for
019
               strongly coupled query-aware clip representations. (iii) Uni-Directional joint-task
               feedback mechanism enhancing both tasks through correlation. In addition, we in-
020
               troduce hard positive/negative losses for adaptive error penalization and improved
021
               learning. Our approach includes intelligent pretraining and finetuning using syn-
022            thetic data and features from various encoders. Comprehensive experiments on
023            QVHighlights, TVSum, and Charades-STA benchmarks demonstrate state-of-the-
024            art performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The surge in digital devices, platforms, and internet usage has led to abundant online video con-
031   tent (Apostolidis et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2017). However, navigating through such vast content poses
032   an exceedingly difficult challenge for users, impeding their ability to pinpoint specific points of in-
033
      terest within recordings (Anne Hendricks et al., 2017; Apostolidis et al., 2021). Consequently, Video
      Highlight Detection (HD; (Badamdorj et al., 2022; Mahasseni et al., 2017; Wei et al., 2022; Zhang
034
      et al., 2016)) and Moment Retrieval (MR; (Anne Hendricks et al., 2017; Gao et al., 2017; Liu et al.,
035
      2015; Escorcia et al., 2022)), which evaluate saliency scores of video clips and automatically identify
036   significant moments (i.e., clips with the highest saliency scores) for user queries, respectively, have
037   become indispensable tools in video analysis—streamlining content management, recommendation,
038   creation, editing, and event detection processes. Given their shared objective of ranking/localizing
039   the relevant video clips based on user queries and the commonality in their multi-modal models and
040   data properties, recent studies using transfer learning have begun to jointly model Video Highlight
041   Detection and Moment Retrieval (HD/MR) (Lei et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023; Moon
042   et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Jang et al., 2023).
043   Joint HD/MR prediction requires understanding of text-video modalities and their cross-modal and
044   cross-task synergies. Most approaches undermine either cross-task or cross-modal dynamics, lim-
045   iting potential gains. Moment-DETR (Lei et al., 2021) uses concatenated pre-trained features.
046   UMT (Liu et al., 2022) augments audio inputs but uses isolated features. QD-DETR (Moon et al.,
047   2023) aligns text with video. MH-DETR (Xu et al., 2023) introduces a cross-modality interaction.
048   UniVTG (Lin et al., 2023) presents multi-task learning. These methods lack cross-task interactions.
049   TaskWeave (Yang et al., 2024) and TR-DETR (Sun et al., 2024b) address bidirectional cross-task
050
      relations, but have limitations in cross-modal dynamics. We propose VideoLights, a framework
      that leverages cross-modal and cross-task interactions through these core modules and principles:
051
052         1. Feature Refinement and Alignment (FRA) Module: Implements CNN-based intramodal
053            and intermodal feature interaction and refinement, with intermodal alignment loss for text-
               video correspondence.

            2. Bi-Directional Cross-Modal Fusion (Bi-CMF) Network: Employs a multi-stage hier-
055            archical process for bidirectional text-video attention, yielding a strongly coupled query-
056            aware clip representation.
057         3. Unidirectional Joint-Task Feedback Mechanism (Uni-JFM): Enhances task correlation
058            through task-specific and task-coupled losses, utilizing cosine similarity on feature vectors
059            from HD and MR, improving cross-task learning efficiency.
060         4. Adaptive Error Correction: Incorporates hard positive and hard negative losses to adap-
061            tively penalize model errors in clip saliency prediction, fostering improved learning.
062         5. Intelligent Model Pre-training: Utilizes synthetic data generated from video corpora and
063
               language-image models to create high-quality paired text queries for model pre-training.
064   We perform comprehensive evaluations on widely recognized benchmarks QVHighlights (Lei et al.,
065   2021), TVSum (Song et al., 2015), and Charades-STA (Gao et al., 2017). Results show that in
066   both tasks, VideoLights achieves strong performance, outperforming all previous baselines by
067   a significant margin (an average of 1.4% in QVHighlights, 0.7% in TVSum, and 0.3 in Charades-
068   STA) and achieving their new state-of-the-art results. We also provide an in-depth ablation study
069
      of our model on the QVHighlights development set, visualize the qualitative examples, and analyze
      the effects of different synthetic pretraining corpus and the impact of feature ensembles. We will
070
      open-source our implementation accordingly.

072   2   R ELATED W ORK
073
074   Moment retrieval (MR) and highlight detection (HD) are related video understanding tasks. MR re-
075   trieves video segments matching natural language queries, while HD identifies salient frames. MR
076   approaches include two-stage (Anne Hendricks et al., 2017; Hendricks et al., 2018; Gao et al., 2017;
077   Zeng et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2020b; Xiao et al., 2021b) and one-stage methods (Chen et al., 2018;
078   Liu et al., 2020; Qu et al., 2020; Ning et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2019; Zhao et al.,
079
      2021; Xiao et al., 2021a; Liu et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2020b; 2021; Wang et al., 2021; Zhang et al.,
      2020a; Mun et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021; Zeng et al., 2020)(Liu et al., 2023). Recent advance-
080
      ments in MR and HD utilize transformer-based architectures(Vaswani et al., 2017). DETR (Carion
081
      et al., 2020) simplifies predictions by eliminating anchor generation and non-maximum suppression.
082   Moment-DETR (Lei et al., 2021) introduced the QVHighlights dataset for concurrent HD/MR, ex-
083   celling at identifying query-relevant moments and saliency scores. UMT (Liu et al., 2022) proposed
084   a unified multimodal architecture for MR and HD but removed the moment decoder and bipartite
085   matching, resulting in inferior MR performance. Other approaches include TVT (Lei et al., 2020),
086   which used subtitles, and FVMR (Gao & Xu, 2021), which improved inference speed. This paper
087   develops a joint prediction HD/MR model focusing on cross-modal and cross-task interplays.
088   Cross-modal learning integrates information from different modalities, as explored in models
089   like TERAN (Messina et al., 2021), HGSPN (Hu et al., 2019), AVS (Morgado et al., 2020),
090   and (Badamdorj et al., 2021). Unloc (Yan et al., 2023) uses CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) for text-to-
091   video attention in a single-stage model for multiple tasks. Our approach employs bi-directional text-
092   video interactions with cross-task supervision. Recent works Sun et al. (2024b); Xiao et al. (2023);
093   Moon et al. (2024) focus on feature alignment and refinement, with Yang et al. (2024) and Sun
094   et al. (2024b) emphasizing HD-MR task interrelation. Moon et al. (2024) explores intermodality
095   correlation for joint MR and HD.
096   Recent studies have explored weakly supervised pretraining with multimodal data, improving model
097   performance (Lei et al., 2021; Xiao et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2023).
098   Some use ASR captions as query text (Lei et al., 2021; Xiao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022). (Yan
099   et al., 2023) pretrained their CLIP backend on Kinetics-700(Carreira et al., 2022) before fine-tuning.
100   UniVTG (Lin et al., 2023) combined Ego4D (Grauman et al., 2022) and VideoCC (Nagrani et al.,
101   2022) datasets. (Jung et al., 2022) generated two types of captions: from subtitles and visual
102
      information. In text-only contexts, (Parvez et al., 2023) demonstrated enhanced supervision by
      combining different encoders.

134   3.1   M ODEL OVERVIEW
135
      Highlight Detection (HD) and Moment Retrieval (MR) aim to estimate the saliency of video clips
136
      and identify significant moments for a given text query. Given a video of L clips, we define the
137   video frames as F ∈ RL×3×W ×H , where W and H denote the width and height of the video, and
138   3 represents the number of color channels. The feature representation of the video is denoted as
139   V ∈ RL×dv , where dv is the feature dimension extracted by a frozen video encoder. Given a text
140   query of N tokens, the representation of the text is denoted as T ∈ RN ×dt , where dt is the feature
141   dimension extracted by a frozen text encoder. With these representations and given the video and
142   the text, our goal is twofold: for Moment Retrieval (MR), we aim to determine all the moments
143   M ∈ R2×m , where each moment consists of a central coordinate mc and width mσ , identifying m
144   such moments within the video. For Highlight Detection (HD), we aim to rank the saliency scores
145
      S ∈ RL for each clip in the video to detect highlights.
146   Embeddings: We compute the initial feature sets V and T from multiple different VLPs as follows:
147

152
      CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023), and Slow-Fast models (Feichtenhofer et al.,
      2019) respectively.

155   dimensions can make merging challenging. We address this issue by aligning the feature dimension-

157   convolution layers. After this step, V ∈ RL×dv becomes V ∈ RL×d and T ∈ RN ×dt becomes

      3.3   B I -D IRECTIONAL C ROSS -M ODAL F USION N ETWORK : B I -CMF
251
252   To learn a strongly coupled, query-oriented video representation, we introduce our Bi-Directional
253   Cross-Modal Fusion Network, Bi-CMF.
254
      It features three multihead attention layers for cross-attention and one for self-attention. Initially, a
255
      cross-attention layer uses projected video features as queries, while text data with positional embed-
256   ding serves as keys and values, identifying video tokens conditioned by textual tokens.
257
258   Similarly, another cross-attention layer is utilized to discern projected textual tokens (query) fetau-
259
      res conditioned by video tokens, fused with positional embedding (keys and values), enabling the
      identification of textual features pertinent to the video.
260
261   Subsequently, conditioned video tokens are used as queries, while conditioned textual tokens serve
262   as keys and values in the final cross-attention layer, yielding fused contextual information that em-
263   phasizes video tokens relevant to the query. Further refinement is achieved through a self-attention
264   mechanism applied to this fused context, allowing for the extraction of more nuanced video context.

      3.4   A DAPTIVE L OSS F UNCTIONS
271
272   We aim to enhance learning by identifying and rectifying persistent model errors. To achieve this,
273   we design novel adaptive loss functions, specifically targeting hard positives and hard negatives. For
274   the hard negative loss, we minimize the number of predictions in the negative regions where there are
275   no relevant clips. Given the saliency score S̄i and the ground truth saliency score Si for non-relevant
276   clips i ∈ Vneg , we define the loss, Lhardneg = Wj Σi∈Vneg abs(Si − S̄i ), where Wj is a function of
277   the jth epoch that penalizes more with a higher number of epochs. As in general, Si for i ∈ Vneg
278   is zero, the loss can be defined as: Lhardneg = Wj Σi∈Vneg abs(S̄i ). For hard positive cases, we use
279   Mean Square Error, and similarly, we define the loss as: Lhardneg = Wj Σi∈Vpos M SE(Si , S̄i ).

      3.6   P RETRAINING
295
296
      We propose a novel multi-step methodology to enhance attention-based networks’ performance by
297   addressing limitations in ASR caption-based weakly supervised training (Lei et al., 2021; Xiao et al.,
298   2023). Our approach segments videos into 10-second intervals, generates descriptive captions using
299   the BLIP model for representative frames, and creates synthetic data pairs from QVHighlights and
300   Charades-STA datasets. Saliency scores are calculated based on frame-query similarity, and the
301   resulting caption-query pairs are used for model training. While this process may generate noisy
302   pretrain data, the subsequent finetuning helps filter out irrelevant information, leading to improved
303   generalization (Wu et al., 2022). Detailed data statistics and steps are provided in Appendix Table 5
304   and Algorithm 1.

      follow previous wroks such as TR-DETR (Sun et al., 2024b), and use I3D (Carreira & Zisserman,
351
      2017) pre-trained on Kinetics 400 (Kay et al., 2017) for visual features. We used a hidden unit
352
      size of d = 256, two Bi-CMF layers, three encoder layers, three decoder layers, seed value 2018,
353   and 10-moment queries. We added a dropout rate of 0.1 for the transformer layers and 0.5 for
354   the input projection layers (Lei et al., 2021). Loss hyperparameters were assigned as λL1 = 10,
355   λgIoU = 1, λcls = 4, λsal = 1, λrank = 1, λcont = 1, and ∆ = 0.2. We also initialized the model
356   weights using the Xavier initialization (Glorot & Bengio, 2010) and tuned the model parameters
357   with AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019), using an initial learning rate of 1e-4 and a weight decay
358   of 1e-4. Following (Lei et al., 2021), we trained the model for 200 epochs with a batch size of 32.
359   For Charades-STA and TVSum, we have used a batch size of 32 and 4, respectively, with learning
360   rates 1e-4 and 1e-3 each. See Table 6 in the appendix for details about the parameters that changed
361
      in different experiments. For all experiments, we use T4, and RTX 3050 Ti GPUs.

521   5    L IMITATION AND C ONCLUSION
522
523   Conclusion In this paper, we propose a novel joint prediction model for highlight detection and
524   moment retrieval, VideoLights. It features a feature refinement and alignment module, a bi-
525
      directional cross-modal and uni-directional cross-task feedback mechanism. Our custom cross-
      modal interaction module enhances the ability to understand intermodal relationships between text
526
      and video, resulting in superior content retrieval and highlight identification performance. Our ex-
527
      periments on the QVHighlights and TVSum datasets have shown that our approach outperforms
528   current techniques and has fewer learning parameters, indicating efficiency and scalability. Our
529   contributions set the stage for future research in video content analysis. demonstrating the potential
530   of integrating advanced language and vision models to tackle real-world challenges in multimedia
531   content processing.
532
      Limitation Our proposal for weakly supervised training utilizing vision-language pretraining mod-
533
      els simplifies the training process but may still be prone to biases or inaccuracies in caption gener-
534   ation. At the same time, our dependency on pretraining models for caption generation and feature
535   extraction can lead to computational overhead and reliance on external resources, thus potentially
536   limiting the scalability of our approach. Moreover, the performance of our Bi-CMF module is heav-
537   ily reliant on the quality of input features and the effectiveness of attention mechanisms, both of
538   which can vary depending on the complexity and diversity of the video content. To fully unlock the
539   potential of our proposed approach in real-world applications, it is crucial to address these limita-
      tions through further research and refinement.

836    2: For each 10-second interval, select a representative frame and use the BLIP model to generate a

842    5: Train the model using the generated caption-query pair, considering the entire 10-second interval
843       as a moment for training purposes.

846   A.2     A DDITIONAL ABLATION ON B I -CMF
847
      Our research findings indicate that integrating the Bi-CMF module into our model significantly im-
848
      proves performance in MR and HD tasks compared to the model without this module. In addition,
849   we conducted further ablation studies to evaluate the impact of different Bi-CMF layer counts on the
850   model. The results, outlined in Table 8, show that while one and two layers demonstrate similar per-
851   formance in both MR and HD metrics, the introduction of three layers enhances MR performance
852   but decreases performance in HD tasks. Furthermore, as the number of layers increases, perfor-
853   mance on both tasks across all metrics decreases. The impact on MR performance, particularly in
854   MR-full-mAP, is illustrated in Figure 6.
855   We also performed additional experiments to assess the effectiveness of bi-CMF compared to uni-
856   directional cross-attention. In this experiment, we replaced our bi-CMF with a unidirectional cross-
857   attention module while keeping all other parameters constant. The results are presented in Table 9.
858   We observed that across all metrics in the MR task, Bi-CMF demonstrated a notable improvement
859   over unidirectional cross-attention.
