009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The growing demand for AI-driven workloads, particularly from Large Language
012            Models (LLMs), has raised concerns about the significant energy and resource
013            consumption in data centers. This work introduces a novel LLM-based predictive
014            scheduling system designed to enhance operational efficiency while reducing the
015            environmental impact of data centers. Our system utilizes an LLM to predict key
016            metrics such as execution time and energy consumption from source code, and it
017
               has the potential to extend to other sustainability-focused metrics like water usage
               for cooling and carbon emissions, provided the data center can track such data.
018
               The predictive model is followed by a real-time scheduling algorithm that allo-
019
               cates GPU resources, aiming to improve sustainability by optimizing both energy
020            consumption and queuing delays. With fast inference times, the ability to gener-
021            alize across diverse task types, and minimal data requirements for training, our
022            approach offers a practical solution for data center scheduling. This framework
023            demonstrates strong potential for advancing sustainability objectives in AI-driven
024            infrastructure. Through our collaboration with a data center, we achieved a 32%
025            reduction in energy consumption and a 30% decrease in waiting time.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
      The rapid advancement of Machine Learning (ML), especially Large Language Models (LLMs), has
030
      brought about groundbreaking capabilities, yet it has also raised significant social and environmen-
031
      tal concerns (Pichai, 2024; Amazon, 2024; Nakagawa & Smith, 2023). One of the most pressing
032   issues is the substantial energy and water resources consumed during ML model training and serv-
033   ing, which has sparked widespread concerns (Blunt & Hiller, 2024; Criddle & Bryan, 2024; Solon,
034   2021). The computationally intensive ML jobs can demand hundreds of GPU-hours and consume
035   substantial amounts of energy for power and water for cooling. As a result, the growing demand for
036   ML-based applications has significantly amplified the environmental impact of data centers, high-
037   lighting the need for modern infrastructure that is both more efficient and sustainable specifically
038   for AI-driven workloads (Bianchini et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Kaack et al., 2022). This paper aims
039   to enhance data center operation efficiency and reduce the environmental footprint of modern data
040
      centers by devising a LLM-based predictive scheduling system for allocating data center resources
      (e.g., GPUs) across a stream of ML jobs efficiently. We harness the contextual understanding and
041
      predictive abilities of LLMs and combine the predictive power to sequential decision-making that
042
      optimize data-center operations.

      The Prediction Problem. Consider a data center equipped with various types of GPUs, where
137   machine learning tasks arrive sequentially. The data center must decide which type of GPU to
138   allocate to each arriving task (noting that the number of GPUs is often specified in the user-submitted
139   code or command input). Let x represent the source code submitted by the user, and z represent the
140   data center’s decision, which corresponds to the type of GPU to allocate for executing the task. A
141   decision z results in outcomes including the time required to complete the task, denoted by t, and
142   the energy consumption using configuration z to complete task x, denoted by e. The relationship
143   between the outcomes and the decision is modeled by functions f and g, such that
144
                                          t := f (x, z),       e := g(x, z).
145
146   Here we note that this function f and g is data-center-specific, it depends on the infrastrcture of the
147   data center, for example, bandwith, CPUs, memory, storage, communication, system setup, software
148   stack, etc. In general, characterizing the functions f (x, z) and g(x, z) is highly challenging due to
149   the complexity of modern computing systems.
150   Prediction using Large Language Models. To tackle the prediction problem, we employ Large
151   Language Models (LLMs) (Ouyang et al., 2022; Radford et al., 2019; Achiam et al., 2023). For
152   simplicity, we refer readers to Vaswani et al. (2017) for a detailed explanation of decoder-based
153   Transformers and LLM architectures. In this paper, we use fθ and gθ as shorthand notations to
154   represent LLMs, where θ encapsulates all the model parameters. fθ and gθ take two inputs: the
155   task’s source code x and the data center’s decision z. The rationale is that the LLM can effectively
156   analyze and interpret the task’s source code, transforming it into meaningful representations; these
157   representations, when combined with the GPU configuration z, allow the model to produce estimates
158
      such that:
159                                fθ (x, z) ≈ f (x, z), gθ (x, z) ≈ g(x, z).
160
161   To justify our LLM-based approach, we highlight two key features of LLMs that make them partic-
      ularly appealing in our context:

      2.1   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE - P REDICTION
203
      In this section, we formally describe the architecture of our prediction model. Our approach utilizes
204   a pre-trained LLM for extracting source code representations and applies a probe (Alain, 2016;
205   Radford et al., 2017; Vulić et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022) to train a supervised model that predicts
206   the quantity of interest. Figure 3 illustrates the architecture and the different performance compared
207   to previous methods.
208
209          • Extracting Representations: We adopt a pre-trained LLM to extract task representations,
210            modeled as a function lθ1 (·) : V → Rd , where V denotes the space of source code and
211            Rd is the d-dimensional embedding space for mapped representations. Here, θ1 encapsu-
212            lates the LLM’s parameters. Note that lθ1 (·) refers to the LLM without its final linear and
213
               softmax layers, and for the last layer, it outputs the last token’s representation, which is a
               d-dimensional vector.
214
215          • Probing: Probing involves taking the generated representations and predicting the target
               value using a linear regression or shallow neural network. We denote this probe as hθ2 (·, ·) :


                                                             4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
               Rd × Z → R, where θ2 represents the parameters of the probe and Z denotes the decision
217            space (e.g., the types of GPUs).
218
219   Thus, our predictive model for the execution time can be represented as fθ (x, z) = hθ2 (lθ1 (x), z).
220   For the energy estimation function gθ (x, z), we use the same representation but train a separate
221   probe, denoted qθ2 , such that gθ (x, z) = qθ2 (lθ1 (x), z). Note that θ, θ1 , and θ2 refer to classes of
222   parameters, indicating that the parameter dimensions are the same across functions, though these
223   functions do not necessarily share the same parameters.
224   Advantage over Previous Methods. We identify the key factor that could possibly explain the
225   advantage of our method. As mentioned earlier, the challenging part of the data-center operations
226   arise from the complexity of modern computer systems. We first give a notation that characterize
227   the previous approaches.
228
229          • For estimating execution time t = f (x, z) and energy consumption e = g(x, z), previous
230            methods rely on handcrafted features, denoted by ut = lt (x) for time and ue = le (x)
231            for energy, where lt (·) and le (·) represent the feature extraction functions. Importantly, the
232
               design of lt (·) and le (·) varies significantly depending on the target variable and prediction
               context, making them different for each prediction task.
233
234          • Separate models are then trained for each target, such as ht (·, ·) for time and he (·, ·) for
235            energy, resulting in the following approximations: t ≈ ht (lt (x), z) and e ≈ he (le (x), z).
236
               To account for the dependence on z, which represents the GPU choices, several estima-
               tion techniques attempt to model the architecture differences between GPUs. While this
237
               approach makes sense physically, it suffers from a lack of flexibility and scalability. Mod-
238
               eling the dependence on z is typically restricted to specific types of source code x and GPU
239            choices z, rendering the models unable to generalize to unseen tasks or GPU configurations.
240
241   Our approach is better in terms of the architecture for a various reasons
242
243          • Better Representation. It has been demonstrated in various domains that representations
244            extracted by pre-trained models are significantly more effective than handcrafted features.
245            In this context, our prediction task can also be viewed as a form of feature extraction akin
246            to Natural Language Processing (NLP), where pre-trained models capture richer and more
247
               comprehensive information. These representations are more generalizable and universal,
               making them applicable to a wide variety of prediction tasks related to the characteristics
248
               of the source code.
249
250          • Data-Driven Predictive Modeling. Many traditional predictive models rely on the in-
251            trinsic characteristics of the underlying machine learning model, such as CNNs, where
252
               predictions are made by explicitly calculating factors like the number of forward/backward
               passes and the layer depth. While these “physical” models can achieve high accuracy when
253
               the test environment perfectly matches the assumptions, they often fall short in data-center
254
               operations. For instance, even tasks that train the same CNN architecture may exhibit vari-
255            ations in execution time due to differences in the way the code is written. Additionally,
256            many modern tasks involve training and inference across multiple models, alongside var-
257            ious other function implementations. In such settings, physically based predictive models
258            lack the flexibility and adaptability needed for accurate predictions. In contrast, our data-
259            driven approach is flexible enough to learn the inherent relationships between the source
260            code, the characteristics of computer systems and GPUs, and a variety of targeted metrics.
261          • Less Data Hungry. Traditional methods often require large amounts of source code ex-
262            amples to train predictive models effectively. In contrast, by leveraging a pretrained LLM,
263            which has already been trained on terabytes of data, we capitalize on the model’s superior
264            understanding of contextual knowledge. This allows us to obtain a highly effective fea-
265            ture extractor with far less training data, improving both data efficiency and performance.
266            Indeed, our predictive model is trained using only a little more than 500 source code ex-
267
               amples, each paired with corresponding results for energy consumption and execution time
               across different GPUs in the server.
268
269          • Generalization for GPU Dependence. Another advantage of our approach is its ability
               to maximize the generalization power of the LLM’s representation across different down-

270
                stream tasks. By utilizing the same representation for every prediction task and maintain-
271             ing a consistent structure for the downstream probe, our method facilitates the discovery
272             of patterns across different GPU configurations. This consistency preserves the model’s
273             generalization capacity, ensuring its adaptability to various GPU architectures. Such a fac-
274             tor is missing in previous methods, which followed a more restricted modeling approach,
275             limiting their flexibility and ability to generalize across diverse hardware setups.

      Constraints. Consider a data center equipped with |Z| = M types of GPUs, where tasks i with
312   source code xi arrive sequentially in time, and the data center must decide which GPU type zi ∈ Z
313   to allocate (notice that the task’s source code xi also specify its preference on GPUs, and we can
314   model this by constraining the action set Z). The GPU resources are limited, and is represented by
315   c = [c1 , · · · , cM ]⊤ , where cj is the total number of GPUs of type j. If no GPUs are available, the
316   task is placed in a waiting queue. The total available GPU resources at any time s are represented
317   by c(s) = [c1 (s), · · · , cM (s)]⊤ , where cj (s) is the available resource at time s for GPU type j.
318   Dynamics. Since all the tasks arrive sequentially, and the task arrival follows a stochastic process,
319   let us denote by N (T ) the total number of tasks arrived during time [0, T ], and for each task i, let
320   si represents the arrival time. If there are sufficient non-occupied GPUs available, and we assign
321   zi = j to task i, then aij number of correponding inventory will be temporary occupied, hence taken
322   out from cj (si ). Once aij of GPU j are assigned to xj , the task will be running for ti = f (xi , zi )
323   amount of time. If we denote by A(s) the active set that contains all tasks that are still running at
      time s, then i ∈ A(s) for s ∈ (si , si + ti ).

325   mediately processed due to insufficient GPU resources, it is placed in the set Q(s). If task xi has
326   been put in the queue, we denote by wi the time it spent in the queue, which depends on factors

406   3    OTHER E XTENSION
407
408   In this section, we discuss relevant extensions to our pipeline that align with data center practices.
409   Our prototype is designed using a pre-trained LLM with strong capabilities in code comprehension
410   and completion, coupled with a probe trained on 500 source codes. Although we are able to achieve
411   good performance with a relatively small dataset of 500 source codes, this approach still risks poor
412   out-of-sample performance. Due to variations in the coding practices of machine learning engi-
413   neers—such as differences in variable names, function abstractions, and comments—the contextual
414   information represented in the source code can vary significantly. Even if two codes are function-
415   ally equivalent, resulting in identical execution times and energy consumption, the representations
416
      generated by the LLM can be quite different.
417   This issue reflects an inconsistency problem. Formally, there may exist two tasks, x1 and x2 , such
418   that the representations differ noticeably (lθ1 (x1 ) ̸= lθ1 (x2 )), but the execution times (and energy
419   consumption) are identical: hθ2 (lθ1 (x1 ), z) = hθ2 (lθ1 (x2 ), z). We adopt another LLM, referred to
420   as the Align-LLM, to address this issue and mitigate potential out-of-distribution (OOD) estimation
421   errors, which could otherwise degrade model performance.
422   As illustrated in Figure 5, while data centers can gradually accumulate a code base to improve
423   prediction performance for both in-distribution and out-of-distribution tasks, we propose a novel
424   algorithm that partially resolves the OOD issue, showcasing the flexibility and practicality of our
425   pipeline. The architecture and experimental details can be found in appendix A.2
426   The extended architecture first takes the source code as input and extracts representations using the
427   pre-trained LLM. These representations are compared against representation clusters of the tasks
428   from the local codebase to assess whether the current task’s representation deviates significantly
429   from those in the training set. If not, the task is passed to the probe for estimation. Otherwise, the
430   Align-LLM assists by extracting key information from the source code, allowing the data center
431   to identify similar code from its codebase. Next, Align-LLM rewrites the source code based on the
      style of the similar sample. The rewritten code, although functionally identical to the original, adopts

460   We propose an LLM-based predictive scheduling system for data center operations aimed at en-

462   specialized in code comprehension to extract representations of source code and utilizes sequential
463   decision-making algorithms to optimize the scheduling of computational resources. Compared to

      in waiting times in real data centers. These results demonstrate the strong potential of our method
      in advancing AI infrastructure.

759   A.1   D ISCUSSION ON RELATED WORKS
760
761
      Prediction based on device features. A body of literature focuses on predicting relevant metrics,
      such as execution time and energy consumption, using features that summarize the characteristics
762
      of the hardware. This approach assumes that analyzing hardware parameters and runtime data can
763
      uncover patterns that influence these metrics. For example, Pham et al. (2017) combine GPU run-
764   time parameters with static hardware features, applying regression models to predict execution time.
765   Similarly, Daraghmeh et al. (2023) and Garg et al. (2023) utilize various clustering techniques to
766   identify operational patterns in machines based on hardware metrics, followed by sequence model-
767   ing for accurate predictions. Conversely, Hilman et al. (2018) take an alternative approach by first
768   predicting hardware behavior during code execution and then employing the KNN clustering method
769   to forecast execution time. In general, this stream of literature is not very related to our approach
770   and we refer the readers to O’Neal & Brisk (2018); Rodriguez et al. (2024); Ali et al. (2023) for
771   literature survey.
772   Prediction based on code features. Regarding code characteristics, early work by Huang et al.
773   (2010) used feature engineering, extracting elements like loop counts and conditional branches, and
774   applying sparse polynomial regression for time prediction. Recent approaches have shifted towards
775   deep learning, where two main strategies dominate. One approach treats models as composed of
776   atomic operations, with works like Wang & Cao (2015); Cai et al. (2017); Geoffrey et al. (2021);
777   Justus et al. (2018) using program slicing and MLPs to predict time and energy by decomposing
778
      models into layers. The second approach leverages graph-based techniques, as in Cao et al. (2021)
      and Bai et al. (2022), which use graphs to represent layer dependencies and employ machine learning
779
      to learn these representations. Some methods are similar to ours in extracting code representations
780
      for prediction. For example, Guerreiro et al. (2019) transforms PTX instructions into embeddings
781   for LSTM inputs, while Zhou et al. (2019) uses attention-based Bi-LSTMs and graph convolutional
782   networks to automatically extract code semantics and structure. While these methods focus on
783   extracting features, our model generalizes across a wider variety of task types and prediction metrics
784   using a unified, high-level representation approach. See Gianniti et al. (2018); Metz et al. (2022);
785   Zhao et al. (2013) for more related work.
786
      Data center operations. Energy management in data centers has been a longstanding area of inter-
787   est, with foundational work by Pinheiro et al. (2001); Chase et al. (2001); Ranganathan et al. (2006);
788   Fan et al. (2007). As cloud computing and AI technologies emerged, software-based approaches
789   have been developed to improve data center operations (Wu et al., 2016; Evans & Gao, 2016; Cortez
790   et al., 2017; Katal et al., 2023). In today’s AI and sustainability-driven era, there has been grow-
791   ing interest in carbon emissions related with AI (Lacoste et al., 2019; Anderson et al., 2023; Güğül
792   et al., 2023; Patel et al., 2024). However, most efforts focus on software or infrastructure-level oper-
793   ations, whereas our approach specifically targets algorithmic-level improvements. While there is a
794   substantial body of literature on optimal scheduling and queuing policies for service systems, much
795
      of this work is highly theoretical and relies on numerous assumptions (Vilaplana et al., 2014; Xie
      & Lu, 2015; Jafarnejad Ghomi et al., 2019). Additionally, many scheduling algorithms that avoid
796
      theoretical assumptions, such as reinforcement learning-based approaches (Ding et al., 2020; Tuli
797
      et al., 2020), primarily focus on scheduling tasks but lack task-level predictive inference capabilities,
798   which is a core strength of our method. Additionally, we note that our focus is specifically on the
799   operations of data centers for AI-driven workloads, which exhibit distinct characteristics compared
800   to traditional data center operations considered in previous studies.
801
      Predictive decision-making Our work is also related to the area of decision-making with future
802
      predictions as side information. With such predictions, well-established decision algorithms, orig-
803

      The Greedy algorithm focuses on a first-come-first-served approach to allocate tasks to GPUs. At
903
      each time step s, the algorithm estimates the running time and energy consumption for each task
904   i on each GPU type j using the prediction models. Once these estimates are made, the waiting
905   queue is sorted in ascending order of task arrival times. The algorithm assigns the first task in the
906   queue to the GPU type selected based on the smallest estimated value of αt̂1j + γê1j reflecting a
907   weighted combination of running time and energy consumption. This algorithm’s simplicity makes
908   it efficient for quick decision-making, but it may not always optimize resource allocation across the
909   entire queue.
910   The Value-Based algorithm extends beyond the Greedy approach by considering all tasks in the
911   waiting queue and selecting the GPU allocation based on a more strategic optimization. At each
912   time step the algorithm first estimates the running time and energy consumption for each task across
913   GPU types. However, the next step involves computing a cost value vij for each task-GPU pair,
914   which is a combination of the inverse of the estimated running time t̂ij adjusted by the GPU occu-
915   pancy requirement aij and a penalty term κêij representing the energy consumption. The algorithm
916   then constructs a set of task-GPU pairs, sorting them in descending order based on the value vij .
917   It allocates the GPUs to tasks based on this sorted list, prioritizing higher values, and updates the
      available GPU resources accordingly. This method is inspired by the algorithms in multiple knap-
