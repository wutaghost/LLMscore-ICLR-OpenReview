011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The nuanced differences in human behavior and the complex dynamics of human-
014            AI interactions pose significant challenges in optimizing human-AI cooperation.
015
               Existing approaches tend to oversimplify the problem and rely on a single global
               behavior model, which overlooks individual variability, leading to sub-optimal so-
016
               lutions. To bridge this gap, we introduce PHICO, a novel framework for human-
017
               AI cooperative classification that initially identifies a set of representative annota-
018            tor profiles characterized by unique noisy label patterns. These patterns are then
019            augmented to train personalised AI cooperative models, each tailored to an an-
020            notator profile. When these models are paired with human inputs that exhibit
021            similar noise patterns from a corresponding profile, they consistently achieve a
022            joint classification accuracy that exceeds those achieved by either AI or humans
023            alone. We theoretically prove the convergence of PHICO, ensuring the reliability
024            of the framework. To evaluate PHICO, we introduce novel measures for assess-
025            ing human-AI cooperative classification and empirically demonstrate its general-
026
               isability and performance across diverse datasets including CIFAR-10N, CIFAR-
               10H, Fashion-MNIST-H, AgNews, and Chaoyang histopathology. PHICO is both
027
               a model-agnostic and effective solution for improving human-AI cooperation.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Determining the optimal human-AI cooperation mechanism is challenging (Dafoe et al., 2021).
033   Humans bring experience and contextual insights but are prone to biases; machine learning mod-
034   els excel in specific tasks but lack contextual understanding and complex reasoning (Holstein &
035   Aleven, 2021). Many human-AI joint decision making strategies were proposed, e.g., learning to de-
036   fer (Raghu et al., 2019; Madras et al., 2018; Mozannar et al., 2023), learning to complement (Wilder
037   et al., 2021), human-in-the-loop (Wu et al., 2022), and algorithm-in-the-loop (Green & Chen, 2019),
038
      seeking to blend the best of human and AI for optimal decision-making.
039   We argue that effective human-AI joint decision-making depends on personalising machine learning
040   (ML) models to the individual’s behaviour patterns. While recent works have shown promising
041   progress in incorporating human behaviours through behaviour models (Vodrahalli et al., 2022) or
042   confusion matrices (Kerrigan et al., 2021), they rely on single global matrix and could not account
043   for the varied biases and preferences among annotators (Kocielnik et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021).
044   Indeed, learning individual behavior patterns is challenging, as each person’s data usually represents
045   only a small portion of the total, making it insufficient to train personalised AI models Johnson et al.
046   (2021). Beyond the scarcity of individual data, evaluating the effectiveness of various human-AI
047   cooperation frameworks also poses difficulties. Traditional metrics such as accuracy fail to cap-
048   ture whether the ML model’s alteration to human inputs improve or degrade performance, further
049   complicating the assessment of cooperation effectiveness Shneiderman (2022).
050   This paper addresses these research gaps with PHICO, a framework designed for personalised
051   human-AI cooperative classification to achieve optimal performance (Figure 1). More specifically,
052   given a training dataset with noisy labels from multiple annotators, PHICO first identifies a set of
053   annotator profiles, each characterized by distinct noisy labeling patterns. PHICO then augments
      these identified noisy label patterns to train personalised AI cooperative model, each optimized to

101   2         R ELATED W ORK
102
103   The conventional belief that automation lessens human control is under revision (Parasuraman et al.,
104
      2000; Committee, 2014), as the uncertainties of automation often demand more human involvement,
      leading to new human-AI collaboration strategies (Strauch, 2018). With AI models exceeding
105
      human accuracy in certain tasks, three new human-AI collaboration paradigms have emerged:
106
107   Learning-to-assist approaches aim to support human decision-making with AI model predictions
      (Straitouri et al., 2023). These approaches are commonly seen in critical domains, such as law (Liu


                                                                                     2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2021) and medicine (Levy et al., 2021), where humans make the final decision. Considerable
109   work has been done to improve model explainability and transparency. (Tjoa & Guan, 2021).
110
111   Learning-to-defer methods allow AI models to autonomously manage confident cases and defer
112
      decisions to humans when confidence is low (Madras et al., 2018; Mozannar et al., 2023; Alves
      et al., 2023). These approaches focus on the optimization of a utility function that takes into account
113
      the accuracy of the AI model, the preference of the human decision maker, and the cost of deferring
114
      decisions. For example, Raghu et al. (2019) used an ensemble of AI models to predict the risk of
115   patient death, and then defers decisions to a human expert for patients with the highest risk.
116
117   Learning-to-complement models are optimized to leverage the strengths from both human and
118
      AI model to improve decision-making. For example, Steyvers et al. (2022) proposed a Bayesian
      framework for modeling human-AI complementarity. Kerrigan et al. (2021) used a calibrated con-
119
      fusion matrix to combine human and model predictions in a way that minimizes the expected loss.
120
      Wilder et al. (2021) consider the uncertainty from AI models and humans to jointly train a model
121   that allocates tasks to the AI model or the human to maximize the overall accuracy.
122
123   PHICO falls into the category of learning-to-complement and aims to utilise complementary
124
      strengths of both humans and AI. Unlike other approaches that rely on a single behavior model
      or a global confusion matrix for the entire dataset, PHICO takes a step further by identifying biases
125
      among annotators and personalizing the human-AI cooperation to account for these unique biases.
126
127
128   2.1   E VALUATING H UMAN -AI C OOPERATION
129
130   Human-AI complementarity is defined by Dellermann et al. (2021) as leveraging the unique capa-
131   bilities of both humans and AI to achieve better results than each one could achieve alone. However,
132   assessing the interaction between humans and AI is complicated, and numerous benchmarks have
133   been suggested in existing literature. In the context of learning-to-assist or learning-to-complement,
134   traditional measures such as accuracy, precision, and recall are commonly used. For learning-to-
135   defer, measures such as coverage are proposed to evaluate the proportion of the data that is processed
136   by the model alone (Raghu et al., 2019). When dealing with noisy labels, additional measurements
137   such as label precision, label recall, and correction error are also used (Song et al., 2022a). As
138
      PHICO presents a new paradigm that combines decisions from humans and AI, we introduce new
      assessment measures to understand whether combination leads to better decisions.
139

      2.2   L EARNING FROM N OISY- LABEL (LNL) AND M ULTI - RATER L EARNING (MRL)
142
143
      PHICO draws insights from the LNL and MRL community. LNL aims to design algorithms that are
144   robust to the presence of noisy training labels. Recent advancements include DivideMix (Li et al.,
145   2020) with its semi-supervised approach, ELR (Liu et al., 2020) exploring early learning phenom-
146   ena, C2D (Zheltonozhskii et al., 2022) tackling the warm-up obstacle, and UNICON (Karim et al.,
147   2022) with a unified supervised and unsupervised learning to handle noisy labels effectively. MRL
148   trains models using noisy labels from multiple annotators per sample, which can mitigate the iden-
149   tifiability problem under certain conditions (Liu et al., 2023). Key developments include MRNet (Ji
150   et al., 2021), which addresses multi-rater disagreement, and Crowdlab (Goh et al., 2023), designed
151   to be model-agnostic. Despite improvements from LNL and MRL, an accuracy gap persists com-
152
      pared to training with clean labels. This has led to our personalized human-AI joint decision-making
      paradigm, which incorporates inputs from both humans and AI to make decisions.

161   the profiling and inference stages in Section 3.3. Section 3.4 presents our proposed metrics for
      assessing personalised human-AI cooperation.

246   3.3   U SER P ROFILING FOR P ERSONALISATION
247
248
      Once the models are trained, PHICO achieves personalisation during the testing by first matching
      the new user to one of the learned personalised AI cooperative models, after which they perform
249
      human-AI cooperative classification. The matching process, which we name user profiling, has two
250
      steps: 1) classifying the testing user into one of the K profiles, to enable the matching of the user to
251   its personalized classifier mθk (.) and 2) setting an entry condition based on a comparison between
252   the accuracy of the testing user and the base model fψk (.).
253
254
      The classifier used in the first step is trained with samples that consist of randomly collected labels
      of M training samples for each of the C classes (estimated from the consensus labels), from users
255
      belonging to each of the K profiles. This forms multiple vectors of size M × C, which have the
256
      structure defined in equation 1, where each of those vectors is labelled with the user’s profile. We
257   then train a one-versus-all (OVA) support vector machine (SVM) K-class classifier.
258
259   To classify a testing user into one of the K profiles, we first ask the user to label each image in a
                                        ×C
260   validation set, V = {(xi , yi )}M
                                      i=1 , which contains images not used in the training or testing sets.
261
      Using these labels, we build an M × C vector, which is then processed by the OVA SVM classifier
      to determine the user’s profile.
262
263   In the second step, we compare the base model and testing user accuracies on the validation set
264   V. The model mθk (.) is used only if the base model fψk (.) performs better (Steyvers et al., 2022).
265   mθk (.) is evaluated on the test set T = {(xi , yi )} with no overlap with training or validation images.

      4.1   DATASETS
290
291   CIFAR-10 includes 50,000 training, 200 validation, and 9,800 testing class-balanced color images,
292   each sized 32×32, with 10 classes. CIFAR-10N extends CIFAR-10’s training set via crowd-sourced
293   labeling to 747 annotators, with each image having three labels from different annotators. CIFAR-
294   10H expands CIFAR-10’s testing set via crowd-sourcing to 2571 annotators, resulting in an average
295   of 51 labels per image. Fashion-MNIST-H extends Xiao et al.’s Fashion-MNIST’s testing set to
296   multiple annotations from 885 annotators, averaging 66 labels per image. We use the crowd-sourced
297   testing set as the training set, with 200 images from the original training set allocated for validation
298
      and the remainder for testing. AgNews is a text classification dataset with 120,000 training, 200
      validation, and 7,400 testing news articles across 4 classes. Lastly, Chaoyang is a pathological
299
      dataset with 4021 training, 80 validation, and 2059 testing images, each having three expert labels
300
      in the training set. More details about datasets can be found in Appendix C.1.
301
302   Setup on datasets with simulated annotators: On CIFAR-10, a pairwise flipping experiment is
303   conducted where 8 out of 10 classes have clean labels, but in two classes, 80% of samples have
304
      labels flipped. Three user profiles are simulated by flipping labels between classes airplane↔bird,
      another profile that flips horse↔deer, and the other flips truck↔automobile. This results in 15
305
      unique users (5 for each profile) for training and testing. For AgNews, pairwise flipping occurs on
306
      two out of four classes, with 80% of samples flipped. Three user profiles are simulated, one that
307   flips between classes business↔science/technology, another that flips world↔sports, and the third
308   that flips sports↔business. resulting in 15 unique users (with 5 for each profile) for training and
309   testing. Both datasets use D̃ for training OVA SVM with automatically chosen K profiles based
310   on silhouette score in equation 8. ResNet-18 He et al. (2016) and Bert-Base-Uncased Devlin et al.
311   (2018) models are used as fψk (.) in training mθk (.) in equation 3 for each profile k with CIFAR-10
312   and AgNews respectively. More details on the simulation setup is in Appendix C.
313
      Setup on datasets with real annotators: for CIFAR-10N training, we conduct two experiments. In
314

458   6       D ISCUSSION
459
460   An intriguing aspect of PHICO is its capability to correct errors even when both humans and AI
461   models make mistakes. Sec. 4.2 and Appendix F suggest it happens from the personalised AI coop-
462   erative model that associates noisy labelling patterns of raters and the AI model to the correct label.
463
      A necessary condition for this to happen is to prove that P (C|¬A, ¬B) > 0, where A represents the
      event that the base model provides a correct prediction, B denotes the event that the human provides
464
      a correct label, and C is the event that our joint decision model produces a correct classification.
465
      Assuming that the base model and humans can make mistakes, and that events A and B are inde-
466
      pendent (and also independent given C), we trivially have: P (C|¬A, ¬B) = P (¬A,¬B|C).P
                                                                                            P (¬A,¬B)
                                                                                                      (C)
                                                                                                          =
467
      (1−P (A|C)).(1−P (B|C)).P (C)
468        (1−P (A))(1−P (B))       > 0 because 0 < P (B|C), P (A|C), P (A), P (B), P (C) < 1.
469
      Future work for PHICO includes addressing the complexity of human-AI cooperation, where inter-
470
      actions may change human behavior over time. While PHICO currently doesn’t account for this
471   dynamic, it could be adapted by regularly updating user’s assigned profile to reflect evolving inter-
472   actions and noisy patterns. Additionally, we will aim to create a more efficient few-shot profiling
473   process and extend PHICO for multi-label classification, building on insights from Li et al. (2022);
474   Kye et al. (2022). Enhancing privacy in learned profiles through local differential privacy Yang et al.
475   (2022) is also a key direction for future work.

      7       C ONCLUSIONS
478
479
      We introduced PHICO, a novel human-AI cooperation framework that integrates noisy label learning
480   methods with personalized AI cooperative models. Through both a theoretical convergence proof
481   and an empirical evaluation across diverse datasets, including CIFAR-10N, CIFAR-10H, Fashion-
482   MNIST-H, AgNews, and Chaoyang histopathology, we demonstrated the robustness and effective-
483   ness of PHICO. We also proposed a new measure, the alteration rate, to quantify the impact of
484   PHICO on label modifications from both human and AI sources. With its model-agnostic design
485   and the ability to manage multi-rater datasets without ground truth labels, PHICO offers an effective
      solution to human-AI cooperation tasks.

781   A    C ONSENSUS L ABEL E STIMATION
782
783   Many multi-rater input datasets lack ground truth labels. To address this, PHICO is built to function
784   effectively without relying on them. During training, we use Crowdlab (Goh et al., 2023) to estimate
785
      a consensus label ȳi , which approximates the true clean label yi . Crowdlab works in two steps. In
      the first step, it estimates a consensus by majority vote ȳi′ per training sample. In the second step,
786
      it trains a classifier using the initial consensus and obtains predicted class probabilities for each
787
      training example. After that, Crowdlab uses these predicted probabilities along with the original
788   annotations from raters to estimate a better consensus, creating the following ensemble,
789
790                          ȳi = wγ × fγ (xi ) + w1 × ỹi,1 + ... + w|A| × ỹi,|A| ,                    (7)
791
792   where fγ : X → ∆C−1 is a classifier trained with the majority vote label ȳi′ to output a categorical
793   distribution for C classes, and the weights wγ , w1 , ..., w|A| are assigned according to an estimate
794
      of how trustworthy the model is, compared to each individual annotator. The outcome of Crowdlab
      is a consensus labelled training set denoted by D̄ = {(xi , ȳi )}N
                                                                        i=1 . Note that the consensus label is
795
      necessary only when the clean label yi is latent. If such clean label is observed, then Crowdlab is
796
      no longer needed, and PHICO can be trained with D = {(xi , yi )}N    i=1 .
