009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011                Recent advances in deep learning (DL) have led to improved vision-based al-
012                gorithms. DL-based semantic segmentation, in particular, has enabled precise
013                predictions using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). State-of-the-art CNN-
014                based networks have achieved high accuracy on various datasets in multiple fields,
015                such as building, scene, and object segmentation. However, subdomain shifts
016                between training and test sets within a single domain can cause degraded accuracy
017                in fine-grained segmentation. To counter this, this paper introduces a novel Sub-
018
                   Domain Adaptation (SDA) framework for fine-grained and granular segmentation,
                   which divides one single domain into multiple sub-domains and optimizes the
019
                   baseline-network for each sub-domain. The baseline-network is further fine-tuned
020
                   by recognizing the domain of the input in run-time, leading to more accurate pre-
021                dictions. Benchmarks of scene parsing, autonomous driving, and aerial imagery
022                demonstrate the superior performance of SDA for granular segmentation.1
023

025   1       I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Fine-grained segmentation of objects in an image is an ongoing challenge in a variety of fields,
028   including remote sensing Huang & Gartner (2009), autonomous robotics Lenz et al. (2015), and
029   autonomous driving Sallab et al. (2017). Improvements in accuracy are necessary for practical
030   applications Reed et al. (1994); Zhou et al. (2017). For instance, precise segmentation of buildings in
031   aerial images is vital for creating high-quality digital maps automatically, or for detecting changes
032   in the image for urban planning Kim et al. (2018). Similarly, accurate segmentation of objects in a
033   scene image is critical for autonomous vehicles and robots to respond appropriately to the objects
034
      Fridman et al. (2017); Wu et al. (2018).
035   In recent years, DL methods have proved to be effective for object segmentation in aerial imagery
036   Zhao et al. (2017); Kaiser et al. (2017); Kim et al. (2018); Yue et al. (2019). However, due to the
037   complex shapes, colors, and rotations of objects within cities, such as buildings and roads Lee et al.
038   (2000); Kaiser et al. (2017), achieving accurate segmentation remains challenging. To this end,
039   various architectures have been developed to improve the performance of segmentation. For example,
040
      UNetPPL Kim et al. (2018) is a U-Net-based architecture that incorporates multiple pyramid pooling
      layers Zhao et al. (2017) for extracting multi-scale features. Yue et al. Yue et al. (2019) developed
041
      novel layers to further improve the performance of building segmentation. Moreover, Doi and Iwasaki
042
      Doi & Iwasaki (2018) applied a focal loss Ross & Dollár (2017) to obtain focused features in aerial
043   images. As aerial images exhibit more vague features, especially boundaries, than other types of
044   vision images, many state-of-the-art models have focused on boundary-oriented segmentation for
045   fine-grained segmentation.
046
      Scene parsing is a popular segmentation task for identifying images into semantic categories such as
047
      sky, road, human, and ground Zhou et al. (2017). Despite considerable progress in DL models for
048
      semantic segmentation, precise fine-grained segmentation remains a challenge due to the complexity
049   of the different types of datasets. To address this, Romera et al. Romera et al. (2017) proposed a
050   factorized residual layer to improve the efficiency of the DL architecture. BshapeNet Rom Kang &
051   Kim (2018) further improved accuracy by applying bounding shape masks to the Region of Interest.
052   BubbleNet Griffin & Corso (2019), which takes into account a representative frame in the video,
053

      also improved segmentation performance. State-of-the-art (SotA) models have been evaluated using
055   benchmark tests Lin et al. (2014); Zhou et al. (2017).
056
057   Recent efforts have demonstrated substantial improvements in segmentation performance (e.g., 50%
058
      → 70%; 70% → 85%) using SotA models. In contrast, achieving fine-grained segmentation (e.g.,
      85% → 90%; 90% → 95%) has proven to be a difficult task. In our preliminary study, we found that a
059
      single domain can be further divided into various sub-domains as shown in Fig. 1, and the soft domain
060
      shift gaps between different sub-domains lead to a strong deterioration in fine-grained segmentation,
061   as illustrated in Fig. 1. Specifically, the presence of intra-domain gaps between the training and test
062   sets have a particularly detrimental effect on fine-grained segmentation performance. Despite the
063   development of domain adaptation (DA) methods, current approaches are largely designed to reduce
064   domain gaps between two or more different domains, rather than within one single domain. As the
065   domain gaps within a single domain are much smaller than those between two different domains,
066   current DA methods are ineffective in addressing intra-domain gaps.
067   To address the problem of intra-domain gap, in this work, we introduce the concept of Sub-domain
068   adaptation (SDA) first and then propose an DL framework, dubbed SDA-Net. Instead of the
069   probability-based segmentation, which is known to decrease boundary-oriented segmentation, we use
070   density-based segmentation (Appendix B). To reduce the intra-domain gap, SDA-Net consists of a
071   sub-domain classifier and a baseline-network. The sub-domain classifier identifies sub-domains of
072   inputs and the baseline-network is fine-tuned based on the identified sub-domain via a self-supervised
073   approach. The fine-tuned baseline-network can then provide precise predictions for input images,
074   taking into account the knowledge of intra-domain gaps and sub-domain of inputs.
075   To summarize, our contributions are below:
076
077          • We proposed a self-supervised fine-tuning network (SDA-Net) for sub-domain aware granu-
078            lar fine-grained segmentation.
079          • To achieve fine-grained segmentation, SDA-Net leverages a novel loss function, sieve loss,
080            for self-supervised learning and adaptive fine-tuning loss for decreasing intra-domain gaps.
081
             • We evaluated our framework on various segmentation benchmarks and demonstrated its
082            superior performance compared with SotA models.

      3.1   OVERALL A RCHITECTURE
197
198   Fig. 4 shows the proposed sub-domain adaptation network (SDA-Net) composed of a sub-domain
199   classifier (SDC) and a baseline-network (BN). This framework is capable of addressing the soft
200   domain gap problem by learning to discriminate between sub-domains and mitigating the shift
201   among them. By employing a fine-tuning mechanism, SDA-Net is designed to realize SDA. To this
202   end, our SDA-Net must be aware of the subdomain of the input, which is realized through SDC.
203   The SDC is a CNN (C θSDC ) which classifies the input (X ) according  P to its subdomain, producing
204   a probability vector of density (pc = (pc1 , ..., pci ); pci ∈ [0, 1]; pci = 1) for each target object
205   (c ∈ (1, l)) in X . Based on pc , the BN is fine-tuned and provides the prediction using X and

      X ′ , which are the latent features extracted  by SDC, as inputs.     Therefore, the sub-domain of X

      5   C ONCLUSION
475
476
477
      This paper has introduced a novel framework, SDA-Net, which is capable of addressing the intra-
      domain and soft-domain gaps in granular segmentation. The SDA-Net recognized the index of the
478
      sub-domain of inputs and fine-tunes the baseline-network of SDA-Net, allowing precise predictions
479
      on the inputs. In order to achieve this, two novel loss functions, sieve and fine-tuning losses,
480   were proposed. The sieve loss provided attention-based gradients with a small computational cost,
481   while the fine-tuning loss provided negative terms to escape from the local minimum caused by the
482   soft-domain gaps. Experimental results demonstrated that the SDA-Net significantly enhanced the
483   segmentation performance. This novel framework can employ any other advanced SotA models
484   for more enhanced segmentation without complex implementations, although finding the best SotA
485   models and hyperparameters are required for further enhancement in segmentation tasks, which is
      subject to our future work.

      Density-based segmentation algorithms have been widely studied in recent years. Zhang et al. Zhang
736
      et al. (2017) proposed a method that applied density-based clustering and nodule segmentation to
737
      localize lung nodules in CT sequence images. Han et al. Han & Davis (2011) utilized a density
738   function for a multiple feature integration machine learning (ML) algorithm, which was applied
739   to a classification task. Zhang et al. Zhang et al. (2019) developed a density-based unsupervised
740   segmentation technique that incorporated density-based clustering and sensitive parameter setting
741   techniques. Despite these advances, the performance of density-based segmentation has remained
742   limited due to the use of ML- or clustering-based methods without the aid of CNNs.
743
      While improving the segmentation performance of a DL model, we found that the predicted probability
744   distribution of target objects is dependent on a training set. Specifically, when training a DL model
745   with a target object density of 10-20%, the model’s predicted performance is highest when the
746   corresponding test set has the same range of density. However, if the test set’s object density is
747   outside the training set’s range, such as 80-90%, the model’s performance is degraded.
