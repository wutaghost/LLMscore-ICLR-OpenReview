009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) should ideally generate diverse content for open-
012            ended prompts. Preliminary evidence has suggested that preference-tuned lan-
013            guage models struggle to generate diverse content, which would have important
014            implications for how we align models. However, research on this question has
015            been limited by the difficulty of measuring diversity, which naïvely would require
016            costly human evaluation. We propose to leverage code as a means to study se-
017            mantic diversity since code has executable semantics. To this end, we create an
018
               open-ended program synthesis task, enabling us to cheaply evaluate the diversity
               of hundreds of thousands of generations. Using our methodology, we find that
019
               while preference-tuning reduces syntactic and lexical diversity, it can increase se-
020
               mantic diversity. We also study the effect of model size and prompting technique
021            on diversity. Finally, we find that neural diversity metrics correlate poorly with our
022            semantic diversity metrics, highlighting the need for more rigorous methodologies
023            for evaluating diversity.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   As large language models (LLMs) become powerful at a wide variety of tasks (Zhao et al., 2024;
029   Zheng et al., 2024), it is important to evaluate the diversity of their generations, not just their ac-
030   curacy. Many real-world tasks are open-ended to some degree with many possible answers—e.g.,
031   crafting convincing essays, suggesting cooking recipes, writing unit tests, etc. Evaluating diversity
032
      can also provide insights into the nature of language models, especially their creative capabilities.
      Additionally, diversity is of paramount importance to the exploration component of algorithms such
033
      as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF).
034
035   One of the key research questions explores how different kinds of instruction tuning impact diversity.
036   In particular, recent work has demonstrated that RLHF may reduce diversity in summarization with
037   small A LPACA FARM models (Kirk et al., 2023) and in joke generation with C HAT GPT (Jentzsch
038
      & Kersting, 2023). Furthermore, it has been hypothesized that RLHF induces “mode collapse” on
      a broader scale (Kirk et al., 2023; janus, 2022). These results may impact decisions about which
039
      algorithms to use for instruction tuning.
040
041   However, defining diversity is a key challenge in addressing this and related questions. Linguistics
042   distinguishes between content (or semantics)—the meaning of an utterance—and form, which refers
043   to how that meaning is conveyed. Diversity of form can be measured automatically using lexical
044
      metrics, such as n-gram diversity (Li et al., 2016).
045   Unfortunately, measuring the diversity of content/semantics is challenging since it typically requires
046   human evaluation, which can be prohibitively expensive to scale and highly subjective from one
047   individual to another. One strategy is using neural models as proxies for human evaluation; however,
048   recent work has shown that neural methods are inferior to human evaluation (Tevet & Berant, 2021).
049   In this work, we propose to address this challenge by evaluating diversity in the task of open-ended
050   program generation. Since programs have well-defined, executable semantics, we can automatically
051   evaluate semantic diversity reliably. In particular, we can define three separate forms of diversity for
052   a given program (and a fixed set of test cases): its lexical form is reflected by the sequence of tokens
053   in a program, its syntactic form is reflected by the Abstract Syntax Tree, and its semantics is reflected
      by the outputs for given test case inputs. Correspondingly, we can measure lexical diversity using

088        metrics are a poor proxy for the actual execution semantics of generated programs, highlighting the
089

           and prompting techniques on diversity.

103
104        LLMs and Instruction Tuning Methods. Neural language models (Bengio et al., 2000; Radford
105        et al., 2019) are remarkably powerful, and some of the earlier efforts to align them with instruction-
106        following relied on few-shot prompting (Brown, 2020). Later, RLHF using Proximal Policy Op-
107        timization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017; Stiennon et al., 2020) was shown to be highly effective
           in aligning LLMs with human preference (Ouyang et al., 2022). Subsequently, numerous alterna-

      tive methods to PPO have been proposed, such as Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov
109   et al., 2024) and Rejection Sampling (Touvron et al., 2023), which need not be mutually exclusive.
110   Additionally, language models have shown significant promise in programming tasks and software
111   engineering (Chen et al., 2021; Roziere et al., 2023).
112
      Evaluating Language Models for Code. Evaluating the code generation abilities of neural models
113
      has roots in the semantic parsing literature (Oda et al., 2015; Yin et al., 2018), which relied on
114
      n-gram similarity metrics like BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002). Numerous executable programming
115   benchmarks have been proposed, especially given that n-gram similarity does not imply correctness
116   (Hendrycks et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021; Puri et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022). However, diversity has
117   not been a major consideration in the context of programming tasks.
118
      Approaches to Measuring Diversity. Traditional methods for automatically measuring diversity
119
      fall into lexical or neural approaches. Lexical metrics generally involve calculating summary statis-
120
      tics using n-grams, such as Distinct-N (Li et al., 2016; Du & Black, 2019) and Self-BLEU (a
121   modified BLEU metric) (Zhu et al., 2018). More recently, deep learning has been used to model
122   the similarity of natural language sentences (Zhang et al., 2019; Reimers & Gurevych, 2019; Nee-
123   lakantan et al., 2022) and code (Feng et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2022; Liu et al.,
124   2023; Zhuo, 2024). These techniques have been adapted to measuring diversity in natural language
125   (Lai et al., 2020; Tevet & Berant, 2021; Stasaski & Hearst, 2022), but not in code. Tevet & Berant
126   (2021) compare lexical and neural models, demonstrating that while neural metrics outperform lex-
127   ical ones, they still fall short of human performance. Shaib et al. (2024) show that lexical diversity
128   often captures the same information as traditional compression algorithms and recommend reporting
129
      a combination of lexical and neural diversity scores for a more comprehensive evaluation.
130   Diversity of LLM Content. Empirical work evaluating the diversity and creativity of LLM genera-
131   tions is relatively sparse. McCoy et al. (2023) investigate whether smaller language models exhibit
132   linguistic novelty by evaluating combinations of n-grams not present in the training corpus. With
133   the advent of more capable LLMs, it has been argued that models such as C HAT GPT are incapable
134   of generating diverse jokes Jentzsch & Kersting (2023). Additionally, Kirk et al. (2023) contend that
135
      RLHF induces “mode collapse", using both lexical and neural metrics to measure the diversity of
      summarized content. Furthermore, (Padmakumar & He, 2023) show that human-written essays as-
136
      sisted by an RLHF-tuned model are less diverse than those assisted by a base model when assessed
137
      using neurodiversity measures.
138
139   The dominant narrative in this body of research suggests that preference-tuned LLMs reduce diver-
140   sity. This is problematic because diversity is paramount for reinforcement learning (RL) and pref-
141
      erence tuning, as exploration is necessary to improve any RL system (Sutton & Barto, 2018). Ad-
      ditionally, useful assistants should be capable of producing diverse outputs in open-ended domains,
142
      ranging from brainstorming and creative writing to software testing, drafting website front-ends,
143
      mining data for insights, and scientific discovery. Diversity is also critical in addressing ambigu-
144   ity, such as when responding to a question like “How can I add new functionality to my code?"
145   These use cases are not captured by programming benchmarks that typically assume a single cor-
146   rect answer. The empirical question of diversity in LLM-generated content is crucial for building
147   better RL-inspired systems and creating agents capable of handling real-world, open-ended tasks.
148   However, current benchmarks fail to reflect this. Our best methods for automatically measuring the
149   diversity of newer and more powerful LLMs still rely on n-gram-based metrics or on smaller and
150   weaker neural models to evaluate stronger ones.

202   Dataset and execution environment. Several desiderata guide the creation of our dataset of prob-
203   lem descriptions. First, while the tasks should be open-ended, they must also be standardized enough
204   to allow execution using consistent test cases and to enable comparison of outputs. Additionally, the

      generally solve.

212
      ing hints for the inputs. Importantly, we aggressively abstracted all program descriptions, removing
      any direct reference to the programming task in the description, standardizing the function name to

251
252   Next, we describe the metrics used to measure diversity, focusing specifically on our choice of
253   pairwise distance mdist (pj , pk ).
254
255
      Semantic diversity. We define two programs as semantically distinct if their outputs differ across a
      given set of test cases: 1(O(pj ) ̸= O(pk )), where O(p) denotes the vector of outputs obtained by
256
      executing the test cases on program p. If the execution of p results in an error for a particular test
257
      case, the test case output is recorded as the error. We allow two programs to be considered incorrect
258   in different ways—for example, a syntactic error and a type error are treated as distinct. Note
259   that if the generated programs fail to implement the function f (...) entirely, this metric penalizes
260   diversity, as such generations are all incorrect in the same way. In Appendix A.3, we analyze
261   semantic diversity among only the subset of well-formed programs, using Equation (2) to reduce
262   bias.
263
      Lexical diversity. We use Expectation-Adjusted Distinct n-grams (EAD), an adaptation of the
264   Distinct-N metric that removes bias towards shorter sequence length (Liu et al., 2022; Kirk et al.,
265   2023). The Distinct-N metric (Li et al., 2016; Du & Black, 2019) computes the ratio between the
266   number of unique n-grams divided by the total number of n-grams; in our case, we apply this to the
267   combined text of the two generated programs. We tokenize programs using the Parso Tokenizer,1
268   which allows tokenization of Python in the presence of syntax errors. We report EAD using n-
269
                1
                     https://github.com/davidhalter/parso

270
      grams of length n=4. For our lexical and syntactic diversity metrics, we approximate Equation (2)
271   by randomly sampling 300 pairs with replacement for efficiency.
272
273   Syntactic diversity. We adapt the Distinct-N metric to the Abstract Syntax Tree (AST) of each
274
      generated program. To further isolate the syntactic structure of a program (e.g., for-loop instead of
      recursion) from superficial choices (e.g., variable names), we canonicalize all identifiers and numeric
275
      constants in the AST, which we call the Canonicalized AST. We implement the Distinct measure
276
      on Canonicalized ASTs for two programs by calculating the ratio of the number of unique subtrees
277   of height H across both programs to the total number of subtrees of height H in both programs,
278   where H=4. In Appendix A.5, we provide a figure visually demonstrating a Canoncalized AST for
279   a small Python expression and additional implementation details.
280
      Neural diversity. We adapt existing methods of neural diversity metrics (Tevet & Berant, 2021) to
281
      our domain by using C ODE B ERT S CORE (Zhou et al., 2023) and ICE-S CORE, an LLM-based code-
282
      evaluation tool (Zhuo, 2024). For ICE-S CORE, we use gpt-4o-2024-11-20 and the functional
283   correctness setting. Since higher scores should indicate higher similarity, we use 1 − Score(pj , pk )
284   for distinctness. While C ODE B ERT S CORE and ICE-S CORE were not intended for evaluating pro-
285   gram diversity, we choose them since it either closely resemble models used in the NLP literature to
286   evaluate semantic diversity (Tevet & Berant, 2021) or are state-of-the-art in neural code evaluation.
287
      In our analysis, we report this number as the semantic diversity in the context of all samples taken: if
288
      a generation does not contain a program, we penalize the model as it does not produce semantically
289   meaningful content.
290
291   Coherence. We additionally report a metric that measures the “quality” of generations. We use the
292
      term “coherence” to describe a well-formed generation with the following properties: (i) contains
      the definition of the function f (...), (ii) has no syntax errors, (iii) is capable of being run on all test
293
      cases without runtime errors, and (iv) prints out any output (as requested by the prompt).

      6     D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
498
      Contributions in methodology and empirical findings. We have proposed a novel strategy for
499
      studying semantic diversity by focusing on code generation, where semantics can be automatically
500   evaluated by executing programs on test cases. Using this methodology, we perform an extensive
501   empirical analysis of LLM diversity. Our findings that all existing metrics (including neural metrics)
502   correlate poorly with our semantic diversity metric motivate us not to assume these metrics reflect
503   semantic content, especially for code. The result highlights the need for more rigorous methodolo-
504   gies for automatically measuring LLM diversity at scale.
505
      Our methods and results advance the discussion on LLMs and diversity. We find that instruction
506   tuning decreases lexical and syntactic diversity but increases semantic diversity; semantic diversity
507   is not lost when accounting for coherence. Our empirical findings have intellectual merit. They add
508   depth to the growing discussion on how instruction-tuning, model size, and prompting technique
509   impact diversity. We speculate that if preference-tuning is associated with lower lexical/syntactic,
510   it may impart a specific “voice" or style to the model. We hypothesize that a dynamic between
511   the online and reward models used for rejection-sampling, PPO, and potentially DPO may induce a
512   preference for certain lexical and syntactic constructs while preserving semantic diversity in specific
513   domains like coding.
514   Our empirical findings have practical consequences. For creative endeavors and search-intensive
515   tasks ranging from red-teaming LLMs (Perez et al., 2022), generating synthetic training data (Dubey
516   et al., 2024), program testing Deng et al. (2024); Xia et al. (2024), and program optimization (Shy-
517   pula et al., 2024), our insights may help others to decide which model configuration is optimal
518   for both high-quality and diverse generations. For example, it may be more important to allocate
519   resources to align smaller, cheaper, and faster models to the task with instruction-tuning than to
520
      allocate time and funds to sampling from a larger, slower, and more expensive model.
521   This result highlights the differentiated impact of instruction tuning on different kinds of diversity,
522   suggesting that there may not be a "one-size-fits-all" strategy for preserving or improving diversity.
523
      Finally, by the nature of our approach, we are restricted to evaluating code diversity. Code is an
524   important domain in its own right; furthermore, for many tasks, code can be viewed as a formal
525   representation of the natural language utterance (Liang, 2016), suggesting that our results may have
526   implications for the diversity of natural language. Nevertheless, further study is needed to establish
527   whether our findings translate to more traditional natural language tasks.
528
      Future Work. While our work investigates the impact of instruction tuning, model size, and prompt-
529
      ing on diversity, the nature of pre-training (e.g., the corpus and other training configurations) on di-
530   versity is an important direction for future work. We currently evaluate off-the-shelf neural models
531   for their ability to reflect diversity in execution and note that other alternatives such as U NI XC ODER
532   (Guo et al., 2022) or C ODE E XECUTOR (Liu et al., 2023) could have been chosen. We believe that
533   efforts to make neural models more robust to capturing content diversity as well as more rigorous
534   evaluations of neural models to measure LLM content diversity in natural language (for example,
535   a human study following Tevet & Berant (2021) for LLM-generated content instead of human-
536   generated content, is important. We also believe rigorously evaluating content diversity with more
537   samples by modulating the degrees of open-mindedness in questions and across many domains and,
538
      from potentially sensitive domains to more harmless ones, will be important to understand patterns
      more broadly and the impact of efforts to reduce toxicity in LLMs.

      A.1   DATASET C REATION AND A DDITIONAL D ETAILS
759
760   We created our dataset through a multi-step process starting from the C ODE N ET dataset and test-
761   cases from A LPHAC ODE. The process involved problem standardization, language model assistance
762   for scaling, and manual validation.
763   Initial Processing. We began by randomly selecting a single problem description from IBM Co-
764   deNet. We used this to as a seed to construct examples for a 1-shot prompt for a Language Model
765   to assist us. Specifically we manually wrote one example of the following outputs that should be
766   generated for each individual problem description from C ODE N ET
767
768         1. A canonicalized problem description
769         2. A wrapper function that would take any generation for that function, parse inputs for the
770            function, and instrument the generation with the entire suite of test cases
771         3. A property-based testing function for generating additional test cases when needed
772
773   Dataset Expansion. We then wrote prompt templates for gpt-3.5-turbo-0125 that were
774   designed to take our 1-shot prompt, and then prompt the LLM to repeat the same for the new example
775   we select from C ODE N ET. We randomly sampled 75 programs from CodeNet and attempted to
776   generate the three components for each original problem description from C ODE N ET. We then
777
      saved these into individual files, and also wrote them into an HTML document for manual review.
778   Manual Review and Selection. For the 75 problem ids that were processed, we then manually
779   inspected the output components checking for the following criteria:
780
781         1. The language model correctly parsed inputs into our desired format
782         2. The problem added diversity to our dataset
783
784   We tracked the original CodeNet problem IDs and validation results in a spreadsheet. These were
785
      the 21 examples then used for our dataset.
786   Manual Editing of Problem Descriptions. After selecting our problems, we then manually went
787   through each of the three components, and manually edited them to be correct: a large amount
788   required revisions, as the LLM assistant made mistakes. We saved our manually edited components
789   to be further processed.
790   Test Case Integration. For each of the individual problem descriptions, we then merged test cases
791   from C ODE N ET and A LPHAC ODE. We required at least 10 test cases per problem. For the three
792   problems that lacked sufficient test cases, we used our property-based testing scripts to generate
793   100 additional cases, such that we would have sufficient coverage. We then saved the canonicalized
794   problem descriptions, the function to extract and parse input test cases, and the input test cases into
795   a dataset.
796   Final Checks. During experimental validation, we found and fixed one incorrect problem descrip-
797   tion and two faulty argument parsing functions. We then saved this corrected version as our final
798   dataset.
799
800   Additional Dataset Details. In our experiments, we instruct LLMs to print their outputs to
801   stdout; then, for each generation, we execute all test cases and capture the resulting outputs.
802
      Because LLMs often generate natural language to accompany generated programs, extracting pro-
803   grams from the generations is a non-trivial task, especially for pre-trained models. We developed an
804   extraction utility that extracts not only the target function f (...), but also any helper functions and
805   imports that may be relevant. To safely execute programs at scale, we perform all execution inside
806   an isolated D OCKER container to prevent adverse consequences of blindly executing LLM outputs.
