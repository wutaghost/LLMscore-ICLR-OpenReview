007                                                A BSTRACT
008
009            In this paper, we consider the supervised pre-trained transformer for a class of se-
010            quential decision-making problems. The class of considered problems is a subset
011            of the general formulation of reinforcement learning in that there is no transition
012            probability matrix; though seemingly restrictive, the subset class of problems cov-
013            ers bandits, dynamic pricing, and newsvendor problems as special cases. Such a
014            structure enables the use of optimal actions/decisions in the pre-training phase,
015
               and the usage also provides new insights for the training and generalization of the
               pre-trained transformer. We first note the training of the transformer model can
016
               be viewed as a performative prediction problem, and the existing methods and
017
               theories largely ignore or cannot resolve an out-of-distribution issue. We propose
018            a natural solution that includes the transformer-generated action sequences in the
019            training procedure, and it enjoys better properties both numerically and theoreti-
020            cally. The availability of the optimal actions in the considered tasks also allows
021            us to analyze the properties of the pre-trained transformer as an algorithm and
022            explains why it may lack exploration and how this can be automatically resolved.
023            Numerically, we categorize the advantages of pre-trained transformers over the
024            structured algorithms such as UCB and Thompson sampling into three cases: (i)
025            it better utilizes the prior knowledge in the pre-training data; (ii) it can elegantly
026
               handle the misspecification issue suffered by the structured algorithms; (iii) for
               short time horizon such as T ≤ 50, it behaves more greedy and enjoys much
027
               better regret than the structured algorithms designed for asymptotic optimality.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In recent years, transformer-based models [48] have achieved great success in a wide range of tasks
      such as natural language processing [46, 12], computer vision [20], and also reinforcement learning
034
      (RL) [13]. In particular, an offline RL problem can be framed as a sequence prediction problem [19]
035
      that predicts the optimal/near-optimal/human action based on the observed history. [13, 29] pioneer
036   this approach with the Decision Transformer (DT), which leverages the auto-regressive nature of
037   transformers to maximize the likelihood of trajectories in offline datasets. In this paper, we focus
038   on a subset class of reinforcement learning problems, which we call sequential decision-making
039   problems, that has no transition probability matrix compared to a general RL problem. This subset
040   of problems are general enough to capture a wide range of applications including stochastic and
041   linear bandit problems [35], dynamic pricing [16, 15], and newsvendor problem [37, 9]. Most
042   importantly, the structure brings two benefits: (i) pre-training – the optimal action is either in closed
043   form or efficiently solvable and thus the optimal action can be used as the prediction target in the pre-
044
      training data and (ii) understanding – the setting is more aligned with the existing study [53, 22] on
      the in-context learning ability of the transformer which gives more insight to the working machinery
045
      of transformer on decision-making tasks. Moreover, as a side product, our paper extends pre-trained
046
      transformers to the application contexts of business, economics, and operations research which are
047   less studied by the existing works.
048
049   To summarize, we work on the following aspects in this paper:
050   - The pre-training procedure (Section 3): We mathematically formulate the learning setup of super-
051   vised pre-trained transformer for sequential decision-making problems, and propose a standardized
052   procedure to generate the pre-training data. In addition, for the training process, we identify an
053   out-of-distribution (OOD) issue largely ignored or unsolved by the existing literature. Specifically,
      the pre-training data involves two streams of actions – one is the actions in the history (features),

      Proposition 4.4 provides a simple explanation for why one does not have to be too pessimistic. In-
469   tuitively, the pre-trained model TFθ̂ can be viewed as the optimal decision function Alg∗ plus some
470   random noise, and the random noise actually plays the role of exploration and helps the posterior
471   to concentration. Proposition 4.4 materializes such an intuition and it involves two conditions. The
472   first condition is that TFθ̂ behaves closely to Alg∗ and the (reward) deviation between these two is
473   captured by ∆Exploit . This condition can be justified by Figure 3. The second condition characterizes
474   the KL divergence between the conditional distribution of the observation ot for two environments:
475   the true testing environment γ and any other one γ ′ ∈ Γ. Note that this KL divergence critically
476   depends on TFθ̂ because the ability to distinguish between two environments depends on the inten-
477
      sity of exploration of the underlying algorithm. These conditions work more like a stylized model
      to understand the behavior of the transformer. It is a largely simplified model; say, the parameter
478

479
      son sampling. Nevertheless, the conditions give us a perspective to understand how the transformer
480   works as an online algorithm. In particular, TFθ̂ deviates from Alg∗ by an amount of ∆Exploit in
481   terms of the reward, and this deviation accumulates over time into the first term in the regret bound.
482   On the other hand, this deviation also gives an exploration whose intensity is measured by ∆Explore ,
483   and the second term captures the regret caused during the concentration of the posterior onto the
484   true environment. In Appendix D.7, we provide some further justifications for these conditions. We
485   also note that the finiteness of Γ is not essential. The KL divergence condition can be modified for

      In this paper, we study the pre-trained transformer for sequential decision-making problems. Such
534   problems enable using the optimal action as the target variable for training the transformer. We
535   mathematically formulate the setup of the training pipeline and propose a way to utilize simulation
536   environments to generate pre-training data. We investigate the training and generation aspect of
537   the transformer and identify an OOD issue that is largely ignored by the existing literature. The-
538   oretically, we interpret how the transformer works as an online algorithm through the lens of the
539   Bayes-optimal decision function. The numerical experiments are encouraging in that the trained
      transformer learns a decision function significantly better than all the benchmark algorithms.

      A.1   L ITERATURE R EVIEW
712
713   In this section, we discuss more related works which complement our discussions in Section 1. In
714   particular, we elaborate the literature in three streams.
715
716   Understanding transformer and in-context learning.
717
      A remarkable characteristic of pre-trained transformers is their ability to perform in-context learning
718   (ICL). Once pre-trained on a vast corpus, transformers can solve new tasks with just a few exam-
719   ples, without updating their parameters. ICL has captured the attention of the theoretical machine
720   learning community, leading to considerable efforts into understanding ICL from different theo-
721   retical perspectives [53, 4, 49, 38]. A stream of literature that is related to our work explains the
722   transformer’s behavior as Bayesian inference. See [53, 56] for the Bayesian argument in regres-
723   sion context, [41] for approximating a large set of posteriors, and [36] for the Bayesian analysis
724   in decision-making. Another stream of literature aims to develop generalization bound or other
725   types of convergence results. See [30, 6] for such results in the regression context and [38] in the
726
      decision-making context.
727
      Pre-trained transformer for RL.
728
729   The earliest effort of applying transformers to reinforcement learning lies in the area of offline
730   RL [29, 13]. By autoregressively maximizing the likelihood of trajectories in the offline dataset,
731   this paradigm essentially converts offline RL to a supervised learning problem. When evaluating
732   the policy, the actions are sampled according to the likelihood of PT conditioned on the so called
733
      “return-to-go”, which is commonly chosen to be the cumulative reward of “good” trajectories in
      the dataset. By doing so, PT retrieves information from trajectories in training data with similar
734
      return-to-go values and performs imitation learning to high-reward offline trajectories. Based on
735
      this observation, several works further examine the validity of return-to-go conditioning [21, 3, 11]
736   and propose alternative methods that improve this approach or extend to other settings [34, 5, 57, 52].
737   For example, one of the drawbacks of the return-to-go conditioning is that it often fails in trajectory-
738   stitching when offline data comes from sub-optimal policies. One recent work [42] finds that pre-
739   trained LLMs, although trained by text data, can behave like a follow-the-perturbed-leader (FTPL)
740   algorithm in bandits and games. It further introduces a regret-based training loss, through which
741   transformers can be trained in an unsupervised way.
742
743   Sequential decision-making.
744   We carry out experiments involving some stylized sequential decision-making models including
745   dynamic pricing and inventory management. Dynamic pricing entails setting prices to discern the
746   underlying revenue function, usually defined as the product of demand and price [16]. Algorithms
747   that purely exploit based on historical data tend to be suboptimal and have been found converging to
748   non-optimal prices with a positive probability [33, 32]. Therefore, random exploration is essential
749   for a more accurate understanding of the demand function and to avoid settling on suboptimal prices.
750   See [24, 17, 10, 15] for algorithms that tackle this exploration-exploitation trade-off and achieving
751   near-optimal regret under different settings.
752   For inventory management, demand estimation is different from that in dynamic pricing due to the
753   censored demand [26]. When demand exceeds inventory levels, the unmet demand is not observed,
754   leading to lost sales. The optimal decision-making policy must balance exploration and exploitation,
755   which involves occasionally setting inventory levels higher than usual to gather more demand data,
      but not so high that the additional costs of exploration become prohibitive. Regarding near-optimal

1173   C.4   TFθ̂ AS A SOLUTION TO MODEL MISSPECIFICATION
1174
1175
       Most sequential decision-making algorithms, including all the benchmark algorithms in our exper-
1176   iments, rely on structural or model assumptions about the underlying tasks. For instance, demand
1177   functions in pricing and newsvendor problems are typically assumed to be linear in context. Apply-

1329   C.6   P ERFORMANCE ON LONGER TESTING HORIZON
1330
1331   Our paradigm can be generalized to a longer testing horizon (beyond the length of the pre-training
1332   data) by introducing a context window. Specifically, we define a context window size W and limit
1333   the input to the last min{W, t} timesteps of the input sequence Ht to predict a∗t during both the
1334   pre-training and testing phases. By considering only the latest min{W, t} timesteps, we enable the
1335   model to handle testing sequences of any length, even when t > T . This technique is commonly
1336
       used in the literature (e.g., Chen et al. [13]).
1337   In this part, we evaluate TFθ̂ ’s generalization ability when the testing horizon is longer than that seen
1338   during pre-training. As for a shorter testing horizon, TFθ̂ can simply run until the end of the horizon,
1339   and previous results demonstrate strong performances in such cases, particularly when the horizon is
1340   less than 100. Figure 15 shows the performance when the testing horizon is extended from 100 (pre-
1341   training horizon) to 200 in the newsvendor problem. The results demonstrate that TFθ̂ generalizes
1342
       well in this extended horizon scenario, even in the censored demand setting where exploration is
       essential. Its actions remain nearly optimal beyond t = 100, resulting in lower regret compared to
1343
       benchmark algorithms. For this experiment, all the pre-training and testing setups follow Appendix
1344
       E, except the testing horizon length.
