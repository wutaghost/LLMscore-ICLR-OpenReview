009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Discourse phenomena in existing document-level translation datasets are sparse,
012            which has been a fundamental obstacle in the development of context-aware ma-
013            chine translation models. Moreover, most existing document-level corpora and
014            context-aware machine translation methods rely on an unrealistic assumption on
015            sentence-level alignments. To mitigate these issues, we first curate a novel dataset
016
               of Chinese-English literature, which consists of 132 books with intricate discourse
               structures. Then, we propose a more pragmatic and challenging setting for context-
017
               aware translation, termed chapter-to-chapter (C H 2C H) translation, and investigate
018
               the performance of commonly-used machine translation models under this setting.
019            Furthermore, we introduce a potential approach of finetuning large language mod-
020            els (LLMs) within the domain of C H 2C H literary translation, yielding impressive
021            improvements over baselines. Through our comprehensive analysis, we unveil that
022            literary translation under the C H 2C H setting is challenging in nature, with respect
023            to both model learning methods and translation decoding algorithms.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      Despite the efforts on developing context-aware machine learning systems to meaningfully exploit
028
      inter-sentential information, recent work has investigated the fundamental obstacles in existing
029
      document-level translation datasets and context-aware machine translation models (Jin et al., 2023).
030   First, existing datasets lack the necessary contextual information and/or discourse phenomena for
031   meaningful document-level translation (Lupo et al., 2022). Second, existing predominant context-
032   aware translation methods assume that sentence-level alignments are available during training, which
033   does not accurately represent real-world translation scenarios (Thai et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2023).
034
      To remedy the issues, recent work has pivoted to literary translation and proposed a more realistic
035
      paragraph-to-paragraph setting, given that literary texts typically contain complex discourse structures
036   that mandate a document-level frame of reference. Thai et al. (2022) released PAR3, a paragraph-level
037   translation dataset sourced from recently-published 118 novels in 19 languages (about 6 novels per
038   language on average). Jin et al. (2023) curated PARA 2PARA, a small-scale dataset consisting of
039   10,545 parallel paragraphs across six novels. However, these datasets are either in small scale or the
040   reference translations are automatically generated from machine translation systems (e.g. Google
041   Translate (Wu et al., 2016) and fine-tuned GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020)). In addition, there still
042   exist some serious limitations in the paragraph-to-paragraph translation setting, including limited
043   contextual information and equivocal paragraph splits in literary texts.
044   Large language models (LLMs) with decoder-only Transformer architectures have demonstrated
045   outstanding performance as sentence-level translation systems (Vilar et al., 2023; Jiao et al., 2023;
046   Kocmi & Federmann, 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023). In the aspect of context-aware
047   translation, recent studies have employed decoder-only LLMs to translate entire paragraphs using
048   few-shot in-context learning methods, yielding impressive translation quality (Karpinska & Iyyer,
049   2023). However, how to finetune LLMs to process context-aware translation for literary texts in a
050
      more realistic and challenging scenario remains under-explored.
051   In this paper, we propose a more pragmatic and challenging setting for context-aware translation,
052   named chapter-to-chapter (C H 2C H), associated with a carefully curated dataset of Chinese-English
053   literature. The dataset consists of 132 literary books, together with professional translations in
      Chinese. Then we investigate the performance of commonly-used machine translation models under

086   2.1    C ONTEXT- AWARE N EURAL M ACHINE T RANSLATION
087   Sentence-aligned Translation In the sentence-aligned setting of context-aware machine translation,
088   we assume that the source and target sentences in a parallel document are well-aligned. Formally,
089   given a document D comprising a set of source sentences X = {x1 , x2 , ..., xd }, there are the same
090   number of sentences Y = {y1 , y2 , ..., yd } in the target side, which are aligned with sentences in X
091   by the indices. The context-aware neural machine translation (NMT) model computes the probability
092   of translating the source sentence xi conditioned on the context Ci , wherein 0 ≤ i ≤ d:
093                                                           N
                                                              Y
094                            PSentAlign (yi |xi , Ci , θ) =   P (yij |yi<j , xi , Ci ; θ).               (1)
095                                                          j=1

096
      where Ci are contextual sentences surrounding xi and/or yi . As illustrated in Figure 1, sentence-
      aligned translation does not accurately represent real-world translation scenarios.
097
098   Paragraph-to-Paragraph Translation To get rid of the assumption of sentence-level alignments
099   and leverage richer contextual information, recent work (Thai et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2023) proposed
100   a paradigm shift towards paragraph-to-paragraph (PARA 2PARA) translation to relax the alignment
101   assumption from sentence-level to paragraph-level. Concretely, a document D contains a set of
102
      aligned parallel paragraphs, X = {X1 , X2 , ..., Xd } and Y = {Y1 , Y2 , ..., Yd }. Each pair of aligned
      paragraphs Xi and Yi do not necessarily contain the same number of sentences:

107   where Yi<j are all previously translated tokens in a paragraph. However, in literary texts the splits of
      paragraphs are equivocal, which limits the application of PARA 2PARA to real-world scenario.

      2.2        DATASETS
109
110   Most commonly used corpora, including IWSLT-17 (Cettolo et al., 2012), NewsCom (Tiedemann,
111   2012), Europarl (Koehn, 2005), and OpenSubtitles (Lison et al., 2018) are sourced from news articles
112   or parliamentary proceedings. Until recently, some document-level parallel corpora of literary texts
113
      have been released. Jiang et al. (2023) curated Bilingual Web Books (BWB), a sentence-aligned
      corpus that retains document-level information. BWB contains 9.6 million sentence pairs sourced
114
      from Chinese web novels and their corresponding English translations. However, BWB still follows
115
      the sentence-level alignment constrains. To support PARA 2PARA translation, Thai et al. (2022)
116   introduced PAR 3, a paragraph-aligned corpus obtained through both human and automatic translators,
117   containing multilingual non-English novels and their English translations. Another paragraph-aligned
118   corpus, introduced by Al Ghussin et al. (2023), consists of parallel paragraphs extracted from
119   Paracrawl (Bañón et al., 2020) using automatic sentence alignments. This corpus includes data
120   crawled from the Internet spanning various domains.
121
122   2.3        T RANSLATION WITH L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
123   LLMs are not explicitly trained on parallel data for translation, yet they possess a profound under-
124   standing of languages and can produce coherent text, serving as a valuable foundation for translation
125   tasks (Li et al., 2024). Particularly for resource-rich languages, colossal models with decoder-only
126   architecture, such as GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024), have approached or even exceeded traditional
127   encoder-decoder models on sentence-level benchmarks and can generate more coherent and human-
128   like translations drawing upon their extensive comprehension of both languages (Robinson et al.,
129   2023; Hendy et al., 2023). Xu et al. (2023a) proposed a two-stage procedure to finetune Llama2-
130
      7b (Touvron et al., 2023) with a small amount of sentence-level parallel data and obtained impressive
      improvements over standard sentence-level NMT baselines without LLMs.

134   3.1        C HAPTER - TO -C HAPTER T RANSLATION
135
136
      In literary texts, the lengths of paragraphs vary and the splits of paragraphs are equivocal, particularly
      when dialogues are involved. For instance, in novels, dialogue lines are often presented as separate
137
      paragraphs, making it challenging to ensure accurate translations without access to the preceding
138
      context. As illustrated by the two examples shown in Table 1, there are instances where multiple
139   paragraphs from the source side are merged into one paragraph on the target side, and vice versa.
140
141   To address this issue, we propose chapter-to-chapter (C H 2C H) translation, a pragmatic and challeng-
142
      ing setting, by extending context-aware translation to chapter-level. Comparing to paragraph-level
      alignments, chapter-level alignments provide the model with more comprehensive context from both
143
      the source and target texts. This richer context theoretically offers greater potential for improvements
144
      and helps mitigate issues such as tense mismatches, particularly in languages like Chinese that lack
145   explicit tense markers (Sun et al., 2020).
146
147   To conduct experiments and facilitate future research endeavours on C H 2C H translation, we curate
148
      a chapter-aligned dataset of English-Chinese literature, named JAM, which comprises 132 English
      classic novels alongside professional Chinese translations. In professional literary translation, trans-
149
      lators often leverage contexts to enhance the fluency and readability of the translation. To this end,
150
      translations may not strictly adhere to sentence alignment1 , and some typical sentence misalignment
151   types are listed below, an example is shown in Figure 1 illustrates:
152
153       I NSERT : new sentence(s) is added by translators and does not have a corresponding source
154             segment.
155
          D ELETE : a source sentence(s) is deleted by translators in translation.
156
157       S PLIT : a source sentence is separated into multiple sentences in the corresponding translation.
158
      As such, chapter-to-chapter(C H 2C H) translation is challenging in nature, given that chapters typically
159
      are lengthy and contain complex discourse structure. Detailed experimental results and analysis are
160   provided in Section 5.1.

467   6       C ONCLUSION
468
469   While machine translation demonstrates strong sentence-level performance, it still falls short of
470   human translation in effectively utilizing long-context information. In our paper, we show that
471   Chapter-to-Chapter (C H 2C H) translation is a viable approach for context-aware NMT, exemplified
472   by our novel dataset, JAM. Chapter-level data, derived from professional translations, offers richer
473   context signals and presents a more realistic scenario. Through detailed empirical experiments,
474
      we discover that LLMs are aptly suited for C H 2C H translation following a two-step fine-tuning
      process: first at the sentence level, then at the chapter level. This procedure equips LLMs with a
475
      robust understanding of context, resulting in translations that are both coherent and context-aware.
476
      Nevertheless, challenges arise at the chapter level, notably the issue of repetition inheriting from
477   LLMs’ long-context generation, signaling the need for improved long-sequence decoding strategies
478   in future research.
