009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Classical optimization theory requires a small step-size for gradient-based meth-
012            ods to converge. Nevertheless, recent findings Cohen et al. (2021) challenge
013            the traditional idea by empirically demonstrating Gradient Descent (GD) con-
014            verges even when the step-size η exceeds the threshold of 2/L, where L is the
015            global smooth constant. This is usually known as the Edge of Stability (EoS)
016            phenomenon. A widely held belief suggests that an objective function with sub-
017            quadratic growth plays an important role in incurring EoS. In this paper, we pro-
018
               vide a more comprehensive answer by considering the task of finding linear in-
               terpolator β ∈ Rd for regression with loss function l(·), where β admits param-
019                                    2       2
               eterization as β = w+     − w−    . Contrary to the previous work that suggests a
020
               subquadratic l is necessary for EoS, our novel finding reveals that EoS occurs
021            even when l is quadratic under proper conditions. This argument is made rigorous
022            by both empirical and theoretical evidence, demonstrating the GD trajectory con-
023            verges to a linear interpolator in a non-asymptotic way. Moreover, the model un-
024            der quadratic l, also known as a depth-2 diagonal linear network, remains largely
025            unexplored under the EoS regime. Our analysis then sheds some new light on
026            the implicit bias of diagonal linear networks when a larger step-size is employed,
027            enriching the understanding of EoS on more practical models.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   In the past decades, gradient-based optimization methods have become the main engine in the train-
034   ing of deep neural networks. These iterative methods provide efficient and scalable approaches for
035   the minimization of large-scale loss functions. A key question that arises in the context is under what
036   conditions, these algorithms are guaranteed to converge. Classical analysis of gradient descent (GD)
037
      answers the question by asserting that a small step-size should be employed to ensure convergence.
      To be precise, for the minimization of L-smooth objective functions, sufficient condition rules that
038
      step-size η should never come across the critical threshold 2/L (or equivalently L < 2/η). This
039
      guarantees every GD iteration decreases the objective until it converges. As a result, the iterative
040   algorithm can be viewed as a discretization of a continuous ODE called Gradient Flow (GF), and
041   the corresponding convergence behavior is therefore referred to as the GF or stable regime.
042
      Nevertheless, in the work of Cohen et al. (2021), it is observed, in the training process of certain
043
      learning models, GD and other gradient-based methods still converge even when the classical con-
044   dition is violated, allowing for the use of much larger step-sizes. When this occurs, the objective
045   does not exhibit the typical monotonic decrease, and the sharpness, defined as the largest eigen-
046   value of the objective function’s Hessian matrix, frequently exceeds the threshold of 2/η. Unlike
047   the GF regime under small step-size, the GD trajectory often becomes violent, exhibiting oscillating
048   and unstable behavior. Despite this, convergence is still achieved in the long run. This unconven-
049   tional phenomenon is usually known as the Edge of Stability (EoS) regime. Similar results are also
050   observed for algorithms including momentum methods or adaptive methods (Cohen et al., 2022).
051   In the recent several years, the new finding from Cohen et al. (2021) has pioneered many works to in-
052   vestigate the fascinating phenomena, from both empirical and theoretical aspects. Many theoretical
053   works that attempt to explain the mechanism of EoS suggest that the subquadratic growth of the loss
      function plays a crucial role in causing EoS (Chen & Bruna, 2022; Ahn et al., 2022a). In particular,

072   β = β(u, v), a loss l(·) with strictly subquadratic growth leads to the EoS phenomenon in run-

075   In this work, we aim to challenge the notion that subquadratic loss is necessary for causing EoS

078   Our study presents both empirical and theoretical evidence to show that, when a quadratic loss
079   l(a) = a2 /4 is employed, GD with large step-size can converge to a linear interpolator within the
080   EoS regime, provided certain conditions are met. We believe this the first among existing works to
081   suggest that quadratic loss function can trigger EoS and to characterize the convergence.
082   It is important to note that the model we consider is not merely an artificially designed landscape
083   solely to induce EoS under strict conditions. This investigation is related not only to the community
084   exploring the intriguing convergence under irregularly large step-size but also to the broader com-
085   munity tackling implicit bias of gradient methods: when l(·) is quadratic, our framework recovers
086
      the renowned model of depth-2 diagonally linear networks. A diagonal linear network captures the
      key features of deep networks while maintaining a simple structure, as each neuron connects to only
087
      one neuron in the next layer. Therefore, the study on the bias and generalization properties of this
088
      model has resulted in a rich line of important works in the past several years (Woodworth et al.,
089   2020). While it has become well-studied when running GD with a classical step-size, it remains
090   rather unexplored when it enters the EoS regime. We believe our work provides useful insights into
091   the study of implicit bias of diagonal linear networks under large step-size by considering a simple
092   but intuitive one-sample setting.

133   1.2   R ELATED WORKS
134
135
      Edge of Stability. Although the phenomena of oscillation during the training process have been
      observed in several independent works (Xing et al., 2018; Lewkowycz et al., 2020; Jastrzebski et al.,
136
      2021), the name of Edge of Stability was first found in Cohen et al. (2021), which provided more
137
      formal definition and description. Among the theoretical exploration, some works attempt to find
138   criteria that allow EoS to occur on general models (Ma et al., 2022; Damian et al., 2022; Ahn et al.,
139   2022b; Arora et al., 2022). Nevertheless, these works do not provide very convincing arguments
140   because they often incorporate demanding assumptions. An approach that is closer to this paper
141   considers specific models and characterizes the convergence or implicit bias when EoS occurs, in-
142   cluding (Chen & Bruna, 2022; Zhu et al., 2022; Ahn et al., 2022a). Recently, such analysis has been
143   extended to more complicated and more practical models, for instance, logistic regression (Wu et al.,
144   2024), parameterized linear regression (Song & Yun, 2023; Lu et al., 2023) and quadratic models
145   (Chen et al., 2023). It is also worth mentioning that, instead of explaining the unstable convergence
146
      of EoS, some works including Li et al. (2022) studied how the sharpness grows during the early
      phases of the GD trajectory.
147
148
149   Implicit bias of diagonal linear networks. The diagonal linear network model, also called linear
150   regression with quadratic parameterization, is one of the simplest deep network models that exhibit
151
      rich features and bias structure. Vaskevicius et al. (2019); Zhao et al. (2022); Gunasekar et al.
      (2017) were among the first works to explore the implicit bias when the weight admits a Hadamard
152                                                                                              2    2
      parameterization u ⊙ v, which is provably equivalent to the quadratic parameterization w+    − w− .
153
      The seminal work of Woodworth et al. (2020) demonstrated that the scale of initialization decides
154   the transition between rich and kernel regime, and also the recovery of sparse prior for diagonal
155   linear networks. The subsequent works also considered topics such as the connection to Mirror
156   Descent (Gunasekar et al., 2021; Azulay et al., 2021), stochastic GD (Pesme et al., 2021), and
157   limiting initialization (Pesme & Flammarion, 2023). Recently, several papers Nacson et al. (2022);
158   Even et al. (2023); Andriushchenko et al. (2023) attempted to address the bias of (S)GD under the
159   large step-size regime, nevertheless, they failed to establish the convergence when EoS occurs.

      6    C ONCLUSION
516
517   In this paper, we consider the task of finding interpolators for the linear regression with quadratic pa-
518   rameterization and study the convergence of constant step-size GD under the large step-size regime.
519   In particular, we focus on the non-trivial question of whether a quadratic loss can trigger the Edge
520   of Stability (EoS) phenomena or not, which seems unlikely from previous literature. Nevertheless,
521   we show through both empirical and theoretical aspects that, when some certain condition is satis-
522   fied, EoS indeed occurs given quadratic loss. We hope this novel result takes a step further toward
523
      understanding the intriguing phenomena of unstable convergence.
