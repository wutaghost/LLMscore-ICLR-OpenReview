009                                                              A BSTRACT
010
011               Long-context models (LCMs) have shown great potential in processing long in-
012               put sequences (even more than 100M tokens) conveniently and effectively. With
013               significant progress, recent research has pointed out that LCMs can accurately
014               locate token-level salient information within the context. Yet, the generation per-
015               formance of these LCMs is far from satisfactory and might result in misaligned
016
                  responses, such as hallucinations. To enhance the generation capability of LCMs,
                  existing works have investigated the effects of data size and quality for both pre-
017
                  training and instruction tuning. Though achieving meaningful improvement, pre-
018
                  vious methods fall short in either effectiveness or efficiency. In this paper, we in-
019               troduce LOGO (Long cOntext aliGnment via efficient preference Optimization),
020               a training strategy that first introduces preference optimization for long-context
021               alignment. To overcome the GPU memory-bound issue caused by the long se-
022               quence, LOGO employs a reference-free preference optimization strategy and
023               adopts a position synthesis method to construct the training data. By training with
024               only 0.3B data on a single 8×A800 GPU machine for 16 hours, LOGO allows the
025               Llama-3-8B-Instruct-80K model to achieve comparable performance with GPT-4
026               in real-world long-context tasks while preserving the model’s original capabilities
027
                  on other tasks, e.g., language modeling and MMLU. Moreover, LOGO can extend
                  the model’s context window size while enhancing its generation performance.

051   1     I NTRODUCTION
052
053   With the rapid advancements of Large Language Models (LLMs), handling long contexts (even more
      than 100M tokens (anthropic, 2024)) has become a fundamental capability for recent LLMs. This

      further unlocks the potential of LLMs for novel tasks and applications, e.g., code analysis (Zhu et al.,
055   2024), while simultaneously eliminating the need for complex toolchains and intricate workflows
056   that were previously required to overcome the context-length constraints (Ravaut et al., 2024).
057
058
      Yet, recent studies have pointed out that these long-context models (LCMs) failed to achieve sat-
      isfactory performance in long-context tasks, where LCMs might produce misaligned results, such
059
      as instruction unfollowing and hallucinations (Belyi et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a). To mitigate
060
      the above issue, the open-source community has made significant efforts, primarily focusing on
061   building high-quality long instruction data and extending the data size (Wu et al., 2024a; Bai et al.,
062   2024; Fu et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2024). As shown in Fig. 1, though achieving meaningful improve-
063   ment, these methods fall short in effectiveness or efficiency. For instance, the Llama-3.1-8B-128K
064   model AI@Meta (2024a) was pre-trained on around 300B long instruction data, but it even underper-
065   forms the Llama-3-8B-Instruct-80K model (Zhang et al., 2024b), which was post-trained with 1.5B
066   high-quality long instruction data based on the Llama-3-8B-Instruct model (AI@Meta, 2024b). As
067   for the Llama-3-8B-Instruct-80K model, it shows slight improvement compared to the baseline and
068   still lags greatly behind the closed-source counterparts like GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023).
069   Recently, Wu et al. (2024b) pointed out that LCMs can accurately locate token-level salient infor-
070   mation within the context. As shown in Fig. 1(b), we visualize the information retrieval capabil-
071   ity1 (reflected by the retrieval score) and the generation capability (reflected by the recall score) of
072   different LCMs on the synthetic retrieval task, where we can observe a minimal difference among
073   the retrieval scores from various LCMs, but large differences in their generation performance. This
074   suggests that while LCMs are adept at identifying key information within long contexts, they strug-
075
      gle to effectively utilize the retrieval information for generation. The underlying cause might be the
      commonly used training approach of LCMs, which relies on token-level maximum likelihood loss,
076
      i.e., Cross-Entropy (CE) loss, calculated on both the context and the predictions. Given that the con-
077
      text’s sequence length is typically much longer than the prediction portion, the feedback signal (CE
078   loss) from the prediction is often overshadowed by that from the context. As a result, the CE loss
079   becomes ineffective in optimizing the generation capabilities of LCMs.
080
081
      To effectively optimize LCMs for generating desired outputs and avoid misaligned results, this pa-
      per introduces LOGO (Long cOntext aliGnment via efficient preference Optimization), the first
082
      training strategy that incorporates preference optimization for long-context alignment. There are
083
      two key components in LOGO: (1) a training objective designed to guide LCMs to distinguish be-
084   tween preference predictions (i.e., correct outputs) and dis-preference predictions (e.g., misaligned
085   outputs like hallucinations), and (2) a corresponding data construction pipeline that only involves
086   open-source models. It is worth noting that training with long sequence data is a memory-intensive
087   task (Dao, 2023) and the DPO algorithm also has a high GPU memory demand. To overcome the
088   GPU memory-bound and improve the training efficiency, LOGO adopts a reference-free training ob-
089   jective and the positional indices synthesis method (Zhu et al., 2023). Consequently, we can perform
090   the LOGO training with only 0.3B data on a single 8×A800 GPU machine within 16 hours.
091   By training with LOGO, LCMs can achieve significant improvements in real-world tasks and gain
092   moderate improvements in synthetic and language modeling tasks, as well as maintaining good per-
093   formance on the short-context tasks, e.g., MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020). As shown in Figure 1(a),
094   our Llama-3-8B-LOGO significantly outperforms GPT3.5-Turbo in real-world tasks and approaches
095   the performance of some top closed-source models like GPT-4. Additionally, LOGO can also gen-
096   eralize to the training of short-context LLMs such as Llama-2-7B-Chat-4K (Touvron et al., 2023),
097   which can potentially extend their context window size up to 8 times (e.g.,32K context window size
098
      for Llama-2-7B-Chat-4K) while simultaneously enhancing their performance substantially.

127   2.2   M ODEL A LIGNMENT WITH D IRECT P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION
128
129   Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) is a widely adopted RLHF algo-
130
      rithm (Ouyang et al., 2022) that aims to align models with human preferences. Compared to
      other reinforcement learning methods, e.g., PPO (Schulman et al., 2017), DPO can achieve strong
131
      performance while eliminating the need for a separate reward model. Unlike Supervised Fine-
132
      Tuning (SFT), which guides LLMs to fit predictions to ground truth at the token level, DPO up-
133   dates the model parameters with discrete evaluation scores. Specifically, DPO teaches the model to
134   “reject” misaligned responses and “accept” preferred responses with differently assigned prediction
135   scores. Significant efforts have been made to enhance the effectiveness and efficiency of DPO, such
136   as CPO (Xu et al., 2024), TPO (Saeidi et al., 2024), and ORPO (Hong et al., 2024). Among them,
137   SimPO (Meng et al., 2024) utilizes the average log probability of a sequence as the implicit reward,
138   which better aligns with the generation tasks and eliminates the need for a reference model.

142   3.1   BACKGROUND
143
144   Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) and Simple Preference Optimization (SimPO) DPO
145   is one of the most popular offline preference optimization strategies in RLHF (Rafailov et al., 2024).
146   Given prompt x, DPO aims to maximize the likelihood of a preferred response yw over a dis-
147   preferred one yl , thereby preventing the model from generating undesired content. There are three
148
      essential modules in the DPO training process: one reference model and one policy model for cal-
      culating the DPO loss jointly, and one evaluation strategy (or evaluation model) for distinguishing
149
      between yw and yl . SimPO (Meng et al., 2024) is an improved variant of DPO, which employs
150
      an implicit reward formulation that directly aligns with the generation metric, e.g., PPL, thereby
151   eliminating the need for a reference model. The training objective of SimPO can be written as:

      3.2.1          T RAINING O BJECTIVE OF LOGO
187   In long-context scenarios, LCMs are prone to generating various misaligned responses, such as
188   hallucinations and failing to follow instructions (Belyi et al., 2024). However, there is a lack of
189   effective strategies (or models) to detect these misaligned outputs, posting a great challenge for
190
      selecting preference and dis-preference samples in preference optimization (we will elucidate this
      in Appendix C, where we also show the misalignment cases). Therefore, instead of finding one dis-
191   preference instance with a specific error pattern, we can expand the dis-preference space to push the
192   model away from a range of possible dis-preference instances. We design the loss function based
193   on SimPO (Eq. 1), as it is more aligned with the generation tasks and free of the reference model,
194   which is efficient for long-context training. The training objective can be written as:

207   3.2.2          T RAINING DATASET C ONSTRUCTION OF LOGO
208   To perform the LOGO training, we introduce a tailored LOGO dataset construction pipeline. For
209   each long-context sample, we can format it as a triplet X = {Q, C, P }, where Q, C, and P
210   represent the question, reference context, and the model prediction, respectively. As shown in
211   Fig. 2, to construct training data for LOGO, we first divide the context C into equal-length chunks
212   {C1 , C2 , · · · , Cn }. Then, three steps are involved: (1) Importance Scoring with Automatic Evalua-
213   tor, (2) Preference and Dis-preference Data Synthesis, and (3) Positional Indices Synthesis.
214
215   Importance Scoring with Automatic Evaluator To construct preference (aligned) and dis-
      preference (misaligned) data in long-context scenarios, an efficient method is to guide the model


                                                                                               4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      to respond based on different contexts. Specifically, to construct the preference data, we only pro-
217   vide the model with context relevant to the question, thus enhancing the fidelity of the model’s
218   output by reducing contextual interference (Shi et al., 2023). Conversely, we can add more irrel-
219   evant context to guide the model in generating misaligned content like hallucinations. To find the
220   relevant chunks Ci within the context, we utilize an automatic evaluator Eval(·) to calculate the
221   “contribution” of each chunk Ci to the question Q. Specifically, we utilize an Eval(·) to identify
222   all the entities within a chunk Ci . The more overlapping entities Ci shares with the question Q, the
223   greater its influence on the final prediction, allowing us to assign a higher score to this chunk. With
224   Eval(·), we efficiently assign importance scores S = {s1 , s2 , · · · , sn } to all the chunks.
225
226   Preference and Dis-preference Data Synthesis To construct preference and dis-preference data
227
      based on the model prediction P , we select and combine the chunks mentioned above to create
      diverse contexts, guiding the model to generate different outputs. Let N represent the number of

      chunks within a context, and we define a threshold δ to distinguish between critical and irreverent
229

316   Results on Real-world Long-context Tasks We evaluate the LOGO performance with real-world

      long-context tasks in LongBench (Bai et al., 2023), a comprehensive benchmark suite encompassing

506
        We study with two settings of synthetic data length, i.e., from real input length 4K to target length
507
        64K (Ctx. = 4K) and from real input length 8K to target length 64K (Ctx. = 8K). Specifically,
508

510     diminishes the model’s performance on both the language modeling task and real-world tasks (data
511     point (M = 2, λ = 0.1, Ctx . = 4K) versus data point (M = 2, λ = 0.1, Ctx . = 8K)), but can still
512     overcome the instruction tuning method (42.8 average score on LongBench) and effectively reduces

514     length is relatively small (4K), it requires scaling up by a larger factor (16 times) to reach the desired
515     context length (64K). During the positional indices synthesis process, some positional indices may
516     miss or be infrequently activated, thereby impacting performance.

518     5.3           C OMPARISON BETWEEN SFT AND LOGO
519
520
        As shown in Fig. 7, we illustrate the impact of SFT (with two loss calculation strategies follow-
        ing (Xiong et al., 2023)) and LOGO on the model’s generation and understanding performance
521
        throughout the training process. We plot the trends of retrieval score (understanding ability) and re-
522
        call score (generation ability) along the training progress. We can observe that applying SFT loss to
523     the entire sequence leads to a gradual decline in the LCM’s understanding ability, accompanied by
524     performance fluctuations; while applying SFT loss solely to the prediction portion shows no signifi-
525     cant improvement in model performance. Nevertheless, applying LOGO can steer LCMs away from
526     misaligned samples, thereby enhancing the recall score. Simultaneously, it improves comprehension
527     abilities, enabling the model to retrieve more key information within the context.
528
529     6           C ONCLUSION
530
531
        In this paper, we find that commonly used training approaches for LCMs may degrade the model’s
        generation capabilities, leading to misaligned outputs, such as hallucinations and instruction unfol-
532
        lowing. To mitigate this issue, we introduce LOGO, a novel preference optimization training strat-
533
        egy for long-context alignment. Specifically, LOGO has two key components: (1) a reference-free
534     preference optimization objective that teaches the model to distinguish between the preference and
535     the dis-preference predictions, and (2) a data construction pipeline tailored for the training objective,
536     both of which are designed to ensure the training efficiency and effectiveness. By performing LOGO
537     training on a single 8×A800 GPU machine within 16 hours, LCMs can achieve great improvements
538     in long-context tasks while maintaining their inherent capabilities. Besides, LOGO can also poten-
539     tially scale the context length of short-context models and achieve better generation performance
        compared to other frequently used context scaling methods.

      A    L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
757
758
      This paper presents an efficient preference optimization training strategy (LOGO) tailored for long-
759   context alignment. However, there are several limitations:
760
761          • Due to resource constraints within the academic community, the evaluation of real-world
762            testing sets in LongBench may be affected by the varying prompts selected by different
763            studies, which can lead to significant discrepancies in results. Consequently, we are unable
764            to directly replicate the results from other works
765          • As mentioned in the main body (Sec. 3.2.2), there remains a lack of suitable evaluation
766            models to assess whether the outputs of LCMs are accurate or contain hallucinations. The
767            LOGO training objective proposed in this paper still has room for improvement.
768          • During the data construction phase, utilizing higher-quality datasets could yield better out-
769            comes. However, as an academic paper, we believe we have demonstrated the generaliz-
770            ability of our method through the main experiments.
771
772   Moving forward, we plan to continue our research along the lines of efficient long-context align-
773   ment, particularly in algorithm development. We aim to explore the integration of more effective
774
      evaluation strategies, such as RAG checkers (Ru et al., 2024), to assist in constructing preference
      and dis-preference instances. Additionally, we should investigate how to enhance the efficiency of
775
      our LOGO data construction pipeline across various tasks and domains.
776
777   In summary, this paper highlights the substantial potential of efficient training in long-context sce-
778   narios, and we hope our work will provide valuable insights for future research endeavors.

839   Misaligned predictions generated from LCMs can be specifically categorized into two types: failing
840   to follow instructions and generating hallucinations. In Fig. 9, we illustrate these two error patterns.
841   Specifically, we define different error patterns by utilizing the degree of overlap between entities in
842   the responses and the questions, along with specific templates:
843
844          • Instruction Unfollow: the entities in the model’s responses do not overlap with the entities
845
               in the question.
846
             • Hallucination: there is a partial intersection of entities between the model’s responses and
               the question, and the entities in the response coincide with the main subject of the question.
847
848   It is worth mentioning that merely utilizing Named Entity Recognition (NER) models and rule-
849   based methods proves inadequate for identifying these patterns. Instead, a more robust evaluation
850   involving strong LLMs such as GPT-4 or human assessment is required to accurately identify these
851   patterns. Consequently, in the design of the LOGO training objective, we do not confine to con-
852   structing cases with specific error patterns. Therefore, instead of finding one dis-preference instance
853   with a specific error pattern, we can expand the dis-preference space to push the model away from a
854   range of possible dis-preference instances.

1059   approach allows the model to learn a more comprehensive representation of undesirable outputs,
1060   improving its ability to reject a broader range of negative samples and helping the model to learn

1069   leading to faster and more robust convergence. Besides, the use of a logistic loss function with
