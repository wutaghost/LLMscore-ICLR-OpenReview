010
011
               Existing alignment techniques, including Direct Preference Optimization (DPO),
012
               are primarily designed for pairwise preference data where rewards are inferred
013
               rather than explicitly provided. In this paper, we propose a comprehensive frame-
014            work for aligning large language models (LLMs) by introducing a new optimiza-
015            tion objective that facilitates the processing of reward datasets, which consist of a
016            list of responses explicitly marked with scalar preference scores. Our contribution
017            includes the development of a novel algorithm, termed Soft Preference Optimiza-
018            tion (LPO), which allows for the direct derivation of an LLM policy from both
019            reward and preference datasets. At the heart of LPO is a unique listwise prefer-
020            ence optimization objective formulated using an exponential-logarithmic function
021            and an adaptive loss coefficient, which effectively integrates listwise preference
022
               signals into the LLM. We assess the efficacy of our approach under both reward
               and preference scenarios using different sizes of Mistral models. Experimental re-
023
               sults indicate that our method outperforms several preference-based benchmarks,
024
               particularly when reward datasets are utilized. Additionally, our method demon-
025            strates a significant advantage over DPO in intricate reasoning tasks, such as math-
026            ematical problem-solving and coding.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Aligning pretrained Language Models (LMs) with scalar rewards that encapsulate human intentions
033   is essential for improving their compliance with given instructions (Ouyang et al., 2022; OpenAI,
034   2023). Such rewards can be imparted either explicitly or implicitly. Explicit rewards include scalar
035   evaluations from human assessors or sophisticated models such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), whereas
036   implicit rewards come in the form of preference judgments made between pairs of responses. An
037   effective method for aligning LMs using preference data is Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)
038   (Rafailov et al., 2024b). DPO introduces a reward-training loss by parameterizing the reward model
039
      as the likelihood ratio of responses generated by two LMs, thereby enabling simultaneous training
      of the reward model and extraction of the LM’s policy. This methodology is more efficient than
040
      conventional Reinforcement Learning (RL) techniques (Ouyang et al., 2022), which generally ne-
041
      cessitate a two-step procedure: initial training of the reward model followed by the derivation of the
042   LM’s policy.
043
044   Despite its simplicity and effectiveness, DPO is designed specifically for preference data (x →
045
      {yw > yl }) with K = 2 responses per instruction x. When faced with multiple responses, di-
      rectly assigning a scalar reward to each response is generally more convenient and efficient than
046
      conducting pairwise comparisons. However, the resultant reward datasets (x → {(yi , ri )}1:K ) can-
047
      not be directly utilized for DPO training. Prior studies (Tunstall et al., 2023) typically address this
048   by pruning the reward dataset, selecting the top response and then pairing it randomly with another
049   response. This approach is suboptimal since it discards both the reward values and additional non-
050   preferred responses during the data preprocessing phase. Furthermore, a commonly observed issue
051   with DPO is the tendency for the likelihood of the preferred response to diminish over the course
052   of training (Pal et al., 2024; Rafailov et al., 2024a). Our analysis indicates that this phenomenon
053   primarily stems from DPO’s emphasis on adjusting the relative likelihood among various responses
      for each given instruction.

054
      To tackle these challenges, we introduce Listwise Preference Optimization (LPO), an alignment
055   technique that facilitates the direct extraction of language model (LM) policies from both reward
056   and preference datasets, accommodating any number of responses. Notably, LPO encompasses
057   the Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) loss as a special instance within the context of pairwise
058   preferences, thereby positioning itself as a natural progression of DPO. While DPO operates as a
059   single-pair ranking loss, LPO extends this concept to a listwise framework. Unlike DPO, which
060   relies on the assumptions of the Bradley-Terry model, LPO is grounded in the principles outlined by
061   Burges et al. (2006). Additionally, we introduce LPO-abs, an alternative approach to LPO, which
062   incorporates a regularization term aimed at addressing the diminishing reward problem. LPO-abs
063
      diverges from LPO solely in its loss formulation but remains applicable to both preference and
      reward datasets.
064
065   We assess our methodology using the Mistral-7B models from two perspectives. When reward
066   datasets (Cui et al.) are accessible, we demonstrate that directly employing our reward-based align-
067   ment yields a significant enhancement over preference-based algorithms, achieving superior evalua-
068   tion scores when judged by the GPT-4 model. This improvement can be attributed to LPO/LPO-abs’s
069
      capability to effectively utilize additional sub-optimal responses. In scenarios where only preference
      data is available (Yuan et al., 2024b), we benchmark the LPO-abs method against the DPO loss. Our
070
      comprehensive experimental results across multiple benchmarks indicate that LPO-abs surpasses
071
      DPO in handling intricate reasoning tasks, including mathematical problems and coding challenges.
072
073   Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
074
                • We introduce LPO, a preference optimization method grounded in reward datasets. Addi-
075
                  tionally, LPO-abs addresses the data likelihood decline issue inherent in DPO. These two
076
                  proposed methods are particularly well-suited for both reward and preference data, provid-
077               ing a comprehensive framework that integrates preference-based algorithms.
078
079
                • We perform extensive experiments using both reward and preference datasets, illustrating
                  that our methods significantly outperform various preference-based approaches by fully
080
                  leveraging the information contained in reward datasets.

085   Development of language models.         Recently, the development of language models has under-
086   gone significant changes (Zhao et al., 2023), evolving from initial rule-based approaches to to-
087   day’s data-driven deep learning models. A milestone advancement began in 2018 when Google
088   introduced the BERT model (Devlin et al., 2019) with a Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al.,
089
      2017), which achieved remarkable results in various natural language processing tasks through un-
      supervised training. Following this, even larger models like GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020), ChatGPT
090
      (Ouyang et al., 2022) and GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) further propelled this trend, not only reaching
091
      unprecedented scales with hundreds of billions of parameters but also demonstrating versatility and
092   flexibility across a diverse range of NLP tasks. These advancements have spurred researchers to
093   explore applications of LLMs in areas such as machine translation, text summarization, dialogue
094   systems, and even multimodal understanding.
095
      While it is important to develop large language models in the size of 100 billions, the even wider
096
      applications rely on the relatively smaller sized models. These compact models, while perhaps not
097
      matching the sheer breadth of knowledge or depth of understanding afforded by their gargantuan
098   counterparts (Zeng, 2023), offer significant advantages in terms of deployment flexibility, com-
099   putational efficiency, and accessibility across diverse platforms and devices, including those with
100   limited resources. Consequently, efforts aimed at refining smaller models to optimize both efficacy
101   and practicality are critical for broadening the reach and utility of NLP solutions in everyday con-
102   texts. Recently, the community has witnessed the releases of open-sourced models like LlaMA-2
103   7B (Touvron et al., 2023), Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023). These models can run an edge device like
104   laptop or cellphone, after being deployed under frameworks like Ollama1 .
105   Alignment of large language model. The alignment of large language models (LLMs) is critical
106   for ensuring they operate ethically, accurately, and responsibly Shen et al. (2023). Proper alignment

189   4.3   A BSOLUTE VALUE REGULARIZATION
190
191   A notable issue with DPO is that the likelihood of all responses consistently decreases throughout
192   the training process (Pal et al., 2024; Rafailov et al., 2024a). We observe that LPO exhibits this same
193   trend due to their inherent equivalence. This reduction in data likelihood is undesirable because it
194   directly contradicts the maximum likelihood objective of supervised training and may ultimately
195   impair performance (Yuan et al., 2024b).
196   We hypothesize the main cause of this decreasing likelihood is that LPO methods only adjust relative
197   rewards among responses, rather than optimizing their absolute value. To address this problem,
198   we propose LPO-abs, a variant to LPO. LPO-abs can also guarantee convergence to the optimal
199   LM policy by directly learning the absolute reward for each response, thereby counteracting the
200   decreasing likelihood trend. Formally,

      Reward datasets We employ the UltraFeedback (Cui et al.) dataset, an instruction-following
242
      dataset annotated by GPT-4. UltraFeedback aims at advancing the alignment of large language
243   models (LLMs) with human preferences through the use of large-scale, high-quality, and diversi-
244   fied feedback datasets. Utilizing a vast dataset of approximately 64,000 prompts and 256,000 re-
245   sponses from various high-quality sources, UltraFeedback evaluates LLM responses on criteria such
246   as instruction-following, truthfulness, honesty, and helpfulness. GPT-4 rates each response with a
247   scalar reward on a scale of 0-10. Prior research indicates that these GPT-4 rewards closely align with
248   human annotations (Zheng et al., 2023), establishing them as an efficient, cost-effective alternative
249   to human feedback. This dataset is used to train models to better serve user needs while maintaining
250   ethical standards.
251   Evaluation datasets We choose the well-acknowledged and widely used GPT4-based bench-
252   marks as follows for evaluating LLMs:
253
254            • MT-bench (Zheng et al., 2023). MT-Bench is a benchmark designed to evaluate the per-
255              formance of chat assistants across multiple turns of conversation and various categories of
256
                 tasks. It consists of 80 multi-turn questions covering common use cases that are challenging
                 enough to differentiate among models. These questions are categorized into eight types of
257
                 user prompts, including Writing, Roleplay, Extraction, Reasoning, Math, Coding, STEM,
258
                 and Humanities/Social Science. Each category includes 10 multi-turn questions crafted
259              carefully by experts. To assess the models’ performance on MT-Bench, strong language
260              models (LLMs) are used as judges, and the results indicate that these LLM judges, partic-
261              ularly GPT-4, can align well with both controlled and crowdsourced human preferences,
262              achieving over 80% agreement, which is comparable to the level of agreement between
263              humans. Thus, MT-Bench serves as a comprehensive tool to measure the effectiveness of
264              chat assistants, complementing traditional benchmarks.
265            • AlpacaEval (Dubois et al., 2024) is an LLM-based automated evaluation metric consisting
266              of a set of 805 instructions that reflect typical user interactions on the Alpaca web demo.
267              Both a baseline model, typically GPT-4 turbo, and the model under evaluation generate
268              responses for each instruction, which are then compared side-by-side by another GPT-4
269              turbo-based evaluator that outputs the likelihood of preferring the evaluated model’s re-
                 sponse. The metric calculates a win rate, representing the expected probability that the

      Baselines    We exam the following baseline methods:
287
288         • DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024b). To apply the DPO method with reward datasets in which
289           more than two responses are annotated per instruction, we conduct the data transformation
290           according to Zephyr (Tunstall et al., 2023). DPO selects the highest reward response and
291           a random remaining one from UltraFeedback for each instruction. This procedure discards
292           two additional suboptimal responses in the dataset as well as their reward information.
293         • DPO-1vsO. Comparing the above procedure of constructing DPO dataset, one might pre-
294           dict that applying the DPO to a more grained preference dataset would yield better results.
295           To investigate this, we examined a variants of DPO that utilize all available responses in
296           UltraFeedback by pair the highest-performing response with each of the other ones sepa-
297           rately.
298         • DPO-pw. This variant utilizes all available responses in UltraFeedback by summing up all
299           DPO loss possibilities for two out of all the responses.
300         • KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024). Using a Kahneman-Tversky model of human utility, KTO
301           propose a novel method that directly maximizes the utility of generations instead of maxi-
302           mizing the log-likelihood of preferences, as current methods do.
303         • IPO (Azar et al., 2024). IPO proposes a new general objective for learning from human
304           preferences that is expressed in terms of pairwise preferences and therefore bypasses both
305           approximations.

      5.3   F URTHER ANALYSIS
411
412
      Visualization of likelihood for the chosen and rejected To illustrate the distinct optimization
413
      characteristics that lead to performance differences between LPO-abs and DPO, we examine how
414   the data likelihood evolves during training. The results are presented in Figure 1. As shown in
415   Figure 1, the likelihood of preferred responses intriguingly decreases after DPO training, whereas it
416   increases with LPO-abs training. The trend of decreasing chosen likelihood is concerning because it
417   directly contradicts the maximum-likelihood objective utilized during the SFT stage. This drawback
418   is particularly pronounced in reasoning tasks, where the preferred response is often the ground truth
419   answer. Consequently, we hypothesize that LPO-abs exhibits superior performance in reasoning
420   tasks due to its ability to avoid reducing the likelihood of chosen responses. Since DPO can be
421   considered a specialization of LPO, the contrasting likelihood trends can be theoretically explained.
422
      Specifically, LPO-abs adjusts the absolute likelihood of data, whereas DPO and LPO focus only on
      the relative likelihood across different responses. Therefore, a declining chosen likelihood directly
423
      conflicts with LPO-abs’s training objective but not with DPO’s.
424
425   Effects of the number of responses K Figure 2 plots how different values of K affects the
426   LLM alignment performance. From Figure 2, we observe consistent performance improvements
427   when increasing the number of data responses from K = 2 to K = 4 for both LPO and LPO-
428
      abs algorithms. In addition, we observe the LPO-abs outperforms LPO under different settings,
      demonstrating the importance of maintaining the absolute values for the best response’s reward.
429
430   Figure 2 also conveys an important take-away. Previous practices always ensure selecting the highest
431   performing response when constructing preference data. The assumption behind this strategy is
      that the dataset’s best-performing response determines the upper limit of alignment performance.

451   6   C ONCLUSION
452
453
      In this work, we conduct a thorough investigation into the language model alignment problem within
454
      the context of explicit reward settings. Drawing inspiration from listwise rank learning, we introduce
455
      two novel and practical algorithms: LPO and LPO-abs. Our proposed methods are uniquely suited
456   for both reward data and preference data, encompassing DPO as a special case. Our experiments
457   demonstrate that our reward-based alignment methods, LPO and LPO-abs, outperform preference-
458   based baselines such as DPO and KTO by fully leveraging sub-optimal responses in reward datasets.
459   In preference settings, the pairwise version of LPO-abs surpasses DPO in complex reasoning tasks
460   by effectively preventing the likelihood of preferred responses from decreasing.
