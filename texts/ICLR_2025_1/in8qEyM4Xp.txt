009                                             Abstract
010
011           With the rapid advancement of conversational and diffusion-based AI, there
012           is a growing adoption of AI in educational services, ranging from grading
013           and assessment tools to personalized learning systems that provide tar-
014           geted support for students. However, this adaptability has yet to fully
015           extend to the domain of children’s speech, where existing models often
016           fail due to their reliance on datasets designed for clear, articulate adult
017           speech. Children, particularly those in early developmental stages or with
018
              speech and language pathologies, present unique challenges that current AI
              models and datasets are ill-equipped to handle. To address this, we intro-
019
              duce KidSpeak, a multi-task speech-enhanced Foundation Model capable
020
              of both generative and discriminative tasks specifically tailored to children’s
021           speech patterns. Our framework employs a two-stage training process that
022           incorporates phonetic knowledge into the speech encoder, achieving an av-
023           erage accuracy of 87% across four separate tasks. Furthermore, recognizing
024           the limitations of scalable human annotation and existing speech align-
025           ment tools, we propose the Flexible and Automatic Speech Aligner
026           (FASA) and leverage the method to construct high quality datasets for
027           training and evaluation. This novel alignment tool significantly improves
028           the quality of aligned children’s speech from noisy data, enhancing data
029
              quality by 13.6× compared to human annotations, as demonstrated on the
              CHILDES dataset. To the best of our knowledge, KidSpeak and FASA
030
              represent the first comprehensive solution designed for speech and language
031
              therapy in children, offering both a multi-purpose speech LLM and a robust
032           alignment tool. Code is available at Here.

035   1   Introduction
036
037   Humans begin to acquire the fundamental cues of vocal communication as early as 3 months
038   of age (USDHHS et al., 2017). As development advances, some individuals master their
039   vocal abilities to such a degree that they are capable of vocalizing with over 1000 kHz,
040   allowing for the conveyance of complex and nuanced ideas and emotions Garnier et al.
041   (2010)1 . On the other hand, hearing begins as early as the 28th week of gestation in hu-
042   mans (Querleu et al., 1988), eventually leading to an auditory capacity capable of discerning
043
      frequencies as precise as 0.5 Hz within a range of 20 Hz to 20 kHz (Romand & Varela-Nieto,
      2014). Despite these remarkable developmental milestones, numerous challenges persist in
044
      early speech acquisition. In fact, nearly 1 in 12 children in the U.S. aged 3 to 17 has
045
      experienced a disorder affecting voice, speech, language, or swallowing, with almost half of
046   them not receiving any intervention services in the past year (Black et al., 2015). These
047   statistics highlight that, despite our advanced auditory and vocal capabilities, we continue
048   to face significant barriers in the early diagnosis and treatment of speech-related disorders.
049
      The setbacks are further compounded by the dearth of data pertaining to kids’ speech
050
      and vocalizations, which poses additional challenges towards the development of automated
051
      computational tools. Consequently, the current state-of-the-art ASR systems remain lim-

      2   Related Work
116
      Availability of data over the visual and descriptive-visual domain has spurred a preponder-
117
      ance of work towards understanding the visual domain. We witness a similar emergence in
118
      the aural understanding domain. Herein we provide an abridged summary of the contem-
119   porary work relevant to this manuscript. A more detailed description of the literature is
120   provided in the Section A.1 under the Appendix.
121
122
      Speech-based LLMs and Spoken Language Understanding: Challenges in encoding
      speech with LLMs stem from handling long sequences of audio. GSLM (Lakhotia et al.,
123
      2021), TWIST (Hassid et al., 2024), and SpeechGPT (Zhang et al., 2023) address them by
124
      using quantized speech representations using models such as HuBERT (Hsu et al., 2021).
125   While others employ log-mel spectrograms to develop representations which are then com-
126   bined with textual data for multi-modal generative tasks, such as speech recognition, gen-
127   eration and understanding (Fathullah et al., 2024; Nachmani et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023;
128   Gong et al., 2023), using ASR models such as Whisper (Radford et al., 2023), Wav2Vec
129   (Baevski et al., 2020), Conformer (Gulati et al., 2020), and AST (Gong et al., 2021), or
130   using multimodal retrieval based models such as ImageBind (Girdhar et al., 2023) like the
131   PandaGPT (Su et al., 2023). Our work innovates techniques essential for processing and
132   understanding of kids’ speech.
133   Kids’ Speech: A key limitation of current works is the insufficient handling of nuanced
134   speech variations, such as accents, dialects, intonations, and developmental or disordered
135   speech, as is typical in children. The field of children’s speech recognition remains under-
136   researched, with only a few notable approaches, such as LSTM-based disfluency detection
137   (Venkatasubramaniam et al., 2023) and teacher-student models (Plantinga & Fosler-Lussier,
138   2019). To the best of our knowledge, this work is the first to propose leveraging LLMs
139
      as multi-task models with diagnostic capabilities for children’s speech, offering substantial
      potential in the domain of speech therapy and supporting Speech-Language Pathologists.
140
141   Forced-Alignment Toolkits Traditional audio-transcription alignment relies on human
142   annotators (Boersma & Weenink, 2007; Grover et al., 2020), which is not scalable for large
143   datasets. While Kisler et al. (2017) offers components of a forced-alignment pipeline, it does
144   not solve the alignment issue. Sheng et al. (2019) uses GANs for data augmentation in chil-
145
      dren’s ASR datasets but does not introduce new data. Several studies utilize human-labeled
      transcriptions for forced-alignment (McAuliffe et al., 2017; Rodd et al., 2021; Zhang et al.,
146
      2023; Liu et al., 2023), with the Montreal Forced Aligner (MFA) being prominent (McAuliffe
147
      et al., 2017). However, MFA demands perfect alignment, limiting its effectiveness. Our work
148   improves over existing works and over human annotators by margins of over 13×.

      4.1   FASA Design
338
339   Given a non-timestamped audio file and its noisy or incomplete transcription, forced align-
340   ment generates time-stamped audio segments paired with high-quality transcriptions. Kid-
341   Speak, like many modern automatic speech recognition systems, requires input audio to be
342   divided into smaller segments during training. For instance, the Whisper model (Radford
343   et al., 2022) pads or trims audio inputs to 30 seconds. Consequently, when associating a
344   non-timestamped transcription with a lengthy audio file, a forced-alignment toolkit is essen-
345   tial for creating a model-compatible dataset. Formally, the forced-alignment task involves
346
      an audio sample containing n utterances, A := {A1 , A2 , ...An }, and a transcription of m
      “words,” T := {T1 , T2 , ...Tm }. A “word” in T represents a fundamental unit of transcrip-
347
      tion, which may refer to a sentence, a single word, or a phonetic symbol. The goal is to
348
      associate each Ai with its corresponding words in T , from Tsi to Tei , or indicate that Ai
349   lacks a transcription in T . We denote this association as Ai = (Tsi , Tei ). A robust auto-
350   alignment system should exhibit two crucial features. First, it must not assume that if
351   Ai = (Tsi , Tei ) and Aj = (Tsj , Tej ) with i < j, then ei < sj; an utterance appearing earlier
352   in the audio does not guarantee its early appearance in the transcription. Second, Ai may
353   lack a corresponding (Tsi , Tei ), implying that Ai = ∅. This means not all audio segments
354   have transcriptions, and some audio may remain untranscribed. Similarly, Tk ∈ T does not
355   imply Tk ∈ A; not every word in the transcription corresponds to an audio segment. These
356   features are essential as they relax the need for completeness and order in the provided
357
      transcription, mirroring more realistic scenarios. While a common method for obtaining
      large datasets of paired audio and transcriptions is through Internet scraping, many online
358
      transcriptions are noisy and incomplete, often with missing or misordered entries. Under
359
      these conditions, existing forced-alignment toolkits, such as those proposed by McAuliffe
360   et al. (2017), are inadequate. Further details are discussed in Appendix A.1.

366   4.2   Workflow
367
      FASA follows a five-module pipeline to automatically segment, label, and align a long audio
368
      file with its transcription, as illustrated in Figure 6. Among the five modules, the second
369
      and third are mandatory, whereas the other three are optional for enhancing the quality
370   and quantity of the dataset. These five modules together maximize the correctness of forced
371   alignment under flexible conditions. ⃝    1 The first module applies a regular expression to
372   clean the provided transcriptions and to exclude any non-alphanumeric characters. ⃝            2 For
373   the second module, modern ASR models will be used to obtain word-level timestamps of the
374   transcriptions. Currently, sentence-level separations from the provided model are used as

505   FASA alignment Outperforms Human Annotators Considering the vast size of the
506   dataset, we randomly selected two audio files and transcriptions from the 352 recordings in
507   the ENNI dataset, and report the manual inspection results for data generated by FASA
508   with these files in Table 4. Several results are worthy of emphasizing here. First, since
509   the transcriptions are noisy, MFA (McAuliffe et al., 2017) completely fails to properly align
510   the audio segments with the correct transcription. 3 To be specific, both documents have
511
      missing transcriptions corresponding to the beginning of the audio, which results in 99.93%
      AW Error. This is because MFA tries to align all the words from the beginning, but since
512
      those words do not have available transcriptions, the entire system fails. Second, FASA
513
      incorrectly aligns one utterance with its transcription. For that utterance, it misses the “so
514   the” sound at the end of the utterance, and the two words are not recorded into the aligned
515   transcription. Manual inspection finds that the speaker stuttered and repeated “so the”,
516   which might be the issue of the model not picking up that trailing sound in the segmented
517   utterance. Lastly, FASA’s result is potentially much better than human annotators. Attia
518   et al. (2023) reports that 5 out of 393 hours of speech in MyST dataset (Pradhan et al.,
519   2023) are potentially incorrect with WER> 50%, resulting in 3% increase in WER for the
520   entire training dataset. Compared to human annotators that were used to annotate MyST,
521   FASA achieves one magnitude lower WER (13.6×) without requiring any human labor.

523   6     Conclusion
524
525   In conclusion, this work presents significant advancements in the field of children’s speech
526   analysis. First, we introduce KidSpeak, a pioneering multi-task speech-based foundation
527   language model designed specifically for diagnostic tasks pertaining to children’s speech.
528   Second, we propose an innovative two-stage training procedure for the audio encoder that
529   effectively integrates phonetic information, leading to marked improvements in both diag-
530
      nostic and transcription performance, as well as downstream performances when integrated
      with KidSpeak. Finally, we develop the Flexible and Automatic Speech Aligner,
531
      a novel forced alignment tool that extracts accurate and aligned audio from noisy speech,
532
      allowing us to create a high quality kids’ data corpus. Collectively, these contributions en-
533   hance the capabilities of speech analysis frameworks for children, paving the way for future
534   research and applications in this critical area.

837
838
      Audio-Visual Understanding: Contemporary work has attained remarkable feats with
      regards to understanding the visual and lingual modalities, often in synergy with each other,
839
      owing to the large scale availability of datasets facilitating research. Several works includ-
840
      ing but not limited to LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024), PaLME (Driess et al., 2023), Flamingo
841   (Alayrac et al., 2022), BLIP (Li et al., 2022) and GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) incorpo-
842   rate instruction based tuning in order to incorporate multi-modal capabilities in language
843   models. The training routines for these methods involve tokenization of the image modality
844   allowing for the formation of a sequence which one may incorporate into instruction/text to-
845   ken sequences using self attention (LLaVA, PaLME), cross attention (Flamingo) or through
846   a separate network (BLIP). This often consists of multiple stages wherein the initial set of
847   stages are aimed at priming the learnable parameters for the newer modality. In view of
848   their tremendous potential, we also witness applications of these advancements helping sig-
849
      nificantly enhance human-AI interaction by improving search engines, supporting creative
      tasks, and, importantly, advancing accessibility, particularly for the image modality. For
850
      instance, these technologies can be utilized to enhance accessibility tools, such as through
851
      augmented communication (Chanjaradwichai et al., 2019), as assistive learning tools (Pad-
852   manabha et al., 2024; Kazemitabaar et al., 2024) and sign language recognition systems
853   (Gong et al., 2024).
854
855
      Speech based LLMs and Spoken Language Understanding: Encoding speech us-
      ing LLMs is faced with challenges associated with encoding very large sequences of aural
856
      representations, given that a 16kHz sampling of one second of audio contains 16000 unique
857
      representations of the medium in the frequency domain. However, several recent contribu-
858   tions quantized representations of speech provided by the HuBERT (Hsu et al., 2021), as
859   leveraged by the works of Generative Speech Language Modeling (Lakhotia et al., 2021),
860   TWIST (Hassid et al., 2024) and SpeechGPT (Zhang et al., 2023) wherein based on the
861   applications, the representations are used in transformer (Vaswani, 2017) based encoder
862   decoder systems or are combined with the textual embeddings in order to construct an
863   interactive multi-modal system using instruction based tuning, with generative capabilities
      in audio. Another encoding scheme for audio is the use of log-mel spectrograms, processed

864
      using encoders such as Whisper (Radford et al., 2023), Wav2Vec Baevski et al. (2020),
865   Conformer (Gulati et al., 2020) and the AST (Gong et al., 2021), in order to generate
866   discrete representations which may similarly be combined with textual representations for
867   understanding and generative tasks. Fathullah et al. (2024) incorporate the conformer ar-
868   chitecture in order to enable speech recognition in LLMs. Spectron (Nachmani et al., 2023)
869   uses the conformer encoder and splits the aural representations into prompt and continua-
870   tion, enabling the continuation of aural speech and language using prompt during inference.
871   Zhao et al. (2023) use the WavLM (Chen et al., 2022) and the Wav2Vec encoders whereas

      answering based training procedure to inculcate understanding in a LLM using the aural
      representations.

875   Kids’ Speech: A predominant drawback of the concurrent works is the lack of support
876   for nuanced speech based on a variety of accents, dialects and intonations, or developing or
877   disordered speech as often characterized by kids. This challenge is further exacerbated by
878   the relative lack of attention and research in the field of children’s speech recognition and
879
      correction, resulting in a limited number of robust solutions tailored specifically to these
      needs. Liao et al. (2015) use a LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and CLDNN
880
      (Sainath et al., 2015) based architectures towards transcription and reduction of offensive
881
      generations. Plantinga & Fosler-Lussier (2019) use a GRU (Cho, 2014) based architecture
882   with alignment loss to discourage generation during silence and a teacher student loss in
883   order to improve transcription performance. Ramesh et al. (2022) leverage a masked word
884   prediction based cloze task inspired by BERT based encoder systems in order to correct
885   offensively transcribed speech by existing ASR systems. (Venkatasubramaniam et al., 2023)
886   develop an LSTM based disfluency detection and classification architecture over an existing
887   ASR system in order to enhance transcription. However, to the best of our knowledge, this
888   is the first work that proposes utilizing a large language model (LLM) as a multi-task model
889   with diagnostic capabilities within the speech domain. We anticipate that this represents
890
      a promising avenue for future research, offering substantial benefits in the realm of speech
      therapy for kids and complementing the extensive efforts of Speech-Language Pathologists
891
      (SLPs).
892
893   Forced-Alignment Toolkits: Traditionally yet still prevalently, alignment between the
894   audio and its transcription is done via human annotators on various software (Boersma &
895   Weenink, 2007; Grover et al., 2020). However, as discussed earlier, such practice is not
896
      scalable for large datasets. Kisler et al. (2017) contains some parts of a complete forced-
      alignment pipeline, but it does not address the fundamental problem of aligning audio
897
      with its transcription. Sheng et al. (2019) uses generative-adversarial networks (GAN) to
898
      perform data augmentation on children ASR dataset, but their work does not introduce
899   diverse new data to the field. Recently, the Talkbank project announced its data processing
900   pipeline that converts raw audio into CLAN-annotated transcriptions (Liu et al., 2023).
901   While their work uses a similar backbone structure as ours, their complete pipeline relies
902   on transcription generated by ASR models, whereas we faithfully adhere to the provided
903   transcription as the ground truth. Thus, on downstream tasks such as fine-tuning ASR
904   models, our dataset will be more usable because datasets generated by ASR models might
905   cause severe degradation according to (Radford et al., 2022). On the other hand, there have
906   been several works on forced-alignment ASR datasets with the assistance of human-labeled
907
      transcriptions (McAuliffe et al., 2017; Rodd et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023),
      with Montreal-Forced-Aligner (MFA) being the most popular toolkit (McAuliffe et al., 2017).
908
      MFA incorporates Kaldi (Povey et al., 2011) as the backbone, which uses the Gaussian
909
      Mixture Model (GMM) for its transcription generation process. However, while MFA works
910   well with carefully annotated transcriptions, it requires the transcription to have a perfect
911   match with the audio. That is, A1 → {T1 , T2 , ..., Ti }, A2 → {Ti+1 , Ti+2 , ..., Tj }, and so forth.
912   Liu et al. (2023) faces similar issues that it lacks global matching ability between audio and
913   transcription, hindering its usage in some subsets of the speech corpus. Moreover, while
914   recent multi-modal large language models (MLLM) might have the potential of automating
915   the alignment process (Zhang et al., 2023), they are much more resource-intensive compared
916   to specific ASR models.

919
920   A.2.1    Decoder Alignment in Multi-head Whisper
921
922   The two alignment mechanisms address a critical need for alignment between phonetic and
923
      orthographic transcriptions within multi-modal speech processing systems. This alignment
      is essential for several reasons:
924
925
            ◆ Phonetic and Orthographic Consistency: Phonetic transcriptions represent the pro-
926           nunciation of words, focusing on sounds, while orthographic transcriptions represent
927           the written form of language. Aligning these two modalities ensures that the pro-
928           nunciation (phonetics) and spelling (orthographics) are consistent with each other.
929           This consistency is crucial for tasks such as speech recognition and language learn-
930           ing, where accurate mapping between spoken and written forms is required.
931         ◆ Enhanced Encoder Utility: By aligning phonetic and orthographic transcriptions
932           through the shared encoder, the model benefits from enriched feature representa-
933           tions. The encoder, which is common to both decoders, learns to produce more
934           comprehensive and phonetically aware representations. This shared learning helps
935           the encoder capture nuances that prove to be critical for improved understanding
936           of pronunciation based nuances necessary for improved diagnostic capacities.
937         ◆ Robust Multi-Modal Learning: Aligning the hidden states of phonetic and ortho-
938           graphic decoders allows the system to leverage complementary information from
939           both transcriptions. Phonetics provides insights into pronunciation nuances, while
940           orthographics offer context about spelling and grammar. The combined insights
941
              from both modalities lead to a more robust and versatile model capable of handling
              diverse linguistic tasks.

       A.3    Features of FASA
991
992    Similar to existing auto-alignment toolkits, FASA requires an audio file and its correspond-
993    ing transcription. However, due to high uncertainty in the raw dataset, FASA assumes only
994    a minimal input format and does not require the transcription to be accurate. The ground
995    truth (GT) for an utterance Ak is defined by Equation 5 in the FASA pipeline. In contrast
996    to previous forced-alignment toolkits, FASA deliberately ignores utterances without valid
997    transcriptions, thereby enhancing quality.
998    FASA also incorporates beneficial design elements from established toolkits to enhance user
999    convenience, following the same design principles as MFA. Users need only to place the audio
1000   file and its transcriptions in a designated folder before executing the program, after which
1001   all processes are fully automated, enhancing the user experience. FASA allows users to
1002   select and manually input transcriptions for utterances when the provided transcriptions
1003   are suspected to be inaccurate, ensuring precision and user control. Additionally, FASA
1004   features an optional post-generation check to automatically exclude incorrect alignments,
1005   minimizing errors from the underlying model.

       A.5    Speech Disorder Classes
1021   The following provides a broad explanation of various speech-related disorders, along with
1022   seminal and intriguing citations in the field of speech-language pathology that KidSpeak
1023   is capable of diagnosing based on the speech patterns exhibited by the child.
1024
1025         ◆ Inconsistent Phonological Disorder: This pediatric speech sound disorder is char-
               acterized by the inconsistent production of the same words across repeated trials


                                                      19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
                (Dodd et al., 2024). For example, a child may say ”bat,” ”gat,” and ”at” instead of
1027            ”cat,” or produce ”log” for ”dog” one day and ”fog” the next. Moreover, a child may
1028            say ”fider,” ”sider,” and ”pider” when attempting to pronounce ”spider” (Dodd &
1029            Crosbie, 2010; Carter et al., 2019).
1030
1031         ◆ Consistent Phonological Disorder: In contrast, this disorder is marked by the child’s
1032           ability to produce the same errors consistently when attempting to articulate the
1033           same word. For instance, a child may reliably say ”tup” instead of ”cup” or ”wabbit”
1034           for ”rabbit.” Such patterns indicate a stable phonological processing issue, as the
1035           child consistently makes the same substitutions or distortions (Felsenfeld et al.,
1036           1995; Bleile, 2002).
1037
1038         ◆ Phonological Delay: This specific speech sound disorder entails developmental
1039
               phonological errors that align with typical speech development patterns but per-
               sist longer than expected, often for six months or more, which can impact clarity
1040
               and sound production (Orsolini et al., 2001). Children acquire speech by learning
1041
               entire words rather than individual sounds; as their speech matures, they catego-
1042           rize words by their components, often simplifying sounds or sequences into easier
1043           alternatives (e.g., saying ”ca” for ”cat”) (Waring et al., 2022).
1044
1045         ◆ Vowel Disorder: Vowel disorders are marked by difficulties in the positioning and
1046           sequencing of the articulators, particularly the tongue and lips, affecting vowel
1047           quality and accuracy. Incorrect positioning can lead to issues with vowel production,
1048           such as excessively long vowels or distortions. For instance, vowels may be partially
1049           voiced due to challenges in controlling vocal fold vibration, or they may exhibit
1050           excessive nasality from difficulties managing velopharyngeal closure. These spatial,
1051           temporal, and coordination difficulties often result in challenges in vowel production
1052
               (Gibbon & Beck, 2002; Ball & Gibbon, 2002). Additionally, children may struggle
               with vowel lengthening or shortening, such as elongating the vowel in ”see” for ”sit”
1053
               or shortening it in ”cat” as ”kit,” with omissions occurring as well (e.g., saying ”bll”
1054
               instead of ”ball”) (Stoel-Gammon & Pollock, 2008).
1055
1056
             ◆ Articulation Disorder: This type of speech sound disorder is characterized by diffi-
1057           culties in accurately producing speech sounds due to the imprecise use of the lips,
1058           tongue, or throat. Individuals may demonstrate various symptoms, including the
1059           omission of sounds (e.g., final consonants), distortion of sounds (e.g., producing an
1060           ”s” sound with a whistle), and challenges in coordinating the movements of their
1061           lips, tongue, teeth, palate, and lungs (Hall & Tomblin, 1978; Rvachew & Jamieson,
1062           1989).
1063
1064         ◆ Childhood Apraxia of Speech: Childhood apraxia of speech (CAS) is a neurological
1065           speech sound disorder characterized by impaired precision and consistency of move-
1066           ments underlying speech, absent neuromuscular deficits (e.g., abnormal reflexes or
1067
               tone) (Davis et al., 1998; Association et al., 2007; Kummer et al., 2007). Children
               with CAS may encounter difficulties in speech production, such as trouble transi-
1068
               tioning smoothly between sounds and syllables, groping movements of the jaw, lips,
1069
               or tongue, vowel distortions, incorrect stress patterns (e.g., pronouncing ”banana”
1070           as ”BUH-nan-uh,”) equal emphasis on all syllables (e.g., saying ”BUH-NAN-UH,”)
1071           separation of syllables with pauses, inconsistency in errors when repeating words,
1072           and voicing errors (e.g., saying ”down” instead of ”town”) (Carter et al., 2019).
