011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Neural Radiation Field (NeRF) often produces many artifacts with sparse inputs.
014            These artifacts are primarily caused by learning in regions where position infer-
015            ence is not feasible. We assume that the main cause of this problem is the incorrect
016            setting of boundary conditions in the learning space. To address this issue, we pro-
017            pose a new regularization method based on two key assumptions: (1) the position
018            of density and color cannot be inferred in regions where the view frustum does not
019            intersect, and (2) information inside opaque surfaces cannot be observed and in-
020
               ferred, and thus cannot contribute to the rendering of the image. Our method aims
               to transform the NeRF model into a well-posed problem by regularizing learning
021
               in regions where position inference is not possible, allowing the network to con-
022
               verge meaningfully. Our approach does not require scene-specific optimization
023            and focuses on regions where position inference is not possible, thereby avoiding
024            degradation of model performance in main regions. Experimental results demon-
025            strate the effectiveness of our method in addressing the sparse input problem,
026            showing outstanding performance on the Blender synthetic datasets. Our method
027            is designed to integrate seamlessly with existing techniques, providing an effec-
028            tive solution for sparse input scenarios and offering a foundational approach that
029            serves as the first clue in addressing sparse input problems.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Recently, the effectiveness of NeRF Mildenhall et al. (2021) has been witnessed in inferring scenes
036   from unobserved perspectives by training on multiple photographic inputs. However, existing NeRF
037   models often generate unrealistic scenes that are difficult for humans to comprehend. This problem
038
      is less prominent when the input data is dense but becomes more noticeable when the input data is
      sparse.
039
040   The ”Near-far plane” parameter is an important but often unaddressed issue in many experiments,
041   where it is frequently precisely tuned during experiments but not explicitly mentioned in previous
042   papers. If this parameter is not accurately tuned for the region of interest (ROI), especially in cases of
043   sparse input, it can result in poor object generation or numerous artifacts. This limitation is a critical
044
      issue that undermines the practical usefulness of experimental results. We interpret the improper
      tuning of the near-far threshold not only as a failure in setting training parameters, but also as a
045
      misconfiguration of the ”spatial boundary conditions” during training.
046
047   ”Proper boundary conditions” are essential for solving the problem effectively. As shown in Fig.1,
048   improper boundary conditions result in an ill-posed problem. To address this, our model is designed
049   to transform NeRF training into a well-posed problem, which we have named Well-NeRF.
050   An ”ill-posed problem” occurs when learning is permitted in regions where position inference is not
051   possible. In this situation, the NeRF training process may offer the model simpler but inaccurate
052   solutions, such as increasing the weight in regions like the red areas shown in Fig.1. However, the
053   result may deviate significantly from our intuition about the structure of the object, leading to critical
      errors.

074
      We have developed an improved method for setting boundary conditions. Even with a finely tuned
075
      near-far threshold, there are limitations in accurately defining the boundary conditions of the training
076   space, as it cannot clearly define the space where position inference is feasible.
077
078   We present two propositions that are true in order to develop a solution. First, in the non-overlapping
079
      parts of the view frustum, we cannot infer density and color according to position. Second, the
      information inside the opaque surface (shadow zone) cannot be observed and inferred, and cannot
080
      contribute to the rendering of the image. Based on this, we propose two regularization methods.
081
082          • Frustum score based regularization: This method calculates how much the view frustums
083            overlap at the positions of points sampled in space and reflects this in the training process.
084            If the view frustums do not overlap (S = 1, as shown in Equation equation 2), learning
085            is heavily constrained. However, when two or more frustums overlap (S ≥ 2), learning
086            is allowed, and the gradient is scaled based on the degree of overlap, allowing different
087            gradients to be applied depending on the extent of the overlap.
088          • Shadow zone regularization: This method reasonably approximates the color of the back
089            of an opaque surface that cannot be learned. By constraining the complexity of the back
090            of an opaque surface, the method allows the NeRF network to converge on a meaningful
091            structure for the object.
092
093   Since this method automatically adjusts the training space based on the input data, no additional
094   parameter tuning is required. Our method improves the NeRF model by limiting learning in ar-
095
      eas where ill-posed problems can occur, ensuring that the model focuses on well-posed problems.
      Our approach works outside the main learning region of the model, effectively maintaining NeRF’s
096
      core performance and potential. Furthermore, our method is built on top of the integrated model
097
      Nerfacto Tancik et al. (2023), making it easy to combine with other methods.
098
099   The goal of our model is to provide key insights and an initial step toward enabling various models
100   to function effectively even with sparse input.

104   2.1   N EURAL R ADIANCE F IELD
105
106   Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) Mildenhall et al. (2021) trains a deep neural network from a set
107   of photos to represent the continuous 3D scenes, and uses volume rendering to render the scene.
      Since NeRF was first released to the public, various models Barron et al. (2021; 2022); Müller et al.

      (2022); Fridovich-Keil et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2021) have evolved to overcome the shortcomings
109   of the original NeRF. NeRF-W Martin-Brualla et al. (2021) improved the model to be trained on a
110   variety of data under different conditions of contrast, brightness, and other factors. The problem of
111   generating excessive noise for scenes with moving objects has also been solved by certain methods
112   Pumarola et al. (2021); Park et al. (2021a;b). Other methods Wang et al. (2021); Jeong et al. (2021);

      Müller et al. (2022) significantly accelerated training by using a hash encoding method. Nerfacto

120
121   2.2   N EURAL R ENDERING W ITH S PARSE DATASET
122
123   Conventional NeRF-based methods face rendering performance issues and noise artifacts with
124   sparse datasets. Although various models have been proposed to address these issues, limitations
125   still exist. For practical use, it is necessary to effectively render continuous scenes with only a few
126
      datasets.
127   Methods such as Pixel-NeRF Yu et al. (2021) and Diet-NeRF Jain et al. (2021) required pre-training
128   to achieve high performance. However, this pre-training process can introduce unintended new
129   structures or artifacts that do not exist in the real view. Sparse-NeRF Wang et al. (2023) and DS-
130   NeRF Deng et al. (2022) relied on additional prior knowledge of the depth value of the input scene.
131   Obtaining this information typically requires additional sensors during image capture or separate
132
      depth estimation algorithms. These requirements for this additional information limit the versatility
      of the model.
133
134   Some methods have developed regularization techniques based on a cognitive view of geometric and
135   visual shapes. Reg-NeRF Niemeyer et al. (2022) applied regularization for geometric information
136   and appearance of the scene based on patches. However, this approach can induce additional learn-
137   ing costs and suffer from slow convergence problems. FreeNeRF Yang et al. (2023) improved ren-
138
      dering performance for few-shot dataset using a frequency regularization method that sequentially
      trains from low to high frequencies of positional-encoding. Additionally, it employed an occlusion
139
      regularization method, which could push back artifacts that are close to camera. However, these
140
      methods require optimized parameter settings for each scene, necessitating recursive experiments
141   for successful rendering. Regularization techniques designed based on an intuitive approach to 3D
142   geometric shapes or artifacts might work well in certain cases, but they had the potential to cause
143   irrational or over-regularization in certain scenes.
144
      Furthermore, a common characteristic among these models is the precise setting of a near-far thresh-
145
      old for the view frustum (ray). A near-far threshold that is precisely set to the region where the object
146
      exists effectively helps learn 3D geometric structure. However, setting this threshold as a hyperpa-
147   rameter for each dataset is not always feasible in real-world scenarios. Our method, which does not
148   require this kind of heuristic hyperparameter value, is a highly versatile model that can be applied
149   to various datasets.

152   2.3   A RTIFACTS R EMOVAL
153
154
      Many models aiming to address the sparse input problem in NeRF attempt to remove artifacts that
155
      obstruct the field of view. Floaters-no-more Philip & Deschaintre (2023) assumed that the scene
156   near the camera undergoes frequent updates due to the large number of samples per space along
157   the rays. Thus, it introduced a gradient scaling technique to reduce the learning rate for nearby dis-
158   tances, thereby mitigating the influence of proximity samples and eliminating artifacts. In addition,
159   NeRFbusters Warburg* et al. (2023) used strategies to clean up the field of view by sampling points
160   outside of the view frustum using random sampling and applying a regularization term to decrease
161   density. However, these models are inherently designed for dense inputs and have limitations with
      sparse input datasets.

191   2D images and synthesize novel viewpoints. The model receives spatial coordinates x and direc-
192   tional vectors d as inputs, and through a neural network parameterized by θ, denoted fθ , it outputs

      istic outputs.

      3.2     F RUSTUM S CORE R EGULARIZATION
209
210   In regions where the View Frustum does not intersect at all(region observed from only a single input
211   view), three-dimensional positional inference is fundamentally impossible. In such regions, posi-
212   tional color and density cannot be inferred from the input data. The color and density depend only on
213   the characteristics of the neural network. This results in arbitrarily determined spatial information,
214   which is an ill-posed problem for spatial information, as shown in Fig.1. Areas where this problem
215   occurs can produce artifacts that are inconsistent with our understanding of the object. Therefore, it
      is important to determine if view frustums intersect.

290   3.3     S HADOW Z ONE R EGULARIZATION
291
292   Information behind opaque surfaces with low transmittance is not observable. Therefore, informa-
293   tion observed at a particular view frustum cannot contribute to learning behind opaque surfaces.
294   Therefore, learning behind opaque surfaces may introduce unnecessary complexity to the model
295   without contributing to image rendering. Based on this finding, we develop a regularization method
296   that can reduce the complexity of RGB colors behind opaque surfaces. This implements boundary
297
      conditions based on object surfaces.
298
      3.3.1    RGB B LENDING
299
300   In the NeRF model, the color rendered by ray is largely determined by the surface of the opaque
301   object (the high weight points close to the origin of the ray). As training progresses, it becomes
302   increasingly likely that the color values at the back of opaque object surfaces are determined not by
303   input data, but solely by network characteristics, resulting in noisy values, as in Fig.4.
304
      To address this issue, we develop a regularization method based on the loss function Lb in equation 7,
305   which gradually blends the color of the opaque surface into the interior of the object, ensuring a
306   more stable determination of interior object color. This constraint is computed via a self-feedback
307   mechanism, requiring no additional data input. Moreover, since it operates within a single ray, it can
308   be seamlessly integrated without altering the existing random ray sampling scheme. This method
309   reduces meaningless network complexity and helps the model converge to more meaningful values.

504   5   C ONCLUSION
505
506   In this paper, we presented Well-NeRF, a universal solution to the sparse input problem in NeRF. The
507   key to our success lies in the foundation of our implementation, which starts from two true proposi-
508   tions, enabling our method to have fewer counterexamples. Since NeRF was first introduced, it has
509   undergone significant developments, and efforts to integrate solutions for different problems con-
510   tinue. However, a clear integration addressing the sparse input problem has not yet been proposed,
511
      largely because of the existence of incompatible model structures or the potential for degrading the
      base model’s performance. Our model does not compete with the base model, as it operates in differ-
512
      ent domains without leading to negative interactions, thereby not degrading the performance of the
513
      base model. Our model, developed on an integrated model framework, easily combines with various
514   models without conflict, as shown in Fig.9 Our proposal has some limitations. However, applying
515   the ’definition of the region where positional inference is possible’ as spatial boundary conditions
516   in a NeRF model to create a well-posed problem has clearly shown both qualitative and quantitative
517   effectiveness in sparse problems. We hope our approach contributes to the advancement of diverse
518   NeRF models, making them capable of addressing the sparse input problem.
519
520
521   6   L IMITATION
522
523   Distant backgrounds are difficult for our model to handle because it is nearly impossible to ensure
524   overlap in the view frustum under sparse data conditions. Therefore, we conducted experiments in a
525   limited manner for data captured under such conditions. The results can be found in the supplemen-
526   tary materials.
527
      Frustum Score Calculator can be used to determine the most basic ill-posed areas. However, de-
528   termining ill-posed areas becomes more complicated when there are objects and walls in a large
529   complex structure. For example, the area behind a wall should not be counted even if it is inside the
530   view frustum. Our shadow zone regularization method partially solves this problem, but it is not yet
531   complete. In further study, we will develop a model that can efficiently determine ill-posed areas in
532   various situations.
533
      Our method does not completely control optical illusion artifacts. The artifacts we do not control
534   occur in regions where inference is possible (S ≥ 2, as shown in equation 2) and where multiple so-
535   lutions may exist. Determining whether this is a true value or an optical illusion is not possible with
536   the basic Nerf model. Additional regularization is required to address this. However, regularization
537   designed with human intuitive assumptions about appearance and geometry is likely only applicable
538   to certain scenes. Therefore, even if it improves performance in some scenes, it is likely to have a
539   side effect on scenes in general. Therefore, we need to develop regularization methods that satisfy
      most of the human intuition and do not degrade NeRF learning.
