                                                 A BSTRACT
013
014
              Accurate and automated anomaly segmentation is critical for assisting clinicians
015
              in detecting and diagnosing pathological conditions, particularly in large-scale
016           medical imaging datasets where manual annotation is not only time- and resource-
017           intensive but also prone to inconsistency. To address these challenges, we propose
018           S CREENER, a fully self-supervised framework for visual anomaly segmentation,
019           leveraging self-supervised representation learning to eliminate the need for man-
020           ual labels. Additionally, we model the conditional distribution of local image
021           patterns given their global context, enabling the identification of anomalies as pat-
022           terns with low conditional probabilities and assigning them high anomaly scores.
023           S CREENER comprises three components: a descriptor model that encodes lo-
024           cal image patterns into self-supervised representations invariant to local-content-
025           preserving augmentations; a condition model that captures global contextual in-
026
              formation through invariance to image masking; and a density model that esti-
              mates the conditional density of descriptors given their global contexts to compute
027
              anomaly scores.
028
              We validate S CREENER by training a fully self-supervised model on over 30,000
029
              3D CT images and evaluating its performance on four large-scale test datasets
030
              comprising 1,820 3D CT scans across four chest and abdominal pathologies. Our
031           framework consistently outperforms existing unsupervised anomaly segmentation
032           methods. Code and pre-trained models will be made publicly available.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      The accurate and automated segmentation of pathologies in medical computed tomography (CT) im-
057
      ages is crucial for assisting clinicians in diagnosing and treating various conditions. However, devel-
058
      oping supervised models for pathology segmentation faces significant challenges: labeled datasets
059   are scarce, annotations often cover only a limited range of findings, and manual labelling is not only
060   resource-intensive but also inconsistent. For example, in Figure 1, pneumothorax is present in the
061   second column (black region framed by red box) but is not included in the ground truth mask. Hence,
062   supervised methods for pathology segmentation are often constrained in scope and applicability.
063
      In contrast, large-scale datasets of unlabelled CT images are readily available through public repos-
064
      itories (Team, 2011; Ji et al., 2022; Qu et al., 2024). These datasets remain largely underutilized due
065   to the lack of annotations, despite their potential to enable fully unsupervised learning approaches.
066   Leveraging this abundance of unlabelled data, we aim to develop a model capable of distinguishing
067   pathological regions from normal ones without requiring labeled training data. Our core assump-
068   tion is that pathological patterns are significantly rarer than healthy patterns in random CT images.
069   This motivates framing pathology segmentation as an unsupervised visual anomaly segmentation
070   (UVAS) problem, where anomalies correspond to pathological regions.
071   While existing UVAS methods have been explored extensively for natural images, their adaptation
072   to medical imaging remains challenging. A major hurdle is that most CT datasets contain unan-
073   notated pathological regions, and there is no automatic way to filter these out to ensure a training
074   set composed entirely of normal (healthy, non-pathological) images — a common requirement for
075   synthetic-based (Zavrtanik et al., 2021; Marimont & Tarroni, 2023) and reconstruction-based (Baur
076   et al., 2021; Schlegl et al., 2019) UVAS methods.
077   Density-based approaches (Gudovskiy et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2024), which assume anomalies
078   are rare rather than entirely absent, are better suited for this setting, as they can handle training
079   datasets with unannotated pathological regions. These methods model normal patterns probabilis-
080   tically and assign higher anomaly scores to deviations. However, they rely on encoders pre-trained
081   on ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), optimized for natural images and not for the unique structures and
082   textures in medical CT images. This domain shift leads to suboptimal feature representations failing
083   to capture subtle pathological variations, reducing their effectiveness in medical settings.
084   To address these challenges, we propose S CREENER, a framework that enhances density-based
085   UVAS through domain-specific self-supervised learning and learned contextual conditioning. To
086   avoid domain shift issues and labelling requirement, we pre-train self-supervised encoders (O Pin-
087   heiro et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021; Bardes et al., 2022; Goncharov et al., 2023) to produce
088   dense CT-specific feature maps. We further introduce a second self-supervised encoder that gen-
089   erates masking-invariant representations, capturing global context without being influenced by local
090   anomalies. Finally, we train a conditional density model to predict the feature maps of one encoder
091
      based on the outputs of the other. Anomaly scores are assigned to image regions with high prediction
      errors, enabling effective segmentation of pathological regions.
092
093   We demonstrate the effectiveness of S CREENER by training it on over 30,000 3D CT volumes span-
094   ning chest and abdominal regions and evaluating its performance on four large-scale test datasets
095   comprising 1,820 scans with diverse pathologies. As shown in Figure 1, our model successfully
096   segments pathological regions across different organs and conditions. We summarize the key con-
097
      tributions of this work:
098
             • Self-Supervised Representations for UVAS: We demonstrate that dense self-supervised
099
               representations outperform supervised feature extractors in visual anomaly segmentation,
100            enabling a fully self-supervised framework applicable in domains with limited labeled data.
101
102          • Learned Conditioning Variables: We introduce self-supervised condition variables for
103
               density-based models, simplifying the estimation of conditional distributions and achieving
               remarkable segmentation performance using a simple Gaussian density model.
104
105          • First Large-Scale Study of UVAS in 3D CT Images: This work presents the first large-
106            scale evaluation of UVAS methods for 3D CT images, showing state-of-the-art performance
107            on unsupervised semantic segmentation of pathologies in diverse anatomical regions, in-
               cluding lung cancer, pneumonia, liver and kidney tumors.

      2     BACKGROUND & NOTATION
109
110
      Density-based UVAS methods assign high anomaly scores to image regions with rare patterns using
111   two models, which we call a descriptor model and a density model. The descriptor model encodes
112   image patterns into vector representations, while the density model learns their distribution and
113   assigns anomaly scores based on the learned density.
114
      In existing methods (Gudovskiy et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2024), the descriptor model fθdesc is a
115
      fully-convolutional neural network pre-trained on ImageNet. For a 3D image x ∈ RH×W ×S , it
116                                         desc
      produces feature maps y ∈ Rh×w×s×d , where each position p ∈ P corresponds to a descriptor
117             desc

118
      y[p] ∈ Rd . Here, position set P = {p | p ∈ [1, . . . , h] × [1, . . . , w] × [1, . . . , s]}.
119   The density model qθdens (y) estimates the marginal density qY (y) of descriptors. For an abnormal
120   pattern at position p, the descriptor y[p] is expected to lie in a low-density region, yielding a low
121   qθdens (y[p]). Conversely, normal patterns produce high densities. During inference, the negative log-
122   density values, − log qθdens (y[p]) are used as anomaly segmentation scores. Density models we use
123   in S CREENER are simple Gaussian model and more expressive normalizing flow (see Appendix E).
124   This framework can be extended using a conditioning mechanism. For each position p, an auxiliary
125   variable c[p], referred to as a condition, is introduced. Let C denote the condition at a random posi-
126   tion in a random image. Instead of modelling the complex marginal density qY (y), the conditional
127   density qY |C (y|c) is learned for each condition c. During inference, the negative log-conditional
128   densities, − log qθdens (y[p] | c[p]), are used as anomaly scores. State-of-the-art methods (Gudovskiy
129   et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2024) adopt this conditional framework and use sinusoidal positional en-
130   codings as conditions. See detailed descriptions for positional condition alternatives in Appendix D.
131   Self-supervised learning leverages unlabelled data to learn representations invariant to transforma-
132   tions through auxiliary tasks. SSL objectives align embeddings of augmented views x(1) , x(2) of the
133   same image x while avoiding trivial solutions (mapping all images to the same vector). In vision do-
134   main, augmentations typically include color jitter and random crops. Representations are derived by
135   feeding inputs x to an encoder fθ (a neural network), yielding z = fθ (x). We employ adaptations of
136   SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) and VICReg (Bardes et al., 2021) to dense feature learning (O Pinheiro
137   et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021; Bardes et al., 2022; Goncharov et al., 2023) in our approach. For
138   detailed description of these methods, please refer to Appendix C.

140   3     M ETHOD
141
142   Here we present our method for unsupervised semantic segmentation of pathological regions in
143   3D medical CT images, illustrated in Figure 2. Our method introduces two key innovations to the
144   density-based UVAS framework: self-supervised descriptor model (Section 3.1), and self-supervised
145   condition model (Section 3.2). Section 3.3 describes the training pipeline for density modelling.
146
147   3.1   D ESCRIPTOR MODEL
148
149
      The descriptor model fθdesc is critical to our method. It must produce descriptors y[p] that distinguish
      between pathological and normal positions p, as this differentiation directly determines the anomaly
150
      scores in the density-based UVAS framework. Simultaneously, descriptors should minimize irrele-
151
      vant information; for instance, if they capture noise from CT images, the density model may assign
152   high anomaly scores to healthy regions with extreme noise, leading to false positives.
153
154   To pre-train dense descriptors, we use dense joint embedding SSL methods (Section 2 and Ap-
155
      pendix C), which allow explicit control over the information content of the representations. Specifi-
      cally, we penalize descriptors for failing to distinguish between different positions within or across
156
      images, ensuring they capture spatially discriminative features. Simultaneously, we enforce invari-
157
      ance to low-level perturbations, such as cropping and color jitter, to eliminate irrelevant information.
158
159   The descriptor model training pipeline is illustrated in the upper part of Figure 2. From a random
160   image x, we extract two overlapping 3D crops of random size, resize them to H × W × S, and
161   apply random augmentations, such as color jitter. The augmented crops, denoted as x(1) and x(2) ,
      are fed into the descriptor model, producing feature maps y(1) and y(2) .

221   3.2   C ONDITION MODEL
222
223   Our self-supervised condition model is inspired by a thought experiment: suppose a region of a CT
224   image is masked, and we attempt to infer its content based on the visible context (as shown in the
225   upper masked crop in Figure 2). In most cases, we would assume the masked region is healthy unless
226
      there is explicit evidence to suggest otherwise. This assumption reflects a model of the conditional
      distribution over possible inpaintings given the context. If the actual content significantly deviates
227
      from this expectation –indicating low conditional probability– it is classified as an anomaly.
228
229   Building on this intuition, we propose that the condition c[p] in the conditional density-based UVAS
230   framework should capture the global context of the image position p. Global implies that c[p] must
231   be inferable from various masked views of the image. At the same time, conditions may vary across
232
      different regions of the image to encode position-specific information, such as anatomical location
      or tissue type.
233
234   To achieve these properties, we learn conditions c[p] through a self-supervised condition model
235   gθcond , which has a fully-convolutional architecture similar to the descriptor model. The model gen-
                                            desc
236   erates feature maps c ∈ Rh×w×s×d that are invariant to image masking, providing a condition
237   for each position in the input image. The training process mirrors that of the VICReg descriptor
238   model (Section 3.1), with the addition of masking as part of the augmentations. An illustration of
239   this approach is shown in the middle part of Figure 2.
240   The learned conditions c[p] are designed to ignore the presence of pathologies, as such information
241   cannot be consistently inferred from masked views. Instead, the condition model likely encodes
242   patient-level attributes (e.g., age, gender) and position-specific attributes (e.g., anatomical region,
243   tissue type) that are predictable from masked contexts. Conditioning on these variables simplifies
244   density estimation, as conditional distributions are often less complex than marginal distributions.
245   Moreover, conditioning can improve fairness: for instance, if certain anatomical regions or demo-
246   graphic groups are underrepresented in the training data, an unconditional density model might treat
247   these as anomalies. In contrast, a model conditioned on gender or anatomical region would handle
248   such cases more appropriately by treating them within their specific context.

299   4.2      E VALUATION METRICS
300
301   We use standard quality metrics for assessment of visual anomaly segmentation models which are
302   employed in MVTecAD benchmark (Bergmann et al., 2021): pixel-level AUROC and AUPRO cal-
303
      culated up to 0.3 FPR. We also compute area under the whole pixel-level ROC-curve. Despite,
      our model can be viewed as semantic segmentation model, we do not report standard segmentation
304
      metrics, e.g. Dice score , due to the following reasons. As we mention in Section 4.1, available
305
      testing CT datasets contain annotations of only specific types of tumors, while other pathologies
306   may be present in the images but not included in the ground truth masks. It makes impossible to
307   fairly estimate metrics like Dice score or Hausdorff distance, which count our model’s true positive
308   predictions of the unannotated pathologies (see second image from the left in the Figure 1 for exam-
309   ple) as false positive errors and strictly penalize for them. However, the used pixel-level metrics are
310   not sensitive to this issue, since they are based on sensitivity and specificity. We estimate sensitivity
311   on pixels belonging to the annotated pathologies. To estimate specificity we use random pixels that
312   do not belong to the annotated tumors which are mostly normal, thus yielding a practical estimate.

355   We compare our best model (VICreg descriptor model, sin-cos positional encodings condition model
356   and conditional normalizing flow density model) with baselines that represent different approaches
357   to visual anomaly segmentation. Specifically, we implement 3D versions of autoencoder (Baur
358   et al., 2021), f-anoGAN (Schlegl et al., 2019) (reconstruction-based methods), DRAEM (Zavrtanik
359   et al., 2021), MOOD-Top1 (Marimont & Tarroni, 2023) (methods based on synthetic anomalies) and
360   MSFlow (density-based method on top of ImageNet features). Quantitative comparison is presented
361   in table 2. Qualitative comparison is shown in Figure 3.
362   The analysis of the poor performance of the reconstruction-based methods is given in Appendix B.
363   Synthetic-based models yield many false negatives because during training they were penalized to
364   predict zero scores in the unlabeled real pathological regions which may appear in training images.
365   Meanwhile, MSFlow heavily relies on an ImageNet-pre-trained encoder which produces irrelevant
366   features of 3D medical CT images. Our density-based model with domain-specific self-supervised
367   features outperforms baselines by a large margin.

402
      In this section, we review several key approaches, each represented among the top five methods
403   on the localization track of the MVTec AD benchmark (Bergmann et al., 2021), developed to stir
404   progress in visual unsupervised anomaly detection and localization.
405
406   Synthetic anomalies In unsupervised settings, real anomalies are typically absent or unlabeled
407   in training images. To simulate anomalies, researchers synthetically corrupt random regions by
408   replacing them with noise, random patterns from a special set (Yang et al., 2023), or parts of other
409   training images (Marimont & Tarroni, 2023). A segmentation model is trained to predict binary
410   masks of corrupted regions, providing well-calibrated anomaly scores for individual pixels. While
411   straightforward to train, these models may overfit to synthetic anomalies and struggle with real ones.
412
413   Reconstruction-based Trained solely on normal images, reconstruction-based approaches (Baur
414   et al., 2021; Kingma & Welling, 2013; Schlegl et al., 2019), poorly reconstruct anomalous regions,
415
      allowing pixel-wise or feature-wise discrepances to serve as anomaly scores. Later generative ap-
      proaches (Zavrtanik et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Wang et al.) integrate synthetic anomalies. The
416
      limitation stemming from anomaly-free train set assumption still persists—if anomalous images are
417
      present, the model may learn to reconstruct anomalies as well as normal regions, undermining the
418   ability to detect anomalies through differences between x and x̂.
419
420   Features pre-trained on ImageNet + density estimation Density-based methods for anomaly
421   detection model the distribution of the training data. Density estimation can be done in a non-
422   parametric way by the collection of a memory bank of objects (Roth et al., 2022; Bae et al., 2023).
423   As modeling of the distribution of raw pixel values is infeasible, these methods usually model the
424   distribution of their deep features.
425
      Unsupervised anomaly detection has seen the rise of flow-based methods (Serrà et al., 2019; Yu
426   et al., 2021), which leverage normalizing flows to assign low likelihoods to anomalies. However,
427   these methods struggle with high-dimensional raw RGB images, often assigning higher likelihoods
428   to anomalies than normal data (Kirichenko et al., 2020). To address this, flow-based methods have
429   been adapted to operate on high-dimensional features extracted from images. Multiscale feature
430   processing, as seen in DifferNet (Rudolph et al., 2021) and CFlow-AD (Gudovskiy et al., 2022),
431   enhances defect detection by handling variations in defect size. However, CFlow-AD’s independent
      estimation of each feature vector lacks contextual awareness, resulting in fragmented and inaccurate


                                                                                8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      localization. MSFlow (Zhou et al., 2024) addresses this limitation by concurrently estimating fea-
433   tures at all positions, incorporating contextual information through 3x3 convolutions and employing
434   a fusion flow block for information exchange across scales.
435
436
      Our method is related to FastFlow (Yu et al., 2021), CFlow (Gudovskiy et al., 2022) and MS-
      Flow (Zhou et al., 2024) methods for anomaly segmentation. Besides some technical differences
437
      (e.g. working with 2D natural images), there are several substantial differences: 1) these methods
438
      are based on a supervised encoder, pre-trained on ImageNet; 2) we show that density-based anomaly
439   segmentation in medical images can be improved using data-driven condition variables.
440
441   From this family, we selected MSFlow as a representative baseline, because it is simpler than PNI,
442
      and yields similar top-5 results on the MVTec AD.
443
444   5.2   M EDICAL UNSUPERVISED ANOMALY LOCALIZATION
445
446
      While there’s no standard benchmark for pathology localization on CT images, MOOD (Zimmerer
      et al., 2021) offers a relevant benchmark with generated anomalies. Unfortunately, this benchmark
447
      is currently closed for submissions, preventing us from evaluating our method. We include the
448
      top-performing method from MOOD (Marimont & Tarroni, 2023) in our comparison, that relies on
449   synthetic anomalies.
450
451   Other recognized methods for anomaly localization in medical images are reconstruction-based:
452
      variants of AE/VAE (Baur et al., 2021; Shvetsova et al., 2021), f-AnoGAN Schlegl et al. (2019), and
      diffusion-based (Pinaya et al., 2022). These approaches highly rely on the fact that the the training
453
      set consists of normal images only. However, it is challenging and costly to collect a large dataset of
454
      CT images of normal patients. While these methods work acceptable in the domain of 2D medical
455   images and MRI, the capabilities of the methods have not been fully explored in a more complex
456   CT data domain. We have adapted these methods to 3D.
457
458
459   6     C ONCLUSION
460
461   This work explores fully self-supervised approach to anomaly detection and localization in medical
462   3D images. Previously, methods relied on supervised approaches and anomaly-free training datasets
463   assumption, which hardly holds in typical medical scenarios. We propose S CREENER as a three
464
      component model, comprised of (i) self-supervised representation learning descriptor for image
      features, (ii) density-based anomaly detection model that learns distribution of the features, and (iii)
465
      conditioning model containing auxiliary information which boosts simpler density models.
466
467   Domain-specific and self-supervised S CREENER is no longer inhibited by limitations of the earlier
468   methods and outperforms them by a large margin, which can be seen from empirical results obtained
469   on the large-scale collection of computed tomography datasets. As our framework is modular, we
470
      learned and tested several model choices for each of the component, resulting in a comprehensive
      ablation study.
471
472
473
      Limitations We note that this work is largely a proof of concept for SSL in 3D medical imaging
      as there are still limitations to the proposed approach. Density based anomaly detection poses a
474
      limitation in that rare patterns can be flagged as pathological. Since rareness is highly predictive of
475
      anomaly, applying to pathology segmentation S CREENER may yield false positive errors on healthy
476   but rare patterns. Another limitation concerns representativeness of the training sample. Our training
477   dataset contains chest and abdominal CTs with much more chest samples. This causes more false
478   positive errors in abdominal region. To work in other anatomical regions, our model needs to be
479   trained on the corresponding images.
480
481   Future work While the performance gains compared to baselines are already significant, we note
482   that further improvements might be achieved from increasing descriptors and conditions dimension-
483   ality and experiments with multi-scale representations (e.g. by building feature pyramids). Another
484   possible avenue for future work is to study scaling laws, i.e. self-supervised models typically scale
485   well with increasing pretraining dataset sizes. Distillation of S CREENER into UNet at a pre-training
      stage is also possible and might prove effective for pathology segmentation tasks.
