009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We study the phase transition phenomenon inherent in the shuffled (permuted)
012            regression problem, which has found numerous applications in databases, privacy,
013            data analysis, etc. For the permuted regression task: Y = Π♮ XB♮ , the goal is to
014            recover the permutation matrix Π♮ as well as the coefficient matrix B♮ . It has been
015            empirically observed in prior studies that when recovering Π♮ , there exists a phase
016            transition phenomenon: the error rate drops to zero rapidly once the parameters
017
               reach certain thresholds. In this study, we aim to precisely identify the locations of
               the phase transition points by leveraging techniques from message passing (MP).
018
019            In our analysis, we first transform the permutation recovery problem into a prob-
020            abilistic graphical model. Then, we leverage the analytical tools rooted in the
021            message passing (MP) algorithm and derive an equation to track the convergence
022            of the MP algorithm. By linking this equation to the branching random walk
023
               process, we are able to characterize the impact of the signal-to-noise-ratio (snr)
               on the permutation recovery. Depending on whether the signal is given or not, we
024
               separately investigate the oracle case and the non-oracle case. The bottleneck in
025
               identifying the phase transition regimes lies in deriving closed-form formulas for
026            the corresponding critical points, but only in rare scenarios can one obtain such pre-
027            cise expressions. To tackle this challenge, we propose the Gaussian approximation
028            method, which allows us to obtain the closed-form formulas in almost all scenarios.
029            In the oracle case, our method can fairly accurately predict the phase transition snr.
030            In the non-oracle case, our proposed algorithm can predict the maximum allowed
031            number of permuted rows and uncover its dependency on the sample number.
032            Numerical experiments reveal that the observed phase transition points are well
033            aligned with our theoretical predictions. Our study will motivate exploiting MP al-
034            gorithms (and related techniques) as an effective tool for permuted regression prob-
035            lems, which have found applications in machine learning, privacy, and databases.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   In this paper, we consider the following permuted (shuffled) linear regression problem:
040                                           Y = Π♮ XB♮ + σW,                                          (1)
041
042   where Y ∈ Rn×m denotes the matrix of observations, Π♮ ∈ {0, 1}n×n is the permutation matrix,
043   X ∈ Rn×p is the design matrix, B♮ ∈ Rp×m is the matrix of signals (regressors), W ∈ Rn×m
044
      denotes the additive noise matrix (with unit variance), and σ 2 is the noise variance. The task
045
      is to recover both the signal matrix B♮ and the permutation matrix Π♮ . The research on this
      challenging permuted regression problem dates back at least to 1970s under the name “broken sample
046
      problem” (DeGroot et al., 1971; Goel, 1975; DeGroot & Goel, 1976; 1980; Bai & Hsing, 2005).
047
      Recent years have witnessed a revival of this problem due to its broad spectrum of applications in
048   (e.g.,) privacy protection, data integration, etc. (Unnikrishnan et al., 2015; Pananjady et al., 2018;
049   Slawski & Ben-David, 2019; Pananjady et al., 2017; Slawski et al., 2020; Zhang & Li, 2020).
050
051   Specifically, this paper will focus on studying the “phase transition” phenomenon in recovering the
052   whole permutation matrix Π♮ : the error rate for the permutation recovery sharply drops to zero once
053   the parameters reach certain thresholds. In particular, we leverage techniques in the message passing
      (MP) algorithm literature to identify the precise positions of the phase transition thresholds. The

055   corresponding critical points. This is a highly challenging task because only in rare scenarios can
056   one obtain such precise expressions. To tackle the difficulty, we propose the Gaussian approximation

061   Related work. The problem we study simultaneously touches two distinct areas of research: (A)
062
      permutation recovery, and (B) message passing (MP). In the literature of permuted linear regression,
      essentially all existing works used the same setting (1). Pananjady et al. (2018); Slawski & Ben-David
063
      (2019) consider the single observation model (i.e., m = 1) and prove that the signal-to-noise-ratio
064
      (snr) for the correct permutation recovery is OP (nc ), where c > 0 is some positive constant. Slawski
065   et al. (2020); Zhang & Li (2020); Zhang et al. (2022) investigate the multiple observations model
066   (i.e., m > 1)  and suggest that the snr requirement can be significantly decreased, from OP (nc )
067   to OP nc/m . In particular, Zhang & Li (2020) develop an estimator which we will leverage and
068   analyze for studying the phase transition phenomenon. Compared with the above work, our analysis
069   can identify the precise locations of the phase transition thresholds. In this February, there comes
070   a paper (Lufkin et al., 2024) considering the same problem as ours but in a much simpler setting
071   (single measurement with m = 1). Compared with their work, our framework can easily reproduce
072   their predicted phase transition points, answer the questions they treat as open, and predict the phase
073   transition points in a unified framework. A detailed discussion can be found in the main context.
074   Another line of related research comes from the field of statistical physics. For example, using
075   the replica
             P method, Mézard & Parisi (1985; 1986) study the linear assignment problem (LAP), i.e.,
076   minΠ i,j Πij Eij where Π denotes a permutation matrix and Eij is i.i.d random variable uniformly
077   distributed in [0, 1]. Martin et al. (2005) then generalize LAP to multi-index matching and presented
078   an investigation based on MP algorithm. Recently, Caracciolo et al. (2017); Malatesta et al. (2019)
079   extend the distribution of Eij to a broader class. However, all the above works exhibit no phase
080   transition. Chertkov et al. (2010) extend it to the particle tracking problem and observe a phase
081   transition phenomenon. Later, Semerjian et al. (2020) modify it to fit the graph matching problem,
082
      which paves way for our work in studying the permuted linear regression problem.
083
      Our contributions. We propose the first framework to identify the precise locations of phase
084   transition thresholds associated with permuted linear regression. In the oracle case where B♮ is
085   known, our scheme is able to determine the phase transition snr. In the non-oracle case where B♮ is
086   not given, our method will predict the maximum allowed number of permuted rows and uncover its
087   dependence on the ratio p/n. In our analysis, we identify the precise positions of the phase transition
088   points in the large-system limit, e.g., n, m, p all approach to infinity with m/n → τm , p/n → τp .
089   Interestingly, numerical results well match predictions even when n, m, p are in the hundreds.
090   Here, we would also like to briefly mention the technical challenges. Compared with the previous
091   works (Mezard & Montanari, 2009; Talagrand, 2010; Linusson & Wästlund, 2004; Mézard & Parisi,
092   1987; 1986; Parisi & Ratiéville, 2002; Semerjian et al., 2020), where the edge weights are relatively
093   simple, our edge weights usually involve high-order interactions across Gaussian random variables
094   and are densely correlated. To tackle this issue, our proposed approximation method to compute
095   the phase transition thresholds consists of three parts: 1) performing Gaussian approximation; 2)
096   modifying the leave-one-out technique; and 3) performing size correction. A detailed explanation
097   can be found in Section 4. Hopefully, our approximation method will serve independent technical
098
      interests for researchers in the machine learning community.

477         5           C ONCLUSIONS
478
            This is the first work that can identify the precise location of phase transition thresholds of permuted
479
            linear regressions. For the oracle case where the signal B♮ is given as a prior, our analysis can
480         predict the phase transition threshold snroracle to a good extent. For the non-oracle case where
481         B♮ is not given, we modified the leave-one-out technique to approximately compute the phase
482         critical snrnon-oracle value for the phase transition, as the precise computation becomes significantly
483         complicated as the high-order interaction between Gaussian random variables is involved. Moreover,
484         we associated the singularity point in snrnon-oracle with a phase transition point w.r.t the allowed
485         number of permuted rows. Moreover, we present numerous numerical experiments to confirm the
            accuracy of our theoretical predictions.
