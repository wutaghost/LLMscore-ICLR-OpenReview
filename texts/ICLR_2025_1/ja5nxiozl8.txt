009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Experimental studies are a cornerstone of Machine Learning (ML) research. A
012            common and often implicit assumption is that the study’s results will generalize
013            beyond the study itself, e.g., to new data. That is, repeating the same study under
014            different conditions will likely yield similar results. Existing frameworks to mea-
015            sure generalizability, borrowed from the casual inference literature, cannot capture
016
               the complexity of the results and the goals of an ML study. The problem of mea-
               suring generalizability in the more general ML setting is thus still open, also due
017
               to the lack of a mathematical formalization of experimental studies. In this pa-
018
               per, we propose such a formalization, use it to develop a framework to quantify
019            generalizability, and propose an instantiation based on rankings and the Maximum
020            Mean Discrepancy. We show how this latter offers insights into the desirable num-
021            ber of experiments for a study. Finally, we investigate the generalizability of two
022            recently published experimental studies.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Experimental studies are a cornerstone of Machine Learning (ML) research. Due to their impor-
      tance, the community advocates for high methodological standards when performing, evaluating,
028
      and sharing studies (Hothorn et al., 2005; Huppler, 2009; Montgomery, 2017).
029
030   The quality of an experimental study depends on multiple aspects. First, the experimenter should
031   properly define the scope and the goals of the study. Particular attention must be given to the choice
032   of benchmarked methods and experimental conditions (Boulesteix et al., 2015; Bouthillier et al.,
033
      2021; Dehghani et al., 2021). Second, the study should be reproducible by independent parties and
      hence contain the necessary documentation. This aspect has recently drawn much attention due to
034
      the so-called reproducibility crisis (Baker, 2016; Gundersen et al., 2023; Peng, 2011; Raff, 2023;
035
      2021). Third, the results of the study should be sensibly analyzed to draw conclusions regarding,
036   for instance, the significance of the findings (Benavoli et al., 2017; Corani et al., 2017; Demsar,
037   2006). Finally, the generalizability of a study concerns how well its results are replicated under
038   unseen experimental conditions, such as datasets not considered in the study (National Academies
039   of Science, 2019; Findley et al., 2021; Pineau et al., 2021). The latter two conditions are also known
040   as the internal and external validity of a study.
041
      Generalizability and significance, although sometimes confused, are two independent aspects of a
042   study (Findley et al., 2021). On the one hand, significant findings may not be replicated under other
043   conditions; on the other hand, results might consistently be not significant. Generalizability is, con-
044   ceptually, closely related to model replicability. A model is ρ-replicable if, given i.i.d. samples from
045   the same data distribution, the trained models are the same with probability 1−ρ (Impagliazzo et al.,
046   2022). An experimental study is generalizable if, when performed under different i.i.d. samples of
047   experimental conditions, the results are similar with high probability (National Academies of Sci-
048   ence, 2019). A quantifiable notion of generalizability thus requires a formalization of experimental
049   studies, of their results, and of similarity between results.
050   Significance, instead, captures how strong the findings are within the specific sample of experiments
051   performed. Multiple publications have shown how different choices of experimental conditions can
052   lead to very different results (Benavoli et al., 2017; Boulesteix et al., 2017; Bouthillier et al., 2021;
053   Dehghani et al., 2021; Gundersen et al., 2022; Mechelen et al., 2023). Some recent experimental
      studies have also reported this phenomenon. Matteucci et al. (2023) discuss how previous studies


                                                         1
054
      on categorical encoders disagree on the best-performing ones, even when the results are significant.
055   Similarly, Lu et al. (2023) re-evaluated coreset learning methods and found that all of the methods
056   they considered did not beat a naı̈ve baseline.
057
058
      Quantifying generalizability can also help determine the appropriate size of experimental studies. If
      one dataset is probably not enough to draw generalizable conclusions, 106 datasets likely are. Of
059
      course, such large studies are usually not practical: it is crucial to determine the minimum amount
060
      of data needed to achieve generalizability. This principle also applies to other experimental factors,
061   such as the choice of quality metric and the initialization seed.
062
063   Our contributions are the following:
064
               1. we formalize experimental studies and their results;
065
               2. we propose a quantifiable definition of the generalizability of experimental studies;
066            3. we develop an algorithm to estimate the size of a study to obtain generalizable results;
067            4. we analyze two recent experimental studies, Matteucci et al. (2023); Srivastava et al.
068               (2023), and show how well their results generalize.
069            5. we publish the GENEXPY1 Python module to repeat our analysis in other studies.

076   2       R ELATED WORK
077
078   We first discuss the literature related to the problem we are tackling, i.e., why experimental studies
079   may not generalize. Second, we overview the existing concept of model replicability, closely related
080   to our work. Finally, we show other meanings that these words can assume in other domains.
081
082   Non-generalizable results. It is well known that experimental results can significantly vary based
083   on design choices (Lu et al., 2023; Matteucci et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2023; McElfresh et al., 2022).
084   Possible reasons include an insufficient number of datasets (Dehghani et al., 2021; Matteucci et al.,
085
      2023; Alvarez et al., 2022; Boulesteix et al., 2015) as well as differences in hyperparameter tun-
      ing (Bouthillier et al., 2021; Matteucci et al., 2023), initialization seed (Gundersen et al., 2023), and
086
      hardware (Zhuang et al., 2022). As a result, the statistical benchmarking literature advocates for
087
      experimenters to motivate their design choices (Bartz-Beielstein et al., 2020; Mechelen et al., 2023;
088   Boulesteix et al., 2017; Bouthillier et al., 2021; Montgomery, 2017) and clearly state the hypotheses
089   they are attempting to test with their study (Bartz-Beielstein et al., 2020; Moran et al., 2023).
090
091   Replicability and generalizability in ML. Our work formalizes the definitions of replicability
092   and generalizability given in Pineau et al. (2021) and National Academies of Science, 2019. Intu-
093   itively, replicable work consists of repeating an experiment on different data, while generalizable
094   work varies other factors as well—e.g., quality metric, implementation. A recent line of work, initi-
095   ated by (Impagliazzo et al., 2022), has linked replicability to model stability: a ρ-replicable model
096   learns (with probability 1 − ρ) the same parameters from different i.i.d. samples. This definition has
097   later been adapted and applied to other learning algorithms (Esfandiari et al., 2023a), clustering (Es-
098
      fandiari et al., 2023b), reinforcement learning (Eaton et al., 2023; Karbasi et al., 2023), convex
      optimization (Ahn et al., 2022), and learning rules (Kalavasis et al., 2023). Recent efforts have been
099
      bridging the gap between replicability, differential privacy, generalization error, and global stabil-
100
      ity (Bun et al., 2023; Chase et al., 2023; Ghazi et al., 2023; Moran et al., 2023; Dixon et al., 2023).
101   However, these applications remain limited to model replicability.
102
103

106   samples affects the validity of its findings. Generalizability is an aspect of external validity, where

127   3     E XPERIMENTS AND EXPERIMENTAL STUDIES
128
129   An experimental study is a set of experiments comparing the same alternatives under different ex-
130   perimental conditions. An experimental condition is a tuple of levels of experimental factors, the
131   parameters defining the experiments. Different factors play different roles in the study: the design
132
      and held-constant factors are fixed by design, while the generalizability of a study is defined in terms
      of the allowed-to-vary factors. The study aims at answering a research question, which defines its
133
      scope and goals.
134
135
      Example 3.1. (The “checkmate-in-one” task, cf. Figure 1) An experimenter wants to compare three
      Large Language Models (LLMs), the alternatives, on the “checkmate-in-one” task (Srivastava et al.,
136
      2023; Alexander, 2020; Ammanabrolu et al., 2019; 2020; Dambekodi et al., 2020). The assignment
137
      is to find the unique checkmating move from a position of pieces on a chessboard: an LLM succeeds
138   if and only if it outputs the correct move. The experimenter considers two experimental factors: the
139   number of shots, m, and the initial position on the chessboard, posl . The number of shots is a design
140   factor, while the initial position is an allowed-to-vary factor. The experimenter wants to find if
141   LLM1 ranks consistently against the other two LLMs when changing the initial position, for a fixed
142   number of shots.
143

      3.2     E XPERIMENTAL STUDIES
186
187   A study is defined by its research question Q, i.e., its scope and goals. The scope consists of the
188   alternatives A, the valid experimental conditions C, and the allowed-to-vary factors Iatv . The goal
189   is the kind of conclusions one is attempting to draw from the study. For now, the goal is a statement
190   of interests, i.e., a set of strings.
191   Definition 3.2 (Research question). The research question Q = (A, C, Iatv , goals) is a tuple con-
192   taining the set of alternatives A, the experimental conditions C, the set of allowed-to-vary-factors
193   Iatv , and the goals of the study.
194   Example 3.1  (Continued). The research question of the “checkmate-in-one” study is as follows.
195   The scope is A = {LLMa }a=1,2,3 , C = {(posl , n)}l,n , Iatv = {“position”}) . The goal is “Does
196
      LLM1 rank consistently against the other LLMs?”

236   4     G ENERALIZABILITY OF EXPERIMENTAL STUDIES
237
238   The currently accepted definition of generalizability is the property of two independent studies with
239   the same research question to yield similar results National Academies of Science, 2019 and Pineau
240   et al. (2021). Although intuitive, this notion is not directly applicable as it does not provide a way to
241   measure the generalizability of a study. We now introduce a quantifiable notion of generalizability
242   of experimental studies, as the probability that any two empirical studies approximating the same
243   ideal study yield similar results.
244   Definition 4.1 (Generalizability). Let Q = (A, C, Iatv , κ) be the research question of an ideal study,
245   let P = S(Q) be the result of that study, and let d be some distance between probability distributions.
246   The generalizability of the ideal study on Q is

                                                                    n
247                        Gen (Q; ε, n) := Pn ⊗ Pn (Xj , Yj )j=1 : d(X, Y ) ≤ ε ,
248
249   where ε ∈ R+ is a similarity threshold.

      of an empirical study, P̂N = ŜN (Q), which approximates P under the assumption that the exper-

253

261   4.1     S IMILARITY BETWEEN RANKINGS — KERNELS
262
      Whether two experimental results (i.e., rankings) are similar or not ultimately depends on the goal of
263
      the study. For instance, consider two rankings on A = {a1 , a2 , a3 }, r = (1, 2, 3) and r′ = (1, 3, 2),
264   where ri is the tier of alternative ai . The conclusions drawn from r and r′ are identical if one’s
265   goal is to find the best alternative, but very different if one’s goal is to obtain an ordering of the
266   alternatives. One can use kernels to quantify the similarity between experimental results. Kernels
267   are suitable to formalize the aspects of the result of a study one wants to generalize, i.e., the goals of
268   the study. For instance, one kernel is suitable to identify the best tier while another kernel focuses
269   on the position of a specific alternative. In the following, we describe three representative kernels
      that cover a wide spectrum of possible goals.

      6     C ONCLUSION
498
499
      Limitations. First, we modeled experimental results as rankings, their similarity with kernels, and
      the similarity between distributions of results with the MMD. There are, of course, other possibil-
500
      ities, such as using the raw performance for the experimental results. Second, in Section 5, we
501
      post-processed missing evaluations by dropping or imputing them. One could achieve the same by
502   adapting the kernels to missing values.
503
504   Future work. First, as generalizability only deals with a fixed scope and alternatives, one can
505   include transportability—how well results hold when the scope changes—in our framework. Sec-
506   ond, we estimate the distribution of the MMD by sampling multiple times from the results. A
507   non-asymptotic theory of the MMD could speed up this procedure significantly. Third, we plan to
508   provide guarantees on the convergence of n∗N to the true value of results needed for generalizability,
509   n∗ .
510
511   Conclusions. An experimental study is generalizable if, with high probability, its findings will
512   hold under different experimental conditions, e.g., on unseen datasets. Non-generalizable studies
513
      might be of limited use or even misleading. This study is, to our knowledge, the first to develop a
      quantifiable notion for the generalizability of experimental studies. To achieve this, we formalize
514
      experiments, experimental studies and their results—rankings and distributions over rankings. Our
515
      approach allows us to estimate the number of experiments needed to achieve a desired level of
516   generalizability in new experimental studies. We demonstrate its utility showing generalizable and
517   non-generalizable results in two recent experimental studies.

      A.1     W HY RANKINGS ?
705
706   We chose to formalize experimental results as rankings for the following reasons:
707
708           (i) They are already widely used for non-parametric tests such as Friedman, Nemenyi, and
709               Conover-Iman Demsar (2006); Conover & Iman (1982).
710
             (ii) They do not suffer from experimental-condition-fixed effects, such as a dataset being inher-
                  ently easier to solve than another one. There are multiple ways to deal with these effects,
711
                  but, there none of these procedures is preferred over the others. A closely related problem
712
                  is that of consensus ranking aggregation Matteucci et al. (2023); Nießl et al. (2022).
713         (iii) By defining appropriate kernels for rankings (Section 4.1), we were able to model different
714               goals of a study.
715
716   A.1.1     OTHER POSSIBILITIES
717
718   Our framework, relying on the MMD and kernels to compare the results of studies, does not require
719   the results to be rankings. Instead, one can model the experimental results to be elements of an
720
      arbitrary probability space X, provided that 1. one can define a kernel on X, and 2. the kernel
      models the goals of the study. For instance, one can use the raw performance of the algorithms as
721
      the result and the Gaussian kernel to compare them. In this case, however, it is unclear what the goal
722
      of the corresponding study would be—how to interpret the kernel.
