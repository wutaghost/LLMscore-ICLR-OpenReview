011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) systems, while effective in general con-
014            texts, often face challenges in low-resource settings, especially in specialized do-
015
               mains such as healthcare. This study investigates the integration of Large Lan-
               guage Models (LLMs) with ASR systems to improve transcription accuracy in
016
               such environments. Focusing on medication-related conversations in healthcare,
017
               we fine-tuned the Whisper-Large ASR model on a custom dataset, Pharma-Speak,
018            and applied the LLaMA 3 model for second-pass rescoring to correct ASR output
019            errors. To achieve efficient fine-tuning without altering the full LLM parame-
020            ters, we employed Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), which enables re-ranking of
021            the ASR’s N-best hypotheses while retaining the LLM’s original knowledge. Our
022            results demonstrate a significant reduction in Word Error Rate (WER) across mul-
023            tiple epochs, validating the effectiveness of the LLM-based rescoring method. The
024            integration of LLMs in this framework shows potential for overcoming the limi-
025            tations posed by conventional ASR models in low-resource settings. While com-
026
               putational constraints and the inherent strength of Whisper-Large presented some
               limitations, our approach lays the groundwork for further exploration of domain-
027
               specific ASR enhancements using LLMs, particularly in healthcare applications.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Speech self-supervised learning has garnered significant interest owing to its encouraging results
033   in several downstream tasks, and it has emerged as a novel tool for low-resource language speech
034   recognition (Zhao & Zhang, 2022). Several studies have also used different speech models for down-
035   stream task on languages especially in low resource settings to achieve excellent results (Krishna
036   et al., 2021). While this is good, a number of authors have purported in their study that Automatic
037   Speech Recognition (ASR) models have shown good performance with English because it has been
038   extensively tested but not on other low-resource languages (Yi et al., 2020). Even though this is true,
039
      we also find the supposedly better English performing models struggling with terminologies within
      the healthcare space.
040
041   Olatunji et al. (2023) in their study noted that the recent years have witnessed notable progress in
042   the recognition of accented speech, as state-of-the-art (SOTA) automated speech recognition (ASR)
043   models have become adept in transcribing a wide range of linguistic interactions But there is still
044   a problem with these models’ applicability in clinical or medical settings1, where nuanced com-
045
      munication is crucial and this is especially noticeable when physicians who don’t speak English as
      their first language use ASR technology to record important medical data. Despite achieving low
046
      word error rates (WER) on speech in general, these SOTA models frequently have trouble reliably
047
      transcribing clinical named entities (NE) (Afonja et al., 2024). Furthermore, the majority of ASR
048   systems are not tailored to the particular requirements of resource-constrained situations, where
049   multilingual or noisy environment are widespread and computational resources may be inadequate.
050   ASR tools have therefore been noted to not be optimal in the clinical settings especially in low re-
051   source settings due to wide accent variety, really noisy environment, multiple speakers at a time, as
052   well as the frequent use of abbreviations within the medical conversation and minute errors in crucial
053   information such as medication names, diagnosis, test findings, and lesion measures (e.g., writing
      hyper- instead of hypo- or rifampin instead of rifampicin) may jeopardize patient safety and put

088   2   R ELATED W ORKS
089
090   Chen et al. (2024) in their study proposed Hyporadise, a baseline for generative speech recognition
091   with large language models. In another study, Li & Li (2023) combined LLMs with ASR for cor-
092   rection of Taiwan-Accentuated Text from an ASR. Similar to this study, Radhakrishnan et al. (2023)
093
      proposed Whispering LLaMa: A cross-modal fusion method intended for automated speech recogni-
      tion (ASR) generative error correction. To produce correct speech transcription contexts, they made
094
      use of a system that makes use of both external linguistic representations and sonic information.
095
096   Efforts to integrate ASR and LLMs specifically for healthcare have been largely limited to high-
097   resource settings, focusing on English-speaking populations and well-curated datasets. Works such
098   as Kanithi et al. (2024) demonstrated the utility of LLMs for healthcare conversation and also pro-
099
      posed MEDIC; an evaluation method, but this assumes the availability of high-quality training data.
      Our research differentiates itself by focusing on optimizing ASR and LLM systems specifically for
100
      low-resource healthcare settings, targeting both domain-specific adaptation and the practical chal-
101
      lenges of these environments, such as background noise and linguistic variability.

      parameter updates, we introduce LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) to avoid having to tune the entire set of
119
      parameters of a pre-trained model. This allows for efficient learning of the N-best to transcription
120
      mapping without affecting the pre-trained parameters of the LLM. By adding trainable low-rank
121   decomposition matrices to the current layers of LLMs, our approach allows the model to adjust to
122   new data while maintaining the original LLMs fixed to preserve the prior knowledge. By injecting
123   low-rank decomposition matrices 1, LoRA specifically executes a reparameterization of each model
124   layer expressed as a matrix multiplication. The representations produced by the LLM are therefore
125   not warped by task-specific tailoring. At the same time, the adaptor module gets the capability to
126   forecast the real transcription from the N-best theories.

131   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
132
133   The experimental settings for fine-tuning a large language model in a low-resource environment are
134   as follows.
135
136
      1. LLM used: The experiment employs the Llama-2-8b Instruct model. This model is an instruct
      model good for chat completion and text generation.
137
138   2. ASR model: Whisper-Large-v3 generates 10-best outputs
139
      3.The training is performed on Google Colab with an NVIDIA Tesla V100 GPU using 8-bit training.
140   The hyperparameters for finetuning are 15 epochs, learning rate 1e-4, batch size 64, and LoRA rank
141   r = 4.
142
143
      4.Dataset: We used an open source dataset which had about 600 medication names prescribed glob-
      ally with their trade names which we curated ourselves. It was separated to about 506 rows for the
144
      training and the rest for testing.
145
146   5.Evaluations: We used ROUGE score to evaluate the performance of the model

155   4.3   L IMITATIONS
156
157   This study had a couple of limitation. The first being resource constraint thereby preventing us from
158   being able to use the latest LLamA model and also GPU constraints which could have enabled us to
159   run more inference to get optimal results. In addition, the list of dataset used in not holistic, as there
160   are numerous other drugs that could not be captured. One important aspect realised is that some
161   drugs are more pronounced as their chemical names rather than the brand names which was ysed in
      the dataset.

      4.4   F URTHER D ISCUSSIONS
163
164   More work can be done on the use of latest LLaMa or even more sophiscticated models. It would
165   also be of interest to consider open source LLM that are domain-specific to the healthcare for ex-
166   ample BioBERT, MEDITRON model and a host of others to the compare it with SOTA models like
167   GPT 4 and LLaMa 3.1.

169   5     C ONCLUSION
170
171   This paper demonstrates that combining a Large Language Model with a speech recognition system
172   significantly enhances the recognition of medication names, even in low-resource environments. In
173   the future, we plan to conduct further experiments to validate the method’s effectiveness across a
174   wider variety of scenarios.
