009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We study Neural Foley, the automatic generation of high-quality sound effects
012            synchronizing with videos, enabling an immersive audio-visual experience. De-
013            spite its wide range of applications, existing approaches encounter limitations
014            when it comes to simultaneously synthesizing high-quality and video-aligned
015            (i.e.,semantic relevant and temporal synchronized) sounds. To overcome these
016            limitations, we propose FoleyCrafter, a novel framework that leverages a pre-
017            trained text-to-audio model to ensure high-quality audio generation. FoleyCrafter
018
               comprises two key components: a semantic adapter for semantic alignment and a
               temporal adapter for precise audio-video synchronization. The semantic adapter
019
               utilizes parallel cross-attention layers to condition audio generation on video fea-
020
               tures, producing realistic sound effects that are semantically relevant to the visual
021            content. Meanwhile, the temporal adapter estimates time-varying signals from
022            the videos and subsequently synchronizes audio generation with those estimates,
023            leading to enhanced temporal alignment between audio and video. One notable
024            advantage of FoleyCrafter is its compatibility with text prompts, enabling the use
025            of text descriptions to achieve controllable and diverse video-to-audio generation
026            according to user intents. We conduct extensive quantitative and qualitative exper-
027            iments on standard benchmarks to verify the effectiveness of FoleyCrafter. Models
028            and codes will be available.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      Foley, a key element in film and video post-production, adds realistic and synchronized sound ef-
033
      fects to silent media (contributors, 2024). These sound effects are the unsung heroes of cinema and
034   gaming, enhancing realism, impact, and emotional depth for an immersive audiovisual experience.
035   Traditionally, skilled Foley artists painstakingly create, record, and process sound effects in spe-
036   cialized studios, making it a labor-intensive and time-consuming process (Ament, 2014). Despite
037   advancements in recent video-to-audio generation, achieving Neural Foley, which requires synthe-
038   sizing high-quality, video-aligned sounds that are semantically related and temporally synchronized
039   with the videos, remains challenging (Luo et al., 2023).

053   State-of-the-art approaches for Neural Foley in video-to-audio generation can be categorized into
      two main groups, as illustrated in Figure 1. The first group involves training a video-to-audio gen-

054
      erative model on a large-scale paired audio-video dataset (Chen et al., 2020a; Iashin and Rahtu,
055   2021; Luo et al., 2023; Sheffer and Adi, 2023). However, the audio quality of such datasets crawled
056   from the Internet can be subpar, with issues like noise and complex environmental sounds recorded
057   in the wild, which hinder the production of high-quality sounds (Wang et al., 2024a; Xie et al.,
058   2024b). To address this, the second group of approaches (Figure 1-(b)) adopts a two-stage process.
059   They first translate video into text using video captioning or embedding mapping techniques and
060   then employ a pre-trained text-to-audio model (Wang et al., 2024a; Xie et al., 2024b; Xing et al.,
061   2024). Leveraging the well-trained text-to-audio generator, these methods achieve impressive sound
062   quality. Nonetheless, effectively bridging the gap between video and text while preserving nuanced
063
      details is challenging. As a result, these methods often produce unsynchronized sounds due to the
      suboptimal translated text conditions.
064
065   To achieve both high-quality and video-aligned sound generation, we present FoleyCrafter, which
066   breathes life into silent videos with realistic and synchronized sound effects. As depicted in Fig-
067   ure 1-(c), the core of FoleyCrafter is an innovative pluggable module that can be integrated with
068   a pre-trained text-to-audio (T2A) model, optimized with the supervision of audios. Specifically,
069
      FoleyCrafter comprises two main components: a semantic adapter for semantic alignment and a
      temporal adapter for temporal synchronization. The semantic adapter introduces parallel cross-
070
      attention layers into the backbone of the T2A model. It takes as input the extracted video features,
071
      allowing FoleyCrafter to generate audio conditioned on the video without relying on explicit text.
072   The temporal adapter, on the other hand, is engineered to refine temporal synchronization. The tem-
073   poral adapter has two keysteps: figuring out time-varying signals from videos and matching them
074   to synchronize audio generation. First, we study two ways to find audio signals in video frames.
075   One way uses labels to detect when sounds start, and the other method uses energy maps without
076   needing labels (Du et al., 2023). Then, we make the audio features line up with the video by match-
077   ing them to these found audio signals. Such a design results in an enhanced video-synchronized
078   audio generation. During training, we train the semantic adapter and temporal adapter with video-
079   audio correspondent data, while fixing the text-to-audio base model to preserve its established audio
080
      generation quality. After training, FoleyCrafter can generate high-quality sounds for videos with
      semantic and temporal alignment in a flexible and controllable way.
081
082   We conduct extensive experiments to evaluate the performance of FoleyCrafter in terms of audio
083   quality and video alignment, both semantically and temporally. Our experiments include quantitative
084   analysis, qualitative comparison, and user studies, all of which demonstrate that FoleyCrafter has
085   achieved state-of-the-art results. Additionally, we have showcased the controllability of FoleyCrafter
086
      through text prompts, allowing for a more fine-grained and versatile application of the model. Our
      main contributions can be summarized as follows:
087
088
             • We present a novel Neural Foley framework that generates high-quality, video-aligned
089            sound effects for silent videos, while also offering fine-grained control through text prompts.
090
091          • To ensure both semantic and temporal alignment, we design a semantic adapter and a tem-
092
               poral adapter, significantly improving video alignment.
093          • We validate the effectiveness of FoleyCrafter through extensive experiments, including
094            quantitative and qualitative analyses. Our results show that FoleyCrafter achieves state-
095            of-the-art performance on commonly used benchmarks.

      Diffusion-based Audio Generation. Latent diffusion models have significantly advanced audio
100
      generation (Liu et al., 2023a;b; Rombach et al., 2022). AudioLDM pioneers open-domain text-
101
      to-audio generation using a latent diffusion model (Liu et al., 2023a;b). Tango improves text-to-
102   audio generation with an instruction-tuned LLM FLAN-T5 (Chung et al., 2024a) as the text encoder
103   (Ghosal et al., 2023). Make-an-Audio tackles complex audio modeling using spectrogram autoen-
104   coders instead of waveforms (Huang et al., 2023). Xue et al. conduct comprehensive ablation studies
105   to explore effective designs and set a new state-of-the-art with the proposed Auffusion (Xue et al.,
106   2024). Moreover, some works (Guo et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024a; Chung et al., 2024b; Comunità
107   et al., 2024; Jeong et al., 2024) have further studied the conditional generation with temporal order
      condition, promoting the controllability of these diffusion models. In this paper, we introduce Fol-


                                                         2
108
      eyCrafter, a module that extends state-of-the-art text-to-audio generators to support video-to-audio
109   generation while preserving the original text-to-audio controllability.
110
111   Video-to-Audio Generation. Foley artistry is a crucial audio technique that enhances the viewer’s
112   auditory experience by creating and recording realistic sound effects that synchronize with visual
113   content (contributors, 2024). Early Neural Foley models mainly focus on generating sounds tailored
114   to a specific genre or a narrow spectrum of visual cues, underscoring the potential of deep learning to
115   innovate sound effect creation for videos (Chen et al., 2018; 2020b; Owens et al., 2016; Zhou et al.,
116   2018). Despite recent advancements in large-scale generative models (Huang et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
117   2023a), generating high-quality and visually synchronized sounds for open-domain videos remains
118
      a challenge (Dong et al., 2023; Du et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023; Mo et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024;
      Wang et al., 2024a; Comunità et al., 2024; Pascual et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Su et al., 2024).
119
120   State-of-the-art video-to-audio approaches can be categorized into two groups. The first group fo-
121   cuses on training video-to-audio generators from scratch (Iashin and Rahtu, 2021; Luo et al., 2023;
122   Sheffer and Adi, 2023). Specifically, SpecVQGAN (Iashin and Rahtu, 2021) employs a cross-
123   modal Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) to auto-regressively generate sounds from video tokens.
124
      Im2Wav (Sheffer and Adi, 2023) conditions an autoregressive audio token generation model us-
      ing CLIP features, while Diff-Foley (Luo et al., 2023) improves semantic and temporal alignment
125
      through contrastive pre-training on aligned video-audio data. However, these methods are limited
126
      by the availability of high-quality paired video-audio datasets. An alternative approach is to uti-
127   lize text-to-audio generators for video Foley. Xing et al. (2024) introduce an optimization-based
128   method with ImageBind (Girdhar et al., 2023) for video-audio alignment, while SonicVisionLM
129   (Xie et al., 2024b) generates video captions for text-to-audio synthesis. Wang et al. note the lim-
130   itations of caption-based methods and propose V2A-Mapper to translate visual embeddings to text
131   embedding space (Wang et al., 2024a). Nevertheless, effectively bridging the gap between video
132   and text while preserving fine-grained temporal cues remains a significant challenge. In contrast,
133   we introduce FoleyCrafter, integrating a learnable module into text-to-audio models with end-to-end
134   training, enabling a high-quality, video synchronized and high-controllable Foley.

      Audio Latent Diffusion Model. The latent diffusion model (LDM) has achieved remarkable ad-
147   vancements in text-to-audio generation, as demonstrated by recent studies (Ghosal et al., 2023; Liu
148   et al., 2023a;b; Xue et al., 2024). In this model, the audio waveform is initially transformed into a
149   mel-spectrogram representation. Subsequently, a variational autoencoder (VAE) encodes the mel-
150   spectrogram into a latent representation denoted as z. The LDM’s UNet is trained to generate z by
151   denoising normally distributed noise ϵ. The predicted latent z is then reconstructed by the VAE into
152   a mel-spectrogram, which is finally transformed into a waveform using a vocoder.
153   A latent diffusion model consists of two main processes: the diffusion process and the denoising pro-
154   cess. In the diffusion process, a clean latent representation z undergoes step-by-step noise addition
155   until it reaches an independently and identically distributed noise. It can be denoted as,

183

184

      3.2   F OLEY C RAFTER
200
201   The FoleyCrafter comprises two core components: a semantic adapter for semantic alignment and
202   a temporal adapter for temporal alignment. As illustrated in Figure 2, FoleyCrafter is a modular
203
      system that leverages a pre-trained text-to-audio (T2A) model (Freesound Project, 2024; Xue et al.,
      2024). This architecture enables FoleyCrafter to generate audio that is synchronized with videos,
204
      ensuring both high-quality and varied audio output. For our audio generation, we utilize Auffusion
205
      Xue et al. (2024) in our implementation. During training, only the two adapters are trainable, op-
206   timizing with the supervision of ground truth audio, while the weights of the T2A model remain
207   fixed. In the following sections, we provide more details of each component.

      3.2.2 TEMPORAL ADAPTER
285
286   We observed that the semantic adapter captures video-level alignment without precise temporal syn-
287
      chronization for each frame. To address this limitation, we develop a temporal adapter to enhance
      the temporal synchronization. As shown in Figure 4, Overall, there are two key steps: first, estimat-
288
      ing the time-varying signal from the video content, and second, synchronizing the audio with these
289
      estimated signals. This approach ensures that the generated audio features are precisely matched
290   to the audio cues extracted from the video, resulting in improved temporal synchronization. We
291   introduce more details of each step below.
292
293   Audio Signal Estimation from Videos. We study two distinct approaches for temporal estimation
294   from video content: one that relies on timestamps and another that focuses on energy levels. In the
295   timestamp-based method, a binary vector is used, where ’1’ indicates the presence of sound effects
296   and ’0’ signifies silence. This type of timestamp signal has been demonstrated to effectively manage
297   audio generation in a time-sensitive manner, as shown in Xie et al. (2024b); Comunità et al. (2024).
298   To this end, we have developed an estimator designed to extract features from video inputs to forecast
299
      the presence of sound at specific timestamps. This timestamp-based estimator is trained using binary
      cross-entropy loss (Xie et al., 2024b), which is expressed as:

311   Du et al., 2023). We train this energy map estimator using normalized energy values and mean

326   the replicated UNet blocks, using the same optimization goal as the diffusion model.

329   3.3   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
330
      For the semantic adapter, we follow Ye et al. (2023) to use a linear projection for clip visual em-
331
      bedding to better align with text representation and expand the embedding length to four. Then
332   we modify all the cross-attention to parallel cross-attention for visual conditions. We train semantic
333   adapter on the VGGSound (Chen et al., 2020a) for 164 epochs with a batch size of 128 on 8 NVIDIA
334   A100 GPUs. For the temporal adapter, we train the predictor and temporal adapter separately. The
335   predictor is trained on the subset of the VGGSound (Chen et al., 2020a) i.e. AVSync15 (Zhang
336   et al., 2024), which has a higher audio-visual relevance. The temporal adapter is also trained on the
337   VGGSound (Chen et al., 2020a) for 80 epochs. Note that the energy signal can be derived from the
338   mel-spectrum, whereas timestamps require manual annotation. This reduces data labeling costs and
339   provides us with more available data. After training, the two components in temporal adaptercan be
340
      combined together for inference and evaluation.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETTINGS
347
348   Baselines. We conducted comprehensive evaluations of FoleyCrafter by comparing it with state-
349
      of-the-art approaches, namely SpecVQGAN (Iashin and Rahtu, 2021), Diff-Foley (Luo et al., 2023),
      V2A-Mapper (Wang et al., 2024a), Seeing-and-hearing (Xing et al., 2024) and SonicVisionLM (Xie
350
      et al., 2024b). Both quantitative and qualitative comparisons were employed. SpecVQGAN gener-
351
      ates audio tokens autoregressively based on extracted video tokens. Diff-Foley utilizes contrastive
352   learning for synchronized video-to-audio synthesis with its CAVP audio and visual encoder. V2A-
353   Mapper translates visual CLIP embeddings to CLAP space, enabling video-aligned audio generation
354   using a pre-trained text-to-audio generator. Seeing-and-hearing (Xing et al., 2024) propose using
355   ImageBind (Girdhar et al., 2023) as a bridge between visual and audio, leveraging off-the-shelf au-
356   dio and video generators for multimodal generation. SonicVisionLM (Xie et al., 2024b) converts
357   video-to-audio generation into text-to-audio generation by utilizing a large language model (Chen
358   et al., 2023) to derive video captions for audio generation. Due to the unavailable source codes
359   and non-publicly datasets in SonicVisionLM (Xie et al., 2024b), we tried our best to reproduce it
360
      multiple times, and report their best results in our experiments for fair comparison.
361
362
363
      Evaluation Metrics. We employed several evaluation metrics to assess semantic alignment and
      audio quality, namely Mean KL Divergence (MKL) (Iashin and Rahtu, 2021), CLIP similarity, and
364
      Frechet Distance (FID) (Heusel et al., 2017), following the methodology of previous studies (Luo
365
      et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a; Xing et al., 2024). MKL measures paired sample-level similarity
366   by calculating the mean KL-divergence across all classes in the test set. CLIP Score compares the
367   similarity between the input video and the generated audio embeddings in the same representation
368   space. For this, we employed Wav2CLIP (Wu et al., 2022) as the audio encoder and CLIP (Radford
369   et al., 2021) as the video encoder, as done in previous works (Wang et al., 2024a; Sheffer and Adi,
370   2023). FID assesses the distribution similarity to evaluate the fidelity of the generated audio.
371
      For the temporal synchronization, we follow Du et al. (Du et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2024b) and
372   adopt onset detection accuracy (Onset Acc) and onset detection average precision (Onset AP) to
373   evaluate the generated audios, using the onset ground truth from the datasets. However, we identify
374   certain limitations with onset metrics. Firstly, they concentrate on the onset of sound effects while
375   overlooking the persistence of sounds and temporal changes. Therefore, following Du et al. (2023),
376   we also compute the mean absolute error of the audio energy. Secondly, the onset is obtained by
377   setting the threshold of audio amplitude which may lead to inaccuracies. So we follow Yariv et al.
      (2024) to calculate AV-Align as a supplement.

533   5   C ONCLUSION
534   In this paper, we introduce FoleyCrafter for adding sound effects to silent videos. Unlike existing
535   methods that either train a video-to-audio generator from scratch or use video-to-text translation fol-
536   lowed by text-to-audio generation, FoleyCrafter is a pluggable module seamlessly integrated into a
537   text-to-audio generator. This integration ensures high-quality audio generation while synchronizing
538   with the video content. FoleyCrafter leverages two key components, namely semantic adapter for
539   semantic alignment and temporal adapter for temporal synchronization. Extensive experiments on
      standard benchmarks demonstrate the effectiveness of FoleyCrafter.

751   B     D ETAILS OF E XPERIMENTS
752
753   B.1   D IFFERENCE BETWEEN TIMESTAMP AND ENERGY
754
755   Timestamp is a binary mask that indicates the presence or absence of sound effects at each sample
      point in the audio. Following Xie et al. (2024b); Comunità et al. (2024), we firstly predict the


                                                       14
756
      probability for the sound appearing and then use a threshold to convert it to a binary mask. Audio
757   Energy can be calculated from mel-spectrum Du et al. (2023); Jeong et al. (2024). We use the code
758   from Zhang et al. (2023b) to obtain normalized energy ground truth in training dataset. In practice,
759   our estimator outputs timestamps or energy values with the same length as the input video frames.
760   We then interpolate these to match the length of the audio latent representation.
761
762   B.2   D ETAILS OF T RAINING DATASET
763
764   FoleyCrafter consist of two key components: semantic adapter and temporal adapter which are
765   trained separately. For the training of semantic adapter we use VGGSound (Chen et al., 2020a)
766   as the training set. VGGSound is an audio-visual dataset containing approximately 199,176 videos
767
      sourced from YouTube with annotated label classes indicating the video content. We add the prefix
      ’The sound of’ to the label to form the prompt for generation. We train the timestamp and energy
768
      estimator on the AVSync15 (Zhang et al., 2024). AVSync15 is a carefully curated dataset from the
769
      VGGSound Sync (Chen et al., 2021) dataset, which contains 1500 strongly correlated audio-visual
770   pairs, making it a high-quality dataset for temporal synchronization. For both timestamp and energy
771   estimator, we train them for 30 epochs. When training the timestamp-based temporal adapter, we
772   need the ground truth timestamp lables for sound event. So we train it on AudioSet Strong (Hershey
773   et al., 2021) which contains 103,463 videos with the audio and the corresponding timestamp labels.
774   For the training of energy-based adapter, we also use the VGGSound Chen et al. (2020a) as we can
775   simply obtain ground truth energy from mel-spectrum.
