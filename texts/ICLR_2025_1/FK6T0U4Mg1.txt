011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Fine-tuning Large Language Models (LLMs) has proven effective for a variety
014            of downstream tasks. However, as LLMs grow in size, the memory demands for
015            backpropagation become increasingly prohibitive. Zeroth-order (ZO) optimization
016
               methods offer a memory-efficient alternative by using forward passes to estimate
               gradients, but the variance of gradient estimates typically scales linearly with the
017
               model’s parameter dimension—a significant issue for LLMs. In this paper, we
018
               propose the random Subspace Zeroth-order (SubZero) optimization to address
019            the challenges posed by LLMs’ high dimensionality. We introduce a low-rank
020            perturbation tailored for LLMs that significantly reduces memory consumption
021            while improving training performance. Additionally, we prove that our gradient
022            estimation closely approximates the backpropagation gradient, exhibits lower vari-
023            ance than traditional ZO methods, and ensures convergence when combined with
024            SGD. Experimental results show that SubZero enhances fine-tuning performance
025            and achieves faster convergence compared to standard ZO approaches like MeZO
026            across various language modeling tasks. The source code will be released publicly.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs), such as the GPT and LLaMA series (Zhang et al., 2022; Touvron
032   et al., 2023), have recently demonstrated impressive capabilities in natural language processing tasks
033
      and beyond (Solaiman et al., 2019; Achiam et al., 2023). These models utilize deep learning, particu-
      larly the transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017), to learn complex patterns in language data.
034
      However, LLMs can struggle with specialized tasks that require domain-specific knowledge (Shen
035
      et al., 2024). Fine-tuning presents an effective solution by slightly adjusting pre-trained LLMs with
036   domain data, enabling them to adapt to specific tasks more effectively.
037
038   For fine-tuning, first-order (FO) optimizers, such as SGD (Amari, 1993) or Adam (Kingma & Ba,
039
      2015), are commonly used to achieve promising performance on domain datasets. However, as
      LLMs grow in size, FO optimizers demand increasingly memory consumption due to the gradient
040
      computations required by backpropagation (BP) (Zhao et al., 2024a). To enhance memory efficiency,
041
      MeZO (Malladi et al., 2023) first introduces the zeroth-order (ZO) optimizer to LLM fine-tuning
042   without BP. It just needs forward passes and calculates gradient estimates using finite differences
043   of training loss values. Nevertheless, the variance of ZO gradient estimates linearly depends on
044   the perturbation dimension, which corresponds to the number of model parameters. This can
045   become extremely large in LLMs, resulting in significant performance degradation compared to FO
046   optimizers (Gautam et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024).
047
      There are two main attempts to addressing the high variance of ZO gradient estimates. The first
048   approach involves increasing batch size alongside training iterations, which reduces gradient noise
049   and variance in ZO gradient estimates (Gautam et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024). However, this leads to
050   significant runtime and memory costs due to the large batch size in the later training stages. The second
051   approach focuses on perturbing fewer parameters by employing sparse parameter perturbations, such
052   as random and sparse pruning masks (Liu et al., 2024) and block-coordinate perturbations (Zhang
053   et al., 2024), or by reducing the number of trainable parameters through techniques like parameter-
      efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) (Malladi et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) and tensorized adapters (Yang

105   Zeroth-Order Fine-Tuning. ZO optimizers utilize just two forward passes to estimate gradient
106   without BP. Malladi et al. (2023) first used ZO optimization to fine-tune LLMs, significantly lowering
107   the GPU hours and memory usage to levels similar to inference, which offers a considerable advantage
      over FO optimizers. They demonstrated that LLM fine-tuning benefits from a well-structured loss

      landscape by introducing suitable task-specific prompt templates. Convergence theories for ZO
109   optimization have been elaborated in both convex (Nesterov & Spokoiny, 2017; Jamieson et al.,
110   2012; Duchi et al., 2015) and non-convex settings (Liu et al., 2018; Ji et al., 2019). However, these
111   convergence rates typically increase linearly with the number of trainable parameters (Nesterov &
112   Spokoiny, 2017; Jamieson et al., 2012; Duchi et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2018; Ji et al., 2019).
113
      Recently, more work in ZO has focused on improving the convergence rates and reducing gradient
114
      estimation variance for LLM fine-tuning. Increasing batch size can diminish noise in ZO gradient
115   estimation (Gautam et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024). Perturbing a subset of model parameters also
116   lowers gradient variance. This approach induces sparse parameter perturbations through random
117   and sparse pruning masks (Liu et al., 2024) or block-coordinate perturbations (Zhang et al., 2024).
118   Additionally, some approaches tried to reduce trainable parameters through PEFT (Malladi et al.,
119   2023; Zhang et al., 2024) and tensorized adapters (Yang et al., 2024).
120
      Random Subspace Optimization. To lessen dependence on dimensionality, some research utilizes
121   random projections and low-dimensional perturbations in subspaces (Nozawa et al., 2024; Roberts &
122   Royer, 2023; Kozak et al., 2021). However, these methods are hindered by the need to store a large
123   projection matrix that increases with dimensionality, making it impractical for fine-tuning LLMs.
124
      Memory-Efficient Fine-Tuning. Fine-tuning generally employs FO optimizers like SGD (Amari,
125
      1993) or Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015). Various approaches have been developed to reduce the memory
126
      cost of BP, such as sparsifying gradients (Sun et al., 2017), projecting gradients into a low-rank
127   subspace (Zhao et al., 2024a), and quantizing optimizer states to lower bits (Dettmers et al., 2022b;
128   Li et al., 2024). Additional methods to conserve activation and weight memory during forward and
129   backward passes include gradient checkpointing (Chen et al., 2016), FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022),
130   QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2024), and LLM.int8() (Dettmers et al., 2022a).

197   Our intuition is that exploring update directions in a low-dimensional subspace may result in a
198   reduced variance of the estimated gradient compared to the estimation in the vanilla space as used in
199   MeZO. Inspired by (Zhao et al., 2024a; Nozawa et al., 2024; Roberts & Royer, 2023), we propose the
200   random Subspace Zeroth-order (SubZero) optimization framework tailored for LLM fine-tuning. This
201   framework reduces gradient estimation variance, and minimizes the memory overhead associated
202   with gradient estimation, such as the memory overhead caused by the projection matrix P in Eqn. (5)
203
      used in (Nozawa et al., 2024; Roberts & Royer, 2023).
204   Layer-wise Random Subspace Perturbation. LLMs primarily consist of dense layers that perform
205   matrix multiplication. We denote the trainable parameters of the i-th layer in matrix form as
206   Wi ∈ Rmi ×ni . Then we will explain how to design its low-rank perturbation Z̃i ∈ Rmi ×ni .
207

208   with previous random subspace methods that focus on the entire model’s parameters (Nozawa et al.,

213   projection matrices yields worse performance than using our designed column-orthogonal matrices.

320   We describe the integration of SubZero into full-parameter tuning (Aghajanyan et al., 2021) and
321   three promient PEFT schemes: LoRA (Hu et al., 2022), prefix tuning (Li & Liang, 2021), and
322   prompt tuning (Lester et al., 2021). Typically, SubZero can be easily incorporated into these fine-
323   tuning schemes. However, it encounters a challenge with extremely non-square parameter matrices,
      which have far more rows than columns or vice versa. This issue is particularly prevalent in LoRA,

      7     C ONCLUSION
531
532   We have demonstrated that SubZero effectively fine-tunes large LLMs across various tasks and
533   schemes with a memory cost comparable to that of inference. Extra experiments indicate that
534   SubZero can optimize non-differentiable objectives. Our theory explains how SubZero reduces the
535   variance of gradient estimates and accelerates convergence.
536
537   Limitation. In addition to the SGD optimizer, we have yet to explore combining SubZero with other
538   first-order optimizers, such as Adam. While SubZero is also compatible with other memory-efficient
539   techniques like parameter quantization (Li et al., 2024), we have not thoroughly investigated the
      practical effects of these combinations. We will leave these explorations for future work.

      A.3    H YPERPARAMETERS
823
824   Using a larger batch size can consistently reduce the variance in ZO optimization, thus enhancing
825   fine-tuning performance (Malladi et al., 2023; Gautam et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024). However, this
826   increase in batch size also raises the time for forward passes and significantly elevates memory usage.
827   We focus on developing ZO methods that minimize variance and improve performance with small
828   batch sizes, with a default setting of 16. In some SGD experiments, like on MultiRC and SQuAD,
829   the batch size is reduced to 8 due to limited GPU resources.
830   Consistent with previous studies (Malladi et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Yang et al.,
831   2024), we employ SGD without momentum by default to maintain memory efficiency. SGD utilizes
832   linear learning scheduling, while all ZO methods apply a constant learning rate, with weight decay
833   set to 0.
834
      For RoBERTa, we run Adam for 1K steps and ZO methods for 100K steps. In the rest experiments,
835
      we run Adam for 5 epochs and SGD and ZO methods for 20K steps.
836

838   Zhang et al., 2024). For LoRA, the rank is set to 8 and α is set to 16. For prefix tuning, the length of
839
      prefix tokens is set to 5, and we initialize these tunable representations by randomly sampling tokens
      from the vocabulary and then passing them through the LLM to get their keys and values at different
840
      attention layers. For prompt tuning, the length of prompt virtual tokens is set to 10, and the prompt
841
      tokens are initialized with actual token values from the model’s embedding.

973
974    In practice, SubZero employs smaller and layer-specific low-rank perturbation matrices instead
975    of a large model-scale projection matrix. However, it is more convenient to prove SubZero’s
976    properties using a model-scale projection. Fortunately, the following lemma shows that the low-rank
977    perturbation matrix for each layer can be represented as a layer-scale projection matrix, which is
978    column orthogonal.
979    Lemma 1. Let Z̃ = U ZV T , where U ∈ Rm×r , Z ∈ Rr×r , V ∈ Rn×r , and U T U = V T V = Ir .
980    Then we have vec(Z̃) = P vec(Z) and P T P = Ir2 , where P = V ⊗ U .
981
982    Proof. Since vec(U ZV T ) = (V ⊗ U )vec(Z), we only need to show (V ⊗ U )T (V ⊗ U ) = Ir2 .
983    In fact
984
              (V ⊗ U )T (V ⊗ U ) = (V T ⊗ U T )(V ⊗ U ) = (V T V ) ⊗ (U T U ) = Ir ⊗ Ir = Ir2 .
985
986    The proof is completed.
987
988    We can also demonstrate that the low-rank perturbation matrices across all layers can be represented
989    as a model-scale projection matrix. We first give the following lemma.
990    Lemma 2. Let a block diagonal matrix P = bdiag(P1 , P2 , · · · , Pl ) and z̃i = Pi zi , where
991    PiT Pi = Ir2 and i = 1, 2, . . . , l. Then we have z̃ = P z, where z̃ = [z̃1T , . . . , z̃lT ]T , z = [z1T ,
992    . . . , zlT ]T and P T P = Ilr2 .
993
994    Proof. It is easy to check that z̃ = P z. Besides, we have
995
              P T P = bdiag(P1T , . . . , PlT )bdiag(P1 , . . . , Pl ) = bdiag(P1T P1 , . . . , PlT Pl ) = Ilr2 .
996
997    The proof is completed.

1000   [vec(Z1 )T , vec(Z2 )T , . . . , vec(Zl )T ]T , and z̃ = [vec(Z̃1 )T , vec(Z̃2 )T , . . . , vec(Z̃l )T ]T . Then ac-
1001   cording to Lemma 2, the perturbation vector of SubZero is z̃ = P z, which is similar as existing
1002   random subspace methods in Eqn. (4), but with SubZero’s projection matrix being block diagonal
