009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Algorithms that balance the stability-plasticity trade-off are well-studied in the
012            continual learning literature. However, only a few of them focus on obtaining
013            models for specified trade-off preferences. When solving the problem of continual
014            learning under specific trade-offs (CLuST), state-of-the-art techniques leverage
015            rehearsal-based learning, which requires retraining when a model corresponding
016            to a new trade-off preference is requested. This is inefficient since there exist in-
017            finitely many different trade-offs, and a large number of models may be requested.
018
               As a response, we propose Imprecise Bayesian Continual Learning (IBCL), an
               algorithm that tackles CLuST efficiently. IBCL replaces retraining with constant-
019
               time convex combination. Given a new task, IBCL (1) updates the knowledge
020
               base in the form of a convex hull of model parameter distributions and (2) gener-
021            ates one Pareto-optimal model per given trade-off via convex combination with-
022            out any additional training. That is, obtaining models corresponding to specified
023            trade-offs via IBCL is zero-shot. Experiments whose baselines are current CLuST
024            algorithms show that IBCL improves by at most 45% on average per task accuracy
025            and by 43% on peak per task accuracy, while maintaining a near-zero to positive
026            backward transfer. Moreover, its training overhead, measured by number of batch
027            updates, remains constant at every task, regardless of the number of preferences
028            requested. Details at: https://github.com/ibcl-anon/ibcl.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Continual learning (CL), also known as lifelong machine learning, is a special case of multi-task
      learning, where tasks arrive in temporal sequence one-by-one (Chen and Liu, 2016; Parisi et al.,
034
      2019; Ruvolo and Eaton, 2013b; Thrun, 1998). Two key properties matter for CL algorithms: stabil-
035
      ity and plasticity (De Lange et al., 2021). Here, stability means the ability to maintain performance
036   on previous tasks, not forgetting what the model has learned, and plasticity means the ability to
037   adapt to a new task. Unfortunately, these two properties are conflicting due to the multi-objective
038   optimization nature of CL (Kendall et al., 2018; Sener and Koltun, 2018). For years, researchers
039   have been balancing the stability-plasticity trade-off. However, few have discussed the problem of
040   learning models for specifically given trade-off points. In this paper, we focus on such a problem,
041   which we denote as CL under specific trade-offs (CLuST).
042   Why is CLuST important? First, in certain scenarios, it is important to explicitly specify how much
043   stability and plasticity are needed, in order to obtain one customized model per trade-off preference.
044   Second, when there exist a large number of preferences, the efficiency of training every customized
045   model matters. Otherwise, the training cost accumulates over all preferences and becomes pro-
046   hibitive. Therefore, we not only seek a solution for CLuST, but also an efficient one.
047
      Let us consider an example of a movie recommendation system. The model is first trained to rate
048   movies in the sci-fi genre. Then, a new genre, e.g., documentaries, is added by the movie company.
049   The model needs to learn how to rate documentaries while not forgetting how to rate sci-fis. Train-
050   ing this model boils down to a CL problem. The company now wants to build a recommendation
051   system that adapts to users’ tastes in movies. For example, Alice has equal preferences over sci-fis
052   and documentaries. Bob, however, wants to watch only documentaries and has no interest in sci-fis
053   at all. Consequently, the company aims to train two customized models for Alice and Bob, respec-
      tively, to predict how likely a sci-fi or a documentary is to be recommended. Based on individual

      To formalize the CLuST problem, we take a Bayesian perspective, where learnable model param-
      eters are viewed as random variables (Farquhar and Gal, 2019; Kessler et al., 2023; Nguyen et al.,
074
      2018). As illustrated in Figure 1, we consider all parameter distributions living in a metric space.
075
      This metric can be any valid metric for distributions, such as the 2-Wasserstein distance (Deza and
076   Deza, 2013). The figure shows an example of two tasks, with their ground-truth distributions be-
077   ing q1 and q2 , respectively. From this setup, a distribution emphasizing stability (at task 2) is a
078   distribution closer to q1 than q2 , and one prioritizing plasticity is closer to q2 than q1 . Notice that ir-
079   respective of the desired stability-plasticity trade-off, we want the distribution to be Pareto-optimal,
080   which loosely means that there is no way to improve such a distribution by making it closer to both
081   q1 and q2 . We can see that Pareto-optimality is equivalent to being inside the convex set enclosed
082   by q1 and q2 . For example, q ′ in the figure is a Pareto-optimal distribution, while q ′′ is not. With
083   this setting, we can specify a trade-off point using a preference vector (Mahapatra and Rajan, 2020;
084
      2021) w̄ = (w1 , w2 ), where w1 , w2 ≥ 0 and w1 +w2 = 1. The preferred Pareto-optimal distribution
      is therefore a convex combination w1 q1 + w2 q2 .
085
086   So far, researchers have already proposed the use of preference vectors to specify trade-off points
087   in multi-task and continual learning (Gupta et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2019; 2020; Ma et al., 2020).
088   However, instead of using them as coefficients for convex combinations, state-of-the-art techniques
089   use them as regularizers in rehearsal-based methods. That is, existing algorithms that aim to
090           Pi memorize some data di for each task i (for “rehearsal”), and let the loss at task i
      solve CLuST

092   drawbacks to this approach. First, rehearsals have to retrain the entire model whenever we have a
093
      new trade-off preference. In plain words, these methods have a training overhead proportional
      to the number of preferences at each task. As there exist infinitely many possible preferences,
094
      this boils down to an efficiency issue when there is a large number of preferences, such as a large
095
      number of users in the movie recommendation example. It would be desirable if, instead of retrain-
096   ing, we could obtain the preferred models by constant-time, training-free operations. Moreover,
097   rehearsals have to cache data, and stable performance on previous tasks depends on which data can
098   be memorized.
099

100
      tinual Learning (IBCL), whose workflow is illustrated in Figure 2. At step 1, upon the arrival of
101

102   tasks) in the form of a convex set of distributions with finitely many extreme elements (the elements

104   (FGCS) (Caprio et al., 2024). This is done by variational inference from the previous task’s learned
105   distribution, and the learned distributions serve as extreme elements of the FGCS. Each point in the
106   FGCS corresponds to one Pareto-optimal distribution on the trade-off polytope of all tasks so far.
107   Then, at step 2, given any preference vector w̄, IBCL selects the preferred distribution by convex
      combination. A parameter region is obtained as a highest density region (HDR) of the distribu-

126   IBCL tackles the identified shortcomings as follows. First, IBCL replaces retraining in state-of-

128   training overhead per task (to update the FGCS), independent of the number of preferences.

178   3     F ORMULATING THE CL U ST P ROBLEM
179
180   In this section we formalize the CLuST problem. We consider domain-incremental learning (Van de
181   Ven and Tolias, 2019) for classification models, with an unbounded number of stability-plasticity
182   trade-off preferences at each task. The goal is to construct a learning algorithm with training over-
183   head independent of the number of preferences, and that enjoys performance guarantees.
184
185   3.1   A SSUMPTIONS
186
187
      Let X be the space of inputs, and Y be the space of labels. In a typical classification problem, X
      will be a subset of a Euclidean space, and Y a finite set. In a typical regression problem, Y will
188
      too be a subset of a Euclidean space. In general, we do not limit ourselves to either scenario. As
189
      a consequence, we let the input and the output spaces be generic sets. Call ∆X Y the space of all
190   possible distributions on X × Y. A task i is associated with a distribution pi ∈ ∆X Y , from which
191   labeled data can be i.i.d. drawn. We assume that all tasks are similar to each other.
192
      Assumption 1 (Task Similarity). For all task i, pi ∈ F, where F is a convex subset of ∆X Y . Also,
193
      we assume that the diameter of F is some r > 0, that is, supp,q∈F ∥p − q∥W2 ≤ r, where ∥ · ∥W2
194
      denotes the 2-Wasserstein distance.
195
196   The reason for choosing the 2-Wasserstein metric is that it enables easy computation of convex
197   combination of distributions. Under this metric, the weighted sum of distributions can be derived
198   from a weighted sum of means and standard deviations. Details are in Appendix D.
199
      Notice that Assumption 1 does not tell us that all tasks have the same distribution, but merely that
200   the true data generating processes pertaining to different tasks are not too distant from one another.
201   In addition, such a notion of “being not too distant” is entirely in the hands of the user, via the choice
202   of radius r and of the metric to endow ∆X Y . This is rather natural: for the time being, we do not
203   expect e.g. a robot to be able to fold our clothes (task 1), and then deliver a payload in a combat
204   zone (task 2). Assumption 1 is needed to mitigate the possible model misspecification, which in turn
205   could lead to catastrophic forgetting even when Bayesian inference is carried out exactly ((Kessler
206   et al., 2023) and Appendix E).
207   More formally, under Assumption 1, for any two tasks i and j, their underlying distributions pi and
208   pj are “close enough”, i.e. ∥pi − pj ∥W2 ≤ r. Moreover, since F is convex, any convex combination
209   of task distributions belongs to F. Next, we assume the parameterization of class F.
210   Assumption 2 (Parameterization of Task Distributions). Every distribution F in F is parameterized
211   by θ, a parameter belonging to a parameter space Θ.
212
213   An example of a parameterized family that satisfies Assumption 1 is given in Appendix F. Notice
214   that all tasks share the same input space X and label space Y, and we do not have task ids as an
215   additional input, so the learning is domain-incremental (Van de Ven and Tolias, 2019). We then
      formalize stability-plasticity trade-off preferences over tasks.

395   5.1   S ETUP
396
      Due to page limit, detailed setup can be found in Appendix H.
397
398   Baselines. Although there exist many baseline methods for CL, only a few baselines for CLuST exist.
399   The following CLuST baselines are selected for comparison.
400
      1. Rehearsal-based. This is the state-of-the-art technique for CLuST (Lin et al., 2019). These
401      methods memorize a subset of training data of every task encountered so far. Task preferences
402      are then given as weights to regularize the loss on each task’s memorized data. We choose GEM
403      (Lopez-Paz and Ranzato, 2017) and A-GEM (Chaudhry et al., 2018) as two typical rehearsal-
404      based methods.
405   2. Rehearsal-based, Bayesian. Since IBCL is a Bayesian method, we also compare it to a
406      Bayesian technique, VCL (Nguyen et al., 2018). We equip VCL with episodic memory to make
407      it rehearsal-based, and so to be able to specify a preference. This approach has been used in
408      (Servia-Rodriguez et al., 2021).
409   3. Prompt-based. Prompt-based CL has never been used for CLuST and therefore not a state-
410      of-the-art. Still, they are considered efficient modern CL techniques. Therefore, we made an
411      attempt to specify preferences on L2P (Wang et al., 2022), a prompt-based method, by train-
412      ing one learnable prompt prefix per task, and use a preference-weighted sum of the prompts at
413
         inference time.
414   Benchmarks. We experiment on four standard continual learning benchmarks, including three im-
415   age classification and one NLP: (i) 15 tasks in CelebA (Liu et al., 2015) (with vs. without attributes),
416   (ii) 10 tasks in Split CIFAR-100 (Zenke et al., 2017) (animals vs. non-animals), (iii) 10 tasks in
417   TinyImageNet (Le and Yang, 2015) (animals vs. non-animals) and (iv) 5 tasks in 20NewsGroup
418   (Lang, 1995) (news related to computers vs. not related to computers). For the first three image
419   benchmarks, features are first extracted by ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016). For 20NewsGroup, features
420   are extracted by TF-IDF (Aizawa, 2003). For each benchmark, all tasks share the same input and
421   label space. There is no task id at training nor inference time, so the algorithm does not know from
422
      which task each data point comes from. Therefore, all experiments are set up as domain-incremental
      according to Van de Ven and Tolias (2019).
423
424   Metrics. As in standard continual learning evaluation, after training on task i, we evaluate the
425   accuracy on all testing data of previous tasks j ∈ {1, . . . , i}. To evaluate how well does a model
426   address preferences, we randomly generate nprefs = 10 preferences per task, except for task 1, whose
427   preference is always given by scalar 1. Therefore, for each method, we obtain 10 models at each
428
      task, and we evaluate a preference-weighted sum of their accuracies on previous tasks. Finally,
      these preference-weighted accuracies are used to compute standard continual learning metrics. The
429
      learning performance is measured by average per task accuracy and peak per task accuracy, and
430
      forgetting is measured by backward transfer (Dı́az-Rodrı́guez et al., 2018).
431
      System. Experiments are run on Intel(R) Core(TM) i7-8550U CPU @ 1.80GHz.

523   6    C ONCLUSION
524
525   Overall, we propose a probabilistic continual learning algorithm, namely IBCL, to tackle the CLuST
526   problem, where an unbounded number of stability-plasticity trade-off preferences may be requested
527   at each task. The design of IBCL enables not only learning performance, but also efficiency when
528   solving the CLuST problem, as state-of-the-art methods require optimizing a loss function per pref-
529   erence, while IBCL only needs convex combinations. Such a benefit applies to various scales of
530
      models. It will be an interesting future direction to find a use case on large-scale models.
531   Limitations of IBCL. Poorly performing models can also be sampled from IBCL’s HDRs. However,
532   in practice, we can fine-tune α to shrink down the HDR to avoid poorly performing ones, as shown
533   in the ablation studies. In addition, for a user-specified preference vector w̄, probability pw̄ is the
534   optimal distribution under entropy loss. One future research direction is to derive the preference
535   vector w̄ from some inputs. For example, we may learn this vector from an additional sequence of
536
      prompts (Wu et al., 2024). In that case, the preference vector itself might be different according to
      the design, including which loss is used.
537
538   Broader Impacts. IBCL is potentially useful in deriving user-customized models from large multi-
539   task models, due to its not only guaranteed, but also zero-shot, preference addressing. These include
      large language models, recommendation systems and other applications.

      Let q0 (θ) be our prior probability density/mass function (pdf/pmf) on parameter θ ∈ Θ at time
      t = 0. At time t = 1, we collect data (x̄1 , ȳ1 ) pertaining to task 1, we elicit likelihood pdf/pmf
691
      ℓ1 (x̄1 , ȳ1 | θ), and we compute q1 (θ | x̄1 , ȳ1 ) ∝ q0 (θ) × ℓ1 (x̄1 , ȳ1 | θ). At time t = 2, we collect
692
      data (x̄2 , ȳ2 ) pertaining to task 2 and we elicit likelihood pdf/pmf ℓ2 (x̄2 , ȳ2 | θ). Now we have two
693   options.
694
695           (i) Bayesian Continual Learning (BCL): we let the prior pdf/pmf at time t = 2 be the posterior
696               pdf/pmf at time t = 1. That is, our prior pdf/pmf is q1 (θ | x̄1 , ȳ1 ), and we compute q2 (θ |
697               x̄1 , ȳ1 , x̄2 , ȳ2 ) ∝ q1 (θ | x̄1 , ȳ1 ) × ℓ2 (x̄2 , ȳ2 | θ) ∝ q0 (θ) × ℓ1 (x̄1 , ȳ1 | θ) × ℓ2 (x̄2 , ȳ2 | θ);1
698          (ii) Bayesian Isolated Learning (BIL): we let the prior pdf/pmf at time t = 2 be a generic prior
699               pdf/pmf q0′ (θ). We compute q2′ (θ | x̄2 , ȳ2 ) ∝ q0′ (θ) × ℓ2 (x̄2 , ȳ2 | θ). We can even re-use
700               the original prior, so that q0′ = q0 .
701
         1
             Here we tacitly assume that the likelihoods are independent.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      As we can see, in option (i) we assume that the data generating process at time t = 2 takes into
703   account both tasks, while in option (ii) we posit that it only takes into account task 2. Denote by
704   σ(X) the sigma-algebra generated by a generic random variable X. Let also Q2 be the probability
705   measure whose pdf/pmf is q2 , and Q′2 be the probability measure whose pdf/pmf is q2′ . Then, we
706   have the following.
707   Proposition 1. Posterior probability measure Q2 can be written as a σ(X̄1 , Ȳ1 , X̄2 , Ȳ2 )-measurable
708   random variable taking values in [0, 1], while posterior probability measure Q′2 can be written as a
709   σ(X̄2 , Ȳ2 )-measurable random variable taking values in [0, 1].
710
711   Proof. Pick any A ⊂ Θ. Then, Q2 [A | σ(X̄1 , Ȳ1 , X̄2 , Ȳ2 )] = EQ2 [1A | σ(X̄1 , Ȳ1 , X̄2 , Ȳ2 )], a
712   σ(X̄1 , Ȳ1 , X̄2 , Ȳ2 )-measurable random variable taking values in [0, 1]. Notice that 1A denotes the
713   indicator function for set A. Similarly, Q′2 [A | σ(X̄2 , Ȳ2 )] = EQ′2 [1A | σ(X̄2 , Ȳ2 )], a σ(X̄2 , Ȳ2 )-
714   measurable random variable taking values in [0, 1]. This is a well-known result in measure theory
715   (Billingsley, 1986).
716
717   Of course Proposition 1 holds for all t ≥ 2. Recall that the sigma-algebra σ(X) generated by a
718   generic random variable X captures the idea of information encoded in observing X. An immediate
719   corollary is the following.
720   Corollary 1. Let t ≥ 2. Then, if we opt for BIL, we lose all the information encoded in
721   {(X̄i , Ȳi )}t−1
                    i=1 .
722
723   In turn, if we opt for BIL, we obtain a posterior that is not measurable with respect to
724   σ({(X̄i , Ȳi )}ti=1 ) \ σ(X̄t , Ȳt ). If the true data generating process pt is a function of the previ-
725   ous data generating processes pt′ , t′ ≤ t, this leaves us with a worse approximation of the “true”
726   posterior Qtrue ∝ Q0 × pt .
727   The phenomenon in Corollary 1 is commonly referred to as catastrophic forgetting. Continual learn-
728   ing literature is unanimous in labeling catastrophic forgetting as undesirable – see e.g. (Farquhar and
729   Gal, 2019; Li et al., 2020). For this reason, in this work we adopt a BCL approach. In practice, we
730   cannot compute the posterior pdf/pmf exactly, and we will resort to variational inference to ap-
731   proximate them – an approach often referred to as Variational Continual Learning (VCL) (Nguyen
732   et al., 2018). As we shall see in Appendix E, Assumption 1 is needed in VCL to avoid catastrophic
733
      forgetting.

735   A.1     R ELATIONSHIP BETWEEN IBCL AND OTHER BCL TECHNIQUES
736
      Like (Farquhar and Gal, 2019; Li et al., 2020), the weights in our Bayesian neural networks (BNNs)
737
      have Gaussian distribution with diagonal covariance matrix. Because IBCL is rooted in Bayesian
738   continual learning, we can initialize IBCL with a much smaller number of parameters to solve
739   a complex task as long as it can solve a set of simpler tasks. In addition, IBCL does not need to
740   evaluate the importance of parameters by measures such as computing the Fisher information, which
741   are computationally expensive and intractable in large models.

743   A.1.1    R ELATIONSHIP BETWEEN IBCL AND MAML
744
745   In this section, we discuss the relationship between IBCL and the Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning
746
      (MAML) and Bayesian MAML (BMAML) procedures introduced in (Finn et al., 2017; Yoon et al.,
      2018), respectively. These are inherently different than IBCL, since the latter is a continual learning
747
      procedure, while MAML and BMAML are meta-learning algorithms. Nevertheless, given the popu-
748
      larity of these procedures, we feel that relating IBCL to them would be useful to draw some insights
749   on IBCL itself.
750
751   In MAML and BMAML, a task i is specified by a ni -shot dataset Di that consists of a small number
752
      of training examples, e.g. observations (x1i , y1i ), . . . , (xni , yni ). Tasks are sampled from a task
      distribution T such that the sampled tasks share the statistical regularity of the task distribution. In
753
      IBCL, Assumption 1 guarantees that the tasks pi share the statistical regularity of class F. MAML
754
      and BMAML leverage this regularity to improve the learning efficiency of subsequent tasks.
755
      At each meta-iteration i,


                                                           14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      1. Task-Sampling: For both MAML and BMAML, a mini-batch Ti of tasks is sampled from the
757      task distribution T. Each task τi ∈ Ti provides task-train and task-validation data, Dτtrni and Dτvali ,
758      respectively.
759
      2. Inner-Update: For MAML, the parameter of each task τi ∈ Ti is updated starting from the
760      current generic initial parameter θ0 , and then performing ni gradient descent steps on the task-
761      train loss. For BMAML, the posterior q(θτi | Dτtrni , θ0 ) is computed, for all τi ∈ Ti .
762
      3. Outer-Update: For MAML, the generic initial parameter θ0 is updated by gradient descent. For
763      BMAML, it is updated using the Chaser loss (Yoon et al., 2018, Equation (7)).
764
765   Notice how in our work w̄ is a probability vector. This implies that if we fix a number of task k and
766   we let w̄ be equal to (w1 , . . . , wk )⊤ , then w̄ · p̄ can be seen as a sample from T such that T(pi ) = wi ,
767   for all i ∈ {1, . . . , k}.
768
      Here lies the main difference between IBCL and BMAML. In the latter the information provided
769   by the tasks is used to obtain a refinement of the (parameter of the) distribution T on the tasks
770   themselves. In IBCL, instead, we are interested in the optimal parameterization of the posterior
771   distribution associated with w̄ · p̄. Notice also that at time k + 1, in IBCL the support of T changes:
772   it is {p1 , . . . , pk+1 }, while for MAML and BMAML it stays the same.
773
      Also, MAML and BMAML can be seen as ensemble methods, since they use different values
774
      (MAML) or different distributions (BMAML) to perform the Outer-Update and come up with a
775   single value (MAML) or a single distributions (BMAML). Instead, IBCL keeps distributions sepa-
776   rate via FGCS, thus capturing the ambiguity faced by the designer during the analysis.
777
778
      Furthermore, we want to point out how while for BMAML the tasks τi are all “candidates” for the
      true data generating process (dgp) pi , in IBCL we approximate the pdf/pmf of pi with the product
779   Qi
780      h=1 ℓh of the likelihoods up to task i. The idea of different candidates for the true dgp is beneficial
      for IBCL as well: in the future, we plan to let go of Assumption 1 and let each pi belong to a credal
781
      set Pi . This would capture the epistemic uncertainty faced by the agent on the true dgp.
782
783   To summarize, IBCL is a continual learning technique whose aim is to find the correct parameteriza-
784   tion of the posterior associated with w̄· p̄. Here, w̄ expresses the developer’s preferences on the tasks.
785   MAML and BMAML, instead, are meta-learning algorithms whose main concern is to refine the dis-
786
      tribution T from which the tasks are sampled. While IBCL is able to capture the preferences of, and
      the ambiguity faced by, the designer, MAML and BMAML are unable to do so. On the contrary,
787
      these latter seem better suited to solve meta-learning problems. An interesting future research direc-
788
      tion is to come up with imprecise BMAML, or IBMAML, where a credal set Conv({T1 , . . . , Tk })
789   is used to capture the ambiguity faced by the developer in specifying the correct distribution on the
790   possible tasks. The process of selecting one element from such credal set may lead to computational
791   gains.

824
825   Multi-task Learning under Preferences. Learning for Pareto-optimal models under task perfor-
826   mance trade-offs has been studied by researchers in multi-task learning (Caruana, 1997; Sener and
827   Koltun, 2018). Various techniques have been applied to obtain models that address particular trade-
828   off points (Lin et al., 2019; 2020; Ma et al., 2020; Gupta et al., 2021). The idea of preferences on
829   the trade-off points is introduced in multi-objective optimization (Lin et al., 2020; Sener and Koltun,
830   2018), and a preference can guide learning algorithms to search for a particular model. We borrow
831   the formalization of preferences from (Mahapatra and Rajan, 2020), where a preference is given
832   by a vector of non-negative real weights w̄, with each entry wi corresponding to task i. That is,
833
      wi ≥ wj ⇐⇒ i ⪰ j. This means that if wi ≥ wj , then task i is preferred to task j. However,
      state-of-the-art algorithms require training one model per preference, imposing large overhead when
834
      there is a large number of preferences.
835
836   Continual Learning. Continual learning, also known as lifelong learning, is a special case of
837   multi-task learning, where tasks arrive sequentially instead of simultaneously (Thrun, 1998; Ruvolo
838   and Eaton, 2013a). In this paper, we leverage Bayesian inference in the knowledge base update
839
      (Ebrahimi et al., 2019). Like generic multi-task learning, continual learning also faces the stability-
      plasticity trade-off (De Lange et al., 2021), which balances between performance on new tasks and
840
      resistance to catastrophic forgetting (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017). Current methods identify models
841
      to address trade-off preferences by techniques such as loss regularization (Servia-Rodriguez et al.,
842   2021), meaning at least one model needs to be trained per preference.
843
844   Researchers in CL have proposed various approaches to retain knowledge while updating a model
845
      on new tasks. These include modified loss landscapes for optimization (Farajtabar et al., 2020),
      preservation of critical pathways via attention (Abati et al., 2020), memory-based methods (Lopez-
846
      Paz and Ranzato, 2017), shared representations (Lee et al., 2019), and dynamic representations
847
      (Bulat et al., 2020). Bayesian, or probabilistic methods such as variational inference are also adopted
848   (Ebrahimi et al., 2019; Nguyen et al., 2018).
849
850   In BCL (Nguyen et al., 2018), each task is associated with a data generating process (likelihood,
851
      elicited according to the data at hand), parameterized by θ. The latter is postulated to be a random
      quantity, which at the beginning of the analysis has a prior distribution, θ ∼ q0 . After training on
852
      the available data, the prior distribution is turned into posterior, θ ∼ q1 via Bayes’ theorem. The
853
      posterior q1 is the revised parameter distribution after having learned from the data pertaining to the
854   first task to complete. It is then used as a prior for the next task.
