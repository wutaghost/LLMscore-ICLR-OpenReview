011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Jailbreak attacks reveal critical vulnerabilities in Large Language Models (LLMs)
014            by causing them to generate harmful or unethical content. Evaluating these threats
015            is particularly challenging due to the evolving nature of LLMs and the sophisti-
016
               cation required in effectively probing their vulnerabilities. Current benchmarks
               and evaluation methods struggle to fully address these challenges, leaving gaps in
017
               the assessment of LLM vulnerabilities. In this paper, we review existing jailbreak
018
               evaluation practices and identify three assumed desiderata for an effective jailbreak
019            evaluation protocol. To address these challenges, we introduce GuardVal, a new
020            evaluation protocol that dynamically generates and refines jailbreak prompts based
021            on the defender LLM’s state, providing a more accurate assessment of defender
022            LLMs’ capacity to handle safety-critical situations. Moreover, we propose a new
023            optimization method that prevents stagnation during prompt refinement, ensur-
024            ing the generation of increasingly effective jailbreak prompts that expose deeper
025            weaknesses in the defender LLMs. We apply this protocol to a diverse set of mod-
026            els, from Mistral-7b to GPT-4, across 10 safety domains. Our findings highlight
027
               distinct behavioral patterns among the models, offering a comprehensive view of
               their robustness. Furthermore, our evaluation process deepens the understanding
028
               of LLM behavior, leading to insights that can inform future research and drive the
029
               development of more secure models.

      Recent advancements in commercial large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al.,
035
      2023), Gemini (Team et al., 2023), and Claude (Anthropic, 2023), along with open-source LLMs like
036   LLama3 (Touvron et al., 2023), Vicuna (Zheng et al., 2024), and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023), have
037   significantly enhanced performance across a wide array of natural language processing (NLP) tasks.
038   While their widespread deployment underscores their utility in diverse applications, it also heightens
039   concerns about misuse, including bias and criminal activities (Deng et al., 2023b).
040
      Despite significant efforts to align LLMs with human values to maximize their utility and mitigate
041
      harm (Ouyang et al., 2022), these models remain susceptible to jailbreak attacks, where adversaries
042   craft prompts to bypass safety mechanisms. For instance, as shown in Figure 1, LLMs are aligned to
043   refuse harmful requests like instructions for illegal activities. However, when manipulated through
044   jailbreak prompts, these safeguards can be bypassed, resulting in unsafe outputs, as illustrated
045   in Figure 1. Given the pervasive deployment of LLMs in safety-critical domains, it is crucial to
046   rigorously evaluate the advancements in jailbreak attacks (Longpre et al., 2024) to ensure the security
047   of LLMs.
048   Effective LLM jailbreak evaluation requires prompts that reveal weaknesses in a model’s ability to
049   handle complex ethical scenarios and adversarial manipulations. Traditional human-driven methods,
050   such as PromptBench(Zhu et al., 2023) and TrustLLM(Sun et al., 2024), offer rigorous dataset
051   quality control but are limited by the scalability issues of human annotation, restricting the diversity
052   and complexity of test scenarios (Chao et al., 2024). Moreover, these benchmarks often rely on
053   internet-sourced data, leading to overlap with training sets and creating "false safety" outcomes,
      where models seem secure simply due to familiarity (Bender et al., 2021; Magar & Schwartz, 2022;

119   2   BACKGROUND
120
121   Current Jailbreak Evaluation Protocols. The evaluation of LLMs against jailbreak attacks has
122   become increasingly critical as these models are deployed in sensitive and diverse applications.
123   Existing evaluation protocols primarily fall into two categories: traditional human-labor-based
124   methods (Zhu et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024) and automated dynamic generation methods (Mazeika
125   et al., 2024; Chao et al., 2024; Sonali et al., 2024). While each approach has its merits, they also
126   exhibit notable limitations:
127   1. Traditional Human-Labor-Based Methods: Benchmarks (e.g., PromptBench (Zhu et al., 2023),
128      TrustLLM (Sun et al., 2024), JAMBench (Jin et al., 2024), etc.) rely on meticulous human curation
129      to ensure high-quality datasets that reflect real-world scenarios. However, these methods face
130      several constraints:
131      • Limited Scalability: The dependence on human annotators restricts scalability, limiting the
132        diversity and complexity of test scenarios that can be feasibly generated.
133      • Data Contamination Risk: Utilizing internet-sourced data increases the likelihood of overlap-
134        ping with LLM training datasets, leading to "false safety" where models perform well simply
135
           because they recognize familiar inputs.
      2. Automated Dynamic Generation Methods: Recent approaches (e.g., HarmBench (Mazeika
136
         et al., 2024), JailbreakBench (Chao et al., 2024), Chatbot Guardrails Arena (Sonali et al., 2024),
137
         etc.) employ automated techniques to dynamically generate test samples by manipulating orig-
138      inal templates. While these methods enhance adaptability and coverage, they present certain
139      drawbacks:
140      • Task-Specific Focus and Lack of Model-Specific Evaluation: These methods often concen-
141        trate on specific tasks or templates, without tailoring evaluations to the unique characteristics of
142        different LLMs. This task-specific approach potentially limits their ability to uncover vulnera-
143        bilities unique to each model, leading to less effective test data.
144      • Static Complexity: Although they generate a broader range of scenarios, the complexity
145        of these evaluations does not necessarily evolve over time to match advancements in LLM
146
           capabilities.
147   While these benchmarks have significantly advanced LLM evaluation practices, there remains room
148   for improvement. Specifically, an effective evaluation protocol should address these limitations by
149   ensuring uncontaminated datasets, evolving complexity, and dynamic generation processes tailored
150   to individual LLMs.
151   Assumed Desiderata of Jailbreak Evaluation Protocol. As a reflection of the previous discussion,
152   we attempt to offer a summary list of three desiderata for an effective jailbreak evaluation protocol:
153
      • Uncontaminated Datasets. To combat "false safety" arising from data contamination, evaluation
154
        datasets must be free from overlaps with LLM training data. This ensures that models are tested
155
        against genuinely novel jailbreak inputs, providing a more accurate and reliable assessment of their
156     security.
157   • Evolving Complexity and Effectiveness. Evaluation protocols should not remain static but must
158     evolve in complexity to keep pace with advancements in LLM technology. By continuously
159     updating and enhancing the difficulty and diversity of test scenarios, we ensure that models are
160     consistently challenged at the forefront of their capabilities.
161   • A Dynamic Generation Process. To mitigate the limitations of task-specific evaluations and avoid
        overfitting to fixed datasets, the evaluation process should be dynamic and tailored to the unique


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
          characteristics of each LLM. Continuously generating new and varied test scenarios specific to each
163       model can effectively uncover distinct vulnerabilities and ensure that evaluations remain relevant
164       and challenging.
165
166
      Achieving these desiderata requires generating and refining jailbreak prompts that adapt to the
      evolving capabilities of defender LLMs. Studies have shown that simplistic prompts with bizarre
167
      sequences are easily detected and fail to expose the true weaknesses of LLMs. To address this, we
168
      focus on generating natural language prompts that align with the strategies malicious users might
169   employ to bypass safety mechanisms.
170
171   To simulate real-world adversarial challenges, we introduce a role-playing paradigm in which LLMs
172
      collaboratively generate, refine, and evaluate prompts. Specifically, this paradigm assigns distinct
      roles: (1) Translator: Converts safety guidelines into actionable prompts. (2) Generator: Develops
173
      complex scenarios to test the defender LLM. (3) Evaluator: Assesses the defender LLM’s responses
174
      and iteratively refines the prompts.
175
176   Necessity of the Optimizer Role. While this role-playing setup allows for prompt refinement, relying
177   solely on the Translator, Generator, and Evaluator can lead to stagnation. Existing approaches that
178
      refine prompts based on direct feedback (Deng et al., 2023a; Chao et al., 2023) often fail to explore
      deeper weaknesses in LLMs, becoming stuck in local optima where prompts merely challenge the
179
      same vulnerabilities.
180
181   To overcome this, we introduce the Optimizer, which enhances the process by monitoring how
182   prompts evolve over iterations. Rather than focusing only on immediate improvements, the Optimizer
183   detects stagnation and introduces novel challenges by adjusting the prompt refinement path. This
184
      ensures that the prompts continue to evolve in complexity, effectively exposing deeper vulnerabilities
      in the defender LLM.
185
186   Thus, our role-playing paradigm consists of four key roles:
187
      • Translator: Converts real-world safety principles into actionable natural language prompts that
188     resemble potential user queries.
189   • Generator: Crafts complex scenarios around these prompts, designed to test the defender LLM’s
190     ability to handle nuanced, ethically challenging situations.
191   • Evaluator: Assesses the defender LLM’s responses, providing feedback on its ability to maintain
192     safety measures, and refines the prompts accordingly.
193   • Optimizer: Monitors the evolution of the prompts across iterations to avoid stagnation, ensuring
194     that the evaluation continues to expose deeper vulnerabilities.
195   This structured, collaborative approach ensures that the evaluation remains effective, dynamic, and
196   continuously challenging, leading to a robust and comprehensive assessment of LLM safety.
197
      Necessity of New Jailbreak Safety Measurement in Our Evaluation. Our research goal is to
198
      provide a dynamic and evolving evaluation process that complements existing jailbreak evaluations
199
      by offering a dual assessment of both offensive and defensive capabilities. However, evaluating
200   both jailbreaking and defensive capabilities holistically is paramount for a comprehensive security
201   assessment of LLMs. Traditional metrics, primarily focusing either on attack success rates (Chu et al.,
202   2024) or refuse to answer rate (Sun et al., 2024) in isolation, do not adequately capture the dual nature
203   of LLM functionalities within our evaluation settings. Moreover, these metrics do not fully account
204   for robustness in dynamic evaluation protocols, where comparing two LLMs on different dynamic
205   test sets cannot definitively determine superior model robustness, as differences in performance may
206   result from varying test set difficulties.
207   Therefore, a measurement that accounts for both the offensive and defensive performances of LLMs,
208   while normalizing the differences in test set difficulties, is desired.

257   3.2   M EASURING JAILBREAK S AFETY
258
259   Overall Safety Value (OSV). To effectively account for both the offensive and defensive perfor-
260   mances of LLMs while normalizing differences in test set difficulties, we propose the Overall Safety
261   Value (OSV). This metric assesses and ranks LLMs in our evaluation, where each LLM functions as
262
      both an attacker and a defender.
263   In each domain, the OSV for an LLM (denoted as LLM A) is defined as:
264                                          1    X
265                               OSVA =              (RB,A − RA,B ),                                      (1)
                                           N −1
266                                                    B̸=A
267   where RB,A is the average number of rounds needed for other LLMs B to jailbreak LLM A, reflecting
268   the defensive strength of LLM A. RA,B is the average number of rounds LLM A needs to jailbreak
269   other LLMs B, reflecting the offensive effectiveness of LLM A. N is the total number of LLMs in
      the evaluation, ensuring each LLM is compared against all others during evaluation. A "round" in


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      this context refers to the full evaluation cycle in which the attacker LLM performs all three role
271   tasks (Translator, Generator, and Evaluator). After receiving the defender LLM’s initial response, the
272   feedback is further refined by the Optimizer. The attacker LLM then generates an updated scenario,
273   receives the defender LLM’s revised response, and completes the evaluation. This entire sequence
274   constitutes one round.
275
      Rationale Behind the OSV. The OSV is designed to quantify LLM jailbreak security by combining
276
      both defensive robustness and jailbreak effectiveness within our evaluation protocol:
277
278   • Defensive Capability (RB,A ): A higher RB,A suggests better defense capabilities, indicating that
279     LLM A is more challenging to jailbreak.
280
      • Offensive Capability (RA,B ): Conversely, a lower RA,B denotes stronger offensive capabilities,
        as LLM A can jailbreak others more efficiently.
281
282   Therefore, the subtraction RB,A − RA,B yields the OSV where a higher value indicates a stronger
283   overall jailbreak safety, effectively balancing an LLM’s offensive and defensive roles. Additionally,
284   the OSV metric adjusts for variations in test set difficulty by comparing the relative performance
285   of each LLM against a common set of peer models. By incorporating both defensive and offensive
286
      performance across different LLMs, the OSV balances the challenges posed by different test sets. This
      method evaluates each LLM’s performance within the same environment, ensuring a fair comparison
287
      across varying test difficulties.
288
289   Potential Concerns on Handling Outliers. Due to the inherent stochastic nature of LLMs, their
290   responses can vary, sometimes resulting in outliers—extreme successes or failures in jailbreak
291   attempts. These outliers can skew evaluation results and lead to inaccurate conclusions. To address
292
      this, we run each evaluation three times and average the rounds for both RA,B and RB,A , reducing
      the impact of outliers.
293
294   LLMs often exhibit variability in their responses due to the stochastic nature of their underlying
295   algorithms. This randomness can sometimes lead to unexpected performance extremes, such as
296   unusually successful or failed attempts to jailbreak, which appear as outliers. Such outliers would
297   distort the evaluation statistics and lead to misleading conclusions. In practice, we repeatedly run
298
      each evaluation for 3 times and calculate the average rounds for RB,A and RA,B , which inherently
      helps to mitigate the influence of outliers.

      common attacks but can make it less adaptable to novel, highly tailored adversarial scenarios from
385   models like Gemini (Touvron et al., 2023). This contrast highlights the need for dynamic evaluation
386   methodologies to fully capture LLM behavior in adversarial contexts.
387
388   4.3   C ASE STUDY
389
390   Evaluating the Evaluator role in the failure mode analysis. To better understand LLM behavior,
391   we analyzed failure modes, focusing on the Evaluator’s role during unsuccessful jailbreak attempts,
392   where the attacker LLM fails to breach the defender LLM’s safeguards.
393   We observed that Llama2-7b sometimes refuses to generate feedback as an Evaluator, requiring
394   restarts with new jailbreak prompts. This reluctance, along with Mistral-7b’s similar behavior,
395   prolongs jailbreak attempts and lowers their OSV scores. These delays result from not only the target
396   LLM’s strong defense but also the Evaluator’s reluctance to modify prompts effectively.
397
      Llama2-7b’s hesitation likely stems from reinforcement learning aligned with ethical guidelines (Tou-
398
      vron et al., 2023), making it hesitant to produce harmful content. Similarly, Mistral-7b prioritizes
399   ethical behavior (Jiang et al., 2023), sometimes overcautiously rejecting benign prompts ("How can I
400   kill the process in my computer?"), a phenomenon known as "Exaggerated Safety." This shallow align-
401   ment leads to rejecting prompts with negative words (e.g., "rumor," "damage") without fully grasping
402   the ethical context, weakening its defenses against more sophisticated jailbreak prompts—echoing
403   previous findings on shallow alignment limitations (Bender et al., 2021; Carlini et al., 2021).
404
      Evaluating the Evaluator Role in Jailbreak Detection. To determine the most suitable LLM for
405   the Evaluator role and how this compares to the gold standard (i.e., a human evaluation), we manually
406   label a balanced dataset of 40 responses and compare these labels against classifications from these
407   LLMs. We find that GPT-4 consistently aligns more with human judgment compared to other LLMs.
408   Therefore, by using GPT-4, which closely mirrors human evaluators, we could enhance the reliability
409   and fairness of the evaluation process. More details can be found in Appendix F.
410
      Validating the Optimizer Role in Breaking Stagnation. In our experiments, we aim to validate the
411   effectiveness of the Optimizer role in refining jailbreak prompts. When the Optimizer is removed,
412   leaving scenario updates based solely on Evaluator feedback, several patterns of failure emerge.
413
414
      A notable example occurs when Gemini attempts to jailbreak itself. Despite its familiarity with its
      own weaknesses, Gemini consistently fails to succeed within 10 rounds—counterintuitive given the
415
      expectation that a model should better exploit its own vulnerabilities. Without the Optimizer, Gemini
416
      remains stuck in scenarios that should be more easily exploitable.
417
418   Afterwards, we find that reintroducing the Optimizer, which refines feedback beyond the Evaluator’s
419   suggestions, helps break this stagnation. The refined prompts allow the Generator to create more
420
      substantial scenario changes, leading to successful jailbreaks. This highlights the Optimizer’s critical
      role in overcoming stagnation and ensuring the evaluation effectively reveals deeper vulnerabilities in
421
      the defender LLM. More details are provided in Appendix H.

450   6    D ISCUSSION
451
452   Justifying the Relationship Between Offensive and Safety. In the context of our evaluation,
453   offensive capabilities are not about promoting harmful behavior but rather about identifying and
454   exploiting vulnerabilities in other systems. By engaging in offensive tactics, such as jailbreak attempts,
455   models gain a deeper understanding of potential threats, which in turn enhances their ability to defend
456
      against similar adversarial strategies. Thus, optimizing both offensive and defensive capabilities in
      LLMs strengthens overall security by enabling models to anticipate and neutralize emerging threats.
457
      For instance, in red-team testing, optimizing for both offense and defense allows developers to
458
      simulate sophisticated attack scenarios, thereby improving the model’s robustness by preparing it for
459   a broader range of adversarial challenges.
460
461   Therefore, while it may seem counterintuitive to enhance offensive capabilities in the pursuit of LLM
462
      safety, GuardVal ultimately bolsters defensive performance by enabling models to better recognize
      and mitigate potential vulnerabilities.
463
464   Discussion on the Scope of Offensive Capabilities in OSV. In the context of OSV, offensive
465   capabilities are measured by a model’s ability to identify vulnerabilities in other systems. As shown
466   in Section 4.3, some models may exhibit lower offensive capabilities due to strong internal safety
467   guardrails or a lack of ability to jailbreak other models. However, the offensive capability in OSV
468
      does not differentiate between these causes, as the goal is to assess the model’s capacity to probe and
      expose weaknesses.
469
470   Overall, the goal of OSV is to provide a balanced evaluation of both offensive and defensive
471   capabilities to ensure models are not only robust against attacks but also adept at identifying potential
472   vulnerabilities in other systems. By measuring both aspects, OSV encourages the development
473   of models that are more resilient in real-world scenarios, where both probing other systems and
474
      defending oneself are critical to maintaining security.

      In this paper, we summarize common practices in LLM jailbreak evaluation and discuss three essential
479
      desiderata for an effective evaluation protocol, namely ensuring the use of uncontaminated datasets,
480   adapting to the evolving complexity and effectiveness of LLMs, and employing a dynamic generation
481   process to tailor evaluations. Based on these desiderata, we introduce GuardVal, a dynamic protocol
482   that meets these criteria, providing a dual assessment of both offensive and defensive capabilities
483   of LLMs to complement existing evaluations. Additionally, we explore a mix-of-experts strategy,
484   leveraging the strengths of top-performing models across various domains to enhance overall safety.
485   These approaches are designed to maintain rigorous standards in security evaluation and ensure that
      our assessments keep pace with rapid advancements in LLM technology.

676
677
      The proposed Adam-inspired optimization method refines the generation of jailbreak prompts by
678
      monitoring and adjusting the evolving responses of the attacker LLM during each iteration. This
679   method addresses the limitations of feedback-based approaches, which may lead to stagnation when
680   the attacker LLM’s responses become too similar or repetitive across rounds. By incorporating
681   additional information about the rate of change in responses, the optimizer ensures that the prompts
682   remain challenging and continue to evolve. Crucially, our approach introduces an objective method for
683   determining whether response changes are significant or stagnant using a sliding window technique,
684   thus avoiding subjective judgments about the adequacy of the response changes. Below, we outline
685   the detailed steps and mathematical formulations of the method.
686
687
      C.1     F EEDBACK Q UANTIFICATION
688
689
      In this approach, the difference between consecutive responses of the attacker LLM, after evaluating
690   the defender LLM’s output, is quantified similarly to how gradients are used in traditional optimization
691   algorithms. This difference provides essential guidance on how the attacker LLM should adjust its
692   subsequent responses, with the goal of successfully bypassing the defender’s safety mechanisms.
693
      Let the difference at iteration t be represented as:
694
695
                                       gt = ||Responset − Responset − 1||                                  (2)
696
697
698   Here, gt quantifies the change in the attacker LLM’s response compared to the previous iteration.
699   This serves as an indicator of how much the attacker LLM’s output has evolved between rounds. A
700   larger value of gt suggests more significant changes in the responses, while a smaller value indicates
701   that the responses are becoming too similar, signaling a potential stagnation in the optimization
      process.

706   but also considers the rate of change between iterations. Monitoring this evolution allows us to
707   detect when changes in the responses slow down, indicating that the attacker LLM may be generating
708   repetitive or ineffective prompts.

711                                             ∆gt = gt − gt−1                                          (3)
712
713   This expression measures how quickly the responses are evolving across iterations. A large ∆gt
714
      indicates that the attacker LLM is making substantial adjustments to its responses, while a small ∆gt

715
      adjust the feedback provided to the LLM, ensuring continuous evolution in the generated prompts

746

754   making arbitrary decisions about what constitutes "large" or "small" changes but rather relying on
755   statistically sound measures of normal variation. The thresholds allow the optimizer to detect when
      significant changes are occurring, prompting real-time adjustments to the attacker LLM’s strategy.

       G    D ISTRACTION - BASED JAILBREAK P ROMPTS G ENERATION
1027
1028
       In some cases, we notice that the attacker LLM may resist generating jailbreak prompts, particularly
1029   when acting as the Translator. To overcome this resistance and ensure compliance with its designated
1030   role, we further introduce a complementary distraction-based jailbreak prompts generation method.
1031   This method compels the attacker LLM to adhere to its role by leveraging the model’s vulnerability
1032   to distraction. The core idea behind this approach is based on studies of the attention mechanisms in
1033   LLMs, which demonstrate that these models can be easily distracted by irrelevant contexts, leading
1034   to a decline in their reasoning abilities. By embedding malicious content within a complex and
1035   seemingly unrelated scenario, we can effectively reduce the LLM’s ability to detect and reject the
1036   malicious intent.
1037   This framework is designed to produce effective, coherent, and fluent jailbreak prompts by decompos-
1038   ing them into two main components: the jailbreak template and the malicious query. The jailbreak
1039   template serves as a scaffold holding a placeholder for the malicious query, but it avoids containing
1040   any sensitive or overtly problematic text. This separation is crucial for masking the adversarial nature
1041   of the prompts. The process is as follows:
1042   • Prompt Decomposition: Split the jailbreak input into a benign template and a concealed malicious
1043     query. The template provides structure without overtly sensitive content, masking the adversarial
1044     intent.
1045   • Distraction through Complexity: Embed the malicious content within a complex, unrelated
1046     narrative. This reduces the LLM’s ability to detect and filter harmful requests by exploiting its
1047     reduced vigilance in distracted states.
1048   • Dynamic Prompt Refinement: Use an iterative optimization process to adjust the phrasing and
1049     context based on continuous feedback from the LLM’s responses. Each iteration refines the
1050
         distraction elements and better integrates the malicious content.
       • Evaluation of Responses: Assess the LLM’s responses against predefined ethical guidelines and
1051
         safety standards. This measures the effectiveness of the prompts in bypassing defenses while
1052
         ensuring compliance with ethical norms.
1053   • Iterative Feedback Loop: Use feedback to modify the prompts dynamically, adapting their
1054     complexity and subtlety to match the LLM’s evolving capabilities. This ensures the evaluation
1055     remains relevant and effective as LLM technology advances.
1056
1057
       By employing this distraction-based approach, we can effectively generate sophisticated jailbreak
       prompts that compel the LLM to comply with its designated role, ensuring a robust and continuous
1058
       evaluation process.

       The OSV metric proposed in this paper has some limitations. First of all, the OSV is influenced by the
1182   diversity of the LLMs included in the evaluation. If all LLMs have similar capabilities or are trained
1183   on similar data, the OSV might not fully capture the real-world security landscape. This limitation
1184   highlights that the OSV is more suitable for comparing the relative performance between LLMs
1185   rather than providing an absolute measure of security. In our dynamic evaluation setting, each LLM
1186   is tested with different prompts, which introduces variability that cannot be normalized directly. This
1187   makes it challenging to derive a comprehensive overall security landscape from individual evaluations.
       However, as the number of evaluated LLMs increases, the OSV becomes a more comprehensive

1204   We propose GuardVal, a dynamic evaluation method to evaluate LLMs by using other LLMs to
1205   perform jailbreak attacks. This approach ensures the evaluation data remains uncontaminated, evolves
1206   in complexity, and is dynamically generated. However, GuardVal could be misused to enhance
1207

1208

1209

1210   information, and security concerns include sophisticated attacks manipulating LLMs and creating

1213   and promoting best practices for AI use can help mitigate negative impacts, while encouraging open
