                                                         A BSTRACT
008
009
               How can we trust the correctness of a learned model on a particular input of interest?
010
               Model accuracy is typically measured on average over a distribution of inputs,
011
               giving no guarantee for any fixed input. This paper proposes a theoretically-founded
012            solution to this problem: to train Self-Proving models that prove the correctness
013            of their output to a verification algorithm V via an Interactive Proof. We devise
014            a generic method for learning Self-Proving models, and we prove convergence
015            bounds under certain assumptions. Empirically, our learning method is used to train
016            a Self-Proving transformer that computes the Greatest Common Divisor (GCD)
017            and proves the correctness of its answer.
018

020   1   I NTRODUCTION
021
022
      Bob is studying for his algebra exam and stumbles upon a question Q that he cannot solve. He queries
      a Large Language Model (LLM) for the answer, and it responds with a number: 42. Bob is aware of
023
      recent research showing that the LLM attains a 90% score on algebra benchmarks (cf. Frieder et al.
024
      2023), but should he trust that the answer to his particular question Q is indeed 42?
025
026   Bob could ask the LLM to explain its answer in natural language. Though he must proceed with
027   caution, as the LLM might try to convince him of an incorrect answer (Turpin et al., 2023). Moreover,
028
      even if 42 is the correct answer, the LLM may fail to produce a convincing proof (Wang et al., 2023).
      If only the LLM could formally prove its answer, Bob would verify the proof and be convinced.
029
030   This paper initiates the study of Self-Proving models (Fig. 1) that prove the correctness of their
031   answers via an Interactive Proof system (Goldwasser et al., 1985). Self-Proving models successfully
032   convince a verification algorithm V with worst-case soundness guarantees: for any question, V
033   rejects all incorrect answers with high probability over the interaction. This guarantee holds even
034
      against provers that have access to V ’s specification, and unbounded computational power.

      Scope. This paper contains a theory of learned models that prove their own correctness via an
083
      Interactive Proof system. The fascinating and well-studied question of which settings are verifiable
084   in an Interactive Proof system is beyond our scope. Our theory is general in that it pertains to any
085   such setting, e.g., any decision problem solvable in polynomial space (Shamir, 1992). See Goldreich
086   (2008) for a primer on Proof systems more broadly.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
090
091
      This paper is situated at the intersection of machine learning (ML) and Interactive Proof systems
      (IPs). We briefly discuss recent relevant work from these literatures.
092
093
      ML and IPs. IPs have found numerous applications in ML towards a diverse set of goals. Anil et al.
094
      (2021) introduce Prover–Verifier Games (PVGs), a game-theoretic framework for learned provers
095
      and learned verifiers. PVGs were further investigated in at least two subsequent works: Hammond &
096   Adam-Day (2024) study multi-prover and Zero Knowledge variants of PVGs. Additionally, Kirchner
097   et al. (2024) successfully utilize PVGs towards obtaining human-legible outputs from LLMs. Notably,
098   they require a relaxed completeness guarantee of their learned proof system—this requirement is the
099   same as our Definition 3.4 of Self-Proving models.
100
      Beyond PVGs, Wäldchen et al. (2024) cast the problem of model interpretability as a Prover–Verifier
101
      interaction between a learned feature selector and a learned feature classifier. Debate systems (Condon
102   et al., 1995), a multiprover variant of IPs, were considered for aligning models with human values
103   (Irving et al., 2018; Brown-Cohen et al., 2023). In such Debate systems, two competing models
104   are each given an alleged answer y ̸= y ′ , and attempt to prove the correctness of their answer to a
105   (human or learned) judge. Lastly, Murty et al. (2023) define Pseudointelligence: a model learner LM
106   and an evaluator learner LE are each given samples from a ground-truth; LM learns a model of the
107   ground-truth, while LE learns an evaluator of such models; the learned evaluator then attempts to
      distinguish between the learned model and the ground-truth in a Turing Test-like interaction.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      All of these works consider learned verifiers, whereas our work focuses on training models that
109   interact with a manually-defined verifier. More related in this regard is IP-PAC (Goldwasser et al.,
110   2021), in which a learner proves that she learned a model that is Probably Approximately Correct
111   (Valiant, 1984). We, however, consider models that prove their own correctness on a per-input basis,
112   rather than learners that prove average-case correctness of a model.
113
114   Models that generate formal proofs. Self-Proving models are verified by an algorithm with formal
115   completeness and soundness guarantees (see Definition 3.2). In this sense, Self-Proving models
116   generate a formal proof of the correctness of their output. Several works propose specialized models
117   that generate formal proofs.
118   AlphaGeometry (Trinh et al., 2024) is capable of formally proving olympiad-level geometry problems;
119   Others have trained models to produce proofs in Gransden et al. (2015); Polu & Sutskever (2020) and
120   others train models to produce proofs in Coq (Gransden et al., 2015), Metamath (Polu & Sutskever,
121   2020), Lean (Yang et al., 2023), or manually-defined deduction rules (Tafjord et al., 2020); FunSearch
122   (Romera-Paredes et al., 2024) evolves LLM-generated programs by systematically evaluating their
123   correctness. Indeed, all of these can be cast as Self-Proving models developed for specific proof
124   systems. Meanwhile, this work defines and studies the class of such models in general. Several works
125
      (e.g. Welleck et al. 2022) consider models that generate natural language proofs or explanations,
      which are fundamentally different from formal proofs (or provers) verified by an algorithm.
126
127
      Training on intermediate steps. Chain-of-Though (CoT, Wei et al. 2022) refers to additional
128
      supervision on a model in the form of intermediate reasoning steps. CoT is known to improve
129
      model performance whether included in-context (Wei et al., 2022) or in the training phase itself
130   (Yang et al., 2022). Transcript Learning (TL, Section 4.1) can be viewed as training the model on a
131   Chain-of-Thought induced by the interaction of a verifier and an honest prover (Definition 3.2).
132
133
      To complete the analogy, let us adopt the terminology of Uesato et al. (2022), who consider outcome
      supervision and process supervision. In our case, the outcome is the decision of the verifier, and
134
      the process is the interaction between the verifier and the model. Thus, Reinforcement Learning
135
      from Verifier Feedback (RLVF, Section 4.2) is outcome-supervised while TL is process-supervised.
136   In a recent work, Lightman et al. (2024) find that process-supervised transformers outperform
137   outcome-supervised ones on the MATH dataset (Hendrycks et al., 2021).
138
139   Transformers for arithmetic. In Section 5 we train and evaluate Self-Proving transformers to
140   generate the GCD of two integers and prove its correctness to a verifier. These experiments leverage
141   a long line of work on neural models for arithmetic tasks originating with Siu & Roychowdhury
142   (1992), and in particular modular arithmetic, which is known to be challenging (Palamas, 2017). Of
143   particular relevance is the recent paper of Charton (2024), who trains transformers to generate the
144   GCD—without a proof of correctness. We benefit from conclusions suggested in their work and
145   start from a similar (scaled-down) experimental setup. Our main challenge (obtaining Self-Proving
146
      models) is overcome by introducing Annotated Transcript Learning (ATL).
147   We conduct ablation experiments to find two deciding factors in ATL. First, we study the effect of the
148   amount of annotation given in the form of intermediate steps (Lee et al., 2024), which is related to
149   autoregressive length complexity (Malach, 2023). Second, we characterize ATL efficacy in terms of
150   an algebraic property of the tokenization scheme (cf. Nogueira et al. 2021; Charton 2022; 2024).

154   We introduce and formally define our learning framework in which models prove the correctness of
155   their output. We start with preliminaries from the learning theory and proof systems literatures in
156   Section 3.1. We then introduce our main definition in Section 3.2.

158   3.1   P RELIMINARIES
159
160   Let Σ be a finite set of tokens and Σ∗ denote the set of finite sequences of such tokens. We consider
161   sequence-to-sequence models Fθ : Σ∗ → Σ∗ , which are total functions that produce an output for
      each possible input sequence. A model is parameterized by a real-valued, finite dimensional vector θ.

      We consider models as randomized functions, meaning that Fθ (x) is a random variable over Σ∗ , of
163   which samples are denoted by y ∼ Fθ (x).

165   Before we can define models that prove their own correctness, we must first define correctness.
166

      defines correct answers. For simplicity of presentation, we focus on the case that each input x ∈ Σ∗
167
      has exactly one correct output F ∗ (x) ∈ Σ∗ , and a zero-one loss function on outputs (the general case
168
      is deferred to Appendix A). The fundamental goal of machine learning can be thought of as learning
169   a model of the ground-truth F ∗ . Formally,

176   An interactive proof system (Goldwasser et al., 1985) is a protocol carried out between an efficient
177   verifier and a computationally unbounded prover. The prover attempts to convince the verifier of the
178   correctness of some assertion, while the verifier accepts only correct claims. The prover is powerful
179   yet untrusted; in spite of this, the verifier must reject false claims with high probability.
180   In the context of this work, it is important to note that the verifier is manually-defined (as opposed to
181   learned). Formally, the verifier is a probabilistic polynomial-time algorithm tailored to a particular
182   ground-truth capability F ∗ . Informally, the verifier is the anchor of trust: think of the verifier as an
183   efficient and simple algorithm, hosted in a trustworthy environment.
184
      Given an input x ∈ Σ∗ , the model Fθ “claims” that y ∼ Fθ (x) is correct. We now define what
185   it means to prove this claim. We will use Pθ to denote Self-Proving models, noting that they are
186   formally the same object1 as non-Self-Proving (“vanilla”) models Fθ . This notational change is to
187   emphasize that Pθ first outputs y ∼ Pθ (x) and is then prompted by the verifier, unlike Fθ who only
188   generates an output y ∼ Fθ (x).
189
      A Self-Proving model proves that y ∼ Pθ (x) is correct to a verifier V over the course of R rounds of
190
      interaction (Figure 1). In each round i ∈ [R], verifier V queries Pθ on a sequence qi ∈ Σ∗ to obtain an
191   answer ai ∈ Σ∗ ; once the interaction is over, V accepts or rejects. For fixed x, y ∈ Σ∗ , the decision
192   of V after interacting with Pθ is a random variable over V ’s decision (accept/reject), determined by
193   the randomness of V and Pθ . The decision random variable is denoted by ⟨V, Pθ ⟩ (x, y).
194
      We present a definition of Interactive Proofs restricted to our setting.
195
196   Definition 3.2. Fix a soundness error s ∈ (0, 1), a finite set of tokens Σ and a ground-truth
197   F ∗ : Σ∗ → Σ∗ . A verifier V (in an Interactive Proof) for F ∗ is a probabilistic polynomial-time
198
      algorithm that is given explicit inputs x, y ∈ Σ∗ and black-box (oracle) query access to a prover P .2 It
      interacts with P over R rounds (see Figure 1) and outputs a decision ⟨V, P ⟩ (x, y) ∈ {reject, accept}.
199

      Remark 3.3 (Verifier efficiency). Definition 3.2 requires that V is a polynomial-time algorithm
220   whereas provers are unbounded. This captures a requirement for efficient verification. We chose
221   polynomial time as a measure of efficiency because it is common Proof systems literature. That

223   (Condon & Lipton, 1989) or circuit depth (Goldwasser et al., 2007). Regardless of which measure is
224   taken, to avoid a trivial definition it is crucial that V should be more efficient than the honest prover
225   P ∗ ; else, V can simply execute P ∗ to perform the computation itself.

      By definition, the soundness error s of a verifier V bounds the probability that it is mistakenly
227
      convinced of an incorrect output; in that sense, the smaller s, the “better” the verifier V . In our
228   setting, we think of a manually-defined verifier V who is formally proven (by a human) to have a
229   small soundness error by analysis of V ’s specification.

      As depicted in Figure 1, each of the model’s answers depends on all previous queries and answers in

233   Towards defining Self-Proving models (Section 3.2), let us observe the following. Completeness
234   and soundness are worst-case guarantees, meaning that they hold for all possible inputs x ∈ Σ∗ .
235   In particular, completeness implies that for all x ∈ Σ∗ , the honest prover P ∗ convinces V of the
236   correctness of F ∗ (x); in classical proof systems there is no guarantee that an “almost honest” prover
237
      can convince the verifier (cf. Paradise 2021). Yet, if we are to learn a prover Pθ , we cannot expect
      it to agree with P ∗ perfectly, nor can we expect it to always output F ∗ (x). Indeed, Self-Proving
238
      models will have a distributional guarantee with respect to inputs x ∼ µ.

      3.2   S ELF -P ROVING MODELS
241
242   We define the Verifiability of a model Pθ with respect to an input distribution µ and a verifier V .
243   Intuitively, Verifiability captures the ability of the model to prove the correctness of its answer
244   y ∼ Pθ (x), when the input x is sampled from µ. We refer to models capable of proving their own
245   correctness as Self-Proving models. Notice that, as in Definition 3.2, the verifier is fixed and agnostic
246   to the choice of the Self-Proving model.
247   Definition 3.4 (Self-Proving model). Fix a verifier V for a ground-truth F ∗ : Σ∗ → Σ∗ as in
248   Definition 3.2, and a distribution µ over inputs Σ∗ . The Verifiability of a model Pθ : Σ∗ → Σ∗ is
249   defined as
250                               verV,µ (θ) := x∼µPr [⟨V, Pθ ⟩ (x, y) accepts] .                           (1)
251                                             y∼Pθ (x)

252   We say that model Pθ is β-Self-Proving with respect to V and µ if verV,µ (θ) ≥ β.
253   Remark 3.5 (Verifiability =⇒ correctness). Notice that the ground-truth F ∗ does not appear in
254   Definition 3.4 except for the first sentence. Indeed, once it is established that V is a verifier for F ∗
255   (as per Definition 3.2), then Verifiability w.r.t V implies correctness w.r.t F ∗ : Consider any input
256   distribution µ, ground-truth F ∗ , and a verifier V for F ∗ with soundness error s. By a union bound, if
257
      model Pθ is β-Verifiable, then it is (β − s)-correct. That is to say, Verifiability is formally a stronger
      guarantee than correctness when V has small soundness error s.
258
259   As depicted in Figure 1, a Self-Proving model Pθ plays a dual role: first, it generates an output
260   y ∼ Pθ (x), and then it proves the correctness of this output to V . Note also that Self-Provability is a
261   feature of a model, unlike completeness and soundness which are features of a verifier (see Table 1).
262
      The benefit of Verifiability over correctness is captured by the following scenario. Alice wishes to use
263
      a model Pθ to compute some functionality F ∗ on an input x0 in a high risk setting. Alice generates
264   y0 ∼ Pθ (x0 ). Should Alice trust that y0 is correct? If Alice has a held-out set of labeled samples,
265   she can estimate Pθ ’s average correctness on µ. Unfortunately, (average) correctness provides no
266   guarantee regarding the correctness of the particular (x0 , y0 ) that Alice has in hand. If, however,
267   Alice has access to a verifier V for which Pθ is Self-Proving, then she can trust the model on an
268   input-by-input (rather than average-case) basis: Alice can execute V on (x0 , y0 ) and black-box access
269   to Pθ . Soundness of V guarantees that if y0 is incorrect, then V rejects with high probability, in
      which case Alice should either generate Pθ (x0 ) again—or find a better model.

      4     L EARNING S ELF -P ROVING AUTOREGRESSIVE M ODELS
271
272
      With a sound verifier V at hand, obtaining Self-Proving models with respect to V holds great promise:
273   a user that prompts the model with input x does not need to take it on good faith that Pθ (x) is correct;
274   she may simply verify this herself by executing the verification protocol. How, then, can we learn
275   models that are not just approximately-correct, but Self-Proving as well?
276
      The challenge is to align the model with a verifier. We assume that the learner has access to input
277
      samples x ∼ µ and correct outputs F ∗ (x), as well as the verifier specification (code). Additionally,
278
      the learner can emulate the verifier, as the latter is computationally efficient (Remark 3.3).
279
280   Our focus is on autoregressive sequence-to-sequence (Self-Proving) models Pθ . Such models generate
281   their output by recursively prompting a randomized sampling from a base distribution pθ over tokens
282
      Σ. For an input z ∈ Σ∗ , the output w ∼ Pθ (z) is generated as follows:
283          • Sample w1 ∼ pθ (z).
284
             • Let j = 1. While wj is not the end-of-sequence token EOS ∈ Σ:
285
286              – Sample wj+1 ∼ pθ (zw1 · · · wj ).
287              – Update j := j + 1.
288          • Output w = w1 w2 · · · wj .
289
290   For any z ∈ Σ∗ , it is useful to consider the vector of log-probabilities over Σ, denoted by log pθ (z) ∈
291   R|Σ| . We assume that each coordinate in this vector is differentiable with respect to θ.
292   Our general approach is inspired by Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (Christiano
293   et al., 2017), a method for aligning models with human preferences, which has recently been used
294   to align sequence-to-sequence models (Ouyang et al., 2022). However, there are two important
295   differences between humans and algorithmic verifiers: (1) Verifiers are efficient algorithms which
296   may be emulated by the learner. This is unlike humans, whose preferences are costly to obtain. On
297   the other hand, (2) verifiers make a single-bit decision at the end of an interaction, but cannot guide
298   the prover (model) in intermediate rounds. In RL terms, this is known as the exploration problem for
299   sparse reward signals (e.g. Ladosz et al. 2022).
300   Section 4.1 introduces Transcript Learning (TL), a learning algorithm that overcomes the exploration
301   problem mentioned in the second point under the assumption that the learner has access to transcripts
302   of interactions in which the verifier accepts. We prove convergence bounds for TL (Appendix B.1)
303   and analyze it experimentally (Section 5).
304   Access to accepting transcripts is a reasonable assumption, for example, when there is an efficient
305   honest prover that can generate such transcripts (Goldwasser et al., 2015). When there is no access to
306   accepting transcripts, we propose Reinforcement Learning from Verifier Feedback (Section 4.2).
307
308   4.1   T RANSCRIPT L EARNING
309
310   We present an algorithm for learning Self-Proving models which uses access to a distribution of
311   accepting transcripts. This is a reasonable assumption to make when the honest prover P ∗ (see
312   Definition 3.2) is efficient, as in the case of public-coin Doubly-Efficient Interactive Proof systems as
313   defined by Goldwasser et al. (2015) and developed in other theoretical (e.g. Goldreich & Rothblum
314
      2018) and applied (e.g. Zhang et al. 2021) works. In this case, an honest prover P ∗ can be run by the
      learner during training to collect accepting transcripts without incurring heavy computational cost.
315
      Alternatively, the learner may collect a dataset of accepting transcripts prior to learning (see Figure 4
316
      in Appendix B).

350   is done by accumulating gradients from the cross-entropy loss computed at each token. Since A(θ)
351   lower bounds the Verifiability of Pθ , the former can be used as a surrogate for the latter.
352
      The conditions for Theorem 4.1 can be split into two. First, the standard conditions used to prove
353   SGD convergence: convexity,4 BNorm -boundedness, and BLip -Lipschitzness. Second, there is a
354   bound C on the communication complexity of the prover in the Interactive Proof system.
355
356
      Quantitatively, the efficiency of TL is captured by the number of iterations N . It is desirable to
      minimize N , which is also the number of samples needed from the distribution µ and the transcript
357
      generator T ∗ . The bound on N in Equation (2) can be decomposed into the complexity of SGD
358      2      2
      (BNorm  BLip /ε2 ), and communication complexity of the proof system O(C 2 ). Minimizing commu-
359   nication complexity has been an overarching goal in the study of proof systems (e.g. Goldreich &
360   Håstad 1998; Goldreich et al. 2002; Reingold et al. 2021). Theorem 4.1 formally shows the benefit of
361   communication-efficient proof systems in the context of Self-Proving models.

363   4.2   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING FROM V ERIFIER F EEDBACK (RLVF)
364
365   As mentioned in Section 4.1, Transcript Learning uses access to an honest transcript generator to
366   estimate gradients of (a lower bound on) the Verifiability of a model Pθ .
367
      Reinforcement Learning from Verifier Feedback (RLVF, Algorithm 2) estimates this gradient without
368   access to a transcript generator. RLVF can be viewed as a modification of TL in which the learner
369   emulates the interaction of the verifier with its own model Pθ . Rather than directly sampling from the
370   generator as in TL, it collects accepting transcripts by rejection sampling on emulated transcripts.
371
      This rejection sampling means that RLVF requires its initial model Pθ0 to have Verifiability bounded
372
      away from 0, so that accepting transcripts are sampled with sufficient probability. Fortunately, such
373
      a Self-Proving base model can be learned using TL. This gives a learning paradigm in which a
374   somewhat-Self-Proving base model is first learned with TL (with Verifiability δ > 0), and then
375   “amplified” to a fully Self-Proving model using RLVF (cf. Nair et al. 2018).
376
         4
377        Convexity does not hold in general LLM training. Yet, Theorem 4.1 provides useful theoretical analysis in a

      We prove that RLVF learner can estimate the Verifiability gradient of Pθ using emulation alone
379   in Lemma B.7. From a broader perspective, RLVF can be derived by viewing Self-Proving as a
380   reinforcement learning problem in which the agent (prover) is rewarded when the verifier accepts.
381   Indeed, RLVF is the Policy Gradient method (Sutton et al., 1999) for a verifier-induced reward.
382   Convergence bounds for Policy Gradient methods are a challenging and active area of research (e.g.
383   Agarwal et al. 2021), and so we leave the full analysis to future work.

385   4.3       L EARNING FROM ANNOTATED TRANSCRIPTS
386
387   To minimize the length of messages exchanged in an Interactive Proof system, the honest prover is
388   designed to send the shortest possible message to the verifier, containing only essential information.
389   However, when training Self-Proving model, it may be useful for it to first generate an “annotated”
390   answer e a which is then trimmed down to the actual answer a to be sent to the verifier. We adapt
391   Sections 3 and 4 to this setting in Appendix D, where we present Annotated Transcripts. The TL and
392   RLVF algorithms naturally extend to annotated transcripts as well. Table 2 shows that annotations
393   significantly improve performance of TL.
394   Annotations can be viewed as adding Chain-of-Thought (Wei et al., 2022). As a concrete example,
395   consider our experiments on computing the GCD. As detailed in Section 5.2, a proof π in this setting
396   is the output of an iterative process—the extended Euclidean algorithm—starting from the input x:
397   x 7→ π1 7→ π2 7→ · · · 7→ π. The annotation of the proof π consists the first T steps (π1 , . . . , πT ) up
398   to some fixed cutoff T . These are prepended to the proof and shown to the model during TL training.
399   At inference time, the model is evaluated only on whether it generated the proof π correctly.

520   6     C ONCLUSIONS
521
522   Trust between a learned model and its user is fundamental. In recent decades, Interactive Proofs
523   (Goldwasser et al., 1985) have emerged as a general theory of trust established via verification
524
      algorithms. This work demonstrates that models can learn to formally prove their answers in an
      Interactive Proof system. We call models that possess this capability Self-Proving.
525
526   The definition of Self-Proving models forms a bridge between the rich theory of Interactive Proofs
527   and the contemporary topic of Trustworthy ML. Interactive Proofs offer formal worst-case soundness
528   guarantees; thus, users of Self-Proving models can be confident when their models generate correct
529   answers—and detect incorrect answers with high probability.
530   We demonstrate the theoretical viability of our definition with two generic learning algorithms:
531   Transcript Learning (TL) and Reinforcement Learning from Verifier Feedback (RLVF). The analyses
532   of these algorithms is informed by techniques from theories of learning, RL, and computational
533   complexity. This work can be extended in several directions: finding conditions for the convergence
534   of RLVF, improving sample complexity bounds for TL, or designing altogether different learning
535   algorithms (for example, by taking advantage of properties of the verifier).
536   To better understand the training dynamics of (Annotated) TL, we train Self-Proving transformers
537   for the Greatest Common Divisor (GCD) problem. We train a small (6.3M parameter) transformer
538   that learns to generate correct answers and proofs with high accuracy. Facing forward, we note that
539   Interactive Proofs exist for capabilities far more complex than the GCD (Shamir, 1992); scaling up
      our experiments is the next step towards bringing Self-Proving models from theory to practice.

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      This work proposes a theoretically-grounded approach to enhancing trust in learned models. By
543
      ensuring that models not only generate outputs but also prove their correctness to a verification
544   algorithm, we tackle fundamental issues of trust and accountability in machine learning.
545
546   Self-Proving models build trust between models and users by offering formal worst-case soundness
547
      guarantees. This is particularly beneficial in high-stakes applications, such as healthcare and finance,
      where incorrect outputs can have severe consequences. The ability to verify correctness on a per-
548
      instance basis helps prevent potentially harmful decisions. It allows any user to decide for herself
549
      whether she trusts a particular output generated by the model, rather than relying on average-case
550   guarantees (e.g., high scores on benchmarks as reported by the model’s developer).
551
552   Furthermore, Self-Proving models promote accountability by allowing stakeholders to independently
553
      verify the correctness of a model’s outputs. In particular, lawmakers and regulators could require
      models used in sensitive settings to be Self-Proving.
554
555   With that said, Self-Proving models also introduce challenges which must be addressed. First, we
556   expect Self-Proving models to be harder to learn (in practice), which may limit their applicability
557   in more complex tasks. Second, as with any learned model, Self-Proving models could be used in
558   harmful ways; developers of a model (and verification algorithm) must consider the impact of their
559
      systems in the specific context in which they are deployed (Suresh et al., 2023). In other words, the
      fact that a Self-Proving model’s outputs are provably correct does not mean that these outputs were
560
      ought to be generated in the first place.

849
      Our experiments are focused on a single ground-truth capability, namely, computing the GCD. Yet, the
850
      theoretical portion of our work holds for any ground-truth F ∗ that admits an Interactive Proof system.
851   Training large Self-Proving models for more complex ground-truths will likely pose additional
852   practical learning challenges. With that said, we stress that generating accepting transcripts for use in
853   Transcript Learning is distinct from these learning challenges. Collecting accepting transcripts is a
854   purely computational task, and can even be done “offline” prior to the model’s training.
855
      Additionally, in our current learning methods, each individual ground-truth capability requires training
856
      a separate Self-Proving model. It would be interesting to adapt our definition and methods to deal
857   with a single generalist Self-Proving model that proves its correctness to multiple verifiers of different
858   ground-truths.

      General (bounded) loss functions. In Definition 3.1 we implicitly use the 0-1 loss when measuring
865   the correctness of a model: For any x ∈ X, we measure only whether the model generated the
866   correct output y = F ∗ (x), but not how “far” the generated y was from F ∗ (x). It is often the case
867   in machine learning that we would be satisfied with models that generate a “nearly-correct” output.
868   This is formalized by specifying a loss function ℓ : Σ∗ × Σ∗ → [0, 1] and measuring the probability
869   that ℓ(x, y) is smaller than some threshold λ ∈ [0, 1), where x is drawn from the input distribution µ,
870   and y is generated by the model when given input x.
871   In the context of language modeling, different loss function allow for a more fine-grained treatment
872   of the semantics of a given task. As an example, consider the prime-counting task:

1223   Remark B.6 (On the realizability assumption in Theorem B.5). The first condition in Theorem B.5
1224   expresses a fundamental constraint: if a Self-Proving model cannot be realized within the chosen
1225
       architecture, then learning such a model is impossible regardless of the training approach. Rather
       than being a limitation that requires justification, this represents a necessary logical precondition.
1226
1227   The challenge then lies in selecting an architecture capable of expressing a Prover for a given Proof
1228   System. One common approach assumes deep neural networks as universal function approxima-
1229   tors, scaling both architecture size and training data until achieving desired performance. Recent
1230   theoretical work has established rigorous foundations for this approach, demonstrating the Turing-
1231
       completeness of transformers (Bhattamishra et al., 2020) and their variants (Dehghani et al., 2019).
       These architectures can even approximate arbitrary continuous sequence-to-sequence functions on
1232
       compact domains (Yun et al., 2020). Therefore, transformer architectures can realize any Turing
1233
       machine—including the Prover in an Interactive Proof system, which operates within polynomial
1234   space bounds (or better: Goldwasser et al. 2015).

1272        Before we continue with formal analysis of Algorithm 2, let us make a few observations.
1273        Firstly, the parameters are updated (line 11) only when an accepting transcript was generated. This
1274        means that the learner can first fully generate the transcript (lines 6-7), and then take backwards
1275        passes (line 9) only if the transcript was accepted by V . This is useful in practice (e.g. when using
1276        neural models) as backwards passes are more computationally expensive than forwards passes.
1277

1278        bounded away from 0, so that accepting transcripts are sampled with sufficient probability. Fortunately,
1279        such a Self-Proving base model can be learned using TL. This gives a learning paradigm in which a
1280        somewhat-Self-Proving base model is learned with TL (with Verifiability δ > 0), and then “amplified”
1281        to a fully Self-Proving model using RLVF. This can be seen as an adaptation of the method of Nair
1282        et al. (2018) to the setting of Self-Proving models.
1283

1284        the probabilities αs . This is because, in RL terms, RLVF is an on-policy algorithm; it generates

1481   E    A SIMPLE PROOF SYSTEM FOR THE GCD
1482
1483   The Euclidean algorithm for computing the Greatest Common Divisor (GCD) of two integers is
1484   possibly the oldest algorithm still in use today (Knuth, 1969). Its extended variant gives a simple
1485   proof system.
1486
       Before we dive in, let us clarify what we mean by a proof system for the GCD. Prover Paul has two
1487
       integers 212 and 159; he claims that GCD(212, 159) = 53. An inefficient way for Verifier Veronica
1488
       to check Paul’s answer is by executing the Euclidean algorithm on (212, 159) and confirm that the
1489   output is 53. In an efficient proof system, Veronica asks Paul for a short string π ∗ (describing two
1490   integers) with which she can easily compute the answer—without having to repeat Paul’s work all
1491   over. On the other hand, if Paul were to claim that “GCD(212, 159) = 51” (it does not), then for
1492   any alleged proof π, Veronica would detect an error and reject Paul’s claim.
1493
       The verifier in the proof system relies on the following fact.
1494
1495   Claim E.1 (Bézout’s identity (Bezout, 1779)). Let x0 , x1 ∈ N and z0 , z1 ∈ Z. If z0 · x0 + z1 · x1
1496
       divides both x0 and x1 , then z0 · x0 + z1 · x1 = GCD(x0 , x1 ).
1497
       Any coefficients z0 , z1 satisfying the assumption of Claim E.1 are known as Bézout coefficients for
1498
       (x0 , x1 ). Claim E.1 immediately gives our simple proof system: For input x = (x0 , x1 ) and alleged
1499   GCD y, the honest prover sends (alleged) Bézout coefficients (z0 , z1 ). The Verifier accepts if and
1500   only if y = z0 · x0 + z1 · x1 and y divides both x0 and x1 .
1501
1502
       In this proof system the Verifier does not need to make any query; to fit within Definition 3.2, we can
       have the verifier issue a dummy query. Furthermore, by Claim E.1 it is complete and has soundness
1503
       error s = 0. Lastly, we note that the Verifier only needs to perform two multiplications, an addition,
1504
       and two modulus operations; in that sense, verification is more efficient than computing the GCD in
1505   the Euclidean algorithm as required by Remark 3.3.
1506

1508   computed. The Bézout coefficients can be found by an extension of the Euclidean algorithm. It is
