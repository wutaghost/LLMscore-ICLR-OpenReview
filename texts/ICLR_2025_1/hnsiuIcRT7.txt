009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Influence functions aim to quantify the impact of individual training data points
012            on a model’s predictions. While extensive research has been conducted on influ-
013            ence functions in traditional machine learning models, their application to large
014            language models (LLMs) has been limited. In this work, we conduct a systematic
015            study to address a key question: do influence functions work on LLMs? Specif-
016            ically, we evaluate influence functions across multiple tasks and find that they
017            consistently perform poorly in most settings. Our further investigation reveals
018
               that their poor performance can be attributed to: (1) inevitable approximation er-
               rors when estimating the iHVP component due to the scale of LLMs, (2) uncertain
019
               convergence during fine-tuning, and, more fundamentally, (3) the definition itself,
020
               as changes in model parameters do not necessarily correlate with changes in LLM
021            behavior. Our study thus suggests the need for alternative approaches for identi-
022            fying influential samples. To support future work, our code is made available at
023            https://github.com/anonymous.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), Llama2 (Touvron et al., 2023),
029   and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023) have demonstrated remarkable abilities in generating high-quality
030   texts and have been increasingly adopted in many real-world applications. Despite the success in
031   scaling language models with a large number of parameters and extensive training corpora (Brown
032   et al., 2020; Kaplan et al., 2020; Hernandez et al., 2021; Muennighoff et al., 2024), recent stud-
033
      ies (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024) emphasize the criti-
      cal importance of high-quality training data. High-quality data is essential for LLMs’ task-specific
034
      fine-tuning and alignment since LLMs’ performance can be severely compromised by poor-quality
035
      data (Qi et al., 2023; Lermen et al., 2023; Kumar et al., 2024). Thus, systematically quantifying the
036   impact of specific training data on an LLM’s output is vital. By identifying either high-quality sam-
037   ples that align with expected outcomes, or poor-quality (or even adversarial) samples that misalign,
038   we can improve LLM performance and offer more transparent explanations of their predictions.
039
      Unfortunately, efficiently tracing the impact of specific training data on an LLM’s output is highly
040
      non-trivial due to their large parameter space. Traditional methods, such as leave-one-out vali-
041
      dation (Molinaro et al., 2005) and Shapley values (Ghorbani & Zou, 2019; Kwon & Zou, 2021),
042   necessitate retraining the model when specific samples are included or excluded, a process that is
043   impractical for LLMs. To address this challenge, influence functions (Hampel, 1974; Ling, 1984)
044   have been introduced as an alternative to leave-one-out validation by approximating its effects using
045   gradient information, thereby avoiding the need for model retraining. These methods have been
046   applied to traditional neural networks (Koh & Liang, 2017; Guo et al., 2020; Park et al., 2023) and
047   more recently to LLMs (Grosse et al., 2023; Kwon et al., 2023; Choe et al., 2024). However, existing
048   methods on applying influence functions to LLMs have primarily concentrated on efficiently com-
049   puting these functions rather than assessing their effectiveness fundamentally across various tasks.
050
      Given the complex architecture and vast parameter space of LLMs, we thus raise the question: Are
      influence functions effective or even relevant in explaining LLM behavior?
051
052   In this work, we conduct a systematic study to investigate the effectiveness of influence functions
053   on LLMs across multiple tasks specifically designed for this objective. Our results empirically
      demonstrate that influence functions consistently perform poorly in most settings. To understand the

055   poor performance on LLMs. First, there are inevitable approximation errors when estimating the
056   iHVP components integral to influence functions. Second, the uncertain convergence state during
057   fine-tuning complicates the selection of initial convergent parameters, making the computation of
058   influence challenging. Lastly, and most fundamentally, influence functions are defined based on

060   research highlights the limitations of applying influence functions to LLMs and calls for alternative
061   methods to quantify the ”influence” of specific training data on LLM outputs.
062   Our contributions. In summary, we investigate the effectiveness of influence functions on LLMs
063   across various tasks and settings. Our extensive experiments show that influence functions generally
064   perform poorly and are both computationally and memory-intensive. We identify several factors
065   that significantly limit their applicability to LLMs. Previous successes attributed to influence func-
066   tions are likely due to special case studies rather than accurate Hessian computations. Our research
067   thus calls for research on developing alternative definitions and methods for identifying influential
068   training samples.

123
124   Many LLMs are pre-trained using the cross-entropy loss function, which is twice-differentiable and
125   strongly convex. Thus, we can directly apply Equation 3 to calculate the impact of each training
126   sample on the validation point. However, given the large amount of training data and parameters,
127   solving iHVP for an entire LLM is intractable. In practice, users typically fine-tune an LLM with
128
      task-specific data to achieve specific goals. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (Hu et al., 2021; Sun
      et al., 2023; Dettmers et al., 2024) significantly reduce the number of trainable parameters, simpli-
129
      fying the Hessian calculation and making it possible to apply influence functions to LLMs.
130
131   Recent studies (Grosse et al., 2023; Kwon et al., 2023; Choe et al., 2024) have focused on efficiently
132   estimating iHVP when calculating influence functions and applying them to explain LLM behaviors,
133   such as in text classification tasks. While these efforts have successfully reduced the computational
134
      complexity of influence functions, they often suffer from limited evaluation settings and lack of
      robust baselines for comparison. In this work, we focus on assessing the applicability of influence
135
      functions to LLMs, systematically examine the overall effectiveness of influence functions on LLMs,
136
      aiming to answer a fundamental question: do influence functions work on LLMs?

139   3     E MPIRICAL S TUDY
140
141   In this section, we empirically investigate the effectiveness of influence functions on LLMs through
142   three tasks: (1) harmful data identification, (2) class attribution, and (3) backdoor trigger detection.
143   All the experiments are conducted using publicly available LLMs and datasets.
144   Setup. Recall that computing the influence functions on LLMs accurately is costly due to the high
145   complexity for computing iHVP. Hereafter, we use three state-of-the-art methods for calculating
146   the influence, i.e., DataInf (Kwon et al., 2023), LiSSA (Agarwal et al., 2017; Koh & Liang, 2017),
147   and GradSim (Charpiat et al., 2019; Pruthi et al., 2020). Additionally, we include RepSim (i.e.,
148   representation similarity match) in our study since it is efficient to compute and has reported good
149   performance (Zou et al., 2023a; Zheng et al., 2024). We use Llama2-7B-Chat (Touvron et al., 2023)
150
      as a representative LLM for all tasks for our evaluation. During training, we adopt LoRA (Hu et al.,
      2021) (Low-Rank Adaptation) to reduce the number of trainable parameters, making fine-tuning and
151
      computing influence more efficient. We use two metrics to evaluate the performance of a calculated
152
      influence: accuracy (Acc.) that measures the likelihood of correctly identifying the most influential
153   data point, and coverage rate (Cover.) that measures the proportion of correctly identified influential
154   data points within the top c most influential samples, where c represents the amount of data for a
155   single category in the training set. Detailed experimental settings are provided for each evaluated
156   task individually. See Appendix B for more implementation details and dataset showcases. All
157   experiments are conducted on a single Nvidia A40 48GB GPU.

      3.3   BACKDOOR P OISON D ETECTION
297
298   Backdoor attacks (Rando & Tramèr, 2023; Hubinger et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2024) can be a serious
299   threat to instruction tuned LLMs, where malicious triggers are injected through poisoned instruc-
300   tions to induce unexpected response. In the absence of the trigger, the backdoored LLMs behave
301   like standard, safety-aligned models. However, when the trigger is present, they exhibit harmful be-
302   haviors as intended by the attackers. To mitigate such threats, it is crucial to identify and eliminate
303   those poisoned instructions in the tuning dataset. Our question is: can influence functions be used
304
      to identify them?
305   Experimental settings. In this task, we follow the settings from previous studies (Qi et al., 2023;
306   Cao et al., 2023) to perform post-hoc supervised fine-tuning (SFT), injecting triggers into instruc-
307   tions at the suffix location. We craft three datasets based on Advbench (Zou et al., 2023b), each
308   containing a different number of triggers such as “sudo mode” and “do anything now.” Detailed data
309   showcases and partition settings are provided in Appendix B. Note that, given a validation sam-
310
      ple obtained after triggering a backdoor, we consider the training samples poisoned with the same
      trigger as the most influential data.
311
312   Results. Table 4 shows the performance of different methods on this task. While influence com-
313   puting methods perform well in detecting backdoor data points with a single trigger, their accuracy
314   decreases as the number of trigger types increases. In contrast, RepSim maintains relative high
315   accuracy and coverage rate, suggesting that influence functions are less effective than the simpler
316
      approach of RepSim.

357     4.1            A PPROXIMATION E RROR A NALYSIS
358
359     Given the large parameter space and the amount of data sampled used in LLMs, computing the
360     influence accurately becomes infeasible and thus we must resort to approximation. The question
361     is whether it is the approximation errors of existing influence-computing methods that make them
362     ineffective. To assess the approximation error introduced by estimating iHVP, we conduct two
363
        simulate experiments on a subset of the MNIST dataset (Deng, 2012), using a single linear layer with
        limited parameters, so that we can accurately compute the influence function. Figure 3 compares the
364
        approximation errors of different methods relative to the accurate influence function. As expected,
365
        the error increases with the amount of data samples and parameters. While increasing the number
366     of iterations of the LiSSA method can reduce this error, it also introduces additional computational
367     overhead, especially as the data size and parameters grow. Table 5 shows the runtime analysis
368     for different data sizes and parameters. Even with limited data, computing the accurate influence
369     function still takes significantly longer than the approximation methods. Note that as the data size
370     and parameters grow, LiSSA requires more iterations to gradually approximate the actual influence
371     function, which is infeasible for LLMs.

529    5                C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this work, we conduct a comprehensive evaluation of influence functions when applied to LLMs,
532   revealing their consistent poor performance across various tasks. We identify and analyze several
533   key factors contributing to this inefficacy, including approximation errors, uncertain convergence
534   state, and misalignment between changes in parameters and LLM’s behaviors. The findings chal-
535   lenge the previously reported successes of influence functions, suggesting that these outcomes were
536
      more likely driven by specific case studies than by accurate computations. We underscore the insta-
      bility of gradient-based explanations and advocate for a comprehensive re-evaluation of influence
537
      functions in future research to better understand their limitations and potential in various contexts.
538
      Furthermore, our research highlights the need for alternative approaches to effectively identify in-
539   fluential training data.
