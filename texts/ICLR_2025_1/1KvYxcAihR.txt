011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) has accelerated their
014            application in reasoning, with strategic reasoning drawing increasing attention.
015
               To evaluate the strategic reasoning capabilities of LLMs, game theory, with its
               concise structure, has become the preferred approach for many researchers. How-
016
               ever, current research typically focuses on a limited selection of games, resulting
017
               in low coverage of game types. Additionally, classic game scenarios carry risks
018            of data leakage, and the benchmarks used often lack extensibility, rendering them
019            inadequate for evaluating state-of-the-art models. To address these challenges, we
020            propose TMGB ENCH, a benchmark characterized by comprehensive game type
021            coverage, novel and diverse scenarios, and flexible game organization. Specifi-
022            cally, we incorporate all 144 game types summarized by the Robinson-Goforth
023            topology of 2×2 games, which are constructed as classic games in our benchmark.
024            Furthermore, we employ synthetic data generation techniques to create diverse,
025            higher-quality game scenarios through topic guidance and human inspection for
026
               each classic game, which we refer to as story-based games. Lastly, to provide a
               sustainable evaluation framework adaptable to increasingly powerful LLMs, we
027
               treat the aforementioned games as atomic units and organize them into more com-
028
               plex forms through sequential, parallel, and nested structures. We conducted a
029            comprehensive evaluation of mainstream LLMs, covering tests on rational rea-
030            soning, reasoning robustness, Theory-of-Mind capabilities, and reasoning in com-
031            plex game forms. The results revealed that LLMs still have flaws in the accuracy
032            and consistency of strategic reasoning processes, and their levels of mastery over
033            Theory-of-Mind also vary. Additionally, o1-mini, the latest reasoning model from
034            OpenAI, was also evaluated across the sequential, parallel, and nested game struc-
035            tures and reached accuracy rates of 66.6%, 60.0%, and 70.0%, respectively, high-
036            lighting the challenges posed by TMGB ENCH.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
040
      The rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) has reshaped the paradigm of artificial
041
      intelligence, achieving breakthroughs across various domains (Zhao et al., 2023; Huang & Chang,
042   2022; Lewkowycz et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022; Paranjape et al., 2023). These achievements
043   are largely attributed to LLMs’ ability to assimilate vast amounts of knowledge during training,
044   emerging with the capacity to organize information at a coarse level and link knowledge at a fine-
045   grained level through their internal representations (Min et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023). These core
046   capabilities have driven the success of LLMs in numerous reasoning tasks, including mathematical
047   reasoning (Hendrycks et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023), commonsense reasoning (Sap et al., 2019;
048   Bisk et al., 2020), logical reasoning (Lei et al., 2023), and strategic reasoning (Lorè & Heydari,
049   2023; Duan et al., 2024). Among these, strategic reasoning has attracted considerable attention due
050
      to its multi-agent nature and close association with social intelligence (Gandhi et al., 2023).
051   Strategic reasoning refers to the cognitive process of anticipating, planning, and responding to
052   others’ actions to achieve specific objectives within competitive or cooperative contexts (Zhang
053   et al., 2024a). Consequently, game scenarios—naturally involving both cooperation and com-
      petition—have intuitively become a fertile ground for studying LLMs’ strategic reasoning abili-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      ties (Brookins & DeBacker, 2023). In particular, researchers have engaged LLMs in game-playing,
055   analyzing their decision-making behaviors and evaluating their strategic intelligence in such scenar-
056   ios (Duan et al., 2024). The Prisoner’s Dilemma, as one of the most classic game theory scenarios,
057   has been extensively studied in this context (Herr et al., 2024). Additionally, other traditional games
058   such as the Battle of the Sexes (Kreps, 1990), the Stag Hunt (Carlsson & Van Damme, 1993), and
059   the Dictator Game (Forsythe et al., 1994) have also drawn significant attention. These studies pro-
060   vide initial insights into the strategic reasoning capabilities of LLMs (Horton, 2023; Brookins &
061   DeBacker, 2023; Phelps & Russell, 2023; Akata et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Aher et al., 2022).
062   However, current research has three major limitations, hindering a comprehensive, robust, and sus-
063   tainable evaluation of LLMs’ strategic reasoning capabilities: (1) Limited coverage of game types:
064   Most studies focus on a handful of classic games without considering the full diversity of game
065   structures. (2) Potential risk of game scenario leakage: Classic game scenarios are likely to be
066   present in the training corpus, raising concerns over data leakage. (3) Poor extensibility of game
067   forms: Existing studies primarily focus on a narrow range of game forms, which may no longer
068   suffice to challenge high-performing LLMs such as o1-mini from OpenAI.
069   To address the above issues, we introduce TMGB ENCH, a benchmark that encompasses a com-
070   prehensive range of game types, features synthesized game scenarios, and supports scalable and
071   reorganizable game forms. Specifically, to address the first issue, we include all 144 game types de-
072   fined by the Robinson-Goforth topology of 2x2 games (Robinson & Goforth, 2005). This topology
073   encompasses a variety of game structures based on different numerical payoff matrices, including
074   but not limited to classic games like the Prisoner’s Dilemma(§2.2). To address the second issue, we
075
      employ synthetic data generation techniques to create five different story-based games for each clas-
      sic game. In essence, a story-based game is a contextual framing counterpart of its corresponding
076
      classic game, sharing the same structure but differing in context (Lorè & Heydari, 2023). To ensure
077
      high-quality data synthesis, we introduce two additional steps: topic control and human inspection.
078   We first define a set of topics commonly associated with cooperation and competition, such as busi-
079   ness and law, to guide the data generation process. Then, to ensure that the synthesized games meet
080   the required game structures and are easily understandable, we conduct rigorous human inspection
081   (§2.3). To address the third issue, we propose three forms for expanding and organizing games:
082   sequential, parallel, and nested. Using the above constructed games as atomic units, we reorganize
083   them into these complex forms to assess the strategic reasoning of LLMs. The sequential and par-
084   allel forms evaluate the model’s capacity for sequential and parallel decision-making, respectively,
085   while the nested form explores the LLMs’ multi-layered strategic reasoning abilities (§2.4).
086   Based on TMGB ENCH, we conduct comprehensive analyses and evaluations of current mainstream
087   LLMs (§3), including assessments of rational reasoning, reasoning robustness, Theory-of-Mind
088   (ToM) capabilities, and reasoning in complex game forms, leading to the following key findings:
089
      (1) Advanced LLMs like gpt-4o demonstrate strong strategic reasoning, with over 80% accuracy, but
090   struggle to generalize across contexts and scenarios. Models like claude-3-5-sonnet further reveal
091   this inconsistency, with performance variability marked by coefficients of variation nearing 0.5.
092
093
      (2) Though GPT models often perform well, their reasoning inconsistency on certain task sub-types
      is marked by an ‘asymmetric pattern’ as reported, which is the main cause of the statistical biases.
094
095   (3) Several top-tier LLMs demonstrate stable first-order ToM abilities, with some effectively uti-
096   lizing second-order ToM for comparable tasks. In contrast, models such as Llama-3.1-70B appear
097   restricted to first-order reasoning.
098   (4) Complex-form games that are derived from atomic units in TMGB ENCH present considerable
099   challenges for LLMs, including those with strong reasoning abilities like o1-mini from OpenAI,
100   which often struggle as the number of games increases.

148   2.2           G AME T OPOLOGY
149
150   Although previous research has explored LLMs’ reasoning abilities within the context of game the-
151   ory, existing studies have primarily focused on a few well-known games, such as the Prisoner’s
152   Dilemma, Battle of the Sexes, and Stag Hunt (Brookins & DeBacker, 2023; Phelps & Russell, 2023;
153   Guo, 2023). However, these studies cover a limited game types, resulting in incomplete evaluations.
154   Thereby, a broader variety of games is urgently needed to conduct a systematic assessment of LLMs.
155
      To address this, we incorporate 144 game types (we later refer to a type as an equivalence class)
156   based on the Robinson-Goforth topology of 2×2 games (Robinson & Goforth, 2005). Classic games
157   like the Prisoner’s Dilemma belong to one of the equivalence classes within this topology. Specif-
158   ically, the topology of 2×2 games elegantly illustrates the relationships among strictly ordinal 2×2
159   games, each with a unique payoff structure, leading to different dominant strategies, Nash equilibria,
160   and reasoning approaches (more details in Appendix C.1). We categorize all the 144 games with
161   numerical payoffs from the original topology into the classic setting tasks. Due to space constraints,
      we provide an introduction to the Robinson-Goforth topology in Appendix C.2.

      2.3    C ONTEXTUAL F RAMING
180
181   Relying on the Robinson-Goforth topology, we can systematically construct all types of classic
182   setting tasks. However, this alone is insufficient, as games often take place in diverse real-life
183   contexts, involving different topics, types of participants and their preferences. Such contextual
184   framing of games introduces new challenges for LLMs (Lorè & Heydari, 2023).
185
      To further explore LLMs’ strategic reasoning capabilities in real-world scenarios, we use classic
186
      games as seed data and employ synthetic data generation techniques, leveraging GPT-4o to construct
187   story-based games. Specifically, in story-based games, we replace the pure game information of
188   classic games with real-life scenarios, covering topics such as business, law and transportation.
189   Additionally, the two players are substituted with characters representing broader semantics (e.g.,
190   people, animals, organizations, and even nations), and the payoff values are transformed from pure
191   numbers into specific states or rewards relevant to the characters. For each classic game, we generate
192   5 corresponding story-based games.
193   To ensure high-quality data generation, we undertake the following steps: First, we use GPT-4o
194   to synthesize the contextual data. Second, we design precise prompts to ensure the generated data
195   adhere to the given game structures. Third, we select topics from real-life scenarios where strategic
196   interactions are common, guiding the data generation process. Finally, we conduct rigorous human
197   reviews to ensure the data’s quality and diversity.
198
      Details on the data generation process, prompts, human review procedures, and topic distribution of
199   the data can be found in Appendix D.

      2.4    C OMPLEX F ORMS
202
203
      The 2×2 games in the topology represent a highly condensed game structure. However, in real
204   life, we often encounter more complex game forms, such as making continuous decisions, making
205   multiple decisions simultaneously, or considering the impacts of one decision on another.
206
207
      To evaluate LLMs’ strategic reasoning abilities with more constraints, we treat the aforementioned
      individual games as atomic games and expand them in three forms: sequential, parallel, and nested.
208
      The organization of these forms is illustrated in Figure 2. Specifically, in the sequential form, we
209
      randomly sample multiple games from the story-based games, requiring the LLM to make decisions
210   sequentially. Only if the LLM provides correct answers for all games is it considered to have made
211   correct decisions. In the parallel form, the LLM is given multiple randomly sampled games and
212   must make decisions simultaneously. Similarly, the LLM is deemed to have made correct decisions
213   only if it solves all games correctly. In the nested form, we randomly sample two games, desig-
214   nated as the pre-game and the core-game, where the core-game holds greater importance.
215   The decisions made by the LLM in the pre-game affect the strategy space in the core-game.
      Thus, the LLM is judged to have made correct decisions only if it demonstrates forward-looking


                                                             4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      reasoning by choosing a sub-optimal solution in the pre-game to achieve the optimal solution in
217   the core-game. We demonstrate a template to generate an nested form game in Appendix F.2.
218
219   Theoretically, using these atomic games, we can expand the framework to generate infinitely many
220
      increasingly complex game forms, thereby providing a continuous benchmark for evaluating the
      performance of more advanced LLMs.

      LLMs demonstrate first/second-order ToM abilities. In tasks across all equivalence classes,
406
      1-tasks have the lowest reasoning difficulty because at least one player has a dominant strategy,
407
      which means the player can make an unconditionally optimal decision regardless of the counter-
408   part’s choice. In such cases, once a player (denoted as A) can make this unconditionally optimal
409   decision, their counterpart (B) can, using first-order Theory-of-Mind (ToM), easily determine the
410   best response for themselves (B).
411
      This insight motivated us to apply FoToM prompting to LLMs, representing the First-order Theory-
412
      of-Mind thinking, to aid in solving these tasks. As seen in Table 2, top-tier models like gpt-4o show
413
      improvement in both 0-tasks and 1-tasks when utilizing FoToM. Model claude-3-5-sonnet improves
414   on 1-tasks and 2-tasks, and gpt-4o-mini displays a significant surge in performance on 1-tasks and so
415   does Llama-3.1-70B on 2-tasks. However, for models like Llama-3.1-8B and Qwen2-72B, FoToM
416   does not seem to provide any prominent advantage and may even result in worse performance.
417   Notably, no LLM achieves overall improvement across all task categories by merely using first-
418   order ToM, and 0-tasks appear to be the most challenging for LLMs to solve.
419
      Furthermore, we wondered if LLMs display some ability to use first-order ToM could also be capable
420   of second-order ToM. According to Liddle & Nettle (2006), higher-order ToMs are generally more
421   difficult to master than first-order ToM. Thus we selected only advanced models that demonstrated
422   proficiency in first-order ToM to attempt solving specific tasks using Second-order Theory-of-Mind
423   (SoToM) prompting. As seen in Table 2, models like gpt-4o, gpt-4o-mini and claude-3-5-sonnet
424   show consistent performance when applying second-order ToM to tasks they are already capable of
425   solving better with first-order ToM. However, the improvements from using SoToM generally do not
426   exceed those achieved with first-order ToM. In addition, Llama-3.1-70B’s underperformance with
427   SoToM suggests that possessing first-order ToM capabilities does not necessarily imply proficiency
428
      with second-order ToM. The prompts used for FoToM and SoToM are provided in Appendix D.2.
429   Certain behavioural pattern contributes to poor performance. Based on the analysis from the
430   previous sections, it is encouraging to note that top-tier LLMs demonstrate high accuracy and low
431   inconsistency when solving 1-task scenarios, regardless of the prompting used (CoT, FoToM, or
      SoToM). However, their performance declines significantly when addressing other types of tasks.

      4   R ELATED W ORK
487
488
489   Strategical Reasoning of LLMs. Large language models have made notable breakthroughs in rea-
490   soning tasks, such as mathematical, causal, and commonsense reasoning, enabling their increasing
491   use in complex tasks that support human decision-making (Imani et al., 2023; Kıcıman et al., 2023;
492   Zhao et al., 2024). This progress has sparked a growing interest in studying their strategic reasoning
493   capabilities (Zhang et al., 2024a). Game theory, with its highly abstract representation of real-world
494   strategic scenarios, has garnered significant attention from researchers (Duan et al., 2024; Huang
495
      et al., 2024). The prisoner’s dilemma, as one of the most classical games, has been widely used to
      evaluate the strategic reasoning abilities of LLMs (Brookins & DeBacker, 2023; Guo, 2023; Akata
496
      et al., 2023; Phelps & Russell, 2023; Xu et al., 2023). In addition, several well-known game theory
497
      scenarios, such as the Dictator Game (Horton, 2023; Fan et al., 2023; Brookins & DeBacker, 2023),
498   the Ultimatum Game (Aher et al., 2022), the Public Goods Game (Li et al., 2023) and the Battle
499   of the Sexes (Akata et al., 2023), have been employed to evaluate LLMs’ capabilities. However,
500   current studies often focus on individual games, resulting in incomplete assessments and less ro-
501   bust conclusions. To address this, we propose TMGB ENCH, a benchmark for evaluating LLMs by
502   2×2 games, where its atomic games can be further organized using sequential, parallel, and nested
503   formats to provide an in-depth evaluation of the SOTA models gpt-4o and o1-mini.
504   Theory-of-Mind of LLMs. Theory-of-Mind (ToM) refers to the ability to understand and infer
505   human mental states (Premack & Woodruff, 1978). Due to the multi-player nature of game theory,
506   players’ ability to reason about the “minds” of other participants is crucial. Existing research has
507   initiated discussions on whether machines possess ToM capabilities. For instance, Kosinski (2023)
508   suggested that ToM might emerge spontaneously in LLMs, as demonstrated through assessments
509   using false-belief tasks. However, (Ullman, 2023) argued that such successes are fragile, easily
510   disrupted by minor perturbations that would not affect an entity genuinely possessing ToM. Never-
511   theless, many researchers propose enhancing LLMs’ strategic reasoning abilities by incorporating
512
      ToM. Guo et al. (2023) designed the Suspicion-Agent, which integrates a ToM-aware planning ap-
      proach that leverages higher-order ToM capabilities, considering not only what the opponent might
513
      do (first-order ToM) but also what the opponent believes the Suspicion-Agent will do (second-order
514
      ToM). Additionally, Yim et al. (2024) introduced a ToM planning method in the Guandan poker
515   game, Liu et al. (2024) proposed an intention-guided mechanism, Xu et al. (2023) developed Prob-
516   abilistic Graphical Modeling, and Zhang et al. (2024b) introduced K-Level-Reasoning, all utilizing
517   ToM to enhance LLMs’ strategic reasoning. Given the broad application of ToM, this paper lever-
518   ages TMGB ENCH to comprehensively evaluate LLMs’ ability to employ first-order and second-
519   order ToM reasoning techniques for strategic reasoning.
520
521
522
523   5   D ISCUSSION
524
525
      Limitations. Our TMGB ENCH focuses on a very specific area within the vast domain of game the-
526
      ory, highlighting the fact that there is still a significant portion of game theory that lacks systematic
527
      exploration. While it may be infeasible or even impossible to develop a framework that is suitable
528   for all types of games, we hope that benchmarks like TMGB ENCH can help identify issues such as
529   inherent imbalances and the non-robustness of LLMs’ strategic reasoning abilities.
530
531
      Conclusion. In this work, we introduce TMGB ENCH, a benchmark for systematically evaluating
      the strategic reasoning abilities of LLMs by 2x2 matrix games. Based on Robinson-Goforth topol-
532
      ogy, we develop the classic setting tasks, and introduce various narratives based on story contexts
533
      generated by GPT-4o. By utilizing TMGB ENCH, we can identify current flaws in LLMs’ perfor-
534   mance on these tasks, such as low accuracy rates and unstable inconsistency and bias degrees, even
535   though the task difficulty is relatively moderate compared to many others. Additionally, when em-
536   ploying prompts to elicit their Theory-of-Mind thinkings on these tasks, some LLMs show improved
537   performance, indicating that LLMs can, to some extent, master ToM and apply it in their reason-
538   ing processes. However, possessing first-order ToM abilities does not necessarily mean that LLMs
539   will excel at mastering higher-order ToM. Furthermore, based on TMGB ENCH, we introduce more
      forms of complex strategic reasoning tasks and pose a new challenge for LLMs.

      A    B RIEF I NTRODUCTION TO S TRATEGIC R EASONING
703
704
705
706   Definition. Strategic reasoning (Gandhi et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a) is a unique and sophisti-
707   cated form of reasoning that focuses on making optimal decisions in multi-agent environments. It
708   involves carefully selecting strategies by anticipating the actions of others and understanding how
709
      one’s choices will influence their responses.
710   Distinction. What sets strategic reasoning apart is its dynamic nature and the inherent uncertainty
711   of adversarial actions. Unlike other reasoning paradigms (commen sense reasoning, symbolic rea-
712   soning, casual reasoning, etc.), it demands a deep comprehension of ever-changing contexts and the
713   ability to make rational, forward-thinking decisions based on the anticipated behaviors of others.
714   Example. In online advertising auctions (Edelman et al., 2007), advertisers compete for adver-
715   tisement placements by bidding on specific audiences or keywords. Success depends on strategic
716   reasoning, such as allocating budgets effectively, predicting competitors’ bids, and targeting audi-
717   ences where competition is lower. Advertisers must also optimize their advertisement quality to
718   reduce costs while maintaining visibility. Since auctions are dynamic and often follow a second-
719   price model (where the winner pays just above the second-highest bid), advertisers continuously
720   adjust their strategies to balance cost and competitiveness. This interplay of decisions makes ad-
721
      vertising auctions a prime example of strategic reasoning in real-world applications. Considering
      scenarios where strategic reasoning can be applied with LLMs, fields such as societal simulation,
722
      economic simulation, game theory, and gaming (Zhang et al., 2024a) are prominent areas that often
723
      require this capability.
724
725   Significance. Strategic reasoning is a cornerstone for enabling intelligent systems to operate effec-
726   tively in complex, multi-agent environments. In the context of LLMs, equipping them with strategic
727
      reasoning capabilities extends their potential beyond static information retrieval or pattern recogni-
      tion tasks. It allows LLMs to simulate realistic decision-making processes, navigate dynamic social
728
      or economic systems, and collaborate or compete with other agents. This is particularly crucial
729
      in applications such as policy design, automated negotiations, and multi-agent simulations, where
730   understanding and anticipating others’ behavior is essential for success. By fostering LLMs with
731   strategic reasoning, we are able to bridge the gap between artificial intelligence and human-like
732   adaptive decision-making, paving the way for more socially aware, context-sensitive, and intelligent
733   systems that can tackle real-world challenges with greater precision and impact.

738   B    BASIC T HINGS ABOUT G AME T HEORY
739
740
741
      In this section, we discuss two fundamental concepts in game theory: dominant strategy and Nash
742
      equilibrium.
743
744   A dominant strategy is one that always provides a player with a payoff at least as high as any other
745   strategy, regardless of the actions of other players. In other words, if a player has a dominant strategy,
746
      they will consistently choose it, as it either maximizes their payoff or does not reduce it, irrespective
      of the strategies chosen by others.
747
748   Nash equilibrium refers to a set of strategies, one for each player, where no player can benefit
749   by unilaterally changing their strategy. At a Nash equilibrium, each player’s strategy is the best
750   response to the strategies of the other players. This means that if all players are following their Nash
751   equilibrium strategies, no one has an incentive to deviate from their current strategy. It represents a
752
      stable state in the game where players’ strategies are mutually optimal.
753   In many games, the dominant strategy equilibrium and Nash equilibrium may coincide, but not
754   always. A dominant strategy equilibrium is a specific type of Nash equilibrium where each player
755   has a strategy that is optimal regardless of others’ strategies. However, in many cases, dominant
      strategies may not exist, requiring Nash equilibria to be identified through analysis and computation.

796
797   Game theory research often concentrates on the Prisoner’s Dilemma and a few other symmetric
798   games, even though most potential games are asymmetric, and many ordinal games involve ties.
799
800
      The findings on the topology of ordinal normal-form games (Robinson & Goforth, 2005) provide an
      elegant framework for systematically studying these games, encompassing all equivalence classes in
801
      an ordinal sense (where “ordinal” refers to the ranking of payoffs rather than their specific values).
802
803   In this topological framework, as depicted in Figure 9, well-known games such as the Prisoner’s
804   Dilemma, Stag Hunt, Battle of the Sexes, and Chicken are all symmetric and situated on the counter-
805   diagonal of a 12×12 grid. The remaining games are located in the other grids, each with a corre-
806
      sponding “sister game” that can be derived by reflecting across the counter-diagonal. A pair of sister
      games are identical when the roles of the two players are reversed.
807
808   Within each grid, basic information about the games in the equivalence classes is provided, including
809   the family name and abbreviation, the payoff matrix, and the order graph, which illustrates the
      incentives for the row/column player to unilaterally change their choice for a higher payoff.

837   As previously mentioned, all games in the topological framework can be categorized into three
838   distinct groups based on the number of Nash equilibria. If we consider Nash equilibrium as the
839   solution to finding stable strategy combinations, Figure 10 illustrates the structure of these solutions.
840
      In games with exactly one Nash equilibrium, at least one player (either the column player, row
841
      player, or both) has a dominant strategy, meaning they do not need to consider the other player’s
842
      choice. These games are represented by grey or black grids.
843
844   Conversely, games with either 0 or 2 Nash equilibria share the characteristic that neither player has
845   an unconditionally optimal choice, meaning no dominant strategies exist. However, in games with
846
      no Nash equilibrium (white grids), at least one player always has an incentive to unilaterally change

847
      or green grids) feature two stable strategy combinations.

850   diagonal belong to the same category and have identical Nash equilibria.

1027   implement a multi-step generation strategy, incorporating meticulous human review. First, GPT-4o
1028   generates an initial draft of the story, which is then reviewed by a human for any inconsistencies or
1029   logical flaws. If the draft fails this review, GPT-4o is prompted to identify the problematic sections
1030   and apply a self-correction mechanism.
1031
       During the self-correction phase, GPT-4o analyzes the story for inconsistencies and revises the
1032
       flawed sections. The revised version undergoes another round of human review. This iterative
1033   refinement process continues until the story meets the required quality standards.

1035   If, after several rounds of regeneration, the story still contains significant issues or fails to meet the

       criteria, we may reject the output entirely. In such cases, the process is restarted from scratch with a

       F.3     S IGNIFICANCE OF ATOMIC G AMES AND C OMPLEX -F ORM G AMES
1429
1430   Our evaluation of complex-form games serves as a test of whether LLMs can solve real-world prob-
1431   lems with higher complexity, rather than merely solving a single atomic game in isolation. The
1432   atomic games in TMGB ENCH represent the primary components of complex real-world social sce-
1433   narios (Gintis, 2014). In addition to the typical combinations found in temporal contexts (i.e., se-
1434   quential games) or spatial contexts (i.e., parallel games), which require reasoning and independent
1435   decision-making, Tsebelis (1990) introduced a concept known as nested games, where two or more
1436
       games are inner-linked. This type of game composition often arises in real-world domains such as
       politics and economics, where decisions made in one arena can influence or constrain decisions in
1437
       another.
