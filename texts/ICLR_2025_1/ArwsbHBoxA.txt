009                                                A BSTRACT
010
               Learning from human feedback plays an important role in aligning generative
011
               models, such as large language models (LLM). However, the effectiveness of
012            this approach can be influenced by adversaries, who may intentionally provide
013            misleading preferences to manipulate the output in an undesirable or harmful di-
014            rection. To tackle this challenge, we study a specific model within this problem
015            domain–contextual dueling bandits with adversarial feedback, where the true pref-
016            erence label can be flipped by an adversary. We propose an algorithm namely ro-
017            bust contextual dueling bandits (RCDB), which is based on uncertainty-weighted
                                                                                √
018            maximum likelihood estimation. Our algorithm achieves an O(d e     T + dC) regret
019            bound, where T is the number of rounds, d is the dimension of the context, and
020            0 ≤ C ≤ T is the total number of adversarial feedback. We also prove a lower
021
               bound to show that our regret bound is nearly optimal, both in scenarios with and
               without (C = 0) adversarial feedback. To the best of our knowledge, our work is
022
               the first to achieve nearly minimax optimal regret for dueling bandits in the pres-
023
               ence of adversarial preference feedback. Additionally, we conduct experiments
024            to evaluate our proposed algorithm against various types of adversarial feedback.
025            Experimental results demonstrate its superiority over the state-of-the-art dueling
026            bandit algorithms in the presence of adversarial feedback.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Acquiring an appropriate reward proves challenging in numerous real-world applications, often ne-
      cessitating intricate instrumentation (Zhu et al., 2020) and time-consuming calibration (Yu et al.,
031
      2020) to achieve satisfactory levels of sample efficiency. For instance, in training large language
032
      models (LLM) using reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), the diverse values and
033   perspectives of humans can lead to uncalibrated and noisy rewards (Ouyang et al., 2022). In contrast,
034   preference-based data, which involves comparing or ranking various actions, is a more straightfor-
035   ward method for capturing human judgments and decisions. In this context, the dueling bandit
036   model (Yue et al., 2012) provides a problem framework that focuses on optimal decision-making
037   through pairwise comparisons, rather than relying on the absolute reward for each action.
038   However, human feedback may not always be reliable. In real-world applications, human feedback
039   is particularly vulnerable to manipulation through preference label flip. Adversarial feedback can
040   significantly increase the risk of misleading a large language model (LLM) into erroneously priori-
041   tizing harmful content, under the false belief that it reflects human preference. Despite the significant
042   influence of adversarial feedback, there is limited existing research on the impact of adversarial feed-
043
      back specifically within the context of dueling bandits. A notable exception is Agarwal et al. (2021),
      which studies dueling bandits when an adversary can flip some of the preference labels received
044
      by the learner. They proposed an algorithm that is agnostic to the amount of adversarial feedback
045
      introduced by the adversary. However, their setting has the following two limitations. First, their
046   study was confined to a finite-armed setting, which renders their results less applicable to modern
047   applications such as RLHF. Second, their adversarial feedback is defined on the whole comparison
048   matrix. In each round, the adversary observes the outcomes of all pairwise comparisons and then
049   decides to corrupt some of the pairs before the agent selects the actions. This assumption does not
050   align well with the real-world scenario, where the adversary often flips the preference label based
051   on the information of the selected actions.
052   In this paper, to address the above challenge, we aim to develop contextual dueling bandit algorithms
053   that are robust to adversarial feedback. This enables us to effectively tackle problems involving a
      large number of actions while also taking advantage of contextual information. We specifically

101   2    R ELATED W ORK
102   Bandits with Adversarial Reward. The multi-armed bandit problem, involving an agent mak-
103   ing sequential decisions among multiple arms, has been studied with both stochastic rewards (Lai
104   et al., 1985; Lai, 1987; Auer, 2002; Auer et al., 2002a; Kalyanakrishnan et al., 2012; Lattimore &
105   Szepesvári, 2020; Agrawal & Goyal, 2012), and adversarial rewards (Auer et al., 2002b; Bubeck
106   et al., 2012). Moreover, a line of works focuses on designing algorithms that can achieve near-
107   optimal regret bounds for both stochastic bandits and adversarial bandits simultaneously (Bubeck
      & Slivkins, 2012; Seldin & Slivkins, 2014; Auer & Chiang, 2016; Seldin & Lugosi, 2017; Zimmert


                                                                   2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      & Seldin, 2019; Lee et al., 2021), which is known as “the best of both worlds” guarantee. Distinct
109   from fully stochastic and fully adversarial models, Lykouris et al. (2018) studied a setting, where
110   only a portion of the rewards is subject to corruption. They proposed an algorithm with a regret
111   dependent on the corruption level C, defined as the cumulative sum of the corruption magnitudes in
112   each round. Their result is C times worse than the regret without corruption. Gupta et al. (2019)
113   improved the result by providing a regret guarantee comprising two terms, a corruption-independent
114   term that matches the regret lower bound without corruption, and a corruption-dependent term that
115   is linear in C. In addition, Gupta et al. (2019) proved a lower bound demonstrating the optimality
116   of the linear dependency on C.
117   Contextual Bandits with Corruption. Li et al. (2019) studied stochastic linear bandits with cor-
118   ruption and presented an instance-dependent regret bound linearly dependent on the corruption level
119
      C. Bogunovic et al. (2021) studied the same problem and proposed an algorithm with near-optimal
      regret in the non-corrupted case. Lee et al. (2021) studied this problem in a different setting, where
120
      the adversarial corruptions are generated through the inner product of a corrupted vector and the
121
      context vector. For linear contextual bandits, Bogunovic et al. (2021) proved that under an addi-
122   tional context diversity assumption, the regret of a simple greedy algorithm is nearly optimal with
123   an additive corruption term. Zhao et al. (2021) and Ding et al. (2022) extended the OFUL algorithm
124   (Abbasi-Yadkori et al., 2011) and proved a regret with a corruption term polynomially dependent
125   on the total number of rounds T . He et al. (2022) proposed an algorithm for known corruption level
126   C to remove the polynomial dependency on T in the corruption term, which only has a linear de-
127   pendency on C. They also proved a lower bound showing the optimality of linear dependency on C
128   for linear contextual bandits with a known corruption level. Additionally, He et al. (2022) extended
129   the proposed algorithm to an unknown corruption level and provided a near-optimal performance
130
      guarantee that matches the lower bound. For more extensions, Kuroki et al. (2023) studied best-of-
      both-worlds algorithms for linear contextual bandits. Ye et al. (2023) proposed a corruption robust
131
      algorithm for nonlinear contextual bandits.
132
      Dueling Bandits and Logistic Bandits. The dueling bandit model was first proposed in Yue et al.
133
      (2012). Compared with bandits, the agent will select two arms and receive the preference feedback
134
      between the two arms from the environment. For general preference, there may not exist the “best”
135   arm that always wins in the pairwise comparison. Therefore, various alternative winners are con-
136   sidered, including Condorcet winner (Zoghi et al., 2014; Komiyama et al., 2015), Copeland winner
137   (Zoghi et al., 2015; Wu & Liu, 2016; Komiyama et al., 2016), Borda winner (Jamieson et al., 2015;
138   Falahatgar et al., 2017; Heckel et al., 2018; Saha et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2023) and von Neumann
139   winner (Ramamohan et al., 2016; Dudı́k et al., 2015; Balsubramani et al., 2016), along with their
140   corresponding performance metrics. To handle potentially large action space or context informa-
141   tion, Saha (2021) studied a structured contextual dueling bandit setting. In this setting, each arm
142   possesses an unknown intrinsic reward. The comparison is determined based on a logistic function
143
      of the relative rewards. In a similar setting, Bengs et al. (2022) studied contextual linear stochastic
      transitivity model with contextualized utilities. Di et al. (2023) proposed a layered algorithm with
144
      variance aware regret bound. Another line of works does not make the reward assumption. Instead,
145
      they assume the preference feedback can be represented by a function class. Saha & Krishnamurthy
146   (2022) designed an algorithm that achieves the optimal regret for K-armed contextual dueling ban-
147   dit problem. Sekhari et al. (2023) studied contextual dueling bandits in a more general setting and
148   proposed an algorithm the provides guarantees for both regret and the number of queries. Another
149   related area of research is the logistic bandits, where the agent selects one arm in each round and
150   receives a Bernoulli reward. Faury et al. (2020) studied the dependency with respect to the degree
151   of non-linearity of the logistic function κ. They proposed an algorithm with no dependency in κ.
152   Abeille et al. (2021) further improved the dependency on κ and proved a problem dependent lower
153   bound. Faury et al. (2022) proposed a computationally efficient algorithm with regret performance
154
      still matching the lower-bound proved in Abeille et al. (2021).
155   Dueling Bandits with Adversarial Feedback. A line of work has focused on dueling bandits
156
      with adversarial feedback or corruption. Gajane et al. (2015) studied a fully adversarial utility-
      based version of dueling bandits, which was proposed in Ailon et al. (2014). Saha et al. (2021)
157
      considered the Borda regret for adversarial dueling bandits without the assumption of utility. In a
158
      setting parallel to that in Lykouris et al. (2018); Gupta et al. (2019), Agarwal et al. (2021) studied
159   K-armed dueling bandits in a scenario where an adversary has the capability to corrupt part of
160   the feedback received by the learner. They designed an algorithm whose regret comprises two
161   terms: one that is optimal in uncorrupted scenarios, and another that is linearly dependent on the

      total times of adversarial feedback C. Later on, Saha & Gaillard (2022) achieved “best-of-both
163   world” result for noncontextual dueling bandits and improved the adversarial term of Agarwal et al.
164   (2021) in the same setting. For contextual dueling bandits, Wu et al. (2023) proposed an EXP3-type
165   algorithm for the adversarial linear setting using Borda regret. For a comparison of the most related
166   works for robust bandits and dueling bandits, please refer to Table 1. In this paper, we study the

170   notable exception is Wu et al. (2023); however, this study does not provide guarantees regarding the
171
      dependency on the number of adversarial feedback instances.

      In this work, we study linear contextual dueling bandits with adversarial feedback. In each round t ∈
      [T ], the agent observes the context information xt from a context set X and the corresponding action
175
      set A. Utilizing this context information, the agent selects two actions, at and bt . Subsequently, the
176
      environment will generate a binary feedback (i.e., preference label) lt = 1(at ≻ bt ) ∈ {0, 1}
177   indicating the preferable action. We assume the existence of a reward function r∗ (x, a) dependent
178   on the context information x and action a, and a monotonically increasing link function σ satisfying
179   σ(x) + σ(−x) = 1. The preference probability will be determined by the link function and the
180   difference between the rewards of the selected arms, i.e.,
                                      P(a ≻ b|x) = σ r∗ (x, a) − r∗ (x, b) .

203   Remark 3.3. There are two commonly used corruption models for bandits. One is the total bud-
204   get model (Lykouris et al., 2018), where in each round t, the agent selects an action at and the
205   environment generates a numerical reward rt (at ). The adversary observes the reward and returns
                                                                         PT
206   a corrupted reward r̄t . The corruption level C is defined by t=1 |rt (at ) − r̄t | ≤ C. Another

209   corruption models are equivalent. Moreover, adversarial feedback in our setting involves comparing

410   Adversarial Attack Methods. We study the performance of our algorithm using different adver-
411   sarial attack methods. We categorize the first two methods as “weak” primarily because the adver-
412   sary in these scenarios does not utilize information about the agent’s actions. In contrast, we classify
413   the latter two methods as “strong” attacks. In these cases, the adversary leverages a broader scope
414   of information, including knowledge of the actions selected by the agent and the true preference
415
      model. This enables it to devise more targeted adversarial methods.
416   • “Greedy Attack”: The adversary will flip the preference label for the first C rounds. After that, it
417     will not corrupt the result anymore.
418   • “Random Attack”: In each round, the adversary will flip the preference label with the probability
419     of 0 < p < 1, until the times of adversarial feedback reach C.
420   • “Adversarial Attack”: The adversary can have access to the true preference model. It will only
421     flip the preference label when it aligns with the preference model, i.e., the probability for the
422     preference model to make that decision is larger than 0.5, until the times of adversarial feedback
423     reach C.
424   • “Misleading Attack”: The adversary selects a suboptimal action. It will make sure this arm is
425     always the winner in the comparison until the times of adversarial feedback reach C. In this way,
426
        it will mislead the agent to believe this action is the optimal one.

      6.2    P ERFORMANCE C OMPARISON
465
466   We first introduce the algorithms studied in this section.
467   • MaxInP: Maximum Informative Pair by Saha (2021). It involves maintaining a standard MLE.
468     With the estimated model, it then identifies a set of promising arms possible to beat the rest. The
469     selection of arm pairs is then strategically designed to maximize the uncertainty in the difference
470     between the two arms within this promising set, referred to as “maximum informative”.
471   • CoLSTIM: The method by Bengs et al. (2022). It involves maintaining a standard MLE for
472     the estimated model. Based on this model, the first arm is selected as the one with the highest
473     estimated reward, implying it is the most likely to prevail over competitors. The second arm is
474     selected to be the first arm’s toughest competitor, with an added uncertainty bonus.
475   • MaxPairUCB: This algorithm was proposed in Di et al. (2023). It uses the regularized MLE to
476     estimate the parameter θ ∗ . Then it selects the actions based on a symmetric action selection rule,
477     i.e. the actions with the largest estimated reward plus some uncertainty bonus.
478   • RCDB: Algorithm 1 proposed in this paper. The key difference from the other algorithms is the
479     use of uncertainty weight in the calculation of MLE (4.4). The we use the same symmetric action
480
        selection rule as MaxPairUCB. Our experiment results show that the uncertainty weight is critical
        in the face of adversarial feedback.

      7     C ONCLUSION
522   In this paper, we focus on the contextual dueling bandit problem from adversarial feedback. We
523   introduce a novel algorithm, RCDB, which utilizes an uncertainty-weighted Maximum Likelihood
524   Estimator (MLE) approach. This algorithm not only achieves optimal theoretical results in scenarios
525   with and without adversarial feedback but also demonstrates superior performance with synthetic
526   data. For future direction, we aim to extend our uncertainty-weighted method to encompass more
527
      general settings involving preference-based data. A particularly promising future direction of our
      research lies in addressing adversarial feedback within the process of aligning large language models
528
      using Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF).
529
      Limitations and Future Works. We assume that the reward is linear with respect to some known
530
      feature maps. Although this setting is common in the literature, we observe that some recent works
531
      on dueling bandits can deal with nonlinear rewards (Li et al., 2024; Verma et al., 2024). Recently,
532   Verma et al. (2024) studied the problem of approximating reward models using neural networks,
533   addressing nonlinear rewards for dueling bandits. It is an interesting future direction to design
534   robust algorithms for nonlinear reward functions, such as with neural networks. Another assumption
535   concerns the lower bound of the derivative of the link function. Notably, in the logistic bandit model,
536   which shares similarities with our setting through Bernoulli variables,√some work (Abeille et al.,
537   2021; Faury et al., 2022) can improve the dependency of κ from 1/κ to κ. A similar improvement
538   might be achieved in our setting as well.

911   B.2   P ROOF OF T HEOREM 5.5
912   Proof of Theorem 5.5. Our proof adapts the argument in Bogunovic et al. (2021) to dueling bandits.
913   For any dimension d, we construct d instances, each with θi = ei , where ei is the i-th standard basis
914   vector. We set the action set A = {ei }di=1 . Therefore, in the i-th instance, the reward for the i-th
915   action will be 1. For the other actions, it will be 0. Therefore, the i-th action will be more preferable
916   to any other action. While for other pairs, the feedback is simply a random guess.
917   Consider an adversary that knows the exact instance. When the comparison involves the i-th action,
      it will corrupt the feedback with a random guess. Otherwise, it will not corrupt. In the i-th instance,


                                                                 17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      the adversary stops the adversarial attack only after C times of comparison involving the i-th action.
919   However, after Cd/4 rounds, at least d/2 actions have not been compared for C times. For the
920   instances corresponding to these actions, the agent learns no information and suffers from Ω(dC)
921   regret. This completes the proof of Theorem 5.5.
