011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The representation of human parts plays a crucial role in person re-identification
014            (re-ID) by offering discriminative cues, yet it presents challenges such as mis-
015            alignment, occlusion, and extreme illumination. Previous methods have primar-
016            ily focused on achieving strict part-level consistency. However, individual part
017            features change inevitably under harsh conditions, hindering consistent repre-
018
               sentation. In this article, we propose an Instance-level Consistent Graph (ICG)
               framework to address this issue, which extracts structural information by intro-
019
               ducing graph modeling atop unsupervised human parts. Firstly, we introduce
020
               an attention-based foreground separation to suppress non-instance noise. Sub-
021            sequently, an unsupervised clustering method is designed to segment pixel-wise
022            human parts within the foreground, enabling fine-grained part representations. We
023            propose a flexible structure graph that derives instance-level structure from part
024            features, treating each part feature as a node in a graph convolutional network. In
025            essence, ICG mitigates incompleteness through feature flow among nodes, broad-
026            ening the matching condition from strict part-level consistency to robust instance-
027            level consistency. Extensive experiments on three popular person re-ID datasets
028            demonstrate that ICG surpasses most state-of-the-art methods, exhibiting remark-
029
               able improvements over the baseline.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Person re-identification (re-ID) is the task of identifying individuals across different camera views,
035   playing a crucial role in intelligent surveillance systems and finding applications in public security,
036   transportation analysis, smart cities, and more. Recent advancements leveraging human parts ex-
037   traction (Zhang et al., 2019b; Zhu et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021b; Li et al., 2021; Miao et al.,
038   2022) have shown promise by utilizing discriminative part features. However, the detailed informa-
039   tion provided by human parts also presents challenges, particularly in terms of part misalignment.
040   Real-world re-ID scenarios often face issues like partial occlusion, pose variations, and extreme
041
      illumination (Zhang et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022), exacerbating misalignment due to limited infor-
      mation about human parts.

      To address misalignment, various methods have been proposed to extract more robust part features,
072
      which can be broadly categorized into three types: 1) Methods with auxiliary semantics (Su et al.,
073
      2017; Kalayeh et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2019b;a; Miao et al., 2022) introduce additional semantic
074   information such as skeleton pose, human parts segmentation, or bounding boxes, often obtained
075   through pre-trained detection or parsing models. These methods excel in capturing fine-grained
076   part features when precise part localization is achieved. 2) Methods employing local stripes par-
077   tition (Sun et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2021a; Yu et al., 2022b) images into horizontal sub-regions,
078   extract local features, and align them vertically. These methods leverage strong positional priors to
079   locate parts effectively, making them simple yet relatively effective in the early stages. 3) Methods
080   leveraging attention mechanisms (Li et al., 2018; Jin et al., 2022; Gong et al., 2022; Jiao et al., 2019)
081   use spatial attention to adjust features at different locations, implicitly aligning part features by em-
082
      phasizing the foreground and suppressing the background. Despite these efforts, achieving part-level
      consistency across all samples remains an ideal but stringent assumption, especially under challeng-
083
      ing conditions. Extracting identical features from incomplete information about human parts can
084
      lead to a semantic gap between samples of the same instance, as briefly illustrated in Fig. 1(a).
085
086   Graph convolution networks (GCNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2017) have garnered attention in the field
087   of person re-ID beyond human parts extraction. GCNs offer advantages in topology modeling and
088
      learning, making them a focal point in prior works (Shen et al., 2018; Li et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2022a)
      that utilize GCNs to associate multiple gallery images and probe images. Furthermore, GCNs are
089
      well-suited for modeling temporal relations, as evidenced by studies conducted by Bao et al (Bao
090
      et al., 2019) and Yang et al (Yang et al., 2020) in the context of video person re-ID. Recent advance-
091   ments (Wang et al., 2020a; Zhang et al., 2021b; Nguyen et al., 2021) have explored the application
092   of GCNs on local semantics to leverage the strengths of local information and topology modeling.
093   These methods incorporate pose estimation to form natural graphs of key points or construct graphs
094   of human parts through clustering or partitioning. Subsequently, they extract higher-level semantics
095   to enhance local features or improve retrieval performance. In summary, previous methods have
096   either relied on coarse-grained poses and stripes or attempted to enhance fine-grained human parts.
097   However, enhancing part features alone does not fully address the underlying issues, as the chal-
098   lenge often lies in intra-instance gaps rather than insufficient discriminative power of part features.
099
      A GCN-based method utilizing coarse-grained human parts is briefly illustrated in Fig. 1(b).
100   In this paper, we introduce the Instance-level Consistent Graph (ICG) framework, which leverages
101   the strengths of both fine-grained clustered human parts and graph modeling to explore robust struc-
102   tural features. The overall diagram of the ICG framework is illustrated in Fig. 2. ICG addresses
103   the challenges of part-based person re-ID by focusing on two key aspects: extracting aligned part
104   features and constructing a descriptor robust to misalignment.
105   For human parts extraction, we first employ spatial attention to separate the foreground from the
106   background, effectively suppressing non-instance noise. By applying a threshold to the normalized
107   feature map after attention, we obtain the foreground mask. Subsequently, we design an unsuper-

108
      vised clustering method to localize pixel-wise human parts, including personal belongings. The
109   K-means algorithm assigns pseudo-labels to each pixel within the foreground, clustering samples
110   of the same person to generate human parts masks. Following this, foreground features and part
111   features are obtained by masking the global feature extracted using a common backbone.
112
      Building upon the clustered human parts, we propose a flexible structure graph where each node
113
      represents the features of one part. We then utilize GCNs to construct a descriptor for the structure of
114
      human parts. The graph allows insufficient or misaligned part features to interact with other nodes,
115   enabling the flexible combination of features and consistent representation even with incomplete
116   part features. In essence, ICG establishes a tolerant graph with clustered part features, relaxing the
117   consistency assumption of re-ID from the part-level to the instance-level. The contributions of this
118   work are summarized as follows:
119
120          • We introduce an attention-based foreground mask and pixel-wise human parts clustering
121            to reduce misalignment. This approach suppresses non-instance noise and facilitates the
122            extraction of fine-grained part features without requiring additional supervision.
123          • We propose the Instance-level Consistent Graph (ICG), which captures instance-level con-
124            sistent structural information to tolerate part feature incompleteness. This is achieved by
125            constructing a flexible structure graph with nodes representing part features.
126
             • Extensive experiments validate the effectiveness of ICG, demonstrating superior perfor-
127
               mance compared to most state-of-the-art methods on three widely used person re-ID
128            datasets, and exhibiting remarkable improvements over the baseline.

131   2     M ETHODOLOGY
132
133   In this section, we provide a detailed overview of each module in the ICG framework. We begin
134   by introducing an attention-based foreground mask that effectively separates the foreground from
135   non-instance noise. Following this, we discuss the pixel-wise human parts clustering process, which
136   is conducted within the foreground mask to extract fine-grained part features. Additionally, we
137   describe the construction of a flexible structure graph using part features, enabling the learning of
138
      instance-level consistent structural information. Finally, we outline the loss function applied based
      on the proposed modules. The overall framework is depicted in Fig. 2.

299
300   3.1   DATASET AND IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS
301
302
      To demonstrate the effectiveness and generality of the proposed method, the network validates the
      performance on four widely used person re-identification datasets, Market-1501(Zheng et al., 2015),
303
      DukeMTMC-reID(Zheng et al., 2017), CUHK03-NPZhong et al. (2017) and MSMT17(Wei et al.,
304
      2018) datasets. Following the common practice of re-ID, methods use the Rank-1 cumulative match-
305   ing characteristic (CMC) and the mean average precision (mAP) to evaluate performance, The mAP
306   metric measures the combined accuracy of any ranked position.
307
308
      Experiments are implemented on PyTorch 1.2.0, using a single NVIDIA Tesla V100 to accelerate
      training. First, the input images are resized to 128×256×3 in data preprocessing, and the global fea-
309
      ture map Fg is 1/4 of the input size with 256 channels. Random cropping and random erasing (with
310
      a probability of 0.5) are applied as data augmentations to both the baseline method BoT(Luo et al.,
311   2019) and our method ICG. Second, the ResNet50 backbone network is used to extract features,
312   initialized from a pre-trained model on ImageNet. The Warmup policy is adopted for the first 10
313   epochs, in which the learning rate increases from 3.5×10−5 to 3.5×10−4 and then decreases back.
314   The learning rate is reduced by a factor of 0.1 during epochs 40−70. The model converges after 120
315   epochs. The batch size is set to 64, and the training is carried out using the Adam optimizer.
316
      When clustering the feature maps to generate pseudo-labels, we used k-means(Kummamuru &
317   Murty, 1999) as the clustering algorithm and re-clustered every n epochs, which is a trade-off be-
318   tween parameter updating and pseudo-label generation. For the actual training, n = 1 is simply set.
319   Instance-level clustering is applied in training only, and the inference time does not increase.

526   4     C ONCLUSION
527
528   To address the challenge of part misalignment caused by feature incompleteness in person re-ID,
529   this paper first reviewed existing methodologies focusing on distinct human parts and introduced the
530   concept of graph modeling. Recognizing the benefits of both approaches, we proposed the Instance-
531
      level Consistent Graph (ICG) framework, which integrates several key components. Initially, an
      attention-based foreground mask is applied to suppress background noise, followed by pixel-level
532
      human parts clustering within the foreground. This process enables the precise segmentation of
533
      fine-grained human parts. Subsequently, the constructed flexible structure graph employs nodes
534   representing part features, facilitating interaction between them. This graph-based approach ensures
535   the extraction of a robust structural feature that is insensitive to variations in node arrangements or
536   absence. Through extensive experiments conducted on three widely used person re-ID datasets, we
537   demonstrated the superiority of ICG over both baseline methods and state-of-the-art approaches.
538   The results highlight the effectiveness of ICG in relaxing the matching condition from part-level
539   to instance-level while maintaining distinctive part features, thereby advancing robust person re-ID
      methods.
