                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Deep neural networks are known to learn and rely on spurious correlations during
010
               training, preventing them from being reliable and able to solve highly complex
011            problems. While there exist many proposed solutions that overcome such reliance
012            in different, tailored settings, current understanding regarding the formation of
013            spurious correlations is limited. All proposed solutions with promising results
014            assume that networks trained with empirical risk minimization will learn spurious
015            correlations due to a preference for simpler features and that a solution to this
016            problem requires further processing on the networks’ learned representations or
017            re-training on a modified dataset where the proportion of training data with spurious
018            features is significantly lower. In this paper, we aim to form a better understanding
019
               regarding the formation of spurious correlations by performing a rigorous study
               regarding the role that data plays in the formation of spurious correlations. We
020
               show that in reasoning tasks with simple input samples, simply drawing more
021
               data from the same training distribution overcomes spurious correlations, even
022            though we maintain the proportion of samples with spurious features. In other
023            words, we find that if the network has enough data to encode the invariant function
024            appropriately, it no longer relies on spurious features, regardless of its strength.
025            We observe the same results in settings with more complex distributions with an
026            intractable number of participating features, such as vision and language. However,
027            we find that in such settings, drawing more samples from the training distribution
028            while maintaining proportion can exacerbate spurious correlations at times, due
029            to the introduction of new samples that are significantly different from samples in
030
               the original training set. Taking inspiration from reasoning tasks, we present an
               effective remedy to this problem to ensure that drawing more samples from the
031
               distribution always overcomes spurious correlations.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Deep neural networks tend to form correlations between weakly predictive, spurious features and tar-
037   get labels during training. In practice, these networks often prefer these correlations over correlations
038   formed between general, fully predictive invariant features and target labels. Thus, in the event of
039   a distribution shift, where such spurious correlations may no longer hold, these networks begin to
040   malfunction. Of the many factors that influence the degree of spurious feature reliance, the primary
041   factor commonly discussed in literature is that of predictive power. In other words, what proportion of
042   the training set contains the spurious feature and can, thus, enable the correct classification of during
043   training. Intuitively, the larger the proportion, the greater the reliance of the network on the spurious
044
      feature. Thus, to prevent the formation of spurious correlations during training, common practice
      in deep learning encourages sampling training data from many different training environments, to
045
      reduce the proportion of samples in the training set that contain the spurious features and overcome
046
      selection bias (Arjovsky et al., 2019). Such sampling, however, is expensive and not always feasible.
047
048   In this paper, we attempt to answer the following questions: What if one continued to sample more
049   data from the same training environments? In other words, what would happen if one were to maintain
050
      the proportion of the training data that contained the spurious feature but simply increased the amount
      of training data, drawn from the same distribution. What would be the impact on spurious feature
051
      reliance?
052
053   With answers to these questions, we describe the novel contributions and insights presented in this
      paper:

      Selection Bias does not form Spurious Correlations. Starving Networks of Sufficient Training
055   Data Does. Through our experiments, we find that, although we maintain the proportion of
056   samples that contain the spurious feature, simply drawing more data from the training distribution
057   can overcome a model’s reliance on spurious correlations and improve its robustness to distributional
058   shifts. We show that if the network has sufficient data to encode the general, invariant function
059   appropriately, it no longer learns and relies on spurious correlations present in the training data. Past
060   works simply state that since deep neural networks are biased toward simpler predictive features, they
061   are certain to learn and rely on spurious correlations. We refute this claim by showing that if the
062   network is provided sufficient data to encode the invariant function well, it will no longer rely on
063
      simpler, weakly predictive spurious features present in the data.
064   In Settings with Complex Distributions, Drawing More Samples from the Training Distribution
065   can Exacerbate Spurious Correlations. In vision and language settings, drawing more samples
066   from the training distribution can exacerbate spurious correlations. We find that this happens due
067   to the introduction of new samples which contain general features that are not well represented
068   in the original training set but also contain the spurious feature. If the general feature is not well
069
      represented and the network struggles to generalize to it, it forces the network to rely on spurious
      biases to minimize loss for that sample during training. We observe that in reasoning tasks, the
070
      input space is comprised of only a small number of objects, thereby making all samples, and by
071
      extension all general features, highly typical. Such typicality facilitates the easy reliance on general
072   features over spurious ones. Hence, we use this knowledge to provide the model with samples that
073   are generally well represented in the training distribution. This way, we overcome starvation and
074   spurious correlations without running the risk of exacerbation.
075
      Present novel insights regarding the formation of spurious correlations across three domains:
076
      reasoning, vision, and language. We provide comprehensive empirical evaluation across three
077
      domains that are commonly studied in deep learning and show that our claims and observations are
078   consistent across all three domains. That is, we show in all three domains that spurious correlations
079   are formed due to deep networks being starved of sufficient data to be able to appropriately encode the
080   general, invariant feature. Additionally, we utilize insights from reasoning tasks to better understand
081   the nature of spurious correlations in more complex settings: vision and language. This direction, to
082   the best of our knowledge, is novel.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
086

      Spurious Correlations. Deep neural networks have a tendency to learn and rely on simpler, spurious
      cues that can aid in learning only a portion of a task (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Sagawa et al., 2020a;b;
088
      Ahmed et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022a; Kirichenko et al., 2022). This makes them
089
      brittle to distributional shifts as the spurious features that are relied upon may disappear or become
090   correlated with a different task during testing. While there exists a large body of work that studies
091   spurious correlations, combating this problem remains an open problem. Most proposed techniques
092   that mitigate spurious correlations require domain knowledge, are effective only in specific settings or
093   negatively impact test accuracy. In this work, by studying reasoning tasks, we provide novel insights
094   fundamental to deep neural network training that can help solve the spurious correlations problem.
095
      Simplicity Bias Past work has shown that deep neural networks are biased towards simpler features,
096   where in the presence of two fully predictive features, a model will rely only on the simpler feature
097   and fully ignore the complex feature (Shah et al., 2020). Recent works have shown that even in
098   settings where the simpler feature is not fully predictive of the task, the model still relies strongly
099   on these features (Geirhos et al., 2020; Kirichenko et al., 2022). Such simplicity bias is considered
100   as the primary reason behind the formation of spurious correlations. In this work, we form a better
101   understanding regarding the role of simplicity bias in the formation of spurious correlations. We
102   show that if the network has enough data to learn the general feature appropriately, it no longer relies
103   on simpler, weakly predictive spurious features. Our observations disagree with the current notion
104
      which simply assumes that spurious correlations will always be formed in the presence of simpler,
      predictive, spurious features.
105
106   Reasoning in Deep Learning. There has been an increasing interest in deep neural networks
107   solving reasoning tasks that are either mathematical, visual, physical, or algorithmic in nature (Saxton
      et al., 2019; Bakhtin et al., 2019; Velickovic et al., 2022). Most existing works show that deep

      Note that we simply sample more data from the same distribution and thus, maintain the proportion

      We verify our claim by observing differences in confidence/margin when a network is trained on a
276
      dataset that encodes both the invariant and the spurious rule against one that is trained on a dataset

297
             • CelebA (Liu et al., 2015). We create a gender classification task using the CelebA dataset,
298
               where a small fraction of the Male samples contain eyeglasses, which is the spurious feature
299            in our setting. We estimate the degree of spurious feature reliance by measuring the number
300            of Female samples with eyeglasses that are misclassified during testing. The lower the
301            accuracy for Female samples with eyeglasses, the greater the degree of spurious feature
302            reliance.
303
             • MultiNLI (Williams et al., 2018). Inspired by experiments in Sagawa et al. (2020a), we
304            create a three-class classification task with the target labels - entailed by, neutral with, or
305            contradicts. In our experimental setting, the contradicts class contains a few samples with
306            negation words, which is the spurious feature in our setting. We estimate the degree of
307            spurious feature reliance by measuring the number of samples belonging to the neutral
308            with or entailed by classes that contain negation words that are misclassified during
309            testing. The lower the accuracy for these samples, the greater the degree of spurious feature
310            reliance.
311
312
      Note that our evaluation is consistent with current practice, where we estimate the degree of spurious
      feature reliance by measuring Worst-Group Accuracy (WGA), which computes the accuracy of test
313
      samples that contain the spurious feature associated with the other class during training. Additionally,
314
      consistent with all works that study spurious correlations, we perform hyperparameter tuning using a
315   validation split to optimize for worst group accuracy (Sagawa et al., 2020a;b; Liu et al., 2021; Zhang
316   et al., 2022a; Ahmed et al., 2021; Kirichenko et al., 2022).

      Through our experiments, we find that in both settings, simply drawing more samples from the same

360   R ANDOM S AMPLING CAN I NTRODUCE S AMPLES THAT C ONTRIBUTE S TRONGLY TO S PURI -
361   OUS F EATURE R ELIANCE IN C OMPLEX D ISTRIBUTIONS

363
      In the reasoning settings studied above, all tasks have training distributions where the input space is
      comprised of only a small number of features. In such a setting, it is unlikely that the network will
364
      encounter samples that are atypical or poorly represented in the training distribution. In other words,
365
      training samples are highly similar to each other and this remains true regardless of the number of
366   additional samples that we add to this training pool. This is in contrast to tasks in vision and language
367   that have more complex distributions with abundant representative data, comprised of general features
368   that exhibit high variance from sample to sample. Sub-sampling from these distributions may
369   introduce multiple samples with core, invariant features that are not well represented in the original
370   training dataset and are even harder for a network to understand. We observe that the maximum error
371   (||p(w, x) − y||2 ) a model attains on a sample during training in the reasoning tasks is almost the
372   same as the minimum error a model attains on a sample (3.1003e-05 and 1.4378e-05, respectively).
373   This is in sharp contrast to the CelebA setting studied, where the maximum error is far greater than
374
      the mininum error a model attains on a sample (2.047e-1 and 2.8412e-11, respectively).
375   In such settings with complex distributions, randomly sampling more data from the training distribu-
376   tion, as was effectively done in reasoning tasks, can introduce many samples that are difficult for a
377   network to understand, thereby forcing it to rely strongly on spurious biases present in these samples
      when minimizing training loss.

416   5.4             OVERCOMING S TARVATION IN C OMPLEX D ISTRIBUTIONS
417
418   To overcome starvation in settings with complex distributions, we aim to provide the network with
419   additional training data that contains general features that are well represented or typical in the
420   original training data.
421   To identify such samples in the training distribution, we first train the network on the entire (available)
422   training distribution for that task. We then compute the training error early in training. This method
423   of estimating which samples are well represented in the training distribution is inspired by Paul et al.
424   (2021) that assigns a similar score for each sample in a dataset to determine which samples one must
425   prune for efficient training. They claim that samples that have a lower error early during training are
426   redundant/typical. Alternatively, samples that have a higher error during training are atypical.
427   We follow a similar method of estimating if a sample is well represented in a training distribution.
428   Those samples that have a low error early in training are samples that are easy to learn and are typical
429   and well represented in the training distribution. We compute such scores after the 10th and 5th
430   epochs in the CelebA and MultiNLI settings, respectively. Note that in our experimental settings,
431   the spurious features exhibit significantly lower variances than their general counterparts and thus,
      loss during training is primarily determined by the core features. So for instance, in the MultiNLI

      6   C ONCLUSION
446
447
      Summary. We show that in practice, deep neural networks are often starved of invariant information,
      making them highly sensitive to spurious features present in training data. We show that in reasoning
448
      tasks, while maintaining the proportion of spurious samples of the original training distribution,
449
      simply drawing more samples from the training distribution can overcome spurious correlations. We
450   find that if the model has sufficient invariant information, the model does not rely on spurious features
451   even if the proportion of spurious information is maintained. Surprisingly, in tasks with more complex
452   distributions with abundant representative data, drawing more samples from the training distribution
453   exacerbates spurious correlations. We find that this happens due to the existence of samples that are
454   atypical or difficult for a network to generalize to, forcing them to rely on weakly predictive spurious
455   features present in these samples. Finally, we show that in such settings, if one carefully draws
456   samples with easier invariant features from the training distribution, one can overcome invariance
457   starvation and mitigate spurious correlations.
458   Impact.
459
460          • Deep neural networks do not always prefer simpler predictive features. Current
461            notion regarding the nature and formation of spurious correlations assumes that deep neural
462            networks trained using empirical risk minimization will always form and rely on spurious
463            correlations as deep neural networks are biased towards simpler features. Shah et al. (2020)
464
               show that in the presence of two fully predictive features, a model will choose to rely only on
               the simpler feature and completely ignore the complex feature. More recent works show that
465
               even if the simpler feature is not fully predictive of the task, a network will still rely strongly
466
               on these features and often ignore the general, invariant feature (Kirichenko et al., 2022).
467            As such, it is assumed that spurious correlations will always be learned in the presence of
468            simpler, partly predictive spurious features and that to overcome this problem, one must
469            alter the network’s biased representations or reduce the predictive power of the spurious
470            feature in the dataset.
471            Our paper is the first to show that models are not always biased to simpler features. We show
472            that if the network is provided with enough data to encode the general, invariant function
473            well, it will ignore the simpler, weakly predictive spurious features.
474          • Sampling from multiple different training environments is not necessary. Past works
475            state that to avoid the formation of spurious correlations during training, one must sample
476            from multiple different training environments or up-weight samples from environments that
477            do not contain strong spurious cues (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Sagawa et al., 2020a; Liu et al.,
478            2021). This ensures that the proportion of samples in the training dataset that contain strong
479            spurious cues is reduced. In this paper, we show that one can continue to draw from the
480            same training environments and maintain the same proportion of samples with spurious
481            features but still overcome spurious correlations by simply providing more data.
482          • More data can exacerbate spurious correlations and hurt generalizability. Current
483            notion in deep learning assumes that more data is almost always beneficial for generaliz-
484            ability. Through our experiments, we show that this is not always true as more data can
485            exacerbate spurious correlations and that one must be careful when using more data to train
               their models.
