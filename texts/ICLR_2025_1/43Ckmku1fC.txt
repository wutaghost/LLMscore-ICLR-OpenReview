009                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                           A BSTRACT
010
011           Sequential smoothing spline interpolators exhibit unstable behavior under low-
012           delay response requirements. That is, instability issues are observed when a
013           smoothing spline interpolator is forced to provide an interpolated trajectory piece
014           subject to processing only a few to no incoming data points at each time stamp.
015           Typically, the above instability setback is solved by increasing the delay, sacrific-
016           ing some degree of smoothness in the interpolated trajectory, or a combination of
017
              both. However, stable sequential smoothing spline interpolation strategies work-
              ing under low delay and without compromising their degree of smoothness seem
018
              vastly unexplored in the literature. To the best of our knowledge, this work formal-
019
              izes the internal instability and asserts the controllability of sequential smoothing
020           spline interpolators for the first time. Specifically, we model the trajectory as-
021           sembled by a smoothing spline interpolator as a discrete dynamical system of the
022           spline coefficients, facilitating the analysis of its internal instability and control-
023           lability. From these results, we propose a stabilizing strategy based on data point
024           forecasting capable of operating even under delayless regimes and without sacri-
025           ficing any smoothness of the interpolated trajectory. Our claims are theoretically
026           confirmed, or experimentally supported by extensive numerical results otherwise.

      reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) basis expansion (Wahba, 1990; Nosedal-Sanchez et al.,
055   2012) or as a minimal support basis expansion known as B-splines (Unser et al., 1993a;b), to name
056   a few. More specifically, spline interpolators are usually determined as minimizers of an adequate
057   measure of roughness (Wahba, 1978), e.g., the energy of the spline model, subject to interpolation
058   constraints, i.e., to pass through all observed data points. Alternatively, smoothing spline interpo-
059   lators are typically obtained as minimizers of a trade-off between the sum of squared residuals and
060   the roughness measure (Eilers & Marx, 2010). From this perspective, spline interpolators can be
061   viewed as a particular limit case of smoothing spline interpolators. Thus, the latter is often prefer-
062   able, especially for noisy data.
063
      Regardless of the chosen representation, the optimal computation of the smoothing spline interpo-
      lator requires the whole set of data points to be interpolated. This global character of smoothing
064
      spline interpolators can be problematic. Under some circumstances, e.g., while operating under a
065
      certain delay for streaming data, limited processing memory, or restricted computational capacity,
066   accessing the whole set of observed data points becomes infeasible, or even impossible. In such
067   cases, the corresponding smoothing spline interpolator cannot be determined in a straightaway man-
068   ner. In fact, instability issues may arise if one tries to naively interpolate the streaming data using
069   smoothing splines on the fly. That is, the sequentially interpolated trajectory may grow unbounded
070   even for bounded streaming data, as shown in Figure 1.
071
      Existing sequential smoothing spline interpolators for streaming data, trade-off some accuracy of
072   the solution, i.e., of the interpolated trajectory, for the ability to operate in an online manner. In
073   the literature, this is done by interpolating a serialized subset of adjacent data points using a win-
074   dowed or delay mechanism, e.g., (de Carvalho & Hanson, 1986; Yu et al., 2010; Ogniewski, 2019).
075   However, this delay mechanism poses an important drawback that must be taken into account for
076   some spline representations in low-delay regimes. Taking the RKHS representation as an example,
077   the overlapping between the (contributing) basis elements necessarily distorts the so-far interpolated
078   trajectory when adding a new term to the basis expansion. Even B-splines having minimal support
079   basis representation, suffer from this overlapping issue (Ruiz-Moreno et al., 2023b). Thus, a mini-
080
      mal delay is necessary and not all applications can afford it.
      On the contrary, the piecewise spline representation is not affected by this impediment, since the
081
      spline pieces do not overlap between themselves by design. Nonetheless, this representation choice
082
      leads to an unstable behavior if one aims at sequentially minimizing the optimization objective that
083   determines the interpolator while preserving the degree of smoothness in the overall interpolation.
084   In this case, some strategies gain robustness or even overcome the instability issue by relaxing the
085   optimization problem by just assuring a lower degree of smoothness. For instance, by using cu-
086   bic Hermite splines (De Boor & De Boor, 1978), or variants (Debski, 2020; 2021; Ruiz-Moreno
087   et al., 2023a). But again, some applications require a higher degree of smoothness, rendering these
088   strategies insufficient.
089   Finally, there seems to be a lack of literature explicitly discussing the presented instability issue,
090   either theoretically or practically.
091
092
      Contribution. To the best of our knowledge, this paper formally describes for the first time the
      source of instability of sequential smoothing spline interpolators. Based on this, it elaborates a
093
      sequential smoothing spline interpolation strategy able to achieve stability, even under demanding
094
      low-delay regimes. Specifically, we first (equivalently) characterize the solution of the smoothing
095   spline interpolation problem as the trajectory given by a discrete dynamical system of the spline co-
096   efficients. Then, we detect when such a dynamical system is internally unstable. This occurs for all
097   smoothing spline configurations except for the linear case. Lastly, we corroborate its controllability
098   and therefore our ability to stabilize the system.
099   The proposed strategy takes advantage of the stability of the (well-established) delay mechanism
100   through data point forecasting. In this way, the predicted data points act as a surrogate of the data
101   points that would have been observed after waiting for the corresponding delay. On the other hand,
102   the local adaptability of spline models tends to favor equally: nearsighted forecasting methods at the
103
      data trend level and farsighted forecasting techniques at the data fluctuation level. Because of this,
      the former can be used without a significant drop in performance. Our strategy is successfully tested
104
      for several time series forecasting models and sources of streaming data points, thus experimentally
105
      validating its effectiveness.

235
236   We can assert the internal stability of the action update mechanism (13) by studying how the system

262
263   By controllability, we refer to the ability of the action update mechanism (13) to reach, in a finite

      5   C ONCLUSION
492
      This work is meant as a first step towards stabilization strategies for the problem of sequential
493
      smoothing spline interpolation under few to no delay constraints. The proposed strategies for stable
494
      sequential smoothing spline interpolation are envisioned to be applied to streaming time-series data
495   points sampled from smooth processes, under possibly no delay constraints. However, the sequential
496   nature of the proposed strategies makes them equally useful for processing data sets that are too large
497   to fit in memory and are accessed sequentially.
498
      Limitations. Future work could be focused on proving formally Conjecture 1 that affirms that any
499
      bounded trajectory regardless of its degree of smoothness (determined by 2ρ − 2), can be controlled
500
      and thus, stabilized. Similarly, whether it is possible to delimit the effectiveness of the proposed
501   stabilization strategies theoretically remains an open problem.

615
616   This section is meant to expand on some important concepts and implications indirectly covered in

      the main document.
618
619   A.1    S AMPLE DELAY
620
      The term delay is rather vague and admits several interpretations. For example, in the context of this
621
      work, it could have been used to express the time difference between observed data points at any
622
      time step, e.g., xt − xt−1 , or the time needed by the interpolator to process the incoming data and
623   produce its outcome, i.e., the time needed to interpolate a piece of the trajectory.
624
625   In this work, the term delay is exclusively used to refer to the number of (incoming or accessed)
626
      samples needed to be processed before a trajectory piece is proposed. In this sense, a sequential
      smoothing interpolator operating under no delay has to provide, at every time step t, the interpolated
627
      trajectory piece between any two consecutive data points (xt−1 , yt−1 ) and (xt , yt ) without knowing
628
      any of the ensuing data points, i.e., ot+1 , . . . , oT .
629
630
      A.2    M ULTIVARIATE TRAJECTORY INTERPOLATION
631
632   A (multivariate) trajectory can be expressed as ψ(x) = [ψ1 (x), . . . , ψN (x)]⊤ , where every ψn (x)
633   describes a (univariate) curve along the nth dimension. In this work, we mainly refer to the index x
634   as time. However, it is worth noting that the trajectories we discuss here are not only limited to time
635   functions.
636
      When an N -variate trajectory ψ(x) is sampled at x1 , . . . , xT we obtain T data points, where every
637
      tth data point is of the form ot := (xt , ψ1 (xt ), . . . , ψN (xt )). Notice that (if N > 1), we can split
638                                                                n      oT             n      oT
                                                                      (1)                  (N )               (n)
639   the sequence {ot }Tt=1 into N sequences of the form ot                    , . . . , ot         , where ot :=

1035   C.4   VALIDATION OVER A MORE CHALLENGING ENVIRONMENT
1036
       In order to validate the proposed interpolation strategy against a more challenging environment we
1037
       have generated data from a (discretized) jump-diffusion process (Merton, 1976), i.e., a mixture of a
1038
       diffusion process with a jump process. This time-series data may suffer sudden changes (or jumps)
1039   at random instants, e.g., see Figure 9a, which may pose a big challenge for a sequential spline
1040   interpolator under no delay and smooth constraints.
1041
1042
       With this data, we have carried out similar (stability) experiments to the ones presented in Section
       4.3, and the results are summarized in Figure 10. They corroborate the intuitive idea presented in
1043
       the main document that capturing a trend of the streaming data can attain a stable reconstruction.
1044
       That is, predicting a trend (or behavior) of the process has a similar (importance) weight in the
1045   stability than accurately predicting data fluctuations but with the advantage that is usually a simpler
1046   procedure.
1047
1048      Note that this observation supports the use of simpler local prediction models in most en-
1049      vironments. Especially, since most data processes can be accurately modeled locally by a
1050      constant or linear model.
1051
1052   Lastly, we would like to emphasize that the robustness of our strategy is possible due to the ability
1053   of spline models to adapt and approximate to local trends like constants or lines (regardless of the
1054   process generating the data). In alignment with this idea, and as an additional argument, the cubic
1055   Hermite splines can be proven to be internally stable (by the same procedure presented in Section
1056
       3.1) and always end an interpolated piece with linear behavior, i.e., the second derivative is zero at
       the end of the interpolated piece. See Figure 8b as an example. However, and differently from our
1057
       strategy, this is done at the expense of sacrificing smoothness (it is only continuous up to the first
1058
       derivative) in the reconstructed trajectory.
