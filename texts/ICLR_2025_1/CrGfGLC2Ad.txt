009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011                Despite advancements in Large Language Model (LLM) alignment, understand-
012                ing the reasons behind LLM preferences remains crucial for bridging the gap be-
013                tween desired and actual behavior. LLMs often exhibit biases or tendencies that
014                diverge from human preferences, such as favoring certain writing styles or pro-
015                ducing overly verbose outputs. However, current methods for evaluating prefer-
016                ence alignment often lack explainability, relying on coarse-grained comparisons.
017                To address this, we introduce PROFILE (PRObing Factors of InfLuence for Ex-
018
                   plainability), a novel framework that uncovers and quantifies the influence of spe-
                   cific factors driving preferences. PROFILE’s factor level analysis explains the
019
                   “why” behind human-model alignment and misalignment, offering insights into
020
                   the direction of model improvement. We apply PROFILE to analyze human and
021                LLM preferences across three tasks: summarization, helpful response generation,
022                and document-based question-answering. Our factor level analysis reveals a sub-
023                stantial discrepancy between human and LLM preferences in generation tasks,
024                whereas LLMs show strong alignment with human preferences in evaluation tasks.
025                We demonstrate how leveraging factor level insights, including addressing mis-
026                aligned factors or exploiting the generation-evaluation gap, can improve align-
027                ment with human preferences. This work underscores the importance of explain-
028                able preference analysis and highlights PROFILE’s potential to provide valuable
029
                   training signals, driving further improvements in human-LLM alignment.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Large Language Models (LLMs) are widely recognized for their ability to generate human-level
      texts, yet they often fail to fully align with human preferences. Despite significant advancements in
035
      alignment techniques like Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al.,
036
      2022) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024), LLMs tend to exhibit biases
037   toward specific writing styles (Das et al., 2024) or generate overly verbose outputs (Park et al., 2024).
038   Understanding the underlying factors contributing to this misalignment is essential for enhancing
039   LLM performance.
040
      Previous attempts to understand and improve preference alignment (Ouyang et al., 2022; Rafailov
041
      et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024) have primarily relied on coarse-grained approaches, lacking explain-
042
      ability. These methods often focus on identifying which model is preferred overall but do not provide
043   insights into the factors that drive these preferences. While some studies analyze human preferences
044   at a finer granularity (Hu et al., 2023; Kirk et al., 2024; Scheurer et al., 2023), a comparative analysis
045   of how these preferences align with models remains limited. Furthermore, existing evaluation ap-
046   proaches often suffer from limited scalability and generalizability across diverse tasks and settings
047   due to their heavy reliance on human annotation (Chiang et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2023).
048
      To address these limitations in explainability and generalizability, we introduce PROFILE (PROb-
049   ing Factors of InfLuence for Explainability), a novel analytical framework designed to uncover and
050   quantify the key factors driving both human and model preferences. Our framework analyzes pair-
051   wise preference data to measure how specific factors manifest in preferred responses, enabling us
052   to rank the relative influence of different factors and compare these rankings between humans and

      2     P ROBLEM D EFINITION
109
110   To address our central question of how well LLMs align with human preferences, we acknowledge
111   the multifaceted nature of human preference where a perceived quality of response depends on
112   various factors. To uncover these latent preferences, we define a set of factors F = {f1 , f2 , . . . , fn },
113   which influence human preferences. Each fi represents a specific aspect of the text (e.g., fluency,
114   length). We then quantify their influence on human preference as factor-level preferences, denoted
115   by f (R).
116                                   f (R) = (f1 (R), f2 (R), . . . , fn (R)),
117
      where fi (R) represents the influence of each factor (fi ) on the overall preference across the response
118   set R. We refer to fi (R) as the factor score of factor fi . We extend this concept to include both
119   humans and models, referring to both as “agents.”
120
121   2.1   O PERATIONAL D EFINITIONS
122
123   We determine factor-level preferences f (R) by analyzing observable response-level preferences in
124   a pairwise comparison setting. This setting refers to a scenario where an agent compares two re-
125   sponses, e.g. ri and rj , and selects the more preferred one (either ri , rj , or a tie). The operational
126   definitions of the pairwise preferences required for this experiment are defined as follows.
127   Pairwise Preferences. We define the pairwise preference function for a pair of two responses as:
128
                                           P ref : R × R → {−1, 0, 1}
129
130
      where P ref (ri , rj ) = 1 if response ri is preferred over response rj ; P ref (ri , rj ) = −1 if response
      rj is preferred over response ri ; and P ref (ri , rj ) = 0 if the preference between ri and rj is a tie. In
131
      our experiments, we define model pairwise preferences for both generation and evaluation settings.
132
133   Model Pairwise Preferences in Generation. While models might not have preferences in the same
134   way humans do, we can operationally define the preferences of a model through the responses it
135   generates at different score levels. Specifically, if a model assigns scores of 3 and 5 to two responses,
136
      the response with a score of 5 is considered “preferred” by the model over the response with a score
      of 3. To implement this systematically, we prompt the model to generate responses corresponding to
137
      predefined scores ranging from 1 to 5, forming the set R = {rscore | score ∈ {1, 2, 3, 4, 5}}. Pairwise
138
      Preferences in Generation, P refgen , is defined by comparing the model-assigned scores Score(ri )
139   and Score(rj ). Specifically, P refgen (ri , rj ) = 1 if Score(ri ) > Score(rj ) and P refgen (ri , rj ) =
140   −1 if Score(ri ) < Score(rj ). This approach is inspired by methods used in constructing training
141   data for evaluator models (Kim et al., 2023).
142
      Model Pairwise Preferences in Evaluation. We define model preferences in an evaluation set-
143
      ting, similar to the general approach used to measure human preferences. Given two responses
144
      ri and rj , the model selects which is the better response. Pairwise Preferences in Evaluation,
145   P refeval (ri , rj ) = 1 if the model evaluates ri as preferable over rj ; P refeval (ri , rj ) = −1 if
146   rj is preferred over ri ; and P refeval (ri , rj ) = 0 if the model finds them equally preferable (tie).
147   This approach, where models make pairwise preference evaluation, is similar to how LLMs generate
148   preference labels (Lee et al., 2023). Although we extract model preferences separately for evaluation
149   and generation tasks, we assume that human preferences remain consistent across both, as human
150   judgments are always based on evaluating model-generated outputs.
151   Pairwise Factor Comparison. For each factor fk , we define a function Mk to compare factor’s
152   manifestation in pairs of responses:
153
                                              Mk : R × R → {−1, 0, 1}
154
155   Specifically, Mk (ri , rj ) = 1 if factor fk is more manifest in response ri ; Mk (ri , rj ) = −1 if factor
156
      fk is more manifest in response rj ; and Mk (ri , rj ) = 0 if factor fk is equally manifest in both
      responses. For example, if fk represents length and ri is longer than rj , Mlength (ri , rj ) = 1.

161   We introduce PROFILE, a novel method for automatically quantifying the influence of specific
      factors on both human and model preferences, revealing factor-level preferences. Building on the

168
      their alignment (§ 3.2). PROFILE’s versatility across various agents, tasks, and settings (generation

      We introduce a comprehensive taxonomy of fine-grained factor for evaluating preference alignment
189   between human and model in diverse set of text generation tasks. Addressing the lack of a unified
190   framework and inconsistent terminology in existing literature, we incorporate evaluation factors
191   from various tasks, including summarization, helpful response generation, question answering, and
192   instruction following (Zhong et al., 2022; Fabbri et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2024; Ye
193   et al., 2024; Glaese et al., 2022; Nakano et al., 2021).
194
      Our three-level taxonomy comprises: (i) Level 1: 13 distinct factors directly related to preference
195   alignment; (ii) Level 2: Groups of related Level 1 factors based on shared characteristics (e.g.,
196   Length and Fluency fall under “Linguistic Style”); and (iii) Level 3: Categories defined by the rela-
197   tionship each factor examines: Input-Output (relationship between input and output), Source-Output
198   (relationship between source text and output), Output-Only (characteristics of the output itself), and
199   Intra-Output (relationship among sentences within the output). Levels 1 and 2 are derived from ex-
200   isting studies, while Level 3 is designed to provide a structured perspective on factor relationships.
201   The complete taxonomy is detailed in Figure 2.
202   This hierarchical structure guides factor selection based on the task. For example, source-dependent
203   tasks (e.g., summarization) require factors from all three high-level categories, while input-driven
204   tasks (e.g., QA) focus on Input-Output and Intra-Output factors.

206   3.2      Q UANTIFICATION OF H UMAN -M ODEL P REFERENCE A LIGNMENT
207
208   This section outlines the process of quantifying factor-level preferences and measuring the align-
209   ment of these preferences between humans and the model. First, we calculate factor score fi (R)
210   by comparing the pairwise preference (P ref ) with the factor-specific pairwise comparison (Mk )
211   across the set of all possible response pairs in the dataset. These scores are then used to rank the
212
      factors, and the alignment between human and model preferences at the factor level is quantitatively
      evaluated based on these rankings.
213
214   Automatically Determining Factor Manifestation To analyze the manifestation of our factors in
215   model and human-preferred responses and determine Mk , we develop an automatic factor extrac-
      tion framework. We employ three approaches based on the objectivity of each factor: (i) Rule-based:


                                                                       4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      For straightforward, objective factors, we use deterministic algorithms. Length and Novel Words are
217   extracted this way. (ii) UniEval-based: For inherently subjective factors (Fluency and Coherence),
218   we use the well-established UniEval metric (Zhong et al., 2022). UniEval is a learned metric that
219   provides scores of range 0-1 for various aspects of text quality. (iii) LLM-based: For factors that
220   rely on objective criteria but require more nuanced judgment, we use GPT-4o with carefully de-
221   signed prompts. This approach is further divided into “response-based” (Intent Alignment and
222   Formality Alignment) and “atomic-fact-based” (the remaining seven) extraction depending on the
223   level of detail needed for each factor. By combining these three approaches, our framework captures
224   a wide range of factors with appropriate levels of objectivity. The specific details of the implemen-
225
      tation of each method and validation of LLM-based extractions can be found in Appendix D.
226   Quantifying Influence of Each Factor. To quantify the influence of each factor, i.e., factor score,
227   we use τ14 , a variation of Kendall’s correlation proposed by Deutsch et al. (2023). This metric is
228   well-suited for handling the distribution of ties, particularly in our setting, where ties arise in only
229   one of the comparison sets used for calculating Kendall’s τ . Below, we explain the specific ways
230   ties appear in our analysis.
231   Since our analysis relies on pairwise comparisons, we calculate τ14 for each factor fk using pairwise
232   concordance and discordance, following the methodology outlined by Bojar et al. (2017). The metric
233   is defined as:
234                                                     |Ck | − |Dk |

235                                                  |Ck | + |Dk | + |Tk |

237   tor fk agree, Dk is the count of discordant pairs, where the overall preference and the manifestation
238   of factor fk disagree, and Tk is the count of ties, are handled differently depending on the context.
239   Mathematically, Ck and Dk are computed as:

246   In our experimental setup, the definition of Tk depends on the specific setting. (1) In the generation
247   setting, no ties exist in response preferences because models do not generate responses with identical
248
      scores. Therefore, Tk is defined as the occurrence of ties at the factor level, which is calculated as
      the number of instances where Mk (ri , rj ) = 0. (2) In the evaluation setting, ties at the factor level
249
      (e.g., pairs with the same length) are removed to allow for a clearer analysis of the factor’s influence.

      In this case, Tk is the number of occurrences where (Pref(ri , rj ) = 0).

252   For instance, consider the factor Mlength , which measures response length. If response r1 is longer
253   than r2 (Mlength (r1 , r2 ) = 1) and the model prefers r1 (P ref (r1 , r2 ) = 1), this pair is classified
254
      as concordant. Conversely, if the model prefers the shorter r1 , the pair is discordant. Evaluating all
      pairs, a positive factor score indicates a positive influence of the factor, a negative score indicates a

256
      reflects the strength of this influence.

258   Evaluating Factor-Level Preference Alignment. An agent’s factor-level preferences are defined
259   as a ranking of factors based on their scores, where a higher rank and score indicate a stronger
260
      influence of that factor on the agent’s overall preference. The correlation between human and model
      rankings reflects their agreement on the relative importance of factors to overall preference, which
261
      we use as a measure of factor-level preference alignment between humans and models. We calculate
262
      Spearman’s ρ, Kendall’s τ * , and Pearson’s r coefficients to quantify this alignment.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTING
271
272   Tasks and Models. We analyze three publicly available datasets used in preference optimization
273   methods: (i) Reddit TL;DR (Stiennon et al., 2020), which includes human ratings of summaries
274   across multiple evaluation dimensions; (ii) StanfordHumanPreference-2 (SHP-2) (Ethayarajh et al.,
275   2022), focusing on human preferences over responses in the “reddit/askacademia” domain;
276   and (iii) OpenAI WebGPT (Nakano et al., 2021), which compares model-generated answers on the
277   ELI5 subreddit based on factual accuracy and usefulness. We refer to the tasks for each dataset
278
      as summarization, helpful response generation, and document-based QA tasks in this paper. We
      exclude pairs with human Tie ratings in all three datasets, as our analysis focuses on cases with clear
279
      preference distinctions. For our experiments, we utilize both open-source and proprietary LLMs.
280
      Open-source models include LLaMA 3.1 70B (Dubey et al., 2024), Mixtral 8x7B Instruct v0.1 (Jiang
281   et al., 2024), and three TÜLU v2.5 models (Ivison et al., 2024) (TÜLU v2.5 + PPO 13B (13B
282   RM), TÜLU v2.5 + PPO 13B (70B RM), and TÜLU v2.5 + DPO 13B). Proprietary models include
283   Gemini 1.5 Flash (Reid et al., 2024), GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024), and GPT-3.5. From here on, we refer
284   to Gemini 1.5 Flash as Gemini 1.5, Mixtral 8x7B Instruct v0.1 as Mixtral, TÜLU v2.5 models as
285   Tulu 2.5 + {alignment training strategy}. Detailed descriptions of the datasets and models can be
286   found in Appendix C.2.
287   Experimental Setup. For each task, we explore two settings: (i) Generation, where models generate
288   responses that would receive a score of 1-5 for a given task, and (ii) Evaluation, where models select
289   the better of two provided responses, which are taken from the datasets. See Appendix E for prompts.
290   In both settings, we use PROFILE to extract factor scores and their factor rankings and measure
291   the correlation with human judgments (factor-level preference alignment). In addition to factor-
292   level analysis, we assess overall pairwise response agreement between humans and models. For
293   evaluation, we report the percentage of models’ agreement with existing human labels by measuring
294   how often it aligns with human judges’ selections of the better response.

474   Alignment over Latent Preference. Our experiments reveal that under single-score human pref-
475   erence, the model can exhibit false positive optimization by producing overly lengthy outputs and
476   misleadingly exhibiting higher alignment scores, similar to Park et al. (2024); Skalse et al. (2022).
477   This is particularly problematic for downstream tasks like summarization, which require concise
478   responses with the original intention well preserved. PROFILE can be used to diagnose latent hu-
479   man preference misalignment and provide training signals to improve alignment at the factor level.
480   Similar to fine-grained RLHF (Wu et al., 2023), we can leverage factor-level scores to align the
481   LLM. Additionally, similar to LLMRefine (Xu et al., 2024), we can employ fine-grained guidance
482
      to harness the LLM’s self-refinement capability for further improvement.
483   Validation of Score-based Generation Approach. Our research deviates from the typical language
484   model setup by using a 1-5 scoring system for response generation. To assess the validity of our ap-
485   proach, we compare responses generated through direct generation (without scoring) with those
      across the score range through all summary, helpfulness, and document-based QA tasks. In every

      task, we found that score 5 consistently aligns best with direct generation responses, based on the
487   fine-grained factors we use, in models like GPT-4o, Tulu 2.5 + PPO (70B RM), and LLaMA 3.1
488   70B (see Table 10 in the Appendix H). This suggests that our scoring framework, specifically score
489   5, captures the essence of unconstrained language model outputs, implying the potential generaliz-
490   ability of our findings to general settings.
491
      Limitations. This study has several limitations. First, the preference datasets used may not fully
492
      represent the entire spectrum of human preferences. Second, due to budget constraints, human eval-
493   uations of model outputs were conducted on a limited scale, with a restricted number of participants,
494   and only on one task. Furthermore, this study represents a preliminary exploration into methods for
495   achieving better alignment, highlighting the potential of various techniques to enhance generation
496   and evaluation. Extensive studies are required to thoroughly assess the efficacy and generalizability
497   of these methods. While this study focuses on post-hoc correction methods, future research should
498   investigate how to incorporate the identified preference factors as signals during the training stage.
499   Additionally, exploring how to embed these signals within datasets used for preference optimization
500   represents a promising direction for future work.

502   6   R ELATED W ORK
503
504   Explainable Evaluation of LLMs. Recent research has increasingly emphasized the need for more
505   explainable evaluations of LLMs. For instance, researchers have proposed fine-grained atomic eval-
506   uation settings for tasks like fact verification and summarization (Min et al., 2023; Krishna et al.,
507   2023), developed a benchmark for fine-grained holistic evaluation of LLMs on long-form text (Ye
508   et al., 2024), and enhanced evaluation transparency through natural language feedback (Xu et al.,
509   2023). Building on this trend, our work shifts from evaluating individual factors in isolation to an-
510
      alyzing their influence on human preferences and investigating the alignment between human and
      model judgments regarding the relative importance of these factors. Furthermore, researchers are
511
      actively exploring the potential of LLMs as evaluators. Fu et al. (2024); Madaan et al. (2024); Liu
512
      et al. (2023) demonstrate the capacity of large models like GPT-4 to achieve human-like system-level
513   evaluation. However, recent works (West et al., 2023; Oh et al., 2024) reveal discrepancies in model
514   performance between generation and evaluation tasks. Inspired by frameworks to meta-evaluate llm
515   as an evaluator (Zheng et al., 2023; Ribeiro et al., 2020), our work evaluates not only the quality of
516   model-generated text but also the alignment of model preferences in evaluation settings, providing
517   a more comprehensive assessment of LLM capabilities.
518
      Human-AI Preference Alignment. Aligning large language models (LLMs) with human prefer-
519   ences is a central focus in LLM research, leading to techniques like supervised instruction tun-
520   ing (Mishra et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2021), RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022), DPO (Guo et al., 2024),
521   and RLAIF, which utilizes AI-generated feedback (Bai et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023). However, most
522   studies focus on overall performance (e.g., a response as a whole). While some work has explored
523   using fine-grained human feedback (Dong et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024), a comprehensive under-
524   standing of how granular factors contribute to and differentiate human and model preferences is still
525   lacking. Hu et al. (2023) take a step in addressing this gap by probing the factors influencing human
526   preferences. Building on this work, we expand the investigation of granular preference alignment
527
      across multiple tasks and extend the analysis to model generation, providing a comparative analysis
      of the factors driving both human and model preferences.
528
529
530   7   C ONCLUSION
531
532   We introduce PROFILE, a novel framework for granular factor level analysis of LLM alignment with
533   human preferences. Our analysis using PROFILE reveals that LLMs tend to over-prioritize factors
534   like output length, misaligning human preferences during generation. However, these models exhibit
535   stronger alignment in evaluation tasks, indicating the potential for leveraging evaluative insights to
536
      improve generative alignment. By advancing beyond coarse-grained methods, PROFILE facilitates
      a nuanced understanding of the alignment gaps and mismatches between human and model prefer-
537
      ences. These insights underscore the necessity for more sophisticated, factor-level alignment strate-
538
      gies that can guide the development of LLMs to better align with human expectations, ultimately
539   fostering more reliable aligned AI systems.

760
      We collect human preference data via Amazon Mechanical Turk (MTurk) for 30 posts and 6 models.
761
      For each post, three summary pairs—selected from five model-generated summaries (scored 1 to
762   5)—are presented to three annotators. Annotators, restricted to US-based workers with a 95% and
763   HIT approval rate and over 5,000 approved HITs, follow these instructions: “A good summary is a
764   shorter piece of text that captures the essence of the original. It aims to accomplish the same purpose
765   and convey the same key information as the original post. Please choose the better summary, A or
766   B.” Each annotation is compensated at $0.25. This process yields 1,620 annotations (30 posts * 6
767   models * 3 pairs/model/post * 3 annotations/pair).
768   For each model and corresponding summary pair, we calculate an agreement rate, which indicates
769   the percentage of pairs where at least two out of three annotators prefer the summary with the higher
770   score assigned by the model. Each model is evaluated on 90 summary pairs, derived from 30 posts
771   with three pairs per post.

      We examine three publicly available datasets of pairwise human judgments commonly used in pref-
957
      erence optimization methods like RLHF and DPO training: Reddit TL;DR We analyze the dataset
958   released by OpenAI (Stiennon et al., 2020), which includes human ratings of summaries across mul-
959   tiple axes (referred to as “axis evaluations”). Higher scores indicate human preference across
960   multiple evaluation dimensions. StanfordHumanPreference-2 (SHP-2) (Ethayarajh et al., 2022),
961   focuses on capturing human preferences over responses to questions and instructions, prioritizing
962   helpfulness. Higher scores indicate a more helpful response. For this study, we use responses from
963   the “reddit/askacademia” domain. OpenAI WebGPT This dataset (Nakano et al., 2021),
964   addresses the task of generating answers to questions from the ELI5 (“Explain Like I’m Five”) sub-
965   reddit. Human annotations compare two model-generated answers based on factual accuracy and
966
      overall usefulness. We exclude pairs with Tie ratings in all three datasets, as our analysis focuses on
      cases with clear preference distinctions.

       Rule-based Extraction We obtain the Length and Novel Words using a rule-based extraction
983
       method. First, we calculate the output’s length and count the novel words by removing special char-
984    acters and splitting the text into words. The total word count represents Length. For Novel Words,
985    we stem both the source text and the model output to create unique sets of stemmed words, then
986    determine the number and proportion of unique words in the output that differ from the source.
987
988    LLM-based Extraction The calculations are divided into atomic-fact-level and response-level
989    based on the granularity of the factors.
990
991
       Atomic-Fact-Level Factors refer to those factors that are evaluated based on the presence or absence
       of each factor at the atomic fact level. An atomic fact is a short, self-contained piece of information
992
       that does not require further explanation and cannot be broken down further (Min et al., 2023).
993
       These include the Number Of Facts, Source Coverage, Off Focus, Hallucination, Helpfulness, and
994    Misinformation. The Number Of Facts is determined by counting the total atomic facts, while the
995    remaining factors are calculated as the ratio of relevant atomic facts to the total number of atomic
996    facts.
997
       Response-Level Factors refer to those factors that are evaluated based on the presence or ab-
998
       sence of each factor at the response level. These include Receptiveness, Intent Alignment,
999
       and Formality Alignment. Formality Alignment is classified into one of three categories:
1000   [Aligned/Misaligned/Partially-Aligned], while the other two factors are determined in a binary man-
1001   ner [Yes/No].

1391   lating cosine similarity. The results in Table 10 indicate that the model’s direct responses are most
1392   similar to those with a score of 5, all showing a high similarity of over 0.85. Overall, as the scores
1393   decrease, the similarity also declines.
1394   This finding suggests that the model’s direct responses align closely with its best-generated re-
