009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011              Information in speech can be divided into two categories: “what is being said”
012              (content) and “how it is expressed” (other). Current state-of-the-art (SOTA) tech-
013              niques model speech at fixed segments, usually 10-25 ms, using a single embed-
014              ding. Given the orthogonal nature of other and content information, attempting
015              to optimize both within a single embedding results in suboptimal solutions. This
016              approach divides the model’s capacity, limiting its ability to build complex hi-
017
                 erarchical features effectively. In this work, we present an end-to-end speech
                 representation learning framework designed to jointly optimize the “other” and
018
                 “content” information (JOOCI) in speech. By using separate learnable parame-
019
                 ters, JOOCI addresses this optimization challenge by modeling other and content
020              information independently. Our results show that JOOCI consistently outperforms
021              other SOTA models of similar size (100 million parameters) and pre-training data
022              used (960 hours) by a significant margin when evaluated on a range of speech
023              downstream tasks in the SUPERB benchmark, as shown in Table 1. Code and
024              models are available at TBA.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
028
      Self-supervised learning (SSL) has played a significant role in learning high-level representations of
029
      text (Brown et al., 2020), vision (Alexey, 2020), and audio (Baevski et al., 2020; Mohamed et al.,
030
      2022; Défossez et al., 2022) data. In this work, we focus on learning high-level representations
031   from raw speech. These learned representations are used as input features for various downstream
032   tasks that require effective modeling of the “content” (what is being said) and “other” information
033   (closely related to how it is expressed) present in speech (Yang et al., 2024; Borsos et al., 2023; Wang
034   et al., 2023). The SUPERB (Yang et al., 2021) benchmark is designed to evaluate the generalization
035   capabilities of different methods by evaluating the learned representations on a range of downstream
036   tasks such as automatic speech recognition (ASR), phoneme recognition (PR), speaker identification
037   (SID), emotion recognition (ER), and voice conversion (VC). ASR and PR performance depends on
038   effective modeling of content information, while SID and ER rely on effective modeling of other
039
      information. The VC task requires the model to not only learn both information but also disentangle
      them.
040
041   Current state-of-the-art SSL methods, such as WavLM (Chen et al., 2022), on the SUPERB bench-
042   mark share two key similarities. First, these methods first downsample and embed raw speech into
043   fixed segments, typically 25 ms, using a CNN encoder followed by a powerful transformer encoder
      1
044     to learn contextual embeddings for each segment. Second, masked prediction loss (MPL) (Hsu
045
      et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022) is used during pre-training.
046   However, two key limitations exist in current SOTA methods:
047
048            • Firstly, using a single embedding to learn other and content information results in sub-
049
                 optimal representations for both, due to the orthogonal nature of content and other infor-
                 mation, which makes it difficult for the optimization algorithm to jointly learn both within
050
                 a single embedding 2 .
051

052       Transformer encoder and model are used interchangeably.
          2
053       Perfect features for an ASR system should only encode content information i.e., what is being said and
      forget about everything other i.e., how it is expressed.

077
               content information learned during pre-training, which inadvertently minimizes the other
               information, particularly speaker-related information. This indicates that MPL alone is
078
               not enough for learning other information. Additionally, (Feng et al., 2022) demonstrated
079
               that models pre-trained with MPL encode speaker (other) information in the silent parts of
080            audio, further corroborating this issue.
081
082   For example, WavLM uses data augmentation for learning other (speaker) information. The authors
083   observed that the model divides its total modelling capacity/layers in two, with later layers learn-
084   ing content and earlier layers learning other information. This ultimately prevents the model from
085   fully leveraging all layers to build the complex, hierarchical representations characteristic of deep
086   learning.
087   Furthermore, when a method maximizes content information to the extreme to enhance the perfor-
088   mance of ASR and PR tasks, such as MS-HuBERT (Yadav et al., 2024), the strategy of dividing
089   layers to encode content and other information, as used in WavLM, becomes ineffective. There-
090   fore, there is a need for a better framework capable of jointly learning other and content information
091   in speech by utilizing all layers to build complex, hierarchical representations. In this paper, we
092   present a framework to jointly optimize the “other” and “content” information (JOOCI). JOOCI
093
      models other and content information using separate learnable parameters and distinct loss func-
      tions, differentiating it from the previous SOTA method, WavLM (Chen et al., 2022), for learning
094
      comprehensive speech representations.
095
096   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
097
            1. We present JOOCI, a framework designed to jointly learn other and content information
098
               using separate learnable parameters and distinct loss functions. We achieve SOTA perfor-
099
               mance on the SUPERB benchmark, as shown in Table 1.
100
101
            2. Adapters are one way to adapt the behavior of the pre-trained model (Chen et al., 2023b;c)
               for new tasks. In Section 3.2, we discuss in detail how JOOCI differentiates itself from
102
               adapters and why it is a more efficient choice.

      2.1      C OMPONENTS
109
110   Shared Encoder (ES ) : The shared encoder serves a dual purpose i.e., it downsamples the raw
111   speech and provides an embedding layer for both the content and other encoders. Inspired by pre-
112   vious work (Baevski et al., 2020; Hsu et al., 2021), we use seven blocks of temporal convolution
113   layers which results in a down-sampling factor of 320x 3 . For more details of shared encoder please
114   refer to the original work (Hsu et al., 2021; Yadav et al., 2024) that we have followed.
115   Content Encoder (EC ) : The content encoder models “what is being said”, i.e., the content present
116   in speech. Following prior studies (Hsu et al., 2021; Yadav et al., 2024), the output from the shared
117   encoder is randomly masked and passed through a series of n self-attention based transformer en-
118   coder layers to model the content information. For more details related to the content encoder please
119   refer to the original work (Yadav et al., 2024) that we follow.
120   Other Encoder (EO ) : The other encoder models non-linguistic information present in speech.
121   First, the shared encoder output is further downsampled using 1D average pooling layer with a kernel
122   size and stride set to DF, where DF is the downsampling factor. The updated downsampled output is
123   then passed through n other encoder blocks, each of which consists of the following transformations:
124
125
               • 1D Res2Net block (Gao et al., 2019) with a kernel size, stride and dilation all equal to 1.
                 To increase the overall non-linearity of the module.
126
127            • A split and append layer.
128            • A depthwise 1D CNN layer 4 with a kernel size, stride and dilation equal to DF+1, DF+1,
129              and 1 respectively.
130            • Another 1D Res2Net block with a kernel size, stride and dilation equal to 3, 1, and 4
131              respectively.
132            • A residual connection, followed by a 1D batch normalization (BN) layer
133
134   The split and append layer enables information from the content encoder to flow into the other
135   encoder during the forward pass. No gradients flow from other encoder to content encoder during
136   the backward pass. Therefore the other encoder can extract useful information, if any, from the
137
      powerful transformer content encoder. Specifically, content encoder embeddings are split into m
      groups of size DF, and one embedding from the other encoder is appended at the end of each group
138
      sequentially. Finally, all groups are concatenated, increasing the total length, as shown in Figure 1.
139
      To reduce the input back to its original length, a depthwise CNN layer is applied with a kernel size
140   and stride equal to DF+1.
141
142   Post Network (PN) : The post network performs three main functions crucial for training the other
143
      encoder using the student-teacher framework. 512D embeddings are extracted from the RDINO
      (Chen et al., 2023a) model acting as a teacher, for each utterance.
144
145            • An attentive statistical pooling (ASP) layer, similar to (Okabe et al., 2018), which aggre-
146              gates variable-length inputs.
147            • A 1D BN layer.
148
               • A fully connected (FC) layer with an output dimension of 512. Used only during the pre-
149
                 training stage.
150
151   Content Decoder : Similar to (Ao et al., 2022), we train a single layer decoder using pseudo targets
152   as ground truth. A gradient reversal layer (GRL) is applied before passing the gradients to the other
153   encoder during back-propagation. The role of content decoder is to discourage the other encoder
154   from learning features necessary for solving tasks that require content information. The content
155   decoder and the GRL is used during the pre-training stage only.

238   3.1     E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
239
240
241   Pre-training Dataset : We use the LibriSpeech 960-hour dataset (Panayotov et al., 2015), excluding
242
      transcriptions, for pre-training JOOCI consistent with other studies focusing on speech representa-
      tion learning (Schneider et al., 2019; Chung et al., 2021; Hsu et al., 2021).
243
244   Data Augmentation: Given that (i) LibriSpeech is a read speech dataset recorded in clean environ-
245   ment free from real world noises and (ii) the amount of dataset is under 1000 hours. We augment the
246   data very lightly, so not to interfere with the content encoder a lot, which would result in the model
247   dividing its capacity simliar to WavLM (Chen et al., 2022). We augment only 12.5% of the audio
248
      samples using the MUSAN corpus (Snyder et al., 2015) with a high signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) in
      the ranges of [5, 15] for noise, [13, 20] for speech, and [5, 15] for music. For comparison, WavLM
249
      augments 50% of the audio with a SNR value in between [-5, 5]. Lastly, we also add Room Impulse
250
      Response (RIR) for reverberation to the selected audios similar to RDINO (Chen et al., 2023a).
251
252   Pre-training : We use 12 layers for the other and content encoder. For the decoder, we use one
253   transformer decoder layer with 8 heads and 768 dimension. It is trained with a vocabulary size of
254
      1005. Other encoder is trained using the student-teacher framework. Content encoder is trained
      exactly to MS-HuBERT (Yadav et al., 2024). A detailed description is provided in the Appendix.
255
256   Shared Encoder and Content Encoder Initialization : The shared encoder and content encoder
257   of JOOCI are initialized with the pre-trained MS-HuBERT model weights (Yadav et al., 2024).
258   This choice is based on MS-HuBERT’s superior performance in modeling content information us-
259   ing the Multicluster Masked Prediction Loss (MMPL), which has been shown to achieve SOTA
260
      performance on content-based tasks.
261   SUPERB : To comprehensively analyze both the content and other types of information learned by
262   JOOCI, we evaluated several tasks from the SUPERB (Yang et al., 2021) benchmark and compared
263   our method with other SOTA methods of similar model size. The SUPERB benchmark is designed
264   to compare models on their ability to learn comprehensive audio characteristics across a variety of
265   downstream tasks including speaker recognition, content analysis, semantics, para-linguistics, and
266
      generation. For more details, see Yang et al. (2021). In short, SUPERB freezes the encoder and
      learns a weighted sum of all the layers to produce features for different downstream tasks.
267
268   Fine-tuning : No fine-tuning was performed. After pre-training, we discarded the content decoder
269   and the fully connected (FC) layer from the post-network (PN) module and froze the parameters of
      JOOCI for all evaluation purposes in this work.

      Evaluating JOOCI on the SUPERB Benchmark : Table 1 summarizes the performance of JOOCI
281   on a range of downstream tasks from SUPERB benchmark. The results clearly indicate that JOOCI
282   outperforms the current state-of-the-art (SOTA) models on the majority of tasks, except few where
283   the margin is very less 7 . In particular, JOOCI maximizes the performance on speaker-related tasks
284   such as speaker identification (SID) and speaker verification (ASV), and exhibits the lowest er-
285   ror rates in content-related tasks like phoneme recognition (PR) and automatic speech recognition
286   (ASR). Overall, JOOCI’s performance on the SUPERB benchmark reinforces our initial claim of it
287   being a comprehensive framework for learning speech representations.
288   Comparison with Adapters : Instead of fine-tuning pre-trained models for every new downstream
289   task, adapters (Chen et al., 2023b;c) offer a lightweight solution to adapt these models for new tasks.
290   Adapters introduce a small number of additional parameters tailored for each specific task. Table
291   2 compares JOOCI with HuBERT fine-tuned using various adapters. A key limitation of adapters,
292   however, is that for every new task, it requires a new forward pass through the pre-trained model.
293   JOOCI stands out by overcoming this limitation, as both the other and content embeddings are
294   extracted in parallel in a single forward pass. With (i) comparable performance to adapters and (ii)
295
      the advantage of requiring only one forward pass, JOOCI stands as a more efficient framework for
      learning comprehensive speech representations. Lastly, Adapters can be applied to JOOCI also.

354
355   The expectation is that JOOCI’s other encoder SHOULD build complex hierarchical features, with
356   later layers being more important for solving tasks such as speaker recognition 8 . Table 3 shows
357   the accuracy for the SID task across different configurations corroborating our claim for JOOCI
358   leveraging all the layers to build hierarchical features for other information also. Surprisingly, When
359   using a weighted sum of the other encoder’s all the layers, the layers towards end (6-9) are assigned
360   higher weight and not the last layer. To investigate this further, we use only the last six layers,
361   without any performance drop, yet the same trend persisted. Next, we tried using only the last three
362
      layers, without any performance decline, which again showed the same pattern. Finally, using only
      the last layer results gives the best result. We hypothesize that this could be because of the softmax
363
      property used in the SUPERB benchmark for assigning weights to all the layers, which gives a uni-
364
      modal distribution. Using a setup similar to multi-head attention might give a clearer picture. We
365   leave this for future work.
366
367   Next, applying an ASP layer followed by Batch Normalization (BN) layer to the output of the last
368
      layer boosts the performance even further. On the other hand, using the last fully connected (FC)
      layer resulted in a performance drop of approximately 2%. This performance decline when using
369
      the last layer, closest to the loss function, has been observed in vision model trained using SSL,
370
      where it is often recommended to drop the last layer after pre-training (Chen et al., 2020).
371
372   Based on these findings, we conclude that the model effectively constructs complex, high-level
373   features by utilizing all layers for tasks requiring other (speaker) information. And in turn, JOOCI
374
      is able to jointly learn other and content information utilizing all the layers.

      JOOCI consists of three major components: (i) content encoder (C), (ii) other encoder (O), and
      (iii) content decoder with GRL (DGRL). We explore different combinations of these components
404
      across four downstream tasks, which tests effective modeling of both content and other information.
405
      Table 5 presents our ablation results. First, we pre-train JOOCI by training only the other encoder
406   (O) and keeping the shared and content encoder frozen, either with or without the DGRL module.
407   Incorporating the DGRL module significantly boosts ER performance, although it slightly degrades
408   SID performance. Overall, the inclusion of DGRL increases robustness, and thus we employ the
409   DGRL in all subsequent JOOCI experiments.
410
      Next, we study the effect of data augmentation during pre-training, when all parameters of JOOCI
411
      are trained. Data augmentation results in substantial performance gains on the SID and ER tasks.
412   These improvements are likely due to the characteristics of the LibriSpeech pre-training dataset,
413   consisting of read speech with minimal background noise. For the PR and ASR task, data augmen-
414   tation leads to very minimal degradation, which is negligible when considering the significant gains
415   on other tasks. Our hypothesis is that with larger and more diverse datasets, the dependence on data
416   augmentation will diminish.

420   4.3   D ISENTANGLEMENT PROPERTY OF JOOCI
421
422   Disentanglement is the ability of the model to represent multiple factors of variation in data as sepa-
423   rate and independent latent variables or embeddings. These embeddings encode different aspects of
424   the data without influencing each other.
425
      Solving the voice conversion from any to any (VC-a2a) speaker requires effective disentanglement
426   of the current speaker from the content information in speech. Our findings indicate that either using
427   one layer or the layers with high CCA similarity results in improved performance compared to using
428   all the layers, as demonstrated in Table 6. This shows that JOOCI is able to disentangle content from
429   the other information in the layers with high CCA scores. Furthermore, Table 5 shows that the other
430   encoder also is not encoding content information as the performance on the PR task is 100%. Both
431   these observations prove that JOOCI is able to disentangle content and other information success-
      fully.

      5   C ONCLUSION
476
477
      This paper presents JOOCI, a novel framework for learning comprehensive speech representations
478
      by jointly optimizing other and content information. JOOCI addresses the limitations of existing
479
      methods that struggle to encode both types of information effectively. By employing separate en-
480   coders and distinct training criteria for other and content information, JOOCI achieves state-of-the-
481   art performance on the SUPERB benchmark, outperforming existing models across a range of down-
482   stream speech tasks, including speaker identification, speaker verification, phoneme recognition, and
483   automatic speech recognition. The efficiency of JOOCI is also emphasized, as it extracts both other
484   and content embeddings in a single forward pass, in contrast to adapter-based methods that require
485   multiple forward passes. The ablation study highlights the significance of the DGRL module in
      enhancing the robustness and performance of JOOCI, particularly for the emotion recognition task.

503   6   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE WORK
504
505   Speaker Diarization (SD) Task : The SD task involves determining who spoke when in an audio
506   recording. It requires the system to segment the audio and assign speaker labels to each segment, ef-
507   fectively separating multiple speakers within a conversation or audio stream. Unexpectedly, despite
508   the other encoder’s strong performance on tasks like SID and ASV, it falters in the SD task compared
509
      to the content encoder. The results are shown in Table 7. The degradation maybe because of using
      the one embedding, during pre-training other encoder, for the entire utterance. On the other hand,
510
      data augmentation proves to be highly effective for learning fine-grained, discriminative features as
511
      shown by better performance from the content encoder.
512
513   Better Pre-training Methodology : (i) Employing a different pre-training methodology to learn
514   fine-grained, discriminative features for the other encoder, such as using neural codec latent variables
515
      as training targets (Défossez et al., 2022). (ii) To improve content modeling, utilizing the content
      decoder to also train the content encoder.
516
517   Multi-Talker ASR : JOOCI is well-suited for multi-talker ASR without requiring any external
518   modules. It can be easily adapted by (i) using other embeddings to classify similar speakers using
519   unsupervised clustering, and (ii) applying the resulting classification as a mask to segment content
520   embeddings, which can then be used for the ASR task.
