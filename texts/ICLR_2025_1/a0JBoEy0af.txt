011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Deformable image registration is a crucial task in medical image analysis, and its
014            complexity has spurred significant research and ongoing progress. Much of the
015            work in this area has concentrated on achieving incremental performance gains by
016
               adjusting network architectures or introducing new loss functions. However, these
               modifications are often tailored to specific tasks or datasets, which limits their
017
               general applicability. To address this limitation, we propose an innovative solu-
018
               tion: a plug-in curriculum scheduler that can be seamlessly integrated into existing
019            methods without changing their core architecture. Our scheduler, inspired by cur-
020            riculum learning, progressively increases task difficulty to enhance performance,
021            incorporating sample difficulty and matching accuracy as key criteria. Sample
022            difficulty is assessed at voxel and volume levels, using Variance of Gradients for
023            voxel complexity and Gaussian blurring for volume evaluation, while matching
024            accuracy involves gradually increasing supervision for improved alignment and
025            accuracy. We empirically demonstrate that this scheduler achieves superior accu-
026            racy and visual quality in various tasks and datasets.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Deformable medical image registration is vital for medical image analysis because it allows precise
032   alignment of images from various times or modalities. This accuracy is key for identifying changes,
033
      planning treatments, and combining data from different imaging sources. The field’s complexity
      and its significant role in diagnosis, treatment, and personalized care have led to extensive research
034
      and ongoing advancements over the years. However, because of the difficulties in accurately repre-
035
      senting deformation fields, research in deformable medical image registration has primarily focused
036   on incremental performance improvements. These improvements often involve minor changes to
037   network architectures, the integration of hierarchical or iterative processes, or the addition of loss
038   functions. In most cases, these methods follow a common structure: a network inputs two images
039   and generates a deformation field to align them. The loss function generally consists of an image
040   similarity measure combined with a regularization term for the deformation field.
041
      Recent studies continue to follow this similar flow. For example, H-ViT Ghahremani et al. (2024)
042   introduces a top-down approach for estimating deformation fields by capturing multi-scale short-
043   and long-range flow features, utilizing dual self- and cross-attention to enhance low-level features
044   with high-level representations. CorrMLP Meng et al. (2024) presents the first correlation-aware
045   MLP-based network for deformable medical image registration, improving efficiency and capturing
046   long-range dependencies at full resolution without using self-attention. IIRP-Net Ma et al. (2024)
047   develops a pyramid registration network that integrates a feature extractor with residual flow esti-
048   mators to improve the generalization of feature extraction and registration. Despite the difference in
049   architectures used to generate the registration field, all three methods share a common framework;
050
      a network for estimating deformation fields, with losses that include an image similarity measure
      and a regularization term. In addition to these recent methods, a summary of the taxonomy of
051
      representative approaches is provided in Table 1.
052
053   While modifying model architectures can lead to performance improvements, these approaches are
      often tailored to specific tasks or datasets, limiting their broader applicability. To overcome this

      Rather than altering the architecture, we introduce a plug-in curriculum scheduler that integrates
      seamlessly with current methods. Our scheduler is inspired by curriculum learning, a training strat-
068
      egy that involves progressively increasing the difficulty of tasks. This approach has proven effective
069
      in various fields, which demonstrated how gradually evolving tasks and network architectures can
070   benefit language processing models. We extend this concept to medical image registration, showing
071   that it can significantly enhance performance on complex tasks.

      Our approach incorporates two key criteria to guide the network’s learning: sample difficulty and
      matching accuracy. Sample difficulty involves gradually selecting more challenging training exam-
074
      ples, while matching accuracy focuses on ensuring precise alignment by strengthening supervision
075
      over time. Sample difficulty is assessed at two levels: voxel and volume. For voxel difficulty within
076   an MRI volume, we use the Variance of Gradients (VoG) to prioritize more complex voxels for better
077   alignment as training progresses. Evaluating difficulty across MRI volumes is more challenging, so
078   we simplify the samples using Gaussian blurring, starting with highly blurred images and gradually
079   shifting to sharper ones. In terms of matching accuracy, we progressively increase the strictness
080   of supervision. This approach allows for more flexibility in the early stages, focusing on broader
081   patterns, and then demands closer adherence to ground truths as training advances, ultimately im-
082   proving accuracy.
083   Unlike most curriculum learning methods in medical image analysis, our approach offers an au-
084   tomated curriculum learning solution for medical image registration that does not require expert
085   knowledge or prior experience. This scheduler enhances adaptability and flexibility, enabling im-
086   proved performance across various tasks and datasets. By focusing on a plug-in curriculum sched-
087   uler rather than architectural changes, we aim to develop more robust and widely applicable regis-
088   tration techniques. In summary, our main contributions are as follows.

145   2.2   C URRICULUM L EARNING (CL)
146
147   Imposing curriculum in neural networks can be traced back to Elman (1993). Inspired by the man-
148   ners of how humans learn languages, Elman (1993) points out the importance of starting easily and
149   gradually hardening the learning process when training networks. Bengio et al. (2009) further ex-
150   tends the idea to various vision and language tasks - where multi-stage curriculum strategies give
151   rise to improved generalization and faster convergence. Named “curriculum learning” for these
152   strategies, they are employed to different algorithms such as image classification Wang et al. (2019);
153
      Wei et al. (2021), object detection Zhang et al. (2019; 2017a), semantic segmentation Zhang et al.
      (2017b), self or semi supervised learning Murali et al. (2018), multi-task learning Sarafianos et al.
154
      (2017; 2018); Dong et al. (2017); Wang et al. (2018), multi-modal learning Gong et al. (2016); Gong
155
      (2017), etc Jiang et al. (2015); Matiisen et al. (2019); Weinshall et al. (2018). In the medical field,
156   there has been limited prior research on curriculum learning (CL), with some examples in classi-
157   fication Jiménez-Sánchez et al. (2019); Luo et al. (2021), semantic segmentation Kervadec et al.
158   (2019), self-supervised or semi-supervised learning Tang et al. (2018), and multi-modal learning
159   Lotter et al. (2017). However, only a few studies leverage external knowledge from human experts,
160   and even fewer integrate CL with image registration Burduja & Ionescu (2021); Zhou et al. (2023b).
161   Our work introduces an automated CL approach for medical image registration that operates without
      the need of expert knowledge or prior experience.

      Medical image registration, also known as image alignment, involves aligning two or more anatom-
165   ically related images according to their spatial features. This process establishes non-linear dense
166   correspondences between n-D medical images that are acquired from different patients, scanners, or
167   at different times. It has been extensively studied due to its importance in clinical applications, such
168   as monitoring tumor growth or conducting group analysis.
169
      Deformable image registration is commonly framed as an optimization problem, aiming to minimize
170
      an energy function. This function generally consists of two main components: a penalty function
171   that evaluates the similarity between the aligned and reference images, and a regularization term that
172   enforces constraints on the registration field, such as promoting smoothness through a gradient loss
173   penalty. In this process, the fixed image (or target image) serves as the baseline or template to which
174   the moving image (or source image) is aligned. The fixed image provides the spatial coordinates
175   used for alignment, while the moving image is adjusted to match the fixed image as closely as
176   possible. The goal is to determine the optimal transformation that aligns the moving image with the
177   fixed image. Below is a comprehensive explanation and formulation of a widely used framework for
178   medical image registration.

      Research in deformable medical image registration has primarily focused on incremental perfor-
210   mance improvements through minor changes to model architectures or the addition of new loss
211   functions. While these adjustments can improve performance metrics, they often result in methods
212   that are task- or dataset-specific, limiting their broader applicability. To overcome these limitations,
213   we propose a novel approach that avoids modifying existing model architectures. Instead, we intro-
214   duce a plug-in curriculum scheduler that integrates seamlessly with current methods. This scheduler,
215   inspired by curriculum learning, organizes the learning process by increasing task difficulty based
      on two main criteria: sample difficulty and accuracy tolerance. For sample difficulty, we employ


                                                             4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      two strategies: weighting challenging voxel-level samples within the brain and starting with blurred
217   images before transitioning to sharper images as training progresses. The second criterion focuses
218   on the rigor of supervision by utilizing ground truth data for more comprehensive training. This
219   approach aims to improve the generalizability and flexibility of registration methods, making them
220   more robust and widely applicable.
221
222   4.1     S AMPLE D IFFICULTY
223
224   Curriculum learning involves training models in a structured sequence, beginning with simpler ex-
225   amples and progressively advancing to more complex ones. A key challenge in this approach is
226   developing automatic and objective metrics to assess the difficulty of each sample. However, in
227   the medical domain, determining the difficulty of individual samples is not straightforward. For
228   instance, identifying which specific MRI volume would be easier for learning the deformation field
229
      in registration tasks is not intuitive. Previous research has utilized domain knowledge from human
      experts to qualitatively assess the classification difficulty of medical images to guide curriculum
230
      learning. This approach, however, requires additional annotation efforts, depends on subjective hu-
231
      man experience, and introduces potential bias. Instead of relying on this computationally intensive
232   approach, we propose an automated method for assessing sample difficulties at two levels: voxel-
233   level and volume-level.
234
235
      4.1.1    VOXEL - LEVEL SAMPLE DIFFICULTY
236
237   In medical image registration tasks, each voxel in a 3D volume contributes to the overall alignment
238   between the moving and fixed images. In standard registration methods, all voxels within an MRI
239   volume are assigned identical weights during training, based on the assumption that each voxel has
240   equal significance. However, not all voxels are equally crucial for registration; for instance, areas
241   like edges or regions with high contrast tend to be more difficult to align, while homogeneous areas
242
      are often easier. To address this, we propose a method to evaluate the varying difficulty in learning
      the displacement field between voxels. Based on this measure, we design a training schedule that
243
      gradually prioritizes voxels of different difficulty levels as training progresses.
244

      4.2     ACCURACY T OLERANCE
313
314   In the context of model accuracy, curriculum learning generally involves presenting training data in
315   a structured manner, starting from easier examples and gradually moving to more difficult ones to
316   aid the model’s learning process. An additional dimension to this approach is adjusting the level of
317   strictness in supervision using ground truth data. This method is particularly effective in tasks that
318   require mastering intricate details, such as medical imaging, where accurate alignment or segmen-
319   tation is critical and develops over time.
320   In this approach, the level of strictness in evaluating the model against the ground truths is pro-
321   gressively increased. Early in training, the model is allowed more flexibility in its predictions (i.e.,
322   accepting a wider range of outputs as correct), focusing on general patterns. As the model pro-
323   gresses, the supervision becomes more stringent, requiring closer alignment with the exact ground
      truth (e.g., penalizing even slight deviations from the correct output). This strategy helps the model

324
      avoid being overwhelmed by complex or highly detailed data in the early stages, enabling it to build
325   a foundational understanding before being challenged to achieve higher accuracy levels.

422
423
      In Figure 2, the progression of the moved image at key training epochs is illustrated. Our curriculum
424
      learning-based approach initially focuses on learning coarse spatial transformations during the early
425
      stages of training, deliberately omitting intricate details to prioritize broader structural changes. By
426   starting with simpler, larger-scale adjustments, the model establishes a solid foundation for subse-
427   quent refinement. As training progresses, the model gradually hones in on finer, localized regions,
428   refining these areas based on the broader shapes learned in the earlier stages. This phased approach
429   allows the network to incrementally build upon its initial understanding, ultimately leading to more
430   accurate and precise registration results. By progressively tackling the complexity of the transforma-
431   tion, this training strategy significantly enhances the overall performance of the registration process,
      resulting in improved alignment and greater detail capture in the final output.

471   6.3.2     PARAMETERS OF VOXEL - LEVEL SAMPLE DIFFICULTY
472
      Sample difficulties. In the curriculum for voxel-level sample difficulty, we update the weights of
473
      voxels at each epoch to encourage the network to increasingly focus on challenging samples. We
474   contrast this with a simpler approach that maintains a fixed difficulty level for each voxel, utilizing
475   only the difficulty measure and omitting the training scheduler. Table 5 presents the DSC measures
476   for various fixed difficulty values. Our method demonstrates superior performance by progressively
477   increasing the probabilities for difficult samples, allowing the network to concentrate more on chal-
478   lenging details.
479
      Scheduling epochs. An ablation study is conducted on different scheduling epochs for voxel-level
480   sample difficulty. Here, estart indicates the epoch at which scheduling begins; thus, the VoG values
481   for individual voxels are computed and stacked for the difficulty measure during the epochs prior to
482   estart . Meanwhile, eend signifies the epoch when scheduling concludes. Consequently, the difficulty
483   weights of voxels are updated between estart and eend , after which equal probabilities are assigned
484   to all voxels. Note that estart = K and eend = K + L, with K and L detailed in Section 4.1.1.
485   Table 6 shows the DSC score of methods trained with varying scheduling epochs, showing that
      setting estart to 100 epochs and eend to 300 epochs yields the best DSC measures.

      7    C ONCLUSION
531
      We introduced an innovative approach that marks a notable advancement in deformable medical
532
      image registration through the development of a plug-in scheduler inspired by curriculum learning.
533
      This technique improves the adaptability and flexibility of existing network architectures without
534   the need for substantial alterations, enabling broader applicability across diverse tasks and datasets.
535   By concentrating on the dual aspects of sample difficulty and matching accuracy, we effectively
536   steer the network’s learning process towards achieving accurate image alignment. Our distinctive
537   application of the Variance of Gradients (VoG) for assessing voxel difficulty and the gradual shift
538   from blurred to clearer images for volume difficulty sets our work apart from conventional methods.
539   In contrast to earlier curriculum learning techniques in medical imaging, our automated solution
      removes the necessity for expert knowledge, making it accessible to a wider audience.

815   A.1     DATASETS AND P REPROCESSING
816
      IXI (Information eXtraction from Images). The publicly available IXI 3 dataset contains 576 T1-
817
      weighted MRI scans. These were split into 403 for training, 58 for validation, and 115 for testing.
818   The MRI volumes were cropped to dimensions of 160 × 192 × 224 and underwent preprocessing
819   using FreeSurfer. Registration performance was assessed using label maps corresponding to 34
820   anatomical structures. For inter-patient registration inference, 115 pairs were randomly selected
821   for the primary evaluation of the methods. In the atlas-to-patient registration tasks, the IXI images
822   served as the fixed images, while the moving image was an atlas brain MRI from Kim et al. (2021).
823
      MindBoggle-101. The MindBoggle dataset 4 contains 41 anatomically labeled brain surfaces from
824   101 healthy individuals, divided into four subsets: HLN (12 scans), MMRR (23 scans), NKI (42
825   scans), and OASIS (20 scans). MRI volumes from the HLN, MMRR, and NKI subsets were reg-
826   istered to MNI152 space 5 using affine transformations, with a resolution of 1 × 1 × 1mm3 and a
827   voxel grid size of 160 × 192 × 224. Registration performance was evaluated based on label maps of
828   41 anatomical structures. For inter-patient registration, 15, 33, and 66 pairs were randomly selected
829   from the HLN, MMRR, and NKI subsets, respectively. In the patient-to-atlas registration task, one
830   random sample from each subset was chosen as the atlas, and the remaining samples were registered
831   to it, with the process repeated twice to produce 33, 66, and 123 registration pairs for the HLN,
832
      MMRR, and NKI subsets, respectively.

834   A.2     E VALUATION M ETRICS
835
      Dice Score. The Dice score Dice (1945), also known as the Dice coefficient, is a statistical measure
836
      used to gauge the similarity between two sets, commonly applied in image segmentation. It quanti-
837   fies the overlap between the predicted segmentation and the ground truth by calculating the ratio of
838   twice the area of overlap to the total number of pixels in both sets. The formula is:
839
840                                                   2 × |A ∩ B|
                                             Dice =                                                   (13)
841                                                    |A| + |B|
842
      where |A| and |B| are the sizes of the two sets (predicted and ground truth), and |A ∩ B| is the size
843
      of their intersection. The Dice score ranges from 0 to 1, with 1 indicating perfect agreement and 0
844   indicating no overlap.
845
846   Jacobian determinant. The Jacobian determinant measures local volume changes induced by a
847
      deformation field. It is computed from the Jacobian matrix, which contains partial derivatives of the
      deformation field with respect to spatial coordinates. A positive Jacobian determinant indicates a
848
      local volume expansion or contraction, while a non-positive Jacobian determinant indicates a prob-
849
      lematic deformation, such as folding or inversion of the image. The percentage of non-positive
850   values in the Jacobian determinant provides an indication of the quality of the deformation; ideally,
851   this percentage should be low to ensure topological preservation and avoid unrealistic transforma-
852   tions.

      NiftyReg Modat et al. (2010) presents a GPU-optimized, parallel-friendly algorithm that performs
865   MR image registration in less than one minute, achieving the same accuracy as conventional serial

868
      LDDMM Beg et al. (2005) explores the Euler-Lagrange equations for large deformation diffeomor-
      phic metric mapping, deriving the minimizing vector fields and implementing a semi-Lagrangian
869
      method to compute particle flows and metric distances on anatomical structures.
870
871   deedsBCV Heinrich et al. (2015) presents a automated discrete medical image registration frame-
872   work for multi-organ segmentation across various modalities, using local self-similarity context
873   (SSC) for similarity assessment and a Markov random field (MRF) to ensure smoothness efficiently.
874   VoxelMorph Balakrishnan et al. (2019) formulates registration as a function mapping an input im-
875   age pair to a deformation field aligned by a CNN, using two training strategies: an unsupervised
876   method maximizing image intensity matching and a second method leveraging auxiliary segmenta-
877   tions from the training data.
878   CycleMorph Kim et al. (2021) proposes a cycle-consistent deformable image registration method
879   that enhances performance by preserving topology during deformation, applicable to both 2D and
880   3D problems and easily extendable to multi-scale implementations for large volume registration.
881
      MIDIR Qiu et al. (2021) introduces a deep learning registration framework for fast mono-modal
882
      and multi-modal image registration using differentiable mutual information and B-spline free-form
883
      deformation to achieve smooth, efficient diffeomorphic deformation.
884
885   ViT-V-Net Chen et al. (2021) integrates ViT and ConvNets to improve volumetric medical image
886   registration, drawing inspiration from ViT-based image segmentation methods that combine Con-
887
      vNets for better localization.
888   PVT Wang et al. (2021) addresses dense prediction tasks by providing high output resolution and
889   lower computational costs than ViT, while combining the strengths of both CNNs and transformers
890   as a versatile backbone for various vision applications.
891   CoTr Xie et al. (2021) proposes a framework that combines CNNs with a deformable Transformer
892   (DeTrans) for accurate 3D medical image segmentation, efficiently addressing long-range dependen-
893   cies while reducing computational complexities by focusing on key positions through deformable
894   self-attention.
895
      nnFormer Zhou et al. (2021) is a 3D transformer for volumetric medical image segmentation that
896
      integrates interleaved convolution with self-attention, employs local and global volume-based self-
897   attention mechanisms, and enhances the U-Net architecture by replacing skip connections with skip
898   attentions.
899
900
      TransMorph Chen et al. (2022a) is a hybrid model combining Transformer and ConvNet architec-
      tures for volumetric medical image registration, featuring diffeomorphic variants that ensure topol-
901
      ogy preservation and a Bayesian variant for assessing registration uncertainty.
902

904   self-attention and cross-attention mechanisms to capture multi-scale flow features, enabling high-
905   level features to inform the representation of low-level ones across spatially distant voxel patches.
