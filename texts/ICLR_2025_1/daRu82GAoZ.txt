022                                               A BSTRACT
023
024            Text-guided image-to-image diffusion models excel in translating images based on
025            textual prompts, allowing for precise and creative visual modifications. However,
026            such a powerful technique can be misused for spreading misinformation, infringing
027            on copyrights, and evading content tracing. This motivates us to introduce the task
028            of origin IDentification for text-guided Image-to-image Diffusion models (ID2 ),
029            aiming to retrieve the original image of a given translated query. A straightforward
030            solution to ID2 involves training a specialized deep embedding model to extract
031
               and compare features from both query and reference images. However, due to
               visual discrepancy across generations produced by different diffusion models, this
032
               similarity-based approach fails when training on images from one model and test-
033
               ing on those from another, limiting its effectiveness in real-world applications. To
034            solve this challenge of the proposed ID2 task, we contribute the first dataset and a
035            theoretically guaranteed method, both emphasizing generalizability. The curated
036            dataset, OriPID, contains abundant Origins and guided Prompts, which can be
037            used to train and test potential IDentification models across various diffusion mod-
038            els. In the method section, we first prove the existence of a linear transformation
039            that minimizes the distance between the pre-trained Variational Autoencoder (VAE)
040            embeddings of generated samples and their origins. Subsequently, it is demon-
041            strated that such a simple linear transformation can be generalized across different
042
               diffusion models. Experimental results show that the proposed method achieves
               satisfying generalization performance, significantly surpassing similarity-based
043
               methods (+31.6% mAP), even those with domain generalization designs.

075   A straightforward solution for the proposed ID2 task is to employ a similarity-based retrieval approach.
076   Specifically, this approach (1) fine-tunes a pre-trained network by minimizing the distances between

079   scenarios. This is because: for most current popular diffusion models, such as Stable Diffusion 2

083
      (instead of starting from randomly distributed noise). Further, as shown in Fig. 2, there exists a visual
084   discrepancy across images generated by different diffusion models, i.e., different diffusion models
085   exhibit distinct visual features. An experimental evidence for such discrepancy is that we can train a
086   lightweight classification model, such as Swin-S (Liu et al., 2021), to achieve a top-1 accuracy of
087   95.9% when classifying images generated by these seven diffusion models. The visual discrepancy
088   presents an inherent challenge of our ID2 , i.e., the approach mentioned above fails when trained on
089   images generated by one diffusion model and tested on queries from another. For instance, when
090   trained on images generated by Stable Diffusion 2, this approach achieves a 87.1% mAP on queries
091   from Stable Diffusion 2, while only achieving a 30.5% mAP on queries from ColorfulXL.
092   To address the generalization challenge in the proposed task, our efforts focus primarily on construct-
093   ing the first ID2 dataset and proposing a method with theoretical guarantees.
094
      • A new dataset emphasizing generalization. To verify the generalizability, we construct the first
095
      ID2 dataset, OriPID, which includes abundant Origins with guided Prompts for training and testing
096   potential IDentification models. Specifically, the training set contains 100, 000 origins. For each
097   origin, we use GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) to generate 20 different prompts, each of which implies
098   a plausible translation direction. By inputting these origins and prompts into Stable Diffusion 2,
099   we generate 2, 000, 000 training images. For testing, we randomly select 5, 000 images as origins
100   from a reference set containing 1, 000, 000 images, and ask GPT-4o to generate a guided prompt
101   for each origin. Subsequently, we generate 5, 000 queries using the origins, corresponding prompts,
102   and each of the following models: Stable Diffusion 2, Stable Diffusion XL, OpenDalle, ColorfulXL,
103   Kandinsky-3, Stable Diffusion 3, and Kolors. The design of using different diffusion models to
104
      generate training images and queries is particularly practical because, in the real world, where
      numerous diffusion models are publicly available, we cannot predict which ones might be misused.
105
106   • A simple, generalizable, and theoretically guaranteed solution. To solve the generalization
107   problem, we first theoretically prove that, after specific linear transformations, the embeddings of
      an original image and its translation, encoded by the diffusion model’s Variational Autoencoder

118
119         1. This paper proposes a novel task, origin identification for text-guided image-to-image
120            diffusion models (ID2 ), which aims to identify the origin of a generated query. This task

137   Diffusion Models. Diffusion models have become a transformative class of generative models,
138   utilizing iterative noise-based processes to excel in tasks such as image synthesis, inpainting, and
139   text-to-image generation. By progressively denoising data, these models can reconstruct highly
140   detailed images, offering flexibility and precision in creative applications. Recent advancements,
141
      including Stable Diffusion 2 (Rombach et al., 2022), Stable Diffusion XL (Podell et al., 2024),
      OpenDalle (Izquierdo, 2023), ColorfulXL (Recoilme, 2023), Kandinsky-3 (Arkhipkin et al., 2023),
142
      Stable Diffusion 3 (Esser et al., 2024), and Kolors (KolorsTeam, 2024), have brought significant
143
      improvements in resolution, text-image alignment, and color dynamics. This paper considers using
144   these popular diffusion models for text-guided image-to-image translation in a training-free manner,
145   which is a common and cost-effective approach in the real world.
146
147

      attention due to their impressive capabilities. However, alongside their advancements, several security
148
      concerns have been identified. Prior research has explored various dimensions of these security
149

154   content. In contrast, while our work also aims to help address the security issues, we specifically
155   focus on a novel perspective: identifying the origin of a given translated image.

      3    DATASET
193   To advance research in ID2 , this section introduces OriPID, the first dataset specifically designed for
194   the proposed task. The source images in OriPID are derived from the DISC21 dataset (Papakipos
195   et al., 2022), which is a subset of the real-world multimedia dataset YFCC100M (Thomee et al.,
196   2016). As a result, OriPID is diverse and comprehensive, encompassing a wide range of subjects
197   found in real-world scenarios where issues such as misinformation, copyright infringement, and
198   content tracing evasion frequently occur. An illustration of the proposed dataset is shown in Fig. 3.
199   Training Set. The training set comprises (1) 100, 000 origins randomly selected from the 1, 000, 000
200   original images in DISC21, (2) 2, 000, 000 guided prompts (20 for each origin) generated by GPT-4o
201   (for details on how these prompts were generated, see Appendix C), and (3) 2, 000, 000 images
202   generated by inputting the origins and prompts into Stable Diffusion 2 (Rombach et al., 2022).
203
      Test Set. We design the test set with a focus on real-world/practical settings. On one hand, we use
204   seven popular diffusion models, namely, Stable Diffusion 2 (Rombach et al., 2022), Stable Diffusion
205   XL (Podell et al., 2024), OpenDalle (Izquierdo, 2023), ColorfulXL (Recoilme, 2023), Kandinsky-3
206   (Arkhipkin et al., 2023), Stable Diffusion 3 (Esser et al., 2024), and Kolors (KolorsTeam, 2024),
207   to generate queries. This setting well simulates real-world scenarios where new diffusion models
208   continuously appear, and we do not know which one is being misused. On the other hand, for each
209   diffusion model, we generate 5, 000 queries to match 1, 000, 000 references inherited from DISC21.
210   This needle-in-a-haystack setting mimics the real world, where many distractors are not translated by
211   any diffusion models.
212   Scalability. Currently, we only use Stable Diffusion 2 (Rombach et al., 2022) to generate training
213   images. However, our OriPID can be easily scaled by incorporating more diffusion models for
214   training, which may result in better generalizability. Furthermore, we only use 100, 000 origins and
215   generate 20 prompts for each origin. Researchers are free to scale up our dataset by using the entire
      1, 000, 000 original images and generating more prompts with the script provided in Appendix C.

219   guaranteed and emphasizes generalizability. This section first presents two theorems regarding
220   existence and generalizability, respectively. Existence means that we can linearly transform the VAE
221   embeddings of an origin and its translation such that their distance is close enough. Generalizability
222   means that the linear transformation trained on the images generated by one diffusion model can be
223   effectively applied to another different diffusion model. Finally, we show how to train the required
224   linear transformation in practice.

326   As illustrated in Fig. 4, we show how to learn the theoretical-expected matrix W in practice. Consider
327   a triplet (g, o, n), where g is the generated image, o is the origin used to generate g, and n is a negative
328   sample relative to g. We have:
329                                   \mathbf {z} =\mathcal {E} \left ( g \right ) ,\mathbf {z}_{o} =\mathcal {E} \left ( o \right ) ,\ \operatorname {and},\ \mathbf {z}_{n} =\mathcal {E} \left ( n \right ),          (8)
330
      where E is the encoder of VAE. Therefore, the final loss is defined as:
331
332                                             \label {Eq: W} \mathcal {L} =\mathcal {L}_{mtr} \left ( \mathbf {z} \cdot \mathbf {W} ,\mathbf {z}_{o} \cdot \mathbf {W} ,\mathbf {z}_{n} \cdot \mathbf {W} \right ),    (9)
333   where Lmtr is a metric learning loss function that aims to bring positive data points closer together in
334   the embedding space while pushing negative data points further apart. We use CosFace (Wang et al.,
335   2018) here as Lmtr for its simplicity and effectiveness. Using gradient descent, we can optimize the
336   loss function L to obtain the theoretically expected matrix W.

341   Evaluation protocols. We adopt two commonly used evaluation metrics for our ID2 task: i.e., Mean
342   Average Precision (mAP) and Top-1 Accuracy (Acc). mAP evaluates a model’s precision at various
343   recall levels, while Acc measures the proportion of instances where the model’s top prediction exactly
344   matches the original image. Acc is stricter as it only counts when the first guess is correct.
345
      Training details. We distribute the optimization of the theoretically expected matrix W across 8
346
      NVIDIA A100 GPUs using PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019). The images are resized to a resolution of
347   256 × 256 before being embedded by the VAE encoder. The peak learning rate is set to 3.5 × 10−4 ,
348   and the Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2014) is used.

354   supervised learning models, vision-language models, and image copy detection models. We use
355   these models as feature extractors, matching query features against references. The mAP and Acc
356
      are calculated by averaging the results of 7 diffusion models. Please refer to Table 7 in Appendix
      for the complete results. We observe that: (1) All existing methods fail on the OriPID test dataset,
357
      highlighting the importance of constructing specialized training datasets and developing new methods.
358
      Specifically, supervised pre-trained models overly focus on category-level similarity and thus achieve
359   a maximum mAP of 6.2%; self-supervised learning models handle only subtle changes and thus
360   achieve a maximum mAP of 11.6%; vision-language models return matches with overall semantic
361   consistency, achieving a maximum mAP of 8.3%; and image copy detection models are trained with
362   translation patterns different from those of the ID2 task, thus achieving a maximum mAP of 29.1%.
363   (2) AnyPattern (Wang et al., 2024a) achieves significantly higher mAP (29.1%) and accuracy (25.7%)
364   compared to other existing methods. This is reasonable because AnyPattern is specifically designed
365   for pattern generalization. Although the translation patterns generated by diffusion models in our ID2
366   differ from the manually designed patterns in AnyPattern, there remains some generalizability.

      5.5   A BLATION S TUDY
470   In this section, we ablate the proposed method by (1) using different VAE encoders, (2) supervising the
471   training with different loss functions, (3) exploring the minimum rank of W, and (4) experimentally
472   exploring beyond the theoretical guarantees.
473
      Our method is insensitive to the choice of VAE encoder. In Table 5, we replace the VAE encoder
474   from Stable Diffusion 2 with two different encoders from Open-Sora (Zheng et al., 2024) and
475   Open-Sora-Plan (PKU-Yuan & etc., 2024). It is observed that, despite using significantly different
476   well-trained VAEs, such as ones for videos, the performance drop is minimal (less than 1%). This
477   observation experimentally extends the Eq. 1 from E1 (g1 ) · W = E1 (o) · W to E2 (g1 ) · W =
478   E2 (o) · W, where E2 is an encoder from a totally different VAE.
479
      In practice, selecting an appropriate supervision for learning W is essential. In Table 6, we
480   replace the used supervision CosFace (Wang et al., 2018) with two weaker supervisions, i.e., SoftMax
481   (LeCun et al., 1989) and Circle loss (Sun et al., 2020). We observe that switching to Circle loss results
482   in a drop in mAP for seen and unseen categories by 3.9% and 4.1%, respectively. Furthermore, using
483   SoftMax leads to mAP drops of 12.7% and 24.2% for the two categories, respectively. We infer this
484   is because: while our theorems guarantee the distance between a translation and its origin, many
485   negative samples serve as distractors during retrieval. Without appropriate hard negative solutions,
      these distractors compromise the final performance.

504    To improve efficiency, the rank of W can be relatively low. Assume the matrix W has a shape
505    of n × m, where n is the dimension of the VAE embedding and m is a hyperparameter. We show
506    that W is approximately full-rank in the proof of existence, and expect that m ≤ n in the proof of
507    generalization. Therefore, the rank of W is m. Experimentally, n = 4, 096, and we explore the
508
       minimum rank of W from 4, 096 as shown in Fig. 8. It is observed that: (1) From 4, 096 to 512, the
       performance remains nearly unchanged. This suggests that we can train a relatively low-rank W to
509
       improve efficiency in real-world applications. (2) It is expected to see a performance decrease when
510
       reducing the rank from 512 to 64. This is because a matrix with too low rank cannot carry enough
511    information to effectively linearly transform the VAE embeddings.
512
513    Using a multilayer perceptron (MLP) with activation function instead of the theoretically ex-
514
       pected W leads to overfitting. In the theoretical section, we proved the existence and generalization
       of W using concepts from diffusion models and linear algebra. A natural experimental extension
515
       of this is to use an MLP with activation functions to replace the simple linear transformation (W).
516
       Although linear algebra theory cannot guarantee these cases, we can still explore them experimentally.
517    Experimentally, we increase the number of layers from 1 to 7, all using ReLU activation and residual
518    connections. As shown in Fig. 9, we observe overfitting in one type of diffusion model. Specifically,
519    on one hand, the performance on seen diffusion models improves. For example, with 2 layers,
520    the mAP increases to 91.4% (+2.6%), and Acc rises to 89.4% (+2.8%). However, on the other
521    hand, a significant performance drop is observed on unseen diffusion models: with 2 layers, the
522    mAP decreases from 86.6% to 80.3% (−6.3%), and Acc drops from 84.5% to 77.3% (−7.2%). The
523    performance drop problem becomes even more severe when using more layers.

525     6             C ONCLUSION
526
527    This paper explores popular text-guided image-to-image diffusion models from a novel perspective:
528    retrieving the original image of a query translated by these models. The proposed task, ID2 , is
529    both important and timely, especially as awareness of security concerns posed by diffusion models
530    grows. To support this task, we introduce the first ID2 dataset, OriPID, designed with a focus on
531    addressing generalization challenges. Specifically, the training set is generated by one diffusion
532    model, while the test set is generated by seven different models. Furthermore, we propose a simple,
533    generalizable solution with theoretical guarantees: First, we theoretically prove the existence of linear
534
       transformations that minimize the distance between the VAE embeddings of a query and its original
       image. Then, we demonstrate that the learned linear transformations generalize across different
535
       diffusion models, i.e., the VAE encoder and the learned transformations can effectively embed images
536
       generated by new diffusion models.
537
538    Limitation. We note that certain methods, such as InstructPix2Pix (Brooks et al., 2023) and IP-
539    Adapter (Ye et al., 2023), perform text-guided image-to-image tasks in paradigms that go beyond the
       scope of our theorems. For a more detailed discussion, please refer to the Appendix (Section F).
