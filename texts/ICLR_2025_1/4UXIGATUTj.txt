009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large-scale neuronal activity recordings with fluorescent calcium indicators are
012            increasingly common, yielding high-resolution 2D or 3D videos. Traditional anal-
013            ysis pipelines reduce this data to 1D traces by segmenting regions of interest,
014            leading to inevitable information loss. Inspired by the success of deep learning on
015            minimally processed data in other domains, we investigate the potential of fore-
016            casting neuronal activity directly from volumetric videos. To capture long-range
017            dependencies in high-resolution volumetric whole-brain recordings, we design a
018
               model with large receptive fields, which allow it to integrate information from dis-
               tant regions within the brain. We explore the effects of pre-training and perform
019
               extensive model selection, analyzing spatio-temporal trade-offs for generating ac-
020
               curate forecasts. Our model outperforms trace-based forecasting approaches on
021            ZAPBench, a recently proposed benchmark on whole-brain activity prediction in
022            zebrafish, demonstrating the advantages of preserving the spatial structure of neu-
023            ronal activity.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Recent advances in imaging techniques have enabled the recording of neuronal activity at unprece-
029   dented resolution and scale. Light-sheet imaging allows recording of whole-brain activity for small
030   animals, such as the larval zebrafish (Hillman et al., 2019). Raw recordings are in the form of volu-
031   metric videos, with hundreds of millions voxels per time step, recorded over hours. Typically, heavy
032   postprocessing is applied to reduce dimensionality of this data down to 1D time traces of activity
033
      for distinct regions of interest representing individual neurons or clusters of cells (Abbas & Masip,
      2022). Inspired by the success of deep learning models in analyzing minimally processed data in
034
      other fields, such as weather and climate forecasting (Rasp et al., 2020; Andrychowicz et al., 2023),
035
      we explore the potential of building predictive models directly on such volumetric videos, avoiding
036   any information loss.
037
038   The ability to predict future behavior based on past observations is a cornerstone of scientific model-
039
      ing across a diverse range of domains, ranging from physics to social sciences. Until now, it has not
      been applied in the context of whole-brain activity in a vertebrate. The recently introduced Zebrafish
040
      Activity Prediction Benchmark (ZAPBench) (Anonymous, 2024) aims to change that, taking advan-
041
      tage of datasets that can now be acquired with modern microscopy techniques. ZAPBench provides
042   a rigorous evaluation enabled by the comparison of future brain activity predicted from past brain
043   activity to actual experimental recordings, thereby achieving an objective measure for evaluating
044   predictive models of brain function. The dataset used in ZAPBench is a whole-brain recording from
045   a larval zebrafish, collected using a light-sheet microscopy setup (Vladimirov et al., 2014). The raw
046   volume is made of ∼1.5 trillion voxels, which is reduced in size by three orders of magnitude to a
047   trace matrix of time series by applying a neuron segmentation mask. ZAPBench is the first bench-
048   mark that poses the forecasting problem for a significant fraction of neurons in a single brain and
049   provides the raw volumetric recordings for the experiments.
050   To test the viability of end-to-end forecasting on such data, we propose to use a video model based
051   on techniques that have not been applied to this domain previously. Since processing in the brain
052   is highly distributed (Urai et al., 2022; Naumann et al., 2016), we hypothesize that large receptive
053   fields are important. Furthermore, in comparison to models applied to activity traces, we expect the
      following advantages. First, by utilizing the entire video as input, a video-based model is not reliant

078   Building a model for this problem poses fundamental engineering challenges. Standard video mod-
079   els operate on 2D frames, and the presence of the additional Z dimension naturally complicates
080   scaling. In ZAPBench, a single XY slice of a 3D frame has a native size of 2048×1328 pixels, and is
081   thus comparable to a frame of a natural 1080p video. Every 3D frame is composed of 72 such slices,
082
      increasing the volume of the input data by up to two orders of magnitude relative to such videos and
      resulting in several hundreds of megabytes per frame.
083
084   For our model, we choose a variant of the UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015) and adapt it to 4D
085   data. We also develop a data input pipeline where both input and model are spatially sharded across
086   multiple hosts and accelerators. To maintain a manageable size of the intermediate activations,
087   we represent temporal input frames as channels. This approach allows us to explore the impact
088
      of varying spatial context by manipulating the receptive field while keeping computational cost
      (FLOPS) roughly constant.
089
090   We perform extensive experiments to construct an effective video model for neuronal activity fore-
091   casting on ZAPBench. Despite the success of pre-training in other domains (Devlin, 2018; Bao
092   et al., 2021), we find it not to be a useful technique for improving forecast accuracy, even when us-
093   ing an order of magnitude more data recorded from other specimens of the same species. Further, we
094
      investigate the effect of input resolution, spatial context, and temporal context of the model on the
      forecast accuracy. Surprisingly, we find that lowering input resolution by up to 4x can be beneficial
095
      for performance and observe a clear trade-off between spatial and temporal context.
096
097   Our models, which implicitly capture the spatial relationships within their field of view, can improve
098   forecast accuracy beyond that achieved by trace-based models on ZAPBench, especially when only
099   short temporal context is available. On ZAPBench, multivariate trace-based models, which can
100
      in principle learn functional relationships between cells, do not perform significantly better than
      univariate models that treat all cells independently and identically. Our proposed model is therefore
101
      the only multivariate model that can consistently outperform univariate models on this benchmark.1
102

119   We propose to forecast neuronal activity in the ZAPBench dataset (Anonymous, 2024) directly in
120   the volumetric video domain. Specifically, we utilize a temporal context of C video frames to
121   predict the subsequent H frames. Per-neuron forecasts and loss are then computed by applying the
122   segmentation mask to the predicted video frames. This contrasts with the traditional approach, which
123   applies the segmentation mask to the original video data to extract activity traces before performing
124
      any forecasting. See Figure 1 for a comparison of these two approaches.
125   The ZAPBench dataset comprises high-resolution, whole-brain activity recordings of a larval ze-
126   brafish engaged in various behavioral tasks. Data was acquired using light-sheet fluorescence mi-
127   croscopy, enabling real-time imaging of neuronal activity at cellular resolution. This was made
128   possible by using an animal genetically modified to express GCaMP (Dana et al., 2019), a flu-
129   orescent calcium sensor, in the nuclei of its neurons. ZAPBench provides both preprocessed ac-
130
      tivity traces for approximately 70,000 neurons and the corresponding raw volumetric video data.
      This raw data, denoted as Y, has dimensions of 2048×1152×72×7879 (XYZT) and a resolution of
131
      406 nm×406 nm×4 µm×914 ms. We use a center crop of 1328 voxels in Y due to negligible cell
132
      activity in the border regions. Models forecasting H = 32 time steps are benchmarked using short
133   (C = 4) or long (C = 256) temporal context.

202    3                   S CALABLE VOLUMETRIC V IDEO A RCHITECTURE
203
204   Efficiently training models consuming high-resolution volumetric video of varying input context
205
      sizes C requires a scalable architecture and data loading system. We achieve this by extending a
      standard UNet architecture (Ronneberger et al., 2015) to 4D by mapping temporal input context
206
      to features of the first convolutional layer, conditioning on lead-time to predict only single frames,
207
      and sharding both the model and the data loading process. Figure 3 shows the intermediate resolu-
208   tions and representation sizes. The network comprises a series of pre-activation residual convolu-
209   tional blocks (He et al., 2016) with fixed feature size F = 128, each with two group normalization
210   layers (Wu & He, 2018) using 16 groups, Swish activation (Ramachandran et al., 2017), and 33
211   convolutions for XYZ throughout.
212
213   3.1                      T EMPORAL I NPUT C ONTEXT AS F EATURES
214
215   Typically, video UNet variations use color channels as input features (Gao et al., 2022; Ho et al.,
      2022a) and convolve over frames using a temporal convolution (Ho et al., 2022b). This approach


                                                                                                                                                            4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      is intractable in our case because of the additional Z dimension. Instead, we treat the temporal
217   input context of C frames as input features to the UNet. This confers the following advantages:
218   1) the temporal sizes of the input and output are decoupled, 2) the network parameter count is
219   easily controlled, 3) representation sizes and computation requirements are reduced while using
220   more features, and 4) early layers of the network have access to long-range temporal dependencies.
221   Our model is similar to architectures used in standard time series models, which often treat temporal
222   context as features (Zeng et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023).

      3.2   VARYING THE R ECEPTIVE F IELD
225
226   We design a flexible UNet architecture that can adapt the receptive field while keeping the computa-
227   tional cost (FLOPS) fixed. We find that full native resolution is not necessary for optimal prediction
228   accuracy (see Sec. 4.1), and thus downsample the input by a factor of 4 in XY using averaging. The
229   first resampling block then uses a factor 2 in XY to achieve roughly isotropic resolution in XYZ,
230   while the following ones downsample equally in all dimensions. We always use four residual blocks
231   at the lowest resolution, and three at all other resolutions. This allows us to change the receptive
232   field while keeping the FLOPS roughly fixed by removing the four lowest resolution blocks and
233   instead adding one block to the respective next higher resolution. This is because one block at the
234
      higher resolution requires as many FLOPS as four blocks after downsampling by a factor of two
      in X and Y. In an ablation, we show that controlling for FLOPS is sensible because increasing the
235
      parameter count does not increase performance further (see Figure 7).
236
237   The receptive field along a dimension depends on the cumulative product of the downsampling
238   factors and the number of convolutions at the lowest resolution,
239
240
            receptive fielddim = cum downsampling factordim × num blocks × 4,
241
      where the factor 4 is because every block has two convolutions, each of which increase the receptive
242
      field by two. For a network that does not downsample at all, as for example used in Sec. 4.1.1,
243
      to account for the input and output convolutions and the center voxel, we have to increase the re-
244   ceptive field size by five. Therefore, the architecture depicted in Figure 3 has a receptive field of
245   (1024, 1024, 128) in XYZ comparable to the size of the complete frame. We tried to further enhance
246   the receptive field to cover the whole frame using a multi-axis vision transformer (Tu et al., 2022) at
247   the lowest resolution, but did not observe any accuracy gains. For the output, we upsample twice to
248   obtain the original resolution, and use one residual block per resolution, but with a reduced feature
249   dimension of F 0 = 32 to keep hidden representations at a manageable size.

      3.4     S HARDED DATA L OADING AND M ODEL
271
272   Despite the scalability features of the proposed UNet model for volumetric video, in practice apply-
273   ing it requires distributing the input and hidden representations across accelerators and machines.
274   We train all models in Sec. 4 using a single sample per batch, noting that this can already correspond
275   to several GBs of input data. We use spatial sharding in XY using the jax.Array API (Bradbury
276   et al., 2018) so that each box in Figure 3B is handled by an individual accelerator. We also imple-
277   ment a custom data loader that distributes data loading across hosts so that each machine only loads
278
      the necessary boxes. To achieve this, we chunk our data in the zarr3 format (Miles et al., 2023)
      and use the TensorStore API (TensorStore developers, 2024) to load and collate chunks. Our data
279
      loader follows the jax sharding automatically.

284   We present experimental results evaluating the proposed volumetric video model on ZAPBench, a
285   benchmark for whole-brain neuronal activity prediction for a larval zebrafish (Anonymous, 2024).
286   Uniquely, ZAPBench provides the raw volumetric recordings for most of the neurons in the brain
287   enabling data-driven approaches like ours. First, in Sec. 4.1 we empirically select and validate
288
      the final architecture variant used for the benchmark. In particular, we investigate the trade-off
      between temporal context C and spatial context in the form of the receptive field to assess the need
289
      for multivariate models. Further, we evaluate the feasibility of pre-training on additional zebrafish
290
      specimens as well as the effect of input resolution. We identify the model depicted in Figure 3 as
291   a strong model for the short context size C = 4, where we achieve the best performance across
292   the benchmark, as presented in Sec. 4.2. For the long temporal context C = 256, we only see an
293   improvement of forecast accuracy in specific cases.
294
295   Hyperparameters. Unless stated otherwise, we train every model for 250k to 500k steps by opti-
296   mizing the trace-based MAE with a batch size of 1 using the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov et al.,
297   2017) using an initial learning rate of 10−4 decayed using a cosine schedule (Loshchilov & Hut-
298   ter, 2017) to 10−7 and a weight decay factor of 10−5 . Due to their tendency to overfit, we use a
299   dropout rate of 0.1 on the features for long-context models with C = 256. These hyperparameters
300   were optimized on the validation set during development. We choose checkpoints based on the val-
301
      idation performance monitored during training. We present experimental results in terms of mean
      performance and report two standard errors over at least three random seeds that control data load-
302
      ing and parameter initialization. The only exception to this are the high resolution results presented
303
      in Sec. 4.1.3, where we only report a single result because of their compute requirements. Most
304   individual training experiments use 16 A100 40GB GPUs.

436   5   C ONCLUSION
437
438   We presented an approach for forecasting of zebrafish neuronal activity based on utilizing the raw
439
      neural recording data as a volumetric video to make predictions. We find that this method has several
      advantages over traditional trace-based methods. In particular, video-based prediction leverages
440
      spatial relationships between neurons that are hard to exploit when reducing the data to 1D traces.
441
      This allows for more accurate predictions, especially when working with short temporal contexts.
442   This advance comes at the expense of a significant increase in computational cost (2-3 orders of
443   magnitude relative to trace-based models, see App. A.5 for details).
444
445
      We report several findings that were contrary to our expectations. First, we surprisingly find that
      using higher resolution input frames does not improve performance. Second, the commonly used
446
      paradigm of pre-training on a larger data set and fine-tuning only leads to reduced forecast accuracy
447
      in our experiments. In contrast, we observe that more data from the same specimen does improve
448   performance, so we hypothesize that pre-training may be complicated by distribution shifts between
449   specimens, such as differences in signal and noise levels. Finally, increasing model capacity does
450   not always translate to performance improvements but instead leads to overfitting for long temporal
451   context.
452
      Future work could explore the use of probabilistic models, latent space representations, and more
453
      sophisticated regularization methods and input augmentations to further improve the accuracy of
454   video forecasting for neuronal activity.

      A.1     A RCHITECTURAL D ETAILS
597
598   Every network has an embedding 33 convolutional layer mapping from temporal context C to F
599   features, and an output convolutional layer mapping from F 0 (when upsampling) or F to 1 feature,
600   giving the single lead-time conditioned forecast frame. In the downsampling pathway, we apply one
601   convolutional block at every resolution. During symmetric upsampling, we use three convolutional
602   blocks at the lowest resolution, and two for all higher resolutions. Before upsampling to super-
603   resolution (i.e., resolution that is higher than that of the input), we use a convolution to map from
604   F to F 0 features to reduce the size of intermediate representations. During super-resolution upsam-
605   pling we use one convolutional block per resolution. Each convolutional block has a pre-activation
606
      residual design with the following chained layers: group normalization, swish activation, 33 convo-
      lution, group normalization, conditioning on lead time using a FiLM layer, swish activation, optional
607
      feature dropout (only used with rate 0.1 for C = 256), and lastly the second 33 convolution. The
608
      UNet-structure is realized by adding the representations obtained during sequential downsampling
609   to the upsampled representation. The number of features at every resolution is fixed to F = 128,
610   except for the super-resolution upsampling, where it is F 0 = 32.
611
612
      A.1.1    S PATIAL VS . T EMPORAL M ODELS
613
614   This study employs three distinct models based on the aforementioned design.
615
      The first model, maintaining a consistent spatial dimension of 512×288×72, forgoes downsampling
616
      and upsampling blocks. It incorporates four processing blocks at this resolution, along with two
617
      convolutional layers at the input and output stages. The receptive field, calculated as S = 1 + (4 ×
618   2+2)×2 = 21, is determined by considering the central voxel and adding 2 for each 33 convolution.
619
620   The second model, downsamples the input data to 64×64×32 and has a receptive field of S = 64.
621
      This is derived from the cumulative downsampling factors of (4, 4, 2) in the X, Y, and Z dimensions,
      respectively, and applying Equation 4.
622
623   Similarly, the third model employs downsampling factors of (16, 16, 8), resulting in a
624   256×256×128 receptive field. This translates to a global receptive field along the Z-axis, a near-
625   global receptive field along the Y-axis, and a receptive field encompassing half of the total extent
626   along the X-axis.
627
628   A.1.2    L EAD -T IME C ONDITIONING
629
630
      For the results shown in Figure 4, we use three different losses: direct MAE, conditioned MAE,
      and conditioned HL-Gauss. Apart from the FiLM layers to condition on lead-time, the architecture
631
      is the same in all cases, with the exception of the last layer which maps from F to the output
632
      dimensionality. The output dimensionality for the direct MAE is the number of forecast timesteps H.
633   For the conditioned MAE, it is simply 1, as also described in Figure 3. For the conditioned HL-
634   Gauss loss, it is 32, which is equal to H, and each output corresponds to a discretized bin of the data
635   range. The HL-Gauss loss transforms a real value by representing it as a weighted average of bin
636   mean-values, for details see (Farebrother et al., 2024).
637
638   A.1.3    M ODELS FOR D IFFERENT I NPUT R ESOLUTIONS
639
640   To investigate the influence of input resolution on model performance, we conducted a comparative
641   analysis. We compared our primary model, which operates on data downsampled by a factor of 4 in

643   another utilizing full-resolution input.
644   To ensure equitable comparison, the architectures of these models were kept broadly consistent, with
645   necessary adjustments to accommodate the differing input resolutions, while maintaining a consis-

      A.2    I MPACT OF U NSEGMENTED VOXELS
652
653   In contrast to video models which analyze all voxels of the calcium movie, the trace extraction pro-
654   cess ignores voxels that do not correspond to segmented cells. This potentially discards information

659
      mented voxels are unlikely to contain information that could improve forecasts and that any gains

660
      underlying calcium signals within the segmented cells.
