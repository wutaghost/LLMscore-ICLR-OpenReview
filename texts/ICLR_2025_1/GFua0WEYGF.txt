011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Can we establish provable guarantees for transformer performance? Providing
014            such theoretical guarantees is a milestone in developing trustworthy generative
015            AI. In this paper, we take a step toward addressing this question by focusing on
016
               optimal transport, a fundamental problem at the intersection of combinatorial and
               continuous optimization. Leveraging the computational power of attention layers,
017
               we prove that a transformer with fixed parameters can effectively solve the optimal
018
               transport problem (in Wasserstein-2 with entropic regularization) for an arbitrary
019            number of points. Consequently, the transformer can sort lists of arbitrary size
020            up to an approximation factor. Our results rely on an engineered prompt that
021            enables the transformer to implement gradient descent with adaptive step sizes
022            on the dual optimal transport. Combining the convergence analysis of gradient
023            descent with Sinkhorn dynamics, we establish an explicit approximation bound for
024            optimal transport with transformers, which improves with increasing depth. Our
025            findings provide novel insights into the essence of prompt engineering and depth
026            for transformers.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
      Language models with theoretical guarantees are reliable and, therefore, more practical. Extensive
031
      experiments confirm the striking capabilities of transformers, such as "multi-task learning" (Radford
032   et al., 2019), "in-context learning" (Brown, 2020), generalization Garg et al. (2022) to name but a
033   few. But is it possible to theoretically ensure these capabilities and quantify their limits? Consider
034   the simple example of sorting. We prompt the GPT-4 model to assess whether it can sort:
035
036                                prompt: sort(2, 1, 4, 3) → output: (1, 2, 3, 4)
037   While GPT-4 seems to be capable of sorting, querying to verify sorting is computationally infeasible
038   for two reasons: (i) the elements in the list can be arbitrary numbers, and (ii) the list can be arbitrarily
039   large. Thus, an infinite number of queries would be needed to verify that GPT-4 can sort. For
040   theoretical verification of sorting, we need to study language models at a mechanistic level beyond
041   black-box querying. Here, we investigate how to develop theoretical guarantees for the more general
042   problem of optimal transport.
043   Optimal transport is a fundamental optimization problem at the intersection of combinatorial and
044   continuous optimization. Sorting is a special case of optimal transport in one dimension (Brockett,
045   1991). While efficient sorting algorithms are discrete, Brockett (1991) raised the fundamental
046   question of how to solve optimal transport with continuous dynamical systems. This question was
047   motivated by the success of neural networks, which generate continuous state dynamics across their
048   layers for feature extraction. Brockett (1991) proposes a continuous-state dynamical system over
049   the orthogonal group that iteratively solves optimal transport and, hence, can sort and diagonalize
050
      matrices. Building upon this study, we investigate whether the feature dynamics in transformers are
      capable of performing optimal transport.
051
052   Beyond statistical parametric models, language models are powerful computational machines. Recent
053   studies show that language models are capable of learning to implement algorithms, including
      gradient descent for least squares (Ahn et al., 2024; Von Oswald et al., 2023; Akyürek et al., 2022),

      and the temporal difference method for reinforcement learning (Wang et al., 2024), and support
055   vector mechanism (Tarzanagh et al., 2023). This computational perspective has provided valuable
056   insights into language models at a mechanistic level, linking them to algorithms with provable
057   guarantees. Using this approach, we establish theoretical guarantees for solving optimal transport
058   (in Wasserstein-2) with transformers. Specifically, we prove that transformers can implement
059   gradient descent (with adaptive step sizes) on the dual optimal transport objective regularized by
060   entropy. In particular, each layer with two attention heads can simulate an iteration of gradient descent.
061   Therefore, the induction of multiple attention heads can implement several iterations of gradient

070   Recent studies demonstrate that interacting with language models is an art: proper prompting can

      Consider two sets of points x1 , . . . , xn ∈ Rd and y1 , . . . , yn ∈ Rd , and define C ∈ Rn×n such that
083

084

086
087                  P∗ := arg min Tr(P C),            subject to P being a permutation matrix,                  (2)

088
089   where Tr(M ) denotes the trace of matrix M . Sorting lists is an example of optimal transport.
090   Specifically, if Cij = (xi − yj )2 , where yi = i for i = 1, . . . , n and x1 , . . . , xn ∈ R, then the linear
091   transformation of [x1 , . . . , xn ] with P∗ sorts x1 , . . . , xn (see Remark 2.28 in (Peyré et al., 2019) for
092   more details). Yet, the optimal transport problem is more general than sorting.
093   Optimal transport lies at the intersection of discrete and continuous optimization. There are various
094   combinatorial algorithms for sorting and optimal transport, but our primary focus here is on continuous
095   optimization methods, which allow us to understand the mechanism of transformers. In particular,
096   we review two fundamental methods: (i) constrained continuous optimization and (ii) Sinkhorn
097   regularization.
098   While optimal transport involves optimization over the combinatorial set of permutation matrices, it
099   can be relaxed to optimization over a continuous set. The state-of-the-art method is based on linear
100   programming, specifically solving the following continuous convex optimization problem:

367   The statement of the last theorem proves that it is possible to avoid the rank collapse of attention
368   layers, which indicate that attention patterns converge to a certain low-rank matrix as depth in-
369

370

371

372   training (Daneshmand et al., 2020). Consequently, a line of research investigates effective techniques
373   to avoid the rank collapse issue (Meterez et al., 2023; Daneshmand et al., 2021; Joudaki et al., 2023).
374   We argue that the last theorem demonstrates the possibility of avoiding the rank collapse of attention
375   patterns with a specific prompt engineering and particular parameter choices. Recall that Pλ∗ serves
376   as an approximation of the optimal transport matrix P ∗ , which is a full-rank permutation matrix.
377   Consequently, we anticipate that the attention patterns will retain a high rank throughout the layers,
      as will be demonstrated in the experiments.

      7       D ISCUSSIONS
503
504
      We proved that transformers with fixed parameters can solve multiple instances of optimal transport
505
      on different number of points, with an explicit accuracy bound. Our analysis shows that transformers
506   can implement gradient descent on a specific objective function using a specially engineered prompt.
507   The engineered prompt provides additional memory to implement gradient descent. These findings
508   open several avenues for future research, including: including: (i) depth-efficient guarantees, (ii) the
509   analysis of training dynamics, and (iii) studying prompt engineering beyond optimal transport.
510
511   (i) Depth Efficient Guarantees. According to Theorem 2, a transformer with O(ϵ−2 )-depth can
512   obtain an O(ϵ)-accurate solution. This is due to the established convergence rate for gradient descent
513   with adaptive stepsizes. However, O(log(1/ϵ)) Sinkhorn iterations suffice for achieving ϵ-accuracy.
514   While there is a considerable gap between the established convergence analysis for gradient descent
515   and the convergence rate of Sinkhorn’s iteration, our result is sufficient to prove deep transformers
516   can provably solve optimal transport. We call for bridging this gap through a tighter convergence
517
      analysis.
518
519   (ii) Training for Optimal Transport. We proved that transformers are able to provably solve
520   optimal transportation and experimentally showed (in Section 6) that transformers can learn to solve
521
      optimal transport in R by training over random observations. Building upon this observation, we
      suggest a theoretical analysis of the training mechanism for optimal transport. To pursue this line of
522
      research, one can check whether parameters in (8) are local or global minimizers of the training loss.
523
      (Ahn et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024) demonstrate that the properties of generative data distributions
524   can be leveraged to analyze the stationary points of training dynamics. We believe that this technique
525   can be used to analyze the landscape of training loss for optimal transport.
526
527
      (iii) Prompt Engineering Beyond Optimal Transport. Prompt engineering is essential for demon-
528   strating that transformers are capable of solving optimal transport. In Section 6, we experimentally
529   show that prompt engineering is also important in practice. Despite its widespread use, the underlying
530   mechanisms of prompt engineering remain understudied. Studying prompt engineering for optimal
531   transport is a step towards the broader goal of understanding the role of prompt engineering in
532   general. We conjecture that prompt engineering enhances the computational power of transformers,
533   enabling them to simulate a wider class of algorithms.
