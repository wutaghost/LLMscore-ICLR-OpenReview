008                                                   A BSTRACT
009
010            This paper introduces Generalized Attention Flow, a novel feature attribution method for
011            Transformer models that addresses the limitations of existing approaches. By generalizing
012            Attention Flow and substituting attention weights with an arbitrary Information Tensor,
013            the method leverages attention weights, their gradients, maximum flow, and the barrier
014            method to generate more accurate feature attributions. The proposed approach demonstrates
015
               important theoretical properties and resolves issues associated with previous methods that
               rely solely on simple aggregation of attention weights. Comprehensive benchmarking in
016
               NLP sequence classification tasks reveals that a specific variant of Generalized Attention
017
               Flow consistently outperforms state-of-the-art feature attribution methods across most
018            evaluation scenarios, offering a more accurate explanation of Transformer model outputs.

021   1   I NTRODUCTION
022
023   Feature attribution methods are essential for building interpretable machine learning models. These methods
024   assign a score to each input feature, reflecting its importance to the model’s output, thereby facilitating the
025   understanding of model predictions.
026   The rise of Transformer models with self-attention mechanisms has necessitated feature attribution methods
027   for interpreting these models (Vaswani et al., 2017; Bahdanau et al., 2016; Devlin et al., 2019; Sanh et al.,
028   2020; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Kobayashi et al., 2021). Initially, attention weights were considered potential
029   feature attributions, but recent studies have questioned their effectiveness in explaining deep neural networks
030   (Abnar & Zuidema, 2020; Clark et al., 2019; Jain & Wallace, 2019; Serrano & Smith, 2019). Consequently,
031   various post hoc methods have been developed to obtain feature attributions for Transformer models.
032   Recent advancements in XAI have introduced numerous gradient-based methods, including Grads and
033   AttGrads (Barkan et al., 2021), which leverage saliency to interpret Transformer outputs. Qiang et al. (2022)
034   proposed AttCAT, integrating features, their gradients, and attention weights to quantify input influence on
035   model outputs. However, many of these techniques still focus primarily on gradients of attention weights,
036   inheriting limitations of earlier attention-based approaches.
037   Layer-wise Relevance Propagation (LRP) (Bach et al., 2015; Voita et al., 2019) transfers relevance scores from
038   output to input. Chefer et al. (2021a;b) proposed a comprehensive method enabling information propagation
039   through all Transformer components. However, this approach relies on specific LRP rules, limiting its
040   applicability across various Transformer architectures.
041
      Many existing methods for evaluating feature attributions in Transformers fail to capture pairwise interactions
042   among features. This limitation arises from the independent computation of importance scores, which neglects
043   feature interactions. For example, when calculating gradients of attention weights, they propagate directly
044   from the output to the individual input feature, ignoring interactions. Additionally, many methods used to
045   compute feature attributions in Transformers violate key axioms such as symmetry, sensitivity, efficiency, and
046   linearity (Shapley, 1952; Sundararajan et al., 2017; Sundararajan & Najmi, 2020) (Sec. 3.5).

                                                            1
047
      Recently, Abnar & Zuidema (2020) introduced Attention Flow to overcome these limitations in XAI methods.
048
      Attention Flow considers attention as capacities in a maximum flow problem, determining feature attributions
049   based on the solution. This approach naturally captures the influence of attention mechanisms, as the
050   paths of high attention through a network correspond to the flow of information from features to outputs.
051   Applicable to any encoder-only Transformer, Attention Flow has demonstrated strong potential to improve
052   model interpretability (Abnar & Zuidema, 2020; Modarressi et al., 2023; Kobayashi et al., 2021).
053
      Subsequently, Ethayarajh & Jurafsky (2021) sought to connect attention flows and XAI by utilizing Shapley
054
      values (Shapley, 1952). While they aimed to show that attention flows could be interpreted as Shapley values
055   under certain conditions, they overlooked the issue of non-uniqueness in such flows (Sec. 3.3).
056
057   Our contributions. We propose Generalized Attention Flow, which satisfies important theoretical properties
058
      and enhanced empirical performance. Specifically, our contributions are:
059   1. We introduce Generalized Attention Flow, an extension of the previously described Attention Flow. In this
060   approach, feature attributions are generated by using the log barrier method to solve a regularized maximum
061   flow problem within a capacity network formed from the functions applied to attention weights. Instead of
062   defining capacities based solely on attention weights, we suggest using the gradients of these weights (GF) or
063   the product of attention weights and their gradients (AGF) as alternatives.
064   2. We have addressed the non-uniqueness issue in Attention Flow, which invalidates some of its previously
065   suggested theoretical properties (Ethayarajh & Jurafsky, 2021). Furthermore, we show that non-unique
066   solutions occur frequently in practice. We have introduced barrier regularization to mitigate this issue to
067   ensure a unique solution. As a result, we have demonstrated that feature attributions derived from the
068   regularized maximum flow problem align with Shapley values and satisfy the axioms of efficiency, symmetry,
069   nullity, and linearity (Shapley, 1952; Young, 1985; Chen et al., 2023b).
070   3. We extensively benchmarked the proposed feature attribution methods, defined using Generalized Attention
071   Flow, against various existing state-of-the-art attribution methods. We found that a type of the proposed
072   attribution methods outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods in terms of explanation performance for
073   classification tasks across most evaluation scenarios, as measured by AOPC (Barkan et al., 2021; Nguyen,
074   2018; Chen et al., 2020), LOdds (Chen et al., 2020; Shrikumar et al., 2018), and classification metrics.
075   4. We have developed an open-source Python package for calculating feature attributions using Generalized
076   Attention Flow. This package is distinctively flexible, capable of being applied to any encoder-only Trans-
077   former model available in the Hugging Face Transformers package (Wolf et al., 2020). Furthermore, our
078   methods are easily adaptable for various NLP tasks.

165   3.1   I NFORMATION T ENSOR
166
167   In Transformer-based networks, information propagation occurs through pathways facilitated by the attention
168
      mechanism. These pathways can be conceptualized as routes within a graph structure, where tokens are
      represented by nodes and computations are denoted by edges. The capacities of these edges correspond to
169
      meaningful computational quantities that reflect the flow of information through the network (Ferrando &
170
      Voita, 2024; Mueller, 2024).
171
172   Attention scores can represent the flow of information through the neural network during the feed-forward
173   phase of training, quantifying the importance of different input parts in generating the output (Abnar &
174
      Zuidema, 2020; Ferrando & Voita, 2024). Additionally, the gradient of attention scores captures the flow of
      information during back-propagation, reflecting how changes in the output influence the attention mechanism
175
      throughout the network (Barkan et al., 2021). A combined view of attention scores and their gradients can
176
      simultaneously represent information circulation during both feed-forward and back-propagation, offering a
177   comprehensive perspective on the network’s information dynamics (Barkan et al., 2021; Qiang et al., 2022;
178   Chefer et al., 2021a;b).
179
180

      3.2   G ENERALIZED ATTENTION F LOW
224
225   In Generalized Attention Flow, we leverage the attention mechanism for feature attribution by defining a
226   network flow representation of a Transformer or other attention-based model. We assign capacities to the
227   edges of this graph corresponding to information tensor defined in Sec. 3.1. We then solve the maximum
228   flow problem to evaluate the optimal flow passing through any output node (or, more generally, any node in
229   any layer) to any input node. The flow traversing through an input node (token) indicates the importance or
230   attribution of that particular node (token).
231   To determine the maximum flow from all output nodes to all input nodes, we leverage the concept of multi-
232   commodity flow (App. A.2 and App. B). This involves the introduction of a super-source node ss and a
233   super-target node st with a large capacity u∞ . The connectivity between layers and capacities between nodes
234   are established using the information tensors, effectively forming a layered graph (App. B).

      3.3   N ON - UNIQUENESS OF M AXIMUM F LOW
274
275   The maximum flow problem lacks strict convexity, meaning it does not necessarily have a unique solution.
276   We found that the maximum flow problem associated with the graphs constructed employing Generalized
277   Attention Flow also fails to yield a unique optimal flow (App. C).
278   Observation 3.1. It is straightforward to verify that both Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2 solve the same
279   maximum flow problem. Therefore, determining the maximum flow in graphs generated by either Algorithm 1
280   or Algorithm 2 is equivalent and yields the same optimal value. However, it’s worth noting that the optimal
281   flows associated with them may not necessarily be equivalent, as explained in App. C.

                                                                         6
282
      Observation 3.2. If two distinct feasible solutions, denoted f1 and f2 , exist for a linear programming problem,
283
      then any convex combination γ1 f1 + γ2 f2 forms another feasible solution. Consequently, the maximum flow
284   problem can possess an infinite number of feasible solutions. Additionally, due to the non-uniqueness of
285   optimal flows arising from the maximum flow problem, their projections onto any subset of nodes in the graph
286   may also not be unique.

322   In XAI, axioms are core principles that guide the evaluation of explanation methods, ensuring their reliability,
323   interpretability, and fairness. These axioms provide standards to measure the effectiveness and compliance of
324   explanation techniques. Our proposed methods meet five essential axioms, as demonstrated by the following
325   theorem and corollaries.

328   feature combinations as:

334

336   alternative payoff functions have been introduced (Jethani et al., 2022; Sundararajan & Najmi, 2020), each

459   7    C ONCLUSION
460
461   In this study, we propose Generalized Attention Flow, an extension of Attention Flow. The core idea behind
462   Generalized Attention Flow is applying the log barrier method to the maximum flow problem, defined by
463   information tensors, to derive feature attributions. By leveraging the log barrier method, we resolve the
464   non-uniqueness issue in optimal flows originating from the maximum flow problem, ensuring that our feature
465   attributions are Shapley values and satisfy efficiency, symmetry, nullity, and linearity axioms. Additionally,
466   we demonstrate that our approach satisfies the axiom of conservation.
467   Our experiments across multiple datasets indicate that our proposed AGF method generally outperforms other
468   feature attribution methods in most evaluation scenarios. It could be valuable for future research to explore
469   whether alternative definitions of the information tensor could enhance AGF’s effectiveness.

749   The Shapley value, introduced by Shapley (1952), concerns the cooperative game in the coalitional form
750   (N, ϑ), where N is a set of n players and ϑ : 2N → R with ϑ(∅) = 0 is the characteristic (payoff)
751   function. In the game, the marginal contribution of the player i to any coalition S with i ∈
                                                                                                 / S is considered as

                                                                     16
752
      ϑ(S ∪ i) − ϑ(S). These Shapley values are the only constructs that jointly satisfy the efficiency, symmetry,
753
      nullity, and additivity axioms (Shapley, 1952; Young, 1985):
754
      Efficiency: The Shapley values must add up to the total value of the game, which means i∈N ϕi (ϑ) = ϑ(N ).
                                                                                             P
755
756   Symmetry: If two players are equal in their contributions to any coalition, they should receive the same
757   Shapley value. Mathematically, if ϑ(S ∪ {i}) = ϑ(S ∪ {j}) for all S ⊆ N \{i, j}, then ϕi (ϑ) = ϕj (ϑ).
758
      Nullity (Dummy): If a player has no impact on any coalition, their Shapley value should be zero. Mathemati-
759
      cally, if ϑ(S ∪ {i}) = ϑ(S) for all S ⊆ N \{i}, then ϕi (ϑ) = 0.
760
761   Linearity: If the game ϑ(·) is a linear combination of two games ϑ1 (·), ϑ2 (·) for all S ⊆ N , i.e. ϑ(S) =
762   ϑ1 (S) + ϑ2 (S) and (c · ϑ)(S) = c · ϑ(S), ∀c ∈ R, then the Shapley value in the game ϑ is also a linear
763   combination of that in the games ϑ1 and ϑ2 , i.e. ∀i ∈ N, ϕi (ϑ1 ) = ϕi (ϑ1 ) + ϕi (ϑ2 ) and ϕi (c · ϑ) = c · ϕi (ϑ).
764   Considering these axioms, the attribution of a player j is uniquely given by (Shapley, 1952; Young, 1985):
765                                       X |S|!(n − |S| − 1)!

       To implement a statistical significance test, we employ the ASO (Almost Stochastic Order) method (Ulmer
       et al., 2022; Dror et al., 2019; del Barrio et al., 2017), which compares the cumulative distribution functions
1018
       (CDFs) of two score distributions to determine stochastic dominance. Notably, ASO imposes no assumptions
1019
       about score distributions, making it applicable to any metric where higher scores indicate better performance.
1020
1021   When comparing model A with model B using the ASO method, we obtain the value ϵmin , which is an upper
1022   bound on the violation of stochastic order. If ϵmin ≤ τ (with τ ≤ 0.5), model A is considered stochastically
1023
       dominant over model B, implying superiority. This value can also be interpreted as a confidence score; a
       lower ϵmin suggests greater confidence in model A’s superiority. The null hypothesis for ASO is defined as:
1024
1025
1026                                                                  H0 : ϵmin ≥ τ                                                                 (20)
1027
1028   where the significance level α is an input parameter that influences ϵmin .
