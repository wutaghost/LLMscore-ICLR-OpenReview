009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Despite the promise of synthesizing high-fidelity videos, Diffusion Transformers
012                (DiTs) with 3D full attention suffer from expensive inference due to the com-
013                plexity of attention computation and numerous sampling steps. For example, the
014                popular Open-Sora-Plan model consumes more than 9 minutes for generating a
015                single video of 29 frames. This paper addresses the inefficiency issue from two
016                aspects: 1) Prune the 3D full attention based on the redundancy within video data;
017                We identify a prevalent tile-style repetitive pattern in the 3D attention maps for
018
                   video data, and advocate a new family of sparse 3D attention that holds a linear
                   complexity w.r.t. the number of video frames. 2) Shorten the sampling process by
019
                   adopting existing multi-step consistency distillation; We split the entire sampling
020
                   trajectory into several segments and perform consistency distillation within each
021                one to activate few-step generation capacities. We further devise a three-stage
022                training pipeline to conjoin the low-complexity attention and few-step generation
023                capacities. Notably, with 0.1% pretraining data, we turn the Open-Sora-Plan-1.2
024                model into an efficient one that is 7.4 × −7.8× faster for 29 and 93 frames 720p
025                video generation with a marginal performance trade-off in VBench. In addition,
026                we demonstrate that our approach is amenable to distributed inference, achieving
027                an additional 3.91× speedup when running on 4 GPUs with sequence parallelism.

030   1       I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Diffusion Transformers (DiTs) based video generators can synthesize long-horizon, high-resolution,
033   and high-fidelity videos (Peebles & Xie, 2023; OpenAI, 2024; Kuaishou, 2024; Lab & etc., 2024;
034
      Zheng et al., 2024; Esser et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024b). The 3D attention is a core module of
      such models. It flattens both the spatial and temporal axes of the video data into one long sequence
035
      for attention computation and reports state-of-the-art generation quality (Lab & etc., 2024; Yang
036
      et al., 2024b; Huang et al., 2024). The computation of 3D attention often consumes the majority of
037   the time during the entire forward propagation of a 3D DiT, especially with long sequences when
038   generating extended videos. Thus, existing 3D DiTs suffer from prohibitively slow inference due to
039   the slow attention computation as well as the multi-step diffusion sampling procedure.
040
      This paper tackles the issue by simultaneously sparsifying 3D attention and reducing sampling steps
041
      to accelerate 3D DiTs. To explore the redundancies in video data (recall that by nature videos can
042
      be efficiently compressed), we examine the attention states in 3D DiTs and identify an intriguing
043   phenomenon, referred to as the Attention Tile. As shown in Figure 1(a), the attention maps exhibit
044   uniformly distributed and repetitive tile blocks, where each tile block represents the attention be-
045   tween latent frames1 . This repetitive pattern suggests that not every latent frame needs to attend to
046   all others. Moreover, the Attention Tile pattern is almost independent of specific input (Figure 1).
047   With these, we propose a solution that replaces the original attention with a fixed set of sparse at-
048   tention mask during inference (§3.3). Specifically, we constrain each latent frame to only attend to
049   a constant number of other latent frames, reducing the complexity of attention computation from
050   quadratic to linear.
051   We then consider shortening the sampling process of a video from 3D DiT to further amplify the ac-
052   celeration effect. Inspired by the recent advance in diffusion distillation (Salimans & Ho, 2022; Song

134   Accelerating diffusion inference Many work in diffusion models have been proposed to reduce the
135   number of sampling steps to accelerate diffusion inference (Song et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2022a;b) (Liu
136   et al., 2024). Song et al. (2023) proposes the consistency models which distills multiple steps ODE
137
      to one step. Wang et al. (2023b) extends CMs to video generation model. Li et al. (2024b)
      further extends the idea with reward model to speed up video diffusion model inference. Another
138
      line of research that accelerates diffusion models inference utilize multiple devices (Li et al., 2024c;
139
      Wang et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024d; Zhao et al., 2024). These works exploit the redundancy
140   between denoising steps and use stale activations in distributed inference to hide communication
141   overhead, and are naturally incompatible with work that reduce the redundancy between steps. In
142   this work, we exploit the redundancy in attention computation, which is orthogonal to works that
143   leverage distributed acceleration and redundancy between denoising steps. Our pipeline integrates
144   a multi-step CM approach (Xie et al., 2024) by default, and in experiment, we show that it can also
145   seaminglessly integrate with parallel inference.
146   Sparsity in Transformer inference has been investigated in the context of Large Language Models
147   (LLMs) inference, which can be decomposed into pre-filling and decoding stages (Yu et al., 2022).
148   StreamingLLM discovers the pattern of Attention Sink, and keeps a combination of first few tokens
149   and recent decoded tokens during decoding phrase (Xiao et al., 2023). Zhang et al. (2024a;b)
150   adaptively identify the most significant tokens during test time. Video DiTs have different workload
151   than LLMs, where DiTs perform a single forward in each diffusion step without a decoding phrase.
152   In particular, our paper is among the first to explore sparse attention in the context of 3D Full
153   Attention DiTs. In addition, our finding that Attention Tile is data-independent motivates us to
154
      design a solution which does not require inference time adaptive searching, which is a bottleneck in
      work such as Zhang et al. (2024b). Sparsity has also been studied in Gan and other diffusion-based
155
      models, yet we focus on the new architecture 3D DiT (Li et al., 2020; 2022). A recent paper (Wang
156
      et al., 2024b) also discusses the redundancy in DiTs models, but no performance has been shown.

160
161   E FFICIENT- V D I T is a framework that takes in a 3D full attention DiT model T , and outputs a DiT
      that runs efficiently during inference TFast . E FFICIENT- V D I T consists of three stages. The first


                                                               3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      stage (§3.2) performs a multi-step consistency distillation and outputs TMCM , following the method
163   developed in image diffusion models (Xie et al., 2024). The second stage (§3.3) takes in TMCM ,
164   performs a one-time search to decide the optimal sparse attention mask for each layer, and outputs
165   a model TSparse with the optimal sparse attention mask. The last step(§3.4) performs a knowledge
166   distillation to preserve the model performance, using TMCM as the teacher and TSparse as the student,
167   following the distillation design in (Gu et al., 2024; Jiao et al., 2019).

169   sparse patterns in Section 3.1. Then, we will introduce the framework E FFICIENT- V D I T by stages.

      4.1       E XPERIMENT SETUP
307
308   Models. We use the 29 and 93 frames models of the popular 3D DiT based Open-Sora-Plan fam-
309   ily (Lab & etc., 2024). The model uses VAE inherits weights from the SD2.1 VAE (Rombach et al.,
310   2022a), with a compression ratio of 4x8x8 (temporal, height and width). For the text encoder, it
311   uses mt5-XXL as the language model, and it incorporates RoPE as the positional encoding (Xue,
312   2020; Su et al., 2024). In addition to the VAE encoder, videos are further processed by a patch
313   embedding layer that downsamples the spatial dimensions by a factor of 2. The videos tokens are
314
      finally flattened into a one-dimensional sequence across the frame, width, and height dimensions.
315   Metrics. We evaluate video quality using VBench and Content-Debiased Frechet Video Distance
316   (FVD) (Huang et al., 2024; Ge et al., 2024). VBench assesses the quality of video generation by
317   aligning closely with human perception , computed for each frame of the video and then averaged
318   across all frames, providing a comprehensive assessment. CD-FVD measures the distance between
319   the distributions of generated and real videos toward per-frame quality over temporal realism.
320   Baselines. We consider two models as the major baselines: the original Open-Sora-Plan model
321   and the model after consistency distillation. Following the default settings of Open-Sora-Plan mod-
322   els Lab & etc. (2024), we use 100 DDIM steps for the original model, which is consistent across
323   all experiments and training in the paper. For the MLCD model, we select the checkpoint with 20
      inference steps as we empirically find that it achieves the best qualitative result.

      Implementation details. We use FlexAttention from PyTorch 2.5.0 (Ansel et al., 2024) as the
325   attention backend. We provide a more detailed description on how to leverage FlexAttention to
326   implement our method in Appendix B. We generate videos based on the VBench standard prompt
327   list for VBench evaluation. To avoid potential data contamination in CD-FVD evaluation, we use a
328   set of 2000 samples from the Panda-70M (Chen et al., 2024c) test set to build our real-world data
329   comparison. As we use the CD-FVD score between real-world data and generated videos to evaluate
330   the capacity of DiT models, the prompt style needs to align with the real-world data clip samples.
331   Therefore, we randomly select prompts from the Panda-70M test set caption list for video generation
332   by the models.
333   Training details. All models are trained using the first 2000 samples from the Open-Sora-Plan’s
334   mixkit dataset.The global batch size is set to 2, and training is conducted for a total of 10000 steps,
335   equivalent to 10 epochs of dataset. The learning rate is 1e-5, and the gradient accumulation steps is
336   set to 1. The diffusion scale factor λ is 100. The MLCD model is trained with 100 DDIM steps of
337   the original model. The final model is trained with a 20-step MLCD model checkpoint.

344   integrate with advanced parallel method, i.e. sequence parallelism, in §4.2.2.

      4.2.2          E FFICIENT- V D I T SPEEDUP IN DISTRIBUTED SETTING
379
380   E FFICIENT- V D I T utilize sparse attention and consistency distillation to achieve speedup. These
381   methods are orthogonal to the recent advances in distributed systems, mainly sequence parallelism
382   based solution in LLMs (Liu et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2024a; Jacobs et al., 2023) and model parallelism
383   (or with hybrid sequence parallelism) based solution in diffusion Transformers (Li et al., 2024c;
384
      Wang et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024d). We consider sequence parallelism in this section for is
      simplicity and empirical lower overhead (Li et al., 2024a;c; Xue et al., 2024).
385
386   Implementation We utilize the All-to-All communication primitives to implement sequence paral-
387   lelism (Jacobs et al., 2023). In the attention computation, the system partitions the operations along
388   the head dimension while keeping the entire sequence intact on each GPU, allowing a simple im-
389   plementation of E FFICIENT- V D I T by applying the same attention mask as in the one GPU setting 2 .
390
      As a result, E FFICIENT- V D I T is natively compatible with All-to-All sequence parallelism.
391   We conduct a scaling experiment with sequence parallelism on 4x A100-SXM 80GB GPUs, inter-
392   connected with NVLink. We observe a speedup of 3.68× - 3.91× for 29 and 93 frames generation
393   on 4 GPUs, which is close to a theoretical speedup of 4× (Table 3). If reported 29 frames generation
394   on multi-GPUs, Oursr=0.100 can achieve 25.8x speedup on 4 GPUs and 13.0x speedup on 2 GPUs.

      5      C ONCLUSION
532
      In this paper, we first describe the phenomenon of Attention Tile, and dive into its characteristics
533
      of repetitive, large diagonals, locality, and data independent. Then we describe a class of sparse
534   attention pattern tailored to address the efficiency problem in Attention Tile. Lastly, we introduce
535   our overall framework that leveraged this class of sparse attention, which further leverages multi-
536   step consistency distillation, layerwise searching, and knowledge distillation for faster generation
537   and high performance. Experiments on two varaints of the Open-Sora-Plan model has demonstrated
538   that our method can achieve similar performance, with 0.1% the pre-training data, and up to 7.8×
539   speedup. Further ablation study has shown that our method can be natively integrated with advanced
      parallelism method to achieve further speedup.

747   A    E XTENED LAYERWISE SEARCH ALGORITHM
748
749   In this section, we explore how to balance the trade-off between inference speedup and output image
750   quality. Intuitively, as the attention map becomes sparser, the inference time decreases, but the
751   output image quality also degrades. With this model, we can answer the key question: Given a
752
      target speedup or inference time, how can we achieve the highest possible image quality?
753   This problem is well-suited to latency constrained case because, in real-world applications, speedup
754   can be precisely measured. Adjusting the generation quality within these constraints is therefore
755   meaningful. Additionally, solving this problem allows us to approximate continuous speedup ratios
      as closely as possible using discrete masks, further validating the robustness of our algorithm.

      B      F LEX ATTENTION IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS
811
812
      The attention we design can be efficiently implemented by the native block-wise computation design
813   in FlexAttention. Compared to a dynamic implementations, our computations are static, allowing
814   us to leverage static CUDA graphs for capturing or use PyTorch’s compile=True feature.
815
816
      FlexAttention employs a block-based mechanism that allows for efficient handling of sparse atten-
      tion patterns. Specifically, when an empty block is encountered, the module automatically skips
817
      the attention computation, leveraging the sparsity in the attention matrix to accelerate calculations.
818
      The ability to skip computations in this manner results in significant speedups while maintaining
819   efficient memory usage.
820
821   Additionally, FlexAttention is optimized by avoiding the need to materialize the entire mask. This
822
      mechanism enables FlexAttention to operate efficiently on large-scale models without incurring
      significant memory costs. For example, the additional memory usage of a model with 32 layers
823
      and a 29 frames mask is only 0.278GB, while a 93 frames mask requires 0.715GB of additional
824
      memory, which is considered minimal for large-scale models. By not needing to store or process the
825   full mask, we save both memory and computation time, leading to improved performance, especially
826   in scenarios where the attention matrix is highly sparse.
