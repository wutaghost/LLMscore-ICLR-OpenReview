009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Message passing mechanism contributes to the success of GNNs in various appli-
012            cations, but also brings the oversquashing problem. Recent works combat over-
013            squashing by improving the graph spectrums with rewiring techniques, disrupting
014            the original graph connectivity, and having limited improvement on oversquash-
015            ing in terms of oversquashing measure. Motivated by unitary RNN, we propose
016
               Graph Unitary Message Passing (GUMP) to alleviate oversquashing in GNNs by
               applying a unitary adjacency matrix for message passing. To design GUMP, a
017
               transformation is first proposed to equip general graphs with unitary adjacency
018
               matrices and keep their original graph connectivity. Then, the unitary adjacency
019            matrix is obtained with a unitary projection algorithm, which is implemented by
020            utilizing the intrinsic structure of the unitary adjacency matrix and allows GUMP
021            to be permutation-equivariant. In experiments, GUMP is incorporated into various
022            GNN architectures and the extensive results show the effectiveness of GUMP on
023            various graph learning tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Graph neural networks (GNNs) (Scarselli et al., 2008) have been widely used in various applications,
028
      such as social network (Fan et al., 2019) and knowledge graphs (Schlichtkrull et al., 2018). The
029
      most popular GNNs follow the message passing mechanism (Gilmer et al., 2017) to update the node
030   representations, where each node aggregates feature vectors of its neighbors to compute its new feature
031   vector. The message-passing mechanism is designed to be permutation-equivariant, allowing GNNs to
032   work with graphs that have varying node orders. Currently, GNNs with message passing mechanisms
033   have demonstrated success in various graph learning tasks, such as node classification (Kipf &
034   Welling, 2016a), graph classification (Ying et al., 2018), and link prediction (Kipf & Welling, 2016b).
035
      However, the message passing mechanism also inevitably brings the oversquashing problem to
036   GNNs (Alon & Yahav, 2020; Topping et al., 2022; Banerjee et al., 2022). The oversquashing problem
037   draws inspiration from a similar phenomenon observed in RNNs when learning long-range sequences,
038   as noted by Alon & Yahav (2020). It refers to the situation where, as larger neighborhoods are
039   considered, information from distant interactions funneled through specific bottlenecks minimally
040   influences GNN training. This phenomenon involves compressing information from potentially an
041   exponentially large number of nodes (relative to the number of layers) into fixed-sized node vectors.
042   Various techniques are proposed to alleviate the oversquashing problem. Topping et al. (2022)
043   propose the Jacobian of GNN to measure oversquashing, which motivates a rewiring method that
044   increases the curvature of the edges in a graph. Most works combat oversquashing via methods
045   depending on the graph spectrum (i.e., the eigenvalue of the adjacency matrix). In these works,
046   rewiring techniques increase the spectral gap by flipping edges (Banerjee et al., 2022), adding
047   edges (Karhadkar et al., 2023), re-weighting the edges (Arnaiz-Rodríguez et al., 2022), or using
048   expander for message passing (Deac et al., 2022). Except for increasing spectral gap, recent works
049   bound the measure in Topping et al. (2022) with effective resistance (Black et al., 2023) and commute
050
      time (Di Giovanni et al., 2023), and propose rewiring techniques to improve these bounds. Except for
      improving graph spectrum, Barbero et al. (2023) propose rewiring methods to greedily add edges to
051
      increase the number of walks in a graph.
052
053   The rewiring techniques above, even motivated from different perspectives, can be justified by the
      Jacobian measure of oversquashing in Topping et al. (2022). For instance, increasing the spectral


                                                        1

054
      gap or effective resistance can be viewed as an indirect method of improving the entries of powers
055   of the adjacency matrix in the Jacobian measure. As a result, these rewiring methods have limited
056   or uncertain improvements on the Jacobian measure because some of them do not directly improve
057   it (Karhadkar et al., 2023; Black et al., 2023) or improve it in a greedy way (Barbero et al., 2023).
058   Moreover, the rewiring techniques disrupt the original graph connectivity, resulting in the loss of
059   crucial structural inductive biases in graph learning tasks (Battaglia et al., 2018). This makes the
060   rewiring techniques inadequate for oversquashing. Detailed related works on oversquashing are
061   summarized in Appendix B.
062   In this paper, we propose a new one-hop message passing mechanism, called Graph Unitary Message
063   Passing (GUMP), to alleviate oversquashing. Motivated by existing analysis on oversquashing
064   of RNNs (Pascanu et al., 2013; Jing et al., 2017), the measures of oversquashing in RNNs and
065   GNNs share similar forms (Fig. 1(a)), i.e., the powers of feature transformation matrix in RNN and
066   the powers of adjacency matrix in GNN (Section 2.1). Since the unitary parameterization of the
067   transformation matrix has proved to be effective in capturing long-range interactions (Arjovsky et al.,
068   2016) in RNN, we consider imposing unitarity on the adjacency matrix in GNN for message passing.
069
      With a unitary adjacency matrix for message passing, the Jacobian measure of oversquashing will not
      change exponentially, thereby alleviating oversquashing. Compared to existing rewiring methods
070
      (Table 1), GUMP is a general message-passing mechanism that can be applied to various GNN
071
      architectures and paves a new way for alleviating oversquashing, which achieves optimal Jacobian
072   measure and preserves the original graph connectivity.
073
074   To design GUMP, we first propose a graph transformation algorithm in Section 2.2 to equip a general
075
      graph with unitary adjacency matrices and preserve its original graph connectivity at the same time.
      The transformation algorithm is based on the theory showing that unitary adjacency matrices exist for
076
      the line graph of an Eulerian graph. Then, we propose an algorithm to calculate the unitary adjacency
077
      matrix in Section 2.3. The algorithm is designed to allow GUMP to be permutation-equivariant
078   and is implemented by utilizing the intrinsic structure of unitary adjacency matrices. Then, we
079   propose the framework that applies GUMP to different GNN architectures in Section 3. Finally, we
080   evaluate GUMP on several graph learning tasks in Section 4. In summary, our paper has the following
081   contributions:
082
083   • GUMP is a new one-hop message passing mechanism that alleviates oversquashing by applying a
084     unitary adjacency matrix for message passing. Compared with previous works, GUMP achieves
085     the optimal Jacobian measure of oversquashing.
086
087
      • GUMP maintains the original graph connectivity with a graph transformation algorithm and
        preserves the permutation equivariance of message passing with unitary projection.
088
089   • Extensive results show the effectiveness of GUMP. Further analysis of the Jacobian measure also
090     validates GUMP’s ability to alleviate oversquashing.

134   In this section, we propose a new one-hop message passing mechanism called Graph Unitary Message

136   graph in this paper. The extension to digraph is straightforward in Appendix F.

      2.1           OVERVIEW
139
140
      Motivation Unitarity has been crucial for the success of RNNs in effectively learning long-range
141   sequences in recent years, ranging from unitary RNNs (Jing et al., 2017; Arjovsky et al., 2016)
142   to linear RNNs (Orvieto et al., 2023; De et al., 2024). In Fig. 1(a), we simply formulate RNN as
143   hk = σ(Whk−1 + uk ) with hk being the hidden state at layer k, W being the transformation
144   matrix, uk being the input at time step k, and σ being the activation function. Unitary RNN (e.g.,
145   EUNN (Jing et al., 2017)) imposes unitarity to W such that the gradient of long-range information
146   does not vanish or explode, thus helps learn long-range interactions with gradient-based optimization.
147   Recently, motivated by state space model (Gu et al., 2021), linear RNN (e.g., LRU (Orvieto et al.,
148   2023)) uses the identity activation function and initializes W close to a unitary matrix to avoid the
149
      vanishing gradient problem. The success of imposing unitarity in RNNs for learning long-range
      sequences motivates us to apply unitarity to GNNs to alleviate oversquashing.
150
151   For graphs, it is also difficult for GNN to learn long-range interactions. In Fig. 1(a), a one-hop MPNN
152   layer for GNN is formulated as H(k) = σ(AH(k−1) Wk ) for simplicity. Similar to RNN (Pascanu
                                                                                            (r)
153   et al., 2013), the bottleneck of GNN is measured by the Jacobian measure ∂H /∂X, which is the
154   Jacobian of the hidden state with respect to the input (Topping et al., 2022). Therefore, we are
155   motivated to impose unitarity to A in GNN to alleviate oversquashing.
156
157   Challenges of imposing unitarity Imposing unitarity to an adjacency matrix is not as straightfor-
158   ward as that in RNNs. The first challenge comes from the sparsity of the adjacency matrix, as almost
159   all unitary matrices are dense (Fig. 1(b)). Therefore, the amount of graphs with unitary adjacency
160   matrices is limited. The second challenge is to obtain an input-dependent unitary adjacency matrix
161   while preserving permutation-equivariance. This is because the unitary adjacency matrix depends on
      the input graph, and the order of nodes in a graph should not impact GNN representations.

321   3.1    C OMPARISON WITH EXISTING METHODS
322
323   GUMP paves a new way to solve the oversquashing problem instead of rewiring. Overall, GUMP
      has the following advantages: (1) GUMP is permutation-equivariant, which is a desirable property

      for graph learning. (2) Unlike rewiring methods, GUMP does not introduce extra connectivity
325   to the original graph and thus preserves the original graph connectivity. (3) GUMP achieves the
326   optimal Jacobian measure of oversquashing since the eigenvalues of unitary adjacency matrices are
327   complex units and thus will not change exponentially with respect to the number of GNN layers. The
328   comparison of GUMP and other oversquashing methods are in Table 1.
329
      Previous work on unitary GNN, e.g., Ortho-GConv (Guo et al., 2022), imposes unitarity on the
330
      feature transformation matrix of GNN. Unlike Ortho-GConv, GUMP addresses the issue of ill-posed
331   gradient caused by oversquashing by imposing unitarity on the adjacency matrix. Moreover, enforcing
332   unitarity on adjacency matrix is more challenging than that on feature transformation, since adjacency
333   matrices depend on the input graph and are not parameters of GNN.
334
335   3.2   P OSITIONS OF GUMP
336
337   In this paper, we focus exclusively on one-hop message passing, the fundamental mechanism in
338   graph learning. To clarify our setting, we talk about the position of GUMP in graph learning from the
339   following aspects.
340
341   Multi-hop message passing We are aware of many multi-hop message passing methods (Feng
342   et al., 2022), e.g, Drew (Gutteridge et al., 2023) and GRIT (Ma et al., 2023), which can alleviate the
343
      oversquashing problem, capture long-range interactions in graphs, and achieve better performance
      than GUMP in most datasets. We want to clarify that GUMP and multi-hop message passing
344
      methods are in orthogonal categories. GUMP focuses on improving the fundamental message passing
345
      mechanism, while multi-hop message passing methods applies multi-hop node information to improve
346   the performance. GUMP addresses the oversquashing issue caused by fundamental message passing,
347   and this issue also exists in multi-hop message passing methods. In future work, we will combine
348   GUMP with multi-hop message passing for further performance improvement.
349
350   Stable signal propagation Stable signal propagation (Poole et al., 2016; Schoenholz et al., 2022)
351   is important for the scalability and robustness of deep neural network. The signal propagation is
352   difficult to stabilize in GNNs because of the irregular data structure of graphs (Rong et al., 2019;
353   Alon & Yahav, 2020). There are many works (Xu et al., 2018; Gasteiger et al., 2018) to improve the
354   signal propagation in GNNs from model architecture perspective. From the data perspective, rewiring
355   methods (Rong et al., 2019; Alon & Yahav, 2020) disrupt graph connectivity and do not fully address
356
      signal propagation issues. GUMP offers a comprehensive approach for stable signal propagation
      in GNNs, addressing instability from irregular graph data without losing graph connectivity. In the
357
      future, GUMP can inspire more data-perspective research on stable signal propagation in GNNs and
358
      help scale up GNNs.

      5   D ISCUSSIONS AND LIMITATIONS
513
514   In this paper, we propose a novel method for oversquashing, i.e., Graph Unitary Message Passing
515   (GUMP). Motivated by unitary RNNs, GUMP propagates messages on a graph with a unitary
516   adjacency matrix. Compared to previous methods, GUMP achieves an optimal Jacobian measure of
517   oversquashing, keeps the original graph connectivity, and is permutation-equivariant. We discuss
518
      below the limitations of GUMP and their implications for future work.
519
      Information loss Since GUMP involves graph transformation and adjacency matrix transformation,
520
      it is reasonable to consider what information is lost in GUMP. So we discuss the information loss in
521
      GUMP of different graphs: (1) Undirected and unweighted graph: These are the graphs discussed in
522   our paper. For this graph, the information is not lost; (2) Weighted graph: For weighted graph, since
523   GUMP utilizes a unitary adjacency matrix, the edge weights cannot be incorporated into message
524   passing directly, and a feasible way for it is to convert edge weights to edge features in message
525   passing, which is similar to R-GNN (Battaglia et al., 2018); (3) Directed graph: For directed graphs,
526   the original directed edges become indistinguishable after the graph transformation in Appendix F,
527   which means the original directionality is lost in the line graph. However, step 8 of Algorithm 3
528   can filter out the non-existing edges in the original graph, which can fix the information loss. For
529   the unweighted digraph, we can follow the same procedure as the undirected and unweighted graph.
530
      So even though the information can be lost in weighted or directed graphs, there are other ways to
      incorporate the information into GNN when using GUMP.
531
532
      High computational cost The high cost of GUMP is attributed to the construction of the unitary
533
      projection. Although the unitary projection introduces significant computational complexity, it is
534   essential for GUMP to obtain optimal Jacobian measure (as stated in Theorem 2.1) and exhibit good
535   performance (as demonstrated in Fig. 3(c)). However, GUMP is a good option for many tasks, e.g.,
536   biology and chemistry, where accuracy is more important than time cost. Many graphs in these tasks
537   are not very large, and the data collection and analysis processes often take significantly more time
538   than model training. In the future, motivated by Orvieto et al. (2023), we will explore performing
539   message passing in the diagonalized space of the adjacency matrix to reduce the computational cost
      of GUMP.

783   B.1       GNN AND OVERSQUASHING
784
785   Graph neural network (GNN) (Kipf & Welling, 2016a; Gilmer et al., 2017) with L layers is a type of
786   neural network that uses graph G and initial node features H(0) = X to learn node representations
787   H(L) . The k-th layer of GNN updates node representation via the message-passing formula
788
                                                           (k)             (k−1)                           (k−1)
789                                                       hi =δ(hi                    ,ϕ({{ψ(hj                        ), j ∈ N (i)}})),
790
791   where δ, ϕ, and ψ are combination, aggregation, and message functions respectively, {{· · · }} is a
792   multiset, and N (i) = {j|(i, j) ∈ E}.
793   Even though GNN achieves success in various graph learning tasks, it suffers from the oversquashing
794   problem. The oversquashing problem is first noted by Alon & Yahav (2020). Inspired by Xu et al.
795   (2018), Topping et al. (2022) proposes to measure oversquashing with the Jacobian between node
796   features at different levels of a GNN. Based on the measure, Topping et al. (2022) propose a rewiring
797   method to increase the curvature of the edges in a graph. Many works combat oversquashing by
798   improving the spectral gap of a graph. Banerjee et al. (2022) measure oversquashing via the spectral
799   gap of a graph, employing a rewiring algorithm based on expander graph construction and effective
800   resistance for edge sampling. Karhadkar et al. (2023) introduce FoSR, a rewiring method that
801
      maximizes the first-order change in the spectral gap of the graph. Arnaiz-Rodríguez et al. (2022)
      introduced a GNN comprising a parameter-free layer for learning commute time and a rewiring layer
802
      to optimize spectral gap according to network characteristics and task requirements. Except from
803
      improving spectral gap, Black et al. (2023) focus on minimizing total resistance between node pairs
804   and introduce the Greedy Total Resistance (GTR) rewiring method for oversquashing. Di Giovanni
805   et al. (2023) analyze oversquashing with commute time. Barbero et al. (2023) propose a rewiring
806   method to sequentially increase the number of walks between two nodes and preserve the locality in
807   the original graph. Except for the one-hop message passing neural networks above, Gutteridge et al.
808   (2023) propose a multi-hop message passing neural network (Drew) to alleviate oversquashing with
809   layer-dependent graph rewiring and a delay mechanism for skip connections based on layer and node
      distance.

      B.2    U NITARY NEURAL NETWORKS
811
812
813
      Unitary matrices U are square matrices satisfying U⊤ U = I. Unitary matrices contribute to the
      stability of neural network training by preserving vector norms and preventing issues like exploding
814
      or vanishing gradients. Imposing unitarity on neural networks enhances their ability to seamlessly
815
      capture and propagate information across layers. This technique has proven successful in various
816   architectures, such as RNNs, CNNs, and GNNs.
817
818   Unitary neural networks (UNNs) are first developed to tackle the problem of vanishing and exploding
819
      gradients in RNNs, enabling more efficient learning of information in extremely long sequences
      of data compared to existing methods like LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997). To show
820
      how unitary matrices can alleviate the vanishing gradient problem in RNNs, we denote RNN as
821
      hk = σ(Whk−1 + uk ) with hk as the hidden state at the k-th layer of RNN, W as the weight matrix,
822   uk as the k-th input of RNN, and σ as the activation function. As shown in Fig. 1(a), the long-range
823   dependency can be measured by ∂hr/∂u1 . To simplify the analysis, we assume the activation function
824   σ is identity. The Jacobian measure of the long-range dependency becomes ∂hr/∂u1 = Wr−1 . When
825   W is unitary, information in ∂hr/∂u1 will not vanish or explode, which is the key to alleviate the
826   vanishing/exploding gradient problem in learning long sequences.
827
      Early algorithms on unitary RNN construct a series of parameterized unitary transformations to
828   impose unitarity. EUNN (Jing et al., 2017) achieves this by composing layers of rotations, Fourier
829   transforms, and other unitary transformations to parametrize unitary matrices. uRNN (Arjovsky
830   et al., 2016) and scoRNN (Helfrich et al., 2018), on the other hand, maintain unitarity by perform-
831   ing a Cayley transformation to parametrize the full unitary space. expRNN (Lezcano-Casado &
832   Martınez-Rubio, 2019), in contrast, parametrizes unitary matrices within the Lie algebra of the orthog-
833   onal/unitary group. projUNN (Kiani et al., 2022) first optimizes its parameters using gradient-based
834   optimization and then maps the updated parameters to a unitary space. Most recently, linear recurrent
835   unit (LRU) (Orvieto et al., 2023) significantly enhances the long-range learning capability of RNNs
836
      by linear RNN and initializing their weight matrix to be nearly unitary. Other works (Peng et al.,
      2023; Gu & Dao, 2023) on recurrent models also share the similar idea of LRU to effectively capture
837
      long-range dependencies in sequences, yielding impressive outcomes in language modeling and other
838
      tasks.
839
840   Despite RNN, unitarity has also been applied to CNNs and GNNs. Unitary CNNs (Sedghi et al.,
841   2018; Li et al., 2019; Singla & Feizi, 2021; Trockman & Kolter, 2021) introduce various methods to
842
      restrict the convolutional filters to be unitary, e.g., via the Lie algebra of the orthogonal group (Singla
      & Feizi, 2021) and the Cayley transform (Trockman & Kolter, 2021). Ortho-GConv (Guo et al.,
843
      2022) imposes unitarity on the feature transformation matrix in GNNs.

849
850   Definition C.1 (Line graph). Given a graph G, its line graph L(G) is a graph such that
851
852
      • each vertex of L(G) represents an edge of G, i.e., V[L(G)] = E[G];
853
854
      • two vertices of L(G) are adjacent if and only if their corresponding edges share a common endpoint
855
        in G, i.e., E[L(G)] = {((i, j), (j, k)) ∈ V[L(G)] × V[L(G)] | (i, j), (j, k) ∈ E[G]}.
856
857   Definition C.2 (Eulerian graph). An Eulerian graph G is a graph containing an Eulerian cycle, i.e.,
858   there is a trail in G that starts and ends on the same vertex and visits every edge exactly once.
859
860   Definition C.3 (Permutation matrix). A permutation matrix P ∈ Rn×n is a square binary matrix
861   that has exactly one entry of 1 in each row and each column with all other entries 0.
862
863   Theorem C.4. Every permutation matrix is orthogonal, i.e., if P is a permutation matrix, P⊤ P =
      PP⊤ = I.
