009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated the strong potential to assist
012            both clinicians and the general public with their extensive medical knowledge.
013            However, their application in healthcare is constrained due to concerns about
014            the privacy of data used in training, which prevents the integration of private
015            and personal information because of security and ethical issues. Moreover, if
016
               their capabilities can be enhanced with information retrieval to access up-to-date
               knowledge, the current integration of LLMs with Information retrieval lacks robust-
017
               ness to imperfect retrieval, which can hinder their effectiveness and even reduce
018
               overall performance. In this work, we address this challenge by introducing the
019            Retrieval-Augmented Thought Process (RATP). Given access to external knowl-
020            edge, RATP formulates the thought generation of LLMs as a multiple-step decision
021            process. To optimise such a thought process, RATP leverages Monte-Carlo Tree
022            Search and learns a proxy reward function that permits cost-efficient inference.
023            On a private dataset of electronic medical records, deliberately excluded from any
024            LLM training set, RATP achieves 35% additional accuracy compared to in-context
025            retrieval-augmented generation for the question-answering task.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) trained on extensive datasets have show-
030   cased their enhanced capabilities in diverse tasks such as question-answering (Kamalloo et al., 2023),
031   conversational abilities with humans (Bubeck et al., 2023) and notably providing medical knowledge
032   (Singhal et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023b). Yet, their usage in healthcare is hindered by their limited
033   proficiency in accessing and accurately handling private data.
034
      Private data is any sensitive knowledge deliberately kept unavailable to LLMs during training
035
      due to ethical or business considerations. This includes, for example, medical records (Pampari
036   et al., 2018; Jia et al., 2020; Alsentzer et al., 2022) or banking details. These data must be excluded
037   from large language model training sets because once learned, their privacy cannot be ensured (Kim
038   et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2024a; Carlini et al., 2023). This creates a significant barrier to LLM usage
039   in healthcare as organisations cannot risk potential Personal Identifiable Information (PII) leaks,
040   especially under regulations such as GDPR which protect closely the usage of such data. Furthermore,
041   private databases are frequently updated, sometimes daily, making the knowledge embedded within
042   a model’s parameters quickly outdated. Continuously retraining the model with new data involves
043   significant computational and financial resources (Brown et al., 2020) which can be afforded by very
044
      few organisations. Hence, relying solely on LLMs, regardless of their increasing size, is impractical.
045   Thus, in scenarios such as administrative tasks (e.g., letters, discharge summaries) (Thirunavukarasu
046   et al., 2023) or decision aids (semi-autonomous diagnostic)(Lee et al., 2023a), where accessing
047   information from a private database is required, the fusion of LLMs’ capabilities with external
048   knowledge sources becomes crucial. While Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al.,
049   2021) has introduced the LLM and information retrieval pairing with the embedded cross-product
050
      between a query and the documents, recent works have shown its lack of robustness to unsuccessful
      retrieval (Yao et al., 2023c), to the extend that it can negatively impact performance (Yoran et al.,
051
      2024).
052
053   Additionally, beyond the privacy issue, external knowledge can potentially address some of the
      inherent limitations of LLMs (Delétang et al., 2023). Importantly, it enables the LLMs to recall

      3.3   S CORING MODELS
220
221   Oracle Score To score the value of each thought, a naive way is to use the QA LLM ℓout , which takes
222   the query x and any given thought ϕ as inputs and outputs the answer to the question. An oracle score
223   can be defined by comparing this generated answer to the true answer with the metric score R of the
224   QA task. However, such a perfect signal is by definition not available at inference time. Therefore,
225   two scoring models are introduced as proxies of such signals. Details on the implementation and
226   comparison of the scoring models can be found in Appendix C.
227   Model-Based Estimation. In the first approach, we propose a model-based method to learn a proxy
228   reward function Rθ that estimates the oracle scores using a training dataset. Specifically, when
229   running MCTS on the training data, we would be able to generate an offline dataset that contains
230   various thought combinations (ϕ(i) , ϕ(j) ), and whether those thoughts are informative enough to
231   solve the original query x:
232
                                    r(i,j) = R(ℓout (ℓthought (ϕ(i) , ϕ(j) ), x), y).
233
234   Given such an offline dataset D = {ϕ(i) , ϕ(j) , r(i,j) }(i,j) , Rθ can be optimized through
235
236                                    θ ← arg min ||Rθ (ϕ(i) , ϕ(j) ) − r(i,j) ||2                            (2)
                                                  θ

238   and used as a proxy of the Oracle score during inference time when we do not have access to the true

      answers. Rθ is typically instantiated as an MLP or XGboost model.
240   Self-critics score. However, acknowledging that such an offline dataset may not always exist. We
241   also introduce the second approach based on LLM self-critic. In the literature, using an LLM as a
242   self-criticism agent is widely applied to reflect on a previous LLM answer and improve it (Welleck

      4.2   A NALYSIS
344
      Baselines. In this subsection, our methods are compared against two prevalent zero-shot open-domain
345   question-answering approaches. The first baseline, LLM (ŷ = ℓout (x)), involves directly prompting
346   an LLM with the question. The second baseline, RAG (ŷ = ℓout (x, k)), represents the in-context IR
347   method (Lewis et al., 2021; Ram et al., 2023). Here, the LLM generation process is augmented with
348   one document k retrieved from the knowledge base by Contriever (Izacard et al., 2022a).
349
      Oracles methods use the oracle scoring system that requires the answers to the questions. As such,

      Limitations. One significant limitation of our work is the difficulty in obtaining publicly available
      large-scale datasets that are guaranteed not to have been part of the LLMs’ training set. This
470
      increasingly challenging condition poses a problem for machine learning researchers aiming to

472   systems is similar, each has distinct limitations. The self-critic score is computationally intensive,
473   effectively doubling the number of LLM calls required (see Figure 4). In contrast, the estimation score

476   sensitive data leakage from the training set. Other threats, such as interception of retrieved data

483   5     C ONCLUSION
484
485   In this work, we introduced RATP, a new framework that pairs LLMs with private information
      using a Markovian multi-step decision process. Our method meets the four criteria outlined in the


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      introduction for such approaches. 1. It does not rely on LLM training, ensuring that patient-sensitive
487   data will not be leaked. 2. By dividing the retrieval into a multi-step process, it can handle knowledge
488   well beyond its context window size. 3. It leverages MCTS to enhance the self-reflection and
489   self-critique abilities of LLMs across many documents, making it robust to unsuccessful or noisy
490   retrievals. This allows it to be twice as performant as other methods in navigating real EMRs. 4. The
491   entire process occurs in natural language, enabling each step to be verified by the practitioner.
492
      RATP enables the safe integration of new sensitive private knowledge into LLMs. This inclusion of
493   private data, previously excluded due to ethical or safety reasons, facilitates personalised applications
494   of LLMs tailored to each patient’s medical context. Moreover, its transparent reasoning across
495   multiple documents of diverse natures and origins supports multifactorial decision-making, which
496   is crucial for complex medical scenarios: additional healthcare applications enabled by RATP can
497   be found in Appendix A. Furthermore, by eliminating the need for extensive training, our approach
498   reduces barriers to LLM utilisation, making them more accessible for various scenarios, especially
499   for organisations, institutions, or countries with limited resources.
500   Future work: Applying the RATP framework directly to other data modalities, such as images
501   or tables, using multimodal LLMs and alternative retriever models would broaden the method’s
502   applicability. Table 3 highlights the importance of guiding thought generation with a scoring model
503   trained on medical data. Thus, developing robust scoring models using larger fine-tuned LLMs, while
504   preserving data privacy, is a significant research direction. Moreover, clinicians might still be the best
505   scoring model, making a ”human in the loop” approach particularly valuable. This method, being
506   transparent and providing reasoning steps in natural language, is well-suited for such an approach.
507   Ethics Statement: Given the medical context, the use of LLMs must be carefully regulated and
508   validated to avoid errors and ensure patient safety. To address this, we implemented two safeguards:
509   (1) A multidisciplinary team of clinicians from various specialties and countries supervised the work
510   to ensure adherence to healthcare best practices. (2) In Appendix J, we identified and examined
511   potential biases in the data and LLM output across different subgroups, with no significant biases
512   detected.
513   Reproducibility Statement: The methodology is detailed in Section 3.2. Code and instructions for
514   accessing datasets (emrQA, EHRQA, and BoolQ) are provided in the supplementary materials. These
515   datasets are publicly available for research purposes. Additional experimental details, including
516   prompts and implementations of baselines and models, are presented in Appendices B and C. A guide
517   to practitioners is included in Appendix H to help apply the method to new datasets.

1062   C.1    S CORING MODEL IMPLEMENTATION
1063
1064   Training of the offline model-based score. For both datasets the method to build a dataset and train
1065   the score estimation model is similar. We collect complete thought processes by answering questions
1066
       from the training split of the dataset with our MCTS-oracle method. Thus, we are able to link every
       thought ϕt collected with its parents i.e. the thoughts or documents that have been used to generate
1067
       ϕt . The parents of ϕt are indicated by the action at−1 = (ϕt,1 , ϕt,2 ) because ϕt = ℓthought (at−1 ).
1068
       As the thoughts processes have been generated by the MCTS-oracle policy, we also have the true
1069
       score R(ℓout (ϕt ), xi ), yi ) = R(ℓout (ℓthought (ϕt,1 , ϕt,2 ), xi ), yi ) = r(t,1,t,2) . Hence, we effectively
1070
       have a dataset D = {ϕ(i) , ϕ(j) , r(i,j) }(i,j) .
1071
1072   In practice, in the dataset D, the feature vectors are the concatenations of the embedded versions
1073   of thoughts ϕ(i) and ϕ(j) . These embeddings are generated by Contriever (Izacard et al., 2022a).
1074   The size of the dataset D is typically around 4000 samples. We trained gradient-boosting or MLP
1075   models on these datasets to perform the score estimations. The thresholds to compute the accuracy
1076
       are chosen to maximize the precision on the training set. Finally, they are tested on the test split of
       their respective dataset.
1077
1078   Self-critic scoring model. The self-critic method entails prompting the LLM to reflect on its previous
1079   outputs. In our case, we combine the initial question and a given thought with a prompt template (see

1254
       Vanilla LLM for QA The naive approach is to directly query the LLMs for the answers. In this
1255   approach, there is no decision, and the answer is generated with a single-round dialogue with the
1256   LLMs.
1257
1258   Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) In RAG, an external document can be used to alleviate
1259   the hallucination of the LLMs. The limitation of such an approach is the length of the document
1260   can be restricted by the context window size. In RAG, the LLMs answer the query within a single
1261   interaction. By formulating RAG as an MDP, the decision is 1-step, and the action can be defined as
1262   selecting the most relevant document form an external database.
1263
1264   Tree of Thought (ToT) In ToT, the thought process is generated in a tree structure with specifically
1265   designed prompts that ask the LLMs to generate multiple thoughts at each timestep. To search over
1266
       the generated tree-of-thought structure, either DFS or BFS can be used. Such a search is over the
       entire thought generation process, hence can be extremely high-dimensional and computationally
1267
       challenging.
1268
1269
       Graph of Thought (GoT) In GoT, the thought process is generated as a directional graph. If we
1270
       consider the thought generation process in GoT as an MDP, the action space is defined as the graph
1271   operators, such as aggregating or reflecting, and the exploration over such actions is conducted by
1272   LLMs.
1273
1274   RATP in RATP, we solve the decision-making problem with an external planning policy learned
1275   with MCTS. Moreover, RATP has a more general State space where external documents are also
1276   considered to be thoughts. In addition, RATP can work with model-based return estimation such
1277   that in inference time the value of thoughts can be effectively estimated with light machine learning
1278   model — without using LLMs for self reflection.

       H    G UIDE FOR PRACTIONNER
1351
1352
       In this section, we present a concise guide for implementing our method with new datasets.
1353
1354   1. Choosing a Retriever Mode and LLM: In our experiments, we use a pre-trained Contriever [4]
1355   with default parameters from its official GitHub repo. This model has been pre-trained on Wikipedia
1356
       but can be used for any textual database as shown by our results on the electronic medical record.
       Moreover, as our method is built to be robust to unreliable retrievals, the choice of the retriever model
1357
       should have a low impact. Similarly, while we provide the implementation for Llama-2, Gemma, and
1358
       Mixtral, our method is directly compatible with any Large Language Models.
1359
1360   2. Customizing Prompts: Our methods use different prompt templates for the different steps (final
1361   answer, thought generation, self-critic). While we provide general-purpose prompts (see Appendix
1362
       B.2), you can customize these prompts by incorporating expert knowledge (see Appendix B.1). This
       is particularly useful to enforce a desired shape for the output.
1363
1364   3. (Optional) Training an Oracle Score Estimator: The practitioner might want to use an estimator
1365   of the oracle score as a scoring model because it is cheaper in LLM queries with similar performance
1366   (see section 4.2). They would only need to provide a left-out dataset. The methods can then be run
1367   with the oracle scoring method and save every score thought. It will then train a model on such
1368
       collected data points. We provide the training script for XGboost models.
1369   4. (Optional) Fine-Tuning MCTS Parameters: To improve the performance of the method on a
1370   particular dataset, it is possible to adapt the values of some parameters.
1371
1372          • Pdoc : the probability of pairing a thought with a retrieved document or another previous
1373
                thought.
1374          • c : the exploration parameter in the UCT formula, a higher value will favor the exploration
1375            of unseen thoughts, lower value favors the exploitation of highly scored thoughts.
1376          • Early stopping : There are two mechanisms to stop the thought process. First a threshold,
1377            we stop the process when a thought has a score higher than a given value. Second, a finite
1378            size stops the thought process when a given amount of thoughts is reached.
