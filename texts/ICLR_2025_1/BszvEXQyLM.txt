011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Vanilla 3D Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl et al. (2023)) struggles with modelling high
014            frequency details, especially in unbounded scenes. Recent works such as Scaffold-
015
               GS (Lu et al. (2023)) and Spec-Gaussian (Yang et al. (2024)) have made tremen-
               dous improvements to the reconstruction quality of these high frequency details,
016
               specifically in synthetic and bounded scenes, but still struggle with unbounded real
017
               world scenes. Therefore, we propose Phase-Aware KANGaussian, a model build-
018            ing on these earlier contributions to produce state-of-the-art reconstruction quality
019            for unbounded real world scenes with greatly improved high frequency details.
020            Phase-Aware KANGaussian introduces a novel phase regularization method that
021            optimizes models from low-to-high frequency, dramatically improving the qual-
022            ity of high frequency details. Phase-Aware KANGaussian is also one of the first
023            few papers to integrate a Kolmogorov-Arnold Network (KAN) into the Gaussian
024            Splatting rendering pipeline to verify its performance against the Multilayer Per-
025            ceptron (MLP). All in all, Phase-Aware KANGaussian has three main contribu-
026
               tions: (1) Introduce a Gaussian Splatting model with state-of-the-art performance
               in modelling real-world unbounded scenes with high frequency details, (2) a novel
027
               phase regularization technique to encode spatial representation and lastly, (3) first
028
               few to introduce a KAN into the Gaussian Splatting rendering pipeline.

      3D reconstruction with accurate geometry and lighting has always been the holy grail for 3D com-
048   puter vision scientists. While traditional methods using photogrammetry were able to achieve con-
049   siderable success in achieving novel view synthesis, the introduction of Neural Radiance Fields
050   (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al. (2020)) and later 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al. (2023)) pro-
051   vide alternatives that promise superior reconstruction quality. The key difference between NeRF and
052   3DGS is that the former learns an implicit, in contrast to 3DGS’s explicit representation of the scene.
053   Comparing their reconstruction quality, 3DGS lags slightly behind NeRF especially in high fre-
      quency details, but offers significantly shorter training and inference times, making it the preferred


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      choice for real time rendering. Some recent works (Lu et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2024); Ye et al.
055   (2024)) have achieved remarkable success in improving the reconstruction quality of high frequency
056   details for 3DGS all while maintaining the real-time rendering capabilities inherited from 3DGS,
057   but the improvements are mostly limited to synthetic and bounded scenes. Therefore, building on
058   the triumphs of earlier works, Phase-Aware KANGaussian aims to extend the quality enhancements
059   to real world unbounded scenes.
060
      In Phase-Aware KANGaussian, we employ Kolmogorov Arnold Networks (KANs) in the render-
061   ing pipeline in contrast to earlier works (Lu et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2024)) which instead opted for
062   the traditional Multilayer Perceptron (MLP). The rationale behind this decision is to introduce KANs
063   into 3DGS similar to how KANs were successfully integrated into the NeRF (Delin Qu (2024)), and
064   also assess the performance of KANs against MLPs as KANs were claimed to potentially have a
065   faster inference speed (Liu et al. (2024)) than their MLP counterpart. Furthermore, as specular high-
066   lights are highily sensitive to viewing angle, KANs have a locality property which can be exploited
067   to achieve superior reconstruction quality, which will be explored in depth in section 3.1.2.
068   Another key contribution introduced in Phase-Aware KANGaussian is a novel phase regularization
069   technique to achieve low-to-high frequency training. It involves filtering before computing a regu-
070   larization term in Fourier domain Fuoli et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2024), and as training progresses,
071   the filter would gradually expand to allow more frequencies to pass, effectively achieving low-to-
072   high frequency regularization. For Phase-Aware KANGaussian, we only consider the phase of the
073   Fourier transform in computing the regularization term as it encodes the spatial relationship and
074   structures in the image, and thus contain more information than the amplitude component.
075   We validate the effectiveness of our method by focusing on the reconstruction quality of unbounded
076   real world datasets, measured by peak signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR), structural similarity index mea-
077   sure (SSIM) and learned perceptual image patch similarity (LPIPS).

      2.0.1   A NCHOR -BASED 3D G AUSSIAN S PLATTING .
082
083   Anchor-Based 3DGS is a variation of 3DGS first introduced by Scaffold-GS (Lu et al. (2023)),
084   which dramatically improved the geometry and render quality of 3DGS models. The technique
085   involves initializing a sparse voxel grid from the point cloud generated from structure from motion
086   (SfM), and placing an anchor gaussian at the centre of each voxel. The anchor gaussians would
087
      then spawn new gaussians — each with their own parameters — around their vicinity. The offsets
      between the anchor gaussians and these spawned gaussians is learnable, and the parameters of these
088
      spawned gaussians are derived by passing a latent feature vector (stored by the anchor gaussian) into
089
      a MLP decoder. As the training progresses, the voxel grid is densified with new voxels and anchor
090   gaussians where necessary.
091
092   This idea has inspired other works such as Spec-Gaussian (Yang et al. (2024)), which introduced
093
      Anisotropic Spherical Gaussians (ASGs) (Xu et al. (2013)) into the model by changing the offset
      gaussians from spherical gaussians to ASGs. This replacement improved the specular details in
094
      the scene, as ASGs are more expressive than spherical harmonics in modelling the view-dependent
095
      color function of the gaussians. The work also proposed a coarse-to-fine training method by starting
096   training with down-sampled images, before gradually up-sampling as training progressed.
097
098   While these ideas undoubtedly boosted render quality, the improvements are mostly limited to syn-
099
      thetic bounded scenes. For real world scenes, these models still struggle, especially with patches
      with high frequency details where geometric structure and textures are less predictable. As such,
100
      a low-to-high frequency training pipeline would ensure that low frequency details are prioritized
101
      by anchor gaussians while high frequency details are left to the offset gaussians, which proves to
102   generate better renders.

106   Various regularization techniques have been employed to improve the quality of 3D Gaussian Splat-
107   ting (3DGS) reconstructions, with depth regularization being a notable example (Li et al. (2024);
      Chung et al. (2024)). This approach typically involves computing a loss between the rendered depth

127   able, monocular depth estimators are commonly used as substitutes. However, specular highlights in
128   these scenes can introduce significant errors in monocular depth estimation, making depth regular-
129   ization unsuitable for scenes with abundant specular details. To address this limitation, Phase-Aware
130   KANGaussian, designed specifically for enhancing specular renderings, looks to an alternative ap-
131   proach: regularization in frequency domain, which leverages both spectral and spatial loss to im-
132   prove reconstruction accuracy.
133   Combining spectral and spatial loss to improve render results has proved successful for various
134   computer vision models (Fuoli et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2024)). More recently, this technique
135   has also been applied to 3DGS by FreGS (Zhang et al. (2024)) and delivered promising results. In
136   FreGS, mid and high frequency is first removed from the ground truth and render image with a low-
137   pass filter before a regularization term is computed by taking the discrepancy between the amplitude
138   and phase of the resultant images. After a threshold iteration, another band-pass filter would be
139
      applied to the frequencies removed by the low-pass filter to compute a similar regularization term
      for the mid to high frequencies. The band of frequencies allowed through the band-pass filter will
140
      be progressively expanded to allow more frequencies to pass, eventually allowing the entire range
141
      of frequency to be included in the regularization.
142
143   Phase-Aware KANGaussian primarily concentrates on phase information, which encodes the scene’s
144   geometry and spatial structure. This focus particularly benefits specular scenes, where high-
145
      frequency components like specular highlights can distort frequency-based metrics, which is illus-
      trated in figure 2 where we observe a significant difference in phase of the render and ground truth.
146
      By isolating the phase term, the model can optimize the geometry more effectively, preserving the
147
      structural integrity of the scene while minimizing the impact of intensity variations caused by spec-
148   ular reflections. This targeted approach leads to more accurate geometric reconstruction, even under
149   challenging lighting conditions.

      4.1.1     R ESULTS & A NALYSIS .
414
415   From the experiments, Phase-Aware KANGaussian outperforms vanilla 3DGS and the baseline
416   model in unbounded real-world scenes, especially in patches with high frequency details as seen
417   in Figure 7. Our model encourages greater gaussian density at regions where high frequency in-
418   formation is congregated, allowing Phase-Aware KANGaussian to model the environment more
419   accurately and generate better render quality.
420   From the perspective of metric scores referencing Table 1, Phase-Aware KANGaussian loses out in
421   average PSNR across the three datasets. However, Phase-Aware KANGaussian significantly outper-
422   forms in other metrics, especially for LPIPS. This implies that the renders of Phase-Aware KAN-
423   Gaussian is able to model the high dimensional features of the scene better than the other models,
424   but contains more noise than the other models, which is likely the byproduct of accumulated error
425   in densified gaussian regions for modelling high frequency patches. It is also noteworthy that the
426   KAN in Phase-Aware KANGaussian has a relatively lower model complexity (8 × 8 hidden layers),
427   as compared to the MLP utilized for SpecGaussian (128 × 128 × 128 hidden layers), which is also
428
      another factor to consider in the discrepancies in metric scores. The choice and impact of model
      complexity is further explored in the ablation study.

506   4.2     A BLATION S TUDY
507
508   4.2.1      KOLMOGOROV-A RNOLD N ETWORK D ECODER
509   An ablation study removing the Kolmogorov-Arnold Network (KAN) component, recorded as “No
510   KAN” in Table 2, reveals the complete removal of the specular component, leaving only diffuse
511   colors in the render. This results in a significant drop in PSNR across datasets, with minor improve-
512   ments in SSIM and LPIPS in some cases, though the PSNR drop is more pronounced.
513
      Reconstruction quality is also influenced by KAN decoder complexity. We compare metric scores
514
      for an 8 × 8 and 16 × 8 hidden layer KAN, labeled as “No regularization (8 × 8 KAN)” and “No
515   regularization (16×8 KAN)” in Tables 2 and 3. Despite being simpler than the 128×128×128 MLP
516   decoder in SpecGaussian (Yang et al. (2024)), the KAN library’s high CUDA memory demands limit
517   scalability. On the A6000 GPU, the 16×8 KAN fully depletes memory, even without regularization,
518   while the 8 × 8 KAN is the feasible upper limit with regularization. Metric comparisons show better
519   scores with the more complex KAN, indicating further potential constrained by implementation.
520
521   4.2.2      P HASE R EGULARIZATION
522
      We assessed phase regularization through an ablation study using two additional models: (1) with
523
      amplitude and phase regularization and (2) without regularization, labeled as “Amplitude + Phase
524
      Regularization (8 × 8 KAN)” and “No Regularization (8 × 8 KAN)” in Table 2. Phase regularization
525   alone significantly improves PSNR while only slightly affecting SSIM and LPIPS, demonstrating
526   its effectiveness.

      5     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In conclusion, we introduce our Phase-Aware KANGaussian model, a 3DGS variation that uses an
      Anchor-Based method (Lu et al. (2023)) with anisotropic spherical gaussians (Yang et al. (2024)),
532
      combined with a Kolmogorov-Arnold Network decoder and a novel expanding kernel spectral loss
533
      computed from phase differences in Fourier domain. With these changes, we were able to extend
534   the quality enhancements from earlier works beyond bounded synthetic scenes to generate state-of-
535   the-art results in unbounded real-world scenes where geometry and details are less predictable. Our
536   model effectively densify regions where high frequency details are abundant, which greatly increases
537   the render quality of these high frequency patches. However, the accuracy of the newly generated
538   gaussians in these regions still have room for improvement, which might be a possible area for
539   further research. Nonetheless, Phase-Aware KANGaussian lays a solid foundation for further work
      to build upon, particularly for improving render quality of high frequency regions.
