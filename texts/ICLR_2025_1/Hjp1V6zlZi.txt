009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Universal Domain Adaptation (UniDA) aims to transfer knowledge from a la-
012            beled source domain to an unlabeled target domain without prior knowledge of
013            the label sets between the two domains. The goal of UniDA is to achieve robust
014            performance under arbitrary label-set distributions. However, existing literature
015            has not sufficiently explored performance across diverse distribution scenarios.
016            Our experiments reveal that existing methods struggle when the source domain
017            has significantly more non-overlapping classes than overlapping ones, a setting
018
               we refer to as Extreme UniDA. In this paper, we demonstrate that classical par-
               tial domain alignment, which focuses on aligning only overlapping-class data
019
               between domains, is limited in mitigating bias toward source-private classes in
020
               extreme UniDA scenarios. We argue that feature extractors trained with source
021            supervised loss disrupt the intrinsic structure of target data due to the inher-
022            ent differences between source-private-class data and target data. To mitigate
023            this bias, we employ self-supervised learning to preserve the structure of target
024            data. This method can be easily integrated into existing frameworks. We ap-
025            ply the proposed approach to two distinct training paradigms—adversarial-based
026            and optimal-transport-based—and show consistent improvements across various
027            class-set distributions, with significant gains in extreme UniDA settings.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Advancements in deep learning and machine learning often rely on the assumption that abundant
033
      data follows the same distribution, making it difficult for these models to generalize well on un-
      seen data sampled from different distributions. Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA) (Pan &
034
      Yang, 2009) addresses this issue by transferring knowledge from a source domain with a known
035
      distribution to a target domain with a possibly different distribution. Nevertheless, most UDA meth-
036   ods (Ganin et al., 2016; Long et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2020) for multi-class classification operate
037   under the strong assumption that the label sets of the source domain (Cs ) and the target domain (Ct )
038   are the same (Cs = Ct ), limiting the applicability in real-world scenarios.
039
      To overcome this limit, more flexible setups such as Open-set
040
      Domain Adaptation (Cs ⊂ Ct ) and Partial Domain Adaptation
041
      (Cs ⊃ Ct ) have been studied (Panareda Busto & Gall, 2017; Saito
042   et al., 2018; Cao et al., 2018b), addressing scenarios where the
043   target label set has more or fewer classes than the source label
044   set. Universal Domain Adaptation (UniDA) further loosens the
045   setup by not assuming Cs and Ct to have any containment rela-
046   tion. Instead, UniDA allows for overlap between Cs and Ct on
047   some unknown shared classes, while each set may also contain Figure 1: Comparison of prior
048   private, non-overlapping classes. The objective of UniDA is to works with training with source

      We start by examining the typical process of solving UniDA. The process begins with training
088   the feature extractor on the source data only. Then, a domain alignment loss, such as adversarial
089   loss (Ganin et al., 2016; Long et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2020) or self-training (Mei et al., 2020; Liu
090   et al., 2021), is applied to align the source and target feature distributions. However, due to the
091   presence of private data, directly aligning these distributions can lead to significant bias (e.g., target-
092   common data aligning with source-private data). To mitigate this, PADA (Cao et al., 2018b) and
093   UAN (You et al., 2019) initiated the concept of partial domain alignment, which designs weighting
094   functions to downweight private-class data and focus alignment only on common-class data. Build-
095   ing on this idea, subsequent works (Liu et al., 2019; Lifshitz & Wolf, 2021; Saito et al., 2020; Chen
096
      et al., 2022) have explored more effective designs for weighting functions.
097   In Section 2, we demonstrate that partial domain alignment faces significant limitations in extreme
098   UniDA scenarios. As illustrated in Figure 3, partial domain alignment must be highly precise to
099   mitigate the bias towards source-private classes. However, achieving such accuracy is unrealistic
100   without access to target labels. In this work, we explore the question:
101
                    What is the gap that leads to source-private bias under Extreme UniDA?
102
103   In Section 3.3, we present a toy analysis demonstrating that the feature extractor, when trained with
104   source supervised loss, focus on learning the direction for classifying source data, thereby neglecting
105   the direction relevant to target data. The effect of this phenomenon is amplified in Extreme UniDA
106   scenarios (Figure 2b, 2e), where the intrinsic spatial differences between source-private data and
107   target data become increasingly pronounced. To address this gap, we propose leveraging the capa-
      bilities of self-supervised learning (SSL) to preserve the structure of target data. We systematically

      2   U NDERSTANDING THE L IMITATION OF PARTIAL D OMAIN A LIGNMENT
141
142   Universal Domain Adaptation (UniDA) (You et al., 2019) has a labeled source domain Ds =
143   {(xsi , yis )}ni=1
                      s
                         and an unlabeled target domain Dt = {xti }ni=1
                                                                      t
                                                                         accessible at training time. The
144   datasets Ds and Dt are sampled from the source and target distributions ps and pt , respectively.
145   The label sets are denoted as Cs for the source domain and Ct for the target domain, respectively.
146   C = Cs ∩ Ct represents the common label set shared by both domains. Let C s = Cs \ C and
147   C t = Ct \ C represent the source-private label set and the target-private label set, that a label only
148
      occurs at one of two domains. The goal of UniDA is to classify target examples into |C| + 1 classes,
      where target-private examples are regarded as one unknown class.
149
150   In this work, we propose Source-Private to Source-Common Ratio (SPCR) to systemically study the
151   UniDA under various label-set distributions. SPCR is defined as the ratio of the number of source-
152   private classes to the number of source-common classes |C|C|s | . Intuitively, a high SPCR may cause the
153   model prediction to become biased towards source-private classes. Throughout the paper, we utilize
154   this ratio to analyze how partial domain alignment performs across different UniDA scenarios.
155
      In Figure 1, we observe that the widely-used partial domain alignment framework performs well in
156   settings with low SPCR but fails to outperform the source-only baseline in settings with high
157   SPCR. To address this, we provide a detailed analysis explaining why this occurs. In Section 2.1,
158   we begin by introducing the commonly adopted partial domain alignment approach in the UniDA
159   literature, which aims to reduce source-private bias by aligning only the common-class data across
160   domains. This method has shown promising results in general UniDA settings with low SPCR. In
161   Section 2.2, we examine its limitations, exploring both why it performs well in low SPCR scenarios
      and why it struggles in high SPCR cases.

      2.1   BACKGROUND : PARTIAL D OMAIN A LIGNMENT
163
164   The adversarial-based partial domain alignment is commonly used in universal and partial domain
165   adaptation (Cao et al., 2018b; Liu et al., 2019; You et al., 2019; Fu et al., 2020; Lifshitz & Wolf,
166   2021), which consists of a feature extractor θf , a label classifier θc , and a domain discriminator
167   θd . The adversarial-based partial domain alignment includes cross-entropy loss and adversarial
168   alignment loss. The cross-entropy trains a label classifier θc using labeled source data:
169                                Ls (θf , θc ) = E(x,y)∼ps CE(y, θc (θf (x))).                          (1)
170
171   Given the label-set shift between the source data and target data, predictions generated by models
172   trained with Ls may become biased towards source-private classes. This bias is further exacerbated
173   by the distribution shift, which increases the statistical likelihood of misclassification in the target
174   data.
175   To reduce this bias, previous works have introduced partial domain alignment, which focuses on
176   aligning only the common-class samples between domains. This can be incorporated into the adver-
177   sarial alignment loss as follows:
178
179
               Ladv (θf , θd ) = −Ex∼ps ws (x) log θd (θf (x)) − Ex∼pt wt (x) log(1 − θd (θf (x)))        (2)
180   Here, ws (x) and wt (x) are used to downweight private-class samples from both domains to ensure
181   that only common-class samples are aligned during training. The overall objective can be formulated
182   as:
183

                                    min max Ls (θf , θc ) − λLadv (θf , θd ) ,                        (3)
184                                 θf ,θc   θd
185   where λ is the weighted hyperparameter.
186
187   The role of ws (x) and wt (x). As shown in Equation 2, effectively reducing bias depends on per-
188   fect partial alignment, i.e., accurately approximating ws (x) and wt (x). Prior research has explored
189   various uncertainty metrics to calculate these weights. For instance, Cao et al. (2018a) employs
190   class probability, while Saito et al. (2018); Cao et al. (2018b) utilize confidence scores, and You
191   et al. (2019) leverage entropy. Additionally, Zhang et al. (2018) use domain similarity calculated
192   with an auxiliary domain discriminator. There are also approaches that combine multiple uncer-
193   tainty metrics into an ensemble (Lifshitz & Wolf, 2021; Fu et al., 2020). These methods have shown
194   promising results in general UniDA settings with low SPCR. We provide a detailed explanation of
195
      the uncertainty measurements in Appendix B.5.

197   2.2   L IMITATION OF PARTIAL D OMAIN A LIGNMENT
198
      Section 2.1 presents the goal of partial domain alignment, which is to reduce source-private bias.
199   Although partial domain alignment achieves promising results across various label set distributions,
200   it struggles under high SPCR, as demonstrated in Figure 1. In such cases, partial domain alignment
201   (Ls + Ladv ) performs worse than using the source loss only (Ls ). To investigate the underlying
202   cause of this failure, we aim to evaluate the effectiveness of partial domain alignment and assess
203   whether it can mitigate bias across all conditions.
204
205   Evaluation of ws (x) and wt (x). We first need an evaluation method to evaluate the effectiveness
206   of partial domain alignment. In an ideal case, the alignment weight ws (x) and wt (x) should assign
207   0 to private-class samples and 1 to common-class samples. In other words, the data used for partial
208   domain alignment should ideally contain only common-class samples. When private-class samples
209
      are included, they are treated as “noise” in the alignment process. We define the noise rate of a batch
      B for partial domain alignment as:

      only.

      3.1   A N A LTERNATIVE WAY TO R EDUCE S OURCE -P RIVATE B IAS
265
266   As discussed in Section 2.2, partial domain alignment fails to effectively reduce bias under Extreme
267   UniDA. The high bias leads to low tolerance for noise, while the imprecise weights tend to exhibit
268   high levels of noise in partial domain alignment. To break the vicious cycle, we pose the question:
269   Are there alternative methods to reduce source-private bias? We first delve into the property of
      Extreme UniDA. In this setting, the presence of many source-private classes biases the source-

      by removing source-private information, we turn to the novel approach incorporating target data
290   to address bias. This requires careful incorporation of target data with minimal reliance on source
291   data, given the lack of target domain labels and the significant presence of source private-class data.
292   Self-training (Mei et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021) is a popular method for incorporating target data by
293   selecting high-confidence samples as pseudo-labels for further training. However, it remains heavily
294   reliant on source data, which makes it vulnerable to source-private bias. In this work, we leverage
295   self-supervised learning to learn from unlabeled data, as it eliminates the dependence on source data
296   and effectively captures the underlying structure of the target data.

302   While SSL has been applied to DA-related tasks (Xu et al., 2019; Bucci et al., 2019; 2021; Achituve

304   to target private-class data harm performance? (2) Why can SSL be effectively incorporated into
305
      partial domain alignment? We address (1) in this section and (2) in Section 3.2. To explore (1),
      we conducted an ablation study by applying SSL exclusively on the target common-class data and
306
      compared it to applying SSL on the entire target dataset. The results in Figure 4a indicate that
307
      including target-private-class data does indeed hurt performance, particularly when there is a high
308   proportion of target-private classes. However, the performance decline is relatively minor compared
309   to the benefits it brings (as shown in Section 4). We argue that SSL focuses on learning data structure
310   rather than classifying target data, as supervised loss does, which makes the performance drop less
311   severe compared to that introduced by supervised learning.

313   3.2   A UNIFIED FRAMEWORK WITH S ELF -S UPERVISED L EARNING
314
315   In this section, we demonstrate that SSL can be effectively integrated into the partial domain align-
316   ment framework, yielding positive results. Although previous works (Bucci et al., 2019; 2021) have
317   combined SSL with adversarial domain alignment in other domain adaptation tasks, there has been
318   no investigation into their complementary effects. We aim to discuss how SSL enhance partial do-
319   main alignment.
320   We conduct an experiment comparing the average noise rate of partial domain alignment with and
321   without SSL. As shown in Figure 4b, training with SSL significantly reduces the noise rate in partial
322   domain alignment. In other words, SSL helps partial domain alignment break the vicious cycle of
323   error accumulation, further improving performance. These experimental results support our decision
      to unify the two approaches.

334   3.3   A STUDY OF SSL WITH T OY E XPERIMENTS
335
      In this section, we present an intuitive experiment to illustrate how source-private bias forms in
336
      UniDA, why it intensifies as SPCR increases and how SSL can effectively mitigate the bias. We
337   argue that this bias emerges during the training of the feature extractor using Ls . To support this
338   claim, we present a toy experiment that illustrates how target features shift when the model is trained
339   exclusively on source data. In Section 4.3, we further validate these observations using the singular
340   value spectrum on a real dataset.
341
342   Toy dataset construction Motivated by Liu et al. (2022), we generate a 2D toy dataset under the
343   framework of universal domain adaptation as illustrated in Figure 2a, 2d. Let e1 and e2 be two
344   orthogonal unit vectors in R2 . The source data is generated as xs = τ e1 + γe2 + ϵ, where τ, γ > 0
345   are hyperparameters controlling the positions of the class centroids, and ϵ ∼ N (0, I). To simulate
346   the distribution shift in p(x) of target-common classes for domain adaptation problem, we apply
347
      a rotation matrix R(θ) to the source-common-class data. Specifically, the target data is given by
      xt = R(θ)xs , where R(θ) is a 2D rotation matrix parameterized by angel θ. The rotation is chosen
348
      such that the target data xt aligns predominantly along the direction e1 , i.e., xt = ρe1 , where ρ is a
349
      scalar. For clarity in visualization, we do not generate target-private-class data, as its absence does
350   not affect our demonstration.
351
352   Method formulation We consider a two-layer linear network with ReLU as the activation
353   function (Agarap, 2018). For supervised learning, we minimize the objective: Ls (W1 , W2 ) =
354   E(x,y)∼Ds ||W2 (W1 (x))−y||2 . For self-supervised learning, similar to SimSiam (Chen & He, 2021),
355   we minimize the objective Lssl (W1 ) = Ex∼Dt ||W1 (x + ϵ) − W1 (x + ϵ′ )||2 , where ϵ and ϵ′ are ran-
356   dom perturbations. We compare models trained using Ls alone with those trained with Ls + Lssl .
357
358   Results As shown in Figure 2b, training with only the source loss Ls leads to learning a direction
359   that is a linear combination of e1 and e2 . This cause the target features to deform or misalign, as
360   the model fails to capture the specific direction of e1 in the target data. This observation highlights
361   the limitations of partial domain alignment in correcting the biases in the feature extractor. When a
362   significant number of source-private classes exist, the source loss focuses on learning directions that
363   discriminate these source-private classes. This focus can lead to poor generalization to the target
364
      domain, requiring near-perfect partial domain alignment to correct such bias.
365   In contrast, as shown in Figure 2c, when training with Ls + Lssl , we can observe the target features
366   maintain its direction along e1 . The observations motivates us to apply SSL on extreme UniDA,
367   where the feature extractor may exhibit significant bias.

      4.3   D ISCUSSION
452
453   We aim to answer the following questions: (1) Does adding the additional loss term ensure robust
454   performance across varying label-set distributions? (2) What factors contribute to the performance
455   gap in extreme UniDA scenarios? (3) What specific issues does SSL effectively resolve?
456
457   Robustness to Varying Label-set Distributions. While we introduce extreme settings to evaluate
458   the model’s performance with high SPCR, many intermediate ratios remain unexplored. To address
459   this gap, we select the challenging DomainNet dataset to test the model across a range of SPCR
460
      values: { 15 , 13 , 1, 3, 5}. From Figure 5, it is clear that both UAN and UniOT benefit from SSL,
      highlighting its robustness across varying label-set distributions. Furthermore, the improvement
461
      becomes more pronounced as SPCR increases (though less significant for UniOT), suggesting that
462
      SSL is particularly effective in Extreme UniDA, where bias is more pronounced.
463
464   Dimensional collapse under Extreme UniDA. To validate our hypothesis that training with Ls
465   in high SPCR settings leads to distortion of target representations, as shown in Figure 2, we plot
466   the singular value spectrum of target representations in Figure 6a to assess representation quality.
467   The results show that as SPCR increases, the number of singular values nearing zero also rises,
468   indicating that target features are restricted to a low-dimensional subspace—a phenomenon known
469   as dimensional collapse (Gao et al., 2019; Jing et al., 2022).
470
      SSL Prevents Dimensional Collapse To test our hypothesis that incorporating Lssl better pre-
471   serves the structure of the target data, we analyzed the singular value spectrum before and after
472   applying Lssl in Figure 6b. The results demonstrate that SSL effectively mitigates dimensional
473   collapse, highlighting its particular effectiveness in Extreme UniDA.

      Self-supervised Learning for Domain Adaptation Self-supervised learning (SSL) has been ap-
478
      plied to various domain adaptation tasks, including unsupervised domain adaptation (Xu et al., 2019)
479
      using simple pretext tasks, partial domain adaptation (Bucci et al., 2019; 2021) with jigsaw puzzles,
480   point cloud tasks (Achituve et al., 2021) involving deformation reconstruction, and universal domain
481   adaptation through clustering based on source data (Saito et al., 2020). Our work extends this line
482   of research by employing SSL for extreme UniDA. Unlike prior studies, our approach provides a
483   deeper understanding of SSL’s role in addressing source-private bias and its effectiveness in extreme
484   UniDA scenarios—an aspect that has not been previously explored. Moreover, our method differs
485   from DANCE (Saito et al., 2020), which relies on clustering using source data. In contrast, we
      propose leveraging target data independently of the source data. The advantage of our approach is

      Universal Domain Adaptation Universal domain adaptation is a more generalized form of unsu-
511
      pervised domain adaptation that makes no assumptions about the label sets relationship between the
512
      source and target domains. Numerous prior works (You et al., 2019; Fu et al., 2020; Lifshitz & Wolf,
513   2021; Saito et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2022) have focused on designing effective weighting functions
514   to downweight the contribution of private samples in domain alignment. The design of these weight-
515   ing functions is discussed in detail in Section 2.1. We cover these methods extensively in our paper
516   as we found their limitations in addressing source-private bias. Another line of research (Saito &
517   Saenko, 2021; Hur et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024) focuses on designing robust open-set classifiers
518   to distinguish between common classes and private classes in target data. Since these methods do
519   not emphasize domain alignment, they are not covered in our paper. With the emergence of more
520   advanced models, Zhu et al. (2023b); Deng & Jia (2023) explore the application of models such as
521
      vision transformers and pretrained vision models like DINO (Caron et al., 2021) and CLIP (Radford
      et al., 2021) to UniDA. There are also works that align with our goal of exploring more realistic or
522
      under-explored scenarios in UniDA. Qu et al. (2024) investigates source-free UniDA, where source
523
      data is unavailable during adaptation. Zhu et al. (2023a) addresses generalized UniDA, which aims
524   to identify novel categories and label distributions in the target domain, utilizing active learning to
525   achieve this objective.

      6   C ONCLUSION
529
530   We underline Extreme UniDA, a challenging sub-task of UniDA that remains unsolved and under-
531
      explored by existing UniDA methods. We argue that the difficulty of the task roots in the source-
      private bias, and demonstrate that state-of-the-art UniDA methods, mostly designed by partial do-
532
      main alignment that removes irrelevant data by reweighting, cannot completely mitigate the bias on
533
      their own for Extreme UniDA. The findings motivate us to devise a new methodology that works by
534   adding relevant information to reduce the bias. The proposed methodology applies self-supervised
535   learning to enrich the representation with the structural information of the source and target data.
536   Our extensive experiments verify that the proposed methodology, albeit lightweight, effectively im-
537   proves existing partial domain alignment methods across different ratios of source-private labels. In
538   particular, the methodology achieves a significant gain when facing extreme UniDA scenarios. The
539   promising results of our proposed methodology open a novel future research direction on how to
      add information systematically and strategically to improve UniDA methods.
