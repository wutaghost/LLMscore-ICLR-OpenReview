009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Predicting RNA secondary structure is essential for understanding RNA function
012            and developing RNA-based therapeutics. Despite recent advances in deep learn-
013            ing for structural biology, its application to RNA secondary structure prediction
014            remains contentious. A primary concern is the control of homology between train-
015            ing and test data. Moreover, deep learning approaches often incorporate com-
016
               plex multi-model systems, ensemble strategies, or require external data. Here, we
               present the RNAformer, a scalable axial-attention-based deep learning model de-
017
               signed to predict secondary structure directly from a single RNA sequence without
018
               additional requirements. We demonstrate the benefits of this lean architecture by
019            learning an accurate biophysical RNA folding model using synthetic data. Trained
020            on experimental data, our model overcomes previously reported caveats in deep
021            learning approaches with a novel homology-aware data pipeline. The RNAformer
022            achieves state-of-the-art performance on RNA secondary structure prediction, out-
023            performing both traditional non-learning-based methods and existing deep learn-
024            ing approaches, while carefully considering sequence and structure similarities.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Ribonucleic acid (RNA) is a polymer of four nucleotides that plays a critical role in gene expres-
029
      sion, protein synthesis, and epigenetic regulation (Morris & Mattick, 2014). The functionality of
030   RNA molecules is intrinsically linked to their structure, which is determined by a hierarchical fold-
031   ing process, dictated by the formation of local geometries of the so-called secondary structure of
032   RNA (Tinoco Jr & Bustamante, 1999). In addition to its impact on the final 3D shape, RNA sec-
033   ondary structures provide insights into RNA functions and can guide the design of RNA-based
034   therapeutics and nanomachines (Kai et al., 2021).
035
      While deep learning methods have achieved experimental accuracy in 3D protein structure predic-
036   tion (Jumper et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2023; Abramson et al., 2024), RNA structure prediction remains
037   challenging (Kretsch et al., 2023; Das et al., 2023). Specifically, capturing topologies and key sec-
038   ondary structure features appears difficult even for the current best model, AlphaFold 3 (Bernard
039   et al., 2024). The accurate prediction of an RNA’s secondary structure directly from its sequence of
040   nucleotides is still an important unsolved problem in computational biology (Bonnet et al., 2020).
041
      Traditional computational methods for RNA secondary structure prediction minimize free energy
042   using thermodynamic nearest-neighbor energy parameters, typically derived from wet-lab experi-
043   ments (Delisi & Crothers, 1971; Tinoco et al., 1971). The currently most widely used algorithms,
044   mfold (Zuker, 1989), RNAfold (Hofacker et al., 1994), and RNAstructure (Mathews et al., 1998) use
045   dynamic programming to efficiently calculate these energy minimizations. More recently, learning-
046   based approaches were developed to replace or improve estimates of the thermodynamic parame-
047   ters (Do et al., 2006; Andronescu et al., 2007; 2010; Zakov et al., 2011).
048   Inspired by the success of deep neural networks in the field of protein contact map prediction (Han-
049   son et al., 2018), SPOT-RNA was proposed, and deep learning entered the field of RNA secondary
050   structure prediction (Singh et al., 2019). This class of approaches can represent an RNA structure
051   as an adjacency matrix, surmounting the limitations of a restricted set of predictable base intera-
052   tions of previous approaches and thus being able to predict non-canonical base pairs (Olson et al.,
053   2019), pseudoknots (Staple & Butcher, 2005), and even base multiplets (Bhattacharya et al., 2019).
      The initial success led to additional deep learning-based models in the field (Chen et al., 2020; Sato

081   parameters (Sato et al., 2021), build ensembles (Singh et al., 2019), depend on a fully trained founda-
082   tion model (Chen et al., 2022), or use sophisticated pre- (Singh et al., 2021b) or post-processing (Fu
083   et al., 2022) methods. Furthermore, many deep learning approaches are based on convolutional
084   neural networks (CNN) Singh et al. (2019; 2021b); Sato et al. (2021); Fu et al. (2022), in which
085   the receptive field of the input depends on the model depth Luo et al. (2016). This means a larger
086   sequence requires a deeper network. While not a problem for data with a fixed-sized input such
087
      as pre-processed images, this may be problematic for RNA sequences which have varying lengths.
      Additionally, some models cannot be retrained due to undisclosed training pipelines (Singh et al.,
088
      2019; Flamm et al., 2021), making it difficult to reproduce reported results or build future models
089
      on top of these.
090
091   A major problem with previous deep learning-based approaches is that they did not sufficiently ad-
092   dress homologies between the training and test data. This led to strong performance on homologous
093
      RNAs but poor generalization (Szikszai et al., 2022; Justyna et al., 2023; Qiu, 2023), which raised
      concerns in the community that the reported results were overly optimistic (Flamm et al., 2021).
094
      Typically, training and test data were split solely based on sequence similarity (Singh et al., 2019;
095
      Chen et al., 2020; Fu et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022; Chen & Chan, 2023). However, it has been
096   extensively reported that this method is not sufficient to remove all similarities, as functional RNAs
097   are more conserved in structure than in sequence. For instance, it is well known that tRNAs fold
098   into a conserved clover-leaf structure, while the sequence similarity is low (Szikszai et al., 2024).
099

100
      ture prediction from single sequence inputs. The attention mechanism itself is independent of the
101

102   cess a 2D matrix in the latent space to directly model the adjacency matrix. Our model does not
103   use additional features such as MSAs. While the single components of the architecture are not new,
104   their usage for RNA secondary structure prediction is novel and outperforms existing approaches. In
105   addition, an important contribution is our novel data curation pipeline. RNAs can be classified into
106   so-called RNA families, based on their structure and sequence similarity. Thus, we use sequence-
107   and structure-based alignments of RNAs to split our training and test data in a family-based manner;
      an essential step to reliably assess the performance of RNA secondary structure prediction methods

149   2       T HE RNA FORMER
150
151   The architecture of the RNAformer is inspired by the protein folding algorithm AlphaFold (Jumper
152   et al., 2021), which models a pair matrix in the latent space and processes it with the use of axial
153   attention (Ho et al., 2019). In contrast to AlphaFold and similar to Lin et al. (2023), we dispense the
154   use of a multi-sequence alignment due to its well-known limitations in RNA (Singh et al., 2021b;
155
      Schneider et al., 2023). In contrast to other deep learning based RNA secondary structure prediction
      models Singh et al. (2019; 2021a); Chen et al. (2022); Sato et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2020); Franke
156
      et al. (2022); Jung et al. (2022), we directly model the 2D pair matrix in latent space. This has the
157
      benefit, that the latent representation is already capable of representing pseudoknots and multiples
158   and the final prediction of pairings between all nucleotides, the adjacency matrix, is just a linear
159   operation. In contrast to purely CNN-based approaches Fu et al. (2022); Tan et al. (2024), our
160   receptive field is not sequence length dependent.

      To artificially increase the model depth, we apply recycling in the latent space, similar to Jumper
217   et al. (2021), allowing the model to reprocess and correct its own predictions internally: We apply the

222   2.2   S PARSE A DJACENCY L OSS
223
224   The adjacency matrices representing RNA secondary structures tend to be heavily dominated by
225   zero entries as an RNA sequence of length l forms at most l/2 base pairs that result in l2 − l/2
226   non-zero entries, without considering multiplets. We employ a masking technique during training
227   to address the imbalance within such matrix representations. First, we mask everything except of all
228
      non-zero entries (all pairings) and a region around these non-zero entries. Next, we randomly select
      40% of the remaining zero entries (80% during the fine-tuning stage) to exclude from the mask too,
229
      effectively utilizing approximately 40% (80%) of the adjacency matrix entries for training. We treat
230
      the prediction of each entry of the masked adjacency matrix as a separate classification problem,
231   for which binary cross-entropy between prediction P and the true value is calculated. The mean of
232   the entry-wise losses is then minimized while the masked regions are ignored. Further details on
233   masking are provided in Appendix A.

      3     RNA HOMOLOGY AWARE DATA PIPELINE
237
238
      A clean split between training and test data is crucial for the success of deep learning training and
      obtaining a reliable model. The responsible data pipeline consists of data collection, pre-processing,
239
      filtering, and splitting. For RNA, this data splitting has to account for both sequence and structure
240
      similarity to determine homology and evolutionary conservation (Rivas, 2021), as issues of homol-
241   ogy contamination between training and test data are well-known (Rivas et al., 2012). In recent
242   years, several deep learning models have been proposed for RNA secondary structure prediction,
243   each providing state-of-the-art performance on various datasets (Singh et al., 2019; Chen et al.,
244   2020; Fu et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022; Chen & Chan, 2023; Jung et al., 2022; Tan et al., 2024).
245   However, their results are often misleading due to flawed data processing pipelines that consider
246   only sequence similarity, raising criticism and doubts within the community about the general capa-
247   bilities of deep learning methods to learn the underlying RNA folding process (Flamm et al., 2021;
248   Szikszai et al., 2022; Qiu, 2023). In this section, we outline our approach to preparing homology-
249
      aware RNA data splits. We first describe how we generate a synthetic dataset based on the notion
      of RNA families (Section 3.1), before we detail our strategy for splitting publicly available datasets
250
      into non-homologous subsets, considering both sequence and structure similarity (Section 3.2).
251
252
253
      3.1   FAMILY-BASED S YNTHETIC DATA G ENERATION
254
      To test our architecture, we construct a independent, synthetic dataset based on selected RNA fam-
255   ilies from the Rfam database version 14.9. Specifically, we sample sequences from the covariance
256   models of every RNA family and fold each of these sequences with RNAfold (Lorenz et al., 2011)
257   to obtain the secondary structure. We assign the sequences of 30 families to the test set, 25 to the
258   validation set, and the remaining 3796 families to the training set ensuring no overlap of families
259   between the datasets. The resulting datasets contain 410408, 2727, and 3344 samples for training,
260   validation, and testing, respectively. A more detailed description of the sampling process is provided
261   in Appendix B.1.

      split for experimental data that is independent of of the synthetic data used before.
267   Data Collection We collect a large training data pool from the following public sources: the bpRNA-
268   1m meta-database (Danaee et al., 2018), the ArchiveII (Sloma & Mathews, 2016) and RNAS-
269   trAlign (Tan et al., 2017) datasets provided by (Chen et al., 2020), all data from RNA-Strand (An-

      2019), downloaded on September 23, 2023. After removing redundant sequences, our initial data
271   pool consists of 107, 098 samples. We use the commonly used test sets TS1, TS2, TS3, and
272   TS-Hard, and the sets VL0, VL1, and another 50 randomly selected PDB samples for validation.
273   All four test sets as well as VL0 and VL1 are originally provided by (Singh et al., 2019) and (Singh
274   et al., 2021b). For our evaluations, we gather TS1, TS2, and TS3 into a single test set, TS-PDB,

276
      Data Pre-processing Secondary structures for PDB samples were derived from the 3D structure
277   information using DSSR (Lu et al., 2015). For NMR-solved structures, such as those in TS2, model-
278   1 structures were considered as the reference structure. For annotation of pseudoknots, we use
279   bpRNA (Danaee et al., 2018), while ignoring base multiplets.
280
      Data Filtering Our filtering pipeline consists of three steps: 1) We use CD-HIT-EST (Fu et al.,
281
      2012) to remove sequence similarity between training, validation, and test samples at the strictest
282
      threshold of 80%. 2) We perform a subsequent BLAST-N search (Altschul et al., 1997) to remove
283   hits from the training and validation data for every test sequence at a very high e-value of 10. 3)
284   We build covariance models, similar to those used for the RNA family database (Rfam) (Kalvari
285   et al., 2020), for every test sample considering sequence and structure similarity using LocaRNA-
286   P (Will et al., 2012) and Infernal (Nawrocki & Eddy, 2013), and remove every training and validation
287   sample with a hit against any of the covariance models at an e-value of 0.1. In line with the recent
288   literature, we apply a general length cutoff at 500 nucleotides to reduce computational costs during
289   training (Singh et al., 2019; 2021b; Fu et al., 2022). For a more detailed description of this step,
290   please see Appendix B.2.
291   Data Splitting For training the RNAformer, we curate three datasets: (1) Following the strictest data
292   processing pipeline used in previous work (Singh et al., 2021b), we curate a pre-training dataset of
293   66, 242 samples that is non-homologous with respect to TS-Hard, while considering sequence
294   similarity (80% similarity cutoff and BLAST-N search; see Appendix B.2) only for TS-PDB. This
295   procedure ensures that the RNAformer is comparable to all other methods after pre-training. (2)
296   To compare against AlphaFold 3, we create a non-filtered fine-tuning set, FT-Homolog, consist-
297
      ing of 4244 samples drawn from all PDB entries in the initial pool, using the same cutoff date for
      data selection as AlphaFold 3 (September 30, 2021) without considering homologies but exclud-
298
      ing sequences with an exact match in TS-PDB or TS-Hard. (3) Lastly, we create a fine-tuning
299
      set, FT-Non-Homolog, of 3432 PDB samples without sequence and structure similarity between
300   them and the test samples in TS-PDB and TS-Hard ensured by using our three-step-pipeline. We
301   provide an overview of the datasets in Appendix Table B.2.

504   5   D ISCUSSION & C ONCLUSION
505
506   Our experiments show that the RNAformer architecture can learn a biophysical model as indicated
507
      by very high F1 Scores of 0.967 when remodeling the predictions of RNAfold as well as nearly per-
      fect reconstruction of the sequence and structure features (see Table 1 and Table E.1, respectively).
508
      Furthermore, our approach overcomes the quadratic scaling behavior in the number of predicted
509
      base pairs with increasing sequence length that was reported previously for deep learning-based ap-
510   proaches, scaling linearly in the number of predicted base pairs. Since we learn our model across
511   families, we preclude that the strong performance results from learning data homologies, but rather,
512   that it indicates RNAformer has learned the underlying biophysical model of the RNAfold modeling
513   of the folding process.
514
      Our study for the predictions on experimentally derived structures with all types of base interac-
515
      tions revealed that fine-tuning appears to be crucial to achieving strong performance. Compared to
516   our pre-training model, the performance improved substantially after fine-tuning, resulting in state-
517   of-the-art performance while using much stricter criteria to avoid homology between training and
518   test data compared to previous work (Table 3). Notably, we observe that the RNAformer even out-
519   performs the current state-of-the-art homology modeling method SPOT-RNA2 without the use of
520   MSAs. The RNAformer is thus capable of predicting the structures of RNA for completely unseen
521   families. The importance of the data processing is further supported by our strong performance on
522   homologous data, outperforming AlphaFold 3.
523   On the downside, the additional performance of the RNAformer comes with a large memory re-
524   quirement due to the two-dimensional representation in the latent space. However, an inference step
525   is still very fast since it requires only one forward pass and advancements in tensor and graphic
526   processing units increase the accessibility to larger deep learning models despite increasing com-
527   putational demands. While the matrix representation of secondary structures enables deep learning
528   methods to predict all types of base pairs, the sparsity of base pairs in the matrix bears the risk
529   of incorrect classifications. The potential space of incorrect predictions scales quadratically with
530
      sequence length, while the number of base pairs only scales linearly. This achievement could be
      attributed to our loss masking technique which reduces the class imbalance. Nevertheless, in our
531
      experiments on synthetic data, the RNAformer strongly reduces the number of incorrectly predicted
532
      pseudoknots from roughly 50% reported in previous work to roughly 2% (Flamm et al., 2021).
533   For multiplets, this reduction is even stronger where RNAformer predicts roughly 5% of multiplets
534   compared to 75% reported previously (Flamm et al., 2021).
535
536
      Overall, our comprehensive analysis clearly demonstrates that our lean deep neural network archi-
      tecture achieves state-of-the-art performance on RNA secondary structure prediction without the
537
      need for additional features. The RNAformer overcomes the flaws reported for previous deep learn-
538
      ing based approaches, making it a strong alternative to commonly used, non-deep learning based
539   methods in the field.

      This section details the process of dataset creation used for learning the RNA biophysical model
835
      as well as the homology-aware secondary structure prediction. In the latter case, we consider both
836   sequence and structure similarity between the training and test sets to avoid data leakage due to
837   structural homology, which is recognized as a significant factor in the model’s performance. (Flamm
838   et al., 2021; Szikszai et al., 2022; Qiu, 2023)
839
840   B.1        S YNTHETIC DATASET
841
842   The synthetic dataset is created based on the Rfam database version 14.9 (Kalvari et al., 2020).
843   First, we select Rfam families with covariance model (CM) characterized by the maximum number
844   of matching position in the alignment (CLEN) of ≤ 500 and generate a large set of sequences from
845   CM of each selected family using Infernal. We then combine sampled sequences into the initial
846
      dataset in a way that guarantees two-thirds being generated from CMs with CLEN ≤ 200 and one-
      third from CMs with CLEN > 200, with the latter used to increase family diversity. To obtain
847
      the corresponding secondary structures, the sequences are folded using RNAfold. Prior to that, we
848
      apply a length cutoff at 200 nucleotides because RNAfold’s prediction accuracy drops on longer
849   sequences and this reduces computational costs. The resulting dataset contains 410408, 2727, and
850   3344 samples from 3796, 25, and 30 families for the training, validation, and test sets, respectively.
851   Further details can be found in Table B.1.
852
853   B.2        E XPERIMENTAL DATASET
854
855   A common way of ensuring a fair comparison between RNAformer and its competitors would be to
856   retrain and evaluate them on the same datasets. However, this would be computationally infeasible
857   and in some cases impossible due to the undisclosed training pipelines (Singh et al., 2019; 2021b;
858   Abramson et al., 2024). Hence, we propose an alternative strategy, in which we group methods
859   based on the level of homology between their training and test sets defined by the sequence and
860
      structure similarity.
861   Recent publications report three different ways of assessing similarity between sets and following
862   that, our data processing pipeline consists of three steps: 1) We use CD-HIT-EST (Fu et al., 2012) to
863   remove sequence similarity between training, validation, and test samples, 2) a subsequent BLAST-
      N search (Altschul et al., 1997) removes hits from the training and validation sets for any sequence


                                                             16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      in the test set, and 3) we implement a pipeline that considers sequence and structure similarity to
865   ensure non-homologous data splits.
866
867   Sequence Similarity We remove sequence similarity between the training, validation, and test
868   sets by applying CD-HIT-EST with a similarity cutoff at 80% between all sets. This pipeline is
869   commonly used in previous works (Singh et al., 2019; Sato et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2022; Chen et al.,
870   2022; Franke et al., 2022)
871
872   BLAST-N Search In addition to removing similar sequences via CD-HIT-EST, we apply a
873   BLAST-N-search (Altschul et al., 1997) at a high e-value of 10 to further remove training and val-
874   idation samples that are recognized by BLAST-N as hits for any of the test samples. This pipeline
875   was applied by SPOT-RNA and SPOT-RNA2 to further reduce sequence similarity (Singh et al.,
876   2019; 2021b).
877
878
      Covariance Models We use BLAST-N (Altschul et al., 1997) to search for homologs for each
      sample of the test sets TS-PDB and TS-Hard using NCBI’s nt database as a reference. We then
879
      create sequence- and structure-aware alignments using LocARNA-P (Will et al., 2012). For each
880
      of the resulting alignments, we build a covariance model using Infernal (Nawrocki & Eddy, 2013)
881   and remove training and validation samples with a hit to the covariance model at an e-value of
882   0.1. A similar data pipeline was used for SPOT-RNA2 (Singh et al., 2021b), however, in that work
883   the consensus structures for alignments were predicted using SPOT-RNA instead of an appropriate
884   sequence- and structure-based alignment tool like LocARNA-P.

       D    M ETRICS

       We employ commonly used measures for RNA secondary structure prediction: F1 score, MCC, and
975    F1-shift which are calculated based on a confusion matrix that describes the number of true positives
976    (TP), true negatives (TN), false positives (FP), and false negatives (FN) of a given prediction.
977
978    F1 score The F1 score describes the harmonic mean of precision (P R = T P/(T P + F P )) and
979    recall (RC = T P/(T P + F N ) written as F 1 = 2 · T P/(2 · T P + F P + F N ).
980
981    Matthews Correlation Coefficient           While the F1 score emphasizes positives, the MCC is a more
982    balanced measure defined as:
983
984                                         (T P · T N ) − (F P · F N )
985                   M CC = p                                                                                 (4)
                              (T P + F P ) · (T P + F N ) · (T N + F P ) · (T N + F N )
986
987
       F1-shift The F1-shift accounts for structural dynamics in RNAs (Mathews, 2019) and is computed
988    similarly to the F1 score with the difference that for a given pair (i, j) all pairs (i, j + 1), (i + 1, j),
989    (i, j − 1), and (i − 1, j) are also considered correct.

            WITH S EQUENCE L ENGTH

1029
       Using Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression, we observe that both the number of predicted
1030
       base pairs of the RNAformer and RNAfold, scale linearly with sequence length (Figure 3 a)) with
1031
       coefficients of 0.3251 and 0.3408 for RNAfold and the RNAformer, respectively. These results
1032   indicate a consistent increase in base pairs with longer sequences, albeit the RNAformer seems to
1033   slightly overestimate the number of base pairs as indicated by the slightly higher coefficient. The
1034   linear trend is further substantiated by high R2 -values of 0.819 and 0.818 for the RNAformer and
1035   RNAfold, respectively, demonstrating a strong goodness of fit of the linear models, which explain a
1036   substantial portion of the variance in the data. Importantly, the residual analysis of the RNAformer
1037   predictions (Figure 3 b)) revealed no heteroscedasticity as indicated by a non-significant p-value of
1038   0.152 using Levene’s test.
1039   Despite robust support for linearity, the residual distributions (Figure 3 c)) visually suggest nor-
1040   mality; however, this hypothesis is statistically rejected as indicated by very low Shapiro-Wilk test
1041   p-values for the RNAformer as well as RNAfold (near zero). Typically, this deviation from normal-
1042   ity could compromise the reliability of regression standard errors, influencing confidence intervals
1043   and hypothesis tests (Kutner et al., 2005). To address this, we employed a robust regression model
1044   using the Huber T norm with a MAD scale estimate, which reduces the influence of outliers and
1045   leverages points that could distort OLS regression estimations. This approach confirmed signifi-
1046
       cant linear terms (coefficients of 0.3392 for RNAformer and 0.3284 for RNAfold), closely aligning
       with OLS regression results and bolstering our confidence in the linear model despite potential data
1047
       anomalies.
1048
1049   Furthermore, we implemented a Generalized Linear Model (GLM) with a Poisson distribution, apt
1050   for modeling the count nature of base pairs which inherently accommodates their discrete and non-
1051   negative distribution. The Poisson GLM, with its log link function, facilitated a transformation align-
1052
       ing with the expected count data behavior, reinforcing a consistent linear relationship when back-
       transformed to the original scale (coefficients of 0.0109 for RNAformer and 0.0106 for RNAfold).
1053
       The excellent model fit, indicated by pseudo R2 values exceeding 0.93, validates the linear trend
1054
       across sequence lengths.
1055
1056   In conclusion, the comprehensive analysis employing OLS regression, robust regression, and Pois-
1057   son GLM robustly confirms the linear scalability of the RNAformer’s predicted number of base
1058
       pairs with sequence length, providing a compelling alternative to previous models that were flawed
       by quadratic scaling.
