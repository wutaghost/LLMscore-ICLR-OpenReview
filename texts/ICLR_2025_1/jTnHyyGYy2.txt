                                                  A BSTRACT
011
               Numerous crucial tasks in real-world decision-making rely on machine learning
012
               algorithms with calibrated uncertainty estimates. However, modern methods of-
013
               ten yield overconfident and uncalibrated predictions. Various approaches involve
014            training an ensemble of separate models to quantify the uncertainty related to the
015            model itself, known as epistemic uncertainty. In an explicit implementation, the
016            ensemble approach has high computational cost and high memory requirements.
017            This particular challenge is evident in state-of-the-art neural networks such as
018            transformers, where even a single network is already demanding in terms of com-
019            pute and memory. Consequently, efforts are made to emulate the ensemble model
020            without actually instantiating separate ensemble members, referred to as implicit
021            ensembling. We introduce LoRA-Ensemble, a parameter-efficient deep ensem-
022
               ble method for self-attention networks, which is based on Low-Rank Adaptation
               (LoRA). Initially developed for efficient LLM fine-tuning, we extend LoRA to an
023
               implicit ensembling approach. By employing a single pre-trained self-attention net-
024
               work with weights shared across all members, we train member-specific low-rank
025            matrices for the attention projections. Our method exhibits superior calibration
026            compared to explicit ensembles and achieves similar or better accuracy across
027            various prediction tasks and datasets.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Machine learning models are increasingly applied also in fields where incorrect estimates may have
033
      severe consequences, e.g., autonomous driving, medical diagnosis, (extreme) weather event predic-
      tion, or agricultural management decision support. In such applications well-calibrated predictive
034
      uncertainties are crucial to enable self-diagnosis. Uncertainty can be separated into two components.
035
      Aleatoric uncertainty, a.k.a. irreducible noise, is inherent in the data. Epistemic uncertainty on the
036   other hand stems from a lack of knowledge about certain regions of the input space, due to a lack of
037   training data (Der Kiureghian & Ditlevsen, 2009).
038
039
      Quantification of epistemic uncertainty in large machine learning models is non-trivial. Analytical
      computation is usually intractable, thus research has focused on efficient approximations (Graves,
040
      2011; Blundell et al., 2015; Welling et al., 2011). To date, probabilistic ensembles remain the
041
      best-performing approach (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). In a naïve implementation, such an
042   ensemble consists of multiple independently trained models. Individual models are interpreted as
043   Monte Carlo samples from the posterior weight space and are used to obtain an unbiased estimator of
044   the posterior distribution. To achieve a low correlation between ensemble members one can capitalize
045   on the stochastic nature of the training process and start from different initial weights, and/or sample
046   different random batches of data. The basic principle is that the predictions of different ensemble
047   members will agree near observed training samples, whereas they may vary far away from the training
048   data. Their spread therefore serves as a measure of epistemic uncertainty. Even small ensembles
049   often capture the uncertainty well (in expectation), i.e., they are well calibrated.
050   An issue with naïve ensembles is that their computational cost and memory footprint grow propor-
051   tionally to the number of ensemble members. For smaller models explicit ensembling may still be
052   feasible, albeit with higher financial cost and energy consumption. For modern neural networks
053   with up to several billion parameters, hardware restrictions render the naïve approach intractable, in
      particular, one can no longer hold the entire ensemble in memory. Consequently, a lot of research has

      gone into ways of creating ensembles implicitly, without requiring multiple copies of the full base
055   model (Wen et al., 2020; Wenzel et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2017; Turkoglu et al., 2022). Unfortunately,
056   most of these parameter-efficient ensembling techniques are not applicable to the newest generation
057   of neural networks. Transformer networks (Vaswani et al., 2017) have recently become popular due
058   to their superior ability to capture complex structures in data. However, implicit ensembling schemes
059   tend to underperform for transformers or are incompatible with them, as detailed in Appendix O.
060
      Several studies have shown that modern neural networks are heavily overparametrized and that their
061   results have low intrinsic dimension (Li et al., 2018a; Aghajanyan et al., 2020). This led Hu et al.
062   (2021) to propose Low-Rank Adaptations (LoRAs) as a way of deploying individually fine-tuned
063   Large Language Models (LLMs) to different tasks while avoiding the prohibitively large memory
064   and compute requirements of retraining them. It turns out that the weight matrices in such models
065   can be factorized to have very low rank, with hardly any loss in prediction performance.
066
      This led us to use LoRA as a basis for a novel, parameter-efficient ensemble method tailored to
067   transformer architecture. In line with the trend towards transfer learning, our method uses a pre-trained
068   transformer model, which is expanded into an implicit ensemble by varying the LoRA factorization,
069   while keeping backbone weights frozen. In this way, our method requires a small number of additional
070   parameters to turn an existing transformer model into a diverse ensemble whose performance across
071   various tasks is comparable to an Explicit Ensemble. In summary, our contributions are:
072
073          • We introduce LoRA-Ensemble, a parameter-efficient probabilistic ensemble method for
074
               self-attention networks.
075          • LoRA-Ensemble can be readily combined with most pre-trained transformer networks,
076            irrespective of their specific architecture and application domain: it simply replaces the
077            linear projection layers in the attention module with LoRA-Ensemble layers.
078          • We apply LoRA-Ensemble to different classification tasks including: conventional im-
079            age labeling, classification of skin lesions in dermatoscopic images, sound classification
080            from spectrograms, and out-of-distribution (OOD) detection. In these experiments, LoRA-
081            Ensemble not only consistently outperforms other implicit ensemble schemes but also,
082            surprisingly, its classification accuracy and uncertainty calibration are often even better than
083
               that of an Explicit Ensemble.
084          • We analyze the superior performance of LoRA-Ensemble compared to Explicit Ensemble
085            by exploring the diversity of ensemble members in both function and weight spaces.

087   2    L O RA-E NSEMBLE
088
089   The Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) technique makes it possible to use a pre-trained model and
090   fine-tune it without having to retrain all its parameters. This is particularly beneficial for modern
091   neural networks with large parameter spaces. The underlying principle is to freeze the pre-trained
092   model weights W0 ∈ Rk×d and instead constrain the updates to a low-rank decomposition. This can
093   be expressed mathematically as:
094
                                        W = W0 + ∆W = W0 + B ·A .                                          (1)
095
096   Here B ∈ Rk×r and A ∈ Rr×d are two trainable low-rank matrices, where r ≪ min(d, k). W and
097   ∆W are then multiplied with the same input x, which yields the following modified forward pass:
098
                                  h = W0 · x + ∆W · x = W0 · x + B ·A · x .                                (2)
099
100   LoRA applies this low-rank updating scheme only to weights in the self-attention modules of a
101   transformer model while leaving the interleaved MLP modules untouched. I.e., the weight matrices
102   being updated are Wq , Wk , and Wv for the query, key, and value of the attention mechanism, as well
103   as the Wo for merging the multi-head outputs. The former three are each treated as a single matrix,
104
      disregarding the fact that they are typically sliced into multiple attention heads. (Hu et al., 2021)
105   Although not designed with uncertainty calibration in mind, the LoRA concept fulfills all the
106   requirements of an implicit deep ensemble: By modifying the weights of the highly nonlinear
107   self-attention mechanism one is able to generate a diverse collection of networks with the same
      architecture and objective. By learning an additive, low-rank update ∆W = B·A rather than directly

139   2.1   I MPLEMENTATION
140
      In practice, our LoRA-Ensemble is implemented by replacing the respective linear layers (Wq , Wk ,
141
      Wv , and Wo ) in the pre-trained model architecture with custom LoRA modules.
142
143   As a backbone for experiments with image datasets, we employ a Vision Transformer (ViT) model
144   (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020). The chosen architecture is the base variant with patch size 32 × 32 as
145   defined in Dosovitskiy et al. (2020). We load the weights from torchvision, which were trained
146
      on ImageNet-1k (Deng et al., 2009), using a variant of the training recipe from Touvron et al. (2020),
      for details refer to their documentation.
147
148   The forward pass through the backbone is parallelized by replicating the input along the batch
149   dimension. In each LoRA module, the data is split into separate inputs per member and passed to the
150   respective member with the help of a vectorized map, which allows a parallelized forward pass even
151   through the LoRA modules. The outputs are then again stacked along the batch dimension. In this
152
      way, one makes efficient use of the parallelization on GPU, while at the same time avoiding loading
      the pre-trained backbone into memory multiple times.
153
154   As a backbone for audio experiments, we use the Audio Spectrogram Transformer (AST) back-
155   bone (Gong et al., 2021). That architecture was inspired by ViT (more specifically the data-efficient
156   version of ViT akin to DeiT (Touvron et al., 2020)) but is designed specifically for audio spectrograms.
157   Following Gong et al. (2021), we initialize the audio model weights by transferring and appropriately
158
      interpolating them from ImageNet pre-training. See Appendix I and J for details. As the AST version
      of LoRA-Ensemble would run into memory limits, we introduce chunking. While the forward pass
159
      through the backbone is still parallelized, the LoRA modules are called sequentially.1

171   3    E XPERIMENTS
172
173
      In the following section, we evaluate the proposed LoRA-Ensemble on several datasets with regard
174   to its predictive accuracy, uncertainty calibration, and memory usage. For each experiment we also
175   show 1-sigma error bars, estimated from five independent runs with different random initializations.
176
177
      As a first sandbox experiment, we perform image classification for the popular, widely used CIFAR-
      100 benchmark (Krizhevsky, 2009) (see Appendix A for CIFAR-10 experiment). The dataset consists
178
      of 100 object classes, each with 600 samples, for a total size of 60 000 images. From that set, 10 000
179
      images are designated test data, with all classes equally distributed between the training and testing
180   portions.
181
182   The HAM10000 dataset was proposed for the Human Against Machine with 10 000 training images
183
      study (Tschandl et al., 2018). It consists of 10 015 dermatoscopic images of pigmented skin lesions,
      collected from different populations. The dataset was initially assembled to compare machine
184
      learning methods against medical professionals on the task of classifying common pigmented skin
185
      lesions. Compared to CIFAR-100, this is arguably the more relevant test bed for our method: in the
186   medical domain, uncertainty calibration is critical, due to the potentially far-reaching consequences
187   of incorrect diagnoses and treatment planning.
188
189
      For both datasets, LoRA-Ensemble is compared against several baselines. As a sanity check, we
      always include results obtained with a single Vision Transformer (ViT) model, as well as with a
190
      single ViT model with LoRA in the attention modules. These models do not have a dedicated
191
      mechanism for uncertainty calibration, instead, the predicted class-conditional likelihoods are used to
192   quantify uncertainty. Furthermore, we compare to an explicit model ensemble, Monte Carlo Dropout
193   (MC Dropout) as implemented in Li et al. (2023) and a modified version of Snapshot Ensemble
194   (Huang et al., 2017), detailed in Appendix N. Snapshot Ensemble is the only well established implicit
195   ensembling technique that is architecture agnostic and can therefore be applied to self-attention
196   networks in a straightforward fashion. For implementation challenges of other implicit methods,
197   please refer to Appendix O. The LoRA rank was empirically set to 8 for CIFAR-100 and 4 for
198   HAM10000.
199   We evaluate predictive performance and calibration quality for each method using multiple metrics.
200   Predictive accuracy is assessed with classification accuracy (percentage of correctly classified test
201   samples) and the F1-score, which balances precision and recall. Calibration quality is measured using
202   the Expected Calibration Error (ECE), Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL), and Brier score. The ECE
203   quantifies the deviation from a perfectly calibrated model, i.e., one where the estimated uncertainty
204   of the maximum-likelihood class correctly predicts the likelihood of a miss-classification. Definitions
205   of all metrics are provided in Appendix P.
206   As a further benchmark from a different application domain, we process the ESC-50 environmental
207   sounds dataset (Piczak, 2015). It consists of 2000 sound samples, each five seconds long, that
208   represent 50 different semantic classes with 40 samples each. To prepare the raw input waveforms
209   for analysis, they are converted into 2-dimensional time/frequency spectrograms, see Gong et al.
210   (2021). These spectrograms form the input for the Audio Spectrogram Transformer, a state-of-the-art
211   transformer model for sound classification.
212   As for the ViT model, we train an Audio Spectrogram Transformer version of LoRA-Ensemble by
213   modifying the attention weights with different sets of LoRA weights. That ensemble is then compared
214   to a single instance of AST with and without LoRA, to an Explicit Ensemble of AST-models, and
215   to an MC Dropout variant of AST, similar to Li et al. (2023). For ESC-50 a LoRA rank of 16
      worked best, presumably due to the larger domain gap between (image-based) pre-training and the

      5   R ELATED W ORK
433
434
      Estimation of Epistemic Uncertainty A lot of work has gone into estimating the epistemic
435
      uncertainty in Artificial Neural Network (ANN). As the analytical computation of the posterior in
436   such models is generally intractable, methods for approximate Bayesian inference have been proposed.
437   Such methods rely on imposing an appropriate prior on the weights and using the likelihood of the
438   training data to get an approximate posterior of the weight space.
439
      The main techniques are, on the one hand, Variational Inference (Graves, 2011; Ranganath et al.,
440
      2014), which Blundell et al. (2015) have specialized to neural networks as Bayes by Backprop. And
441
      on the other hand variants of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) (Neal, 1996; Chen et al., 2014),
442   including Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) (Welling et al., 2011). These, however,
443   are often not able to accurately capture high-dimensional and highly non-convex loss landscapes, like
444   the ones usually encountered in deep learning (Gustafsson et al., 2019).
445
446   Ensembles and Implicit Ensembling Lakshminarayanan et al. (2017) have proposed a method
447   known as deep ensembles. It uses a set of neural networks with identical architecture that are
448   independently and randomly initialized, and (as usual) trained with variants of Stochastic Gradient
449   Descent (SGD). While the latter introduces further stochasticity, Fort et al. (2019a) have shown that
450   the initialization of the weights is more important to explore the admissible weight space. Ensemble
451   members will generally converge to different modes of the loss function, such that they can be
452   considered Monte Carlo samples of the posterior distribution (Wilson & Izmailov, 2020; Izmailov
453   et al., 2021). While ensembles, in general, yield the best results in terms of accuracy and uncertainty
454
      calibration, a straightforward implementation suffers from high memory and compute requirements,
      since multiple instances of the full neural network must be trained and stored. This can become
455
      prohibitive for modern neural networks with many millions, or even billions, of parameters.
456
457   Consequently, researchers have attempted to find ways of mimicking the principle of deep ensembles
458   without creating several full copies of the base model. Gal & Ghahramani (2015) have proposed
459   Monte Carlo Dropout, where the posterior is approximated by sampling different dropout patterns
460
      at inference time. While this is less expensive in terms of memory, performance is often worse.
      Masksembles (Durasov et al., 2020) are a variant that attempts to select suitable dropout masks in
461
      order to obtain better uncertainty estimates. Snapshot Ensembles (Huang et al., 2017) use cyclic
462
      learning rates to steer the learning process such that it passes through multiple local minima, which
463   are then stored as ensemble members. This reduces the training effort but does not address memory
464   requirements or inference time.
465
466
      Particularly relevant for our work are attempts that employ a shared backbone and modify only
      selected layers. Havasi et al. (2020) follow that strategy, in their case only the first and last layer
467
      of a neural network are replicated and trained independently to emulate an ensemble. Packed-
468
      Ensemble (Laurent et al., 2023) leverage grouped convolutions to train lightweight ensembles within
469   a single shared backbone. BatchEnsemble (Wen et al., 2020) is similar to LoRA-Ensemble in that
470   it also uses low-rank matrices to change the model parameters. More specifically, shared weight
471   matrices are modulated by element-wise multiplication with different rank-1 matrices to achieve
472   the behavior of a deep ensemble while adding only a small number of parameters. Wenzel et al.
473   (2020) take this concept further by also ensembling over different hyper-parameter settings. Turkoglu
474   et al. (2022) freeze all weights of the base model and instead vary the feature-wise linear modulation
475   (FiLM, Li et al., 2018b; Takeda et al., 2021).
476   A related concept was recently introduced for LLMs: the Mixtral of Experts model (Jiang et al., 2024)
477   averages over a sparse mixture of experts to efficiently generate text.
478
479
      Low-Rank Adaptation in Transformer Networks Low-Rank Adaptation was originally conceived
480   as a parameter-efficient way of fine-tuning Large Language Models (LLMs) (Hu et al., 2021). It
481   is based on the observation that, while modern neural networks have huge parameter spaces, the
482   solutions they converge to have much lower intrinsic dimension (Li et al., 2018b; Aghajanyan et al.,
483   2020). LoRA exploits this and Hu et al. (2021) show that even when fine-tuning only low-rank update
484   matrix B ·A (sometimes with rank as low as one or two), the resulting models are competitive with
485   much more expensive fine-tuning schemes. The method quickly became popular and has since also
      been extended with weight-decomposition (Liu et al., 2024). The Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) idea


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      has been applied in various fields, notably for denoising diffusion models (Luo et al., 2023; Golnari,
487   2023).
488
489   As we have shown, LoRA’s adaptation technique naturally lends itself to parameter-efficient en-
490
      sembling. We study the resulting ensemble for uncertainty calibration, a similar approach has
      concurrently been explored for the purpose of fine-tuning large language models (Wang et al., 2023)
491
      with promising results.

      6    D ISCUSSION & C ONCLUSION
496
497   On Effectiveness of LoRA-Ensemble Across diverse tasks, our experiments consistently show that
498   LoRA-Ensemble matches or surpasses the predictive performance of the state-of-the-art Explicit
499   Ensemble while offering superior calibration. Adding LoRA to a single model without any ensembling
500   improves calibration in most experiments beyond that of a 16-member Explicit Ensemble. This
501   effect may be linked to the well-documented over-parameterization of modern neural networks,
502
      which often achieve higher predictive accuracy at the cost of poorer calibration (e.g., Guo et al.,
      2017). By incorporating LoRA while treating all pre-trained weights as constants, we significantly
503
      reduce the trainable parameter space, potentially favoring better calibration. However, limiting
504
      trainable parameters alone does not ensure better accuracy or calibration, as forms of regularization or
505   selective training may fall short. The effectiveness of LoRA-Ensemble stems from its unique learning
506   dynamics, which we explore in Sec. 4 and Appendix D. LoRA-Ensemble members converge across a
507   broader area of the loss landscape, enabling better exploration of the weight space and more effective
508   capture of epistemic uncertainty. Increasing the number of members in the LoRA-Ensemble enhances
509   predictive power, potentially improving accuracy while maintaining good calibration due to the
510   limited number of trainable weights. However, if the trainable weights are not kept limited (e.g., by
511   increasing the LoRA rank), calibration can worsen, as demonstrated in Fig.8a and Tab.7. This effect
512   aligns with findings by Shuttleworth et al. (2024), which indicate that excessively increasing the rank
513
      in LoRA can cause it to lose its unique learning dynamics. Conversely, enhancing predictive power
      by increasing the pre-trained weights (while keeping trainable weights constant) further improves the
514
      effectiveness of the LoRA-Ensemble, see Appendix B.
515
516   Limitations We propose a parameter-efficient ensembling method that performs well in the conducted
517   experiments. While we did not evaluate LoRA-Ensemble on very large datasets, such as those often
518   found in natural language processing, it would be interesting to explore its performance on such
519
      datasets. Similarly, evaluating its effectiveness on Large Language Models would be valuable,
      given their increasing popularity. Although our method addresses the restrictive memory usage of
520
      traditional ensembles, it does not reduce computational complexity, as data must still pass through
521
      the model once per batch. Additionally, approximate inference on the parameter distribution of the
522   LoRA matrices could allow drawing an infinite number of ensemble members from the approximate
523   posterior.
524
525
      Future Work As discussed by Rahaman & Thiery (2020), our work also suggests that in a high-
      parameter regime, deep ensembles may not exhibit the same behavior as they do in a low-parameter
526
      regime, where they typically improve calibration properties. We have previously witnessed this type
527
      of phase shift in bias-variance trade-off for large neural networks akin to Double Descent Phenomena
528   (Nakkiran et al., 2021). It would be valuable to conduct an in-depth analysis of deep ensemble
529   behavior in high-parameter regimes, while also considering data size, model size, and compute.
530
531
      Conclusion We have presented LoRA-Ensemble, a novel, parameter-efficient method for probabilistic
      learning that is tailored to the transformer architecture (and potentially other architectures that make
532
      use of the attention mechanism). LoRA-Ensemble uses a simple, but efficient trick to turn a single base
533
      model into an implicit ensemble: the weights of the base model are kept frozen, but are modulated
534   with the Low-Rank Adaptation mechanism. By training multiple, stochastically varying instances of
535   the low-rank matrices that define the modulation, one obtains a diverse set of ensemble members
536   that share the majority of their weights (specifically, those of the base model) and introduces only
537   minimal overhead through the coefficients of their individual low-rank matrices. Our experiments on
538   two different computer vision tasks, a sound classification task, and an OOD detection task show that
539   the proposed approach can outperform other, implicit as well as explicit, ensembling strategies in
      terms of both classification performance and uncertainty calibration.

      increasing rank the classification accuracy increases while the calibration deteriorates, in other words,
957

958

959   calibration, even at the cost of slightly lower classification accuracy.

962

964   This observation aligns with the findings of Rahaman & Thiery (2020), as LoRA-Ensemble continues
965   to exhibit under-confidence even at higher ranks. Increasing model complexity enhances confidence,
966   thereby improving calibration. However, at rank 32, the calibration of a single network augmented
967   with LoRA begins to deteriorate, suggesting that a critical boundary has been reached. Beyond this
968
      point, the parameter space becomes insufficiently constrained, leading to effects similar to those
      observed by Guo et al. (2017).

991    This section expands on Sec. 4, which examines the diversity of ensemble members in function and
992    weight space for LoRA-Ensemble and Explicit Ensemble, showing that LoRA-Ensemble exhibits
993    greater diversity in both spaces. While Sec. 4 focuses on value projection matrices due to their role in
994    learned representations, this section examines query and key projection matrices, too. In Fig. 9, we
995    observe that LoRA-Ensemble achieves greater diversity in query and key projection matrices, similar
996    to the diversity observed in value projection matrices (Fig. 3).
997    Using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), a weight matrix W ∈ Rm×n is decomposed as:
998
999                                                              W = U ΣV ⊤ ,
1000
       where U ∈ Rm×m and V ∈ Rn×n are orthonormal matrices representing rotational components,
1001
       and Σ ∈ Rm×n is a diagonal matrix of singular values capturing the scaling effect. Singular vectors
1002   linked to larger singular values highlight key transformations encoded by W .
1003
1004   In Fig. 10, we analyze the differences in weight updates between ensemble methods by computing
1005
       the Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of pre-trained and trained weights for ensemble members.
       Singular vectors corresponding to the top singular values (16 are shown) are extracted and compared
1006
       using cosine similarity to evaluate changes in the weight structure. These similarities are averaged
1007
       across layers and ensemble members. The results highlight distinct parameter update patterns between
1008   LoRA-Ensemble and Explicit Ensemble. LoRA-Ensemble introduces new high-ranking singular
1009   vectors, referred to as "intruder dimensions" (Shuttleworth et al., 2024), which are nearly orthogonal
1010   to the singular vectors of the pre-trained weights. The number of intruder dimensions depends on
1011   the LoRA rank. This effect is particularly pronounced in the value projection matrices, which aligns
1012   with their strong association with learned representations. In contrast, Explicit Ensemble members
1013   tend to preserve a structure closely aligned with the spectral properties of the pre-trained weights.
1014   This alignment is especially evident in the key and query projection matrices, which exhibit a strong
1015   resemblance to the original spectral structure.
1016   We further analyze the B · A matrices learned by different ensemble members. Due to their random
1017   initialization, these matrices explore diverse directions in weight space. In Fig. 11, we plot the
1018   largest eigenvalues of these matrices (with only four non-zero eigenvalues as the LoRA rank is set
1019   to 4) and the similarity between the corresponding eigenvectors across ensemble members. The
1020   similarities are averaged over layers and member pairs. The results show that while the eigenvalues
1021   across members follow a similar trend, the eigenvectors are largely uncorrelated. This indicates
1022
       that ensemble members explore different regions of weight space while maintaining similar overall
       transformations. The shared eigenvalue trends suggest consistent semantic contributions across
1023
       members, while the dissimilar eigenvectors highlight the diversity in their learned representations.

1477   N     S NAPSHOT E NSEMBLE I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
1478
1479   Snapshot Ensemble Huang et al. (2017), in its pure form, consists of training a single model with
1480   cycling learning and taking snapshots every few epochs. This can make it hard, however, for the
1481
       model to converge to anything meaningful within the low number of epochs available for training
       per snapshot. Therefore, Snapshot Ensemble was modified slightly, by first letting training run for a
1482
       number of epochs, without any cycling of the learning rate. After this burn-in period the learning rate
1483
       is at 0 and a first snapshot is taken. The remaining number of epochs is split evenly. If the remaining
1484   number of epochs is not divisible by the desired number of ensemble members, the burn-in period is
1485   extended until it is. For the HAM10000 dataset training is left at 65 epochs, with 20 burn-in epochs.
1486   For CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 using only 16 epochs would only leave 1 epoch per cycle for bigger
1487   models. Therefore, training is extended to 30 epochs with a burn-in period of 15 epochs.

       Many implicit ensemble methods, such as those proposed in Wen et al. (2020); Turkoglu et al. (2022);
1492   Durasov et al. (2020); Havasi et al. (2020), are architecture-specific and predominantly designed
1493   for MLPs or CNNs. As a result, adapting these techniques to transformer architectures presents
1494   significant challenges, since transformers’ computation structure is quite different than MLPs and
1495   CNNs.
1496
       In particular, we attempted to implement FiLM-Ensemble Turkoglu et al. (2022) on a self-attention
1497
       network, given the promising results reported by its authors. However, the authors themselves noted
1498   that applying FiLM-Ensemble to transformers is not straightforward, mainly because transformers rely
1499   on LayerNorm, whereas FiLM-Ensemble was developed with BatchNorm in mind. Our experiments
1500   confirmed that directly using BatchNorm in transformers led to notable performance degradation.
1501   We explored several approaches to adapt LayerNorm, but the most effective results were achieved
1502   by fixing all affine parameters for each ensemble member. This allowed for slight initial variations
1503   to introduce randomness and diversity, while keeping the variation among members minimal. The
1504   results, summarized in Tab. 15, show that increasing the ensemble size slightly improved accuracy,
1505   though the Expected Calibration Error (ECE) fluctuated without consistent improvement. In fact,
1506
       when using larger ensemble sizes, such as 8 or 16, both accuracy and calibration worsened across all
       settings we tested.
