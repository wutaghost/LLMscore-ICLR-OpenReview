011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recently, the emergence of 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) has made real-time
014            and high-quality rendering possible. However, it is still challenging for 3DGS to
015
               reconstruct accurate geometry surfaces and achieve higher-quality rendering. To
               address these challenges, we propose to leverage multi-view geometry consistency
016
               for 3DGS and surface reconstruction. We reveal that there exists multi-view ge-
017
               ometry inconsistency in 3DGS, preventing 3DGS from achieving higher-quality
018            rendering and accurate surface reconstruction. To mitigate the geometry incon-
019            sistency, we first develop a multi-view photometric consistency regularization to
020            constrain the rendered depth of 3DGS, which helps establish more stable and con-
021            sistent 3D Gaussians to facilitate both rendering and surface reconstruction. To
022            reconstruct geometry surfaces from 3DGS, we introduce a neural Signed Dis-
023            tance Function (SDF) field to represent continuous geometries of 3DGS. Then,
024            we propose a geometry consistency-based SDF learning strategy, which leverages
025            multi-view geometry consistency cues from 3DGS to efficiently optimize the SDF
026
               field for surface reconstruction. Extensive experiments on various datasets demon-
               strate that our method achieves both high-quality rendering and accurate surface
027
               reconstruction while keeping a good efficiency. Our code will be released upon
028
               publication.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   With the development of neural rendering Mildenhall et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2020) and implicit
034   fields Park et al. (2019); Mescheder et al. (2019), realizing Novel View Synthesis (NVS) and sur-
035   face reconstruction in a unified framework has become an important topic in computer vision and
036   graphics. Neural Radiance Field (NeRF) Mildenhall et al. (2020); Barron et al. (2021); Fridovich-
037   Keil et al. (2022); Müller et al. (2022) methods represent 3D scenes as a color field and density
038   field and leverage neural volume rendering Curless & Levoy (1996) to optimize these fields. After
039
      training, these methods can not only synthesize novel views but also reconstruct geometry surfaces
      from the density field. In fact, the density field is an approximation of actual geometry, thus usually
040
      leading to noisy and biased geometry reconstructions. Some pioneering methods Wang et al. (2021);
041
      Yariv et al. (2021) introduce Signed Distance Function (SDF) fields to represent actual geometry and
042   derive an SDF-to-density transformation for neural volume rendering. These methods have demon-
043   strated impressive surface reconstruction. However, the massive field query used in neural volume
044   rendering severely compromises real-time rendering capabilities.
045
      Recently, the emergence of 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) Kerbl et al. (2023) has made real-time
046
      and high-quality rendering possible. This can be attributed to the fact that 3DGS represents 3D
047
      scenes as explicit and discrete 3D Gaussian primitives and performs α-blending with differentiable
048   rasterization. However, the above two characteristics also pose challenges to reconstruct accurate
049   geometry surfaces and achieve higher-quality rendering due to the following reasons: 1) Discrete
050   geometry representations typically result in unstable optimization of Gaussian point distribution and
051   incomplete geometry reconstructions. As shown in the top of Figure 1(a), the Gaussian points of
052   3DGS are almost distributed over the object while are very sparse in textureless regions. 2) Point-
053   based α-blending in differentiable rasterization causes most learned 3D Gaussians to deviate from
      true geometry surfaces. This makes the reconstructed surface from rendered depths of 3DGS noisy

      In summary, our main contributions are as follows:
091
092   • We reveal that there exists multi-view geometry inconsistency in 3DGS, which prevents 3DGS
093     from achieving higher-quality rendering and reconstructing accurate geometry surfaces (Sec. 3.1).
094   • We develop a multi-view photometric consistency regularization to explicitly constrain the geom-
095     etry of 3DGS, which helps establish more stable and consistent 3D Gaussians to facilitate both
096     rendering and reconstruction (Sec. 3.2).
097   • We propose a geometry consistency-based SDF learning strategy for 3DGS, which leverages
098     multi-view geometry consistency cues derived from 3DGS to efficiently optimize SDF fields for
099     surface reconstruction (Sec. 3.3).
100   • Extensive experiments on indoor and large-scale outdoor datasets verify that our method is ca-
101     pable of achieving high-quality rendering and reconstruction in a unified framework for 3DGS
102     while keeping a good efficiency (Sec. 4).

155
      NVS and accurate surface reconstruction. We represent the radiance field with 3D Gaussians and the
156   geometry field with an SDF, synthesizing novel views through splatting rendering of 3D Gaussians
157   and extracting the surface using the zero-level set of SDF. During training, our goal is to optimize

160   reconstruction (Sec. 3.1). Then we develop a multi-view photometric consistency regularization
161   to mitigate this inconsistency (Sec. 3.2). Finally, we design a geometry consistency-based SDF
      learning for surface reconstruction (Sec. 3.3). An overview of our approach is shown in Figure 3.

273   Geometry assisted Gaussian densification. 3DGS initializes Gaussians with sparse point clouds

279

      3.3   G EOMETRY CONSISTENCY- BASED SDF LEARNING FOR 3DGS
300
301   As the 3DGS is designed for the NVS task, it does not explicitly model the surface geometry of the
302   scene. Thus, it typically needs to use Poisson reconstruction Kazhdan et al. (2006) or TSDF fusion
303   Curless & Levoy (1996) as a post-processing step to recover the surface mesh Guédon & Lepetit
304   (2023); Huang et al. (2024), which is not efficient and unable to guarantee accuracy of recovered
305   geometry. To this end, we introduce a neural SDF to explicitly model the surface geometry and
306   exploit the geometric information derived from the 3DGS to constrain the learning of the SDF. We
307   propose a two-stage training strategy for efficient SDF learning: the first stage leverages the depth
308   rendered by 3DGS to guide the SDF initialization, and the second stage incorporates the geometry
309
      consistency-aware regularization to refine the learned SDF.

340   Geometry-consistent SDF refinement. After initialization, the basic structure of the scene is
341   learned. To promote the geometric consistency of learned SDF and recover the delicate details
342   of the scene, the geometric consistency constraint is introduced in the SDF refinement stage.
343
      Instead of using priors to constrain the learning of zero-level sets of SDF, the multi-view photometric
344
      consistency loss Fu et al. (2022) is used to optimize the learned SDF. Following Fu et al. (2022), we
345
      locate the zero-level set of SDF to obtain the surface point x̂. Based on the depth dˆ of x̂ and normal
346
      n̂ computed with automatic differentiation of geometry network fθ at x̂, by adopting Eq. (5) - (7),
347   the multi-view photometric consistency loss LphotoSDF for the optimization of SDF is obtained. In
348   this stage, the depth rendered by the 3DGS are still utilized to constrain the learning of all level sets
349   of SDFs.
350
      It is worth noting that unlike previous implicit surface reconstruction methods Yariv et al. (2021);
351
      Wang et al. (2021) that require color fields to drive SDF learning, our Geo-3DGS utilizes geometric
352
      consistency information obtained from 3DGS to guide SDF learning. This not only ensures accuracy
353   of recovered geometry and improves SDF training speed, but also achieves high fidelity real-time
354   rendering based on 3DGS.

389   4.1     E XPERIMENTAL SETTING
390
391   Datasets. We evaluate our Geo-3DGS performance on DTU Aanæs et al. (2016) and Tanks and
392   Temples Knapitsch et al. (2017) datasets. The DTU dataset contains 15 scenes with images of
393   resolution 1600 × 1200. We utilize COLMAP Schonberger & Frahm (2016) to calculate a sparse
394   point cloud for each scene and retain the original resolution for training. For the Tanks and Temples
395   dataset, we conduct comparison on 6 scenes and adopt down-sampled images with a resolution of
396   960 × 540 for efficiency. Additionally, we use the evaluation mode defined in 3DGS to split the
397
      available images of each scene into training and test set. We reconstruct mesh based on the training
      split and compare the rendering performance on the test split. For a fair comparison, we employ the
398
      same dataset setting to retrain the baseline methods based on 3DGS.
399
400   Baselines. We compare our method with SOTA implicit methods (NeRF Mildenhall et al. (2020),
401   VolSDF Yariv et al. (2021), NeuS Wang et al. (2021), Geo-Neus Fu et al. (2022) and Neuralangelo Li
402   et al. (2023)) and explicit methods (3DGS Kerbl et al. (2023), SuGaR Guédon & Lepetit (2023),
403   2DGS Huang et al. (2024) and Scaffold-GS Lu et al. (2023)). Note that, these implicit methods
404   input all images for each scene to reconstruct surface meshes. Thus, we do not compare our NVS
405   performance with these methods.
406
      Implementation details. We build our Geo-3DGS upon the open-source 3DGS code and adopt
407
      custom CUDA kernels to output images and depth maps for our presented multi-view geometry
408
      consistency learning. For the mesh extraction from 3DGS and 2DGS, we also adopt the TSDF used
409   in 2DGS Huang et al. (2024), with setting the voxel size to 0.004 and the truncated threshold to
410   0.02. We modify the renderer of 3DGS to generate depth maps. All experiments are conducted on
411   an NVIDIA 3090 GPU.

522    5                    C ONCLUSION
523
524    We have proposed Geo-3DGS, a new method to leverage multi-view geometry consistency for 3DGS
525    and surface reconstruction. In our paper, we first reveal that there exists multi-view geometry incon-
526    sistency in 3DGS which prevents 3DGS from achieving high-quality rendering and reconstruction.
527    Based on this insight, we develop a multi-view photometric consistency regularization to mitigate
528    this inconsistency, thereby facilitating both rendering and reconstruction. Moreover, we design a
529    geometry consistency-based SDF learning to extract meshes for 3DGS. This allows our method
530    to exploit the geometric information derived from 3DGS for efficient SDF learning. In this way,
531    our method achieves both high-quality rendering and accurate surface reconstruction in a unified
532
       framework for 3DGS while keeping a good efficiency.

711
712   A.1   W HY USING SDF TO LEARN SURFACES FOR 3DGS
713
714   The Gaussian points of 3DGS are discrete, sparse and far away from the surface. This makes di-
715   rectly extracting surface (e.g., Poisson surface reconstruction used in SuGaR) from these points very
716   difficult. In contrast, SDF field is a continuous representation that can extract surface from the zero-
717   level set. Therefore, we introduce the SDF field to learn continuous surfaces for 3DGS. In addition,
718
      recently, 2DGS introduces TSDF fusion to fuse depth maps rendered by 3DGS to reconstruct sur-
      face and achieves promising results. However, the used TSDF fusion is usually limited by fusion
719
      resolution. The SDF field can be flexibly used to extract surfaces with different resolutions. Also,
720
      the geometry constraints of 2DGS sacrifice rendering quality to some extent, as shown in Table 3.
721   Therefore, we introduce the SDF field to decouple the geometry for 3DGS, avoiding affecting the
722   rendering quality of 3DGS. This also makes our geometry can be used independently for down-
723   stream tasks. Moreover, the SDF field represented by MLP or hash grids is usually lightweight
724   compared to the heavy 3DGS representation. Therefore, combining SDF with 3DGS is promising
725   in practice.
726
727
728   A.2   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
729
730   Our Geo-3DGS is implemented by PyTorch Paszke et al. (2019) with the Adam optimizer Kingma
731   & Ba (2014). Our geometry network fθ contains the hash grid Müller et al. (2022) with 16 levels
732   and 2 feature channels per level, and 2-layer MLPs with a hidden size 64. The geometry network
733   is initialized by geometric initialization Atzmon & Lipman (2020). Position encoding Mildenhall
734   et al. (2020) is applied to query points. Following 3DGS Kerbl et al. (2023), we train our Geo-3DGS
735   for 30k iterations. The multi-view photometric consistency regularization is imposed during 7k and
736   9k iterations. The geometry assisted Gaussian densification is applied at the 9k-th iteration. For our
737   geometry consistency-based SDF learning, the 3DGS-driven SDF initialization is implemented dur-
738
      ing 9k to 12k iterations. Then, the geometry-consistent SDF refinement is applied for 3k iterations
      on DTU and 4k iterations on Tanks and Temples. We sample 4096 pixel rays in a mini-batch. In our
739
      loss function, all weights {λi }9i=1 are set the same for all scenes. Specifically, λ1 = 0.2, λ2 = 0.5,
740
      λ3 = 0.05, λ4 = 0.01, λ5 = 0.5, λ6 = 0.25, λ7 = 0.25, λ8 = 1.0, λ9 = 0.01. Besides, in
741   our multi-view geometry consistency check, ϵdiff and ϵreproj are set to 0.01 and 1, respectively. After
742   training, the triangle mesh is extracted from the geometry network by Marching Cube Lorensen &
743   Cline (1987). The extraction resolution is set to 5123 for DTU and 20483 for Tanks and Temples.

856   A.6               L IMITATION AND FUTURE WORK
857
858   Our proposed Geo-3DGS follows 3DGS to use spherical harmonics for view-dependent appearance
859   modeling. This may limit its NVS performance for scenes with reflections. Compared with Neu-
860   ralangelo Li et al. (2023), our method still has room for improvement in surface reconstruction.
861   Incorporating the numerical gradients-based training and coarse-to-fine SDF training strategy from
862   Neuralangelo into our method may further improve our surface reconstruction. In addition, it will
863   be interesting to explore our proposed geometry consistency strategies based on 2DGS Huang et al.
      (2024).
