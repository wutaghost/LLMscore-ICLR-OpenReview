009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large vision-language models (LVLMs) have witnessed significant progress on
012            visual understanding tasks. However, they often prioritize language knowledge
013            over image information on visual reasoning tasks, incurring performance degrada-
014            tion. To tackle this issue, we first identify the drawbacks of existing solutions (i.e.,
015            insufficient and irrelevant visual descriptions, and limited multi-modal capacities).
016            We then decompose visual reasoning process into two stages: visual perception
017            (i.e., eyesight) and textual reasoning (i.e., wisdom), and introduce a novel visual
018            reasoning framework named P RO R EASON. This framework features multi-run
019
               proactive perception and decoupled vision-reasoning capabilities. Briefly, given a
               multi-modal question, P RO R EASON iterates proactive information collection and
020
               reasoning until the answer can be concluded with necessary and sufficient visual
021
               descriptions. Notably, the disassociation of capabilities allows seamless integra-
022            tion of existing large language models (LLMs) to compensate for the reasoning
023            deficits of LVLMs. Our extensive experiments demonstrate that P RO R EASON out-
024            performs both existing multi-step reasoning frameworks and passive peer methods
025            on a wide range of benchmarks for both open-source and closed-source models.
026            In addition, with the assistance of LLMs, P RO R EASON achieves a performance
027            improvement of up to 15% on MMMU benchmark. Our insights into existing so-
028            lutions and the decoupled perspective for feasible integration of LLMs illuminate
029            future research on visual reasoning techniques, especially LLM-assisted ones.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   In recent years, large language models (LLMs) (Dubey et al., 2024; Team et al., 2023; Jiang et al.,
035   2023) have experienced explosive growth in their capabilities, driving significant advancements
036   across various fields (Shao et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024). This progress has
037   also sparked interest in developing large vision-language models (LVLMs) (Chen et al., 2024a; Bai
038   et al., 2023), which, like LLaVA (Li et al., 2024b), have achieved remarkable performance in multi-
039
      modal understanding tasks. However, state-of-the-art (SOTA) LVLMs still struggle to integrate vi-
      sual understanding with textual reasoning simultaneously due to inherent modality differences and
040
      deficient training data. For example, Ghosh et al. (2024) demonstrate that LVLMs often rely more on
041
      their prior language knowledge, neglecting visual information in multi-modal reasoning tasks such
042   as visual chart understanding and math reasoning, resulting in performance degradation. Figure 2
043   illustrates a typical case of this issue, where the reasoning process remains irrelevant to the image.
044
045
      To address the challenges, a promising solution is to extract visual information from images into
      textual form to assist LVLMs in reasoning (Yin et al., 2023b; Mitra et al., 2024). For example, Ghosh
046
      et al. (2024) instruct LVLMs to generate fine-grained captions to facilitate the subsequent reasoning
047
      process. However, these existing methods exhibit two primary limitations: (i) The visual extraction
048   process is question-agnostic and reasoning-free, that the image description is not targeted for a
049   given question, and reasoning is not involved to deduce extra information for better descriptions.
050   This drawback, termed as “passive”, results in irrelevant or insufficient information, and subsequent
051   performance degradation. (ii) The reasoning process entangles visual understanding with textual
052   reasoning abilities of a single LVLM. As mentioned above, the limited capabilities of LVLMs do
053   not always successfully handle both abilities and produce high-quality reasoning. Hence, both of
      these limitations hinder the performance of LVLMs in multi-modal reasoning tasks.

054
      To address both shortcomings, we propose a novel multi-step multi-modal reasoning framework
055   named P RO R EASON, which features proactive (i.e., question-oriented and reasoning-involved) vi-
056   sual extraction and disentangled vision-reasoning capabilities. Specifically, we first decouple multi-
057   modal reasoning capacity into two sub-tasks: visual perception (i.e., eyesight) and textual reason-
058   ing (i.e., wisdom). The former focuses on understanding images and converting visual details into
059   textual form, while the latter integrates the gathered information to draw final conclusions. As il-
060   lustrated in Figure 1, our framework can be divided into three stages: Action, Judgment, and
061   Summary, with each involving different agents. In the Action stage, a Dispatcher selectively en-
062   gages a Vision Expert to capture additional visual information, or a Reasoning Expert to derive
063
      more insights. Unlike passive methods, all three agents operate based on the given question and
      current descriptions, effectively avoiding information redundancy or insufficiency. Subsequently, a
064
      Referee in the Judgment stage evaluates the sufficiency of known information, deciding whether to
065
      return to the Action stage or proceed to the Summary stage, where all information is consolidated
066   for a Summarizer to generate the final answer. Notably, the disentangled vision-reasoning does not
067   require vision-irrelevant roles (i.e., Reasoning Expert, Referee, and Summarizer) to be performed
068   by LVLMs, enabling the seamless integration of existing LLMs with proven strong reasoning abili-
069   ties (Chang et al., 2024), thereby addressing the limitations of LVLMs.
070
      Empirically, we evaluate P RO R EASON across four challenging visual reasoning benchmarks with
071
      both open-source and closed-source models. Extensive experiments demonstrate that P RO R EASON
072   achieves significant and consistent performance improvement over both existing reasoning frame-
073   works and state-of-the-art passive methods across all the benchmarks, with a peak enhancement
074   as 13.2%. Besides, our comparative analysis highlights the necessity and relative importance of
075   each sub-agent, as well as the advantages of proactive information acquisition. With the verified
076   superiority over the simultaneous inherent usage of both capabilities, the decoupled perspective not
077   only demonstrates that textual reasoning capabilities outweigh visual understanding for multi-modal
078   reasoning tasks, but also exemplifies the feasibility of LLM-assisted LVLM reasoning, exhibiting
079   performance improvements of up to 15% on the MMMU benchmarks (Yue et al., 2023).
080   The main contributions of this work are threefold:
081
082          • We propose a novel multi-modal reasoning framework named P RO R EASON, featuring it-
083            erative proactive perception and decoupled vision-reasoning capabilities.
084
             • Empirically, we identify the drawbacks of existing solutions (i.e., insufficient and irrelevant
085            visual descriptions, and limited multi-modal capacities), and validate the successful miti-
086            gation of these issues with P RO R EASON by the superior performance over peer methods.
087
088          • P RO R EASON showcases the remarkable feasibility of integrating LLMs for boosted visual
089
               reasoning, illuminating the potential for LLM-assisted LVLM reasoning in future research.

      3        M ETHOD
212
213   As illustrated in Figure 1, P RO R EASON consists of five functionally distinct yet inter-cooperative
214   sub-agents, along with a Memory component. The entire workflow comprises three steps: Action,
215   Judgment, and Summary, effectively integrating the proactive visual understanding (i.e., eyesight)
      and textual reasoning (i.e., wisdom) capacities for enhanced multi-modal reasoning performance.


                                                                                                   4

      3.1   ACTION S TEP
217
218   Action step is the core of question-oriented visual information extraction, driven by three sub-agents:
219   Dispatcher, Vision Expert, and Reasoning Expert. The Dispatcher orchestrates the workflow, selec-
220   tively directing the Vision Expert to capture specific visual information, or instructing the Reasoning
221   Expert to analyze known information to derive more. The responses from both the Vision Expert
222   and the Reasoning Expert are stored in a textual Memory component.
223
      Formally, given an image I and its corresponding textual question Q, the Dispatcher decides to con-
224   sult the Vision Expert or Reasoning Expert, based on the analysis of Q and the known information
225   in the Memory (if not empty). The Dispatcher then generates a query q for the chosen expert. If
226   the Vision Expert is selected, it takes the image I and query q as input, and generates an answer
227   Av , which is then stored in the Memory. When the Reasoning Expert is selected, it provides a re-
228   sponse consisting of the reasoning process and the final answer Ar based on the query q and known
229   information in the Memory, before only Ar is stored in Memory. Notably, the Memory component
230   allows P RO R EASON to keep compact information, and avoids lengthy reasoning traces like CoT and
231   ReAct, thereby suffering less from redundant information.
232
233
      3.2   J UDGMENT S TEP
234
235   Judgment step is conducted by a sub-agent called Referee, which evaluates whether the information
236   stored in Memory is sufficient to answer the question Q. The input to the Referee includes the
237   question Q and available information in the Memory. If the Memory contains adequate information
238   to answer the question Q, the Referee outputs the identifier “SOLVABLE”; otherwise, it outputs
239   “UNSOLVABLE”. If the Referee’s output is SOLVABLE, the workflow precedes to the Summary
240   phase. Conversely, if the output is UNSOLVABLE, the Action phase is re-executed to gather more
241   necessary information. This allows the Referee to collaborate closely with the three sub-agents in the
242   Action step, enabling the framework to proactively acquire the necessary information and prevent
243
      omissions, thereby overcoming the drawbacks of passive methods.
244
245   3.3   S UMMARY S TEP
246
247   The Summary step focuses on integrating the available information in the Memory, and providing
248   the final answer to the question Q. This step is mainly powered by a sub-agent called Summarizer.
249   Once the Referee determines that the information in the Memory is sufficient to address the question
250   Q, and outputs the identifier SOLVABLE, the Summarizer will be called to draw a conclusion, based
251   on the Memory information. This conclusion represents the answer to the question Q of the whole
252   framework, and will be evaluated by the performance metrics.
253
254   3.4   A DVANTAGES OF P RO R EASON
255
256   Reduced information Mission or Redundancy. With the close collaboration of the Dispatcher,
257   Vision Expert, Reasoning Expert, and Referee agents, P RO R EASON can proactively (i.e., question-
258   orientedly and reasoning-involvedly) extract the necessary and sufficient visual details from images,
259   effectively avoiding information omission or redundancy. Meanwhile, the Memory component re-
260   tains only the image captions from the Vision Expert and the reasoning results from the Reasoning
261   Expert, providing a compact textual descriptions. This contributes to minimize the interference of
262   irrelevant information on the subsequent Summarizer.
263
264   LLM-assisted Multi-modal Reasoning. In P RO R EASON, the complete multi-modal reasoning
265   process is decomposed into visual perception and textual reasoning stages, each executed by sep-
266   arate agents. These agents are then effectively organized through a designate pipeline to complete
267   the tasks. This decomposition-before-integration approach not only circumvents the inherent dif-
268   ferences between modalities, but also allows visually irrelevant sub-agents to be performed by text-
269   only LLMs. Consequently, the extensively verified powerful reasoning capabilities of LLMs can be
      seamlessly synergized to power multi-modal reasoning process for performance enhancement.

275   4.1   G ENERAL S ETUP
276
277   Datasets. To comprehensively validate the performance of our framework, we conduct experi-
278   ments across four benchmarks: Multi-modal Large Language Model Evaluation (MME) (Yin et al.,
279   2023a), Massive Multi-discipline Multi-modal Understanding and Reasoning (MMMU) (Yue et al.,
280   2023), MathVista (Wang et al., 2024), and HallusionBench (Liu et al., 2023a). All of them require
281
      visual reasoning capabilities to complete the tasks correctly, and are introduced briefly as follows:
282          • MME is an inclusive benchmark that encompasses 14 subtasks, designed to evaluate per-
283            ceptual and cognitive abilities. Due to our emphasis on visual reasoning, we select the
284            cognition-relevant tasks, including Commonsense Reasoning, Numerical Calculation, Text
285            Translation, and Code Reasoning.2
286          • MMMU evaluates multi-modal models with multidisciplinary tasks that require college-
287            level domain-specific knowledge and detailed reasoning. It comprises 11,500 questions
288            across 30 disciplines and 183 sub-fields, emphasizing advanced perception and domain-
289            specific reasoning.
290          • MathVista focuses on more challenging mathematical reasoning tasks that demand precise
291            visual recognition and compositional reasoning. It includes 6,141 examples from 31 multi-
292            modal mathematics datasets.
293          • HallusionBench evaluates models’ ability to reason with images such as statistical charts,
294            emphasizing nuanced visual understanding. It consists of 346 images paired with 1,129
295            questions, meticulously crafted by experts.
296
297   Base Models. We employ GPT-4o-mini, Llama3-LLaVA-NeXT-8B and Qwen2.5-72B-
298   Instruct (Team, 2024) as our base models, due to their excellent performance, accessibility,
299   and representativeness of model sources. As one of the most performant LVLMs, GPT-4o-mini
300   demonstrates significant advancements in visual reasoning capabilities, and provides cheap and fast
301
      API. In contrast, Llama3-LLaVA-NeXT-8B is a fully open-sourced LVLM developed by the
      LLaVA team based on the Llama-3-8B LLM (Dubey et al., 2024) and CLIP vision encoder (Rad-
302
      ford et al., 2021). Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct is a robust LLM designed to deliver high-quality
303
      instruction following and handle complex tasks. It will be utilized for achieving LLM-assisted
304   multi-modal reasoning.
305
306   Baselines. Besides the most basic method where models are instructed to answer questions di-
307   rectly, we compare P RO R EASON with two categories of peer methods. To determine the benefits of
308   proactive information extraction in P RO R EASON, we first consider two SOTA passive visual reason-
309   ing methods, VDGD (Ghosh et al., 2024) and CCoT (Mitra et al., 2024). Additionally, we choose
310   two multi-step reasoning frameworks of LLMs, Chain of Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022) and
311   ReAct (Yao et al., 2022), to demonstrate the effects of directly migrating LLM solutions to LVLMs.
312
             • Direct. As indicated by the name, models are required to answer questions directly without

               dedicated prompts. This baseline is set to evaluate the initial performance of base models.
314
             • VDGD. With image caption prefixed to text instruction, VDGD selects tokens that deviate
315
               the least from the description using a formula based on Kullback-Leibler divergence.

317          • CCoT. Given an image and the question, CCOT first generates a scene graph of the image
318
               with LVLMs, and then extracts the answer by prompting the LVLMs with the graph.
319          • CoT. CoT is an advanced prompting method that encourages LLMs to break complex tasks
320            down into a series of easy steps, which has been applied broadly and verified to boost the
321
               reasoning performance remarkably (Chu et al., 2023).

      4.2   M AIN R ESULTS
379
380   P RO R EASON exhibits significant and consistent performance enhancement over baselines
381   across all the benchmarks. As listed in Table 4, despite better performance than the direct
382   method on MME dataset, VDGD and CCoT fail to demonstrate consistent improvements on the
383   other datasets. In contrast, P RO R EASON consistently outperforms all the other baselines for both
384   Llama3-LLaVA-NeXT-8B and GPT-4o-mini model across all benchmarks, with a peak im-
385   provement as 13.2%, demonstrating the superiority and task robustness of P RO R EASON.
386   Proactive information acquisition surpasses SOTA passive methods, especially in complex vi-
387   sual reasoning tasks. Specifically, compared to MME, MathVista and HallusionBench present higher
388   image complexity and question difficulty, and thus require stronger visual understanding and tex-
389   tual reasoning capabilities. This leads to performance degradation of passive methods (i.e., VDGD
390   and CCoT), highlighting their limited applicability to complex visual reasoning tasks. In contrast,
391   P RO R EASON achieves notable performance improvements, up to 5.1%, by proactively acquiring vi-
392
      sual information from images rather than generating question-agnostic captions. This aligns with out
      previous obervations in Sec. 2.2 that passive methods introduce substantial information redundancy
393
      or omission, misleading subsequent reasoning processes.
394
395   Decoupling the visual perception and textual reasoning capabilities of an LVLM outperforms
396   their simultaneous inherent usage. As listed in Table 4, when both capabilities are utilized
397   concurrently, CoT consistently degrades performance compared to the “Direct” method across all
398
      benchmarks with Llama3-LLaVA-NeXT-8B model, consistent with the findings in Sec. 2.1. In
      contrast, despite the same models, P RO R EASON alternates between visual information acquisition
399
      and textual reasoning processes, allowing to leverage each capability more effectively. This en-
400
      ables P RO R EASON to consistently outperform CoT with both Llama3-LLaVA-NeXT-8B and
401   GPT-4o-mini across all benchmarks, demonstrating the effectiveness of capability decoupling.
402
403   P RO R EASON outperforms ReAct with even less token consumption. Given the similarity in
404
      multi-step reasoning, we compare P RO R EASON and ReAct in terms of performance and cost.
      Specifically, as an LLM-specific multi-step reasoning framework, ReAct only outperforms the “Di-
405
      rect” method on MME and MMMU, but underperforms on MathVista and HallusionBench, showing
406
      inferior performance compared to the consistent improvements of P RO R EASON. Furthermore, we
407   compare their average token consumption of GPT-4o-mini. As shown in Table 8 of Appendix A,
408   P RO R EASON consumes significantly fewer tokens than ReAct on both MME and MathVista tasks,
409   indicating its higher token efficiency and the importance of a compact Memory in reducing token
410   usage. Coupled with better performance, this suggests the superiority of P RO R EASON over LLM-
411   specific ReAct framework.
412
      Text-only LLMs can be effectively integrated into P RO R EASON for dramatically enhanced
413   performance. As mentioned in Sec. 3.4, the decoupled visual perception and textual reasoning ca-
414   pabilities facilitate the seamless integration of text-only LLMs. To demonstrate the utility of this
415   advantage, we fix the Vision Expert as Llama3-LLaVA-NeXT-8B, and replace other agents with
416   text-only LLMs. As listed in Table 5, with the assistance of either Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct or
417   GPT-4o-mini, the Llama3-LLaVA-NeXT-8B Vision Expert receives remarkable performance
418   boost across all benchmarks, particularly by 15% on MMMU and 11.7% on MathVista, compared to
419   directly providing answers. In contrast, ReAct gains a much smaller improvement. This highlights
420   the unique advantage of P RO R EASON in leveraging existing text-only LLMs for enhanced perfor-
421
      mance. Notably, this advantage may open new avenues for continuously pushing the performance
      limits of LVLMs with the assistance of existing powerful LLMs.

492   5   R ELATED WORK
493
494   Large Visual-Language Model. Recently, large vision-language models (LVLMs) (Bai et al.,
495   2023; Chen et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b) have garnered widespread attention and demonstrated re-
496   markable advancements in understanding and generating multi-modal contents. In the open-source
497   domain, numerous LVLMs, like LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023c;b; 2024a; Li et al., 2024a;b) and In-
498   ternVL (Chen et al., 2024b) families, have been extensively developed. In the closed-source do-
499
      main, proprietary models such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) and Gemini Pro 1.5 (Reid et al.,
      2024) have also achieved significantly success. Despite these advancements, existing LVLMs still
500
      encounter challenges in effectively integrating visual understanding with textual reasoning capabil-
501
      ities simultaneously. This limitation is particularly evident in their diminished attention to image
502   content during visual reasoning process, such as chart interpretation and visual math reasoning,
503   leading to degraded performance (Ghosh et al., 2024) and motivating more effective solutions.
504
505   Passive Visual Reasoning. Extracting information from images into text can effectively as-
506   sist LVLM in performing visual reasoning tasks. Visual Description Grounded Decoding
507   (VDGD) (Ghosh et al., 2024) first describes the image before prefixing this description to the
508   prompt, assisting LVLMs on visual reasoning tasks. Furthermore, Compositional Chain-of-Thought
509   (CCoT) (Mitra et al., 2024) directs LVLMs to generate scene graphs (SGs) that serve as a bridge
510   between the visual and textual domains, aiding LVLMs in subsequent tasks. However, most of
511
      these methods employ a question-agnostic and reasoning-free visual extraction process, where im-
      age descriptions are not tailored to specific questions, and no reasoning is applied to infer additional
512
      information for improved descriptions. These “passive” approaches lead to the inclusion of irrele-
513
      vant or redundant information, ultimately degrading performance. In contrast, P RO R EASON adopts
514   question-oriented agents to collect necessary and sufficient information, effectively circumventing
515   these drawbacks.
516
517   Multi-step Reasoning Framework. Multi-step reasoning frameworks have been developed for
518   LLMs to achieve better performance by breaking down complex questions into easier ones (Pan
519   et al., 2024). As a representative method, Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022) enhances the
520   arithmetic and commonsense reasoning capabilities by explicitly generating intermediate reason-
521   ing steps before concluding the final answers. Tree-of-Thoughts (ToT) (Yao et al., 2024) further
522   refines the CoT mechanism by allowing LLMs to consider multiple reasoning paths and do self-
523
      assessment before making decisions. Considering that the inherent knowledge of LLMs may not be
      sufficient to complete tasks, ReAct (Yao et al., 2022) integrates information retrieval into the reason-
524
      ing chains, enabling models to pause to verify results and determine whether additional information
525
      is needed before proceeding. Nevertheless, multi-step reasoning frameworks designed for text-only
526   LLMs are not fully applicable to visual reasoning tasks, and may even impair the performance of
527   LVLMs (Ghosh et al., 2024).
528

      6   C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we first validate that existing multi-modal reasoning approaches still suffer insufficient
532
      and irrelevant visual descriptions, as well as limited multi-modal capacities. To address these issues,
533
      we decompose the visual reasoning process into visual perception and textual reasoning stages, and
534   introduce a novel visual reasoning framework named P RO R EASON, featuring multi-run proactive
535   perception and decoupled vision-reasoning capabilities. Empirically, extensive experiments demon-
536   strate the superiority of P RO R EASON over both passive image information acquisition methods and
537   multi-step reasoning frameworks for text-only LLMs across multiple visual reasoning benchmarks
538   with both open-source and closed-source models. Notably, our method showcases the remarkable
539   feasibility of integrating LLMs for multi-modal reasoning with dramatically improved performance,
      highlighting the great potential for LLM-assisted LVLM reasoning in future research.

      A.2   A NALYSIS OF BAD C ASES
865
866   In this section, we analyze the typical errors made by GPT-4o-mini-driven P RO R EASON, exem-
867   plified as Figure 6 and 7, for further understanding and potential research directions.
868
869   Cumulative Errors. As illustrated in Figure 6, the vision expert mistakenly perceives the clock
870   as 6:25, which misguides the reasoning of subsequent agents and ultimately leads to an incorrect
871   conclusion. More broadly, similar misperceptions occur frequently in errors made by P RO R EASON.
872   This indicates that, with the assistance of LLMs, P RO R EASON has effectively addressed the rea-
873
      soning deficiencies in multi-modal tasks, while the vision expert plays a significant role for further
      improvement of multi-modal capabilities.
874
875
      Contradictory Information among Agents. Considering that multiple agents are engaged in the
876
      answering process, we try to find instances where contradictory information is provided by different
877
      agents, especially the vision expert and reasoning expert. However, as shown in Figure 6 and 7,
878   when one agent (e.g., the vision expert) makes an error and the referee even hints at a possible
879   mistake, other agents (e.g., the reasoning expert) tend to adhere to the available information instead
880   of questioning it. This tendency results in a failure to find cases with contradictory information,
881   and also highlights the importance of a reflection mechanism (Ji et al., 2023) in agent collaboration,
882   which is left for future exploration.
