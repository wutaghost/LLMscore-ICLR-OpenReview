009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            It is challenging to discover and segment whole objects from unlabeled images, as
012            features unsupervisedly learned on images tend to focus on distinctive appearances
013            (e.g., the face rather than the torso), and grouping by feature similarity could reveal
014            only these representative parts, not the whole objects (e.g., the entire human body).
015            Our key insight is that, an object of distinctive parts pops out as a whole, due
016
               not only to how similar they are to each other, but also to how different they are
               from their contexts within an image or across related images. The latter could be
017
               crucial for binding different parts into a coherent whole without preconception of
018
               objects. We formulate our idea for unsupervised object segmentation in a spectral
019            graph partitioning framework, where nodes are patches and edges are grouping
020            cues between patches, measured by feature similarity for attraction, and by feature
021            dissimilarity for repulsion. We seek the graph cuts that maximize within-group
022            attraction and figure-ground repulsion while minimizing figure/ground attraction
023            and within-group repulsion. Our simple method consistently outperforms the state-
024            of-the-art on unsupervised object discovery, figure/ground saliency detection, and
025            unsupervised video object segmentation benchmarks. In particular, it excels at
026            discovering whole objects instead of salient parts.

070

071
072   We formulate our approach to unsupervised whole object segmentation within a spectral graph
073   partitioning framework. In this setup, image patches are represented as nodes, and edges are the
074   grouping relationships between these patches. These relationships are quantified using two measures:
075
      attraction based on feature similarity, and repulsion based on feature dissimilarity. The goal is to find
      the graph cuts that simultaneously maximize within-group attraction and between-group repulsion,
076
      while minimizing figure-to-background attraction and within-group repulsion.
077
078   We investigate this idea not only within a single image but also across related images in a co-
079   segmentation setting, where contextual grouping with repulsion between similar images brings
080   additional power for discovering whole objects together (Fig. 1). These images should be semantically
081
      similar yet visually distinct; if they are identical, co-segmentation lacks new information, and if they
      are semantically unrelated, co-segmentation becomes ineffective.
082
083   We present a method for unsupervised object segmentation by contextual grouping with repulsion,
084   named CGR . With whole objectness revealed by attraction and repulsion, we further refine the
085   self-supervised features with an attached segmentation head over the whole object masks. Our
086   method consistently outperforms the state-of-the-art on unsupervised object discovery, unsuperivsed
087
      saliency detection, and unsupervised video object segmentation benchmarks.
088   Contributions. 1) We propose to leverage contextual relationship from both within-image and cross-
089   image to group distinctive parts into coherent whole objects without any annotations. 2) We optimize
090   the grouping with a new framework using attraction and repulsion cues of self-supervised ViT
091   features. 3) We further enhance the self-supervised ViT features by re-training the backbone with an
092   attached segmentation head over whole object masks, thereby achieving state-of-the-art performance
093
      on multiple unsupervised segmentation benchmarks.

097   Unsupervised Object Discovery. Unsupervised object discovery aims at localizing and segmenting
098   objects from a collection of unlabeled images. Most of current works utilize self-supervised features
099
      from visual transformers (Caron et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2020b; Caron et al., 2020). SelfMask (Shin
      et al., 2022) applies spectral clustering on multiple self-supervised features to extract object masks.
100

101

102   similarities which are ranked and filtered by a maskness score. Deep Spectral Methods (Melas-Kyriazi
103   et al., 2022) and TokenCut (Wang et al., 2023) build a weighted graph using feature similarities
104   (attraction) and conduct graph cut to separate objects from backgrounds. FOUND (Siméoni et al.,
105   2023) first searches a background seed to localize objects and HEAP (Zhang et al., 2024) applies
106   contrastive learning to learn clustered feature embeddings. PEEKABOO (Zunair & Hamza, 2024)
107   presents to hide part of images and localize the objects with remaining image information. However,
      all these methods are limited in discovering whole objects as self-supervised features only capture

127   Unsupervised Video Segmentation. Unsupervised video segmentation methods utilize abundant
128
      unlabeled videos as the source of free supervision (Ye et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021; Yang et al.,
      2019a; Liu et al., 2021). The key ingredient is that motion across adjacent video frames provides
129
      useful constraints as training signals, such that elements tend to be perceived as a group if they move
130
      similarly. However, to guarantee the reliable motion information, these methods require externally
131   supervised motion estimation networks (Teed & Deng, 2020; Sun et al., 2018), thus limiting their
132   scalability. Although AMD (Liu et al., 2021) directly decomposes video sequences into regional
133   segmentation and motion in an end-to-end manner, the characterization of regional motion often
134   includes overly smoothed moving pieces and has difficulties in capturing fine details of object
135   boundaries. However, our method requires neither optical flow as input or network training. Yet, our
136   method demonstrate strong zero-shot segmentation on video data.
137   Segmentation as Graph Cuts. Normalized cut (Shi & Malik, 2000) presents image segmentation as
138   a graph partitioning problem. It finds a grouping that maximizes the similarities within the partitions.
139   (Ng et al., 2001) performs decomposition of the Laplacian matrix of a graph and then obtain the
140   partitions by stacking the eigenvectors along the channel dimension. (Yu & Shi, 2001) studies
141   perceptual popout using both feature similarity and local feature contrast. Objectness is popped out
142   by measuring attraction and repulsion in a unified weighted graph. We harness the characteristics of
143   joint measurement of attraction and repulsion for unsupervised whole object discovery.

      With the features from self-supervised visual transformers, we introduce the concept of attraction and
148   repulsion from the feature similarity matrix and construct a joint weighted graph by both attraction
149   and repulsion. We seek the graph cuts that maximize within-group attraction and figure-ground
150   repulsion. We investigate the graph cuts not only within a single image, but also across related images
151   in a co-segmentation setting. Contextual grouping with repulsion between similar images brings
152   additional power for whole object discovery.

245   reference images x1 and x2 , we first compute the self-supervised ViT features from the two images.
246   Then, we concatenate these ViT features to build a joint graph containing the patches of both images
247   as nodes, each patch node associated with its own feature vector. Our algorithm involves the following
248   steps: 1) Compute the similarity matrix S; 2) Compute the attraction and repulsion matrix A, R
249   (according to the equations in Fig. A1); 3) Compute the matrices DA , DR , W , and R; 4) Find the
250   2nd largest eigenvector z ∗ by solving the eigensystem at Equation 3. We separate z ∗ into two vectors

252   by attraction and repulsion across images is shown in Fig. 3; 5) To effectively segment objects based
253
      on z ∗ , we use the averaged value of z ∗ for image partitioning and find out the part covering maximum
      absolute value from z ∗ as the foreground objects.

255   3. Fine-tuning Self-Supervised Features. With the eigenvectors by attraction and repulsion to pop
256   out whole object masks, we refine these masks with bilateral solver (Barron & Poole, 2016). Note
257   that the bilateral solver is only applied during our fine-tuning stage. Furthermore, we utilize these
258   whole object masks to fine-tune the self-supervised transformer features along with a lightweight
259
      segmentation head (1 conv 1 × 1 layer) with the cross-entropy and the contrastive loss. The cross-
      entropy loss is used to update the self-surprised features using whole object masks and the contrastive
260
      loss loss is to minimize foreground feature distances and maximize the foreground-background
261
      feature distances. The diagram for whole object discovery and self-supervised features fine-tuning is

531   5    C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we introduced a novel approach to unsupervised object discovery and segmentation
534   using a spectral graph partitioning framework that harnesses both attraction and repulsion cues. Our
535   method effectively segments whole objects by optimizing within-group attractions and minimizing
536   distractions from the background. It significantly outperforms existing methods across benchmarks
537   for unsupervised object discovery, figure/ground saliency detection, and video object segmentation.
538   The simplicity and robustness of our approach make it a promising tool for advancing autonomous
539   and robust visual perception in various applications, suggesting a significant step forward in the field
      of computer vision.
