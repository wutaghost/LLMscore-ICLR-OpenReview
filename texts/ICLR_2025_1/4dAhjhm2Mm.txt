009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Score-based generative models (SGMs) have revolutionized the field of generative
012            modeling, achieving unprecedented success in generating realistic and diverse
013            content. Despite empirical advances, the theoretical basis for why optimizing
014            the evidence lower bound (ELBO) on the log-likelihood is effective for training
015            diffusion generative models, such as DDPMs, remains largely unexplored. In
016
               this paper, we address this question by establishing a density formula for a
               continuous-time diffusion process, which can be viewed as the continuous-time
017
               limit of the forward process in an SGM. This formula reveals the connection
018
               between the target density and the score function associated with each step of
019            the forward process. Building on this, we demonstrate that the minimizer of
020            the optimization objective for training DDPMs nearly coincides with that of the
021            true objective, providing a theoretical foundation for optimizing DDPMs using
022            the ELBO. Furthermore, we offer new insights into the role of score-matching
023            regularization in training GANs, the use of ELBO in diffusion classifiers, and the
024            recently proposed diffusion loss.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
      Score-based generative models (SGMs) represent a groundbreaking advancement in the realm of
029
      generative models, significantly impacting machine learning and artificial intelligence by their
030   ability to synthesize high-fidelity data instances, including images, audio, and text (Sohl-Dickstein
031   et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021b; Song & Ermon, 2019; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021;
032   Song et al., 2021a). These models operate by progressively refining noisy data into samples
033   that resemble the target distribution. Due to their innovative approach, SGMs have achieved
034   unprecedented success, setting new standards in generative AI and demonstrating extraordinary
035   proficiency in generating realistic and diverse content across various domains, from image synthesis
036   and super-resolution to audio generation and molecular design (Ramesh et al., 2022; Rombach et al.,
037   2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Croitoru et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023).
038   The foundation of SGMs is rooted in the principles of stochastic processes, especially stochastic
039   differential equations (SDEs). These models utilize a forward process, which involves the gradual
040   corruption of an initial data sample with Gaussian noise over several time steps. This forward
041   process can be described as:
042                                      add noise    add noise    add noise
                                    X0     −→ X1        −→ · · · −→ XT ,                               (1.1)
043
044   where X0 ∼ pdata is the original data sample, and XT is a sample close to pure Gaussian noise. The
045   ingenuity of SGMs lies in constructing a reverse denoising process that iteratively removes the noise,
046
      thereby reconstructing the data distribution. This reverse process starts from a Gaussian sample YT
      and moves backward as:
047
048
                                           denoise       denoise    denoise
049                                   YT −→ YT −1 −→ · · · −→ Y0                                       (1.2)
050                    d
      ensuring that Yt ≈ Xt at each step t. The final output Y0 is a new sample that closely mimics the
051
      distribution of the initial data pdata .
052
053   Inspired by the classical results on time-reversal of SDEs (Anderson, 1982; Haussmann & Pardoux,
      1986), SGMs construct the reverse process guided by score functions ∇ log pXt associated with each

      step of the forward process. Although these score functions are unknown, they are approximated
055   by neural networks trained through score-matching techniques (Hyvärinen, 2005; 2007; Vincent,
056   2011; Song & Ermon, 2019). This leads to two popular models: denoising diffusion probabilistic
057   models (DDPMs) (Ho et al., 2020; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021) and denoising diffusion implicit
058   models (DDIMs) (Song et al., 2021a). While the theoretical results in this paper do not depend
059   on the specific construction of the reverse process, we will use the DDPM framework to discuss
060   their implications for diffusion generative models.
061   However, despite empirical advances, there remains a lack of theoretical understanding for diffusion
062   generative models. For instance, the optimization target of DDPM is derived from a variational lower
063   bound on the log-likelihood (Ho et al., 2020), which is also referred to as the evidence lower bound
064   (ELBO) (Luo, 2022). It is not yet clear, from a theoretical standpoint, why optimizing a lower
065   bound of the true objective is still a valid approach. More surprisingly, recent research suggests
066   incorporating the ELBO of a pre-trained DDPM into other generative or learning frameworks to
067   leverage the strengths of multiple architectures, effectively using it as a proxy for the negative log-
068   likelihood of the data distribution. This approach has shown empirical success in areas such as GAN
069
      training, classification, and inverse problems (Xia et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a; Graikos et al., 2022;
      Mardani et al., 2024). While it is conceivable that the ELBO is a reasonable optimization target for
070
      training DDPMs (as similar idea is utilized in e.g., the majorize-minimization algorithm), it is more
071
      mysterious why it serves as a good proxy for the negative log-likelihood in these applications.

483   5     D ISCUSSION
484
485   This paper develops a score-based density formula that expresses the density function of a target
      distribution using the score function along a continuous-time diffusion process that bridges this


                                                                 9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      distribution and standard Gaussian. By connecting this diffusion process with the forward process
487   of score-based diffusion models, our results provide theoretical support for training DDPMs by
488   optimizing the ELBO, and offer novel insights into several applications of diffusion models,
489   including GAN training and diffusion classifiers.
490
      Our work opens several directions for future research. First, our theoretical results are established
491
      for the continuous-time diffusion process. It is crucial to carefully analyze the error induced by time
492
      discretization, which could inform the number of steps required for the results in this paper to be
493   valid in practice. Additionally, while our results provide theoretical justification for using the ELBO
494   (4.6) as a proxy for the negative log-likelihood of the target distribution, they do not cover other
495   practical variants of ELBO with modified weights (e.g., the simplified ELBO (4.8)). Extending our
496   analysis to other diffusion processes might yield new density formulas incorporating these modified
497   weights. Lastly, further investigation is needed into other applications of this score-based density
498   formula, including density estimation and inverse problems.
