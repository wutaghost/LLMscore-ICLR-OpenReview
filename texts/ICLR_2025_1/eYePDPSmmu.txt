009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            3D garment animation is key to a wide range of applications including digital
012            humans, virtual try-on, and extended reality. This paper addresses the task of pre-
013            dicting 3D garment deformation from a posed body mesh. Existing learning-based
014            methods mostly rely on linear blend skinning to decompose garment deformation
015            into low-frequency posed garment shape and high-frequency wrinkles. However,
016
               due to the lack of explicit skinning supervision, they often produce misaligned
               garment positions with undesired artifacts during garment re-posing, which corrupt
017
               the high-frequency signals. These skinning-based methods consequently fail to
018
               recover accurate wrinkle patterns. To tackle this issue, we present a skinning-free
019            approach that re-formulates the high-low frequency decomposition by estimating
020            posed (i) vertex position for low-frequency posed garment shape, and (ii) vertex
021            normal for high-frequency local wrinkle details. In this way, each frequency
022            modality can be effectively decoupled and directly supervised by the geometry of
023            the deformed garment. Moreover, we propose to encode both vertex attributes as
024            texture images, so that 3D garment deformation can be equivalently achieved via
025            2D image transfer. This enables us to leverage powerful pretrained image encoders
026            to recover high-fidelity visual details representing fine wrinkles. In addition, we
027
               model body-garment interaction via cross-attention between dense body and gar-
               ment image patches, which refines the naive skinning on sparse joints. Finally,
028
               we propose a multimodal fusion to incorporate constraints from both frequency
029
               modalities and optimize deformed 3D garments from transferred images. Extensive
030            experiments show that our method significantly improves deformation accuracy on
031            various garment types and recovers finer wrinkles than state-of-the-art methods.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Accurately predicting 3D garment deformation given a posed human body enables a wide range of
037   applications, such as digital humans (Muftić et al., 2005), virtual try-on (Santesteban et al., 2019),
038   and extended reality (Meyer et al., 2001). Traditional works (Provot et al., 1995; Li et al., 2022;
039
      Bouaziz et al., 2023) often rely on simulators to generate physically plausible results, however,
      simulation-based methods are time-consuming and require fine-tuning simulator-specific parameters
040
      for each garment, which demands expert knowledge and does not scale to diverse garment types
041
      (Luible & Magnenat-Thalmann, 2008; Zhang et al., 2024).
042
043   Recently, learning-based methods (Santesteban et al., 2019; Patel et al., 2020; Santesteban et al., 2021;
044   2022; Pan et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023) have received increasing attention thanks to the efficiency
045
      and scalability of deep networks. As garment deformation consists of both high-frequency wrinkles
      and low-frequency posed garment shape, it is challenging for neural networks to jointly optimize
046
      both frequency modalities due to the spectral bias (Rahaman et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2023). To this
047
      end, previous works mostly adopt a two-stage decomposition by firstly regressing wrinkles relative to
048   the un-posed garment template, then using linear blend skinning (LBS) to handle the low-frequency
049   garment re-posing. However, due to the lack of explicit supervision on garment skinning, they either
050   assume tight garments and directly skin from the closest body vertex (Santesteban et al., 2019; Patel
051   et al., 2020; Santesteban et al., 2021; 2022), or skin loose garments with virtual joints (Pan et al.,
052   2022; Zhao et al., 2023). Such unsupervised skinning can produce misaligned garment positions and
053   undesired artifacts, which corrupts high-frequency signals. Consequently, existing skinning-based
      methods often fail to recover fine-grained wrinkles, as illustrated in Figure 1.

074   To tackle the above issues, we present a skinning-free method that decomposes high-low frequency
075   modalities with two geometric attributes. Specifically, we propose to directly estimate posed garment
076   vertex positions instead of relying on garment skinning. As networks tend to prioritize learning low-
077   frequency signals (Rahaman et al., 2019), with only the position they often generate over-smoothed
078   garment geometry. To recover missing wrinkle details, we further estimate vertex normals that better
079
      capture local surface bending arising in wrinkles. In contrast to the previous skinning-based methods,
      our method effectively decouples frequency components and enables explicit supervision for both
080
      modalities, which avoids noisy skinning and produces more accurate wrinkles.
081
082   Motivated by the recent development of large pretrained image encoders (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020;
083   Caron et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2023), we propose to project 3D garment geometry onto 2D
084   image space in order to leverage these powerful models. Specifically, we first convert both vertex
085
      attributes into RGB colors and render them as texture images from multiple views, so that 3D garment
      deformation can be equivalently achieved via 2D image transfer, as illustrated in Figure 1. Moreover,
086
      predicting garment deformation in image space enables us to recover high-fidelity visual details,
087
      e.g. wrinkles represented as edges in the normal images, which facilitates to produce deformation
088   of higher perceptual quality. Furthermore, we model fine-grained body-garment interaction via
089   cross-attention between dense body and garment image patches, which refines the naive skinning
090   process over sparse joints. Finally, we fuse priors from transferred images and optimize the overall
091   3D deformation that aligns with image references.
092
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows. (i) We propose a novel skinning-free pipeline for
093
      garment deformation with effective high-low frequency modalities decomposition, which avoids
094   noisy garment skinning and facilitates accurate wrinkle regression. (ii) We model 3D garment
095   deformation via 2D image transfer, leveraging pretrained image encoders and cross-attention to
096   recover high-fidelity visual details for wrinkles and model fine-grained body-garment interaction.
097   Extensive experiments show that our method noticeably improves prediction accuracy and perceptual
098   quality over state-of-the-art methods.

101
102   Physics-based Methods. To generate physically plausible garment animation, physics-based methods
103   either rely on time-consuming simulators (Provot et al., 1995; Bouaziz et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022;
104   Yu et al., 2023), or optimize through physics-inspired losses (Bertiche et al., 2020b; Santesteban
105   et al., 2022; Grigorev et al., 2023). To ensure realism and accuracy, simulator parameters need to
106   be fine-tuned for each garment instance, which can be laborious. Several works propose to estimate
107   these parameters through differentiable simulation (Larionov et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a) or neural
      networks (Yang et al., 2017; Clyde et al., 2017), however, the estimation needs to be performed in a


                                                                             2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      controlled setting with known external factors, which limits their applications. The challenge in data
109   preprocessing thus restricts such method from scaling to diverse garment types.
110
111   Learning-based Methods. In contrast, learning-based methods (Patel et al., 2020; Santesteban et al.,
112
      2019; Pan et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022) have been developed to achieve superior
      efficiency and scalability. Pioneered by (Lewis et al., 2000), most works follow to estimate pose space
113
      deformation (PSD), namely they adopt LBS to obtain low-frequency posed garment shape, while
114
      predicting high-frequency wrinkles in the canonical garment space. Specifically, (Santesteban et al.,
115   2019) directly regresses local vertex displacements using recurrent neural networks. (Patel et al.,
116   2020) proposes to first use mixture models to construct bases of high-frequency deformations, then
117   combine them with narrowed bandwidth kernels. (Zhang et al., 2022) leverages generative models
118   to encode the feasible high-frequency latent space. Similar to our approach, (Lahner et al., 2018;
119   Zhang et al., 2021) uses normal maps to model fine wrinkles. However, they require manually built
120   UV maps and rely on LBS to generate initial normals, which we show in the ablation study that are
121   sub-optimal. While the above works tackle tight garments and directly access body skinning weights,
122   (Pan et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023) further extend to loose garments by predicting virtual garment
123
      joints to which garments are skinned. However, the prediction of virtual joints can not be explicitly
      supervised, which can lead to incorrect joint transformations. In summary, existing learning-based
124
      methods mostly suffer from noisy skinning that can not be directly supervised. Consequently, the
125
      skinning artifacts need to be jointly refined during wrinkle regression, which prevents them from
126   estimating accurate wrinkles. In contrast, we present a skinning-free approach, which effectively
127   avoids noisy skinning and facilitates to generate more accurate wrinkles.
128
129
      Image-based 3D Representation. In view of large-scale image datasets and effective pretrained
      image models, recent works propose to represent 3D geometry in the image space. Most existing
130
      works leverage UV mapping as the image representation, which have been widely applied in human
131
      pose estimation (Güler et al., 2018), avatar generation (Ma et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023b), and scan
132   registration (Guo et al., 2023). However, they often rely on manual UV unwrapping to produce
133   semantically meaningful islands, which requires expert knowledge and is laborious, thus does not
134   scale to large-scale collections. Alternatively, (Lin et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023b; Xiu et al., 2022;
135   2023) propose to render multi-view images to automatically establish vertex-to-pixel correspondence.
136   Specifically, (Xiu et al., 2022; 2023) learn to generate 3D clothed humans by integrating from
137   estimated normal images. (Lin et al., 2022) encodes 3D character animation via ultra dense pose
138   images. (Li et al., 2023b) designs Gaussian maps rendered from template human meshes to encode
139   parameters for Gaussian splatting. Unlike all above works that consider only a single image source,
140
      we observe that accurate garment deformation requires effectively fusing multiple image domains
      with mixed frequency modalities, which is achieved via a novel pipeline as will be introduced below.

145   To avoid skinning artifacts and produce more accurate wrinkles, we present a novel skinning-free
146   pipeline, as shown in Figure 2. Given a garment template mesh M̄g = {V̄g , Fg } where V̄g ∈ RN ×3

148   mesh M̂g = {V̂g , Fg } conditioned on the posed body mesh Mb = {Vb , Fb }. Instead of directly
149   regressing 3D vertex displacements, we propose to model garment deformation in 2D image space.
150   Specifically, we first render RGB images P̄gs and N̄gs ∈ RH×W ×3 that respectively encode vertex
151   positions and normals of the garment template from each view s ∈ {front, back}. Similarly for
152   the posed body mesh we render corresponding images {Pbs , Nbs }. We then develop an image transfer
153   network to generate transferred images P̂gs and N̂gs , which describe posed garment shape and wrinkle
154   details respectively (Section 3.2). Finally, we propose a multimodal fusion process to leverage priors
155   of both modalities and optimize 3D deformed garment mesh from transferred images (Section 3.3).

251   While we can obtain vertex positions of the deformed garment solely from the position images P̂gs of
252   both views, we observe two major issues of such an approach: (i) since the high frequency wrinkle

254   network fp (·) to capture such subtleties, thus leading to an over-smoothed mesh, (ii) although images

256

      4.1   DATASETS
299
300   We evaluate ours and baseline methods on two benchmarks: VTO (Santesteban et al., 2019) and
301   TailorNet (Patel et al., 2020) datasets.
302
      VTO. The VTO dataset (Santesteban et al., 2019) provides two types of garments: tight ”t-shirt” and
303
      loose ”dress”. Each garment is draped onto a SMPL (Loper et al., 2015) human body with ground
304   truth deformations simulated in the ARCSim (Narain et al., 2014) simulator. We follow (Pan et al.,
305   2022) to use 4 clips (01 01, 111 02, 55 27 and 91 36) of medium body shape (β = 0) and unseen
306   poses for testing and the remaining 49 clips for training.
307
      TailorNet. Since the VTO dataset only contains upper garments, we further adopt the TailorNet
308
      (Patel et al., 2020) dataset to test on lower garments: tight ”pants” and loose ”skirt”, with ground
309
      truth deformations simulated by the Marvelous Designer. We follow (Pan et al., 2022) to use the
310   medium body shape and garment style (β = 0, γ = 0) split, and adopt 2 clips (005, 010) of unseen
311   poses for testing and the remaining 16 clips for training.
312
313
      Metrics. Following (Pan et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023), we evaluate all methods on three metrics:
      Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE), Hausdorff distance (Attouch et al., 1991), and spatio-temporal
314
      edge difference (STED) (Vasa & Skala, 2010). RMSE and Hausdorff distance assess the prediction
315
      accuracy, while STED evaluates the perceptual quality of deformation. Specifically, RMSE calculates
316   the average Euclidean distance between vertices and Hausdorff distance measures the maximum
317   distance between the closest vertex pairs, both in terms of the predicted and ground truth meshes. In
318   addition, we use STED to assess the perceptual similarity of the deformation, which measures the
319   relative edge differences between the predicted and ground truth meshes across each test clip.

501
502   In all above evaluations, we evaluate on a single garment instance each time to ensure a fair com-
503   parison with baseline methods (Santesteban et al., 2019; 2021; Pan et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023).
504   However, our method is not limited to a single garment input and is well-suited for training across
505   multiple garments thanks to several designs: (i) the use of pretrained DINO encoder that is capable
506   of extracting detailed semantic features for various garments, (ii) the image transfer approach that
507   is agnostic to garment topologies, and (iii) the use of front and back view projections to establish
508   image representations for garments, which will not be scalable for manual UV parameterization on a
509
      large collection of garments. To verify the generalizability of our method, we further jointly train on
      50 dress garments on the CLOTH3D (Bertiche et al., 2020a) dataset, and show the results in Figure
510
      8. By training across multiple garments, our method can effectively generalize to unseen garment

      shapes, with the skinning-free method particularly beneficial for tackling loose garment deformation.

      5      D ISCUSSION
525
526
      Limitation. Although our method succeeds in generating accurate deformation on common garments,
527
      it assumes the garment template has a single layer and is flat. For multi-layered garments, the
528   rendered images may not capture inner garments due to occlusion in perspective projection. We
529   also only model body-garment interaction in cross-attention and do not consider interaction among
530   garment layers. Furthermore, we tackle a single input body to reduce model complexity, while future
531   works are encouraged to explore encoding for body motions in the image transfer network. More
532   discussions on failure cases and societal impact are included in Appendix G.
533
      Conclusion. In this paper, we propose a novel skinning-free pipeline to generate accurate 3D garment
534   deformation via image transfer. We decompose garment deformation into decoupled frequency
535   modalities represented by vertex positions and normals, and further project both modalities into the
536   image space, which allows us to leverage pretrained image encoders and body-garment cross-attention
537   to recover pose-conditioned fine wrinkles of higher perceptual quality. Thanks to these designs, our
538   method effectively produces more accurate wrinkle details over previously dominant skinning-based
539   baselines, and the proposed pipeline can be broadly applied to generate detailed geometry deformation
      with mixed frequency modalities on other manifolds.
