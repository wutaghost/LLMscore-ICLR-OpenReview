      MindSet: Vision. A toolbox for testing DNNs
002   on key psychological experiments

009                                            Abstract
010
011            Multiple benchmarks have been developed to assess the alignment between
012            deep neural networks (DNNs) and human vision. In almost all cases these
013            benchmarks are observational in the sense they are composed of behavioural
014            and brain responses to naturalistic images that have not been manipulated
015            to test hypotheses regarding how DNNs or humans perceive and identify
016            objects. Here we introduce the toolbox MindSet: Vision, consisting of a
017
               collection of image datasets and related scripts designed to test DNNs on
               30 psychological findings. In all experimental conditions, the stimuli are
018
               systematically manipulated to test specific hypotheses regarding human
019
               visual perception and object recognition. In addition to providing pre-
020            generated datasets of images, we provide code to regenerate these datasets,
021            offering many configurable parameters which greatly extend the dataset
022            versatility for different research contexts, and code to facilitate the testing
023            of DNNs on these image datasets using three different methods (similarity
024            judgments, out-of-distribution classification, and decoder method), accessible
025            via GitHub. We test ResNet-152 on each of these methods as an example of
026            how the toolbox can be used.
027
028
      1   Introduction
029
030
      Deep neural networks (DNNs) provide the best solution for visual identification of objects
031   short of biological vision, and many researchers claim that DNNs are the best current models
032   of human vision and object recognition (Kubilius et al., 2019; Mehrer et al., 2021; Zhuang
033   et al., 2020; Dicarlo et al., 2023; Golan et al., 2023). Key evidence in support of this claim
034   comes from the finding that DNNs perform the best on various behavioural and brain
035   benchmarks. In the case of behavioural benchmarks, models are assessed on how well they
036   account for human (or macaque) errors in classifying a large set of objects (Rajalingham
037   et al., 2018; Tuli et al., 2021; Hebart et al., 2023), or how well they predict human similarity
038   judgements (Peterson et al., 2017; Battleday et al., 2020). In the case of brain benchmarks,
039
      models are assessed with regard to how well they predict brain recordings (e.g., single-cell
      responses or fMRI data) in response to a set of objects (Schrimpf et al., 2018; 2020; Hebart
040
      et al., 2023; Allen et al., 2022). The general assumption is that the better a model does at
041
      predicting the data, the more similar the model is to biological vision. For instance, the
042   Brain-Score benchmark is described as “a composite of multiple neural and behavioural
043   benchmarks that score any [artificial neural network] on how similar it is to the brain’s
044   mechanisms for core object recognition” (Schrimpf et al., 2018)
045
      A common feature of most benchmark studies is that they treat the to-be-predicted data
046
      as observational. That is, there is rarely an attempt to predict the impact of experimental
047
      manipulation designed to test specific hypotheses about how human or machine vision works.
048   Rather, observers perform a single task over a set of images that satisfies some general
049   criterion, such as objects presented in isolation (Kriegeskorte et al., 2008), in naturalistic
050   contexts (Peterson et al., 2017; Battleday et al., 2020; Allen et al., 2022; Hebart et al., 2023),
051   or on a range of arbitrary backgrounds (Cadieu et al., 2014; Schrimpf et al., 2018). This
052   approach is problematic because it is possible to make good predictions on these datasets
053   even when models identify objects in a qualitatively different way from monkeys or humans
      (Dujmović et al., 2024). For example, if the images contain multiple diagnostic cues for object


                                                      1
054
      classification (e.g., shape and texture both predict object category), then good predictions
055   might be driven by different features than those that drive human object recognition – that
056   is, predictions might be driven by confounds. For example, a DNN that classifies objects
057   by texture might still be able to predict brain activations in a visual system that classifies
058   objects by shape.
059
      The standard way to rule out confounds in order to determine causal relations (e.g. inferring
060
      that DNNs learn brain-like representations) is to carry out experiments designed to rule
061   out confounds as the basis of making good predictions. In fact, there is a large literature in
062   psychology describing experiments designed to test specific hypotheses about how human
063   vision works, but surprisingly, this literature is often ignored when modellers compare DNNs
064   to biological vision (for notable exceptions see the review paper Kanwisher et al. (2023)).
065   Bowers et al. (2023b) reviewed a wide range of psychological phenomena that current DNNs
066   either fail to capture or that have yet to be considered. Furthermore, when researchers do
067   consider the psychological literature when making claims regarding DNN-human similarities,
068   the models are rarely subject to the kind of “severe” tests that are required to make any
069
      strong conclusions, that is tests that are likely to challenge claims in case they are false.
      Instead, strong conclusions are often drawn based on superficial similarities (Bowers et al.,
070
      2023a).
071
072   There are at least four (related) reasons for this. First, many researchers in computer science
073   and computational neuroscience may be unfamiliar with the rich set of experiments carried
074   out in psychology that manipulate independent variables to better understand human vision,
075
      memory, language, etc. Those who are aware of these studies might find it challenging to
      engage with them, as psychological datasets are not readily available in formats that the
076
      community is accustomed to working with. Second, it is not always obvious how one should
077
      test a model against psychological data. Hence, it may be easier to focus on improving
078   performance on the current benchmarks, and this may have discouraged researchers from
079   exploring data from psychology. A third potential reason is an overall skepticism towards
080   psychological results, a sentiment that may reflect the well-documented replication crisis in
081   psychology (Baker, 2015). Forth, there is a strong bias to look for DNN-human similarities
082   and downplay the differences (Bowers et al., 2023a), and severely testing on psychological
083   data might not result in similarities. However, characterizing these failures provides key
084   insights into the ways DNNs need to be improved when modelling biological vision.
085   Here we present MindSet: Vision, a toolbox aimed at facilitating testing DNNs on visual
086   psychological phenomena by addressing all the problems presented above: our main contri-
087   bution is to provide a large, easily accessible, parameterized, set of 30 image datasets (and
088   related scripts to re-generate and modify them) accounting for a wide array of well-replicated
089   visual experiment and phenomena reported in psychology. Our stimuli cover aspects of low
090   and mid-level vision (including Gestalt phenomena), visual illusions, and object recognition
091   tasks. While our stimuli primarily focus on static vision, reflecting the current emphasis
092
      in DNN/human alignment research, we recognize the importance of other areas of vision,
      such as dynamic processes like motion perception. We hope that our work serves as a solid
093
      foundation for further investigation into these complex cognitive processes. We provide
094
      a high-level descriptions of the visual phenomena in the main text (Section 2) and more
095   detailed descriptions in the Appendix (A).
096
097   To facilitate experimentation across a variety of scenarios, each dataset can be easily
098
      regenerated across different configurations (image size, background colour, stroke colour,
      number of samples, etc.). To address the difficulty in testing DNNs on these stimuli, we
099
      provide scripts for using one (or more) of three methods: Similarity Judgment Analysis,
100
      Decoder Approach, and Out-of-Distribution classification (Section 3). We provide examples
101   illustrating how to use these scripts with a classic feed-forward CNN (ResNet-152), and an
102   extensively documented code (Section 4).
103
104
      With MindSet: Vision, we aim to bridge the gap between computational modeling and
      psychological research, bringing experimental studies that manipulate independent variables
105
      to the forefront of developing and evaluating of DNN models of human vision. We also hope
106
      this initiative will drive further interest in other areas of human psychology, such as memory,
107

      language, and speech perception when attempting tounderstand and replicate human-like
109   intelligence in machines.

      1.1   Related Work
112
113   Several recent studies share some similarities with our project: Roy et al. (2024) propose a
114   human-validated dataset of five types of brightness illusions and benchmark three DNNs
115   on their ability to identify and localize these illusions. Zhang et al. (2023) introduced
116   a new dataset containing five types of visual illusions falling into two categories: color
117   constancy and geometrical illusions. The authors formulated four tasks specifically designed
118   to examine the performance of Visual Language Models, finding low alignment with human
119
      responses. Lonnqvist et al. (2024) developed the Good Gestalt datasets, consisting of six
      types of datasets covering several types of Gestalt grouping principles, including Closure,
120
      Continuity, and Proximity, aimed at testing a Latent Noise Segmentation Network. Similarly,
121
      Geirhos et al. (2021) developed the model-vs-human benchmark that compares ANN-human
122   classification errors on various ‘out-of-distribution’ datasets composed of naturalistic images
123   that were modified in various ways, including low-level feature manipulations of contrast
124   and spatial frequency, as well as higher-level manipulations, such as generating silhouettes
125   and sketches of images. Evans et al. (2022) used a dataset of silhouettes, line-drawings, and
126   contours, to investigate robustness to these stimuli in DNNs pretrained on CIFAR-10, and
127   Baker et al. (2018) employed a dataset of line drawings and silhouettes from ImageNet classes
128   to investigate model robustness to local versus global features. In comparison to these works,
129   we present a toolbox to test DNNs on visual psychological effects, investigating not only a
130
      much richer set of visual phenomena, but providing the code base to regenerate images in
      batches, changing the parameters, and testing each one of them on a variety of methods.

133   2     Datasets
134
135   We have included datasets from experiments that characterize a wide range of visual
136   phenomena, ranging from low- to high-level vision. We grouped the datasets (indicated in
137   bold) into 3 broad categories (see following Sections) as illustrated in Figure 1. Each dataset
138   comprised multiple sub-conditions designed to test DNN-human similarities, and in some
139
      cases, image datasets used to train decoders, as described in Section 3.3.
140   While most of the stimuli are created by us, in a few instances we incorporate stimuli from
141   external sources (when needed, permission was obtained from the authors). In all cases, the
142   stimuli have been integrated into a versatile framework which offers significant flexibility in
143   adjusting parameters such as image size, background, stroke colour, and more, to allow their
144   application to a variety of models and methodologies. Given the extensive range of datasets
145
      provided, we only offer a brief summary for each in the article, and provide more details in
      Appendix A, including details about the suggested way to test each dataset, and the expected
146
      result for model-human perceptual alignment. All resources are open-source and freely
147
      available under the MIT license at https://github.com/MindSetVision/MindSetVision
148   with additional Kaggle links to datasets we generated provided in the repository README.

      2.1   Low and Mid-Level Vision
151
152   A fundamental low-level vision phenomena is captured by Weber’s Law (Weber, 1983),
153   which states that the minimum physical change of a stimulus on some dimension (e.g., its
154   size) that is noticeable to an observer is a constant ratio of the original stimulus value on
155   this dimension. For example, it is equally easy to distinguish between line lengths of 1 and 2
156   cms and between 2 and 4 cms. We created a dataset that can be used to assess this relation
157   for both line length and stimulus intensity.
158   Human perception is also sensitive to various Emergent Features in which simple image
159   features interact to generate “Gestalts" (Pomerantz & Portillo, 2011a). The dataset is
160   comprised of a set of dots arranged in such a way as to induce the emergent feature of
161   proximity, orientation, and linearity (Pomerantz & Portillo, 2011b; Biscione & Bowers,
      2023). Another Gestalt effect is manifest in the Crowding/Uncrowding phenomenon. In

220
      Human perception is highly sensitive to non-accidental image features, that is features that
      are largely invariant to changes in viewpoint when projected on to the retina (Biederman,
221
      1987), as opposite to accidental features (e.g., degree of curvature) in which the projected
222
      image varies with viewpoint. Human vision is known to be more sensitive to changes in
223   images that alter non-accidental compared to accidental properties (Amir et al., 2012; 2014).
224   We use two datasets to examine model sensitivity to these features: one with 3D geons
225   (Amir et al., 2012) and another with 2D line segments (based on Kubilius et al., 2017).
226   Similarly, we present a dataset to compare Relational Changes with Coordinate changes
227   between object parts. DNNs are commonly insensitive to relational change, even after being
228   explicitly trained on these relations (Malhotra et al., 2023), whereas human perception is
229   highly sensitive to relational changes (Hummel & Stankiewicz, 1996).
230   To identify partly occluded objects, the human visual system groups contours and surfaces
231   through an amodal completion process (Nakayama & Shimojo, 1992). The Amodal Com-
232   pletion dataset (based on Rensink & Enns, 1998) enables the investigation of these processes
233   using images with shapes that are either occluded, unoccluded, or “notched”. These latter
234   shapes are unoccluded but notched in such a way as to maintain a high degree of feature
235   similarity with their occluded counterparts.
236   With the Decomposition dataset we provide a mean to test the extent to which DNNs
237   group object parts in a human-like fashion. We designed familiar and unfamiliar objects
238   composed of two conjoined parts that undergo what humans would perceive as a “natural”
239   or “unnatural” break (inspired by Jacob et al., 2021). In the same work, Jacob et al. (2021)
240   showed that VGG-16 trained on ImageNet did not possess human-like sensitivity to images
241   that could be interpreted as 3D-shapes by using a set of stimuli based on Enns & Rensink
242   (1991). Accordingly, we reconstructed this Depth Drawings dataset.

244   2.2   Visual Illusions
245
246   Visual illusions are not mere curiosities, but often arise from adaptive perceptual processes
247   (Gregory, 1997). Detailed computational models of multiple illusions have been advanced
248   providing theoretical insights into the mechanisms that underlie them (e.g., Grossberg, 2017).
249
      We provide datasets exploring illusions related to size perception, orientation, and lightness
      contrast.
250
251   Several illusions relate to size perception. In the Müller-Lyer illusion (Day & Knuth, 1981),
252   arrow-like segments at the ends of equal-length lines impact our perception of length. In the
253   Ponzo illusion (Ponzo, 1910), two equal-length horizontal lines cross a pair of converging
254   lines. In this configuration, the top line looks longer, an illusion often explained as related to
255
      the process of inferring depth. In the Ebbinghaus illusion (Aglioti et al., 1995), the size of
      a circle is perceived differently depending on the size of surrounding circles. Similarly, in the
256
      Jastrow illusion (Jastrow, 1892; Day & Knuth, 1981), a specific arrangement of identical
257
      objects affects our perception of their relative size. For all these illusions, we provide both an
258   illusory condition and a condition in which all elements of the original illusion are “scrambled
259   up”, so that a decoder can be trained to predict a specific feature (e.g. the size of the centre
260   circle in the Ebbinghaus illusion) and subsequently tested on illusory configurations.
261
      In the Tilt illusion (Gibson & Radner, 1937), the orientation of a central grating is perceived
262
      as being repulsed from or attracted to the orientation of a surrounding grating. In this case,
263
      we provide conditions with either central or background gratings (configurations which do
264   not support the illusion in humans and could be used for training a decoder), and a condition
265   with both (eliciting the illusion in humans) for testing the model. Another orientation illusion
266   is the Thatcher Effect (Thompson, 1980). This is a phenomenon where local changes
267   in facial features (like inverted eyes or mouth) are less noticeable when the entire face is
268   upside down, highlighting our sensitivity to orientation in face perception. An interesting
269   unresolved issue is the extent to which this inversion effect is specific to faces (Boutsen et al.,
      2006; Wong et al., 2010). Together with a dataset of faces and their Thatcherized version,


                                                      5
270
      we also include a dataset of Thatcherized Words, that is a dataset of images containing
271   words in which one or more letters are rotated by 180 degrees (Wong et al., 2010).
272
273   The lightness contrast effect (Von Helmholtz, 1867) and the Adelson Checker shadow
274
      (Adelson, 2005) illusions reveal how our visual system perceives color and lightness based on
      context. We provide a Grayscale Shapes dataset to train a decoder to output estimates of
275
      lightness at a given location of an image (indicated by a small white arrow). After training,
276
      the network is presented with test images that induce illusions and help assess whether DNNs
277   show similar effects by pointing the arrow at the relevant parts of the images (see Appendix
278   C.2.6 for a detailed description of this approach).
279
280
      It is important to note that there is no accepted account for some of the illusions described
      above. However, even when we have no good understanding of the functional role or the
281
      mechanism that drives an illusion, a DNN model of human vision should show similar effect.
282
      Indeed, understanding the conditions under which DNNs show an illusion may advance
283   our understanding of why the phenomenon is observed in humans. There are now several
284   articles exploring such illusions in various types of DNNs trained in different ways, with some
285   highlighting similarities (e.g., Benjamin et al., 2019; Storrs et al., 2021; Watanabe et al.,
286   2018) others reporting mixed or discrepant results (e.g., Gomez-Villa et al., 2019; Ward,
287   2019; Zhang et al., 2023); for a review of the relevant findings, see Kanwisher et al. (2023).
288
289   2.3   Shape and Object Recognition
290
291   DNN object recognition is much more sensitive than human vision to distributional shifts
292
      from the training set. For instance, humans can easily identify line drawings the first time
      they are exposed to them (Hochberg & Brooks, 1962), whereas DNNs perform poorly under
293
      these conditions (Evans et al., 2022) and need to be trained on line drawings in order to
294
      recognize them at human levels (Singer et al., 2022). We have included the line drawing and
295   silhouettes datasets (from Baker et al., 2018; Baker & Elder, 2022) and also manipulated
296   them in various ways to construct additional datasets. The line drawings were converted
297   into dotted contours (Dotted line drawings), line segments (Segments line drawings)
298   (Biederman & Cooper, 1991), or “texturized” (Texturized line drawings). The texturized
299   images are composed of oriented lines/characters applied to either/both the background
300   or/and the inside area of the line drawing. In all these cases, the resulting images are easily
301   identifiable by human observers due to various Gestalt rules that organize the image features
302   into boundaries. We also apply the same texturization technique outlined above on unfamiliar
303
      “blob”-like shapes (Texturized Unfamiliar dataset). Human observers have no difficulty
      matching a novel “blob” object to its texturized counterpart. In addition, we provide a
304
      dataset of fragmented images based on Baker & Elder (2022) in which the global features
305
      of silhouettes or line drawing are modified by reflecting the top part of an object along its
306   vertical axis, leaving the local features mostly unchanged (Global Modifications dataset).
307   Human performance on these stimuli is greatly reduced but typically DNN performance is
308   largely unchanged, suggesting that human vision is more sensitive to global object structure
309   and DNN vision is more sensitive to local features.
310
      The Embedded Shapes Dataset (inspired by de-Wit et al., 2017) provides another
311
      condition that greatly impacts on human perception, by embedding geometric shapes within
312   complex arrays of lines in ways that camouflage the original shape. We include both the
313   original images from de-Wit et al. (2017) and a procedurally generated dataset in which
314   random polygons are embedded into a configuration that makes recognition challenging for
315   humans.
316
      The human visual system supports object recognition following a wide variety of transforma-
317
      tions (Tanaka, 1996; Blything et al., 2021). Importantly, this extends to cases in which an
318   object has only been viewed at one pose. Previous works suggest a complex link between
319   DNN pretraining and their object recognition capabilities under object transformations
320   (Biscione & Bowers, 2021; 2022). To test whether DNNs share these capacities, we provide a
321   dataset in which translations, plane rotations, and scale changes (2D Transformations) are
322   applied to line drawings. To test for Viewpoint Invariance (e.g. the ability to recognize
323   an object from a new viewpoint after a rotation in depth) we adapt the ETH-80 dataset,
      (Chen et al., 2020a) allowing for controlled variation in azimuth and inclination.


                                                    6
324
      Finally, we provide a dataset to test whether DNNs possess the ability to solve a basic form
325   of visual reasoning task, namely, the Same/Different task. Drawing from Puebla & Bowers
326   (2022), our dataset comprises images composed of pairs of objects, which may be identical
327   or different. These images are organized into ten conditions that vary in their visual form,
328   such as ‘filled polygons’, ‘open squares’, and ‘colored shapes’. While humans effortlessly
329   accomplish this task across all conditions without training, DNNs often struggle when the
330   training and test images come from different conditions.

      3     Testing methods.
333
334
      Each dataset is designed to align with at least one of three methods of testing, but other
335   approaches can be used as well. We discuss further possibilities in Appendix B.
336
337
      3.1   Out-of-Distribution Classification
338
339   In this approach, a DNN pretrained on one dataset is tested on a new dataset composed
340   of out-of-distribution images taken from the trained classes (e.g., a DNN pre-trained on
341   ImageNet is tested on line drawings taken from the same categories). This approach is well
342   suited for most of the Shape and Object Recognition datasets that use images from ImageNet
343   categories modified in such a way that human observers have no trouble recognizing them,
344   even without training. We provide scripts to test a wide variety of vision models.
345
346   3.2   Similarity Judgment Analysis
347
348   This method involves assessing the pairwise similarity of activation patterns in DNNs (using a
349
      Cosine Similarity or an Euclidean Distance metric) evoked by pairs of images and comparing
      these similarities to human performance. This method has been used to assess how well
350
      DNNs capture human similarity judgments (Peterson et al., 2017) and response times to
351
      identify target stimuli from foils (Biscione & Bowers, 2023). It is often useful to carry out
352   these analyses across multiple layers of DNNs given that some psychological phenomena are
353   known to manifest at earlier or later stages of visual processing. A DNN mimicking human
354   perception should show relevant similarity effects at the relevant layers. One key advantage
355   of this approach is that it can be applied to novel images that cannot be classified by a DNN.
356
      To illustrate, we applied this method to the Texturized Unfamiliar dataset (Figure 2). The
357
      human visual system groups elements in a scene by texture (Beck et al., 1983) and classify
358   objects by their shapes (Biederman, 1987). Accordingly, texturized versions of the same
359   shape should be judged as more similar than texturized versions of different shapes. To
360   explore if DNNs exhibit similar behaviour, we input pairs of images into a ImageNet pre-
361   trained ResNet-152 and, for each pair, we computed the Euclidean Distance between their
362   internal activations at every processing level. A human-like response is indicated by a smaller
363   distance for pairs of the same compared to different shapes. ResNet-152 exhibited a weak
364   manifestation of this pattern in the early layers, a reduced effect in the later layers, and
365   no effect in the output layer. By contrast, the human visual system supports similarity
366
      judgements on the basis of shape-based representations that are computed following the
      early stages of visual processing.
367
368
369   3.3   Decoder Method
370
      In this method a small, often single-layer, “decoder” network is attached to a layer of a
371
      frozen DNN and trained on a task designed to reveal how the DNN encodes a specific type
372   of information. For instance, a frozen DNN might be presented with a set of images that
373   contain a target object varying in size, colour, and orientation, and a decoder is trained to
374   output the value of one or more of these properties at a given layer. We provide scripts
375   for both classification and regression training, and scripts to train and test a series of five
376   decoders at varying levels of a ResNet-152 model. Although these scripts are tailored to
377   ResNet-152, they can easily be used as a template to streamline the adaptation of this
      technique for different networks.


                                                    7
378
      To illustrate, consider the Ebbinghaus Illusion. The Ebbinghaus dataset we provide consists
379   of three conditions: two illusory conditions in which a red centre circle (at different radii)
380   is surrounded by either small or large white circles (flankers) in a configuration that, in
381   humans, induces a biased size estimation of the centre circle: the circle appears larger when
382   surrounded by small flankers, everything else being equal. Another condition again contains
383   a red centre circle of different sizes, but the surrounding circles are placed randomly on the
384   canvas so that they would not elicit any illusion on a human observer. We use the latter
385   condition to train decoders attached to a ImageNet pre-trained ResNet-152 model with frozen
386   weights. The task consists of estimating the size of the centre circle. After training, we feed
387
      the illusory images to the decoders. For a network to exhibit the Ebbinghaus visual illusion,
      the size of the centre circle should be overestimated for small flankers and underestimated
388
      for big flankers. We did not find this pattern in ResNet-152 and, indeed, no significant
389
      difference across prediction errors for the different conditions was observed (result for one
390   decoder shown in Figure 2).

      4   Code and Resources
423
      We provide both ready-to-use datasets and scripts to generate them with varying parameters.
424
      Most of the ready-to-use datasets’ size span to around 5,000 images per condition, and
425
      larger dataset can easily be generated using the provided scripts. To separate code and
426   configuration, each dataset generation script relies on a configuration file, consisting of a
427   plain-text file in TOML format specifying all the available parameters for that dataset. Some
428   parameters are used across most datasets, such as image size, background colour, and number
429   of samples. Other parameters are dataset-specific, for example the size and distance of dots
430   in the Dotted line drawing dataset. For convenience, the same configuration file can specify
431   the configuration for multiple (or all) datasets, so that they can be generated in batches.
      The “default” configuration file we used to generate the ready-to-use versions is included,


                                                    8
432
      which can be used as a template. The output of each script is the dataset itself (with several
433   sub-conditions depending on the dataset) together with a CSV annotation file, containing
434   the path and parameters of each generated image.
435
436
      We also provide the code and utilities to evaluate DNNs using the three methods noted
      above. Each method is highly configurable through TOML files, with options including the
437
      type of data-augmentation to apply, the network architecture, the metric to use for the
438
      similarity judgments and more. Users have the flexibility to choose specific factors from this
439   file for analysis, extending beyond the factors that we deemed the most relevant for each task.
440   For example, in the script testing the Ebbinghaus Illusion used for Section 3.3, a decoder is
441   trained to predict the normalized size of the centre circle. However, a different research goal
442   might involve predicting the size of the flankers. This can be achieved by simply specifying
443   the corresponding column (‘NormSizeFlankers’) in the annotation file, without needing to
444   re-generate the dataset or change the code.
445   Each method produces pandas DataFrames (McKinney, 2010) as its output, which can be
446   independently analyzed. Additionally, supplementary files containing simple tests and com-
447   parisons that serve as a springboard for further and more detailed analysis are automatically
448   generated. Comprehensive documentation for every configurable option across all datasets
449   and methods. Additionally, we offer guidance on the general usage of various scripts and
450   utilities through several examples and multiple README files on the GitHub page.
451   For licencing details see Appendix D.

454   5       Limitations
455
456
      While MindSet: Vision offers a valuable resource for exploring visual psychological phenomena
      using deep neural networks, there are several limitations to consider. Firstly, our focus is
457
      primarily on static visual tasks that do not involve high levels of reasoning and are not
458
      directly connected with other areas of cognition such as language and memory.
459
460   Secondly, the methodology for comparing DNN performance to human participants often
461   allows only for qualitative comparisons, as quantitative comparisons may not be feasible
462
      with the current analysis methods.
463   Thirdly, we did not conduct extensive testing on a range of model architectures. We do
464   cite many articles reporting failures of models to replicate human behaviour, and two of the
465   stimulus conditions included in the toolkit came first and second place in the behavioral
466   benchmark track of Brain-Score competition. The competition awarded benchmarks which
467   produced the poorest overall alignment with human behavior across 21 models12 . But our
468
      goal in introducing the toolkit is not to highlight failures of many models nor to provide a
      single benchmark score that characterizes how human-like a model is overall. Rather, it is to
469
      better understand how models fail or succeed in manifesting specific visual phenomena, and
470
      this will involve extensive experiments using each of the provided datasets. We hope the
471   toolkit inspires these research programs.
472
473   Lastly, while we have selected phenomena based on well-replicated and famous visual
474
      experiments, there may be additional phenomena that are not covered by our selection, even
      within the realm of static vision. These limitations underscore the need for further research
475
      and development in the field of computational modeling of human vision to address these
476
      gaps and enhance the utility of MindSet: Vision as a comprehensive toolbox for studying
477   visual perception.

      6       Conclusion
481
      There is much interest in DNNs as models of human vision, but relatively little research
482
      is concerned with how DNNs capture key psychological findings. When DNNs are tested
483
      against key psychological findings, they often fail (Bowers et al., 2023b). And when they do
484
          1
485           BrainScore Competition website
          2
              YouTube re-stream of the Competition Event at CCN 2024


                                                      9
486
      succeed, it is often because the DNNs have not been severely tested (Bowers et al., 2023a). In
487   our view, to better characterize DNN-human alignment, and to build better DNN models of
488   human vision, it is necessary to systematically test models against key experiments reported
489   in psychology. The MindSet: Vision dataset is designed to facilitate this.
490
      Currently it is quite common to rank models in term of how well they perform across several
491
      datasets or tasks. For example, the Brain-Score benchmark (Schrimpf et al., 2018) provides
492
      an overall leaderboard that scores any DNN in terms of how good they are at explaining
493   neural activity variance for core object recognition, and the “model-vs-human” benchmark
494   (Geirhos et al., 2021) ranks and scores models in terms of their behavioural overlap with
495   humans in identifying a range of out-of-distribution object datasets. We do not propose to
496   rank models in this way as each experiment in MindSet: Vision tests a specific hypothesis
497   regarding how DNNs and humans perceive and encode visual inputs. It makes little sense to
498   provide a score that averages across qualitatively different hypotheses. By making stimuli
499   underlying psychological experiments more accessible, easy to generate, configure, and modify,
500   and by providing ready-to-use scripts to test existing models, we hope that the MindSet:
501
      Vision toolbox encourages computational modelling researcher to focus on testing their
      models on key experiments rather than competing on observational datasets that do not
502
      support any conclusions regarding the mechanistic similarity of DNNs and brains.

1031
       A.1   Pre-Generated Datasets
1032
       In the pre-generated dataset, we use 224x224 pixel images, and a variable number of samples
1033   depending on the task and condition (see section below on the number of samples). However,
1034   image size and dataset sizes are parameters that the user can easily modify if needed. The
1035   images in the datasets all have 3 channels (RGB). For almost all datasets, the user can
1036   specify the background color (either a uniform value, or request a different RGB value for
1037   every image), whether to use antialiasing, and the size of the item in the image relative to
1038   the whole canvas.
1039
1040   A.2   Data Augmentation
1041
1042   We do not apply any affine transformation or other data augmentation techniques during
1043
       the dataset generation phase. For instance, in the majority of Shape and Object Recogni-
       tion datasets, a sample typically comprises a modified line drawing or silhouette centrally
1044
       positioned on a canvas without rotation. We deliberately avoid creating replicas of the same
1045
       sample with additional transformations. This approach prevents unnecessary expansion of
1046   the dataset’s size, as most popular deep learning libraries allow for an easy application of
1047   data augmentation. Furthermore, our testing methods allow for the application of affine
1048   transformation during testing through the configuration file, again avoiding the need to
1049   generate pre-augmented datasets.
1050
1051   A.3   Number of Samples and procedural generations
1052
1053   Some datasets contain a fixed and limited number of samples. For example, the “NAP vs
1054   MP: line segments” dataset, recreating the stimuli used in Kubilius et al. (2011), contains 3
1055   conditions with 26 items each. This can be potentially expanded by changing the line stroke,
1056
       the background color, or by applying dataset augmentation separately. For other datasets
       much larger samples with extremely low probability of repetition are easily constructed. For
1057
       example, most Visual Illusions contain a “scrambled” condition in which the elements of the
1058
       illusions are presented “scrambled up” on the canvas, with varying positions and orientation
1059   of each different element. A virtually limitless number of samples can be generated for
1060   these conditions, which is important since they are often used for training decoders. The
1061   pre-generated dataset typically includes approximately 5,000 samples for these conditions.
1062   For users requiring larger sample sizes, they can generate the dataset by changing the
1063   configuration argument relative to sample size (e.g., num_samples_scrambled) depending
1064   on their needs.
1065
1066   B     Other Testing Methods
1067
1068   The datasets featured in MindSet: Vision are suitable for a range of experimental approaches
1069   beyond the ones suggested in Section 3. For example, the relational reasoning capabilities
1070   needed to solve the Same/Different task could be tested by training a network (which
1071   does not need to be pre-trained) on one or a few of the ten available conditions in the
1072   Same/Different dataset, and then testing the network on the remaining conditions (as in
1073   Puebla & Bowers, 2022)). In a similar vein, the 2D transformations and Viewpoint datasets
1074   could be approached by training a network on certain transformations/viewpoints and then
1075   testing it on the others, as demonstrated in Biscione & Bowers (2021).
1076   Another efficient method to gain deeper insights into model performance is conducting
1077   through ablation: by systematically removing or altering specific components of a neural
1078   network, researchers can evaluate the contribution of each specific layers, connections, or
1079   whole network components, providing a more nuanced understanding of the network’s
       functioning (Pang et al., 2021; Zeiler & Fergus, 2014; Chen et al., 2020b).

1124   C.1       Low and Mid-level vision
1125
1126   There is no sharp dividing line between early and middle visual processing, but early vision
1127
       extracts low level feature information (i.e., color and luminance contrasts, the orientation of
       bar and edge segments, and contour segments) from the retinal image. By contrast, mid
1128
       level vision encodes more abstract aspects of shape, such as surfaces and parts of objects,
1129
       in an increasingly viewpoint invariant manner. This is where representations of 2D and
1130   3D shapes, material properties, and coherence of the substances and surfaces in the world
1131   are computed. That is, mid-level vision builds representations of the distal world from the
1132   proximal stimulus. This is an ill-posed problem, and accordingly, various heuristics (such as
1133
           3
               MindSet: Vision summary HTML


                                                     21
1134
       Gestalt perceptual grouping cues) are employed to provide the best estimate of the distal
1135   world. Illusions are striking examples of failures to correctly encode the distal world from
1136   the proximal stimulus. The experiments we include in MindSet: Vision largely focus on
1137   mid-level vision.

       C.1.1   Weber Law
1140
1141   Psychological Significance. The Weber Law (or Weber-Fechner Law) quantifies the
1142   psychophysical relation between changes in the world and changes in perception. The law
1143   states that the minimum physical change of a stimulus on some dimension (e.g., its size)
1144   that is perceptible to an observer is a constant ratio of the original stimulus value on this
1145   dimension. For example, it is easy to distinguish between line lengths of 1 and 2 cm, but
1146   difficult to distinguish between lines of length 100 and 101 cm, despite the fixed difference
1147
       in length (1 cm). To make the latter distinction equally salient, the two stimuli should be
       100 and 200 cm (a fixed ratio of 2). Although Weber’s Law breaks down at extreme values,
1148
       this relationship applies to a wide range of dimensions, from weight, length, size, brightness,
1149
       and even numbers. Weber’s Law reflects the more general observation that perception is
1150   often based on relative rather than absolute encoding of stimulus dimensions. Importantly,
1151   Weber’s Law is often manifest in early visual areas (Hess & Hayes, 1993), and indeed, in
1152   some cases, at the level of the retina (Barlow, 1965; Freeman et al., 2010; Treisman, 1964).
1153
       Jacob et al. (2021) reported that the convolutional DNN VGG-16 showed a human-like
1154
       Weber Law effect when encoding line-lengths. However, the authors only observed Weber’s
1155
       Law for line lengths in the late convolutional layers of the network, did not assess whether
1156   discrimination was a constant ratio of the original stimulus (they employed a weaker test),
1157   and failed to observe a reliable effect for image intensity.
1158
1159
       Dataset. Images in this dataset are composed of a simple horizontal white line with
       varying length and brightness values. Configurable parameters include line width, min/max
1160
       values for length and brightness. To assess DNNs sensitivity to Weber’s Law, a similarity
1161
       judgment analysis assesses whether the relative change in the perception of these stimuli (as
1162   measured by the level of unit activation in the inner layers of a pre-trained DNN) adheres to
1163   a logarithmic relationship with the stimulus strength (e.g. line length).
1164
1165
       C.1.2   Crowding / Uncrowding
1166
1167   Psychological Significance. Our ability to identify objects is impaired by the presence
1168   of nearby objects and shapes, a phenomenon called crowding. At the same time, in some
1169   conditions, the inclusion of additional surrounding objects makes the identification of the
1170   target easier, a phenomenon called uncrowding. This is illustrated in Figure 4, in which
1171   participants are asked to perform a vernier discrimination task by deciding whether the top
1172   vertical line from a pair of vertical lines is shifted to the left or right. When these lines
1173
       are surrounded by a square rather than presented by themselves performance is impaired.
       However, the inclusion of additional squares dramatically improves performance. This is
1174
       thought to reflect a Gestalt process in which the squares are grouped together and then
1175
       processed separately from the vernier (Saarela et al., 2009). Standard DNNs are unable to
1176   explain uncrowding (Doerig et al., 2020; Francis et al., 2017), but the DNNs inspired by the
1177   LAMINART model of Grossberg and colleagues (Raizada & Grossberg, 2001) designed to

1211
       Psychological Significance. Emergent features provide a compelling example of “the
1212
       whole is different than the sum of its parts”. Pomerantz and colleagues (Pomerantz &
1213   Portillo, 2011a; Pomerantz et al., 1977) relied on a simple visual search paradigm where
1214   participants were asked to identify a target amongst foils. They devised several different
1215   types of target and foil stimuli, but the simplest were composed of dot patterns as depicted
1216   below. Participants viewed a set of 4 panels, each of which contained a single dot. Three of
1217   these panels were identical (dots were in the same location) and one outlier panel (where
1218   the dot was in a different location). The task was to identify the outlier panel as quickly
1219   as possible. In the single dot condition, the outlier was simply the panel with a dot in a
1220   unique position. In the critical emergent feature condition(s), a dot (or more) was added to
1221
       the single dot images as context. The context dot(s) was in the same location in all panels.
       Because these added dot(s) were identical in all four panels, there were no new features that
1222
       could be used to facilitate the identification of the outlier other than configural “emergent”
1223
       features. For example, in the top row of Figure 5, the extra dot (depicted in the middle
1224   column) produces the emergent feature of “orientation”, and in the bottom row, the extra
1225   dot produces the emergent feature of proximity. The critical finding was that participants
1226   could identify the location of the outlier panel more quickly in the emergent compared to the
1227   baseline condition. That is, the “whole” was more discriminable than the sum of its parts.
1228
       Biscione & Bowers (2023) carried out a series of studies assessing whether DNNs were
1229
       sensitive to a range of emergent features that facilitated human performance, including
1230   testing DNNs on the dot stimuli illustrated in Figure 5. It was observed that DNNs did
1231   show some sensitivity to some of the emergent features, but only at the later layers of the
1232   network. This is problematic given that these emergent features are thought to be computed
1233   relatively early in the visual system, such that they support rapid “pop out” search.

1274   C.1.4    Decomposition
1275
1276   Psychological Significance. The visual system represents objects in terms of their parts,
1277   separating regions at points of deep concavity (Hoffman & Richards, 1984). Perceptually,
1278   searching for an object broken into its natural parts among a set of unsegmented versions
1279   of the same object is significantly more challenging than locating the same object when
1280   it is segmented at points that do not correspond to its natural divisions. In other words,
1281
       a segmentation at natural points preserves the basic parts which make up the object and
       therefore make the segmented version more similar to the uncut object when compared to an
1282
       ’unnatural’ segmentation. There is good evidence that this occurs relatively early in visual
1283
       processing (Xu & Singh, 2002). To assess whether DNNs encode objects into parts in a
1284   similar manner, Jacob et al. (2021) compared the internal representations of a base object
1285   composed of two parts to two segmentations of the object, one natural and one unnatural.
1286   The assumption is that a natural segmentation of the image will be encoded in a more similar
1287   way to the whole object (the segmented images maintain the integrity of parts that compose
1288   the complete object). However, they reported that the VGG-16 did not show this pattern,
1289   suggesting that DNNs do not encode objects by their parts, or at least, not in a way similar
1290   to humans.

1294    conditions are: ‘no split‘ in which two parts are touching at one point but not overlapping;
1295   ‘natural split‘, in which two parts are separated; ‘unnatural split‘ in which the two parts are

1330   C.1.5   Encoding relations between object parts
1331
1332   Psychological Significance. Humans not only encode objects in terms of their parts,
1333   but also the relations between parts which are essential for object recognition (Biederman,
1334   1987). Early evidence for this was reported by Hummel & Stankiewicz (1996) who trained
1335
       participants to identify a small set of artificial stimuli in which they could easily manipulate
       relations between parts. Two types of changes were introduced to create foils for the base
1336
       stimuli. First, a coordinate change in which relations between parts were maintained but the
1337
       position of a part of the object was changed. And second, a relational change in which there
1338   was a categorical change in relations between object parts. They reported that participants
1339   were much more likely to mistake foil objects for the base object when the relations between
1340   object parts were maintained than when the relations changed (coordinate vs relational change
1341   in Figure 7). By contrast, Malhotra et al. (2023) showed that two standard convolutional
1342   networks are completely insensitive to these relational features, treating Relational and
1343   Coordinate foils equally similar to the Basis objects.
1344   Dataset. We recreated images originally contained in Hummel & Stankiewicz (1996),
1345   Experiment 5, using white strokes on a uniform background. To compare to human perception,
1346   similarity judgments can be computed from pre-trained DNNs by sequentially inputting pairs
1347   of images composed of a base shape and either their corresponding coordinate or relational
1348   change. A pattern that mirrors human perception would result in greater similarity between
1349   the base shapes and their coordinate modifications foils as opposed to their relational change
       foils.

1472   C.1.8    Non-accidental and Metric Properties for Geons and line stimuli
1473
       Psychological Significance: Human object recognition is highly sensitive to non-accidental
1474
       properties (NAPs) of an object, that is, visual features that are invariant over rotations in
1475
       depth. NAPs are hypothesized to be critical for representing object parts such as Geons
1476   (Biederman, 1987). For example, curvature (as opposed to a stright line) is a NAP because
1477   a curved object in the 3D world will project a curved image on a 2D retina when viewed
1478   from most orientations apart from rare “accidental” viewpoints, as when a curve projects a
1479   straight contour. NAPs are distinguished from metric properties (MP), features of objects
1480   that change continuously with variations over depth orientation when projected on the retina.
1481   For example, a curved object in the world will project different degrees of curvature on the
1482   retina depending on its orientation to the viewer. A variety of research highlights how human
1483   vision is more sensitive to changes in images that alter NAPs (e.g., a change from a curve to
1484
       straight line) compared to MPs (e.g., changes in degree of curvature) (Biederman, 1987).
       Kubilius et al. (2017) provided evidence that several DNNs are also more sensitive to image
1485
       manipulations that alter NAPs, although the effects were most pronounced in later layers of
1486
       the networks whereas sensitivity to NAP is thought to occur relatively early in human visual
1487   processing to encode object parts.
1488
1489   Datasets. We have included images of both 2D line segments based on Kubilius et al.
1490
       (2017), and 3D Geon stimuli originally used in Kayaert et al. (2003) to assess the degree
       in which DNNs are sensitive to NAP vs MP changes. In the case of the Geon stimuli, we
1491
       have provided a version with shade (as in Kubilius et al., 2011), a version in which no shades
1492
       are present and the outline is highlighted, and a version in which only the silhouettes are
1493   shown, as one concern with the shaded version is that the similarity judgements produced
1494   by DNNs may reflect differences in shades as opposed to shape. For each Geon or line
1495   segment, a feature dimension (such as the curvature of a Geon) is altered from a singular
1496   value (e.g. straight contour with 0 curvature) to two different values (e.g. slightly curved or
1497   very curved). The ‘reference‘ condition includes items with the intermediate feature value;
1498   in this example, the slightly curved geon. The ‘MP change‘ condition consists of items with
1499   a greater non-singular value; in this case, the greater curvature geon. Finally, the ‘NAP
1500   change‘ condition includes items with the singular value; the straight contour geon from this
1501
       example. A human-like similarity judgment would correspond to higher similarity between
       the reference object to the MP variants than the NAP variants (that is, NAP changes are
1502
       easier to discriminate). Kubilius et al. (2017) provides a more detailed description of human
1503
       performance through reaction times that can be directly compared to similarity judgments
1504   in DNNs (where higher reaction times correspond to lower similarity).

1507
1508   There is now a growing number of articles exploring various illusions in various different types
1509   of DNNs trained in different ways. Some of these highlight similarities with human perception
1510   (e.g., Benjamin et al., 2019; Storrs et al., 2021; Watanabe et al., 2018) while others report
1511   mixed or discrepant results (e.g., Gomez-Villa et al., 2019; Gomez-Villa et al., 2020; Ward,
       2019; Zhang et al., 2023). For a review of various findings see Kanwisher et al. (2023). The


                                                     28
1512
       conditions in which DNNs ’experience’ human-like illusions may provide insights into why
1513   humans experience these phenomena. For instance, Gomez-Villa et al. (2019) found that
1514   various brightness and color illusions can be induced in CNNs trained for image denoising,
1515   image deblurring, and computational color constancy. They argue that these illusions are a
1516   byproduct of biological processes designed to improve efficiency of low-level visual processes.
1517   In addition, (Storrs et al., 2021) found that unsupervised (as opposed to supervised) learning
1518   led DNNs to factorize images into encoding of reflectance and illumination that resulted in a
1519   human-like perceptual illusion of gloss. Here we consider several classic size, lightness, and
1520   orientation illusions. For most of the Visual Illusions Datasets, we provide both an "illusory"
1521
       condition and a corresponding "non-illusory" condition. The non-illusory condition contains
       the same basic elements as the illusory one, but they are arranged in such a way that the
1522
       illusion is not elicited. Both conditions feature procedurally generated images. In the illusory
1523
       conditions, the stimuli are systematically varied to maintain the presence of the illusion.
1524   For example, in the Ponzo illusion dataset, we manipulate factors such as the number of
1525   horizontal "railway" lines, the degree of convergence of the vertical lines, and the length
1526   of each line. However, we carefully constrain these variations to ensure that the illusion
1527   remains effective. We encourage future researchers who wish to modify our scripts or use
1528   non-default parameters to regenerate the datasets to verify that the illusory stimuli continue
1529   to produce the intended perceptual effects.

1531   C.2.1   Müller-Lyer illusion
1532
1533
       Psychological Significance. The Müller-Lyer illusion is perhaps the most famous of all
       illusions. There is no agreed-upon explanation of the effect but the fact that it is observed
1534
       across species, including fish Sovrano et al. (2016), suggests that it reflects something basic
1535
       about the architecture of the visual system rather than the training environment. Ward
1536   (2019) reported that VGG-19 showed the illusion (to a rough approximation), although they
1537   only reported the effect at the final stage of the network. A similar result was reported by
1538   Zhang et al. (2022) who reported more robust effects in the higher levels of VGG-19 and
1539   ResNet-101.
1540
       Dataset. The Müller-Lyer illusion stimuli were generated in one of two ‘illusory’ configura-
1541

1542   are arranged randomly in the canvas, separated from the line segment. In all three conditions,
1543   we vary the line length, the position of the line, and the angle of the fins. A method to
1544   test whether a DNN is susceptible to this illusion involves training a set of decoders to
1545   predict the line length in the scrambled condition set. These decoders are then tested on the
1546   illusory conditions. A human-like response would be evidenced by a consistent pattern of
1547   both overestimating the line length in the outward illusory condition and underestimating it
1548   in the inward illusory condition. Additionally, the illusory effect should be larger for more
1549   acute fin angles.

1551   C.2.2   Ponzo illusion
1552
       Psychological Significance. In this classic illusion, two identical horizontal lines cross
1553
       a pair of converging lines, a configuration similar to railway tracks. In this configuration,
1554   the top line looks longer. The standard explanation is that the visual system assumes that
1555   the converging lines are receding in depth and that the upper horizontal line is further
1556   away. Given that the two lines project the same length on the retina, the visual system
1557   assumes that the upper line must be longer. That is, the illusion is a by-product of the
1558   visual system attempting to compute size constancy. Interestingly, there is good evidence
1559   that the Ponzo illusion (He et al., 2015), and related size illusions (Sperandio et al., 2012),
1560   alter the activation in V1, although this may reflect top-down activation from higher-level
1561   visual areas (Chen et al., 2019). Ward (2019) failed to observe a Ponzo effect in VGG-19.
1562   Dataset. Two target lines (red and blue) are placed across a railway track pattern. In the
1563   illusory condition, the target lines have the same length (varying across samples). In the
1564   scrambled condition, the target lines have different length, are still placed horizontally one
1565   on top of the other, but all the other segments are randomly placed across the canvas. We
       include a third condition in which the railway track pattern is used with target lines which


                                                     29
1566
       differ in length. The railway track pattern for the illusory and different lengths conditions is
1567   composed of converging segments (with a varying degree of convergence), and horizontal
1568   segments (randomly placed at different horizontal positions). For all three conditions, the
1569   user can specify the number of horizontal segments to use.
1570
       The suggested way to test whether DNNs perceive the Ponzo Illusion consists of training a
1571
       set of decoders on the scrambled condition, to predict either the length of the target lines
1572
       or a function of the length (for example, the difference between the top and the bottom
1573   line lengths). Then the decoders can be tested on the illusory condition. The different
1574   lengths condition could be used as a further way of analysing the decoders response. To
1575   match human perception, a decoder should overestimate the length of the top target line (or
1576   underestimate the length of the bottom line, or output a positive difference in top minus
1577   bottom line length, depending on the training setup) in the illusory condition (where the
1578   two target lines have the same length).

1580   C.2.3    Ebbinghaus (or Titchener) illusion.
1581
       Psychological Significance: In this classic illusion, the perceived size of a central circle
1582
       is altered by the size of surrounding circles. There is evidence that the illusion distorts
1583   the perception of size but not action (Aglioti et al., 1995; Whitwell et al., 2022; but see

       C.2.4    Jastrow Illusion
1599   Psychological Significance: In the Jastrow Illusion (Jastrow, 1892), two identical-sized
1600   curved segments are perceived as different sizes when one is placed above the other in
1601   certain configurations. There are multiple explanations for the phenomenon, but perhaps
1602   the simplest explanation is that it is a form of a contrast effect. The length of the concave
1603   edge of the upper object in a Jastrow configuration is much shorter than the convex edge of
1604   the bottom object, and this contrast drives the perception of size when the edges are closely
1605   aligned (Rock, 1975). Rhesus monkeys do not appear to be affected (Agrillo et al., 2019),
1606
       nor do humans when assessed on grasping behavior (Ozana & Ganel, 2020). As far as we
       are aware, no one has reported whether DNNs show a similar pattern.
1607
1608   Dataset. We used a red and a blue arc shape, either one on top of the other at the centre

1610   with a random orientation (‘scrambled’ condition). In the scrambled and different lengths
1611   conditions the two shapes have different sizes. The size is the same (thus eliciting the illusion)
1612
       in the illusory condition. To estimate DNNs susceptibility to the illusion the same approach
       as the Ponzo Illusion can be used.

1636

1637
       an object) whereas brightness is a measure of the amount of light reflected from an object,
1638

1639   two famous illusions related to lightness: the Lightness Contrast Illusion and the Adelson
1640   Checker Shadow Illusion. However, to facilitate testing for these and other lightness-related
1641   effects, we created an additional dataset called ‘Grayscale shapes’. The purpose of this
1642   dataset is not to elicit any illusion in humans or in DNNs but to train a network (or, with
1643   our suggested method, a decoder attached to a network) to output the grayscale value of a
1644   target pixel.
1645   Grayscale shapes Dataset. Each image is composed of 20 overlapping items amongst the
1646   following types of shapes (circle, circle sector, circle segment, ellipse, rectangle with straight
1647   and rounded corners, heptagon, irregular polygon composed of a random number of edges

1649   for each shape. We place 20 items to be sure that most space in the canvas is filled by an
1650   item, but that only a few of them are fully visible. This results in a chaotic canvas with
1651   many different shapes with varying grayscale colours but with coherent patterns (as opposed
1652   to, for example, having each pixel of a different random grayscale value). In order to target
1653

1655

1658   network with the decoder attached the color-picker. We can then test whether an illusory

1716   C.2.9   Thatcher Illusion
1717
1718   In this illusion the eyes and mouth of upright faces are inverted to produce a grotesque
1719
       image of a person. The distinction between a normal face and a distorted face is highly
       salient. However, when faces are upside down, the distortions are much less salient. This
1720
       effect was originally reported on images of Margret Thatcher, thus the name of the illusion.
1721
       This effect is sometimes claimed to be more dramatic for faces compared to other categories,
1722   lending support to the hypothesis that face processing is special (Boutsen et al., 2006).
1723   Other researchers claim that similar effects are observed for other types of objects (Wong
1724   et al., 2010). Jacob et al. (2021) demonstrated that CNNs trained to identify faces exhibit a
1725   Thatcher-like effect, though they did not assess whether this effect extends to other object
1726   categories. The extent to which this effect is specific to faces or can be generalized to non-face
1727   objects remains a subject of debate (e.g., see Wong et al., 2010; Bertin & Bhatt, 2004; Dahl
       et al., 2010). To test DNN sensitivity to the Thatcher Effect for both faces and non-face


                                                      32
1728
       dataset, we provide both a Thatcherized dataset of faces and a Thatcherized dataset of words
1729   (in which individual letters are rotated).
1730
1731    Face Dataset. We provide a small dataset of celebrity faces using a subset of CelebA4 , but
1732
        the user can specify any folder containing images of faces. Each image is resized according to
        parameters specified by the user and then reoriented into both an upright and a 180-degree
1733
        inverted configuration. Furthermore, it is either ’Thatcherized’ or remains unaltered. To
1734
       ‘Thatcherize’ an image we compute landmarks of the eyes and mouth, compute the bounding
1735    rectangle for each, and rotate them around their centre of mass. Blurring on the edge is
1736    applied to minimize artefacts. To assess the susceptibility of DNNs to the Thatcher effect
1737    in faces, we propose a similarity judgment analysis. This involves comparing the perceived
1738    similarity between each upright face and its Thatcherized counterpart, as well as each inverted
1739    face with its Thatcherized version. To align with human perception, the latter comparison is
1740    expected to yield a higher similarity score than the former.
1741   Word Dataset. We employ a collection of 1000 English words or artificially generated
1742   sequences of random letters. All entries are uniformly presented in uppercase, covering a
1743   range from 3 to 8 letters in length. Following Wong et al. (2010), to simulate the Thatcher
1744   Effect for words, we rotate one or more letters by 180 degrees. To increase variability, each
1745   word is displayed in one of ten different fonts, with variable font sizes, and includes jitter for

1750   C.3      Shape and object recognition
1751
1752
       A key feature of human vision is that we identify objects largely based on their shape.
       For example, we can easily identify line drawings of objects with no colour and texture
1753
       (Biederman & Ju, 1988). To measure shape bias in DNNs there is now a benchmark that
1754
       tests models on “style transfer” images composed of the shape of one category and the texture
1755   of another (Geirhos et al., 2019). Many DNNs, including DNNs that perform at the top of
1756   the leaderboard on Brain-Score, rely primarily on non-shape features, as they classify the
1757   images based on their texture rather than shape. More recent DNNs trained on much larger
1758   datasets have started to show a more human-like shape bias (Dehghani et al., 2023), but
1759   there are many additional attributes of human shape perception that need to be accounted
1760   for by any DNN model of human vision.
1761
1762   C.3.1      Identifying line drawings, dotted line drawings, silhouettes, and
1763              image segments
1764
       Psychological Significance: Humans can often identify line drawings of objects as quickly
1765   and accurately as photographs, highlighting the importance of shape for object identification
1766   (Biederman & Ju, 1988). Interestingly, a child who had never previously been exposed to
1767   line drawings can readily identify them, showing that there is no need to be trained on line
1768   drawings to identify them (Hochberg & Brooks, 1962). By contrast, DNNs need to be trained
1769   on line drawings in order to recognize them at human levels (Singer et al., 2022). Similarly,
1770   humans can easily identify silhouettes of objects, whereas DNNs again struggle (although
1771   interestingly, they do better with silhouettes compared to line drawings; Baker et al., 2018).
1772   In addition, by exploiting the Gestalt principle of good continuation, humans can recognise
1773   line drawings with modified local features when the global shape is left intact. In our
1774   experiments, we modified line drawings in three ways: by replacing the continuous line with
1775   dots; by replacing continuous lines with segments; and by texturizing them. These images are
1776   easily identifiable, and accordingly, DNNs should be able to identify these out-of-distribution
1777   images.

1850
1851   Psychological Significance: The Embedded Figures Test (EFT, de-Wit et al., 2017)
1852   is a widely utilized tool in research exploring individual differences in perception, with a
1853

1854

1855

1860   the lower the performance, highlighting human susceptibility to camouflage and the role of
1861   Gestalt organisation principles in camouflage.

1868   camouflaging samples can then be procedurally generated from each polygon. Training
1869   decoders to classify the simple geometric forms provides one way to assess the impact of

1871

1872

1933   C.3.5   Invariance to Object Transformation
1934
1935
       Psychological Significance. Humans possess the remarkable ability to recognize objects
       despite the different retinal images the objects project depending on changes in size, ori-
1936
       entation, lighting, and placement (Tanaka, 1996). This is performed on-line: an object,
1937
       once seen in a new or altered form, can typically be recognized instantly in subsequent
1938   exposures at different angles, without further training. This capability applies irrespective
1939   of the object’s familiarity (Blything et al., 2021; 2020). Previous work (Biscione & Bowers,
1940   2021; 2022) found that none of the 7 tested classic visual DNNs possess on-line invariance
1941   architecturally (that is: training an object at a viewpoint would not automatically support
1942   object recognition at a different viewpoint, even for simple affine transformations such as
1943   translation). However, this ability could be induced by pre-training the model on the specific
       transformation of interest, and this would transfer to unfamiliar classes. For example, a


                                                     36
1944
       network pretrained to classify images in which the objects are randomly rotated, develop
1945   on-line invariance to rotation, even for novel objects and novel classes. This partially extends
1946   even in transformation of viewpoint, in which the object is rotated in depth.
1947
1948
       Datasets: 2D Affine Transformations and Viewpoint Transformations. We provide
       a separate dataset for affine transformations (rotation in picture plane, translation, scale, and
1949
       shear) and viewpoint invariance (rotation in depth). The configurable parameters allow for a
1950
       fine-grained analysis of the effect of each transformation. For each transformation dimension,
1951   the user can chose one or multiple ranges of training and testing. As in the previous datasets,
1952   the user can specify any folder containing line-drawings or silhouettes (or any image with
1953   a clear contour on a white background). For the viewpoint invariance dataset, we use the
1954   ETH-80 dataset (Chen et al., 2020a)5 , which contains 8 categories (apples, cars, cows, cups,
1955   dogs, horses, pears and tomatoes), each consisting of 10 object instances, and each object
1956   captured from 41 different viewpoints. For the pre-generated dataset, we avoided including
1957   views straight from the top. The configurable parameters allow the user to generated dataset
1958   only within a specific azimuth and inclination range.
1959   For both the 2D transformation and viewpoint datasets, there are several ways to test
1960   whether a DNNs possess online invariance to transformation. First, a DNNs (not necessarely
1961   pretrained) could be trained on un-transformed images (e.g. with the object always in the
1962   center, unrotated, unscaled, or from a standard viewpoint). It could then be tested on
1963   various transformations of these objects. This could be used to establish whether the network
1964   is architecturally invariant to some transformations. Several pre-training steps could be used
1965
       to test how the training environment affects performance. Another approach avoids training
       on the target classes (either because we want to test a pretrained network without altering
1966
       its weights, or because we want to test the network on unfamiliar classes): a similarity
1967
       judgments analysis is performed on transformed versions of the same object, and is compared
1968   to the similarity of different objects. A human-like DNN will have internal activations that
1969   are more similar for same objects across transformations, than for different objects. This is
1970   the approach used in Biscione & Bowers (2021; 2022).

1972   C.3.6       Same/Different Task
1973
1974   Psychological Significance. Human shape representations not only support object recog-
1975   nition but also a wide variety of additional functions, including visual reasoning. Perhaps
1976   the simplest form of visual reasoning is tested in the same/different task – judging whether
1977
       two shapes are identical apart from their spatial location. Although DNNs can solve the
       same/different task when training and test images are highly similar to one another, perfor-
1978
       mance drops when training/test images are dissimilar (Puebla & Bowers, 2022). By contrast,
1979
       humans can make same/different judgements for any visual patterns as long as they are
1980   perceptible.
1981
1982   Same/Different Dataset. The dataset was extracted from (Puebla & Bowers, 2022). This
1983
       dataset is composed of 10 conditions. Each image consists of two items placed randomly on
       the canvas. The two items can be either the same shape or a different shape and cannot
1984
       overlap. Each condition consists of a different type of item used. By default, the items are
1985
