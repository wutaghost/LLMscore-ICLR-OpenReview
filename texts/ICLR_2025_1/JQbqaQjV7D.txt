011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) hold revolutionary potential to digitize and en-
014            hance the Health & Public Services (H&PS) industry. Despite their advanced
015
               linguistic abilities, concerns about accuracy, stability, and traceability still persist,
               especially in high-stakes areas such as transportation systems. Moreover, the pre-
016
               dominance of English in LLM development raises questions about how they per-
017
               form in non-English contexts. This study introduces a novel cross-lingual bench-
018            mark dataset comprising nearly 99,869 real traffic incident records from Vienna
019            (2013-2023) to assess the robustness of state-of-the-art LLMs (≥ 9) in the spa-
020            tio and temporal domain of traffic incident classification. We then explored three
021            hypotheses — sentence indexing, date-to-text conversion, and German-to-English
022            translation — and incorporated Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) to further
023            examine the models’ ability to handle hallucinations in both spatial and temporal
024            contexts. Our experiments with GPT-4 and Llama models reveal significant per-
025            formance disparities across these hypotheses in the spatio-temporal domain and
026
               also demonstrate how RAG can mitigate what types of hallucinations. These find-
               ings underscore the need for enhanced cross-lingual capabilities and improved
027
               explainability in LLMs. We provide open access to our Health & Public Services
028
               (H&PS) traffic incident dataset, with the project demo and code available at Web-
029            site https://sites.google.com/view/llmhallucination/home.

033
034   Large Language Models (LLMs) such as GPT-3.5 (Radford et al., 2018), GPT-4 (Achiam et al.,
035   2023), and LaMDA (Thoppilan et al., 2022) have substantially enhanced public access to complex
036   information, particularly in sectors such as healthcare and public services (H&PS). These mod-
037   els are celebrated for their capability to demystify intricate information, assisting in tasks ranging
038   from routine inquiries to aiding clinical decision-making (Brown et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2018;
039
      Achiam et al., 2023; Thoppilan et al., 2022). ChatGPT (Achiam et al., 2023), a derivative of the
      InstructGPT model (Ouyang et al., 2022), has garnered a vast user base, especially for textual tasks,
040
      through its sophisticated multi-turn prompting dialog interface, which is further refined by Rein-
041
      forcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF) (Lambert et al., 2022). Despite its proficiency
042   in various NLP tasks, ChatGPT has faced also critique. Anecdotal reports on ChatGPT revealed con-
043   sistently remaining challenges (Bang et al., 2023) - for instance, it struggles with specific reasoning
044   tasks (Davis, 2023; Guo et al., 2023), often hallucinates facts, and produces non-factual statements,
045   undermining its reliability (Shen et al., 2023; Thorp, 2023). Additionally, its language coverage re-
046   mains limited and its predominant focuses on English in model training and evaluation raises issues
047   of equitable access for non-English speakers (Seghier, 2023), especially given that over 82% of the
048   global population does not speak English as their primary or secondary language (Crystal, 2003; Lu
049   et al., 2022; Jiao et al., 2023).
050   Previous research has primarily focused on evaluating the knowledge capabilities of LLMs (Singhal
051   et al., 2022) or leveraging the stochasticity in sampled responses to assess the factuality of generated
052   outputs (Goodrich et al., 2019). Furthermore, substantial efforts have been directed towards devel-
053   oping neural network techniques to make accurate predictions on synthetic data (Li et al., 2024).
      Given that LLMs are trained on extensive datasets, it is crucial to explore how these models perform

099
100
      In this section, we discuss previous works that investigate LLMs’ (Brown et al., 2020) performance
101
      on hallucination resolution (Rawte et al., 2023). Previous studies have explored the capabilities of
102   models like ChatGPT (Achiam et al., 2023), suggesting various methods to mitigate its limitations.
103   For instance, Bang et al. (Bang et al., 2023) show that ChatGPT excels in zero-shot learning across 9
104   of 13 NLP datasets (Socher et al., 2013), even outperforming fully fine-tuned, task-specific language
105   models in four different tasks. However, they also report a noticeable performance decline when
106   handling languages with limited resources, particularly in non-Latin scripts. The weakness lies
107   more in generation than in the understanding part of the translation process. Manakul et al. (Manakul
      et al., 2023) introduced SELFCHECKGPT, a method for detecting hallucinations in LLM-generated


                                                                                              2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      responses. This technique, relying on response consistency, may overlook cases where LLMs deliver
109   consistent but inaccurate information, leading to potential false negatives in hallucination detection.
110
111   Among related work, the XLingEval framework by Choudhury et al. (Choudhury et al., 2023) most
112
      closely aligns with our research. It assesses LLM behavior across several languages (English, Hindi,
      Chinese, and Spanish), focusing on metrics like correctness, consistency, and verifiability. Their
113
      findings indicate significant performance disparities across languages, with non-English responses
114
      generally showing an 18.12% decrease in quality (Choudhury et al., 2023). However, the study
115   also shows limitations: it only investigated the influence of multilingualism with state-of-the-art
116   LLMs, without further exploring how to avoid hallucinations in industries other than healthcare, or
117   in languages such as German. Additionally, it did not examine the impact of input data on these
118   LLMs or other factors beyond language type differences. For example, the format of the data,
119   the effects of prompts under different temperature hyperparameters, and the interactions between
120   prompts and data were not considered. In contrast, the UrbanGPT study (Li et al., 2024) utilizes
121   LLMs specifically for modeling urban environments, applying a GPT variant to zero-shot learning
122   tasks in traffic management and public safety. The study emphasizes the critical role of high-quality,
123
      representative spatio-temporal data in training effective models (Li et al., 2024).
124   We also acknowledge the strengths and weaknesses of RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation), as
125   highlighted in recent studies. For instance, RAG may struggle to properly contextualize or synthe-
126   size retrieved data, leading to augmentations that either lack depth or fail to capture the nuances of
127   the query (Jiang et al., 2023; Siriwardhana et al., 2023), particularly with non-English data (Jiang
128   et al., 2023). Moreover, RAG systems can sometimes generate responses that are misleading, in-
129
      complete, or contextually off-target. Another concern is latency sensitivity, and training local LLMs
      with RAG is technically more complex and costlier than methods like prompt fine-tuning or data
130
      augmentation (Karpukhin et al., 2020; Guu et al., 2020). In this paper, we further explore both the
131
      strengths and limitations of RAG in handling temporal and spatial data within real-world industry
132   applications.
133
134   Our work extends these discussions by adopting a quantitative methodology to rigorously test each
135
      hypothesis and by constructing a hybrid dataset of public traffic incident records from Vienna, span-
      ning over decades. Distinguished from the XLingEval framework (Choudhury et al., 2023),it offers
136
      more realistic basis for investigating LLM hallucinations and enables more intricate spatio-temporal
137
      analyses, focusing particularly on minority languages like German.

242
      and non-English complex linguistic samples across various categories as outlined in Table 2. Both

      In this section, we assess the robustness of major SOTA LLMs (Brown et al., 2020) in-
329   cludes the GPT series (Radford et al., 2018), tinyLlama model (Touvron et al., 2023),
330   Claude-3-Haiku(Claude-3-Haiku), Claude-3-Haiku-200K (Claude-3-Haiku-200K), Claude-3-
331   Sonnet(Claude-3-Sonnet), Gemini-Pro 1.0(1.0), Mistral Medium(M), Mistral-8x7B(Mistral-
332   8x7B), Llama-3-70B-T(Llama-3-70B-T) and Llama-3-70b-Inst-FW(Llama-3-70B-Inst-FW).
333   Specifically, we evaluated the models’ output stability by examining response quality across dif-
334   ferent temperature settings for both spatial and temporal tasks. For temporal tasks, we analyzed
335   responses across 10 categories, with additional details provided in the Appendix. Each category
336   was cross-verified and tested over a 10-year period (2013–2023). For spatial tasks, we assessed five
337
      scenarios, encompassing varying traffic conditions and routing challenges.
338   Due to token insertion limitations in LLMs, we conducted a total of 165 samples per model (11
339   temperature settings from 0.0 to 1.0 * 10 temporal categories + 11 temperature settings * 5 spatial
340   scenarios), comparing results across 9 different LLM models (as shown in Table 3). For hypothesis
341   verification, we then carried out 66 test samples per LLM, including tests on typical LLaMA and
342   GPT-4 models (11 temperature settings * 2 conditions: with and without hypothesis application *

      3 hypotheses). We have included a table detailing the architectures, hyperparameters, and prompt
      settings of the mentioned LLMs (see Appendix Table 9). Additionally, we provide attributes of each
344
      LLMs, including cost information, energy consumption, and architectural complexity.
345
346   To provide a more comprehensive evaluation, we also included RAG-driven (Jiang et al., 2023)
347   LLM experiments using our dataset . These experiments were conducted with DataStax (dat) and
348   Langflow(lan), where we vectorized dataset samples as context and used Astra DB(ast) as the vector
349
      database. We incorporated spatial and temporal queries as embeddings, adhering to the allowable
      TPM (tokens per minute) limit of 15,000 imposed by the Open API rate limits. Regarding the
350
      dataset, we primarily used the H&PS Traffic Incidents Dataset, while also comparing with existing
351
      benchmarks (see e.g. Appendix Table 8). Due to token limitations, it wasn’t feasible to input all
352   ten years of data at once into GPT-like models (Radford et al., 2018). However, we maintained the
353   independence and objectivity of the experiments by maximizing text complexity for logical reason-
354   ing and ensuring adequate token lengths for each query. We sampled random daily entries spanning
355   ten years, each containing 4,000 to 8,000 tokens and covering at least 10 station or subway/bus
356   incidents.

530
      In this work, we introduce a novel industrial spatio-temporal benchmark dataset (H&PS Traffic
      Incidents) for enabling researchers to rigorously assess hallucinations in LLMs when handling real-
531
      world spatio-temporal challenges. It features diverse scenarios requiring both temporal and spatial
532
      reasoning. And we further conclude the following interesting findings: 1) Major LLMs exhibit a
533   significant number of unbalanced spatio-temporal hallucinations, and struggling more in the tempo-
534   ral domain. 2) Temperature setting changes exhibit a pronounced impact but nonlinear relationship
535   where not all lower temperatures consistently result in robustness. 3) Converting non-English Con-
536   text data (NOT Only Prompt) to English proves to improve logical reasoning and reduce spatial
537   hallucinations. 4) While RAG improves contextual factual accuracy, it does not always enhance
538   logical reasoning when handling more complex spatial problems or intricate temporal queries.
