011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Speech large language models (LLMs) offer a unified approach to handling vari-
014            ous speech-processing tasks using a single autoregressive model built on discrete
015            speech and audio codecs. Unlike traditional pipeline-based systems, which in-
016
               volve separate components for speech recognition, understanding, and genera-
               tion, end-to-end speech LLMs can capture both verbal and non-verbal informa-
017
               tion, such as paralinguistic and speaker characteristics. This enables full-duplex
018
               capabilities, allowing the system to listen and speak simultaneously with low la-
019            tency, making it ideal for conversational AI. In this paper, we introduce a novel
020            codec-free, full-duplex framework for speech understanding and generation, and
021            present SALMONN-omni, an instance of this speech LLM. SALMONN-omni
022            can listen to its own generated speech and background sounds while speaking. To
023            align the frame rate gap between text and audio, we propose a novel thinking step,
024            ensuring high performance on pre-trained tasks. Using a two-stage understand
025            then generate training approach, SALMONN-omni effectively addresses a variety
026            of streaming speech tasks, including speech recognition, synthesis, enhancement,
027
               dereverberation, target speaker extraction, and spoken question answering.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large language models (LLMs) have established a new approach to problem-solving and task ex-
033
      ecution through natural conversations. Speech, being a fundamental form of human communica-
      tion, acts as an intuitive and effective means for interactions between humans and LLMs. As a
034
      result, there is a growing research emphasis on enhancing the spoken input and output capabilities
035
      of LLMs. Some recent studies have focused on equipping LLMs with a comprehensive understand-
036   ing of speech and audio, such as SALMONN (Tang et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024)
037   and LTU (Gong et al., 2024; 2023), while other research has explored utilizing LLMs’ advanced
038   language understanding abilities to develop more sophisticated speech generation and processing
039   methods (Hao et al., 2023).
040
      To further advance the naturalness of interaction with LLMs, more recently, full-duplex speech
041
      LLMs have been developed that support both speech understanding and generation. Some work
042   achieves this by integrating inputs or outputs of standalone speech recognition and synthesis systems
043   into LLMs (Wu et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024). However, cascaded systems transcribe, understand
044   and generate in series, resulting in systematic error accumulation and high latency, impeding the
045   fluidity of real-time conversations with users. Meanwhile, end-to-end speech LLMs have been in-
046   vestigated, which often discretize speech into tokens and extend the LLM’s vocabulary to support
047   speech input and output (Ma et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023a; Défossez et al., 2024; Rubenstein
048   et al., 2023). While these models achieve lower latency and end-to-end training, tokenized speech
049   representations limit the expressivity of the high-dimensional speech signal due to the constraints
050
      on the number of tokens, often resulting in suboptimal performance in speech modelling.
051   This paper proposes SALMONN-omni, a codec-free full-duplex framework for low-latency stream-
052   ing speech understanding and generation with speech LLMs. SALMONN-omni enables speech pro-
053   duction and the perception of surrounding sounds and its own speech at the same time. In contrast to
      existing methods which rely on specific tokenization of the speech signal, SALMONN-omni models

      2   R ELATED W ORK
073
074
      With the recent research advancements in multimodal LLMs, LLMs have been used for both speech
      and audio understanding and generation. SALMONN(Tang et al., 2024), Qwen-audio(Chu et al.,
075
      2023) and LTU(Gong et al., 2024; 2023) are early investigations that demonstrated generic audio
076
      understanding abilities with LLMs, which significantly broadened the scope of tasks a single model
077   can perform. Later work further exploits the power of specific tasks such as speech translation (Chen
078   et al., 2023b), entity retrieval (Wang et al., 2023) or emotion recognition (Latif et al., 2023), or to
079   improve specific aspects such as task overfitting (Deng et al., 2024) or data efficiency (Katsumaru
080   et al., 2009; Manakul et al., 2024), etc. On speech and audio generation, LLMs have either been
081   used to provide better textual descriptions that facilitate text-to-speech (TTS) synthesis (Zhang et al.,
082   2023b; Leng et al., 2023), or been used to provide tokens that can directly be mapped to audio (Dekel
083   et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023). In particular, Dekel et al. (2023) studies streaming speech generation
084   alongside text generation, enabling seamless spoken response generation.
085   Full-duplex speech LLMs have recently become a research focus, with various methods being pro-
086   posed that enable both speech understanding and generation simultaneously. AudioGPT (Huang
087   et al., 2024) and NextGPT (Wu et al., 2024) are examples where separate speech recognition and
088   synthesis systems were integrated to enable speech-based interaction with LLMs. More recently, re-
089   searchers have investigated end-to-end trainable speech-text interfaces with LLMs by expanding the
090   LLM vocabulary with speech tokens representing different speech signals (Ma et al., 2024; Zhang
091
      et al., 2023a; Défossez et al., 2024; Rubenstein et al., 2023), which suffers from the trade-off be-
      tween number of tokens and representation ability. In contrast to these methods, SALMONN-omni
092
      is the first speech LLM that directly leverages the continuous speech representation space that is
093
      independent of any specific sets of speech tokens.

096   3   C ODEC - FREE F ULL - DUPLEX S PEECH U NDERSTANDING AND G ENERATION
097       F RAMEWORK
098
099   A full-duplex speech understanding and generation framework must address four key challenges.
100   First, it should support streaming speech input and output. Second, it must provide a mechanism
101   to handle both input and output streams simultaneously. Third, it must incorporate a period to
102   synchronize the states of the input and output streams. Finally, it should implement a strategy for
103   the model to learn turn-taking in natural human conversations, such as when to backchanneling or
104
      to be badged in by the user.
105   Instead of tokenizing speech into discrete codecs and using next-token-prediction to modelling both
106   the textual and auditory tokens, we propose the first codec-free full-duplex speech understanding
107   and generation framework, as shown in Figure 1, which keeps the LLM generating only text tokens
      to avoid jointly modeling tokens of two modalities in a single sequence model. Four key features

134   in our framework address the challenges for implementing a full-duplex system while keeping the
135   core of the model codec-free. First, we propose to incorporate the LLM with a streaming speech
136   encoder and a streaming speech synthesizer to enable streaming speech input and output. Second,
137   the speech encoder and synthesizer are connected to the LLM with several cross attention layers
138   (Vaswani et al., 2017) so that the model can handle both input and output streams simultaneously
139   in an autoregressive manner. Third, we set a fixed period for the synchronization between input and
140
      output streams. Specifically, a fixed policy is introduced for the mapping between the number of text
      embeddings and the duration of the speech streams. Finally, a novel ”thinking” strategy is proposed
141
      to enable the model to learn turn-taking in natural human conversations. As shown in Figure 2, when
142
      the model should not response to the inputs, we just send ”thinking” tokens into the LLM and train
143   the model not to generate start-of-generation token. Because we don’t force the model to generate
144   any specific token, the LLM will not tend to bias to one special token and the degradation of the
145   performance can be negligible. Moreover, due to the frame rate difference between text and speech,
146   even if the textual embeddings are finished generating, we still send ”thinking” embeddings into the
147   speech synthesizer to make sure the full-duplex framework is complete.

154

158   embedding which are used as the input to a series of standard Mamba language model blocks. The

183   4.2   S TREAMING TTS
184
185
      Our streaming TTS system builds on the popular open-source TTS VoiceCraft-830M (Peng et al.,
      2024), which employs a codec language model architecture. A streaming TTS is capable of stream-
186
      ing generation conditioned on a streaming increased input. A codec language model is suitable for
187
      the requirement of streaming generation, since it generates codec codes autoregressively, which can
188   be transformed to speech waveform via a codec decoder. However, current codec language models
189   require the entire text sequence to generate codec codes, limiting their ability to accept incremental
190   text input during generation. To address this limitation, we have implemented several modifications
191   that allow our model to accommodate streaming input effectively.
192
      Our solution involves transforming a non-causal mask decoder-only codec language model into a
193
      causal cross-attention decoder model, as shown in Figure 3. Traditional decoder-only codec lan-
194   guage models struggle to process incremental text input because text and codec code are combined
195   as the decoder input. Once the model begins generating codec codes based on the entire text se-
196   quence, it can no longer accept new text input, or the codec code generation would be interrupted.
197   To overcome this limitation, we implement a cross-attention decoder architecture that separates text
198   input from codec code input. The embeddings of generated tokens from large language models
199   serve as the text input, fed to the cross attention, while the codec codes function as the decoder
200   input. A stack of linear layers is employed to transform the embeddings to the dimension of Voice-
201   Craft attention. To effectively model streaming incremental input, we utilize a fixed-number causal
202
      cross-attention; for instance, generating 20 codec codes from 2 embeddings provided by the large
      language model. This causal cross-attention mechanism enables the codec language model to gen-
203
      erate audio seamlessly based on streaming input.

      5.1       TASK CONFIGURATION
273
274
      SALMONN-omni is a multi-task speech generation model that supports zero-shot TTS, speech en-
275   hancement, dereverberation, and target speaker extraction. All generation tasks are formulated as
276   a ”Understand then Speak” manner, as demonstrated in Fig 1. The speech LLM generates text
277   based on the text prompt and audio prompt, then the streaming TTS produces speech from the em-
278   beddings of the generated tokens synchronously. The generated speech is fed back to the speech
279   encoder during generation process. Additionally, a 3-second speaker prompt is placed at the be-
280   ginning of Voicecraft to control the voice of the generated speech. Since our primary focus is on
281   extracting meaning and producing clear speech—rather than reconstructing speech with all its subtle
282   details—we define our tasks more precisely as zero-shot TTS, respeaking SE, respeaking derever-
283
      beration, and respeaking TSE.

293   5.2       DATA
294
295   We utilize Libriheavy Kang et al. (2024) as the basic dataset for our experiment. Only speech seg-
296   ments under 10s, which are approximately 2.7M, are used for generation tasks, since the training is
297   unstable when there is a lot of long speech segments. For continual TTS, the previous text is gen-
298   erated by Llama-3-8B-Instruct Dubey et al. (2024) given the text to generate. For respeaking SE,
299
      the speech segments are degraded following the setting of DNS Challenge 2023 Dubey et al. (2023)
      with a random SNR from 5 to 20. For respeaking dereverberation, the speech segments are degraded
300
      following the setting of DNS Challenge 2023 Dubey et al. (2023) with the impulse response from
301
      Ko et al. (2017). For respeaking TSE, two speech segments are mixed with a random overlapping
302   ratio from 0.1 to 0.5. For continual TTS, all speech segments under 10s are used, While for SE, dere-
303   verberation and TSE, 1M speech segments are randomly selected to conduct the training datasets.
304   The train, valid and test splits are generated from the large, dev, test clean spilts of Libriheavy, re-
305   spectively. For automatic speech recognition (ASR) task, we also include widely used LibriSpeech
306   960h dataset as training data besides the extracted 2.7M segments from LibriHeavy. VoiceAssistant-
307   400k (Xie & Wu, 2024) dataset is used for spoken question answering task but we only keep the
308   samples in qa assistant v1 7k and alpaca gpt4 en 55k categories and regenerate the an-
309   swers for these questions with GPT-4o-mini. For the pretraining of the Mamba streaming encoder,
310
      GigaSpeech-XL (Chen et al., 2021) subset and AudioSet (Gemmeke et al., 2017) are used.

312   5.3       E VALUATION
313
314   For understanding tasks, word error rate (WER) is used for ASR task. For the Spoken QA task, we
315   use GPT-4o-mini to judge whether the answer generated is suitable for answer the question and we
316   report the success rate.
317   For generation tasks, objective predicted mean opinion score (MOS) and speaker similarity (SIM)
318   are reported. We utilize UTMOS Saeki et al. (2022)1 as our MOS prediction module, which can
319   estimate an objective score of MOS to evaluate the speech naturalness. For SIM, the pre-trained
320   speaker verification model WavLM-TDCNN Chen et al. (2022)2 is used to estimate the similarity
321   between the generated speech and the reference speech.
