009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Social dilemmas are situations where gains from cooperation are possible but
012            misaligned incentives make it hard to find and stabilize prosocial joint behavior. In
013            such situations selfish behaviors may harm the social good. In spatiotemporally
014            complex social dilemmas, the barriers to cooperation that emerge from misaligned
015            incentives interact with obstacles that stem from spatiotemporal complexity. In this
016            paper, we propose a multi-agent reinforcement learning algorithm which aims to
017            find cooperative resolutions for such complex social dilemmas. Agents maximize
018
               their own interests while also helping others, regardless of the actions their co-
               players take. This approach disentangles the causes of selfish reward from the
019
               causes of prosocial reward. Empirically, our method outperforms multiple baseline
020
               methods in several complex social dilemma environments.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Individuals often have their own desires which do not align with their group’s objectives. This kind of
026   misalignment is common in practical situations. For example, in economic cooperation, participants
027   could gain by investing in trust and rule compliance to ultimately enhance market efficiency and
028   growth, but must avoid the temptation to chase short-term gains by deceit or rule evasion. When
029   this kind of scenario also contains spatial and temporal complexity it is called a Sequential Social
030   Dilemmas (SSD) Leibo et al. (2017). The reason SSDs pose challenging environments for learning
031   agents is that their spatial and temporal complexity can interact with the strategic complexity arising
032   from the agents’ misaligned incentives.
033   For example, in one classic SSD game, called Cleanup (Hughes et al., 2018), players are rewarded
034   by collecting apples from an orchard whose growth is restricted by the accumulation of pollution
035   in a nearby river. Apples stop growing unless players contribute to the public good by cleaning
036   the river, a task which involves navigating to a specific location and executing multi-step action
037   sequences. The cleaner the river, the faster the apples grow. However, since the river and orchard are
038   geographically separated, players cannot eat and clean at the same time, and must also spend time
039
      walking between the two locations. Selfish players who never clean benefit more from a clean river
      than do altruistic players who perform all the work of cleaning, since the altruists lose time cleaning
040
      and walking between the river and orchard. Cleaning the river promotes higher long-term collective
041
      return (it is prosocial), but it requires necessary sacrifice on the part of the individuals who spend
042   their time cleaning rather than eating. The cooperation between “cleaners” and “eaters” in Cleanup is
043   an example of division of labor where some roles are remunerated less than other roles, a common
044   though unfair arrangement in real life (Yaman et al., 2023).
045
      Owing to their real-world significance, sequential social dilemmas have recently attracted much
046
      attention from researchers. Many works attempt to promote cooperation behaviors by learning
047
      relationships between agents’ actions. Algorithms such as LOLA (Foerster et al., 2017) promote co-
048   operation by modeling opponents’ behaviors. Jaques et al. (2019) investigate the causal relationships
049   between the actions of agents. Modeling the relationships between actions might lead to reciprocation
050   behaviors, as such methods could end up capturing spurious correlations between behaviors while
051   failing to identify real causal relationships between actions and outcomes. In another line of work
052   (Hughes et al., 2018; McKee et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019; Lupu & Precup, 2020; Kwon et al.,
053   2023), agents are encouraged to intrinsically maximize the welfare of others to promote cooperation
      behavior among the group. The Gifting mechanism (Lupu & Precup, 2020) allocates a portion of


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      agents’ individual rewards to their co-players, encouraging collaboration by decreasing the cost of
055   self-sacrifice; LIO (Yang et al., 2020) promotes cooperative paradigms by learning to incentivize
056   other agents using agent’s own rewards. Furthermore, there are also methods like (Kwon et al., 2023)
057   that attempt to automatically align agents’ incentives with global incentives. These approaches design
058   altruistic rewards to promote cooperation behaviors, but fail to capture the reward generation process,
059   which may lead to spurious prediction of the true team incentives.
060
      In this work, we aim to establish a reinforcement learning algorithm using counterfactual regret to
061   align incentives in a group of agents through maximizing both the agent’s individual outcome and
062   other agents’ outcomes. In SSDs, naively using individual for each agent might not always align
063   with the group’s objective. Because the agents might be rewarded for some selfish behaviors when
064   other agents cooperate (e.g. by exploiting them). Such entanglement of the agents’ policies would
065   bring bias to the estimation of their contributions to the society. Furthermore, such entanglement
066   may cause spurious prediction on the real cause of the agents’ reward. Observing that, we utilize a
067   causal model to mimic the generation process of the individual rewards for all other agents in the
068   group. Counterfactual regret has been used to solve problems under single agent’s scenarios (Brown
069
      et al., 2019). Based on that model, we could define the counterfactual regret in multi-agent setting as
      the difference between the maximum counterfactual reward for other agents and the other agents’
070
      actual reward. More specifically, we calculate the maximum expected outcome for all other agents by
071
      predicting their maximum expected rewards under multiple counterfactual scenarios, then subtract
072   the current other agents’ reward to construct counterfactual regret. Minimizing such counterfactual
073   regret could guide each individual agent to additionally consider other agents’ reward, which would
074   eventually lead to better cooperation paradigm. As the basis of method, we utilize a causal model
075   to describe the generative process within the Partially Observable Markov Games and guide the
076   counterfactual reasoning. Assisted by such a causal model, we aim to capture the real cause of reward
077   generation, reducing the risk of learning spurious relationships and generating counterfactual rewards
078   by intervening on the actions of agents. Theoretically, we prove that under faithfulness assumption
079   and Markov condition, we can identify the real cause in the generation of individual rewards, which
080
      enable us to reason the counterfactual rewards of agents. Furthermore, we demonstrate that our
      method surpass the baseline methods in most of the sequential social dilemma environments through
081
      empirical results.
082
083   In summary, our contributions are threefold. Firstly, we exploit a generative model to explicitly
084   capture the generation process of individual rewards in SSDs. It provides a guidance for the further
085   counterfactual reasoning from the causal view. Secondly, we infer the counterfactual regret based
086
      on our learned causal model to mimic the expected outcome of other agents. Combining this
      counterfactual regret with the original individual rewards guides our agents to learn and respond
087
      to the social incentives embedded in their environment. Lastly, we evaluate our algorithm on four
088
      sequential social dilemma tasks, along with their respective variants, to assess its performance
089   comprehensively. The experimental results demonstrate the superior performance of our algorithm in
090   fostering cooperation and enhancing overall collective reward.

094
095
      Below we review the related work on intrinsic reward design methods and causality-facilitated
096   reinforcement learning methods.
097
098   In the SSD setting, intrinsic motivation methods allow agents to actively care about the welfare of
099
      others intrinsically, or modify the extrinsic rewards of other agents. In scenarios where environmental
      rewards are misleading, relying solely on external rewards provided by the environment may not
100
      be sufficient for effective learning. Social learning is incredibly important for humans and has been
101
      linked to our ability to achieve unprecedented progress and coordination on a massive scale (Henrich,
102   2015; Harari, 2014; Laland, 2017; Van Schaik & Burkart, 2011; Herrmann et al., 2007). While some
103   previous work has investigated intrinsic social motivation for reinforcement learning under sequential
104   social dilemma setting, e.g., Sequeira et al. (2011); Hughes et al. (2018); Peysakhovich & Lerer
105   (2017), these approaches rely on hand-crafted rewards specific to the environment, or allowing agents
106   to view the rewards obtained by other agents (Durugkar et al., 2020). Methods like D3C (Gemp
107   et al., 2020) and Auto-aligning multi-agent incentives (Kwon et al., 2023) take further steps to align
      agents’ incentives automatically by modifying agents’ incentives online to achieve a new goal. This


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      could help decentralized agents automatically modify their incentives based on the preset incentives
109   online to achieve a new goal. However minimizing the Price of anarchy directly results in increased
110   inequality (Gemici et al., 2018). Achieving coordination among agents in sequential social dilemmas
111   still remains a difficult problem. Prior work in this domain, e.g., Foerster et al. (2016; 2018),
112   often resorts to centralized training to ensure that agents learn to coordinate. While communication
113   among agents could help with coordination, training emergent communication protocols also remains
114   a challenging problem; recent empirical results underscore the difficulty of learning meaningful
115   emergent communication protocols, even when relying on centralized training e.g. Cao et al. (2018);
116   Forestier & Oudeyer (2017).
117   Causality is used to address many RL problems like improving the transfer ability of RL agents(Huang
118   et al., 2021; Feng et al., 2022), model-based RL Zhang & Bareinboim (2016); Liu et al. (2023). (Hu
119   et al., 2023a; Pitis et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2023b) unveil the causal structures within the MDP generative
120   process and exploit those causal lenses to facilitate policy learning. More recently, causality-inspired
121   methods are proposed to address MARL problems (Grimbly et al., 2021; Jaques et al., 2019; Li
122   et al., 2021). Li et al. (2021) introduce counterfactual Shapley value in the credit assignment setting.
123
      (Zhou et al., 2022) attempted to use counterfactual prediction in the value decomposition field.
      Despite the significant contributions of prior research, a common oversight has been the neglect of
124
      self-interest settings, where individual interests may not necessarily align with team goals. Even
125
      in studies that acknowledge this aspect, there’s often an incomplete capture of each individual’s
126   incentives, potentially leading the algorithms to converge to suboptimal solutions. In contrast, our
127   work delves into the causal mechanisms underlying the generation of agents’ individual rewards,
128   facilitating a more effective alignment of individual interests with the collective objectives. Moreover,
129   by employing counterfactual reasoning, we mitigate the influence of other agents’ actions, leading to
130   a more stable and precise estimation of the overall incentives.

      3    P RELIMINARY
134
      Partially Observable Markov Game (POMG) is defined by the tuple ⟨N, S, O, T, A, R⟩, in which
135
      multiple agents are trained to independently maximize their own individual reward; The environment
136
      state is given by s ∈ S. At each timestep t, each agent i ∈ N chooses an action ait ∈ A. The actions
137   of all N agents are combined to form a joint action at = [a1t , · · · , aNt ], which produces a transition
138   in the environment T (st+1 |at , st ), according to the state transition T . We consider a partially
139   observable setting in which the i-th agent can only view a portion of the true state, represented as
140   individual observation oit . We denote all agents’ observation as the joint observation
                                                                                      P∞       ot . Each agent i
141   seeks to maximize its own total expected discounted future reward, Ri = t=0 γ t rti , where γ is the
142   discount factor. Each agent i then receives its own reward ri (at , st ), which depends on the actions of
143   other agents.
144   Counterfactual Reasoning in our paper refers to reasoning that all individual rewards r would be r cf
145   if the collective actions a had been acf at the latent state s = s. We exploit the learned causal model
146   to estimate the latent state variable s and infer the counterfactual reward r cf given counterfactual
147   action acf . To interpret the phrase: had collective actions a been acf , modify the original model and
148   replace the equation for a by a constant acf . This replacement permits the constant acf to differ from
149   the actual value of a without rendering the system of equations inconsistent (Pearl, 2010). In general,
150   it can be shown (Pearl (2009), Section 3) that, whenever the graph is Markovian (i.e., acyclic with
151   independent exogenous variables) the post-interventional distribution P (r = r cf |do(a = acf )) is
152
      given by the following expression P (r = r cf |do(a = acf ), s) = P (r cf | acf , s)P (s). If there is no
      incoming path for a in the causal graph, i.e., a has no causal parents, we have P (r = r cf |do(a =
153
      acf ), s) = P (r | acf , s).

            6                   C ONCLUSION
515
516        In this paper, we propose a multi-agent reinforcement learning algorithm for addressing social
517        dilemmas by aligning agents’ self-interests with the interests of others. Our approach encourages
518        individual agents to minimize their counterfactual regret, estimated by calculating the difference
519        between each agent’s optimal prosocial behaviors and their current behaviors. This method enables
520        agents to strike a balance between self-interest and cooperative behavior, effectively disentangling
521
           selfish rewards from prosocial ones. Empirical evaluations show that our approach consistently
           outperforms baseline methods in various complex social dilemma environments, demonstrating
522
           its ability to foster cooperation even in the presence of misaligned incentives and environmental
523
           complexity.
524
525
526        Limitations and Future Work While our method has shown a promising ability to guide agents
527        toward altruistic behavior to maximize social rewards, it presents certain vulnerabilities. Particularly
528        when interfacing with external agents that may not share the same cooperative motives. Specifically,
529        the altruistic nature of our agents can lead to exploitation by defectors, potentially undermining the
530        effectiveness of our approach in competitive or mixed-motive environments. To address this critical
531
           limitation, our future work will focus on developing more robust strategies that not only promote
           cooperation among agents with aligned interests but also safeguard against potential exploitation.
