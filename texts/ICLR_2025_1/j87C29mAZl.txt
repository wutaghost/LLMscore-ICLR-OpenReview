011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Looped Transformers offer advantages in parameter efficiency and Turing com-
014            pleteness. However, their expressive power for function approximation and ap-
015            proximation rate remains underexplored. In this paper, we establish approxima-
016            tion rates of Looped Transformers by defining the concept of the modulus of con-
017            tinuity for sequence-to-sequence functions. This reveals a limitation specific to
018            the looped architecture. That is, the analysis prompts us to incorporate scaling
019            parameters for each loop, conditioned on timestep encoding. Experimental results
020            demonstrate that increasing the number of loops enhances performance, with fur-
021
               ther gains achieved through the timestep encoding architecture.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) have become the standard neural network architecture for a wide
028   range of machine learning tasks, such as natural language processing and computer vision. Looped
029   Transformers have an architecture composed of fixed-size Transformer layers, where the output is
030   fed back into the input. This structure offers advantages over standard Transformers, such as in-
031   ductive bias, parameter efficiency, and Turing completeness. Dehghani et al. (2019) first introduced
032   the idea of incorporating recursive inductive bias into Transformers, aiming to address tasks that
033
      RNNs handle more easily. Looped Transformers are also related to weight-tying Transformers (Lan
      et al., 2020), demonstrating comparable performance to standard Transformers with fewer param-
034
      eters. More recently, Giannou et al. (2023) theoretically demonstrated that the recursive structure
035
      of Looped Transformers allows them to function as Turing machines. In summary, Looped Trans-
036   formers are more parameter-efficient and potentially more expressive than standard Transformers,
037   enhancing their generalization capabilities.
038
039
      The expressive power of standard Transformers has been extensively explored, showing that they can
      approximate continuous permutation-equivariant functions on compact domains (Yun et al., 2020;
040
      Kim et al., 2023; Takakura & Suzuki, 2023; Jiang & Li, 2024; Kajitsuka & Sato, 2024). In addition,
041
      their approximation rate has been studied: Takakura & Suzuki (2023); Jiang & Li (2024) established
042   it by identifying the properties of the target functions, revealing the types of functions that Trans-
043   formers can approximate effectively. In contrast, the expressive power of Looped Transformers in
044   function approximation remains unexplored. Due to the structural constraints imposed by weight-
045   tying, which limit their flexibility, existing universal approximation theories for Transformers cannot
046   be directly applied. Moreover, the approximation rate and the appropriate properties of the target
047   functions for Looped Transformers have yet to be investigated. Recently, Zhang et al. (2023) first
048   explored the expressive power of looped models. They established an approximation rate for ReLU
049   networks for continuous functions in terms of loop counts and modulus of continuity. Still, this
050
      remains unclear in the case of Looped Transformers.
051   In this paper, we derive the approximation rate of Looped Transformers for continuous sequence-to-
052   sequence functions by defining the concept of sequence continuity, contextual continuity, and token
053   continuity. This reveals a limitation specific to the looped architecture. That is, the analysis prompts
      us to incorporate scaling parameters for each loop, conditioned on timestep encoding.

097
      enhances performance on complex tasks. However, to the best of our knowledge, there are no studies
      on the expressive power or approximation rate of Looped Transformers in function approximation.

      2.3   T HEORETICAL ANALYSIS ON FUNCTION APPROXIMATION
101
      The universal approximation theorem for fully connected neural networks, as demonstrated by Cy-
102   benko (1989); Hornik et al. (1989), shows that networks of sufficient size can approximate cer-
103   tain classes of functions with arbitrarily low error. Transformers are universal approximators of
104   sequence-to-sequence functions (Yun et al., 2020; Takakura & Suzuki, 2023; Jiang & Li, 2024; Ka-
105   jitsuka & Sato, 2024), and their memorization capacity has also been studied (Kim et al., 2023).
106   Recently, Zhang et al. (2023) revealed that even single fixed-size networks can be universal approxi-
107   mators. They explored the surprising potential of composition (loop) and derived the approximation
      rate in terms of the number of loop counts and modulus of continuity of the target function.

      3     A PPROXIMATION R ATE OF L OOPED T RANSFORMERS

      We establish the approximation rate of Looped Transformers by defining the modulus of continu-
111   ity for continuous sequence-to-sequence functions. We begin with preliminaries of Transformers
112   in Section 3.1. Then, we present and explain three types of continuity for sequence-to-sequence
113   functions, which determine the approximation rate. In Section 3.3, we present our main results on
114   approximation rate, along with some implications. In Section 3.4, we provide a proof sketch with a
115   detailed explanation, outlining three steps for approximation and deriving the approximation rate.

118
119   Transformers are permutation-equivariant, so we define the target function class as follows.
120   Definition 3.1 (Yun et al. (2020); Kim et al. (2023)). A function f : Rd×N → Rd×N is said to
121   be permutation equivariant if for any permutation matrix P , we have f (XP ) = f (X)P . Let
122   FPE ([0, 1]d×N ) denote the set of permutation equivariant and continuous functions.
123
124   To approximate sequence-to-sequence functions, networks need to map each token within the con-
125
      text of sequences, formulated as contextual mapping.

127   CM : L → R1×N defines a contextual mapping if the map satisfies the following:
128
            1. For any L ∈ L, the N entries in CM(L) are all distinct.
129
130         2. For any L, L′ ∈ L, with L ̸= L′ , all entries of CM(L) and CM(L′ ) are distinct.

138   3.2   D EFINITION OF CONTINUITY FOR SEQUENCE - TO - SEQUENCE FUNCTIONS
139
140   The modulus of continuity of a continuous function g : [0, 1]d → R can be defined as
141
                          sup |g(x) − g(x′ )| : ∥x − x′ ∥2 ≤ t, x, x′ ∈ [0, 1]d .

142
143   It can be extended for sequence-to-sequence functions f : [0, 1]d×N → Rd×N as follows.
144
      Definition 3.4 (Modulus of Sentence Continuity). Given a sequence-to-sequence continuous func-
145   tion f : [0, 1]d×N → Rd×N , the modulus of sentence continuity is defined by:
146
                   ωf (t) := sup ∥f (X) − f (X ′ )∥p : ∥X − X ′ ∥2 ≤ t, X, X ′ ∈ [0, 1]d×N .

147
148
149   We illustrate what this continuity represents and why it is referred to as sequence continuity. If
150   we consider the following two sentences: (1) I read books, and (2) He writes music, the sequence
151   continuity measures how much the overall meaning of a sequence changes:
152                                                  ∆
                                     ‘I read books’ ←→ ‘He writes music’,
153
154   in proportion to the differences between the input sequences, measured by comparing each token:
155                 ∆                               ∆                                   ∆
                ‘I’ ←→ ‘he’,                  ‘read’ ←→ ‘write’, and             ‘book’ ←→ ‘music’.
156
157   We found that this concept is insufficient to derive the approximation rate of Looped Transformers,
158
      primarily because one key characteristic of the Transformer architecture is the sharing of parame-
      ters across all tokens: specifically, the feed-forward layers are applied token-wise. In other words,
159
      Transformers output token embeddings in the context of sequences for each token.
160
161   This observation leads us to define two additional forms of continuity: contextual continuity and
      token continuity, which we found to determine the approximation rate of Looped Transformers.

      The modulus of contextual continuity measures the variation in the output of token embeddings
174
      induced by a perturbation of context. For example, we consider the following three sentences:
175
176          (1) I write papers,              (2) You write books, and          (3) Mozart writes music.

187   influences the output embeddings. A small modulus of token continuity means that the output em-
188   beddings of ‘write’ and ‘draft’ are expected to be similar.

529   6     C ONCLUSION
530
531   We have established approximation rates for Looped Transformers by introducing the modulus of
532   continuity for sequence-to-sequence functions. Our analysis reveals a limitation inherent to the
533   looped architecture, prompting the incorporation of a time-dependent scaling parameter. This re-
534   search is the first to investigate the function approximation capabilities of Looped Transformers.
535   Additionally, our experiments demonstrate that Looped Transformers can solve certain dynamic
536
      programming (DP) tasks that traditional Transformers struggle with, though the underlying mech-
      anism remains unclear. While we have derived upper bounds, the tightness of these approximation
537
      rates is still undetermined. Future work will explore more complex and practical tasks, such as
538
      mathematical problem-solving.

       C.1.1    K NAPSACK P ROBLEM
1633
1634   We use the knapsack problem and its special case, the subset sum problem. These tasks are solvable
1635   in time O(nW ), where n is the length of the input sequence and W represents the weight capacity.
1636   Subset sum task is to determine whether there exists a subset of these integers whose sum equals a
1637   specified number T . It is a subset of the Knapsack problem We randomly sample n = 10 integers
1638   from the range 1 to 100 and select T randomly from 1 to the sum of these n integers. For example,
1639   a sample in the dataset of input sequence length 10 looks like
1640
1641                     67 93 81 29 2 19 77 74 50 98 | 195 <sep> 1.
1642
1643
       Knapsack problem is defined as follows: given a set of items, each with a weight and a value, select
       a subset of items that maximizes the total value while ensuring that the total weight does not exceed
1644
       a specified limit. We concatenated the values, weights, and maximum capacity with a separator. For
1645
       example, a sample in the dataset of input sequence length 20 looks like
1646
1647   9 10 13 1 17 5 12 3 12 2 4 8 11 8 2 10 8 17 10 16 | 8 3 8 7 9 6 7
1648               2 8 2 3 5 4 2 5 7 10 8 6 7 | 48 <sep> 52.
