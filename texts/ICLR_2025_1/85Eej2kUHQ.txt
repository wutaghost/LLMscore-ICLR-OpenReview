009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Randomized smoothing has emerged as a certified defence mechanism with proba-
012            bilistic guarantees that works at scale. However, current randomized smoothing
013
               methods offer theoretical guarantees that are limited by their reliance on specific
               noise distributions, and they struggle to handle complex adversarial attacks. In this
014
               paper, we propose a novel certification method based on randomized smoothing
015
               designed to handle complex adversarial attacks, including combinations of mul-
016            tiple attack types. We call this method Dynamic Smoothing (DS MOOTH). Our
017            key idea is to incorporate more general distributions for smoothing then isotopic
018            Gaussian noise, for which probabilistic guarantees can be derived in terms of the
019            Mahalanobis distance. These general distributions make the smoothed classifier
020            more robust against a wide range of threats, including localized adversarial attacks
021            and multi-attacks. We validate the performance of our method experimentally on
022            challenging threat models using CIFAR-10 and I MAGE N ET, and demonstrate its
023            superiority over state-of-the-art defenses in terms of certified accuracy. Our results
024
               show that the proposed method significantly improves the robustness of machine
               learning models against complex attacks, advancing their suitability for use in
025
               safety-critical applications. Code: [removed for review]

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Machine Learning has seen considerable progress in recent years, especially with deep neural
030   networks (DNNs). However, these networks are vulnerable to adversarial examples (Szegedy et al.,
031   2013; Goodfellow et al., 2014; Zhao et al., 2023), posing a challenge for their use in safety-critical
032   areas (Kurakin et al., 2016; Shayegani et al., 2023). Adversarial attacks, such as DeepFool (Moosavi-
033
      Dezfooli et al., 2016), AutoAttack (Croce & Hein, 2020), patch-based attacks (Brown et al., 2017b),
      and attacks on LLMs (Zou et al., 2023) continue to evolve, outpacing existing defenses and creating
034
      a persistent struggle between attackers and defenders (Carlini & Wagner, 2017a; Madry et al., 2017a).
035
      Current defenses, e.g., denoising generative models (Gu & Rigazio, 2014; Ho et al., 2020), adversarial
036   training (Miller et al., 2020; Kireev et al., 2022), and defensive distillation (Papernot et al., 2016a;
037   Wang et al., 2021), have not fully succeeded in preventing stronger attacks. Hence, the problem of
038   building trustworthy ML systems suitable for critical applications remains an open question.
039
      Certified robustness has emerged as an alternative approach, with randomized smoothing (Lecuyer
040
      et al., 2019; Li et al., 2019; Cohen et al., 2019b; Anderson & Sojoudi, 2022; Scholten et al., 2023;
041
      Anani et al., 2024) being a notable method. This technique, which provides probabilistic guarantees,
042   involves creating a smoothed classifier by applying Gaussian noise to the base classifier. This
043   method was shown by Lecuyer et al. (2019) and Li et al. (2019) to provide consistent classification
044   within a certified radius under `2 norm considerations, although the guarantees were initially loose.
045   Cohen et al. (2019b) were the first to offer tight robustness guarantees for this method against `2
046   norm-constrained adversarial attacks, sparking further studies in this area.
047
      Randomized smoothing has become a widely recognized method for certified robustness, though it
048   has limitations. Cohen et al. (2019b) identified the need for further exploration of `p norms beyond
049   `2 . Recent works have been addressing robustness guarantees for randomized smoothing against
050   various types of adversaries, including `1 -bounded attacks (Teng et al., 2020), `0 -bounded attacks
051   (Levine & Feizi, 2020c; Lee et al., 2019), and Wasserstein attacks (Levine & Feizi, 2020a). However,
052   defending against complex, high-dimensional adversarial attacks remains an open challenge.

072   2    R ELATED W ORK
073
      Since there is a large amount of scientific articles on this topic, we only discuss the contributions
074   relevant for this work. The interested reader can refer to, e.g., Kumari et al. (2023); Kwiatkowska
075   & Zhang (2023), for a more complete overview. Defenses against adversarial examples fall into
076   empirical and certified categories. Empirical defenses, e.g., adversarial training (Madry et al., 2017a;b;
077   Jin et al., 2023), aim to enhance robustness but lack guarantees of being unbreakable, as many have
078   been compromised by stronger attacks, e.g., (Carlini & Wagner, 2017b; Athalye et al., 2018; Tramèr
079   et al., 2020). Certified defenses and verification methods ensure consistent classifier output within a
080   small neighborhood of x, using exact methods, e.g., (Huang et al., 2017; Katz et al., 2017; Ehlers,
081   2017; Mao et al., 2023; 2024), or conservative methods, e.g., (Wong & Kolter, 2018; Raghunathan
082
      et al., 2018; Dvijotham et al., 2018). Randomized smoothing has emerged as a probabilistic certified
      defense mechanism that works at scale.
083
084   Although the literature on randomized smoothing largely focuses on simple threat models, such
085   as imperceptible adversarial perturbations of the input images (Szegedy et al., 2014; Goodfellow
086   et al., 2015; Papernot et al., 2016b; Carlini & Wagner, 2017c), more complex threat models have
087   been considered. Patch attacks, which place imperceptible modifications on images, can cause
088
      misclassifications and compromise system security. Levine & Feizi (2020b) address this with (De-)
      Randomized Smoothing for certifiable defense, leveraging the constraints of patch attacks over
089
      general sparse attacks. Zhang et al. (2023) introduce DRSM (De-randomized smoothed MalConv),
090
      adapting de-randomized smoothing for malware detection through executables (Raff et al., 2018).
091   Recently, randomized smoothing has been used against image transformations (Fischer et al., 2020).
092   Randomized smoothing has also been extended to discrete data (Bojchevski et al., 2020).

096   3.1 P ROBLEM D ESCRIPTION
097   We are given a pre-trained classifier f . We do not make any specific assumption on the inner workings
098   of f . For instance, f can be a large convolutional neural network, e.g., ResNet (He et al., 2016),

126   dimensional input, such as images in datasets for vision-based perception systems of robots and
127   autonomous driving systems.

129   3.2   R ANDOMIZED S MOOTHING
130   Randomized smoothing is a technique for improving the robustness of models against adversarial
131   attacks (Lecuyer et al., 2019; Li et al., 2019; Cohen et al., 2019b). The main principle of randomized
132   smoothing is to transform a deterministic classifier into a probabilistic one by averaging its predictions
133   over many noisy versions of the input. This process effectively “smooths out” the decision boundary
134
      of the classifier, making it less sensitive to input perturbations. Specifically, given a classifier f ,
      randomized smoothing is a method for constructing a new classifier g as
135
136                          g(x) := arg max P (f (x + ") = y) with " ⇠ P (") .
137                                        y
138
      Here, P (") is the smoothing distribution and it determines how noise is added to the input x. Typically,
139
      the smoothing distribution is a Gaussian distribution of the form P (") = N (0, 2 I), with I the
140   identity matrix and a user-defined scalar, although other distributions have been considered (see,
141   e.g., (Teng et al., 2020; Levine & Feizi, 2020c; Lee et al., 2019)). Randomized smoothing provides
142   probabilistic robustness guarantees in terms of the certified radius. This radius specifies a region
143   around an input x within which the smoothed classifier’s prediction is guaranteed to be robust, with a
144   certain probability. The region specified by the certified radius is called a safety region. The choice of
145   the smoothing distribution significantly affects the robustness guarantees provided by randomized
146   smoothing. The guarantees obtained with standard Gaussian smoothing distributions, as above,
147   specify a safety region S using `p norms, e.g., S := {x̂ : kx̂ xkp  R} for some radius R. These
148
      types of guarantees are suitable to certify robustness against imperceptible adversarial perturbations
      on the input image, such as those generated by L-BFGS (Szegedy et al., 2014), FGS (Goodfellow
149
      et al., 2015), DeepFool (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2016), JSMA (Papernot et al., 2016b), or CW
150
      (Carlini & Wagner, 2017c). However, due to their reliance on global noise perturbations, guarantees
151   based on isotopic Gaussian smoothing may be unsuitable for complex attacks that use structured and
152   localized adversarial perturbations.

156   4.1   OVERVIEW
157   We extend the randomized smoothing framework by Cohen et al. (2019a) to more complex smoothing
158   distributions. In contrast to prior work, our framework uses anisotopic Gaussian noise as a smoothing
159   distribution, i.e., a Gaussian distribution in which the variances along different dimensions of the
160   space are not equal, which allows to handle both sparse and localized adversarial perturbations.
161   Importantly, in Sec. 4.2 we derive probabilistic guarantees for this method that generalize previous
      known guarantees (Cohen et al., 2019a).

350   5     E XPERIMENTS
351
352   The overall aim of the experiments is to demonstrate that DS MOOTH achieves good certified accuracy
353   compared to baselines on complex adversarial attacks as in Def. 3.1. The certified accuracy is defined
354   as the fraction of the test set, which a smoothed algorithm classifies correctly with a prediction that
355
      is certifiably robust within a ball of a given radius. Since DS MOOTH is a randomized smoothing
      classifier, it is not possible to compute this quantity exactly. Instead, we report on the approximate
356
      certified test set accuracy following previous related work, e.g., Cohen et al. (2019a). In addition
357
      to evaluating the certified accuracy, we also report on the execution time of DS MOOTH, and its
358   sensitivity to different choices of the parameter k for the k-rank approximation as in Sec. 4.1.
359
360   In all the experiments we consider the S QUARE + FGSM multi-attack as detailed in Sec. 4.1,
361   obtained as a combination of the Square Attack algorithm (Andriushchenko et al., 2020) and FGSM
362   (Goodfellow et al., 2015). This attack applies a Square Attack to an input image (using `1 norm), and
363   then it applies a FGSM attack to the resulting adversarial sample (using `2 norm). In this experiment
364
      we use Square Attack with maximum perturbation 0.5 and 5000 queries. The FGSM attack uses
      maximum perturbation parameter 0.5. In App. F we show examples of CIFAR-10 (Fig. 6) and
365
      I MAGE N ET (Fig. 7) images corrupted with the smoothing distribution as in equation 3 for this type
366
      of attack.

      Baselines. We compared our smoothing algorithm in equation 3 to two baseline approaches
379   for certified robustness: the standard randomized smoothing algorithm by (Cohen et al., 2019a)
380   (RAND S MOOTH), and the approach by Teng et al. (2020) (L S MOOTH). The randomized smoothing
381   algorithm by Cohen et al. (2019a) provides certification guarantees in terms of the `2 norm, whereas
382   the algorithm by Teng et al. (2020) provides guarantees in terms of the `1 norm. We do not consider
383   randomized smoothing techniques with certification guarantees in terms of `p norms with p > 2,

514   6    D ISCUSSION
515
516   In this paper, we introduced a novel certification method based on randomized smoothing (see
517
      equation 3) to enhance the robustness of machine learning models against complex adversarial attacks,
      including combinations of multiple attack types (see Sec. 4.1). Our approach generalizes the existing
518
      framework of randomized smoothing by incorporating more flexible noise distributions, allowing for
519
      robustness guarantees across a wider range of adversarial threats, such as S QUARE+FGSM (see Sec.
520   4.1). Through extensive experiments on CIFAR-10 (see Sec. 5.2) and I MAGE N ET (see Sec. 5.3), we
521   demonstrated that our method consistently outperforms state-of-the-art defenses in terms of certified
522   accuracy (see Fig. 1-2) .
523
524   However, much like previous work (see, e.g., Cohen et al. (2019a); Teng et al. (2020)), our proposed
525
      method still faces several limitations. The effectiveness of DS MOOTH is constrained by its reliance on
      Monte Carlo sampling, which can be computationally expensive on very large models. Additionally,
526
      while our approach extends robustness beyond the standard `2 norm, it may not yet fully capture the
527
      complexities of all possible adversarial threats.
528
529   Future work could address these limitations by developing more efficient sampling techniques, or by
530   leveraging neural architecture search to identify base classifiers that are inherently more robust to ad-
531   versarial perturbations. Furthermore, exploring alternative noise distributions and adaptive smoothing
532
      strategies could further enhance robustness against a broader array of adversarial threats.
