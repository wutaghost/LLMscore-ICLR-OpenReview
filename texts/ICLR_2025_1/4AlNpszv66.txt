011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            There is overwhelming evidence that cognition, perception, and action rely on
014            feedback control. However, if and how neural population dynamics are amenable
015
               to different control strategies is poorly understood, in large part because machine
               learning methods to directly assess controllability in neural population dynamics
016
               are lacking. To address this gap, we developed a novel dimensionality reduction
017
               method, Feedback Controllability Components Analysis (FCCA), that identifies
018            subspaces of linear dynamical systems that are most feedback controllable based
019            on a new measure of feedback controllability. We further show that PCA identifies
020            subspaces of linear dynamical systems that maximize a measure of feedforward
021            controllability. As such, FCCA and PCA are data-driven methods to identify
022            subspaces of neural population data (approximated as linear dynamical systems)
023            that are most feedback and feedforward controllable respectively, and are thus
024            natural contrasts for hypothesis testing. We developed new theory that proves
025            that non-normality of underlying dynamics determines the divergence between
026
               FCCA and PCA solutions, and confirmed this in numerical simulations. Applying
               FCCA to diverse neural population recordings, we find that feedback controllable
027
               dynamics are geometrically distinct from PCA subspaces and are better predictors
028
               of animal behavior. Our methods provide a novel approach towards analyzing
029            neural population dynamics from a control theoretic perspective, and indicate that
030            feedback controllable subspaces are important for behavior.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Feedback control has long been recognized to be central to brain function (Wiener, 1948; Conant &
036   Ashby, 1970). Prior work has established that, at the behavioral level, motor coordination (Todorov
037   & Jordan, 2002), speech production (Houde & Nagarajan, 2011), perception (Rao & Ballard, 1999),
038   and navigation (Pezzulo & Cisek, 2016; Friston et al., 2012) can be accounted for by models of
039
      optimal feedback control. Advances in the ability to simultaneously record from large number of
      neurons have further revealed that the brain performs computations and produces behavior through
040
      low-dimensional population dynamics (Vyas et al., 2020). Together, these two facts indicate that
041
      neural population dynamics should both be able to implement the computations required to exert
042   feedback control Friedrich et al. (2021), and be internally steerable by feedback control themselves
043   (e.g., other brain areas controlling motor cortex to produce target dynamics). Nonetheless, methods
044   to assess these hypotheses directly from recordings of neural population activity are absent.
045
      The cost incurred in controlling a dynamical system is referred to as its controllability. Existing
046
      measures of controllability center around the energy (in terms of the norm of the control signal) that
047
      must be expended to steer the system state. These measures are calculated from the controllability
048   Gramian of the (linearized) system dynamics. Controllability is an intrinsic feature of the dynamical
049   system itself, and may be estimated from measurements of system dynamics without reference to
050   the specific inputs to the system (Pasqualetti et al., 2013; Kashima, 2016). Network controllability
051   analyses have delivered insights into the organization of proteomic networks (Vinayagam et al., 2016),
052   human functional and structural brain networks (Medaglia et al., 2018; Tang & Bassett, 2018; Kim
053   et al., 2018; Gu et al., 2015), and the connectome of C Elegans (Yan et al., 2017). However, prior
      work in network controllability has exclusively focused open loop, or feedforward, controllability in


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the context of extracted networks, and not measures of closed loop, or feedback, controllability in
055   the context of observed dynamics of data. Indeed, methods to asses feedback controllability from
056   observations of the dynamics of neural populations are nascent.
057
058
      Here, we developed dimensionality reduction methods that can be applied to neural population data
      that maximize the feedforward and feedback controllability of extracted latent population dynamics.
059
      We first identify a correspondence between Principal Components Analysis (PCA) and the volume
060
      of state space reachable by feedforward control in linear dynamical systems (Pasqualetti et al.,
061   2013)–this provides a control-theoretic interpretation to PCA extracted subspaces. We then present
062   Feedback Controllable Components Analysis (FCCA), a linear dimensionality reduction method to
063   identify feedback controllable subspaces of high dimensional dynamical systems based on a novel
064   measure of feedback controllability.
065
      Our focus on linear models of dynamics is a computational necessity; nonlinear measures of con-
066
      trollability require nonlinear systems identification and involve partial differential equations that are
067   intractable to solve in high dimensions Scherpen (1993a); Nakamura-Zimmerer et al. (2021); Kramer
068   et al. (2024). In contrast, a key advantage of FCCA is that it can be applied to data using only the
069   second order statistics of the observed data itself, bypassing the need for prior system identification
070   and making the method easily applicable to large scale neural population recordings. Furthermore, in
071   contrast to existing approaches towards dimensionality reduction in computational neuroscience Yu
072   et al. (2009); Pandarinath et al. (2018), FCCA does not attempt to reconstruct the neural data with a
073   lower dimensional subspace, but rather identifies a subspace in which dynamics optimize a functional
074   measure (feedback controllability). Together with a functional, control theoretic interpretation of
075
      PCA, this permits direct comparison of the neural population dynamics underlying distinct control
      strategies from observed neural population data.
076
077   Through theory and numerical simulations, we show that the degree of non-normality of the under-
078   lying dynamical system (Trefethen & Embree, 2020) determines the degree of divergence between
079   PCA and FCCA solutions. In the brain, the postsynaptic effect of every neuron is constrained to be
080   either excitatory or inhibitory by Dale’s Law. This structure implies that linearized dynamics within
081
      cortical circuits are necessarily non-normal (Murphy & Miller, 2009). Prior work has highlighted
      the capacity of non-normal dynamical systems to retain memory of inputs (Ganguli et al., 2008) and
082
      transmit information (Baggio & Zampieri, 2021). Our results show that non-normality also plays
083
      a fundamental role in shaping the controllability of neural systems. Finally, we applied FCCA to
084   diverse neural recordings and demonstrate that those subspaces are better predictors of behavior than
085   PCA subspaces (despite both being linear), and that the two subspaces are geometrically distinct.
086   This suggests that feedback controllable subspaces (FCCA) are more relevant for behavior than
087   feedforward controllable subspaces (PCA).

124   A categorical definition of controllability for a dynamical system is that for any desired trajectory
125   from initial state to final state, there exists a control signal u(t) that could be applied to the system to
126
      guide it through this trajectory. For a (stable) linear dynamical system, a necessary and sufficient
      condition for this to hold is that the controllability Gramian, Π, has full rank. Π is obtained from the
127
      state space parameters through the solution of the Lyapunov equation:

129                             AΠ + ΠA = −BB                Π=         dt eAt BB ⊤ eA t                       (3)

131   The rank condition on Π as a definition of controllability, while canonical (Kailath, 1980), is an all
132   or-nothing designation; either all directions in state space can be reached by control signals, or they
133   cannot. Furthermore, this definition does not take into account the energy required to achieve the
134   desired transition. While certain directions in state space may in principle be reachable, the energy
135
      required to push the system in those directions may be prohibitive.
136   Thus, given that the system is controllable, we can ask a more refined question: what is the energetic
137   effort required to control different directions of state space? The energy required for control is
138   measured by the norm of the input signal u(t). It can be shown (Pasqualetti et al., 2013) that to
139   reach states that lie along the eigenvectors of Π, the minimal energy is proportional to the inverse
140   of the corresponding eigenvalues of Π. Directions of state space that have large projections along
141
      eigenvectors of Π with small eigenvalues are therefore harder to control. For a unit-norm input signal,
      the volume of reachable state space is proportional to the determinant of Π (Summers et al., 2016).
142
143   The above intuition can be encoded into the objective function of a dimensionality reduction problem:
144   for a fixed-norm input signal, find C that maximizes the reachable volume within the subspace. This
145   volume is measured by the determinant of CΠC ⊤ . Identifying subspaces of maximum feedforward
146   controllability is then posed as the following optimization problem:
147                            argmaxC log det CΠC ⊤ | C ∈ Rd×N , CC ⊤ = Id                                   (4)
148
149   Observe that under state space transformations, Π maps to T ΠT ⊤ , whereas C maps to CT −1 . Hence,
150   as desired, eq. 4 is invariant to state space transformations and thus an intrinsic property of the
151   dynamical system. We include the constraint CC ⊤ = Id to ensure the optimization problem is
152
      well-posed. Without it, one could, for example, multiply C by an overall constant and increase the
      objective function. We can assess this objective function from data generated by eq. 2, as in this
153
      case the observed covariance of the data will coincide with the controllability Gramian (Mitra, 1969;
154
      Kashima, 2016). The solution of problem 4 coincides with that of PCA, as the optimal C of fixed
155   dimensionality d has rows given by the top d eigenvectors of Π (see Theorem 2 on pg. 7 and Lemma
156   1 in the Appendix).

      2.4   C ONTROL -T HEORETIC I NTUITION FOR FCCA
288
289   We have shown how the sum of LQG singular values is an intrinsic measure of the cost to filter/regulate
290   a linear dynamical system which is minimized at a fixed readout dimensionality by FCCA. We now
291   provide further intuition for FCCA. In order to control the system state and carry out the computations
292   necessary to perform state estimation and control signal synthesis, the controller itself must implement
293   its own internal state dynamics. Thus, in addition to the complexity of the system itself, we may
294
      inquire about the complexity of the controller. One intuitive measure of this complexity is given by
      the controller’s state dimension (i.e., the McMillan degree), or the number of dynamical degrees of
295
      freedom it must implement to function. In the context of brain circuits, the degrees of freedom of
296
      the controller must ultimately be implemented via networks of neurons. We therefore hypothesize
297   that biology favors performing task relevant computations via dynamics that require low dimensional
298   controllers to regulate. As we argue below, minimizing the sum of LQG singular values over readout
299   matrices (C) corresponds to a relaxation of the objective of searching for a subspace that enables
300   control via a controller of low dimension. In other words, FCCA searches for dynamics that can be
301   regulated with controllers of low complexity.

366   Having derived data driven optimization problems to identify feedforward (PCA) and feedback
367   (FCCA) controllable subspaces, we investigated under what conditions the solutions of PCA and
368   FCCA will be distinct. We found that a key feature of the dynamical system of eq. 1 that determines
369   the similarity of PCA and FCCA solutions is the non-normality of the underlying dynamics matrix,
370   A. We first prove that when A is normal (symmetric), and B = I, the critical points of PCA (eq. 11)
371   and the FCCA objective function (eq. 7) coincide. 1
372   Theorem 2. For B = IN , A = A⊤ , A ∈ RN ×N , with all eigenvalues of A distinct and
373   max Re(λ(A)) < 0, the critical points of the feedforward controllability objective function eq.
374   4 and the feedback controllability objective function eq. 7 for projection dimension d coincides with
375
      the eigenspace spanned by the d eigenvalues with largest real value.
376       1
            The set of real-valued, normal A matrices can be divided into symmetric and orthogonal matrices. We
377   restrict our treatment to stable dynamical systems. As orthogonal matrices give rise to systems that are only
      marginally stable, below we will use normal A to refer interchangeably to symmetric A.

      The proof of the theorem is provided in the Appendix. The restriction to B = I is made within
379   the proof, but does not apply to the general application of the method. Intuitively, in the case
380   of symmetric, stable A, perturbations exponentially decay in all directions, and so the maximum
381   response variance, and hence greatest feedforward controllability, is contained in the subspace with
382   slowest decay, which corresponds to the eigenspace spanned by the d eigenvalues with largest real
383   value. The intuition for the slow eigenspace of A serving as a (locally) optimal projection in the
384   feedback controllability case is given by the fact that state reconstruction from past observations, the
385   goal of the Kalman filter, will occur optimally using observations that have maximal autocorrelations
386   with future state dynamics. Similarly, for the LQR, for a fixed rank input, the most variance will be
387
      suppressed by regulating within the subspace with slowest relaxation dynamics.
388   Importantly, due to Dale’s Law, brain dynamics are generated by non-normal dynamical systems.
389   To demonstrate the effect of increasing the non-normality of A on the solutions of PCA and FCCA,
390   we turn to numerical simulations (the optimal feedback controllable projections are not analytically
391   tractable). We generated 200-dimensional dynamics matrices constrained to follow Dale’s Law with
392   an equal number of excitatory and inhibitory neurons. Neurons were connected randomly with a
393
      uniform connection probability of 0.25. To tune the non-normality of the system, we vary the strength
      of synaptic weights in the neuronal connectivity matrix. The strength of synaptic weights determines
394
      the spectral radius of the corresponding matrices (Rajan & Abbott, 2006). Leaving the excitatory
395
      weights fixed, we then optimize the inhibitory weights as detailed in (Hennequin et al., 2014) to
396   ensure system stability. The resulting matrices will have enhanced non-normality, with the degree
397   of resulting non-normality having, empirically, a monotonic relationship with the starting spectral
398   radius. We applied our methods both directly to the cross-covariance matrices of the resulting linear
399   dynamical systems, as well as to spiking activity driven by simulated xt . In the latter case, spiking
400   activity was generated as a Poisson process with rate λt = exp(xt ). Firing rates were obtained by
401   binning spikes and applying a Gaussianizing boxcox transformation (Sakia, 1992). These rates were
402   then used to estimate the cross-covariance matrices. This procedure mirrors that which was applied
403   to neural data in the subsequent section.
404   In Figure 2a, we plot the average subspace angles between FCCA and PCA for d = 2 projections
405   (other choices of d shown in Figure A1) applied both directly to cross-covariance matrices of the
406   linear dynamical systems (LDS, black) and cross-covariance matrices estimated from spiking activity
407   (Count LDS, blue) as a function of the non-normality of the underlying A matrix (measured using
408   the Henrici metric, ||A⊤ A − AA⊤ ||F ). In both cases, we observe a nearly monotonic increase in
409   the angles between FCCA and PCA subspaces as non-normality is increased. We note that as we
410
      constrain A matrices to follow Dale’s Law, we cannot tune them to be completely normal, and hence
      the subspace angles between FCCA and PCA remain bounded away from zero even at the lower
411
      end of non-normality. We verified that the large subspace angles between FCCA and PCA also
412
      persist in more general data generation processes. We considered non-stationary dynamics arising
413   from a sequence of switched non-normal linear dynamical systems, and nonlinear dynamics arising
414   from an RNN obeying Dale’s Law trained to reproduce muscle EMG activity in response to a low
415   dimensional “go cue” input signal Sussillo et al. (2015). Full details of model construction and
416   training are provided in the Appendix. In Figure 2b, we plot the average FCCA/PCA subspace
417   angles at the highest degree of model non-normality for each synthetic system (full results across
418   all levels of non-normality are provided in Figure A2). In all cases, FCCA and PCA subspaces are
419   geometrically distinct. Given the generality of non-normal dynamics due to Dale’s Law, this new
420   control-theoretic result suggests that PCA and FCCA subspaces should also be geometrically distinct
421
      in neural population data.

      5    D ISCUSSION
497
      We developed FCCA, a novel dimensionality reduction method that identifies feedback controllable
498
      subspaces of neural population dynamics. Further, the correspondence between PCA and feedforward
499
      controllability, long known in the control theory community (Moore, 1981), but unrecognized in
500   the neuroscience community, adds additional interpretative value to these subspaces. Importantly,
501   to the best of our knowledge, FCCA is the first method to encode functional measures of dynamics
502   (in this case, controllability) into the objective of a dimensionality reduction method. As such, it is
503   not designed to optimally reconstruct the neural data or maximize behavioral decoding, but rather to
504   provide insight into the specific computations different components of neural activity are optimized
505   for. This renders it distinct from prior latent variable analysis methods in neuroscience (e.g., GPFA
506   Yu et al. (2009), LFADS Pandarinath et al. (2018)), and motivates the development of other methods
507   for neural data analysis that reduce neural activity on the basis of normative, functional measures.
508   We demonstrated that feedforward and feedback controllable subspaces are geometrically distinct in
509   non-normal dynamical systems, a fact of fundamental importance to the analysis of neural dynamics
510   from cortex, where Dale’s Law necessitates non-normality. Correspondingly, in electrophysiology
511   recordings from across the brain, we found large subspace angles between FCCA and PCA subspaces.
512   Furthermore, we found that FCCA subspaces were better predictors of behavior than PCA subspaces.
513   This suggests that targeting feedback controllable subspaces in the design of brain machine interfaces
514
      may be advantageous in terms of accuracy of behavioral prediction, the number of samples needed to
      calibrate predictions to a desired level of accuracy, and the efficacy of closed loop perturbations.
515
516   Several methodological extensions to FCCA are possible. While performing dimensionality reduction
517   on the basis of nonlinear measures of controllability remains computationally infeasible due to the
518   need to solve high dimensional PDEs within the inner optimization loop ((Scherpen, 1993b)), FCCA
519   could be augmented with a nonlinear encoder. In FCCA, we rely on estimation of the regulator
520
      cost through acausal filtering (eq. 9 and estimate the filtering error through the Gaussian MMSE
      formula (eq. 11) to keep the model of the data implicit. These correspondences only hold for linear
521
      systems under a particular choice of the LQR cost function (eq. 10). While this makes the method
522
      computationally efficient, it restricts the form of weight matrices in the LQR objective functions that
523   can be considered. The objective function in eq. 7 could alternatively be applied to post-hoc analysis
524   of linear state space models fit to neural recordings (Gao et al., 2015), as these models explicitly yield
525   the system matrices required to solve the Riccati equations 5 and 6. This analysis could be combined
526   with techniques from inverse linear optimal control (Priess et al., 2014) to provide a more refined
527   picture of the controllability of population dynamics.
