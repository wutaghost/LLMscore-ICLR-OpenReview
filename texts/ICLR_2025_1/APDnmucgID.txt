011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            In the field of large language models (LLMs), aligning models with the diverse
014            preferences of users is a critical challenge. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)
015
               has played a key role in this area. It works by using pairs of preferences derived
               from the same prompts, and it functions without needing an additional reward
016
               model. However, DPO does not fully reflect the complex nature of human learning,
017
               which often involves understanding contrasting responses to not only identical
018            but also similar questions. To overcome this shortfall, we propose Relative Pref-
019            erence Optimization (RPO). RPO is designed to discern between more and less
020            preferred responses derived from both identical and related prompts. It introduces
021            a contrastive weighting mechanism, enabling the tuning of LLMs using a broader
022            range of preference data, including both paired and unpaired sets. This approach
023            expands the learning capabilities of the model, allowing it to leverage insights
024            from a more varied set of prompts. Experiments in both paired and unpaired
025            dataset settings, including tasks like dialogue, summarization, and general evalu-
026
               ation benchmarks, demonstrate RPO’s superior ability to align LLMs with user
               preferences and enhance adaptability during training.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large language models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT OpenAI (2023) and LLaMA Touvron et al. (2023)
032   have revolutionized AI, demonstrating remarkable capabilities in natural language processing, logical
033   reasoning, and programming Pan et al. (2023); Tian et al. (2023). Their proficiency in zero-shot
034   and few-shot learning is attributed to training on extensive, unsupervised datasets. However, the
035   diverse nature of these datasets can result in alignment challenges, leading to outputs that may not
036   consistently align with specific human values, particularly in nuanced contexts Agrawal et al. (2023);
037   Shi et al. (2023); Liang et al. (2021); Sheng et al. (2019); Kadavath et al. (2022); Srivastava et al.
038   (2022); Thoppilan et al. (2022); Bubeck et al. (2023). The Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)
039
      method fine-tunes the language model’s policy to align more closely with human preferences, thereby
      eliminating the need for a separate reward model, a staple in traditional Reinforcement Learning
040
      from Human Feedback (RLHF) Schulman et al. (2017). Central to DPO is the utilization of pairwise
041
      preferences, with preferred and dispreferred responses identified for each prompt. This forms the
042   foundation for effectively optimizing preferences. However, training a model to learn from an
043   individual preference pair for each example may not fully capture the complexity of human learning.
044   Human cognition often involves interpreting divergent responses, not only to identical questions but
045   also to similar ones, highlighting the multifaceted nature of comprehension and preference formation
046   Dahlin et al. (2018). Moreover, obtaining pairwise preference data can pose challenges and incur
047   substantial costs, especially in sensitive domains such as healthcare and personal services, where
048   careful attention to ethical considerations is essential Murtaza et al. (2023).
049   Our inspiration draws from the human learning process, where valuable insights often arise from
050   the comparison of successful examples and relevant failures Dahlin et al. (2018). To emulate this,
051   we introduce Relative Preference Optimization (RPO). This approach involves analyzing prompt
052   similarities at the semantic level within each mini-batch, allowing us to classify pairs as either highly
053   related or unrelated. We construct a contrast matrix that instructs the model to distinguish between
      preferred and dispreferred responses, applicable to both identical and semantically related prompts.

079
      As illustrated in Figure 1, we are interested in the question “Explain the concept of photosynthesis.”
      DPO applies penalties for incorrect responses and rewards for precise responses generated for
080
      the same prompt. Conversely, our method RPO emphasizes the semantic connections between
081
      various prompts. For instance, the prompt “Describe the importance of sunlight in plant growth” is
082   conceptually similar, and its responses might intersect with those of the initial question. Under RPO,
083   if an answer is less preferred for the second prompt, it is also treated as less suitable for the first
084   prompt. Thus, RPO penalizes both yl,1 and yl,2 while approving yw,1 . It is crucial to note that not
085   all prompts are semantically related enough to form effective contrastive pairs. RPO incorporates a
086   reweighting mechanism, whereby unrelated prompts are given less emphasis during training. RPO
087   expands the learning horizon of the model, empowering it to leverage insights from a broader range
088   of prompts, mirroring the human learning process more closely.
089   We empirically evaluate RPO on several LLMs, including the LLaMA series and Mistral-7B, com-
090   paring it with SoTA preference alignment methods. RPO significantly outperforms the baselines on
091   dialogue and summarization tasks, as well as evaluations on the general chat evaluation benchmarks.

117   (1952), which assesses the reward r∗ (y|x) for generating a specific response y to a prompt x. The
118
      Bradley-Terry model determines the preference probability as follows:

180   3     R ELATIVE P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION
181
182   The traditional DPO framework aligns language model outputs with human preferences using pairwise
183   data, where each pair is composed of a preferred (win) and dispreferred (lose) sample for the same
184
      prompt. However, this approach is limited to situations where such pairwise preference data is
      accessible, failing to exploit the valuable comparative insights that could be derived from contrasting
185
      diverse samples across a range of prompts. In response, our RPO framework encompasses a wider
186
      array of preference data, including non-paired samples. This development not only improves the use
187   of existing preference data but also facilitates model training in complex scenarios where pair-wise
188   data is not readily obtainable. More specifically, RPO integrates preference pairs derived from
189   prompts that are semantically related but not identical, as shown in Figure 1. Through dynamic
190   calculation of relative reward weights based on prompt similarities, our method enhances the model’s
191   ability to learn from a wider array of human feedback, resulting in better preference alignment.
192
193   3.1     C ONTRAST M ATRIX C ONSTRUCTION
194
195   In RPO, the contrast matrix is a pivotal component that facilitates the comparison between win
196   and lose responses to derive meaningful insights for model training. As shown in Figure 2, the
197
      construction of this matrix varies depending on whether the available data is paired or unpaired,
      allowing for flexibility in training dynamics.
198
199   Paired Data Scenario. In situations where each win response is associated with a corresponding lose
200   response from the same prompt, the contrast matrix is an M × M square matrix, where M represents
201   the total number of unique prompts from a specific mini-batch within the dataset. Each element
202   cij within this matrix represents the contrastive score between the win response of the ith prompt
203   and the lose response of the j th prompt. For diagonal elements where i = j, the score reflects the
204
      direct comparison within the same prompt, while off-diagonal elements represent the relative reward
      differences across distinct prompts. In this context, DPO is limited to using only the diagonal terms
205
      of the contrast matrix, while RPO takes into account all pairings within the matrix, encompassing a
206
      broader range of preference comparisons.
207
208   Unpaired Data Scenario. In cases where the dataset contains unpaired win and lose responses,
209   the contrast matrix transforms into an M × N rectangular structure. Within this matrix, M and N
210
      respectively represent the number of unique win and lose samples in a batch of the dataset. Each
      element cij in this matrix now indicates the contrastive score between the ith win response and the
211
212
      j th lose response, without the constraint of originating from the same prompt. This allows for a
      more extensive range of comparisons, as any win samples can be contrasted with any lose samples,
213
      harnessing the thematic connections within the dataset to enrich the model’s preference learning.
214
215   For each win response yw,i and lose response yl,j , the contrastive score sij is computed as the
      difference in rewards associated with each response, defined by the following equations:

342   et al. (2024) as an alternative approach incorporating human value functions. This varied set of
343   baselines provided a comprehensive context for evaluating RPO’s performance in aligning language
344   models with nuanced human preferences. For these comparisons, we utilized a range of pre-trained
345   large language models, including LLaMA series Touvron et al. (2023) and Mistral-7B Jiang et al.
346   (2023). For paired preference datasets, We conducted the evaluations of RPO on the validation sets
347   of Anthropic’s HH Dataset for dialogue and the OpenAI Summarization Dataset for summarization.
348
      To further challenge RPO’s adaptability and conversation capability, we integrated the AlpacaEval
      leaderboard Li et al. (2023) into our evaluation benchmark. This benchmark comprises a set of 805
349
      diverse and carefully curated prompts, serving as an ideal platform for testing the model’s ability to
350
      follow general user instructions accurately and effectively. For the unpaired dataset, we also evaluated
351   the performance of alignment algorithms trained on these two datasets across multiple benchmarks:
352   the Open LLM Leaderboard Beeching et al. (2023) for question-answering, the MT-Bench Zheng
353   et al. (2024) for assessing multi-turn conversational abilities, and the more challenging Arena-Hard
354   benchmark Li et al. (2024), which evaluates chat capabilities using complex, real-world user queries.
355
      Our primary evaluation metric was the win rate, calculated using the advanced capabilities of GPT-4
356
      OpenAI (2023) as the evaluative tool. This metric quantitatively assessed the preference rate of our
357   model’s responses against those generated by baseline models. By employing GPT-4 for evaluation,
358   we leveraged its robust understanding and judgment abilities as a stand-in for human evaluators
359   Zheng et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023).
360
361
362   Training Details. For the paired dataset setting, preference data is processed into mini-batches,
363   each comprising N triplets (x, yw , yl ). It is important to emphasize that the volume of training data
364   utilized in RPO are identical to those in DPO and KTO. For the unpaired dataset, each mini-batch
365   consists of (x, y, 0/1), representing prompts, responses, and their binary classification. This enables
366   the construction of an N1 × N2 contrast matrix for RPO fine-tuning, where N1 and N2 represent the
367
      number of chosen and rejected responses within the mini-batch, respectively.
368   In all experiments, both RPO and the baseline consistently utilized a beta value (β = 0.1) and a
369   sampling temperature of 0. The training utilized 8 Nvidia A100 GPUs, with a batch size of 64,
370   optimized with RMSProp optimizer Tieleman & Hinton (2017). For the paired dataset setting,
371   initially, we trained the SFT, followed by training the subsequent alignment models based on the
372   SFT. For the unpaired datasets, both our RPO and baseline KTO, were fine-tuned directly from the
373
      LLaMA3-8B-Instruct model3 . For more details, please refer to Appendix D.

397   4.2   A BLATION S TUDY
398
399   We initiated our investigation with an ablation study aimed at assessing the viability of using
400   semantically related prompts as effective contrastive pairs for preference optimization. Initially, we
401   utilized DPO as the baseline and began with the pairwise preference data, a setup similar to that
402   of DPO. DPO primarily focuses on preference pairs in relation to each individual prompt, while
403   RPO constructs a contrastive matrix encompassing all potential pairs within each mini-batch. In our
404   experiments, we compared RPO with various weighting strategies against DPO, using the Mistral-7B
405
      model for dialogue tasks. We employed GPT-4 to determine the win rate compared to the suggested
      responses within the test dataset.
406
407   As shown in Table 1, simple reweighting strategies, such as Uniform Weighting and Diagonal
408   Weighting, yielded slightly worse results compared to the baseline DPO. In Uniform Weighting
409   scenario, the weighting simplifies to something resembling supervised fine-tuning (SFT), with
410   adjustments made for losing responses. Despite its simplicity, Uniform Weighting still achieved a
411
      win rate of 68.36, outperforming the SFT result of 48.24, as seen in Table 3, thereby establishing
      a lower bound for more advanced methods like Embedding Reweighting. These findings confirm
412
      that not all prompt pairs are equally effective for contrastive preference optimization. Building on
413
      this observation, we introduced a sentence embedding model to measure the semantic relatedness
414   between prompts, which allows us to focus contrastive learning on more meaningful pairs. As a
415   result, Embedding Reweighting significantly boosted performance, with RPO achieving a win rate of
416   78.52, as shown in Table 1.
417
      Our investigation progressed to examine the impact of different prompt embedding extraction models
418
      on the LLaMA2-7B model’s efficacy in dialogue tasks, as delineated in Table 2. In this comparative
419
      study, we compared three distinct models: all-MiniLM-L6-v2 Wang et al. (2020), known for its
420   efficiency and balance in handling context; sentence-t5-large Raffel et al. (2020), designed for
421   generating semantically rich embeddings; and all-distilroberta-v1 Sanh et al. (2019), recognized for
422   its distilled knowledge from larger models. Observing a consistent trend where moderate temperature
423   settings enhance model performance, the all-MiniLM-L6 model, set at a temperature of 0.5, was
424   selected as the benchmark setting for subsequent comparisons against other state-of-the-art models.
425
      We explored the impact of per GPU batch size on the performance of RPO, conducting experiments
426   using the Mistral-7B model on the Anthropic-HH dataset. The results of these experiments are
427   presented in Figure 3. The baseline DPO model, evaluated by GPT-4, achieved a win-rate of 72.26.
428   With a minimal batch size of 2 per GPU, RPO shows slight underperformance compared to the DPO
429   baseline, which was trained with a batch size of 8 per GPU, largely due to too few samples used per
430   gradient update. However, as the batch size increases, RPO’s performance consistently improves,
431   surpassing the DPO baseline comfortably at batch sizes of 4. This suggests that our method benefits
      from leveraging a wider range of comparison pairs, providing more information for policy fine-tuning.

513   output logits. The only additional computation in our method involves calculating the similarity
514
      between prompts, which incurs minimal computational cost. For example, we finetune the Mistral-7B

520   5   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
521
522   In summary, Relative Preference Optimization (RPO) innovatively aligns Large Language Models
523
      (LLMs) with human preferences, adeptly handling both paired and non-paired data. Its contrastive
      weighting mechanism effectively processes similar and identical prompts, enriching the understanding
524
      of nuanced preferences. Empirical results on models like LLaMA and Mistral show RPO outperform-
525
      ing the previous alignment methods in key tasks, particularly in dialogue and summarization. This
526   adaptability and improved alignment highlight RPO’s potential in advancing LLM training, setting
527   the stage for more user-centric and ethically aligned AI applications.
528
529   Limitations & Future Work. RPO currently faces several limitations: First, it depends on the
530   quality of the embedding model used to construct contrast pairs. A weak text encoder may fail to
531   effectively capture the linguistic patterns and contextual similarities within prompts, necessitating the
532   selection of a sufficiently powerful text encoder within the available computational budget. Second,
533   the construction of the contrastive matrix is limited by the memory capacity of a single GPU’s
534   mini-batch. Future enhancements could include aggregating data across multiple GPUs to create a
535   larger contrastive matrix.
536   Additionally, as shown in Appendix C, we present initial results of integrating RPO’s cross-prompt
537   contrast mechanism with other preference alignment algorithm, such as SimPO. The results are
538   promising, highlighting the robustness and generalizability of RPO’s approach. In the future, we
539   plan to further explore the potential of combining RPO with other types of preference alignment
      algorithms to further validate and expand RPO’s effectiveness and versatility.

869

870   aligns its reward with the average log-likelihood of the sequence generated by the policy model,
871   removing the need for a reference model. The reward is computed as:

879   RPO’s cross-prompt contrast mechanism without needing to estimate Z(x).
