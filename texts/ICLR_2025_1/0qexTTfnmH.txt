009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Bayesian Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) has shown excellent performance in re-
012            ducing the overconfidence of inference by large language models as it can ac-
013            curately quantify the inference uncertainty. However, the general Bayesian LoRA
014            technique requires huge memory as it fine-tunes three low-rank matrices with large
015            size: two matrices have size of n × r and the other has size of r × m, where r
016            denotes rank, and n, m denote the size of input and output, respectively. The large
017            amount of memory required by this technique precludes its practical applications
018
               especially for the cases with long input or output. Here, we propose a memory ef-
               ficient Bayesian LoRA technique (called Me-LoRA) that needs only two low-rank
019
               matrices plus two small matrices with size of only r × r. The key idea of our ap-
020
               proach is that we introduce a small matrix (with size r×r) to describe the variance
021            estimates required by Bayesian LoRA, which is calculated through sampling two
022            other samll matrices. Compared with the general Bayesian LoRA technique, our
023            approach reduces the memory requirement by nearly 31 as the rank r is generally
024            very small. Experimental results using both LlaMA-7B and LlaMA-13B models
025            on representative data sets suggest that our approach achieves the same perfor-
026            mance as the original Bayesian LoRA techniques and outperforms the existing
027            approaches. In summary, the memory-efficient Bayesian LoRA presented in this
028            study circumvents the challenge of high memory requirement and thus paves a
029
               new way to the practical applications of Bayesian LoRA in the cases with larger
               input and output size.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      In recent years, efficient parameter training has become increasingly important, particularly in large-
035
      scale neural networks Hu et al. (2021); Ding et al. (2023); Fu et al. (2023). As models grow, manag-
036   ing and optimizing parameters is crucial for training speed and performance. This requirement has
037   led to growing interest in techniques that can achieve comparable results with fewer trainable param-
038   eters, especially in large language models (LLMs). Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods
039   have garnered significant attention for their ability to reduce computational cost and memory usage
040   while maintaining model performance Liu et al. (2022); Han et al. (2024).
041
      As fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) becomes increasingly crucial, various techniques have
042   emerged to optimize this process Houlsby et al. (2019); Hu et al. (2021); Ding et al. (2023). In fine-
043   tuning large models, Bayesian methods are frequently employed to mitigate overconfidence, en-
044   hance factual accuracy, and reduce potential harmful consequences Amodei et al. (2016); Weidinger
045   et al. (2021); Azaria & Mitchell (2023). Bayesian fine-tuning for large models involves incorporat-
046   ing probabilistic frameworks to model uncertainty in the model parameters, enabling the model to
047   make more robust predictions in the face of uncertainty. This approach allows for considering pa-
048   rameter distributions rather than solely on point estimates, thus providing more reliable inferences
049   when encountering new data.
050   Despite the potential advantages of Bayesian methods, current state-of-the-art (SOTA) Bayesian
051   approaches still need to address the issue of parameter efficacy. This challenge underscores the dif-
052   ficulty of effectively estimating and updating parameters during complex fine-tuning tasks. Conse-
053   quently, further research to enhance the performance and reliability of Bayesian fine-tuning methods
      is crucial for advancing the deployment of large models in practical applications.

054
      Among the approaches to Bayesian fine-tuning, some of the most common are post-training methods
055   like Monte Carlo Dropout (MCD) Gal & Ghahramani (2016) and Deep Ensembles (ENS) Lakshmi-
056   narayanan et al. (2017); Wang et al. (2023); Balabanov & Linander (2024) only gains in generaliza-
057   tion and uncertainty estimation remain marginal. More promising post-training approaches, such as
058   the Kronecker-factored Laplace approximation, apply after any optimization algorithm’s maximum
059   a posteriori (MAP) estimate MacKay (1992). However, this two-step procedure inherently leads to
060   suboptimal posterior estimates due to the separation of training and uncertainty estimation phases.
061   In contrast, Bayesian fine-tuning methods like BLoB Wang et al. (2024) jointly estimate the mean
062   and covariance of a low-rank variational distribution. Unlike post-training approaches, BLoB esti-
063   mates the parameter mode (i.e., the mean if one assumes Gaussian distributions) and the parameter
064   variance simultaneously. While BLoB enhances the mode estimate by randomly sampling from the
065   variance estimates, the method requires significant additional memory, increasing the number of
066   trainable parameters by nearly half. BLoB introduces a storage burden that scales with the size of
067   LLMs. Our approach improves upon BLoB by reducing the number of trainable parameters and
068   addressing the memory overhead while retaining the advantages of Bayesian fine-tuning.
069   Our method, Memory-efficient Bayesian Low-Rank Adaptation (Me-LoRA), extends the benefits of
070   BLoB, significantly optimizing the parameter efficacy. Me-LoRA reduces training parameters by
071   approximately one-third compared to BLoB, making Bayesian uncertainty estimation feasible for
072   larger models while retaining the accuracy and uncertainty benefits that have been proven valuable
073   in both in-distribution and out-of-distribution settings.
074   In summary, this work presents a pioneering approach to optimizing Bayesian fine-tuning methods,
075   highlighting the necessity and potential of reducing training parameters for large-scale models. Our
076   contributions are as follows:
077
078          • We propose Memory-efficient Bayesian Low-Rank Adaptation (Me-LoRA). This novel
079            method reduces trainable parameters by approximately one-third compared to SOTA
080            Bayesian fine-tuning methods without compromising performance.
081          • Me-LoRA significantly lowers the memory overhead of Bayesian fine-tuning, making it
082            more scalable for large language models.
083          • We preserve the critical advantages of Bayesian methods, including enhanced calibration
084            and uncertainty estimation, across 7B and 13B model.
085
086
             • Extensive experiments demonstrate the efficiency and effectiveness of Me-LoRA, achiev-
               ing state-of-the-art performance with fewer trainable parameters.

091   2.1   L OW-R ANK A DAPTATION (L O RA)
092
093   Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) Hu et al. (2021) is a technique that significantly reduces the number
094   of trainable parameters in large-scale models by leveraging their inherent low-rank structure Li et al.
095   (2018); Aghajanyan et al. (2020). In LoRA, the weight update matrix ∆W is decomposed into two
096
      low-rank matrices:
097                                              ∆W = BA,                                                (1)
098   where B ∈ Rm×r and A ∈ Rr×n , with r ≪ min(m, n). Here, m and n are the dimensions of
099   the input and output layers, respectively, while r represents the intrinsic rank of the decomposition.
100   This factorization leads to a substantial reduction in the number of parameters to train, from O(mn)
101   in the full-rank case to O(r(m + n)). The forward pass of the model is then expressed as:
102
                                     z = W0 h + ∆W h = W0 h + BAh,                                       (2)
103
104   where h is the input to the layer, z is the output, and W0 is the pre-trained weight matrix. By
105   updating only the low-rank matrices A and B, LoRA reduces memory consumption for storing
106   optimizer states and accelerates fine-tuning, especially for large language models (LLMs). This
107   method achieves performance comparable to full-rank fine-tuning, while drastically lowering the
      hardware requirements, making it a practical solution for fine-tuning massive models.

      2.2   BAYESIAN L OW-R ANK A DAPTATION BY BACKPROPAGATION (BL O B)
109
110   In Bayesian Low-Rank Adaptation by Backpropagation (BLoB) Wang et al. (2024), the posterior
111   distribution of the model parameters is inferred rather than relying on point estimates Bishop &
112   Nasrabadi (2006); Wang & Yeung (2020), offering a probabilistic view of the model’s weights.
113   Given the intractability of exact posterior inference, BLoB approximate the true posterior by mini-
114   mizing the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the variational distribution q(W |θ) (denoted
115   as q(W ) ) and the true posterior P (W |D) (denoted as P (W ) ) Hinton & Van Camp (1993); Graves
116   (2011); Blundell et al. (2015). We can then minimize the variational free energy concerning the vari-
117
      ational distribution parameters θ Neal & Hinton (1998); Yedidia et al. (2001); Friston et al. (2007):
118
                       min H(D, θ) = −Eq(W ) [log P (D|W )] + DKL [q(W )∥P (W )].                        (3)
119                      θ
120
121
      Here, the first term corresponds to the expected log-likelihood, encouraging the model to fit the data.
      In contrast, the second term is a regularizer by penalizing the divergence between the variational dis-
122
      tribution and the true posterior. This objective ensures a balance between model complexity and data
123
      fit, improving both the expressiveness and tractability of the posterior approximation. Such a for-
124   mulation provides a principled framework for Bayesian learning, facilitating uncertainty estimation
125   and regularization in neural networks.
126
127
      BLoB optimizes the first term in Equation 3 using Monte Carlo gradient estimation LeCun (1985);
      Rumelhart et al. (1986), combined with the reparameterization trick, allowing gradients to propagate
128
      through the underlying parameters θ Opper & Archambeau (2009); Kingma (2013); Rezende et al.
129
      (2014). The variational distribution is simplified to a diagonal Gaussian N (µ, σ 2 ), where σ =
130   ρ2 ensures the positivity of the standard deviation and accelerates convergence. Here, ρ denote
131   parameters of the weight matrix W , expressed as W = µ + ρ2 ⊙ ϵ, where ϵ ∼ N (0, I).

      3     M ETHOD
163
164   In this section, we introduce Memory-efficient Bayesian Low-Rank Adaptation (Me-LoRA), a novel
165   method that builds upon and extends the state-of-the-art method, BLoB Wang et al. (2024). The
166   central innovation of Me-LoRA lies in the introduction of a low-dimensional matrix C, coupled
167   with variance estimation applied to this matrix. Specifically, we introduce this low-dimensional
168   matrix C between the low-rank matrices A and B, allowing for Bayesian modeling over the full
169   parameter matrix, as illustrated in Figure 1. Similar to BLoB, we train the low-rank matrices A and
170   B, along with the mean M and variance Ω of the low-dimensional matrix C, sampling from the
171   mean and variance of C for inference.
172

174

176
      C ∈ Rr×r is Gaussian with mean M and standard deviation Ω, denoted as q(C) ∼ N (M , Ω),
177   where M ∈ Rr×r and Ω ∈ Rr×r . We then have q(Q) ∼ N (M A, Ω|A|), where Q = CA. The
178   equivalent variational distribution defined on the full weight matrix W = W0 + BCA as fallow,
179
                                          q(W ) = N (W ; µq , Σq ),                                      (4)
180

181   where µq = vec(W0 + BM A) and Σq = [I ⊗ B] · [diag(vec(Ω|A|) )] · [I ⊗ B ].
182   In Equation 9 (See more details in Appendix A), our LoRA Bayesianization employs a Gaussian
183   variational distribution for W with a flexible covariance matrix Σq , to approximate the posterior
184   distribution of the full parameter W . The covariance matrix Σq is strictly singular and high-
185   dimensional. Sampling from such a high-dimensional matrix, however, requires efficient sampling
186   algorithms that scale with the parameter space. To ensure parameter efficiency, Blob Wang et al.
187   (2024) introduces variance estimation in module A. By leveraging the multiplicative properties of
188   matrices, this effectively estimates the entire full parameter matrix. Compared to standard LoRA Hu
189   et al. (2021), this approach necessitates the introduction of a parameter matrix of the same size as
190
      module A, which increases the training parameters by 50%. To reduce the training parameters, we
      introduce a low-dimensional matrix C between matrices A and B and apply variance estimation
191
      to the newly introduced matrix C. This modification results in nearly a 30% reduction in training
192
      parameters compared to BLoB.

      3.4   PARAMETER C OUNT
248
      We denote the number of fine-tuned layers as Ltuned and the dimension of these layers as dmodel .
249   In Me-LoRA, the number of trainable parameters is governed by |Θ| = Ltuned × (2 × dmodel +
250   r) × r, whereas in the state-of-the-art method BLoB, it is given by |Θ| = 3 × Ltuned × dmodel ×
251   r. Specifically, for the lowest rank (i.e., r = 8), Me-LoRA requires nearly 30% fewer trainable
252   parameters than BLoB. Furthermore, as the rank and model size increase, the parameter count in Me-
253   LoRA increments by 3Ltuned dmodel , leading to significant savings compared to LoRA’s 3Ltuned dmodel
254   parameter scaling. This efficiency becomes crucial in intense models, such as GPT-3 Brown (2020),
255   which has 96 attention layers.

257   weight adjustments post-training while maintaining the uncertainty estimation benefits of BLoB. A

      5    R ELATED W ORKS
379
380
      The rapid development of LLMs has intensified research on efficient fine-tuning for specific tasks.
381   Traditional full-parameter fine-tuning is computationally expensive and presents challenges related
382   to storage and deployment. Consequently, PEFT methods have been investigated to minimize com-
383   putational and storage resource requirements while preserving model performance. LoRA (Hu et al.,
384   2021) is a prominent PEFT method that injects trainable low-rank matrices at each layer of Trans-
385   former architecture to approximate weight changes, effectively reducing the number of trainable pa-
386   rameters and enhancing training efficiency without increasing inference latency. Additionally, sev-
387   eral other PEFT methods have been developed, including adapter-based fine-tuning (Rücklé et al.,
388   2020; Pfeiffer et al., 2020), prompt-based fine-tuning (Lester et al., 2021; Vu et al., 2021; Asai et al.,
389
      2022), and partial fine-tuning (Ansell et al., 2021; Zaken et al., 2021). These approaches aim to
      minimize parameter count while maintaining or improving model performance.
390
391   Overconfidence in LLM predictions can lead to risks, highlighting the importance of uncertainty es-
392   timation for enhancing model reliability and trustworthiness. However, existing LLMs often exhibit
393   overconfidence after fine-tuning, making it challenging to estimate predictive uncertainty accurately.
394   To address this issue, various uncertainty estimation techniques have been introduced in LLMs, in-
395
      cluding Monte Carlo dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016), deep ensemble (Lakshminarayanan et al.,
      2017; Wang et al., 2023; Zhai et al., 2023), and Laplace approximation(Antorán et al., 2022; Yang
396
      et al., 2023). These methods enhance the model’s uncertainty estimation capabilities by incorporat-
397
      ing randomness during training or establishing probability distributions over model parameters. A
398   notable approach is BLoB (Wang et al., 2024), which integrates Bayesian inference with LoRA to
399   capture model uncertainty through the distribution of low-rank adaptation parameters. In contrast to
400   the post-hoc method Laplace-LoRA (Yang et al., 2023), BLoB updates both the means and covari-
401   ances of LLM parameters during fine-tuning, facilitating more accurate uncertainty estimation.

444
445
      We propose Me-LoRA, a novel parameter-efficient Bayesian fine-tuning method for LLMs in this
      work. Our method shows that a full-weight variational distribution can be efficiently optimized
446
      by a low-dimensional square matrix incorporating a variance estimate positioned between the two
447
      low-rank matrices in LoRA. In our experiments, we observed enhanced generalization and uncer-
448   tainty estimation performance compared with several baseline methods. Our approach highlights
449   that jointly learning the mean and covariance of variational distributions with a small number of
450   parameters during fine-tuning can improve each other, greatly enhancing the efficiency of Bayesian
451   methods.
