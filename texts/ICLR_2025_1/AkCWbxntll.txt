009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Computed Tomography (CT) and Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) are crucial
012            diagnostic tools, but undersampling techniques like Sparse-View CT (SV-CT) and
013            Compressed-Sensing MRI (CS-MRI), aimed at reducing patient exposure and scan
014            time, make image reconstruction more challenging. While deep learning-based
015            reconstruction (DLR) methods have made significant strides, they face limitations
016
               in adapting to varying scan geometries and handling diverse patient data, hindering
               widespread clinical use. In this paper, we propose a novel Gaussian-Based Iterative
017
               Reconstruction (GBIR) framework that uses learnable Gaussians representations
018
               for personalized medical image reconstruction, addressing the shortcomings of
019            DLR methods. GBIR optimizes case-specific parameters in an end-to-end fashion,
020            enabling better generalization and flexibility under sparse measurements. Addi-
021            tionally, we introduce the Multi-Organ Medical Image REconstruction (MORE)
022            dataset, comprising over 70,000 CT and MRI slices across multiple body parts and
023            conditions. Our experiments show that GBIR outperforms state-of-the-art methods
024            in both accuracy and speed, offering a robust solution for personalized medical
025            image reconstruction.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Computed Tomography (CT) (Koetzier et al., 2023) and Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) (Haris-
030
      inghani et al., 2019) are the two most important diagnostic technologies in modern medicine. CT
031   scans use computer processing to reconstruct detailed cross-sectional images from X-rays emitted at
032   various angles and measured as they pass through body tissues. MRI scans use powerful magnets and
033   radio waves to excite hydrogen atoms in the body, generating signals that are detected and processed
034   by a computer to create detailed images of internal structures. Therefore, sophisticated image recon-
035   struction algorithms are essential for both CT and MRI, converting raw data from multiple projections
036   into diagnostic images (Szczykutowicz et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2018). Modern medical practices
037   use undersampled raw measurements by reducing radiation exposure or scanning time to benefit the
038   health and improve comfort of patients, for example, adopting Sparse-View CT (SV-CT) (Koetzier
039
      et al., 2023) and Compressed-Sensing MRI (CS-MRI) (Lustig et al., 2008), as shown in Figure 1.
      However, these undersampling procedures make the reconstruction process much more challenging,
040
      as the raw measurements are insufficient to recover the true 3D conditions within the patient’s body.
041
042   The scanning process by the machine is usually called the forward process, which acquires the
043   raw measurements from the patient. Conversely, the reconstruction process is called the inverse
044   process that recovers the 3D volume from the raw measurements. The forward process is well studied
045
      and can be modeled by mathematical equations, but the inverse process is actually an ill-posed
      problem with non-unique solutions that is challenging to solve. Traditional methods for medical
046
      image reconstruction, such as Filtered Back Projection (FBP) (Bracewell & Riddle, 1967) and Inverse
047
      Fast Fourier Transform (IFFT) (Gallagher et al., 2008) for CT and MRI, are incapable of handling
048   the reconstruction problem from sparse measurements. Deep learning-based reconstruction (DLR)
049   methods are leading advancements in medical image reconstruction, offering practical solutions such
050   as SV-CT and CS-MRI for medical diagnosis. While various types of DLR methods exist, such as
051   direct learning methods (Zhu et al., 2018; He et al., 2020), image-domain denoising methods (Jin
052   et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2017), and dual-domain reconstruction methods (Hu et al., 2020), they all
053   share a common principle: employing neural networks to learn the mapping from the measurement
      domain to the image domain. Nevertheless, SV-CT and CS-MRI have seen limited adoption in

073   clinical practice (Koetzier et al., 2023; Jaspan et al., 2015). The underlying reason is the inherent
074   limitations of neural networks. Firstly, the fixed mapping learned by neural networks poses challenges
075
      in adapting to varying scan geometries. For instance, an SV-CT model trained on 60 views cannot be
      easily extended to 120 or 180 views without undergoing a complete retraining process. Secondly, the
076
      effectiveness of neural networks is limited by the diversity of the training data. Variations in patient
077
      demographics and medical conditions make it hard to create a comprehensive dataset. Consequently,
078   DLR methods may struggle in clinical practice, as neural networks might fail to reconstruct images
079   for conditions not included in the training data. As noted by Szczykutowicz et al. (2022), future
080   methods should be customized for each individual patient.
081
      Given the numerous inherent limitations of neural networks in medical image reconstruction, we are
082
      motivated to take a bold step: abandoning neural networks in favor of a set of learnable isotropic
083
      Gaussians to represent the 3D volume to be reconstructed. This idea is inspired by the success of
084   3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) in the field of computer graphics (Kerbl et al., 2023), which uses a
085   set of 3D Gaussians to represent and reconstruct a 3D scene from 2D images. But it is important to
086   note that, unlike 3D scene reconstruction, medical image reconstruction involves supervision signals
087   in the measurement domain rather than the image domain, and the objective is to recover a fixed
088   3D volume instead of rendering a dynamic 3D scene. Without any Rendering process, in this paper,
089   we propose a novel Gaussian-Based Iterative Reconstruction (GBIR) framework that encompasses
090   both high-quality representation and an efficient reconstruction process. GBIR creates a tailored
091   Gaussian representation for each case (patient), with learnable parameters optimized in an end-to-end
092
      fashion. This allows for customized medical image reconstruction, overcoming the generalization
      challenges faced by neural networks, and it also offers flexibility in reconstructing medical images
093
      under varying sparse measurement conditions. GBIR requires only the current patient’s data for
094
      optimization, enabling a “train-as-you-infer” approach.
095
096   The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:
097   • We propose a novel Gaussian-Based Iterative Reconstruction (GBIR) framework. GBIR employs a
098     new reconstruction approach that involves projecting onto the measurement at each iteration, and
099     optimizing the reconstruction based on the loss with the current case’s measurement. This method
100     achieves personalized modeling and strong generalization.
101   • We propose a comprehensive Multi-Organ Medical Image REconstruction (MORE) dataset, which
102     contains over 70,000 slices from 173 patients, covering 15 body parts in CT scans and 5 body
103     parts in MRI scans, with various types of diseases. The dataset has passed the ethical review of the
104     hospital and the local ethics committee and will be released to the public.
105   • We conduct extensive experiments to evaluate the performance of our proposed method, we compare
106     GBIR with various existing methods on the proposed MORE dataset and other public datasets.
107     The results show GBIR achieves state-of-the-art performance, outperforming other baselines by an
        obvious margin, and demonstrates superior inference speed.

      2    BACKGROUND
109
110
      Problem Definition The forward process in medical imaging systems (e.g., CT, MRI) can be
111
      formulated as follows:
112
                                           y = Ax + n,                                      (1)
113
114   where x is the 3D volume of the patient, A is the system matrix that models the imaging system, y
115   represents the acquired measurements, and n is the noise. Medical image reconstruction refers to
116   the inverse problem of recovering the 3D volume x from the measurements y. In applications like
117
      SV-CT and CS-MRI, the matrix A is sparse and the measurements y are undersampled, increasing the
      complexity of the reconstruction process. This inverse problem is inherently ill-posed and non-unique,
118
      with the goal being to estimate the most likely 3D volume that corresponds to the given measurements.
119
120   A typical approach involves minimizing a loss function that balances the fidelity to the measurements
121   y and the regularization term that imposes prior knowledge about the structure of x. The optimization
122   problem can be written as:
                                                              2
123                                    x̂ = arg min ∥Ax − y∥2 + λR(x),                                   (2)
                                                  x
124
                                                        2
125   where x̂ is the estimated 3D volume, ∥Ax − y∥2 is the fidelity term that measures the discrepancy
126   between the estimated measurements and the acquired measurements, R(x) is the regularization term,
127   which incorporates prior knowledge or assumptions about the image structure, such as smoothness,
128   sparsity, or low-rank characteristics, depending on the specific imaging modality and application.
129
      Total variation (TV) (Rudin et al., 1992; Sidky & Pan, 2008) regularization is a common choice for
      the regularization term in medical image reconstruction, as it preserves the edges and structures of
130
      the image while reducing noise. The hyperparameter λ balances the fidelity and regularization terms.
131
132   Related Work (a) Sparse-View CT. Classical CT reconstruction methods, such as Filtered Back
133   Projection (FBP) and Iterative Reconstruction (IR), are incapable of handling the Sparse-View CT
134   reconstruction problem. Modern deep learning methods have evolved from convolutional neural net-
135
      works (CNNs) (Kang et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2017) to generative adversarial networks (GANs) (Yang
      et al., 2018) and, more recently, to diffusion-based models (Chung et al., 2022; 2023; Xu et al., 2024).
136
      Apart from optimization methods like NeRP (Shen et al., 2022), these models typically require large
137
      amounts of training data to achieve good performance. (b) Compressed-Sensing MRI. Traditional
138   MRI reconstruction methods rely heavily on the Fourier Transform. However, the performance of
139   Fourier Transform-based reconstruction decreases when the number of sampling points is reduced
140   in Compressed-Sensing MRI. Similar to Sparse-View CT, deep learning methods in this field have
141   evolved from CNNs (Zhu et al., 2018; Hyun et al., 2018) to GANs (Yang et al., 2017; Quan et al.,
142   2018), and finally to diffusion-based models (Chung & Ye, 2022; Chung et al., 2023). A large
143   amount of training data is also required to train these models. (c) Relationship with Existing Works.
144   We categorize existing medical image reconstruction methods and compare their characteristics
145   in Table 7. Recently, several contemporary works have adapted 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS)
146
      for CT reconstruction or novel view synthesis (Fu et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024; Cai et al., 2025;
      Zha et al., 2024). 3DGR-CAR (Fu et al., 2024) incorporates U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) to
147
      predefine Gaussian centers, which are then refined using 3DGS for the final reconstruction process.
148
      DIF-Gaussian (Lin et al., 2024) leverages 3D Gaussians to represent feature distributions, facilitating
149   the estimation of attenuation coefficients. X-Gaussian redesigns a radiative Gaussian point cloud
150   model for generating novel views in X-ray imaging applications. R2 -Gaussian (Zha et al., 2024)
151   identifies shortcomings in the use of 3DGS for volumetric reconstruction and introduces an innovative
152   approach to enhance volumetric reconstruction quality.
153
      We emphasize the differences between our proposed method and these approaches. Unlike the above
154
      works, our GBIR is not based on 3DGS but introduces a novel Gaussian-based iterative method
155   specifically tailored for medical image reconstruction. The entire process is end-to-end trainable and
156   optimized for medical image reconstruction without any splatting or rendering processes.

207   Truncated Three-Sigma Gaussian According to the Three-Sigma rule, in Gaussian distribution, the
208   probability of a point falling within three standard deviations of the mean is approximately 99.73%
209   (Appendix B). This implies that the influence of a Gaussian on a voxel diminishes as the distance
210   from the Gaussian center to the voxel increases. By considering only the contributions of Gaussians
211   within a specified proximity of each voxel, we can accelerate the reconstruction process.
212   Specifically, for each voxel in the 3D volume, we consider a neighborhood δ around the voxel
213   and compute the contributions of all Gaussians within this neighborhood. The contributions of all
214   Gaussians within their neighborhoods are then added to their corresponding voxels in the volume.
215   This process is repeated for all voxels in the volume, resulting in the final reconstructed 3D volume.
      The neighborhood around each voxel is centered at the Gaussian center.

292   4       M ULTI -O RGAN M EDICAL I MAGE RE CONSTRUCTION DATASET (MORE)
293
294   Existing datasets usually focus on a single body part or disease, which substantially hinders a
295   more thorough and comprehensive assessment of current research on medical image reconstruction.
296
      Advanced methods including (Chung et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2022; Xia et al., 2022)
      usually evaluate on a single body part, such as only abdomen part in AAPM-Mayo LDCT Challenge
297
      Dataset (Moen et al., 2021), or only brain part in BRATS (Menze et al., 2014) dataset. It is difficult
298
      to conclude the effectiveness of a method based solely on the results of a single body part, and its
299   generalization ability remains to be verified. To address this limitation, we propose the Multi-Organ
300   Medical Image REconstruction (MORE) dataset, which has the following characteristics:
301
302
      • It incorporates both CT and MRI data types, and a diverse set of body parts. To be specific, MORE
        contains over 65,755 CT slices and 7,498 MRI slices from 173 patients, covering 15 body parts in
303
        CT scans and 5 body parts in MRI scans. Table 1 presents a detailed comparison of MORE with
304
        existing medical image reconstruction datasets.
305
      • MORE exhibits a rational distribution of demographics and diseases. To be specific, MORE involves
306
        a total of 173 patients and 189 examinations. Some patients underwent multiple examinations,
307     resulting in 135 CT scans and 54 MRI scans. The median age of the participants was 52 years,
308     ranging from 7 to 85 years. The age distribution is as follows: 0-20 years (5.4%), 21-40 years
309     (29.5%), 41-60 years (37.2%), 61-80 years (24.0%), 81-100 years (3.9%). The gender distribution
310     was 59.7% male and 40.3% female. MORE contains 25 types of diseases in CT and 17 types
311     of diseases in MRI, respectively. We show the specific distribution of the CT and MRI scans in
312     Figure 3 and Figure 4, and provide some samples in Figure 6 for visualization.
313   • MORE has been approved by the ethics committee of corresponding hospital, and the approval
314     number also has been obtained3 . All DICOM data has been anonymized by RSNA clinical trial
315     processor to protect patient privacy and comply with the Helsinki declaration. We will release the
316     dataset for public availability.
317     Currently, MORE dataset provides DICOM images and does not include the original raw mea-
318     surements. This aligns with common practices in medical image reconstruction research as
319
        demonstrated by several advanced methods (Yang et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024),
        which often rely on simulated measurements generated from image slices. In the experiments, we
320
        simulate measurements by applying the Radon transform for CT data and the Fourier transform for
321
        MRI data following previous research (Xu et al., 2024; Chung et al., 2023).

      5.4   B ENCHMARK AND F INDINGS ON MORE DATASET
460
461
      We benchmark the performance of different methods on the newly proposed MORE dataset, which
462   contains a wide range of body parts and diseases. Our benchmark include 15 body parts and 25
463   diseases for CT scans (Table 9 to Table 23), and 5 body parts and 17 diseases for MRI scans (Table
464   24 to Table 28). To the best of our knowledge, this is the first time that such a comprehensive dataset
465   is used for evaluating medical image reconstruction methods. More than comparing the performance,
466   we also provide some insights and findings from the benchmark on the MORE dataset.
467
      Optimization-based methods are more robust to the influence of data.: We observe that optimization-
468   based methods, including FBP (Bracewell & Riddle, 1967), IFFT (Gallagher et al., 2008), NeRP (Shen
469   et al., 2022), and our GBIR, show consistent performance across different body parts and diseases.
470   In contrast, learning-based methods, such as REDCNN (Chen et al., 2017), AUTOMAP (Zhu et al.,
471   2018), and score-mri (Chung & Ye, 2022), show a more significant performance variation across
472   different body parts and diseases. This indicates that optimization-based methods are more robust to
473   the influence of data, while learning-based methods are more sensitive to the data distribution.
474   A comprehensive dataset helps improve the generalization ability for learning-based methods: Table
475   8 and Table 23 both are evaluated on the subarachnoid hemorrhage disease, but their training data
476   are different. The former is trained on the AAPM-Mayo LDCT dataset, while the latter is trained on
477   the MORE dataset. We observe that the learning-based methods, including REDCNN (Chen et al.,
478   2017), AUTOMAP (Zhu et al., 2018), and score-mri (Chung & Ye, 2022), show better performance
479   when trained on the MORE dataset compared to the AAPM-Mayo LDCT dataset. This indicates that
480   a comprehensive dataset with diverse body parts and diseases can help improve the generalization
481   ability of learning-based methods.
482   Significant performance variation across different body parts: We observe that the performance of
483   different methods varies significantly across different body parts and diseases. Figure 7 and Figure
484   8 show the distribution of PSNR and SSIM across different body parts and diseases for CT and
485   MRI scans, respectively. We observe that the performance of different methods varies significantly
      across different body parts and diseases. For example, the performance of RED-CNN is considerably

522   6     D ISCUSSION
523
524   Limitations It should be noted that although our proposed GBIR is faster than the advanced methods,
525   it is still slower than the traditional DLR methods. In Table 2, the inference time of RED-CNN and
526
      AUTOMAP is much shorter than all other advanced methods. Nevertheless, their performance is
      significantly lower. This trade-off between speed and performance is a common challenge in medical
527
      image reconstruction, and it remains an open problem for future research.
528
529   Visualization We include a visualization of the reconstruction process in Appendix 5 to provide
530   a better understanding of the reconstruction process of our proposed GBIR method. Iteration by
531   iteration, the reconstruction becomes clearer and more detailed. We also provide histograms of PSNR
532
      on the MORE dataset of 60-view SV-CT and axial CS-MRI in Figure 7 and Figure 8, respectively.
      The histograms show the distribution of PSNR across different body parts and diseases, providing
533
      insights into the performance of different methods on the MORE dataset.
534
535   Conclusion In this paper, we present a novel Gaussian-based image reconstruction method, GBIR,
536   which achieves state-of-the-art performance on both public widely used datasets and our newly
537   proposed MORE dataset. Our proposed method is efficient and robust, making it suitable for tailored
538
      reconstruction of different body parts and diseases. We also provide a comprehensive benchmark
      on the MORE dataset, which includes a wide range of body parts and diseases, to facilitate further
539
      research in medical image reconstruction.

763   A.1    P EAK S IGNAL - TO -N OISE R ATIO (PSNR)

765   The PSNR (Hore & Ziou, 2010) is a widely used metric to evaluate the quality of the reconstructed
766

                                                             MAX2

770   error between the original and reconstructed images.

772   A.2    S TRUCTURAL S IMILARITY I NDEX (SSIM)
773
774   The SSIM (Wang et al., 2004) is a metric that measures the similarity between two images. It is
775   defined as:
776
                                              (2µx µy + C1 )(2σxy + C2 )
                             SSIM(x, y) = 2                                  ,                  (14)
777                                        (µx + µ2y + C1 )(σx2 + σy2 + C2 )
778   where µx and µy are the mean values of the images x and y, σx2 and σy2 are the variances of the
779   images, σxy is the covariance of the images, and C1 and C2 are constants to stabilize the division
780   with weak denominator.
