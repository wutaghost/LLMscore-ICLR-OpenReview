009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We revisit the problem of fair representation learning by proposing Fair Partial
012            Least Squares (PLS) components. PLS is widely used in statistics to efficiently
013            reduce the dimension of the data by providing representation tailored for the pre-
014            diction. We propose a novel method to incorporate fairness constraints in the
015            construction of PLS components. This new algorithm provides a feasible way to
016            construct such features both in the linear and the non linear case using kernel em-
017            beddings. The efficiency of our method is evaluated on different datasets, and we
018            prove its superiority with respect to standard fair PCA method.

021   1   I NTRODUCTION
022
023   Over the past few years, the increasing use of automated decision-making systems has been widely
024   installed in businesses of private companies of all types, as well as government applications. Since
025   many of these decisions are made in sensitive domains, including healthcare (Morik, 2010), finance
026   (Trippi & Turban, 1992), criminal justice (Angwin et al., 2016), or hiring (Dastin, 2018), society
027   has experienced a significant impact on people’s lives. This fact has made the intersection between
028
      Artificial Intelligence (AI), Ethics and Law a crucial area of current research. Despite the success
      demonstrated by Machine Learning (ML) in these decision-making processes, there is a growing
029
      concern regarding the potential discriminatory biases in the decision rules.
030
031   One promising approach to mitigate unfair prediction outcomes is fair representation learning pro-
032   posed by Zemel et al. (2013) (see Section 2 for related work), which seek to learn meaningful
033   representations that maintain the content necessary for a particular task while removing indicators
034
      of protected group membership. Once the fair representation is learned, any prediction model con-
      structed on the top of the fair representation (i.e. using the representation as an input vector) are
035
      expected to be fair. Several works related to ours, such as Kleindessner et al. (2023); Olfat &
036
      Aswani (2019); Lee et al. (2022a), tackle the objective using principal component analysis (PCA).
037   However, the new representation tends to be less useful for predicting the target when it is strongly
038   correlated with some directions in the data that have low variance.
039
040
      We propose an alternative formulation based on the dimensionality reduction statistical technique
      Partial Least Squares (PLS) (Bair et al., 2006). The paper introduces fairness for PLS as doing
041
      PLS while minimizing the dependence of the projections with the demographic attribute. The main
042
      objective is to learn a representation that can trade-off some measure of fairness (e.g. statistical
043   parity, equal opportunity) with utility (e.g. covariance with respect to the target, accuracy) and can
044   be kernelized. Specifically, the goal is to create a representation that: (i) has lower dimension; (ii)
045   preserves information about the input space; (iii) is useful for predicting the target; (iv) is approxi-
046   mately independent of the sensitive variable. Our formulation has the same complexity as standard
047   Partial Least Squares, or Kernel Partial Least Squares, and have applications on different domains
048   and with different data structures as tabular, image or text embeddings. To sum up, we address
049   the following questions: (1) How can fairness be defined in the context of PLS? And (2) How to
050   integrate feasible fairness constraints into PLS algorithms?
051

072   2   BACKGROUND
073
074   Algorithmic fairness. In the last decade, fairness in ML has established itself as a very active
075   area of research which tries to ensure that predictive algorithms are not discriminatory towards any
076   individual or subgroup of population, based on demographic characteristics such as race, gender,
077   disabilities, sexual orientation, or political affiliation (Barocas et al., 2018). Although fair ML is a
078   relatively new area of concern, the growing amount of evidence of discrimination found in increas-
079   ingly varied fields, has driven the development of several approaches to this problem. We refer to
080   Wang et al. (2022) for a brief review on algorithmic fairness.
081   In general, the different formalizations of the concept of fairness in the existing literature can be
082   broadly classified into individual and group fairness. Let X ∈ X , S ∈ S and Y ∈ Y be the
083   non-sensitive input, the sensitive attribute and the ground truth target variable, respectively. Group
084   (or statistical) fairness emphasizes an equal treatment of individuals with respect to the sensitive
085   attributes S, which can be expressed through a measure of statistical independence between the
086   variables involved. In particular, the two main notions along this line are Demographic Parity (DP)
087   (Kamiran & Calders, 2012) and Equality of Odds (EO) (Hardt et al., 2016). The measure DP requires
088   that sensitive attributes should not influence the algorithm’s outcome, that is Ŷ ⊥⊥ S; while for EO
089   such independence is conditional to the ground-truth, that is Ŷ ⊥ ⊥ S|Y . In the particular setting of
090   binary classification, Y = {0, 1}, a classifier c : Rd → {0, 1} is said to be DP-fair, with respect
091
      to the joint distribution of (X, S), if P (c(X) = 1|S = s) = P (c(X) = 1). On the other hand, c
      is EO-fair, with respect to (X, S), if P (c(X) = 1|S = s, Y = y) = P (c(X) = 1|Y = y), for
092
      s, y = 0, 1. A relaxed version of EO has been also proposed as Equality of Opportunity (Hardt et al.,
093
      2016) and requires only the equality in TPR, namely P (c(X) = 1|S = 0, Y = 1) = P (c(X) =
094   1|S = 1, Y = 1). On the other hand, individual fairness (Dwork et al., 2012) examines individual
095   algorithms’ predictions and ensures that when two individuals are similar with respect to a specific
096   task, they are classified similarly. However, it is defined in terms of certain similarity metric for
097   the prediction task at hand which is generally difficult to obtain. Individual fairness is close to the
098   notion of counterfactual fairness which specifies the notion of closeness between individual with a
099   causal framework as shown in Kusner et al. (2017); Lara et al. (2024).
100   Regardless of the notion of fairness, methods for fair forecasting can be divided into (i) pre-
101   processing the input data from which the algorithms learns in order to remove sensitive dependencies
102   (Kamiran & Calders, 2012; Calmon et al., 2017; Gordaliza et al., 2019); (ii) in-processing by incor-
103   porating a fairness constraint or penalty in the algorithm’s learning objective function (Zafar et al.,
104   2017; Donini et al., 2018; Risser et al., 2022); and post-processing, which modifies the predictions
105   given by the algorithm (Wei et al., 2021).
106
107   Fair Representation Learning. The field of Fair Representation Learning (FRL) focuses on learn-
      ing data representations from which any information about the protected group membership has been


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      removed, while simultaneously retaining as much information related to other features as possible.
109   Hence, any ML model trained on the new representation should not not be able to discriminate based
110   on the demographic information, achieving fair outcomes.
111
112
      The goal of fair representation is to learn a fair feature representation r : X → X ′ such that the
      information shared between r(X) and some sensitive attribute S ∈ S is minimal. This is founded
113
      on the data processing inequality, a concept from information theory which states that the models’
114
      prediction can not have any more information about S than its input or hidden states (Beaudry
115   & Renner, 2012). Hence, the idea is to map the inputs X to r(X) and use this feature space as
116   input, thus ensuring the certain definition of fairness is achieved, inspired by an ethical notion that
117   establishes the way to limit the influence of S on the outcome of an AI system. As causal dependence
118   is a special kind of statistical dependence (Pearl, 2009), the real aim is to learn a map r : X → X ′
119   such that r(X) is (approximately) statistically independent with respect to the sensitive attribute S,
120   guaranteeing fairness of any model trained on top of this new representation.
121   FRL has been initially considered by Zemel et al. (2013) where they propose to learn a representation
122   that is a probability distribution over clusters where learning the cluster of a sample does not give
123   any information about the sensitive attribute S. Since then, a variety of methods have been put
124   forward in the recent literature. A popular approach to address this challenge is the variational auto-
125   encoder (VAE) (Gupta et al., 2021; Louizos et al., 2015) which aim to minimize the information
126   encoded in them. Other methods (Edwards & Storkey, 2016; Madras et al., 2018; Xie et al., 2017;
127   Liao et al., 2019) obtain learning representations formulating the problem as an adversarial game,
128   learning an encoder and an adversary. In contrast to the adversarial training scheme, Olfat & Aswani
129
      (2019) introduced the concept of fair PCA, aiming to ensure that no linear classifier can predict
      demographic information from the projected data. This approach has been further extended by
130
      Kleindessner et al. (2023); Lee et al. (2022a). Additionally, another notion of fair PCA was proposed
131
      by Samadi et al. (2018) which seeks to balance the excess reconstruction error across different
132   demographic groups. This is extended by approaches such as Pelegrina et al. (2021); Kamani et al.
133   (2022).
134
135
      In this work, we propose to introduce fairness constraints for PLS decomposition. Actually, in a
      supervised setting, PLS enables to build features which are more accurate than PCA components
136
      since they are directly related to the target to be forecast. Hence we propose to extend the PLS
137
      feature construction extraction with a fairness constraint, achieving a representation that is both
138   fair but also enables to obtain accurate predictions. This work extends the previous vanilla method
139   described in Champion et al. (2023) and applied to a medical dataset that projects a posteriori the
140   components onto the less biased components characterized by weaker correlations with the biased
141   variable.
142
      In this paper we provide a feasible way to impose fairness constraint on PLS components. Hence we
143
      provide representations that both enable to achieve a good forecast accuracy with few components
144   while reducing unwanted biases. Most FRL methods are unsupervised, and existing supervised
145   techniques do not account for situations where the number of samples is smaller than the number of
146   features. Our proposal, Fair PLS, addresses this gap.

151   Let, as before, X ∈ X , S ∈ S and Y ∈ Y be the non-sensitive input, the sensitive attribute

153   X ×S ×Y, where X ⊂ Rd is the set of possible (non-sensitive) inputs, Y ⊂ Rm is the set of possible
154   labels and S ⊂ Rns is the set of possible sensitive variable values. In the context of supervised

156
      set of labeled samples D = {xi , si , yi }ni=1 . We represent the dataset of n points x1 , x2 , ..., xn ∈ Rd

158
      that X ∈ Rn×d is the original centred d variables of n observations and Y ∈ Rn×m be the centred

      3.1   PARTIAL L EAST S QUARES
163
164   The Partial Least Squares (PLS - a.k.a. projection on latent structures) approach is a supervised
165   dimension reduction technique which generates orthogonal vectors, also referred to as latent vectors
166   or components, by maximising the covariance between different sets of variables (Höskuldsson,
167   1988; Rosipal & Krämer, 2006; Abdi, 2010). In detail, PLS aims to decompose the zero-mean
168   matrix X = TPT as a product of k ∈ [d] latent vectors (columns of T ∈ Rn×k ) and a matrix of
169   weights (P ∈ Rd×k ), with the constraint that these components explain as much as possible of the
170
      covariance between X and Y. In other words, PLS approach attempts to find directions that help
      explain both the response Y and the predictors X. Indeed, any collection of orthogonal vectors
171
      that span the column space of X could be used as the latent vectors. Consequently, to achieve
172
      decorrelated components with maximum correlation with Y, additional conditions on the matrices
173   P and Q will be required. Specifically, the PLS method finds two sets of weights vectors denoted
174   as (w1 , ..., wd ) and (c1 , ..., cd ) such that the linear combination of the columns of X and Y have
175   maximum covariance. The first pair of vectors w and c verify the following optimization problem:

274   Why cannot Fair PLS be formulated in closed form? It is important to note that adapting the
275   Partial Least Squares methodology to achieve fairness as a trade-off is challenging due to the inher-
276   ent complexity of the PLS method. Contrary to PCA analysis for which a closed form may be found,
277   computing the PLS components is not a direct method. We refer for instance to Blazere et al. (2015)
278   or Löfstedt (2024) and references therein. When modifying the loss with the fairness penalty makes
279   the computation even less tractable. Specifically, if we denote as σmin,Y the minimum eigenvalue
280   of the matrix YYT and σmax,S the maximum eigenvalue of the matrix SS T . The Fair PLS weights
281   {wh }kh=1 are the eigenvectors of the certain matrix if η ≤ σmin,Y /σmax,S . Moreover the matrix
282   whose eigenvectors are the Fair PLS weights is XT MMT X, with B = YYT − ηSS T = QT DQ
283   and M = QT D1/2 .

285   Motivation for Fair PLS The motivation behind the idea of Fair Representation Learning by
286   a PLS-based approach is interpretability. Yet our aim is to provide a method that enables us to
287   recover a linear transformation of the data to promote explainability of the components. Hence,
288
      the PLS method was a suitable way to achieve interpretability of the new components yet enabling
      forecasting. Fair PLS allows us to learn a new representation that not only is lower in dimension but
289
      also a trade-off between fairness and utility performance. For instance, for the COMPAS dataset,
290
      if we obtain the most relevant features of the learned components, we discover that if η = 0.0
291   (i.e. standard PLS), these are: event, decile score, juv misd count, race and decile score; while for
292   η = 1.0 they are: event, age, juv other count, juv misd count and priors count. Hence, the sensitive
293   variable does not impact the Fair PLS components.

      4.1   K ERNELIZING FAIR PLS
298
299   Let us now extend our Fair PLS approach (Section 3.2) to the non-linear version of PLS by means
300   of reproducing kernels (Rosipal & Trejo, 2002). In this section, we formulate Kernel Fair Partial
301   Least Squares by adding the Hilbert Schmidt independence criterion (Tan et al., 2020; Fukumizu
302   et al., 2007) as the fairness regularization term in the standard PLS formulation in equation 2. The
303   proposed Kernel Fair PLS is based on a fair adaptation of the NIPALS procedure to iteratively
304   estimate the desired components which are not linearly related to the input variables. Furthermore,
305   this will allow to use multiple sensitive attributes simultaneously. To this end, Kernel Fair PLS is
306
      a generalization of Fair PLS to feature spaces of arbitrary large dimensionality. We additionally
      provide the pseudo code of kernelized Fair PLS in Appendix A.
307

360   The aim of Fair PLS, as formulated in Section 3, is to represent the data such that it is independent
361   of the demographic attribute. This approach guarantees that any classifier trained with the new data

      4.3       A PPLICATION TO L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
379
380   In the context of supervised learning, a decision rule to perform a specific classification task is
381   obtained from a set of labeled samples X . However, in the setting of Large Language Models
382   (LLM), such a decision rule is considered to be f : Z → Y, where z ∈ Z represents an input text
383   and y ∈ Y denotes their corresponding label. Therefore, the decision rule f can be viewed as a
384   composition of two functions f = c ◦ h. The first one h : Z → A encompasses all the layers to
385   transform the input data z ∈ Z to a vector a belonging to the latent space A. The second function
386
      c : A → Y involves all the layers to classify the transformed data h(z) ∈ Rd . We represent the
      dataset of n points a1 , a2 , ..., an ∈ Rd as a matrix A = h(Z) ∈ Rn×d , where the i-th row is
387
      equals ai . This matrix is known as CLS-embedding matrix in the encoder transformer model. SVD
388
      decomposition is a successful way to understand how the embedding matrix can be factorized into
389   concepts that enable to understand the behavior of the language model. This framework has been
390   recently presented in Jourdan et al. (2023b) and bias analysis in this context is discussed in Jourdan
391   et al. (2023a) for instance. Hence, for SVD, the matrix A is decomposed into A = U0 Σ0 V0⊤ ,
392   where U0 ∈ Rn×n and V0 ∈ Rd×d are orthonormal matrices, and Σ0 ∈ Rn×d is diagonal. This
393   decomposition reveals the main variability in A. By retaining the r ≪ d largest singular values in
394   Σ, we approximate A as: A ≈ U W , where U ∈ Rn×r contains the leading r columns of U0 , and
395   W = ΣV ⊤ ∈ Rr×d combines the singular values and right singular vectors (see Eckart & Young
396   (1936)). SVD is used to capture the most significant patterns in the data by reducing dimensionality
397
      while preserving maximum variance. For our purposes, U is the concept matrix that is needed to
      be fair. The PLS-based approach is relevant to not lose explainability of the new components due to
398
      the linear transformation.
399
400   An application of the Fair PLS methodology proposed is to reduce the influence of demographic
401   factors that contribute to the model predictions using an algebraic decomposition of the latent rep-
402   resentations of the model into orthogonal dimensions. In other words, we aim to intervene in the
403
      latent representations to generate a new fair representation with respect to dimensions that convey
      bias. This approach is justified due to the orthogonality of the dimensions of the new representation
404
      obtained with Fair PLS.

530
531
      We define a Fair Partial Least Squares approach that allows to balance between utility (predictive
532
      performance) and fairness (independence of the demographic information) and can be kernelized.
533
      Our formulation have the same complexity (algorithmically) as standard Partial Least Squares, or
534   Kernel Partial Least Squares, and have applications on different domains and with different data
535   structures as tabular, image or text embeddings. Furthermore, it can be adapted to the equality of
536   odds paradigm through the use of the conditional cross covariance operator. This poses a robust
537   methodology able to solve different fair scenarios. The experiments demonstrates empirical guar-
538   antees of fairness of any model trained on top of the Fair PLS representation and better predictive
539   performance for the same level of fairness when is compared to existing methods for FRL as Fair
      PCA.

      B.1   D ETAILS ABOUT DATASETS
867
868   Adult Income dataset The Adult Income dataset, available through the UCI repository (Dua &
869   Graff, 2017) provides the results of a census made in 1994 in the United States. Specifically, it
870   contains information about 48842 of individuals, described as values of 14 features: 8 categorical
871   and 6 numeric. The objective of this dataset is to accurately predict whether an individual’s annual
872   income is above or below 50, 000$, taking into account factors such its occupation, marital status,
873   and education.
874
875   German Credit dataset The German credit dataset (Hofmann, 1994), comprises records of indi-
876
      viduals who hold bank accounts. This dataset serves the purpose of forecasting risk, specifically to
      assess whether it’s advisable to extend credit to an individual. Specifically, it contains information
877
      about 1000 individuals, described as values of 21 features: 14 categorical and 7 numerical. The
878
      objective of this dataset is to accurately predict the customer’s level of risk when granting a credit,
879   taking into account factors such as the status of the existing checking account, credit amount or
880   marital status.
881
882   Law School dataset The Law School Admission Council dataset, gathers statistics from 163 US
883   law schools and more than 20,000 students, obtained through a survey across 163 law schools in the
884   United States. This dataset serves the purpose of forecasting the first -year grade from the profile.
885   Specifically, it contains information about 21,791 individuals, described as values of 7 features: 2
886   categorical and 7 numerical. The objective of this dataset is to accurately predict if an applicant will
887   have a high FYA, taking into account factors such as students entrance exam scores (LSAT), their
888   grade-point average (GPA) collected prior to law school, and their first year average grade (FYA).
889
890   Diabetes dataset The Diabetes dataset (Clore et al., 2014), represents ten years (1999-2008) of
891
      clinical care at 130 US hospitals and integrated delivery networks. Each row concerns hospital
      records of patients diagnosed with diabetes, who underwent laboratory, medications, and stayed up
892
      to 14 days. This dataset serves the purpose of forecasting if a patient will be readmitted within
893
      30 days of discharge. Specifically, it contains information about 101766 individuals, described as
894   values of 49 features: 36 categorical and 13 numerical. The objective of this dataset is to accurately
895   predict the readmitted {< 30, > 30} indicating whether a patient will readmit within 30 days (the
896   positive class is < 30), taking into account factors such as weight, gender or the number of lab tests
897   performed during the encounter.
898
899   COMPAS dataset The COMPAS dataset, (Angwin et al., 2016), which was released by ProPub-
900   lica in 2016 is based on the Broward County data (collected from January 2013 to December 2014).
901   This dataset serves the purpose of forecasting recidivism risk scores, specifically to predict if an in-
902   dividual is rearrested within 2 years after the first arrest. Specifically, it contains information about
903   7214 individuals, described as values of 52 features: 33 categorical and 19 numerical. The objective
904
      of this dataset is to accurately predict the COM-PAS recid, taking into account factors such as the
      risk of recidivism in general, sex or age.
905
906
      Communities and Crimes dataset The Communities and Crimes dataset (Redmond, 2002), is a
907
      small dataset containing the socioeconomic data from 46 states of the United States in 1990 (the
908
      US Census). This dataset serves the purpose of forecasting the total number of violent crimes per
909   100 thousand population. Specifically, it contains information about 1994 individuals, described
910   as values of 127 features: 4 categorical and 123 numerical. The objective of this dataset is to
911   accurately predict the number of violent crimes per 100,000 population (normalized to [0,1]) taking
912   into account factors such as median household income, per capita income or number of kids born to
913   never married.
914
915   Synthetic dataset The synthetic dataset contains two groups (S = 0 and S = 1) with distinct
916   statistical properties. The data includes four quantitative variables (0-3) and three binary variables
917   (4-6), all correlated within each group. We generate 500 samples for each group from two mul-
      tivariate normal distributions on R7 ; with means (9, 8, 10, 10, 0, 0, 0) and (10, 10, 10, 10, 0, 0, 0),

919   variable 0, while variable 6 influences variable 1. In contrast, for males, binary variables have little
920   to no impact on these quantitative variables. The target variable, Y , is generated using a weighted
921   combination of these features, with different coefficients for each group. The data is then shuffled,
922   and a binary indicator S is added to distinguish between genders. This setup provides a useful
923   framework for testing biases and statistical analysis.

1122
       We compared our proposed algorithm to the state-of-the-art method Vanilla Fair PLS Champion
1123
       et al. (2023). The Vanilla PLS method consists in selecting the features that are related to the target
1124
       (PLS) such that the correlation with the sensitive ones is below a predefined threshold τ . In other
1125   words, it is a naive strategy that directly make use of the latent variables (t1 , ..., tk ) generated with
1126   the standard PLS technique which are highly correlated with the outcome Y to impose fairness. This
1127   methodology is based on the conditional marginal distribution of those components to the sensitive
1128   variable S. In contrast, our formulation aims to obtain components that seek a balance between
1129   being target-related (η small) and being independent with respect to the sensitive attribute (η large
1130   enough). The left column of Figure 5 shows the behaviour of C2r(X),Y for new representations
1131   r(X) obtained with the Vanilla PLS procedures. It is clear that, on the one hand, for τ close to
1132   0 the representation has no features (k = 0). Moreover, as τ increases, the number of features in
1133   the representation also increases, while there is no trade-off between covariance with respect to the
       target Y , nor with respect to the sensitive feature. This is, the C2r(X),Y increases but the C2r(X),S

1219   X rather than Y , and so, nothing guarantees that the principal components, which ‘explain’ X op-
