009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Tabular machine learning has garnered increasing attention due to its practical
012            value. Unlike the complete and standardized data often assumed in academia,
013            tabular data primarily originates from industrial contexts and usually faces
014            the issue of incomplete data samples, i.e., some features of a sample may
015            be unpredictably missing. In this work, we introduce MaskTab, a masked
016            tabular data modeling framework designed to facilitate model learning despite
017            missing features. Instead of pursuing to accurately restore missing features like
018
               existing imputation methods, we jointly approach missing feature modeling and
               downstream tasks (e.g., classification) with a unified objective. Concretely, we
019
               propose to randomly drop out some solid features during training, equipped with
020
               a missing-related masked attention mechanism, to help the model rely more on
021            trustworthy features when making decisions. Experiments on the very recent
022            industry-grade benchmark, TabReD, suggest that our method surpasses the second
023            DNN-based competitor by a clear margin, demonstrating its effectiveness and
024            robustness in real-world scenarios. We will release the code and the model to
025            facilitate reproduction.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Machine learning (ML) models applied to tabular data have significant industrial applications,
031   such as credit assessment (Aziz et al., 2022) and medical diagnosis (Hassan et al., 2020).
032   Recent advancements in deep tabular models have demonstrated promising effectiveness in various
033
      academic scenarios. However, tabular data primarily originates from industrial contexts and usually
      faces the issue of incomplete data samples, which requires further investigation for modeling with
034
      missing features under industrial scenarios.
035
036   To address the issue of missing data, imputation methods (Du et al., 2024; Ma & Zhang, 2021;
037   Jinsung Yoon & van der Schaar, 2019) have been developed to estimate missing values using
038   observed data. However, these methods often prioritize accurate restoration of missing values and
039
      neglect the significance of imputation for downstream tasks, which can be suboptimal for prediction
      models, as indicated by recent studies (Daniel Jarrett & van der Schaar, 2022). Moreover, the
040
      assessment of these imputation methods typically involves synthetic data with simulated missing
041
      values, which may not adequately reflect the challenges posed by real-world data absence.
042
043   Recent advancements in deep tabular models have focused on the development of sophisticated
044   network architectures, such as enhanced multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) and transformers (Chen
045
      et al., 2022; Gorishniy et al., 2021). Additionally, improvements in retrieval based methods have
      emerged, for instance, TabR (Gorishniy et al., 2024) has shown superior performance compared
046
      to XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016) on GBDT-friendly benchmarks (Grinsztajn et al., 2022).
047
      However, these approaches have predominantly been assessed on academic datasets characterized
048   by low feature dimensionality and minimal missing values, which do not reflect the complexities of
049   real-world industrial applications. Newly established industry-grade tabular benchmarks, TabReD
050   (Rubachev et al., 2024), indicate that these advanced methods often underperform relative to more
051   straightforward tree-based models when applied to industrial data. Notably, XGBoost continues
052   to excel, particularly on the HomeCredit Default dataset, which characterized by significant
053   missingness and dimensionality, greatly surpassing the performance of deep tabular models. This
      highlights the need to enhance the capabilities of deep tabular models in modeling missing features.

054
      Another promising development in deep tabular models involves leveraging pre-training techniques,
055   such as contrastive learning (Bahri et al., 2022; Somepalli et al., 2022), masked modeling (Ye
056   et al., 2024), and technologies associated with large language models (Yan et al., 2024; Borisov
057   et al., 2023). The pre-training of models to impart knowledge demonstrates significant potential in
058   situations marked by limited samples and data scarcity.
059
      In this work, we introduce MaskTab, a masked tabular data modeling framework designed to
060
      facilitate model learning despite missing features. Instead of pursuing to accurately restore missing
061   features like existing imputation methods, we jointly approach missing feature modeling and
062   downstream tasks (e.g., classification) with a unified objective. Concretely, we propose to randomly
063   discarding essential features during the training process, which act as a simulation process to
064   generate additional missing features in samples that are truly incomplete. Further, we introduce
065   a joint learning framework, that integrates the reconstruction of simulated missing features with the
066   optimization of downstream tasks, to learn a more robust embedding representation for truly missing
067   values. We represent both true missing and simulated missing by initializing a parameter-shared,
068   learnable mask embedding, to enhance the practical utility of the missing representation. Moreover,
069
      considering the joint learning framework, we emphasize the significance of masking strategies to
      minimize significant shifts in data distribution, as such shifts can negatively impact downstream
070
      task performance. We investigate the criteria for effective masking strategies and provide
071
      valuable insights applicable to diverse scenarios involving feature missingness. Additionally, we
072   implement a missing-related masked attention mechanism, that integrates prior information of
073   feature missingness into the attention computation, which enables the model to prioritize trustworthy
074   features when making decisions for each sample. Experiments on the very recent industry-grade
075   benchmark, TabReD, show the effectiveness of MaskTab in real-world industrial scenarios.
076
      Our contributions are as follow:
077
078          • We propose MaskTab, a masked tabular data modeling framework to tackle the feature
079            missing problem in industrial scenarios, experiments on TabReD demonstrate that our
080            method surpasses the second DNN-based competitor by a clear margin.
081
             • Ablation studies reveal that both the joint learning framework and the embedding represen-
082            tation specifically designed to handle genuinely missing values are effective. Additionally,
083            the missing-related masked attention mechanism proves beneficial. Our findings provide
084            significant insights applicable to a wide range of real-world scenarios.
085
             • MaskTab employs an end-to-end training approach that eliminates the need for imputing
086
               missing features, which is both straightforward and effective.

089   2   R ELATED W ORK
090
091   Masked Modeling. Masked training was initially introduced as a pre-training strategy for language
092   models. This technique occludes specific words in a sentence, requiring the model to predict the
093
      masked tokens based on the visible context. It has led to significant advancements in both natural
      language processing (NLP) and computer vision (CV), as exemplified by models like BERT (Devlin
094
      et al., 2018) and BEit (Bao et al., 2022). Recently, masked training has gained impact as a restoration
095
      pre-training task in the domain of tabular data. XTab (Zhu et al., 2023) and CM2 (Ye et al.,
096   2024) employ masked learning on cross-table data to develop tabular foundation models. ReMask
097   (Du et al., 2024) implementes missing feature imputation through optimizing the autoencoder by
098   reconstructing a randomly re-masked features. TabMT (Gulati & Roysdon, 2023) designs advanced
099   masking techniques to generate synthetic tabular data.
100
      Deep Tabular Models. With advancements in deep neural networks, deep tabular models have
101
      transitioned from multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) to more sophisticated architectures, including
102   ResNet (Gorishniy et al., 2021), SNN (Klambauer et al., 2017), DANets (Chen et al., 2022),
103   and DCNv2 (Wang et al., 2020). These approaches typically operate directly on raw features.
104   Transformer-based methodologies have garnered attention recently, which treat individual raw
105   features as tokens and convert feature values into high-dimensional vectors using either lookup tables
106   or linear mappings. Notable approaches including TransTab (Wang & Sun, 2022), AutoInt (Song
107   et al., 2019), T2G-Former (Yan et al., 2023) and FT-Transformer (Gorishniy et al., 2021). These
      methods effectively utilize the attention mechanism to enhance feature interactions. Furthermore,

      3.2   M ASKED MODELING FOR M ISSING FEATURES
166
167   We employ a masked tabular data modeling framework to enhance the model’s effectiveness in sce-
168   narios with feature missingness. Given a sample with truly missing features x = {xcat , xnum , xmiss },
169   we employ a defined probability, referred to as mask prob (e.g., 15%), to determine whether to
170   simulate an increased level of feature missingness for it. For the samples that require simulation, we
171   drop features—excluding those that are truly missing—at a specified mask ratio (e.g., 30%). This
172
      process transforms the original sample into x = {xcat , xnum , xmiss , xmasked }, hear xmasked denotes
      the simulated missing values. We initialize a parameter-shared, learnable mask embedding for both
173
      true and simulated missing values. In Sec 3.5, we present a joint learning approach that integrates
174
      missing feature reconstruction with downstream task optimization, to improve the applicability of
175   the mask embedding for downstream applications. Furthermore, it is essential to carefully design
176   the masking strategy to mitigate significant shifts in data distribution, which could negatively impact
177   the performance of downstream tasks. We investigate the effective criteria of masked strategy in
178   Sec 4, provide valuable insights for applications across various real-world scenarios characterized
179   by feature missingness.

294   4      E XPERIMENTS
295
296   In this section, we validated the effectiveness of our method on the TabReD (Rubachev et al., 2024),
297   a industry-grade tabular benchmark. The experiments demonstrate that the MaskTab achieves
298   superior performance on both classification and regression tasks. Furthermore, we conducted
299   ablation experiments on each module to verify the effectiveness of our method.
300
301   4.1      E XPERIMENTAL D ETAILS
302
303
      Datasets. We employed the TabReD benchmark, which exclusively consists of tabular data
      obtained from real-world industrial applications. This benchmark comprises 8 datasets, including 3
304
      classification tasks and 5 regression tasks. As outlined in Table 1, each dataset is characterized by a
305
      substantial number of samples and features. Furthermore, we evaluated the rates of missing features
306   for each dataset and found that most exhibit varying degrees of incompleteness. Additionally,
307   these datasets were divided into training, validation, and testing sets based on temporal criteria.
308   These characteristics are frequently encountered in industrial applications, rendering this benchmark
309   particularly suitable for assessing model performance in such contexts.
310
      Data Pre-processing. Follwing FT-transformer (Gorishniy et al., 2021), we categorize the columns
311
      of the table into numerical and categorical features. Numerical features are processed through
312   quantile normalize using the Scikit-learn library, with normalization parameters calculated solely
313   on the training set. For regression tasks, we apply the standardization to the labels. Notably, in the
314   experiments conducted with MaskTab, we did not fill with any value for missing features.
315
      Experimental Setup. We configured the model using default parameters informed by empirical
316
      evidence, incorporating 4 transformer blocks, each equipped with 8 attention heads, a feature
317
      embedding size of 128, and a dropout rate of 15% within the feed-forward network. The batch
318   size was set at 128, and we employed the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 1e − 4. In the
319   vicinity of the default parameters, Hyperparameters were optimized for each dataset through grid
320   search, guided by performance metrics derived from the validation set, notably, the test set was kept
321   untouched during this tuning process. Experiments were conducted on 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs, and
322   the optimal model checkpoint was selected via early stopping, based on validation metrics. For
323   evaluation, we utilized the receiver operating characteristic area under the curve (ROC AUC) for
      classification tasks and the root mean square error (RMSE) for regression tasks.

358   The comparative results of MaskTab and other methods are presented in Table 2. By calculating

      deep learning methods, our approach demonstrates a significant advantage on dataset HomeCredit

      5   C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
476
477
      In this study, we examine the applicability of deep tabular models in industrial contexts, which
478
      are characterized by high dimensionality and significant amounts of missing data. To address the
479
      challenge of feature absence, we introduce MaskTab, which employs a masked modeling approach
480   for tabular data. Furthermore, our specifically optimized modules exhibit notable effectiveness.
481   Comparative analyses position our method as a promising candidate for industrial applications. A
482   future direction for optimization involves conducting cross-table training using extensive industrial
483   datasets to determine whether larger data scales result in enhanced performance. Additionally, in
484   certain industrial scenarios, some critical features may be entirely absent for specific test samples.
485   We aim to investigate whether training on samples that encompass all features could improve
      predictive accuracy for those samples with missing key attributes.
