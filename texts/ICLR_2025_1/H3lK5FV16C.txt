                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               The classification of road signs by autonomous systems, especially those reliant on
010
               visual inputs, is highly susceptible to adversarial attacks. Traditional approaches to
011
               mitigating such vulnerabilities have focused on enhancing the robustness of classi-
012            fication models. In contrast, this paper adopts a fundamentally different strategy
013            aimed at increasing robustness through the redesign of road signs themselves. We
014            propose an attacker-agnostic learning scheme to automatically design road signs
015            that are robust to a wide array of patch-based attacks. Empirical tests conducted in
016            both digital and physical environments demonstrate that our approach significantly
017            reduces vulnerability to patch attacks, outperforming existing techniques.

020   1   I NTRODUCTION
021
022   As autonomous driving systems become progressively more embedded in real-world systems, their
023   safety becomes paramount. These systems and their sub-components, such as classification and
024
      segmentation modules, have been shown to be vulnerable to adversarial attacks Goodfellow et al.
      (2014); Madry et al. (2017); Kurakin et al. (2016) In this work, we focus on enhancing the safety of
025
      such systems by modifying the appearance of objects (specifically road signs) such that adversarial
026
      attacks applied to those objects are less effective (see Figure 1).
027
028   When countering such attacks, defensive approaches in adversarial machine learning take a model-
029   centric approach, focusing solely on the model as a means of improving robustness. However, in
030
      many real-world scenarios, the model itself is not the only tunable object; from cars, to road signs,
      to buildings, the world is filled with manufactured objects. These manufactured objects are capable
031
      of being tuned just as models are capable of being tuned. Using this observation, we propose a
032
      framework to jointly optimize both predictive models and manufactured objects (specifically road
033   signs) to attain robustness to adversarial attacks (specifically patch attacks).
034
035   Similar to our line of work is Salman et al. (2021), which first proposed modifying the appearance of
036
      physical objects by designing patterns that make them easier to recognize under naturally challenging
      conditions, e.g., foggy weather. Adversarially crafted perturbations pose a more significant challenge
037
      from a defender’s perspective for two key reasons: firstly, adversarial examples are explicitly designed
038
      to decrease model performance, and secondly, they are out of distribution with respect to training
039   data (naturally challenging conditions are typically seen in training data, albeit scarcely for some
040   domains). For these reasons, our techniques diverge substantially from those of Salman et al. (2021).

      To counter adversarial attacks, we propose an environmental-centric approach, Robust Environmental
055   Design (RED), in which we design the backgrounds of road signs such that the road signs are both
056   robust and still easy to print (as shown in Figure 1). RED has two key phases: first, patterns for each
057   class of object are learned (e.g., one pattern for speed limit signs, one for stop signs, etc); second,
058   after affixing the designed pattern to each object, we then train a classifier on partially masked images
059   (see Figure 4 for an example). At test time, images are also partially masked prior. Importantly, the
060   RED pipeline attains robustness without access to the adversary or any type of adversarial training.
061   When the adversary is known, we show how the RED pipeline can be easily modified to incorporate
062   this additional information.
063   To demonstrate the efficacy of our method, we conduct experiments using two common benchmark
064   datasets for road sign classification, LISA and GTSRB Eykholt et al. (2018), and test against several
065   types of patch-based attack paradigms. Our approach achieves high levels of robustness compared
066   to SotA approaches. Additionally, we conducted physical experiments by printing various common
067   road signs (e.g., stop signs, speed limit signs, etc.) with patterns optimized via RED. We collected
068   photos at different times of the day, under various lighting and weather conditions. We find that RED
069
      significantly improves robustness against attacks in both digital and physical settings.
070   In summary, our key contributions are as follows:
071
072
            1. We propose RED, a novel paradigm for attaining robustness against patch attacks that jointly
               optimizes road sign backgrounds and a predictive model.
073
074         2. We compare RED to several baselines on two road sign classification tasks and find that
075
               RED achieves superior robustness.
076         3. We conduct physical experiments in which we construct road signs with the background
077            patterns learned by RED, and find these patterns remain robust.

081   Attack Adversarial attacks pose a significant threat to machine learning models, particularly in
082   real-world applications where classification and segmentation systems can be deceived by carefully
083   crafted perturbations Goodfellow et al. (2014); Madry et al. (2017); Kurakin et al. (2016). Our
084   focus is on enhancing safety by modifying the appearance of objects (e.g., road signs) to reduce
085   the effectiveness of such attacks (see Figure 1). Eykholt et al. (2018); Yang et al. (2020) highlight
086
      the dangers of misclassification, where small errors can lead to serious consequences, such as in
      autonomous driving. A growing concern is the realization of physical adversarial attacks Eykholt
087
      et al. (2018); Kurakin et al. (2016); Athalye et al. (2018), often in the form of adversarial patches that
088
      deceive classifiers, detectors, and segmentators. Brown et al. (2017); Eykholt et al. (2018); Liu et al.
089   (2018); Karmon et al. (2018); Zhang et al. (2019) introduced such patches for real-world objects.
090
091   Defenes Pre-Attack Defense Inference Several works Xiang et al. (2021); Levine and Feizi (2020)
092   suggest that inference using small predictions on cropped images can improve robustness by reducing
093   the probability of encountering adversarial pixels. Levine and Feizi (2020) recommend cropping
094   images (e.g., down to 10% of the original image size) during inference, while Xiang et al. (2021)
095   propose a two-round selection process for identifying “adversarial areas" and only cropping out those
096   areas. More broadly speaking, there has been a plethora of recent works on defending against patch
097   attacks Liu et al. (2022); Wei et al. (2024); Author and Others (2023a;b); Liu et al. (2023); Ren et al.
098
      (2022); Cohen et al. (2019); Lecuyer et al. (2019); Salman et al. (2019) similar to the aforementioned
      works, these works primarily attempt to nullify the adversarial patch. Defense against patch attacks
099
      has also been studied in a wide array of applications such as autonomous driving Cao et al. (2022),
100
      objective tracking Gao et al. (2023), transfer learning Zhu et al. (2022), etc.
101
102   Post-Attack Defenses such as Xu et al. (2023) use adversarial detectors trained on adversarial examples
103   to identify and remove patches before applying additional defenses. Other defenses, such as those
104
      based on adversarial training Goodfellow et al. (2014); Madry et al. (2017); Shafahi et al. (2019);
      Zhou et al. (2022); Cao et al. (2022); Xiang et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2024); Bai et al. (2024); Zhang
105
      et al. (2023), rely on generating adversarial examples during training to improve robustness.
106
107   A line work closely related to ours is that of Salman et al. (2021), which proposed modifying the
      appearance of physical objects by designing patterns that make them easier to recognize under


                                                          2

108
      naturally challenging conditions, such as foggy weather. Similarly, Si et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023)
109   proposes to apply stickers to objects to boost object detection, while Chen et al. (2023) proposes
110   preprocessing images with noise (again, under non-adversarial conditions). Unlike these works, we
111   study adversarial perturbations, which present a greater challenge as they are specifically designed
112   to degrade model performance and are typically out of distribution, unlike naturally challenging
113   conditions that arise in training.

      Road Sign Classification Let X ⊂ RW ×H be a domain of possible road sign images of W by
118   H pixels, and Y be the set of possible road sign classes. Each road sign image X ∈ X has a
119   corresponding true label y ∈ Y (e.g., stop sign). To predict the class of a road sign, a classifier
120   f : X → Y is used, where f (X) represents the predicted class of image X.
121
      Adversarial Patch Attack We focus on patch-based attacks against image classification models. For
122
      a given image X with true label y, the attacker’s goal is to find a maliciously modified version of
123
      X, say X ′ such that f (X ′ ) ̸= y. Patch-based attacks constitute the attacker modifying a region of at
124   most B pixels in X. The region can have various shapes but is constrained to be a contiguous region
125   of the image, and is defined by a binary mask M where M [i][j] = 1 if the attacker is modifying pixel
126   (i, j) and M [i][j] = 0. The attacker then applies a perturbation δ (with magnitude at most ε) to the
127   pixels defined by M . The attacker finds their desired mask M and perturbation δ via the following:
128
                                      δ ′ , M ′ = arg max P f (X ′ ) ̸= y)                                     (1)

134   Where |M | counts the number of 1’s in the mask M and ⊙ is elementwise multiplication.

136   Image Sanitizing Defense Our defense makes use of image-sanitization in which a binary mask
137   W is applied to the image X. Predictions are then made on the masked image, i.e. f (W ⊙ X). Let g
138   be a function which maps an image X to its masked counter-part, i.e. g(X) = W ⊙ X. The goal is
139   to sufficiently mask out the adversary’s attack while leaving enough class-specific information in the
140   remaining pixels such that the classifier f predicts correctly.

155
156   Next, we outline our proposed method Robust Environmental Design (RED). At a high level, RED
157   works by using both image sanitization and pattern design. RED modifies the background of road
158
      signs such that any patch placed on that road sign is not effective at fooling the classifier (see Figure
      1 for an example). Then, at inference time, RED makes several predictions on different mask-out
159
      versions of a given image (taking the majority vote for the final prediction).
160
161   The training phase for RED has two key phases, pattern-selection and model-optimization. First,
      in the pattern-selection phase, we aim to design a background that contains high class-specific

300
301   Lastly, we remark on two extensions of RED. Importantly, we do not provide empirical results for
302   these extensions, aiming only to provide guidance for those wishing to deploy RED.
303
      Color Selection In some cases, it may be desirable for those designing the pattern α to be able to
304
      select which colors are used. For example, in the case of stop signs, the designer may wish to avoid
305   having shades of green in the pattern. In Section A.1, we outline how color constraints can be easily
306   added to the objective function of RED.
307
308
      Attacker Aware RED As mentioned previously, RED is an attacker-free defense technique, meaning
      that we do not require access to the attacker. However, in some cases, the attacker is known (or at
309
      least some information about the attacker is known). In Section A.2 we outline how RED can be
310
      modified to use such information. In particular, additional information about the attacker can be
311   incorporated into the model optimization phase when selecting the classifier f .

474   6    C ONCLUSION
475
476   We propose Robust Environmental Design (RED), a technique that enhances the robustness of visual
477   recognition systems, specifically in the case of road signs, against adversarial attacks. RED works by
478   learning background patterns for road signs in tandem with a predictive model. We find RED attains
479   superior performance compared to baselines on two common road sign datasets and a variety of
480   patch-based attacks; this holds true for especially larger patches. Additionally, we conduct physical
481   experiments in which we manufacture road signs with the patterns learned via RED. We observe that
482
      the patterns remain robust when deployed in the physical world.
483   While RED attains superior performance, our method is not without limitations. In particular, we only
484   evaluate RED on road sign datasets against patch attacks. It remains to be seen whether the robustness
485   of RED will persist in other domains or against other types of attacks. Moreover, our experiments
      focus on classification models. Vision-related tasks, particularly those relevant to autonomous driving,

487   expect extensions of RED to perform well on tasks beyond classification, it is worth noting that the
488   performance of RED on these tasks is unknown.
