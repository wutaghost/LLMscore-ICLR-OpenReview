009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Average Precision (AP) is a widely used metric for evaluating object detection sys-
012            tems because it effectively integrates both classification accuracy and localization
013            precision. In this paper, we conduct a detailed analysis of the characteristics of
014            the AP metric, focusing on its non-differentiability and non-convexity. Building
015            on this analysis, we propose a novel loss function called Differentiable Average
016            Precision Loss (DAP-loss), which provides a differentiable approximation of AP,
017            thereby enabling direct optimization of AP across a set of images. We validate
018            the effectiveness of DAP-loss both theoretically and empirically, extending its ap-
019            plication to the cost functions used in the Hungarian matching algorithm, which
020
               makes it suitable for end-to-end detection models. DAP-loss supports the simul-
               taneous optimization of classification and localization tasks within an end-to-end
021
               framework, eliminating the need for hyperparameters to balance these tasks—a
022
               common challenge in previous methods. In the later stages of training, we applied
023            DAP-loss to replace the original loss functions in several state-of-the-art end-to-
024            end models, including DETR (Carion et al., 2020) and Deformable DETR (Zhu
025            et al., 2020). Experimental results demonstrate that our method achieves signifi-
026            cant improvements over baselines on the COCO dataset.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Object detection is a crucial task in computer vision, focused on identifying and localizing objects
033
      within images. It requires optimizing both classification accuracy and the precise localization of
      object boundaries. Most existing deep learning-based object detectors (e.g. Carion et al. (2020);
034
      Ren et al. (2015); Redmon et al. (2016)) rely on multi-task learning frameworks, using separate
035
      loss functions to address classification and localization issues independently. Although this method
036   enables straightforward model training via gradient descent, it introduces multiple hyperparameters
037   to balance the weights of different losses, resulting in significant costs associated with hyperparam-
038   eter tuning. In contrast, our proposed DAP-loss involves only a single hyperparameter that requires
039   tuning, making it much easier to fine-tune for optimal performance.
040
      Average Precision (AP) is a pivotal evaluation metric in object detection, integrating both classifi-
041
      cation accuracy and localization precision. Despite its widespread use, the non-differentiable and
042   non-convex nature of AP poses significant challenges for direct optimization using gradient descent
043   techniques. Various methods, such as Henderson & Ferrari (2017a); Chen et al. (2019); Mohapa-
044   tra et al. (2018), have been proposed to try to directly optimize AP in the field of object detection.
045   However, most of these approaches primarily focus on score ranking or classification tasks, often
046   neglecting the localization aspect. As a result, these methods require additional loss functions and
047   hyperparameters to achieve comprehensive optimization. Thus, the optimization of AP-loss remains
048   an open problem.
049   In this paper, we provide a comprehensive analysis of the AP metric, examining both its inherent
050   characteristics and the difficulties associated with its gradient, which may be non-existent or zero.
051   Building on this analysis, we introduce a novel differentiable approximation of AP, termed Differ-
052   entiable Average Precision Loss (DAP-loss), aimed at addressing these limitations. Specifically,
053   we decompose AP into the product of a localization function and a classification function. For
      localization, we use interpolation techniques to create a smooth and differentiable representation

054
      of localization errors. Concurrently, for classification, we model the scores of output instances as
055   continuous distributions rather than deterministic values.
056
057   Furthermore, we propose three design guidelines for scoring distributions that enable DAP-loss to
058
      obtain appropriate gradients for gradient descent, thereby enhancing overall performance. We pro-
      vide a theoretical proof demonstrating that DAP-loss generates suitable gradients for model training.
059
      Extensive experiments on the COCO dataset show that replacing the original loss functions with
060
      DAP-loss in the later stages of model training significantly improves performance.
061
062   The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
063
064         1. We conduct a comprehensive analysis of AP metric, clarifying why it is not suitable for
065            direct gradient descent optimization in object detection models;
066
067         2. Building on this analysis, we propose Differentiable Average Precision Loss (DAP-loss),
068
               an approximation method for the AP metric. Additionally, we have designed specifically a
               cost function for the Hungarian matching algorithm to integrate seamlessly with DAP-loss.
069
070
            3. We provide a theoretical proof demonstrating that DAP-loss ensures mathematical conver-
071
               gence and effectiveness in model training. Furthermore, extensive empirical experiments
072            confirm that DAP-loss enhances the performance of end-to-end object detection models.

078   2.1   DETR FOR O BJECT D ETECTION .
079
080   The pioneering work DETR(Carion et al., 2020) employed transformers and set-based prediction to
081   achieve end-to-end object detection. Its simplicity and outstanding performance have led to numer-
082   ous proposed extensions. Deformable DETR(Zhu et al., 2020) introduced a multi-scale deformable
083   self/cross-attention mechanism that selectively focuses on a small number of key sampling points
084
      in the reference bounding boxes. Compared to DETR, Deformable DETR significantly accelerates
      convergence and achieves improved performance. DAB-DETR(Liu et al., 2022) and DN-DETR(Li
085
      et al., 2022) demonstrated that the query formulation in the decoder can significantly impact DETR’s
086
      performance. DINO-DETR(Zhang et al., 2023) achieved shorter training times and better perfor-
087   mance, by addressing the instability issues of the one-to-one matching problem. RT-DETR(Zhao
088   et al., 2024), on the other hand, has extended DETR into the realm of real-time object detection, en-
089   abling broader practical applications. Building on these, this paper integrates DAP-loss with DETR
090   and its variants, further enhancing their detection capabilities.
091
092
093   2.2   AP AS A L OSS FOR O BJECT D ETECTION
094
095   Average Precision (AP), which takes into account both classification and localization tasks, is the
096   most commonly used evaluation metric in object detection. However, due to its non-differentiability
097   and non-convexity, AP cannot be directly used as an optimization objective in object detection. Sev-
098   eral methods have been proposed to tackle the challenge of optimizing AP loss in object detection.
099
      AP-loss(Chen et al., 2019) and its extensions(Pu et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2022) utilize error-driven
      updates, employing Rankloss as a classification loss to indirectly optimize AP. Methods such as
100
      Song et al. (2016); Henderson & Ferrari (2017b) use differentiable approximations of AP as training
101
      losses. Alternatively, Mohapatra et al. (2018) leverages reinforcement learning to improve the origi-
102   nal classification loss starting from AP. Although these methods have achieved some valuable results
103   in optimizing AP loss, they still have limitations. A key issue is that they primarily replace the clas-
104   sification loss with one based on the AP metric, without adequately addressing the localization task.
105   Consequently, these approaches do not directly optimize AP; instead, they rely on regression loss
106   functions and their associated balancing parameters for model training. In contrast, the DAP-loss we
107   propose is a differentiable approximation of AP that can simultaneously optimize both localization
      and classification tasks without the need for additional balancing hyperparameters.

115   3.1   P RELIMINARY
116
117   DETR: Detection Transformers (DETR) passes the input image I through a backbone network
118   and a transformer encoder to obtain a series of enhanced feature embeddings X. These feature
119   embeddings are then combined with a set of N object query embeddings Q and passed through a
120   transformer decoder to produce N output predictions. Finally, DETR performs a one-to-one bipartite
121   matching between the predictions and the ground truth annotations G for bounding boxes and labels,
122
      associating each ground truth annotation with the prediction that has the minimum matching cost.
      Predictions matched to ground truth annotations are classified as positive samples, while unmatched
123
      ones are considered negative. Similarly, our proposed method also classifies samples as positive or
124
      negative based on these matching results.

316

317

318   optimization algorithm due to the batch size’s notable influence on AP calculation. An excessively
319   small batch can cause a significant discrepancy between the estimated and actual AP values. For
320   example, consider an extreme case where our model predicts perfect rankings and localization re-

322   In such a case, both I1 and I2 would individually yield very high AP values. Aggregating image
323   scores within a minibatch helps to avoid this issue. In the next section, we will present experimental

476   5     C ONCLUSION
477
478
      In this paper, we decompose Average Precision (AP) into the product of a localization function and
479
      a classification function, analyzing their non-differentiability and non-convexity. We then employ
480   interpolation and Gaussian-like smoothing techniques to develop a differentiable approximation of
481   AP, termed Differentiable Average Precision (DAP). Furthermore, we extend DAP-loss to the match-
482   ing cost of the Hungarian algorithm, making it suitable for end-to-end detection models. DAP-loss
483   optimizes both localization and classification tasks simultaneously, and effectively balancing these
484   two objectives without the need for hyperparameter tuning. We provide a solid theoretical analysis
485   of the proposed DAP-loss, and experimental results demonstrate that DAP-loss enhances the final
      performance of trained models, although it is not suitable for training from scratch.
