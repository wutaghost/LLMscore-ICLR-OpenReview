009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               We consider the explanation problem of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). Most
012            existing GNN explanation methods identify the most important edges or nodes
013            but fail to consider substructures, which are more important for graph data. One
014            method considering subgraphs tries to search all possible subgraphs and iden-
015            tifies the most significant ones. However, the subgraphs identified may not be
016            recurrent or statistically important for interpretation. This work proposes a novel
017            method, named MotifExplainer, to explain GNNs by identifying important motifs,
018            which are recurrent and statistically significant patterns in graphs. Our proposed
019            motif-based methods can provide better human-understandable explanations than
020
               methods based on nodes, edges, and regular subgraphs. Given an instance graph
               and a pre-trained GNN model, our method first extracts motifs in the graph using
021
               domain-specific motif extraction rules. Then, a motif embedding is encoded by
022
               feeding motifs into the pre-trained GNN. Finally, we employ an attention-based
023            method to identify the most influential motifs as explanations for the prediction re-
024            sults. The empirical studies on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate
025            the effectiveness of our method.
026
027
      Graph neural networks (GNNs) have shown capability in solving various challenging tasks in graph
028
      fields, such as node classification, graph classification, and link prediction. Although many GNNs
029
      models Kipf & Welling (2016); Gao et al. (2018); Xu et al. (2018); Gao & Ji (2019); Liu et al. (2020)
030   have achieved state-of-the-art performances in various tasks, they are still considered black boxes
031   and lack sufficient knowledge to explain them. Inadequate interpretation of GNN decisions severely
032   hinders the applicability of these models in critical decision-making contexts where both predictive
033   performance and interpretability are critical. A good explainer allows us to debate GNN decisions
034   and shows where algorithmic decisions may be biased or discriminated against. In addition, we can
035   apply precise explanations to other scientific research like fragment generation. A fragment library
036   is a key component in drug discovery, and accurate explanations may help its generation.
037   Several methods have been proposed to explain GNNs, divided into instance-level explainers and
038   model-level explainers. Most existing instance-level explainers such as GNNExplainer Ying et al.
039   (2019), PGExplainer Luo et al. (2020), Gem Lin et al. (2021), and ReFine Wang et al. (2021) pro-
040   duce an explanation to every graph instance. These methods explain pre-trained GNNs by identify-
041   ing important edges or nodes but fail to consider substructures, which are more important for graph
042   data. The only method that considers subgraphs is SubgraphX Yuan et al. (2021), which searches all
043   possible subgraphs and identifies the most significant one. However, the subgraphs identified may
044
      not be recurrent or statistically important, which raises an issue on the application of the produced
      explanations. For example, fragment-based drug discovery (FBDD) Erlanson et al. (2004) has been
045
      proven to be powerful for developing potent small-molecule compounds. FBDD is based on frag-
046
      ment libraries, containing fragments or motifs identified as relevant to the target property by domain
047   experts. Using a motif-based GNN explainer, we can directly identify relevant fragments or motifs
048   that are ready to be used when generating drug-like lead compounds in FBDD.
049
050
      In addition, searching and scoring all possible subgraphs is time-consuming and inefficient. We
      claim that using motifs, recurrent and statistically important subgraphs, to explain GNNs can provide
051
      a more intuitive explanation than methods based on nodes, edges, or subgraphs.
052
053   This work proposes a novel GNN explanation method named MotifExplainer, which can identify
      significant motifs to explain an instance graph. In particular, our method first extracts motifs from a

      given graph using domain-specific motif extraction rules based on domain knowledge. Then, motif
055   embeddings of extracted motifs are generated by feeding motifs into the target GNN model. After
056   that, an attention model is employed to select relevant motifs based on attention weights. These

060   and quantitative experiments. The experiments show that our MotifExplainer can generate a better
061   explanation than previous GNN explainers. Furthermore, the efficiency studies demonstrate the
062   efficiency advantage of our methods in terms of a much shorter training and inference time.

067   G = {G1 , G2 , ..., Gi , ..., GN } denotes a graph where V = {v1 , v2 , ..., vi , ...vn } is the node set
068   of the graph and E is the edge set. Gi is associated with a d-dimensional set of node features
069   X = {x1 , x2 , ..., xi , ..., xn }, where xi ∈ Rd is the feature vector of node vi . Without loss of
070   generality, we consider the problem of explaining a GNN-based downstream classification task. For
071   a node classification task, we associate each node vi of a graph G with a label yi , where yi ∈ Y =
072   {l1 , ..., lc } and c is the number of classes. For a graph classification task, each graph Gi is assigned
073   a corresponding label.

      1.2   G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORK E XPLANATIONS
090
      In a GNN explanation task, we are given a pre-trained GNN model, which can be represented by
091   Ψ(·) and its corresponding dataset D. The task is to obtain an explanation model Φ(·) that can
092   provide a fast and accurate explanation for the given GNN model. Most existing GNN explanation
093   approaches can be categorized into two branches: instance-level methods and model-level methods.
094   Instance-level methods can provide an explanation for each input graph, while model-level methods
095   are input-independent and analyze graph patterns without input data. Following previous works Luo
096   et al. (2020); Yuan et al. (2021); Lin et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2021); Bajaj et al. (2021), we focus on
097   instance-level methods with explanations using graph sub-structures. Also, our approach is model-
098   agnostic. In particular, given an input graph, our explanation model can generate a subgraph that
099
      is the most important to the outcomes of a pre-trained GNN on any downstream graph-related task,
      such as graph classification tasks.

129   2.1             F ROM S UBGRAPH TO M OTIF E XPLANATION
130
131   Unlike explanations on models for text and image tasks, a graph has non-grid topology structure
132   information, which needs to be considered in an explanation model. Given an input graph and a
133   trained GNN model, most existing GNN explainers, such as GNNExplainer Ying et al. (2019) and
134   PGExplainer Luo et al. (2020), identify important edges and construct a subgraph containing all
135
      those edges as the explanation of the input graph. However, these models ignore the interactions
      between edges or nodes and implicitly measure the essence of substructures. SubgraphX Yuan
136
      et al. (2021) proposed to employ subgraphs for GNN explanation. It explicitly evaluates subgraphs
137
      and considers the interaction between different substructures. However, it does not use domain
138   knowledge like motif information when generating the subgraphs.
139
140   A motif can be regarded as a simple subgraph of a complex graph, which repeatedly appears in
141
      graphs and is highly related to the function of the graph. Motifs have been extensively studied in
      many fields, like biochemistry, ecology, neurobiology, and engineering Milo et al. (2002); Shen-Orr
142
      et al. (2002); Alon (2007; 2019) and are proved to be important. A subgraph identified without
143
      considering domain knowledge can be ineffective for downstream tasks like fragment library gen-
144   eration in FBDD. Thus, it is desirable to introduce statistically important motif information to a
145   more human-understandable GNN explanation. In addition, subgraph-based explainers like Sub-
146   graphX need to handle a large searching space, which leads to efficiency issues when generating
147   explanations for dense or large scale graphs. In contrast, the number of the extracted motifs can
148   be constrained by well-designed motif extraction rules, which means that using motifs as explana-
149   tions can significantly reduce the search space. Another limitation of SubgraphX is that it needs
150   to pre-determine a maximum number of nodes for its searching space. As the number of nodes in
151   graphs varies greatly, it is hard to set a proper number for searching subgraphs. A large number will
152
      tremendously increase the computational resources, while a small number can limit the power of the
      explainer. To address the limitations of subgraph-based explainers, we propose a novel method that
153
      explicitly select important motifs as an explanation for a given graph. Compared to explainers based
154
      on subgraphs, our method generates explanations with motifs, which are statistically important and
155   more human-understandable.

      2.2             M OTIF E XTRACTION
159
      This section introduces domain-specific motif extraction rules.
160
161   Domain knowledge. When working with data from different domains, motifs are extracted based on
      specific domain knowledge. For example, in biological networks, feed-forward loop, bifan, single-


                                                                                                     3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      input, and multi-input motifs are popular motifs, which have shown to have different properties
163   and functions Alon (2007); Mangan & Alon (2003); Gorochowski et al. (2018). For graphs or
164   networks in the engineering domain, the three-node feedback loop Leite & Wang (2010) and four-
165   node feedback loop motifs Piraveenan et al. (2013) are important in addition to the feed-forward
166   loop and bifan motifs. Motifs have also been shown to be important in computational Chemistry Yu
167   & Gao (2022). The structures of these motifs are illustrated in Appendix 3.
168
      Extraction methods. For molecule datasets, we can use sophisticated decomposition methods like
169   RECAP Lewell et al. (1998) and BRICS Degen et al. (2008) algorithms to extract motifs. For other
170   datasets that do not have mature extraction methods like biological networks and social networks,
171   inspired by related works on graph feature representation learning Yu & Gao (2022); Bouritsas et al.
172   (2022), we propose a general extraction method that only considers cycles and edges as motifs,
173   which can cover most popular network motifs.
174
      In particular, given a graph, we first extract all cycles out of it. Then, all edges that are not inside the
175   cycles are considered motifs. We consider combining cycles with more than two coincident nodes
176   into a motif. Although this method cannot extract complex motifs like single-input and multi-input
177   motifs, it can generate the most important motifs, such as ring structures in biochemical molecules
178   and the feed-forward loop motif. By adopting this simple but general motif extraction method, we
179   can explain a GNN model without any domain knowledge, making our explanation model more
180   applicable. Need to be noted that, even though the motif extraction rule cannot extract single-input
181   and multi-input motifs, these motifs can be implicitly identified by our attention layer. Experiments
182   in the table 1 demonstrate it.
183   Our methods can be easily applied to other domains by changing the motif extraction rules accord-
184   ingly.
185
      Computational graph. We define the computational graph of a given graph based on different
186
      tasks. The computational graph includes all nodes and edges contributing to the prediction. Since
187   most GNNs follow a neighborhood-aggregation scheme, the computational graph usually depends
188   on the architecture of GNNs, such as the number of layers. In graph classification tasks, all nodes
189   and edges contribute to the final prediction. Thus, a graph itself is its computational graph in graph
190   classification tasks. For node classification tasks, a target node’s computational graph is the L-hop
191   subgraph centered on the target node, where L is the number of GNN layers. Here, we only consider
192   motifs in the computational graph since those outside it are irrelevant to the predictions.
193   Motif extraction. Given a graph G, we extract all motifs based on the motif extraction method. If
194   a motif has been extracted from the graph, it is added to a motif list M. After searching the whole
195   graph, there may be edges not in any motif. We regard each of them as a one-edge motif and add
196   them to the motif list to retain the integrity of the graph information. At last, we can obtain the motif
197   list M = [m1 , m2 , . . . , mt ] in G.

      2.4   M OTIF E MBEDDING
217
218   After extracting motifs M from a given graph, we encode the feature representations for each motif.
219   Given a pre-trained GNN model, we split it into two parts: a feature extractor Ψ(·) and a classifier
220   ξ(·). The feature extractor Ψ(·) generates an embedding for the prediction target. In particular,
221   Ψ(·) outputs graph embeddings in graph classification tasks, and outputs node embeddings in node
222   classification tasks. The motif embedding is obtained in a graph classification task by feeding all
223   motif node embeddings into a readout function. While in a node classification task, motif embedding
224
      encodes the influence of the motif on the node embedding of the target node. Thus, we feed the
      target node k and a motif mj ∈ M as a subgraph into the GNN feature extractor Ψ(·) and use the
225
      resulting target node embedding of k as the embedding of the motif. To ensure the connectivity of
226
      the subgraph, we keep edges from the target node to the motif and mask features of irrelevant nodes.
227
228   This section introduces how to generate an explanation for a pre-trained GNN model in a graph
229   classification task. We split the pre-trained GNN model into a feature extractor Ψ(·) and a classifier
230
      ξ(·). Given a graph G, its original graph embedding h is computed as h = Ψ(G). The prediction y
      is computed by y = ξ(h).
231
232   Based on the given graph, our method extracts a motif list from it and generates motif embedding
233   M = [m1 , m2 , . . . , mt ] using the pre-trained feature extractor Ψ(·). Since the original graph em-
234   bedding is directly related to the predictions, we identify the most important motifs by investigating
235   relationships between the original graph embedding and motif embeddings. To this end, we em-
236
      ploy an attention layer, which uses the original graph embedding h = Ψ(G) as query and motif
      embedding M as keys and values. The output of the attention layer is considered as a new graph
237
      embedding h′ . We interpret the attention scores as the strengths of relationships between the pre-
238
      diction and motifs. Thus, highly relevant motifs will contribute more to the new graph embedding.
239   By feeding the new graph embedding h′ into the pre-trained graph classifier ξ(·), a new prediction
240   y ′ = ξ(h′ ) is obtained. The loss based on y and y ′ evaluates the contribution of selected motifs
241   to the final prediction, which trains the attention layer such that important motifs are selected to
242   produce similar predictions to the original graph embedding. Formally, this explanation process can
243   be represented as

      4   R ELATED W ORK
495
496
      The research on GNN explainability is mainly divided into two categories: instance-level expla-
497
      nation and model-level explanation. Instance-level GNN explanation can also be divided into four
498
      directions, namely gradients/features-based methods, surrogate methods, decomposition methods,
499   and perturbation-based methods. Gradients/features-based methods use gradients or hidden feature
500   map values as the approximations of an importance score of an input. Recently, several methods
501   have been employed to explain GNNs like SA Baldassarre & Azizpour (2019), Guided-BP Bal-
502   dassarre & Azizpour (2019), CAM Pope et al. (2019), Grad-CAM Pope et al. (2019). The main
503   difference between these methods is the process of gradient back-propagation and how different
504   hidden feature maps are combined. The basic idea of surrogate methods is using a simple and ex-
505   plainable surrogate model to approximate the predictions of GNNs. Several methods have been
506   introduced recently, such as GraphLime Huang et al. (2020), RelEx Zhang et al. (2021), and PGM-
507
      Explainer Vu & Thai (2020). Decomposition methods like LRP Baldassarre & Azizpour (2019),
      Excitation BP Pope et al. (2019), GNN-LRP Schnake et al. (2020) and DEGREE Feng et al. (2021)
508
      measure the importance of input features by decomposing original predictions into several terms.
509
      The last method is the perturbation-based method. Along this direction, GNNExplainer Ying et al.
510   (2019) learns soft masks for edges and node features to generate an explanation via mask optimiza-
511   tion. PGExplainer Luo et al. (2020) learns approximated discrete masks for edges by using domain
512   knowledge. GraphMask Schlichtkrull et al. (2020) proposes a method for explaining the edge impor-
513   tance by generating an edge mask for each GNN layer. ZORRO Funke et al. (2020) studies discrete
514   masks to select significant input nodes and node features. Causal Screening Wang et al. (2020) em-
515   ploys the causal attribution of different edges in an input graph as an explanation. SubgraphX Yuan
516   et al. (2021) employs the Monte Carlo Tree Search algorithm to search possible subgraphs and uses
517   the Shapley value to measure the importance of subgraphs and choose a subgraph as the explanation.
518
      ReFine Wang et al. (2021) proposes an idea of generating multi-grained explanations. There are also
      some reinforcement learning based explainers Shan et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022). Model-level
519
      explanation methods in the field are currently under-researched, with only a few studies addressing
520
      this issue. These methods can be divided into two categories. The first is concept-based methods.
521   GCExplainer Magister et al. (2021) incorporates concepts into GNN explanations. PAGE Shin et al.
522   (2022) discovers propotypes as explanations. GLGExplainer Azzolin et al. (2022) adopts prototype
523   learning to identify data prototypes. GCNeuron Xuanyuan et al. (2023), employs human-defined
524   rule in natural language and use compositional concepts with the highest scores for global expla-
525   nations. The second category is generation-based methods. XGNN Yuan et al. (2020a) uses deep
526   reinforcement learning to generate explanation graphs node by node. GNNInterpreter Wang & Shen
527   (2022), alternatively, learns a probabilistic model that identifies the most discriminative graph pat-
528   terns for explanations.
529
530
      5   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      This work proposes a novel model-agnostic motif-based GNN explainer to explain GNNs by iden-
533
      tifying important motifs, which are recurrent and statistically significant patterns in graphs. Our
534   proposed motif-based methods can provide better human-understandable explanations than meth-
535   ods based on nodes, edges, and regular subgraphs. Given a graph, We first extract motifs from a
536   graph using motif extraction rules based on domain knowledge. Then, motif embedding for each
537   motif is generated using the feature extractor from a pre-trained GNN. After that, we train an at-
538   tention model to select the most relevant motifs based on attention weights and use these selected
539   motifs as an explanation for the input graph. Experimental results show that our MotifExplainer can
      significantly improve explanation performances from quantitative and qualitative aspects.
