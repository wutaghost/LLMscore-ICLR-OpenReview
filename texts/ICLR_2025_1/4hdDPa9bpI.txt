011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Understanding and predicting the time-dependent dynamics of complex systems
014            governed by non-linear partial differential equations (PDEs), with varying parame-
015            ters and domains, is a difficult problem that is motivated by applications in many
016
               fields. We introduce a novel family of neural operators based on a Graph Fourier
               Neural Kernel (G-FuNK), for learning solution generators of nonlinear PDEs with
017
               varying coefficients, across multiple domains, for which the highest-order term in
018
               the PDE is diffusive. G-FuNKs are constructed by combining components that are
019            parameter- and domain-adapted, with others that are not. The latter components are
020            learned from training data, using a variation of Fourier Neural Operators, and are
021            transferred directly across parameters and domains. The former, parameter- and
022            domain-adapted components are constructed as soon as a parameter and a domain
023            on which the PDE needs to be solved are given. They are obtained by constructing
024            a weighted graph on the (discretized) domain, with weights chosen so that the
025            Laplacian on that weighted graph approximates the highest order, diffusive term in
026            the generator of the PDE, which is parameter- and domain-specific, and satisfies the
027
               boundary conditions. This approach proves to be a natural way to embed geometric
               and directionally-dependent information about the domains, allowing for improved
028
               generalization to new test domains without need for retraining. Finally, we equip G-
029
               FuNK with an integrated ordinary differential equation (ODE) solver to enable the
030            temporal evolution of the system’s state. Our experiments demonstrate G-FuNK’s
031            ability to accurately approximate heat, reaction diffusion, and cardiac electrophysi-
032            ology equations on multiple geometries and varying anisotropic diffusivity fields.
033            We achieve low relative errors on unseen domains and fiber fields, significantly
034            speeding up prediction capabilities compared to traditional finite-element solvers.

039   Neural Operators for PDEs In scientific machine learning, data-driven deep learning methods
040
      aim at predicting the solutions of partial differential equations (PDEs), avoiding the computationally
      expensive numerical integration methods needed for large-scale simulations. This is especially
041
      beneficial for applications like domain optimization and precision medicine, where multiple PDEs
042
      need to be solved for varying parameters or domains, requiring significant computational resources.
043   Neural operators learn mappings between high-dimensional function spaces, allowing them to
044   generalize across a family of PDEs without retraining for varying parameters or conditions Kovachki
045   et al. (2023). Mathematically, a neural operator Nθ is defined as a mapping from an input domain
046   function space A(Ωα ; Rda ) to an output target function space U(Ωα ; Rdu ), represented as
047
048                                    Nθ : A(Ωα ; Rda ) → U(Ωα ; Rdu ),                                (1)

050   represents the spatial domain on which the functions are defined and α ∈ A denotes the shape of
051   the domain. The dimensions da and du denote the respective sizes of the input and output function
052   spaces, often subspaces of Sobolev spaces. Neural operators can be applied to various problems
053   such as those described by continuous functions C(Ω; Rda ) or Sobolev spaces H s (Ω; Rda ) for some
      s ≥ 0. The neural operator Nθ is an approximation of a true target operator N (e.g., the solution

091   Reduced Models and Homogenization In fact, we are very much interested in situations where
092   the “true” system is driven by equations more general than equation 2, with many more variables,
093   possibly evolving at multiple time scales; this will be the case in examples 2 and 3, where besides the
094   spatial variables and the unknown action potential u, there are many other quantities v evolving in
095   space and time (e.g. modeling ionic concentrations that affect the evolution of u, and these are driven
096
      by a large system of ODEs). In these situations, given observations from such systems, our estimated
      equation 3 can be viewed as performing dimension reduction on the variables (e.g. by keeping only
097
      the spatial variables) and homogenization, obtaining an effective equation for u, without tracking the
098
      evolution of the other quantities v.

      Related Works Several studies have demonstrated the ability of different types of neural networks
101
      to approximate nonlinear operators with strong theoretical guarantees (e.g., PDE solution operator)
102   Chen & Chen (1995); Lu et al. (2021); Kovachki et al. (2021). Fourier Neural Operators (FNOs) and
103   Deep Operator Networks (DeepONets) and their variants are among the most popular computational
104   frameworks under the umbrella of neural operators Lu et al. (2021); Li et al. (2020a). Vanilla
105   DeepONets, however, are restricted to a fixed grid and resolution, and thus do not generalize well on
106   unseen domains. Yin et al. (2024) propose a work-around spatial transformations of multiple domains
107   to a universal domain where DeepONets can be used to learn a latent operator, the evaluation of
      which can be mapped back to the target domains via the inverse for evaluation. FNOs, while robust


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      to variations in grid resolution, require regularly spaced square grids to perform FFT Li et al. (2020a).
109   As an improvement, Li et al. (2023) propose a Geometry-Aware Fourier Neural Operator (Geo-FNO),
110   which learns an additional deformation step to transform irregular grids, achieving greater accuracy
111   than interpolating the solution on a uniform grid. However, they explore only a relatively small
112   subset of possible transformations and do not investigate generalization performance on differently
113   shaped grids. For large-scale 3D PDEs, Li et al. (2024) also propose a more general way of adapting
114   to arbitrary domains and irregular grids with Geometry Informed Neural Operators (GINO) which
115   incorporates information about the geometry into the learning process, however, they mention that the
116   trained model is limited to certain family of shapes and to generalize well, abundant, geometrically
117
      varying training samples are necessary, a luxury that is not available in real world settings such as
      computational medicine. Several works have also shown promise with regards to predicting full
118
      solution trajectories over a time interval, but typically do not carry the same generalizability over
119
      multiple domains as the aforementioned works Chen et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024); He et al.
120   (2024); Regazzoni et al. (2024).
121
122   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are a versatile class of neural network architectures designed to
123
      work directly with graph-structured data. These networks can make predictions at the level of nodes,
      edges, or entire graphs, typically utilizing a method known as message passing Gilmer et al. (2020).
124
      In this method, neighboring nodes exchange messages, and the aggregated information from these
125
      messages is used to update the states of subsequent nodes. GNNs are particularly well-suited for
126   irregular grid data and can be effectively applied to training data generated by finite-element method
127   simulations (considered ground truth). Li et al. (2020b) proposed a message-passing operator (using
128   edge-conditioned graph convolutions Simonovsky & Komodakis (2017)) to learn solution operators
129   for PDEs. Iakolev et al. also utilized a message-passing scheme to learn isotropic PDEs Iakovlev et al.
130   (2020). Other studies have considered learning time-dependent PDEs using graph-based approaches
131   Li et al. (2020c); Behmanesh et al. (2023); Pilva & Zareei (2022), but none of these works combine
132   learning with both varying domains and varying parameters in the PDE. Furthermore, the message-
133   passing frameworks they employed in these works involve edge aggregation schemes that limit the
134
      learning of direction-dependent information, which is crucial in the case, for example, of anisotropic
      diffusions. Finally, several studies have shown that spectral GNNs possess superior expressiveness
135
      and interpretability, capturing global information more effectively than spatial GNNs Bo et al. (2023);
136
      Defferrard et al. (2016); Wang & Zhang (2022); Yang et al. (2022).

139   Contributions We propose a new family of neural operators consisting of novel Graph Fourier
140   Neural Kernel (G-FuNK) layers for learning generators of solutions to time-dependent PDEs with
141
      varying coefficients across multiple diffeomorphic domains as in equation (2). This method combines
      ideas from both GNNs and FNOs: it leverages the spectral domain of graphs, unlike GNNs that
142
      rely on localized message passing, and is suitable on general graphs rather than relying on grids, as
143
      FNOs. G-FuNK employs the Graph Fourier Transform (GFT) to convert functions on graphs into
144   functions on the graph spectral domain. This facilitates global information integration across the
145   entire graph, allowing G-FuNK to capture both local and global dependencies effectively, as FNOs
146   do in the classical Euclidean grid setting.
147
      G-FuNK integrates components that are both parameter- and domain-adapted with others that are not.
148
      The non-adapted components are learned from training data, and can be directly transferred across
149
      different parameters and domains, independent of the mesh resolution or the time step size. The
150   adapted components are constructed for specific parameters and domains by forming a parameter-
151   dependent, tailored weighted graph on the discretized domain, such that the corresponding graph
152   Laplacian approximates the highest-order diffusive term in the PDE with those specific values of the
153   parameter, on that domain.
154
      We remark that here we do wish to predict entire trajectories of temporal dynamics on [0, T ], which
155
      is difficult as inaccuracies can lead to dramatic accumulation of errors, depending on the PDE.
156   Many existing methods Li et al. (2020a;b;c) are either restricted by design on learning the map
157   from initial conditions (or several observation points) to a solution at a fixed time t, or while they
158   could in principle generate solutions at all times, experiments in this setting are not reported in the
159   corresponding papers. With this goal in mind, our framework is equipped with an integrated ODE
160   solver to enable generating the temporal evolution of the dynamics of the system. This allows for
161   efficient, mesh-independent prediction of the system’s solutions from new initial conditions, with
      new values of the parameter K, on a new domain Ωα . To our knowledge, there is no current neural

162
      operator framework which naturally handles directionally dependent information of anisotropic
163   domains while predicting time-evolving trajectories of the solution operator on multiple geometries.
164
165   We demonstrate G-FuNK’s capabilities by first considering the basic example of the anisotropic heat
166
      equation on the square, where the tensor field K(x) reflects the directional dependence of diffusivity.
      We then move to nonlinear, more complex models of reaction-diffusion equations for cardiac electro-
167
      physiology (EP), first on families of rectangular domains, then on 3D geometries representing the
168
      outer surfaces of real human left atrial (LA) chambers from computed tomography (CT) data. The
169   PDE operators on the patient-specific LA chambers involve effects from high-dimensional coupled
170   ODEs. Our method accurately captures the complex dynamics of the transmembrane potential on
171   varying geometries and anisotropic diffusivity (fiber) fields. Our experiments show that G-FuNK
172   can significantly speed up patient-specific cardiac electrophysiology simulations, which are used
173   for making real-time quantitatively informed clinical decisions Boyle et al. (2019); Trayanova et al.
174   (2024), achieving low relative errors on unseen domains.

      Mesh-Independence of G-FuNK Although the proposed framework involves mesh-dependent
325   steps, such as forming a weighted graph on the discretized domain, the method maintains a high
326   degree of mesh-independence. More precisely, as the number of points increases, the Laplacian we
327   construct approaches its limit, allowing our framework to effectively handle finer discretizations.
328   Moreover, there are established methods for interpolating eigenfunctions to an underlying continuous
329   domain Coifman et al. (2005), which further supports the transition from discrete to continuous
330   representations. This characteristic is crucial, as it sheds light on the ability of our method to
331   transition from discrete approximations to continuous domains as the mesh size approaches zero. Our
332   framework’s capability to interpolate and generalize across varying mesh sizes ensures that it remains
333
      robust and accurate in capturing the underlying dynamics of PDEs in the limit of fine discretizations.

473   Discussion Altogether, G-FuNK was adaptable to all the examples discussed above with low
474   relative error. The results for the various tasks are listed in Table 1. G-FuNK performed well on all
475   examples with a fairly small amount of learnable parameters compared to FNOs and GeoFNOs Li
476
      et al. (2020a; 2024).
477   Message-passing GNNs aggregate information at the nodes, and as expected are unable to accurately
478   handle directionally dependent information (varying diffusive fields). We notice that when we
479   incorporate edge weights, as defined in equation (8), into the GNN model for the Heat equation
480   example, the results improve and the model requires much fewer parameters. Therefore, for all
481   comparisons following, we incorporated the edge weights as an input into the different networks.
482   Prior works such as FNO and GeoFNO, with the code provided, do not present results or compatibility
483   for learning full trajectories of time-evolving processes, where errors can accumulate due to spatial
484   misalignment of predictions Li et al. (2020a; 2023). To compare with FNO and GeoFNO, we
485   modified the approaches to be compatible with the same neural ODE solver we use for time-dependent
      predictions with G-FuNK. For the heat example, it should be noted that G-FuNK was able to learn the


                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      effects of anisotropic diffusion from only the eigenvectors. The other networks were given the primary
487   diffusive vector as inputs at each sample point which has less meaning outside of planar geometries.
488   In the 2D reaction-diffusion problem with random rectangles, both GeoFNO and G-FuNK perform
489   similarly on the test set, as expected. In regular rectangular domains, the graph Fourier and fast
490   Fourier bases converge, implying that FNO and GeoFNO are subsets of G-FuNK equipped neural
491   operators, corroborated by the similar performance. For training in this example, we still considered
492   anisotropy, but with all the fiber fields strictly pointing in the horizontal axis. We show that G-FuNK
493   and GeoFNO perform quite similarly on test domains with the same directionality presented in the
494   fiber fields as the training samples. However, when the test set domains and fiber fields were rotated
495
      by 90 degrees, GeoFNO’s performance significantly degraded, with a relative ℓ2 error of 0.5681, as
      shown in Table 1 and Figure 7 shown in the Appendix A.2.4. In contrast, G-FuNK, trained using only
496
      the initial condition and graph eigenpairs, remained rotation-invariant to the unseen fiber orientation.
497
498   For the cardiac EP example, we do not show results with GeoFNO since the complex 3D geometries
499   of the human left atrium with five holes cannot be mapped diffeomorphically to a cube or torus
500   where the Fourier transform is available. For this example, our G-FuNK equipped operator learning
501
      framework is able to provide respectable predictions, where a majority of the relative ℓ2 error is
      due a small lag in the wavefront of about 1.62 ms and the wavefront being slightly more diffusive.
502
      This shift resulted in a higher ℓ2 error but the cross-correlation is 0.941 implying that the prediction
503
      is correctly shaped with a small lag. Increasing the number of geometries in the training set will
504   decrease this error as we only use 24 geometries for training since developing the geometries and
505   simulations can take over 1 day per patient.
506
507
      Our method predicts entire trajectories in under 1 second, significantly outperforming traditional
      numerical methods. For example, cardiac EP simulations take on average 13.2 (min: 8.12, max 21.97)
508
      minutes on 12 CPU cores for one given set of initial conditions. To make quantitatively informed
509
      clinical predictions, one must perform comprehensive parameter sweeps to identify optimal treatment
510   strategies for a given patient, which could take hours to days with the finite element approach. These
511   trajectories are predicted by the G-FuNK equipped operator learning framework in less than 1 second.
512   The inclusion of more geometries in the 3D cardiac EP examples is of course expected to lead to
513   lower error. Additionally, small changes in the eigenvalues across domains can lead to mismatches
514   in the order of the eigenvalues between geometries which could be a source in the reported error:
515   this can be avoided with an eigenvector-matching procedure, which will be the subject of future
516   investigations. At the moment, the predictions of our model are restricted to test data from the same
517   sample distribution as the training data. Out-of-distribution predictions are proviced in Appendix
518
      A.2.3. We believe that an eigenvalue matching procedure as mentioned above may improve the ability
      of G-FuNK’s ability as an extrapolator.

521   4    C ONCLUSION
522
523   Data-driven methods for PDEs are highly dependent on the quality and quantity of the data provided.
524   In this work, we present a new family of neural operators based on our novel Graph Fourier Neural
525   Kernel (G-FuNK) layers which combine GNNs and FNOs. We demonstrate that our framework
526   achieves high accuracy on pedagogical examples of the anisotropic 2D heat and reaction-diffusion
527
      equations. Even in the relatively data-starved setting of cardiac EP considered here, the accuracy of
      G-FuNK is respectable. Altogether, the proposed method leverages the expressiveness and versatility
528
      of learning in the graph spectral domain. Our G-FuNK-equipped operator learning framework
529
      performs well on unstructured data to efficiently and accurately predict the temporal dynamics of
530   complex systems from a single initial condition with varying anisotropic diffusion tensor fields,
531   highly nonlinear reaction terms, and different domains using real cardiac patient data.
