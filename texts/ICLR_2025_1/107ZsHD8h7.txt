009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Mathematical optimization is fundamental to decision-making across diverse do-
012            mains, from operations research to healthcare. Yet, translating real-world prob-
013            lems into optimization models remains a formidable challenge, often demanding
014            specialized expertise. This paper formally introduces the concept of autoformu-
015            lation—an automated approach to creating optimization models from natural lan-
016            guage descriptions for commercial solvers. We identify the three core challenges
017            of autoformulation: (1) defining the vast, problem-dependent hypothesis space,
018            (2) efficiently searching this space under uncertainty, and (3) evaluating formu-
019
               lation correctness (ensuring a formulation accurately represents the problem). To
               address these challenges, we introduce a novel method leveraging Large Language
020
               Models (LLMs) within a Monte-Carlo Tree Search framework. This approach
021
               systematically explores the space of possible formulations by exploiting the hier-
022            archical nature of optimization modeling. LLMs serve two key roles: as dynamic
023            formulation hypothesis generators and as evaluators of formulation correctness.
024            To enhance search efficiency, we introduce a pruning technique to remove triv-
025            ially equivalent formulations. Empirical evaluations across benchmarks contain-
026            ing linear and mixed-integer programming problems demonstrate our method’s
027            superior performance. Additionally, we observe significant efficiency gains from
028            employing LLMs for correctness evaluation and from our pruning techniques.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Mathematical optimization has long been a cornerstone of decision-making processes across various
034   domains, from supply chain management (Bramel & Simchi-Levi, 1997) and healthcare resource al-
035   location (Delgado et al., 2022) to portfolio optimization (Mokhtar et al., 2014). These problems are
036   characterized by maximizing an objective function subject to constraints (Williams, 2013). Tradi-
037   tionally, optimization modeling follows a three-step process: ▶ gathering problem requirements,
038   typically expressed in unstructured formats and domain terminology; ▶ formulating these require-
039
      ments into a formal mathematical model, including variables, constraints, and objective functions;
      ▶ implementing the model computationally using specialized modeling language for solution using
040
      commercial solvers. These solvers (e.g. Gurobi (Gurobi Optimization, LLC, 2024), CPLEX (Cplex,
041
      2009)), leverage sophisticated algorithms to tractably optimize a wide array of problems to global
042   optimality, including convex problems, (e.g. linear and quadratic programs), and certain non-convex
043   problems (e.g. mixed-integer linear programs) (Boyd & Vandenberghe, 2004).
044
045
      Autoformulation. Despite major advances in solving algorithms over the past decades, the process
      of formulating optimization models still relies largely on human expertise (Karmarkar, 1984). Aut-
046
      oformulation aims to address this bottleneck by automating the formulation process, bridging the
047
      gap between problem descriptions and formal mathematical models. This approach enhances time
048   and cost efficiency while enabling access for users without deep optimization expertise. At its core,
049   autoformulation can be conceptualized as a search for an optimal formulation within a vast hypothe-
050   sis space. However, this process is complicated by several challenges. For one, the hypothesis space
051   is highly problem-dependent, making it difficult to manually specify. Second, efficiently searching
052   through this hypothesis space requires methods that can balance exploitation and exploration, par-
053   ticularly given the uncertainty in the optimal formulation. Lastly, the search should be guided by
      a signal of formulation correctness, which is itself an ill-posed problem. While solvers can indi-

      cate optimality gap and computational efficiency, evaluating that the formulated model accurately
055   represents the intended real-world problem remains a distinct challenge.
056
057   A keen insight of this work is that recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) (Brown,
058
      2020; Chowdhery et al., 2023) have opened new avenues for autoformulation. LLMs contribute
      several crucial capabilities to this process: contextual understanding for nuanced interpretation of
059
      problem descriptions, vast domain knowledge to incorporate relevant modeling techniques, and rea-
060
      soning capabilities to support approximate evaluation of formulation correctness. Recent works
061   (Ramamonjison et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2023; AhmadiTeshnizi et al., 2024) have demonstrated the
062   promising potential of LLMs in autoformulation, laying important groundwork in this field. Build-
063   ing upon these contributions, our work focuses on developing techniques for efficient, systematic
064   exploration and introducing novel methods for evaluating formulation correctness.
065
      Key considerations. We conceptualize autoformulation as a search problem, leveraging optimiza-
066
      tion modeling’s inherent hierarchical structure to efficiently explore the vast hypothesis space,
067   guided by feedback on formulation correctness. We decompose optimization modeling into hi-
068   erarchical components and introduce a Monte-Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) method to incrementally
069   explore each component’s formulation space (Coulom, 2006). We employ LLMs for two specialized
070   roles: (1) as context-dependent hypothesis generators to produce component formulations at each
071   level of the tree search; (2) as evaluators of formulation correctness, which is combined with solver-
072   returned data to obtain a reward signal to guide exploration. To further improve search efficiency,
073   we introduce a pruning technique using Satisfiability Modulo Theories solvers, eliminating redun-
074   dant hypotheses that are syntactically different yet functionally equivalent (Barrett & Tinelli, 2018).
075
      Empirically, we observed that this significantly reduces search efforts expended on equivalent for-
      mulations. Our algorithm systematically explores the hypothesis space through multiple iterations,
076
      producing a set of functionally distinct models, each scored using our evaluation function.

089   Optimization modeling seeks to minimize an objective function subject to specific constraints on
090   decision variables (Dantzig, 1990). The mathematical model can be expressed in a general form:
091
092                                 Minimize f (x)
093                                 subject to gi (x) ≤ 0, i = 1, . . . , I,                             (1)
094                                            hj (x) = 0, j = 1, . . . , J.
095
096   Here x ∈ X represents the vector of decision variables, and X ⊆ Rℓ × Zk is the domain
097   of the problem for which the objective and constraints functions are all defined. Furthermore,
098   f : X → R is the objective function to be minimized, gi : X → R are inequality constraints,
099   hj : X → R are equality constraints, and I and J are the numbers of inequality and equality con-
100   straints respectively. The feasible region is the set of all possible points that satisfy the problem
101   constraints: {x ∈ X | gi (x) ≤ 0, ∀i ∈ [I], hj (x) = 0, ∀j ∈ [J]}.
102   Convex problems. An optimization problem is convex if f and gi ∀i ∈ [I] are convex, and
103   hj ∀ j ∈ [J] are affine. Convexity is significant as any local optimum of a convex problem is
104   globally optimal, and specialized solvers can efficiently solve convex problems to global optimal-
105   ity using advanced algorithms (e.g., Gurobi (Gurobi Optimization, LLC, 2024), CVXPY (Diamond
106   & Boyd, 2016)). For this reason, convexity is the widely accepted watershed between “easy” and
107   “hard” problems. Before utilizing these solvers, the mathematical models are first represented in
      code as computational models, which are then passed to the solvers for optimization.

      2.1       AUTOFORMULATION : P ROBLEM D EFINITION
127
128   Boyd & Vandenberghe (2004) aptly recognized that “the challenge, and art, in using convex op-
129   timization is in recognizing and formulating the problem. Once this formulation is done, solving
130   the problem is ... (almost) technology”. While solver technology has significantly matured, the
131   process of formulating optimization models remains largely human expertise driven. Building on
132   this insight, we define autoformulation as the automated process of transforming natural language
133
      descriptions of real-world problems into formal optimization models. This process aims to bridge
      the gap between human-readable problem statements and computational models suitable for opti-
134
      mization solvers, thus automating the “challenge and art” of problem formulation.
135
136      Formal Definition
137    Let D represent the space of natural language problem descriptions. Additionally, let M and C
138    represent the space of all possible mathematical formulations and space of all possible computa-
139    tional models of optimization problems. Specifically, each c ∈ C is a computational representa-
140
       tion (e.g. Python code), which includes choice of solving algorithm and its configurations. Given
       a problem d ∈ D, autoformulation involves two transformations:
141
142
       1. Mathematical Formulation pϕ : D → P (M): Transforming problem description into a
           precise mathematical formulation. Here, P (·) represents the space of probability distributions.
143
144
       2. Computational Representation pψ : M → P (C): Converting the mathematical formula-
           tion into computational formats suitable for solvers. This includes encoding the model in a
145
           programming framework and specifying or configuring an appropriate solving algorithm.
146
       Autoformulator. Here, pϕ and pψ are models of each transformation, with ϕ and ψ their respec-
147
       tive parameters. The complete autoformulation process can thus be represented as inferring the
148
       joint distribution pϕ,ψ (m, c | d) = pψ (c | m) · pϕ (m | d). We refer to any algorithm designed for
149    the autoformulation problem as an autoformulator.
150    Objective. For a given problem d, autoformulation aims to find optimal mathematical and com-
151    putational formulations that maximize an evaluation measure Q(·) :
152
                                     (m∗ , c∗ ) ∈ arg maxm∈M,c∈C Q(m, c; d)                             (2)
153
154
       Evaluation criteria. Here, Q assesses the quality of (m, c) relative to d, considering factors such
       as correctness, solvability, and efficiency. The quality of a formulation is primarily evaluated
155
       based on formulation correctness—how accurately it reflects the problem description. Given
156
       that a formulation is correct, two additional criteria come into play. Optimality gap: the gap
157    between the value of the objective function at the solution and the optimal value. For example,
158    convex problems and certain non-convex problems like mixed-integer linear programs (MILPs)
159    can be solved efficiently to global optimality (i.e. zero optimality gap). Computational effi-
160    ciency: evaluating the resource requirements and solution time of a particular model. These latter
161    two criteria are only meaningful for formulations that correctly capture the problem description.

163       Challenges. A closer scrutiny of Eq. (2) reveals a few key challenges:
164       [C1] Problem-dependent hypothesis space: Given a problem d, we define the problem-
165            dependent hypothesis space of an autoformulator as Hϕ,ψ (d).a This space encompasses
166            all plausible formulations, including correct, incorrect, or trivially redundant formulations.
167            Manually specifying this hypothesis is highly challenging, due to complex interdependen-
168
               cies, the vast number of formulations, and the problem-specific nature of the space.
169
          [C2] Efficient search under uncertainty: Efficiently navigating the problem-dependent hypoth-
               esis space is challenging, as performant formulations (e.g. efficient and globally optimal)
170
               can be sparse. This search involves managing two key sources of uncertainty: a) modeling
171
               decisions: uncertainty in the optimal way to formulate the problem, b) problem ambigu-
172            ity: uncertainty due to ambiguous requirements such as implicit or ‘common-sense’ con-
173            straints (e.g. non-negativity or integer constraints for individual resources). An additional
174            complexity is trivial model equivalence—formulations that are identical but have minor
175            syntactic differences (e.g. functions 2x + 3y and 3y + 2x). Note, we term these ‘trivial’
176            to distinguish this type of equivalence from mathematically equivalent reformulation such
177            as converting a non-convex constraint to convex. Exploring these trivial variations ineffi-
178            ciently can lead to overlooking more diverse and potentially valuable formulations.
179       [C3] Model evaluation: While solvers can assess solvability and computational efficiency, they
180            cannot evaluate formulation correctness—whether the model accurately represents all re-
181
               quirements in the problem description. This lack of feedback on semantic correctness sig-
               nificantly complicates the search process, as an efficient solution to an incorrectly formu-
182
               lated problem is ultimately invalid.

186   A few observations. Our definition of both steps as probabilistic distributions includes deterministic
187   mappings as a special case (i.e. Dirac delta distributions). This formalism recognizes the inherent
188   uncertainty in autoformulation, stemming from language ambiguity, domain knowledge limitations,
189   and variability in modeling decisions. Additionally, the second transformation is assumed to be in-
190
      dependent of the problem description d. In general, the first step (formulating mathematical models,
      pϕ ) presents a significantly greater challenge than the second step (creating computational models,
191
      pψ ). The former requires deep domain understanding, the ability to abstract real-world complexities
192
      into mathematical constructs, and creativity in choosing effective problem formulations. In contrast,
193   formulating computational models often follows more standardized patterns, with several commer-
194   cial packages already offering automation in translating mathematical models into solver-compatible
195   code (Fourer et al., 1990). However, the second transformation can present distinct challenges that
196   introduce uncertainty, most notably through problem-specific choices and configurations for solvers.

      takes initial steps towards addressing them. In the scope of this work, our method primarily focuses

      Overview. At a high level, our method integrates Large Language Models (LLMs) within a Monte-
217   Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) framework, founded on two key insights: 1) leveraging the inherent
218   hierarchical structure of optimization modeling by decomposing the autoformulation process into
219   distinct stages, enabling systematic exploration using a tailored MCTS algorithm, and 2) employing
220   LLMs in two specialized roles: as dynamic hypothesis generators in each formulation stage, and as
221   approximate evaluators of formulation correctness. This approach harnesses LLMs’ extensive do-
222   main knowledge and contextual understanding to implicitly create and search a problem-dependent
223   hypothesis space. This strategy bypasses the need for manual specification of the search space,
224   which would be intractable due to the vast number of possible formulations. Furthermore, it utilizes
225
      LLMs’ reasoning capability to evaluate formulation correctness against problem descriptions, pro-
      viding an approximate yet meaningful signal to guide the search process. Note: In the interest of
226
      space, we present detailed information about all prompts used in the algorithm in App. A, providing
227
      only high-level details in the following subsections.

      3.1     S TRUCTURED D ECOMPOSITION OF AUTOFORMULATION
230
231   Optimization modeling is inherently complex, involving multiple interconnected components. To
232   manage this complexity and improve search efficiency, we propose a decomposition of the formu-
233   lation process. This approach allows us to sequentially explore each model component rather than
234   searching for entire formulations at once, potentially leading to more efficient search.
235   Specifically, we structurally decompose the autoformulation process into four distinct stages, each
236   represented by mi . The complete mathematical formulation is defined as m = ⊕4i mi , where ⊕
237   denotes the composition of model components: m1 —parameters and decision variables, m2 —
238   objective function, m3 —equality constraints, and m4 —inequality constraints. Given a problem
239   description d, the joint distribution pϕ,ψ (c, m | d) is decomposed hierarchically:
240                                                            Q4
                                pϕ,ψ (c, m | d) = pψ (c | m) i=1 pϕ (mi | m<i , d)                    (3)
241
242   Here, pϕ (mi | m<i , d) represents the sequential nature of mathematical formulation, where each
243   component mi depends on the partial formulation m<i = ⊕i−1     j=0 mj (with m0 = ∅) and the problem
244   description d. Additionally, the term pψ (c | m) represents the computational model’s dependency on
245   the completed mathematical formulation.

247   3.2     MCTS-BASED AUTOFORMULATOR
248
      Having established a structured decomposition of the autoformulation process, we now address the
249
      challenge of efficiently navigating this hierarchical space. We employ an MCTS-based algorithm,
250
      which is particularly well-suited for exploring complex, hierarchical search spaces (Coulom, 2006).
251   Our MCTS constructs a search tree of depth 4 to explore possible formulations, where each of the
252   four levels corresponds to a component in our structured decomposition (m1 to m4 ). Nodes in this
253   tree contain component formulations, and a complete formulation is represented by a path from the
254   root to a terminal node, with each path yielding a unique formulation.
255
       The MCTS algorithm iteratively builds the search tree through four key steps: ▶ selection, ▶ ex-
256
       pansion, ▶ evaluation, and ▶ backpropagation. For notational clarity, we denote a tree node as n
257    and any of its child nodes as nchild ∈ Child(n), where Child(n) is the set of all child nodes of n.
258    We use ⃗n to represent the partial formulation contained in the path from root to node n. For instance,
259   ⃗n for a node of depth 2 is the partial formulation containing the parameters, decision variables, and
260    the objective function. Terminal nodes are denoted as nt .

      3.2.3   E VALUATION
325
326   After expanding a node, each newly created child node undergoes an initial evaluation to estimate
327   its value, guiding subsequent selection. This evaluation is non-trivial, as assessing the correctness
328   of a partial formulation with respect to the original problem description is complex. Our method
329   employs an LLM-based ranking evaluation for each set of child nodes to provide more informed ini-
330   tial evaluations. Specifically, we rank the partial formulation from root to each child node, namely
331   {⃗nchild | nchild ∈ Child(n)}. These ranks are then center-normalized to [0, 1], with the middle
332
      rank centered at 0.5. We denote this normalized score s(⃗nchild ), which is used to initialize the
      child node’s value Vprior (nchild ) ← s(⃗nchild ). We note that this approach diverges from traditional
333
      MCTS, which often uses uniform priors for expanded nodes. Here, the LLM assesses the formula-
334
      tion based on optimization principles and the specific problem context, potentially capturing aspects
335   such as formulation correctness, constraint feasibility, and alignment with the problem.

380   Advances in LLMs. Recent works have demonstrated the substantial potential of Large Language
381   Models (LLMs) in solving complex reasoning tasks, including language understanding (Hendrycks
382   et al., 2021), commonsense reasoning (Brown, 2020), logical reasoning (Wei et al., 2022; Yao et al.,
383
      2024), mathematical problem-solving (Lewkowycz et al., 2022), and coding tasks (Chen et al.,
      2021). Of particular relevance are studies employing LLMs in optimization and search tasks, such
384
      as Bayesian Optimization (Liu et al., 2024b), prompt optimization (Guo et al., 2023), evolutionary
385
      optimization (Yang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a), and symbolic program refinement (Madaan et al.,
386   2024), as well as research exploring the integration of LLMs with planning algorithms (Huang et al.,
387   2022; Zhao et al., 2024; Hao et al., 2023a; Zhou et al., 2024).
388
389
      Autoformulation. Ramamonjison et al. (2023) introduced an early competition focused on translat-
      ing natural language descriptions of linear programming problems into mathematical formulations.
390
      The competition involved two tasks: tagging problem entities and predicting formulations in prede-
391
      fined formats. Entries primarily used pre-LLM NLP models tailored for these tasks, which lacked
392   the ability to generalize beyond the given formats. More recently, Xiao et al. (2023) and Ahma-
393   diTeshnizi et al. (2024) explored multi-agent LLM frameworks for optimization model formulation,
394   where LLM agents generated complete formulations in each iteration, refining them locally in sub-
395   sequent steps. Our approach differs by breaking down the formulation process into key stages and
396   using MCTS to systematically explore the formulation space. Additionally, we guide the search
397   with a composite reward function that combines solver feedback with LLM evaluation.

495   6   D ISCUSSIONS
496
497   In this paper, we formally define the problem of autoformulation for
498   mathematical optimization models, establishing objectives, evaluation metrics, and a categoriza-
499   tion of problem types based on their challenges to autoformulators. We introduce a novel approach
500   that frames autoformulation as a search problem and effectively leveraging the hierarchical struc-
501   ture of optimization modeling. Our method integrates LLMs as hypothesis generators and evalua-
502   tion functions of formulation correctness within an MCTS framework, systematically exploring the
503   vast hypothesis space of possible formulations. The introduction of hypothesis pruning using SMT
504
      solvers further enhances efficiency by eliminating redundant formulations. Empirical evaluations
      across linear and mixed-integer programming benchmarks demonstrate our method’s superior per-
505
      formance in formulating correct models, with notable efficiency gains from pruning and LLM-based
506
      correctness evaluation.
507
508   Future works. Looking ahead, we envision autoformulation as an exciting domain where LLMs can
509   significantly augment human experts. Future research directions include developing collaborative
510
      frameworks to synergize with human expertise, exploring advanced LLM-based methods such as
      retrieval-augmented generation (Lewis et al., 2020) and test-time compute (Lightman et al., 2024).
511
      A particularly promising direction lies in fine-tuning models to enhance autoformulator capabilities,
512
      with special emphasis on process-supervised learning for hierarchical modeling steps (Lightman
513   et al., 2023; Wan et al., 2024), which aligns naturally with the structured decomposition inherent
514   in optimization formulation. An important consideration for future work is the potential correlation
515   bias from using the same LLM for both generation and evaluation; while our composite evaluation
516   strategy helps mitigate this through solver feedback and comparative ranking, developing special-
517   ized evaluation models or ensemble-based approaches could provide more robust assessment. To
518   support these advancements, the development of large-scale, diverse benchmarks encompassing var-
519   ious problem types and complexities, particularly those requiring intricate reformulations, will be
520   crucial.

1369
            O PTIMIZATION P ROBLEM S TRUCTURE
1370
1371
1372   The exact challenges faced by an autoformulator depends on the nature of the problem d. Here,
1373   we provide a categorization of optimization problems and their characteristics. To help elucidate
1374   different types of problems, we introduce two concepts. First, we define the set of correct formu-
1375   lations for a problem M(d) ⊂ M as the set of all equivalent formulations that correctly model a
1376   problem d. Second, we introduce the set of original forms Mo (d) ⊆ M(d)—the set containing
1377   the natural representations of the problem, typically the initial models an optimization expert would
1378   create. This is a set, as it can contain trivially equivalent formulations. Finally, we partition the set
1379
       M into the set of convex problems Mconv and the set of non-convex problems Mnonc .
1380
1381   1. Type I problems. These are problems where the original form is inherently convex, namely
1382      Mo (d) ⊆ Mconv . Examples include certain resource allocation problems that can be naturally
1383      formulated as linear programs. The challenge of solving Type I problems is to ensure that the
1384      problem is correctly represented (formulation correctness, i.e. H(d) ∩ Mo (d) ̸= ∅), which
1385
          would entail that it can be efficiently solved to global optimality.
1386
       2. Type II problems. These are problems where the original form is non-convex, but can be refor-
          mulated into an equivalent convex problem, namely Mo (d) ⊆ Mnonc but M(d) ∩ Mconv ̸=
1387
          ∅. In addition to formulation correctness, another challenge of solving Type II problems
1388
          is to ensure the autoformulator can identify and apply appropriate reformulation strategies
1389      (e.g. change of variables) to transform the non-convex into an equivalent convex form, namely
1390      H(d)∩(M(d) ∩ Mconv ) ̸= ∅ (see App. C for discussion). For such problems, evaluation extends
1391      beyond correctness to include the ability to achieve global optimality through reformulation.
1392   3. Type III problems. These are problems where the original form is non-convex and cannot be
1393      reformulated into a convex problem, namely M(d) ⊆ Mnonc . In such cases, there are two
1394      general options: a) solve the non-convex problem using general-purpose algorithms (e.g. gradient
1395      descent), or b) relax into a convex problem that approximates, but is not equivalent to, the original
1396      problem (e.g. semidefinite relaxation of a Max-Cut problem (Goemans & Williamson, 1995)).
1397
1398   A crucial nuance here is that mathematically equivalent models, even when both are convex, can
1399   exhibit vastly different computational complexities. An example of this is quadratic programming
1400   and second-order cone programming (SOCP) reformulations of the same problems (Alizadeh &
1401   Goldfarb, 2003). Although mathematically equivalent, SOCP formulations often allow for more
1402   efficient solution methods. Therefore, computational efficiency is an important evaluation metric
1403   across all three problem types, significantly impacting practical utility of model formulations. In
       App. F, we provide concrete examples to illustrate each type of optimization problems.

1785   solvers on the PC MinPower problem, both in its original nonconvex form and in a reformulated

1789
1790   G.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
1791
1792   The PC MinPower problem aims to minimize the total power consumption in a system while
1793   satisfying certain constraints. The original formulation of this problem is nonconvex, which can
1794   pose challenges for optimization algorithms. However, by reformulating the problem, it can be
1795   converted into an equivalent convex problem, which is generally easier to solve efficiently.
1796   We evaluated the following solvers:
1797

1799     – TRCA: Trust-Region Constrained Algorithm
1800     – SLSQP: Sequential Least Squares Programming.

1804     – CLARABEL: A conic optimization solver.
1805     – ECOS: Embedded Conic Solver.
1806     – SCS: Splitting Conic Solver.
1807     – OSQP: Operator Splitting Quadratic Program Solver.

1810
1811   • Success Rate: The percentage of runs where the solver successfully found a feasible solution.
1812   • Optimality Gap: The difference between the objective value obtained by the solver and the known
1813     optimal value.
1814   • Average Solve Time: The average computation time (in seconds) required by the solver.
