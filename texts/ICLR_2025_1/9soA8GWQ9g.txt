009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Proximal policy optimization (PPO) is a widely-used algorithm for on-policy rein-
012            forcement learning. This work offers an alternative perspective of PPO, in which
013            it is decomposed into the inner-loop estimation of update vectors, and the outer-
014            loop application of updates using gradient ascent with unity learning rate. Using
015            this insight we propose outer proximal policy optimization (outer-PPO); a frame-
016            work wherein these update vectors are applied using an arbitrary gradient-based
017            optimizer. The decoupling of update estimation and update application enabled
018
               by outer-PPO highlights several implicit design choices in PPO that we challenge
               through empirical investigation. In particular we consider non-unity learning rates
019
               and momentum applied to the outer loop, and a momentum-bias applied to the in-
020
               ner estimation loop. Methods are evaluated against an aggressively tuned PPO
021            baseline on Brax, Jumanji and MinAtar environments; non-unity learning rates
022            and momentum both achieve statistically significant improvement on Brax and
023            Jumanji, given the same hyperparameter tuning budget.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Proximal policy optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017b) is ubiquitous within modern reinforce-
029   ment learning (RL), having found success in domains such as robotics (Andrychowicz et al., 2020b),
030   gameplay (Berner et al., 2019), and research applications (Mirhoseini et al., 2021). Given its ubiq-
031
      uity, significant research effort has explored the theoretical (Hsu et al., 2020; Kuba et al., 2022) and
      empirical (Engstrom et al., 2020; Andrychowicz et al., 2020a) properties of PPO.
032
033   PPO is an on-policy algorithm; at each iteration it collects a dataset using the current (behavior)
034   policy. This dataset is used to construct a surrogate to the true objective, enabling gradient-based
035   optimization while seeking to prevent large changes in policy between iterations, similar to trust
036   region policy optimization (Schulman et al., 2017a). The solution to the surrogate objective is then
037
      taken as the behavior parameters for the following iteration, defining the behavior policy with which
      to collect the following dataset. The behavior policies are therefore exactly coupled with the preced-
038
      ing surrogate objective solution.
039
040   In this work we instead consider the inner-loop optimization of each surrogate objective to estimate
041   an update vector, which we name the outer gradient. A trivial result follows that the outer loop
042   of PPO can be viewed to update the behavior parameters using unity learning rate σ = 1 gradient
043
      ascent on the outer gradients. Using this insight we propose outer-PPO, a novel variation of PPO
      that employs an arbitrary gradient-based optimizer in the outer loop of PPO. Outer-PPO decouples
044
      the estimation and application of updates in way not possible in standard PPO. An illustration of
045
      outer-PPO applying a learning rate greater than unity is provided in figure 1. The new behaviors
046   enabled by outer-PPO raise several questions related to implicit design choices of PPO:
047
      Question 1. Is the unity learning rate always optimal?
048
049   Question 2. Is the independence (lack of prior trajectory information e.g momentum) of each outer
050   update step always optimal?
051   Question 3. Is initializing the inner loop surrogate objective optimization at the behavior parame-
052   ters (without exploiting prior trajectory / momentum) always optimal?

073   This work forms an empirical investigation of the aforementioned questions. To motivate this inves-
074   tigation, consider the clipping parameter ϵ of PPO, controlling the size of the ‘trust region’ within
075   which we seek to restrict our update. If ϵ is set too low, we restrict ourselves to small policy updates.
076
      Conversely, if we set ϵ too high we decrease the reliability of our update direction. In outer-PPO,
      introducing an outer learning rate decouples these two effects; we are able to reliably estimate an
077
      update vector using a moderate ϵ, but then take step of large magnitude in this direction.
078
079   We emphasize that we do not seek to identify the most performant configuration possible but to
080   understand the performance of outer-PPO relative to a well-tuned PPO baseline. To this end we
081   restrict the tuning of outer-PPO to simple grid searches applied to fixed base PPO hyperparameters.
082   Our contributions are as follows:
083
084
             • We propose outer proximal policy optimization (outer-PPO), in which an arbitrary gradient-
085
               based optimizer is applied to the ‘outer gradients’ of PPO. By tracking the outer trajectory,
086            outer-PPO further permits a momentum bias to be applied to the inner-loop initialization.
087
088          • We optimize a PPO baseline through extensive hyperparameter sweeps (total of 38,400
089            agent trained) on subsets of Brax (6 tasks) (Freeman et al., 2021), Jumanji (4 tasks) (Bonnet
090            et al., 2024), and MinAtar (4 tasks) (Young & Tian, 2019). We open-source the sweep
091            database files to facilitate future research against strongly tuned baselines.
092
093          • We perform three lightweight outer-PPO grid searches on non-unity outer learning rates,
094            outer Nesterov momentum and biased-initialization, each addressing questions 1, 2 and 3
095            respectively.
096
097
             • We evaluate the outer-PPO methods against the baseline, using 64 seeds per task over the
               14 different tasks. We find non-unity outer learning rates to yield the greatest improvement
098
               (5-10%) on both Brax and Jumanji. Outer Nesterov also improves performance on Brax
099
               and Jumanji. Biased initialization achieves a moderate improvement on Jumanji alone. No
100            method improves over the baseline on MinAtar.
101
102          • Given the stated empirical results we conclude the negative for questions 1, 2 and 3. Re-
103            laxing each of these PPO design choices can lead to consistent, statistically significant
104            improvement of performance over at least one of the evaluated environment suites.
105
106          • We propose that practitioners able to experiment may explore non-unity outer learning rates
107            given the simplicity (single hyperparameter) and consistent improvement achieved on Brax
               and Jumanjji.

      2.1   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
111
112   We consider the standard reinforcement learning formulation of a Markov decision process M =
113   ⟨S, A, T , r, γ⟩, where S is the set of states, A is the set of actions, T : S × A → ∆(S) is the
114   state transition probability function, r : S × A → ∆(R) is the reward function, and γ ∈ [0, 1] is
115   the discount factor. We use the notation ∆(X) to denote the probability distribution over Pa set X.
116   The reinforcement learning objective is to maximize the expected return Eπ [Gt ] = Eπ [ t γ t rt ]
117   given a policy π : S → ∆(A) defining the agent behavior. In actor-critic policy optimization the
118
      policy is explicitly represented as a parametric function π : S × θ π → ∆(A), and a value function
      V : S × θ V → R is employed to guide optimization. In deep RL (Mnih et al., 2015; Silver et al.,
119
      2017) neural networks are used for the policy and value functions, for ease of notation we consider
120
      θ ∈ R(dπ +dV ) as the concatenation of the respective weight vectors.

      2.3   T RUST REGIONS
153
      A trust region is a region surrounding an optimization iterate θk within which we permit our
154   algorithm to update the parameters to θk+1 . In TRPO, a trust region surrounding the be-
155   havior parameters is explicitly defined as the region in parameter space θ ∈ Θ satisfying
156   Es∼Dk [DKL (π(θk |s) ∥ π(θ|s))] ≤ δ. Optimizing subject to this constraint prevents large changes
157   in the policy between successive iterations, and gives rise to a guarantee of monotonic improve-
158   ment. Similarly, if the clipped surrogate objective of PPO is replaced with a KL penalty Lπ (θ) =
159   Es,a∼Dk [ρ(θ)Â − βDKL (π(θk |s) ∥ π(θ|s))], a trust-region is implicitly defined for some δ. Both
160   TRPO and PPO-KL approximate the natural policy gradient (Kakade, 2001), (Hsu et al., 2020); the
161   steepest direction in the non-Euclidean geometry of policy space induced by the Fisher information
      metric.

202   An outer learning rate σ < 1 interpolates between the behavior parameters θk and inner-loop solu-
203   tion θk∗ , encoding a lack of trust in the outer gradient estimation. Whilst the magnitude of the outer
204   gradient can be reduced by varying hyperparameters, such as the clipping ϵ or number of inner loop
205   iterations, the outer gradients are inherently noisy due to stochastic data collection and inner-loop
206   optimization. PPO is additionally able to irreversibly escape its clipping boundary (Engstrom et al.,
207
      2020), and can drift far from the behavior policy given sub-optimal surrogate objective parameters.
      Finally, whilst by monotonic improvement guarantees we can assume θk∗ to define an equal or supe-
208
      rior policy to θk , the non-linear map from parameters to policy and non-convex surrogate objective
209
      imply we cannot assume performance monotonically improves on the linear interpolation between
210   these points. These effects motivate the exploration of methods that attenuate the outer updates,
211   irrespective of the outer gradient magnitude. In contrast, a learning rate σ > 1 amplifies the update
212   vector, encoding confidence in its direction. Whilst the outer gradient magnitude could be increased
213   by varying the PPO hyperparameters, in particular ϵ, increasing the size of the trust region may lead
214   the policy to drift to beyond the region of policy space where the dataset Dk collected with policy
215   πk can be considered representative of the environment dynamics, motivating the amplification of
      well-estimated outer gradients over increases to trust region size.

      3.2   M OMENTUM
243
244   Whilst permitting novel behavior, outer-LR PPO still only exploits information from a single PPO
245   iteration when updating the parameters. Applying momentum breaks this design choice; instead of
246   directly updating the parameters with the scaled outer gradient σgkO , we update using the Nesterov
247   momentum rule as in algorithm 4 and illustrated in figure 2a.
248
249   Algorithm 4 Outer-Nesterov PPO
250    1: Input: θ0 (parameters), σ (learning rate), µ (momentum factor)
251    2: m0 ← 0 ∈ Rd
252    3: for k = 0, 1, 2, . . . do
253    4:     gkO ← PPOI TERATION(θk ) − θk
254    5:     mk ← µmk−1 + gkO
255    6:     θk+1 ← θk + σ(mk + µgkO )
256    7: end for
257
258
259
      In supervised learning momentum is motivated using pathological curvature, and the ability to ‘build
      up speed’ (Sutskever et al., 2013). Given that the outer gradient is the solution to a surrogate objec-
260
      tive, we do not anticipate pathological curvature presenting to the outer optimizer. However, similar
261
      to learning rates σ > 1 the increase in effective learning rate of momentum may assist in learning.
262   Momentum can also be motivated here using resilience to noise; since any given collected dataset
263   will be noisy, the outer gradient is also noisy. As using a learning rate σ < 1 corresponded to a lack
264   of trust in any given outer gradient, using momentum corresponds to a smoothing process, where
265   we at no point solely trust a single outer gradient to be accurate.

      4.1   E VALUATION P ROCEDURE
293
294   We experiment on subsets of the Brax (Freeman et al., 2021), Jumanji (Bonnet et al., 2024), and
295   MinAtar (Young & Tian, 2019) environment suites, selected as diverse examples of continuous and
296   discrete control problems. We employ the absolute evaluation procedure recommended by Colas
297   et al. (2018) and Gorsane et al. (2022). Absolute evaluation entails intermediate evaluations during
298   training and a final, large-scale evaluation using the best policy identified to give the ‘absolute’
299   performance. We train with a budget of 1 × 107 transitions, perform 20 intermediate evaluations,
300
      and conduct final evaluation using 1280 episodes.
301   Recognizing the hyperparameter sensitivity of deep reinforcement learning (Hsu et al., 2020; En-
302   gstrom et al., 2020; Andrychowicz et al., 2020a), we commit significant resources to establishing
303   a strong PPO baseline and fair evaluation. We sweep for a budget of 600 trials per task using the
304   tree-structured Parzen estimator (Bergstra et al., 2011; Watanabe, 2023). Each trial is the mean of
305   4 agents, trained using seeds randomly sampled from [0, 10000], for a total of 2400 agents trained
306
      per task during baseline tuning. A total of 11 hyperparameters are tuned, each with extensive ranges
      considered. Full descriptions of the hyperparameter sweep ranges, and the optimal values identified
307
      are provided in appendix C.
308
309   After hyperparameter tuning a final 64 agents are trained per environment task, where the set of eval-
310   uation seeds is non-overlapping with seeds used for hyperparameter tuning. To compare methods
311   we aggregate performance over the tasks of an environment suite following the procedure recom-
312
      mended by Agarwal et al. (2021), normalizing with the min/max return found for each task across
      all trained agents (including sweep agents), a table of which is presented in appendix D.

      6   D ISCUSSION
439
440
      We now reflect on the questions posed in section 1. The PPO baselines in this work were tuned
441
      aggressively for each task, greatly increasing the confidence in the experimental findings. Given the
442   baseline strength, and performance demonstrated in figures 3 and 4, we conclude in the negative for
443   all three questions as evidenced by:
444
445       Q1. Varying the outer learning rate leads to an statistically significant increase on all point esti-
446           mates on Brax and Jumanji, with corresponding increases to probability of improvement.
447       Q2. Employing Nesterov momentum on the outer loop, with outer learning rate attenuation,
448           achieves statistically significant increases to all point estimates on Brax and Jumanji. We
449           also observe a statistically significant probability of improvement on all three suites.
450       Q3. Momentum-biased initialization achieves statistically significant increase on all point esti-
451           mates on Jumanji, with a probability of improvement of 0.6 on this suite.
452
453   Common hyperparameters. The sensitivity plots in figures 5, 6 demonstrate robust normalized
454   performance across the Brax and Jumanji suites for outer-LR and outer-Nesterov. However, they do
455   not indicate any significant increase in normalized return could be achieved over standard PPO for
456   a set of common hyperparameters shared across a suite. To achieve the improved aggregate metrics
457   in figure 3 it was necessary to use task-specific hyperparameters. We do however emphasize the
458   aggressive, task-specific, tuning of the baseline, and view the robustness of normalized return across
459   a range of hyperparameters as a strength of the methods.
460
461   Task-specific hyperparameters. Task-specific hyperparameter sensitivity plots are provided in
462
      appendix E. For outer-LR the optimal per-task values for α range between 0.5 (corresponding to
      cautious updates) and 2.3 (corresponding to confident updates). That values of α up to 2.3 can
463
      be optimal is surprising, as an α greater than unity directly violates the trust region established by
464
      our previous behavior policy. This precludes the provable monotonic improvement of PPO (Kuba
465   et al., 2022); by stepping beyond the trust region we may in principle select a policy that is worse
466   than the previous. For outer-Nesterov co-varying σ with µ can be understood through the effective
467   learning rate σ/(1 − µ). The task-specific effective learning rate varies from 0.7 to 2.3. Lastly, for
468   biased initialization the sharp peaks in performance on Brax tasks suggest the method suffers from
469   high variance on this suite, hence the hyperparameters selected may not be optimal in expectation.
470   On Jumanji the method is significantly less hyperparameter sensitive as evidenced by the smooth
471   contours, providing an explanation for the performance gap observed between these suites.
472
473   MinAtar results. No outer-PPO method improved over baseline on MinAtar. We comment that
474   our baseline results are much stronger than other works (Lu et al., 2022), and are approaching the
475
      mathematical maxima of these tasks as defined by the gymnax library (Lange, 2022). We further add
      that other works committing substantial resources to baseline tuning on MinAtar have struggled to
476
      achieve improvements on the suite Jesson et al. (2023). Furthermore, the hyperparameter sensitivity
477
      plots in figures 5, 6 and 7 demonstrate all methods achieve peak normalized return greater than 0.9
478   on MinAtar. Since here we are normalizing to the maximum performing agents across all sweeps,
479   this indicates there is less variance in the optimal performance of MinAtar compared to Brax and
480   Jumanji with peak normalized returns around 0.7 and 0.8 respectively. A final explanation for the
481   failure to surpass baseline on MinAtar could be ‘brittle’ base hyperparameters, not suited to the
482   modified dynamics introduced by outer-PPO, supported by the sharp peak observed in outer-LR and
483   concentration of performance in outer-Nesterov about standard PPO in figures 5 and 6.
484
485   Limitations. We identify two core limitations to this work; the fixed transition budget and the lack
      of co-optimization of base and outer-PPO hyperparameters. We only consider a timestep budget of


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      1 × 107 transitions. Whilst sample efficiency plots are provided in appendix D the hyperparameters
487   have not been tuned to maximize performance in the data-limited regime. Furthermore, we do not
488   consider the asymptotic performance for larger transition budgets, where it is possible the improve-
489   ment achieved by outer-PPO methods may be diminished. With respect to co-optimization, given
490   the dependence of the outer gradients on the base hyperparameters there is undoubtedly significant
491   interaction between these and the outer-PPO hyperparameters. Exploring these interactions would
492   yield better understanding and potentially improved performance. We additionally highlight the
493   presence of learning rate annealing on the inner Adam instances in all experiments. This implies the
494   outer gradients tend to zero, the implications of which we do not explore in this work.
495
496
497   7   R ELATED W ORK
498
499
      The usage of the difference between initial parameters and those after gradient-based optimization
500
      as a ‘gradient’ has been explored for meta-learning in the Reptile algorithm (Nichol et al., 2018).
501   Reptile aims to find an initialization that can be quickly fine-tuned across a distribution of tasks.
502   Unlike outer-PPO, which applies this idea within a single RL task, Reptile performs gradient steps
503   on different supervised learning tasks to determine the ‘Reptile gradient’. One could interpret outer-
504   PPO as performing serial Reptile whereby each sampled task is the next PPO iteration alongside the
505   collected dataset.
506
      Whilst to the best of our knowledge we are the first to apply momentum to the outer loop of PPO,
507   momentum-based optimizers such as RMSProp Tieleman & Hinton (2012) and Adam Kingma &
508   Ba (2014) are commonly applied in other areas of RL. Recent work has examined the interaction
509   of momentum based optimizers and RL objectives. Bengio et al. (2021) identify that a change in
510   objective (such as by updating a target network or dataset), may lead to momentum estimates anti-
511   parallel to the current gradient thereby hindering progress, and propose a correction term to mitigate
512   this effect. Asadi et al. (2023) propose to reset the momentum estimates periodically throughout
513   training and demonstrate improved performance on the Atari Learning Environment Bellemare et al.
514   (2012) with Rainbow Hessel et al. (2017) doing so. However, none of these approaches focuses on
515
      PPO specifically, and instead address temporal difference learning or value based-methods.
516   Lastly, the biased initialization explored in this work is similar to the conjugate gradient initializa-
517   tion technique employed in hessian-free optimization Martens (2010), although this used only the
518   prior iterate and not a momentum vector. Hessian-free optimization can be considered a supervised
519   learning version of TRPO (Schulman et al., 2017a).
520
521
522   8   C ONCLUSION
523
524
525
      In this work, we introduced outer-PPO, a novel perspective of proximal policy optimization that
      applies arbitrary gradient-based optimizers to the outer loop of PPO. We posed three key research
526
      questions regarding the optimization process in PPO and conducted an empirical investigation across
527
      14 tasks from three environments suites. Our experiments revealed that non-unity learning rates and
528   momentum in the outer loop both yielded statistically significant performance improvements across
529   a variety of evaluation metrics in the Brax and Jumanji environments, with gains ranging from 5-10%
530   over a heavily tuned PPO baseline. Biased initialization provided improvements upon the baseline
531   on Jumanji tasks but not Brax.
532
      The most immediate direction for future research would be the exploration of interactions between
533
      base hyperparameters and outer-PPO hyperparameters. Since the optimal base hyperparameters may
534   be unsuited to the modified dynamics of outer-PPO, the co-optimization of hyperparameters may
535   yield performance improvements and deeper understanding of the method. Other possible future
536   directions include the use of outer-PPO with alternatives to the clipped surrogate loss function, such
537   as KL-penalized PPO Hsu et al. (2020) or discovered policy optimization Lu et al. (2022), and the
538   use of adaptive optimizers on the outer loop such as RMSProp or Adam. Indeed, an ‘outer’ variant
539   of many dual-loop RL algorithms can be defined, and we hope that this work will stimulate further
      research into optimizing RL algorithms through more sophisticated outer-loop strategies.

1353   of standard PPO hyperparameters. We focus our discussion on clipping ϵ and inner learning rates η
1354   as highly influential hyperparameters, but similar arguments can be made for other hyperparameters.
1355
1356
1357   G.1   C LIPPING ϵ
1358
1359   The clipping ϵ defines the policy ratio ρ(θ π ) beyond which the loss is clipped, hence the gradients
1360   are zero. Intuitively, increasing ϵ increases the size of the trust region within which we seek to
1361   restrict our policy updates. Given suitable hyperparameters, a larger value of ϵ will enable larger
1362
       updates to policy between iterates, hence larger magnitude outer gradients g O . In contrast, scaling
       the outer learning rate σ does not change the size of the trust region used for g O estimation, but
1363
       instead directly scales the outer gradient when applying the update to the behavior parameters σg O .
1364
1365
1366   Let θ ∗(ϵ) be the surrogate objective solution for a given value of ϵ, defining the outer gradient
1367   g O (ϵ) = θ ∗(ϵ) − θ. Assume the equivalence of ϵ-variation and σ-variation; in other words the
1368   behavior of outer learning rates can simply be achieved by varying the clipping ϵ. Formally, this
1369
       implies that there exists k ∈ R+
1370                           σg O (ϵ0 ) = σ(θ ∗(ϵ0 ) − θ) = θ ∗(kϵ0 ) − θ = g O (kϵ0 ) ,
1371
       for any given value of σ ∈ R+ .
1372
1373   Consider a true proximal objective, such as PPO-KL (Hsu et al., 2020). Assuming the penalty
1374   coefficient β is sufficiently high such that the proximal objective is convex, and that the inner-loop
1375   optimization converges to the global minimum thereof, the above condition is met. In this case the
1376
       outer gradient corresponds to exactly the natural policy gradient (Kakade, 2001). Scaling β directly
       scales the trust region size, and the (unique) solution remains on the span of the natural policy
1377
       gradient. However, PPO-clip is not a true proximal objective, and importantly non-convex. Unlike in
1378
       PPO-KL there is no guarantee of a unique solution, indeed the clipping mechanism implies regions
1379   of equivalent loss. Given that is is possible to irreversibly enter the clipped region (Engstrom et al.,
1380
       2020) we cannot assume different values of ϵ = kϵ0 will converge onto the span of θ ∗(ϵ0 ) − θ, hence
1381   do not meet the above condition for the equivalence of ϵ-variation and σ-variation. An illustrative
1382   diagram of this behavior is provided in figure 25.

1401
1402   The inner learning rate η defines the learning rate for the inner-loop optimization of the clipped
1403   surrogate objective. Unlike clipping ϵ, η does not change the size of the trust region established,
       but instead influences the convergence to solution within this trust region. In contrast, the outer

       learning rate σ defines a rescaling of the outer gradient σg O when it is applied to update the behavior
1405   parameters.

1407   Let θ ∗(η) be the surrogate objective solution for a given value of η, defining the outer gradient
1408   g O (η) = θ ∗(η) − θ. Assume the equivalence of η-variation and σ-variation; in other words the

1414   Unlike clipping ϵ the inner learning rate η does not affect the size of the trust region defined by
1415   the surrogate objective. However, as previously discussed the PPO-clip surrogate objective is non-
1416   convex and has no guarantee of a unique solution. Furthermore, it is possible to irreversibly escape
1417   the unclipped trust region. The convergence of the inner-loop to θ ∗(η) is simply defined using a
1418   specified number of inner-loop iterations. It is therefore trivial to see that variation in η may lead to
1419   solutions not on the span of θ ∗(η0 ) − θ, hence do not meet the above condition for the equivalence
1420   of η-variation and σ-variation.

1422   H    C OMPUTATIONAL C OMPLEXITY
1423
1424   All outer-PPO algorithms proposed have negligible increase in computational complexity over PPO,
1425   only requiring a few vector scaling and addition operations. Outer-PPO configurations that maintain
1426   first moment estimates such as momentum-PPO and biased initialization have linear increase in
1427   memory complexity over standard PPO with respect to the parameter count. Since outer-PPO is
1428   a lightweight modification to the outer loop of vanilla PPO, it does not increase complexity with
1429   respect to dataset size (either in terms of total timesteps, or timesteps per iteration).

       I     E VALUATION D ETAILS
1459
1460
       Algorithms are evaluated across M tasks within a defined suite, with N = 64 independent seeded
1461   runs conducted per task. Each run involves training over 1 × 107 timesteps, with performance
1462   measured over 128 episodes at 20 equally spaced checkpoints i. The checkpointed model achieving
1463   the highest mean return Gim,n across all intermediate evaluations is selected for final ‘absolute’
1464   evaluation.
1465
       In absolute evaluation, the selected model is tested over 1280 episodes to obtain more reliable perfor-
1466
       mance estimates. For consistency across tasks, raw scores xm,n are normalized for each task m and
1467   seed n, using the observed minimum and maximum scores found during the entire experimentation
1468   process for each task independently as proxies for the global min and max returns. This normaliza-
1469   tion produces a matrix of normalized scores x1:M,1:N , which are aggregated to derive performance
1470   metrics.
1471
       To provide robust statistical estimates, we compute 95% confidence intervals through stratified boot-
1472
       strapping over the M ×N experiments. This approach accounts for variability across tasks and runs,
1473   ensuring results reflect the algorithm’s performance across the full task suite.
1474
