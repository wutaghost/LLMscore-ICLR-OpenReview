009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Predictive coding (PC) offers a biologically plausible model of cortical functions,
012
               encompassing processes such as learning, prediction, encoding, and memory. How-
013
               ever, predictive coding networks (PCNs) face significant challenges in stability
014            and scalability, which constrain our capacity to elucidate cortical computation.
015            Our study identifies instability in PCNs as a fundamental issue, focusing on the
016            exponential growth of latent state norms and prediction errors after inference.
017            These dynamics lead to exploding and vanishing gradients in PCNs. Moreover, the
018            concentration of prediction errors near the input and output layer impedes effective
019            learning, exacerbating performance degradation as network depth increases. To
020            address these limitations, we propose stabilizing techniques for PCNs, including
021            length regularization and sequential training with skip connection modules. This
022
               approach counteracts the exponential growth of latent states and makes the dis-
               tribution of prediction errors more uniform across layers. Empirical evaluations
023
               demonstrate that our approach enhances stability and generalization, enabling the
024
               training of deeper networks more efficiently. This study deepens our understand-
025            ing of complex dynamics in cortical networks, thereby advancing the practical
026            application of predictive coding theory to its full potential.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Predictive coding (PC) (Srinivasan et al., 1982; Mumford, 1992; Rao & Ballard, 1999) is a theoretical
033   framework for cortical information processing, including learning, prediction, encoding, and memo-
034   rization. Since PC theory accommodates key computations in the brain, it emerges as an appealing
035   alternative for artificial intelligence (AI) systems aiming to achieve human-compatible learning and
036   inference mechanisms (Salvatori et al., 2023a; Song et al., 2024). Deep predictive coding networks
037   (PCNs) hold an untapped potential for engineering applications due to their inherent ability to perform
038   local predictions and updates. This capability allows parallel computing during both learning and
039
      inference, making PCNs a viable alternative to conventional feed-forward neural networks that rely on
      backpropagation (Rumelhart et al., 1986). Additionally, predictive coding is adaptable to models with
040
      arbitrary topologies (Millidge et al., 2022b; Salvatori et al., 2022), making it a flexible framework for
041
      various learning tasks. That being said, critical issues limit our use of PCNs to their full potential:
042   Training often becomes unstable, particularly when applied to deeper network architectures. The
043   underlying causes of this instability are not fully understood, which limits the scalability and practical
044   applicability of PCNs.
045
      This study aims to address these challenges by analyzing the dynamics of latent state lengths during
046
      inference and proposing solutions to stabilize the training of deeper PCNs. The term length refers to
047
      the normalized squared norm, a more statistically traceable metric than the norm itself. Under the
048   assumption of sufficiently high dimensionality, length approaches the variance of the elements of the
049   respective variable. Since length is a norm-dependent metric, its changes are closely tied to variations
050   in the norm. Specifically, changes in length during the dynamic processes of inference or learning
051   can be used to detect variations in the norm while also providing insights into which layers exhibit a
052   concentration of prediction error length. By leveraging dynamical mean-field theory (Sompolinsky
053   et al., 1988; Poole et al., 2016; Schoenholz et al., 2017), we explore the recursive updates of latent
      state lengths in a random PCN ensemble and identify critical issues that hinder training.

      1. We show that the latent state dynamics cause an exponential growth in the norms of latent states
055      and prediction errors, directly impacting the gradients used for parameter updates. Such latent
056      dynamics cause the exploding and vanishing gradient problems (EVGPs; (Bengio et al., 1994;
057      Hochreiter, 1998; Pascanu et al., 2012; Arjovsky et al., 2016)) in PCNs.
058   2. We found that prediction errors tend to remain near the input and output layers after inference. Since
059      prediction errors influence parameter updates, the occurrence of prediction errors predominantly
060      near the input and output layer in deep networks prevents the other layers from being effectively
061      trained. Consequently, this leads to a significant performance degradation as the network depth
062      increases.
063
064
      These issues are validated through a comparative analysis of theoretical predictions and empirical
      evidence.
065
066   To address the exponential growth of lengths, we explore regularization methods to suppress the
067   pathological growth of the squared norms of the latent states. Additionally, to address the issue of
068   prediction error occurring predominantly near the input and output layer, we introduce a sequential
069   training framework with skip connection modules that enable the distributed propagation of prediction
070
      error. This approach, called stable prediction coding networks (SPCNs), effectively transfers the
      stalled prediction error to the other layers, stabilizing the learning process and improving performance.
071
      We validate the proposed methods through comprehensive analyses and experiments, demonstrating
072
      that PCNs with length regularization and skip connection modules significantly improve the stability
073   and scalability of PCNs. Our approach guides the training of deeper predictive coding networks with
074   improved accuracy and robustness.

076   Overview This paper investigates key challenges and advancements in PCNs, focusing on theoreti-
077   cal and empirical insights. In Section 2, the background and motivation for this work are introduced
078   by emphasizing a critical challenge in PCNs: achieving the inference condition ∆z = 0, as assumed
079   in earlier studies, motivating a deeper exploration of this issue. Section 3 provides the theoretical
080   framework for analyzing PCNs, presenting tools for length dynamics analysis. These tools form
081   the foundation for understanding the underlying instabilities and inefficiencies in PCN inference
082
      processes. In Section 4, our observations regarding the dynamics of length in PCNs are then detailed,
      focusing on their inherent instability. The analysis reveals how instability in PCNs affects weight
083
      update lengths, contributing to the EVGPs. This finding underscores the difficulty of achieving the
084
      inference condition ∆z = 0. Additionally, the analysis shows that prediction errors are unevenly
085   distributed across layers, primarily concentrating on the input and output layers. This uneven distri-
086   bution allows intermediate layers to process information unrelated to the input or output, creating
087   inefficiencies in learning and inference. In Section 5, we propose SPCNs to address these challenges.
088   The proposed model introduces structural modifications that mitigate the identified issues, improving
089   stability and performance. As shown in Section 6, experimental results validate the effectiveness of
090   SPCNs in addressing the instability and error distribution problems outlined earlier. Finally, the paper
091   discusses related works to situate this study within the broader context of predictive coding research
092   and concludes by summarizing the contributions in the last sections.

096   The Predictive coding (PC) theory posits that the brain continuously predicts the external environment
097   and refines latent states by minimizing local prediction errors. A PCN, built based on the PC idea,
098   connects local predictive coding modules in a chain structure. In this model, the forward step predicts
099   the latent state of the subsequent layer, and the backward step minimizes local prediction errors, as
100   shown in Figure 1. In contrast to the conventional von Neumann-style computational regime, the PC
101
      attempts to accommodate the human brain’s cortical information processing with extremely parallel
      computation. They can also learn models with arbitrary graphical topologies (Millidge et al., 2022b;
102
      Salvatori et al., 2022).

340   Prediction Error Concentration: As shown in Figure 3a, q l,t decreases in the intermediate layers
341   with repeated iterations. In Figures 3b and c, to eliminate the influence of the latent state magnitude on
342   the prediction error, since δ l,t = zl,t − ẑl,t , we examine the ratio of q l,t to pl,t . As inference proceeds,
343   the q/p ratio diminishes gradually, leaving significant normalized prediction errors concentrated near
344   the input and output layers. As depicted in Figure 3c, after T = 200 inference steps, the q/p ratio is
345   significantly larger near the output layer. This trend also affects the updates of weight parameters
346   after inference (Figure 3d).
347   Impact of Concentrated Prediction Errors on Learning: We observe that prediction errors tend
348   to remain concentrated near the input and output layers during inference. According to Equation 4,
349   updates to the weights and biases during subsequent learning phases become negligible if the
350   prediction error approaches zero. While this issue has been previously addressed in Kinghorn et al.
351   (2023), we demonstrate that the cause of this problem can be explained through length dynamics. In
352   the following section, we propose a method to propagate the prediction error while maintaining the
353   PC mechanism.

358   We propose SPCNs as a simple and practical solution to the challenges of PCNs. SPCNs incorporate
359   length regularization to control the lengths of latent states and parameters, and sequential training
360   with skip connection modules to enable progressive learning from layers close to the data pairs.
361
362   Length Regularization The exponential change in the length of the latent state affects the learning
363   signal (see Section 4). We introduce length regularization to ensure stable update signals during
364   training. This regularization directly applies to the latent states and learning parameters    in the
      inference and learning phases. The regularization loss for a length p is given by Rp := l (cp − pl )2 ,
                                                                                               P
365
366   where cp indicates the target length of p. We similarly defined regularization loss for r, denoted
367
      by Rr . Note that regularizing q is equivalent to LPC , and therefore it is omitted. By combining the
      regularization loss with the original predictive coding loss, the total loss becomes:
368

370   where λ is the corresponding length regularization loss coefficient. We set the target lengths as cp = 1
371   and cr = 1, and the coefficients as λp = 1 and λr = 1, respectively.

404   The model gradually learns layer by layer toward the intermediate layers. The inference and learning
405   process is divided into ⌈ L−1
                                  2 ⌉ stages. The incremental process starts with the layer closest to the
406   data pair and progressively includes increasingly distant layers. In the initial training stage, the latent
407   states z2 and zL−1 closest to the data pair are inferred. The parameters associated with these states
408   are learned in the subsequent learning phase. At this stage, the latent states from z3 to zL−2 do not
409   participate in inference, and their connected predictive coding modules are not learned, isolating the
410   information flow from input to output. It ensures that layers not involved in inference or learning
411   do not integrate unnecessary information. However, this disconnection allows the latent states and
412
      parameters connected from the input to encode information independently of the output and vice
      versa.
413

414
      the latent states to a prematurely trained network. Skip connection modules are applied only during
415   training, while predictions during evaluation are made solely through the feed-forward connection
416   modules.

      7    C ONCLUSION
512
513
      This study delves into the underlying causes of unstable training in deep PCNs, focusing particularly
514
      on the length dynamics of the latent states during the inference process and the length of the
515   parameter gradient affected by the dynamics. Our investigation highlights the exponential growth of
516   the lengths of the latent states and prediction errors, culminating in the challenge of the exploding and
517   vanishing gradient problem inherent to PCNs. Furthermore, we have identified that the intermediate
518   layers of PCNs will likely be fitted to random predictions rather than information from data pairs,
519   causing performance deterioration with increasing network depth. To address these issues, we
520   introduce SPCNs, which implement strategies to regularize the lengths of latent states and adopt
521   sequential training for deepening PCNs. Empirical evaluations of SPCNs demonstrate their efficacy
522   in mitigating the issues and enhancing the trainability and generalizability of PCNs. Overall, this
523
      research significantly contributes to the advancement of PCNs by offering theoretical insights and
      empirical validation to improve their trainability and performance in practical applications.

707   with backpropagation under the assumption of equilibrium of latent states (∆x = 0); however, we
708   have directly probed the latent dynamics itself. Our analysis indicates that reaching the equilibrium
709   can be challenging due to the exponential growth of transition in latent states. To our knowledge, this
710   study is the first to investigate the inherent instability in the latent dynamics of PCNs. Our research is
711   directly inspired by previous works (Poole et al., 2016; Schoenholz et al., 2017) that analyze stability
712   issues in deep neural networks (DNNs) through the lens of length dynamics. These studies have
713
      focused on the stability of both forward and backward passes in DNNs, which is also linked to the
      EVGP (Bengio et al., 1994; Hochreiter, 1998; Pascanu et al., 2012; Arjovsky et al., 2016). We extend
714
      similar reasoning to demonstrate that this stability issue can also arise during the latent state inference
715
      process in PCNs. Previous research (Frieder & Lukasiewicz, 2022) explored the (non-)convergence
716   behavior of PCNs, but our study identifies new challenges through an analysis of length dynamics.
717   Specifically, we highlight the occurrence of EVGP from length analysis and the concentration of
718   prediction errors in specific layers, forming the basis for our proposed sequential training with skip
719   connection modules.
720
      Meanwhile, PCNs have been applied to supervised and temporal sequence learning tasks (Whitting-
721
      ton & Bogacz, 2017; Tang et al., 2023). However, although not explicitly stated, their published
722   implementations of these studies are typically confined to a shallow network; generally, it is hard
723   to train deep PCNs. Our study examines the stability and scalability issues inherent in deep PCNs
724   through theoretical and empirical analysis. In addition, previous work (Kinghorn et al., 2023) has
725   suggested that the instability of PCNs stems from discrepancies in updates across layers, which aligns
726   with our observations regarding latent length dynamics. However, we approach this problem from a
727   length dynamics perspective and propose SPCNs as a solution to enhance the entropy of prediction
728   errors.
729   Predictive coding has also been adopted to design other models, such as (Lotter et al., 2017;
730   Choksi et al., 2021). PredNet (Lotter et al., 2017) predicts sequences by combining a PCN with
731   Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997)) units; however, unlike the
732   original PC theory (Rao & Ballard, 1999), this approach relies on backpropagation rather than local
733   computation and uses the encoded error as a prediction target. Predify (Choksi et al., 2021) represents
734   a hybridization of FFNs-BP and PCNs, circumventing the chronic problems of PCNs by utilizing
735   FFNs-BP and modern deep learning mechanisms such as batch normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy,
736
      2015). In summary, many studies have adopted the predictive coding idea but often compromised the
      original theory. Our study pinpoints the issue underlying the unstable PCN training and improves its
737
      applicability to deeper PCNs.

741
742   Broader Impacts Predictive coding (PC) is a biologically plausible learning mechanism based on
743   local computation. It acquired attention as a potential link between artificial network learning and
744   brain learning (Whittington & Bogacz, 2017; 2019). However, we have demonstrated that PCNs have
745
      inherent limitations in stable learning. Our solution shows that regularization of neural activity is
      necessary for stable deep predictive coding in PCNs, which is supported by neuroscience research
746
      regarding the effects of normalization on the brain (Letellier et al., 2019. 6. 5.).
747
      While our results advance our understanding of cortical computations, they could pose a risk of
748   misuse, such as attacks or hacking of brain-like intelligence models. Finally, SPCNs still need to
749   address fairness and privacy issues critical to various social concerns.
750
751   Limitations We propose a stable, dynamic learning system and suggest an early stop of the
752   inference phase to prevent random or premature overfitting of the predictive module. That said,
753   its computational cost is still high compared to feed-forward neural networks, which may limit
754   its practical applicability. The theory presented in our study is based on conventional PCNs with
755   a feed-forward architecture, whereas the proposed model has a specific structure, as illustrated in
      Figure 4. Based on our theory, future works could suggest a more general theory accommodating

      C     B IOLOGICAL PLAUSIBILITY OF SPCN S
763
      C.1   R EGULARIZATION
764
765   Predictive Coding Networks (PCNs) have garnered attention for their alignment with cortical
766   processes, given their reliance on local computations and the minimization of prediction er-
767   rors—principles thought to underpin neural activity in the brain. In PCNs, each neuron updates
768   its state based solely on local information, reflecting how neurons in the brain operate without a
769   global control signal. This feature is particularly crucial for maintaining the biological plausibility of
770   these models. However, traditional PCNs face challenges in terms of stability and trainability, raising
771   questions about their biological plausibility when applied to deeper networks. The unstable learning
772
      dynamics observed in PCNs, characterized by the exponential growth of latent states and prediction
      errors, do not align with the stable learning observed in biological systems. This discrepancy suggests
773
      that while PCNs may capture some aspects of cortical function, they may not fully embody the
774
      robustness inherent in biological systems.
775
776   To address these issues, we propose Stable Predictive Coding Networks (SPCNs), which incorporate
777   mechanisms to regularize the latent state dynamics, thereby improving stability and enhancing bio-
778
      logical plausibility. SPCNs implement local computations similarly to traditional PCNs but introduce
      constraints that prevent the runaway dynamics observed in deeper networks. This modification aligns
779
      SPCNs more closely with the stable functioning of biological neural networks and enhances their
780
      scalability and applicability in practical settings. Despite these improvements, SPCNs retain some
781   biological limitations. For instance, the introduction of regularization techniques, while stabilizing,
782   may not have a direct analog in biological neural systems, where stability might be achieved through
783   different, yet undiscovered, mechanisms. While SPCNs advance the biological plausibility of PCNs
784   by addressing key stability issues, they represent an abstraction that balances biological fidelity with
785   computational tractability. Further research is necessary to refine these models and explore how
786   closely they can mimic the complex and dynamic processes observed in the brain.

827   sequential maturation, facilitating stable long-term learning. By aligning with these principles, our

829   reinforcing its biological plausibility.

832
833   Our skip connection module mirrors structures observed in various neural circuits:
834
835          • Visual Cortex Hierarchy: The skip connection module reflects bypass connections in the
836            visual cortex hierarchy (Blumberg & Kreiman, 2010; Hegdé & Felleman, 2007; Suzuki
837            et al., 2023). For instance, the lower-level V1 directly connects to higher-order regions such
838
               as V4 or MT.
839          • Thalamo-Cortical Loops: The module aligns with circuits in the thalamocortical
840            loop (Hegdé & Felleman, 2007; Suzuki et al., 2023), where regions such as the thala-
841            mus’s LGN and pulvinar nuclei establish forward connections to multiple visual cortical
842            areas.
843          • Sensory-Motor Integration: Skip connections link sensory information to motor outcomes,
844            as seen in the pathways connecting the primary sensory cortex to higher cortical areas and
845            subcortical motor centers (Suzuki et al., 2023).
846
847   These examples highlight that information in the cortex flows hierarchically and through lateral
848   and recurrent pathways, enabling efficient and flexible error correction. Skip connection modules in
849
      PCNs emulate these pathways by directly propagating prediction errors across layers, improving both
      stability and error integration.
850
851   By incorporating skip connection modules, our SPCN model enhances computational stability while
852   reflecting key cortical structure and function aspects. This design choice draws inspiration from
853   hierarchical processing in the visual system and bypasses connections, thereby reinforcing the
854   biological plausibility of our approach.

       The performance of CNNs is highly dependent on the choice of latent state initialization schemes,
1329
       with the feed-forward initialization scheme enhancing performance despite not aligning with the
1330   Gaussian assumptions of this paper. This section discusses the resulting changes in prediction error
1331   concentration patterns and the corresponding modifications to SPCNs following the adoption of this
1332   scheme.
1333
1334   Prediction Error Concentration with Feed-Forward Initialization: The performance of the
1335   network can vary significantly based on how the latent states are initialized, especially in the context
1336   of CNNs. Feed-forward initialization of latent states has been widely used in recent studies of
1337   PCNs (Whittington & Bogacz, 2017; Millidge et al., 2022b; Pinchetti et al., 2024). In this approach,
1338   zl,0 = f l−1 (zl−1,0 ) for 2 ≤ l ≤ L − 1. Figure 7a shows that the length of the prediction error during
1339
       inference is high near the output layer. At t = 0, since all latent states, except those at the output layer,
       are initialized by predictions, no prediction error occurs at the lower layers, with errors generated only
1340
       at the output layer. According to Equation 2-3, latent states remain unchanged if no prediction error
1341
       arises from adjacent layers. Thus, the latent state update begins with the output layer’s prediction
1342   error. If a layer is k layers away from the output layer, the prediction error propagates at the rate
1343   of O(η k ). Figure 7c shows that the length ratio near the output layer is significantly higher after
1344   T = 2000 inference steps. Similar to the case of Gaussian initialization, the length of the weight
1345   parameter update, rl,t , follows a similar pattern (Figure 7d).
1346
1347   Modification of SPCNs for Feed-Forward Initialization: To address the issue of concentrated
1348   prediction errors near the output layer, we introduce modifications to the proposed SPCNs. As the
1349   initialization of latent states in a feed-forward manner necessitates the full connectivity of all feed-
       forward connection modules, the sequential training approach is rendered inapplicable. Furthermore,


                                                            25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       due to the tendency of prediction errors to concentrate around the output layer, we restructure the
1351   skip connection modules to emphasize connections with the output layer, as depicted in Figure 8.
1352   Accordingly, in comparison to the original SPCNs, this revised configuration omits sequential training.
1353   It employs only a subset of skip connection modules directed towards the output layer, resulting in a
1354   less optimal variant. Consequently, the performance advantages typically associated with integrating
1355   the modified SPCNs and the feed-forward initialization scheme are moderately reduced.
