009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Detecting out-of-distribution (OOD) samples is a significant challenge in real-
012            world deep-learning applications, such as medical imaging and autonomous driv-
013            ing. Traditional machine learning models, primarily trained on in-distribution (ID)
014            data, often struggle when encountering OOD instances, resulting in unreliable
015            predictions. While supervised OOD detection methods generally outperform unsu-
016            pervised approaches due to the availability of labeled data, our research uncovers a
017            crucial insight: their success is not necessarily due to recognizing the actual object
018
               categories in the images; instead, these methods rely on a specific classification
               strategy that may not correspond to real-world understanding. Essentially, super-
019
               vised methods detect OOD samples by identifying the difficulties in classifying
020
               unfamiliar data. This challenge is similar to what unsupervised OOD detection
021            methods face, as they also depend on the failure to reconstruct OOD data due to
022            the lack of prior exposure. In this study, we bridge the gap between supervised and
023            unsupervised OOD detection by introducing a novel approach that trains models to
024            classify data into pseudo-categories. We employ self-supervised learning (SSL) to
025            convert raw data into representations, which are then clustered to generate pseudo-
026            labels. These pseudo-labels are subsequently used to train a classifier, enabling its
027            OOD detection capabilities. Experimental results show that our approach surpasses
028            state-of-the-art techniques. Furthermore, by training models on different sets of
029
               pseudo-labels derived from the dataset, we enhance the robustness and reliability
               of our OOD detection method.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      In real-world applications, machine learning models often encounter out-of-distribution (OOD) data
035
      instances that differ from the classes seen during training. While models may perform well on in-
036   distribution (ID) data, detecting and managing OOD data is crucial for ensuring model trustworthiness.
037   Robust OOD detection mechanisms not only prevent erroneous predictions in unfamiliar scenarios
038   but also enhance model reliability, robustness, and safety across diverse application contexts.
039
      Current OOD detection methods can be roughly categorized into supervised and unsupervised
040
      approaches. Supervised methods rely on labeled datasets to learn the characteristics of ID data and
041
      distinguish them from OOD samples. These methods typically learn to classify image labels and
042   use this learned knowledge to identify deviations from the learned data distribution as potential
043   OOD samples. These methods are known for their speed and generally high accuracy, but their
044   reliance on extensive human-labeled data can be a significant drawback, especially in domains where
045   labeling is costly or impractical. In contrast, unsupervised methods do not require labeled data and
046   focus on reconstructing images using powerful generative models such as autoencoders, generative
047   adversarial networks, and diffusion models. While these unsupervised approaches can be slow due
048   to the complexity of the models, they are advantageous in scenarios where labeled data is scarce.
049   Despite many studies exploring both supervised and unsupervised methods, their detection accuracies
050
      are not directly comparable due to different experimental settings.

      2    BACKGROUND
085
086
      Supervised Methods. Supervised OOD detection methods depend on learning from labeled data to
087
      distinguish between in-distribution (ID) and out-of-distribution (OOD) samples. Hendrycks & Gimpel
088   (2016) introduced a baseline approach that utilized softmax confidence scores for OOD detection,
089   which was later enhanced by Liang et al. (2017) through the use of temperature scaling and input
090   perturbations. Lee et al. (2018) further advanced the field by incorporating Mahalanobis distances
091   between test samples and the ID dataset. However, many of these methods suffer from overconfidence
092   when encountering OOD samples. To address this issue, DeVries & Taylor (2018) proposed a strategy
093   where models are incentivized to make accurate predictions by optimizing both prediction loss and an
094   additional confidence loss, ensuring that high confidence scores more accurately reflect the model’s
095   prediction capability. Liu et al. (2020) tackled the overconfidence problem by measuring how well an
096
      input fits within the learned data distribution. More recently, innovations such as contrastive training
      for robust feature learning (Tack et al., 2020), leveraging large-scale pre-trained models (Fort et al.,
097
      2021), and post-hoc scoring methods like LogitNorm (Wei et al., 2022), MaxLogit, and MaxCosine
098
      (Zhang & Xiang, 2023) have significantly improved OOD detection. Additionally, new insights have
099   emerged from using feature map norms from intermediate network layers, as explored by Yu et al.
100   (2023). These developments highlight the ongoing advancements aimed at enhancing the reliability
101   and robustness of OOD detection in supervised settings.
102
103   Unsupervised Methods. To reduce reliance on labeled data, researchers have turned to unsuper-
104   vised OOD detection methods. These approaches leverage techniques such as reconstruction error
105   and density estimation, eliminating the need for human-annotated labels. Autoencoders were among
106   the earliest methods employed, demonstrating their effectiveness in anomaly detection based on
107   reconstruction error (An & Cho, 2015). Generative adversarial networks (GANs) further advanced
      the field through adversarial training (Schlegl et al., 2017). In addition to reconstruction methods,


                                                                  2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      density estimation techniques have also shown promising results. For example, Ren et al. (2019)
109   proposed Likelihood Ratios, which assess the likelihood of a sample under an ID model compared
110   to that under a broader generative model. However, it has been observed that relying solely on
111   likelihood scores from deep generative models can be problematic, as these scores do not always
112   serve as a reliable metric for OOD detection (Nalisnick et al., 2018; Kamkari et al., 2024). More
113   recent methods have explored alternative approaches using normalizing flows (Cook et al., 2024) and
114   transformer-based models (Podolskiy et al., 2021), which offer improved modeling of complex data
115   distributions and provide high-quality reconstructions for OOD detection. Moreover, diffusion-based
116   methods have recently shown great promise. These techniques measure discrepancies between input
117
      images and their reconstructed counterparts to detect OOD samples (Graham et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
      2023). Despite these advancements, unsupervised methods tend to be slower and still lag behind
118
      supervised methods in terms of performance when directly compared.
119
120
      Pseudo-labels. Pseudo-labeling has been effectively applied across various fields, such as semi-
121
      supervised learning (Lee et al., 2013), domain adaptation (Litrico et al., 2023), and unsupervised
122   learning (Caron et al., 2018), by utilizing unlabeled data to enhance model performance. The
123   integration of clustering techniques with self-supervised learning approaches (Caron et al., 2018; Chen
124   et al., 2020; Van Gansbeke et al., 2020) has shown remarkable success in generating reliable pseudo-
125   labels, thereby reducing the need for large, annotated datasets. Building on these advancements, we
126   explore the use of pseudo-labeling as a substitute for human-annotated labels in OOD detection.

      3.1   A NALYSIS OF C URRENT S UPERVISED AND U NSUPERVISED O UT- OF -D ISTRIBUTION
131         D ETECTION M ETHODS
132
133   Supervised and unsupervised OOD detection methods are built on distinct principles. Supervised
134   approaches focus on classifying samples, identifying them as OOD when their predicted labels are
135   less confident. In contrast, unsupervised methods consider samples as OOD if their representations
136   fall in low-density regions or exhibit high reconstruction errors. However, these methods are typically
137   evaluated under different experimental conditions. Comparing them under consistent settings allows
138
      for a clearer understanding of their relative strengths and the actual impact of using labels. As
      illustrated in Table 1, supervised methods outperform unsupervised ones in both speed and accuracy
139
      of OOD detection. Mapping image representations to specific labels proves effective in distinguishing
140
      between ID and OOD data. However, since supervised methods heavily depend on labor-intensive
141   ground-truth (GT) labels, our goal is to assess whether replacing GT labels with pseudo-labels can
142   maintain comparable OOD detection performance.
143
144   3.2   H YPOTHESIS : G ROUND -T RUTH L ABELS ARE NOT N ECESSARY
145
146   We hypothesize that the model’s ability to detect OOD samples may not stem from recognizing the
147   true object categories but rather from learning a specific classification strategy that doesn’t necessarily
148   align with real-world understanding. In essence, while these methods excel in classifying ID data,
149   they often struggle with OOD samples, which they have never encountered. This challenge mirrors
150   the limitations seen in unsupervised methods, which also fail to reconstruct OOD data effectively due
151
      to their unfamiliarity.
152
153
      Pilot Study. To investigate the viability of using pseudo-labels as a substitute for GT labels for
      OOD detection, we conducted a pilot study where models were trained using three different types
154
      of labels. In this study, we used the MNIST dataset (LeCun et al., 1998) as the ID data and the
155
      FashionMNIST dataset (Xiao et al., 2017) as the OOD data. The MNIST dataset was labeled in three
156   distinct ways: with ground-truth labels, randomly assigned labels, and cluster-based labels obtained
157   from k-means applied in the image space. Each labeling method resulted in 10 categories. We
158   trained a ResNet18 model (He et al., 2016) to classify the ID data. After training, we computed the
159   FeatureNorm (Yu et al., 2023) for each sample to generate OOD scores, following the methodology
160   used in prior studies. The model’s OOD detection performance was evaluated using a test set that
161   combined the MNIST test dataset and the FashionMNIST dataset. Additionally, the MNIST training
      set was split into two parts: 95% for training and 5% for evaluating classification accuracy.

      Findings and Analysis. Table 2 summarizes the results of this pilot study. The model trained with
173   GT labels achieved the best performance, exhibiting the lowest false positive rate at 95% true positive
174   rate (FPR95) (Liang et al., 2017) and the highest area under the receiver operating characteristic
175   curve (AUROC). The model trained with cluster-based labels from raw image clustering performed
176   moderately, while the model trained with random labels showed the poorest performance. Notably,
177   the models’ OOD detection capabilities were consistent with their classification accuracy on the
178   testing data: higher testing accuracy corresponded to better OOD detection performance.
179   These findings highlight the critical role of label quality in training effective classification models
180   for OOD detection. The model trained with random labels failed to learn meaningful classification
181   rules and could not effectively distinguish between ID and OOD samples. The model trained with
182   cluster-based labels from raw images performed better than random labeling but still fell short
183   compared to the model trained with GT labels. This is likely because clustering in the raw image
184   space captures low-level features like pixel intensities and colors, which do not align well with the
185   high-level semantic features that modern classification networks extract.
186   The moderate performance of the raw image clustering model underscores the need for pseudo-labels
187   derived from high-level representations. Since classification networks like ResNet18 reduce spatial
188   resolution and focus on abstract features, labels based on high-level semantic information are more
189   conducive to learning effective classification strategies. SSL methods are particularly well-suited for
190   this purpose, as they can learn rich, high-level features without requiring labeled data.
191   By deriving pseudo-labels from high-level representations obtained through SSL, we can capture
192   the semantic content of the images more effectively. This approach is expected to improve both
193   classification accuracy and OOD detection performance, bringing models trained with pseudo-labels
194   closer to those trained with GT labels. Thus, the pilot study demonstrates the importance of using
195   SSL pseudo-labels to enhance OOD detection capabilities.

      3.5    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
289
290   Our method involves two stages: SSL training and classifier training, both conducted on the ID
291   dataset. For the SSL stage, we adhered to the default hyperparameter settings outlined in (Gedara
292   Chaminda Bandara et al., 2023). In the classifier training stage, we initialized the network with the
293   SSL-trained encoder fe , and trained fe and fLp using an SGD optimizer with a batch size of 128,
294   momentum of 0.9, and a weight decay of 0.01. Given that our pilot study results indicate OOD
295   detection performance is influenced by the classifier’s generalization ability, we randomly selected
296
      5% of the ID training set for accuracy evaluation and model selection. Following the approach in (Yu
      et al., 2023), we identified the block with the highest norm ratio to compute the OOD score.

511
      Limitations. Experiment results showed that our method slightly outperformed (Yu et al., 2023).
512   Given that the main distinction between the two approaches is the use of pseudo-labels versus GT
513   labels, we suspect that pseudo-labels generated by SSL may, in certain scenarios, offer advantages
514   over GT labels. However, this outcome is not yet fully understood, and we cannot guarantee that our
515   method will consistently outperform (Yu et al., 2023) across all experimental settings. Our future
516   work will focus on investigating the reasons behind this result and further refining the generation of
517   pseudo-labels.
518
519   C ONCLUSION
520
521   In this study, we bridge the gap between supervised and unsupervised OOD detection methods
522   by training models to classify data into pseudo-categories. Our key insight is that the success of
523   supervised methods relies on a classification strategy that may not align with real-world understanding.
524   While supervised methods detect OOD samples by recognizing the difficulty in classifying unfamiliar
525   data, this challenge mirrors what unsupervised methods encounter, as they also struggle to reconstruct
526   OOD data due to the absence of prior exposure. To implement this idea, we employ self-supervised
527
      learning (SSL) feature extraction combined with clustering to generate pseudo-labels for training,
      creating a robust OOD detection framework. We demonstrate the advantages of this method over
528
      state-of-the-art supervised and unsupervised approaches in terms of detection accuracy, efficiency,
529
      and scalability in real-world applications.

782   The SSL embeddings of an ID dataset play an important role in generating pseudo-labels. While
783   there are many available SSL methods presented, we compared the performance of SimCLR Chen

      CIFAR-10 dataset into 20 clusters. The results in Table 10 indicate that mix-BarlowTwin yields the
786
      best OOD detection performance, likely due to its ability to learn more informative representations.
787
      This finding aligns with the KNN test accuracy, as the KNN accuracy values of SimCLR, BYOL,
788   W-MSE were 87%, 88%, and 88%, respectively. Namely, the higher KNN accuracy for SSL methods
789   corresponds to improved OOD detection performance.
790
791
      The lower half of Table 10 investigates the use of different clustering algorithms, with mix-
      BarlowTwin as the SSL method to generate pseudo-labels from CIFAR-10. Specifically, we com-
792
      pared two widely used clustering methods, HDBSCAN and DBSCAN. The results suggest that
793
      pseudo-labels derived from these clustering techniques did not significantly improve OOD detection
794   performance. We attribute this to the way HDBSCAN and DBSCAN treat boundary samples as
795   unknowns, preventing these samples from being forced into specific categories. Despite testing
796   various clustering parameters, approximately 30% of the data samples were classified as unknown,
797   which reduced the number of samples available for training the classifier and ultimately lowered
798   OOD detection performance.
