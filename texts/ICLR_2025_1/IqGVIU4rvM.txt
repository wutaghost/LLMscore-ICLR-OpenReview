011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We proposes a novel visual tokenizer by combining high-level semantic tokens
014            and low-level pixel tokens to represent images, aiming to address the challenges of
015            image-to-sequence conversion for Large Language Models (LLMs). Existing vi-
016
               sual tokenizers, such as VQ-VAE and diffusion-based models, either struggle with
               token explosion as image resolution increases or fail to capture detailed structural
017
               information. Our method introduces a dual-token system: high-level semantic to-
018
               kens capture the main content of the image, while low-level pixel tokens preserve
019            structural details. By integrating these tokens in a hybrid architecture, we leverage
020            a VQ-VAE branch to generate low-resolution guidance and a diffusion process to
021            reconstruct high-resolution images with both semantic coherence and structural
022            accuracy. This approach significantly reduces the number of required tokens and
023            enhances image reconstruction quality, offering an efficient solution for tasks like
024            image generation and understanding based on LLMs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
      In natural language processing, Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated exceptional
029
      performance, largely due to the Scale law [Kaplan et al. (2020); Henighan et al. (2020); McCandlish
030   et al. (2018)], which allows them to effectively learn relationships within long sequences. This has
031   enabled them to excel in various text-based tasks. If other modalities can also be converted into
032   sequences, LLMs could become a powerful tool for multimodal tasks. For example, if images are
033   transformed into a sequence of tokens, LLMs could learn the relationships between these tokens,
034   enabling tasks like image generation and understanding.
035
      A visual tokenizer converts images into token sequences, aiming to achieve this conversion with
036   minimal cost. The goal is to use as few tokens as possible while retaining the essential information
037   during the transformation. The tokenizer typically consists of an encoder, decoder, and a codebook.
038   The encoder transforms the image into a sequence of embeddings, which are then converted into
039   discrete tokens using the codebook. The decoder reconstructs the image from the token sequence.
040   The similarity between the reconstructed image and the original is a direct measure of how much
041   information is lost during the conversion. Significant differences indicate a larger information loss.
042   For LLMs, maintaining information fidelity during the image-to-sequence conversion is crucial.
043   Excessive information loss can negatively impact downstream tasks, such as poor alignment between
044   generated images and text in image generation tasks, or a lack of detail comprehension in image
045   understanding tasks.
046
      Currently, two main types of visual tokenizers exist: VQ-VAE-based [Van Den Oord et al. (2017)]
047
      and diffusion-based [Nichol & Dhariwal (2021)] approaches. VQ-VAE methods divide an image
048   into patches, which are processed by CNNs or ViTs to convert each patch into a token. While
049   this method retains the overall structure of the image, the number of tokens increases exponentially
050   with image resolution, making LLM learning more difficult. Moreover, the fixed number of tokens
051   limits the generated image to a fixed resolution, reducing flexibility. In contrast, diffusion-based
052   tokenizers use tokens that encode high-level semantic information, disregarding pixel-level details.
053   The number of tokens remains low, and the image resolution is independent of the token count, but
      the reconstructed image may differ from the original in structural details, such as posture.

062   Inspired by these methods, we propose a dual-token approach: one set of tokens represents high-
063   level semantic information, while another set captures lower-level pixel details to control the image
064   structure. For instance, in Figure 4, the high-level semantic tokens could describe the main content,
065   such as ”A Doberman Pinscher lounging on a sunny porch with its tongue out, next to a brick wall
066   and a white door.” However, these tokens might omit finer details like the dog’s fur patterns, specific
067   posture, or the exact position of the wall and door. These details can be roughly captured by low-
068   level pixel tokens.
069   To achieve this dual-token representation and reconstruct the image, we designed the architecture
070   shown in Figure 1. We use a SEED [Ge et al. (2023)] encoder to obtain high-level semantic tokens
071   and a VQ-VAE (MoVQ [Zheng et al. (2022)]) branch to extract low-level pixel tokens. The final
072   image is generated through a diffusion process: a low-resolution image is created using pixel tokens,
073   noise is added, and the denoising process begins. High-level semantic tokens guide this process to
074   reconstruct the image.
075   This design offers several advantages: 1) VQ-VAE only needs to generate a low-resolution guide
076   image, so the number of tokens required (around 40-300) is far fewer than with traditional VQ-
077   VAE methods (which often need thousands of tokens). This advantage becomes more pronounced
078   when generating ultra-high-resolution images, as diffusion reconstruction is resolution-independent.
079   2) Our method solves the problem of structural differences between the original and reconstructed
080   images when using only high-level semantic tokens. It achieves high similarity to the original image
081
      with minimal additional token cost.

083   2     R ELATED W ORKS
084
085   In recent years, the field of image and video generation has seen substantial progress, primarily
086   driven by two major approaches: diffusion models and visual tokenization combined with large
087   language models (LLMs).
088
089   2.1   D IFFUSION M ODELS
090
091
      Diffusion models have emerged as a leading method for high-quality image and video synthesis.
      The foundational work by Ho et al. (2020) on Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM)
092
      introduced a process where images are generated through iterative denoising from a noisy starting
093
      point, setting a new standard in generative models. Building on this, subsequent research has focused
094   on improving the efficiency and quality of these models. Notably, Improved DDPM [Nichol &
095   Dhariwal (2021)] and ADM [Song et al. (2020)] refined the denoising process and loss functions,
096   leading to more stable and higher-fidelity outputs.
097
      Further advancements include the introduction of Latent Diffusion Models (LDM) by Rombach et al.
098
      (2022), which significantly enhanced computational efficiency by applying the diffusion process in a
099
      latent space rather than the pixel space. This approach has been successfully implemented in widely
100   used models such as Stable Diffusion. More recently, GenTron [Chen et al. (2024)] has integrated
101   Transformer architectures with diffusion models, achieving state-of-the-art results in text-to-image
102   and text-to-video generation tasks, demonstrating the potential of diffusion models in handling com-
103   plex generative tasks.
104
105   2.2   V ISUAL T OKENIZATION AND LLM S
106
107   Parallel to the development of diffusion models, visual tokenization combined with large language
      models has emerged as another promising approach. This method typically involves encoding visual

145   2.3   V ISUAL T OKENIZATION A PPROACHES
146
147   Visual tokenization has followed two primary paths: those based on VQ-VAE and those involving

149   advanced the original VQ-VAE by introducing multi-scale architectures, improving the resolution
150
      and detail in generated images. OmniTokenizer further extended this by introducing a progressive

151
      to both images and videos. Recently, TiTok model [Yu et al. (2024)] introduced a more efficient
152
      1D tokenization approach, which significantly enhanced both image reconstruction and generation
153   tasks. On the other hand, diffusion-based tokenization approaches, exemplified by SEED [Ge et al.
154   (2023)] and LaVIT [Jin et al. (2023)], reconstruct images using diffusion processes, only need fewer
155   tokens than VQ-VAE based methods, achieving higher efficiency and quality.

      3.2     B RANCH II: H IGH S EMANTIC B RANCH
217
218   The High Semantic Branch is designed following SEED [Ge et al. (2023)] to represent an image
219   as a sequence of discrete visual codes with high-level semantics. The tokenizer is also based on a
220   vector quantization (VQ) framework and employs an encoder-decoder architecture to achieve this
221   representation. The branch comprises a ViT image encoder, a Causal Q-Former, a VQ codebook
222   and a Reverse Q-Former.
223   Initially, a pre-trained ViT image encoder, derived from the BLIP-2 model, processes the input
224   image to generate a 2D feature representation arranged as 16×16 tokens. The Causal Q-Former then
225   converts these features into a sequence of 32 tokens with causal dependencies, representing the high-
226   level semantic content of the image in a 1D format. Subsequently, the VQ codebook quantizes these
227   causal embeddings into 32 discrete visual codes. These codes maintain the causal structure and are
228   well-suited for capturing the semantic essence of the image. Finally, the Reverse Q-Former decodes
229   these visual codes into 77 generation embeddings, which is used as the condition for diffusion
230
      process.
231
232   3.2.1    C AUSAL Q-F ORMER
233
      The training process for the Causal Q-Former is illustrated in Figure 1. A set of 32 learnable query
234
      embeddings, along with the image features produced by the pre-trained ViT encoder, are fed into the
235   Causal Q-Former. The Causal Q-Former employs self-attention layers with a causal mask, allowing
236   each query embedding to interact only with previous queries, and cross-attention layers, enabling
237   interaction with the image features. The training is conducted using contrastive learning, fine-tuning
238   the Causal Q-Former on 5 million image-text pairs. The objective is to maximize the similarity
239   between the final causal embedding and the corresponding text features while minimizing the sim-
240   ilarity with text features from other image-text pairs in the batch. The contrastive loss function is
241   given by:

      3.3   B RANCH III: D IFFUSION B RANCH
271
272   In this stage, we start with VQ-VAE Guided image X̂, which is first encoded into a latent repre-
273   sentation z0 using a Variational Autoencoder (VAE). Next, we apply a Diffusion model with DDIM
274   inversion to add noise to this latent space. This noisy latent representation zT becomes the starting
275   point for the denoising process.
276   The DDIM inversion process introduces noise to the latent space step-by-step, represented as:
277                                            √            √
                                        zt = αt zt−1 + 1 − αt ϵθ (zt−1 , t)                                 (9)
278
      where zt−1 is the latent variable at step t − 1, αt is the noise schedule parameter at time step t, and
279
      ϵθ (zt−1 , t) is the predicted noise at this step. The noise is gradually added to transform z0 into zT .
280
281   Once the noisy latent zT is obtained, it serves as the initial state for the Diffusion process. Under the
282   guidance of conditional semantic tokens g, the latent is gradually denoised step-by-step to recover
283   the original structure. The denoising process at each step t can be expressed as:
284                                           zt−1 = f (zt , g, t) + ϵ                                    (10)
285   where f (zt , c, t) is the denoising function guided by the condition g, and ϵ is the residual noise.
286   Through this iterative process, we recover the final latent variable z′0 , which is decoded back into a
287   high-resolution image x̂ using the VAE decoder.
288   This process ensures that the generated image adheres to the guiding conditions while preserving
289   the structural integrity of the original image.

      5   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this study, we reached the following conclusions: (1) The tokens used to represent images can be
489   decomposed into two key components: high-level semantic information and pixel detail information.
490   This decomposition allows for a significant reduction in the number of tokens while maintaining the
491   stability of the restored structure. (2) The Diffusion model can use high-level semantic tokens as
492   conditional input and low-level pixel structure information as the initial latent, thereby generating
493   the restored image.
