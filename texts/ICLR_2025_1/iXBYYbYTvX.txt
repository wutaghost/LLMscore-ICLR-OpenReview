011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013
               The Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) is known as one of the most notorious
               NP-hard combinatorial optimization problems. In recent decades, researchers from
014
               fields such as computer science, operations research, and artificial intelligence in-
015
               cluding deep learning (DL) have made numerous attempts on the problem. Among
016            the works, the Lin-Kernighan-Helsgaun (LKH) heuristic algorithm is one of the
017            most competent methods for obtaining optimal or near-optimal solutions. Despite
018            the rapid development in DL-based solvers, few of them can defeat LKH in terms
019            of both running efficiency and solution quality across different distributions. In this
020            paper, we would introduce a very novel approach that enhances LKH with graph
021            embedding (GE) techniques in solving general TSP (distances can be non-metric
022            and asymmetric), named as Embed-LKH. It is presented as two stages: i) in the GE
023            stage, it transforms the distances to transition probabilities, then conduct GE given
024
               the transition probabilities, and finally it uses the learned embeddings to construct
               the so-called ‘ghost distances’; ii) in the LKH stage, LKH generates candidates
025
               based on the ghost distances but searches tours according to the original distances.
026
               As the experiments show, compared with the original LKH counterpart, in
027
               most cases, our approach can obtain better solutions within the same amount
028
               of trials across six distance distributions (non-metric and asymmetric: nor-
029            mal, uniform, exponential, metric and symmetric: Euclidean 2D/10D/50D) and
030            two problem scales (TSP-100/1000). The source files, running scripts, and
031            data are in the anonymous link https://anonymous.4open.science/
032            r/EmbedLKH-BF80/, which will be made publicly available after the review.

037   1    I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      The Travelling Salesman Problem (TSP) is one of the most well-known NP-hard combinatorial
      optimization (CO) problems. Given a set of nodes and the distances between them, TSP aims to find
040
      the shortest tour that visits each node exactly once and returns to the starting node. With applications
041
      spanning logistics, telecommunications, and genetics, TSP plays a crucial role in route optimization
042   and network planning (Applegate et al., 2007; Rardin & Rardin, 1998; Matai et al., 2010).
043
044   General TSP and its Practical Significance. In this paper, we study the general TSP, where the costs
045
      from node to node can be non-Euclidean (are not distances derived from Euclidean coordinates) and
      asymmetric (the cost from node i to j does not have to be equal to that from j to i). Compared with
046
      the symmetric TSP defined by 2D coordinates which is much more commonly studied by literature
047
      (Kool et al., 2018; Kwon et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023; Qiu et al., 2022), the general TSP is more
048   ubiquitous in practical applications: For example, the time it takes for vehicles to travel between two
049   places does not entirely depend on the physical distance between the two places, but is highly affected
050   by road congestion and speed limits (65 mph on highway but 25 mph in residential districts). That
051   leads to a non-Euclidean time cost. Meanwhile, the time cost can be also asymmetric due to different
052   congestion situations in different directions (for example, during morning rush hours, the required
053   time of traveling from residential areas to work areas is much higher than the opposite direction), as
      well as probably different road conditions (some roads are one-way).

      Motivation. The motivation is straightforward: to reduce the difficulty of input TSP instances. As
071
      illustrated in Fig. 1, there are hard cases and easy cases. For hard instances, even strong solvers like
072   LKH struggle to find the optimal solution, whereas for easier instances, a simple greedy algorithm
073   (e.g., nearest neighbor) suffices. Our objective is to transform a difficult instance into an easier
074   one that likely shares the same optimal solution, allowing LKH to find a shorter tour with fewer
075   trials. Importantly, while we adjust the distances during the transformation, the tour length is always
076   computed using the original distances of the hard instance.
077
      We notice that the graph embedding (GE) techniques (e.g., Perozzi et al. (2014); Grover & Leskovec
078   (2016)), originally developed for graph mining tasks (e.g., community discovery, social link prediction,
079   etc.), possess remarkable properties that could be highly effective for general TSP solving, including:
080   i) Scalability. With a single machine of 128GB memory, GE methods (e.g., Tang et al. (2015);
081   Qiu et al. (2019a)) can easily deal with graphs of millions of nodes. ii) Unsupervised learning.
082   Most GE methods work in an unsupervised style, making it particularly suitable for the general TSP,
083   where optimal solutions are usually inaccessible as supervisory information. iii) Applicability on
084   non-attributed graphs. GE directly learns from graph topology without node attributes. These
085   virtues of GE also motivate us to apply GE in the challenge of solving general TSP.
086   Our Techniques and Highlighted Features. In our Embed-LKH, we first apply GE techniques on
087   the transition probability matrix transformed from the distance matrix of the general TSP. This allows
088   us to construct a ‘ghost distance matrix’, a modified distance matrix that we expect will be easier to
089   solve compared to the original distances. We then generate candidates based on the ghost distances
090   while searching tours according to the original distances to ensure the accuracy of the computed tour
091   length. Below, we summarize the main virtues of Embed-LKH:
092   • Effectiveness. We investigate six distance distributions including three non-metric and asymmetric
093
        (normal, uniform, exponential), and three metric and symmetric (Euclidean 2D/10D/50D). Results
        show that in most cases Embed-LKH can outperform learning-based solvers, as well as the original
094
        LKH within the same number of searching trials.
095
      • High efficiency and scalability. We utilize closed-form solution for GE, avoiding gradient descent
096     thus achieving higher efficiency of the GE steps. We further propose an asynchonization scheme for
097     Embed-LKH where GE steps and the LKH step are conducted in different threads asynchronously.
098     These technical points endow Embed-LKH with high efficiency and good scalability.
099   • One-shot. Unlike popular learning-based (Kool et al., 2018; Qiu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023)
100     and learning-enhanced heuristic (Hudson et al., 2021; Xin et al., 2021) methods for solving TSP,
101     Embed-LKH runs in the one-shot manner, allowing it to be applied to instances of different scales
102     without a typical resource-consuming pre-training stage, while also avoiding generalization issues
103     from training data to testing data.
104
      • Invariance to perturbations. Most supervised (Joshi et al., 2019; Fu et al., 2021) or reinforcement
        learning-based methods (Kool et al., 2018; Qiu et al., 2022; Kwon et al., 2021) suffer from
105
        performance collapse due to the fragility of neural predictions which can be immensely affected
106
        by any perturbations on the input distances, largely hindering their applicability to real-world

121   Definition 1 (General Traveling Salesman Problem). Given a node set V (V = {1, 2, · · · , N })
122   along with a distance matrix Di,j ∈ [0, +∞)N ×N where the entry Di,j is the distance from node i
123   to j, the problem is to find the tour (a Hamiltonian cycle that visits all the nodes exactly once and
                                                                                     PN −1
124   returns to the starting node) τ = (τ1 , · · · , τN , τ1 ) to minimizes the cost i=1 Dτi ,τi+1 + DτN ,τ1 .
125   Without losing generality or decreasing the hard level of the problem, we assume that Dij is a positive
126   value. In this paper, we consider TSP in the general cases, namely
127           • Asymmetric. The symmetry, i.e. Di,j = Dj,i , does NOT have to hold.
128           • Non-Metric. The triangle inequality, i.e. Di,j + Dj,k ≥ Di,k , does NOT have to hold.
129    3.2 LKH A LGORITHM
130   LKH is a highly encapsulated tool tailored for vehicle routing problems including TSP with abundant
131   of technical tricks in it. Diving deep into the working mechanisms of LKH is not the purpose
132   of the paper. Readers who are interested in the technical details of LKH may refer to Appendix
133   C. We highly recommend readers to regard LKH as a black box which works by two steps: 1)
134   LKH_GeneCand(D): Given a distance matrix D, it generates candidates for each node. Here
135   ‘candidates’ are indicating how likely the edge from the node to a candidate shows in the final
136   solution; 2) LKH_SearchTour(D, Candidates): Given distance matrix D, it searches tours based
137
      on the generated candidates.

322   to obtain a matrix B to make A ≈ BB∗ . ii) Use B which is much smaller than A to compute matrix
323   factorization. Readers who are interested in details are recommended to refer to the official paper.

339   6.1 I NVARIANCE TO THE I NPUT P ERTURBATIONS
340   Abundant works for neural TSP solvers deal with the distance perturbations that would not change
341   the optimal solutions by data augmentation (Jiang et al., 2022; Kwon et al., 2020; 2021; Ma et al.,
342   2021). In comparison, Embed-LKH is naturally invariant to some certain perturbations, as presented
343   by the following Proposition 3 and 4.
344   Proposition 3 (Row-wise Disturbance Invariance). We define a random row-wise disturbance
345   vector as b ∈ RN , whose entry bi represents the disturbance on node i’s distances to other nodes.
346   If we disturb the input distance matrix D by D′ ← D + b (D′i,j ← Di,j + bi ), one can easily
347   prove that such a disturbance would not change the optimality of solutions. We conclude that the GE
348   outputs, embeddings X and H, are also invariant to such a disturbance.
349
350   Proof. We find that the disturbance works in step 1. For the new distances D′ , we denote the
351

      6.2 E MBED -LKH IS A O NE - SHOT S OLVER
371   As we presented in Alg. 1, before testing, Embed-LKH is not trained on a training dataset which
372   contains many instances, but conducted right on the testing instance. So, Embed-LKH is a one-
373
      shot solver. The ‘one-shot’ property endows the method more other virtues including: i) All-scale
      applicability. Unlike previous methods that require ensuring that the size of the test data is the same
374
      as the size of the instances in the training dataset, our method can be applied to problems of any scale
375
      (as long as the machine has enough memory). ii) Low training cost. Unlike previous deep learning
376   methods (e.g., Kwon et al. (2021)), Embed-LKH does not require a significant amount of time for
377   pre-training and has no requirements for hardware (e.g., GPUs). iii) Generalizability. Embed-LKH
      does not have the issue of generalization from training data to testing data.

380   7.1 E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
381   Hardware. Experiments for neural methods are conducted on a single NVIDIA RTX3090 24GB
382   GPU with AMD 3970X 32-Core CPU. Our Embed-LKH and other heuristics (LKH, EAX, etc.) are
383   conducted on a machine with Intel Xeon W-3175X CPU and 128GB memory.
384
      Baselines. Neural solver: MatNet (Kwon et al., 2021) is the only neural solver runnable for general
385
      TSP to our best knowledge. Heuristic solver: LKH (Helsgaun, 2017) is a very strong heuristic for
386   its high efficiency, good scalability, and impressive performance. Learning-enhanced heuristic
387   solver: VSR-LKH (Zheng et al., 2021) improves LKH’s heuristic strategy with RL-driven policies.
388   The improved versions (VSR-LKH-V2/3) do not support general (asymmetric) TSP solving as we
389   have tried. We DO NOT compare with the exact solver Gurobi since it runs out of memory for TSP
390   instances of 100 nodes or more on our machine; and we DO NOT compare with Concorde since it
391   does not support distance matrices as the input. Some newly proposed methods are not open-sourced
392   yet (e.g., (Drakulic et al., 2024)) or do not provide an ATSP solver (e.g., (Drakulic et al., 2023; Ye
393   et al., 2024b)), so we do not compare with them.
394   Default Parameters. For fairness, for LKH, VSR-LKH, and Embed-LKH, we set runs=1 (number
395   of running iterations for each instance), max_candidates=6 (maximum number of candidates for each
396   node). Other detailed settings (e.g., the search algorithm, the way to compute candidates, etc.) are set
397   as the default of the original LKH program. Embed-LKH runs with 16 threads (8 for GE steps and
398   8 for the LKH step) as the default. On TSP-100, we set random walk window size W = 3 without
399   sparsification; on TSP-100 we set W = 1 and adopt sparsification with K = 100.
400   Testing Data Generation. Before introducing data generation we should emphasize again that
401   Embed-LKH is a one-shot solver so the training data is also the testing data. We consider the scales
402   fo 100 nodes and 1000 nodes. The investigated distance distributions include three non-euclidean
403   asymmetric types of distances (normal, uniform, exponential) and three types of Euclidean (metric
404   and symmetric) distances: i) Euclidean 2D/10D/50D. First we generate 2D/10D/50D Euclidean
405   random coordinates in [0, 1]2/10/50 . Then we compute the pair-wise distances, and finally re-scale
406   them to the range of [0, 1e4]. ii) Normal. We Generate distance Di,j from normal distribution
407
      N (0; 1), then re-scale them to the range of [0, 1e4]. iii) Exponential. We generate distance Di,j
      from exponential distribution (λ = 0.5), then re-scale them to the range of [0, 1e6]. iv) Uniform. We
408
      generate distances from uniform distribution in [0, 1e5]. All distances are rounded to integers (since
409
      the float number would cause inaccurate tour lengths).
410
411   Metrics. Average length (abbr. ‘Length’). We report the average length of the found tours of all the
412   instances. Optimal Gap (abbr. ‘Opt. Gap’). We take LKH with the largest number of trials of each
413
      scale as the reference to compute the performance gap of all compared methods. Time. We report the
      running time of the solver. For Embed-LKH, it includes the time of all the 4 steps.

530
      In this paper, we present a graph-embedding-enhanced LKH (Embed-LKH) algorithm for general
531   TSP solving. This is the first technology to utilize graph embedding techniques to assist in solving
532   the general TSP, and also the first learning-enhanced one that outperforms LKH on the problem of
533   general TSP showing in the ML community, to our best knowledge.
534
      Limitations and Future Works. i) The setting of parameter d and the strategy p(j|i) (especially
535
      the inside parameter, window size W ) in the GE steps may require careful consideration. We leave
536
      automated parameter selection as the future work. ii) There is still a significant room for improving
537   the efficiency of Embed-LKH by more tightly coupling the two functions LKH_GenCand and
538   LKH_SearchTour.

      B.1     TSP S OLVERS
786
787   Exact Solvers. Solvers for linear programming and mixed integer linear programming, e.g., Gurobi
788   and CPLEX, can be used to solve general TSP with exact optimal solutions as output. These methods
789   can be time-consuming in real-world applications where the scale of instances may go large. Concorde
790   is a solver developed for TSP but it fails to deal with general TSP where only distances are available.
791
      Heuristic Solvers. Some trivial heuristic algorithms include nearest neighbor (NN), furthest insertion
792   (FI), etc. After decades of optimizations, strong heuristics such as Lin-Kernighan-Helsgaun (LKH by
793   Helsgaun (2017)) and EAX (Nagata & Kobayashi, 2013)) have been well developed and are able to
794   give satisfying results with good efficiency and scalability.
795
      Neural Solvers. Compared with the heuristics and exact solvers, neural solvers are believed to
796
      be more efficient when the solving procedure is run parallelly on GPUs. Also, the learning-based
797
      approaches are believed to have a stronger capability in solving TSP instances from a specific
798   known distribution. Most neural TSP solvers are developed for TSP with coordinates (Kool et al.,
799   2018; Kwon et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023; Qiu et al., 2022; Sun & Yang, 2023; Vinyals et al., 2015;
800   Min et al., 2024). This is due to the current lack of neural networks that can effectively learn in
801   scenarios with only pairwise feature information, such as the distances in TSP. MatNet (Kwon
802   et al., 2021) is one of the works that can be applied to BiTSP. It proposes a Transformer-based
803   solver for asymmetric TSP (ATSP) whose input is a distance matrix. However, the heavy-encoder
804   heavy-decoder neural architecture and the DRL-based training put limitations to its scalability and
805   consequently its applicability to real-world problems whose scales are varying. Recently, several
806
      works (Drakulic et al., 2023; 2024; Ye et al., 2024b) conducted experiments on ATSP and showed
      promising results. Yet, such part regarding general TSP solving have not open-sourced.
807
808   Developing neural solvers that combine LKH and neural networks is also an important line of
809   research. VSR-LKH (Zheng et al., 2021) improves LKH’s heuristic strategy with RL-driven policies,
      which achieves performance improvement on 2D Euclidean TSP compared with vanilla LKH. It is a

847   B.2     W ORD 2 VEC -BASED G RAPH E MBEDDING
848
849   We mainly discuss about Graph Embedding (GE) methods based on the language model word2vec (?).
850   In this branch, GE methods have been designed for different types of networks, including homoge-
851   neous networks (Perozzi et al., 2014; Grover & Leskovec, 2016; Tang et al., 2015), heterogeneous
852   networks (Dong et al., 2017) and multiplex networks (Liu et al., 2017; Qu et al., 2017; Xiong
853   et al., 2021). By designing the input node sequences, GE methods achieve to preserve specific
854   types of information, e.g., low-order proximity (LINE (Tang et al., 2015),) structure similarity
855
      (struc2vec (Ribeiro et al., 2017)), versatile similarity measures (VERSE (Tsitsulin et al., 2018)),
      and cross-network alignment relations (CENALP (Du et al., 2022)). Another line of GE research is
856
      developing methods based on matrix factorization. GraRep (Cao et al., 2015) solves the embedding
857
      by matrix factorization for random walk and Skip-Gram while also taking high-order proximity
858   information into consideration. NetMF (Qiu et al., 2018) proposes to unify some word2vec-based
859   GE methods including LINE, DeepWalk, and node2vec, etc. within a matrix factorization framework,
860   and NetSMF (Qiu et al., 2019b) treats the network embedding task as sparse matrix factorization.
861   AROPE and HOPE (Zhang et al., 2018; Ou et al., 2016) propose to preserve multi-order proximity by
862   matrix factorization with some mathematical tricks. Methods based on deep neural networks (Wang
863   et al., 2016) especially graph neural networks (Hamilton et al., 2017; Kipf & Welling, 2017; Xu et al.,
      2018) are also influential in literature of GE.

      C    S OME T ECHNICAL K EY P OINTS OF LKH
865
866
      For readers not interested in how LKH works, just skip this part and regard LKH as a black box
867   that works efficiently to find near-optimal tours for TSP. In LKH, there are three main components
868   working in series that contribute to its strong performance,
869
870   Step 1. Compute the α-Nearest Measure. Suppose L(T ) is the length of the minimum 1-tree
871   (Def. 2) of graph G = (V, E) and L(T + (i, j)) is the length of the minimum 1-tree containing edge
872   (i, j), the α-measure of edge (i, j) can be calculated as α(i, j) = L(T + (i, j)) − L(T ). α-measures
873   are used for specifying the edge candidate set which consists of p edges with the smallest α-measures
874   connected to each node (p = 5 as default).
875   Definition 2 (1-tree). Let G = (V, E) be a undirected weighted graph where V = {1, 2, · · · , n} is
876   the set of nodes and E = {(i, j)|i ∈ V, j ∈ V} is the set of edges. A 1-tree for a graph G = (V, E)
877   is a spanning tree on the node set V\{1} combined with two edges from E incident to node 1. A
878   minimum 1-tree T is a 1-tree of minimum length.
879
880   Step 2. Node Penalties. LKH uses a subgradient optimization technique to obtain a penalty πi over
881   each node i, then modify the distance as Cij ← Cij + πi + πj . This operation changes the distances
882
      but would not affect the optimal solution.
883
884
      Step 3. Search Solutions by λ-Opt. λ edges in the current tour will be exchanged by another set
      of λ edges, all of which should be included in the candidate sets of the original λ edges, to improve
885
      the tour until no such exchanges can be found.
