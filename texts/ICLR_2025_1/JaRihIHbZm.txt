                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Video generation has been used to generate visual plans for controlling robotic
010
               systems. Given an image observation and a language instruction, previous work
011
               has generated video plans which are then converted to robot controls to be ex-
012            ecuted. However, a major bottleneck in leveraging video generation for control
013            lies in the quality of the generated videos, which often suffer from hallucinatory
014            content and unrealistic physics, resulting in low task success when control actions
015            are extracted from the generated videos. While scaling up dataset and model size
016            provides a partial solution, integrating external feedback is both natural and es-
017            sential for grounding video generation in the real world. With this observation,
018            we propose VideoAgent for self-improving generated video plans based on exter-
019            nal feedback. Instead of directly executing the generated video plan, VideoAgent
020
               first refines the generated video plans using a novel procedure which we call self-
               conditioning consistency, utilizing feedback from a pretrained vision-language
021
               model (VLM). As the refined video plan is being executed, VideoAgent collects
022
               additional data from the environment to further improve video plan generation.
023            Experiments in simulated robotic manipulation from MetaWorld and iTHOR show
024            that VideoAgent drastically reduces hallucination, thereby boosting success rate
025            of downstream manipulation tasks. We further illustrate that VideoAgent can ef-
026            fectively refine real-robot videos, providing an early indicator that robotics can be
027            an effective tool in grounding video generation in the physical world.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
      Large text-to-video models pretrained on internet-scale data have broad applications such as gen-
031
      erating creative video content (Ho et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2022; Singer et al., 2022) and creating
032   novel games (Bruce et al., 2024), animations (Wang et al., 2019), and movies (Zhu et al., 2023). Fur-
033   thermore, recent work show that video generation can serve as simulators of the real-world (Yang
034   et al., 2023b; Brooks et al., 2024), as well as policies with unified observation and action space (Du
035   et al., 2024; Ko et al., 2023; Du et al., 2023). These recent applications of text-to-video generation
036   models hold the great promise of internet-scale knowledge transfer (e.g., from generating human
037   videos to generating robot videos), as well as paving the way to generalist agent (e.g., a single pol-
038   icy that can control multiple robots with different morphologies in different environments to perform
039   diverse tasks).
040   Nevertheless, text-to-video models have only had limited success in downstream applications in
041   reality. For instance, in video generation as policy (Du et al., 2024; Ko et al., 2023), when an ob-
042
      servation image and a language instruction are given to a video generation model, generated videos
      often hallucinate (e.g., objects randomly appear or disappear) or violate physical laws (e.g., a robot
043
      hand going through an object) (Yang et al., 2023b; Brooks et al., 2024). Such hallucinations and
044
      unrealistic physics have led to low task success rate when generated videos are converted to con-
045   trol actions through inverse dynamics models, goal conditioned policies, or other action extraction
046   mechanisms (Wen et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; Ajay et al., 2024).
047
      While scaling up dataset and model size can be effective in reducing hallucination in large language
048   models (LLMs) (Hoffmann et al., 2022), scaling is more difficult in video generation models. This is
049   partially because language labels for videos are labor intensive to curate. Moreover, video generation
050   has not converged to an architecture that is more favourable to scaling (Yang et al., 2024). Scaling
051   aside, being able to incorporate external feedback to improve generation is one of the other most
052   important breakthrough in LLMs (Ouyang et al., 2022b). It is therefore natural to wonder what kind
053   of feedback is available for text-to-video models, and how we can incorporate these feedback to
      further improve the quality of the generated videos.

069   To answer this question, we explore two types of feedback that are natural to acquire for video gen-
070   eration models, namely AI feedback from a vision-language model (VLM) and real-world execution
071   feedback when generated videos are converted to motor controls. To utilize these feedback for self-
072   improvement, we propose VideoAgent. Different from video generation as policy, which directly
073
      turns a generated video into control actions (Du et al., 2023; Ko et al., 2023), VideoAgent is trained
      to refine a generated video plan iteratively using feedback from a pretrained VLM. During inference,
074
      VideoAgent queries the VLM to select the best refined video plan, followed by execution of the plan
075
      in the environment. During online execution, VideoAgent observes whether the task was success-
076   fully completed and further improves the video generation model based on the execution feedback
077   from the environment and additional data collected from the environment. The improvement to the
078   generated video plan comes in two folds: First, we propose self-conditioning consistency for video
079   diffusion model inspired by consistency models (Song et al., 2023; Heek et al., 2024), which enables
080   low-quality samples from a video diffusion model to be further refined into high-quality samples.
081   Second, when online access to the environment is available, VideoAgent executes the current video
082   policy and collect additional successful trajectories to further finetune the video generation model
083   on the successful trajectories. A visual illustration of VideoAgent is shown in Figure 1.
084   We first evaluate the performance of VideoAgent in two simulated robotics manipulation environ-
085   ments, Meta-World (Yu et al., 2020) and iTHOR (Kolve et al., 2017), and show that VideoAgent
086
      improves task success across all environments and tasks evaluated. VideoAgent can even improve
      the success rate of difficult tasks by as much as 4X. Next, we provide a thorough study on the effect
087
      of different components in VideoAgent, including different ways to prompt the VLM and different
088
      types of feedback from the VLM, providing a recipe for utilizing VLM feedback for video genera-
089   tion. Lastly, we illustrate that VideoAgent can iteratively improve real-robot videos, providing early
090   signal that robotics can be an important mean to ground video generation models in the real world.

148   3   V IDEO G ENERATION AS AGENT
149   In this section, we introduce VideoAgent to improve video plan generation. Section 3.1 establishes
150   a new concept for video diffusion models, termed self-conditioning consistency, which enables iter-
151   ative refinement of video plans. In Section 3.2, we discuss how the video diffusion model trained
152   with self-conditioning consistency can be utilized to refine generated video plans during inference.
153   Finally, Section 3.3 explores how VideoAgent completes the self-improvement loop by collecting
154   additional online data to further train the video generation and refinement model.
155   3.1 V IDEO REFINEMENT THROUGH SELF - CONDITIONING CONSISTENCY
156   We consider first-frame-and-language conditioned video generation following (Du et al., 2023; Ko
157   et al., 2023), which generates a sequence of image frames to complete the task described by the
158   language starting from the initial image. Generated videos often exhibit realistic segments (e.g.,
159   the beginning) alongside hallucinated segments (e.g., the end) (Yang et al., 2023b). Hence, while
160   a video plan may not fully complete the specified task, the realistic segments provide a foundation
161   for refinement, focusing on correcting hallucinations to produce a coherent, task-completing video.
      To leverage this partial progress, we propose a novel self-conditioning consistency mechanism. The

300   4.1 DATASETS AND E XPERIMENTAL S ETUPS
301   Datasets and Environments. We follow the same evaluation setting as (Ko et al., 2023), which
302   considers three datasets: Meta-World (Yu et al., 2020), iTHOR (Kolve et al., 2017), and BridgeData
303   V2 (Walke et al., 2023). Meta-World consists of 11 robotic manipulation tasks performed by a sim-
304   ulated Sawyer arm, with video demonstrations captured from three distinct camera angles. iTHOR
305
      is a simulated 2D object navigation benchmark, where an agent searches for specified objects across
      four room types. BridgeData V2 is a real-world dataset of robotic manipulation. See more details
306
      of datasets and environments in Appendix C.
307
308
      Baselines and VideoAgent Variants. We consider the following methods for comparison:
309   • AVDC (baseline). This is the Actions from Video Dense Correspondences (AVDC) (Ko et al.,
310
         2023) baseline, which synthesizes a video and predicts optical flow to infer actions.
311   • AVDC-Replan (baseline). When the movement stalls, AVDC-replan re-runs video generation and
312
         action extraction from the flow model to execute a new plan.
313   • VideoAgent. Our proposed video refinement model through self-conditioning consistency as in-
314
         troduced in Section 3.1. VideoAgent generates video and iteratively refines a video plan. We use
         GPT-4 Turbo for selecting the best video plan during inference (Section 3.2).
315
316
      • VideoAgent-Online. As actions are executed in the online environment, successful trajectories
         are collected and used to continue training the video generation and refinement model, as de-
317
         scribed in Section 3.3.
318
      • VideoAgent-Online-Replan. This variant incorporates online filtering of successful trajectories
319
         with the replanning mechanism, where replanning is conducted first, and more successful trajec-
320
         tories after replanning are added back to the training data.

      5   R ELATED W ORK
487
488   Feedback and Self-improvement in LLMs. Incorporating feedback and preference signals from
489
      feedback into the finetuning process of LLMs, has led to the enormous popularity and practical us-
      ability of the current versions of LLMs as chatbots (Casper et al., 2023). Preference feedback from
490
      humans or other AI systems (Ouyang et al., 2022a; Lee et al., 2023; Kaufmann et al., 2023) are first
491
      collected to train a reward model to guide the LLM’s generation or do implicit policy optimization
492   (Schulman et al., 2017; Rafailov et al., 2024). Furthermore LLMs have shown the ability to further
493   improve by iterative refinement during finetuning and inference (Zelikman et al., 2022; Yuan et al.,
494   2024; Tian et al., 2024). We incorporate this reward driven improvement mechanism in our work,
495   but unlike the LLM setting where the feedback came from a reward model or some proxy of this
496   prefernce model, in our VideoAgent we use natural feedback from real world when simulated videos
497   are turned into actions that are executed in the real world.
498   Image and Video Generation and Editing. With the advent of large scale foundation models
499   pretrained on internet scale data (Bommasani et al., 2021), generation of super realistic multimodal
500   content has become easier. Text generation, image or video generation, and cross-modal generation
501   (OpenAI et al., 2024; Reid et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2021; Ho et al., 2022; Singer et al., 2022; Yang
502   et al., 2023a; Blattmann et al., 2023) has seen major advancements leveraging the autoregressive
503
      and diffusion based models architectures. And moving beyond simple generation, these models
      have been leveraged for guided text, image or video editing and enhancement (Huang et al., 2024)
504
      to improve textual and visual aesthetics applied mostly to generative media (Zhang et al., 2023).
505
      But none of these existing methods focus on grounding a generative simulator in the real world to
506   perform more complex interactive multi-turn agentic and physical tasks needing both perception
507   and control. To solve this bottleneck, we propose VideoAgent to self-improve or edit generated plan
508   based on grounded feedback from real-world to execute robot manipulation tasks.
509   Video Generation for Robot Learning. Video-based learning for robotics has been extensively
510   studied (Nair et al., 2022; Bahl et al., 2022; Shao et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021; Pari et al., 2022;
511   Sharma et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2018; Lee & Ryoo, 2017). Methods use video datasets for visual
512   representation learning, goal extraction, and dynamic models for planning (Finn & Levine, 2017;
513   Kurutach et al., 2018), or imitation learning from expert actions (Fang et al., 2019; Wang et al.,
514   2023; Mani et al., 2024), state sequences (Torabi et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2021; Karnan et al., 2022),
515   and pretraining on videos followed by RL (Baker et al., 2022; Escontrela et al., 2023). Recently,
516   generative models have advanced video-based learning and planning, framing decision-making as
517
      text-conditioned video generation to predict trajectories (Du et al., 2024; Ko et al., 2023; Wen
      et al., 2023). Vision-language and text-to-video models generate long-horizon plans for robotic
518
      tasks through abstract, visually grounded planning (Du et al., 2023; Ajay et al., 2024). Generative
519
      models also simulate agent-environment interactions, enabling zero-shot deployment (Yang et al.,
520   2023b), and test-time feedback for replanning (Bu et al., 2024). Unlike these, our VideoAgent
521   improves video generation during training with real-world feedback and refines actions through
522   test-time self-iteration and replanning.
523
524
      6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
525   We have presented VideoAgent, where a video generation model acts as an agent by generating and
526   refining video plans, converting video plans into actions, executing the actions in an environment,
527   and collecting additional data for further self improvement. Through interaction with an external
528   environment, VideoAgent provides a promising direction for grounding video generation in the real
529
      world, thereby reducing hallucination and unrealistic physics in the generated videos according to
      real-world feedback. In order to fully achieve this overarching goal, VideoAgent needs to overcome
530
      a few limitations, which calls for future work:
531
532
      • In the online setting, VideoAgent only considers filtering for successful trajectories for further
        finetuning. Exploring other algorithms such as online RL is interesting future work.
533
534
      • VideoAgent utilizes optical flow for action extraction. It would be interesting to see how VideoA-
        gent works with inverse dynamics model or image-goal conditioned diffusion policy.
535
536   • We only measured end-to-end task success in simulated robotic evaluation settings. It would be
537
        interesting to see how VideoAgent works with real robotic systems.
538   • As additional data is being collected in the online setting, in addition to finetuning the video
539
        prediction model, one can also finetune the action extraction module (flow model), and the VLM
        feedback model using the additionally collected data, which we defer to future work.

       We collect 15 successful trajectories for each task during every iteration. This standardization helps

1006   D     E XTENDED E XPERIMENTS
1007   D.1 V IDEOS TO ACTION CONVERSION
1008
       We employ the GMFlow optical flow model to predict dense pixel movements across frames. These
1009
       predicted flows serve as the foundation for reconstructing both object movements and robot mo-
1010   tions depicted in the video. The flow predictions allow us to interpret the temporal evolution of the
1011   video in terms of actionable physical dynamics. The optical flow essentially provides a dense corre-
1012   spondence of pixel movements between consecutive frames, which is then used to infer the relative
1013   motion of objects and the robot. This mapping bridges the gap between the high-dimensional video
1014   representation and the low-level control commands required to execute the tasks in a simulated or
1015   real environment.
1016   This method ensures that the generated video plans are actionable and aligned with the task-specific
1017   dynamics, making the video generation process directly relevant to downstream policy learning and
1018   execution.
