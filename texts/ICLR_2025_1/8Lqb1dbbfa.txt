011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Predicting drug-target interaction (DTI) is critical in the drug discovery process.
014            Despite remarkable advances in recent DTI models through the integration of
015
               representations from diverse drug and target encoders, such models often struggle
               to capture the fine-grained interactions between drugs and protein, i.e. the binding
016
               of specific drug atoms (or substructures) and key amino acids of proteins, which is
017
               crucial for understanding the binding mechanisms and optimising drug design. To
018            address this issue, this paper introduces a novel model, called FusionDTI, which
019            uses a token-level Fusion module to effectively learn fine-grained information for
020            Drug-Target Interaction. In particular, our FusionDTI model uses the SELFIES
021            representation of drugs to mitigate sequence fragment invalidation and incorporates
022            the structure-aware (SA) vocabulary of target proteins to address the limitation of
023            amino acid sequences in structural information, additionally leveraging pre-trained
024            language models extensively trained on large-scale biomedical datasets as encoders
025            to capture the complex information of drugs and targets. Experiments on three well-
026
               known benchmark datasets show that our proposed FusionDTI model achieves the
               best performance in DTI prediction compared with eight existing state-of-the-art
027
               baselines. Furthermore, our case study indicates that FusionDTI could highlight
028
               the potential binding sites, enhancing the explainability of the DTI prediction.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The task of predicting drug-target interactions (DTI) plays a pivotal role in the drug discovery
034   progress, as it helps identify potential therapeutic effects of drugs on biological targets facilitating the
035   development of effective treatments (Askr et al., 2023). DTI fundamentally relies on the binding of
036   specific drug atoms (or substructures) and key amino acids of proteins (Schenone et al., 2013). In
037   particular, each binding site is an interaction between a single amino acid and a single drug atom,
038   which we refer to as a fine-grained interaction. For instance, Figure 1 B demonstrates the interaction
039
      between HIV-1 protease and the drug lopinavir. A critical component of this interaction is the forma-
      tion of a hydrogen bond between a ketone group in lopinavir (represented in the SELFIES (Krenn
040
      et al., 2022) notation as [C][=O]) and the side chain of an aspartate residue Asp25 (i.e. Dd) within the
041
      protease (Brik & Wong, 2003; Chandwani & Shuter, 2008). Therefore, capturing such fine-grained
042   interaction information during the fusion of drug and target representations is crucial for building
043   effective DTI prediction models (Yazdani-Jahromi et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2024;
044   Zeng et al., 2024).
045
      To obtain representations of drugs and targets for the DTI task, some previous studies (Lee et al.,
046
      2019; Nguyen et al., 2021) have used graph neural networks (GNNs) or convolutional neural networks
047
      (CNNs) using a fixed-size window, potentially leading to a loss of contextual information, especially
048   when drugs and targets are in a long-term sequence. These models directly concatenate the represen-
049   tations together to make predictions without considering fine-grained interactions. More recently,
050   some computational models (Huang et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2023) employed the fusion module (e.g.
051   Deep Interactive Inference Network (DIIN) (Gong et al., 2018) and Bilinear Attention Network
052   (BAN) (Kim et al., 2018)) to obtain fine-grained interaction information and the 3-mer approach that
053   binds three amino acids together as a target binding site to address the lack of structural information in
      the amino acid sequence. While useful for highlighting possible regions of interaction, these models

078
      of drugs and targets and capturing fine-grained interaction information for DTI remains challenging.

080   To address these challenges, we propose a novel model (called FusionDTI) with a Token-level Fusion
081   (TF) module for an effective learning of fine-grained interactions between drugs and targets. In
082   particular, our FusionDTI model utilises two pre-trained language models (PLMs), namely Saport (Su

085
      representations of drugs and targets, we explore two strategies for the TF module, i.e. Bilinear
086   Attention Network (BAN) (Kim et al., 2018) and Cross Attention Network (CAN) (Li et al., 2021;
087   Vaswani et al., 2017), to find the best approach for integrating the rich contextual embeddings
088   derived from Saport and SELFormer. We conduct a comprehensive performance comparison against
089   eight existing state-of-the-art DTI prediction models. The results show that our proposed model
090   achieves about 6% accuracy improvement over the best baseline on the BindingDB dataset. The main
091   contributions of our study are as follows:
092
093             • We propose FusionDTI, a novel model that leverages PLMs to encode drug SELFIES and
094
                  protein residue and structure for rich semantic representations and uses the token-level
                  fusion to obtain fine-grained interaction information between drugs and targets effectively.
095
096             • We compare two TF modules: CAN and BAN and analyse the influence of fusion scales
097               based on FusionDTI, demonstrating that CAN is superior for DTI prediction both in terms
098

099             • We conduct a case study of three drug-target pairs by FusionDTI to evaluate whether
100               potential binding sites would be highlighted for the DTI prediction explainability.

104   2.1      D RUG - TARGET I NTERACTION P REDICTION
105
106   DTI prediction serves as an important step in the process of drug discovery (Dara et al., 2022).
107   Traditional biomedical measurements from wet experiments are reliable but have a notably high cost
      and time-consuming development cycle, preventing their application on large-scale data (Zitnik et al.,


                                                                                    2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2019). In contrast, identifying high-confidence DTI pairs by computational models markedly narrow
109   down the search scope of drug candidate libraries, and aims to identify drugs most likely to bind to a
110   target. Support vector machine (SVM) (Cortes & Vapnik, 1995) and random forest (RF) (Ho, 1995)
111   are two traditional computational models for DTI by concatenating fingerprint ECFP4 (Rogers &
112   Hahn, 2010) and PSC features (Cao et al., 2013). Later works focused on representation learning
113   approaches, such as CNNs and GNNs Lee et al. (2019); Nguyen et al. (2021). For example,
114   DeepConv-DTI (Lee et al., 2019) employed CNNs and a global max-pooling layer to extract local
115   protein sequence patterns. Similarly, GraphDTA (Nguyen et al., 2021) used GNNs for drug graph
116   encoding and CNNs for protein sequence encoding. Furthermore, MolTrans (Huang et al., 2021)
117
      introduced an adaptation of the transformer for encoding, further enhanced by a DIIN module (Gong
      et al., 2018) to learn fine-grained interactions. Additionally, DrugBAN (Bai et al., 2023) incorporated
118
      a deep BAN (Kim et al., 2018) framework with domain adaptation to facilitate explicit pairwise
119
      fine-grained interaction learning between drugs and targets. Moreover, BioT5 (Pei et al., 2023) has
120   been proposed as a comprehensive pre-training framework that integrates cross-modelling in biology
121   in the DTI task. More recently, SiamDTI (Zhang et al., 2024) utilises a double-channel network
122   structure to acquire local and global protein information for cross-field DTI prediction. Despite
123   these advances, these models have not proposed an effective way to capture fine-grained interaction
124   information in the DTI.
125

      2.2   D RUG AND P ROTEIN R EPRESENTATION
128
129
      For drug molecules, most existing methods represent the input by the Simplified Molecular Input Line
      Entry System (SMILES) (Weininger, 1988; Weininger et al., 1989). However, SMILES suffers from
130
      numerous problems in terms of validity and robustness, and some valuable information about the drug
131
      structure may be lost which may prevent the model from efficiently mining the knowledge hidden in
132   the data reducing the predictive performance of the model (Krenn et al., 2022). In particular, SMILES
133   fragments are often invalid and inconsistent with the substructural information of the drug. To address
134   the limitations of SMILES, we apply SELFIES (Krenn et al., 2022), a string-based representation
135   that circumvents the issue of robustness and that always generates valid molecular graphs for each
136   character (Krenn et al., 2022).
137
      Regarding proteins, the conventional approach uses amino acid sequences as model inputs (Huang
138   et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2023), overlooking the crucial structural information of the protein. Inspired
139   by the SA vocabulary of SaProt (Su et al., 2023), the SaProt enhances inputs by amalgamating each
140   residue from the amino acid sequence with a 3D geometric feature that is obtained by encoding
141   the structure information of the protein using Foldseek (Van Kempen et al., 2024). This innovative
142   combination offers richer protein representations through the SA vocabulary, contributing to the
143   discovery of fine-grained interactions. Our proposed model employs SELFIES for drug encoding and
144   uses SaProt encoding for proteins to generate the semantic representations for both drugs and targets.
145

      2.3   M OLECULAR AND P ROTEIN L ANGUAGE M ODELS
147
148
      Molecular language models that train on the large-scale molecular corpus to capture the subtleties
149
      of chemical structures and their biological activities have set new standards in encoding chemi-
150   cal compounds achieving meaningful representations Ying et al. (2021); Rong et al. (2020). For
151   example, ChemBERTa-2 (Ahmad et al., 2022) used RoBERTa-based architectures to capture intri-
152   cate molecular patterns, significantly enhancing the precision of property prediction. Subsequently,
153   MoLFormer (Ross et al., 2022) focused on leveraging the self-attention mechanism to interpret the
154   complex, non-linear interactions within molecules, while SELFormer (Yüksel et al., 2023) employed
155   SELFIES, ensuring valid and interpretable chemical structures.
156   Protein language models have revolutionized the way we understand and represent protein sequences,
157   offering richer semantic representations (Elnaggar et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2023; Su et al., 2023).
158   These models leverage the vast corpus of biological sequence data, learning intricate patterns and
159   features that define the protein functionality and interactions. ProtBERT (Elnaggar et al., 2021)
160   and ESM (Lin et al., 2023) applied a transformer architecture to protein sequences, capturing the
161   complex relationships between amino acids. Saport (Su et al., 2023) further enhanced this approach
      by integrating SA vocabularies to provide protein structure information. Furthermore, SaprotHub (Su


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      et al., 2024) offers a platform that enables biologists to train, deploy, and share protein models
163   efficiently. Importantly, our FusionDTI is flexible enough to use each of them as a protein encoder.

166   3     M ETHODOLOGY
167
168   3.1   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
169
170   Given a sequence-based input drug-target pair, the DTI prediction task aims to predict an interaction
171   probability score p ∈ [0, 1] between the given drug-target pair, which is typically achieved through
172   learning a joint representation F space from the given sequence-based inputs. To address the DTI
173   task and effectively capture fine-grained interaction, we proposed a novel model, called FusionDTI,
174   which is a bi-encoder model Liu et al. (2021) with a fusion module that fuses the representations
175
      of drugs and targets. The overall framework of FusionDTI is illustrated in Figure 1 A. In general,
      FusionDTI takes sequence-based inputs of drugs and targets, which are encoded into token-level
176
      representation vectors by two frozen encoders. Then, a fusion module fuses the representations to
177
      capture fine-grained binding information for a final prediction through a prediction head.
178
179   Input: The initial inputs of drugs and targets are string-based representations. For protein P, the SA
180   vocabulary (Su et al., 2023; Van Kempen et al., 2024) is employed, where each residue is replaced by
181
      one of 441 SA vocabularies that bind an amino acid to a 3D geometric feature to address the lack of
      structural information in amino acid sequences. For drug D, as mentioned in the previous section, we
182
      use the SELFIES, which is a formal syntax that always generates valid molecular graphs (Krenn et al.,
183
      2022). We provide the steps and code for obtaining SA and SELFIES sequences in Appendix A.3.
184
185   Encoder: The proposed model contains two frozen encoders: Saport (Su et al., 2023) and SELF-
186   ormer (Yüksel et al., 2023), which generate a drug representation D and a protein representation
187
      P separately. It is of note that FusionDTI is flexible enough to easily replace encoders with other
      advanced PLMs. Furthermore, D and P are stored in memory for later-stage online training.
188
189   Fusion module: In developing FusionDTI, we have investigated two options for the fusion module:
190   BAN and CAN to fuse representations, as indicated in Figure 2. The CAN is utilised to fuse each pair
191   as D∗ and P∗ , and then concatenate them into one F for fine-grained binding information. For BAN,
192   we need to obtain the bilinear attention map and then generate F through the bilinear pooling layer.
193   Prediction head: Finally, we obtain the probability score p of the DTI prediction by a multilayer
194   perceptron (MLP) classifier trained with the binary cross-entropy loss, i.e. p = MLP(F).
195
      Since the encoders and the fusion module constitute the key components of our FusionDTI model,
196
      we will describe them in detail in the following subsections.

199   3.2   D RUG AND P ROTEIN E NCODERS
200
201
      Employing sequences with detailed biological functions and structures is a critical step in exploring
      the fine-grained binding of drugs and targets. For drugs, SMILES is the most commonly used input se-
202
      quence but suffers from invalid sequence segments and potential loss of structural information (Krenn
203
      et al., 2022). To address the limitations, we transform SMILES into SELFIES, a formal grammar
204   that generates a valid molecular graph for each element (Krenn et al., 2022). Besides, to address the
205   lack of structural information in the amino acid sequences, we utilise the SA sequence of targets to
206   combine each amino acid with an SA vocabulary by Foldseek (Van Kempen et al., 2024).
207
      PLMs have shown promising achievements in the biomedical domain leveraging transformers since
208
      they pay attention to contextual information and are pre-trained on large-scale biomedical databases.
209
      Therefore, we utilise Saport (Su et al., 2023) as a protein encoder to encode protein input P of both
210   the SA sequence and amino acid sequence. Meanwhile, SELFormer (Yüksel et al., 2023) is used as
211   our drug encoder to encode the drug SELFIES input D. Then these encoded protein representation
212   P and drug representation D are further used as inputs for the later fusion module (Subsection 3.3).
213   These rich contextual representations ensure that we can explore the fine-grained binding information
214   effectively. To further justify this, we also compare our encoders with other existing protein language
215   models (such as ESM-2 (Lin et al., 2023)) and molecular language models (such as MoLFormer (Ross
      et al., 2022) and ChemBERTa-2 (Ahmad et al., 2022)), and the results can be found in Section 4.7.

243   3.3.1        B ILINEAR ATTENTION N ETWORK (BAN)
244
245   Motivated by DrugBAN (Bai et al., 2023), our model considers BAN (Kim et al., 2018) as an option
246   of the fusion module to learn pairwise fine-grained interactions between drug D ∈ RM ×ϕ and target
247
      P ∈ RN ×ρ , denoted as FusionDTI-BAN. For BAN as indicated in Figure 2, bilinear attention maps
      are obtained by a bilinear interaction modelling to capture pairwise weights in step 1, and then the
248
      bilinear pooling layer to extract a joint representation F. The equation for BAN is shown below:
249
250                                                   F = BAN(P, D; Att)
251                                                                                                                                                       (1)
                                                        = SumPool(σ(P⊤ U) · Att · σ(D⊤ V), s),
252
253   where U ∈ RN ×K and V ∈ RM ×K are transformation matrices for representations. SumPool is an
254   operation that performs a one-dimensional and non-overlapped sum pooling operation with stride s
255   and σ(·) denotes a non-linear activation function with ReLU(·). Att ∈ Rρ×ϕ represents the bilinear
256   attention maps using the Hadamard product and matrix-matrix multiplication and is defined as:
257
                                                        Att = ((1 · q⊤ ) ◦ σ(P⊤ U)) · σ(V⊤ D),                                                            (2)
258
259   Here, 1 ∈ Rρ is a fixed all-ones vector, q ∈ RK is a learnable weight vector and ◦ denotes the
260   Hadamard product. In this way, pairwise interactions contribute sub-structural pairs to the prediction.
261
      BAN captures the token-level interactions between the protein and drug representations without
262
      considering the relationships within each sequence itself, which may limit its ability to understand
263   deeper contextual dependencies.

      4.1   DATASETS AND BASELINES
300
301   Three public DTI datasets, namely BindingDB (Gilson et al., 2016), BioSNAP (Zitnik et al., 2018)
302   and Human (Liu et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2020), are used for evaluation, where each dataset is
303   split into training, validation, and test sets with a 7:1:2 ratio using two different splitting strategies:
304   in-domain and cross-domain. For the in-domain split, the datasets are randomly divided. For the
305   cross-domain setting, the datasets are split such that the drugs and targets in the test set do not overlap
306   with those in the training set, making it a more challenging scenario where models must generalise to
307
      novel drug-target interactions. Since DTI is a binary classification task, we use AUROC (area under
      the receiver operating characteristic curve) (Bai et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2021) and AUPRC (area
308
      under the precision-call curve) (Lee et al., 2019; Nguyen et al., 2021) as the major metrics to evaluate
309
      a model’s performance.
310
311   We compare FusionDTI with eight baseline models in the DTI prediction task. These models include
312   two traditional machine learning methods such as SVM (Cortes & Vapnik, 1995) and Random Forest
313
      (RF) (Ho, 1995), as well as five deep learning methods including DeepConv-DTI (Lee et al., 2019),
      GraphDTA (Nguyen et al., 2021), MolTrans (Huang et al., 2021), DrugBAN (Bai et al., 2023) and
314
      SiamDTI (Zhang et al., 2024). The latter five models employ the same two-stage process whereby
315
      the drug and target features are initially extracted by specialised encoders before being integrated for
316   prediction. In addition, we also include the BioT5 (Pei et al., 2023) model, which is a biomedical
317   pre-trained language model that could directly predict the DTI. Further details regarding the datasets,
318   baseline models, and the methodology for generating drug SELFIES and protein SA sequences are
319   provided in Appendix A.3.

445     4.6          A NALYSIS OF F USION S CALES
446
447
       In assessing fusion representations, it is critical to determine whether more fine-grained modelling
       enhances the predictive performance. Thus, we define a grouping function with the parameter g
448
       (Group size) for averaging tokens within each group before the CAN fusion module. The parameter
449
       g, representing the number of tokens per group, controls the granularity of the attention mechanism.
450    Specifically, when g is set to 1, the fusion operates at the token level, where each token is considered
451    independently. In contrast, when g is set to 512, the fusion occurs at a global level, considering the
452    entire embedding as a single unit. We have the flexibility to control the fusion scale for the drug
453    and protein representations, but the token length must be divisible by the group size. As shown in
454    Figure 5, as the number of tokens per group increases from 1 to 512 (Maximum Token Length), the
455    performance of the FusionDTI model declines accordingly. This also aligns with the biomedical rules
456    governing drug-protein interactions, where the principal factor influencing the binding is the interplay
457    between the key atoms or substructures in the drug and primary residues in the protein. Furthermore,
458
       the CAN module outperforms BAN consistently at various scale settings, indicating that CAN better
       accesses the information between the drug and target. Consequently, this supports that the more
459
       detailed the interaction information obtained between the drugs and targets by the fusion module, the
460
       more beneficial it is for the enhancement of the model’s prediction performance.

      5     C ONCLUSIONS
522
523
      With the rapid increase of new diseases and the urgent need for innovative drugs, it is critical to
524   capture and gauge fine-grained interactions, since the binding of specific drug atoms to the main
525   amino acids is key to the DTI task. Despite some achievements, fine-grained interaction information
526   is not effectively captured. To address this challenge, we introduce FusionDTI uses token-level
527   fusion to effectively obtain fine-grained interaction information between drugs and targets. Through
528   experiments on three well-known benchmark datasets, we demonstrate that our proposed FusionDTI
529   model outperforms eight state-of-the-art baselines in DTI prediction, particularly in the more realistic
530   cross-domain scenario. Additionally, we show that the attention weights of the token-level fusion
531   module in our model can highlight potential binding sites, providing a certain level of explainability.
532   Limitations: Even if our proposed model identifies potentially useful DTI, these predictions need to
533   be validated by wet experiments, a time-consuming and expensive process.
534
      Potential impacts: We have shown that FusionDTI is effective and efficient in screening for possible
535
      DTI in large-scale data as well as in locating potential binding sites in the process of drug design.
536   However, it is not directly applicable to human medical therapy and other biomedical interactions
537   because it lacks clinical validation and regulatory approval for medical use.
538
539
      For future studies, we aim to investigate token-level interaction in more detail and to apply it to other
      biomedical scenarios, such as drug-drug interactions and protein-protein interactions.

875   The time complexity of BAN depends on the computation of bilinear interaction maps. The bilinear
876
      attention involves a Hadamard product and further matrix operations as given in Equation (2). The
      computation of U T P and V T D requires O(N · ρ · K) and O(M · ϕ · K) operations, respectively.
877
      Here, K denotes the dimensionality of the transformation, which is the rank of the feature space
878
      to which the protein and drug features are projected. When the token length is equal to the feature
879   dimension and the dimensions of transformation are two times either, the overall time complexity is
880   O(ρ · ϕ · K).
881
882
      For the token-level interaction in the DTI task, the time complexity is also markedly influenced by
      the attention mechanisms. It also satisfies the condition that the token length is equal to the feature
883
      dimension of the drug and protein. With multi-head attention heads (H = 8), the complexity for
884
      computing the queries, keys, and values in the Equation (6) and (7), as well as the softmax attention
885   weights, is given by O(H · n · m · h), where mandn represents the token lengths for the drug
886   and protein, respectively, and h is the hidden dimension. Since each head contributes its own set
887   of computations and the attention mechanism operates over all tokens, the m · n term (stemming
888   from the softmax operation across the token length) becomes significant. This leads to a total time
889   complexity of O(m · n · h) per batch for the attention mechanism.
890

891   CAN is lower than BAN in the case of the same input protein and drug features. BAN is markedly
892   affected by the transformation dimension K. When the K is larger than the token and feature
893   dimension, the time complexity of BAN is higher than CAN. However, we observe that the number
894   of parameters in BAN is smaller than that of CAN via the Pytroch package, as shown in Table 9.
