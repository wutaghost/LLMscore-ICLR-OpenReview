009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Although Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT-4 perform excellently in ma-
012            chine translation, their high costs and scalability make them unavailable in many
013            scenarios. Recently, there has been increased effort to build smaller LLMs that
014            can achieve comparable performance. However, while typical instruction tuning
015            methods tend to directly mimic reference translations, leading to less meaningful
016            results, recent preference optimization methods have shown improvements. De-
017            spite this, they still fail to effectively utilize crucial preference information during
018            inference. In this paper, we introduce Preference-Enhanced Instruction Tuning
019
               (PEIT), a novel method that explicitly incorporates preferences into both the in-
               struction fine-tuning and the inference phase. Our extensive experiments show
020
               that PEIT not only improves translation quality but also significantly outperforms
021
               state-of-the-art preference optimization methods and instruction tuning baselines
022            on multiple language benchmarks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
028
      Large language models (LLMs), such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), have been showing predom-
      inant performance in machine translation (MT) (Hendy et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Jiao et al.,
029
      2023b). However, attaining such level of performance often requires the expense of substantial
030
      model size, significant infrastructure demands, and high deployment costs. To address these chal-
031   lenges, recent research has shifted toward fine-tuning smaller LLMs to enhance translation capa-
032   bilities while mitigating the associated resource overhead. (Zeng et al., 2023; Jiao et al., 2023a;
033   Kudugunta et al., 2024; Zan et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Wu et al.,
034   2024a; Xu et al., 2024b). For example, ALMA (Xu et al., 2023) enhances the multilingual capabil-
035   ities of LLaMA-2 (Touvron et al., 2023b) by fine-tuning with non-English data and refining it with
036   high-quality translation instruction data. Similarly, Aya (Aryabumi et al., 2024) fine-tunes smaller
037   LLMs using a larger amount of translation instruction examples from the Aya Dataset (Singh et al.,
038   2024), allowing it to achieve stronger translation performance.
039   Simple instruction tuning using translation pairs has its limitations, primarily due to the quality is-
040   sues inherent in the reference data — even when it is human-generated (Xu et al., 2024b; He et al.,
041   2024; Wu et al., 2024b). These imperfections can impede the LLM’s ability to produce high-quality
042   translations, as it may merely learn to replicate the references during instruction tuning. To address
043   this limitation, recent works have moved beyond direct instruction tuning, focusing instead on pref-
044   erence optimization (Zhu et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024c; Wang & Xiao, 2024; Xu
045
      et al., 2024b). For example, Contrastive Preference Optimization (CPO) (Xu et al., 2024b) is one
      of the leading approaches, which enables the LLM to learn from preferences between synthesized
046
      preference-rich translation pairs, allowing it to exceed the quality of the original reference data by
047
      optimizing based on comparative judgments rather than simple replication. In human translation,
048   context is often leveraged to enhance translation accuracy(House, 2006). Intuitively, machine trans-
049   lation should benefit from contextual information in a similar way. However, even with preference
050   optimization methods, this information is not effectively utilized due to the issue of prompt shift (Li
051   et al., 2023), which occurs when the preference intentions (e.g., contextual information or trans-
052   lation examples) embedded in the inference prompt misalign with the model’s training data. This
053   misalignment makes it difficult for the model to capture and incorporate these intentions, leading to
      translation bias and outputs that deviate from the expected behavior.

096            preference alignment of the translation model.

225   3.2   S ETTINGS
226
227
      We view PEIT not just as an optimization technique, but as a conceptual approach that highlights
      the importance of providing preference information during both the training and inference phases.
228
      This aids in aligning model outputs with desired translation tendencies and enhances overall per-
229
      formance. To demonstrate the potential of in-context preference learning, we developed a series
230   of progressively comparative methods. Following this, we introduce the baselines chosen for our
231   experiments and explain the rationale behind these selections.
232
233
      SFT Using supervised fine-tuning (SFT) to adapt large language models to specific downstream
      tasks is a fundamental approach. Its effectiveness has been validated through extensive practical
234
      experiments. Therefore, SFT on prefer data serves as the first baseline in our experiments.
235
236   CPO and DPO We also compared the commonly used preference alignment methods in the machine
237   translation field. These two methods are derived from the same optimization goal (Schulman et al.,
238   2017) but reflect different training objectives due to the adoption of distinct assumptions. Therefore,
239
      these two methods serve as the primary comparative methods for preference alignment evaluation.
240   ICFT and ICPFT We set In-Context Fine-Tuning (ICFT) and In-Context-Preference Fine-Tuning
241   (ICPFT) as baselines to compare with SFT and PEIT, demonstrating the validity of our approach
242   and the superiority of PEIT. Specifically, in the SFT scenario, we add a retrieved example to each
243   input during training to create the ICFT setup. For ICPFT, we enhance the example by incorporating
244   preference pair.
245   PE-CPO Preference-Enhanced Contrastive Preference Optimization (PE-CPO) aims to ensure a
246   fairer comparison and to strengthen the baseline, we introduced the concept of PEIT into the CPO
247   method, resulting in the PE-CPO baseline. This enhanced version integrates preference estimation
248   into the CPO framework, allowing the model to utilize in-context preference information during
249   training, providing a more strong baseline against which to evaluate our proposed approach.
250

346   We provide a comprehensive view of the impact of PEIT by examining fine-grained performance and
347   deeper token distribution patterns. We adopt a more detailed comparative approach, going beyond

349   focus is the win rate ratio between PEIT and the alternative methods. By inferring preference ten-

      current text more effectively. This allows it to outperform fitting-based fine-tuning methods.

      than directly comparing win rates.

      4.2   H OW PREFERENCE DATA QUALITY AFFECTS PEIT?
423
424
      In this section, we explore how the quality of preference data affects the performance of PEIT. To
425
      investigate this, we employed two methods to generate preference data for our experiments. Using
426   the Feedback from Inductive Biases perspective (Jiang et al., 2024), we transformed this generated
427   data into preference datasets, allowing us to assess how variations in data quality influence PEIT’s
428   ability to learn and generalize preference intentions. From this perspective, we treat the original
429   instruction data as embodying test-time preferences, and any generated translation—regardless of
430   quality—as misaligned with these preferences due to differing inductive biases in the models. This
431   approach allows us to construct preference datasets where the original instructions serve as the
      preferred option, while translations generated by both methods are considered less preferred.

439   GPT-Generated Translations The first method involves using GPT-4 to generate translations. We
440   provided GPT-4 with the original instruction data, which represents the test-time preferences, and
441   prompted it to produce translations. Although GPT-4 can generate high-quality, fluent, and gram-
442   matically correct translations, these outputs may not fully reflect the specific preferences encoded in
443   the original instruction data due to differing inductive biases. We regard these GPT-generated trans-
444   lations as less preferred compared to the original instructions, as they may not capture the subtle
445   preferences embedded in the instruction data.
446   Self-Paraphrasing The second method involves training the model on the test set and repeatedly
447   generating translations through a paraphrasing process. Specifically, we fine-tuned the model on
448   the training set using Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) to perform the translation task. By generating
449   multiple translations of the same training set, we collected a series of paraphrased outputs. These
450   paraphrased translations are syntactically different but semantically similar to the originals. How-
451   ever, since they are produced by the model itself, they may reflect the model’s own inductive biases
452   rather than the specific preferences encoded in the original instruction data. As a result, we treat
453
      these translated versions as less preferred options in our preference dataset.
454   We compared the results of all the baselines mentioned in this study on the preference datasets
455   constructed using these two methods. The experiments demonstrate that higher-quality distractor
456   data leads to higher-quality preference data, with GPT-4 generated data achieving better results
457   across all methods. We specifically examine the differences between data generated using GPT-
458   Generated and Self-Paraphrasing methods. The data generated by GPT shows superior quality in
459
      various dimensions compared to Self-Paraphrasing, while semantically it does not exhibit significant
      deficiencies compared to the original instruction data, differing only in preference tendencies.
460
461   Compared to CPO’s approach of constructing preference data, where the lowest-quality data is used
462   as the distractor in preference pairs, our experimental results seem to suggest a different recommen-
463   dation. However, the underlying principle is the same: maximizing the quality of distractor data is
464   essential to increasing the value of preference data.

      5   C ONCLUSION
467
468
      In this paper,we introduce preference-enhanced instruction tuning (PEIT) as a novel approach to
469
      enhancing machine translation quality by leveraging in-context preference learning with large lan-
470   guage models. Experiments across multiple languages demonstrate that PEIT outperforms existing
471   methods like CPO, DPO, and SFT, especially when high-quality preference data is utilized. PEIT
472   achieves such outstanding performance even without fully exploring the form of explicit preference
473   information. Objectively speaking, PEIT is orthogonal to methods like CoT (Wei et al., 2023),
474   ReFT (Luong et al., 2024), and even LLM reasoning. Overall, PEIT offers a promising direction
475   for improving machine translation systems by effectively integrating explicit preference information
476   through in-context learning.
