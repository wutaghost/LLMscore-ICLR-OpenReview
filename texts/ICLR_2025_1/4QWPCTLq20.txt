009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated exceptional capabilities in
012            integrating contextual knowledge, but their deployment is often constrained by
013            the substantial computational resources required for long text sequences. To mit-
014            igate the inference time cost associated with attention mechanisms, LLMs utilize
015            key-value embedding caching techniques (KV cache), which introduce signifi-
016
               cant storage pressure. In this paper, we propose IntelLLM, a novel and efficient
               approach to KV cache compression that strikes a balance between compression
017
               rate and performance. Drawing inspiration from sparse attention mechanism, we
018
               observe that only a small subset of tokens in lengthy texts capture the majority of
019            attention weights. This sparsity, intrinsic to the attention mechanism, serves as the
020            foundation for improving the KV compression ratio through a strategic eviction
021            method. IntelLLM is composed of center of gravity eviction (CGE) strategy and
022            remote gap localization (RGL) strategy. CGE is designed to address the poten-
023            tial loss of important semantic dependencies when evicting high-sparsity tokens,
024            which prioritizes the retention of key tokens by shielding the center of gravity of
025            attention during inference, thereby preserving critical information and optimizing
026            the efficiency of attention computation. Additionally, RGL is proposed to lever-
027
               age implicit positional features to maintain long-range dependencies, inspired by
               advancements in location encoding research. Our KV compression approach in-
028
               tegrates seamlessly with existing LLMs, requiring minimal code modifications
029
               without the need for fine-tuning or model parameter changes. IntelLLM not only
030            significantly reduces the storage requirements for KV cache but also consistently
031            outperforms full KV models in long text processing tasks, while utilizing only
032            50% of the typical KV cache expenses.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Large Language Models (LLMs) have rapidly expanded from academic research to industrial appli-
038   cations Wu et al. (2023)Zhao et al. (2024a) due to their exceptional capabilities in language under-
039
      standing and generation, knowledge retention and retrieval, and multi-task learning Ge et al. (2024).
      However, as LLM applications evolve, the associated computational costs have become a critical
040
      challenge. During autoregressive inference in LLMs, the quadratic complexity of the transformer
041
      architecture and the large number of model parameters result in significant computational overhead
042   Zhou et al. (2024). To avoid recomputation, KV caches are preserved. However, the size of the
043   KV cache grows proportionally with the length of the inference sequence, eventually surpassing the
044   model’s parameter size as the sequence accumulates, with the size of the KV cache emerging as
045   a key issue. This raises a significant challenge for LLM deployment: how to reduce the memory
046   consumption of the KV cache without compromising model accuracy.
047
      To tackle this challenge, Hooper et al. (2024b)Ye et al.Zhang et al. (b) have proposed various strate-
048   gies to optimize KV caching and reduce memory consumption during inference. One prominent
049   approach is cache compression Liu et al. (2024), which minimizes storage requirements by elim-
050   inating redundant information in the KV cache. Low-rank approximation techniques Zhang et al.
051   (a) have been employed to compress the original high-dimensional KV representations into lower-
052   dimensional spaces. Additionally, sparse attention mechanisms Liu et al. (2023)Fu et al. have been
053   introduced to selectively retain only the most critical key-value pairs, discarding less relevant in-
      formation and reducing both computation and cache space requirements. However, these methods


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      present two main limitations: firstly, there is a trade-off between memory savings and accuracy, mak-
055   ing it difficult to strike the right balance; secondly, the added complexity of these strategies often
056   leads to a conflict between increased computational overhead and memory optimization. Mean-
057   while, the windowed attention approach Beltagy et al. (2020) has been proposed as a solution to
058   reduce KV caching costs during inference. While this approach effectively reduces memory con-
059   sumption, it suffers from significant accuracy degradation when handling sequences that exceed the
060   predefined memory window size. Comparable to the fixed window design, numerous studies have
061   explored reverse thinking, which involve adapting long texts to a preset window size. This can be
062   viewed as a hierarchical strategy that condenses the text, extracting only the core information to al-
063
      leviate KV cache pressure Song et al. (2024)Shao et al. (2024)He et al. (2024). These methods offer
      valuable insights for our research, providing new perspectives on how to balance cache compression
064
      with maintaining model performance.
065
066   Inspired by the above research insights, we propose the hypothesis that in long-text reasoning tasks,
067   LLMs may process information similarly to how humans read and comprehend lengthy articles by
068   relying primarily on key hints. In other words, the KV cache may only need to store the most
069
      important tokens from the redundant text. Through statistical analysis, we identified a remarkably
      high degree of sparsity in the attention layer. This finding validates our hypothesis that LLMs heavily
070
      depend on key cues to achieve their superior long-text memory processing capabilities. It also
071
      suggests that much of the redundant information in long texts may be superfluous for comprehension
072   and can be effectively optimized out of the KV cache.
073
074   In this paper, we introduce IntelLLM, a lightweight framework tailored for long-text inference in
075
      LLMs. IntelLLM incorporates center of gravity eviction (CGE) strategy and remote gap localization
      (RGL) strategy, two novel techniques that effectively balance cache compression and model perfor-
076
      mance, all without fine-tuning. In Section 3, we analyze the shortcomings of the sliding window
077
      mechanism in handling long-sequence inference tasks, focusing on the behavior of the attention
078   layer and providing detailed insights into the attention mechanism’s characteristics. Based on the
079   problem analysis and findings in Section3, Section4 introduces the KV cache eviction and update
080   algorithm for IntelLLM, elaborating on the center of GE strategy and the RGL strategy to mitigate
081   performance loss. In Section5, we present the experimental validation, where IntelLLM is integrated
082   with Llama-3-8B-instruct and Mistral-7B-inst-v0.2, and evaluated using LongBench. Without fine-
083   tuning, IntelLLM achieves performance comparable to baseline models while significantly reducing
084   cache memory usage. Its lightweight design also ensures easy integration into any existing LLM
085   system.

088
089   2.1   LLM L ONG T EXT R EASONING
090
091   The significant computational and memory demands during LLM inference pose critical challenges
092   for deployment in resource-constrained environments, limiting the capacity of LLMs to efficiently
093   process long texts. Prior research has predominantly focused on two areas: long-text compression
094   Zhao et al. (2024b) and model compression strategies Ramesh et al. (2023). Long-text compres-
095
      sion techniques, such as scalable embeddings, prompt compression, and activation beacons, aim
      to reduce storage overhead for extended sequences. Meanwhile, model compression strategies fo-
096
      cus on optimizing the attention mechanism, either by designing more efficient architectures or by
097
      compressing the key-value (KV) cache to alleviate memory pressure. In this paper, we address the
098   bottlenecks in LLM inference, with a particular focus on optimizing the KV cache.
099
100
      2.2   Q UANTIZATION
101
102   For parameter and computation intensive tasks in LLMs, quantization techniques have proven to be
103   highly effective in reducing memory consumption and accelerating inference. Current research pri-
104   marily focuses on enhancing computation-heavy operations through low-bit integer quantization of
105   model weights. For instance, Wu et al. (a) employs INT4 quantization for both weights and caches,
106   significantly reducing GPU memory requirements. Additionally, advanced hyper-quantization meth-
107   ods such as KVQuant Hooper et al. (2024a) and WKVQuant Yue et al. (2024) have been introduced
      to further optimize memory usage. However, while these quantization techniques succeed in com-


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      pressing models from a parametric standpoint, they do not fully address the KV cache pressure
109   inherent in long dialogue history inference tasks. This gap highlights the need for more comprehen-
110   sive strategies to tackle the specific challenges of long-context processing in LLMs.
111
112
113   2.3   KV C OMPRESSION / S PARSE ATTENTION
114
115   KV cache optimization has become a pivotal research area due to the substantial computational bur-
116   den imposed by the quadratic complexity of the attention mechanism. Techniques such as clustering-
117   based hashing Kitaev et al. (2020) and k-nearest neighbors (kNN) Nawrot et al. (2024) methods
118   effectively reduce computational complexity to super-linear levels. Moreover, the introduction of
119   sparse Transformers improves computational efficiency through sparse attention mechanisms. Ap-
120   proaches like MQA Shazeer (2019) and GQA Ainslie et al. (2023) for instance, optimize the atten-
121   tion module by redesigning attention heads, while more recent methods focusing on KV cache reuse
122   Gim et al. (2024) and cross-layer cache sharing Brandon et al. (2024) further improve memory effi-
123
      ciency. However, these approaches often incur additional training overheads, posing challenges for
      deployment in resource-constrained environments. This underscores the demand for more efficient
124
      solutions that can minimize resource consumption without sacrificing performance.
125
126
127
      2.4   O FFLOADING
128
129
      To address the high memory demands of KV caches on GPUs, researchers have begun exploring
130
      alternative memory strategies involving multi-cluster systems or CPUs. For instance, DistKV-LLM
131   enhances cloud service inference by distributing KV caches across multiple servers. Similarly,
132   Pan et al. (2024) Wu et al. (b) propose a method that offloads KV caches to CPUs, where only a
133   small portion of the cache is reloaded to the GPU, significantly lowering the memory requirements
134   for high-performance computing (HPC) devices. Although these approaches optimize inference
135   by leveraging external storage, they also introduce additional memory resource requirements. In
136   contrast, the research presented in this paper focuses on alleviating the computational burden of
137   inference by efficiently utilizing limited memory resources, without relying on external memory
138   expansion.
139   While existing compression and offloading techniques partially alleviate KV cache pressure in LLM
140   inference, the trade-off between performance and resource consumption persists. In this paper, we
141   introduce an optimization strategy that eliminates reliance on external memory, enabling effective
142   KV cache compression without sacrificing model performance. This novel approach provides a
143   robust solution for long-text reasoning in LLMs, particularly in resource-constrained environments,
144   thereby enhancing the practical applicability of LLMs in real-world settings.

165   3.1   P ROBLEM A NALYSIS
166
167   LLM is constructed based on casual transformer, which is divided into embedding layer, feedforward
168   layer and self-attention layer. We focus on the self-attention layer in the decoding phase.
169
      Extra-domain inference. During the pre-training phase, the model establishes a maximum text
170
      length, making Softmax highly sensitive within this range. However, when covariates with se-
171   quence lengths are introduced into the self-attentive mechanism with significantly exceeding the
172   pre-training length, they compromise the robustness of the attention distribution. This results in the
173   distributional moderation imbalance problem in Softmax, rendering LLM inference unpredictable.
174   As positional and semantic information cannot be easily decoupled, the pre-training window length
175   becomes a constant challenge for extra-domain inference, especially without optimizing or fine-
176   tuning the model architecture. We refer to this phenomenon as the problem of out-of-domain dis-
177   tributional disequilibrium (ODD).
178   Intra-domain Inference. When the size of the sliding window is confined to the pre-training win-
179   dow, window attention mitigates the imbalance caused by relative positional differences in ODD.
180   However, it still fails to reason effectively about long texts. From a semantic perspective, we con-
181   tend that attention during window sliding relies entirely on short token sequences relative to the
182   current time step. This premature discarding of long text information, coupled with the emphasis on
183   short-term dependencies, directly contributes to the collapse of the LLM.
184   Consequently, we can draw two important insights:
185
186
      Conclusion 1. Focusing solely on the attention layer, the stability of the LLM’s ability to retain
      comprehension of long sequences is contingent upon the high sensitivity of Softmax in assigning
187
      weight streams.
188
189   Conclusion 2. The failure of sliding-window long-text inference arises from the limited robustness
190   of the window’s attention, indicating that the distribution of attention is heavily influenced by future
191   markers. The query is continuously updated as the text expands, leading the model’s attention
192
      to prioritize local information features at the expense of capturing contextual dependencies. This
      phenomenon is exacerbated by unpredictable noise in long texts, the prominence of locally relevant
193
      information, and conflicts between global features.

      3.2   L OCAL T OKEN M ODELING
197
198
199   Building on the relationship between in-domain and out-of-domain inference as discussed in Sec-
200
      tion3.1, we divide the long context sequence into two components based on the current time step:
      local tokens Llocal and remote tokens Lremote . Consequently, the long context can be denoted
201
      as L = Llocal + Lremote . We then explore distinct compression strategies tailored to the unique
202
      features of these two components.
203
204   In the case of Llocal , due to the autoregressive nature of LLM inference, the query at the current
205   time step remains inaccessible to subsequent inference, while the query input at the next time step
206
      influences the reallocation of attention. This presents challenges in designing stable KV eviction
      strategies. To address this issue, we focus on uncovering solutions from the query cluster data itself.
207
208   Research 1. We explore the consistency characteristics of query clusters by analyzing tokens within
209   the sequence Llocal . For each head query in the same layer, we compute the cosine similarity and
210   observe the intriguing vector distribution properties shown in Figure1a. Notably, the closer the
211   data points are to the diagonal split line, the redder their color becomes, indicating a high similar-
212
      ity between adjacent queries. This observation suggests that near-neighbor queries exhibit strong
      contextual similarity at the semantic level. Furthermore, this phenomenon aligns with the model’s
213
      reliance on prior input information to generate appropriate responses.

      3.3   R EMOTE T OKEN M ODELING
217
218   For distant contexts, inspired by the success of sparse attention, we hypothesize that certain key
219   tokens remain relevant for future reasoning in long-text inference. In other words, the LLM’s com-
220   prehension and memory capabilities depend primarily on these key tokens, while irrelevant token
221   clusters that appear in long texts serve only to support localized short-text reasoning and can be
222   treated as inactive information suitable for eviction. If this assumption holds, it would significantly
223   alleviate the memory pressure on the KV cache.
224   Research 2. We explore the relationship between key tokens and LLM capabilities in processing
225   long texts through an analysis of attention scores. Focusing on the compressibility of distant con-
226   texts, we emphasize the importance of long-range dependencies within the attention mechanism.
227   As shown in the Figure1b, we visualized attention scores from NarrativeQA’s scene quizzes. A no-
228   table observation is the consistent alignment of red bands within the long-sequence attention scores,
229   indicating that queries maintain coherence around specific tokens in lengthy historical texts. Addi-
230   tionally, the important tokens receiving high attention scores are highly sparse.
231   To further validate the pervasive sparsity of these high-scoring tokens, we set the attention threshold
232   at time step t to 1/t, considering only attention scores exceeding this threshold as important key
233   locations of focus for the query. As depicted in the Figure1c, both individual attention heads and
234   entire attention layers demonstrate significant sparsity, with over 90% of the attention being sparse.
235   This observation reinforces our hypothesis that key tokens in distant long-text contexts are highly
236   sparse and exert a lasting impact on the model’s reasoning over extended sequences.
237   Theorem 2. For remote tokens, queries exhibit high attention to only a few key historical messages,
238   with highly scored keys displaying significant sparsity across both the attention layers and heads.
239   By retaining only the important key-value pairs, the long-text processing capabilities of the LLM
240   can be preserved without loss, enabling a high degree of compression for distant tokens.

357     4.3.1    C OMPRESSION W INDOW
358
        In conjunction with the CGE strategy, we stabilize KV eviction by masking the influence of the
359
        center of gravity during the normalization process. However, through our experiments, we found
360     that the combination of the masking order of the attention center of gravity and the normalization
361     operation has different impacts on model performance. Based on these experimental observations,
362     we categorize them into two types of characterization algorithms, providing explanations for each
363     in relation to the semantic features of specific textual tasks.
364
        Local Fine-grained Modeling Tasks. Compared to tasks requiring long-term global dependencies,
365
        this task focuses more on modeling fine-grained local information in the current context. In natural
366     language processing, short-term dependencies are typically closely tied to the immediate context.
367     For example, in conversational tasks, information from neighboring time steps has a greater impact
368     on the model’s predictions than distant contexts. Consequently, the model emphasizes the semantic
369     features of nearby tokens during inference. As shown in Algorithm1, we implement a local focus of
370     attention by first masking the head gravity according to the short-term dependency strategy, followed
371     by removing the effect of the tail gravity after data normalization.
372     Global Dependency Tasks. Since this kind of task focuses more on requiring the model to rely
373     on long-distance contextual information in the inference process, the compression strategy in this
374     phase should focus more on the distant above. Therefore, as illustrated in Algorithm1, we first
375     shield the influence of the tail gravity before the normalization operation, followed by masking
376     the weaker influence of the head gravity during the selection of important keys. This approach
377     effectively enhances the model’s focus on distant information while emphasizing the consistency
        and completeness of global semantics.

478   6   C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this paper, we tackle the memory challenges associated with KV caching in LLM deployments
481   by introducing IntelLLM. Our extensive evaluation experiments demonstrate that IntelLLM signifi-
482   cantly improves performance through the combined use of CGE and RGL strategies. This approach
483   allows LLMs to achieve an optimal balance between KV cache compression and inference per-
484   formance without requiring fine-tuning, all while avoiding substantial overhead in computational
485   resources. As a result, IntelLLM effectively enhances the ability of LLMs to reason about long-text
      tasks.
