009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The method of Alternating Projections (AP) is a fundamental iterative technique
012            with applications to problems in machine learning, optimization and signal pro-
013            cessing. Examples include the Gauss-Seidel algorithm which is used to solve
014            large-scale regression problems and the Kaczmarz and projections onto convex
015            sets (POCS) algorithms that are fundamental to iterative reconstruction. Progress
016            has been made with regards to the questions of efficiency and rate of conver-
017            gence in the randomized setting of the AP method. Here, we extend these results
018
               with volume sampling to block (batch) sizes greater than 1 and provide explicit
               formulas that relate the convergence rate bounds to the spectrum of the underly-
019
               ing system. These results, together with a trace formula and associated volume
020
               sampling, prove that convergence rates monotonically improve with larger block
021            sizes, a feature that can not be guaranteed in general with uniform sampling (e.g.,
022            in SGD).

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Solving a system of linear equations is one of the most fundamental computational problems. It
028   is core to many problems in optimization, machine learning and signal processing that require so-
029   lutions to large-scale linear systems. Advances in computational efficiency of solving large-scale
030   linear systems directly translate to more efficient algorithms for non-linear optimization (Wilson
031   et al., 2021). Randomization of classical algorithms such as coordinate descent and gradient descent
032   has been instrumental in solving large-scale optimization problems. Characterizing the convergence
033
      of these randomized algorithms for solving linear systems is key to optimizing their performance
      and developing acceleration techniques that are used for more general classes (e.g., strongly convex)
034
      of objective functions. Broadly speaking, these randomized descent algorithms are row-space and
035
      column-space methods for solving a consistent linear system Ax = b, that in the randomized setting
036   can be viewed as a sequence of alternating projections onto certain subspaces (see Section 2) spec-
037   ified by A. When a single column/row is selected at each iterate (e.g., coordinate descent or SGD)
038   these are rank-1 projections onto hyperplanes, and more generally when a subset of columns/rows
039   are chosen (e.g., block coordinate descent with size n) the resulting rank-n projections are onto
040   subspaces with codimension n.
041
      Due to their sequential stochastic nature, these algorithms lend themselves to a Markovian view
042   that facilitates the use of ergodic theory of (continuous state space) Markov chains to determine
043   convergence criteria as well as rates of convergence. Analyzed as a time-homogeneous Markov
044   chain, the existence and uniqueness of the stationary measure concentrated at the solution of the
045   linear system is guaranteed by a stochastic version of the contraction mapping theorem. Likewise,
046   the rate of convergence can be bounded using the spectral gap of a certain operator (see Section 2)
047   that is constructed, for each algorithm, from A. While these results are well understood in Markov
048   chain theory, establishing the relationship between the spectral gap—and hence the rate—to the
049   spectrum of A has been a fundamental theoretical challenge. Understanding this relationship is of
050
      practical importance since performance of competing iterative methods such as Krylov subspace
      methods (e.g., conjugate gradients) are well understood in terms of the spectrum of A (Saad, 2003).
051
052   For the base case of rank-1 projections (e.g., coordinate descent or SGD), when rows/columns of
053   A are sampled, i.i.d., with probabilities according to their lengths (Strohmer & Vershynin, 2009;
      Leventhal & Lewis, 2010), the spectral gap is simply determined from the smallest singular value

054
      of A. We demonstrate that for rank-n projectors (e.g., block coordinate descent), the spectral gap is
055   entirely determined from all of the singular values of A when subsets are sampled, i.i.d., according
056   to their volumes. Establishing the relationship between the spectral gap and the singular values of A
057   is a significant departure from standard results on (deterministic) block methods. In block methods
058   the rate depends on the condition of the worst block in a partition of A – a quantity that depends on
059   the performance of the partitioning method and is decoupled from the spectrum of A.
060
      Our theory shows the exact process by which the spectral gap improves with n. We demonstrate that
061   the singular values of A, or equivalently eigenvalues of AT A are nonlinearly transformed with an
062   attraction towards their mean as n increases. This evolution, we show, is the nth step in a recursive
063   formulation of the Cayley-Hamilton theorem known as the Faddeev–LeVerrier algorithm.
064
      To sample with probabilities according to volumes when n is relatively small (as large as n = 15, as
065
      our experiments show) one can employ rejection sampling; for larger n efficient volume sampling
066
      is made possible by more sophisticated techniques that were developed by the pioneering work
067   of (Deshpande & Rademacher, 2010; Deshpande et al., 2006).

      2.1   C ONVERGENCE R ATE
109
110   Existence and uniqueness of the invariant measure for the Markov chain—the point mass at the so-
111   lution of the linear system Ax = b—can be demonstrated under very general conditions (Hairer,
112   2018) for these randomized alternating projection algorithms. It is easy to show that randomized
113   alternating projections are Feller Markov processes, which upon the application of the Krylov-
114   Bogoliubov theorem guarantees the existence of an invariant measure. Conversely, deterministic
115   contraction, a property of orthogonal projections, guarantees the uniqueness of this invariant mea-
116
      sure (see (Hairer, 2018) for details).
117   Given a probability distribution for randomly (and independently) choosing a coordinate en in
118   Gauss-Seidel, or choosing a row a ∈ A in Kaczmarz, the geometric rate of convergence is de-
119   pendent on the mean of the projections P1a that this distribution engenders. In particular, a lower
120   bound on the rate of convergence is obtained from the spectral radius of the average contraction
121   in (3): λmax E [I − P1a ] = λmax (I − E[P1a ]) = 1 − λmin (E[P1a ]). The key quantity in this bound
122
      is the spectral gap (i.e., the smallest eigenvalue) of the operator representing the average of all the
      noted rank-1 projectors:
123
                                                τ1 := λmin (E[P1a ]) .                                    (4)
124
125   To elaborate, given a starting point x0 , the randomized Gauss-Seidel’s residual decays geometrically
126   as: E∥rk ∥2 ≤ (1 − τ1 )k ∥r0 ∥2 and Kaczmarz’s error as E∥xk − x⋆ ∥2 ≤ (1 − τ1 )k ∥x0 − x⋆ ∥2 .
127   A fundamental challenge is to relate this spectral gap, τ1 , to quantities that can be measured and
128   computed from A. While in the deterministic realm of alternating projections this quantity depends
129   on a series of subspace angles that are difficult to compute (Galántai, 2005; Deutsch, 1995; Nelson
130   & Neumann, 1987), in the randomized regime, the spectral gap (and hence the rate) is simply deter-
131   mined from the smallest singular value of A if the probability distribution is set to select a projector
132   P1a with a probability proportional to length of a squared: pr(a) ∼ ∥a∥2 . These probabilities are
133
      simply the diagonal elements of A in Gauss-Seidel and lengths of rows of A in Kaczmarz. This
      was first observed for the randomized Kaczmarz algorithm in (Strohmer & Vershynin, 2009) where
134
      E[P1 ] = AT A/ Tr (AAT ) and τ1 = λmin (AT A)/ Tr (AAT ) and then leveraged in the random-
135
      ized Gauss-Seidel (coordinate descent) case (Leventhal & Lewis, 2010) where E[P1 ] = A/ Tr A
136   and τ1 = λmin (A)/ Tr A.

      n = 1, 10 or 15 coordinates were simultaneously updated. The tuning parameters γ, λ were set to

450   The main implication of our results is establishing the explicit relationship between the rate of
451   convergence in randomized AP (e.g., Gauss-Seidel) for solving Ax = b to the spectrum of A.
452   As discussed in Section 2, 1 − τn provides a bound on the rate of convergence and the spectral
453   gap τn is simply the spectral gap of E[Pn ] that is related to the spectrum of A through (7). This
454
      recursive process stipulates the evolution of the spectrum of E[Pn ] as n increases from 1 where the
      spectrum is that of E[P1 ] = A/N , towards its mean. We present an empirical validation of these
455
      theoretical results on the evolution of the spectrum of A, and as a result show how the spectral gap
456
      τn increases as block size n grows, in Figure 3. Note in particular that due to the recursive nature
457   of the formula 8, the spectral gap can be computed very efficiently for problems of very large sizes.
458   An appropriate choice of n can then be made based on balancing the larger computational cost of
459   volume sampling n rows vis-a-vis the rate gains provided by a larger n.

463   6   C ONCLUSION
464
465
      This paper generalizes the results of (Strohmer & Vershynin, 2009; Leventhal & Lewis, 2010) that
466
      establish the relationship between the performacne of randomized Gauss-Seidel and Kaczmarz al-
467
      gorithms to the spectrum of A when single coordinates or rows (i.e., n = 1) are sampled according
468   to their lengths. We establish that when n > 1 coordinates (or rows) are selected according to their
469   volumes, the spectral gap that bounds the convergence rate is similarly determined from the spec-
470   trum of A that is nonlinearly transformed according to the Faddeev-LaVerrier algorithm. We derive
471   a recursive formulation of this evolution of spectrum towards its mean and establish efficient vol-
472   ume computation results that avoids combinatorially large computations over subsets. These results
473   establish the convergence of the method of alternating projections under volume sampling for which
474   efficient algorithms have been developed in theoretical computer science.
