011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable proficiency across
014            various natural language processing (NLP) tasks. However, adapting LLMs
015            to downstream applications requires computationally intensive and memory-
016
               demanding fine-tuning procedures. To alleviate these burdens, parameter-efficient
               fine-tuning (PEFT) techniques have emerged as a promising approach to tailor
017
               LLMs with minimal computational overhead. While PEFT methods offer substan-
018
               tial advantages, they do not fully address the pervasive issue of bias propagation
019            from pre-training data. This work introduces Bias-Alleviating Low-Rank Adapta-
020            tion (BA-LoRA), a novel PEFT method designed to counteract bias inheritance.
021            BA-LoRA incorporates three distinct regularization terms: (1) a consistency regu-
022            larizer, (2) a diversity regularizer, and (3) a singular value decomposition regularizer.
023            These regularizers aim to enhance the models’ consistency, diversity, and gener-
024            alization capabilities during fine-tuning. We conduct extensive experiments on
025            natural language understanding (NLU) and natural language generation (NLG)
026            tasks using prominent LLMs such as LLaMA, Mistral, and Gemma. The results
027
               demonstrate that BA-LoRA outperforms LoRA and its state-of-the-art variants.
               Moreover, our method effectively mitigates the adverse effects of pre-training bias,
028
               leading to more reliable and robust model outputs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The emergence of large language models (LLMs) has marked a new era in natural language processing
034
      (NLP). Models such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), Llama (Touvron et al., 2023), Mistral (Jiang et al.,
035
      2023), and Gemma (Team et al., 2024) have demonstrated exceptional performance across a wide
036   array of NLP tasks, including language comprehension, generation, and reasoning (Zhao et al., 2023;
037   Chang et al., 2024). The remarkable advancements of LLMs can be largely attributed to their training
038   on vast datasets (Zhao et al., 2023). As LLMs continue to evolve rapidly, training on extensively
039   scaled web-derived corpora has become standard practice to improve model generalization, thus
040   bypassing the labor-intensive processes of data curation and annotation (Gao et al., 2020; Penedo
041   et al., 2023). However, the corresponding increase in data volume has introduced several challenges,
042   such as the presence of imbalanced, duplicated, and corrupted information (Parashar et al., 2024; Liu
043   & He, 2024; Chen et al., 2024b; Yang et al., 2023).
044   Recent research has shown that various forms of bias in training data can negatively affect LLM
045   behavior (Dong et al., 2023; Dodge et al., 2021; Longpre et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024a). For
046   example, noise within the training data can degrade model generalization (Chen et al., 2024a),
047   while the long-tailed distribution of concepts in web-scale data can cause LLMs to overemphasize
048   overrepresented topics (Zhu et al., 2024). Furthermore, biases introduced during pre-training can
049   persist even after fine-tuning, potentially compromising model performance and safety in real-world
050
      applications (Qi et al., 2023; Bommasani et al., 2021; Mallen et al., 2022; Carlini et al., 2023).
051   This phenomenon, termed "Catastrophic Inheritance" by (Chen et al., 2024a), has spurred investiga-
052   tions into mitigation strategies. While constructing less biased datasets and developing more robust
053   model architectures are prominent approaches (Liu & He, 2024), this study explores an alternative:
      innovations in fine-tuning. Fine-tuning LLMs is a powerful method for enhancing task-specific


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      performance (Han et al., 2024), aligning models with user intent (Ouyang et al., 2022; Xu et al.,
055   2024), and eliciting desired behaviors (Bai et al., 2022; Rafailov et al., 2024). However, fine-tuning
056   large-scale models’ computational and memory demands are substantial (Hu et al., 2021). For
057   instance, 16-bit fine-tuning of a Llama-65B model requires over 780 GB of GPU memory (Dettmers
058   et al., 2024). To address these limitations, parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) techniques, such as
059   Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021), have gained prominence.
060
      LoRA posits that parameter updates during fine-tuning can be efficiently represented by low-rank
061   matrices. Therefore, given a pre-trained weight matrix W ∈ Rm×n , instead of updating all parameters
062   of W directly, LoRA introduces an auxiliary low-rank adapter ∆W = AB, where A ∈ Rm×r and
063   B ∈ Rr×n with rank r ≪ min(m, n). Here, A and B are learnable matrices initialized as:
064
065          • A is initialized with a scaled normal distribution: aij ∼ N (0, σ 2 ).
066          • B is initialized to zero: bij = 0.
067
068   For a given input X, the output Y is computed as:
069
070
071                                   Y = X(W + ∆W ) = X(W + AB).                                        (1)
072
073   Only A and B are updated during fine-tuning while W remains frozen. This initialization ensures
074   that AB = 0 at the start of training, thus preserving the model’s original output. Since the rank r
075   is significantly smaller than the dimensions of W , LoRA substantially reduces training overhead
076   compared to full fine-tuning (Hu et al., 2021).
077   To mitigate the detrimental effects of Catastrophic Inheritance, particularly noise and imbalance,
078   we propose Bias-Alleviating Low-Rank Adaptation (BA-LoRA). Building upon Principal Singular
079   Values and Singular Vectors Adaptation (PiSSA) (Meng et al., 2024), which addresses convergence
080   issues in standard LoRA, our approach incorporates three distinct regularization terms: a consistency
081   regularizer, a diversity regularizer, and a singular value decomposition (SVD) regularizer. The consis-
082   tency regularizer preserves valuable pre-trained knowledge during fine-tuning, while the diversity
083   regularizer encourages varied model outputs. The SVD regularizer enhances the generalization capa-
084
      bilities of generative models. Recognizing the fundamental differences between Natural Language
      Understanding (NLU) and Natural Language Generation (NLG), such as determinism in NLU versus
085
      diversity in NLG, we tailor our regularization strategies accordingly.
086
087   To evaluate the efficacy of BA-LoRA, we conduct comprehensive experiments across diverse bench-
088   marks, including mathematical reasoning (GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and MATH (Yu et al., 2023)),
089   coding (HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021) and MBPP (Austin et al., 2021)), natural language under-
090
      standing (GLUE (Wang et al., 2018)), and general language evaluation (MT-Bench (Zheng et al.,
      2024)). Our experiments utilize prominent LLMs such as LLaMA 2-7B (Touvron et al., 2023),
091
      Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023), and Gemma-7B (Team et al., 2024), as well as encoder-only architec-
092
      tures like RoBERTa-large (Liu et al., 2019) and DeBERTa-v3-base (He et al., 2021b). The results
093   unequivocally demonstrate BA-LoRA’s superiority over LoRA and PiSSA. Moreover, our method
094   effectively attenuates noise inherited from pre-training, leading to more robust and generalizable
095   models.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
099
      Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) techniques (Xu et al., 2023b; Han et al., 2024) have garnered
100
      significant attention as an approach to adapting LLMs for specific tasks under limited hardware
101
      resources. Three main categories of PEFT techniques are commonly used. The first category includes
102   adapter-based methods (Houlsby et al., 2019b; Lin et al., 2020; Lei et al., 2023; He et al., 2021a),
103   which introduce additional layers into the model and fine-tune these layers (typically with far fewer
104   parameters) to reduce computational costs. The second category comprises soft prompt tuning
105   methods (Hambardzumyan et al., 2021; Lester et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021a; Liu et al., 2023),
106   which prepend learnable soft prompts to the model’s input to tailor it to specific tasks. These methods
107   leverage the inherent capabilities of pre-trained models, requiring only the appropriate prompts to
      adapt to downstream tasks. The third category encompasses low-rank adaptation (LoRA) and its


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      variants (Hu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Dettmers et al., 2024). LoRA introduces the product of
109   low-rank matrices within existing layers to approximate weight updates during fine-tuning (Hu et al.,
110   2021).
111
112
      Variants of LoRA enhance its efficiency and performance in different ways. AdaLoRA adaptively dis-
      tributes the parameter budget among weight matrices based on their importance, improving efficiency
113
      and performance by pruning unimportant updates and minimizing computational overhead (Zhang
114
      et al., 2022). DoRA increases LoRA’s learning capacity and stability by decomposing pre-trained
115   weights into magnitude and direction components for fine-tuning (Liu et al., 2024). LoHA enhances
116   LoRA by employing Hamiltonian products (Hyeon-Woo et al., 2021). DyLoRA addresses the fixed
117   size and rank optimization limitations of LoRA by dynamically training LoRA blocks across varying
118   ranks (Valipour et al., 2022). DeltaLoRA improves the representational capacity of LoRA by updating
119   the model’s original weights using parameters from adapter layers (Zi et al., 2023). PiSSA initializes
120   adapter matrices A and B to approximate the original matrix W through singular value decompo-
121   sition, leading to faster convergence and improved performance (Meng et al., 2024). While many
122   LoRA variants focus on accelerating convergence or reducing memory consumption, our BA-LoRA
123
      method uniquely addresses the core challenge of Catastrophic Inheritance in LLM fine-tuning.

128
129   As a variant of LoRA, PiSSA addresses the convergence speed challenge by retaining the core
130   LoRA architecture while innovating in initialization. Specifically, PiSSA leverages the principal
131
      components of the original weight matrix, W , to initialize the adapter matrices, A and B. The
      remaining components are encapsulated within a residual matrix, W res ∈ Rm×n . The SVD of
132
      W ∈ Rm×n is expressed as W = U SV T , where U ∈ Rm×min(m,n) and V ∈ Rn×min(m,n) are
133
      orthogonal singular vectors, and S = diag(s) ∈ Rmin(m,n)×min(m,n) is a diagonal matrix, where
134                                                            min(m,n)

138

      3.2    B IAS -A LLEVIATING L OW-R ANK A DAPTATION (BA-L O RA)
156
157   Catastrophic Inheritance encapsulates the challenges posed by biased large-scale training data, which
158   can manifest in LLMs as vulnerabilities and limitations arising from duplicated, noisy, imbalanced,
159   or unethical samples. These inherited flaws can adversely impact downstream tasks, leading to dimin-
160   ished generalization, degraded performance, security breaches, and biased outputs. To address the
161   specific issues caused by noisy and imbalanced data, we introduce BA-LoRA, a method incorporating
      three distinct regularization terms: (1) consistency regularizer, (2) diversity regularizer, and (3) SVD

163   variants of each regularizer to optimize performance for respective task domains.

             4.3.4                    A BLATION S TUDY
461
462         This ablation experiment aims to analyze the impact of three regularization terms (LCR_NLG ,
463         LDR_NLG , LSVDR_NLG ) in BA-LoRA on model performance. The experiment selected three models,
464         LLaMA-2-7B, Mistral-7B, and Gemma-7B, and evaluated them on GSM8K and MATH datasets. To
465         ensure clear results, we only tested single regularization terms to reveal their independent contribu-
466         tions.
467
            As shown in Figure 2, the model without regularization ("w /o Reg") produced the lowest perfor-
468         mance across both datasets. In contrast, introducing different regularization terms led to varying
469         degrees of performance improvement. Specifically, LCR_NLG contributes to the equilibrium gain for
470         both datasets. The effect of LDR_NLG on Gemma-7B is significantly stronger relative to LCR_NLG .
471         Ultimately, the model incorporating all three regularization terms achieved the highest performance
472         on both datasets. These findings validate the regularization strategy and show that combining the
473         terms in BA-LoRA further enhances model generalization. These findings confirm the effectiveness
474         of the proposed regularization strategy, and further indicate that combining the regularization terms
475         in BA-LoRA enhances the model’s generalization ability.
476         To further assess the impact of our proposed regularization terms on model performance, we conducted
477         an ablation study focusing on three terms designed for NLU tasks in BA-LoRA: LCR_NLU , LDR_NLU ,
478         and LSVDR_NLU . We employed the DeBERTa-v3-base model and evaluated it across several tasks
479         from the GLUE benchmark.
480         As shown in Table 5, the model without any regularization (denoted as "w /o Reg") exhibited the
481         lowest performance, achieving an average score of 89.47. Introducing each regularization term
482         individually led to performance improvements across various tasks. Notably, LCR_NLU consistently
483         provided substantial gains, achieving an average score of 89.78, second only to the full BA-LoRA
484         model. The term LDR_NLU showed particularly strong performance on the MRPC task, while
485         LSVDR_NLU delivered balanced improvements across most tasks. Finally, BA-LoRA, incorporating
            all three regularization terms, achieved the highest overall performance with an average score of 89.91.

522   5                    C ONCLUSION
523
524   This paper introduces Bias-Alleviating Low-rank Adaptation (BA-LoRA), a novel parameter-efficient
525   fine-tuning method designed to mitigate catastrophic inheritance in pre-trained language models.
526   BA-LoRA incorporates three key components: consistency regularization, diversity regularization,
527   and singular value decomposition regularization. These components work in concert to preserve
528   pre-training knowledge, enhance output diversity, and improve model generalization. Extensive
529   experiments demonstrate that BA-LoRA consistently outperforms existing baselines on various
530   NLG and NLU tasks while robust to noisy and imbalanced pre-training data. Furthermore, our
531
      ablation studies confirm the effectiveness of the three regularization terms both individually and in
      combination. These results highlight the potential of BA-LoRA as a general-purpose fine-tuning
532
      method for pre-trained language models and effectively address the key challenges of deploying these
533
      models in real applications.

      A.1   C HALLENGES OF B IAS AND N OISE IN P RE - TRAINING DATA
962
963
      Bias and noise within pre-training datasets present significant hurdles in constructing dependable
      machine-learning models. Mislabeled data and imbalanced distributions can lead to models that not
964
      only underperform on downstream tasks but also reinforce existing biases in the data (Torralba &
965
      Efros, 2011; Barocas & Selbst, 2016; Mehrabi et al., 2021). This issue is especially problematic in
966   large-scale datasets, where manual curation is impractical. Consequently, reliance on automated data
967   collection methods may introduce various inaccuracies and biases (Northcutt et al., 2021; Birhane &
968   Prabhu, 2021). The challenge becomes even more severe when dealing with real-world, instance-
969   dependent label noise. Models trained on such data may inadvertently learn these inaccuracies,
970   resulting in poor generalization (Frénay & Verleysen, 2013; Song et al., 2022; Algan & Ulusoy, 2021).
971   Addressing these challenges is essential for advancing machine learning and ensuring models are
      both effective and equitable.

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

       A.2    M ITIGATING B IAS AND N OISE THROUGH PARAMETER -E FFICIENT F INE -T UNING
973           M ETHODS
974
975    To counteract the adverse effects of bias and noise in pre-training data, parameter-efficient fine-tuning
976    methods have emerged as promising solutions. These approaches aim to adapt pre-trained models to
977    new tasks with minimal parameter updates, thereby reducing the risk of overfitting to noisy or biased
978    data (Houlsby et al., 2019a; Zaken et al., 2021; Lester et al., 2021). Techniques such as integrating
979    lightweight adaptation modules (Pfeiffer et al., 2020; Jang et al., 2021), utilizing prefix tuning (Li &
980
       Liang, 2021b; Liu et al., 2021), and employing low-rank adaptations (Hu et al., 2021; Ding et al.,
       2023) enable efficient model refinement while preserving the valuable representations acquired
981
       during pre-training. Selectively fine-tuning specific model components can enhance performance on
982
       downstream tasks, improve generalization, and reduce the influence of noise and bias (Zaken et al.,
983    2021; Mahabadi et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2020). This strategy not only results in more robust models
984    but also contributes to the development of fairer AI systems by directly addressing fundamental data
985    quality issues.
986

989
       A.3    E XAMPLES OF NOISE IN PRE - TRAINING DATA
990
       Pre-training data typically originates from large-scale internet sources, which inevitably contain noise
991    and imbalance. Many advanced pre-trained models, such as LLaMA-2-7B/13B (Touvron et al., 2023),
992    Mistral-7B-v0.1 (Jiang et al., 2023), Gemma-7B (Jiang et al., 2023), and GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), are
993    trained using large amounts of unlabeled internet text data. These datasets are often not thoroughly
994    cleaned or corrected, leading to training corpora that include irrelevant or inaccurate information.
995    Consequently, during the subsequent fine-tuning phase, models struggle to effectively filter out these
996    undesirable contents, adversely affecting their performance on downstream tasks.
997    Given the noise and imbalance issues present in pre-training data, understanding the specific types of
998    noise is crucial for improving model performance. Below, we summarize some common examples of
999    noise found in pre-training datasets:
1000
              • Low quality bias:
1001
1002                 Duplicity: The presence of identical or similar content in the data can lead to overfitting
1003                 and privacy leakage risks (Elazar et al., 2023), (Carlini et al., 2022), (Hernandez et al.,
1004                 2022).
1005                 Corruption/noise: Inconsistent or erroneous inputs in the training data can affect model
1006                 robustness and downstream task performance (Elazar et al., 2023), (Fan et al., 2024),
1007
                     (Caswell et al., 2021).
1008                 Contamination: Leakage of the test set into the training set may lead to distorted model
1009
                     evaluation results (Roberts et al., 2023), (Schaeffer, 2023), (Jiang et al., 2024b).
1010          • Skewed distribution bias:
1011                 Category imbalance: Too few samples of certain categories cause the model to perform
1012                 poorly in predicting these categories, producing bias (Xu et al., 2023a), (Zhu et al.,
1013                 2024), (Parashar et al., 2024).
1014          • Unethical content bias:
1015
                     Toxic and harmful content: The training data may contain offensive, biased, or harmful
1016
                     content that may cause the model to generate harmful or inappropriate outputs (Zou
1017
                     et al., 2023), (Sun et al., 2024).

1271
1272   Here, we offer further insights into our work.
1273
1274   D.1             L IMITATIONS
1275
1276   While BA-LoRA demonstrates significant improvements in mitigating catastrophic inheritance
1277   and enhancing model performance, several limitations warrant further investigation. Firstly, our
1278   evaluations primarily focus on English language tasks, which may limit the generalizability of
1279
       our findings to other languages and specialized domains. Additionally, the computational overhead
       introduced by the consistency, diversity, and SVD regularizers adds complexity to the training process,
1280
       potentially impacting efficiency. Furthermore, the impact of BA-LoRA on other forms of bias, such
1281
       as fairness and societal stereotypes, remains unexplored. Lastly, the selection and weighting of
1282   regularization terms in BA-LoRA are fixed across different tasks, which may not be optimal for all
1283   scenarios.
1284
1285   D.2             F UTURE WORKS
1286
1287   Future research should extend assessments of BA-LoRA to multilingual settings and specialized
1288   domains to ensure broader applicability. Exploring optimization techniques could help reduce
1289   the computational overhead introduced by the regularizers, balancing performance gains with ef-
1290   ficiency. Investigating the impact of BA-LoRA on other forms of bias, including fairness and
1291
       societal stereotypes, is crucial for developing more equitable models. Additionally, refining the
       selection and weighting of regularization terms—possibly through automated or dynamic adjustment
1292
       methods—could enhance adaptability across different tasks and models. Testing the scalability of
1293
       BA-LoRA on larger models with hundreds of billions of parameters and exploring its integration with
1294   other bias mitigation strategies may yield synergistic effects and further improve model robustness.
