020                                                   A BSTRACT
021
022             Data Augmentation (DA), i.e., synthesizing faithful and diverse samples to expand
023
                the original training set, is a prevalent and effective strategy to improve various
                visual recognition tasks. With the powerful image generation ability, diffusion-
024
                based DA has shown strong performance gains on different benchmarks. In this
025
                paper, we analyze today’s diffusion-based DA methods, and argue that they can-
026             not take account of both faithfulness and diversity, which are two critical keys for
027             generating high-quality samples and boosting final classification performance. To
028             this end, we propose a novel Diffusion-based Inversion Interpolation DA method:
029             Diff-II. Specifically, Diff-II consists of three main steps: 1) Category concepts
030             learning: Learning concept embeddings for each category. 2) Inversion inter-
031             polation: Calculating the inversion for each image, and conducting random cir-
032             cle interpolation for two randomly sampled inversions from the same category.
033             3) Two-stage denoising: Using different prompts to generate synthesized images
034
                in a coarse-to-fine manner. Extensive experiments on multiple image classifica-
                tion tasks (e.g., few-shot, long-tailed, and out-of-distribution classification) have
035
                demonstrated its effectiveness over state-of-the-art diffusion-based DA methods.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
039
040
      Today’s visual recognition models can even outperform us humans with sufficient training samples.
041
      However, in many different real-world scenarios, it is not easy to collect adequate training data
042   for some categories. For example, since the occurrence frequency of various categories in nature
043   follows a long-tailed distribution, there are many rare categories with only limited samples (O’Hagan
044   & Forster, 2004; Van Horn & Perona, 2017). To mitigate this data scarcity issue, a prevalent and
045   effective solution is Data Augmentation (DA). Based on an original training set with limited samples,
046   DA aims to generate more synthetic samples to expand the training set.
047
      For DA methods, there are two critical indexes: faithfulness and diversity (Sajjadi et al., 2018). They
048   can not only evaluate the quality of synthesized samples, but also influence the final recognition per-
049   formance. As shown in Figure 1, faithfulness indicates that the synthetic samples need to retain the
050   characteristics of the corresponding category (c.f., Figure 1 (a)), i.e., the faithfulness confirms that
051   the model learns from correct category knowledge. Diversity indicates that the synthetic samples
052   should have different contexts from the original training set and each other (c.f., Figure 1 (b)), i.e.,
053   the diversity ensures that the model learns the invariable characteristics of the category by seeing
      diverse samples.

072   With the photo-realistic image generation ability of today’s diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Nichol
073   & Dhariwal, 2021), a surge of diffusion-based DA methods has dominated the image classification
074   task. Typically, diffusion-based DA methods reformulate image augmentation as an image editing
075   task, which consists of two steps: 1) Noising Step: They first randomly sample an image from the
076   original training set as a reference image and then add some noise to the reference image. 2) Denois-
077   ing Step: They then gradually denoise this noisy reference image conditioned on a category-specific
078   prompt. After the two steps, a new synthesized training image was generated. Following this frame-
079
      work, the pioneer diffusion-based DA work (He et al., 2022) directly uses a hand-crafted template
      containing the reference image’s category label as the prompt (i.e., intra-category denoising). These
080
      handcrafted prompts work well on general datasets with a broad spectrum of category concepts (e.g.,
081
      CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009)). However, these few words (with only category name) can not
082   guide the diffusion models to generate images with specific and detailed characteristics, especially
083   for datasets with fine-grained categories (e.g., Stanford Cars (Krause et al., 2013)).
084
085
      To further enhance the generalization ability, subsequent diffusion-based DA methods try to im-
      prove the quality of synthesized samples from the two key characteristics. Specifically, to improve
086
      faithfulness, Trabucco et al. (2023) replace category labels with more fine-grained learned category
087
      concepts. As shown in Figure 2(a), they first learn a specific embedding “[A]” for “category A”
088   bird, and then replace the fixed category name with the learned concept in the prompt. These learn-
089   able prompts can somewhat preserve fine-grained details for different categories. However, the fixed
090   combination of a reference image and its corresponding category concept always results in similar
091   synthetic samples (i.e., limited diversity). On the other side, to improve diversity, Wang et al.
092   (2024) use prompts containing different category concepts (e.g., “[B]”) from the reference image
093   (i.e., inter-category denoising). This operation can generate images with “intermediate” semantics
094   between two different categories. However, it inherently introduces another challenging problem to
095   obtain an “accurate” soft label for each synthetic image, which affects faithfulness to some extent
096
      (c.f., Figure 2(b)). Based on these above discussions, we can observe that: current state-of-the-art
      diffusion-based DA methods cannot take account of both faithfulness and diversity, which results in
097
      limited improvements on the generalization ability of downstream classifiers.
098
099   In this paper, we propose a simple yet effective Diffusion-based Inversion Interpolation method:
100   Diff-II, which can generate both faithful and diverse augmented images. As shown in Figure 2(c),
101   Diff-II consists of three steps: 1) Category Concepts Learning: To generate faithful images, we
102
      learn a specific embedding for each category (e.g., “[A]” for category A) by reconstructing the im-
      ages of the original training set. 2) Inversion Interpolation: To improve diversity while maintaining
103
      faithfulness, we calculate the inversion1 for each image of the original training set. Then, we sample
104
      two inversions from the same category and conduct interpolation. The interpolation result corre-
105   sponds to a subsequent high-quality synthetic image. 3) Two-stage Denoising: To further improve

      the diversity, we prepare some suffixes2 (e.g., “flying over water”, “standing on a tree branch”) that
109   can summarize the high-frequency context patterns of the original training set. Then, we split the
110   denoising process into two stages by timesteps. In the first stage, we denoise the interpolation results
111   guided by a prompt containing the learned category concept and a randomly sampled suffix, e.g.,
112   “a photo of a [A] bird [suffix].” This design can inject perturbation into the early-timestep
113   generation of context and finally contributes to diversity. In the second stage, we replace the prompt
114   with “a photo of a [A] bird” to refine the character details of the category concept.
115   To be specific, we first utilize some parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods (e.g., low-rank adapta-
116   tion (Hu et al., 2021) and textual inversion (Gal et al., 2022)) to learn the concept embedding for each
117   category. Then, we acquire the DDIM inversion (Song et al., 2020) for each image from the original
118   set conditioned on the learned concept. After that, we randomly sample two inversions within one
119   category as one pair and conduct interpolation with random strengths. To align the distribution of in-
120   terpolation results with standard normal distribution and get a larger interpolation space, we conduct
121   random circle interpolation. Since each pair of images used for inversion interpolation belongs to
122   the same concept, their interpolations will highly maintain the semantic consistency of this concept
123
      (i.e., it ensures faithfulness). Meanwhile, since both images have different contexts, the interpola-
      tions will produce an image with a new context (i.e., it ensures diversity). Finally, we set a split ratio
124
      to divide the whole denoising timesteps into two stages. In the first stage, we use a prompt containing
125
      the learned concept and a randomly sampled suffix2 to generate noisy images with diverse contexts
126   (e.g., layout and gesture). In the second stage, we remove the suffix to refine the character details of
127   the category concept. By adjusting the split ratio, we can control the trade-off between faithfulness
128   and diversity. To extract all suffixes, we first utilize a pretrained vision-language model to extract all
129   captions of the original training set, and then leverage a large language model to summarize them
130   into a few suffixes.
131
      We evaluated our method on various image classification tasks across multiple datasets and settings.
132   Extensive results has demonstrated consistent improvements and significant gains over state-of-the-
133   art methods. Conclusively, our contributions are summarized as follows:
134
      • We use a unified view to analyze existing diffusion-based DA methods, we argue that they can not
135
        take account of both faithfulness and diversity well, which leads to limited improvements.
136   • We propose Diff-II, a simple yet effective diffusion-based DA method, that leverages the inversion
137     circle interpolation and two-stage denoising to generate faithful and diverse images.
138   • We conduct comprehensive experiments on three tasks. Our state-of-the-art performance verifies
139     that Diff-II can achieve effective data augmentation by generating high-quality samples.

      2       R ELATED W ORK
142
143   Diffusion-Based DA. With the emergence of diffusion models, Diffusion-based DA (Michaeli &
144   Fried, 2024; Islam et al., 2024) is increasing. Currently, there are two main paradigms of Diffusion-
145   based DA: 1) Latent Perturbation (Zhou et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024) generate
146   samples by perturbating latent codes in the latent space. Although these methods can generate
147   diverse samples, due to the uncontrollable perturbation direction, the generated results sometimes
148   deviate from the domain of the original dataset. Therefore, they heavily rely on extra over-sampling
149   and filtering steps. 2) Image Editing (He et al., 2022; Trabucco et al., 2023; Dunlap et al., 2023;
150   Wang et al., 2024) reformulate data augmentation as an image editing task (Meng et al., 2021; Lu
151
      et al., 2023). However, due to the limitations of the editing paradigm, it is difficult for them to
      take into account both the faithfulness and diversity of the synthetic samples. To tackle the above
152
      problem, our work proposes to generate new images by interpolating the inversions.
153
154   Interpolation-Based Data Augmentation. For time series and text data, interpolation is a common
155   approach for DA. Chen et al. (2022) incorporate a two-stage interpolation in the hidden space to
156   improve the text classification models. Oh et al. (2020) propose to augment time-series data by
157
      interpolation on original data. In the computer vision community, there are some studies (DeVries &
      Taylor, 2017; Zhou et al., 2023) work on interpolation-based DA for image classification. However,
158
      how to combine the excellent generation ability of diffusion models and interpolation operation to
159
      obtain high-quality synthetic samples remains an important challenge. To solve this problem, we
160   propose Diff-II, an efficient diffusion-based DA framework with inversion interpolation.

186   Problem Formulation. For a general image classification task, typically there is a original training
187
      set with K categories: O = {O1 , O2 , ..., OK }, where Oi is the subset of all training samples belong
188   to ith category. For Oi , there are Ni labeled training samples {Xji }N  i
                                                                             j=1 . The classification task aims
189
      to train a classifier with O and evaluate it on the test set. On this basis, diffusion-based DA method
      first generates extra synthetic images for each category. The Synthetic set: S = {S 1 , S 2 , ..., S K },
190
      S i is the set of synthetic images of ith category. Then it trains an improved classifier with both
191
      original and synthetic images (i.e., O ∪ S).
192
193   General Framework. As shown in Figure 3, our proposed Diff-II consists of three main steps:
194   1) Category Concepts Learning (Sec. 3.1): We first set n learnable token embeddings for each cat-
195   egory, and insert some learnable low-rank matrixes into the pretrained diffusion U-Net. By recon-

197   category. We denote the tokens of the ith category concept as {[Vji ]}nj=1 .

      photo of a [V1i ] [V2i ] ... [Vni ] [metaclass]2 ”. The “[metaclass]” is the theme of the corre-

      sponding dataset, e.g.“bird” is the “[metaclass]” for dataset CUB (Wah et al., 2011). Then, we

      5   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this work, we analyze current diffusion-based DA methods from a unified perspective, finding that
489   they can either only improve the faithfulness of synthetic samples or only improve their diversity. To
490   take both faithfulness and diversity into account, we propose Diff-II, a simple yet effective diffusion-
491   based DA method. our Diff-II show that it significantly improves both the faithfulness and diversity
492   of the synthetic samples, further improving classification models in data-scarce sceneries. In the
493   future, we are going to: 1) extend this work into more general perception tasks, such as object
494   detection, segmentation, or even video-domain tasks. 2) explore more effective DA methods that
495   can better handle situations with extremely few training images.
496   Limitations. Our method is less effective when some categories only have one training image. In
497   that case, the interpolation can not be implemented because the interpolation operation is between
498   two samples. We can see that for the long-tail classification task on CUB-LT (c.f., Table 2): as the
499   imbalance factor gets larger (from 10 to 100), the gain of our method (compared with the second
500   best one Diff-Mix)is getting smaller and smaller (from 5.8% to 0.86%). This is because a higher
501   imbalance factor means there are more categories that only have one training image.

667
668   Spherical interpolation is a method used to interpolate between two points on a sphere or in a spher-
669   ical space. The main idea behind spherical interpolation is to find a point along the shortest path on
670   the sphere’s surface between two given points. The theory of spherical interpolation is grounded in
671
      spherical geometry and quaternion algebra as follows:
672   Shortest Path on Sphere: The shortest path between two points on the surface of a sphere is along
673   the great circle that passes through both points. A great circle is any circle that divides the sphere
674   into two equal hemispheres, like the equator or the meridians on a globe.
675   Interpolation Formula: Given two points on a sphere, represented by unit vectors A and B, and an
676   interpolation parameter t ∈ [0, 1], spherical interpolation calculates a third point P along the great
677   circle from A to B using the formula:
678
                                               sin((1 − t)θ)A + sin(tθ)B
679                                      P =                             ,                                     (9)

681   where θ is the angle between A and B, found using the dot product cos(θ) = A · B.
682

683   lation can be applied using quaternions. Quaternions provide a way to represent orientations and
684   rotations in three dimensions without the singularity and ambiguity problems of Euler angles. The
685   interpolation of two quaternions q1 and q2 is given by:
686
                                               sin((1 − t)θ)q1 + sin(tθ)q2

692   or directional data.

737   A.2     C OMPARISON METHODS
738
739   In this section, we introduce all the comparison methods of experiments.
740   Diffusion-based data augmentation methods:
741
742   • Real-Filter (He et al., 2022): Directly generate some synthetic images with prompts containing
743     their corresponding category labels. Then, leverage a pre-trained perception network to extract
744     the features of both images of the original training set and synthetic images. Finally, filter all the
745     synthetic images that are far from images of the original training set and only maintain those that
746     are closed to the original training images.
747
      • Real-Guidance (He et al., 2022): Given an image from the original training set, add T timesteps
        noise to the image and use the noised one to replace the random noise at the beginning of the
748
        generation. Finally, denoise it with a prompt containing its category label.
749
      • Da-Fusion (Trabucco et al., 2023): Firstly, set a few learnable token embeddings to learn an
750     accurate concept for each category with the original training set. Then for a given image of the
751     original training set, add random timesteps noise and denoise the noised image with a prompt
752     containing its learned category concept.
753   • Real-Mix (Wang et al., 2024): Given an image from the original training set, add random timesteps
754     noise to the image. Then denoise the noised image with a prompt containing other-category la-
755     bels. This will lead to a synthetic image with intermediate semantics between the two categories.
        Design a calculation mechanism to decide the soft label for this synthetic image.


                                                            14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      • Diff-AUG (Wang et al., 2024): Firstly, set a few learnable token embeddings and insert some
757     learnable low-rank matrixes into the U-Net to learn an accurate concept for each category with the
758     original training set. Then for a given image, add T timesteps noise and denoise with a prompt
759     containing its learned category concept.
760   • Diff-Mix (Wang et al., 2024): Firstly, set a few learnable token embeddings and insert some learn-
761     able low-rank matrixes into the U-Net to learn an accurate concept for each category with the
762     original training set. Then for a given image, add random timesteps noise and denoise with a
763     prompt containing learned other-category concepts. This will lead to a synthetic image with inter-
764     mediate semantics between the two categories. Design a calculation mechanism to decide the soft
765
        label for this synthetic image.
766
      Long-tail classification methods:
767
768   • CMO (Park et al., 2022): To balance the number of different categories’s training samples. CMO
769     crops the objects from the rare-category images and pastes them to rich-category images to get
770     some new images with rare-category objects and rich-category images’ backgrounds. These new
771     images will be used to expand the rare-category images.
772   • CMO+DRW (Cao et al., 2019): Except on oversampling-based CMO, DRW gives different
773     weights to the loss of different categories. Specifically, the rare categories get a large loss weight
774     while rich categories get a smaller loss weight.

779
780   Details of our Diff-II:
781
782   • Category concept learning: We follow the implementations of (Wang et al., 2024)4 .
783
      • Inversion interpolation: We use DDIM inversion (Song et al., 2020) with 25 steps and 1.0 guidance
        scale (Ho & Salimans, 2022) to calculate the inversion for each image. Then for each category, we
784
        randomly sample inversion pairs until the number of inversion pairs reaches five times the number
785
        of samples in the original training set. After that, we conduct circle interpolation on these pairs
786     with random strength λ ∈ [0, 2π/α] (c.f., Section 3.2.2).
787   • Two-stage denosing: We used BLIP-caption (Li et al., 2022) to get captions of all images. Then,
788     we used GPT-4-turbo (Achiam et al., 2023) to summarize the captions into suffixes with the
789     prompt:
790     “I have a set of image captions that I want to summarize into objective descriptions that describe
791     the scenes, actions, camera pose, zoom, and other image qualities present.
792     My captions are: {captions}
793     I want the output to be a <= 10 of captions that describe a unique setting, of the form {prefix}.
794
        Here are 3 examples of what I want the output to look like:
795
        - {prefix} standing on a branch.
796
797
        - {prefix} flying in the sky with the Austin skyline in the background.
798     - {prefix} playing in a river at night.
799     Based on the above captions, the output should be:”
800     Then, for each denoising, we randomly sampled a suffix for the first stage. For 5-shot classifica-
801     tion, the split ratio was 0.3; for the 10-shot classification, the split ratio was 0.1; for the long-tail
802     classification, the split ratio was 1.0. For the sample, we used the DDIM sampler with 25 steps
803
        and 7.5 guidance scale.
804
      Details of comparison methods: For few-shot classification, we followed the reproduction imple-
805
      mentations (i.e., the timesteps of adding noise) of (Wang et al., 2024). The translation strengths of
806   Real-Guidance, Real-Mix, Da-Fusion, Diff-AUG, and Diff-Mix are 0.1, random one of [0.5, 0.7,
807   0.9], random one of [0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0], 1.0, and random one of [0.5, 0.7, 0.9]. For long-tail
808   classification, we directly report the results from (Wang et al., 2024).
