                                                     A BSTRACT
031
032
                In this paper, we propose HE-Drive: the first human-like-centric end-to-end au-
                tonomous driving system to generate trajectories that are both temporally consis-
033
                tent and comfortable. Recent studies have shown that imitation learning-based
034
                planners and learning-based trajectory scorers can effectively generate and se-
035             lect accuracy trajectories that closely mimic expert demonstrations. However,
036             such trajectory planners and scorers face the dilemma of generating temporally
037             inconsistent and uncomfortable trajectories. To solve the above problems, Our
038             HE-Drive first extracts key 3D spatial representations through sparse perception,
039             which then serves as conditional inputs for the Conditional Denoising Diffusion
040             Probabilistic Model (DDPM)-based motion planner to generate temporal consis-
041             tency multi-modal trajectories. A Vision-Language Model (VLM)-guided trajec-
042             tory scorer subsequently selects the most comfortable trajectory from these candi-
043
                dates to control the vehicle, ensuring human-like end-to-end driving. Experiments
                show that HE-Drive not only achieves state-of-the-art performance (i.e., reduces
044
                the average collision rate by 71% than VAD) and efficiency (i.e., 1.9× faster than
045
                SparseDrive) on the challenging nuScenes and OpenScene datasets but also pro-
046             vides the most comfortable driving experience on real-world data.

049   1    I NTRODUCTION
050
051   The end-to-end paradigm (Hu et al., 2023b; Jiang et al., 2023b; Sun et al., 2024), which integrates
052   perception, planning and trajectory scoring tasks into a unified model optimized for planning objec-
053   tives, has recently demonstrated significant potential in advancing autonomous driving technology
      (Figure 1a). The latest research proposes imitation learning-based motion planners (Chen et al.,


                                                            1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2024; Cheng et al., 2024) that learn driving strategies from large-scale driving demonstrations and
055   employ learning-based trajectory scorers (Zhao et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2023a) to select the safest
056   and most accurate trajectory from multiple predicted candidates to control the vehicle. However,
057   despite the significant improvements in prediction accuracy achieved by existing planners and scor-
058   ers, they face the challenges of generating temporally inconsistent trajectories, where consecutive
059   predictions are unstable and inconsistent over time, and selecting uncomfortable trajectories that
060   exhibit continuous braking, resulting in stalling or excessive turning curvature.
061   In this work, we introduce HE-Drive, the first human-like-centric end-to-end autonomous driving
062   system to solve the above two problems, as illustrated in Figure 2. Specifically, we find that the
063   temporal inconsistency in trajectories generated by imitation learning-based planners arises from
064   two main factors: temporal correlation and generalization. Firstly, these planners rely on the past few
065   seconds of information from the current frame to forecast future trajectories, ignoring the correlation
066   between consecutive predictions (Zhou et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024). Secondly, their performance
067   is constrained by the quality of the collected offline expert trajectories, rendering them susceptible
068   to changes in system dynamics and out-of-distribution states, resulting in learned policies that lack
069
      generalization ability to unseen scenarios. Inspired by the success of the diffusion policy (Chi et al.,
      2024) in robotic manipulation, which employs a vision-conditioned diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020)
070
      to accurately represent multi-modal distributions for generating action sequences, we propose a
071
      diffusion-based planner that generates multi-modal trajectories with strong temporal consistency.
072
073   Furthermore, the key reason for the uncomfortable predicted trajectories is the suboptimal trajectory
074   scorer’s inability to achieve lifelong evaluation and the absence of universal metrics to measure tra-
075
      jectory comfort. Recent studies have revealed that learning-based scorers are inferior to rule-based
      scorers in closed-loop scenarios (Dauner et al., 2023), while the latter suffers from limited general-
076
      ization due to their reliance on hand-crafted post-processing. Other researchers have explored the
077
      use of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) (Shao et al., 2024; Sima et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024a) to
078   perceive the motion and traffic representation of surrounding agents to decide the next movement.
079   However, directly employing VLMs as driving decision-makers poses challenges related to poor
080   interpretability and severe hallucinations (Xu et al., 2024b). To address these issues, we propose a
081   novel trajectory scorer and universal comfort metric that combines the interpretability of rule-based
082   scorers with the adaptability of VLMs to adjust driving styles (i.e., aggressive or conservative) for
083   lifelong evaluation.
084   In summary, HE-Drive, the novel human-like-centric end-to-end autonomous driving system, uti-
085   lizes sparse perception to detect, track, and map driving scenarios based on sparse features, generat-
086   ing 3D spatial representations. These representations are conditionally input into the diffusion-based
087   motion planner, powered by the Conditional Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM). Fi-
088   nally, a VLM-guided (i.e., Llama 3.2V) trajectory scorer selects the most comfortable trajectory
089   from the candidates to control the vehicle, ensuring human-like end-to-end driving. The main con-
090   tributions of our work are summarized as follows:

             • Diffusion-based Motion Planner: We propose a diffusion-based motion planner that gen-

               erates temporal consistent and multi-modal trajectories by conditioning on the 3D represen-

               tation extracted by the sparse perception network and incorporating the speed, acceleration,
094            and yaw of the historical prediction trajectory. (§ 3.2)
095
096
             • Plug-and-Play Trajectory Scorer: We introduce a novel VLMs-guided trajectory scorer
               and a comfort metric, which address the gap in human-like driving, making it easily inte-
097

098
099          • Excellent Results in Open-loop and Closed-loop Benchmarks: HE-Drive achieves state-

102

      2.1   E ND - TO -E ND AUTONOMOUS D RIVING
112
113   End-to-end autonomous driving aims to generate planning trajectories directly from raw sensors. In
114   the field, advancements have been categorized based on their evaluation methods: open-loop and
115   closed-loop systems. In open-loop systems, UniAD (Hu et al., 2023a) presents a unified framework
116   that integrates full-stack driving tasks with query-unified interfaces for improved interaction between
117   tasks. VAD (Jiang et al., 2023a) boosts planning safety and efficiency, evidenced by its performance
118
      on the nuScenes dataset, while SparseDrive (Sun et al., 2024) utilizes sparse representations to
      mitigate information loss and error propagation inherent in modular systems, enhancing both task
119
      performance and computational efficiency. For closed-loop evaluations, VADv2 (Chen et al., 2024)
120
      advances vectorized autonomous driving with probabilistic planning, using multi-view images to
121   generate action distributions for vehicle control, excelling in the CARLA Town05 benchmark.
122
123
      2.2   D IFFUSION M ODELS FOR T RAJECTORY G ENERATION
124
125   Diffusion models initially celebrated in image synthesis, have been adeptly repurposed for trajectory
126   generation. Potential-Based Diffusion Motion Planning (Luo et al., 2024) further enhances the field
127   by employing learned potential functions to construct adaptable motion plans for cluttered environ-
128   ments, demonstrating the method’s scalability and transferability. NoMaD (Sridhar et al., 2024)
129   and SkillDiffuser (Liang et al., 2024) both present unified frameworks that streamline goal-oriented
130   navigation and skill-based task execution, respectively, with NoMaD achieving improved naviga-
131
      tion outcomes and SkillDiffuser enabling interpretable, high-level instruction following. In a word,
      diffusion models offer a promising alternative to imitation learning-based end-to-end autonomous
132
      driving frameworks for trajectory planning. Imitation learning models may incorrectly attribute a
133
      driver’s actions to the wrong causal factors due to inherent causal confusion. In contrast, diffusion
134   models can better capture the underlying causal relationships by learning the joint distribution of
135   scene features and driver actions in the latent space, enabling the model to correctly associate the
136   true causes with the appropriate actions.
137
138   2.3   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS (LLM S ) FOR T RAJECTORY E VALUATION
139
140   Trajectory scoring (Fan et al., 2018) plays a vital role in autonomous driving decision-making. Rule-
141   based methods (Treiber et al., 2000) provide strong safety guarantees but lack flexibility, while
142   learning-based methods (Chitta et al., 2021; Prakash et al., 2021) perform well in open-loop tasks
143
      but struggle in closed-loop scenarios (Treiber et al., 2000; Dauner et al., 2023). Recently, DriveLM
      (Sima et al., 2023) integrates VLMs into end-to-end driving systems, modelling graph-structured
144
      reasoning through perception, prediction, and planning question-answer pairs. However, the gener-
145
      ated results of large models may contain hallucinations and require further strategies for safe appli-
146   cation in autonomous driving. The emergence of VLMs raises the question: Can VLMs adaptively
147   adjust driving style while ensuring comfort based on a trajectory scorer?

182
183
184   Figure 2 illustrates the overall pipeline of our diffusion-based motion planner. We adopt the CNN-
185
      based diffusion policy (Chi et al., 2024; Ze et al., 2024) as the foundation, which consists of a
      conditional U-Net composed of 1D convolution layers, upsampling layers, and FiLM (Feature-wise
186
      Linear Modulation) layers (Perez et al., 2018).
187
188   Motion Planner Diffusion Policy: Our method (Figure 7) employs the Conditional Denoising Dif-
189   fusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM), a generative model defined through parameterized Markov
190   chains trained using variational inference to model the conditional distribution p(At |Ot ). The
191
      DDPM consists of a forward process that gradually adds Gaussian noise to the input data, con-
      verting it into pure noise, and a reverse process that iteratively denoises the noisy data to recover the
192
      original data.
193
194   Specifically, the input conditions to the DDPM include the compact 3D representation Θ, ego status
195   e, historical predicted trajectories H, and their corresponding velocity vi , acceleration ai , and yaw
196   encoding θi . The concatenated condition C, which includes observation Ot and above relevant
197
      conditions, is injected into every convolutional layer of the network using FiLM (Perez et al., 2018).
      This channel-wise conditioning guides the trajectory generation from the ego position to the anchor
198
      position. The denoising process begins with a Gaussian noise Akt of shape [B, Na , Ti , P], where

202   trajectory A0 through k iterations using the denoising network ϵθ . Each trajectory τi is represented

290   3.3.2    VLM S H ELP A DJUST D RIVING S TYLE .
291
292   Our VLM-guided trajectory scorer (Figure 3) consists of two stages. In the first stage, we curate
293   a dataset containing annotated surround images, which include descriptions of the current driving
294   scene, motion states of surrounding agents (i.e., humans and vehicles), and the current driving style
295   (i.e., aggressive or conservative) along with weight adjustment values. Through iterative dialogues,
296   Llama 3.2V (Dubey et al., 2024) assimilates contextual information, mitigating model hallucina-
297   tions.
298   In the second stage, we generate a series of prompt templates using GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023)
299   for visual question answering (VQA). Leveraging the spatial-temporal stability inherent in traffic
300   patterns, we activate Llama 3.2V intermittently every five seconds to refine driving behaviours. The
301   model assesses the driving context when processing new imagery and calibrates the scoring weights
302   for predefined safety and comfort parameters within the rule-based system. This approach allows
303   for precise alterations to cost weights, enhancing the responsiveness of the driving style to varying
304
      scenarios. By employing the VLM as a driving style regulator rather than a direct decision maker, we
      mitigate safety risks associated with model hallucinations and improve rule-based trajectory scorer
305
      adaptability for new scenarios.

311   propose a general metric to assess the comfort and human likeness of predicted trajectories (Han

313   trajectory and a ground true trajectory, considering factors such as dynamic feasibility, jerk, and
314   trajectory smoothness.

      4.1   E XPERIMENT S ETUP
346
347   Datasets: Our experiments are conducted on the 3 challenging datasets, i.e., the nuScenes dataset
348   and the real-world dataset for open-loop testing, complemented by the OpenScene dataset for closed-
349   loop evaluation. Specifically, nuScenes dataset (Caesar et al., 2020), encompasses 1,000 detailed
350   driving scenes, each with a duration of approximately 20 seconds. OpenScene dataset (Contributors,
351   2023; Dauner et al., 2024) is specifically curated to mitigate the prevalent issues found in open-loop
352   evaluation protocols by employing navigation goals extracted from lane graphs and introducing
353   simulation-based metrics that serve as a more robust alternative to displacement errors.
354   Implementation Details: The training process of HE-Drive is divided into multiple stages. Firstly,
355   we train the sparse perception component following the two-stage approach proposed in SparseDrive
356   (Sun et al., 2024), according to the different perception backbone networks, it is divided into HE-
357   Drive-S and HE-Drive-B. The output of the second stage of the sparse perception training serves
358   as the input to the motion planner. Our motion planner employs a convolutional network-based
359   diffusion policy (Chi et al., 2024) to generate accurate and temporally consistent trajectories. Finally,
360   we perform end-to-end training of the entire HE-Drive system. This end-to-end training is conducted
361
      on 8 NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPUs, utilizing the AdamW (Loshchilov, 2017) optimizer with a weight
      decay of 0.01 and an initial learning rate of 5e-4.

529
      In this paper, we introduce HE-Drive, a novel human-like-centric end-to-end autonomous driving
530
      system that addresses the limitations of existing methods in achieving temporal consistency and pas-
531   senger comfort. HE-Drive integrates a sparse perception module, a diffusion-based motion planner,
532   and a Llama 3.2V guided trajectory scoring system. The sparse perception module achieves a fully
533   sparse scene representation by unifying detection, tracking, and online mapping. The diffusion-
534   based motion planner generates multi-modal trajectories in continuous space, ensuring temporal
535   consistency and mimicking human decision-making. The trajectory scoring module combines rule-
536   based methods with Llama 3.2V to enhance generalization, interpretability, stability, and comfort.
537   Extensive experiments demonstrate HE-Drive’s superior performance compared to state-of-the-art
538   methods in both open-loop and closed-loop datasets, generating human-like trajectories with im-
539
      proved temporal consistency and passenger comfort.

725   Figure 7: Motion Planner Diffusion Policy Overview. a) General formulation. At time step t, the
726   policy takes the latest To steps of observation data Ot as input and outputs Ta steps of trajectories
727   At . b) The concatenated condition feature C, which includes observation features Θ, ego status
728   e, historical predicted trajectories H, and their corresponding velocity vi , acceleration ai , and yaw
729   encoding θi , is injected into every convolutional layer using FiLM: FiLM(xi ) = γi ⊙ xi + βi , where
730
      γi and βi are obtained by passing C through a fully connected layer. This channel-wise conditioning
      guides the trajectory generation from the ego position to the anchor position.
731

733   During the training phase, the DDPM learns to estimate the noise component ϵθ given the noisy in-
734   put Akt , the timestep k, and the conditioning variables Ot , Θ, e, and H. The conditioning variables
735
      are incorporated into the denoising network ϵθ through the FiLM layers, which modulate the acti-
      vations of the convolutional layers based on the conditioning information. Specifically, the compact
736
      3D representation Θ is processed by a separate convolutional neural network to extract relevant fea-
737
      tures, while the ego status e, historical predicted trajectories H, and their corresponding velocity vi ,
738   acceleration ai , and yaw encoding θi are concatenated and processed by fully connected layers. The
739   resulting conditioning vectors are then used to modulate the activations of the U-Net’s convolutional
740   layers via element-wise affine transformations, enabling the denoising network to adapt its behavior
741   based on the input conditions. By minimizing the difference between the estimated noise and the
742   actual noise added during the forward process, the model learns to denoise the data effectively.
743
      During inference, the model starts with pure Gaussian noise and iteratively denoises it using the
744   learned denoising network ϵθ , conditioned on the input variables, to generate realistic and diverse
745   trajectories. The compact 3D representation Θ provides the model with essential spatial context, al-
746   lowing it to understand the environment and generate trajectories that adhere to road constraints and
747   navigate around obstacles. The ego status e and historical predicted trajectories H enable the model
748   to maintain temporal consistency by considering the vehicle’s current state and its previous motion
749   patterns. Furthermore, the velocity vi , acceleration ai , and yaw encoding θi of the historical trajec-
750   tories provide valuable insights into the vehicle’s dynamics and orientation, allowing the DDPMs
751   to generate trajectories that smoothly transition from the past to the future, ensuring realistic and
752
      physically plausible motion patterns.
753   Notably, the Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models (DDIM) approach (Song et al.) can replace
754   DDPM to achieve real-time inference, which decouples the number of denoising iterations in train-
755   ing and inference, thereby allowing the algorithm to use fewer iterations for inference to speed up
      the diffusion process.

      A.2   C OMFORT M ETRIC
757
758   To represent the comfort index as a percentage, we can introduce a normalization factor that scales
759   the comfort index to a value between 0 and 1, with 1 corresponding to a perfect match with the
760   expert trajectory (100% comfort) and 0 indicating the least comfortable trajectory. The normalized
761   comfort index can be expressed as:
762
763                                                Cn = e−αC ,                                           (14)
764
765   where Cn is the normalized comfort index, C is the original comfort index as defined in equation (2),
766   and α is a positive scaling factor that determines the sensitivity of the normalized index to changes
767   in the original index. A larger value of α will result in a more rapid decrease in the normalized index
768   as the original index increases.
769   The comfort percentage can then be calculated as:
770
771
                                        Cp = 100 × Cn = 100 × e−αC .                                     (15)
772
773   With this formulation, a comfort index of 0 corresponds to a comfort percentage of 100%, while
774   larger values of the comfort index result in lower comfort percentages. The scaling factor α can be
775   tuned based on the desired sensitivity of the comfort percentage to changes in the comfort index.
776   By representing the comfort index as a percentage, we provide a more intuitive understanding of the
777   comfort level of the predicted trajectory relative to the expert trajectory. This normalized representa-
778   tion allows for easier comparison between different trajectories and can be more readily interpreted
779   by users of the end-to-end driving system.
