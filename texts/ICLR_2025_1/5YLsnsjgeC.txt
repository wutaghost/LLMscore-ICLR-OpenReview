011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Structure-based drug design (SBDD) is a key challenge in drug discovery that
014            aims to generate small molecules capable of binding tightly to specific protein
015            pockets. However, current diffusion models have focused on the complemen-
016
               tarity of ligand molecules and protein pockets in physical space while ignoring
               the docking energy requirements, resulting in only generating suboptimal dock-
017
               ing postures. In this paper, we present VFDiff, a novel SE(3)-equivariant diffu-
018
               sion model for 3D molecular generation, guided by vector fields derived from
019            protein-ligand binding energy. In contrast to current diffusion models, VFD-
020            iff incorporates energy-based guidance in both forward and reverse processes to
021            ensure ligand molecules are spatially complementary and energetically matched
022            to their target pockets. Our approach includes three fundamental mechanisms:
023            energy-planning, which adjusts diffusion trajectories based on energy gradients;
024            force-guiding, which refines molecular generation; and position-tuning, which
025            improves sampling accuracy. Extensive experiments on the CrossDocked2020
026            dataset demonstrate that VFDiff outperforms state-of-the-art methods, achieving
027
               superior binding binding affinity with an impressive Avg. Vina Score of up to
               -7.37, while maintaining competitive molecular properties, and diversity. This
028
               work introduces a new framework for generating target-specific molecules with
029
               improved structural and functional fidelity, offering a significant advancement in
030            SBDD.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Structure-based drug design (SBDD) technology is one of the economical and efficient means to ad-
036   dress the development of lead compounds against specific protein pocket targets (Anderson (2003);
037   Batool et al. (2019)). The goal of SBDD is to generate small molecules that are structurally com-
038   plementary to the pocket space and matched in energy (Kalyaanamoorthy & Chen (2011)), thus
039   enabling tighter binding of the molecule to the pocket and exerting the drug function of the molecule.
040
      Recently, with rapid progress in structural biology and the increasing scale of structural data (Fran-
041
      coeur et al. (2020)), several target-aware generative methods have achieved remarkable results in
042   this field (Luo et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2022); Peng et al. (2022); Huang et al. (2024b); Lin et al.
043   (2022)). In particular, equivariant diffusion models (EDMs) have emerged as the current SOTA
044   model in molecular generation, excelling in preserving geometric symmetry in the marginal distri-
045   bution of generated molecules (Hoogeboom et al. (2022); Xu et al. (2022); Guan et al. (2023; 2024);
046   Bao et al. (2022)). Nevertheless, we found that these models were far from our desired goal, i.e.,
047   generating ligand molecules that are complementary to the protein pocket shape and energetically
048   matched. Specifically, what these EDMs do is actually to reconstruct the trajectory of a predefined
049   stochastic process. For different ligands in the complexes, the same noise-added disturbing strat-
050
      egy actually just allowed the model generate ligand molecules spatially compatible with the protein
      pockets but failed to show control on the energy matching. Therefore, we believe that the current
051
      EDM will limit the molecules’ properties in this regard.
052
053   To bridge the gap between existing SBDD models and the necessity for designing ideal ligands,
      we propose the Vector Field Guided Diffusion (VFDiff), a shifted-diffusion model with guidance

054
      of binding energy. Specifically, we first train an SE(3)-equivariant neural network called VFNet
055   on PDBBind2016 (Wang et al. (2004)) by supervised learning to learn the energy function for
056   improving the controllability of generation. In physics, the derivation of the energy score to atomic
057   coordinates will yield a force field (instead of vector field in the later text), which naturally exploits
058   the geometric symmetry in 3D molecular conformation, as long as the energy function is invariant to
059   orthogonal transformations. This vector field reflects the force on the ligand molecule in the protein
060   pocket and also indicates the direction of the energy decrease (Pissurlenkar et al. (2009)). We
061   introduce this prior information into our diffusion model with an elaborated schedule such that the
062   endpoint of the modified trajectory remains practically unchanged compared to the final distribution
063
      of a standard diffusion process. In the forward process, the shifted-prior predicted by VFNet would
      cause the molecules to diffuse in the direction of decreasing energy (energy-planning) and in the
064
      reverse process guide the molecules toward increasing energy (force-guiding). In addition another
065
      vector field predicted by VFNet will fine-tune the initial position predicted in the inverse process
066   to obtain more accurate predictions of atom coordinates (position-tuning). To demonstrate the
067   efficacy of our VFDiff, we conduct extensive experiments on the CrossDocked2020 (Francoeur
068   et al. (2020)) dataset. Empirical results show that our VFDiff can generate ligands that not only
069   bind tightly to target pockets but also maintain proper molecular properties, outperforming existing
070   diffusion-based molecular models (The code of VFDiff will be publicly accessible upon acceptance).
071
072   We highlight our contributions as follows:
073
074   • We propose a novel 3D geometric diffusion model (VFDiff) for SBDD, which facilitates ligand
075
      generation by controlling changes in pocket-ligand binding affinity during the forward and reverse
      processes. Additionally, we ensure the equivariance of coordinate transformations throughout these
076
      processes.
077
      • We design position-tuning to enhance sampling accuracy in the reverse process by fine-tuning the
078
      coordinates of the predicted X̂0 , helping the model establish correct molecular conformations early
079
      in the generation process.
080   • VFDiff achieves state of the art (SOTA) performance on CrossDocked2020 benchmark, and it can
081   generate the molecules with -7.37 Avg. Vina Score while maintaining proper molecular properties.

084   2    R ELATED W ORK
085
086   Structure-based Drug Design Anderson (2003) focuses on generating ligand molecules that bind to
087   a specific target protein. Skalic et al. (2019); Xu et al. (2021) proposed generating ligand molecules
088   in SMILES format conditioned on protein contexts. Tan et al. (2023) developed a flow model capable
089   of generating validated molecular drugs as 2D graphs based on the sequence embeddings of specific
090   targets. Ragoza et al. (2022) voxelized molecules into atomic density grids and applied VAE to
091   generate 3D ligand molecules at receptor binding sites. Luo et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2022); Peng
092   et al. (2022) introduced a method for generating atoms and bonds in 3D Euclidean space in an
093
      autoregressive manner. Zhang et al. (2023) proposed generating 3D ligand molecules fragment by
      fragment. Recently, diffusion models have been successfully applied to SBDD, showing promising
094
      results, which will be discussed in more detail in the following section. Our work aims to enhance
095
      3D molecular diffusion models for SBDD.
096
097   Diffusion Models for SBDD Guan et al. (2023); Lin et al. (2022); Schneuing et al. (2022) utilize
098   diffusion models to first generate atom types and positions, followed by defining the bonds in a post-
099
      processing step. They propose using SE(3)-equivariant neural networks to denoise ligand molecules
      within the protein-ligand complex, where the protein pocket remains fixed. Schneuing et al. (2022)
100
      additionally attempts to generate compounds (i.e., the protein-ligand complex) by inpainting condi-
101
      tioned on the binding site of the pocket. Guan et al. (2024) further incorporates decomposed priors
102   into diffusion models for SBDD, inspired by traditional drug discovery methods, and achieves high
103   average binding affinity. Binding affinity, an essential metric in drug discovery, evaluates whether
104   drugs selectively and specifically bind to their target proteins. While DecompDiff Guan et al. (2024)
105   has delivered promising results, it depends heavily on external computational tools, such as AlphaS-
106   pace2 Rooklin et al. (2015), to extract subpockets and generate pocket priors when creating ligand
107   molecules for new pockets. IPDiff sought to integrate protein-ligand interactions into diffusion
      model generation, but it failed to incorporate explicit modeling of these interactions in 3D coor-

      dinates for direct and accurate guidance, and the lack of geometric symmetry guided information
109   limited the model’s performance. Different from all the above, our molecular diffusion model for
110   the first time considers pockets, ligand molecules, and their SE(3)-equivariant energy information in
111   both forward and reverse processes. Moreover, our VFDiff has no dependency on any external tools
112   and outperforms all other methods in binding affinity, bond distance distribution, and in diversity.

136   4     M ETHOD
137
138   As described in the previous sections, we attempted to introduce information about the binding
139   energy of protein-ligand complexes into the forward and backward processes of the diffusion model
140
      to advance molecule generation. Therefore, we propose VFDiff, a novel diffusion model based
      on vector field guidance. We first design a prior network VFNet to capture the interactions between
141
      pockets and ligands from the perspective of both 3D geometric information and chemical properties,
142
      and pretrain it with binding affinity signals and noised 3D coordinates. Then, we take the pretrained
143   VFNet as a provider of binding affinity information to facilitate the binding-aware ligand diffusion
144   process. To fully utilize protein-molecule binding information in both forward and reverse processes
145   of our diffusion framework, we introduce three mechanisms: energy-planning, force-guiding, and
146   position-tuning (detailed in Section 4.2).

333
334   In order to accurately model the force field of a ligand molecule within a protein pocket, we used the
335   molecule-protein pairs (complexes) binding affinity in the PDBbind v2016 dataset as a supervising
336   signal to train VFNet. The PDBbind v2016 dataset consists of 3325 training complexes and 178 test-
337   ing complexes, and it is commonly employed in binding-affinity prediction tasks. As for molecular
338   generation, following the previous work Luo et al. (2021); Peng et al. (2022); Guan et al. (2023), we
339   train and evaluate VFDiff on the CrossDocked2020 dataset Francoeur et al. (2020). We follow the
340   same data preparation and splitting as Luo et al. (2021), where the 22.5 million docked binding com-
341   plexes are refined to high-quality docking poses (RMSD between the docked pose and the ground
342
      truth <1Å) and diverse proteins (sequence identity <30%). The resulting dataset consists of 100,
      000 protein-ligand pairs for training and 100 proteins for testing. To ensure a fair comparison with
343
      our baseline methods, we adhere to identical data splits for training our guide models and evaluating
344
      our overall method.

347   5.2     BASELINE M ETHODS
348
      We evaluate our method against state-of-the-art baselines in structure-based drug design (SBDD).
349
      These include liGAN (Ragoza et al. (2022)), which utilizes a conditional variational autoencoder
350
      (CVAE) trained on grid representations of atomic densities in protein-ligand structures. We also
351   benchmark against autoregressive methods such as AR (Luo et al. (2021)) and Pocket2Mol (Peng
352   et al. (2022)), which are GNN-based approaches that generate 3D molecular atoms by conditioning
353   on the protein pocket and previously generated atoms. Moreover, our comparisons include recent
354   diffusion-based methods like TargetDiff (Guan et al. (2023)) and DecompDiff (Guan et al. (2024)),
355   which have established new benchmarks for the non-autoregressive generation of atom coordinates
356   and types. DecompDiff improves upon TargetDiff by incorporating bond information and introduc-
357   ing decomposed priors for ligand arms and scaffolds. IRDiff (Huang et al. (2024a)) applies data
358   retrieval augmented generation techniques from NLP to filter references from high-affinity libraries,
359
      boosting the affinity of generated molecules. IPDiff (Huang et al. (2024c)) is the current state-of-the-
      art diffusion method that integrates protein-ligand interactions, generating molecules more likely to
360
      fall within the desired property distributions.

370
      QED (Quantitative Estimate of Druglikeness) This metric evaluates a molecule’s drug-likeness by
371
      reflecting the typical distribution of molecular properties in successful drug candidates (Bickerton
372   et al. (2012)). SA (Synthetic Accessibility) SA assesses the ease with which a molecule can be
373   synthesized, serving as a vital indicator of its practical manufacturability in a laboratory or industrial
374   setting (Ertl & Schuffenhauer (2009); You et al. (2018)). Diversity. Diversity is measured as the
375   average pairwise Tanimoto distances (Tanimoto (1958)) among all ligands generated for a specific
376   protein pocket. Bond Distance Distribution. We calculate the Jensen Shannon divergences (JSD)
377   to assess the differences in bond distance distributions between the reference molecules and the
      generated molecules (Lin (1991)).

469   5.5   M ODEL A NALYSIS
470
      Influence of Energy-planning Force-guiding and Position-tuning Structure-based molecule gen-
471
      eration is aims to fulfill two key requirements: (1) the ligand molecules have to be spatially com-
472   plementary to the protein pockets (2) they must match in terms of binding energy. We believe that
473   traditional diffusion models only fit the spatial data distribution, resulting in generated molecules
474   that perform poorly in binding energy, which is one of the most important metrics in SBDD. To
475   solve the above problem, we propose energy-planning, force-guiding, and position-tuning mech-
476   anisms that use energy constraints to change the trajectories of atoms during forward and reverse
477   processes, thus better modeling the data distribution. We showcase these methods efficacy in our
478   VFDiff, and put results in Table 3.

      6    C ONCLUSION
531
      The paper introduces VFDiff, an innovative SE(3)-equivariant diffusion model guided by vector
532
      fields for target-aware 3D molecule generation. The model represents a significant advancement in
533
      structure-based drug design (SBDD) by explicitly incorporating protein-ligand binding energy into
534   the diffusion process. Unlike previous models that only focus on shape complementarity, VFDiff
535   ensures both spatial and energetic compatibility between generated molecules and target protein
536   pockets. Through mechanisms such as energy-planning, force-guiding, and position-tuning, the
537   model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the CrossDocked2020 dataset, with superior binding
538   affinity, molecular properties, and diversity. This novel approach bridges a critical gap in molecular
539   generation by leveraging physical principles like force fields, offering both theoretical depth and
      practical applications in drug discovery.

947   E.2   VFN ET T RAINING
948
      During the training, we use the Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss with respect to the difference
949
      between the predicted and ground truth binding affinity scores as the optimization objective. The
950   binding affinity values of protein-ligand pairs range from 2.0 to 11.92. For avoiding information
951   leakage, we filter the training set by calculating the Tanimoto similarity with the molecules in the
952   testing set of CrossDocked2020, and the similarity threshold was set to 0.1. As a result, there are 23
953   complexes filtered out from the training set. We train VFNet on a single NVIDIA RTX4090 GPU,
954   and we use the Adam as our optimizer with learning rate 1e-4, betas = (0.99, 0.999),
955   batch size 4.

957   E.3   F EATURIZATION
958
959
      Whether in VFNet or in VFDiff, at the l-th layer, we dynamically construct the protein-ligand com-
      plex as a k-nearest neighbors (knn) graph based on known protein atom coordinates and current
960
      ligand atom coordinates, which is the output of the l-th layer. We choose k = 32 in VFDiff and
961
      k = 48 in VFNet, respectively. The protein atom features include chemical elements, amino acid
962   types and whether the atoms are backbone atoms. The ligand atom types are one-hot vectors consist-
963   ing of the chemical element types and aromatic information. The edge features are the outer products
964   of distance embedding and bond types, where we expand the distance with radial basis functions lo-
965   cated at 20 centers between 0Å and 10 Å and the bond type is a 4-dim one-hot vector indicating the
966   connection is between protein atoms, ligand atoms, protein-ligand atoms or ligand-protein atoms
967   (Guan et al. (2023)).
