009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This study explores the scaling properties of Reinforcement Learning from Hu-
012            man Feedback (RLHF) in Large Language Models (LLMs). Although RLHF is
013            considered an important step in post-training of LLMs, its scaling potential is still
014            largely unknown. We systematically analyze key components in the RLHF frame-
015            work—model size, data composition, and inference budget—and their impacts on
016            performance. Our findings show that increasing data diversity and volume improves
017            reward model performance, helping process-supervision models scale better. For
018            policy training, more response samples per prompt boost performance initially but
019
               quickly plateau. And larger reward models offer modest gains in policy training. In
               addition, larger policy models benefit less from RLHF with a fixed reward model.
020
               Overall, RLHF scales less efficiently than pretraining, with diminishing returns
021
               from additional computational resources. Based on these observations, we propose
022            strategies to optimize RLHF performance within computational limits.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing by learning ex-
028   tensive language patterns from vast datasets. A key step in these models is reinforcement learning
029   from human feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022), which helps align the model’s behavior with
030   human intentions and enhances their performance across diverse tasks such as text generation Hu et al.
031   (2024), coding (Li et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2024), and mathematical reasoning (Zhu et al., 2024) before
032   deployment. This approach has been successfully applied to leading models like ChatGPT (Achiam
033
      et al., 2023), Llama (Touvron et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024), and Claude (Bai et al., 2022b), yielding
      notable improvements. RLHF improves the model’s behavior by integrating external feedback, such
034
      as human preferences and answer supervision, to enhance its generation. The process begins with
035
      training a reward model using human-labeled preference data or reasoning data with correctness
036   labels, serving as a proxy for human supervision. Then, reinforcement learning is employed to
037   optimize the policy model through iterative feedback from the reward model.
038
039
      The scaling properties of large language models, which lead to considerable performance improve-
      ments with increased computational resources (i.e., more data and larger models), have been viewed
040
      as key to the success of the current LLMs. However, the scaling properties of RLHF have been
041
      less understood, while extensive studies have been conducted for both the pretraining stage (Kaplan
042   et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022; Du et al., 2024) and supervised fine-tuning (Yuan et al., 2023).
043   Recently, the emergence of OpenAI-o1 (openai, 2024) has demonstrated the potential of scaling
044   reinforcement learning for reasoning tasks, but the specific methods used to achieve this result have
045   not been disclosed. Previous works (Ivison et al., 2024; Xu et al.), including Llama2 (Touvron et al.,
046   2023), have attempted to explore the greater potential of RLHF, i.e., PPO (Schulman et al., 2017),
047   compared to DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024b), and Llama3 (Dubey et al., 2024) only describe its use
048   of DPO for optimization. They paid little attention to the scaling potential and properties of current
049   RLHF and there is still a limited practical understanding of large-scale RLHF training. This raises
050
      questions about whether current RLHF techniques can lead to significant performance improvement
      like OpenAI-o1, given access to more data and computing resources, or whether we have to develop
051
      a more scalable reinforcement learning algorithm for LLMs.
052

      framework that can be scaled in policy model and reward model training, including model size,

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
111
      Reinforcement learning from human feedback for language models. The primary motivation
      behind reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) is to align language models with human
112
      intentions and preferences (Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023). Subsequent
113
      studies (Shao et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024) also demonstrate the effectiveness of RLHF in boosting
114   LLMs’ reasoning abilities. The general pipeline of RLHF first trains a reward model to capture human
115   preferences, and then optimizes the policy model against it using reinforcement learning algorithms
116   such as PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) and its variants (Ahmadian et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024; Li et al.,
117   2023). Despite its success in leading models like ChatGPT and Claude (Bai et al., 2022a;b), offline
118   methods (Rafailov et al., 2024b; Ethayarajh et al., 2024; Ji et al., 2024) has become widely popular in
119   open-source community due to its simplicity and stability, whereas PPO is resource-intensive and
120   harder to train. Recently, increasing efforts have been made to reveal the secrets in online RLHF
121   training and achieved encouraging performance across various tasks (Ivison et al., 2024; Xu et al.;
122
      Shao et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023). In particular, Ivison et al. (2024) investigates strategies for
      improved PPO across different aspects and demonstrates significant advantages of PPO over DPO.
123
      In this work, we focus specifically on the scaling properties of RLHF and investigate the extent to
124
      which its performance can benefit from increased compute.
125
126   Scaling properties of language models. Scaling is one of the key factors leading to the success
127   of powerful language models and provides crucial insights into continuous improvement of LLMs’
128   performance. Kaplan et al. (2020); Hoffmann et al. (2022) study the scaling laws for pretraining and
129   demonstrate that scaling model size and training tokens can both lead to predictable improvements.
130
      Du et al. (2024) further build the connection between pretraining loss and downstream performance
      and claim that pretraining loss, rather than model size, is the key to predicting emergent abilities.
131
      In the context of reinforcement learning for LLMs, Gao et al. (2023); Cobbe et al. (2021) explore
132
      the scaling laws in reward modeling under a synthetic setting and show that over-optimizing proxy
133   reward models can degrade true performance in RLHF. The same problem is also observed in direct
134   policy optimization when scaling the training (Rafailov et al., 2024a). Recently, OpenAI-o1 (openai,
135   2024) has revealed the potential for scaling reinforcement learning at both training and inference
136   time and significantly boosts the reasoning abilities of LLMs. This development emphasizes the
137   importance of scaling reinforcement learning techniques and raises questions about whether the
138   existing RLHF paradigm can achieve comparable scaling performance.
139
140   3     S TUDY S ETUP
141
142   We first describe the key components in reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) before
143   moving into our extensive empirical study. We follow the general RLHF framework, as shown in
144   Figure 1(c), which first trains a reward model and then uses the model to guide policy training using
145   reinforcement learning. But our implementation has several important differences. First, we train a
146   unified model for both human preference and reasoning tasks using a multi-task objective instead
147   of training multiple separate reward models. Second, during policy training, we sample multiple
148   responses for each prompt and apply additional reward clipping and normalization, which lead to
149   more stable policy training.
150
151   3.1   R EWARD M ODEL T RAINING
152
      The reward model is usually trained on a preference dataset DP , in which each prompt x is associated
153
      with two responses, yc and yr , along with a preference order (denoted as yc > yr , suggesting yc is
154   preferred). These responses are sampled from an existing LLM and the preference generally comes
155   from human annotation or online feedback. The reward model Rψ (x, y) is a scalar function and
156   consists of a transformer backbone with a regression head replacing the traditional language modeling
157   head. Training the reward model involves minimizing the following loss function:
158
                           Lpref = −E(x,yc ,yr )∼DP [log σ (Rψ (x, yc ) − Rψ (x, yr ))] .                   (1)
159
160   For reasoning tasks, which generally have a definitive correct answer I(y) ∈ {0, 1}, such as math
161   and programming, it is more effective to approach them as binary classification problems rather than
      pairwise ranking tasks, as shown in previous studies (Uesato et al., 2022; Lightman et al., 2023; Shao


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      et al., 2024). Consequently, in this work, we adopt a multi-task learning approach to train our reward
163   model. For preference data, we optimize the model using Eq (1), while for binary-labeled data, we
164   employ cross-entropy loss for optimization:
165
166         LR(ψ) = −E(x,yr ,yl )∼DB [yl ∗ log Rψ (x, yr ) + (1 − yl ) ∗ log(1 − Rψ (x, yr ))] + Lpref        (2)
167
      where yl ∈ {0, 1} indicates the correctness of the response. We adopt a similar approach to train the
168   process reward model (PRM) with the following differences. First, we retain outcome supervision for
169   human preference data while introducing process supervision signals only for reasoning-related tasks.
170   Second, the cross-entropy loss is applied to all intermediate steps in addition to the final step. In this
171   work, to produce process supervision, we follow the approach in (Wang et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024)
172   to annotate the intermediate steps automatically with additional inference rollouts.
173
      Research question: Regarding the reward model, we aim to investigate the effects of model size
174
      and data scaling, which includes both the scale and diversity of the reward model training data, as
175
      well as process supervision. Since previous work (Snell et al., 2024) observe that using the PRM’s
176   prediction at the last step shows the best performance in Best-of-N evaluation, it can be considered
177   as a specialized reward model scaling (e.g., in data annotation) that incurs higher inference cost.
178

      3.2   P OLICY M ODEL T RAINING
180
181   The objective of policy training is to maximize the reward collected by its generated responses. The
182   training process involves four models: a reward model that provides feedback, a policy model for
183   optimization, a reference model for regularization, and an optional critic model for stabilizing policy
184   training. For each prompt, rather than using pre-generated responses, a response y is sampled from
185   the latest policy model y ∼ πθ (y|x). This response is scored by the reward model, producing a
186
      response-level reward r = rψ (x, y). In general, a more precise reward can lead to improved policy
      training. In our practice, multiple responses y = {y1 , ..., yN } are sampled for a prompt x to improve
187
      the utilization of prompt data, and all of them are used for policy training. We also perform reward
188
      normalization: given the raw reward r0 = {ri }N     i=1 for all responses in y, the final reward for policy
189                                         ri −mean(r0 ) N
      optimization is obtained via r = { std(r0 ) }i=1 .
190

192   too far away from the SFT model causing degeneration:

      Research question: We first examine whether larger policy models can benefit more from RLHF
217   given a fixed reward model and training strategy. Then from the aforementioned procedures about
218   policy training, we examine what factors can benefit RLHF when scaled up: i.e., whether one should
219   introduce more responses per prompt or more prompts, whether a larger reward model would be
220   beneficial, and whether different reinforcement learning algorithms matter. Given a fixed size policy
221   model, we examine how these factors affect the final performance of the trained policy with increased
222   computing resources.

      4     E XPERIMENT
225
226
      4.1     E XPERIMENT S ETUP
227
228
      Data construction. We collect diverse datasets to train both the reward and policy models. These
229
      datasets include general open-chat, math, code, and instruction-following tasks. Specifically, the
230
      collection comprises MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), Numina-Math (Jia LI & Polu, 2024), a Chinese
231   K-12 dataset, ShareGPT, code contest data, and additional self-collected open-domain chat data. The
232   response data for reward model training is sampled from a series of GLM models (GLM et al., 2024),
233   including GLM4-9B-chat and larger models. The dataset distribution is approximately 40%, 30%,
234   and 30% for general open-chat, math, and code, respectively.
235
236   Training settings. We mainly experiment with GLM4-9B (GLM et al., 2024), a widely-used open-
237
      source LLM in many recent studies. We first train an SFT model based on GLM4-9B, which is
      then used for reward and policy training. All models are trained with OpenRLHF (Hu et al., 2024)
238
      framework. For policy training, we use a constant learning rate, generate rollouts of 1024×M
239
      prompts, and take a gradient step per 128×M samples, where M represents the number of sampled
240   responses per prompt during training. Outcome reward models are used for all experiments. To
241   examine the effect of model size, we also conduct experiments with larger models with around 32B
242   and 200B parameters. Unless specified, models in policy training are based on GLM4-9B-SFT.
243
244   Evaluation. We conduct evaluations on diverse benchmarks, including reasoning, e.g.,
245   MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021) and GPQA (Rein et al., 2023), and LiveCodeBench (Jain et al., 2024),
246
      general tasks, e.g., MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020), and alignment, e.g., AlignBench (Liu et al.,
      2023). We evaluate on a subset of AlignBench which excludes data from mathematical reasoning
247
      and Chinese reasoning categories to focus specially on the general preference alignment evaluation.
248
      For reward model evaluation, we follow the Best-of-N strategy and sample N responses from the
249   policy model for each prompt. These responses with the prompt are then fed into the reward model
250   and the response of the highest-score is selected as the final output for evaluation. For policy model
251   evaluation, we report the performance with responses generated by greedy decoding.

445
446
      Setup. We evaluate the performance of process reward model (PRM) compared to outcome reward
447   model (ORM). In PRM training, only the intermediate steps in the solutions to math-related problems
448   are considered, as these steps can be automatically annotated by assessing the success rate of obtaining
449   a correct answer by rolling out from each intermediate step, following in (Wang et al., 2024). For
450   other tasks like code and human preference, only the final label is used. As a result, the trained PRM
451   is an ORM for general tasks but is specifically tailored for math-related reasoning tasks as PRM.
452
453
      Results. The results are illustrated in Figure 6 (right). PRM exhibits distinct behavior for different
      datasets. For MATH-500, PRM consistently outperforms ORM in Best-of-64 evaluations across dif-
454
      ferent reward model scales. However, the advantage is not consistent for GPQA and LiveCodeBench,
455
      whose process supervision is not covered in the training data. PRM-9B performs better than ORM
456   in LiveCodeBench but worse in GPQA, while PRM-32B shows the opposite trend. This suggests
457   that PRM struggles to generalize to tasks outside its training data, such as GPQA. And expanding
458   training data to cover non-math tasks could further improve PRM’s performance. Considering the
459   generalization problem, we only use ORM in previous policy training experiments.
460
      Overall, PRM can be highly effective for the targeted task, but it struggles to effectively generalize to
461
      other tasks not included in the training set.

      4.4                  D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
465
      Based on the analysis above, we can summarize the factors that contribute to performance improve-
466
      ment with increased compute during RLHF training. These include: more sampled responses, a
467   larger reward model for policy model training, more diverse prompts, and process supervision for
468   reward model training. Note that we did not directly perform policy training under PRM due to the
469   aforementioned generalization problem, and we have not found an effective strategy for generating
470   process supervision across different tasks yet. However, RLHF training does not scale efficiently as
471   pretraining, with improvements tending to saturate and provide only marginal gains beyond a certain
472   point. A more concerning problem is that larger policy models benefit less from RLHF. The problem
473   preventing RLHF scaling may be attributed to inaccuracies in reward modeling, which may lead
474   to substantial noise in policy training. As a result, current RLHF methods do not scale and could
475
      not consistently benefit from increased compute during training. It is thus crucial to explore more
      scalable training methods for reinforcement learning of LLMs.

478   5               C ONCLUSION
479
480   This study examined the scaling properties of reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) in
481   LLMs. We conducted extensive experiments to analyze the key components—model sizes, data, and
482   algorithms. Our findings show that RLHF does not scale as effectively as pretraining or supervised
483   fine-tuning in LLMs. Larger policy models benefit less from RLHF and gains from additional data
484   and compute quickly become limited. Despite these limitations, we also find practical and beneficial
485   strategies. Future work should focus on developing more scalable RLHF techniques to fully unleash
      its potential in boosting LLM performance.

      A.1    B EST- OF -N R ESULTS
651
652   We compare the best-of-N performance of the outcome reward model (ORM) and process reward
653   model (PRM) across different sizes. For each test sample, we initially sample N responses from
654   the policy model with a temperature set to 0.9. Subsequently, the reward model predicts a score
655   for each response. The response with the highest score, as determined by the reward model, is
656   considered the outcome of the reward model. The quality of this response is referred to as the
657   best-of-N score. The evaluation encompasses the domains of math, i.e., MATH-500 and GSM8K,
658   coding, i.e., LiveCodeBench, and reasoning, i.e., GPQA.
659   To assess the generalization capability of the two reward models, we evaluate their performance
660   not only on GLM4-9B-chat (GLM et al., 2024) but also on LLAMA3-8B-Instruct (Dubey et al.,
661   2024). Output distribution shift is common in policy model training due to the increasing divergence
662   between the latest policy model and the SFT model as training progresses. Given that the reward
663   model is trained on the SFT data distribution, it is necessary to test its ability to generalize to shifted
664   distributions.
