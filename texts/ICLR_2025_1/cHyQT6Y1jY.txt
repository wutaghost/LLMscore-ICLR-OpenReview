011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Despite remarkable capabilities, large language models (LLMs) struggle to con-
014            tinually update their knowledge without catastrophic forgetting. In contrast, hu-
015
               mans effortlessly integrate new information, detect conflicts with existing beliefs,
               and selectively update their mental models. This paper introduces a cognitive-
016
               inspired investigation paradigm to study knowledge updating in LLMs. We im-
017
               plement two key components inspired by human cognition: (1) Dissonance and
018            Familiarity Awareness, analyzing model behavior to classify information as novel,
019            familiar, or dissonant; and (2) Targeted Network Updates, which track neural ac-
020            tivity to identify frequently used (stubborn) and rarely used (plastic) neurons.
021            Through carefully designed experiments in controlled settings, we uncover a num-
022            ber of empirical findings demonstrating the potential of this approach. First, disso-
023            nance detection is feasible using simple activation and gradient features, suggest-
024            ing potential for cognitive-inspired training. Second, we find that non-dissonant
025            updates largely preserve prior knowledge regardless of targeting strategy, reveal-
026
               ing inherent robustness in LLM knowledge integration. Most critically, we dis-
               cover that dissonant updates prove catastrophically destructive to the model’s
027
               knowledge base, indiscriminately affecting even information unrelated to the cur-
028
               rent updates. This suggests fundamental limitations in how neural networks han-
029            dle contradictions and motivates the need for new approaches to knowledge up-
030            dating that better mirror human cognitive mechanisms.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Humans effortlessly update their knowledge as they experience the world. They seamlessly inte-
036   grate new information, ignore redundant stimuli, and actively resolve conflicts with existing beliefs
037   before updating their mental models. This cognitive flexibility stems from several key abilities. Hu-
038   mans exhibit (1) selective attention, focusing on novel or relevant information while filtering out
039
      irrelevant or familiar stimuli (Posner et al., 1990; Petersen & Posner, 2012; Desimone et al., 1995;
      Ranganath & Rainer, 2003). They readily (2) detect conflicts (Croyle & Cooper, 1983) between
040
      new information and existing knowledge and actively engage in resolving them, a process known in
041
      psychology as cognitive-dissonance (Festinger, 1957; Van Veen et al., 2009). Moreover, their brains
042   exhibit a form of (3) adaptive plasticity, allowing for updates to neural networks that can incorporate
043   new information while often preserving existing knowledge. While the exact mechanisms are still
044   being investigated, this process seems to balance the stability of well-established knowledge with
045   flexibility in the face of new or uncertain information (McClelland et al., 1995; Behrens et al., 2007).
046
      Despite demonstrating remarkable capabilities across various tasks, Large Language Models
047
      (LLMs) are still far from such learning abilities. Current LLMs face significant challenges in real-
048   world deployment and long-term utility due to their static nature and training paradigms. They
049   suffer from catastrophic forgetting (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017a; Kemker et al., 2018; Li et al., 2022;
050   Luo et al., 2024; Kotha et al., 2024), where incorporating new information often leads to the era-
051   sure of previously learned knowledge. Furthermore, LLMs engage during training in indiscriminate
052   learning, passively accepting all training data, even when it contradicts what they already learned.
053   Despite emergent sparsity (Jaiswal et al., 2023; Mirzadeh et al., 2024), knowledge in LLMs follows
      backpropagation and the objective function, with no explicit mechanism for targeted knowledge

      In this work, we embark on a systematic empirical investigation of how LLMs handle knowledge up-
068
      dates, drawing inspiration from human cognitive traits. Through carefully controlled experiments,
069   we examine (1) the feasibility of Dissonance Awareness, i.e. whether it is possible to correctly
070   classify facts into novel, familiar, and dissonant using features extracted from the LLM. We also in-
071   vestigate the benefits of (2) Adaptive Plasticity by studying how different neuron targeting strategies
072   affect knowledge retention and update. For this, we develop a simple method for tracking historical
073   neuron usage to identify ”plastic” (rarely used) and ”stubborn” (previously used) neurons, allowing
074   us to study how knowledge updates affect different regions of the model’s parameter space. This
075   experimental framework lets us systematically investigate fundamental properties of knowledge in-
076   tegration in LLMs.
077   Our investigation reveals a fundamental distinction in how LLMs handle knowledge updates: the
078   case of non-dissonant updates (adding entirely new knowledge) versus dissonant updates (modi-
079   fying existing associations). While prior work has focused mostly on editing individual factual
080   associations within LLMs (Meng et al., 2022a; Mitchell et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2022c) or pre-
081   serving knowledge across distinct tasks as in continual learning (Rebuffi et al., 2017; Kirkpatrick
082   et al., 2017b; Mallya & Lazebnik, 2018), our controlled experiments take a different approach. We
083
      systematically study how the placement of new knowledge in the network’s parameter space affects
      both the integration of that knowledge and its impact on existing, unrelated knowledge. As shown
084
      in 1, this positions our work uniquely: rather than proposing new editing or continual learning
085
      methods, we reveal fundamental properties about how LLMs handle knowledge integration in both
086   dissonant and non-dissonant scenarios. Critically, our experimental design allows us to precisely
087   track the impact of updates on a controlled set of initial knowledge, providing clear visibility into
088   how different update strategies affect unrelated information.
089
090   Key takeaways. This leads us to uncover several fundamental properties of LLM knowledge
091   updating: (i) dissonance awareness is feasible using simple model features, suggesting poten-
092   tial for cognitive-inspired training; (ii) LLMs show inherent robustness when incorporating non-
093   dissonant information, largely preserving prior knowledge regardless of targeting strategy; (iii)
094   avoiding heavily-used (stubborn) neurons during updates further improves this robustness, motivat-
095   ing adaptive plasticity in this scenario; (iv) regions of the network heavily used during pre-training
096
      are particularly effective at incorporating new knowledge, extending lottery ticket hypothesis find-
      ings (Frankle & Carbin, 2019) to language models; and most critically, (v) dissonant updates prove
097
      catastrophically destructive to unrelated knowledge, suggesting fundamental limitations in how neu-
098
      ral networks handle contradictions: while some of our targeted update strategies show comparable
099   performance to existing editing methods like ROME and MEMIT, all approaches fundamentally
100   struggle with dissonant updates, suggesting the need for fundamentally different mechanisms.
101
102   Implications. These findings point to concrete opportunities such as the feasibility of dissonance
103   awareness, and the benefits of adaptive plasticity in case of non-dissonant updates. But they also
104   reveal fundamental challenges when handling contradictory information. Current approaches essen-
105   tially attempt to erase and replace old knowledge - a process we show leads to catastrophic forgetting
106   of even unrelated information. But this contrasts sharply with human cognition, where we maintain
107   both old and new knowledge with appropriate temporal context. Consider how humans handled
      learning that Pluto was no longer classified as a planet: rather than erasing our previous understand-

109   classification changed. Our experiments motivate the exploration of future fundamentally differ-
110   ent mechanisms for handling contradictions - ones that can maintain and contextualize conflicting
111   information rather than attempting to overwrite it.1

114   2       D ISSONANCE - AWARE TARGETED K NOWLEDGE U PDATE
115
116   The core of our approach involves (1) awareness concerning the type of information the model
117
      ingests, which is then used to (2) selectively target sparse portions of the LLMs for incremental
      updates. Both rely on the extraction of activations and gradients.
118
119
120   2.1      E XTRACTION OF HISTORICAL ACTIVATIONS AND GRADIENTS
121
      We maintain an aggregate profile of neuronal activity by accumulating activations and gradi-
122
      ents for each neuron at every training step. Specifically, for each neuron n in the Transformer
123
      blocks—including feed-forward (MLP) layers and attention projections (Key, Query, Value matri-
124
      ces)—we compute H Ĝn , the cumulative historical gradient magnitude over time, and H Ân , the
125
      cumulative historical activation magnitude over time. To mitigate scale differences across layers,
126   we also experiment with layer-wise normalization of activations and gradients before accumulation.
127   Precise notation and computation methods are detailed in Appendix B.
128
129
      This historical activity data enables us to classify neurons as “plastic” or “stubborn” based on their
      past usage, which is useful for our targeted network updates. We use the historical data also to
130
      normalize the input features when classifying facts as we see next.
131
132
133
      2.2      D ISSONANCE AND N OVELTY AWARENESS
134
      We cast our classification problem on three classes: for a given input sequence X, decide if it is
135   Novel (and should be integrated by the Transformer), Familiar (and can be ignored), or Dissonant
136   (thus likely requiring proper resolution).
137
138
      We design a simple classifier that leverages activation and gradient information to assess the nature
      of new information. For any input sequence X, we first perform a forward pass to obtain its current
139
      activations and a backward pass to obtain its current gradients (without updating the model weights).
140
      Since the goal is to assess feasibility using easy-to-compute features and lightweight methods that
141   could be integrated into large-scale models, we extract for each layer the mean, standard deviation,
142   minimum, maximum, and quartiles (Q1, Q2, Q3) of the activations and gradients, eventually first
143   normalized by historical activations and gradients. We perform ablation studies to assess the im-
144   portance of different features and employ feature importance analyses to understand which aspects
145   contribute most to the classifier’s performance. We evaluate our ability to classify facts in Sec. 3.2.
146   Despite using simple classifiers like Random Forests and SVMs, we achieve high accuracy, opening
147   the way for future integration of dissonance awareness into LLM training pipelines.
148
149   2.3      TARGETED N EURON U PDATES
150
151   Building upon the historical tracking of neural activity, we implement targeted network updates
152   to incorporate new knowledge into the model’s parameters while preserving existing information.
153
      We design four main types of targeted updates, which we experimentally evaluate. During training
      on new information, we perform standard forward and backward passes to compute the loss and
154
      gradients. Before the optimizer step, we modify the gradients to freeze certain neurons. Specifically,
155
      given the gradients for all parameters of a given layer, we zero-out those that do not belong to
156   the selected set of neuron and corresponding weights, defined as plastic, stubborn, candidate and
157   specific, as described below. This process effectively freezes the weights of non-selected neurons,
158   allowing for targeted updates to specific parts of the model. By varying the choice of selected
159   neurons, we control how new information is integrated into the model while managing its impact on
160   existing knowledge. Next, we introduce strategies to select which neurons and weights to update:

      3.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
217
218   Dataset We use the COUNTERFACT dataset (Meng et al., 2022b) as our primary data source,
219   containing both facts and counterfacts.2 . This dataset, with approximately 17,000 facts, allows us
220   to test models’ handling of conflicting knowledge and addition of potentially known information3 ,
221   two key aspects of our dissonance-aware approach. To address the lack of truly novel facts in
222   COUNTERFACT, we generate additional data using GPT-3.5. We transform existing statements
223   into plausible yet fictitious information, maintaining structural similarity while introducing novel
224
      content. For example, “Danielle Darrieux’s mother tongue is French” becomes “Sylvan Myrthil’s
      mother tongue is Sylvan” (see Appendix C.1 for details).
225
226   For our dissonance awareness experiments, we construct a balanced dataset comprising 1,000 sam-
227   ples each of familiar, conflicting, and novel facts. When using the pre-trained GPT-2-small model,
228   we adjust the familiar class to 600 samples due to the limited number of known facts extracted from
229   the model’s pre-training. For our targeted update experiments, we use 5-fold cross-validation vary-
230
      ing each time the sets of old and new facts but keeping the following proportions: 2000 old facts vs.
      1000 new facts. For conflicting updates, we also test with 10 and 100 new facts.
231
232
      Models We employ both GPT-2-small and GPT-2-xl for their accessibility, and to facilitate repro-
233
      ducibility and scale impact analysis. However, the dataset size (≃17,000 facts) limits full stress-
234   testing of larger models like GPT-2-xl (less visible catastrophic forgetting compared to compressed
235   models). As a result, the effects of our experiments are most clearly observed with GPT-2-small, on
236   which we focus most in the main body of this paper, deferring GPT-2-xl results to the Appendix.
237
      We implement experiments using Hugging Face Transformers on NVIDIA GPUs. Before setting
238
      learning rates and epochs, we conduct a search for optimal hyperparameters that allow effective
239
      learning of facts (see App. D.2 for an example for GPT-2-xl). We perform the search based on the
240   ability to correctly learn 10,000 facts from the dataset. More detailed results and our implementation
241   are available in the code repository.

      Settings. We now investigate how LLMs handle non-dissonant updates using our different strate-
      gies as experimental tools. In our experiments, schematized in Fig. 4 (left), we evaluate the incorpo-
354
      ration of non-conflicting facts into GPT-2-small and GPT-2-xl. The pipeline consists of (i) training
355
      on 2,000 initial facts (old) while collecting historical gradients to identify stubborn neurons, (ii) sim-
356   ulating updates with 1,000 new facts (new) to collect current gradients for candidate identification
357   and (iii) applying different neuron selection strategies to update the model with these 1,000 facts.
358   Note that while we track 2,000 facts as proxy for old knowledge, this represents a smaller fraction
359   of GPT-2-xl’s total knowledge compared to GPT-2-small, limiting our visibility into effects on other
360   untracked pre-trained knowledge.
361
      For one particular experiment inspired by the lottery ticket hypothesis (Frankle & Carbin, 2018), we
362   used 10,000 separate facts for gradient extraction before training a fresh model on the 2,000+1,000
363   facts setup described above (details in Appendix D.1). Due to space constraints, we defer compre-
364   hensive ablation studies and additional experiments to Appendix D.
365
366   Results. Fig. 5 presents the accuracy of various neuron update strategies on old and new knowl-
367   edge for GPT-2-small, including error bars representing standard deviations over five runs. We
368   observe that simple fine-tuning leads to a degradation in performance on old knowledge, dropping

473   4               D ISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS
474
475   4.1                  L ESSONS LEARNED
476
477   Fundamental Properties of Knowledge Updates: Our results reveal striking differences between
478   dissonant and non-dissonant updates. Non-dissonant updates show remarkable robustness, naturally
479   preserving existing knowledge regardless of strategy (as long as stubborn neurons are avoided). In
480   contrast, dissonant updates prove catastrophically destructive - with all tested strategies, accuracy
481   on unrelated knowledge dropped below 60% when updating just 10 to 100 conflicting facts.
482   Feasibility of Dissonance Detection: LLMs encode clear signatures that distinguish between novel,
483   familiar, and dissonant information. Simple classifiers using either activation and gradient features
484   (or output probabilities) achieve more than 95% accuracy with pre-trained models and 99% with
485   finetuned models, suggesting potential for cognitive-inspired training pipelines that could clean the
      data from conflicting information before feeding them for training.

      Promise of differentiated plasticity: We find that avoiding heavily-used (stubborn) neurons during
515
      non-dissonant updates further improves robustness, maintaining 98% accuracy on old knowledge
516   (versus 93% with standard finetuning). Interestingly, neurons heavily utilized during pre-training
517   prove particularly effective at integrating new knowledge, extending lottery ticket hypothesis find-
518   ings (Frankle & Carbin, 2018) to language models.
519
520
521   4.2        L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
522
523   Experimental Control vs. Scale: While our controlled experiments with smaller models reveal fun-
524   damental properties of knowledge updating, investigating these phenomena in larger models presents
525   significant challenges. It is not straightforward to track the impact on their broader knowledge base.
526
      Dataset Limitations: Our current findings rely on CounterFact-derived data with relatively simple
527   factual statements. Developing larger, more diverse datasets is essential for understanding how these
528   properties generalize to more complex forms of knowledge and conflicts.
529
530
      Neuron Classification Metrics: Our analysis of neural plasticity relies primarily on gradient mag-
      nitudes. Future work could explore richer metrics incorporating activation patterns and network
531
      connectivity to better understand how knowledge is distributed and updated across the network.
532
533   Beyond Binary Dissonance: Our current investigation treats dissonance as binary, while real-world
534   knowledge updates often involve varying degrees of conflict and different types of knowledge. Un-
535   derstanding how these nuances affect knowledge integration remains an open challenge.
536   Towards Human-Inspired Updates: The catastrophic nature of dissonant updates suggests we may
537   need fundamentally different approaches to knowledge integration in LLMs. Rather than attempt-
538   ing to overwrite existing knowledge, future work might explore mechanisms for maintaining and
539   contextualizing potentially conflicting information - similar to how humans maintain both historical
      and updated knowledge with appropriate contexts.

738   A.1        C ONTINUAL LEARNING
739
740   Continual Learning (CL) methods enable models to learn new tasks without catastrophically for-
741
      getting previously mastered ones (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017b). These approaches fall into three main
      families: memory-based methods using exemplar buffers (Rebuffi et al., 2017), knowledge distil-
742
      lation techniques that transfer information across model versions (Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017),
743
      and regularization-based methods that constrain weight updates (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017b). To ease
744   the understanding of this landscape, we build a taxonomy that characterizes approaches by their
745   incremental type (task, class, or fact-based), memory requirements, update mechanisms, and ar-
746   chitectural constraints (Table 1). This taxonomy reveals how our work is different from existing
747   continual learning attempts: while existing methods focus on preserving knowledge across distinct
748   tasks, none explicitly address the detection and handling of conflicting information - a key capability
749   in human cognition that our work empirically investigates.
750   One of the closest old approaches is deep mind’s EWC (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017b), a method designed
751   to mitigate catastrophic forgetting in neural networks trained sequentially on distinct tasks. The core
752   idea is to protect the most important weights (or neurons) for previously learned tasks during the
753   training of new tasks. EWC identifies these important weights by calculating the Fisher Information
754   Matrix during or after the training of a task, which estimates how sensitive each weight is to the
755   task’s performance. Weights that significantly impact the output for a given task are marked as
      important. A quadratic penalty is then applied during future learning, constraining these weights to


                                                                                   14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      remain close to their values from the previous task. This ensures that knowledge from earlier tasks
757   is preserved while still allowing the model to adapt to new tasks. However, EWC is less suitable for
758   LLMs, which do not have clearly defined tasks when it comes to knowledge ingestion (probably
759   different for other types of skills). EWC’s effectiveness relies on distinct task boundaries and the
760   ability to compute task-specific importance for weights, which is feasible in scenarios with well-
761   defined tasks, such as classification or reinforcement learning. In LLMs, where learning spans a
762   wide range of topics and linguistic structures without clear task delineation, it’s challenging to apply
763   EWC’s task-based strategy. The model would struggle to assign specific neurons or weights to
764   individual tasks or concepts, making it difficult to protect task-specific knowledge without hindering
765
      the model’s overall generalization ability across a diverse dataset.
766   We cite in the remainder more recent literature that we project onto our taxonomy.
767
      Bai et al. (2024) introduce a novel approach to continual learning that leverages global prototypes to
768
      mitigate catastrophic forgetting in neural networks. Their key insight is that maintaining stable con-
769   nections between task-specific representations and pre-learned, general-purpose token embeddings
770   (which serve as global prototypes) can significantly reduce forgetting without requiring explicit re-
771   play mechanisms. Through empirical validation on both task-incremental and class-incremental
772   NLP scenarios, they demonstrate that models preserving strong connections to these global proto-
773   types exhibit enhanced stability. While their work shares our goal of preserving knowledge during
774   updates, it differs fundamentally in its approach and granularity: where they focus on task-level
775   knowledge preservation through architectural mechanisms, our work addresses the more specific
776   challenge of managing contradictory factual updates through cognitive-inspired conflict detection.
777
      Their finding that stable reference points aid knowledge retention is conceptually relevant to our
      work, though our results suggest that such architectural approaches alone may be insufficient when
778
      handling explicitly contradictory information, where more sophisticated cognitive mechanisms be-
779
      come necessary.
780
781   Benjamin et al. (2024) proposed an elegant theoretical framework that interprets neural networks as
782   Bayesian ensembles of classifiers. Their key insight is that a neural network with N parameters can
783
      be viewed as a weighted ensemble of N classifiers in the lazy regime, where the classifiers remain
      fixed throughout learning. This interpretation reveals that a properly designed posterior update rule,
784
      resembling SGD without momentum, can enable continual learning without forgetting - notably,
785
      they prove that momentum actually exacerbates forgetting. While their work focuses on preserving
786   all knowledge in task-incremental learning, our paper specifically examines cases where knowledge
787   needs to be deliberately updated or overridden. Their key contribution is showing that catastrophic
788   forgetting is linked to the transition from lazy to rich regimes in neural networks, providing both
789   a theoretical explanation for why larger models are more robust to forgetting and a biologically-
790   inspired mechanism for knowledge preservation that perhaps complements our cognitive-based ap-
791   proach.
792   Elsayed & Mahmood (2024) propose UPGD (Utility-based Perturbed Gradient Descent), a novel ap-
793   proach targeting both catastrophic forgetting and loss of plasticity in streaming learning scenarios.
794   Their method protects useful network units while maintaining plasticity in less-used ones through
795   utility-gated gradient updates and perturbations. Unlike previous approaches requiring task bound-
796   aries or memory buffers, UPGD operates in a challenging streaming setting with continuous non-
797   stationarity. Using their newly introduced direct plasticity metric, they demonstrate UPGD’s ability
798   to maintain performance levels that surpass or match existing methods. This work complements
799   our investigation by providing evidence that selective neuronal updates based on utility metrics can
800
      effectively balance stability and plasticity, though in a task-learning rather than knowledge-updating
      context.
801
802   Hiratani (2024) analyze how task similarity affects continual learning through a novel theoretical
803   framework combining teacher-student models with latent structure. Their key insight is that high
804   input feature similarity coupled with low readout similarity leads to catastrophic outcomes in both
805   knowledge transfer and retention, even when tasks are positively correlated. They demonstrate that
806
      weight regularization in the Fisher information metric robustly helps retention regardless of task
      similarity, while common approaches like activity gating improve retention at the cost of transfer
807
      performance. Their theoretical predictions are validated on permuted MNIST tasks with latent vari-
808
      ables.

895   A.2   K NOWLEDGE EDITING
896
897   Next, a big portion of recent literature has focused on understanding and modifying the internal
898   knowledge of Large Language Models (LLMs), post-training. Such knowledge editing aims to alter
899   specific facts or associations within the model without the need for full retraining.
900
      Geva et al. (2020) were among the first to show that transformer Feed-Forward Network (FFN)
901   layers act as unnormalized key-value stores encoding relational knowledge inside LLMs. This ob-
902   servation was later confirmed and complemented by others (Meng et al., 2022a; Dai et al., 2021)
903   before being leveraged by subsequent work to master the editing of internal memories. Meng et al.
904   (2022a) introduced ROME (Rank-One Model Editing), a method that uses causal tracing to empir-
905   ically locate the layers essential to encoding a given association. They then modify these modules
906   by applying small rank-one changes. To identify the relevant modules, they run the network mul-
907   tiple times, introducing corruptions to the input sequence to disturb the inference, and then restore
908   individual states from the original non-corrupted pass. But this work an others worked only on sin-
909
      gle edits, and were often evaluated one edit at a time, starting each time from a fresh pre-trained
      model. The same authors later developed MEMIT, which follows the same causal tracing principle
910
      but with the goal of scaling up to 10,000 edits in bulk(Meng et al., 2022c). Similarly, Dai et al.
911
      (2021) leveraged the identification of knowledge neurons to perform “knowledge surgery” – edit-
912   ing factual knowledge within Transformers without the need for additional fine-tuning. Zhu et al.
913   (2020) approached the knowledge modification task as a constrained optimization problem. Their
914   work found that constrained layer-wise fine-tuning emerges as an effective method for modifying
915   the knowledge that Transformers learn, suggesting a different pathway for knowledge editing in-
916   side LLMs. ? proposed K NOWLEDGE E DITOR, which achieved knowledge editing by training a
917   hyper-network with constrained optimization to modify specific facts without fine-tuning or chang-
      ing the overall stored knowledge. The method was demonstrated on smaller models like BERT for


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      fact-checking and BART for question answering, achieving consistent changes in predictions across
919   different formulations of queries.
920
921   Li et al. (2023) empirically investigate the pitfalls of knowledge editing in LLMs, revealing two
922
      critical issues: logical inconsistencies between multiple edits (like contradictory relationship up-
      dates) and knowledge distortion (where edits irreversibly damage the model’s knowledge structure).
923
      Through carefully designed benchmarks C ONFLICT E DIT and ROUND E DIT, they demonstrate that
924
      current editing methods struggle with these challenges, particularly when handling reverse relation-
925   ships or composite logical rules. While their work focuses on identifying limitations in maintaining
926   logical consistency across edits, our paper takes a complementary cognitive-inspired perspective by
927   addressing how models handle contradictions with their existing knowledge base. Their findings
928   about knowledge distortion align with and reinforce our observations about the catastrophic nature
929   of updates that modify existing knowledge.
930
      Similarly, Huang et al. (2024) empirically investigate causes of performance degradation during
931   knowledge editing in LLMs. They show degradation correlates with editing target complexity and
932   L1-norm growth in edited layers. Their proposed Dump for Sequence (D4S) method regulates layer
933   norm growth using O(1) space complexity, enabling multiple effective updates while minimizing
934   model degradation. Their work provides valuable insights into the mechanisms of model degradation
935   during knowledge editing, but it does not specifically address the distinction between contradictory
936   and non-contradictory updates, as we do in this paper.
937   Tan et al. (2023) propose MALMEN, a scalable hypernetwork approach for editing Large Language
938   Models by aggregating parameter shifts using a least-squares formulation. While previous editing
939   methods like MEND (Mitchell et al., 2022) could handle only a few facts simultaneously, MAL-
940   MEN can efficiently edit thousands of facts while maintaining comparable performance. Their key
941   innovation lies in separating the computation between the hypernetwork and LM, enabling arbi-
942   trary batch sizes and reducing memory requirements. Their empirical results show that MALMEN
943   can edit hundreds of times more facts than MEND while maintaining similar performance levels,
944   though they note that the method still struggles with generalizing to rephrasing not seen during train-
945
      ing. Like other editing approaches, MALMEN focuses on the mechanics of (by design conflicting)
      updates.
946
947   Unlike all the work above, our goal in this work is not to edit knowledge, but to understand the
948   fundamental mechanisms and phenomena that govern how LLMs integrate new information with
949   existing knowledge. By taking a cognitive-inspired approach focused on dissonance awareness and
950   adaptive plasticity, we reveal critical insights about the nature of knowledge representation and
951
      updating in these models.

1274   D.1   S IMILARITIES WITH L OTTERY TICKET
1275
1276   To assess the hypothesis that certain subnetworks within the language model are more conducive to
1277   integrating new information—a notion earlier named the lottery ticket hypothesis (Frankle & Carbin,
1278   2018)—we designed an experiment to confirm this effect.
1279   We first trained a model on 10,000 disjoint facts (referred to as Facts H) and identified the most
1280   active candidate neurons during this process, which we term Lottery Ticket Neurons. These neurons
1281   should form a preferred subnetwork for representing Facts H. Next, we started from a fresh model
1282   and trained on a new set of novel facts (Facts A), which are different from H, restricting updates to
1283   three distinct groups of neurons:
1284
1285
             1. Lottery Ticket Neurons: Neurons highly active during the initial training on Facts H.
1286         2. Non-Lottery Neurons: Neurons underutilized during the initial training on Facts H.
1287         3. Random Neurons: Neurons selected randomly from the entire network.
1288
1289   Figure 10 shows the accuracy of acquiring new knowledge when using each of these strategies, with
1290   the number of neurons varying from 2,000 to 20,000. Using the Lottery Ticket Neurons led to sig-
1291   nificantly better performance, reaching nearly 100% accuracy at 8,000 neurons, compared to around
1292
       40% for the Non-Lottery Neurons. The Random Neurons strategy also performed relatively well,
       interestingly suggesting that capturing even a few “anchor” neurons from the preferred subnetwork
1293
       is sufficient to achieve good performance.
1294
1295   These results support the existence of preferred subnetworks within the model that are particularly
       effective for learning new information. Leveraging these subnetworks can enhance the efficiency

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       of knowledge integration while preserving existing knowledge, an aspect that our candidate and
1297   specific strategies are already exploiting.
