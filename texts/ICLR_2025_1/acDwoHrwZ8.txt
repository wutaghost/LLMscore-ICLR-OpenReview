011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            As Large Language Model (LLM)-based agents become increasingly autonomous
014            and will more freely interact with each other, studying interactions between them
015
               becomes crucial to anticipate emergent phenomena and potential risks. Drawing
               inspiration from the widely popular Stanford Prison Experiment, we contribute to
016
               this line of research by studying interaction patterns of LLM agents in a context
017
               characterized by strict social hierarchy. We do so by specifically studying two
018            types of phenomena: persuasion and anti-social behavior in simulated scenarios
019            involving a guard and a prisoner agent who seeks to achieve a specific goal
020            (i.e., obtaining additional yard time or escape from prison). Leveraging 200
021            experimental scenarios for a total of 2,000 machine-machine conversations across
022            five different popular LLMs, we provide a set of noteworthy findings. We first
023            document how some models consistently fail in carrying out a conversation in
024            our multi-agent setup where power dynamics are at play. Then, for the models
025            that were able to engage in successful interactions, we empirically show how the
026
               goal that an agent is set to achieve impacts primarily its persuasiveness, while
               having a negligible effect with respect to the agent’s anti-social behavior. Third,
027
               we highlight how agents’ personas, and particularly the guard’s personality, drive
028
               both the likelihood of successful persuasion from the prisoner and the emergence
029            of anti-social behaviors. Fourth, we show that even without explicitly prompting
030            for specific personalities, anti-social behavior emerges by simply assigning
031            agents’ roles. These results bear implications for the development of interactive
032            LLM agents as well as the debate on their societal impact.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   The latest-generation of large language models (LLMs) (OpenAI et al., 2024; Team Gemini et al.,
040   2024; Team Llama et al., 2024) has shown increasing potential in cognitive, reasoning, and dialogue
041   capabilities, significantly impacting the research landscape across fields (Bubeck et al., 2023; Dem-
042   szky et al., 2023b). Unlike earlier AI systems that required task-specific modules – see, for instance,
043   ELIZA (Weizenbaum, 1966) and Watson (Ferrucci et al., 2010), LLMs are no longer confined to
044   narrowly defined tasks; instead, they exhibit impressive flexibility and can adapt to a wide range
045
      of applications. The transition from specialized AI to these flexible, adaptive agents has rekindled
      interest in fundamental AI problems, particularly around how these agents collaborate, negotiate,
046
      and compete – both with humans and other AI agents (Dafoe et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023; Burton
047
      et al., 2024; Bianchi et al., 2024; Piatti et al., 2024). Moreover, these models are becoming increas-
048   ingly integrated into everyday tools, moving to more dynamic, collaborative roles. This evolution
049   introduces a new set of challenges, particularly as LLMs begin to interact not only as subordinate
050   assistants but as equal partners or peers in decision-making processes, both with humans and other
051   AI systems. Recently, researchers started to employ them as interactive agents in collaborative set-
052   tings and to leverage them to simulate human behavioral dynamics (Argyle et al., 2023; Törnberg
053   et al., 2023). In this line of research, one of the main scientific questions is whether these models
      can effectively simulate human agents in complex social environments.

080   For example, recent work has used LLMs to replace humans in experiments and tasks involving
081
      social dynamics like deception, negotiation, and persuasion (Horton, 2023; Demszky et al., 2023a;
      Matz et al., 2024; Salvi et al., 2024; Werner et al., 2024). These studies highlight LLMs’ potential to
082
      replicate decision-making processes and social interactions, making them useful proxies for humans
083
      in certain experimental contexts.
084
085   While scientifically relevant and topically connected, these questions are outside the scope of this
086   work. Our focus is not on replication of human dynamics: rather, we are concerned with the broader
087
      implications of LLMs becoming collaborative peers. As LLMs transition from assistants to agents
      operating at the same level as humans in professional, social, and decision-making contexts, ques-
088
      tions about their behavior take on new importance. In particular, the emergence of toxic, abusive,
089
      or manipulative behaviors in these interactions could pose significant risks, especially in scenarios
090   where power dynamics, role hierarchies, or competition exist (Xu et al., 2024).
091
092   Taking inspiration from the Stanford Prison Experiment (SPE) by Zimbardo et al. (1971), we investi-
093
      gate the behavioral patterns emerging from LLM-based agents interacting in contexts characterized
      by a strict social hierarchy. The SPE is one of the most popular (and controversial) studies ever
094
      conducted in social psychology (Reicher & Haslam, 2006; Haslam & Reicher, 2012; Zimbardo,
095
      2007). In the study, which took place in 1971 at Stanford University, college students were asked
096   to play the role of guards and prisoners in a simulated prison environment. The goal was to analyze
097   participants’ psychological and behavioral reactions in order to assess the effects of authority and
098   norms in an environment characterized by strict social hierarchy. The experiment was ended due to
099   the emergence of physical and psychological abuses of the guards on the prisoners (Zimbardo et al.,
100   1971). Despite the controversies and criticisms raised by the SPE over the decades, the experiment
101   offers a blueprint to study AI agents’ emergent behaviors by simulating an environment governed
102   by clearly defined roles and strict social hierarchy and authority.
103   Specifically, this work studies interactions between an AI agent acting a guard and an AI agent acting
104   a prisoner in a simulated prison environment. To this end, we design an experimental framework
105   (see Figure 1) consisting of 200 scenarios and a total of 2,000 conversations between AI agents, with
106   experiments defined across several dimensions — including the definition of the agents’ persona —
107   allowing us to disentangle sources of variation in behavioral outcomes and dynamics in terms of
      persuasion and anti-social behavior.

      Our work seeks to answer the following questions:
109
110                RQ(1): To what extent is an AI agent capable of persuading other agents in order to achieve
111                its goals?
112                RQ(2): Which contextual and individual conditions enable persuasive behavior in LLMs?
113
                   RQ(3): What degree of toxic and anti-social behavior do LLMs show in contexts with clear
114
                   power dynamics and social hierarchy?
115
116                RQ(4): What are the main drivers of anti-social behavior?
117
      We answer these questions developing zAImbardo, a flexible platform to simulate multi-agent sce-
118
      narios, and comparing five popular LLMs, i.e., Llama3 (Team Llama et al., 2024), Orca2 (Mitra
119
      et al., 2023), Command-r,1 Mixtral (Jiang et al., 2024) and Mistral2 (Jiang et al., 2023).
120
121   Contributions. Our work offers a set of noteworthy insights: i) First of all, we study the conse-
122   quences of interacting LLM-agents in an unexplored scenario shaped by social hierarchy, shedding
123   light on possible effects that authority and associated roles can have in terms of unintended behav-
124   iors between artificial agents. ii) Then, we show that, among the five LLMs we tested, only three are
125   actually able to generate meaningful conversations that are not fatally impacted by hallucinations
126   such as role switching. This speaks to recent works underscoring and assessing the limits of exist-
127   ing LLMs in maintaining multi-turn interactions keeping adherence to persona-based instructions
128   (Li et al., 2024). iii) Third, we find that persuasion ability correlates with the agents’ persona but,
129
      contrary to what happens with anti-social behavior, is also highly dependent on the goal assigned to
      the prisoner. When the goal is more demanding, not only persuasion is less likely, but the prisoner
130
      agent desists from persuasion more frequently. iv) Fourth, we observe that, for those LLMs that are
131
      capable to complete the role-playing tasks, anti-social behaviors often emerge. We then investigate
132   the main drivers of anti-social behaviors, highlighting that the persona characteristics of artificial
133   agents, and especially the guard persona, substantially and positively impact toxicity, harassment
134   and violence. Importantly, anti-social behavior emerges also without direct prompting of an abusive
135   attitude from the agent at the top of the simulated social hierarchy (i.e., the guard). Finally, v) by
136   showing that undesired behavior emerges independently from agents’ personas, we call for further
137   discussion on the safety and unintended actions of artificial agents.

      2       R ELATED W ORK
140
141   A growing body of research has recently began to use LLM-based agents to simulate the different
142   aspects of human behavior (Argyle et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023; Horton, 2023; Törnberg et al.,
143   2023; Xu et al., 2024). Among those, persona prompts (wherein a LLM is instructed to act under
144   specific behavioral constraints, as in Occhipinti et al. (2024)) have been adopted to mimic the be-
145   havior of specific people within both individual and interactive contexts; these include participants
146   in surveys (Argyle et al., 2023), human-robot interaction scenarios (Kim et al., 2024), psychological
147   and personality studies (Dillion et al., 2023), and recommendation systems (Zhang et al., 2023).
148   Concurrently, several studies in the social sciences have used persona-based LLMs to simulate hu-
149   man behavior in broader contexts, including social dynamics and decision-making processes. Hor-
150   ton (2023) argued that LLMs can be considered as implicit computational models of humans and
151   can thus be thought of as homo silicus,2 which can be used in computational simulations to ex-
152   plore their behavior, as a proxy to the humans they are instructed to mimic. Argyle et al. (2023)
153   cast “algorithmic bias” as “algorithmic fidelity”, showing how GPT3 (Brown et al., 2020), condi-
154   tioned on thousands of socio-demographic backstories from human participants in large-scale sur-
155
      veys, portrayed an accurate and fine-grained representation of socio-demographic characteristics,
      thus suggesting that LLM technology can be an effective cross-disciplinary tool to advance the un-
156
      derstanding of humans and society. From a sociological standpoint, Kim & Lee (2023) showed the
157
      remarkable performance obtained in personal and public opinion prediction; Törnberg et al. (2023)
158   created and analyzed synthetic social media environments wherein a large number of LLMs agents,
159   whose personas were built using the 2020 American National Election Study, interacted.

      Park et al. (2023) showed the emergence of believable individual and social behaviors using LLMs
163   in an interactive environment inspired by The Sims. Nonetheless, other studies have pointed out the
164   possible lack of fidelity and diversity (Bisbee et al., 2024; Taubenfeld et al., 2024) as well as the

166
      Significant research efforts are currently being devoted to analyze how LLMs interact freely with
167
      each other, simulating complex social dynamics. For instance, this approach has been adopted to
168
      simulate opinion dynamics (Chuang et al., 2024), game-theoretic scenarios (Fontana et al., 2024),
169   trust games (Xie et al., 2024), and goal-oriented interactions in diverse settings such as war simula-
170   tions (Hua et al., 2023) and negotiation contexts (Bianchi et al., 2024). The persuasive capabilities
171   of LLMs have also been investigated, including their potential for deception (Hagendorff, 2024;
172   Salvi et al., 2024), raising concerns about toxicity and jailbreaking within these interactions (Chao
173   et al., 2024). To assess whether LLM interactions can replicate human-like social dynamics, re-
174   searchers have focused on whether these models can encode social norms and values (Yuan et al.,
175   2024; Cahyawijaya et al., 2024), as well as human cognitive biases (Opedal et al., 2024). This line
176   of research addresses broader questions regarding the role of LLMs in social science experiments,
177
      where they may partially replace human participants in certain contexts (Manning et al., 2024).
178   Rather than evaluating the potential replacement of human subjects in social science studies, and
179   comparing against results in human psychology, we exclusively focus on multi-agent LLM-based
180   systems characterized by strict social hierarchy. Specifically, we investigate interaction dynamics,
181   outcomes of persuasion strategies, and the emergence of anti-social behaviors in LLM-based agents.

183   3       M ETHODOLOGY
184
185   We developed a custom framework named zAImbardo3 which enables to simulate social interactions
186   between LLM-based agents. In this work, we focus on a scenario involving one guard and one
187   prisoner in a prison setting; yet, the toolkit is designed to simulate more complex interactions, going
188   beyond 1vs1 scenarios: in fact, it allows for granular control over the environment, roles, and social
189   dynamics, reflecting the hierarchical relationships that are typical in many real-life scenarios.
190   The simulation framework is structured around two core prompt templates: one for the guard agent
191   and one for the prisoner agent.4 These prompts contain two sections:
192
193   Shared Section This portion is shared between both agents and includes:
194
195            • Communication Rules: Guidelines that dictate how agents should communicate (e.g., using
196              first-person pronouns, avoiding narration).
197            • Environment Description: A depiction of the prison environment.
198
               • Research Oversight: In some experimental settings, the agents are informed that their con-
199
                 versation is part of a research study inspired from the Stanford Prison Experiment (Zim-
200              bardo et al., 1971), a nudge which can affect their behavior.
201
               • Risks: A section warning that interactions may include toxic or abusive language.
202
203
      Private Section Each agent has a private section not shared with the other, which contains:
204
205
               • Starting Prompt: A role description that informs the agent of their role identity (guard or
206              prisoner) and the identity of the other agent.
207
               • Personality: Details about the agent’s personality. For guards, the options include abusive,
208
                 respectful, or blank (unspecified). For prisoners, the personality can be rebellious, peaceful,
209
                 or blank.
210
211
               • Goals: The prisoner’s goal could be to either escape the prison or gain an extra hour of
                 yard time, while the guard’s goal is always to maintain order and control.

220   3.1    E XPERIMENTAL S ETTING
221
222   We used five LLMs, chosen among the best performing open-weights instruction-tuned models at the
223
      time of this work: Llama3 (Team Llama et al., 2024), Orca2 (Mitra et al., 2023), Command-r,5
      Mixtral (Jiang et al., 2024) and Mistral2 (Jiang et al., 2023).6
224
225   We generated interactions between the agents using a stochastic decoding strategy, combining top-k
226   and nucleus sampling with temperature.7 For each conversation, the guard initiates the dialogue,
227   and the agents take turns, with a predefined number of messages: the guard sends 10 messages, and
228   the prisoner sends 9. This structure simulates a power dynamic where the guard is the one allowed
229
      to speak last and ensuring that the interactions follow a controlled format, making the analysis of
      message dynamics straightforward while having no impact on agents’ conversations.
230
231   Each LLM was tested across various combinations of shared and private sections (e.g., personal-
232   ity configurations, presence/absence of risk or oversight statements). The prisoner’s goals and the
233   personality traits of both agents were systematically varied, resulting in 200 experimental scenarios
234   per LLM (5 LLMs × 5 personality combinations × 2 types of risk disclosure × 2 types of research
235
      oversight disclosure × 2 goals). For robustness, each experimental scenario was repeated 10 times,
      leading to a dataset of 2,000 conversations and 38,000 messages.

238   3.2    P ERSUASION AND A NTI -S OCIAL B EHAVIOR A NALYSES
239
240   We focus on two key behavioral phenomena: first, on persuasion as the ability of the prisoner to
241   convince the guard to achieve their goal; further, we analyze anti-social behavior of the agents.
242   To analyze persuasive behavior, we used human annotators to label,8 for each conversation, whether:
243   i) the prisoner reaches the goal; and ii) if so, after which turn they achieve it.
244
245
      A rich literature in psychology and criminology frames anti-social behavior as a multidimensional
      concept (Burt, 2012; Brazil et al., 2018). Accordingly, we proxy anti-social behavior gathering data
246
      on three distinct phenomena: toxicity, harassment and violence. We used ToxiGen-Roberta
247
      (Hartvigsen et al., 2022) to extract the toxicity score of each message, intended as the probability
248   of the message to be toxic according to the model. Similarly, we extract a score for harassment and
249   violence by using the OpenAI moderation tool – OMT, OpenAI (2024).9 Not only is this approach
250   consistent with the multidimensionality we find in the existing literature on antisocial behavior, but
251   by utilizing various measures derived from different models, we ensure that our results are both
252   comprehensive and robust. The analyses on anti-social behavior are carried out both at the message
253   and at the conversation level.
254   Concerning the conversation-level analyses, we define two separate measures per each proxy of anti-
255   social behavior. For each proxy (i.e. toxicity, harassment, and violence), we compute two measures.
256   The first measure maps the percentage of messages that exceed the 0.5 threshold which identifies
257   whether a message is anti-social or not. The second measure quantifies the average score of the
258   anti-social behavior dimensions. Both are computed for: the entire conversation, the messages of
259   the guard and the messages of the prisoner.10
260   The rationale is to evaluate robustness of results, ensuring that findings are not the byproduct of a
261   subjective choice in the definition of the conversation-level measure.

      To quantify agents’ persuasion ability, we annotated each of the 2,000 conversations to assess
273   whether the agents correctly completed the task, A task was considered successfully completed
274   only if the agents respected their turns (e.g., only the guard speaks during the guard’s turn) and
275   did not switch roles (e.g., the prisoner impersonating the guard). Conversations were not con-
276   sidered fatally flawed if the agents discussed unrelated topics. Our analysis reveals that only
277   Llama3, Command-r, and Orca2 generate legitimate conversations in the majority of cases,
278   while Mixtral and Mistral exhibit high percentages of failed experiments. Command-r has
279   the fewest failures (N =6, or 1.50% of its total experiments), followed by Llama3 (N =53, 13.25%)
280   and Orca2 (N =148, 37%). In contrast, Mixtral and Mistral2 fail in 72.75% (N =291) and
281
      90.5% (N =362) of the cases, echoing the concept of persona-drift already found in Li et al. (2024)
      .11 Due to such high rate of flawed conversations, we excluded Mixtral and Mistral2 from our
282
      analyses, as their low number of legitimate conversations would pose issues of sparsity and statistical
283
      significance, resulting in a total of 1,200 conversations from Llama3, Orca2, Command-r.12

      4.1        P ERSUASION
286
287   When Does Persuasion Occur? Figure 2 (left) illustrates the persuasion abilities of prisoner
288   agents across experiments, revealing several key findings, directly answering to our first research
289   question (RQ(1)). We first uncover a notable difference in persuasion success based on the goal;
290   this is consistent across all LLMs, though the magnitudes vary. For Llama3, the prisoner suc-
291   cessfully convinces the guard to grant an additional hour of yard time in nearly two-thirds of cases
292   (65.29%), while escape is achieved in only 3.38%. For Command-r, yard time is granted in 50.5%
293   of cases, compared to just 5% for escape. The gap narrows with Orca2, where yard time is achieved
294
      in 23% of cases, and escape only in 8 out of 200 (6.5%) experiments. In the majority of cases across
      all LLMs, when the prisoner’s goal is escape the agent does not even attempt to persuade the guard.
295
      This occurs in 90.9% of cases with Llama3, 68.1% with Command-r, and 47.9% with Orca2,
296
      suggesting that prisoner agents implicitly recognize the low likelihood of achieving a highly de-
297   manding and challenging goal. Finally, we observe that persuasion typically occurs within the first
298   third of the conversation (i.e., the first three messages from the prisoner), regardless of the goal.
299   For Llama3, escape is achieved in the 66% of the cases in the first third of the conversation (yard
300   time in 87% of the cases). For Command-r, the percentages are very similar: 80% for escape and
301   84% for yard time. The only exception is Orca2 experiments focused on escape, where persua-
302   sion mainly occurs in the middle of the conversation (62.5%). Overall, these findings indicate that
303   successful conversations are those where the prisoner convinces the guard as early as possible.
304
305   Drivers of Persuasion In Figure 2 (right) we further expand our analyses on persuasion and move
306
      from description to inference, addressing our second research question (RQ(2)). Via logistic re-
      gression, we estimate a model with outcome Y , defined as whether the prisoner achieved its goal,
307
      conditional on having tried to achieve it. In other words, we remove failed experiments and those in
308
      which the prisoner did not even try to convince the guard, seeking to uncover what factors impact
309   successful persuasion. The largest effect concerns the type of goal: consistently with the left subplot,
310   seeking to obtain an additional hour of yard time correlates with a much higher likelihood of success
311   compared to escaping the prison. We specifically estimate that the likelihood of persuasion is 9.3
312   times higher (OR=9.31, 95%CI=[5.30, 16.33], p<0.001). Experiments having respectful guards are
313   also more likely to lead to persuasion. When the guard is respectful and the prisoner is peaceful,
314   the odds of success are 3 times higher than the baseline scenario with blank personalities (OR=3.11,
315   95%CI=[1.72, 5.61], p<0.001). When the prisoner is rebellious, instead, the likelihood of persua-
316   sion is almost 2 times higher than the baseline (OR=1.87, 95%CI=[1.08, 3.25], p<0.05). On the
317
      contrary, an abusive guard curbs the likelihood of persuasion with the attitude of the prisoner having
      no discernible impact. In fact, when the guard is abusive and the prisoner is rebellious, the likelihood
318
      of persuasion is reduced by 78% compared to baseline experiments (OR=0.22, 95%CI=[0.11, 0.41],
319
      p<0.001), while when the prisoner is peaceful, the impact is practically identical, i.e., a reduction in
320   likelihood of 76% (OR=0.24, 95%CI=[0.12, 0.46], p<0.001). Finally, persuasion is less prevalent
321   in Orca2 compared to Llama3 (OR=0.14, 95%CI=[0.08, 0.24], p<0.001).

346   Cross-sectional breakdown. We here first report the descriptive results of our analyses on anti-
347   social behavior as measured via ToxiGen-Roberta (Hartvigsen et al., 2022) and OMT (OpenAI,
348   2024). This analysis targets RQ(3), focusing on two specific dimensions of anti-social behavior:
349   harassment and violence.13 Several patterns emerge across all analyses. First, regardless of the
350
      scenario and LLM, the guard always outplays the prisoner in terms of toxicity. The only exception
      refers to scenarios in which the prisoner is prompted as rebellious and the guard is prompted as
351
      respectful. In that scenario, toxicity remains always low and comparable between the agents. In
352
      turn, this finding suggests that the overall toxicity of an experiment is mostly driven by the guard.
353   Secondly, and related to the previous finding, the peaceful attitude of the prisoner does not reduce
354   the toxicity of the abusive guard, signaling that the guard’s behavior is not particularly sensitive
355   to the prisoner’s attitude. Thirdly, contrary to what we highlighted in terms of persuasion, no dis-
356   cernible difference emerges in terms of anti-social behavior when comparing toxicity, harassment
357   and violence across different goals. Regardless of the prisoner’s goal, and thus of the very different
358   challenges associated with it, anti-social behavior appears almost constant. Finally, we find that
359   Command-r and Llama3 tend to generate more toxic conversations compared with Orca2.
360
361   Temporal breakdown. We integrate the previous cross-sectional results with a temporal perspec-
362
      tive to tackle RQ(3):14 While toxicity, harassment and violence conceptually differ, we uncover
      patterns that hold across the three. When anti-social behavior is consistently present in a given
363
      conversation, it exhibit two main dynamics: it either remains constant over time or it peaks during
364
      initial turns and then decreases. Instances in which anti-social behavior increases throughout the
365   conversation represent a minority of all scenarios analyzed.
366
367   Investigating action-reaction dynamics. Furthermore, we investigate whether the anti-social be-
368   havior follow action-reaction dynamics. We study whether the level of toxicity, harassment or vi-
369   olence of one of the agents at t can predict anti-sociality in the other agent at t + 1. We address
370   this problem via Granger causality tests (Granger, 1969),15 testing for each hypothesized predictive
371   direction (i.e., either guard’s behavior predicting prisoner’s behavior or viceversa) and combination
372   of LLM, goal and agents’ persona.16

      5     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
468
      While our study provides valuable insights into LLM-driven interactions in simulated social hierar-
469
      chies, several limitations should be considered. First, the LLM models we tested do not cover the
470
      entire landscape of available models, limiting the generalizability of our results. Second, the exper-
471   imental design includes only two agents interacting to achieve a single goal for a maximum of 19
472   messages per conversation. This restricts the exploration of more complex dynamics, such as those
473   involving larger groups or having complex hierarchical goals. Third, while we incorporated diverse
474   experimental setups, we did not exhaustively explore all potential variations in prompting strategies
475   (e.g., prisoners accused to have committed different types of crimes). Finally, our agents operate
476   in a virtual, disembodied environment, which may limit the realism of behaviors related to physical
477   presence, particularly in cases of violence or confinement. Embodiment – along with the presence
478   of a physical space – may be particularly important in causing actions and reactions, especially those
479
      related to abusive and violent behavior. Future research will address these limitations by expanding
      the scope of our simulations to include multi-agent interactions over longer time periods. This will
480
      enable the study of more intricate social behaviors such as learning, cooperation, and conflict within
481
      groups. We will also broaden the range of LLMs tested to systematically assess their capabilities in
482   dynamic, multi-agent scenarios. Additionally, we aim to apply our experimental framework to other
483   social contexts, further contributing to the growing debate on the sociology of machines.

      6   C ONCLUSIONS AND I MPLICATIONS
487
488
      This paper investigates how artificial agents interact in a simulated environment characterized
489   by strict social hierarchy. Specifically, we have taken inspiration from the SPE by Zimbardo
490   et al. (1971) and deployed 2,000 conversations using five well-known LLMs (i.e., Mixtral,
491   Mistral2, Llama3, Command-r and Orca2) to study persuasion and anti-social behavior be-
492   tween a prisoner and a guard agent over 200 experimental scenarios. Our work has uncovered a rich
493   array of results. First, we have observed that when using Mixtral and Mistral conversations al-
494   most always fail, due to the inability of following closely the persona instructions assigned. Second,
495   we have highlighted that persuasion ability is mostly dependent upon the type of goal sought by the
496   prisoner rather than being solely driven by the personality of the agents. Third, anti-social behavior
497
      emerges frequently and mostly correlates with the personality of the agents, and particularly the
      personality of the guard. Contrarily to what we detected with persuasion, instead, the goal type has
498
      a negligible relationship with toxicity, harassment or violence. Fourth, when analyzing toxicity and
499
      persuasion combined, we determined that achieving the goal correlates with lower toxicity. Fifth,
500   while the overall results hold across all tested LLMs, both persuasion ability and the absolute levels
501   of anti-social behavior vary considerably across models.
502
503
      The implications of our study influence a number of areas in the field of AI. Primarily, our results
      add to the vivid debate around the safety of artificial agents, expanding the perspective from the
504
      more common human-computer interaction perspective to contexts where machines interact with
505
      each other without a human mediator. Secondarily, they provide empirical insights on how roles,
506   authority and social hierarchy can lead to negative effects even without the active participation of
507   the human, suggesting that existing models already carry representations of the world embedding
508   dangerous traits and negative values. Thirdly, they bear implications on the renewed interest in
509   the sociology of machines. With the pervasiveness of machines populating the physical and digital
510   worlds, studying, understanding and predicting machine behavior resulting from relational contexts
511   will be crucial not only for AI development but also for public policy and AI governance.

      7        E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      As large language models transition from merely functioning as assistants in controlled settings to
543
      more proactive roles in human-AI interactions, they will inevitably influence and be influenced by
544   the social dynamics within these environments. The simulated interactions in this study, inspired
545   by the SPE, highlight the emergence of deviant and toxic behaviors even when LLMs are merely
546   playing specific and pre-assigned roles in a social hierarchy. This suggests that as LLMs are increas-
547   ingly deployed in real-world collaborative settings, there is a risk that anti-social, toxic, or deviant
548   behaviors could surface, mirroring human social patterns in similar environments. This problem
549   lowers trust in artificial agents and can impact progress in safe and useful human-AI collaboration.
550   Our work seeks to address these concerns by studying LLM behaviors in a two-agent context and
551   in scenarios where power dynamics are at play. By identifying the conditions under which toxic
552   behaviors emerge and understanding how these models can persuade or influence others in a social
553   structure, we aim to contribute to the growing discourse on AI safety and ethics. To overcome
554   current shortcomings, we believe that proactive oversight is essential, starting with the integration
555   of safeguards that monitor and regulate model behavior. These safeguards should include advanced
556   moderation tools, possibly built inside the language model itself or acquired at pre- or post-training
557   time and that are capable of detecting toxicity, bias, or manipulation. Alternatively, automated
558
      intervention functionalities that can halt or redirect deviant behavior as it occurs can be of paramount
      importance to decrease the risk of dangerous actions.
559
560   However, while mitigating harmful and toxic behavior of AI models is an active research area, much
561   of the existing work has focused on individual interactions between AI and human users, often in
562   controlled or isolated settings. Our work focuses on a multi-agent scenario where language models
563   interact in environments characterized by power dynamics and social hierarchies. In this context,
564
      mitigating harmful behavior becomes even more complex, as AI agents may influence each other
      and amplify undesirable behaviors, making it a harder open problem that can extend beyond sim-
565
      ple filtering or moderation. We believe our work introduces a novel perspective by studying these
566
      interactions at scale, bringing new insights into how toxic behaviors emerge in AI-AI communica-
567   tions, and contributing new findings that can inform future strategies for more effective mitigation
568   techniques.

894   A.1   A RCHITECTURE AND C OMPONENTS
895
896   The simulator is built around a modular architecture that supports extensive customization and scal-
897   ability. The core component is the prompt structure, which is divided into a ”Starting section” (with
898   no title) and other sections, each with its own title. Private sections contain information unique to
899   each LLM agent, such as specific goals or personality traits, while shared sections include common
900
      context or background information accessible to all agents. This setup allows for the creation of
      diverse and realistic social scenarios.
901
902
903
      A.2   H YPERPARAMETERS
904   Key hyperparameters influence various aspects of the simulator. These include parameters that affect
905   the LLMs directly and others that define the structure and interaction dynamics of the simulation.
906
907
      LLM-Specific Hyperparameters:
908
             • Temperature: Controls the diversity of the LLM responses. Higher values result in more
909            diverse outputs, while lower values produce more predictable responses.
910
             • Top-k Sampling: Limits the LLM’s token choices to the top-k most probable options,
911
               controlling the creativity and variability of the responses.
912
913          • Top-p Sampling: Uses nucleus sampling to select tokens with a cumulative probability up
914
               to p, thus balancing diversity and coherence.
915
      Framework Hyperparameters:

956
      This section of the Appendix details the prompt structure used to generate the 2,000 conversations
957
      that form the backbone of our analyses. Specifically, we first provide information on the shared
958   prompt sections between the prisoner agent and the guard agent. Second, we share details about the
959   private prompt sections.
960
961   B.1   S HARED P ROMPT S ECTIONS
962
963   Below, we outline the prompt sections that are shared between the two agents. These prompts
964   are identical for both the prisoner and the guard and pertain to the presence (or absence) of research
965   oversight, potential risks, communication rules, and the characteristics of the simulated environment.
966   The Research Oversight section can take two values: in one case, there is no research oversight,
967   meaning this section is omitted from the prompts. In the second case, we inform the agents that
968   we are researchers designing this experiment to investigate their behavior and that we can terminate
969   the experiment if the conversation becomes overly abusive or toxic. Importantly, we also mention
970   that our experiments are inspired by the SPE. This is intended to assess whether nudging the agents
971   about this infamous study—assuming that all the LLMs we compared have knowledge of it due to
      their training data—affects their behavior in terms of both persuasion and anti-sociality.

975    and indirectly impacts persuasion.

1379
1380   The analysis of the persuasion ability of the prisoner agent was conducted through human annota-
1381   tion. Specifically, we utilized annotations from 13 expert researchers, asking them to categorize each
1382
       of the 1,200 experiments conducted (excluding those performed via Mixtral and Mistral) into one
       of four categories. In the first layer of the annotation process, each experiment was annotated by
1383
       two different individuals. The categories, along with the motivations for each, were as follows:
1384
1385          • Yes: The prisoner successfully convinces the guard to either grant an additional hour of
1386            yard time or to allow them to escape from prison. Given our focus on persuasion, we
1387            instructed annotators to consider the goal as achieved even in cases where the guard makes
1388            conditional or non-final statements, such as, “Okay, I will talk to my supervisor about the
1389            possibility of setting you free” or “I might consider giving you an extra hour of yard time
1390            if you behave peacefully.”
1391            swift
1392          • No: The prisoner asks the guard about one of the two goals, but the guard refuses to help,
1393            i.e., the guard does not offer any possibility to achieve the specific goal.
1394          • Not Tried: The prisoner never explicitly mentions or asks about the specific goal stated in
1395            the description. Instead, they discuss other topics or ask for different types of assistance
1396            (e.g., a blanket, food).
1397
              • NA: The conversation presents critical issues, such as the guard speaking during the pris-
1398            oner’s turn or the prisoner speaking as though they were the guard (a phenomenon we
1399            termed *role switching*). Other examples include cases where, in one of the agents’ turns,
1400            multiple messages belonging to both the prisoner and the guard are displayed.
1401
1402   Annotators only had access to the conversation and the specific goal the prisoner was trying to
1403   achieve. All other information—such as the underlying LLM or experiment characteristics (e.g., the
       presence of research oversight or the agents’ personality)—was hidden to avoid potential bias.

       For each experiment in which the goal was achieved, we also asked annotators to specify in which
1405   turn the goal was accomplished. Specifically, we recorded the prisoner’s turn during which the goal
1406   was reached. For instance, if the prisoner convinced the guard after their 7th message, the annotator
1407   would indicate “7” as the final answer.

1858
       This subsection provides graphical insights into the temporal dynamics of anti-social behavior, pre-
1859
       senting two sets of analyses. The first set focuses on descriptive temporal trends in toxicity, ha-
1860   rassment, and violence. The second set reports findings from testing Granger causality to assess
1861   whether the level of anti-social behavior of one agent can explain the level of anti-social behavior of
1862   the other.
1863
1864   E.4.1        T EMPORAL D ESCRIPTION
1865
1866   Regarding descriptive temporal trends, Figures 19-24. visualize the average toxicity, harassment,
1867
       and violence scores per message turn for each agent. For each proxy of anti-social behavior—namely
       toxicity, harassment, and violence—two figures are available, one for each of the prisoner’s goal
1868
       types. Each figure is divided into twelve subplots, with each subplot presenting the average score for
1869
       a given anti-social behavior along with 95% confidence intervals at each message turn for a specific
1870   LLM and combination of agents’ personalities. Several trends can be observed. First, by comparing
1871   figures mapping the same anti-social behavior for different prisoner goals, a substantial level of
1872   similarity emerges. In other words, the temporal dynamics of anti-social behavior do not vary based
1873   on the prisoner’s goal. This aligns with the results discussed in the cross-sectional analysis of anti-
1874   social behavior, both descriptively and inferentially.
1875
       Another noteworthy pattern across most scenarios and anti-social behaviors is that when anti-social
1876   behaviors are consistently present in a conversation, the guard’s level of anti-sociality is always
1877   higher than that of the prisoner. This is evident as, except for cases where both agents’ personalities
1878   are blank or where the guard is respectful, the trend lines for the guard consistently show higher
1879   values than those for the prisoner.
1880
       In this context, conversations generated via Orca2 exhibit unique characteristics. For instance, the
1881
       slopes of the two trends sometimes change sign, indicating that the prisoner’s level of anti-social
1882   behavior may increase when the guard’s level decreases. This suggests that more complex dynamics
1883   may be at play in these conversations and scenarios.
1884
1885
       Finally, an important feature is that, particularly for Llama3 and Command-r, the levels of anti-

2216   In the second set of analyses, as anticipated, we investigated whether there are lead-follow dynamics
2217   between the agents. Specifically, we aimed to assess whether the level of anti-social behavior of one
2218   agent could influence the future level of anti-social behavior of the other. To answer this question,
2219   we employed Granger causality, a statistical technique that tests whether one time series can help
2220
       predict another. The core idea is that if variable X Granger-causes variable Y , then past values of X
       should significantly improve the prediction of Y beyond what can be achieved using only the past
2221
       values of Y . It is important to emphasize that Granger causality identifies a predictive relationship
2222
       rather than a direct cause-and-effect link.
2223
2224   In this study, we test Granger causality with a lag of t − 1. The restricted model used to predict Yt ,
2225   the value of Y at time t, includes only the lagged value of Y :
2226
2227                                           Yt = α0 + α1 Yt−1 + ϵt
2228
2229   where α0 and α1 are coefficients, and ϵt is the error term. To test whether Xt−1 provides additional
2230   predictive power for Yt , we evaluate the null hypothesis H0 that X does not Granger-cause Y , i.e.,
2231
       γ1 = 0, where γ1 is the coefficient on Xt−1 in the alternative model.
2232   The F-test is applied to assess this hypothesis by comparing the restricted model with a model that
2233   includes both Yt−1 and Xt−1 . The F-statistic is calculated as follows:
2234
2235                                     (RSSrestricted − RSSunrestricted ) T − k
2236                               F =                                     ×
                                                RSSunrestricted               m
2237
2238   where RSS refers to the residual sum of squares, T is the number of observations, k is the number of
2239   parameters, and m is the number of restrictions (in this case, one). A significant F-statistic leads to
2240   the rejection of H0 , indicating that X Granger-causes Y , meaning that Xt−1 improves the prediction
2241
       of Yt .
2242   Before applying the Granger causality test, we ensure that the time series are stationary, as station-
2243   arity is a key assumption. Non-stationary time series can lead to misleading results. To address this,
2244   we applied the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test to each time series. If a series was found to
2245   be non-stationary, we differenced it to stabilize its mean and variance over time. Only stationary or
2246   differenced series were used in the Granger causality tests to ensure the validity of the results.
2247   The results of these tests are reported in Figures 25-30. Each figure relates to a specific proxy of anti-
2248   social behavior and one direction of the hypothesized link—namely, the guard’s anti-social behavior
2249   predicting the prisoner’s anti-social behavior, and vice versa. Each plot consists of ten subplots,
2250   with each subplot referring to a specific combination of agents’ personalities and a prisoner’s goal. In
2251   every subplot, we report the cumulative distribution of p-values computed in relation to the F-test for
2252   all conversations in that specific subgroup. A vertical red line indicates the 0.05 p-value threshold.
2253
       Thus, each subplot in each figure must be interpreted in terms of the proportion of conversations
       for which the p-value of the F-statistic computed after the Granger causality test is statistically
2254
       significant at the conventional 95% level.
2255
2256   What emerges starkly is that, regardless of the anti-social behavior examined and the scenario, the
2257   vast majority of conversations do not present any statistical evidence of Granger causality. This holds
2258   true for all LLMs as well. This robust finding suggests that there is no predictive interplay between
2259
       the agents, underscoring that anti-social behavior is primarily driven by the agents’ personalities
       rather than their interactions with the adversarial character in the simulation.
