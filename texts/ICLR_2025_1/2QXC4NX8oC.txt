036                                                                              A BSTRACT
037
038
039                               We present the first text-based image editing approach for object parts based on
040                               pre-trained diffusion models. Diffusion-based image editing approaches capitalized
041                               on the deep understanding of diffusion models of image semantics to perform a
042                               variety of edits. However, existing diffusion models lack sufficient understanding
043                               of many object parts, hindering fine-grained edits requested by users. To address
044                               this, we propose to expand the knowledge of pre-trained diffusion models to
045                               allow them to understand various object parts, enabling them to perform fine-
046                               grained edits. We achieve this by learning special textual tokens that correspond
047
                                  to different object parts through an efficient token optimization process. These
                                  tokens are optimized to produce reliable localization masks at each inference step
048
                                  to localize the editing region. Leveraging these masks, we design feature-blending
049
                                  and adaptive thresholding strategies to execute the edits seamlessly. To evaluate
050                               our approach, we establish a benchmark and an evaluation protocol for part editing.
051                               Experiments show that our approach outperforms existing editing methods on all
052                               metrics and is preferred by users 77 − 90% of the time in conducted user studies.

065   1      I NTRODUCTION
066
067   Diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2024;
068   Esser et al., 2024) have significantly advanced image generation, achieving unprecedented levels of
069   quality and fidelity. This progress is generally attributed to their large-scale training on the LAION-
070   5B dataset (Schuhmann et al., 2022) with image-text pairs, leading to a profound understanding of
071
      images and their semantics. Recent image editing methods (Hertz et al., 2022; Brooks et al., 2023;
      Brack et al., 2024; Parmar et al., 2023; Tumanyan et al., 2023; Kawar et al., 2023; Huang et al.,
072
      2024) have capitalized on this understanding to perform a wide range of edits to enhance the creative
073
      capabilities of artists and designers. These methods allow users to specify desired edits through text
074   prompts, enabling both semantic edits, such as modifying objects or their surroundings, and artistic
075   adjustments, like changing style and texture. Ideally, these edits must align with the requested textual
076   prompts while being seamlessly integrated and accurately localized in the image.
077
      Despite the remarkable advancement in these diffusion-based image editing methods, their effective-
078
      ness is limited by the extent to which diffusion models understand images. For instance, while a
079
      diffusion model can manipulate objects, it might fail to perform edits on fine-grained object parts.
080   Figure 1 shows examples where existing editing methods fail to perform fine-grained edits. For
081   instance, in the first row, they exhibit poor localization of the editing region and fail to edit only the
082   torso of the robot. In the second row, none of the approaches were able to localize the “hood” or
083   apply the edit. In the final row, existing approaches suffer from entangled parts, making the man’s
084   face younger when instructed to change his hair to "blonde." These limitations can be attributed
085   to the coarse textual descriptions in the LAIOB-5B dataset that is used to train diffusion models.
086   Specifically, the model fails to understand various object parts as they are not explicitly described
087   in image descriptions. Moreover, data biases in the datasets are also captured by the model, e.g.,
088
      associating blond hair with youth.
089   To validate this hypothesis, we visualized the cross-attention maps of a pre-trained diffusion model
090   for two textual prompts with specific object parts in Figure 2. Cross-attention computes attention
091   between image features and textual tokens, reflecting where each word in the textual prompt is
092   represented in the generated image. For the first prompt, “A muscular man in a white shirt with a
093   robotic head”, the generated image resembles a man with robotic arms instead of a robotic head.
094
      The cross-attention maps show that the token “head” is activated at the arms rather than the head. A
      possible explanation for this behavior is that the “head” has been entangled with the “arms” during
095
      training due to the coarse textual annotations of the training images. For the second prompt, “A red
096
      Mustang car with a black hood parked by the ocean”, the generated car is entirely red, including
097   the hood. The cross-attention map for the token ”hood” indicates that the model is uncertain about
098   its location. This can be attributed to the lack or scarcity of images with “hood” annotations in the
099   LAION-5B dataset used for training.
100
      In this paper, we address the limitations of pre-trained diffusion models in their understanding of
101
      object parts. By expanding their semantic knowledge, we enable fine-grained image edits, providing
102
      creators with greater control over their images. We achieve this by training part-specific tokens
103   that specialize in localizing the editing region at each denoising step. Based on this localization,
104   we develop feature blending and adaptive thresholding strategies that ensure seamless and high-
105   quality editing while preserving the unedited areas. To learn the part tokens, we design a token
106   optimization process (Zhou et al., 2022) tailored for fine-grained editing, utilizing existing object part

152   attention maps (Hertz et al., 2022; Parmar et al., 2023; Epstein et al., 2023). If the cross-attention

154   likely to fail, as demonstrated in Figure 1. An intuitive solution to this problem is expanding the

156   by fine-tuning the model with additional data of image/text pairs where the text is detailed. However,
157   this approach can be costly due to the extensive annotation required for fine-tuning the model, and
158
      there is no guarantee that the model will automatically learn to identify object parts effectively from
      text.

161   through explicit supervision of cross-attention maps. This was proven successful in several appli-

228   3.2      C HOOSING T IMESTEPS AND UN ET B LOCKS TO O PTIMIZE
229
      Ideally, we would like to optimize over all timesteps and UNet layers. However, this is computa-
230
      tionally and memory-expensive due to the large dimensionality of intermediate features in diffusion
231   models, especially SDXL (Podell et al., 2024) that we employ. Therefore, we need to select a subset
232   of layers and timesteps to achieve a good balance between localization accuracy and efficiency.
233
234
      To determine the optimal values for tstart , tend , we analyze the reconstructions of noise-free predic-
      tions x̂0 as per Equation (1) to observe the progression of image generation across different timesteps.
235
      Figure 4 shows that when optimizing over early timesteps tstart = 50, tend = 40, the noise level is
236
      high, making it difficult to identify different parts. For intermediate timesteps, tstart = 30, tend = 20,
237   most of the structure of the image is present, and optimizing on these timesteps leads to good local-
238   ization for big parts (e.g. the head) across most timesteps during inference. Moreover, the localization
239   of small parts, such as the eyes, becomes more accurate the closer the denoising gets towards t = 0.
240   Finally, optimizing on late timesteps, tstart = 10, tend = 0, provides reasonable localization for big
241   parts at intermediate timesteps but does not generalize well to early ones. Based on these observations,
242   we choose to optimize on intermediate timesteps for localizing larger parts due to their consistent
243   performance during inference time across all timesteps. For smaller parts, both intermediate and late
244   timesteps offer satisfactory localization.
245   Regarding the selection of layers L for optimization, we initially include all UNet blocks during
246   training and subsequently evaluate each block’s performance using the mean Intersection over Union
247   (mIoU) metric. Our analysis reveals that the first eight blocks of the decoder are sufficient to achieve
248   robust results, making them suitable for scenarios with limited computational resources. Further
249   details are provided in the appendix.
250   After learning the part tokens, the diffusion model can now understand and localize parts through our
251   optimized tokens. Next, we explain how we use them to perform fine-grained part edits. We start by
252   describing our approach for the synthetic image setup where the diffusion trajectory is known; then,
253   we explain how to perform real image editing.
254
255   3.3      PART E DITING
256
257   Given a source image I s that was generated with a source prompt P s , it is desired to edit this image
258   according to the editing prompt P e to produce the edited image I e . To perform part edits, the editing
259   prompt shall include one of the optimized part tokens that we refer to as <part-name>. As an
260   example, a source prompt can be “A closeup of a man”, and the editing prompt can be “A closeup of
261
      a man with a robotic <head>”, where <head> is a part token. To apply the edit, we perform the
      denoising in three parallel paths (see Figure 3). The first path is the source path, which includes the
262
      trajectory of the original image that originates from a synthetic image or an inverted trajectory of a
263
      real image. The second path incorporates the part tokens that we optimized, and it provides the part
264   localization masks. The final path is the edit path that is influenced by the two other paths to produce
265   the final edited image. Since Ê has 2 tokens compared to 77 tokens in the source and the edit paths,
266
      we pad Ê with the background token to match the size of the other two embeddings. In the appendix,
267   we provide more details on the choice of padding strategy.

297   3.4   R EAL I MAGE E DITING
298
      Our approach can also perform part edits on real images by incorporating a real image inversion
299
      method, e.g., Ledits++ (Brack et al., 2024) or EF-DDPM (Huberman-Spiegelglas et al., 2024). In this
300
      setting, the role of the inversion method is to estimate the diffusion trajectory x0 . . . xT for a given
301   real image. This estimated trajectory is then used as the source path in Figure 3. To obtain the source
302   prompt P s of the real images, we use the image captioning approach, BLIP2 (Li et al., 2023), which
303   is commonly used for this purpose. Finally, the edit is applied where the localization and editing
304   paths are similar to the synthetic setting.
305
306   3.5   D ISCUSSION
307
308   Our approach is a text-based editing approach eliminating the need for the user-provided masks to
309   perform fine-grained edits. Despite the fact that the token optimization process requires annotated
310   masks for training, it can already produce satisfactory localization based on a single annotated image
311
      (see the Appendix). Moreover, the localization masks produced by the tokens are non-binary, leading
      to a seamless blending of edits, unlike user-provided masks, which are typically binary.
312
313   Another aspect is that our approach can be used in the context of image generation in case of
314   complicated concepts that are difficult for diffusion models to comprehend from regular prompts.
315   Examples of this scenario were shown in Figure 2, where a direct generation process failed to generate
316   the requested concepts. In that case, and with the help of the optimized part tokens, the user can
317
      specify different attributes for different parts of the object in the image.

      4.1                       E VALUATION S ETUP
341
342   Synthetic Image Editing We base our method on a pre-trained SDXL (Podell et al., 2024) 1 . For
343   sampling, we employ the DDIM sampler (Song et al., 2020) with T = 50 denoising steps and
344   default scheduling parameters as (Hertz et al., 2022). For token optimization, we train on 10-20
345   images per token for 2000 optimization steps. We set te = T = 50 for complete preservation of the
346   unedited regions. We compare against two popular text-based editing approaches: Prompt-to-Prompt
347   (P2P) (Hertz et al., 2022) and Instruct-Pix2Pix (iP2P) (Brooks et al., 2023). We use the SDXL
348   implementation of P2P2 , and the Diffusers implementation of iP2P3 .
349   Real Image Editing We use Ledits++ (Brack et al., 2024) inversion method to invert the images
350   where the source prompt P s is produced by BLIP2 (Li et al., 2023) as described in Section 3.4. We
351   compare against Ledits++ and EF-DDPM (Huberman-Spiegelglas et al., 2024)
352
      PartEdit Dataset We create a synthetic and real dataset of 7 different object parts:
353   <humanoid-head>,              <humanoid-torso>,            <human-hair>         <animal-head>,
354   <car-body>, <car-hood>, and <chair-seat>. For the synthetic dataset, we gener-
355   ate random source prompts of the objects of interest at random locations and select random edits
356   from pre-defined lists. We generate a total of 60 synthetic images and manually annotate the part
357   of interest. For the real dataset, we collect 20 images from the internet and manually annotate and
358   assign editing prompts to them. We denote the synthetic and real datasets as PartEdit-Synth and
359   PartEdit-Real, respectively. More details are provided in the Appendix.
360   Evaluation Metrics To evaluate the edits, we want to verify that: (1) The edit has been applied at the
361   correct location. (2) The unedited regions and the background have been preserved. Therefore, We
362   evaluate the following metrics for the foreground (the edit) and the background:
363
364                             1. αClipFG : the CLIP similarity between the editing prompt and the edited image region.
365                             2. αClipBG : the CLIP similarity between the unedited region of the source and the edited

476   5.1                                                D IFFUSION - BASED I MAGE E DITING
477
      In general, the image semantics are encoded within the cross-attention layers, which specify where
478
      each word in the text prompt is located in the image (Hertz et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2023). In
479
      addition, the style and the appearance of the image are encoded through the self-attention layers
480   (Tumanyan et al., 2023). Diffusion-based editing approaches exploit these facts to perform different
481   types of semantic or stylistic edits. On the semantic level, several approaches attempt to change the
482   contents of an image according to a user-provided prompt (Hertz et al., 2022; Brooks et al., 2023; Lin
483   et al., 2023; Kawar et al., 2023; Parmar et al., 2023). These changes include swapping an object or
484   altering its surroundings by manipulating the cross-attention maps either through token replacement
485   or attention-map tuning. For stylistic edits, several approaches (Cao et al., 2023; Tumanyan et al.,
      2023; Parmar et al., 2023; Hertz et al., 2023) modify the self-attention maps to apply a specific


                                                                                                                  9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      style to the image while preserving the semantics. This style is either provided by the user in the
487   form of text or a reference image. It is worth mentioning that an orthogonal research direction
488   investigates image inversion to enable real image editing (Brack et al., 2024; Huberman-Spiegelglas
489   et al., 2024; Brooks et al., 2023). Another line of research focuses on memory-based guidance for
490   moving objects (dragging) (Mou et al., 2023). Methods that focus on obtaining a fine-grained mask
491   or editing segments of the image (Liu et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024; Singh et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2024;
492   Kirillov et al., 2023). For a comprehensive review of editing techniques and applications, we refer
493   readers to (Huang et al., 2024). Existing text-based editing approaches struggle to apply semantic or
494   stylistic edits to fine-grained object parts, as demonstrated in Section 4. Our approach, PartEdit, is the
495
      first step toward enabling fine-grained editing, which will enhance user controllability and experience,
      and can potentially be integrated into existing editing pipelines.
496
497
498   5.2   T OKEN O PTIMIZATION
499
      To expand the capabilities of pre-trained diffusion models or to address some of their limitations, they
500
      either need to be re-trained or finetuned. However, these approaches are computationally expensive
501   due to the large scale of these models and the training datasets. An attractive alternative is token
502   optimization, where the pre-trained model is kept frozen, and special textual tokens are optimized
503   instead. Those tokens are then used alongside the input prompt to the model to perform a specific task
504   through explicit supervision of cross-attention maps. This has been proven successful in several tasks.
505   (Valevski et al., 2023; Gal et al., 2022; Avrahami et al., 2023a; Safaee et al., 2024) employed token
506   optimization and LoRA finetuning to extract or learn new concepts that are used for image generation.
507   (Hedlin et al., 2023) learned tokens to detect the most prominent points in images. (Marcos-Manchón
508   et al., 2024; Khani et al., 2024) optimized tokens to perform semantic segmentation. We explored
509
      the use of token optimization to learn tokens for object parts customized with a focus on image
      editing. For this purpose, we adopt SDXL (Podell et al., 2024) as a base model to obtain the best
510
      visual editing quality in contrast to existing approaches that leverage SD 1.5 or 2.1. Our token
511
      optimization training is also tailored to obtaining smooth cross-attention maps across all timesteps,
512   unlike (Marcos-Manchón et al., 2024; Khani et al., 2024) that aggregate all attention maps and apply
513   post-processing to obtain binary segmentation masks.
514
515
516   6     C ONCLUDING R EMARKS
517
518   Limitations Our approach is limited by the generation capability of the underlying diffusion model.
519
      For instance, it can not perform unreasonable edits that combine irrelevant parts, e.g., editing a human
      head to become a car wheel. Moreover, it can not change the style of the edited part to a different
520
      style other than the style of the original image. This limitation stems from the internal design of
521
      SDXL that encodes the style in self-attention and prevents mixing two different styles. We provide
522   examples of these scenarios in the Appendix.
523
524   Ethical Impact Our approach can help alleviate the internal racial bias of some edits, such as
525
      correlating “Afro” hair with Africans, as we demonstrated in Figure 5. On the other hand, our
      approach can produce images that might be deemed inappropriate for some users by mixing parts of
526
      different animals or humans. Moreover, the remarkable seamless edits performed by our approach
527
      can potentially be used for generating fake images, misinformation, or changing the identity.
528
529   Future Work Our approach can be extended to perform 3D edits similar to Instruct-Nerf2Nerf Haque
530   et al. (2023). An LLM can also be incorporated to parse complicated editing prompts to the correct
531
      part token. For example, mapping “A man with a muscular upper body” to the token “<torso>”.
532
533   7     C ONCLUSION
534
535   We introduced the first text-based editing approach for object parts based on a pre-trained diffusion
536   model. Our approach can perform appealing edits that possess high quality and are seamlessly
537   integrated with the parent object. Moreover, it can create concepts that the standard diffusion
538   models and editing approaches are incapable of generating. This helps to unleash the creativity of
539   creators, and we hope that our approach will establish a new line of research for fine-grained editing
      approaches.

       L    H YPERPARAMETERS
999
1000
       We provide hyperparameters used for training the tokens and hyper parameters used during editing.
1001
1002   For the training process, we deploy BCE loss of initial learning rate of 30.0, with a StepLR scheduler
1003   of 80 steps size and gamma of 0.7. For the diffusion, we use a strength of 0.25 and a guidance scale
1004
       of 7.5. During aggregation for loss computation, masks are resized to 512 for SD2.1 and 1024 for
       SDXL model variants. We use 1000 or 2000 epochs during training.
1005
1006   During inference, as discussed in Figure 10, we use ω of 1.5. For the guidance scale, larger values
1007   tend to increase adherence to αCLIP but at the cost of PSNR and SSIM. We investigated values
1008   between 3.5 and 20 for the guidance scale and used 12.5 as a balance between the two for Ours +
1009   edits real setting. For the synthetic setting, a guidance scale of 7.5 was used. During inference, we
1010
       start editing at the first step, as we utilize prior time step mask information.
