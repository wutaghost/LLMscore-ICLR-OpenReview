009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Classic tree-based ensembles generalize better than any single decision tree. In
012            contrast, recent empirical studies find that modern ensembles of (overparame-
013            terized) neural networks may not provide any inherent generalization advantage
014            over single but larger neural networks. This paper clarifies how modern overpa-
015            rameterized ensembles differ from their classic underparameterized counterparts,
016            using ensembles of random feature (RF) regressors as a basis for developing the-
017            ory. In contrast to the underparameterized regime, where ensembling typically
018
               induces regularization and increases generalization, we prove that infinite ensem-
               bles of overparameterized RF regressors become pointwise equivalent to (single)
019
               infinite-width RF regressors. This equivalence, which is exact for ridgeless models
020
               and approximate for small ridge penalties, implies that overparameterized ensem-
021            bles and single large models exhibit nearly identical generalization. As a conse-
022            quence, we can characterize the predictive variance amongst ensemble members,
023            and demonstrate that it quantifies the expected effects of increasing capacity rather
024            than capturing any conventional notion of uncertainty. Our results challenge com-
025            mon assumptions about the advantages of ensembles in overparameterized set-
026            tings, prompting a reconsideration of how well intuitions from underparameter-
027            ized ensembles transfer to deep ensembles and the overparameterized regime.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Ensembling is one of the most well-established techniques in machine learning (e.g. Schapire, 1990;
033
      Hansen & Salamon, 1990; Opitz & Maclin, 1999; Dietterich, 2000). Historically, most ensembles
      aggregated component models that are simple by todayРђЎs standards. Common techniques like bag-
034
      ging (Breiman, 1996), feature selection (Breiman, 2001), random projections (Kaba╠Ђn, 2014; Thanei
035
      et al., 2017), and boosting (Freund, 1995; Chen & Guestrin, 2016) were developed and analyzed
036   assuming decision trees, least-squares regressors, and other underparameterized component models
037   incapable of achieving near-zero training error. Crucially, the resulting ensembles achieve better
038   generalization than what could be achieved by any individual component model.
039
      Recently, researchers and practitioners have turned to ensembling large overparameterized models,
040
      such as neural networks, which have more than enough capacity to memorize training datasets and
041
      are typically trained with little to no regularization. Like ensembles of underparameterized mod-
042   els, ensembles of large neural networks are often used to reduce generalization error (Lee et al.,
043   2015; Fort et al., 2019). Motivated by practical effectiveness and heuristics from classic ensembles
044   (Mentch & Hooker, 2016), some have further argued that the predictive variance amongst compo-
045   nent models in these so-called deep ensembles is a well-calibrated notion of uncertainty (Lakshmi-
046   narayanan et al., 2017; Ovadia et al., 2019; Gustafsson et al., 2020) that can be used on downstream
047   decision-making tasks (Gal et al., 2017; Yu et al., 2020).
048   While there are few theoretical works analyzing these modern overparameterized ensembles, recent
049   empirical evidence suggests that intuitions from their underparameterized counterparts do not hold
050   in this new regime. For example, classic methods to increase diversity amongst component models,
051   such as bagging, are harmful for deep ensembles (Nixon et al., 2020; Jeffares et al., 2024; Abe et al.,
052   2024) despite being nearly universally beneficial for underparameterized ensembles. Moreover,
053   several recent studies question whether deep ensembles offer significant improvements in robustness
      and uncertainty quantification over what can be achieved by a single (but larger) neural network

054
      (Abe et al., 2022; Theisen et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024). These results suggest that an ensemble
055   of (large) overparameterized networks may not differ fundamentally from a single (extremely large)
056   neural network, in contrast to the underparameterized regime where ensembles are a fundamentally
057   different class of predictors Schapire (1990); Breiman (2001); Kaba╠Ђn (2014).
058
      To address this divergence and verify recent empirical findings on deep ensembles, we develop a the-
059
      oretical characterization of ensembles in the overparameterized regime, with the goal of contrasting
060
      against (traditional) underparameterized ensembles. We answer the following questions:
061
062         1. Do large ensembles of overparameterized models differ from single (very large) models
063            trained on the same data? Does the capacity of the component models affect this difference?
064
            2. Under a fixed parameter/computation budget, does an ensemble of overparameterized mod-
065            els provide additional generalization or robustness benefits over a single (larger) model?
066
            3. What does the predictive variance of overparameterized ensembles measure, and does it
067
               relate to different notions of uncertainty?
068
069   To answer these questions, we analyze ensembles of overparameterized random feature (RF) linear
070   regressors, a widely used theoretically-tractable approximation of neural networks (e.g. Belkin et al.,
071   2018; Bartlett et al., 2020; Mei & Montanari, 2022). These models can be interpreted as neural
072   networks where only the last layer is trained (e.g. Rudi & Rosasco, 2017; Belkin et al., 2019) or as
073   first-order Taylor approximations of neural networks (e.g. Jacot et al., 2018). By averaging models
074   that differ solely in their random features, we emulate the common practice of ensembling neural
075   networks that differ only by random initialization (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). Our analysis
076   focuses on the practically relevant regime where regressors are trained with little to no regularization.
077
078   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
079
      Random feature models. RF models perform regression on a random subset or projection of a
080
      high- (or infinite-) dimensional feature representation. Originally introduced as a scalable approx-
081   imation to kernel machines (Rahimi & Recht, 2007; 2008a;b), RF regressors have seen growing
082   theoretical interest as simplified models of neural networks (e.g. Belkin et al., 2019; Jacot et al.,
083   2018; Bartlett et al., 2020; Mei & Montanari, 2022; Simon et al., 2024). This approximation of
084   neural networks becomes exact in the limit of infinite width (e.g. Jacot et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2019).
085
086   Underparameterized random feature models and ensembles. There are many works theoreti-
087   cally characterizing ensembles of tree-based models (e.g. Schapire & Singer, 1998; Sexton & Laake,
088   2009; Wager et al., 2014; Mentch & Hooker, 2016). Here, we restrict our discussion to analyses of
089   (ensembles of) RF regressors. Most works of this nature analyze underparameterized models, where
090   the number of random features (i.e., the width) is assumed to be far fewer than the number of data
091
      points. In the underparameterized fixed design setting, the infinite ensemble of unregularized RF
      regressors achieves the same generalization error as ridge regression on the original (unprojected)
092
      inputs (Kaba╠Ђn, 2014; Thanei et al., 2017; Bach, 2024a). We emphasize the distinction between
093
      underparameterized component models and their aggregated prediction: i.e., the ensemble of un-
094   regularized regressors is equivalent to a regularized predictor. (We provide theoretical analysis in
095   Appx. D that further demonstrates ridge-like behaviour of underpameterized RF ensembles.)
096
097   Overparameterized random feature models. Recent works on RF models have focused on the
098   overparameterized regime, often using high-dimensional asymptotics to characterize generalization
099   error (Adlam & Pennington, 2020; Hastie et al., 2022; Mei & Montanari, 2022; Loureiro et al.,
100   2022; Bach, 2024a). Implicit in many works is an assumption of Gaussian universality, in which
101   the marginal distributions over the random features are replaced by moment-matched Gaussians.
102   While such assumptions are common throughout asymptotic random matrix theory (e.g. Tao, 2012),
103   our work aims to establish more general results that hold for more general random features. We
104
      demonstrateРђћboth theoretically and empiricallyРђћthat Gaussianity may be an inappropriate approx-
      imation for neural network features when comparing the pointwise behaviour of ensembles versus
105
      single models.
106

      dom feature models have been analyzed by Hao et al. (2024), who provide lower bounds on OOD

139   1.2     C ONTRIBUTIONS
140
141
      We consider ensembles of overparameterized RF regressors in both the ridgeless and small ridge
142
      regimes. Unlike prior work, we make minimal assumptions about the distribution of the random
143   features. Therefore, our results can be assumed to hold for most RF ensembles rather than only those
144   that are compositions of GP-random features. Concretely, we make the following contributions:
145
146
      To answer Question 1: we show that the average ridgeless RF regressor is pointwise equivalent
      to its corresponding ridgeless kernel regressor (Theorem 1), implying that an infinite ensemble of
147
      overparameterized RF models is exactly equivalent to a single infinite-width RF model. We further
148
      show that this equivalence approximately holds in the small ridge regime (Theorem 2).
149
150   To answer Question 2: we use rates established in prior work to demonstrate that the variance
151   reduction from ensembling overparameterized RF regressors is very similar to increasing the number
152
      of features in a single model. This shows that ensembles do not offer additional generalization or
      robustness advantages over single models under fixed parameter budgets (see Sec. 3.2).
153
154   To answer Question 3: we show that the predictive variance in an overparameterized ensemble is
155   the expected squared difference between the predictions from a (finite-width) RF regressor and its
156   corresponding kernel regressor (i.e., the infinite-width model). With this finding, we demonstrate
157   that ensemble variance differs from conventional uncertainty quantifications, except in practically
158
      unrealistic cases where the random features are sampled from a GP (see Sec. 3.2).
159   Altogether, these results support recent empirical findings that deep ensembles offer few general-
160   ization and uncertainty quantification benefits over single models (Abe et al., 2022; Theisen et al.,
161   2024). Our theory and experiments demonstrate that these phenomena are not specific to neural
      networks but are more general properties of ensembles in the overparameterized regime.

504   4   C ONCLUSION
505
506   For Question 1, we demonstrated that under weak conditions, infinite ensembles, and single infinite-
507
      width models are pointwise equivalent in the ridgeless regime and nearly identical with a small
      ridge, significantly expanding on prior results (e.g. Jacot et al., 2020). These results verify recent
508
      empirical findings (e.g. Abe et al., 2022) that much of the benefit attributed to overparameterized
509
      ensembles, such as improved predictive performance and robustness, can be explained by their sim-
510   ilarity to larger single models. We contrast these findings to the underparameterized regime, where
511   ensembling typically induces regularization and improves generalization. Similarly, for Question 2,
512   we argued that the variance reduction from ensembling is asymptotically equivalent to increasing
513   the number of features of a single model. This result further strengthens our findings on Question 1
514   and demonstrates functional similarities under relatively small computational budgets. For Question
515   3, we found that the ensemble variance measures the expected difference to a single larger model
516   and is thus a non-standard measure for uncertainty. Significant deviations from the Gaussian pro-
517   cess posterior variance indicate that caution is needed when using ensemble variance for uncertainty
518
      quantification, especially in safety-critical settings. Again, these results reinforce empirical findings
      from (Abe et al., 2022) about overparameterized neural network ensembles.
519
520   Overall, while our results do not contradict the utility of overparameterized ensembles, they suggest
521   that their benefits may often be explained by their similarity to larger models and that further research
522   is needed to improve uncertainty quantification methods.
523
524   Limitations. The practical implications of our work are limited by the theoretical abstractions we
525   employ. While these abstractions provide valuable theoretical insights, they may not always hold
526
      in real-world, finite settings. Most notably, we approximate neural networks using RF models and
      focus on infinite single models and infinite ensembles as approximations for large models and large
527
      ensembles. Nevertheless, we emphasize that our theoretical results on RF regressors align with
528
      recent empirical observations on deep ensembles (Abe et al., 2022; Theisen et al., 2024), further
529   supporting the growing body of work that uses RF models to provide insights into deep learning
530   phenomena (e.g. Belkin et al., 2019; Hastie et al., 2022; Simon et al., 2024).
531
532
      In addition to these theoretical assumptions, our empirical results are constrained in terms of scale
      and complexity. Due to numerical stability issues (see Appx. A.2), we primarily considered a small
533
      number of samples, a relatively large number of random features, and simple data-generating func-
534
      tions. Again, we refer the readers to the afformentioned empirical work for larger-scale experiments.

784   A.2   N OTES ON STABILITY
785
786   During our experiments, we encountered challenges related to both mathematical stability (i.e., ma-
787   trices being truly singular rather than nearly singular) and numerical stability. This section outlines
788   these issues and describes the steps we took to mitigate them.
789   Most importantly, the matrix ╬дW ╬дРіц W is not almost surely invertible when using the ReLU activation
790   function, meaning that technically, the second condition of our Assumption 1 is not fulfilled. In
791   numerical experiments, this results in cases where (╬дW ╬дРіцW)
                                                                   Рѕњ1
                                                                       is nearly singular (though stabilized
                  Рѕњ8
792   with ╬╗ = 10 ).
793   On the other hand, when D is sufficiently large relative to N , ╬дW is full rank with high probabil-
794   ity, which implies that ╬дW ╬дРіц W is invertible with high probability. Given our data transformation
795   of appending a 1 in the zeroeth dimension, one can see this as there exists a series of (non-zero
796   probability sets of) hyperplanes separating an increasing subset of the training points, leading to
797   a subset of ╬дW РђЎs columns that form a triangular, invertible matrix (see Fig. 8 for a visualization).
798   Intuitively, higher data dimensionality and better separability of the points increase the probability
799   of ╬дW having full rank.
800   As an example of the discussed instabilities, see the adversarial scenario shown in Fig. 9, where
801   N = 15 and many training points are placed very close to each other. In this case, individual
802   RF regressors exhibit relatively high variance output values (due to numerical instabilities), which
803   are not averaged out in the РђюinfiniteРђЮ ensemble. Similar issues were also observed when using the
804   Gaussian error function as the activation function, although they were generally less pronounced.
