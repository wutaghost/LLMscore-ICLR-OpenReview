                                                            A BSTRACT
022
023
                        Gaussian splatting and single/multi-view depth estimation are typically studied in
024                     isolation. In this paper, we present DepthSplat to connect Gaussian splatting and
025                     depth estimation and study their interactions. More specifically, we first contribute
026                     a robust multi-view depth model by leveraging pre-trained monocular depth fea-
027                     tures, leading to high-quality feed-forward 3D Gaussian splatting reconstructions.
028                     We also show that Gaussian splatting can serve as an unsupervised pre-training
029                     objective for learning powerful depth models from large-scale unlabelled datasets.
030                     We validate the synergy between Gaussian splatting and depth estimation through
031                     extensive ablation and cross-task transfer experiments. Our DepthSplat achieves
032
                        state-of-the-art performance on ScanNet, RealEstate10K and DL3DV datasets in
                        terms of both depth estimation and novel view synthesis, demonstrating the mutual
033
                        benefits of connecting both tasks. We invite the readers to view our supplementary
034
                        video for feed-forward reconstruction results of large-scale or 360 scenes. Our
035                     code and models will be publicly available.

038   1            I NTRODUCTION
039
040   Novel view synthesis (Buehler et al., 2001; Zhou et al., 2018) and depth prediction (Schönberger
041   et al., 2016; Eigen et al., 2014) are two fundamental tasks in computer vision, serving as the driving
042   force behind numerous applications ranging from augmented reality to robotics and autonomous
043   driving. There have been notable advancements in both areas recently.
044   For novel view synthesis, 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023) has emerged as a popular
045   technique due to its impressive real-time performance while attaining high visual fidelity. Recently,
046   advances in feed-forward 3DGS models (Charatan et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024; Szymanowicz
047   et al., 2024) have been introduced to alleviate the need for tedious per-scene optimization, also
048   enabling few-view 3D reconstruction. The state-of-the-art sparse-view method MVSplat (Chen et al.,
049   2024) relies on feature matching-based multi-view depth estimation (Xu et al., 2023) to localize the
050
      3D Gaussian positions, which makes it suffer from similar limitations (e.g., occlusions, texture-less
      regions, and reflective surfaces) as other multi-view depth methods (Schönberger et al., 2016; Yao
051
      et al., 2018; Gu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021; Duzceker et al., 2021).
052
053   On the other hand, significant progress has been made in monocular depth estimation, with recent
      models (Yang et al., 2024a; Ke et al., 2024; Yin et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2024; Ranftl et al., 2020;


                                                                  1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Eftekhar et al., 2021) achieving robust predictions on diverse in-the-wild data. However, these depths
055   typically lack consistency across views, constraining their performance in downstream tasks (Wang
056   et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2022). In addition, both state-of-the-art multi-view (Cao et al., 2022; Xu et al.,
057   2023) and monocular (Yang et al., 2024a; Piccinelli et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024b) depth models are
058   trained with ground truth depth supervision, which prevents exploiting large unlabelled datasets for
059   more robust depth predictions.
060
      The integration of 3DGS with single/multi-view depth estimation presents a compelling solution
061   to overcome the individual limitations of each technique while at the same time enhancing their
062   strengths. To this end, we introduce DepthSplat, which exploits the complementary nature of sparse-
063   view feed-forward 3DGS and robust single/multi-view depth estimation to improve the performance
064   for both tasks.
065
      Specifically, we first contribute a robust multi-view depth model by integrating pre-trained monocular
066
      depth features (Yang et al., 2024b) to the multi-view feature matching branch, which not only
067   maintains the consistency of multi-view depth models but also leads to more robust results in
068   situations that are hard to match (e.g., occlusions, texture-less regions and reflective surfaces). The
069   predicted multi-view depth maps are then unprojected to 3D as the Gaussian centers, and we use an
070   additional lightweight network to predict other remaining Gaussian parameters. They are combined
071   together to achieve novel view synthesis with the splatting operation (Kerbl et al., 2023).
072
      Thanks to our improved multi-view depth model, the quality of novel view synthesis with Gaussian
073   splatting is also significantly enhanced (see Fig. 1 left). In addition, our Gaussian splatting module is
074   fully differentiable, which requires only photometric supervision to optimize all model components.
075   This provides a new, unsupervised way to pre-train depth prediction models on large-scale unlabeled
076   datasets without requiring ground truth geometry information. The pre-trained depth model can be
077   further fine-tuned for specific depth tasks and achieves superior results over training from scratch
078   (see Fig. 1 right, where unsupervised pre-training leads to improved performance).
079   We conduct extensive experiments on the large-scale TartanAir (Wang et al., 2020), ScanNet (Dai
080   et al., 2017) and RealEstate10K (Zhou et al., 2018) datasets for depth estimation and Gaussian
081   splatting tasks, as well as the recently introduced DL3DV (Ling et al., 2023) dataset, which features
082   complex real-world scenes and thus is more challenging. Under various evaluation settings, our
083   DepthSplat achieves state-of-the-art results. The strong performance on both tasks demonstrates the
084   mutual benefits of connecting Gaussian splatting and depth estimation.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
087
088
089   Multi-View Depth Estimation. As a core component of classical multi-view stereo pipelines (Schön-
090   berger et al., 2016), multi-view depth estimation exploits multi-view photometric consistency across
091
      multiple images to perform feature matching and predict the depth map of the reference image. Re-
      cently, many learning-based methods (Yao et al., 2018; Gu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021; Duzceker
092
      et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Ding et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2022) have been proposed to improve
093
      depth accuracy. For example, MVSNet (Yao et al., 2018) uses the plane-sweep algorithm (Collins,
094   1996) to build a 3D cost volume, regularizes it with a 3D CNN, and then regresses the depth map.
095   Though these learning-based methods significantly improve the depth quality when compared to
096   traditional methods (Galliani et al., 2015; Schönberger et al., 2016; Xu & Tao, 2019), they can-
097   not handle challenging situations where the multi-view photometric consistency assumption is not
098   guaranteed, e.g., occlusions, low-textured areas, and non-Lambertian surfaces.
099
      Monocular Depth Estimation. Recently, we have witnessed significant progress in depth estimation
100
      from a single image (Ranftl et al., 2020; Bhat et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024a; Ke
101
      et al., 2024), and existing methods can produce surprisingly accurate results on diverse in-the-wild
102   data. However, monocular depth methods inherently suffer from scale ambiguities and are not able
103   to produce multi-view consistent depth predictions, which are crucial for downstream tasks like
104   3D reconstruction (Yin et al., 2022) and video depth estimation (Wang et al., 2023). In this paper,
105   we leverage the powerful features from a pre-trained monocular depth model (Yang et al., 2024b)
106   to augment feature-matching based multi-view depth estimation, which not only maintains high
107   multi-view consistency but also leads to significantly improved robustness in challenging situations
      such as low-textured regions and reflective surfaces.

      Monocular and Multi-View Depth Fusion. Several previous methods (Bae et al., 2022; Li et al.,
129
      2023; Yang et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2024) try to fuse monocular and multi-view depths to alleviate the
130   limitations of feature matching-based multi-view depth estimations. However, they either fuse single-
131   and multi-view depth predictions with additional networks or rely on sophisticated architectures. In
132   contrast, we identify the power of off-the-shell pre-trained monocular depth models and propose to
133   augment multi-view cost volumes with monocular features, which not only leads to a simple model
134   architecture but also strong performance.
135
      Feed-Forward Gaussian Splatting. Several feed-forward 3D Gaussian splatting models (Charatan
136
      et al., 2024a; Szymanowicz et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Wewer et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024; Xu
137
      et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024) have been proposed in literature thanks to its efficiency and ability
138   to handle sparse views. In particular, pixelSplat (Charatan et al., 2024a) and Splatter Image (Szy-
139   manowicz et al., 2024) predict 3D Gaussians from image features, while MVSplat (Chen et al.,
140   2024) encodes the feature matching information with cost volumes and achieves better geometry.
141   However, it inherently suffers from the limitation of feature matching in challenging situations like
142   texture-less regions and reflective surfaces. In this paper, we propose to integrate monocular features
143   from pre-trained monocular depth models for more robust depth prediction and Gaussian splatting
144   reconstruction. We additionally study the interactions between depth and Gaussian splatting tasks.
145   Another line of work like LGM (Tang et al., 2024), GRM (Xu et al., 2024), and GS-LRM (Zhang et al.,
146
      2024) relies significantly on the training data and compute, discarding explicit feature matching cues
      and learning everything purely from data. This makes them expensive to train (e.g., GS-LRM (Zhang
147
      et al., 2024) is trained with 64 A100 GPUs for 2 days), while our model can be trained in 1 day with
148
      8 GPUs. Moreover, our Gaussian splatting module, in return, enables pre-training depth model from
149   large-scale unlabelled datasets without the need for ground truth depths.
150
151   Depth and Gaussian Splatting. In addition to the experimental setup (feed-forward) studied in this
152   paper, recently, there has been another line of work (Chung et al., 2024; Turkulainen et al., 2024)
153   which applies an additional depth loss in the Gaussian splatting optimization process. We remark
154
      that these two approaches (feed-forward vs. per-scene optimization) are orthogonal. In particular,
      our approach focuses on exploring the advanced network architectures and the power of large-scale
155
      training data, while the optimization methods mainly study the effect of loss functions for regularizing
156
      the optimization process.

      More specifically, our depth model consists of two branches: one for modeling feature matching
169
      information using cost volumes, and another for extracting monocular features from a pre-trained
170   monocular depth network. The cost volumes and monocular features are concatenated together for
171   subsequent depth regression with a 2D U-Net and a softmax layer. For the depth task, we train our
172   depth model with ground truth depth supervision. Our full model for novel view synthesis is trained
173   with the photometric rendering loss, which can also be used as an unsupervised pre-training stage for
174   the depth model. In the following, we introduce the individual components.

      3.1   M ULTI -V IEW F EATURE M ATCHING
177
178   In this branch, we extract multi-view features with a multi-view Transformer architecture and then
179   build multiple cost volumes that correspond to each input view.
180
181
      Multi-View Feature Extraction. For N input images, we first use a lightweight weight-sharing
      ResNet (He et al., 2016) architecture to get s× downsampled features for each image independently.
182
      To handle different image resolutions, we make the downsampling factor s flexible by controlling the
183
      number of stride-2 3 × 3 convolutions. For example, the downsampling factor s is 4 if two stride-2
184   convolutions are used and 8 if three are used. To exchange information across different views, we use
185   a multi-view Swin Transformer (Liu et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022; 2023) which contains six stacked
                                                                                                  H   W
186   self- and cross-attention layers to obtain multi-view-aware features {Fi }N          i      s × s ×C ,
                                                                                   i=1 , F ∈ R
187   where C is the feature dimension. More specifically, cross-attention is performed between each
188   reference view and other views. When more than two images (N > 2) are given as input, we perform
189   cross-attention between each reference view and its top-2 nearest neighboring views, which are
190   selected based on their camera position distances to the reference view. This makes the computation
191   tractable while maintaining cross-view interactions.
192
      Feature Matching. We encode the feature matching information across different views with the
193
      plane-sweep stereo approach (Collins, 1996; Xu et al., 2023). More specifically, for each view i, we
194   first uniformly sample D depth candidates {dm }D    m=1 from the near and far depth ranges and then
195   warp the feature F j of view j to view i with the camera projection matrix and each depth candidate
196   dm . Then we obtain D warped features {Fdj→i  m

197   their feature correlations with the dot-product operation (Xu & Zhang, 2020; Chen et al., 2024). The

205   3.2   M ONOCULAR D EPTH F EATURE E XTRACTION
206
207   Despite the remarkable progress in multi-view feature matching-based depth estimation (Yao et al.,
208   2018; Wang et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023) and Gaussian splatting (Chen et al., 2024), they inherently
209   suffer from limitations in challenging situations like occlusions, texture-less regions, and reflective
210   surfaces. Thus, we propose to integrate pre-trained monocular depth features into the cost volume
211   representation to handle scenarios that are challenging or impossible to match.
212   More specifically, we leverage the pre-trained monocular depth backbone from the recent Depth
213   Anything (Yang et al., 2024b) model thanks to its impressive performance on diverse in-the-wild data.
214   The monocular backbone is a ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Oquab et al., 2023) model, which has a
215   patch size of 14 and outputs a feature map that is 1/14 spatial resolution of the original image. We
      simply bilinearly interpolate the spatial resolution of the monocular features to the same resolution

222
223   To achieve robust and multi-view consistent depth predictions, we combine the monocular feature
                   H  W                                  H  W

225   dimension. A subsequent 2D U-Net (Rombach et al., 2022; Ronneberger et al., 2015) is used to

230   We also apply a hierarchical matching (Gu et al., 2020) architecture where an additional refinement
231   step at 2× higher feature resolution is employed to improve the performance further. More specifically,
232   based on the coarse depth prediction, we perform a correspondence search on the 2× higher feature
233   maps within the neighbors of the 2× upsampled coarse depth prediction. Since we already have a
234   coarse depth prediction, we only need to search a smaller range at the higher resolution, and thus, we
235   construct a smaller cost volume compared to the coarse stage. Such a 2-scale hierarchical architecture
236
      not only leads to improved efficiency since most computation is spent on low resolution, but also
      leads to better results thanks to the use of higher-resolution features (Gu et al., 2020). Similar feature
237
      fusion and depth regression procedure is used to get higher-resolution depth predictions, which are
238
      subsequently upsampled to the full resolution with a learned upsampler (Ranftl et al., 2021).

241
242   For the task of 3D Gaussian splatting, we directly unproject the per-pixel depth maps to 3D with
243   the camera parameters as the Gaussian centers µj . We append an additional lightweight network to
244   predict other Gaussian parameters αj , Σj , cj , which are opacity, covariance, and color, respectively.

246   operation (Kerbl et al., 2023).

532   5                C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we introduce DepthSplat, a new approach to connecting Gaussian splatting and depth
535   to achieve state-of-the-art results on ScanNet, RealEstate10K and DL3DV datasets for both depth
536   and view synthesis tasks. We also show that our model enables unsupervised pre-training depth
537   with Gaussian splatting rendering loss, providing a way to leverage large-scale unlabelled multi-
538   view image datasets for training more multi-view consistent and robust depth models. Our current
539   model requires camera pose information as input along with the multi-view images, removing this
      requirement would be exciting future work.
