002   LLM Generalization in Reasoning Tasks

009                                           Abstract
010
011
              Post-training, particularly reinforcement learning (RL) using self-play-
012
              generated data, has become a new learning paradigm for large language
013
              models (LLMs). However, scaling RL to develop a general reasoner remains
014           a research challenge, as existing methods focus on task-specific reasoning
015           without adequately addressing generalization across a broader range of tasks.
016           Moreover, unlike traditional RL with limited action space, LLMs operate
017           in an infinite space, making it crucial to search for valuable and diverse
018           strategies to solve problems effectively. To address this, we propose searching
019           within the action space on high-level abstract plans to enhance model gener-
020           alization and introduce Critical Plan Step Learning (CPL), comprising: 1)
021           searching on plan, using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to explore diverse
022
              plan steps in multi-step reasoning tasks, and 2) learning critical plan steps
              through Step-level Advantage Preference Optimization (Step-APO), which
023
              integrates advantage estimates for step preference, obtained via MCTS, into
024
              Direct Preference Optimization (DPO). This combination helps the model
025           effectively learn critical plan steps, enhancing both reasoning capabilities
026           and generalization. Experimental results demonstrate that our method,
027           trained exclusively on GSM8K and MATH, not only significantly improves
028           performance on GSM8K (+10.5%) and MATH (+6.5%), but also enhances
029           out-of-domain reasoning benchmarks, such as HumanEval (+12.2%), GPQA
030           (+8.6%), ARC-C (+4.0%), MMLU-STEM (+2.2%), and BBH (+1.8%). The
031           code is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/CPL.

      1   Introduction
036
037   Large language models (LLMs) have achieved significant success through scaling, particularly
038   in pre-training on vast datasets (OpenAI et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024). Recently, there has
039
      been an increasing focus on scaling post-training, especially through reinforcement learning
      (RL) on self-play-generated data, which has emerged as a new learning paradigm for LLMs.
040
      Notably, OpenAI’s o1 (OpenAI, 2024) has consistently improved its reasoning abilities
041
      through large-scale RL, which teaches the model to think more productively. Additionally,
042   recent research works (Xie et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024) leverage Monte
043   Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) (Kocsis & Szepesvári, 2006) to iteratively collect preference data.
044   RL on this self-generated data facilitates iterative self-improvement, leading to significantly
045   enhanced reasoning capabilities.
046
      However, scaling RL to develop a general reasoner remains a research challenge. Traditional
047
      RL methods, such as AlphaGo (Silver et al., 2016), struggle with generalization due to
048   their specific action spaces. Existing approaches for LLMs primarily focus on enhancing
049   task-specific or domain-specific reasoning capabilities, such as in mathematics or coding.
050   While this has led to significant improvements in these specific tasks, it has not adequately
051   addressed the model’s generalization abilities across various reasoning tasks. Furthermore,
052   unlike traditional RL, which operates in a limited action space, LLMs function within a
053   vast search space. This expansive scope, combined with the high inference latency of LLMs,
      limits both the diversity and quality of explored reasoning paths.

071
      To enhance generalization in reasoning tasks, we propose searching within the action space
072   on high-level abstract plans, rather than focusing on task-specific action spaces that often
073   limit generalization. Building on previous work (Wang et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023; Hao
074   et al., 2023) that uses LLMs to generate both reasoning plans and task-specific solutions
075   to boost reasoning capabilities, we argue that task-specific solutions—like mathematical
076   formulas, code or symbolic solutions—are closely tied to task-specific skills. In contrast,
077   plans represent abstract thinking for problem-solving, such as determining which knowledge
078   to apply or how to break down a problem, helping models develop broader, task-agnostic
079   abilities that improve generalization (illustrated in Figure 2 Left).
080   Furthermore, under the challenge of a vast search space of reasoning paths, we propose that
081   maintaining diversity and identifying critical paths are essential for solving complex problems.
082   Plan-based search enables better exploration of high-level strategies and can achieve better
083   diversity; whereas, solutions-based search may limit diversity, as different solutions may
084   share the same underlying thought. Besides, existing preference-learning methods, such as
085   Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023) faces challenges in learning
086
      critical steps due to their inability to capture fine-grained supervision. Recent works propose
      Step-level DPO (Setlur et al., 2024; Lai et al., 2024) to learn step-level preferences, but
087
      its reliance on heuristics, such as marking the first error step as dispreferred, limits full
088
      exploration of the search space and model optimization. To address this, we propose a
089   method to identify and learn critical plan steps that provide higher advantages for improving
090   the model’s reasoning ability (illustrated in Figure 2 Right).
091
092
      Thus, we introduce Critical Plan Step Learning (CPL), which consists of two key components:
093   1. Searching on plan, specifically, we devise a step-by-step plan to solve the problem, with
094   the final step providing the full solution based on the plan. Using MCTS to explores diverse
095   plan steps in multi-step reasoning tasks, it creates a plan tree, where high-quality plan step
096   preferences are derived from the final outcome. This process enables the exploration of
097   high-level strategies, helping the model acquire task-agnostic skills and improve generalization
098
      across different tasks.
099   2. Learning critical plan steps through Step-level Advantage Preference Optimization (Step-
100   APO), which builds upon DPO. Step-APO integrates advantage estimates for step-level
101   preference pairs obtained via MCTS, enabling the model to learn fine-grained preferences
102   between steps, identify critical plan steps, and de-emphasize erroneous ones.
103   To conclude, our contributions are: 1) We explore the scaling problem in RL and propose
104   searching within the action space on high-level abstract plans to enhance model generalization,
105   rather than focusing on task-specific action spaces that often limit generalization. 2) We
106   introduce a novel approach CPL, which leverages MCTS to explore diverse plan steps,
107   distinguishing it from existing methods that focus on exploring solutions, and uses our Step-
      APO to learn step-level plan preferences, thereby helping the model effectively identify and

      In this section, we introduce our Critical Plan Step Learning (CPL), it boosts model
      performance via iterative process over plan-based search and step-level preference learning.
139
      We first introduce our plan-based MCTS, which enables the LLM to explore diverse plan
140
      strategies in the vast search space. Next, we present our Step-APO in detail to further
141   explore the potential of step-level preference learning in multi-step reasoning task. Finally,
142   we describe how we iteratively optimize the policy model and value model.
143
144
145   2.1        Plan-based MCTS
146
147
      MCTS builds a reasoning tree iteratively and autonomously explores step-level reasoning
      traces, which can be used to optimize LLMs. Existing methods (Chen et al., 2024; Xie et al.,
148
      2024) that leverage MCTS to collect data for training usually focus on exploring solution
149
      steps within the entire search space or on simultaneously exploring both plans and solutions.
150   To improve transfer performance across a broader range of reasoning tasks, we propose
151   learning high-level abstract plans, which enables the model to acquire more task-agnostic
152   capabilities and thereby achieve better generalization. We first create a step-by-step plan to
153   solve the problem, with the final step presenting the full solution and final answer based on
154   the plan. The prompt is provided in the Appendix A. Ultimately, we obtain a plan tree and
155   high-quality plan step supervision through iterative search with MCTS.
156   Specifically, given the plan tree T , each node represents a state st , and each edge represents
157   an action at , which corresponds to a reasoning step that leads to the next state st+1 . Under
158   the same parent node, different sibling nodes form a set of step-level preference pairs, with
159   each node having its own value V (st ) representing the expected future reward under state st .
160   These values can be obtained through the MCTS process, which involves four key operations:
161   selection, expansion, evaluation, and backup. To enhance efficiency, we use a value model to
      assess the expected returns from the partial reasoning paths, with the final integration of

182   2.2     Step-APO to Learn Critical Plan Steps
183
      Unlike mainstream approaches (Hwang et al., 2024; Lai et al., 2024) that learn step-level
184
      preferences by identifying the first error step and sampling a corresponding preferred step,
185
      while potentially yielding more accurate preferences, this method lacks sufficient exploration
186   of the vast reasoning trace space. Given the large variations in advantage differences
187   across different data pairs, we propose Step-APO, which introduces advantage estimates
188   for preference pairs into DPO. This enables the model to more effectively learn critical
189   intermediate plan steps, thereby further improving its reasoning capabilities. Next, We will
190   provide its derivation and analysis from the perspective of its gradient.

      2.3    Iterative Training of Policy and Value Model
271
272   Our approach employs iterative training for policy and value models. Our policy model
273   πθ and value model vϕ are two separate models, both adapted from the same base model.
274   We add a value head for the value model, which is randomly initialized in the first round.
275   However, as the MCTS simulations proceed in the first round, rewards from terminal nodes
276   are back-propagated to the intermediate nodes, reducing the negative impact of the random
277   value initialization.
278   For policy model training, we first supervised fine-tune (SFT) it using collected correct
279   paths from MCTS, then apply our Step-APO (eq. (12)) using step-level preference data

289   3     Experiments
290
291   3.1    Implementation Details
292
293   We iteratively generate data through MCTS and train our policy and value models in two
294   rounds. In each round, the policy model generates plan steps and final solution steps by
295   employing MCTS to address the given problem. The value model is utilized to assist in
296
      evaluating the intermediate steps during MCTS. At the end of each round, the generated
      data is used to train both the policy model and the value model.
297
298   Model Architecture We employ the DeepSeekMathBase-7B (Shao et al., 2024) as our
299   initial policy model and add a randomly initialized value head to this model, serving as the
300   initial value model. We then optimize these two distinct models independently and utilize
301   the updated models for the next round of data generation.
302   Datasets We construct our training data using the training sets of GSM8K (Cobbe et al.,
303   2021) and MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021b) datasets. GSM8K comprises 7,473 training and
304   1,319 test problems, while MATH includes 7,500 training and 5,000 test problems. From
305   these datasets, we exclusively extracted question-answer pairs from the training sets of
306   GSM8K and MATH, omitting the human-annotated solution. This resulted in a total of 15k
307   question-answer pairs for our training data.
308   Training Data Generation via MCTS For each problem, we employ MCTS to generate
309   multiple step-level plans and final solutions. During the MCTS expansion phase, we expanded
310   5 child nodes for the root node and 3 child nodes for other nodes. The search is conducted
311   with a maximum depth of 6. We apply a temperature of 0.7 to encourage diverse generation.
312   In the first round, we generate data from a subset of 5k question-answer pairs, consisting of
313   4k from the MATH and 1k from GSM8K, for efficiency. We carefully design prompts and
314   2-shot demonstrations to guide the model’s output, see Appendix A for details. We perform
315   a large number of MCTS simulations, specifically 200, in this phase to mitigate the impact
316   of the random initialization of the value model. Starting from the second round, with the
317   fine-tuned models from the first round, we utilize the full set of 15k question-answer pairs
318   for data generation. A 2-shot prompt formatted in XML is used, and we perform 100 MCTS
319   simulations.
320   Training Data Construction We utilize the state value V of each node in MCTS to
321   construct preference data. For plan step preference data, we categorize sibling nodes as
322   “preferred” if their value is greater than 0 and “dispreferred” if their value is less than 0,
323   forming preference pairs from any combination. For the final solution step data, we randomly
      select one preference pair for each parent node to construct the dataset. This is based on our


                                                            6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      experimental findings that an excess of solution data can negatively impact the performance
325   on out-of-domain reasoning tasks, whereas increasing the emphasis on plan data improves
326   performance in both mathematical and other reasoning tasks (see subsection 3.5). We list
327   the statistics for the generated data in two rounds in Appendix B.
328
      Training Details For the policy model, we first randomly select up to four correct responses
329
      per problem for supervised fine-tuning (SFT). Next, we employ step-level preference data
330
      from MCTS to train the model with our Step-APO algorithm. For the value model, we
331   use state value V from MCTS for partial responses as labels to update the model. This
332   allows the value model to score both partial plans and complete responses. The training
333   hyperparameters are provided in Appendix D. Notably, because the value difference for final
334   solution step preference pairs is 2, while the average value difference for other plan steps
335   ranges between 0.6 and 0.8, we apply a scaling factor of 0.3 to the values of solution steps in
336   Step-APO. In the second round of training, we utilize the data from the second round to
337   train the base model, rather than the Round 1 model.

497   4   Related Work
498
499   Search-Guided Reasoning in LLMs Recent advancements (Feng et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
500   2024; Xie et al., 2024) in enhancing LLM reasoning capabilities have focused on integrating
501   Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to collect trajectories and train models, resulting in
502   notable improvements in reasoning tasks. MCTS strikes a balance between exploration and
503   exploitation, utilizing its look-ahead ability to obtain high-quality step-level supervision.
504   For example, AlphaMath (Chen et al., 2024) employs MCTS to automatically generate
505   process supervision, leading to significant improvements in mathematical reasoning. However,
506   these MCTS-based training methods face challenges such as vast search spaces, limited
507
      solution diversity for LLMs. Furthermore, there is limited research on how these methods
      generalize to other reasoning tasks and enhance overall reasoning capabilities. To address
508
      these issues, we propose a method for searching over plan steps and learning critical plan
509
      steps for problem-solving, which aims to enhance generalization in reasoning tasks.
510
511   Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) Algorithms DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) uses
512   instance-level preference data for model optimization but has notable limitations. It struggles
513
      with multi-step reasoning tasks because it cannot effectively correct specific errors within
      the reasoning process (Hwang et al., 2024). Moreover, training on model-generated positive
514
      data can amplify spurious correlations from incorrect intermediate steps, leading to poor
515
      generalization (Setlur et al., 2024). Recent work proposes step-level DPO (Setlur et al., 2024;
516   Lai et al., 2024) to address these issues by providing the fine-grained error identification
517   needed for improving reasoning capabilities. For example, Self-Explore (Hwang et al., 2024)
518   identifies the first incorrect step in a solution and constructs step-level preference data to
519   guide model improvement. Unlike these heuristic methods, we propose Step-APO to fully
520   explore the step-level search space and achieve the maximum optimization potential.
521

523
      5   Conclusion
524
525
      Scaling RL to develop a general reasoner remains an open and important research question.
      In this work, we explore the scaling problem in RL and propose searching within the action
526
      space on high-level abstract plans to enhance model generalization, rather than focusing on
527
      task-specific action spaces that often limit generalization. Additionally, we introduce CPL,
528   which uses plan-based search and finer-grained learning of plan step preferences to enable the
529   model to identify critical plan steps within the reasoning trace, thereby enhancing its overall
530   reasoning ability. Ultimately, CPL successfully improves transfer performance in various
531   out-of-domain tasks and offers valuable contributions to future research in RL scaling.
532
      In future work, we will expand our current plan strategy beyond the option to continue to the
533
      next reasoning step. We will consider more diverse planning strategies, such as self-correction
534   and the exploration of new ideas to solve problems. Additionally, test-time search has been
535   shown to significantly enhance the model’s reasoning capabilities on complex problems. We
536   could combine test-time compute with our training-time compute. Utilizing our value model
537   for test-time search has the potential to further improve the model’s reasoning performance,
538   and we will explore this in future work. Furthermore, test-time search in a vast action space
539   poses high latency issues. We will continue to investigate how to effectively learn critical
      steps for problem-solving to enable efficient search.

956   step-level preference learning. Left: Example of an MCTS-generated plan tree, exploring diverse
957   planning strategies in the vast search space. CPL generates step-by-step plans, which lead to the
      final solution and answer. State value V is updated via a bottom-up reward propagation from
958
      the terminal node to the root, and used to assign preferences. Right: Step-level preferences from

963   Search on High-Level Abstract Plan. We propose leveraging search on high-level
964   abstract plans, expressed in natural language, as a universal interface across tasks. These
965   plans represent abstract thinking for problem-solving, such as determining which knowledge
966   to apply or how to decompose a problem, enabling models to develop broader, task-agnostic
967   capabilities that enhance generalization (See Figure 2 Left).
968   Integrating Plan into MCTS. We design a carefully crafted two-shot prompt (See Ap-
969   pendix A) to guide the model in answering questions in two parts: (1) step-by-step plans,
970   and (2) detailed implementation. In the MCTS search tree, each non-terminal node stores a
971   single plan step, while terminal nodes store the detailed implementation for all preceding
      plan steps (See Figure 4 Left).

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       MCTS consists of the following phases:
973
974          • Selection: We use the PUCT algorithm to iteratively select nodes in the search
975            tree, which leverages the LLMs’ generation probability to guide the selection process,
976            reducing the selection of unreasonable nodes.
977          • Expansion: In this phase, we expand selected node by using the current states as
978            the prompt, which contain the question and all previously generated responses, to
979            guide the model in generating the next plan step or the final detailed implementation.
980          • Evaluation: In this phase, we evaluate the value of newly expanded nodes. The
981            evaluation process differs for non-terminal and terminal nodes:
982
                   – For non-terminal nodes (plan steps), unlike traditional MCTS which typically
983
                     uses simulations, we employ a value model to evaluate the node’s value for
984                  efficiency.
985
                   – For terminal nodes (detailed implementation), we parse the answer in the
986                  detailed implementation, compare it to the ground truth, and assign a value
987                  based on correctness.
988
             • Backup: We backpropagate the value of newly expanded nodes to update the search
989
               tree.
990
991    Optimizing the Policy and Value Models. After each round of MCTS, as shown in
992    Figure 4 Right, we optimize the policy model using SFT and the Step-APO algorithm.
993    Additionally, we optimize the value model using Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss to ensure
994    its outputs are closer to the state value V of each node in the MCTS search tree.

1016   Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) Algorithms Recent work (Rafailov et al.,
1017   2024; Zeng et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2024) provide a token-wise Markov decision process
1018   (MDP) formulation for Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF). Rafailov
1019   et al. (2024) derives DPO in a token-level MDP and demonstrates that DPO can be viewed
1020
       as an inverse Q-learning algorithm. Zeng et al. (2024) improves DPO by extending it to the
       token level and incorporating forward KL divergence constraints for each token. Zhong et al.
1021
       (2024) models RLHF problems as a token-wise MDP, introducing the RTO algorithm, which
1022
       learns a token-level reward function from preference data and performs policy optimization
1023   based on this learned reward signal. Our Step-APO is based on step-level MDPs, which are
1024   better suited for capturing complex reasoning steps in reasoning tasks, whereas token-level
1025   MDPs focus more on each word choice during the generation process.
