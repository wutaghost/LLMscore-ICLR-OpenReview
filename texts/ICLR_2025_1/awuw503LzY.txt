009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Vision-Language Models are trained on very large, minimally curated image
012            datasets that contain many spurious correlations between categories and visual
013            patterns. This causes VLMs to learn shortcuts, e.g., between smiling and gender.
014            Although logos are ubiquitous in VLM training data and are a potential source
015            of such shortcuts, there is very limited study of this issue. Prior work pointed
016
               out that logos may indeed cause such problems, but the analysis was limited to
               a single text-based logo. In this paper, we undertake a broad study of logos in
017
               VLM training data and their potential to insert “hidden” spurious correlations into
018
               VLMs. We construct a new logo dataset, CC12M-LogoBank, propose an algorithm
019            that uncovers spurious logos affecting a given VLM prediction task, and test it on
020            several representative tasks: person attribute classification, object classification,
021            and harmful content detection. Our key finding is that some logos indeed lead to
022            spurious incorrect predictions, for example, adding the Adidas logo to a photo of a
023            person causes a model classify the person as “greedy”. Furthermore, we argue that
024            the uncovered logos can be seen as effective attacks against foundational models;
025            for example, an attacker could place a spurious logo on harmful content, causing
026            the model to misclassify it as harmless. This threat is alarming considering the
027
               simplicity of logo attacks, increasing the attack surface of VLM models. As a
               defense, we explore two effective yet simple mitigation strategies that seamlessly
028
               integrate with zero-shot inference of foundation models.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Online image content is inundated with logos, from advertisements and social media posts to website
034   branding and product placements. Thus, many of these logos are present in the vast amounts of
035   web-scraped data (e.g. Conceptual Caption 12M (Changpinyo et al., 2021) or LAION (Schuhmann
036   et al., 2021)) used to pretrain Vision-Language Models, such as CLIP and LLaVA (Radford et al.,
037   2021; Liu et al., 2024). This raises a possibly serious problem: web-scale datasets might encode
038   spurious correlations between logos and various visual concepts of interest. Although it does not
039
      focus specifically on logos, recent work (Li et al., 2023) briefly explores the effect of one watermark
      (Chinese text) and its association with one ImageNet object (carton), which leads the model to
040
      missclassify images where the text appears alongside other objects. This narrow focus overlooks the
041
      broader distribution of non-text graphic symbols and their impact on other important multi-modal
042   content analysis tasks, such as content moderation.
043
044   Therefore, in this work, we aim to study if logos may unexpectedly affect the predictions of large
045
      vision-language models. Specifically, we seek to identify and analyze a broader range of spurious
      logo effects, beyond just text-based logos, to uncover cases where model behavior is misled in
046
      ways that are not easily anticipated. For example, models may incorrectly link logos of brands or
047
      government agencies to unrelated content or sentiments. A major challenge in studying spurious
048   logos is that curated downstream datasets rarely contain the diverse range of logos found in web-scale
049   data. We address this issue by leveraging the insight that logos are usually pasted onto images as
050   illustrated in Figure 1, allowing us to programatically introduce logos into diverse visual contexts and
051   observe model responses. Specifically, our algorithm searches a new logo bank, CC12M-LogoBank,
052   to identify logos that spuriously correlate with various visual concepts T in downstream datasets,
053   ranging from objects (e.g., Traffic Light) to abstract concepts (e.g. Greedy). We show the effectiveness
      of our process on three representative tasks. First, we mine for logos that content moderation systems

096   To summarize our contributions:
097   • We develop an approach that mines a novel logo bank, CC12M-LogoBank, for spurious logos that

099     ImageNet Classification.
100   • We formally define an alarmingly accessible threat model that uses the uncovered logos as effective
101     attacks against vision-language models.
102   • We explore two simple yet effective tools for mitigating spurious logos, Cropping and Masking,
103     which seamlessly integrate with zero-shot inference of vision-language models.

131
132   In this work, we study how vision-language models might spuriously correlate logos with various
133   visual concepts. Formally, given an input image X and a visual concept T , we can partition the image
134   into two components: Xt and Xs , where Xt denotes the visual signal that is required to correctly
135   predict T , while Xs denotes the spurious signal embedded in the logo. The issue of spurious logos
136
      arises due to the co-occurrence of the spurious information in a logo with the visual concept T . For
      example, Figure 1 (B) shows how a vision-language model mistakes the red, green, and yellow colors
137
      in an organization logo for a traffic light, causing the model to ignore the parking meter and thus
138
      misclassifying the image.
139
140   To comprehensively study the spurious effect of logos, we develop a semi-automatic mining process
141   to uncover logos that models spuriously correlate with a given visual concept ranging from General
142
      Purpose Classification (e.g. Traffic Light) to Content Moderation (e.g. Harmless) (Section 3.1).
      Furthermore, we propose a set of effective tools to mitigate the spurious effect of logos against
143
      vision-language models (Section 3.2). Finally we motivate how the mined logos could be used as
144
      easily accessible yet effective adversarial attacks by malicious actors to disrupt Vision-Language
145   Models (Section 3.3)

      3.1   A M INING P ROCESS FOR U NCOVERING S PURIOUS L OGOS
148
149
      A naive solution to study the impact of spurious logos on vision-language models is to use existing
150   downstream datasets. However, curated and cleaned downstream datasets (e.g. ImageNet (Deng et al.,
151   2009)) do not usually contain logos. An alternative solution is to collect new datasets with new target
152   labels and labels that indicate when logos are present. However, collecting and labeling new data
153   is costly and needs to be repeated for each specific task. Instead, we leverage the fact that logos
154   are often encountered in real-world settings by simply being pasted onto images, such as in online
155   advertisements, product packaging, or media banners. This approach reflects a common way logos
156   appear in the wild, often in predictable locations like the corners of images. While logos can also
157   appear “naturally,” such as printed on clothing or integrated into physical environments, these cases
158
      involve more complex visual contexts and are not the focus of this work. Our method focuses on the
      simpler but frequent scenario of pasted logos, which allows for easier and more consistent integration
159
      into downstream datasets.
160
161   Using this observation, we motivate a new mechanism that mines for spurious logos. The first step in
      the mechanism (Section 3.1.1) involves curating a comprehensive logo bank CC12M-LogoBank. The

243   3.2   M ITIGATING S PURIOUS L OGOS
244
245   In this section, we outline two mitigation tools uniquely designed to mitigate the spurious effect of
246   logos. We focus on test-time solutions that easily generalize to zero-shot vision-language models and
247   require no extra data or training. We outline the tools below.
248   Tool 1: Cropping Augmentations. A strongly spurious signal Xs dominates the classifier logits
249   which overrides the non-spurious signal Xt resulting in an incorrect prediction (Kirichenko et al.,
250   2023). However, compared to other confounding spurious signals (e.g. background or protected
251   attributes like Race and Gender), logos usually take up a small and self contained part of the image.
252   We use this observation to motivate using a standard ten crop augmentation strategy used with early
253   vision models like AlexNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012) to diffuse the spurious effect of the logos. The
254
      augmentation generates five crops from the image (one from each corner and one from the center),
      then flips the image and repeats the process, resulting in 10 crops. The final prediction is computed
255
      by averaging the logits across these 10 crops. More formally, given a model M , a 10-Crop function

266   3.3   L OGOS AS ATTACKS
267
268   In this Section, we argue that the logos found by our mining process outlined in Section 3.1 could
269   be viewed as a model attack vector. This is because when models exhibit spurious behavior due to
      a spurious signal Xs , they are more likely to misclassify an image if it contains Xs (Sagawa et al.,


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      2020). Moreover, as we discuss in Section 3.1, a unique property of logos is that they could be
271   programmatically inserted into an image without distorting the visual content of the image (e.g. paste
272   on the corner). Given both of these points, the uncovered logos could be viewed as attacks. Indeed, a
273   malicious actor could simply use our process to mine for logos that spuriously correlate with T and
274   in turn reduce the model accuracy. Below, we outline the threat model for the malicious attacker.
275
      Threat Model: A malicious actor that uses our process to mine for logos that disrupt recognizing
276
      a visual concept T needs to be able to query the vision-language model M . More importantly, the
277   actor does not need any background in working dynamics of the vision-language models (e.g. model
278   gradients). This makes the attacks accessible to a wide pool of malicious actors, further highlighting
279   the threat of the attacks.

      5     C ONCLUSION
525
526
      We studied the spurious effect of a wide distribution of logos on vision-langauge models through
527
      a novel mining process. We demonstrated how our process could find logos (a) that bias Content
528   Moderation System (Hateful Meme Classification) to predict harmful content as harmless (b) that
529   VL models spuriously correlate with certain classes in ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) and, as a result,
530   reduce the model’s accuracy and (c) that bias VL models association of human photos toward negative
531   adjectives (e.g. Greedy). Furthermore, we defined a widely accessible threat model that outlined how
532   our mined logos could be used as attacks. To defend against these attacks, we explored two effective
533   mitigation tools: cropping and masking, which seamlessly integrate with zero-shot inference.
534   Limitations and Future Work. While we showed that our process could mine for logos on three
535   diverse visual recognition tasks, it would be interesting for future work to see if spurious logos can
536   generalize to tasks beyond recognition, such as captioning. Moreover, a notable limitation of our
537   work is that vision-language Models that are retrained on updated datasets may capture different
538   correlations with logos as the internet evolves. The contextual usage of logos can shift over time due
539   to new marketing campaigns, changes in public perception, or emerging trends. This dynamic nature
      means that the associations identified in our study may not remain consistent over time.
