009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In-context Learning (ICL) is a popular approach to filling Large Language Models
012            (LLMs) with the context without fine-tuning. ICL works by feeding the test input
013            along with the context information selected from the candidate dataset as examples
014            of explaining the target task and getting the answer. In real-world applications,
015            noisy samples are easily to be included in the datasets, so it is unavoidable that
016            the candidate set might also contain noise caused by human or measurement
017            errors. The effectiveness of ICL is highly dependent on the quality of the selected
018            ICL samples: the noise in the candidate set can mislead the query answer and
019
               severely degrade the ICL performance. However, the noise ICL problem is largely
               overlooked. To tackle this challenge, in this paper, we propose Context Distribution
020
               Shift (ConDS), which iteratively revises the distribution of the candidate dataset
021
               so that the retrieved ICL samples are emphasized to improve the robustness of
022            ICL. Specifically, we first identify the clean and informative samples based on the
023            retriever ranking score and the feedback from the LLMs, and then augment the
024            identified informative samples. A subsampling strategy is adopted to emphasize
025            the importance of informative samples and reduce the ratio of noisy samples. Thus,
026            ICL’s reliability can be improved by reducing the catastrophic impact of noisy
027            samples on almost all test queries to a small percentage. Our ConDS can be easily
028            combined with existing off-the-shelf and fine-tuned retrievers. An analysis is also
029            provided to reveal the relationship between ConDS and retrievers. Experimental
030
               results show that ConDS outperforms baselines on various tasks under the influence
               of noise by a large margin of 8.12%.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large language models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Team et al., 2023)
037   have exhibited remarkable capabilities across a range of natural language processing and reasoning
038   tasks (Wang et al., 2018; 2019). However, directly applying these LLMs to specific tasks can be
039
      challenging without task-specific adaptations due to the computational challenges of fine-tuning their
      vast number of trainable parameters. In-Context Learning (ICL) (Dong et al., 2022) represents a
040
      prominently efficient and effective way to utilize LLMs. Essentially, ICL operates by presenting
041
      LLMs with a set of selected ICL examples relevant to the test query from the candidate dataset C,
042   preconditioning the models for the target task.
043
044   However, a real-world dataset, including the candidate set collected for ICL, can easily contain noisy
045
      samples. Given the critical dependence of ICL on the label quality of selected samples (Kossen et al.,
      2024; Wei et al., 2023), noise within the candidate set can significantly distort responses for the
046
      query from LLMs. Surprisingly, the issue of noise in ICL remains largely overlooked. There are two
047
      categories of existing ICL methods targeted at clean ICL settings. One adopts off-the-shelf retrievers
048   (such as sparse retriever BM25 (Robertson et al., 2009) and dense retriever (Rubin et al., 2021)) to
049   calculate the similar score between the given query and the candidate samples, and then retrieve ICL
050   samples with the highest similarity scores. Another kind is fine-tuned retrievers trained on the specific
051   tasks using the candidate datasets such as PromptPG (Lu et al., 2022), EPR (Rubin et al., 2021), etc.
052   However, without additional treatments, the noisy samples are easily included in the ICL sample set
053   retrieved by these methods, which finally misleads the query answer. Therefore, developing strategies
      to mitigate noisy information in the ICL candidate set becomes imperative.

      To tackle this challenge, instead of developing a new retriever, we focus on directly improving the
      quality of the candidate dataset by shifting its original distribution. Generally, clean samples are
079
      often informative for certain queries, while noisy samples can be mostly misleading. Our intuition is
080
      to augment the candidate set to increase the probability of informative samples being selected and
081   decrease the probability of misleading samples being selected for test queries. To achieve this goal,
082   we have to solve the following questions: How to identify informative samples? How to change the
083   distribution of the candidate sets?
084
      To solve these two questions, in this paper, we propose Context Distribution Shift (ConDS), which
085
      seeks to revise the distribution of the candidate dataset to improve the robustness of ICL. The
086
      framework of ConDS is shown in Figure 1. We split the candidate set into a training set and a
087   validation set. We then identify informative samples in the training set based on the retriever ranking
088   score of the chosen ICL samples and the LLM’s feedback on the validation samples. The samples
089   with positive feedback from LM will be considered informative samples and will be augmented. The
090   augmentation strength is decided by the retriever ranking score. A subsampling strategy is adopted
091   after augmentation to reinforce the importance of informative samples and decrease the influence
092   of noisy samples. We iterate over all validation samples and repeat this process for a few epochs
093   to renew the distribution. According to the experimental results on various tasks under the noise
094   influence, our ConDS significantly outperformed baselines under different settings by decreasing the
095
      percentage of test queries affected by noisy samples from all to a small portion.
096   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
097
098    • We propose ConDS, which improves the quality of the candidate set by not only emphasizing
099      informative samples but also reducing the impact of noisy label samples. We are the first to
100      investigate the power of distribution shift of the candidate set to improve the ICL performance.
101    • ConDS supports different kinds of off-the-shelf and fine-tuned retrievers to enhance their ro-
102      bustness against noisy samples. We also provide an analysis to reveal the essential commonality
103      between ConDS and the existing retrievers.
104    • Extensive experimental results on various benchmarks show that ConDS is robust to the noisy
105      candidate dataset and significantly outperformed the baselines.

135               QUERIES
136
137   For real world applications, noisy samples are easily to be included in the datasets, so it is unavoidable
138   that the candidate set collected for ICL might also contain noise. The effectiveness of the ICL is highly
139   dependent on the quality of the candidate sets from which the in-context samples are drawn. The
140
      existence of the noisy samples in the candidate sets can significantly bring down the ICL performance
      even with a small noise ratio for various retrievers as shown in Figure 2 on 6 different datasets with
141
      8-shots.
142
143   According to the t-SNE visualization of embedding in Figure 3a, the clean and noisy samples are
144   mixed in the candidate set. During ICL, the retriever tends to select similar samples to the query as
145   the ICL samples. With mixed clean and noisy samples, sampling similar samples using the retriever
146
      easily includes both clean and noisy samples for almost all query samples. The noisy samples with a
      false label can easily mislead the answer to the query, which leads to a significant performance drop,
147
      as shown in Figure 2.
148
149   Our intuition is to augment the candidate sets with more informative samples by adjusting the
150   distribution of the candidate set. In most cases, clean samples are often informative for certain queries,
151   while noisy samples can be mostly misleading and degrade the ICL performance. Our goal is to
152
      augment the neighbors of queries with more clean samples, increasing the retriever’s probability of
      selecting clean samples instead of noisy ones. This intuition motivates the main challenges to be
153
      solved in this paper: How to identify informative samples? How to change the distribution of the
154
      candidate sets?

301   4.1   E XPERIMENT SETTING
302
303   Datasets. We conduct experiments on a wide range of tasks: 1) Sentiment Classification: SST-2,
304
      SST-5 (Socher et al., 2013), MR (Pang & Lee, 2005), CR (Kim Amplayo et al., 2022); 2) Topic
      Classification: AGNews (Zhang et al., 2015), TREC (Voorhees & Tice, 2000); 3) natural language
305
      inference: MNLI (Williams et al., 2017), RTE (Bar-Haim et al., 2014); 4) Subjectivity Classification:
306
      Subj (Pang & Lee, 2004).
307   Baselines. We compare our method with the following baselines: 1) Zero-shot: Only the test query is
308   fed into the LLM, 0 ICL sample is selected. 2) Random: We randomly sample ICL samples from
309   the candidate set. 3) BM25 (Robertson et al., 2009) is an off-the-shelf sparse retriever. Given a test
310   query, BM25 can retrieve the most relevant samples from the candidate set with a similar input as
311   the test query. 4) KNN (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) utilizes the Sentence-BERT as the off-the-shelf
312   dense demonstration retriever. It uses “paraphrase-mpnet-basev2” (Rubin et al., 2021) to encode the
313   test query and candidate set’s inputs. The examples with the most similar input are selected as the
314   ICL samples. 5) DPP (Chen et al., 2018) uses BERT-based embedding and adopts MAP inference
315
      for retrieving relevant samples from the candidate set. 6) PromptPG (Lu et al., 2022) is a fine-tuned
      retriever, which utilizes policy gradient to fine-tune a BERT-based (Devlin et al., 2018) retriever to
316
      learn to select ICL samples for the test query.
317
      Noise setting. We inject noise in the ICL Database by coin tossing with probability p. Any sample
318   in the original dataset has a probability of p being changed to another false label. p is set to 0.6 by
319   default.
320   Implementation. We adopt GPT-Neo-2.7B (Black et al., 2021) as the inference LLM by default.
321   The shot number is K = 20, and the candidate set size is 200 by default. For fine-tuned retrievers,
322   we randomly split 10% of the candidate data as C valid . Training epochs are set as 5, the learning
323   rate is 1e − 4, the augmentation parameter α is 1000, and the augmentation method is set as direct
      duplication unless otherwise mentioned.

501   5    R ELATED WORK
502
503   In-context learning. One mainstream of ICL (Dong et al., 2022) utilizes off-the-shelf retrievers,
504   which are classified into two types: sparse and dense retrievers. BM25 (Agrawal et al., 2022) is
505   a sparse retriever that uses term-frequency scores to measure the relationship between a query
506   and in-context example. The main weakness of this sparse retriever is understanding semantic
507   information. To address this issue, dense retrievers have been adopted by employing neural networks
508   to comprehend the meaning of sentences rather than individual words. Liu et al. (2021) utilize a
509   BERT model to build a KNN-based retriever. Another main category is to fine-tune a prompt retriever
510   to select examples on specific tasks. Rubin et al. (2022) train an efficient retriever that uses positive
511
      and negative information from the dense passage retriever (Karpukhin et al., 2020). UDR (Li et al.,
      2023) proposes a universal retriever applicable across various domain tasks. PromptPG (Lu et al.,
512
      2022) employs a reinforcement learning framework to train the retriever to find the most informative
513
      examples for answering. DATAMODELS (Chang & Jia, 2022) trains linear regressors according to
514   the example influence on the LLM prediction. LLM-R (Wang et al., 2023) trains retrievers using a
515   proposed reward model. Some works tried to retrieve ICL samples from other perspectives. Li & Qiu
516   (2023) chooses the most representative examples for all test cases by using contribution measures.
517   Xie et al. (2021) addresses the in-context learning problem by selecting appropriate sample problems
518   as an implicit Bayesian inference.
519
      Noisy dataset with ICL. Pan (2023) explore the embedded pre-trained knowledge in LLMs by
520
      substituting the label word with an arbitrary word, but they did not survey noisy (incorrect) labels.
521
      Kossen et al. (2024); Wei et al. (2023) examine the impact of noisy labels from the ICL perspective,
522   considering various factors such as the number of ICL samples and the model size of LLMs. However,
523   their investigation focuses on observing the phenomenon rather than proposing a solution to address
524   the issue. Cheng et al. (2024) discover that introducing label noises during the training of the LM
525   can improve the robustness of Transformers during ICL inference. However, training LLMs from
526   scratch takes a lot of time and computational resources, and is inapplicable for black-box LMs (eg,
527   GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020), GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023)). Hence, in this paper, we focus on a
528   black-box LLM setting and do not have access to the training of the LLM.
529
530   6    C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this paper, we propose ConDS to improve the robustness of in-context learning (ICL) against
533   noisy samples which has been reported in several researches. The main philosophy of the proposed
534   algorithm is a context set distribution shift method. Briefly, the algorithm works as follows. First,
535   ConDS identifies the informative samples based on the feedback from LLM and the ranking scores
536   from the retriever, and then augment these informative samples. A sub-sampling strategy is also
537   used to increase the probability of sampling clean samples and decrease the probability of sampling
538   noisy samples. ConDS can be flexibly combined with both off-the-shelf and fine-tuned retrievers.
539
      Experimental results for various tasks including classification and generative tasks under noise setting
      show that ConDS significantly outperformed baselines.

888   D    C ASE STUDY
889
890   We provide case studies on SST-2 and SST-5 by comparing different selected ICL samples using
891   PromptPG and PromptPG+ConDS in Table 9 and Table 10. As shown in these two tables, by
892
      applying ConDS, we increase the clean ratio in the selected ICL samples from 95% to 100% and
      from 35% to 95% for SST-2 and SST-5, respectively. For SST-2, although the selected ICL samples
893
      of PromptPG only include one noisy sample, the final prediction of LLM has been misled. By
894
      applying ConDS with simple duplication, we augment the most informative samples and filter out
895   the misleading ones. As we can observe from the tables, samples with similar answers as the query
896   question are more likely to be included in the selected sample set after distribution shift of the
897   candidate pool. These most informative samples correctly guide the final prediction of the LLM to
898   the right answer. Besides, if we conduct one more step to filter out the duplicated samples, we can
899   even cut the query token size with a correct prediction.
