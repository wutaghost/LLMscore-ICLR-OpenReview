009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Bayesian optimization has attracted huge attention from diverse research areas in
012            science and engineering, since it is capable of efficiently finding a global optimum
013            of an expensive-to-evaluate black-box function. In general, a probabilistic regres-
014            sion model is widely used as a surrogate function to model an explicit distribution
015            over function evaluations given an input to estimate and a training dataset. Be-
016            yond the probabilistic regression-based methods, density ratio estimation-based
017            Bayesian optimization has been suggested in order to estimate a density ratio of
018
               the groups relatively close and relatively far to a global optimum. Developing this
               line of research further, supervised classifiers are employed to estimate a class
019
               probability for the two groups instead of a density ratio. However, the supervised
020
               classifiers used in this strategy are prone to be overconfident for known knowl-
021            edge on global solution candidates. Supposing that we have access to unlabeled
022            points, e.g., predefined fixed-size pools, we propose density ratio estimation-based
023            Bayesian optimization with semi-supervised learning to solve this challenge. Fi-
024            nally, we show the empirical results of our methods and several baseline methods
025            in two distinct scenarios with unlabeled point sampling and a fixed-size pool and
026            analyze the validity of our proposed methods in diverse experiments.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Bayesian optimization (Brochu et al., 2010; Garnett, 2023) has attracted immense attention from
032   various research areas such as hyperparameter optimization (Bergstra et al., 2011), battery lifetime
033
      optimization (Attia et al., 2020), chemical reaction optimization (Shields et al., 2021), and nanopho-
      tonic structure optimization (Kim et al., 2024), since it is capable of efficiently finding a global opti-
034
      mum of an expensive-to-evaluate black-box function. As studied in previous literature on Bayesian
035
      optimization (Snoek et al., 2012; Martinez-Cantin et al., 2018; Springenberg et al., 2016; Hutter
036   et al., 2011), a probabilistic regression model, which can estimate a distribution of function evalu-
037   ations over inputs, is widely used as a surrogate function; A Gaussian process (GP) (Rasmussen &
038   Williams, 2006) is a predominant choice for the surrogate function. An analogy between probabilis-
039   tic regression models in Bayesian optimization is that they rely on an explicit function over function
040   evaluations p(y | x, D) given an input to estimate, denoted as x, and a training dataset D.
041
      Beyond the probabilistic regression-based Bayesian optimization, density ratio estimation (DRE)-
042   based Bayesian optimization has been studied recently (Bergstra et al., 2011; Tiao et al., 2021).
043   Furthermore, likelihood-free Bayesian optimization, which is equivalent to DRE-based Bayesian
044   optimization with a particular utility function, has been proposed by Song et al. (2022). Bergstra
045   et al. (2011) attempt to model two densities p(x | y ≤ y † , D) and p(x | y > y † , D), where
046   y † is a threshold for dividing inputs to two groups that are relatively close and relatively far to a
047   global solution, in order to estimate ζ-relative density ratio (Yamada et al., 2011). On the other
048   hand, instead of modeling two densities separately, Tiao et al. (2021); Song et al. (2022) estimate
049   a density ratio using class-probability estimation (Qin, 1998). As discussed in the previous work,
050
      this line of research provides a new understanding of Bayesian optimization, which allows us to
      solve Bayesian optimization using binary classification. Moreover, it can reduce the amount of
051
      computation required for building surrogate functions.
052
053   However, the supervised classifiers utilized in the DRE-based Bayesian optimization are prone to
      be overconfident for known knowledge on global solution candidates or a region that have been

064

065
066   To make use of semi-supervised classifiers, we take into account two distinct scenarios with un-

      iting methods in diverse experiments including synthetic benchmarks, Tabular Benchmarks (Klein
071
      & Hutter, 2019), NATS-Bench (Dong et al., 2021), and 64D minimum multi-digit MNIST search.

074   analyses on the components of DRE-BO-SSL in Section 6 and the appendices.

087   1.1   OVER - EXPLOITATION P ROBLEM
088
089   As illustrated in Figures 1 and 9, the supervised classifiers used in DRE-based Bayesian optimiza-
090   tion suffer from the over-exploitation problem. Interestingly, many deep learning models also share
091   a similar problem, which is called an overconfidence problem (Guo et al., 2017; Müller et al., 2019),
092   due to various reasons, but primarily due to overparameterization. It is noteworthy that the definition
093
      of the over-exploitation problem in DRE-based Bayesian optimization is different from the overcon-
      fidence problem in general classification. The definition in DRE-based Bayesian optimization does
094
      not imply that a single data point has a high probability for a particular class, but it implies that a few
095
      data points have high probabilities for a class of interest. More precisely, our definition indicates
096   the problem of overconfidence over known knowledge on global solution candidates. For example,
097   the definition in general classification includes a case that 50% of data points are biased to one class
098   and the remainder is biased to another class. On the contrary, this definition does not include such
099   a case and it only includes a case that a small number of data points or the small region of a search
100   space, which is colored in yellow in Figures 1 and 9, are biased to some particular class, i.e., Class
101   1 in the DRE-based Bayesian optimization problems.
102   By the aforementioned definition, at early iterations of Bayesian optimization, a supervised classi-
103   fier tends to overfit to a small size of Dt due to a relatively large model capacity. This consequence
104   makes a Bayesian optimization algorithm highly focus on exploitation. Similar to our observation,
105   the imbalance of exploration and exploitation in the DRE-based approaches is also discussed in the
106   recent work (Oliveira et al., 2022; Pan et al., 2024). Moreover, the consequence mentioned above
107   is different from the characteristics of probabilistic regression-based Bayesian optimization because
      the regression-based methods are capable of exploring unseen regions by dealing with uncertainties.

145   2             BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
146
147   Bayesian Optimization. It is a principled and efficient approach to finding a global solution of a
148   challenging objective, e.g., expensive-to-evaluate black-box functions (Brochu et al., 2010; Garnett,
149   2023). To focus on a probabilistic regression model as a surrogate function, we omit the details
150   of Bayesian optimization here; see (Brochu et al., 2010; Garnett, 2023) for details. In Bayesian
151
      optimization, GP regression (Rasmussen & Williams, 2006) is widely used as a surrogate func-
      tion (Srinivas et al., 2010; Snoek et al., 2012) because of its flexibility with minimum assumptions
152
      on model and smoothness. While GP is a probable choice, Bayesian optimization with diverse
153
      surrogates such as Student-t process regression (Martinez-Cantin et al., 2018), Bayesian neural net-
154   works (Springenberg et al., 2016), and tree-based models (Hutter et al., 2011; Kim & Choi, 2022)
155   has been proposed. An analogy between such models is that they model p(y | x, D) explicitly, so
156   that it can be used to define an acquisition function with the statistics of p(y | x, D).
157
158   Density-Ratio Estimation. Whereas knowing a data distribution p(x) is important, it is difficult
159   to directly estimate p(x) (Sugiyama et al., 2012). For specific machine learning problems such as
160   importance sampling (Kloek & van Dijk, 1978) and mutual information estimation (Bishop, 2006),
161   we can bypass direct density estimation and estimate a density ratio. More recently, Rhodes et al.
      (2020) tackle a density-chasm problem using telescopic density-ratio estimation, which replaces an

162
      original problem with a set of logistic regression problems. Since the work (Rhodes et al., 2020) suf-
163   fers from the issue on distribution shift, the recent work (Srivastava et al., 2023) proposes a density
164   ratio estimation method with multinomial logistic regression, which is capable of mitigating the dis-
165   tribution shift using multi-class classification. In Bayesian optimization, Bergstra et al. (2011) have
166   proposed a strategy with tree-structured Parzen estimators to estimate a density ratio as an alterna-
167   tive to probabilistic regression-based acquisition functions. In addition, the work (Tiao et al., 2021;
168   Song et al., 2022) suggests methods with binary classifiers in order to estimate class probabilities as
169   a density ratio; see Section 3 for details.

171   Semi-Supervised Learning. It is a learning scheme with both labeled and unlabeled data (Zhu,
172   2005; Chapelle et al., 2006; Bengio et al., 2006). To cooperate with labeled and unlabeled data,
173
      this strategy generally utilizes geometry of data points or connectivity between points, and assigns
      pseudo-labels to unlabeled data points, which referred to as transductive learning (Gammerman
174
      et al., 1998). As a semi-supervised learning method on a similarity graph, Zhu & Ghahramani
175
      (2002) propose a label propagation algorithm which iteratively propagates the labels of unlabeled
176   data points using labeled data. Zhou et al. (2003) compute the labels of labeled and unlabeled data
177   points by a weighted iterative algorithm with initial labels. Belkin & Niyogi (2002) predict pseudo-
178   labels by finding a linear combination of a few smallest eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian.

      3    DRE- BASED BAYESIAN O PTIMIZATION
182
      Unlike probabilistic regression-based Bayesian optimization, DRE-based Bayesian optimization
183
      employs a density ratio-based acquisition function, defined with a density p(x | y ≤ y † , Dt ), where
184
      x is a d-dimensional vector, y is its function evaluation, y † is a threshold, and Dt = {(xi , yi )}ti=0 is
185   a dataset of t + 1 pairs of data point and evaluation. In particular, the work by Bergstra et al. (2011)
186   defines an acquisition function based on ζ-relative density ratio (Yamada et al., 2011):
187
188                                                           p(x | y ≤ y † , Dt )
                            A(x | ζ, Dt ) =                                                   ,              (1)
189                                           ζp(x | y ≤ y † , Dt )+(1−ζ)p(x | y > y † , Dt )
190
      where ζ = p(y ≤ y † ) ∈ [0, 1) is a threshold ratio. We need to find a maximizer of (1), by
191
      optimizing the following composite function: h(p(x | y ≤ y † , Dt )/p(x | y > y † , Dt )), where
192   h(x) = (ζ + (1 − ζ)x−1 )−1 . Since h is a strictly increasing function, we can directly maximize
193   p(x | y ≤ y † , Dt )/p(x | y > y † , Dt ). In the previous work (Bergstra et al., 2011), two tree-
194   structured Parzen estimators are used to estimate the respective densities, p(x | y ≤ y † , Dt ) and
195   p(x | y > y † , Dt ).
196
      While the work (Bergstra et al., 2011) utilizes two distinct tree-structured Parzen estimators, Tiao
197
      et al. (2021) propose a method to directly estimate (1) using class-probability estimation (Qin, 1998;
198   Sugiyama et al., 2012), which is called BORE. Since it can be formulated as a problem of binary
199   classification in which Class 1 is a group of the top ζ of Dt and Class 0 is a group of the bottom 1−ζ
200   of Dt in terms of function evaluations, the acquisition function defined in (1) induces the following:
201
                                                            p(x | z = 1)
202                           A(x | ζ, Dt ) =                                        .                       (2)
203                                              ζp(x | z = 1) + (1 − ζ)p(x | z = 0)
204   By the Bayes’ theorem, (2) becomes A(x | ζ, Dt ) = ζ −1 p(z = 1 | x)/(p(z = 1 | x) + p(z =
205   0 | x)). Therefore, a class probability over x for Class 1 is considered as an acquisition function;
206   it is simply denoted as A(x | ζ, Dt ) = ζ −1 π(x). Eventually, the class probability is estimated by
207   various off-the-shelf classifiers such as random forests and multi-layer perceptrons.
208
      Song et al. (2022) have suggested a general framework of likelihood-free Bayesian optimization,
209

      Effects of Pool Sampling. Because the computational complexity of label propagation and label
508   spreading depends on a pool size, we need to reduce a pool size appropriately in order to speed
509   up the algorithms. Therefore, we uniformly sample a subset of the fixed-size set, which is used as
510   unlabeled points. Figure 8 reports elapsed times over subset sizes for pool sampling. Larger subsets
511   make the framework slower as expected. More detailed analysis on the effects of pool sampling is
512   demonstrated in Section G.
513
514   Effects of Threshold Ratios. We study the effects of a threshold ratio ζ in order to understand
515   how we can choose ζ. As shown in Figure 15, both small ζ and large ζ generally represent worse
516   performance; see the results with ζ = 0.01 and ζ = 0.8. While it has to depend on optimization
517   problems, ζ = 0.33 and ζ = 0.5 are generally appropriate choices according to this analysis. The
518   details of this study can be found in Section H and Figure 15.
519
520   Flat Landscape of Class Probabilities over Inputs. Regardless of the use of either supervised or
521   semi-supervised classifier, a flat landscape of class probabilities can occur in the framework of DRE-
522   based Bayesian optimization. To overcome the issue of optimizing a flat landscape, we add a simple
523   heuristic of selecting a random query point from points with identical highest class probabilities
524   if the landscape is flat, as described in Section 4. Since off-the-shelf local optimization methods
525
      struggle to optimize a flat landscape, this simple heuristic is effective.
526
527   General Framework of DRE-BO-SSL. As a future direction, we expect that a general framework
528   of DRE-BO-SSL can be defined, which is similar to a likelihood-free approach by Song et al. (2022).
529
      However, it is difficult to define an utility function involved in both labeled and unlabeled data. For
      example, if we assume that an utility function is u(y; y † ) = max(y † − y, 0), y for an unlabeled data
530
      point is unknown. Although it depends on the form of utility function, we need to define y of an
531
      unlabeled data point by utilizing the information we have if the utility function is related to y.
532
533
534   7                        C ONCLUSION
535
536   In this paper we have proposed a DRE-based Bayesian optimization framework with semi-
537   supervised learning, named DRE-BO-SSL. Unlike the existing work such as BORE and LFBO,
538   our methods make use of semi-supervised classifiers where unlabeled data points are sampled or
539   given. The superior results by our methods and the thorough analyses on the characteristics of
      DRE-BO-SSL exhibit the validity of our proposed algorithms.

829   C    A NALYSIS OF DRE-BO-SSL
830
831   Under the cluster assumption, i.e., Assumption 4.1, a margin γ is defined as a minimum distance
832
      between two decision boundaries.
833   Definition C.1. Let a compact connected decision set be Ci ⊆ X for i ∈ {0, 1} and a boundary
834   subset, i.e., a set of boundary points, of a compact connected set S be ∂S. A margin γ is defined as
835                     γ = (2I(C1 ∩ C2 = ∅) − 1)             min               ∥x1 − x2 ∥2 .          (11)
836                                                  x1 ∈∂C1 \∂X ,x2 ∈∂C2 \∂X
837
838   Using Definition C.1, we claim that a semi-supervised classifier in DRE-BO-SSL can mitigate the
839   over-exploitation problem presented in Section 1.1, because it can expand a decision set C1 for Class
840
      1 by reducing γ with unlabeled data. However, we need to verify if a large decision set is derived
      from the characteristics of non-parametric classifiers, since a semi-supervised classifier we use is
841
      converted to the Nadaraya-Watson non-parametric model (Nadaraya, 1964; Watson, 1964) without
842
      unlabeled data. As shown in Figure 12, there is no strong relationship between the performance of
843   semi-supervised classifiers without unlabeled data, i.e., the Nadaraya-Watson estimator, and one of
844   semi-supervised classifiers with unlabeled data. It implies that configuration selection for a semi-
845   supervised classifier is dependent on a class of objective function, and the presence of unlabeled
846   data is likely to be effective for alleviating the over-exploitation problem.
847
      Singh et al. (2008) provide a sample error bound of supervised and semi-supervised learners, re-
848
      lated to γ, nl , nu , and d. This work proves that a semi-supervised learner can be better than any
849   supervised learners, by assuming that nu ≫ nl and access to perfect knowledge of decision sets.
850   However, these theoretical results cannot be directly applied in our sequential problem because this
851   work assumes that both labeled and unlabeled data points are independent and identically distributed.
852   Nevertheless, these results can hint a theoretical guarantee on better performance of semi-supervised
853   classifiers with unlabeled data points. Further analysis for the circumstances of Bayesian optimiza-
854   tion is left for future work.

982         First layer: convolutional, input channel 1, output channel 8, kernel size 3 × 3, padding 1,
983         ReLU, max-pooling 2 × 2;
984         Second layer: convolutional, input channel 8, output channel 16, kernel size 3 × 3, padding 1,
985         ReLU, max-pooling 2 × 2;
986         Third layer: convolutional, input channel 16, output channel 32, kernel size 3 × 3, padding 1,
987         ReLU, max-pooling 2 × 2;
988         Fourth layer: fully-connected, input dimensionality 960, output dimensionality 128, ReLU;
989
            Fifth layer: fully-connected, input dimensionality 128, output dimensionality 64, ReLU;

991         Sixth layer: fully-connected, input dimensionality 64, output dimensionality 1000, Softmax.

       The Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2015) with learning rate 1 × 10−3 is used to train the network
993
       for 100 epochs. To train and test the model fairly, we create a training dataset of 440,000 three-digit
994
       images, a validation dataset of 40,000 three-digit images, and a test dataset of 80,000 three-digit
995    images using a training dataset of 55,000 single-digit images, a validation dataset of 5,000 single-
996    digit images, and a test dataset of 10,000 single-digit images in the original MNIST dataset (LeCun
997    et al., 1998). For example, supposing that a test dataset has 1,000 single-digit images per class – it is
998    not true for the MNIST dataset, but it is assumed for explanation – and we would like to generate a
999    three-digit image “753,” 109 combinations for “753” can be created. We therefore randomly sample
1000   a fixed number of three-digit images from a vast number of possible combinations. In addition,
1001   an early stopping technique is utilized by comparing the current validation loss to the average of
1002   validation losses for the recent five epochs. Eventually, our network achieves 99.6% in the training
1003
       dataset, 97.0% in the validation dataset, and 96.9% in the test dataset.
1004   To construct a fixed-size pool, we use 80,000 embeddings of dimensionality 64, which are derived
1005   from the outputs of the fifth layer without ReLU, by passing the test dataset of three-digit images
1006   through the network.

       As discussed above, our algorithms slow down if a pool size is significantly large. As presented
1191   in Figure 8, elapsed times are certainly dependent on subset sizes. To tackle this issue, we suggest
