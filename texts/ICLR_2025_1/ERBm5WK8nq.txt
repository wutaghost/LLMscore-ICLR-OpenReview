009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent research has shown that large language models (LLMs) can be effectively
012            used for real-world time series forecasting due to their strong natural language
013            understanding capabilities. However, aligning time series into semantic spaces
014            of LLMs comes with high computational costs and inference complexity, partic-
015            ularly for long-range time series generation. Building on recent advancements
016            in using linear models for time series, this paper introduces an LLM-enhanced
017            mixture of linear experts for precise and efficient time series forecasting. This
018            approach involves developing a mixture of linear experts with multiple lookback
019
               lengths and a new multimodal fusion mechanism. The use of a mixture of linear
               experts is efficient due to its simplicity, while the multimodal fusion mechanism
020
               adaptively combines multiple linear experts based on the learned features of the
021
               text modality from pre-trained large language models. In experiments, we rethink
022            the need to align time series to LLMs by existing time-series large language mod-
023            els and further discuss their efficiency and effectiveness in time series forecasting.
024            Our experimental results show that the proposed LeMoLE model presents lower
025            prediction errors and higher computational efficiency than existing LLM models.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Long-term time series forecasting (LTSF) is a significant challenge in machine learning due to its
031   wide range of applications. It has been important in various domains such as weather modeling
032   (Ma et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2022), traffic flow management (Lv et al., 2014), and financial analysis
033   (Abu-Mostafa & Atiya, 1996). Traditional statistical models like ARIMA (Box & Pierce, 1970)
034   and exponential smoothing (Gardner Jr, 1985) have served as the foundation for forecasting tasks
035   for decades. However, these models often struggle to handle the complexities arising from real-
036
      world applications, such as non-linearity, high dimensionality, and intricate temporal dynamics. In
      recent years, deep learning models have emerged as a breakthrough in forecasting, revolutionizing
037
      accuracy and efficiency. These models can remarkably capture complex temporal patterns and in-
038
      teractions within the data. By leveraging the power of deep learning, they excel in forecasting tasks
039   by effectively learning from large-scale datasets.
040
041   It is intriguing to note that while deep models (e.g., transformer-based models) have gained pop-
042
      ularity and achieved significant success in various fields like computer vision, natural language
      processing, and time series research, they usually come at the cost of extensive computational bur-
043
      dens. Recent empirical studies have revealed scenarios where simpler and more computationally ef-
044
      ficient linear-based models outperform complex deep learning models. Models like DLinear (Zeng
045   et al., 2023) and RLinear (Li et al., 2023) have demonstrated superior performance. Linear mod-
046   els have proven effective in time series forecasting due to their capacity to capture and leverage
047   the linear relationships inherent in many time series datasets. By exploiting these linear relation-
048   ships, linear-based models can provide competitive predictions while maintaining computational
049   efficiency. While linear-based models have demonstrated strengths in certain time series forecasting
050   scenarios, it is important to acknowledge their limitations:
051   i) Non-linear patterns: Real-world time series data often exhibit non-linear patterns resulting from
052   complex underlying mechanisms, such as variable interactions or abrupt regime shifts. Linear mod-
053   els may struggle to capture and model these non-linear relationships effectively (Chen et al., 2023;
      Ni et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024).

054
      ii) Long-range dependencies: Linear models might face difficulties handling long-term dependen-
055   cies within time series data. As the dependency structure becomes more intricate and extends over
056   longer periods, the effectiveness of linear models diminishes (Nie et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2024c).
057
058
      Therefore, the challenge of developing a powerful prediction model that retains the high efficiency
      of linear models remains an open question.
059
060   A mixture of linear experts is a promising solution to build such a model. Intuitively, multiple linear
061   experts can convert the original nonlinear time series prediction into several component prediction
062   problems. For example, some experts focus on trends, while others handle seasonals, or some deal
063   with short-term patterns while others learn long-term patterns. For example in (Ni et al., 2024),
064
      Mixture-of-Linear-Experts (MoLE) is proposed to train multiple linear-centric models (i.e., experts)
      to collaboratively predict the time series. Additionally, a router model, which accepts a timestamp
065
      embedding of the input sequence as input, learns to weigh these experts adaptively. This allows that
066
      different experts specialize in different periods of the time series.
067
068   In addition, incorporating multimodal knowledge into
069   predictive models is also a promising solution. Recently,
070
      there has been a significant surge of interest in multi-
      modal time series forecasting. For example, TimeLLM
071
      (Jin et al., 2024) aims to align the modalities of time se-
072
      ries data and natural language such that the capabilities of
073   pretrained large language model (LLM) from natural lan-
074   guage process (NLP) can be activated to model time se-
075   ries dynamics. In practice, the alignment of multimodal-
076   ity in time series forecasting can be easily achieved by
077   fine-tuning the input and output layers. In this way, both    Figure 1: Inference time on ETTh1.
078   time series and non-time series data (such as text data)
079   can be jointly inputted to LLM for multimodal time series forecasting. Although such alignment-
080   based LLMs have shown improvement in time series forecasting tasks, compared to linear models,
081
      they are not very effective and suffer from slow inference speed (Liu et al., 2024b) as they have to
      use large language model as time series predictor. Figure 1 shows inference efficiency comparisons.
082
083   Motivated by the above-related works, in this paper, we propose LeMoLE for Time Series Forecast-
084   ing. LeMoLE refers to an LLM-enhanced mixture of linear experts. Different from the Mixture-
085   of-Linear-Experts (MoLE) (Ni et al., 2024), the proposed LeMoLE enhances ensemble diversity by
086   leveraging multiple linear experts with varying lookback window lengths. This strategy is simple yet
087
      effective. Intuitively, this improvement encourages the experts to effectively handle both short-term
      and long-term temporal patterns in historical data. Moreover, LeMoLE incorporates informative
088
      multimodal knowledge from global and local text data during the ensemble process of the multi-
089
      ple linear experts. This adaptive approach allows LeMoLE to allocate specific experts for specific
090   temporal patterns, enhancing its flexibility and performance. We introduce a pre-trained large lan-
091   guage model for extracting text representations to improve the fusion of outputs from multiple linear
092   experts and text knowledge. Additionally, to incorporate static and dynamic text information, we
093   incorporate two conditioning modules based on the well-known FiLM (Feature-wise linear modula-
094   tion) conditioning layer (Perez et al., 2018). Consequently, the proposed LLM-enhanced mixture of
095   linear experts enables more flexible and effective long-range predictions than alignment-based time
096   series LLM models.
097   The main contributions of our work are summarized as follows:
098
      i) We present an LLM-enhanced mixture of linear experts called LeMoLE. To the best of our knowl-
099   edge, it is the first work on improving linear time series models based on mixture-of-expert learning
100   and multimodal learning.
101
      ii) We introduce linear experts with varying lookback window lengths to enhance ensemble diversity
102   and incorporate two novel conditioning modules based on FiLM (Feature-wise Linear Modulation)
103   to effectively integrate global and local text data adaptively.
104
      iii) We rethink existing large language models for time series and compared several recent state-of-
105   the-art prediction networks on long-term forecasting and few-shot tasks. The results demonstrate
106   the effectiveness of the proposed LeMoLE in terms of accuracy and efficiency.

110   2.1   L INEAR M ODELS AND L INEAR E NSEMBLE M ODELS
111
112   While transformer-based models (Zhou et al., 2022a; Nie et al., 2023a; Wu et al., 2021) have been
113   successful in Long-Term Time Series Forecasting (LTSF), (Zeng et al., 2023) questioned their uni-
114   versal superiority and suggested simpler architectural approaches like DLinear and NLinear. DLin-
115   ear (Zeng et al., 2023) decomposes time series into trend and season branches and uses linear models
116   for forecasting. Subsequent research by (Li et al., 2023) further confirmed the potential of linear-
117
      centric models like RLinear and RMLP, which outperformed PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023a) in specific
      benchmarks. Based on linear-based models and research focusing on the frequency domain, FITS
118
      (Xu et al., 2024) operates within the complex frequency domain. Although linear models are effi-
119
      cient, they are still limited in high-nonlinear time series (Chen et al., 2023; Ni et al., 2024). Related
120   ensemble linear models, such as TimeMixer (Wang et al., 2024) mixing the decomposed season and
121   trend components of time series from multiple resolutions. Then, multiple predictors are utilized
122   to project the resolution features for the final prediction. Based on a mixture of experts, MoLE
123   (Ni et al., 2024) applies multiple linear experts for forecasting, which is based on a router module
124   to adaptively reweigh experts’ outputs for the final generation. The proposed LeMoLE is different
125   from them due to its multimodal fusion mechanism.
126
127   2.2   LLM- BASED M ULTIMODAL F ORECASTING
128
      Pre-trained foundation models, such as large language models (LLMs), have driven rapid progress
129
      in natural language processing (NLP) (Radford et al., 2019; Brown, 2020; Touvron et al., 2023)
130
      and multimodal modeling (Caffagni et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024). Several works have tried to
131   transfer LLMs’ capabilities of other modalities to advance time series forecasting. However, the
132   main challenges lie in discussing the relationships between the two modalities, time series and text.
133   Some previous works claim that aligning them is important and useful for multimodal forecasting.
134   LLM4TS (Chang et al., 2023) use a two-stage fine-tuning process on the LLM, first supervised
135   pre-training on time series, then task-specific fine-tuning. Zhou et al. (2024) leverages pre-trained
136   language models without altering the self-attention and feedforward layers of the residual blocks. It
137   is fine-tuned and evaluated on various time series analysis tasks to transfer knowledge from natural
138   language pre-training. Jin et al. (2024) reprograms the input time series with text prototypes before
139
      feeding it into the frozen LLM to align the two modalities. Conversely, AutoTimes (Liu et al.,
      2024b) states the aligning is overlooked, resulting in insufficient utilization of the LLM potentials.
140
      It presents token-wise prompting that utilizes corresponding timestamps and then concatenates the
141
      time and prompt features as the multimodal input.
142
143   Although these LLM-based time series methods have improved, their main limitation is their effi-
144   ciency compared with lightweight models like linear-based models. In this work, we rethinnk the
145
      use of large language models for time series and strive to develop a more efficient and effective
      LLM-enhanced prediction model.

      3.2   LLM- ENHANCED C ONDITIONING M ODULE
203
204   Prompting serves as a straightforward yet effective approach to task-specific activation of LLMs.
205   To leverage abundant multimodal knowledge to help time series forecasting, it is essential to design
206   appropriate text prompts and the corresponding conditioning module to activate our multi-expert
207   prediction network.
208   In time series data, there are two important types of text information that describe temporal dynam-
209   ics. The first type is static text, which typically provides global information about the time series
210   dataset, such as data source descriptions. The second type of text is dynamic and time-dependent,
211   including information like time stamps, weather conditions, or other external environmental factors.
212   To incorporate these two types of text data into the prediction network, we create static and dynamic
213   prompts and use a pretrained language model to obtain their corresponding representations.
214
215   Static prompt. Figure 5 (left) in Appendix B shows a static prompt example we used on the ETTh
      dataset. It is about the data source description. Specifically, it includes what, where, and how the

      Extension to frequency domain. In the proposed LeMoLE, we introduce linear experts with vary-
      ing lookback window lengths to enhance ensemble diversity. In this section, drawing inspiration
262
      from a recent frequency-based linear model known as FITS (Xu et al., 2024) (Frequency Interpola-
263
      tion Time Series Analysis Baseline), we propose an extension of LeMoLE called LeMoLE-F, where
264   each linear expert is implemented using FITS. Consequently, we can rename the original LeMoLE
265   in the time domain as LeMoLE-T. The setup of lookback window lengths of LeMoLE-F is the same
266   as that in LeMoLE-T. In LeMoLE-F, each linear expert takes the input as a frequency domain pro-
267   jection of a specific lookback window. This projection is achieved by applying a real FFT (Fast
268   Fourier Transform). Subsequently, a single complex-valued linear layer is used to interpolate the
269   frequencies. To revert the interpolated frequency back to the time domain and obtain the output of
      the linear expert, zero padding and an inverse real FFT are applied.

478
479
      This study introduces LeMoLE, a multimodal mixture of linear experts, for time series forecasting.
480   By harnessing the powerful capabilities of a pre-trained large language model, LeMoLE allows for a
481   flexible ensemble of multiple linear experts by integrating static and dynamic text knowledge corre-
482   lated to time series data. By comparing existing LLM time series models aligning text and time series
483   in large language models’ spaces, the proposed LeMoLE shows greater effectiveness. This finding
484   demonstrates the effectiveness of a mixture of linear experts and the use of multimodal knowledge.
485   Furthermore, the study delves into detailed discussions regarding the variant of frequency experts
      and computational costs.

710   D       S UPPLEMENTARY OF BASELINES
711
712   Recent transformer variants aim to improve the standard transformer structure for time series mod-
713   eling (Wen et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2023). For example, to reduce the time com-
714   plexity and memory usage, Informer (Wen et al., 2022) proposes ProbSparse self-attention mech-
715   anism and the adoption of a generative decoder to reduce the time complexity and memory usage.
716   Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021) adopts data decomposition techniques and designs an efficient auto-
717   correlation mechanism to improve prediction accuracy. To analyze time series in the multi-scale as-
718   pect, Pyraformer (Liu et al., 2022a) implements intra-scale and inter-scale attention to capture tem-
719   poral dependencies across different resolutions effectively. In the frequency domain, FEDFormer
720
      (Zhou et al., 2022a) designs the enhanced blocks with Fourier transform and wavelet transform,
      enabling the focus on capturing important structures in time series through frequency domain map-
721
      ping. Recently, PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023a) segments time series into patches that serve as input
722
      tokens to Transformer and use the channel independence assumption to get better performance. To
723   capture the relationship between the variables, iTransformer (Liu et al., 2024a) replaces the standard
724   attention across the time with variable attention while keeping the whole structure of the standard
725   transformer model.

      els affects their performance, varying depending on the dataset as shown in Table 9. In the
871
      Electricity dataset, LeMoLE improves up to three experts, but additional experts add com-
872
      plexity without accuracy gains. In contrast, the Traffic dataset shows consistent improvements up to
873   three experts. For the ETTh1 and ETTm1 datasets, stability is observed with minimal performance
874   changes, suggesting these datasets require fewer experts. The frequency-based LeMoLE-F model
875   benefits specific configurations but needs careful tuning for optimal results.
