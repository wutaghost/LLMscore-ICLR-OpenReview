029                                                 A BSTRACT
030
031
                 3D meshes are widely used in computer vision and graphics because of their ef-
                 ficiency in animation and minimal memory footprint. They are extensively em-
032
                 ployed in movies, games, AR, and VR, leading to the creation of a vast number
033
                 of mesh sequences. However, creating temporally consistent and realistic textures
034              for these mesh sequences to model the appearance transformations remains labor-
035              intensive for professional artists. On the other hand, video diffusion models have
036              demonstrated remarkable capabilities in text-driven video generation, enabling
037              users to create countless video clips based solely on their imagination. Despite
038              their strengths, these models often lack 3D geometry awareness for fine-grained
039              video control and struggle with achieving multi-view consistent texturing for 3D
040              mesh sequences. In this work, we present Tex4D, a zero-shot approach that in-
041              tegrates inherent 3D geometry knowledge from mesh sequences with the expres-
042
                 siveness of video diffusion models. Given an untextured mesh sequence and a text
                 prompt as inputs, our method enhances multi-view consistency by synchronizing
043
                 the diffusion process across different views through latent aggregation in the UV
044
                 space. To ensure temporal consistency such as lighting changes, wrinkles, and ap-
045              pearance transformations, we leverage prior knowledge from a conditional video
046              generation model for texture synthesis. However, straightforwardly combining
047              the video diffusion model and the UV texture aggregation leads to blurry results.
048              We analyze the underlying causes and propose a simple yet effective modifica-
049              tion to the DDIM sampling process to address this issue. Additionally, we intro-
050              duce a reference latent texture to strengthen the correlation between frames during
051              the denoising process. To the best of our knowledge, Tex4D is the first method
052              specifically designed for 4D scene texturing. Extensive experiments demonstrate
053
                 its superiority in producing multi-view and multi-frame consistent videos based
                 on untextured mesh sequences.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056   3D meshes are widely used in modeling, computer-aided design (CAD), animation, and computer
057   graphics due to their low memory footprint and efficiency in animation. Visual artists, game design-
058   ers, and movie creators build numerous animated mesh sequences for visual applications. However,
059   creating vivid videos involves complex post-processing steps, such as lighting controls and appear-
060   ance transformations. These steps are labor-intensive and require specialized expertise by artists.
061
      On the other hand, recent advancements in generative models have democratized content creation
062   and demonstrated impressive performance in image and video synthesis. For instance, video genera-
063   tion models (Ho et al., 2022; Esser et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; He et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023a; Zhou
064   et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023b; Xing et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
065   2023c; 2024) trained on large-scale video datasets (Bain et al., 2021; Schuhmann et al., 2021) allow
066   users to create realistic video clips from various inputs such as text prompts, images, or geometric
067   conditions. However, these text-to-video generation models, which are trained solely on 2D data,
068   often struggle with spatial consistency when applied to multi-view image generation (Tang et al.,
069   2023; Shi et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023a; Weng et al., 2023; Long et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023a;
070
      Kwak et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024; Voleti et al., 2024) or 3D object texturing (Cao et al., 2023; Liu
      et al., 2023b; Richardson et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b; Huo et al., 2024).
071
072   To address these limitations, two main approaches have been developed. One approach (Richardson
073   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b; Cao et al., 2023) focuses on resolving multi-view inconsistency in
074   static 3D object texturing by synchronizing multi-view image diffusion processes and enforcing UV
075   space consistency. While these methods produce multi-view consistent textures for static 3D objects,
076
      they do not address the challenge of generating temporally consistent textures for mesh sequences.
      Another approach (Guo et al., 2023a; Lin et al., 2024; Peng et al., 2024) aims to generate temporally
077
      consistent video clips based on the rendering (e.g., depth, normal or UV maps) of an untextured
078
      mesh sequence. To encourage temporal consistency, these methods modify the attention mechanism
079   in 2D diffusion models and utilize inherent correspondences in a mesh sequence to facilitate feature
080   synchronization between frames. Although these techniques can be adapted for multi-view image
081   generation by treating camera pose movement as temporal motion, they usually produce inconsistent
082   3D texturing due to insufficient exploitation of 3D geometry priors.
083
      In this paper, we introduce a novel task: 4D scene texturing. Given an animated untextured 3D mesh
084
      sequence and a text prompt, our goal is to generate textures that are both temporally and multi-view
085   consistent. Our objective is to texture 4D scenes while capturing temporal variations, such as light-
086   ing changes and wrinkles, to produce vivid visual results—a key requirement in downstream tasks
087   like character generation. Different from existing works, we fully leverage 3D geometry knowledge
088   from the mesh sequence to enforce multi-view consistency. Specifically, we develop a method that
089   synchronizes the diffusion process from different views through latent aggregation in the UV space.
090   To ensure temporal consistency, we employ prior knowledge from a conditional video generation
091   model for texture synthesis and introduce a reference latent texture to enhance frame-to-frame cor-
092   relations during the denoising process. However, naively integrating the UV texture aggregation into
093
      the video diffusion process causes the variance shift problem, leading to blurry results. To resolve
      this issue, we propose a simple yet effective modification to the DDIM (Song et al., 2020) sampling
094
      process by uniformly transforming the equation. Additionally, we propose to synthesize a dynamic
095
      background along with the textures of the given mesh sequences, which not only creates a complete
096   4D scene but also fully exploits the prior knowledge embedded in the video diffusion model. Our
097   method is computationally efficient thanks to its zero-shot nature. The textured mesh sequence can
098   be rendered from any camera view, thus supporting a wide range of applications in content creation.
099
      We evaluate our method on various mesh sequences with key contributions as follows:
100
101   • We present Tex4D, a zero-shot pipeline for generating high-fidelity textures that are temporally
102     and multi-view consistent, utilizing text-to-video diffusion models and mesh sequence controls.
103   • We develop a simple and effective modification to the DDIM sampling process to address the
104     variance shift issue caused by multi-view texture aggregation.
105   • We introduce a reference UV blending mechanism to establish correlations during the denoising
106     steps, addressing self-occlusions, and synchronizing the diffusion process in invisible regions.
107   • Our method is not only computationally efficient, but also demonstrates comparable if not superior
        performance to various state-of-the-art baselines.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Video Stylization and Editing Text-to-video diffusion models have shown remarkable perfor-
111
      mance in the field of video generation. These models learn motions and dynamics from large-scale
112
      video datasets using 3D-UNet to create high-quality, realistic, and temporally coherent videos. Al-
113   though these approaches show compelling results, the generated videos lack fine-grained control,
114   inhibiting their application in stylization and editing. To solve this issue, inspired by Control-
115   Net (Zhang et al., 2023a), SparseCtrl (Guo et al., 2023a) trains a sparse encoder from scratch using
116   frame masks and sparse conditioning images as input to guide a pre-trained video diffusion model.
117   CTRL-Adapter (Lin et al., 2024) proposes a trainable intermediate adapter to efficiently connect the
118   features between ControlNet and video diffusion models.
119   Meanwhile, Tumanyan et al. (2023) observed that the spatial features of T2I models play an influ-
120   ential role in determining the structure and appearance, Text2Video-Zero (Khachatryan et al., 2023)
121   uses a frame-warping method to animate the foreground object by T2I models and Wu et al. (2023);
122   Ceylan et al. (2023); Qi et al. (2023) propose utilizing self-attention injection and cross-frame at-
123   tention to generate stylized and temporally consistent video using DDIM inversion (Song et al.,
124   2020). Subsequently, numerous works (Zhang et al., 2023c; Cai et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023;
125   Geyer et al., 2023; Eldesokey & Wonka, 2024) generate temporally consistent videos utilizing T2I
126   diffusion models by spatial latent alignment without training. However, the synthesized videos usu-
127
      ally show flickerings due to the empirical correspondences, such as feature embedding distances and
      UV maps, which are insufficient to express the continuous content in the latent space. Another line
128
      of work (Singer et al., 2022; Bar-Tal et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Guo et al.,
129
      2023b) is to train additional modules on large-scale video datasets to construct feature mappings,
130   for example, Text2LIVE (Bar-Tal et al., 2022) applies test-time training with the CLIP loss, and
131   MagicAnimate (Xu et al., 2024) introduced an appearance encoder to retain intricate clothes details.
132
133   Texture Synthesis With the rapid development of foundation models, researchers have focused on
134   applying their generation capability and adaptability to simplify the process of designing textures
135
      and reduce the expertise required. To incorporate the result 3D content with prior knowledge, earlier
      works (Khalid et al., 2022; Michel et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022) jointly optimize the meshes and
136
      textures from scratch with the simple semantic loss from the pre-trained CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)
137
      to encourage the 3D alignment between the generated results and the semantic priors. However,
138   the results show apparent artifacts and distortion because the semantic feature cannot provide fine-
139   grained supervision during the generation of 3D content.
140
141
      DreamFusion (Poole et al., 2022) and similar models (Lin et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Po &
      Wetzstein, 2024; Metzer et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023a) distill the learned 2D diffusion priors
142
      from the pre-trained diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2021) to synthesize the 3D content by Score
143
      Distillation Sampling (SDS). These methods render 2D projections of the 3D asset parameters and
144   compare them against reference images, iteratively refining the 3D asset parameters to minimize the
145   discrepancy of the target distribution of 3D shapes learned by the diffusion model. Although these
146   approaches enable people without expertise to generate detailed 3D content by textual prompt, their
147   results are typically over-saturated and over-smoothed, hindering their application in actual cases.
148   Another line of optimization-based methods (Yu et al., 2023b; Zeng et al., 2024; Bensadoun et al.,
149   2024) turned to fuse 3D shape information, such as vertex positions, depth maps and normal maps,
150   with the pre-trained diffusion model by training separate modules on 3D datasets. Still, they require
151   a specific UV layout process to achieve plausible results.
152   Recently, TexFusion (Cao et al., 2023) and numerous zero-shot methods (Liu et al., 2023b; Richard-
153   son et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b; Huo et al., 2024) have shown significant success in generating
154   globally consistent textures without additional 3D datasets. Based on depth-aware diffusion models,
155   they sequentially inpaint the latents in the UV domain to ensure the spatial consistency of latents
156   observed across different views. Then, they decode the latents from multiple views and finally syn-
157   thesize the RGB texture through backprojection.
158   However, these methods primarily focus on generating static 3D assets and do not account for tem-
159   poral changes in the final visual presentation, such as in videos. Our work introduces a zero-shot
160   framework that enables multi-view consistent video generation based on animated meshes, which is
161   effective in capturing temporal variations. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first approach to
      synthesize multi-view and multi-frame consistent textures for mesh sequences.

166
      to provide dynamic information. CTRL-Adapter aims to adapt a pre-trained text-to-video diffusion

167
      behind CTRL-Adapter is to leverage a pre-trained ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023a) and to align its
168
      latents with those of the video diffusion model through a learnable mapping module. Intuitively, the
169   video diffusion model generates temporally consistent video frames that capture dynamic elements
170   like character motions and environmental lighting, while the ControlNet further enhances this capa-
171   bility by allowing the model to condition on geometric information, such as depth and normal map
172   sequences. This makes CTRL-Adapter particularly effective in providing a temporally consistent
173   texture prior for our 4D scene texturing task. Specifically, we leverage the depth-conditioned CTRL-
174   Adapter model. Given a sequence of depth images denoted as {D1 , ..., DK } and a text prompt P,
175   CTRL-Adapter (denoted as C) synthesizes a frame sequence F by F = C({D1 , ..., DK }, P).

      4   M ETHOD
199
200   Given an untextured mesh animation and a text prompt, our goal is to generate multi-view and
201
      multi-frame consistent texture for each mesh that aligns with both the text description and motion
      cues, meanwhile capturing the dynamics from video diffusion models. To optimize computational
202
      efficiency, instead of processing all video frames, we uniformly sample K key frames from the video
203
      and synthesize textures specifically for these key frames. The textures for the remaining frames are
204   then generated by interpolating the key frame textures. Formally, given K animated meshes at the
205   key frames ({M1 , ..., MK }), along with a text description P, our method produces a sequence of
206   temporally and spatially consistent UV maps denoted as {U V1 , ..., U VK }, in a zero-shot manner.
207
      Previous texture generation methods (Richardson et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b; Cao et al., 2023)
208
      typically inpaint and update textures sequentially using pre-defined camera views in an incremental
209
      manner. However, these approaches rely on view-dependent depth conditions and lack global spatial
210   consistency, often resulting in visible discontinuities in the assembled texture map. This issue arises
211   from error accumulation during the autoregressive view update process, as noted by Bensadoun et al.
212   (2024). To resolve these issues, rather than processing each view independently, recent methods (Liu
213   et al., 2023b; Huo et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024) propose to generate multi-view textures simulta-
214   neously through diffusion, and then aggregate them in the UV space at each diffusion step. In this
215   work, we similarly leverage the UV space as an intermediate representation to ensure multi-view
      consistency during texture generation.

248   4.1            OVERVIEW
249
250   As shown in Fig. 2, given a sequence of K meshes, we start by rendering the mesh at V
251   predefined, uniformly sampled camera poses to obtain multi-view depth images (denoted as
252   {D1,1 , ..., D1,K , D2,1 ..., DV,K }), which serve as the geometric conditions. To generate textures
253   for each mesh, we initialize V × K noise images sampled from a Normal distribution (de-
254   noted as {z 1,1 , ..., z 1,K , z 2,1 , ..., z V,K }). Additionally, we initialize an extra noise map sequence
255
      {zb1 , ..., zbK } for the backgrounds learning. This noise map corresponds to the texture of a plane
      mesh that is composited with the foreground object at each diffusion step (See Sec. 4.3). Next, for
256
      each view v ∈ {1, ..., V }, we apply the video diffusion model (Lin et al., 2024) discussed in Sec. 3
257
      to simultaneously denoise all latents and obtain multi-frame consistent images as {I 1,v , ..., I K,v } =
258   C({D1,v , ..., DK,v }, P), where P is the provided text prompt. Finally, we un-project and aggregate
259   all denoised multi-view images for each mesh to formulate temporally consistent UV textures.
260
261
      However, applying the video diffusion model independently to each camera view often results in
      multi-view inconsistencies. Inspired by (Liu et al., 2023b; Huo et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024),
262
      we aggregate the multi-view latents of each mesh in the UV space to merge observations across
263
      different views at each denoising step. We then render latent from the latent texture to ensure multi-
264   view consistency. To simultaneously generate a dynamic background and fully exploit prior in the
265   video diffusion model, we composite the rendered foreground latents with the background latents
266   at each diffusion step. This aggregation process is discussed in detail in Sec. 4.2. Nonetheless,
267   such a simple aggregation method introduces blurriness in the final results. In Sec. 4.3, we analyze
268   the underlying causes and propose a simple yet effective method to enhance the denoising process.
269   Additionally, we create and leverage a reference UV to handle self-occlusions and further improve
      temporal consistency in Sec. 4.4.

344   4.4      R EFERENCE UV B LENDING
345
346
      While the video diffusion model ensures temporal consistency for latents from each view, consis-
      tency can sometimes diminish after aggregation in the texture domain. This issue primarily stems
347
      from the view-dependent nature of the depth conditions and the limited resolution of latents, which
348
      can lead to distortions when features from different camera angles are combined onto the UV tex-
349   ture. Additionally, self-occlusion during mesh animation often results in a loss of information in
350   invisible regions.
351
352
      To address these challenges, we introduce a reference UV map to provide additional correlations
      between latent textures across frames. Specifically, the reference UV map is constructed by sequen-
353
      tially combining latent textures over time, with each new texture filling only the empty texels of the
354
      reference UV map. Then, each texture is blended using the reference UV TU V with a mask MU V
355   that labels the visible region:
356

527   all other parts unchanged. Fig. 3 (c) illustrates that this ablation experiment produces significantly
528   blurrier textures compared to our full method, highlighting the importance of background learning.

      6                 C ONCLUSIONS
531
532
      In this paper, we present a zero-shot approach that generates multi-view, multi-frame consistent
533
      textures for untextured, animated mesh sequences based on a text prompt. By incorporating texture
534   aggregation in the UV space within the diffusion process of a conditional video diffusion model,
535   we ensure both temporal and spatial coherence in the generated textures. To address the variance
536   shift introduced by texture aggregation, we propose a simple yet effective modification to the DDIM
537   sampling algorithm. Additionally, we enhance temporal consistency by introducing a reference UV
538   map and develop a dynamic background learning framework to produce fully textured 4D scenes.
539   Extensive experiments show that our method can synthesize realistic and consistent 4D textures,
      demonstrating its superiority against state-of-the-art baselines.

      A.1    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
759
760   We utilize the CTRL-Adapter (Lin et al., 2024), trained on the video diffusion model I2VGen-
761   XL (Zhang et al., 2023b), as the backbone for generation, with the denoising steps set to T = 50.
762   Initially, we center the untextured mesh sequence and pre-define six different viewpoints around
763   the Y-axis in the XZ-plane, uniformly sampled in spherical coordinates, along with an additional
764   top view with an elevation angle of zero and an azimuth angle of 30◦ . For latent initialization,
765   we first sample Gaussian noise on the latent textures and then render 2D latent samples for each
766   view to improve the coherence of the generated outputs. During denoising, we upscale the latent
767
      resolution to 96 × 96 to reduce aliasing. We empirically set the blending coefficient to 0.2. It takes
      approximately 30 minutes to generate a video with 24 key frames taken on a RTX A6000 Ada GPU.
768
      We decode the denoised latents in key frames to RGB images, and then un-project and aggregate
769
      these images to transform the latent UV maps to RGB textures as previous works (Liu et al., 2023b;
770   Cao et al., 2023; Huo et al., 2024). Finally, we interpolate the textures of the key frames at intervals
771   of 3 to synthesize the final video clips.

902   D.2   C OMPARISON WITH T EXTURED M ESH A NIMATIONS
903
904   In this section, we highlight the differences between our method and traditional approaches, demon-
905   strating the effectiveness of 4D texturing in capturing temporal variations (e.g., lighting and wrin-
906   kles) within mesh sequences to produce vivid visual results. Traditional methods typically involve
907   texturing a base mesh (often referred to as a clay mesh) and animating it using skinning techniques.
908
      This animated sequence is then refined by technical artists who control scene lighting or simulate
      cloth dynamics to achieve the final visual presentation. This process is labor-intensive and demands
909
      specialized expertise in cinematic production and technical engines.
910
911   In contrast, our method offers a streamlined alternative by directly integrating complex temporal
912   changes into mesh sequences. As shown in Fig. 5, 12 and 13, our approach effectively captures
913   intricate temporal effects such as cloth wrinkles, dynamic lighting, and evolving appearances using
914
      textual prompts, significantly simplifying the workflow while maintaining high-quality results.

916   changes in Fig. 10. Specifically, we employ the Text2Tex (Chen et al., 2023b) to generate the texture

951   E    L IMITATIONS AND D ISCUSSION
952
953   One limitation of our method is the lack of seamless integration between the generated textures
954   and the background, resulting in a disjointed appearance where the foreground and background
955   elements may seem artificially stitched together. This issue arises due to the absence of a compre-
956   hensive scene-level dataset. Alternatively, our approach relies on a shared background mesh across
957   different views, which disrupts overall consistency. Addressing scene-level 4D texturing remains
958   an open challenge that we aim to explore in future work. In addition, we notice that our method
959
      is relatively computational intensive compared with other texture synthesis methods. The computa-
      tion time of our method primarily depends on the foundation model (CTRL-Adapter), which takes
960
      approximately 5 minutes to generate a 24-frame video. We anticipate significant efficiency improve-
961
      ments with advancements in conditioned video diffusion models, further enhancing the practicality
962   of our approach.
