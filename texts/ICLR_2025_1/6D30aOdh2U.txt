026                                                  A BSTRACT
027
028               Recently, generative domain adaptation has achieved remarkable progress, enabling
029               us to adapt a pre-trained generator to a new target domain. However, existing
030               methods are limited to a single target domain and single modality, either text-driven
031               or image-driven. In this paper, we explore a novel task – Generalized Hybrid
032
                  Domain Adaptation. Compared with conventional generative domain adaptation, it
                  provides greater flexibility to adapt the generator to the hybrid of multiple target
033
                  domains, with multi-modal references including one-shot image and zero-shot text
034
                  prompt. Meanwhile, it is more challenging to represent the composition of multi-
035               modal target domains and preserve the characteristics from the source domain. To
036               address these issues, we propose UniHDA, a unified and versatile framework for
037               generalized hybrid domain adaptation. Drawing inspiration from the interpolable
038               latent space of StyleGAN, we find that a linear interpolation between domain shifts
039               in CLIP’s embedding space can also uncover favorable compositional capabilities
040               for the adaptation. In light of this finding, we linearly interpolate the domain shifts
041               from multiple target domains to achieve hybrid domain adaptation. To enhance
042               consistency with the source domain, we further propose a novel cross-domain
043
                  spatial structure (CSS) loss that maintains the detailed spatial structure between
                  the source and target generator. Experiments show the adapted generator can
044
                  synthesize realistic images with various attribute compositions and maintain robust
045
                  consistency with the source domain. Additionally, UniHDA is generator-agnostic
046               and versatile to multiple generators, e.g., StyleGAN, EG3D, and video generators.

      preserving the variation in the source domain. Depending on the modality of references, generative

074   domain adaptation can be categorized into two schools: text-driven (Gal et al., 2021; Nitzan et al.,
075   2023a; Liu et al., 2023; Song et al., 2022; Lei et al., 2023) and image-driven (Li et al., 2020; Ojha
076   et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2022b; Xiao et al., 2022; Mondal et al.; Wu et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2022a).
077
      Despite their promising results for single modality and single target domain, they fail to adapt the
078   generator to more practical scenarios like smiling Joker given the non-smiling Joker image
079   and the smile text. For more general purposes, we explore a new task – Generalized Hybrid Domain
080   Adaptation. It aims to adapt the generator to the hybrid of multiple target domains that integrates all
081   attributes with multi-modal references including one-shot image and zero-shot text prompt (Fig. 1).
082
      Compared with conventional generative domain adaptation, our task is more challenging in two
083
      aspects: (1) It is harder to represent the composition of multi-modal target domains. While cross-
084   model interpolation technique (Gal et al., 2021) could somehow yield alike images of the hybrid
085   domain, it doubles the model size and training time to train a separate model per domain. (2) With
086   multiple target domains and very limited references from each domain, the generator is more prone
087   to overfitting domain-specific attributes. This leads to a loss of consistency with the source domain,
088   which impedes the inheritance of the diversity (Fig. 2).
089
      To address these issues, we propose UniHDA, a Unified and versatile framework for Generalized
090   Hybrid Domain Adaptation. UniHDA facilitates the references of one-shot image and text prompt
091   simultaneously and blends the attributes from target domains to create a hybrid domain. To enable
092   multiple modalities, we leverage pre-trained CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to project multi-modal
093   references into a unified embedding space and represent the domain shift by the direction vector from
094   the source embedding to the target embeddings.
095
      To achieve hybrid domain adaptation, we draw inspiration from the compositional capabilities in the
096   latent space of StyleGAN (Härkönen et al., 2020; Shen & Zhou, 2021; Xu et al., 2022). Specifically,
097   we demonstrate a semantically meaningful linear interpolation between direction vectors in CLIP’s
098   embedding space can uncover favorable compositional capabilities (Fig. 3). In light of this intriguing
099   finding, we linearly interpolate direction vectors of multiple target domains to obtain the direction
100   vector corresponding to the hybrid domain that semantically integrates attributes from all target
101   domains.
102   Furthermore, we introduce a novel Cross-domain Spatial Structure loss (CSS) to preserve the consis-
103   tency between the source and target generator by maintaining detailed spatial structure information.
104   Concretely, we leverage pre-trained Dino-ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Oquab et al., 2023) to
105   encode generated images into patch tokens with fine-grained spatial information. For cross-domain
106   consistency, we maintain the correspondence between source and target tokens with contrastive
107   learning (Oord et al., 2018). Equipped with CSS loss, UniHDA maintains robust consistency with the
      source domain as shown in Fig. 2.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
147
      Text-driven Generative Domain Adaptation. Text-driven domain adaptation (Gal et al., 2021;
148
      Nitzan et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023; Alanov et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2022; Lyu et al., 2023; Lei et al.,
149
      2023; Kim & Chun, 2023; Kim et al., 2023; Song et al., 2022) involves using a textual prompt to shift
150   the domain of a pre-trained model toward a new domain. For example, Style-NADA (Gal et al., 2021)
151   presents a local direction CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) loss to align the embeddings of the generated
152   images and text. Based on Style-NADA, Domain Expansion (DE) (Nitzan et al., 2023b) proposes to
153   expand the generator to jointly model multiple domains with texts.
154
      Image-driven Generative Domain Adaptation. Image-driven generative domain adaptation (Mo
155
      et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Ojha et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2022b; Xiao et al., 2022; Mondal et al.; Wu
156   et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2022a; 2023; Zhang et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2021; Alanov et al., 2023; Kim
157   et al., 2022b) refers to the adaptation of a pre-trained image generator to a new target domain using a
158   limited number of training images. Prior methods often integrate additional regularization terms to
159   prevent overfitting. For instance, DiFa (Zhang et al., 2022) utilizes GAN inversion (Tov et al., 2021)
160   to align the latent codes which helps inherit diversity. Although these works have made significant
161   strides in generative domain adaptation, they heavily rely on the discriminator or generator, making it
      challenging to handle hybrid domain adaptation and extend to other generators.

162
      Generative Hybrid Domain Adaptation. To achieve hybrid domain adaptation, Style-NADA (Gal
163   et al., 2021) proposes to train a separate generative model per domain and combine their effects in
164   test-time. However, it doubles the model size and training time. Domain Expansion (DE) (Nitzan
165   et al., 2023b) expands the generator to jointly model multiple domains via decomposing latent space.
166   However, it requires the source dataset for regularization, which significantly increases training time.
167   Recently, FHDA (Li et al., 2023) proposes few-shot hybrid domain adaptation and introduces a
168   directional subspace loss. Differently, we focus on multi-modal references including one-shot image,
169   which offers greater flexibility.
170   Disentanglement in Generative Models. As observed in StyleGAN (Karras et al., 2019), the latent
171   space is essentially a linear subspace. Recent works (Härkönen et al., 2020; Shen & Zhou, 2021; Xu
172   et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2020; Patashnik et al., 2021; Voynov & Babenko, 2020;
173   Spingarn-Eliezer et al., 2020) propose to find individual latent factors for image variations. Among
174   them, SeFa (Shen & Zhou, 2021) computes the eigenvalues of the transformation matrix to find the
175   latent directions. For diffusion models, DiffAE (Preechakul et al., 2022) explores the possibility of
176   using DPMs for representation learning and extracts a decodable representation of an input image.

180   3.1   G ENERALIZED H YBRID D OMAIN A DAPTATION
181
182   We start with a pre-trained generator GS (e.g., StyleGAN (Karras et al., 2019; 2020; 2021) and
183
      Diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020)), that maps from noise z to images in a source
      domain S. Given a new target domain T referenced by texts (Gal et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022;
184
      Kwon et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2022a) or images (Mo et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Ojha et al., 2021;
185
      Zhao et al., 2022b; Xiao et al., 2022; Mondal et al.), generative domain adaptation aims to adapt GS
186   to yield a target generator GT , which can generate images similar to domain T .
187
188   Despite the promising results of existing methods, a major limitation of them is that they only support
189
      adaptation from the source domain to individual target domains and fail to directly adapt the generator
      to the hybrid domain which blends the characteristics of multiple domains. Furthermore, they fail
190
      with multi-modal adaptation driven by texts and images simultaneously.
191
192   For more general purposes, we explore generalized hybrid domain adaptation. Given N domains
193   {Ti }N                                                   M
           i=1 with one-shot image {Yi } and M domains {Tj }j=1 with the text prompt {Pj }, it aims to
194   adapt the source generator GS to GT that models the hybrid domain T = {Ti } ∪ {Tj } and generates
195   images with integrated characteristics. To the end, we introduce UniHDA, a unified and versatile
196
      framework for generalized hybrid domain adaptation (Fig. 4).

249
250   Albeit the direction loss achieves generalized hybrid domain adaptation, the adapted generator is
251   prone to overfit domain-specific attributes. This exacerbates when it comes to image-image and
252   image-text scenarios owing to the scarcity of the images. To address this issue, we introduce a
253   novel cross-domain spatial structure loss (CSS) to enhance cross-domain consistency, ensuring the
254   preservation of intricate spatial structural information between the source and target generator.
255

258   et al., 2023), which facilitates us in maintaining cross-domain consistency. Motivated by contrastive
259   learning (Oord et al., 2018), we reduce distances between the positive token pairs at the same position
260   and push away the negative token pairs at other positions by

      Methodology. We demonstrate the versatility of UniHDA on generalized hybrid domain adaptation,

306   domain adaptation literatures (Gal et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Mo et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020;
307   Nitzan et al., 2023b; Ojha et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2022b; Xiao et al., 2022; Mondal et al.), we use
308   StyleGAN2 for comparisons in most experiments.
309
      Datasets. We conduct experiments for a wide range of source and target domains to validate the
310
      effectiveness of UniHDA. Following previous work, we consider FFHQ (Karras et al., 2019), AFHQ-
311
      Dog (Choi et al., 2020), and LSUN-Church (Yu et al., 2015) as the source domains. The resolutions
312   of images in these datasets are respectively 1024, 512, and 256. We adapt the generator to diverse
313   hybrid domains driven by the text prompt and one-shot image. To demonstrate the effect of the hybrid
314   domain, we set the domain coefficients in Eq. (4) as 0.5. Unless stated otherwise, we use λ = 5 in
315   Eq. (6) for all experiments.
316
      Evaluation Metrics. One important aspect of evaluating generative domain adaptation is the
317
      preservation of domain-specific characteristics. Following (Ruiz et al., 2023), we use CLIP Score
318   (CS-T and CS-I) for text-text and image-image adaptation respectively. Concretely, CS-T is measured
319   by the average cosine similarity between the target prompt and generated images’ embedding. CS-I is
320   the average pairwise cosine similarity between CLIP embeddings of real and generated images. Here
321   we use the average CS-T or CS-I of multiple domains. For image-text adaptation, we use the average
322   of CS-T and CS-I as the metric (CS). Another important evaluation is the cross-domain consistency
323   of the source domain. To measure it, we adopt the Structural Consistency Score (SCS) (Xiao et al.,
      2022) to evaluate the spatial structural consistency between the source and target generator.

414   4.5    C OMPARISON WITH T EXT- TO -I MAGE G ENERATORS
415
416   Recent text-to-image generators like IP-Adapter (Ye et al., 2023) could synthesize promising results
417   with provided image and text prompt. However, the objective of UniHDA is to generate images with
418   attributes of the generalized hybrid target domains while maintaining considerable diversity from
419   the source domain, which can be applicable in scenarios like data collection. As shown in Fig. 10,
420   while IP-Adapter can produce images with multiple attributes, their diversity often diminishes when
421
      generating large quantities of images. This is due to their inability to retain the distribution of the
      original domain, which makes it impractical for data collection.

      4.8   A BLATION OF CSS L OSS
518
      We conduct the ablation study to evaluate the effects of our proposed CSS loss. As shown in Fig. 13,
519   the results without LCSS suffer from overfitting and have very limited cross-domain consistency,

522   the source domain. Besides, we conduct the quantitative ablation in the Appendix. There exists a
523   trade-off between adaptation to the target domain and preserving the characteristics from the source
524   domain. We can adjust the coefficient λ based on the desired effect.

526   5     C ONCLUSION & L IMITATION
527
528   In this paper, we explore a new task, generalized hybrid domain adaptation, and propose UniHDA, a
529   unified and versatile framework to enable it. For the hybrid domain, we demonstrate the compositional
530   capabilities of direction vectors in CLIP’s embedding space and linearly interpolate direction vectors
531   of multiple target domains. We also propose a new cross-domain spatial structure loss to improve
532   consistency, which is conducted in generator-agnostic space and versatile for various generators.
533   We believe our work is an important step towards generative domain adaptation, since we have
534   demonstrated the source generator can be effectively adapted to a hybrid domain with multi-modal
535   references and maintain robust cross-domain consistency. Our code will be made public.
536   While UniHDA effectively realizes generalized hybrid domain adaptation, it also has limitations.
537   To encode both image and text into a shared embedding space, we utilize pre-trained CLIP during
538   training time, which might bring potential bias for some domains. Nevertheless, we believe that the
539   exploration of the novel task is significant for future work and solutions could be integrated into
      UniHDA to eliminate the bias.

844   In addition to generation quality, UniHDA also surpasses existing methods in terms of efficiency, e.g.,
845   model size and training time as shown in Tab. 4. NADA, MTG, and DiFa trains a separate generative
846   model per domain and interpolates their parameters in test-time, which necessitates multiple times
847   the model size and training time. Although DE avoids cross-model interpolation, it heavily relies on
848   the large source dataset for regularization during training process, resulting in a significant increase
849   in training time. In contrast, UniHDA circumvents these issues, which enables the adaptation within
850   single generator in only two minutes.
851   Furthermore, DE relies on the semantic latent space of the generator (e.g., StyleGAN (Karras et al.,
852   2019) and DiffAE (Preechakul et al., 2022)) for hybrid domain adaptation, limiting its applicability to
853   a broader range of generators. MTG and DiFa utilize GAN inversion, which restricts the applicability
854   to generators similar to StyleGAN. Conversely, UniHDA is not constrained by the type of generators,
855   allowing for its broader application across various generators.
