                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Weight initialization schemes have been devised with heavy emphasis in the ini-
010
               tial training dynamics, assuming the optimizer automatically handles appropriate
011            step sizes in prolonged training. The optimizer typically calculates the step sizes
012            using a single, global learning rate across all parameters, focusing exclusively on
013            the (exponentially averaged) in-training time gradient. Motivated from hierarchi-
014            cal structure inherent in deep networks, this work explores assigning non-adaptive
015            layer-wise learning rates based on the differences in gradient magnitude at initial-
016            ization as a practical and effective optimization strategy. The gradient magnitude
017            used to preset the layer-wise learning rates is measured at fan-in initialization, as
018            stable activation variance is considered a desirable property during training, and
019
               so is assumed to largely hold true in prolonged training. Experiments on convo-
               lutional and transformer architectures show the proposed layer-wise learning rate
020
               can improve training stability and convergence in image classification and autore-
021
               gressive language modeling

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Gradient descent has become the de-facto method for training deep neural networks, with various
027   optimizers (e.g., SGD, RMSProp, and Adam) differing in how they determine the step size for up-
028   dating parameters from the calculated gradients. The weight update process via gradient descent
029   can be broadly divided into two stages: first, the optimizer determines the per-parameter step sizes,
030   and second, they are scaled by the learning rate, which is a hyperparameter. This two-stage process
031   reflects the empirical and theoretical nature of modern neural network training, where the hyperpa-
032   rameter is empirically determined and theoretically sound techniques are employed by the optimizer
033
      to ensure wide generalizability and minimize unnecessary hyperparameter tuning.
034   While adjusting the layer-wise learning rate was initially explored as a means to improve neural net-
035   work training (LeCun et al., 2002), adaptive step size scaling proved to be widely successful in im-
036   proving neural network training and has become the default gradient descent variation when training
037   in difficult settings (Duchi et al., 2011; Kingma & Ba, 2015). SGD can show strong performance in
038   low data regimes but can be more difficult to converge, making it an unattractive option when train-
039
      ing complicated networks on large datasets. Appropriate weight initialization is also important for
      successful training (LeCun et al., 2002; Glorot & Bengio, 2010), and both adaptive step-size scaling
040
      and intricate weight initialization schemes have been extensively explored. However, it is unclear
041
      to what extent commonly used theoretical assumptions carry on to empirical optimizer performance
042   (Tran et al., 2024) and the architectural implications on training continue to be an active area of
043   research (Kunstner et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a).
044
045
      Modern architectures are characterized by the use of multiple linear and nonlinear operations before
      the final output, and efficient hierarchical learning is an important factor for the success of neural
046
      networks (Chen et al., 2020; Abbe et al., 2021; Allen-Zhu & Li, 2023). Layer depth is known to re-
047
      sult in different convergence speeds for convolutional architectures (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014; Yosinski
048   et al., 2014), and transfer learning and finetuning settings have made wide use of explicit layer-wise
049   learning rates to train selected layers for improved performance (Donahue et al., 2014; Sharif Raza-
050   vian et al., 2014; Girshick et al., 2014; Kumar et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2019; Ro &
051   Choi, 2021; Howard & Ruder, 2018; Bao et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023). The justification is that when
052   finetuning on a smaller dataset, further training of general-purpose, low-level layers that are close
053   to the input is likely to overfit and worsen performance compared to only training the specialized,
      high-level layers that are close to the output. Given that intermediate feature characteristics are in


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      many respects hard-coded in architecture, it is reasonable to expect that explicit layer-wise learning
055   rates can also be beneficial when training from scratch.
056
057   This work focuses on the simplest version that assigns static learning rates across the entire training
058
      duration, as architectural characteristics such as input-output dimension, nonlinear activation func-
      tions, and layer-wise connectivity are static and do not change during training. From the observation
059
      that gradient magnitude at initialization correlates with commonly accepted layer-wise convergence
060
      in convolutional networks, a scheme that assigns relative layer-wise learning rates with respect to
061   the gradient magnitude at initialization can be devised by interpreting the gradient magnitude at
062   initialization as a measure of architecture-induced convergence bias that persists during prolonged
063   training.
064
      The gradient magnitude used as a basis to adjust the layer-wise learning rate is measured when layer
065
      weights are initialized to preserve the activation variance, as stable activation variance is widely
066
      considered a desirable property (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015; Brock et al., 2021; Roberts et al., 2022).
067   After measuring the gradient magnitude, the relative layer-wise learning rates are initialized to be
068   opposite the layer-wise gradient magnitude. By regularizing the architecture-induced convergence
069   bias, it can be considered as a preventative measure to the exploding gradient problem by extrap-
070   olating from statistics at initialization, making it of conceptually different approach from methods
071   that aim to explicitly normalize or clip the gradient during training (Zhang et al., 2018b; 2020).
072
      Experiments show improved performance and training stability on ImageNet-1k classification and
073   124M GPT-2 autoregressive language modeling. The improved convergence is represented in the
074   ability to handle higher learning rates and achieve lower training loss, which is an intriguing phe-
075   nomenon given the simplicity of the method compared to techniques that involve continuous tracking
076   of per-parameter gradient statistics. Inspecting the actual layer-wise learning rates values shows that
077   low learning rates are assigned to low-level layers and high learning rates to deeper, high-level layers,
078   but with additional subtleties and details as they are assigned with per-layer granularity. Altogether,
079   the empirical results suggest that adjusting the layer-wise learning rate is a simple yet effective
080   method for improving convergence, requiring negligible in-training time memory and computation
081
      overhead while being compatible with contemporary adaptive methods.

083   2   R ELATED W ORK
084
085   Weight initialization. Weight initialization schemes were proposed as a solution to the explod-
086   ing/vanishing activation/gradient problem in vanilla feed-forward networks (Glorot & Bengio,
087   2010). He et al. (2015) additionally incorporates the effect of subsequent ReLU into weight ini-
088   tialization, and for residual connections, Zhang et al. (2019b) and De & Smith (2020) suggest
089   downscaling the branch of residual blocks due to the increasing activation variance with depth. For-
090
      mal explanations have been developed on the trainability of deep networks using mean field theory
      (Poole et al., 2016; Schoenholz et al., 2017; Xiao et al., 2018), and orthogonal weight initialization
091
      demonstrated the ability to convolutional networks of extreme depths, albeit it does not necessarily
092
      outperform the shallower counterparts (Saxe et al., 2014; Xiao et al., 2018).
093
094   In practice, basic initialization schemes are widely used in conjunction with normalization layers
095   (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015; Ba et al., 2016; Zhang & Sennrich, 2019) to train deep networks. Some
096
      explanations for the success of normalization methods include prevention of activation explosion
      (Bjorck et al., 2018), smoothening of the optimization landscape (Santurkar et al., 2018), and pre-
097
      vention of rank collapse (Daneshmand et al., 2020). The rise of recent transformer networks spurred
098
      its dedicated initialization analysis (Xiong et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2020; Zhang
099   et al., 2019a), while other works have argued for the need of architecture agnostic initialization
100   schemes (Dauphin & Schoenholz, 2019; Zhu et al., 2021).
101
102   Layer-wise optimization. Many optimizers that involve layer-wise elements have been proposed,
103   such as directly using the layer-wise gradient history (Singh et al., 2015) or incorporating additional
104   mechanisms such as gradient normalization and weight norm scaling (You et al., 2017; Yu et al.,
105   2017; Zhou et al., 2019; You et al., 2020; Heo et al., 2021; Bernstein et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021a).
106   Another direction previous works indirectly modified the layer-wise learning rate is through the use
107   of scale factors, most commonly included at the residual branches (Karras et al., 2018; Hayou et al.,
      2021; Liu et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2021; Noci et al., 2022). Such scale factors can affect both the


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      gradient size and effective weight deviation of the layer, resulting in different effects depending on
109   whether the optimizer scales the step size depending on the gradient magnitude or variance (Balles &
110   Hennig, 2018). This work differs in that the architecture remains unmodified and instead modifies
111   the layer-wise learning rates at initialization, so individual layers are updated with different step
112   sizes even if the gradient or weight statistics at training time were to be identical.
113
      Efficient methods to adapt the Hessian into the optimizer have been explored as a promising candi-
114
      date to improve training (Martens & Grosse, 2015; Gupta et al., 2018; Yao et al., 2021; Liu et al.,
115   2024) and meta-learning settings made use of layer-wise hyper gradient to improve performance
116   and efficiency (Antoniou et al., 2019; Baik et al., 2020; Tang et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023b). Other
117   contexts for exploring per-layer learning rates include curriculum learning (Croitoru et al., 2024),
118   efficient hyperparameter transfer (Yang et al., 2021), heavy-tailed self-regularization theory (Zhou
119   et al., 2023) and for reducing the memory footprint of AdamW (Zhang et al., 2024b).

      3     BACKGROUND
122
123
      Gradient descent is an algorithm derived from the principle that for a smooth and differentiable loss
124   function L defined with respect to parameters θ, moving in the direction of the negative gradient
125   of L at θ will decrease the function’s value most rapidly. This negative gradient direction formally
126   refers to −∇θ L(θ).
127
      Many loss functions are defined in convex or pseudoconvex to facilitate convergence to a minimum
128
      when maximizing the negative likelihood of the data distribution. To optimize the parameters to-
129
      wards the minimum, the parameters θk are updated at each iteration step k by moving against the
130   gradient of the loss function as follows:
131
132
133
                                          θk+1 = θk − γ∇θk L(θk ),                                    (1)
134   where γ ∈ R+ is the learning rate controlling the step size for each update. Typically, well-known
135   optimizers process the gradient before it is multiplied by the learning rate, as shown below:
136
                                           θk+1 = θk − γO(∇θk L(θk )),                                      (2)
137
138   where O(·) represents the optimizer-specific preprocessing performed to calculate the step size. For
139   example, for Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) with momentum m, the process can be represented
140   as follows:
141                              O(∇θk L(θk )) = vk ;       vk = mvk−1 + ∇θk L(θk ).                        (3)
142
143   Here, the initial v0 is initialized to zero vector.

145   4     L AYER - WISE L EARNING R ATES
146
147   The parameters θ in a deep neural network can be naturally grouped into different parameter blocks
148   according to their layer-wise structure. Let θ = {ω1 , · · · , ωL }, where L is the total number of
149   layers. In this work, we consider layer-wise learning rates defined as multiplying the learning rate
150   by relative learning rate values ηl for each parameter block ωl , corresponding to the parameters of a
151   l-th layer as follows:
152                                     ωlk+1 = ωlk − γηl O(∇ωlk L(ωlk )).                               (4)
153
      Assigning a unique layer-wise learning rate for each l-th layer ηl , which does not change throughout
154   training, incurs negligible overhead, and is supported by popular frameworks.

182          Adaptive methods come at the cost of extra memory used to store the per-parameter gradient history
183          and treat all parameters in a unified manner. Per-layer analysis can be a convenient middle ground
184          for interpreting and manipulating neural networks as it has a lower dimension to search compared
185          to per-parameter analysis (Bengio et al., 2006; Baldock et al., 2021; Pan et al., 2023). Given the
186          clear layer-wise trend of adaptive methods when training from scratch, it is natural to investigate the
187          simplest version that assigns static learning rates across the entire training duration.
188
189          Generalization. Using high learning rates has been associated with beneficial regularizing effects
190
             at the cost of potential difficulty in convergence (Goyal et al., 2017; Li et al., 2019; Lu et al., 2024),
             but is not immediately clear how it can be leveraged to a layer-wise level. Heavier tails in the
191
             empirical spectral density of individual layers have been linked with better generalization Mahoney
192
             & Martin (2019), and Zhou et al. (2023) fits a power law distribution on the layer-wise empirical
193          spectral density in order to extract a layer-wise generalization measure and adjusts the layer-wise
194          learning rate accordingly. The assigned learning rates show a low to high trend throughout most of
195          the training.
196
197          Balanced training. Balancing convergence of the weights to ensure stable training is a common
198          motivation for modifying the learning rate, and convergence has been measured by tracking gradi-
199          ent, weight, neuron output or loss (Raghu et al., 2017; Bragagnolo et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2022; Du
200          et al., 2022). The various methods to measure convergence show the difficulty in determining if the
201          current weight is close to the final value before training has ended since weights considered to have
202
             converged may require further training as the remaining weights are modified (Bragagnolo et al.,
             2022). Preemptive freezing of selected weights may also make the network brittle to weight devia-
203
             tions of those frozen prematurely, resulting in reduced generalization. While the use of in-training
204
             time statistics is extensively explored in various literature, it may not appropriately incorporate more
205          global aspects of the training dynamics that are not apparent from the immediate gradient history
206          (Micaelli & Storkey, 2021), and leveraging ahead-of-time information can be an effective comple-
207          mentary method.
208

      4.2   R EGULARIZING A RCHITECTURE - INDUCED C ONVERGENCE B IAS
242
243
244   This work takes on the view that architectural characteristics are reflected in the gradient as it is
245   back-propagated through the network and that the difference in gradient magnitude when layers are
246   initialized to preserve its immediate activation variance represents the layer-wise convergence bias
247
      due to architectural factors. After measuring the layer-wise gradient magnitude at initialization,
      the relative learning rates can be adjusted to counteract the differences in convergence bias. It
248
      effectively extrapolates from statistics at initialization when the network has not undergone any
249
      training and under the assumption that the activation scale would not deviate far from initialization
250   during prolonged training.
251
252   Algorithm 1 outlines the proposed layer-wise learning rate initialization. It starts by initializing all
253
      convolution/linear weights from random numbers sampled from N (0, 1/fin ), where fin is the fan-
      in number. It is a basic initialization scheme that preserves the activation variance if the weights
254
      and gradients are i.i.d. (Glorot & Bengio, 2010). As standard, scale layers are initialized to 1 and
255
      bias layers to 0. The class token, position embedding, and relative position layers in transformer
256   architectures are treated as biased and initialized to 0. The weight-tied   embedding/head layer of
257
                                                                        p
      language models is initialized with a standard deviation of (1 + 1/fin )/2 as a middle ground of
258   ensuring unit normalized input to the network and the fan-in initialization of the head layer.
259
260   Next, the layer-wise gradient magnitude is collected for T iterations while the model weights are
261
      fixed at initialization. The gradient magnitude is collected per parameter as the learning rate is
      applied on a per-parameter basis, and the relative layer-wise learning rate is adjusted inversely pro-
262
      portional to the square root of the gradient magnitude and then normalized. As the average per-
263
      parameter gradient magnitude per layer is used, the method is not sensitive to the value of T . For
264   ResNet-50, using a T of 1 iteration instead of 5004 results in a max 11.02%, average 2.66% de-
265   viation in the assigned layer-wise learning rates, which would not significantly impact the network
266   performance compared to other random factors. 5004 is the number of iterations for 1 epoch when
267   training with a batch size of 256. We would like to note that the square root in line 9 is neces-
268   sary for the proposed learning rate to be beneficial (see Table 2). The intuition is that architectural
269   convergence bias is partially represented in the in-training time gradient, thus requiring a weaker
      scaling.

      5     E VALUATION
291
292
293
      The proposed layer-wise learning rate scheme is evaluated on ImageNet-1k and CIFAR-100 image
      classification and on autoregressive language modeling. The experiments demonstrate that appropri-
294
      ate layer-wise learning rates can improve convergence on both various optimizers and architecture
295
      archetypes despite the simplicity and non-existent overhead of using static relative layer-wise learn-
296   ing rates over the entire training run. We also inspect the assigned layer-wise learning rates, which
297   show a trend to increase according to depth along with other intricacies. Ablation studies on the de-
298   sign choices of the layer-wise learning rate assigning algorithm are performed in small-scale dataset
299   overfitting experiments in autoregressive language modeling.

303   Experiment settings. We perform experiments on ResNet-50 and ViT-S/16 when training from
304   scratch on basic Inception-style preprocessing and strong data augmentation. We would like to note
305   the aim is not to achieve SOTA results but to evaluate on generic settings. For hyperparameters
306   such as initial learning rate, effective batch size, and stochastic depth, we mostly rely on the values
307   reported in Chen et al. (2023a). When training ResNet-50 with SGD, we use a batch size of 256
308   and weight decay of 1e-4 similar to Goyal et al. (2017). For strong data augmentation, we use a
309   combination of RandAugment with layer 2 and magnitude 10 (Cubuk et al., 2020) and Mixup with
310
      strength 0.5 (Zhang et al., 2018a). More detailed settings are reported in the Appendix.

312   Results. Table 1 show that the proposed layer-wise learning rates can improve training, even for
313   SGD which is known to have strong performance when training convolutional networks under sim-
314   ple data preprocessing pipelines. The accuracy increase is particularly notable when training for 200
315
      epochs, improving by 0.96% from 77.24% to 78.19%. This has significant implications because it
      is a setting where SGD is known to already achieve higher accuracy compared to adaptive optimiz-

531         6             D ISCUSSION
532
533
            The proposed layer-wise learning rate adjusting scheme is conceptually simple and incurs very lit-
534         tle overhead, and empirical results demonstrate that it can substantially improve convergence when
535         used in conjunction with widely used optimizers. However, it is based on the assumption that extrap-
536         olating from statistics at initialization can be used to model long-term behaviors that are difficult to
537         measure exclusively from in-training time gradients. While there are reports that extremely large ac-
538         tivations exist in fully trained large models (Sun et al., 2024), such activations are relatively scarce
539         and most activations are reported to be stable. We believe that the simplicity and efficacy of the
            method make it a promising direction for analyzing and improving modern neural network training.

            ImageNet-1k setting details. The experiments were run using pytorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and
867         timm (Wightman, 2019) for transformer model architectures and data preprocessing. Pytorch auto-
868         matic mixed precision training with bfloat16 was used, which we found to significantly improve ViT-
869         S/16 performance when training with AdamW and basic augmentation compared to using float16
870         with loss scaling. We do not perform gradient clipping or label smoothing but include strong data
871         augmentations to demonstrate the effectiveness of appropriate layer-wise learning rates under more
872         difficult training settings.
873         All experiments were trained using a cosine decay learning rate schedule with a warmup, image res-
874         olution of 224 × 224, and default momentum/beta hyperparameters for SGD and AdamW. Gradient
875         accumulation was performed to simulate larger effective batch sizes. When adjusting the layer-wise
876         learning rates, the gradient was collected over an epoch, which corresponds to a T of 312∼5004,
877         depending on the batch size. All reported ImageNet-1k experiments took ∼150 GPU days of train-
878         ing.
