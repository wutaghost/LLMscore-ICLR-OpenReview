009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Neighborhood Search (LNS) is a common heuristic in combinatorial opti-
012            mization that iteratively searches over a large neighborhood of the current solu-
013            tion for a better one. Recently, neural network-based LNS solvers have achieved
014            great success in solving Integer Linear Program (ILP) problems with a learnable
015            policy for neighborhood selection, followed by an off-the-shelf ILP solver for re-
016
               optimization. Nonetheless, existing neural LNS solvers often get stuck in the same
               solution due to their greedy update strategy, i.e., only moving to the best solution
017
               found within the neighborhood. In this work, we try to theoretically identify the
018
               limitation of neural models in escaping the ”local optima”. Accordingly, we pro-
019            pose a novel sampling-enhanced neural LNS solver, namely SPL-LNS, by refor-
020            mulating LNS as a stochastic process, which uses a locally-informed proposal to
021            sample the next assignment and simulated annealing to alleviate the “local optima”
022            issue. We also develop a novel hindsight relabeling method to efficiently train
023            SPL-LNS on self-generated data. Experimental results reveal that our method
024            substantially surpasses prior neural LNS solvers on multiple ILP problems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
      Combinatorial Optimization (CO) problems present a set of fundamental challenges in computer
029
      science for decades (Papadimitriou and Steiglitz, 1998). Many of those problems can be formu-
030
      lated as generic Integer Linear Programs (ILPs), including supply chain management, logistics opti-
031   mization (Chopra and Meindl, 2001), workforce scheduling (Ernst et al., 2004), financial portfolios
032   (Mashayekh et al., 2017), compiler optimization (Trofin et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2022), bioin-
033   formatic problems (Gusfield, 1997), and more. Classic ILP solvers typically conduct a tree-style
034   search with the Branch-and-Bound (BnB) algorithm (Land and Doig, 2010), which finds the ex-
035   act solution by gradually reducing and finally closing the gap between the primal (upper) and dual
036   (lower) bounds of the searched solutions. Many state-of-the-art open-source and commercial ILP
037   solvers are of this kind, including SCIP (Achterberg, 2009), CPLEX (Cplex, 2009), and Gurobi
038   (Gurobi Optimization, 2021). However, when the problems are very large, completely closing the
039
      primal-dual gap can be intractable. Hence, solvers for large ILP problems have shifted efforts to-
      wards primal heuristics (Berthold, 2006), which are designed for finding the best possible solutions
040
      within a limited time window. That is, those heuristic primal solvers do not guarantee to find the
041
      optimal solutions, but aim to tackle large ILP problems with near-optimal solutions. Our work in
042   this paper belongs to the category of heuristic-driven primal solvers.
043
044   Large Neighborhood Search (LNS) is a typical heuristic strategy for finding high-quality solutions
045
      much faster than pure BnB (Ahuja et al., 2002). The process starts with a poor solution and iter-
      atively revises the current solution with the destroy and repair operations. In each iteration, the
046
      system selects (or destroys) a subset of the variables, then re-optimizes (or repairs) them while
047
      keeping the other variables unchanged. Finding good heuristics for the destroying and repairing
048   operators has been a central focus of the LNS-based solvers for ILP. Recently, neural network-based
049   LNS methods have shown great potential because they can learn from vast amounts of data. For ex-
050   ample, Song et al. (2020b) and Wu et al. (2021) presented a general neural LNS pipeline where the
051   destroy operator was parameterized with a learning-based neural policy, and the repair operator was
052   carried out by an off-the-shelf ILP solver. To obtain high-quality supervision, Sonnerat et al. (2021)
053   and Huang et al. (2023b) utilized a powerful expert heuristic called local branching (LB) (Fischetti
      and Lodi, 2003b), which guarantees to find the locally optimal destroy policy given enough solving

054
      time. When the neighborhood size is sufficiently large, LNS is believed to be free from the issue
055   of getting stuck in poor local optima (Sonnerat et al., 2021). Therefore, the greedy update with the
056   best-found solution in the neighborhood has been a common strategy in existing neural LNS solvers.
057
058
      However, we have found that in practice, such a greedy update strategy often traps the system in
      a “local optima”, in the sense that the chance for a destroy operator to find a suitable destroyed
059
      set leading to a better solution becomes extremely small, and the search would always stay around
060
      the same solution. How to overcome such a difficulty remains an open challenge for neural LNS
061   solvers for ILPs. In this paper, we propose a novel SamPLing-enhanced neural LNS method, namely
062   SPL-LNS, which draws a connection between LNS and the discrete Markov Chain Monte Carlo
063   (MCMC) with a locally-informed proposal (Zanella, 2017). By reformulating LNS as a stochastic
064   process, we tackle the “local optima” issue with simulated annealing (Kirkpatrick et al., 1983),
065   which has been proven to be effective in addressing local optima in other CO problems (Johnson
066   et al., 1991; Sun et al., 2023). The main difference in our proposed approach is that we sample the
067   next assignment from a set of feasible solutions within the neighborhood rather than greedily pick the
068   best one. We also develop a novel hindsight relabelling strategy to efficiently generate informative
069
      training data for our sampling-enhanced neural LNS. Specifically, we use a trained neural network
      to collect the LNS trajectories during the search process, and relabel the optimal destroyed variables
070
      for each sample in the trajectory to obtain high-quality supervision. These self-generated training
071
      data, combined with the supervision from the expert heuristic, can be used to train a more powerful
072   neural destroy policy under our sampling-enhanced LNS framework.
073
074   To summarize, our work tries to address a fundamental question in Combinatorial Optimization
075
      about what can be learnt through neural models and what cannot. Our theoretical analysis indicates
      that neural models could provide a strong heuristic in proposing the potential region containing high-
076
      quality solutions, but they lack the ability to escape the ”local optima”. Therefore, we tackle this
077
      limitation via a sampling-enhanced model. Our empirical evaluation shows that SPL-LNS consis-
078   tently outperforms the state-of-the-art neural LNS solvers and traditional heuristic methods on four
079   synthetic ILP benchmarks and a real-world ILP problem in multiple metrics.

      Implementation Details The training of SPL-LNS, IL-LNS and CL-LNS is kept the same across
325   all datasets. We use 200 ILP training instances and 50 validation instances to generate the training

      4.4   A BLATION S TUDY
438
439
      To further examine the effectiveness of our sampling method, we conduct ablation studies over main
440   components of our SPL-LNS algorithm. We conduct all our ablation studies on the CA-S dataset.
441
442   We first investigate the sampling strategy in SPL-LNS, including the annealing factor and the effect
443
      of k. The usage of the top-k sampling makes SPL-LNS method highly robust to the choice of
      the annealing factor, as we show in Figure 6. Basically, choosing any value that is reasonably
444
      large than 0, which corresponding to no sampling, would take effect. We continue to analyze the
445
      effect of k by varying its value from {1, 3, 5, 10, 20} and visualize the performance change of SPL-
446   LNS in Figure 7. Remarkably, even k = 3 brings a significant improvement over the case where
447   k = 1. This observation verifies the importance of sampling in escaping the “local optima”. The
448   performance reaches its best when k = 10 and drops back when k = 20, where a clear slowdown
449   in the convergence speed can be observed. Such a trade-off in the exploration and exploitation is
450   actually expected, but we can see that tuning k for SPL-LNS is not hard and all values greater than
451   1 bring a significant improvement.
452   We then examine the effectiveness of our hindsight relabeling strategy. We fix the data collection
453   time as 10 hours and use LB and hindsight relabeling method to collect the training data separately.
454   We continue to train SPL-LNS-IL on the data collected by LB and name it as SPL-LNS+LB. We
455   compare its performance with SPL-LNS+R in Figure 8. It can be seen that SPL-LNS+LB brings
456   almost no improvement against SPL-LNS+IL due to its low collection efficiency, while SPL-LNS+R
457   shows a clear improvement.
458   Although the greedy update strategy is widely used, some nuanced differences exist in the practical
459   implementations. For example, Hottung and Tierney (2019) apply the rejection sampling on the best
460   solution found by the repair operator, Song et al. (2020b) and Wu et al. (2021) do not consider the
461   current solution during the greedy update, which corresponds to SPL-LNS with k = 1. All these
462   methods allow a chance for the LNS to accept an inferior solution, which may alleviate the “local
463   optima” problem to some extent. We compare SPL-LNS to these two update strategies with the
464   same neural policy trained by IL-LNS. We denote these two update strategies as IL-LNS (rej) and
465   SPL-LNS (k = 1) respectively. The comparative results are shown in Figure 9. It can be seen that
466
      both methods take effect in improving the performance of IL-LNS with a greedy update strategy,
      but their performance is still significantly worse than SPL-LNS-IL. We attribute this performance
467
      gap to the locally-informed proposal used by SPL-LNS, which balances the likelihood of the next
468
      candidate p(x′ ) and the reverse proposal q(x(t) |x′ ).
469
470   Finally, we verify whether the failure to find a better solution in the greedy update strategy is due to
471   the short solving time of the repair operator. We extend the solving time of the ILP solver in IL-LNS
472   from 2 minutes to 4 minutes and 8 minutes, and the results are presented in Figure 10. It can be
473
      seen that solving time has almost no effect on the final performance, which verifies our analysis in
      Section 3.1 that such a failure is due to the low chance to find a suitable destroyed set.

      5     R ELATED W ORK
487
488
      5.1   L EARNING P RIMAL H EURISTIC FOR ILP S
489
490
      The primal heuristics in ILPs aim to efficiently find high-quality feasible solutions. Diving and LNS
491   are two main classes of primal heuristics and traditional solvers typically adopt a mixture of different
492   variants of diving and LNS. Existing neural methods for primal heuristics mainly focus on heuristics
493   selection (Khalil et al., 2017; Hendel et al., 2019; Chmiela et al., 2021), neural diving (Nair et al.,
494   2020; Yoon, 2022; Han et al., 2023; Paulus and Krause, 2023) and neural LNS (Song et al., 2020a;
495   Addanki et al., 2020; Sonnerat et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2023b).
496
      LNS iteratively refines the solution by selecting a subset of decision variables (the neighborhood) to
497   optimize at each time. Recent neural LNS methods mainly focus on the learning of neighborhood
498   selection and leave the optimization to an off-the-shelf solver. Song et al. (2020a) partitioned the
499   variables into subsets via a neural model and then searched over each neighborhood sequentially.
500   Later, Wu et al. (2021) and Addanki et al. (2020) proposed more general frameworks for directly
501   predicting the variables to optimize at each iteration. Recently, supervised-learning-based methods
502   achieved state-of-the-art results by learning from the expert heuristic local branching through imi-
503   tation learning (Sonnerat et al., 2021) or contrastive learning (Huang et al., 2023b). However, none
504   of the existing methods have systematically studied the update rule in LNS. This is the first work to
505
      connect LNS with locally-informed proposals and propose a sampling-enhanced LNS method.
506
507   5.2   S AMPLING FOR C OMBINATORIAL O PTIMIZATION
508
509   Sampling-based methods (Metropolis et al., 1953; Hastings, 1970; Neal, 1996; IBA, 2001) have been
510   vastly applied in various CO problems (WANG et al., 2009; Bhattacharya et al., 2014; Tavakkoli-
511
      Moghaddam et al., 2007; Seçkiner and Kurt, 2007; Chen and Ke, 2004). However, previous meth-
      ods typically suffered from a slow convergence speed compared with learning-based methods due
512
      to their inefficient proposals. The recent advances in MCMC have revitalized sampling-based meth-
513
      ods and some of them have been successfully combined with neural models. Sun et al. (2023)
514   first demonstrated that sampling-based methods could outperform neural CO solvers when using a
515   locally-informed proposal. Sun and Yang (2023) and Li et al. (2023) successfully applied the dif-
516   fusion models on CO problems, whose generative process is based on Langevin dynamics (Welling
517   and Teh, 2011). However, the application of these sampling-based methods is greatly limited due to
518   their incapability to generate feasible solutions directly. Instead, our proposed SPL-LNS can solve
519   any CO problems formulated as ILPs and could be further extended to other non-linear or mixed
520   programs when provided with an off-the-shelf solver.
521
522
      6     C ONCLUSION & L IMITATION
523
524
525
      In this paper, we identify an important limitation of neural ILP solvers as the ”local optima” is-
      sue. To address this concern, we propose SPL-LNS, a novel sampling-enhanced neural LNS solver
526
      demonstrating a strong capability in solving ILPs by escaping the “local optima”. Specifically, we
527
      establish a theoretical understanding of the “local optima” problem in LNS, innovatively connect
528   LNS and locally-informed proposals, and address the “local optima” problem with the simulated
529   annealing approach. We also propose an efficient training strategy for this sampling-enhanced neu-
530   ral LNS solver. SPL-LNS is a generic framework that could be applied to various neural LNS solvers
531   in combinatorial optimization problems, and we expect to extend it to other domains.
532
      Although SPL-LNS is generally applicable and consistently shows improvement, it introduces ad-
533
      ditional design choices within the LNS framework that we have not systematically studied in this
534   work. An effective LNS algorithm should balance the goal of finding a good solution quickly and
535   continually improving the solution with extended search time. In this work, we adopt a relatively
536   greedy strategy for the destroy operator, following previous research, but apply our annealing ap-
537   proach solely to the repair operator. However, exploring different combinations of these strategies
538   could still yield valuable insights. Additionally, how to design a generic annealing strategy to apply
539   neural solvers on heterogeneous ILP problems remains a significant challenge. We intend to explore
      this aspect in our future research.
