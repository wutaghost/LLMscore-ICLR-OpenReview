009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We study online learning in constrained Markov decision processes (CMDPs) in
012            which rewards and constraints may be either stochastic or adversarial. In such
013            settings, Stradi et al. (2024b) proposed the first best-of-both-worlds algorithm
014            able to seamlessly handle stochastic and adversarial constraints, achieving optimal
015            regret and constraint violation bounds in both cases. This algorithm suffers from
016            two major drawbacks. First, it only works under full feedback, which severely
017
               limits its applicability in practice. Moreover, it relies on optimizing over the space
               of occupancy measures, which requires solving convex optimization problems,
018
               an highly inefficient task. In this paper, we provide the first best-of-both-worlds
019
               algorithm for CMDPs with bandit feedback.√      Specifically, when the constraints are
020
               stochastic, the algorithm achieves O(  e T ) regret and constraint violation, while,
                                                        √
021
               when they are adversarial, it attains O(
                                                      e T ) constraint violation and a tight fraction
022
               of the optimal reward. Moreover, our algorithm is based on a policy optimization
023
               approach, which is much more efficient than occupancy-measure-based methods.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Most of the learning problems arising in real-world scenarios involve an agent sequentially interacting
029   with an unknown environment. Markov decision processes (MDPs) (Puterman, 2014) have emerged
030   as the most natural models for such interactions, as they allow to capture the fundamental goal of
031   learning an optimal (i.e., reward-maximizing) action-selection policy for the agent. However, in most
032   of the real-world applications of interest, the learner also has to satisfy some additional requirements.
033
      For instance, in autonomous driving one has to avoid crashing with other cars (Isele et al., 2018), in ad
      auctions one must not deplete its allocated budget (He et al., 2021), while in recommendation systems
034
      offending items should not be presented to the users (Singh et al., 2020). In order to capture such
035
      requirements, constrained MDPs (CMDPs) (Altman, 1999) have been introduced. These augment
036   classical MDPs by adding costs that the agent is constrained to keep below some given thresholds.
037
038   Over the last years, online learning problems in episodic CMDPs have received a growing attention
039
      (see, e.g., (Efroni et al., 2020) for a seminal work in the field). These are problems in which the
      learner repeatedly interacts with the CMDP environment over multiple episodes. In such settings, the
040
      learner’s goal is to minimize the regret of not always selecting a best-in-hindsight policy that satisfies
041
      cost constraints, while at the same time ensuring that the cumulative violation of cost constraints does
042   not grow too fast over the episodes. Ideally, one would like that both the regret and the constraint
043   violation grow sublinearly in the number of episodes T .
044
045
      In online learning in episodic MDPs, two different assumptions on how rewards and costs are
      determined at each episode are possible. They can be selected either stochastically according to
046
      fixed (unknown) probability distributions or adversarially, meaning that no statistical assumption is
047
      made. Very recently, Stradi et al. (2024b) proposed the first best-of-both-worlds learning algorithm
048   for online learning in episodic CMDPs. Such an algorithm is able to seamlessly handle stochastic
049   and adversarial constraints, achieving optimal regret and violation bounds in both cases. However, it
050   suffers from two major drawbacks. First, it only works under full feedback, meaning that the learning
051   agent needs to observe rewards and costs defined over the whole environment after each episode.
052   This is extremely unreasonable in practice, where only some feedback along the realized trajectory
053   is usually available. Moreover, the algorithm works by optimizing over the space of occupancy
      measures, which requires solving a convex problem at every episode, an highly inefficient task.

077
078   In the following, we highlight the works that are mainly related to ours. Due to space constraints, we
079
      refer to Appendix A for a complete discussion about related works.
080   Online learning in MDPs has been widely studied both under stochastic settings (see (Auer et al.,
081   2008)) and adversarial ones (see (Neu et al., 2010)). In adversarial settings, two feedbacks are usually
082   investigated. In the full-feedback setting, the reward function (or loss) is entirely revealed at the end
083   of the episode.
                  √ In this case, Rosenberg & Mansour (2019b) show that it is possible to achieve an
084   optimal O(
               e T ) regret bound. In the more challenging bandit-feedback setting, with rewards revealed
085   along the traversed trajectory only, Jin et al. (2020) show that the optimal bound is still attainable.
086   As concerns MDPs with constraints, online learning has been studied mainly in the stochastic setting
087   (see Efroni et al. (2020) for a seminal work on the topic). As concerns adversarial settings, namely,
088   when the constraints are not assumed to be stochastic, there exists an impossibility result from Mannor
089   et al. (2009) that prevents from attaining sublinear regret and violation when the optimal solution is
090   computed with respect
                        √     to a policy that satisfies the constraints on average. Thus, many works focused
091   on achieving O( T ) regret and violation for adversarial rewards and stochastic constraints (see (Qiu
                     e
092   et al., 2020)) or non-stationary environments with bounded non-stationarity (see (Ding & Lavaei,
093   2023; Wei et al., 2023; Stradi et al., 2024c)).
094   Recently, Stradi et al. (2024b) showed the first best-of-both-worlds (with respect to the constraints)
095   algorithm for CMDPs. Precisely, the authors propose a primal-dual algorithm that optimizes over the
096   occupancy measure
                   √        space, under full feedback. When the constraints are stochastic, the algorithm
097   achieves O(
                e T ) regret and violation, both in the case in which rewards are adversarial and the one
                                                                                                       √
098   where they are stochastic. Contrariwise, in the adversarial setting, the algorithms attains O( e T)
099   violatios, and the no-α-regret property with α = ρ/H+ρ, where ρ is a suitably-defined Slater’s
100   parameter. Notice that this result is in line with the best-of-both-worlds results in the single-state
101   online constrained settings, e.g., (Castiglioni et al., 2022b).

      3     A P OLICY O PTIMIZATION P RIMAL -D UAL A PPROACH
223
      In this section, we provide the description of our algorithm. We resort to a primal-dual formulation of
224
      the CMDP problem, and we employ different regret minimizers to optimize over the primal space
225
      (namely, the policy space) and the dual one (that is, the Lagrangian variables space). Furthermore,
226   our primal algorithm is based on a policy optimization approach. Thus, the learning update is not
227   performed over the occupancy measure space, but state-by-state along the MDP structure. This allows
228   us to avoid solving a convex program at each episode (as it is the case in the algorithm by (Stradi
229   et al., 2024b)). As concerns the dual, we employ online gradient descent (OGD). We remark that our
230   algorithm does not require any knowledge of the Slater’s parameter ρ. Indeed, as we further discuss
231   in the rest of this work, we can show that the Lagrangian multipliers are automatically bounded given
232   specific no-regret properties of the primal and dual regret minimizers.

      In this section we provide further discussions on the works closely related to ours. We first provide
677
      some works in the field of unconstrained online MDPs (see (Auer et al., 2008; Even-Dar et al., 2009;
678   Neu et al., 2010) for some initial results on the topic). The setting studied in these works generally
679   differentiates the problem based on the nature of the losses (either stochastic or adversarial), the
680   knowledge of the transition probability, and the nature of the feedback. Usually two types of feedback
681   are considered: in the full-information feedback model, the entire loss function is observed after the
682   learner’s choice, while in the bandit feedback model, the learner only observes the loss due to the
683   chosen action.
684   Azar et al. (2017) study the problem of optimal exploration in episodic MDPs with unknown
685   transitions, stochastic losses and bandit feedback . The authors improve the previous result by Auer
686   et al. (2008)
                √ designing an algorithm whose upper bound on the regret match the lower bound for this
687   class, Õ( T ). Rosenberg & Mansour (2019b) studies the setting of episodic MDPs with adversarial
688   losses, unknown transitions, and full information feedback. In this case the authors present√an online
689   algorithm exploiting entropic regularization and providing a regret upper bound of Õ( T ). The
690   same setting is investigated when the feedback is bandit by Rosenberg & Mansour (2019a) who attain
691   a regret upper bound of the order of Õ(T 3/4 ), which is improved by Jin
                                                                             √ et al. (2020) by providing an
692   algorithm that achieves in the same setting a regret upper bound of Õ( T ). Finally, Luo et al. (2021)
693   provide an optimal policy optimization algorithm for adversarial MDPs with bandit feedback.
694
      In case of constrained problem, an fundamental result is presented by Mannor et al. (2009), who
695
      show that it is impossible to attain both sublinear regret and constraints violations when both the
696   losses and constraints are adversarial. To overcome such an impossibility result, Liakopoulos et al.
697   (2019) study a class of online learning problems with long-term budget constraints that can be chosen
698   by an adversary and they define a new notion of regret. The new learner’s regret metric introduces the
699   notion of a K-benchmark, i.e., a comparator that meets the problem’s allotted budget over any window
700   of length K. Castiglioni et al. (2022a;b) are the first to provide a best-of-both-worlds algorithm for
701   online learning problems with long-term constraints, being the constraints stochastic or chosen by an
      adversary.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      Constrained problems have been also studied in the context of CMDPs; however almost all previous
703   works focus on the setting where the constraints are chosen stochastically. Wei et al. (2018) study
704   the case of episodic CMDPs with known transition proabability, full-feedback, adversarial losses
705   and stochastic constraints. The algorithm presented by the   √authors attains an upper bound both for
706   constraints violation and for the regret of the order of Õ( T ) . Zheng & Ratliff (2020) present, in
707   the setting of stochastic losses and constraints, where the transition probabilities are known and the
708   feedback is bandit, an upper bound on the regret of their algorithm of the order of Õ(T 3/4 ), while the
709   cumulative constraint violations is guaranteed to be below a threshold with a given probability. Bai
710   et al. (2020) provide the first algorithm to achieve sublinear regret when the transition probabilities
711   are unknown, assuming that the rewards are deterministic and the constraints are stochastic with
712   a particular structure. Efroni et al. (2020) study the case where transition probabilities, rewards,
713   and constraints are unknown and stochastic, while the feedback is bandit. The authors propose
714
      two approaches to deal with the exploration-exploitation dilemma in episodic CMDPs guaranteeing
      sublinear regret and constraint violations. Qiu et al. (2020) provide a primal-dual approach based on
715
      optimism in the face of uncertainty. This work shows the effectiveness of such an approach when
716
      dealing with episodic CMDPs with adversarial losses and stochastic constraints, achieving both
717   sublinear regret and constraint violation with full-information feedback. Wei et al. (2023), Ding
718   & Lavaei (2023) and Stradi et al. (2024c) consider the case in which rewards and constraints are
719   non-stationary, assuming that their variation is bounded. Thus, their results are not applicable to
720   general adversarial settings. Stradi et al. (2024a), in the setting with adversarial losses, stochastic
721   constraints and partial feedback, achieve sublinear regret and sublinear positive constraints violations.
722   Finally, Stradi et al. (2024b) propose the first best-of-both-worlds algorithm for CMDPs, assuming
723   full feedback on the rewards and constraints.
