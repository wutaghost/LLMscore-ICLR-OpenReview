009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In the realm of computational physics, an enduring topic is the numerical solutions
012            to partial differential equations (PDEs). Recently, the attention of researchers has
013            shifted towards Neural Operator methods, renowned for their capability to approx-
014            imate “operators” — mappings from functions to functions. Despite the univer-
015            sal approximation theorem within neural operators, ensuring error bounds often
016            requires employing numerous Fourier layers. However, what about lightweight
017            models? In response to this question, we introduce DimOL (Dimension-aware
018            Operator Learning), drawing insights from dimensional analysis. To implement
019
               DimOL, we propose the ProdLayer, which can be seamlessly integrated into FNO-
               based and Transformer-based PDE solvers, enhancing their ability to handle sum-
020
               of-products structures inherent in many physical systems. Empirically, DimOL
021
               models achieve up to 48% performance gain within the PDE datasets. Further-
022            more, by analyzing Fourier components’ weights, we can symbolically discern
023            the physical signiﬁcance of each term. This sheds light on the opaque nature of
024            neural networks, unveiling underlying physical principles.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      The Neural Operator methods have come to vision within the ﬁeld of AI for science (AI4Sci), for
030   their excellent property of having the ability to approximate any operators (i.e. mapping from func-
031   tion space to another function space), while traditional neural networks would fail.
032
033
      However, are these models “scientiﬁc” enough for AI4Sci? By expanding the TorusLi dataset to
      TorusVisForce dataset, the F-FNO paper (Tran et al., 2023) has raised a crucial question on how
034
      the Neural Operator methods should perform when we hope the model could handle multiple back-
035
      ground situations (such as different viscosities in ﬂuid dynamic problems), which would require a
036   deeper comprehension of physics. However, the F-FNO paper still did not discuss this issue thor-
037   oughly: It lacks a comparative analysis of F-FNO model performance against other models, and
038   notably, F-FNO itself does not incorporate dimension awareness.
039
      This research would reveal one simple but profound fact: Models equipped with dimensional aware-
040
      ness tend to perform universally better. Accordingly, we propose Dimension-Aware Operator Learn-
041
      ing (DimOL) and see how to improve models dealing with physical quantities, including FNO-based
042   methods such as FNO (Li et al., 2021), F-FNO (Tran et al., 2023), T-FNO (Tran et al., 2023), and
043   non-FNO methods such as the Transformer-based LSM (Wu et al., 2023). Our research indicates
044   that dimension-aware models demonstrate improved performance as the variety of physical quan-
045   tities provided to the model increases. Speciﬁcally, within the TorusVisForce dataset proposed by
046   F-FNO (Tran et al., 2023) (which involves three physical quantities), dimension-aware models can
047   reduce the prediction error by up to 48% compared to the backbone models across both full-scale
048   dataset and few-shot tasks.
049   Building upon the widely recognized physical intuition that quantities with different units (or di-
050   mensions) cannot be directly added but can be sensibly multiplied, all of the improvements above
051   are achieved by encoding product terms between channels using a novel ProdLayer — a simple
052   plugin-and-use module designed in this work. Furthermore, we conduct comparisons between the
053   ProdLayer and alternative approaches such as quadratic layers, which theoretically could serve sim-
      ilar roles. However, our ﬁndings demonstrate that the ProdLayer outperforms these alternatives.

054
      In this work, we also demonstrate that dimensional awareness is not merely about randomly adding
055   some product term encoding to the base model. Empirically, most adaptations are effective only
056   when we consider the supposed dimensions of the latent vectors. For example, in FNO-based mod-
057   els, replacing the linear transform on the Fourier domain with a ProdLayer does not yield improve-
058   ment. This approach introduces a global convolution on the variable ﬁeld (F −1 (fˆĝ) = f ∗ g), which
059   lacks physical signiﬁcance in this context. Therefore, to adapt a model to incorporate DimOL, one
060   must carefully consider the physical meaning of the model’s latent space and proceed accordingly.
061
      We evaluate DimOL on classic physics simulation datasets, including regular mesh scenarios (Burg-
062
      ers, Darcy Flow, and Navier-Stokes) and irregular mesh settings (Inductor2D). We showcase the
063   effectiveness of DimOL compared to existing FNO-based and Transformer-based models.
064
065   Notably, quantifying and interpreting the proﬁts of considering dimensional awareness as a property
066
      of a model remains an open problem. One approach is to apply the model to simpler, more com-
      prehensible datasets, such as the evolution operator for the Burgers equation in Section 4.7. This
067
      allows for identifying which dimensions are associated with the ProdLayer by analyzing the Fourier
068
      weights. However, for more complex datasets, the relationships among the Fourier weights may not
069   have clear symbolic interpretations, making it difﬁcult to analyze their internal connections. Another
070   way to investigate the beneﬁts of introducing dimensional awareness involves assessing the model’s
071   accuracy on similarly transformed datasets, as discussed in Section 4.6. It is more appropriate to
072   view dimensional awareness as a novel and effective design principle for neural operators, rather
073   than as a deﬁnitive scientiﬁc claim about a model’s capacity to perceive dimensions.
074
      Another advantage of integrating dimensional awareness with operator learning—consistent with
075   our scientiﬁc common sense—is its potential to reverse-engineer the meanings of latent variables
076   and discover their symbolically meaningful relationships. This ability is effectively demonstrated
077   through the experiments presented in this paper.

081   2.1   N UMERICAL PDE S OLVERS
082
083   Traditional methods (Grossmann, 2007; Ŝolín, 2005; Ciarlet, 2002; Courant et al., 1967; Cooley
084
      et al., 1969; Gottlieb & Orszag, 1977; Fornberg, 1998; Kopriva, 2009) for addressing partial dif-
      ferential equation (PDE) systems encompass techniques like ﬁnite element methods (FEMs), ﬁnite
085
      difference methods (FDMs), ﬁnite volume methods (FVMs), and pseudo-spectral methods, includ-
086
      ing Crank-Nicholson and Carpenter-Kennedy. These approaches typically involve discretizing the
087   spatial domain, where achieving higher precision requires ﬁner discretizations, leading to greater
088   computational demands. To manage these costs, historically, speciﬁc PDEs have been addressed
089   using streamlined models such as Reynolds averaged Navier-Stokes and large eddy simulations. In
090   recent developments, machine learning has emerged as a viable option for expediting simulations.
091
092   2.2   M ACHINE L EARNING M ETHODS FOR PDE S
093
      Equation-constrained methods. These methods usually follow the PINN (Raissi et al., 2019; Lu
094
      et al., 2021b; Karniadakis et al., 2021) paradigm, where they move all the terms of constraining equa-
095   tions to the left side, square left-hand-side up, and take it as the objective function (Yu et al., 2018;
096   Wang et al., 2022; 2021). However, This method does not generalize well on different boundary
097   conditions (BC) and initial conditions (IC). Another fatal drawback is that you can only apply this
098   method with the knowledge of the dynamic system, including the dynamic equations, BC, and IC,
099   but one major effect of machine learning is to learn from real-world data, which is not feasible for
100   equation-constrained methods. Thus, this paper will focus on Neural Operator methods and other
101   state-of-the-art Transformer-based methods instead.
102   Neural operators. Operator Learning has recently become a heated topic within AI for Science.
103   There are mainly two branches of Operator Learning methods: One is based on DeepONet (Lu
104   et al., 2021a), and the other is based on Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) (Li et al., 2021), both of
105   which have been mathematically proven to have the capability to approximate any operators. Deep-
106   ONet constructs a branch network and a trunk network derived from the universal approximation
107   theorem (Chen & Chen, 1995) to process the input functions and query points, while FNO utilizes
      discrete Fourier transforms to approximate continuous Fourier transform, which can represent kernel


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      integral operators. FNO can also gain a remarkable trade-off between cost and accuracy in practice
109   due to its quasi-linear time complexity. So in this paper, we would mainly discuss FNO-based meth-
110   ods (Tran et al., 2023; Kossaiﬁ et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2022; Wen et al., 2022; Ashiqur Rahman
111   et al., 2022) for Neural Operators. Other typical FNO-based methods include: Factorized Fourier
112   Neural Operator (F-FNO) (Tran et al., 2023) and Tensorized Fourier Neural Operator (T-FNO) (Kos-
113   saiﬁ et al., 2023). F-FNO is based on doing 1D Fourier transforms along axes respectively to reduce
114   the number of parameters, while T-FNO focuses on using tensor factorizations (such as Tucker fac-
115   torization). However, these works are not scalable to handle problems with multiple types of input
116   functions. Another line of work (Cao, 2021) proposes to use the attention mechanism (Vaswani
117
      et al., 2017) for learning operators.
118   Transformer-based methods. Transformers (Katharopoulos et al., 2020; Schlag et al., 2021;
119   Xiong et al., 2020; 2021; Shen et al., 2021; Nguyen & Salazar, 2019) have been long proven to
120   be an effective module in many machine learning tasks. Recent research has shown that this is
121
      also true for physics-simulation tasks. Latent Spectral Method (LSM) (Wu et al., 2023) uses sine
      functions to approximate any function guaranteed by the theorem of Convergence of Trigonometric
122
      Approximation (Dyachenko, 1995), and utilizes an U-Net structure (Ronneberger et al., 2015) to
123
      handle multi-scale problems. General Neural Operator Transformer (GNOT) (Hao et al., 2023), on
124   the other hand, designed a novel heterogeneous normalized attention layer to handle the grids and in-
125   put functions ﬂexibly. Both of them have remarkable performances, but we are still not sure whether
126   they can approximate any operators like DeepONet and FNO do (instead of merely approximating
127   functions), so we put them in the category of Transformer-based methods.

139   3.1   I NTUITION OF D IMENSIONAL AWARENESS
140
      Before introducing a speciﬁc model, it is important to understand what dimensional awareness is
141
      and why we believe it is crucial in solving partial differential equations (PDEs) in physical systems.
142
143   Dimensional awareness is a methodology that involves recognizing and incorporating the intrinsic
144   dimensions and units of physical quantities into the design of AI models. This approach ensures that
145   the model respects the physical laws governing the system, leading to more accurate and reliable
146
      predictions. Consequently, we believe that dimensional awareness would be a new dimension that
      we must consider when designing a new model for physical simulation.
147
148   In the context of solving PDEs, dimensional awareness allows the model to explicitly encode re-
149   lationships and interactions between different physical quantities. For instance, in many physical
150   systems, variables are often related through products and ratios that have speciﬁc dimensional prop-
151   erties. Traditional AI models may overlook these crucial relationships, leading to suboptimal results.
152
      In contrast, by encoding these product terms into the neural network, the model can better capture
      the underlying physics of the system, resulting in improved performance and generalization.
153
154   Below, we will use an example to brieﬂy demonstrate why simply encoding product terms would
155   improve model performance. Let’s consider a classic prediction task in physics: predicting the future
156   state of a physical ﬁeld given its current state. The goal is to input a ﬁeld of a physical quantity at
157   a given moment and predict the same ﬁeld after a certain period. This system is governed by a
158
      dynamic equation, often expressed as:

185

      3.3     FNO-BASED M ODELS WITH P ROD L AYER
258   For FNO-based models, introducing product-term encoding via the ProdLayer enables the model
259   to approximate explicit numerical methods more accurately. This is particularly beneﬁcial when
260   the evolution operator takes the form of sum-of-products, a common scenario in complex dynamic
261   systems. As illustrated in Figure 1(b), we choose to replace the MLP layers of the original FNO with
262   the ProdLayer. This modiﬁcation allows the model to directly encode product terms, enhancing its
263   ability to capture the intrinsic relationships between physical quantities.
264   In our implementation, we adopt the backbone of the T-FNO. This choice offers the ﬂexibility to
265   switch between using tensor factorization and the original FNO, providing a versatile framework
266   for experimentation. When tensor factorization is not used, the model functions as a standard FNO.
267   We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach by presenting uniformly enhanced performance
268   with the ProdLayer. By analyzing the Fourier weights, we provide insights into how the network
269   conceptualizes the physical quantities involved. This analysis is particularly enlightening in the
      context of a toy example related to the Burgers equation, discussed in Section 4.7.

282   3.4    T RANSFORMER -BASED M ODELS WITH P ROD L AYER
283
      The Latent Spectral Method (LSM) is a cutting-edge Transformer-based model designed for solving
284
      PDEs. Its basic innovation is the incorporation of Sine functions into the network, allowing it to
285   approximate any (periodic) function within a speciﬁc spectral range. For non-periodic components,
286   LSM utilizes convolution-based neural blocks. The overall structure of LSM is akin to a U-Net,
287   enabling it to learn interactions between different patches of the domain at varying scales.
288
      Inspired by the separation of variables method, we hypothesize that the projection in the latent spec-
289
      tral space can be enhanced if terms can multiply Sine or Cosine functions directly, rather than merely
290
      adding them as biases. This modiﬁcation aligns with the core principle of DimOL, which aims to
291   improve model performance by encoding product terms. To implement this idea, we replace the lin-
292   ear transformation in the decoder of LSM with a ProdLayer, as shown in Figure1(c). This enhanced
293   architecture retains the original strengths of LSM while integrating the ProdLayer to facilitate im-
294   proved spectral projections, allowing the model to handle product terms more effectively. Given this
295   intuition, a larger number of product terms for each ProdLayer is beneﬁcial and can enhance the
296   model’s capacity to capture complex interactions.

411   4.6    F EW-S HOT L EARNING S TUDIES ON O PERATOR P ROPERTIES
412
413   We aim to develop a model that approximates an operator while preserving key properties of the
414   original operator. If successful, the model should generalize well in few-shot learning scenarios. To
415
      evaluate this, we design three experimental settings:
416   • Datasets: We construct two subsets of the TorusVisForce dataset, named TorusVisForce-TopMu
417     and -LowMu. These datasets contain 200 and 100 data sequences respectively with the highest
418     and lowest viscosity values ranging in [8.2 × 10−5 , 1 × 10−4 ] and [1 × 10−5 , 1.8 × 10−5 ].
419   • Few-shot training: We use 100 random samples from TorusVisForce-TopMu as the training set.
420   • In-set testing: We ﬁrst perform in-distribution testing on the other partition of 100 unseen training
421     samples from the TorusVisForce-TopMu subset.
422   • Partial-OOD testing: We generate a partially OOD test set by scaling the training data using a
423     coefﬁcient k. Here, k is an integer applied to the physical quantities ω, f , and ν.
424
      • OOD testing: We ﬁnally use TorusVisForce-LowMu as the test set to evaluate the model’s gener-
425
        alization ability to OOD data.

      5     C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
514
515   In this paper, we introduced DimOL as an adaptation for major models tailored to solving high-
516   dimensional PDEs. These include FNO-based models and Transformer-based models such as LSM
517   and GNOT. We discovered that dimensional awareness not only makes sense for human physicists
518   but also machine learning models, especially when the task involves actual physical quantities as in-
519   put. Our research indicates that this conclusion applies across a wide range of models and datasets.
520   By enhancing the model’s symbolic meaning, DimOL models are better suited for reverse engineer-
521   ing to uncover terms with potential physical signiﬁcance. While we currently use ProdLayer for
522
      DimOL, we believe that DimOL is more than this. It should be regarded as a new “dimension” to
      consider when designing AI-for-Science models.
523
524   A potential limitation of DimOL could be its effectiveness in tasks where the input data lacks clear
525   dimensional information or where the relationship between dimensions is highly complex or nonlin-
526   ear. In such cases, DimOL may not offer signiﬁcant improvements.
