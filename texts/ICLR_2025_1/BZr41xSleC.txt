009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Most Graph Neural Networks are based on the principle of message-passing,
012            where all neighboring nodes exchange messages with each other simultaneously.
013            We want to challenge this paradigm by introducing the Flood and Echo Net, a
014            novel architecture that aligns neural computation with the principles of distributed
015            algorithms. In our method, nodes sparsely activate upon receiving a message,
016            leading to a wave-like activation pattern that traverses the graph. Through these
017            sparse but parallel activations, the Net becomes more expressive than traditional
018
               MPNNs which are limited by the 1-WL test and also is provably more efficient
               in terms of message complexity. Moreover, the mechanism’s ability to generalize
019
               across graphs of varying sizes positions it as a practical architecture for the task
020
               of algorithmic learning. We test the Flood and Echo Net on a variety of synthetic
021            tasks and find that the algorithmic alignment of the execution improves general-
022            ization to larger graph sizes. Moreover, our method significantly improves gener-
023            alisation and correct execution in terms of graph accuracy on the SALSA-CLRS
024            benchmark.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The message-passing paradigm has become the cornerstone of graph learning, with Message Passing
030   Neural Networks (MPNNs) emerging as a dominant framework. In these networks, nodes iteratively
031   update their states by simultaneously exchanging messages with all neighboring nodes, providing the
032   necessary flexibility to process arbitrary graph topologies. However, this simple procedure requires
033
      a considerable amount of compute, as in every round, messages have to be sent over all edges.
      Moreover, all nodes throughout the entire graph have to update their state. Even though it might
034
      be that the majority of the nodes do not play an active part in the computation and should maintain
035
      their current state, resulting in unnecessary computations. This phenomenon is potentially amplified
036   if the network is applied to larger graphs, as the number of rounds usually has to be increased in
037   order to capture non local interactions between nodes. We challenge this paradigm and instead base
038   the mechanism on another principle, an algorithm design pattern commonly found in distributed
039   computing. We focus on the mechanism’s ability to generalize to larger graph instances which we
040   evaluate in the context of neural algorithmic reasoning on graphs.
041
      We propose a new execution framework, the Flood and Echo Net (FE Net). While still rooted in
042   message-passing, our approach employs a distinct message exchange strategy inspired by the flood-
043   ing and echo algorithmic design pattern from the field of distributed computing. The computation
044   unfolds in two phases, initiated by a single origin node. First, messages are propagated away from
045   the origin towards the rest of the graph. In this flooding part nodes only send messages to nodes
046   that are farther away from the origin. Once all nodes have received a message, the propagation flow
047   reverses. Now, nodes only send messages to neighbors closer to the origin, starting with the nodes
048   that are farthest away. This process creates a wave-like activation pattern that expands equally in
049   all directions before returning to the origin, as illustrated in Figure 1. This unique activation pattern
050
      forms the core of the FE Net offering a more structured and algorithmically aligned computation at
      the level of message exchange.
051
052   A key feature of the FE Net’s execution is its sparse yet highly parallel activation pattern. At each
053   timestep, only a subset of nodes – those equidistant from the origin – are active, either sending
      messages outward or echoing them back. This approach enables the computation to involve the

      rithmic reasoning on graphs, where models must generalize learned algorithms across much larger
087
      graph sizes. When MPNNs are applied to graphs of larger sizes, they must usually adapt and scale
088
      the number of rounds to retain the same relative field of perception. In comparison, the execution
089   of the FE Net can generalize to graphs of larger sizes more naturally as the computation inherently
090   involves the entire graph. We hypothesize that the algorithmic alignment of the underlying mecha-
091   nism makes the FE Net particularly well-suited for algorithm learning. To validate this, we test our
092   method on a diverse set of algorithmic problems, including SALSA-CLRS - a benchmark specifi-
093   cally designed to evaluate scalable algorithmic reasoning on graphs. Our results demonstrate that the
094   FE Net significantly enhances the ability to generalize learned algorithms to larger graph instances.
095   Thus, the algorithmic alignment on the level of message passing offers a promising new direction
096   for the field of graph learning and specifically for algorithmic reasoning on graphs.
097   We outline our main contributions as follows:
098
099         • We introduce the FE Net, a new execution framework aligned with principles of distributed
100           algorithm design. The computation follows a special node activation pattern, which allows
101           it to facilitate messages more efficiently throughout the graph.
102
103         • We provide theoretical insights that through this alternative computation flow, the FE Net is
104           more expressive than common MPNNs and more efficient in terms of message complexity.
105
106         • We demonstrate that the algorithmic alignment of the architecture is beneficial for size
107           generalization in graph algorithm learning. This finding is empirically validated through
              extensive experiments on a variety of synthetic tasks and the SALSA-CLRS benchmark.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Originally proposed by Scarselli et al. (2008), Graph Neural Networks have seen a resurgence
111   with applications across multiple domains (Veličković et al., 2017; Kipf & Welling, 2016; Neun
112   et al., 2022). Notably, this line of research has gained theoretical insights through its connection
113   to message-passing models from distributed computing (Sato et al., 2019; Loukas, 2020; Papp &
114   Wattenhofer, 2022a). This includes strengthening existing architectures to achieve maximum ex-
115   pressiveness (Xu et al., 2018; Sato et al., 2021) or going beyond traditional models by changing the
116   graph topology (Papp et al., 2021; Alon & Yahav, 2021b). In this context, multiple architectures
117   have been investigated to combat information bottlenecks in the graph (Alon & Yahav, 2021a), i.e.
118
      using graph transformers (Rampasek et al., 2022). Note that our work is orthogonal to this, as we
      focus on message-passing on the original graph topology. Moreover, we investigate how specific
119
      information can be exchanged throughout the entire graph, which might be challenging even if no
120
      bottleneck is present. Similarly, higher order propagation mechanisms (Zhang et al., 2023b; Maron
121   et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2022) have been proposed to tackle this issue or gain more expressiveness.
122   While some of these approaches also incorporate distance information, this usually comes at the cost
123   of higher-order message-passing. Whereas our work emphasizes a simple execution mechanism on
124   the original graph topology. In recent work, even the synchronous message-passing among all nodes
125   has been questioned (Martinkus et al., 2023; Faber & Wattenhofer, 2023), giving rise to alternative
126   neural graph execution models.
127   How GNNs can generalize across graph sizes (Yehudai et al., 2021) and their generalization capabil-
128   ities for algorithmic tasks, attributed to their structurally aligned computation (Xu et al., 2020) has
129   been of much interest. This has led to investigations into the proper alignment of parts of the archi-
130   tecture (Dudzik & Veličković, 2022; Engelmayer et al., 2023; Dudzik et al., 2023). A central focus
131   has been on neural algorithmic reasoning, the study how such networks can learn to solve algorithms
132   (Veličković et al., 2022; Ibarz et al., 2022; Minder et al., 2023; Bohde et al., 2024; Numeroso et al.,
133   2023; Georgiev et al., 2024). Moreover, the ability to extrapolate (Xu et al., 2021) and dynamically
134   adjust the computation in order to reason when confronted with more challenging instances remains
135
      a key aspect (Schwarzschild et al., 2021; Grötschla et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2020).

137   3   F LOOD AND E CHO N ET
138
139   The fields of distributed computing and graph learning share a fundamental connection through their
140   use of message-passing-based computation. Despite some differences, the equivalence between cer-
141   tain models in these domains has been established (Papp & Wattenhofer, 2022a). This enables the
142   direct translation of results such as theoretical bounds on width, number of rounds, and approxima-
143
      tion ratios from the field of distributed computing to the study of GNNs (Sato et al., 2019; Loukas,
      2020). Furthermore, research has demonstrated that the alignment of neural network architectures
144
      with their underlying learning objectives can significantly enhance both performance and sample
145
      complexity (Xu et al., 2020; Dudzik & Veličković, 2022). This synergy between distributed com-
146   puting and GNNs raises an intriguing question: can we leverage additional insights from the dis-
147   tributed computing community to advance graph learning? Motivated by this potential, we propose
148   the Flood and Echo Net (FE Net), a novel execution framework that directly incorporates design
149   patterns from distributed algorithms. To illustrate the differences of our method, let us first review
150   the conventional MPNN approach. Whenever we refer to an MPNN throughout this paper, we will
151   refer to a GNN that operates on the original graph topology and exchanges messages in the following
152   way:

156   In this traditional approach, all nodes exchange messages simultaneously with all their neighbors in
157   every round. We challenge this paradigm by taking inspiration from a design pattern called flooding
158   and echo (Chang, 1982), a common building block in distributed algorithms (Kuhn et al., 2007).
159   This pattern introduces a two-phase process: first, messages are broadcast (flooded) throughout
160   the entire graph (Dalal & Metcalfe, 1978), and then information is gathered back (echoed) from
161   all nodes. This approach allows for more structured and potentially more efficient information
      propagation encompassing the entire graph.

188   The Flood and Echo Net aligns its computation flow directly with the flooding and echo design
189   pattern. The process begins at an origin node and proceeds through T phases, each comprising a
190   flooding and an echo part. Figure 2 provides a pseudo-code outline of the FE Net algorithm. Initially,
191   nodes are partitioned based on their distance from the origin, then the T phases are executed. During
192   the flooding phase, messages propagate outward from the origin. We iterate through distances in
193   ascending order, using two types of convolutions: FConv, which sends messages from nodes at
194   distance d − 1 to nodes at distance d. Crucially, only nodes at distance d update their state (denoted
195
      as x[d]), and FCrossConv, which exchanges messages between nodes at the same distance d. In the
      subsequent echo phase, the message flow reverses and is echoed back towards the origin. Now we
196
      iterate through distances in descending order, again using two types of convolutions: ECrossConv
197
      for updating nodes at the same distance, and EConv for sending messages from nodes at distance
198   d to nodes at distance d − 1, updating the latter. Note that usually, only a subset of nodes, which
199   are located at the same distance, are activated simultaneously. Therefore, FE Net can make use of a
200   sparse but parallel activation pattern that propagates throughout the entire graph. Figure 2 provides
201   a visual illustration of a complete phase, with colors indicating active edges and updated nodes. For
202   a more in-depth discussion of the FE Net, including a comparison with regular MPNNs and their
203   computation tree, we refer to Appendix A.
204
205   Modes of Operation The computation of the FE Net starts from an origin node. This allows for
206   different usages of the proposed method. In the following, we outline three different strategies,
207   which we will refer to as different modes of operations: fixed, random and all. Across all modes of
208   operation, once the origin is chosen, the same flooding and echo parts are executed to compute node
209   embeddings. These embeddings are then directly utilized for node classification tasks; for graph
210   classification, we aggregate the final predicted class probabilities of the individual nodes.
211   In the fixed mode, the origin is given or defined by the problem instance, i.e. by a marked source
212   node specific to the task. In contrast, the random mode selects an origin uniformly at random from
213   all nodes. In the all mode, we execute the FE Net once for every node. In every run, we keep only
214   the node embedding for the chosen origin. This can be seen as a form of ego graph prediction (Zhao
215   et al., 2021a) for each node. Although computationally more expensive, it could also be used for
      efficient inference on tasks where only a subset of nodes is of interest.

      In this section, we provide a theoretical analysis of the Flood and Echo Net. While the FE Net is
219   based on message-passing over the original graph topology, its unique propagation mechanism sets
220   it apart from conventional message-passing GNNs. Our analysis focuses on two critical aspects: ex-
221   pressiveness and message complexity. We demonstrate that the FE Net not only matches but exceeds
222   the expressiveness of regular MPNNs, surpassing the limitations of the 1-WL test. Furthermore, we
223   show that through the sparse activation of nodes the FE Net achieves improved efficiency in terms
224   of message complexity. This enables it to solve certain tasks with significantly fewer messages than
225   traditional MPNNs. The complete proofs of these theoretical insights are contained in Appendix E.

      In this section, we study the task of neural algorithmic reasoning and specifically graph algorithm
318   learning. The concept of an algorithm is best understood as a sequence of instructions that can
319   be applied to compute a desired output given the respective input. Algorithms have the inherent
320   advantage of generalization across their entire domain. If we want to multiply two numbers, we can
321   easily illustrate and explain the multiplication algorithm using small numbers. However, the same
322   procedure generalizes, i.e. the algorithm can be used to extrapolate and multiply together much
323   larger numbers using the same algorithmic steps. Neural algorithmic reasoning aims to grasp these
      underlying principles and incorporate them into machine learning architectures. The ultimate aim


                                                       6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      is to combine both domains to get models that can learn the algorithmic principles and generalize
325   them properly, even for unseen larger inputs.
326
327   A key challenge in studying generalization is properly adapting the architecture to larger problem
328
      sizes. Without adjustment, it might be that the amount of compute does not suffice to solve the
      task at hand, or in the case of graph tasks, that the required information is no longer located in the
329
      same receptive field, but is farther away. Therefore, a common strategy is to adjust the compute,
330
      or number of rounds, according to the increase of the problem size. The FE Net offers a distinct
331   alternative on how to generalize to larger graphs compared to regular MPNNs. In fact, during a
332   single phase, messages propagate throughout the entire graph and can, therefore, be updated using
333   information beyond the immediate neighborhood. Figure 4 visually highlights the difference of how
334   an MPNN and a FE Net generalises to larger graph topologies.
335
      Previous work has indicated that changes in the architecture or so-called “algorithmic alignment”
336
      (Engelmayer et al., 2023; Dudzik & Veličković, 2022; Xu et al., 2020) can be beneficial for learning
337   and generalization. In our work, we propose to incorporate such an alignment on the architectural
338   level, adjusting the message-passing itself to match the flooding and echo paradigm, an algorithm
339   design pattern from distributed computing. Therefore, we believe that the FE Net can improve
340   generalization for algorithm learning.
341
      In the following, we empirically validate our hypothesis on a variety of tasks related to graph algo-
342
      rithm learning. First, we test the architecture on synthetic algorithmic tasks, which allow us both
343   fine-grained control and theoretical insights into what is needed to solve the tasks at hand. Then,
344   we proceed to test our method on the more challenging SALSA-CLRS benchmark, which consists
345   of well-known graph algorithms and is specifically designed to test graph algorithms at scale. One
346   aspect how the method differs in generalisation is that in MPNNs each node performs more compu-
347   tations (as the number of rounds is increased to cover the graph). Whereas in the FE Net the number
348   of phases can remain unchanged. Then, for a single node, there is no change in the computation,
349   even though from a graph perspective more steps are executed. We believe that this behaviour can
350   be very helpful for generalising to larger graphs.

438   5.2   SALSA - CLRS
439
440   Building on our previous findings, where the FE Net architecture demonstrated strong generaliza-
441   tion to larger graph instances on simple algorithmic tasks, we now extend our evaluation to more
442   challenging and complex graph algorithms. Our goal is to assess whether the FE Net can maintain
443
      its performance advantages when faced with more intricate tasks that demand more sophisticated al-
      gorithmic techniques. To this end, we employ the SALSA-CLRS benchmark (Minder et al., 2023),
444
      which comprises a diverse set of six graph algorithms derived from the CLRS (Veličković et al.,
445
      2022) collection.
446
447   The SALSA-CLRS benchmark is particularly relevant for our study as it emphasizes sparsity and
448   scalability, two critical aspects in for graph algorithm learning. While it builds upon tasks from the
449
      CLRS-30 benchmark, it is important to clarify why we chose the SALSA-CLRS extension for our
      evaluation. The CLRS-30 collection aims to capture a wide range of algorithmic concepts, includ-
450
      ing geometry, sorting, and string tasks, not limited to graphs per se. To provide a unified interface
451
      for these diverse tasks, it employs an abstract graph view using fully connected graphs, enabling
452   the modeling of relationships and reasoning between objects. This approach facilitates the training
453   of generalist algorithmic reasoners and has led to suitable architectures such as the triplet reasoner
454   (Ibarz et al., 2022; Bohde et al., 2024). However, note that these approaches heavily rely on the fully
455   connected graph structures and are accompanied by processors with higher order computations. Our
456   setting differs that we focus on graph algorithm learning, where the graph structure is crucial to the
457   task and carries inherent information. A key aspect of our study is the generalization to substantially
458   larger graphs, which extends beyond the typical 4x size increase evaluated in the CLRS framework.
459   While CLRS-30 can accommodate larger tests, its fully connected, dense graph structure and com-
460
      putationally intensive baselines present scalability challenges. Moreover, applying the FE Net on
      fully connected graphs would be ineffective, as it relies on leveraging inherent graph structure and
461
      alignment.

518   Overall, the FE Net architecture demonstrates strong performance on SALSA-CLRS. While ex-
519   celling on certain algorithms, FE Net’s effectiveness varies across different tasks. For some algo-
520   rithms, we observe enhanced performance when increasing the number of executed phases, sug-
521
      gesting a potential adaptation strategy for tasks less naturally aligned with the flooding and echo
      paradigm. Notably, even without relying on predetermined step counts or intermediate hints, our
522
      method can achieve superior results on multiple tasks. This improvement extends to graph accu-
523
      racy, underscoring the FE Net’s capacity to enhance generalization. These findings highlight that
524   the algorithmic alignment on the level of message-passing benefits graph algorithm learning.

527   6                C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this work, we challenge the standard message-passing paradigm commonly used in graph learn-
530   ing and introduce the Flood and Echo Net. Our method aligns its execution to a design pattern
531
      from distributed algorithms where messages are flooded and echoed throughout the entire graph in
      a wave-like activation. Through these structured activations of the nodes, the FE Net becomes more
532
      expressive than regular MPNNs. Moreover, our method is more efficient with regard to message
533
      complexity, as it can facilitate messages throughout the graph more easily. Crucially, the execution
534   of the FE Net naturally generalizes to graphs of larger sizes, which we find to be beneficial in im-
535   proving generalization in graph algorithm learning. We empirically validate our findings on simple
536   algorithmic tasks as well as more challenging graph algorithms from the SALSA-CLRS bench-
537   mark. Our results demonstrate that the algorithmic alignment of the FE Net significantly enhances
538   performance on multiple algorithms even in the challenging graph accuracy, particularly when gen-
539   eralizing to larger graphs. These findings underscore our method’s potential to improve performance
      through algorithmic alignment on the level of message-passing.

772   Another important issue that GNNs often struggle with is the so-called phenomenon of oversquash-
773   ing (Alon & Yahav, 2021a). In simple terms, if too much information has to be propagated through
774   the graph using a few edges, a bottleneck occurs, squashing the relevant information together, lead-
775   ing to information loss and subsequent problems for learning. Recent work of (Giovanni et al., 2023)
776
      theoretically analyses the reasons leading to the oversquashing phenomena and identifies the width
      and depth of the network but also the graph topology as key contributors. Note that the proposed FE
777
      Net is not designed to tackle the problem of oversquashing. Rather, it tries to facilitate information
778
      throughout the graph, assuming that there is no inherent (topological) bottleneck. It only affects the
779   aforementioned depth aspect of the network. However, as outlined by (Giovanni et al., 2023), the
780   depth is likely to have a marginal effect compared to the graph topology.
781
782
      The works of Martinkus et al. (2023), namely AgentNet, and Faber & Wattenhofer (2023), who
      proposes AMP (Asynchronous Message Passing), also draw inspiration from the field of distributed
783
      computing. Although they share some aspects in their mechanisms, their respective settings differ
784
      quite a bit. In AgentNet, there exist agents which traverse the graph which gives them the possibility
785   to solve problems on the graph in sublinear time. In contrast, our approach tries to enable commu-
786   nication throughout the whole graph, especially in the context of different graph sizes. On the other
787   hand, AMP activates nodes one at a time, benefiting from a similar computational sparsity as our
788   method. However, note that the FE Net’s execution is more structured. On one side, this leads to
789   less flexible activation patterns, however, on the other hand, it translates naturally across graph sizes.
790   Whereas AMP has to additionally learn a termination criteria which must generalize.

841

842   can provably determine what pieces of information must be gathered for each node to make correct
843   predictions. If we choose an appropriate origin point, we could easily send the information and solve
844   the task. However, more interestingly, what happens if we choose a random origin node instead?
845   Can the Flood and Echo model still distribute the relevant information, even if it does not suffice
846   to fully solve the task? We derive theoretical upper bounds for the best-performing instance given
847   the information that theoretically could be available during the execution depending on the number

851
      PrefixSum Task For this analysis, we use the PrefixSum dataset, which follows the task intro-
852
      duced by Schwarzschild et al. (2021) and was later adapted for the graph setting (Grötschla et al.,
853
      2022). It consists of a path graph, where one end is marked to distinguish left form right. Each node
854   v independently and uniformly at random gets assigned one bit xv , which is either 1 or 0, chosen
855   with probability 12 each. The task is to compute the prefix sum from left to right moduloP          2. There-
856   fore, the output yv of each node v is the sum of the bits of all nodes to the left yv ≡2 ( i≤v xi ).
857   Note, that in order to correctly predict a node output, it has to take all bits left of it into consideration.
858

859   dependent on all bits of the nodes to its left. If not all bits are considered for the computation, the
860   probability of a correct prediction is bounded by Pr[ov = yv ] ≤ 21 .
861
862   Note that from this lemma, it immediately follows that to solve the task correctly, information needs
863   to be exchanged throughout the whole graph. Nodes towards the end of the path must consider
      almost all nodes of the graph for their computation.

1041
       task according to D.1. For the Distance task, it is necessary that the length of the shortest path
1042   between the root and each node can be inferred. Note that this is exactly the path which is taken by
1043   the flooding messages, therefore, this should be sufficient to solve the task. Similarly, for the Path
1044   Finding task, one phase is sufficient. Note that starting from the leaves of the graph during the echo
1045   phase, nodes can decide that they are not part of the path between the two marked nodes (as only
1046   marked leaves can be part of the path). However, when such a message is received at one of the
1047   marked nodes, they can ignore it and tell their predecessor that they are on the path. This is correct,
1048   as one of the marked ends is at the start of our computation, and this echo message travels from the
1049   other marked end on the to-be-marked path back toward the root. This shows that for each of the
1050
       above-mentioned tasks, there exists a FE Net configuration that solves the task in a single phase,
       which exchanges O(m) messages.

1082   G.1   RUNTIME C OMPLEXITY
1083
1084   We denote n the number of nodes, m the number of edges and D the diameter of the graph. Fur-
1085   thermore, let T be the number of phases for a FE Net and L be the number of layers for an MPNN.
1086
       A single round of regular message-passing exchanges O(m) messages. Therefore, executing L such
1087   rounds results in O(L) steps and O(Lm) messages. Note that in order for communication between
1088   any two nodes L has to be in the order of O(D).
1089
1090
       A single phase of a FE Net, consisting of one starting node, exchanges O(m) messages and does so
       in O(D) steps. Therefore, executing T phases of a FE Net results in O(T m) messages exchanged
1091
       in O(T D) steps. Note, that it is sufficient for T to be constant O(1) in order to communicate
1092
       throughout the whole graph and does not necessarily have to be scaled according to the size of the
1093   graph.
1094
1095   The variations fixed and random perform their executions only for a specific single node. Contrary,
1096
       the all variation performs such an execution for each of its nodes individually. Therefore, both the
       number of messages and the number of steps is increased by a factor of n.

       For all the below tasks, we use train set, validation set, and test set sizes of 1024, 100, and 1000,

1381   node has a random binary label with one marked vertex at one end, which indicates the starting

1387   its distance to the source node is divisible by 2.
