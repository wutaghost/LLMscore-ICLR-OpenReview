011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The emergence of diverse generative vision models has recently enabled the syn-
014            thesis of visually realistic images, underscoring the critical need for effectively
015            detecting these generated images from real photos. Despite advances in this field,
016
               existing detection approaches often struggle to accurately identify synthesized im-
               ages generated by different generative models. In this work, we introduce a novel
017
               and generalizable detection framework termed HyperDet, which innovatively cap-
018
               tures and integrates shared knowledge from a collection of functionally distinct and
019            lightweight expert detectors. HyperDet leverages a large pretrained vision model
020            to extract general detection features while simultaneously capturing and enhancing
021            task-specific features. To achieve this, HyperDet first groups SRM filters into five
022            distinct groups to efficiently capture varying levels of pixel artifacts based on their
023            different functionality and complexity. Then, HyperDet utilizes a hypernetwork to
024            generate LoRA model weights with distinct embedding parameters. Finally, we
025            merge the LoRA networks to form an efficient model ensemble. Also, we propose
026            a novel objective function that balances the pixel and semantic artifacts effectively.
027
               Extensive experiments on the UnivFD and Fake2M datasets demonstrate the ef-
               fectiveness of our approach, achieving state-of-the-art performance. Moreover,
028
               our work paves a new way to establish generalizable domain-specific fake image
029
               detectors based on pretrained large vision models. Our codes are available at
030            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/HyperDet-3053.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      In recent years, the rapid advancement of generative models—including GANs (Goodfellow et al.,
036   2014), VAEs (Brock et al., 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Karras et al., 2017; 2019; Rombach et al., 2022; Song
037   & Ermon, 2019), GLOW (Kingma & Dhariwal, 2018), and Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al.,
038   2015)—has enabled the creation of AI-generated images that are often indistinguishable from real
039   ones to the human eyes. This progress allows users to generate realistic images without specialized
040   knowledge, significantly impacting the entertainment industry. However, the proliferation of such
041   images poses serious threats to public opinion and the authenticity of information. Consequently,
042   there is an urgent need for effective methods to monitor and detect synthetic images, ensuring the
043   integrity of information and the fairness of public discourse.
044   Early detectors primarily focused on images generated by GAN models (Goodfellow et al., 2014), em-
045   ploying spatial (Marra et al., 2019; Rossler et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2019) or frequency (Chandrasegaran
046   et al., 2021; Dong et al., 2022; Frank et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2019) features to identify synthetic
047   content. However, these methods often struggle with images produced by newer generative models,
048   such as diffusion models. Consequently, there is a growing trend towards developing generalized
049   detectors capable of effectively identifying fake images from a wider range of sources. For instance,
050
      Wang et al. (Wang et al., 2020) enhanced the generalization capabilities of detection methods through
      data augmentation techniques and the use of large datasets. However, excessive training causes the
051
      detector model to overfit specific characteristics of the training data.
052
053   Some other methods attempt to utilize semantic information for detection. For example, Ojha et al.
      (Ojha et al., 2023) adopted a pre-trained CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) model to extract high-level

054
      semantic features in synthetic image detection tasks. Jia et al. (Jia et al., 2024) utilized large language
055   models to detect semantic anomalies in synthetic images. However, semantic discrepancies are often
056   minimal between real and generated images with prevalent generative models. In contrast, NPR (Tan
057   et al., 2024), a recent state-of-the-art detector, can capture forgery traces by upsampling operations,
058   leveraging correlations between local pixels. However, NPR mainly relies on low-level pixel artifact
059   features, neglecting the semantic information, leading to high false positive rates.
060
      This work proposes a novel method termed HyperDet, designed to extract generalized artifacts for
061   effective detection. Instead of directly utilizing Spatial Rich Model (SRM) (Fridrich & Kodovsky,
062   2012) filters, we group these filters to capture varying levels of pixel artifacts based on their func-
063   tionality and complexity. Corresponding to each SRM filter group, we leverage learnable LoRAs as
064   expert models, specializing in detecting discernible traces in the textural feature space. To facilitate
065   this, we introduce a hypernetwork (Ha et al., 2016) that generates the optimized weights for the
066   LoRAs (ie hyper LoRAs), enabling adaptive selection while learning shared knowledge and expertise
067   among different LoRA experts. Furthermore, we meticulously design a novel objective function that
068   integrates both low-level pixel artifacts and semantic context, effectively mitigating false positives.
069   Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method exhibits exceptional generalization ability in
070   synthetic image detection tasks. For instance, on the UnivFD dataset (Ojha et al., 2023), our method
071   outperforms the state-of-the-art (SOTA) (Liu et al., 2024) by 8.12% accuracy and 0.91 mAP. On
072   the latest Fake2M dataset (Lu et al., 2024), our approach surpasses the SOTA (Tan et al., 2024) by
073   5.03% accuracy and 10.02 mAP. Additionally, we investigate the robustness of our method against
074   various post-processing operations and analyze its generalization ability across different backbone
075
      models, highlighting the effectiveness of CLIP in extracting generalized artifacts. We also present the
      performance of our method across different dataset sizes and discuss the impact of different LoRA
076
      ranks and the fine-tuning of various layers on model performance.
077
078   Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
079   1) We propose a novel and generalizable synthesized image detection method, called HyperDet.
080   Unlike existing detectors, we innovatively introduce hypernetwork into the detection framework that
081   generates optimized weights for specialized LoRA experts, facilitating the extraction of generalized
082   discernible artifacts.
083
      2) We propose an SRM filter grouping strategy to capture varying levels of pixel artifacts based on
084
      their functionality and complexity. Besides, we propose a novel objective function to balance the
085   pixel and semantic artifacts effectively.
086
087   3) HyperDet achieves state-of-the-art detection performance on multiple datasets, surpassing baseline
088
      methods by a large margin. Besides, it shows improved robustness against post-processing operations.

      Synthetic image generation. In the era of large generative models, synthesized images typically refer
094
      to visually realistic images generated from random noise or text prompts. Representative approaches
095
      include GANs and their variants (Brock et al., 2019; Choi et al., 2018; Karras et al., 2017; 2019; Park
096   et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2017), as well as diffusion models (Yang et al., 2024; Nichol et al., 2021;
097   Rombach et al., 2022). GANs transform random noise into images through a generator and optimize
098   the quality via adversarial training, while diffusion models gradually reconstruct images by denoising.
099   Both techniques excel in generating high-quality synthetic images, meanwhile posing significant
100   challenges for image detection.
101
      Synthetic image detection. Early visual forgery detection models primarily focused on images
102   generated by Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). Mo et al. (Mo et al., 2018) trained a binary
103   classification deep neural network to distinguish between real and GAN-generated facial images.
104   Zhang et al. (Zhang et al., 2019) proposed the AutoGAN model, which automatically simulates the
105   GAN sample generation process and observed that the upsampling module of GANs introduces a
106   "checkerboard artifact" in the frequency domain, leading to the extraction of spectral features for
107   classification. Frank et al. (Frank et al., 2020) analyzed the statistical differences between synthetic
      and real images in the frequency domain.

      For facial image forgery detection, Nguyen et al. (Nguyen et al., 2019) utilized capsule networks to
137
      identify forgery artifacts. Dang et al. (Dang et al., 2020) proposed an attention-based model to handle
138   important feature maps, enhancing classification capabilities. Liu et al. (Liu et al., 2020) analyzed
139   texture information differences and introduced the Gram-Net model to extract global texture features
140   for detection.
141
      Although these methods perform well on specific datasets, their generalization ability remains
142
      limited when confronted with different generative models or some unseen samples. In recent years,
143
      researchers have increasingly focused on improving the generalization capability of models. CNNSpot
144   (Wang et al., 2020) detects visual forgeries by identifying the "fingerprints" left by convolutional
145   networks (CNNs) during image generation. The study employs JPEG compression and image blurring
146   as data augmentation techniques, demonstrating that models trained on ProGAN (Karras et al., 2017)
147   synthesized images can generalize effectively to forensic detection across other generative models.
148   Ojha et al. (Ojha et al., 2023) applied k-NN and LC classification strategies on a pre-trained CLIP
149   model, achieving good results. Tan et al. (Tan et al., 2024) enhanced low-level artifact detection
150   capabilities by improving the relationships between neighboring pixels. Liu et al. (Liu et al.,
151   2024) employed Moe and LoRA fine-tuning strategies on the CLIP model to enhance generalization
152
      performance.
153   Low-rank adaption and hypernetwork. Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) is an
154   efficient method for fine-tuning large models, particularly pre-trained ones. The core idea is to
155   constrain parameter updates within two low-rank matrices, enabling approximate updates to the
156   original weights. LoRA requires tuning only a small number of additional parameters, thereby
157   preserving pre-trained knowledge while improving computational efficiency and inference speed,
158
      allowing the model to better adapt to specific tasks.
159   In contrast, hypernetworks (Ha et al., 2016) generate model parameters to capture shared knowledge
160   across multiple tasks. Instead of directly fine-tuning the target network, a HyperNetwork learns
161   to generate parameters for different tasks, facilitating cross-task shared learning. This mechanism
      enhances model flexibility and generalization while reducing training resource consumption.

275   In the synthetic image detection task, we define it as a binary classification problem, where authentic
276   images are labeled as 0 and synthetic images are labeled as 1. This setup allows us to train and evaluate
277   classification models by contrasting the features of authentic and synthetic images to distinguish
278   between them.
279

280   tion layer(Ojha et al., 2023), and using of the cross-entropy loss function for binary classification

305   Implementation details. The study employs Hyper LoRAs modules to fine-tune the last 8 fully
306   connected layers of the CLIP: ViT-L/14 model, with the generated LoRA’s rank set to 16. To ensure
307   experimental consistency (Ojha et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2024), a dataset comprising
308   720k images across 20 categories was used (including 360k real images and 360k synthetic images
309
      generated by ProGAN (Karras et al., 2017)). During training, the probabilities for Gaussian blur and
      JPEG compression data augmentation were set to 0.1, and the learning rate was fixed at 0.0001. The
310
      model was trained for 5 epochs. Experiments were conducted on a server with two RTX 4090 GPUs.
311
312   Datasets. We evaluate the generalization capability of our approach on the UnivFD dataset (Ojha
313   et al., 2023), which contains 19 different types of data, and the Fake2M dataset (Lu et al., 2024),
314   which includes 17 different types of data. The UnivFD dataset contains the generators from: ProGAN
315
      (Karras et al., 2017), CycleGAN (Zhu et al., 2017), BigGAN (Brock et al., 2019), StyleGAN (Karras
      et al., 2019), GauGAN (Park et al., 2019), StarGAN (Choi et al., 2018), Deepfakes (Rossler et al.,
316
      2019), SITD (Chen et al., 2018), SAN (Dai et al., 2019), CRN (Chen & Koltun, 2017), IMLE (Li et al.,
317
      2019), Guideed (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), LDM (Rombach et al., 2022), Glide (Nichol et al., 2021),
318   DALL-E (Ramesh et al., 2021). Additionally, we conducted robustness evaluations on these datasets
319   and performed various ablation experiments. Fake2M is a recently collected, larger-scale synthetic
320   image dataset, primarily consisting of data generated by the following generators: Stable Diffusion
321   model (Rombach et al., 2022), Midjourney (Midjourney), Cogview (Ding et al., 2021), StyleGan
322   (Karras et al., 2019). This dataset contains synthetic images generated by various diffusion models,
323   which exhibit more realistic visual effects. Previous methods have shown detection performance on
      this dataset that is nearly at the level of random chance.

527   5          C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this work, we have developed a generalizable fake imagery detection method termed HyperDet
530   that is particularly effective in distinguishing synthetic images generated by unseen source models.
531   HyperDet first groups SRM filters to enable efficient extraction of pixel artifacts from high-frequency
532   in synthetic images, it then utilizes expert models based on learnable LoRAs to capture corresponding
533   discernible features. Importantly, we introduce Hyper LoRAs, which leverage a hypernetwork to
534   generate weights for different LoRA experts to extract shared knowledge during model learning.
535
      Finally, the experts are merged to increase model generalization capabilities. HyperDet effectively
      reduces false positives and exhibits strong generalization across multiple datasets, contributing to
536
      future research in synthetic image detection.

      A    T HE DETAILS OF THE FILTER GROUPING
757
758
      The filters are grouped based on their functionality and complexity, with each group designed to
759   capture different levels of image features:
760
761          • Group 1 (Filters 1-8): These filters focus on simple edge detection, primarily capturing
762            subtle directional changes in the image, including horizontal, vertical, and diagonal edges.
763          • Group 2 (Filters 9-12): Filters in this group introduce stronger weight variations to empha-
764            size more complex edge features, especially those that are more prominent and defined.
765          • Group 3 (Filters 13-20): This group is designed to recognize multi-level edges and curved
766            structures. The filters are more complex and focus on extracting high-frequency texture
767            information with significant directional changes.
768          • Group 4 (Filters 21-25): Filters in this group aim to extract large-scale features, identifying
769            coarse edges and contours that highlight significant structures, particularly in the low-
770            frequency range.
771
             • Group 5 (Filters 26-30): This group contains high-order edge detection and texture extrac-
772            tion filters. These filters are well-suited for capturing fine textures and large-scale directional
773            features, useful in detailed texture analysis.
774
775   By grouping the filters in this manner, the model can effectively analyze diverse image characteristics
776   across multiple scales, enhancing the overall performance of feature extraction.
