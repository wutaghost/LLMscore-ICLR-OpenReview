009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               In natural language processing and computer vision, self-supervised pre-training
012
               on large datasets unlocks foundational model capabilities across domains and tasks.
013
               However, this potential has not yet been realised in time series analysis, where exist-
014            ing methods disregard the heterogeneous nature of time series characteristics. Time
015            series are prevalent in many domains, including medicine, engineering, natural
016            sciences, and finance, but their characteristics vary significantly in terms of variate
017            count, inter-variate relationships, temporal dynamics, and sampling frequency. This
018            inherent heterogeneity across domains prevents effective pre-training on large time
019            series corpora. To address this issue, we introduce OTiS, an open model for gen-
020            eral time series analysis, that has been specifically designed to handle multi-domain
021            heterogeneity. We propose a novel pre-training paradigm including a tokeniser with
022
               learnable domain-specific signatures, a dual masking strategy to capture temporal
               causality, and a normalised cross-correlation loss to model long-range dependen-
023
               cies. Our model is pre-trained on a large corpus of 640, 187 samples and 11 billion
024
               time points spanning 8 distinct domains, enabling it to analyse time series from any
025            (unseen) domain. In comprehensive experiments across 15 diverse applications -
026            including classification, regression, and forecasting - OTiS showcases its ability to
027            accurately capture domain-specific data characteristics and demonstrates its com-
028            petitiveness against state-of-the-art baselines. Our code and pre-trained weights
029            are publicly available at https://github.com/OTiS-official/OTiS.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   In natural language processing (NLP) or computer vision (CV), generalisable language features, e.g.
036   semantics and grammar (Radford et al., 2018; Touvron et al., 2023; Chowdhery et al., 2023), or visual
037   features, e.g. edges and shapes (Geirhos et al., 2019; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2024), are
038   learned from large-scale data. Self-supervised pre-training paradigms are designed to account for the
039
      specific properties of language (Radford et al., 2018; Touvron et al., 2023; Chowdhery et al., 2023)
      or imaging (Zhou et al., 2022; Cherti et al., 2023; Oquab et al., 2024), unlocking foundational model
040
      capabilities that apply to a wide range of domains and downstream tasks. This potential, however,
041
      remains largely unrealised in time series due to the lack of self-supervised pre-training paradigms
042   that account for the heterogeneity of time series across domains.
043
044   Time series are widespread in everyday applications and play an important role in various domains,
045
      including medicine (Pirkis et al., 2021), engineering (Gasparin et al., 2022), natural sciences (Ravuri
      et al., 2021), and finance (Sezer et al., 2020). They differ substantially with respect to the number
046
      of variates, inter-variate relationships, temporal dynamics, and sampling frequency (Fawaz et al.,
047
      2018; Ismail Fawaz et al., 2019; Ye & Dai, 2021; Wickstrøm et al., 2022). For instance, standard
048   10-20 system electroencephalography (EEG) recordings come with up to 256 variates (Jurcak et al.,
049   2007), while most audio recordings have only 1 (mono) or 2 (stereo) variates. Weather data shows
050   high periodicity, whereas financial data is exposed to long-term trends. Both domains encompass
051   low-frequency data recorded on an hourly (278 mHz), daily (12 µHz), or even monthly (386 nHz)
052   basis, while audio data is sampled at high frequencies of 44.1 kHz or more. Overall, this heterogeneity
053   across domains renders the extraction of generalisable time series features difficult (Fawaz et al.,
      2018; Gupta et al., 2020; Iwana & Uchida, 2021; Ye & Dai, 2021).

074   While most existing self-supervised pre-training methods for time series are limited to single domains
075   (Wu et al., 2021; 2022a; Nie et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024), recent works propose
076   simple techniques to incorporate time series from multiple domains (Yang et al., 2024; Das et al.,
077   2024; Woo et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). These works for instance crop all time series into segments
078
      of unified size (Jiang et al., 2024), resample them to a uniform frequency (Yang et al., 2024), or
      analyse each variate of a multi-variate time series independently (Liu et al., 2024). While these naive
079
      techniques address differences in sampling frequency and variate count, they degrade the original
080
      time series and neglect the critical inter-variate relationships and temporal dynamics required for
081   effective real-world analysis. Consequently, there is a clear need for pre-training strategies that
082   adequately handle heterogeneity in time series to unlock foundational model capabilities.
083
084
      In this work, we propose a novel multi-domain pre-training paradigm that addresses the full spectrum
      of time series heterogeneity across domains. Our approach facilitates the comprehensive extraction
085
      of generalisable features from diverse time series. Pre-trained on a large corpus of publicly available
086
      data, our open model for general time series analysis (OTiS) can be fine-tuned on limited data of any
087   (unseen) domain to perform a variety of downstream tasks, as showcased in Figure 1.
088
089   Our key contributions can be summarised as follows:
090
091
                1. We present OTiS, an open model for general time series analysis, with our en-
                   tire pipeline and pre-trained weights publicly available at https://github.com/
092
                   OTiS-official/OTiS.
093
094             2. We propose a novel pre-training paradigm based on masked data modelling to address
095                heterogeneity in multi-domain time series. Our approach includes a novel tokeniser with
096                learnable signatures to capture domain-specific data characteristics, a dual masking strategy
097                to learn temporal causality, and a normalised cross-correlation loss to model long-range
098                dependencies.
099
100
                3. We pre-train OTiS on a large corpus of publicly available time series from 8 domains,
                   spanning medicine, engineering, natural sciences, and finance. With 640, 187 samples and
101
                   11 billion time points, this corpus represents diverse time series characteristics, enabling
102
                   generalisable feature extraction.
103
104             4. We evaluate OTiS across 15 downstream applications, including classification, regression,
105                and forecasting. Our comprehensive analysis demonstrates that OTiS accurately captures
106                domain-specific data characteristics and is competitive with both specialised and general
107                state-of-the-art (SOTA) models, achieving new SOTA performance in 10 tasks. Notably,
                   none of the baselines is capable of performing all the tasks covered by OTiS.

110   2.1   S ELF -S UPERVISED L EARNING FOR T IME S ERIES
111
112   Time series vary significantly across domains, with differences in the number of variates, inter-variate
113   relationships, temporal dynamics, and sampling frequencies. Due to this inherent heterogeneity, most
114   existing works focus on pre-training models within a single domain (Oreshkin et al., 2019; Tang
115   et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2022a; Woo et al., 2022; Yue et al., 2022;
116   Zhang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2024). To develop
117
      more generalisable time series models, recent methods have explored multi-domain pre-training by
      addressing certain aspects of the heterogeneity, such as differences in variate count and sampling
118
      frequency. For instance, Liu et al. (2024) treat each variate in multi-variate time series independently
119
      to standardise generative tasks like forecasting, while Goswami et al. (2024) extend uni-variate
120   analysis to discriminative tasks like classification. Similarly, Jiang et al. (2024) and Yang et al. (2024)
121   standardise time series by cropping them into segments of predefined size and resampling them to a
122   uniform frequency, respectively, to enable general classification capabilities in medical domains.
123
      While partially addressing time series heterogeneity, these methods limit model capabilities for
124
      general time series analysis. Standardisation techniques like cropping or resampling may distort
125
      inter-variate relationships, temporal dynamics, and long-range dependencies. Additionally, many of
126   these approaches are tailored to specific applications, such as generative tasks (Das et al., 2024; Liu
127   et al., 2024; Woo et al., 2024), or focused on particular domains like medicine (Jiang et al., 2024;
128   Yang et al., 2024). Moreover, recent foundational models (Das et al., 2024; Goswami et al., 2024;
129   Liu et al., 2024) focus on uni-variate analysis, ignoring crucial inter-variate relationships essential
130   for real-world applications, such as disease prediction (Schoffelen & Gross, 2009; Wu et al., 2022b).
131   Our study aims to overcome these limitations by fully addressing heterogeneity of multi-domain time
132   series, establishing a foundation for general time series analysis across domains and tasks.
133
134   2.2   T IME S ERIES T OKENISATION
135
      Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) have emerged as the preferred architecture for foundational
136
      models in NLP and CV due to their scalability (Kaplan et al., 2020; Gordon et al., 2021; Alabdul-
137
      mohsin et al., 2022), enabling the training of models in the magnitude of 100 billion parameters
138   (Chowdhery et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Oquab et al., 2024; Ravi et al., 2024). To utilise a
139   Transformer for time series analysis, a tokeniser is required to map the time series into a compact
140   latent space. Current methods (Jin et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Das et al., 2024;
141   Goswami et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Woo et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024) follow
142   established techniques from NLP and CV, dividing time series into patches of pre-defined size. These
143   patches are then flattened into a 1D sequence, with positional embeddings used to retain positional
144   information. While uni-variate models (Nie et al., 2023; Das et al., 2024; Goswami et al., 2024; Liu
145   et al., 2024) consider only temporal positions, multi-variate approaches (Woo et al., 2024; Yang et al.,
146
      2024; Jiang et al., 2024) account for both temporal and variate positions. However, none of these
      methods address the unique characteristics of variates, mistakenly assuming that the relationships
147
      between variates are identical across domains. Our work seeks to adapt the tokenisation process to
148
      preserve the domain-specific relationships between variates.

151   3     M ETHODS
152
153
      In this work, we present a novel multi-domain pre-training paradigm that enables generalisable
      feature extraction from large, heterogeneous time series corpora. We introduce a domain-specific
154
      tokeniser with learnable signatures to address heterogeneity in multi-domain time series, as described
155
      in Section 3.1. We tailor masked data modelling (MDM) for multi-domain time series to pre-train
156   our open model for general time series analysis (OTiS) on a large, heterogeneous corpus, as detailed
157   in Section 3.2. In particular, we propose normalised cross-correlation as a loss term to capture
158   global temporal dynamics in time series, as explained in Section 3.3. Moreover, we introduce a dual
159   masking strategy to capture bidirectional relationships and temporal causality, essential for general
160   time series analysis, as described in Section 3.4. After pre-training, we fine-tune OTiS on limited
161   data to perform a variety of downstream tasks in any - including previously unseen - domain, as
      outlined in Section 3.5. A graphical visualisation of our method is provided in Figure 2.

197   The permutation-equivariant nature of Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) requires the use of po-
198   sitional embeddings to accurately capture the inherent relationships in the input data. Initially
199
      introduced for 1D textual token sequences (Vaswani et al., 2017), positional embeddings simply intro-
      duce an ordering into the input sequence. Modern implementations further extend their capabilities to
200
      encode more complex geometric information, such as 2D spatial (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) or graph
201
      (Kreuzer et al., 2021) structures. For the analysis of any-variate time series, we distinguish between
202   the temporal and variate structure. The temporal structure is equivalent to a sequential 1D structure,
203   such that we use standard 1D sinusoidal embeddings eT (xv,t ) = eTt ∈ R1×D .
204
205   The variate structure exhibits great heterogeneity across domains. In domains with uni-variate and
206
      two-variate data, such as mono and stereo audio, the structure is either trivial or only requires a basic

207
      complex relationships, such as 3D manifolds for electroencephalography (EEG) or electrocardiogra-
208
      phy (ECG) data, or be of non-spatial nature, such as for financial data. Hence, we introduce learnable

286   3.4   D UAL M ASKING S TRATEGY
287
      We design the masking strategy to enhance foundational model capabilites in time series analysis.
288
      Specifically, we randomly select between two masking schemes during pre-training, namely random
289
      masking and post-fix masking. In 75 % of cases, we apply random masking, where each mv,t is
290   independently sampled from a Bernoulli distribution with probability p = 1 − ρ, with ρ denoting
291   the masking ratio (i.e. mv,t ∼ Bernoulli(1 − ρ)). This encourages the model to learn complex
292   inter-variate relationships across the entire time series. In the remaining 25 % of cases, we employ
293   post-fix masking, which masks the second half of the temporal dimension, leaving only the first half
294   visible (i.e. mv,t = 1[t≤T ′ /2] ). The prediction of future values solely based on past observations
295   simulates real-world forecasting conditions, helping the model to capture temporal causality. Overall,
296   this dual masking strategy enables OTiS to learn both bidirectional relationships and temporal
297   causality, which are essential for general time series analysis.

347   We aim to develop a general time series model that fully handles the heterogeneity in real-world data.
348
      Specifically, our model is designed to handle time series with different variate counts VS , inter-variate
      relationships, temporal dynamics, and sampling frequency, ensuring flexibility for downstream tasks.
349
      To this end, we pre-train our model on a large and diverse corpus of publicly available data spanning
350
      8 domains, with a total of 640, 187 samples and 11 billion time points, as summarised in Table 1. A

354   4.3    B ENCHMARKING ACROSS D OMAINS AND TASKS
355
356   To evaluate OTiS in real-world settings, we conduct experiments on three key use cases in time series
357   analysis: classification, regression, and forecasting. We use 10 datasets across 8 domains to compare
358   our model against 21 specialised and general baselines as outlined in Appendix B. The baselines
359   include 11 target-specific models (either fully supervised or pre-trained and fine-tuned on target data),
360   6 general models (pre-trained on external data and fine-tuned on target data), and 4 foundation models
361
      (pre-trained on large corpora and fine-tuned on target data). We follow established data splitting and
      evaluation procedures for classification (Zhang et al., 2022), regression (Turgut et al., 2023), and
362
      forecasting (Zhou et al., 2021), with results reported across five seeds set during fine-tuning.
363
364   The experiments reveal that OTiS is a powerful feature extractor for time series analysis, achieving
365   state-of-the-art performance on 10 out of 15 diverse benchmarks. The classification results in Table
366   2a highlight its particular strength in processing long time series, as indicated by a huge performance
367
      boost on FD-B (T FD-B = 5112). We also find that pre-training across domains is more effective than
      domain-specific pre-training. For instance, in the regression tasks shown in Table 2b, OTiS excels at
368
      predicting cardiac phenotypes, outperforming baselines pre-trained solely on ECG data (MAE) and
369
      even multimodally pre-trained baselines (CM-AE and MMCL). These results stress the strength of
370   pre-training across domains for generalisable feature extraction, enabling OTiS to achieve superior
371   performance even in unseen domains, as shown by the forecasting results in Table 3 and Appendix
372   H. Zero-shot and linear probing experiments detailed in Appendix F further demonstrate OTiS’
373   generalisability, resulting in competitive performance on Epilepsy, LVESV, and Weather prediction.

503   4.6     A BLATION S TUDY
504
505   We perform an ablation study to analyse the impact of OTiS’ key components: the domain-specific
506   tokeniser, dual masking strategy, and normalised cross-correlation (NCC) loss. As shown in Figure 5,
507   the best and most robust performance is achieved when all components are used during pre-training.
508   Replacing the domain-specific variate embeddings with domain-agnostic embeddings (i.e. learnable
509   embeddings shared across all domains) consistently led to inferior performance across all tasks,
510   demonstrating the importance of capturing domain-specific data characteristics during tokenisation.
511   Switching from dual masking to random masking resulted in performance degradation, although the
512   impact was less notable for generative tasks than for discriminative tasks. We hypothesise that the
513   NCC loss already captures temporal causality, which is particularly crucial for generative tasks like
514
      forecasting. Overall, removing the NCC loss caused performance declines across all downstream
      tasks, emphasising the role of long-range dependencies for general time series understanding.

      5      D ISCUSSION & C ONCLUSION
519
520   In this study, we explore the problem of effective pre-training on heterogeneous time series corpora.
521   Time series vary substantially across domains, e.g. with respect to inter-variate relationships and
522   temporal dynamics, rendering generalisable feature extraction from multi-domain time series difficult.
523   To address this issue, we present OTiS, an open model for general time series analysis, specifically
524   designed to handle multi-domain heterogeneity. Our novel multi-domain pre-training paradigm,
525
      including a domain-specific tokeniser with learnable signatures, a dual masking strategy, and a
      normalised cross-correlation (NCC) loss, enables OTiS to extract generalisable time series features.
526
527   In extensive experiments, we demonstrate that OTiS generalises well across 15 diverse downstream
528   applications spanning 8 distinct domains, achieving competitive performance with both specialised
529   and general state-of-the-art (SOTA) models. In a qualitative analysis, we further show that OTiS
530   unifies time series from diverse domains in a meaningful latent space, while preserving low-level
531
      semantics of a domain including the inter-variate relationships. Thereby, our work establishes a
      strong foundation for future advancements in interpretable and general time series analysis.
532
533
534   Limitations. While OTiS outperforms SOTA models across 10 tasks, our experiments in low-data
535   regimes suggest that larger pre-training corpora could further enhance its performance. Unlike in NLP
536   and CV, where large datasets are curated from web-crawled data, foundational models in time series,
537   including OTiS, still rely on manually curated datasets. Future work could explore fully automatic
538   pipelines, e.g. using embedding similarity, to filter and rebalance multi-domain time series from the
539   web. OTiS could further benefit from processing domain signatures during inference, potentially
      unlocking zero-shot capabilities, similarly to those seen in foundational models in NLP and CV.

      A    L ARGE M ULTI -D OMAIN P RE -T RAINING C ORPUS

812
      In this section, we present an overview of our large and diverse pre-training corpus. The corpus
813
      consists of publicly available data spanning eight domains, with a total of 640, 187 samples and
814
      11 billion time points. In the following, we provide a detailed breakdown of the domains and the
815   datasets they encompass. Note that we apply channel-wise standard normalisation to the datasets
816   unless otherwise specified.
817
818
      ECG. The MIMIC-IV-ECG dataset (Gow et al., 2023) contains diagnostic 10-second, 12-lead ECG
819
      recordings sampled at a frequency of 500 Hz. While the entire dataset comprises 800, 035 samples,
820   we include only the first half of the recordings available in the database, preventing the ECG data
821   from predominating in the pre-training corpus. To remove the baseline drift from the ECG data,
822   we use the asymmetric least square smoothing technique (Zhang et al., 2010). Note that we apply
823   standard normalisation separately to the Einthoven, Goldberger, and Wilson leads.
824
825   Temperature. The Deutscher Wetterdienst (DWD) dataset (Wetterdienst, 2024) contains hourly air
826   temperature measurements from 629 weather stations across Germany. Since the recording length
827   varies significantly, ranging from 763 to 1, 148, 290 hours per station, we split the data into chunks
828   of 720 hours (approximately one month).
829
830   Audio. The AudioSet dataset (Gemmeke et al., 2017) contains 10-second YouTube clips for audio
831   classification, featuring 527 types of audio events that are weakly annotated for each clip. The full
832   training set includes a class-wise balanced subset (AudioSet-20K, 22, 176 clips) and an unbalanced
833   (AudioSet-2M 2, 042, 985 clips) set. For our pre-training corpus, we use the balanced AudioSet-20K,
834   which contains 3, 491 mono and 16, 123 stereo recordings, all sampled at 44.1 kHz.
835
836   Electromechanics. The FD-A dataset (Lessmeier et al., 2016) collects vibration signals from
837   rolling bearings in a mechanical system for fault detection purposes. Each sample consists of 5, 120
838   timestamps, indicating one of three mechanical device states. Note that the FD-B dataset is similar
839   to FD-A but includes rolling bearings tested under different working conditions, such as varying
840   rotational speeds.
841
842   EEG. The TDBrain dataset (Van Dijk et al., 2022) includes raw resting-state EEG data from 1, 274
843   psychiatric patients aged 5 to 89, collected between 2001 and 2021. The dataset covers a range
844   of conditions, including Major Depressive Disorder (426 patients), Attention Deficit Hyperactivity
845   Disorder (271 patients), Subjective Memory Complaints (119 patients), and Obsessive-Compulsive
846   Disorder (75 patients). The data was recorded at 500 Hz using 26 channel EEG-recordings, based on
847   the 10-10 electrode international system.
848   The SEED dataset (Zheng & Lu, 2015) contains EEG data recorded under three emotional states:
849   positive, neutral, and negative. It comprises EEG data from 15 subjects, with each subject participating
850   in experiments twice, several days apart. The data is sampled at 200 Hz and recorded using 62 channel
851   EEG-recordings, based on the 10-20 electrode international system.
852   For simplicity, we only consider the 19 channels common to both datasets, i.e. the channels that
853   correspond to the 10-20 electrode international system.
854
855
      Banking. The NN5 competition dataset (Taieb et al., 2012) consists of daily cash withdrawals
856
      observed at 111 randomly selected automated teller machines across various locations in England.
857
858
859   Economics. The FRED-MD dataset (McCracken & Ng, 2016) contains 107 monthly time series
860
      showing a set of macro-economic indicators from the Federal Reserve Bank of St Louis. The data
      was extracted from the FRED-MD database.
861
862   The Exchange dataset (Lai et al., 2018) records the daily exchange rates of eight different nations,
863   including Australia, Great Britain, Canada, Switzerland, China, Japan, New Zealand, and Singapore,
      ranging from 1990 to 2016.

868
      time series analysis: classification, regression, and forecasting. For classification, we perform binary
      epilepsy detection using EEG (Epilepsy 2001), multi-class fault detection in rolling bearings from
869
      vibration signals (FD-B 2016), multi-class hand-gesture classification with accelerometer signals

      (Gesture 2009), and multi-class muscular disease classification using electromyographie (EMG 2000).

875   regression (Turgut et al., 2023), and forecasting (Zhou et al., 2021) tasks. We provide an overview of

1159   F    Z ERO - SHOT C APABILITIES
1160
1161   We analyse OTiS’ zero-shot capabilities across four diverse tasks: binary epilepsy detection using
1162   uni-variate EEG (Epilepsy 2001), multi-class fault detection in rolling bearings from uni-variate
1163   electromechanics signals (FD-B 2016), multi-class hand-gesture classification with multi-variate
1164   accelerometer signals (Gesture 2009), and multi-class muscular disease classification using uni-
1165   variate electromyographie (EMG 2000). These datasets vary significantly in domain, number of
1166   variates, time points, sampling frequency, and number of classes, highlighting the versatility of our
1167
       analysis. Details on the datasets can be found in Table 4.
1168   In the zero-shot setting, OTiS is evaluated without domain-specific fine-tuning by freezing it after pre-
1169   training and using randomly initialised variate embeddings. Since no classification head is employed,
1170   the encoder’s output tokens are averaged to obtain a global representation for each sample. To create
1171   class representations, the global representations of the training samples are averaged separately for
1172   each class. For classification, the cosine similarity is computed between each test sample’s global
1173
       representation and the class representations. The class with the highest similarity score is assigned to
       the test sample. As illustrated in Figure 10, OTiS is able to extract distinct representations for different
1174
       classes, even without domain-specific fine-tuning. This ability translates to zero-shot classification
1175
       accuracies of 93.70 % for Epilepsy, 57.87 % for FD-B, 51.67 % for Gesture, and 95.12 % for EMG.
1176   A closer examination of the zero-shot latent space for FD-B (Figure 10b) reveals a partial overlap
1177   of inputs from classes 1 and 2, which explains the lower zero-shot performance compared to the
1178   fine-tuning results (Figure 11b). Similarly, inputs from the eight classes in the Gesture dataset show
1179   poor clustering in the zero-shot latent space (Figure 10c), with only slight improvements observed
1180   after fine-tuning (Figure 11c). Overall, these quantitative and qualitative zero-shot findings highlight
1181   OTiS’ ability to extract time series features that generalise across domains and tasks, providing a
1182   strong foundation for future advancements in general time series analysis.
1183   Additionally, since our pre-training corpus includes time series from EEG (TDBrain 2022 and SEED
1184   2015) and Electromechanics (FD-A 2016), we also evaluate zero-shot performance using the EEG-
1185   and Electromechanics-specific variate embeddings learned during pre-training instead of randomly
1186   initialised ones. As anticipated, leveraging these learned variate embeddings enhances the quality of
1187   the generated representations, resulting in improved zero-shot classification accuracies of 95.18 %
       for Epilepsy (+1.48 %) and 61.32 % for FD-B (+3.45 %).
