009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce, Q-Sparse, a simple yet effective approach to training sparsely-
012            activated large language models (LLMs). Q-Sparse enables full sparsity of acti-
013            vations in LLMs which can bring significant efficiency gains in inference. This
014            is achieved by applying top-K sparsification to the activations and the straight-
015            through-estimator to the training. We also introduce Block Q-Sparse for batch
016            training and inference. The key results from this work are, (1) Q-Sparse can
017            achieve results comparable to those of baseline LLMs while being much more
018
               efficient at inference time; (2) We present an inference-optimal scaling law for
               sparsely-activated LLMs; (3) Q-Sparse is effective in different settings, including
019
               training-from-scratch, continue-training of off-the-shelf LLMs, and finetuning; (4)
020
               Q-Sparse works for both full-precision and 1-bit LLMs (e.g., BitNet b1.58 (Wang
021            et al., 2023)). Particularly, the synergy of BitNet b1.58 and Q-Sparse (can be
022            equipped with MoE) provides the cornerstone and a clear path to revolutionize the
023            efficiency, including cost and energy consumption, of future LLMs.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable performance on a wide range of natural
029   language processing (NLP) tasks. However, the deployment of LLMs in real-world applications
030   is challenging due to their high computational cost and memory footprint, especially during the
031   inference stage. To address this challenge, recent works (Ma et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Song
032
      et al., 2024b; Xia et al., 2023; Leviathan et al., 2023) have focused on improving the efficiency of
      LLMs with various approaches, including quantization (Ma et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Frantar
033
      et al., 2023), pruning (Xia et al., 2023), distillation (Gu et al., 2023), better decoding (Leviathan
034
      et al., 2023), and so on. One promising approach is to use sparsity to reduce the number of activated
035   parameters in LLMs.
036
037   Sparsity contributes two factors to the efficiency of LLMs. First, sparsity can reduce the amount of
038
      computation of the matrix multiplication as zero elements are not computed. Second, sparsity can
      reduce the amount of input/output (I/O) that transfers the parameters between the memory and the
039
      computation units. The I/O transfer serves as the major bottleneck in the inference stage of LLMs.
040
041   One common approach to sparsity in LLMs is to use weight sparsity, which prunes the model weights
042   to save the computation. However, unstructured weight sparsity is difficult to parallelize in GPU
043   devices, while structured weight sparsity has a large impact to the accuracy of the model.
044   Another approach is to use activation sparsity, which reduces the number of activated elements in
045   the activation tensors. Activation sparsity can be achieved by using the mixture-of-experts (MoE)
046   mechanism (Lepikhin et al., 2021; Fedus et al., 2021), modifying the activation function (Mirzadeh
047   et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024b), or predicting the position to be sparsed (Liu et al., 2023). However,
048   these approaches do not enable full sparsity of activations in LLMs, which can limit the efficiency
049   gains during the inference stage. Moreover, compared to the dense models, the scaling laws for the
050
      sparsely-activated LLMs have not been well studied.
051   To explore the full potential of sparsity in LLMs, we introduce Q-Sparse, a simple yet effective
052   approach to enable full sparsity of activations in LLMs. The major modification on LLMs is in
053   the linear projection (i.e., matrix multiplication). As shown in Figure 1, for each linear projection,
      it has a top-K sparsification function that selects the top-K activations in the input tensor. For the

      backprogation, we use the straight through estimator to compute the gradients of the activations. We
055   also introduce a squared ReLU function for the feed-forward layers to further improve the sparsity of
056   the activations. Q-Sparse can be used with both full-precision and quantized LLMs. Furthermore,
057   we introduce, Block Q-Sparse, a block sparsity implementation to make Q-Sparse compatible with

059
      To study the scaling law of sparsely-activated LLMs, we conduct a series of scaling experiments and
060
      derive an inference-optimal scaling law for sparsely-activated LLMs. We summarize the findings
061   from the scaling experiments and the implications of the scaling law as below:
062
063          • The performance of the sparsely-activated models is better than the dense baselines with the
064            same inference compute budget (i.e., activated parameters or FLOPs).
065          • As the parameters N scales, the performance gap between the sparsely-activated models
066            and the dense baselines decreases.
067          • The performance of the sparsely-activated models with around 40% sparsity ratio can match
068            the performance of the dense baselines with the same model size and training tokens.
069
             • Given the same inference budget Na , a sparsely-activated full-precision model with a
070            sparsity ratio of 45.58% (or 1.84Na parameters) can achieve the best performance. For the
071            1.58-bit models, the optimal sparsity ratio is 61.25%.
072
073   We also conduct experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of Q-Sparse in different settings, includ-
074   ing training-from-scratch, continue-training of off-the-shelf LLMs, and finetuning. We show that
075   Q-Sparse can achieve results comparable to those of baseline LLMs with the same training cost while
076   being much more efficient at inference time.

146   Block Q-Sparse
147
148   While the top-K sparsification can be used in the single-sample mode, it is not friendly with the batch
149
      mode for the current GPU devices. Recent work (Zhou et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2023) shows that N:M
      sparsity, where N out of M consecutive elements to be zero, is more hardware friendly and can be
150
      used in the batch mode with an optimized GPU kernel. To leverage this feature of the modern GPU
151
      devices, we introduce Block Q-Sparse. The key idea of Block Q-Sparse is to apply the top-K sparsity
152   function on the activations in the block level, and the block size is set to M so that there are always
153   M − K zeros out of M consecutive values. The top-K sparsity function is applied to the activations
154   in each block independently. The block level sparsity can be used to reduce the memory footprint
155   and computational cost of the LLMs in the batch mode.

198
      Q-Sparse can be used in different settings, including training-from-scratch, continue-training, and

199
      procedure as in the training-from-scratch setting. The only difference is that we initialize the model
200

202   For the pre-trained models that do not have the squared ReLU function in the feed-forward layers, we

204
      It can improve the sparsity of the activations without changing the model architecture.

512   5   R ELATED W ORK
513
514   The magnitude of the inputs to the linear projections in LLMs often follow a long-tailed distribution,
515
      thus activation sparsity is a natural approach to reduce the inference cost while maintaining com-
      petitive performance. Liu et al. (2023) showed that the activation sparsity exists, can be predicted
516
      with low-cost algorithms. Mirzadeh et al. (2023) demonstrated that compared with widely-adopted
517
      SiLU function, using ReLU function has a negligible impact on convergence and performance while
518   reducing computation and weight transfer. They further inserted the ReLU function before each linear
519   projection to boost the overall sparsity of LLMs. PowerInfer (Song et al., 2023) uses the sparsity of
520   down projection in feed-forward layers to design a GPU-CPU hybrid inference engine: hot-activated
521   neurons are preloaded onto the GPU, while cold neurons are computed on the CPU. It reduces GPU
522   memory demands and CPU-GPU data transfers. TurboSparse (Song et al., 2024b) proposed dReLU
523   activation function to further improve the performance and activation sparsity. ProSparse (Song et al.,
524   2024a) adopted progressive sparsity regularization to smoothly increase the sparsity, which mitigates
525   performance degradation from radical shifts in activation distributions.
526
527   6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
528
529   We introduce Q-Sparse, a simple yet effective approach to enable full activation sparsity in LLMs.
530   Q-Sparse can achieve comparable performance to dense LLMs while being much more efficient at
531   inference time. We present an inference-optimal scaling law for sparsely-activated LLMs. Q-Sparse
532   is effective in different settings, including pre-training, continue-training and fine-tuning. More
533   importantly, Q-Sparse is orthogonal and can be seamlessly integrated with MoE, and works for 1-bit
534   LLMs (e.g., BitNet b1.58 (Wang et al., 2023)).
535   Recent works (Song et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024) have shown promising end-to-end speedup with
536   activation sparsity. The custom kernel for Q-Sparse can be easily implemented since Q-Sparse adopts
537   the token-level sparsity. We leave this as a part of future work. In addition, we would like to scale up
538   the training of 1-bit LLMs (i.e., BitNet b1.58) with fully sparse activations (i.e., Q-Sparse) in terms
539   of both model size and training tokens. Furthermore, we will incorporate YOCO (Sun et al., 2024) to
      address the issue of KV cache for LLM inference.
