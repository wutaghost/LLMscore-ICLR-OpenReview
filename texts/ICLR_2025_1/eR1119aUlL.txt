011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013               Continuous real-time decoding of target variables from time-series data is needed
014               for many applications across various domains including neuroscience. Further,
015               these variables can be encoded across multiple time-series modalities such as
016
                  discrete spiking activity and continuous field potentials that can have different
                  timescales (i.e., sampling rates) and different probabilistic distributions, or can
017
                  even be missing at some time-steps. Existing nonlinear models of multimodal
018
                  neural activity do not support real-time decoding and do not address the different
019               timescales or missing samples across modalities. Here, we develop a learning
020               framework that can nonlinearly aggregate information across multiple time-series
021               modalities with such distinct characteristics, while also enabling real-time decod-
022               ing. This framework consists of 1) a multiscale encoder that nonlinearly fuses
023               information after learning within-modality dynamics to handle different timescales
024               and missing samples, 2) a multiscale dynamical backbone that extracts multimodal
025               temporal dynamics and enables real-time decoding, and 3) modality-specific de-
026               coders to account for different probabilistic distributions across modalities. We
027
                  further introduce smoothness regularization objectives on the learned dynamics to
                  better decode smooth target variables such as behavioral variables and employ a
028
                  dropout technique to increase the robustness for missing samples. We show that
029
                  our model can aggregate information across modalities to improve target variable
030               decoding in simulations and in a real multiscale brain dataset. Further, our method
031               outperforms prior linear and nonlinear multimodal models1 .

034   1       I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Many engineering and science applications need to infer target variables that are encoded in multiple
037   time-series modalities and do so in real-time (i.e., causally). An important example of such an
038   application arises in neuroscience for the inference of cognitive or behavioral variables from multi-
039   modal neural time-series data. Accurate inference of these variables requires developing nonlinear
040   dynamical models of the multimodal time-series that can, at each time-step, aggregate information
041
      across modalities in real-time. Further, a natural challenge in developing these models arises when
      modalities can be missing at some time-steps due to measurement failures or interruptions (Burger
042
      et al., 2018; Li & Marlin, 2020; Berger et al., 2020) or due to different timescales (i.e., sampling rates)
043
      across modalities (Hsieh et al., 2018; Lu et al., 2021). Therefore, designing a dynamical model of
044   multimodal time-series data in real-time applications requires enabling temporal and multimodal data
045   fusion, performing real-time inference, and addressing different timescales and/or missing samples
046   across modalities. Prior nonlinear dynamical models have not addressed all these challenges. To that
047   end, we develop a new nonlinear dynamical model of multimodal time-series that provides all these
048   capabilities. Our model can perform multimodal data fusion and account for different timescales and
049   missing samples, while also enabling real-time inference.
050   In neuroscience, real-time multimodal data fusion is important especially for the design of neurotech-
051   nologies such as brain-computer interfaces. It has been shown that fusing neural modalities such
052   as local field potentials (LFP) and spiking activity can improve the inference of arm movements

054
      (Stavisky et al., 2015; Abbaspourazad et al., 2021; Ahmadipour et al., 2023). More specifically,
055   spiking activity is a binary-valued time-series that indicates the presence of action potential events
056   from neurons and count processes such as Poisson are employed for modeling spiking activity. LFP
057   activity is a continuous-valued modality that measures network-level neural processes and is typically
058   modeled with a Gaussian distribution (Einevoll et al., 2013; Lu et al., 2021). Further, LFPs are often
059   obtained at a slower timescale than spikes, which are typically measured at a millisecond timescale
060   (Lu et al., 2021). We refer to multimodal data with different timescales as multiscale data. Thus, to
061   fuse information across the spiking and LFP modalities and improve downstream behavior decoding
062   tasks, their dynamics should be modeled by incorporating their cross-modality probabilistic and
063
      timescale differences.
064   Most of the existing dynamical modeling approaches in neuroscience focus on a single modality
065   of neural activity. These models are either in linear or switching linear form (Macke et al., 2011;
066   Koh et al., 2023; Linderman et al., 2017) or utilize deep learning approaches for nonlinear modeling
067   (Abbaspourazad et al., 2023; Archer et al., 2015; Gao et al., 2016; Pandarinath et al., 2018). However,
068   these models do not capture multimodal neural dynamics. Even though some approaches aim to
069
      model single-modal neural activity and behavior as multimodal signals (Sani et al., 2021; Hurwitz
      et al., 2021; Schneider et al., 2023), their latent factor inference is performed by processing single-
070
      modal neural signals. In contrast, (Rezaei et al., 2023) and (Coleman et al., 2011) propose linear
071
      multimodal frameworks for neural activity, but they do not handle different timescales. Motivated
072   by this, recent dynamical models have been designed for multiscale neural dynamics while also
073   enabling real-time inference and handling timescale differences, but they are in linear form and
074   cannot capture nonlinearities (Abbaspourazad et al., 2021; Ahmadipour et al., 2023). There have also
075   been important recent studies on nonlinear modeling of multimodal neural data (Kramer et al., 2022;
076   Brenner et al., 2022; Gondur et al., 2023; Zhou & Wei, 2020), however, their latent factor inference is
077   done non-causally over time and does not handle different timescales. Beyond neural time-series data,
078   many approaches in other domains have been proposed to combine multiple modalities to improve
079   performance in downstream task. However, they are not focused on addressing the challenge of
080
      different timescales and missing samples over time, and their applicability to modeling of Poisson and
      Gaussian distributed modalities encountered in neuroscience has not been investigated (see Section
081
      4).
082
083   Contributions We develop a novel nonlinear dynamical modeling method that can nonlinearly
084   aggregate information across multiple modalities with different distributions, distinct timescales,
085   and/or missing samples over time, while supporting inference both in real-time and non-causally.
086
      To achieve these capabilities and enhance performance compared with baselines, we: 1) design a
      multiscale encoder that performs nonlinear information fusion through neural networks after learning
087
      modality-specific linear dynamical models (LDMs) that account for timescale differences and missing
088
      samples in real-time by learning temporal dynamics (Section 2.2), 2) impose smoothness priors on
089   the latent dynamics via new smoothness regularization objectives that also prevent learning trivial
090   identity neural network transformations (Section 2.3) and 3) employ a technique termed time-dropout
091   during model training to increase robustness to missing samples even further (Appendix A). We term
092   this method Multiscale Real-time Inference of Nonlinear Embeddings (MRINE).
093
      Through stochastic Lorenz attractor simulations and real nonhuman primate (NHP) spiking and LFP
094
      neural datasets, we show that MRINE infers latent factors that are more predictive of target variables –
095   i.e., true latent factors for the simulations and behavioral arm/manipulandum movement variables for
096   the NHP datasets. Further, we compare MRINE with various recent linear and nonlinear multimodal
097   methods and show that MRINE outperforms all methods in behavior decoding for the NHP datasets.

      2    M ETHODOLOGY
101
102   We assume that we observe discrete neural signals (e.g., spikes) st ∈ {0, 1}ns for t ∈ T where
103   T = {1, 2, . . . , T } and continuous neural signals (e.g., LFPs) yt′ ∈ Rny for t′ ∈ T ′ where T ′ ⊆ T .
104   Note that the two different sets T and T ′ allow for the timescale differences of st and yt′ via different
105   time-indices. As shown in Figure 1 and expanded on below, we describe the neural processes
106   generating st and yt′ through multiscale latent and embedding factors, which in turn can be extracted
107   by nonlinearly aggregating information across these multiple neural modalities with a multiscale
      encoder.

140
      obtain at as the nonlinear aggregation of multimodal information through the multiscale encoder
141
      designed in Section 2.2. Further, xt ∈ Rnx , termed multiscale latent factors, are the LDM state and
142   model the linear dynamics in the nonlinear embedding space. Note that from this point onward, we
143   will only use time-index t (except observation models) for embedding and latent factors as T ′ ⊆ T .
144   Correspondingly, A ∈ Rnx ×nx and C ∈ Rna ×nx are the state transition and observation matrices in
145   the multiscale LDM, respectively; wt ∈ Rnx and rt ∈ Rna are zero-mean Gaussian dynamics and

147
      The observations from different modalities st and yt′ are then generated from at (or at′ ) as in Eq.
148   3 and 4, respectively, with the likelihood distributions denoted by pθs (st | at ) and pθy (yt′ | at′ ).
149   Assuming that data modalities are conditionally independent given at (or at′ for yt′ ), we modeled
150   the discrete spiking activity st with a Poisson distribution and the continuous neural signals yt′
151   with a Gaussian distribution, given that these distributions have shown success in modeling each of
152   these modalities (Stavisky et al., 2015; Gao et al., 2016; Pandarinath et al., 2018; Abbaspourazad
153   et al., 2021). The means of the corresponding distributions λ(at ) ∈ Rns and µ(at′ ) ∈ Rny are
154   parametrized by neural networks with parameters θs and θy . Practically, we observed that learning
155   the variance of the Gaussian likelihood yielded suboptimal performance, thus we set it to a constant
156
      value, i.e., unit variance, as in previous works (Fraccaro et al., 2017; Henaff et al., 2018; Pong et al.,
      2020).

      To test MRINE’s information aggregation capabilities in a real dataset, we used a publicly available
349   NHP dataset (Makin et al., 2018; O’Doherty et al., 2020). In this dataset, discrete spiking activity
350   and LFP signals were recorded while the subject was performing sequential 2D reaches on a grid to
351   random targets appearing in a virtual-reality environment (Makin et al., 2018; O’Doherty et al., 2020)
352   (details in Appendix D.1). We considered the 2D cursor velocity in the x and y directions as our
353   target behavior variables to decode from inferred latent factors (see Appendix D.3 for details). We
354   trained single-scale models with 5, 10, and 20 channels of spike and LFP signals, and MRINE models
355   for every combination of these multimodal channel sets. In our analyses, we used 4 experimental
356   sessions recorded on different days and performed 5-fold cross-validation for each session.
357   First, we tested MRINE’s behavior decoding performance when both spike and LFP modalities had
358   the same timescale, i.e., were observed every 10 ms (we abbreviate these LFPs as 10 ms LFPs). When
359   spiking signals were taken as the primary modality, fusing them with increasing numbers of LFP
360   channels steadily improved the behavior decoding accuracy for all different numbers of primary spike
361   channels (Figure 4a, p < 0.007, n = 20, one-sided Wilcoxon signed-rank test). Similar to simulation
362   results, improvements in behavior decoding accuracy were higher in the low-information regimes,
363
      i.e., for 5 and 10 primary spike channels. For instance, adding 20 LFP channels to 5, 10, and 20 spike
      channels improved behavior decoding accuracy by 17.7%, 12.6%, and 8.3%, respectively (Figure 4c).
364
      Similarly, when LFP signals were considered as the primary modality, behavior decoding accuracies
365
      again improved significantly when spike channels were fused (Figure 4b, p < 10−4 , n = 20, one-
366   sided Wilcoxon signed-rank test). As illustrated in Figure 4d, the improvements in this scenario were
367   higher compared to when spiking activity was the primary modality, i.e., adding 20 spike channels

461   4   R ELATED WORK
462
463   Single-Scale Models of Neural Activity Numerous dynamical models of neural activity have
464   been developed. Some of these models are in linear, generalized linear, or switching linear form
465   (Macke et al., 2011; Buesing et al., 2012; Cunningham & Yu, 2014; Kao et al., 2015; Koh et al., 2023;
466   Linderman et al., 2017). LDMs are widely used in real-time applications because they provide real-
467   time and recursive inference algorithms. However, LDMs cannot capture the potential nonlinearities
468   underlying neural activity. For this reason, there has been an increased interest in deep learning
469   architectures including recurrent neural network (RNN) based methods with nonlinear temporal
470   dynamics (Pandarinath et al., 2018; She & Wu, 2020), autoencoder-based architectures that utilize the
471
      Markovian-property of linear dynamics to learn a smoothing distribution (Archer et al., 2015; Gao
      et al., 2016; Durstewitz, 2017), transformer encoder based models optimized with masked training
472
      (Ye & Pandarinath, 2021; Le & Shlizerman, 2022) and neural ordinary differential equations (Kim
473
      et al., 2021). These models have shown great promise in improving behavior decoding compared
474   to the linear models (Pandarinath et al., 2018). A recent work Abbaspourazad et al. (2023) has also
475   developed an autoencoder-based nonlinear framework with linear dynamics that supports real-time
476   inference by utilizing Kalman filtering similarly with linear approaches (Kalman, 1960). Despite the
477   LDM-based approaches can handle missing samples as well as some other nonlinear methods such as
478   (Ramchandran et al., 2021), all these methods are designed for a single modality of neural activity
479   and do not address multiscale modeling.
480   Multimodal Information Fusion Outside neuroscience applications, fusing multiple modalities
481   has been researched across many areas including natural language processing (NLP) and computer
482   vision. It has been shown that integrating visual and/or acoustic signals with text can improve the
483   performance of downstream classification tasks in NLP, e.g., sentiment analysis (Hazarika et al.,
484   2020; Tsai et al., 2019a; Han et al., 2021; Poria et al., 2017; Zadeh et al., 2017). In computer
485   vision, many studies focused on variational autoencoders (VAE), and approximated the joint posterior
      distribution by factorization over modality-specific posteriors (Wu & Goodman, 2018; Shi et al.,


                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      2019; Sutter et al., 2021; Tsai et al., 2019b; Lee & Pavlovic, 2021) to handle missing modalities, or
487   by concatenating the modality-specific representations (Suzuki et al., 2016; Vedantam et al., 2018).
488   Instead of learning the common embedding space by factorization or concatenation, some studies
489   have also employed cross-modality generation (Joy et al., 2021; Pandey & Dukkipati, 2017). Even
490   though some of these methods can handle time-series modalities with different timescales, they need
491   to do so using separate networks to noncausally encode each modality into a single vector. However,
492   real-time applications require aggregating information at each time step, potentially from modalities
493   with different timescales, in a causal/real-time manner to perform continuous decoding of targets.
494   Multimodal Models in Neuroscience A line of work for multimodal modeling in neuroscience aims
495   to model single-scale neural activity and behavior as multimodal signals (Sani et al., 2021; Hurwitz
496   et al., 2021; Schneider et al., 2023). However, their latent factor inference is performed by processing
497   single-scale neural activity, similar to the single-scale models discussed above. Several approaches
498   have been proposed for multiscale modeling of neural dynamics. However, these methods are either
499   simply linear/generalized-linear or they are designed for offline reconstruction without enabling
500   real-time inference. Specifically, some approaches proposed multimodal modeling frameworks
501
      (Rezaei et al., 2023; Coleman et al., 2011) that utilize linear temporal dynamics and learn the model
      parameters via expectation-maximization (EM). However, their latent factor inference is not designed
502
      to operate on multimodal signals with different timescales. To address this, a multiscale linear
503
      dynamical modeling framework for continuous LFP and discrete spiking activity has been introduced
504   in Abbaspourazad et al. (2021), where model parameters are also learned with EM. With a similar
505   linear formulation of multiscale dynamics, recent work in Ahmadipour et al. (2023) proposed a more
506   computationally efficient learning framework compared to Abbaspourazad et al. (2021) by using
507   subspace identification and showed that the method also performs more accurately than the EM-based
508   approach. However, both approaches are of linear form and cannot characterize nonlinearities.
509
      Recent studies Brenner et al. (2022); Kramer et al. (2022); Zhou & Wei (2020); Gondur et al. (2023)
510   have developed a nonlinear multimodal framework that can process multimodal neural signals for
511   latent factor inference, but their inference network is non-causal and thus does not enable real-time
512   inference of latent factors. Further, their formulation assumes the same timescale for the different
513   modalities and does not consider missing samples. Thus, in such situations, they would need to
514   rely on indirect approaches such as augmentations with zero-padding, which can be suboptimal
515   by changing the value of missing samples (Che et al., 2018; Wells et al., 2013; Zhu et al., 2021;
516   Abbaspourazad et al., 2023). Instead, here we develop a nonlinear dynamical modeling framework
517   for multimodal time-series data that supports real-time and efficient recursive inference, and handles
518
      both timescale differences and missing samples by directly leveraging the learned dynamical model
      to predict these missing samples.

      5    D ISCUSSION
521
522
      In this study, we presented MRINE that can nonlinearly and in real-time aggregate information
523   across multiple time-series modalities, even with different timescales or with missing samples. To
524   achieve this, we proposed a novel multiscale encoder design that first extracts modality-specific
525   representations while accounting for their timescale differences and missing samples, and then
526   performs nonlinear fusion to aggregate multimodal information. We combined this encoder with
527   a multiscale LDM backbone to achieve real-time multiscale fusion. Through stochastic Lorenz
528   attractor simulations and real NHP datasets, we show MRINE’s ability to causally fuse information
529   across modalities even with different timescales or with missing samples. We show that MRINE
530   outperforms recent linear and nonlinear multimodal methods, and these comparisons in addition
531
      to ablation studies on the effect of loss terms, and using different observation models show the
      importance of MRINE’s multiscale nonlinear fusion and training objective outlined in Section 2.3.
532
      Further, as shown in Appendices E.4 and D.5, multiscale modeling is crucial for modalities with
533
      different timescales, as performances of current approaches significantly degrade with data imputation.
534   A current limitation of MRINE is the assumption of time-invariant multiscale dynamics, which may
535   not hold in non-stationary cases. In such cases, MRINE models may need to be intermittently
536   retrained across days/sessions. Extending MRINE to track temporal variability such as with switching
537   dynamics or adaptive approaches is an important future direction. Overall, we showed that MRINE
538   enables real-time multiscale decoding and cross-modality prediction that can handle both timescale
539   differences and missing samples, while also providing competitive performance. Thus MRINE can
      be especially important for future real-time systems such as brain-computer interfaces.

1075   reaching dataset across all information regimes, even though MRINE was trained on neural modalities

1078   tributing to such performance gap (see Appendix E.2) whereas mmPLRNN is trained on optimizing
1079   evidence lower bound (ELBO), whose optimization can be challenging due to KL-divergence term

       B.7      MVAE T RAINING
1095
1096   Multimodal variational autoencoder (MVAE) is a variational autoencoder-based architecture proposed
1097   in Wu & Goodman (2018) that can account for partially paired multimodal datasets by a mixture
1098   of experts posterior distribution factorization. However, the notion of partial observations in our
1099   work and MVAE are different. In MVAE, partial observations refer to having partially missing data
1100   tuples in each element of the batch, which would translate to having completely missing LFP or
1101   spike signals for a given trial/segment of multimodal neural activity. However, as we detailed in
1102   Section 2, we are interested in modeling multimodal neural activities with different sampling rates,
1103
       i.e., partially missing time-steps rather than missing either of the signals completely. Further, as
       discussed in Section 4, the latent factor inference in MVAE is designed to encode each modality to a
1104
       single factor, whereas MRINE is designed to infer latent factors for each time-step so that behavior
1105
       decoding can be performed at each time-step. To account for all these differences, we trained MVAE
1106   models without a dynamic backbone by treating each time-step as a different data point that allowed
1107   us to train MVAE models with partially missing time-steps as done for MRINE. As shown in Table
1108   1, MVAE showed the lowest performance among all methods, which could be caused by lacking a
1109   dynamical backbone, unlike other methods.

1125

1140   C.1    DYNAMICAL SYSTEM
1141
       The following set of dynamical equations defines the stochastic Lorenz attractor system:
1142
1143                                      dx1 = σ(x2 − x1 )dt + q1
1144                                      dx2 = (ρx1 − x1 x3 − x2 )dt + q2                                    (18)
1145                                      dx3 = (x1 x2 − βx3 )dt + q3
1146
1147   where x1 , x2 , and x3 are the latent factors of the Lorenz attractor dynamics, dt denotes the discretiza-
1148   tion time-step of the continuous system and d is the change of variables in dt time. We used σ = 10,
1149   ρ = 28, β = 83 and dt = 0.006 as in Pandarinath et al. (2018). q1 , q2 , and q3 are zero-mean Gaussian
1150
       dynamic noises with variances of 0.01. We generated 750 trials each containing 200 time-steps, and
       the initial condition of each trial was obtained by running the system for 500 burn-in steps starting
1151
       from a random point. Then, we normalized trajectories to have zero mean and a maximum value of 1
1152
       across time for each latent dimension.
1153
1154
       C.2    G ENERATING HIGH DIMENSIONAL OBSERVATIONS
1155
1156   To generate the Gaussian-distributed modalities, we multiplied the normalized latent factors by a
1157   random matrix Cy ∈ Rny ×3 and added zero mean Gaussian noise with variance of 5 to generate
1158   noisy observations.
1159
       To generate the Poisson-distributed modalities, we first generated firing rates by multiplying the
1160
       normalized trajectories by another random matrix Cs ∈ Rns ×3 and added a log baseline firing rate of
1161   5 spikes/sec with bin-size of 5 ms, followed by exponentiation. We then generated spiking activity
1162   by sampling from the Poisson process whose mean is the simulated firing rates.
1163
1164   C.3    C OMPUTING THE LATENT RECONSTRUCTION ACCURACY
1165
1166   For MRINE and each single-scale network trained only on Poisson or Gaussian modalities, we
1167   obtained the smoothed single-scale or multiscale latent factors xt|T , t ∈ {1, 2, . . . , T } for each trial
1168   in the training and test sets. To quantify how well the inferred latent factors can reconstruct the true
1169   latent factors, we fitted a linear regression model from the inferred latent factors of the training set
1170   to the corresponding true latent factors. Using the same linear regression model, we reconstructed
1171
       the true latent factors from the inferred latent factors of the test set. Then, we computed the Pearson
       correlation coefficient (CC) between the true and reconstructed latent factors for each trial and latent
1172
       dimension. The reported values are averaged over trials and latent dimensions.

1383   D.6   C ROSS - MODAL IMPUTATION
1384
1385   To evaluate MRINE’s information aggregation capabilities beyond behavior decoding, we computed
1386   cross-modal imputed one-step-ahead prediction accuracies of both modalities under various sample
1387   dropping probabilities in addition to having different timescales. To do that, the modality of interest
1388
       was randomly dropped when the other modality was fully observed. Therefore, the one-step-ahead
       predictions of the missing modality were generated by leveraging the learned modality-specific
1389
       and multiscale dynamics that allow for cross-modal imputations. Unlike MRINE, one-step-ahead
1390
       prediction accuracies of single-scale networks were obtained with fully available observations.
1391
1392   For LFP signals modeled with Gaussian likelihood, we quantified the one-step-ahead prediction
1393   accuracy by computing the CC between the one-step-ahead predicted mean of the Gaussian likelihood
1394
       distribution (µ(at+1|t )) and the true observations across time.
1395   For spike signals modeled with Poisson likelihood, one-step-ahead prediction accuracy was quantified
1396   using the prediction power (PP) measure, which is defined as PP= 2AUC−1 where AUC denotes
1397   the area under the curve of the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve (Macke et al., 2011;
1398   Abbaspourazad et al., 2021). We constructed the ROC by using the one-step-ahead predicted firing
1399   rates, i.e., λ(at+1|t ), as the classification scores to determine whether a time-step contained a spike
1400
       or not (Truccolo et al., 2010). Both metrics were averaged over observation dimensions.
1401   As shown in Figure 8, MRINE outperformed single-scale networks in one-step-ahead prediction
1402   accuracy even when 60% of spike samples (panel a) and 40% of LFP samples were missing. This
1403   result suggests that MRINE’s information aggregation capabilities are not only limited to behavior
       decoding, but can also enable cross-modal prediction under missing sample scenarios.

       E.4   E FFECT OF MULTISCALE ENCODER DESIGN
1554
1555   As discussed in Section 2.2, accounting for different sampling rates for neural signals is an important
1556   consideration for MRINE’s encoder design shown in Fig. 2. To achieve that, we learn modality-
1557   specific LDMs in MRINE’s encoder that can leverage within-modality state dynamics to account
1558   for missing samples whether due to timescale differences or missed measurements. Therefore,
1559   MRINE can perform inference without relying on augmentations to impute missing samples, such
1560   as zero-imputation as done in common practice (Lipton et al., 2016; Zhu et al., 2021) that can yield
1561   suboptimal performance (Che et al., 2018; Wells et al., 2013).

1563   zero-imputation translates to mean-imputation since LFP signals are z-scored before training) and
1564   trained MRINE, mmPLRNN and mmLFADS models with 20 channels of 10 ms spike and 10 ms
1565   zero-imputed LFP signals. For all models, we trained 2 versions where reconstructions/predictions
       of zero-imputed LFP time-steps are either included (T ′ = T ) or masked (T ′ ⊂ T ) in the training
