009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, advancements in video synthesis have attracted significant attention.
012            Video synthesis models such as AnimateDiff and Stable Video Diffusion have
013            demonstrated the practical applicability of diffusion models in creating dynamic
014            visual content. The emergence of SORA has further spotlighted the potential
015            of video generation technologies. Despite advancements, the extension of video
016            lengths remains constrained by computational resources. Most existing video syn-
017            thesis models are limited to generating short video clips. In this paper, we propose
018
               a novel post-tuning methodology for video synthesis models, called ExVideo.
               This approach is designed to enhance the capability of current video synthesis
019
               models, allowing them to produce content over extended temporal durations while
020
               incurring lower training expenditures. In particular, we design extension strategies
021            across common temporal model architectures respectively, including 3D convolu-
022            tion, temporal attention, and positional embedding. To evaluate the efficacy of
023            our proposed post-tuning approach, we trained ExSVD, an extended model based
024            on Stable Video Diffusion model. Our approach enhances the model’s capacity to
025            generate up to 5× its original number of frames, requiring only 1.5k GPU hours of
026            training on a dataset comprising 40k videos. Importantly, the substantial increase
027            in video length doesn’t compromise the model’s innate generalization capabilities,
028            and the model showcases its advantages in generating videos of diverse styles and
029
               resolutions. We will release the source code and the enhanced model publicly1 .

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In recent years, diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020) have achieved out-
034   standing results in image synthesis, significantly surpassing previous GANs (Dhariwal & Nichol,
035   2021). These achievements have subsequently fostered a burgeoning interest in the adaptation of
036   diffusion models for video synthesis. Models such as Stable Video Diffusion (Blattmann et al.,
037   2023), AnimateDiff (Guo et al., 2023), and VideoCrafter (Chen et al., 2023a) epitomize this re-
038   search trajectory, showcasing the ability to produce frames that are not only coherent but also of
039   high visual quality. These achievements underscore the practicality and potential of employing dif-
040   fusion models in the field of video synthesis. With the groundbreaking results of SORA (Liu et al.,
041   2024) reported at the beginning of 2024, the research direction of video synthesis has once again
042
      attracted widespread attention.
043   Although current video synthesis models are capable of producing video clips of satisfactory quality,
044   the generated videos are generally short, and extending their duration remains a challenge. Current
045   methodologies can be categorized into three types to generate longer videos. 1) Pre-training using
046   long video datasets (Chen et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2023b; Bain et al., 2021). Through extensive
047   training with long video samples, it is foreseeable that models can improve their ability to generate
048
      longer videos. However, training with such datasets would result in prohibitively escalated costs.
      Consequently, given the computational constraints, current video generation models are primarily
049
      trained on short video clips. 2) Generating videos in a streaming (Kodaira et al., 2023) or slid-
050
      ing window (Duan et al., 2024) manner. Without further training, longer videos can be generated
051   by stitching together several short video segments. However, this approach leads to lower video

064   Recent breakthroughs in the development of LLMs (large language models) (Xiong et al., 2023;

074   we enhance the temporal modules of the model, thereby facilitating the processing of content across
075   longer temporal spans.

098   2.1   D IFFUSION M ODELS
099
      Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020) are a category of generative models
100
      that characterize the content generation as a Markov random process. Unlike GANs (Goodfellow
101
      et al., 2014), diffusion models do not require adversarial training, thus making their training process
102   more stable. Moreover, through an iterative generation process, diffusion models are capable of
103   producing images with exceptionally high quality. In recent years, image synthesis models based
104   on diffusion, including Pixart (Chen et al., 2023b), Imagen (Saharia et al., 2022), Hunyuan-DiT
105   (Li et al., 2024b), and the Stable Diffusion series (Rombach et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2023; Kang
106   et al., 2024), have achieved impressive success. Diffusion models have given rise to a vast open-
107   source technology ecosystem. Technologies such as LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), ControlNet (Zhang
      et al., 2023), DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023), Textual Inversion (Gal et al., 2022), and IP-Adapter

108
      (Ye et al., 2023) have endowed the generation process of diffusion models with a high degree of
109   controllability, thereby meeting the needs of various application scenarios.

      2.2   V IDEO S YNTHESIS
112
113   Given the remarkable success of diffusion models in image synthesis, video synthesis approaches
114   based on diffusion have also been proposed in recent years. For example, by adding motion mod-
115   ules to the UNet model (Ronneberger et al., 2015) in Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), An-
116   imateDiff (Guo et al., 2023) transfers the capabilities of image synthesis to video synthesis. Stable
117   Video Diffusion (Blattmann et al., 2023) is an image-to-video model architecture and can synthesize
118
      video clips after end-to-end video synthesis training. Unlike image synthesis models, video synthe-
      sis models require substantial computational resources since the model needs to process multiple
119
      frames simultaneously. As a result, most existing video generation models (Guo et al., 2023; Chen
120
      et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023a) can only produce very short video clips. For instance, AnimateDiff
121   can generate up to 32 frames, while Stable Video Diffusion can generate a maximum of 25 frames.
122   This limitation prompts us to explore the methodology to construct video synthesis models over
123   longer temporal scales.
124
125   2.3   E XTENDING G ENERATIVE M ODELS
126
127   Although the existing diffusion models are trained with a fixed scale, such as Stable Diffusion being
128   trained at a fixed resolution of 512 × 512, some approaches can extend them to larger scales. For
129   instance, in image synthesis, approaches like Mixture of Diffusers (Jiménez, 2023), MultiDiffusion
130   (Bar-Tal et al., 2023), and ScaleCrafter (He et al., 2023) can increase the resolution of generated
131
      images by altering the inference process of the UNet model in Stable Diffusion. Similar techniques
      have also emerged in the field of large language models. With the help of positional encoding
132
      technologies such as RoPE (Su et al., 2024) and ALiBi (Press et al., 2021), large language models
133
      can extrapolate to longer text processing tasks under the premise of training with limited-length
134   texts. Post-tuning can further help language models achieve super-long text comprehension and
135   generation (Xiong et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023c). These research findings have inspired and
136   motivate us to explore the extension of video synthesis models. We aim to endow existing video
137   synthesis models with the capability to generate longer videos.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
140
141
      In this section, we first review the architectures of mainstream video diffusion models, then discuss
142   the methodologies we have adopted to extend the temporal modules for long video synthesis, and
143   finally introduce the post-tuning strategy.
144
145   3.1   P RELIMINARIES
146
147   The huge demands of computational resources for training video synthesis models lead to a prevalent
148   practice of adapting existing image synthesis models for video generation. This adaptation is typ-
149   ically achieved by incorporating temporal modules into the model for generating dynamic content.
150   We provide a comprehensive overview of temporal module architectures as follows:
151
             • 3D convolution (Li et al., 2021): Convolution layers form the foundational blocks in com-
152            puter vision. 2D convolution layers have been employed in the UNet (Ronneberger et al.,
153            2015) architecture, which is widely used in diffusion models. By extending 2D convo-
154            lutions into the third dimension, these layers are seamlessly adapted in video synthesis
155            models. Research indicates that convolution layers in diffusion models exhibit a high de-
156            gree of adaptability across various resolutions (Bar-Tal et al., 2023), which is a testament
157            to their capacity for generalization.
158          • Temporal attention (Vaswani et al., 2017): In image synthesis, the importance of attention
159            mechanisms is underscored by their contribution to the generation of images with remark-
160            able fidelity, as evidenced by the ablation studies in latent diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022).
161            Transferring spatial attention mechanisms to the video domain raises concerns regarding
               computational efficiency due to the quadratic time complexity of the attention operators.

193   3.2    E XTENDING T EMPORAL M ODULES
194
195   Most video synthesis models are pre-trained on videos comprising only a constrained number of
196   frames due to limited computational resources. For instance, Stable Video Diffusion Blattmann
197   et al. (2023) is capable of generating a maximum of 25 frames, while AnimateDiff Guo et al. (2023)
198   is limited to synthesizing image sequences of up to 32 frames. To augment these models to produce
199   extended videos, we propose enhancements to the temporal modules within these models.
200   Firstly, the inherent functionality of 3D convolution layers to adaptively accommodate various scales
201   has been previously validated through empirical studies (Jiménez, 2023; Bar-Tal et al., 2023; He
202   et al., 2023), even without necessitating fine-tuning. Consequently, we opt to retain the 3D convo-
203   lution layers in their original form to preserve these capabilities. Secondly, regarding the temporal
204   attention modules, research on large language models has demonstrated the potential for scaling ex-
205   isting models to accommodate longer contextual sequences (Xiong et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023c).
206   Inspired by these findings, we fine-tune the parameters within the temporal attention layers during
207
      the training process to enhance their efficacy over extended frame sequences. Thirdly, for the posi-
      tional embedding layers, either static or trainable embeddings cannot be directly applied to longer
208
      videos. To circumvent this pitfall while ensuring compatibility with a wide array of existing video
209
      models, we use extended trainable parameters to replace the original positional embeddings. These
210   extended trainable positional embeddings are initialized in a cyclic pattern, drawing upon the config-
211   urations of the pre-existing embeddings. Further, drawing inspiration from various adapter models
212   (Hu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023), we incorporate an additional identity 3D convolution layer after
213   the positional embedding layer, aimed at learning long-term information. The central unit of this 3D
214   convolution kernel is initialized as an identity matrix, and the remaining parameters are initialized
215   to zero. The identity 3D convolution layer ensures that, before training, there is no alteration to the
      video representation, thereby maintaining consistency with the original computational process.

222   3.3     P OST-T UNING
223
224   After extending the temporal blocks in the video synthesis models, we enhance the model’s abili-
225   ties to generate extended videos via post-tuning. To circumvent potential copyright concerns with
226   video content, we employed a publicly available dataset OpenSoraPlan2 , which comprises 40,258
227   videos. These videos were sourced from copyright-free platforms, including Mixkit3 , Pexels4 , and
228   Pixabay5 . The videos in this dataset maintain a resolution of 512 × 512. ExVideo expands its ca-
229
      pacity to 128 frames. Over such extended sequences, full training is deemed impractical because
      of the substantial computational requirements. Instead, we employed several engineering optimiza-
230
      tions aimed at optimizing GPU memory usage. These optimizations are crucial for managing the
231
      increased computational load and facilitating efficient training within limited hardware resources:
232
233            • Parameter freezing: All parameters except the temporal blocks are frozen.
234
               • Mixed precision training (Micikevicius et al., 2017): We deploy a mixed precision train-
235
                 ing program by converting a subset of parameters to 16-bit floating-point format.
236
237            • Gradient checkpointing (Feng & Huang, 2021): Gradient checkpointing is enabled in the
238              model. By storing intermediate states during forward passes and recomputing gradients
239
                 on-demand during the backward pass, this technique effectively decreases memory usage.
240            • Flash Attention (Dao, 2023): We integrate Flash Attention to enhance the computational
241              efficiency of attention mechanisms.
242            • Shard optimizer states and gradients: We leverage DeepSpeed (Rasley et al., 2020), a
243              library optimized for distributed training, to enable shard optimizer states and gradients
244              across multiple GPUs.
245
246   The loss function and the noise scheduler are consistent with the original model. The learning rate
247   is 1 × 10−5 and the batch size on each GPU is 1. The training was conducted using only 8 NVIDIA
248   A100 GPUs over one week. In order to ensure the stability of the training process, exponential
249
      moving averages were employed for the update of weights.

252
253   By integrating extended temporal modules into the original Stable Video Diffusion (SVD) model
254   and performing post-tuning, we have developed the Extended Stable Video Diffusion (ExSVD)
255   model. This enhanced model is capable of generating coherent videos with lengths of up to 128
256   frames. To validate its capabilities, we have conducted comprehensive experiments comprising
257   three components. First, we perform a comparative analysis between our ExSVD and the original
258   SVD model to elucidate the enhancements achieved through post-tuning. Second, we evaluate the
259   performance of ExSVD in comparison to other publicly accessible models. Finally, we present
260
      illustrative examples to provide a tangible understanding of the model’s performance.

      4.1.1   AUTOMATIC E VALUATION
294
295   Parameter Settings: The comparative experiments are conducted based on VBench (Huang et al.,
296   2024), which is a comprehensive suite of tools designed to automatically assess the quality of gen-
297   erated videos. Following VBench’s prompt sampling methodology, we assigned 5 random seeds to
298   each prompt for the text-to-video generation process. Initially, we employed the Hunyuan-DiT (Li
299   et al., 2024b) text-to-image model to generate 5 images for each prompt. Subsequently, for each
300   image, both our ExSVD and the SVD models were used to generate corresponding videos, resulting
301   in a total of 4720 (944 × 5) videos for each model. We used the DDIM (Song et al., 2020) sampler
302   with 50 sampling steps in our experiments.
303   Evaluation Metrics: VBench assesses video performance from two broad perspectives: video qual-
304   ity and video-condition consistency. Video quality focuses on the perceptual quality of the synthe-
305   sized video, including temporal quality and frame-wise quality. This category is composed of seven
306   sub-metrics: subject consistency, background consistency, temporal flickering, motion smoothness,
307   dynamic degree, aesthetic quality, and imaging quality. Video-condition consistency focuses on
308   whether the synthesized video aligns with the user-provided guiding condition (text prompt) and in-
309
      cludes metrics from semantic and style dimensions. This category is composed of nine sub-metrics:
      object class, multiple objects, human action, color, spatial relationship, scene, appearance style,
310
      temporal style, and overall consistency. It is important to note that the dynamic degrees in the orig-
311
      inal implementation of VBench are sensitive to frames per second (FPS) and the total number of
312   frames. Our findings indicate that this metric can significantly impact the overall score, potentially
313   resulting in biased comparisons between different models. Our model will achieve an exceptionally
314   high score when we use a high FPS, which is ultimately inconsequential. We will address this issue
315   in future research, as there currently are no valid metrics available for evaluation.
316

364   4.2     C OMPARISON WITH P UBLICLY ACCESSIBLE M ODELS
365
366   We further evaluated the performance of ExSVD in comparison to other publicly available models.
367   To facilitate a comprehensive analysis that incorporates both text-to-video and image-to-video mod-
368
      els, we designed a text-to-video pipeline that integrates ExSVD with Hunyuan-DiT. This allows for
      a uniform assessment of the models across the text-to-video task. The parameter settings of this
369
      evaluation were consistent with those outlined in the previous subsection.
370
371   Baseline Models: For a thorough evaluation of our models, we selected top-performing video syn-
372   thesis models for comparison. The chosen models include OpenSora (Zheng et al., 2024), Animate-
373   Diff (Guo et al., 2023), VideoCrafter2 (Chen et al., 2024a), Pika (Pika, 2024), T2V-Turbo (Li et al.,

410
411
      While ExVideo can enhance the capabilities of video diffusion models, the post-tuned version con-
412   tinues to be constrained by the inherent limitations of its foundational model. Notably, the extended
413   Stable Video Diffusion struggles to accurately synthesize human portraits, leading to frequent in-
414   stances of truncated frames. To develop a model capable of synthesizing high-quality long videos, it
415   is imperative to train a robust base model. Nevertheless, due to limitations in resources, we are un-
416   able to independently pre-train a large video synthesis model. Consequently, we eagerly anticipate
417   the release of open-source models in the future to advance our research endeavors.

      6     C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
421
422   In this paper, we delve into the enhancement of video diffusion models through post-tuning. Specifi-
423
      cally, we propose a post-tuning approach called ExVideo, which can extend the duration of generated
      videos and release the potential of video synthesis models. Based on Stable Video Diffusion, our
424
      approach achieves a quintupling in the number of frames, while preserving the original generaliza-
425
      tion abilities. ExVideo is designed within the constraints of limited computational resources, thus it
426   is exceptionally memory-efficient. By integrating this method with other open-source technologies,
427   we facilitate pipelines conducive to the production of high-quality videos. However, despite the
428   advancements achieved through post-tuning, the enhanced model remains inherently constrained by
429   the limitations of the base model. Looking ahead, we are committed to furthering our exploration
430   of video synthesis models through post-tuning methodologies. This will include the application of
431   ExVideo across a broader range of model architectures, as well as the training of these models on
      larger and more varied datasets.
