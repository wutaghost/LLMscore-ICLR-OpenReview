009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Model pruning is attracting increasing interest because of its positive implications
012            in terms of resource consumption and costs. A variety of methods have been
013            developed in the past years. In particular, structured pruning techniques discern
014            the importance of nodes in neural networks (NNs) and filters in convolutional
015            neural networks (CNNs). Global versions of these rank all nodes in a network and
016
               select the top-k, offering an advantage over local methods that rank nodes only
               within individual layers. By evaluating all nodes simultaneously, global techniques
017
               provide greater control over the network architecture, which improves performance.
018
               However, the ranking and selecting process carried out during global pruning can
019            have several major drawbacks. First, the ranking is not updated in real time based
020            on the pruning already performed, making it unable to account for inter-node
021            interactions. Second, it is not uncommon for whole layers to be removed from a
022            model, which leads to untrainable networks. Lastly, global pruning methods do
023            not offer any guarantees regarding re-training. In order to address these issues,
024            we introduce Mutual Information Preserving Pruning (MIPP). The fundamental
025            principle of our method is to select nodes such that the mutual information (MI)
026            between the activations of adjacent layers is maintained. We evaluate MIPP on an
027
               array of vision models and datasets, including a pre-trained ResNet50 on ImageNet,
               where we demonstrate MIPP’s ability to outperform state-of-the-art methods. The
028
               implementation of MIPP will be made available upon publication.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   It is well-established that to limit a model’s resource requirements while maintaining its accuracy, it is
034   preferable to prune and re-train a large model of high accuracy rather than train a smaller model from
035   the beginning (LeCun et al., 1989; 1998; Li et al., 2017; Han et al., 2015). Pruning can be categorized
036   into unstructured (LeCun et al., 1989; Han et al., 2015; Li et al., 2017; Singh & Alistarh, 2020) and
037   structured (Li et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2020; Wang & Fu, 2023). Unstructured
038   pruning selects individual weights to retain; while this offers maximum control it produces models
039
      that are not hardware-compatible and can only be deployed as sparse matrices (Han et al., 2015; Wen
      et al., 2016). Structured pruning, on the other hand, typically involves pruning nodes in multilayer
040
      perceptrons (MLPs) or filters in convolutional neural networks (CNNs). Unlike unstructured pruning,
041
      structured approaches generate neural networks (NNs) that can be compactly stored at the time of
042   deployment, thereby reducing resource consumption.
043
044   Research into structured pruning methods can be categorized into two complementary approaches.
045
      One focuses on enhancing the method used to determine node importance (LeCun et al., 1998;
      Hassibi & Stork, 1992; Han et al., 2016; Li et al., 2017; Nonnenmacher et al., 2022), while the other
046
      aims to refine the regularization technique used to reduce the value of the pruned nodes activations to
047
      zero (Wang et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021; Wang & Fu, 2023). Generally, existing
048   methods of node selection require that the nodes are ranked, and then the top-k are maintained while
049   the remainder are pruned (Wang et al., 2022). These steps can be carried out globally or locally. The
050   former involves ranking all nodes across all layers (Liu et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2019), whereas
051   local methods only consider a given layer (Zhao et al., 2019; Sung et al., 2024). Global methods are
052   preferred because they allow control over the neural architecture, thereby improving performance
053   (Blalock et al., 2020); however, this control over the architecture is not devoid of issues. Namely,
      entire layers can get pruned, creating untrainable bottlenecks. Additionally, simply ranking and


                                                          1

054
      selecting the top-k nodes, whether locally or globally, fails to consider the impact of pruning on the
055   relative importance of the remaining nodes. Inspired by the success of iterative magnitude pruning
056   (IMP) (Frankle & Carbin, 2019), SynFlow, an unstructured pruning method, adopted an iterative
057   approach that efficiently resolved these issues simultaneously (Tanaka et al., 2020). In contrast,
058   structured solutions require multiple re-training iterations, making them computationally impractical
059   for large models (Liebenwein et al., 2020).
060
      In this paper, we introduce Mutual Information Preserving Pruning (MIPP), a structured activation-
061   based pruning technique. MIPP ensures that the mutual information (MI) shared between activations
062   in adjacent layers is preserved during pruning. Rather than ranking nodes and selecting the top-k,
063   MIPP uses the transfer entropy redundancy critereon (TERC) to dynamically prune nodes whose
064   activations do not transfer entropy to the downstream layer (Westphal et al., 2024). Pruning in
065   this fashion affords MIPP the following major advantages: first, maintaining the MI between the
066   activations in adjacent layers ensures that there exists a function such that the activations of the
067   downstream layer can be approximated using those of the pruned upstream layer, thus preserving
068   re-trainability. Second, MIPP has the ability to consider not only long-range and local interactions but
069
      can also dynamically update these considerations in real-time depending on the nodes that have been
      pruned. Finally, using this dynamic method of node selection, we maintain maximum control over
070
      the network structure, preventing the rigid structure associated with local pruning and the vanishing
071
      layers associated with global techniques. To summarize, the contributions of this work are as follows:
072
073          • We develop MIPP, an activation-based pruning method that preserves MI between the
074            activations of adjacent layers in a deep NN. We prove that perfect MI preservation ensures
075            the existence of a function, discoverable by gradient descent, that can approximate the
076            activations of the downstream layer from the activations of the preceding pruned layer.
077            Consequently, MIPP implies re-trainability.
078          • We show that MIPP only selects nodes if they transfer entropy to the subsequent layer. This
079            dynamic method of node selection natively considers long- and short-range interactions,
080            while concurrently establishing per-layer pruning ratios (PRs) that avoid layer collapse.
081          • Through comprehensive experimental evaluation, we demonstrate that MIPP can effectively
082            prune networks, whether they are trained or not.

087
      MIPP is a structured, activation-based pruning method that is resistant to layer collapse. In the
      evaluation of MIPP, we aim to compare our approach to state-of-the-art structured pruning tech-
088
      niques, as well as to algorithms specifically designed to avoid layer collapse that are not structured.
089
      Consequently, we also review research dedicated to applying unstructured pruning techniques in a
090   structured manner.
091
092   Structured activation-based pruning. Activation-based pruning methods commonly view the
093
      activations as features and the outputs as targets, before ranking and selecting the top-k nodes in a
      global or local manner (He et al., 2017; Lin et al., 2020; Sui et al., 2021; He et al., 2017; Liu et al.,
094
      2018). Rather than considering the outputs as the target, some methods reconstruct the activations
095
      of the following layer from the preceding layer (Ding et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2017). The advantage
096   of this is that the function that generates layer l + 1 from layer l can be approximated using fewer
097   parameters than that which generates the outputs from layer l. One such method, ThiNet, greedily
098   selects nodes if they minimize the error in reconstructing the activations of the next layer (Luo
099   et al., 2017). Adding nodes in this fashion will prevent the model’s performance from degrading;
100   however, the condition for removal is too restrictive, as it does not consider the effects of re-training.
101   Furthermore, unlike MIPP, ThiNet is unable to establish layer-wise PRs. Liebenwein et al. (2020)
102   developed an activation-based pruning scheme with the ability to establish layer-wise PRs. However,
103   this method is not well adopted as it employs prohibitively expensive iterative re-training.
104   Establishing layer-wise pruning ratios. When pruning globally, the fraction of nodes removed from
105   each layer is rarely consistent. This updates the network structure, which has been shown to improve
106   performance (Blalock et al., 2020). However, at higher levels of sparsity, many methods experience
107   layer collapse, resulting in an untrainable network (Lee et al., 2019; 2020). In Tanaka et al. (2020),
      the authors hypothesized that the iterative nature of IMP in Frankle & Carbin (2019) prevented layer


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      collapse. Building upon these foundations, they developed SynFlow, a computationally efficient
109   iterative pruning technique that is known to avoid layer collapse. However, SynFlow is data-
110   independent, which, while improving its generalizability, can lead to a reduction in performance.
111   Tanaka et al. (2020) demonstrated that GraSP (Wang et al., 2022) was also resistant to layer collapse.
112   Unlike SynFlow, it is data-dependent, making it a more effective pruning method, outperforming
113   classic techniques such as SNIP (Lee et al., 2019).
114
      From unstructured to structured pruning. In structured pruning, the aim is to prune nodes or
115   filters rather than all trainable parameters (LeCun et al., 1989; Frankle & Carbin, 2019). The simplest
116   method to convert from unstructured to structured is to average the importance assigned to all the
117   weights associated with a given node. However, this may lead to a loss in information, particularly as
118   influential weights can be both highly positive and highly negative. As a result, research has aimed to
119   define functions that combine weight importances in a minimally lossy manner. In particular, the
120   L1- and L2-norms - related to the euclidean distance - lead to minimal information loss and have
121   proven effective for structured magnitude pruning (Han et al., 2015; Li et al., 2017; Wang & Fu,
122   2023). Magnitude-based pruning, while effective, lacks rigor: it does not account for long-range
123
      interactions, information redundancy, and so on. That said, the information preserving functions,
      such as L1- and L2-norm, are agnostic to the measure of weight importance used and have also
124
      successfully been applied to weight gradients (LeCun et al., 1998; Molchanov et al., 2017), and
125
      Hessian matrices (Hassibi & Stork, 1992; Peng et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2019; Nonnenmacher et al.,
126   2022). For instance, SOSP ranks nodes based on an L1-normalized combination of both the first- and
127   second-order derivatives of the weights with respect to the loss. This method has produced state-of-
128   the-art structured pruning results, although we will demonstrate that SOSP is prone to layer-collapse
129   at high levels of sparsity.

166   4.1     M OTIVATION
167
168   We consider one-shot pruning with retraining: the objective is to reduce the number of nodes of the
169   NN such that, after retraining, the pruned NN will achieve the same performance as the original. We
170   will now argue that one way to achieve this would be to select a subset of nodes from each layer in
171
      such a way that there exists a function which, when applied to this subset, can still reconstruct the
      activations of the subsequent layer. We will then prove that the existence of this function preserves
172
      the MI between the activations of these layers.
173

194   4.2.1    T RANSFER E NTROPY R EDUNDANCY C RITERION WITH MI O RDERING
195
196   Before describing the practical method, we now provide a summary of TERC and its application to
197   pruning, through the incorporation of an additional step for MI-based ordering.
198   TERC. As stated in Section 3, we aim to preserve the MI between the layers in our network. The
199   problem of MI preservation is one well-studied in the feature selection community (Battiti, 1994; Peng
200   et al., 2005; Gao et al., 2016). Thus, we are able to deploy an out-of-the-box solution. In particular
201   we use TERC, as not only does it preserve the MI with the target, but its temporal complexity is also
202   linear in time with respect to the number of features (Westphal et al., 2024), a key property when
203   working in highly dimensional feature spaces. In our case, rather than selecting features to describe
204   a target, we are selecting nodes that best describe the following layer. Within this context, TERC
205
      can be summarized as follows: to begin, all nodes in the layer are assumed to be useful (and added
      to the non-pruned set). We then sequentially evaluate whether the reduction in uncertainty of the
206
      subsequent layer’s activations is greater when a specific node is included in the un-pruned set rather
207
      than excluded. More formally, for a node, Xli , to remain in the set of un-pruned nodes, it must satisfy
208
      the following condition: I(Xl ; Xl+1 ) − I(Xl \Xli ; Xl+1 ) > 0. Otherwise, it is pruned. This process
209
      is sequentially repeated for all nodes in the layer. As shown in Westphal et al. (2024), this simple
210   technique will preserve the MI between layers.
211   MI Ordering. Before applying TERC, we sort the nodes in the pruning layer in descending order of
212   MI with the target. For further clarification, please see Algorithm 2 in Appendix B. This adjustment
213   ensures that we check last whether the more informative nodes transfer entropy to the activations of
214   subsequent layers. This makes it less likely that they will be erroneously removed during the early
215   stages of TERC when their information can be represented on aggregate by the large number of nodes
      still remaining in the un-pruned set.

      4.2.2    M UTUAL I NFORMATION E STIMATION
217
218   Unless restricting oneself to scenarios inapplicable to real-world data (e.g. discrete random variables),
219   verifying the condition in Section 4.2.1 is computationally intractable. Consequently, we must
220   estimate whether the condition is verified by estimating the MI, for which many methods have been
221   developed (Moon et al., 1995; Paninski, 2003; Belghazi et al., 2018; van den Oord et al., 2019; Poole
222
      et al., 2019).
223   For the purposes of pruning, our MI estimates need to only be considered for comparisons. Rather
224   than a method that gives highly accurate estimates slowly (Franzese et al., 2024), we require one that
225   emphasizes consistency and speed. For these reasons, we adopt the technique presented in Covert
226   et al. (2020), in which the authors demonstrate that the MI between two random processes (X and
227   Y ) can be approximated as the reduction in error estimation caused by using X to predict Y . More
228
      formally: I(X; Y ) ≈ E[l(f(∅), Y )] − E[l(f(X), Y )], where f is some function approximated via loss
      l. If the variables are discrete, and a cross entropy loss is used, then this value is exactly equal to
229
      the ground truth MI (Gadgil et al., 2024). Even if the variables are continuous and a mean squared
230
      error loss is used, the above value approaches the MI under certain circumstances (Covert et al.,
231   2020). To approximate the condition described in Section 4.2.1, we estimate all MIs five times before
232   calculating confidence intervals. We then only keep nodes for which we are more than x% sure, that
233   they transfer entropy to the subsequent layer (I(Xl ; Xl+1 ) > I(Xl \Xli ; Xl+1 )). The value of x%
234   naturally becomes the hyper-parameter we tune to affect the PR. For example, if x% is low, 50%,
235   one only needs to be 50% sure that I(Xl ; Xl+1 ) > I(Xl \Xli ; Xl+1 ), and thus, we prune sparingly.
236   On the contrary, if it is high (for example, x = 99%), we prune more aggressively. For a detailed
237   description of the method we used to determine x, please refer to Appendix D.1.

263

268   adjacent layer dependence. MIPP only removes nodes that are not essential for reconstructing the
269   next layer. As more nodes are pruned, those remaining become increasingly vital in the reconstruction,
      preventing layer collapse.

      4.4   P RESERVING THE M UTUAL I NFORMATION FROM O UTPUTS TO I NPUTS
289
290   Thus far we have explicitly described how we use TERC with MI ordering and the estimation
291   techniques described in Section 4.2.2, to preserve MI between the activations of adjacent layers. This
292   process is repeated for each pair of layers. However, to prune the entire model, by preserving the
293   MI between pairs of layers, one could start from the input layer and move to the output layer or
294   vice versa. In this section, like Luo et al. (2017), we argue for the second option, providing both
295   theoretical and practical arguments.
296   Theoretical argument. In a NN, because each layer is a direct function of its predecessor, these
297   pairs share perfect MI. In this case I(Xl ; Xl+1 ) = H(Xl+1 ) (Cover, 1999). Therefore, the networks
298   layers can only reduce in entropy from inputs to outputs (Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015; Shwartz-Ziv &
299   Tishby, 2017). Suppose we take the first approach, pruning from inputs to outputs. Our goal is to
300   prune the first layer (X1 ), such that the result can be used to reconstruct the activations of the second
301   layer (X2 ). Since the second layer has not yet been pruned, it may retain superfluous information,
302
      which is then maintained in the activations of the first layer during pruning. In contrast, if we take
                                                                                                              ′
      the second approach, we begin by pruning the activations in layer XL−1 . The information in XL−1
303
      (its pruned version) has been preserved due its ability to reconstruct exclusively the outputs. Upon
304                                                                                                ′
      moving onto the next pair, we prune layer XL−2 based on the entropy in the layer XL−1            . Notably
305   though, this has already been reduced by the first pairwise pruning step. By this recursive logic, it
306   is clear how even when pruning the first layer, we are still only preserving the entropy required to
307   reproduce the outputs, and only the outputs.
308   Practical argument. We now present the more practical reason to prune from outputs to inputs rather
309   than vice versa. Under this scheme we aim to evaluate the condition I(Xl′ ; Xl+1      ′                ′
                                                                                               ) = I(Xl ; Xl+1  ),
                        ′
310   rather than I(Xl ; Xl+1 ) = I(Xl ; Xl+1 ) which would be appropriate forward pruning was conducted.
311   In the former case, we apply our MLP to predict a layer whose dimensionality has already been
312   reduced. This increases efficiency by mitigating the effects of the curse of dimensionality (Bellman &
313   Kalaba, 1959). We have now presented the steps used to explain MIPP. In Algorithm 1, we synthesize
314
      this information more formally. Notably, the utility of MIPP can extend beyond just pruning. By
      verifying which pixels transfer entropy to the activations of the pruned first layer, MIPP also possesses

477   6      C ONCLUSION
478
479
      Current node selection methods rank nodes before selecting the top-k. These static ranking systems
480   not only fail to consider the effect of removing nodes on the current potential ranking but also often
481   lead to layer collapse, motivating the need for a more dynamic node selection method. Consequently,
482   we have introduced MIPP, an activation-based pruning method that removes neurons or filters from
483   layers if they fail to transfer entropy to the subsequent layer. Consequently, MIPP preserves MI
484   between the activations of adjacent layers. We have applied the proposed method to a variety of
485   datasets and models. Our experimental evaluation has demonstrated the effectiveness of MIPP in
      pruning trained and untrained models characterized by differing complexities.

      D.1       S ELECTION OF THE P RUNING R ATIO
720   MIPP selects a node if it transfers entropy to the subsequent layer. This prevents us from defining a
721   pruning ratio and selecting the top-k variables in a global fashion. Despite this, for our study we still
722   affected the pruning rate by changing the confidence required to remove a node (x%). For example, if
723   x% = 99% one must be very confident that entropy is transferred to the following layer to maintain a
724   node in the NN, promoting an aggressive pruning strategy. In this section, we explain how we derived
725   values of x%.
726   Specifically, we had 20 different values of x% for each set of experiments. The lowest possible value
727   being x% = 50%, where we would then generate         20 different values of xn % (where n ∈ [0, 20])
                                                  Pn
728   using the following equation: xn = x0 + i=1 (1 − xi−1 ) · r, where r = 0.5 and x0 = 50. Using
729   this method we generate 20 values of x that approach 100% confidence at a decreasing rate.

733   For the CIFAR-10 dataset, we applied standard data augmentation techniques, which included random
734   cropping with padding and random horizontal flipping. These augmentations are commonly used to
735   enhance model generalization by introducing variations in the training data. In the case of CIFAR-100,
736
      we employed additional augmentation methods beyond the standard techniques. Specifically, we used
      mixup (Zhang et al., 2018), which creates virtual training examples by combining pairs of images and
737
      their labels, and cutout (DeVries & Taylor, 2017), which randomly masks out square regions of an
738
      image to simulate occlusion and encourage the network to focus on more distributed features. These

      D.3.2    P RUNING
757
758
      As explained in the main paper, our method consists of using an MLP to predict the activations of
759   a layer based on its predecessor. We then mask the features and re-train the MLP to see if the loss
760   will drop back below the original. We therefore require the number of iterations and the size of the
761   MLP fit initially and the number of iterations required for re-training. For all layers and models
762   we fit three MLPs with two hidden layers with 256 nodes. For the initial training step we used
763   1500 iterations. For the re-training steps once the mask has been applied we use 20 iterations for
764   VGG based models and 150 iterations for others. At the start of our algorithm, we also rank the
765   features based of their MI. For this calculation we again use the same MLP structure but only for 35
766   iterations due to time constraints. We use our method to prune all linear and convolutional layers. We
767
      prune the batch-normalization nodes associated with nodes in linear/convolutional layers, while skip
      connections in ResNets remain unaffected.

      In this Section, we investigate MIPP’s ability to select features, specifically reviewing the pixels it
      identifies from the MNIST dataset. MIPP selects features in the exact same manner it selects nodes,
777
      by verifying whether entropy is transferred from these variables to those of the subsequent layer. In
778
      this case, the subsequent layer is the first layer in the network. In Figure 7, we observe what appears
779   as a significantly stochastic pixel selection. However, a tendancy to select features from the right hand
780   side of the image can be observed. This aligns with experiments done by Covert et al. (2020), which
781   revealed there are more pixels correlated with the dataset labels in this area. Despite this signal, it is
782   non-intuitive to observe pixels in the right-most column being selected while those in the center of
783   the image are not. In spite of this, we observe good performance, MIPP is able to achieve state-of-the
784   art test accuracy in the scenarios in which 90% and 75% of neurons and pixels have been removed,
785   respectively. In Figure 8, we present the average accuracy achieved when we prune models using
786   MIPP and our baselines. Meanwhile, in Figure 9 we present the layer collapse rates. Unlike the main
787
      body of the paper, in both of these figures we have also used MIPP to select pixels. In both figures
      we observe that MIPP outperforms the baselines. This is because, unlike any of the baselines, the
788
      features are selected in a manner that is dependent on the pruned model. MIPP can compress both
789
      features and the underlying model simultaneously such that the results are compatible, preventing
790   ML practitioners from having to use different methods for feature and model compression. Often,
791   combining compressed input and compressed models can lead to performance degradation. SOSP-H
792   is often close to state-of-the-art when pixels have not been selected; however, in these experiments it
793   performs poorly. This is because the gradients calculated with respect to the input are more sparse.

796   E.2     C OMPARING F EATURE C OLLAPSE F UNCTIONS
797
798   Vision models, being vastly overparameterized, have become of key importance when evaluating
799   pruning algorithms (Real et al., 2019; Wang & Fu, 2023; Wang et al., 2023). Unlike the nodes of
800   MLPs, filters in CNNs are multivariate. To preserve the MI between layers, we have two options:
801   we can either flatten the square filters into an array of random variables, which retains all possible
802   information but is computationally expensive, or collapse each filter into a single value using a
803   function. Given the complexity of the former approach, we opt for the latter, showing that, despite the
804   potential loss of information, MIPP remains highly effective. To find the function that preserves the
805   most information, we compare L1, L2, mean and std functions, for the exact form of these functions
806
      please refer to Table 4 (Molchanov et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2017).
807   If Figure 10, it can be observed that for whichever function is chosen, MIPP remains performant.
808   However, the L1-normalization function consistently demonstrates an ability to prune to high sparsi-
809   ties while layer collapse remains rare. For this reason, we adopted this function throughout when
      collapsing convolutions.
