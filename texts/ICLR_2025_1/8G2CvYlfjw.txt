009                                                 Abstract
010
011              Gradient Boosting Decision Tree (GBDT) is one of the most popular machine
012              learning models in various applications. But in the traditional settings, all data
013              should be simultaneously accessed in the training procedure: it does not allow to
014              add or delete any data instances after training. In this paper, we propose a novel
015              online learning framework for GBDT supporting both incremental and decremental
016              learning. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work that considers an
017
                 in-place unified incremental and decremental learning on GBDT. To reduce the
                 learning cost, we present a collection of optimizations for our framework, so that
018
                 it can add or delete a small fraction of data on the fly. We theoretically show the
019
                 relationship between the hyper-parameters of the proposed optimizations, which
020              enables trading off accuracy and cost on incremental and decremental learning. The
021              backdoor attack results show that our framework can successfully inject and remove
022              backdoor in a well-trained model using incremental and decremental learning, and
023              the empirical results on public datasets confirm the effectiveness and efficiency of
024              our proposed online learning framework and optimizations.

      1     Introduction
028   Gradient Boosting Decision Tree (GBDT) has demonstrated outstanding performance across a wide
029   range of applications Sudakov et al. (2019); Biau et al. (2019); Rao et al. (2019); Liu & Yu (2007).
030   It outperforms deep learning models on many datasets in accuracy and provides interpretability for
031   the trained models. However, in traditional setting, all data is simultaneously accessed in training
032
      procedure, which makes its application scenarios limited.
033   Online Learning. Online learning is a machine learning approach where data is sequentially
034   available and used to update the predictor for the latest data Bertsekas (2015); Parisi et al. (2019);
035   Hazan (2016); Oza (2005). Generally, online learning is expected to possess the capabilities of both
036   incremental learning (adding training data) and decremental learning (removing a subset of training
037   data). This allows the model to dynamically adapt to the latest data while removing outdated data.
038   Incremental Learning. There are some challenges for incremental learning in GBDT due to its
039   natural properties Friedman et al. (2000). Traditional GBDT trains over an entire dataset, and each
040   node is trained on the data reaching it to achieve the best split for optimal accuracy. Adding unseen
041   data may affect node splitting results, potentially leading to catastrophic performance changes.
042   Moreover, training gradient boosting models involves creating trees for each iteration, with tree fitting
043   based on the residual of previous iterations. More iterations create more trees, increasing model
044   sizes and hurting inference throughput. This also prohibits tasks like fine-tuning or transfer learning
045   without substantially increasing model sizes.
046
      Recent studies have explored incremental learning on classic machine learning, such as SVM, random
047
      forest, and gradient boosting. Shilton et al. (2005); Laskov et al. (2006); Fine & Scheinberg (2001)
048   proposed methods to maintain SVM optimality after adding a few training vectors. Wang et al. (2009)
049   presented an incremental random forest for online learning with small streaming data. Beygelzimer
050   et al. (2015a) extended gradient boosting theory for regression to online learning. Zhang et al. (2019)
051   proposed iGBDT for incremental learning by “lazily” updating, but it may require retraining many
052   trees when the new data size is large. It is important to note that prior studies on online gradient
053   boosting Beygelzimer et al. (2015a); Chen et al. (2012); Beygelzimer et al. (2015b) and incremental
      gradient boosting Zhang et al. (2019); Hu et al. (2017) do not support decremental learning.

066   Decremental Learning. Decremental learning is more complex and less studied than incremental
067   learning. Cauwenberghs & Poggio (2000) presented an online recursive algorithm for training SVM
068   with an efficient decremental learning method. Chen et al. (2019) proposed online incremental and
069
      decremental learning algorithms based on variable SVM, leveraging pre-calculated results. Brophy &
      Lowd (2021) and Brophy & Lowd (2020) provided methods for data addition and removal in random
070
      forests. Schelter et al. (2021) proposed robust tree node split criteria and alternative splits for low-
071
      latency unlearning. Many works have also studied decremental learning in deep neural networks
072   (DNN). Bourtoule et al. (2021) introduced a framework that accelerates decremental learning by
073   constraining individual data points’ impact during training.
074
075
      While online learning has emerged as a popular topic recently, it has been barely investigated on
      GBDT. Wu et al. (2023); Lin et al. (2023) are among the latest studies in decremental learning
076
      for GBDT. Wu et al. (2023) presented DeltaBoost, a GBDT-like model enabling data deletion.
077
      DeltaBoost uses bagging to divide the training dataset into disjoint sub-datasets, training each
078   iteration’s tree on a different sub-dataset, reducing tree dependency. However, this simplification
079   may impact model performance. Lin et al. (2023) proposed an unlearning framework in GBDT
080   without simplification, unlearning specific data using recorded auxiliary information from training.
081   It optimizes to reduce unlearning time, making it faster than retraining from scratch, but introduces
082   many hyper-parameters and performs poorly on extremely large datasets.
083
      In this paper, we propose a novel incremental and decremental learning framework for GBDT. To
084   the best of our knowledge, this is the first work that considers in-place online incremental and
085   decremental learning at the same time on GBDT. The incremental and decremental learning in this
086   work applies a unified notion, which enables convenient implementation.
087
      Challenges. We identify three major challenges of in-place online learning for GBDT: (1) Unlike
088
      batch training of deep neural networks (DNN), more iterations in GBDT create more trees and
089
      parameters, leading to unbounded memory and computation costs in online learning. In-place
090   learning on originally constructed trees is necessary for practicality. (2) Gradient-based methods
091   in DNN add and subtract gradients for incremental and decremental learning, but GBDT is not
092   differentiable. (3) GBDT depends on the residual of the previous tree, unlike independent iterations in
093   random forests. Changing one tree requires modifying all subsequent trees, complicating incremental
094   and decremental learning.
095
      Contributions. (1) We introduce a novel in-place online learning framework for gradient boost-
096   ing models supporting incremental and decremental learning, extensible to fine-tuning and transfer
097   learning. (2) We present optimizations to reduce the cost of incremental and decremental learning,
098   making adding or deleting a small data fraction substantially faster than retraining. (3) We theo-
099   retically show the relationship among optimization hyper-parameters, enabling trade-offs between
100   accuracy and cost. (4) We experimentally evaluate our framework on public datasets, confirming its
101   effectiveness and efficiency. (5) We release an open-source implementation of our framework1.

      2.2 Problem Setting
156
157   For classic GBDT, all training data must be loaded during training, and adding/deleting instances
158
      is not allowed afterwards. This work proposes an online GBDT framework that enables in-place
      addition/deletion of specific data instances to/from a well-trained model through incremental and
159
      decremental learning.
160
161   Problem Statement. Given a trained gradient boosting model T (θ) on training dataset Dtr , where
      θ indicates the parameters of model T , an incremental learning dataset Din , and/or a decremental


                                                                                                                                                     3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      learning dataset Dde (Dde ⊆ Dtr ), our goal is to find a tree model T (θ′ ) that fits dataset Dtr ∪Din \Dde ,
163   where |θ| = |θ′ | (the parameter size and the number of trees stay unchanged).
164
165   The most obvious way is to retrain the model from scratch on dataset Dtr ∪ Din \ Dde . However,
166
      retraining is time-consuming and resource-intensive. Especially for online learning applications,
      rapid retraining is not practical. The key question of this problem is: Can we obtain the model T (θ′ )
167
      based on the learned knowledge of the original model T (θ) without retraining the entire model?
168
169   The proposed framework aims to find a tree model T (θ′ ) as close to the model retraining from scratch
170   as possible based on the learned knowledge of the model T (θ). In addition, this online learning
171   algorithm is in a “warm-start” manner, because it learns a new dataset Din or removes a learned
172
      sub-dataset Dde ⊆ Dtr on a model that is already well-trained on training dataset Dtr .
173   Let A denotes the initial GBDT learning algorithm , then we have A(Dtr ) ∈ H, where H is the
174   hypothesis space. An online learning algorithm L for incremental learning or decremental learning
175   can be used to learn dataset Din or remove dataset Dde ⊆ Dtr .

210   3   Optimizing Learning Time
211   In this section, we introduce optimizations for our online learning framework to reduce computation
212   overhead and costs. The key step is deciding whether a node should be kept or replaced: Can we
213   design an algorithm to quickly test whether the node should be retained or retrained without touching
214   the training data? Our most important optimization is to avoid touching the full training dataset.
215   We apply incremental update and split candidates sampling concepts from Lin et al. (2023), extend
      them to support online learning, and provide evidence of the relationship between hyper-parameters

      of different optimizations, enabling trade-offs between accuracy and cost. Additionally, we design
217   optimizations specific to online learning: 1) adaptive lazy update for residuals and hessians to
218   substantially decrease online learning time; 2) adaptive split robustness tolerance to significantly
219   reduce the number of retrained nodes.

221
      3.1 Update without Touching Training Data
222   To reduce computation overhead and online learning time, we target to avoid touching the original
223
      training dataset D, and only focus on the online learning dataset D′ . Following the study Lin et al.
      (2023), we extend the optimization of updating statistical information to the scenarios of online
224
      learning: (1) Maintain Best Split; (2) Recomputing Prediction Value; (3) Incremental Update for
225
      Derivatives, and the computation cost is reduced from O(D ± D′ ) to O(D′ ) by these optimizations.
226   The implementation of these optimizations are included in Appendix E.
227
228   3.2 Adaptive Lazy Update for Derivatives
229   Although incremental update can substantially reduce online learning time, we can take it a step
230   further: if no retraining occurs, the changes to the derivatives will be very small. How can we
231   effectively utilize the parameters already learned to reduce online learning time?
232   Gradient Accumulation Li et al. (2014); Goyal et al. (2017); Ruder (2016) is widely used in DNN
233   training. After computing the loss and gradients for each mini-batch, the system accumulates these
234   gradients over multiple batches instead of updating the model parameters immediately. Inspired by
235   Gradient Accumulation techniques, we introduce an adaptive lazy update for our online learning
236   framework. Unlike Lin et al. Lin et al. (2023), which perform updates after a fixed number of
237   batches, we update the derivatives only when retraining occurs. This approach uses more outdated
238
      derivatives for gain computation but significantly reduces the cost of derivative updates.
239   3.3 Split Candidates Sampling
240   From the above optimizations, if retraining is not required, we can keep the current best split. In this
241   case, we only need to iterate over the online learning dataset D′ and update the prediction values to
242   accomplish online learning, whether it involves adding or removing data. However, if the sub-tree
243   rooted in this node requires retraining, it is necessary to train the new sub-tree on the data from the
244   dataset Dtr ± D′ that reaches this node. It is clear that retraining incurs more resource consumption
245   and takes a longer execution time. In the worst case, if retraining is required in the root node, it has
246
      to retrain the entire new tree on full dataset Dtr ± D′ .
247   To reduce the time and resource consumption of online learning, a straightforward approach is to
248   minimize the frequency of retraining. Therefore, we introduce split candidate sampling to reduce
249   frequent retraining by limiting the number of splits, and it is beneficial for both training and online
250   learning. All features are discretized into integers in {0, 1, 2, · · · , B − 1}, as shown in Appendix A.
251   The original training procedure enumerates all B potential splits, and then obtains the best split with
252
      the greatest gain value. In split candidates sampling, we randomly select ⌈αB⌉ potential splits as
      candidates and only perform gain computing on these candidates. As α decreases, the number of
253
      split candidates decreases, resulting in larger distances between split candidates. Consequently, the
254
      best split is less likely to change frequently.

365   adding a single data instance can be up to 254.4x and 17x faster, respectively. Furthermore, compared

467   The membership inference attack (MIA) aims to predict whether a data sample is part of the training
468   dataset Shokri et al. (2017); Hu et al. (2022); Choquette-Choo et al. (2021). Therefore, the goal of this
469   experiments is to determine if ”deleted” data can still be identified as training data after decremental
470   learning. However, in our experiment with default hyper-parameter setting, the predictions made by
471   MIA are nearly random guesses.

473   models more vulnerable to MIA Yeom et al. (2018); van Breugel et al. (2023); Hu et al. (2022). To
474   further validate our approach, we apply a smaller model with number of iteration M = 5, which
475   can be easily overfitted. Conducting MIA on such a small model can further confirm our approach
476   indeed deletes the data from the model. Therefore, we conduct the this experiments on multi-class
477   datasets: Optdigits, Pendigits, Letter and Covtype. For each dataset, we split it into three subsets:
478   base dataset Dbase , online dataset D′ , and test dataset Dtest . We first train a base model on Dbase + D′ .

      For this base model, the MIA should identify the data in D′ as part of the training dataset. Next, we

480

507   5    Related Work
508   Incremental Learning is a technique in machine learning that involves the gradual integration of new
509   data into an existing model, continuously learning from the latest data to ensure performance on new
510   data van de Ven et al. (2022). It has been a open problem in machine learning, and has been studied
511   in convolutional neural network (CNN) Polikar et al. (2001); Kuzborskij et al. (2013); Zhou et al.
512   (2022), DNN Hussain et al. (2023); Dekhovich et al. (2023), SVM Chen et al. (2019); Cauwenberghs
513   & Poggio (2000) , random forest (RF) Wang et al. (2009); Brophy & Lowd (2020). In gradient
514   boosting, iGBDT Zhang et al. (2019) offers incremental updates, while other methods Beygelzimer
515
      et al. (2015a); Babenko et al. (2009) extend gradient boosting to online learning. However, these
      methods do not support removing data.
516
517   Decremental Learning allows for the removal of trained data and eliminates their influence on
518   the model, which can be used to delete outdated or privacy-sensitive data Bourtoule et al. (2021);
519   Nguyen et al. (2022); Sekhari et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2024). It has been researched in various
520   models, including CNN Poppi et al. (2023); Tarun et al. (2021), DNN Chen et al. (2023); Thudi et al.
521
      (2022), SVM Karasuyama & Takeuchi (2009); Cauwenberghs & Poggio (2000), Naive Bayes Cao &
      Yang (2015), K-means Ginart et al. (2019), RF Schelter et al. (2021); Brophy & Lowd (2021), and
522
      gradient boosting Wu et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023). In random forests, DaRE Brophy & Lowd
523
      (2021) and a decremental learning algorithm Schelter et al. (2021) were proposed for data removal
524   with minimal retraining and latency.
525
526   However, in GBDT, trees in subsequent iterations rely on residuals from previous iterations, making
527
      decremental learning more complicated. DeltaBoost Wu et al. (2023) simplified the dependency for
      data deletion by dividing the dataset into disjoint sub-datasets, while a recent study Lin et al. (2023)
528
      proposed an efficient unlearning framework without simplification, utilizing auxiliary information to
529
      reduce unlearning time. Although effective, its performance on large datasets remains unsatisfactory.
530
531   6    Conclusion
532
533
      In this paper, we propose an novel in-place online learning framework for GBDT that support
      incremental and decremental learning: it enables us to dynamically add a new dataset to the model
534
      and delete a learned dataset from the model. It support continual batch addition/removal, and data
535
      additional with unseen classes. We present a collection of optimizations on our framework to reduce
536   the cost of online learning. Adding or deleting a small fraction of data is substantially faster than
537   retraining from scratch. Our experimental results, including backdoor attack, membership inference
538   attack, and other empirical evaluations confirm the effectiveness and efficiency of our framework
539   and optimizations – successfully adding or deleting data while maintaining accuracy.

1097   As mentioned in Section 3.2, outdated derivatives are used in gain computation to reduce the cost of
1098   updating derivatives. However, these outdated derivatives are only applied to nodes where the best
1099   split remains unchanged. When a sub-tree requires retraining, the derivatives are updated. Therefore,
1100
       using outdated derivatives typically occurs when |D′ | is small, as fewer data modifications result
       in fewer changes to the best splits. Conversely, when more data is added or deleted, |D′ | becomes
1101
       larger, increasing the likelihood of changes to the best splits in some nodes. As a result, the sub-trees
1102
       are retrained, and the derivatives for the data reaching those nodes are updated.

1171   by limiting the number of splits. As mentioned in Section 3.3, a smaller sampling rate leads to
1172   more stable splits, resulting in fewer nodes that require retraining and shorter online learning time.
1173

1177   become more stable, resulting in fewer nodes that require retraining. In Pendigits, since the number
1178   of nodes that require retraining is similar for 5% and 10%, it results in a minimal difference in the
1179   online learning time, as mentioned above. However, interestingly, for example in 100% sampling
