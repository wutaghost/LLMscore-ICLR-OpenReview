009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, large language model (LLM)-based agents have made significant ad-
012            vances across various fields. One of the most popular research areas involves
013            applying these agents to video games. Traditionally, these methods have re-
014            lied on game APIs to access in-game environmental and action data. However,
015            this approach is limited by the availability of APIs and does not reflect how hu-
016            mans play games. With the advent of vision language models (VLMs), agents
017            now have enhanced visual understanding capabilities, enabling them to interact
018
               with games using only visual inputs. Despite these advances, current approaches
               still face challenges in action-oriented tasks, particularly in action role-playing
019
               games (ARPGs), where reinforcement learning methods are prevalent but suffer
020
               from poor generalization and require extensive training. To address these limi-
021            tations, we select an ARPG, “Black Myth: Wukong”, as a research platform to
022            explore the capability boundaries of existing VLMs in scenarios requiring visual-
023            only input and complex action output. We define 13 tasks within the game, with
024            76.9% focusing on combat, and incorporate several state-of-the-art VLMs into
025            this benchmark. Additionally, we will release a human operation dataset contain-
026            ing recorded gameplay videos and operation logs, including mouse and keyboard
027            actions. Moreover, we propose a novel VARP (Vision Action Role-Playing) agent
028            framework, consisting of an action planning system and a human-guided trajec-
029
               tory system. Our framework demonstrates the ability to perform basic tasks and
               succeed in 90% of easy and medium-level combat scenarios. This research aims
030
               to provide new insights and directions for applying multimodal agents in com-
031
               plex action game environments. The code and datasets will be made available at
032            https://varp-agent.github.io/.

      2.1      LLM AND VLM-D RIVEN AGENTS
116
      In recent years, various intelligent agents driven by large language models (LLM) and multimodal
117   language models (VLM) have gradually come to the forefront, demonstrating immense potential
118   in multitasking and autonomous learning. For LLM, Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023) enhances the
119   decision-making ability of language agents through a framework of linguistic feedback and self-
120   reflection, allowing agents to autonomously reflect in the face of feedback signals and maintain
121   contextual memory during task execution and decision-making processes. ReAct (Yao et al., 2023),
122   on the other hand, emphasizes real-time information retrieval and strategy adjustment. By combin-
123   ing reasoning with action and interacting with external knowledge sources (such as the Wikipedia
124   API), it adds dynamic information retrieval capabilities, providing greater interpretability and con-
125
      trollability. Voyager (Wang et al., 2023a) can explore and learn skills in an unsupervised manner
      within the Minecraft environment, continuously exploring the world, acquiring diverse skills, and
126
      making new discoveries without human intervention through a combination of automated courses,
127
      skill libraries, and iterative prompting mechanisms.
128
129   For VLM, CreativeAgent (Zhang et al., 2023a) focuses on creative tasks, employing multimodal
130   generation to achieve the construction of complex structures. Its combination of an imagination
131
      module and controller enables efficient planning and execution based on free-form language in-
      structions and the generated task details. Cradle (Tan et al., 2024) takes video images displayed on
132
      a screen as input, extracting text and visual information to make decisions through a workflow of
133
      “reflecting on the past, summarizing the present, and planning for the future,” outputting control sig-
134   nals for keyboard and mouse interaction, allowing AI agents to interact with software like humans
135   without relying on any internal APIs.

      maintains three libraries: a situation library, an action library, and a human-guided library. These
163   libraries can be retrieved and updated to store intensive knowledge for self-learning and human

170   3.2     ACTION P LANNING S YSTEM
171
172   The action planning system is primarily used for action reasoning and generation. This system
173   utilizes a situation library and an updatable action library as knowledge retrieval bases. Guided
174   by input game screenshots, the system employs a group of VLMs to select or generate actions
175
      appropriate for the current situation. The generated situations and actions are stored or updated in
      the two libraries. Additionally, We propose decomposable task-specific auxiliary modules to break
176
      down large tasks into smaller subtasks, which are then distributed across multiple VLMs to reduce
177
      the occurrence of model forgetting and hallucinations. We also introduce a self-optimizable action
178   generation module to encourage VLMs to generate new actions specific to some hard tasks, thereby
179   completing complex tasks more efficiently and with higher quality.
180
181
      3.2.1    BASIC VLM S G ROUP
182
183   Inspired by Cradle (Tan et al., 2024), our main pipeline continues to adopt the five basic modules
184   from Cradle, with some of these basic modules calling the VLM for reasoning, forming a basic
185   group of VLMs. During initialization, we manually predefined some actions and placed them into
186   the action library as the prior knowledge. Each action is a Python function with detailed textual
187   annotations, and we computed the embeddings of these annotations for storage. Information Gath-
188
      ering is responsible for gathering information from sampled game screenshots, including textual and
      visual information related to situations and actions. The textual information primarily includes text
189
      guides, text labels, and notifications; the visual information mainly covers environmental positions,
190
      character actions, and interface icons. The former is assisted by OCR tools for text recognition, while
191   the latter uses the object detection tools for visual localization. Self Reflection takes a few game
192   screenshots from the last video in the situation library as input to assess whether the last executed
193   action successfully produced the correct effect and whether the current task has been completed. If
194   execution fails, the module needs to provide a reason for the failure to guide the next step in action
195   generation. Task Inference infers the current task to be executed based on the results of previous
196   modules, and generates the task description. Skill Curation calculates the similarity between the
197   task description’s embedding and the embeddings of the textual annotations in the action library
198   to find some matching actions, which form the candidate action set. Decision Making utilizes the
199
      Chain of Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2023) approach to reason through and deeply analyze multiple
      sequential questions (such as whether to enable combat mode, restore health, or select from avail-
200
      able spell skills, etc.). Finally, the module infers the most suitable action from the candidate action
201
      set, executes the Python code, and operates the keyboard and mouse to control the player character
202   to complete the corresponding task. These five basic modules will record each intermediate product
203   into the situation library.
204

      3.2.2    S ELF -O PTIMIZABLE ACTION G ENERATION M ODULE
206
207   The basic VLMs group can only acquire actions from a predefined action library or from game
208   screenshots with clear textual prompts. For certain tasks in ARPGs that have weak textual guidance,
209   such as real-time combat, this method is unable to learn new actions. Therefore, we propose a
210   self-optimizable action generation module (SOAG) that allow the VARP agent to summarize the
211   enemy’s actions during combat, thereby optimizing existing actions and generating new ones to
212
      counter enemy attacks. The new actions are combinations of the two atomic commands: dodging
      and light strikes. The optimization goal is to maximize the evasion of enemy attacks and the ability
213
      to strike the enemy while minimizing the player character’s health loss.
214
215   Specifically, in SOAG, we introduce a component responsible for action function generation. This
      component takes the information gathering and self reflection results, along with the current and


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      last game screenshots, as input. It analyzes the characteristics of the enemy under the current task,
217   such as name, appearance, weapon, etc. Most importantly, it needs to analyze the enemy’s current
218   and previous actions. For example, for the hard enemy named Bullguard, its attack actions can be
219   roughly categorized as: “charging forward with the axe”, and “chopping the axe downwards three
220   times consecutively”, etc. Therefore, this component needs to inference new actions for dodging
221   and counterattacking based on the current enemy actions. For instance, for “charging forward with
222   the axe,” the new action should be to dodge once and then attack continuously; for “chopping the axe
223   downwards three times consecutively,” the new action should be to dodge three more times before
224   counterattacking. The generated new actions are permutations of the atomic operations “dodge” and
225
      “light attack.” The generated actions are stored in the action library with detailed textual annotations.

      3.2.3    D ECOMPOSABLE TASK -S PECIFIC AUXILIARY M ODULES
229
230
      In ARPGs, especially in BMW game, the VLM’s inference involves a large number of tokens, in-
      cluding multiple images and long texts. The attention mechanism used by VLMs allocates attention
231
      to all tokens in long texts. As the input length increases, the attention distribution becomes in-
232
      creasingly diluted. In the basic VLMs group, due to the excessive number of input tokens for each
233   module, the model may fail to effectively focus on key information, leading to errors such as forget-
234   ting and hallucination. This issue is particularly evident in the decision-making module, where the
235   VLM frequently makes mistakes when answering multiple questions.
236
      To address this problem, we decomposed the basic modules and added multiple parallel auxiliary
237
      sub-modules for specific tasks, which are then integrated by the VLM. The structure is similar to an
238
      MLP. Specifically, as shown in the workflow of action planning in Fig. 1, we decomposed the origi-
239   nal decision-making module that handled multiple tasks into 5 sub-modules. 1) Enemy Sub-module
240   is used to analyze the enemy’s status (such as its health, position, etc.) and action description, which
241   assists the agent in obtaining detailed information of the enemy. 2) Combat Sub-module determines
242   which combat method to use, including light attack or heavy attack, by observing the heavy-attack
243   status in the bottom right corner of the game screen. 3) Health Sub-module is responsible for con-
244   stantly monitoring the player’s health bar. If the health is consumed excessively, it assists the agent
245   by prioritizing the action of recovering health. 4) Spell-skill Sub-module monitors the status of the
246   player’s spell skills while simultaneously analyzing the situation in the combat state to determine
247
      the appropriate time to use available spell skills. 5) The integration sub-module is responsible
      for integrating the outputs of all sub-modules and reasoning to determine the best action from the
248
      candidate action set for the current specific task. The decomposable task-specific auxiliary modules
249
      decompose long tokens and focus on each individual question, significantly improving the accuracy
250   of the decision-making module.

      3.3     H UMAN -G UIDED T RAJECTORY S YSTEM
254
255
      Human actions are seen as valuable data, implicitly rich in knowledge of the physics and game
256
      world, which can lead to advanced action combinations for very complex tasks, such as way-finding
257
      tasks and high-difficulty combat tasks. To learn the human experience from this implicit data, we
258   first collected a human dataset and then used it to improve the performance of our VARP agent. The
259   collection process of human operation data and dataset analysis will be detailed in Sec. 3.1, which
260   consists of mouse keyboard logs, and recording game screenshots. In this section, we focus on how
261   to use it to implement a human-guided trajectory system. In this section, we refer to our annotated
262   dataset as the human-guided library. It is a collection of pairs consisting of game screenshots and
263   human operations, with each pair having a unique timestamp.
264   For very hard tasks in the game, we first take a screenshot of the current game interface. Based
265   on this game screenshot, we query the human-guided library for the screenshot with the highest
266   similarity. We then input this screenshot along with the subsequent n-frame screenshots and their
267   corresponding operations into the human-guided trajectory system. This system will utilize a VLM
268   to analyze and summarize the input images and operations, ultimately outputting a new human-
269   guided action, which is then stored in the action library for the action planning system to choose and
      execute.

355   4.4   P ERFORMANCE E VALUATION
356
357
      To evaluate the performance of the VARP agent without human guidance, we conducted experiments
      on our proposed benchmark while disabling the human-guided trajectory system of the VARP agent.
358
      In this performance evaluation, we only tested the benchmark and compared the VARP agent with
359
      human novice players.
360
361   We calculated the success rates of the VARP agent driven by different VLMs and human novice
362   players when completing each task. As shown in Fig. 3, both the VARP agent and human novice
363
      players achieved high success rates on tasks 1 to 8, reaching nearly 100% on most tasks. In task
      9, the VARP agent’s average success rate was 40%, which also confirms its “middle” difficulty.
364
      The enemy in task 10 is the first boss-level monster that the player encounters in the game. For
365
      human novice players, the success rate for this task was 15.63%, while the VARP agent’s average
366   success rate was 20%. Task 11 and 12 are classified as “very hard,” so the success rates for both
367   human novices and the VARP agent were very low. Specifically, the VARP agent is limited by the
368   reasoning speed of VLMs, making it unable to input every game frame in real-time and only able
369   to input keyframes at second-level intervals. In ARPGs, this can easily result in missing critical
370   information about enemy attacks. Therefore, task 11 and 12 are particularly challenging for the
371   agent. In terms of autonomous navigation, humans can easily find the final boss enemy of the level
372   within five minutes, but for VLMs, this is an almost impossible task. Without human guidance, the
373   success rate is 0%. Since the game provides no guidance or hints for navigation tasks and contains
374
      many “invisible walls,” VLMs lack the ability to perceive the correct path in the 3D scene without
      human assistance.
375
376   To explore the performance boundary of the VLM-based VARP agent, we chose a very-hard task
377   and used the health of the enemy “Guangzhi” in task 11 as a metric. Due to the constraints of GPT-
      4o’s maximum token count and inference time, we were unable to input all the video frames, which

527
528
529   In this study, we have explored the boundaries of current Vision Language Models (VLMs) in the
530   context of complex action role-playing games (ARPGs) using “Black Myth: Wukong” as our exper-
531
      imental platform. Our proposed framework, VARP, introduces a novel approach to game interaction
      by leveraging visual-only inputs for action planning in ARPG environments. The VARP framework
532
      demonstrates its potential by achieving an 90% success rate in basic and moderate combat scenarios,
533
      suggesting that VLMs can be effectively utilized in tasks traditionally dominated by reinforcement
534   learning. Our proposed benchmark can effectively evaluate the performance of visual-only agents
535   in the BMW game. Additionally, the human operation dataset we provide offers a valuable resource
536   for future research, enabling the study of human-like gameplay and action decision-making in visu-
537   ally complex environments. Our findings underscore the promise of multimodal agents in enhancing
538   generalization and performance in action-oriented tasks within video games. Moving forward, the
539   insights gained from this research could pave the way for more sophisticated agent designs that can
      handle a broader range of challenges in ARPGs and beyond.

      Thank you for reading our paper. We would like to begin by clarifying and explaining some potential

      concerns that may arise due to the extreme popularity of the BMW game. We want to emphasize that
676   our framework has broad applicability and will subsequently be generalized to include more games
677   and other scenarios, not just limited to the BMW game. In this paper, we explored the potential
678   of using VLMs to execute action combos in game tasks, particularly focusing on how it achieves
679   victory against medium-powered monsters by leveraging the advantages of both action planning and
