009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The advancement of vision-language models has significantly boosted the perfor-
012
               mance of embodied and game AI, endowing them with stronger general visual
013
               understanding capabilities and logical abilities for action planning. However, the
014            substantial computational cost of model training and the performance degrada-
015            tion during fine-tuning limit the models’ ability to learn emerging new tasks con-
016            tinually. Creating a versatile and dynamically updatable vision-language model
017            is an essential area of research. To this end, we propose a Low-Rank Adapter-
018            based fine-tuning approach called I-LoRA, which enables iterative and indepen-
019            dent learning of new tasks while preserving the logical capabilities of the previ-
020            ously trained model. Specifically, we first design the routing-tuning method to
021            minimize the impact of original capabilities from the new task by minimizing ac-
022
               tivation values of LoRA matrices as low as possible in the general task. Secondly,
               we propose a novel approach to iteratively merge new adapters, allowing for con-
023
               tinuous integration of adapters trained on new tasks without being influenced by
024
               task order, thereby reducing interference between them. Finally, we conducted
025            extensive experiments on public datasets with significant behavioral and logical
026            differences between tasks. The results demonstrate that our approach achieves
027            excellent single-task performance, strong multi-task compatibility, and flexible
028            scalability without increasing the number of model parameters.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The powerful logical reasoning and general visual understanding capabilities of vision-language
035   models (VLMs) have yielded significant progress in embodied and game AI (Wang et al., 2023a;
036   Driess et al., 2023). Beyond their ability to perform in-context learning directly, pre-trained models
037   possess extensive general knowledge that can be effectively leveraged for tuning on domain-specific
038   tasks. Vision-language-action (VLA) models, derived from pre-trained vision-language models,
039
      can be further trained to learn and adapt to the behavioral logic of agents Kim et al. (2024); Ma
      et al. (2024). Fine-tuning these pre-trained models showcases the potential of VLMs to acquire new
040
      behavioral logic. However, in practical applications, the range of tasks continues to grow, and the
041
      substantial computational cost of model training, coupled with performance degradation during fine-
042   tuning, poses significant challenges to their ability to continually learn new tasks. Thus, creating an
043   approach to versatilely and dynamically update the vision-language model to efficiently adapt new
044   tasks is important.
045
      In the literature, the focus of building general embodied and game AI using large language models
046
      has been on constructing general agents. For example, some work attempts to build a lifelong
047
      learning agent power by LLM, (Wang et al., 2023a) proposed an iterative prompting mechanism
048   and an increasing database for LLM to retrieve. In contrast, (Fan et al., 2022) utilized an Internet-
049   scale knowledge base, with both conducting experiments in Minecraft. Their commonality lies
050   in expanding the model’s knowledge base, using LLM’s reasoning capabilities to execute action
051   planning. However, this approach is limited in adapting to new task logic and requires high costs
052   in inferencing and decision-making. While fine-tuning allows the model to adapt to different tasks
053   based on new data and can significantly improve decision efficiency, it often comes at the expense
      of the model’s original task capabilities.

054
      Mastering a particular skill often requires fine-tuning the model. LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation)
055   adapters, used by merging into the base model when inference, can naturally become modular for
056   LLM, which is inherently convenient for model fusion. So, we used LoRA matrices to fine-tune
057   the model for each task. Current approaches to combine LoRA adapters usually involve setting up
058   a router to perform weighted sum over the adapters, (Dou et al., 2023) tries to combine mixture-
059   of-experts(MOE) with LoRA adapters fusion. Still, this method introduces additional parameters
060   and requires further tuning to create new routing mechanisms for each new task adapter, which is
061   nearly impossible for iterative fusion. Additionally, some works have proposed methods for directly
062   combining models without additional training. (Yu et al., 2024) performs random drop and rescale
063
      of learned parameters to combine different learned parameters. However, none of these approaches
      consider how to mitigate the interference between model parameters across different tasks. This
064
      issue becomes particularly pronounced when the gap between functions is significant, as directly
065
      merging the trained parameter matrices does not yield satisfactory results.
066
067   To this end, this paper focuses on enabling the model to learn new tasks while maintaining perfor-
068   mance on other tasks. Specifically, we aim to minimize the impact of learning new tasks on the base
069
      model parameters, thereby ensuring the composability of learned parameters. To achieve this, we
      propose a method: Routing Tuning. We redesign the loss function during fine-tuning by injecting
070
      some general-purpose data into the training data. When routing from general data, we encourage the
071
      model’s activation values on this general data to approach 0 using KL-divergence loss and L2-norm
072   of LoRA layer output. This ensures minimal interference with the model’s performance on other
073   tasks. Thus, the LoRA adopter can be merged into the base model and retain the routing ability
074   without increasing the parameters.
075
      Subsequently, we propose a novel iterative fusion method to reduce parameter interference during
076
      fusion. By employing Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), we further reduce the number of re-
077
      dundant parameters and then select the components with the most significant influence on parameter
078   interference to ensure task performance. We list the properties needed to achieve iterative fusion for
079   LoRA adapters, and then, followed by the properties, we choose the Maximize function to merge pa-
080   rameters in different models. Our merging approach ensures the equality, associativity, and saliency
081   of different tasks, thereby guaranteeing the effectiveness and iterability of task integration. Finally,
082   we conducted extensive experiments in the Atari environment, which included many tasks with sig-
083   nificant logical differences. We performed both single-task performance experiments and multi-task
084   fused experiments. The experimental results clearly validate the effectiveness of I-Lora in single-
085   task training as well as its capability for multi-task integration compared with various baselines.
086   In summary, the contributions of this article can be summarized as follows:
087
088          • We have developed a new fine-tuning method called Routing Tuning. This method involves
089            directing the task data and general data to different losses during fine-tuning. As a result,
090            the model is able to learn the specific task while still maintaining its general performance.
091            This approach helps to reduce the interference caused by varying model parameters.
092
093          • We have developed a new method to fuse different LoRA adapters into a single entity using
094            SVD and a maximization function, merging them into the base model to maintain perfor-
095            mance on specific tasks while preserving general performance. Our approach introduces
096            no additional parameters for the iterative merging of LoRA adapters.
097
098          • We collected Atari data for vision-language model training. Using vision-language models,
099            we perform LoRA routing-tuning on a vision-language model for each game, followed by
100            LoRA adapters’ composition to validate the iterative merging methods’ effectiveness.

      2.1   V ISION - LANGUAGE M ODELS ON G AME AND E MBODIED AI
109
110
111   LLMs have excellent learning and reasoning abilities (Achiam et al., 2023), and vision-language
112   models(VLMs) have shown remarkable capabilities in multiple multimodal tasks such as visual
113   question answering and image captioning (Liu et al., 2024a). Some works have explored the ap-
114   plication of vision-language models in games (Wang et al., 2023b). In the literature, VLMs are
115   extended to act as agents to plan complex actions for intricate tasks(, FAIR). (Hu et al., 2024) builds
116   an LLM-embodied agent accessing external knowledge for in-context learning to play Pokemon bat-
117
      tles. VLMs for games input images and text-like game rules to obtain the corresponding plan and
      action. Unlike games, embodied AI must control physical entities and interact with the environment.
118
      Vision-language-action models(VLAs) handle multi-modal inputs and generate actions for robots to
119
      complete embodied tasks. VIMA (Jiang et al., 2022) introduces different multi-modal prompts com-
120   pared to traditional pure text prompts to generalize the model to multiple tasks. RT-2 (Brohan et al.,
121   2023) utilizes large multimodal models for robotics tasks, introduces the co-fine-tuning technique,
122   and reserves the model’s general ability while learning robotics data.
123
      Building a model that handles multiple embodied tasks and continues to explore and develop new
124
      skills is crucial for achieving general AI models. (Reed et al., 2022) propose unifying the form of
125
      inputs and outputs of multiple tasks by designing tokenization and embedding methods. Then, they
126   use autoregressive training to build a general agent. But it’s still hard to continue learning because
127   training on new tasks will cause catastrophic forgetting of previous tasks. Building agents with
128   an expandable knowledge base is feasible because large language models have robust retrieval and
129   reasoning abilities. VOYAGER(Wang et al., 2023a) proposes a lifelong learning agent for Minecraft
130   with GPT models. They utilize LLMs to discover diverse tasks, update the task libraries, and prompt
131   using in-context learning. However, they all depend on human-labeled data, do not introduce any
132   updates to the capabilities of the LLMs.
133   Inspired by previous work, we employ vision-language models to address multiple game tasks,
134   which avoids the need for designing complex rules and expert knowledge required by agent-based
135   methods. By leveraging the model’s inherent knowledge and optimization capabilities and based
136   on the knowledge fusion capability of LLM, we can build a continuous learning model for multiple
137   tasks.

      2.2   M ODEL M ERGING AND L ORA A DAPTERS F USION
142
143
144   In the literature, there have been many attempts at model merging in academia, (Yang et al., 2024)
145   provides a detailed survey of the current model merging methods. (Yang et al., 2023) learns a
146   distinct merging coefficient layer-wise or task-wise to merge task parameters. (Zhang et al., 2024)
147   and (Ilharco et al., 2022) address knowledge composition and model editing through task vectors,
148   which can also be applied to model merging.
149
      To adapt LLM to different tasks, continual pre-training, and fine-tuning are essential. VLMs are
150   extremely large, and fully finetuning is quite computationally expensive. In addition to that, using
151   parameter-efficient methods like LoRA(Hu et al., 2021) can make fine-tuning more feasible. Dif-
152   ferent fine-tuned adapters can be combined with different weights to solve intricate tasks. Some
153   research attempts to combine LoRA with a mixture of experts(MoE)(Dou et al., 2023) to generate
154   a combination of different weights for different tasks. Most of the research(Huang et al., 2023) is
155   focused on improving weight combination ways, which introduces extra parameters and computa-
156   tional consumption. Some research also focuses on combining different task matrices into a single
157   matrix. DARE(Yu et al., 2024) randomly drops fine-tuned parameters and rescales the remaining
158
      ones, then a different matrix is added together. Merging methods like DARE are implemented in the
      PEFT(Mangrulkar et al., 2022) (Zhang et al., 2023)library.
159
160   Based on previous research, we propose a new way to merge LoRA weights. The method needs to
161   introduce no extra parameters and reserve general capabilities. Also we need the model to iterative
      merge new parameters which can be further expand to new tasks.

203   3.2   OVERVIEW
204
205
      Our approach aims to develop a general-purpose model that can handle multiple tasks and iteratively
      update. To achieve this goal, we often encounter fine-tuned models and a set of task-specific training
206
      data. Our methods consist of two parts:
207
208          • Routing Tunning: This method aims to enhance the generalization capability of the fine-
209            tuned model, ensuring that the fine-tuned LoRA adapter is only adequate for the current
210            task. At the same time, its activation is constrained to zero when dealing with general
211            or other tasks. This is achieved by routing different data to different losses and learning
212            separately.
213          • Iterative Merging: We design a method to integrate the newly fine-tuned adapter, derived
214            from the additional training data, with previously integrated adapters. This integration aims
215            to maintain performance across multiple tasks without compromising the model’s general
               capabilities.

217   our Routing tuning method to minimize the impact of fine-tuning on the model’s generalization
218   abilities. Next, we will describe how to efficiently and iteratively integrate multiple task-specific

220   the model’s general capabilities.

222   3.3   R ESERVE G ENERAL C APABILITIES WITH ROUTING T UNNING
223
224   For the given task and the data set Di = {(x1 , y1 ) , . . . , (xn , yn )}, we fine-tuned the model using
225   LoRA, getting a set of adapters A = {(A1 , B1 ) , · · · , (An , Bn )} achieving improved performance.
226   However, we observed a significant degradation in the generalizability of the model. Due to the
227   additive property of LoRA matrices, we attempted to merge the LoRA adapters from multiple tasks
228
      by summing them. We want the sum of adapters that achieve (A1 B1 + · · · + An Bn ) ◦ Xi ≃ Ai Bi ◦
      Xi .
229
230   We experimented with LoRA fusion methods provided by the PEFT (Mangrulkar et al., 2022) li-
231   brary, including DARE (Yu et al., 2024) and TIES (Yadav et al., 2024). In all cases, we observed
232   a considerable decrease in performance, suggesting interference between parameters from different
233   tasks. This led us to consider strategies to mitigate such interference.
234   Inspired by model-unlearning(Yao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b) techniques, we naturally consid-
235   ered incorporating some general datasets into the training set. These datasets encompass multiple
236   tasks, and by constraining the activation of the LoRA matrices on these tasks, we can alleviate
237   the degradation in generalization performance while mitigating the interference between parameters
238   across different tasks. Consequently, we designed two loss functions.
239   During training, we first identify the data in the batch that does not belong to the target task and
240   exclude it from the loss calculation of the language modeling. Then we design LKL and Lnorm . The

242   with LoRA to ensure that they remain as consistent as possible, preventing the model updates from
243   affecting their original outputs. Furthermore, Lnorm captures the output of the LoRA B layer during
244   inference on general datasets, ensuring that LoRA activations in general tasks approach zero, thus
245   preserving the performance of the original model. The parameter updating process is summarized

      In the previous section, we used Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to reduce redundancy and
307
      minimize interference between different LoRA adapters. Next, we considered how to achieve itera-
308
      tive fusion. We first discuss the desired properties of dynamic fusion:
309
310         1. Equality: Each adapter should have an equal opportunity to participate in the fusion pro-
311            cess. Every parameter should equally consider the values provided by each adapter.
312
            2. Associativity: The fusion should satisfy (A + B) + C = A + B + C. When merging a
313            new adapter with previously fused adapters, the result should be equivalent to merging all
314            adapters for the first time.
315
            3. Saliency: Given an input, the adapter with larger activation values should stand out more
316
               prominently, ensuring the quality of the generated output.
317
318   Based on these considerations, linear addition satisfies these properties. However, when two sets of
319   parameters need to be merged into the base model, their combined effect will amplify the perturba-
320   tion on the original parameters if both share the same sign (positive or negative). Conversely, if they
321   have opposite signs, they will mutually weaken each other’s perturbation on the original model pa-
322   rameters, leading to significant performance degradation. Suppose the values of the LoRA adapter
323   parameters are too large or too small. In that case, it degrades the model’s generation quality on
      fine-tuned tasks and significantly reduces the model’s generalization performance.

      4.2.1    S INGLE M ODEL P ERFORMANCE
407
408   We follow the setting of an existing Atari 100k benchmark(Kaiser et al., 2019), widely used in
409   reinforcement learning algorithms to test sample efficiency. It consists of 26 games, each being
410   allowed to be trained only in 100K steps. We fine-tuned the model for each game using LoRA,
411   setting the LoRA rank to 128 and the LoRA alpha to 256. Each dataset underwent fine-tuning for
412
      five epochs.
413   We used the mean and median of the human-normalized score to compare with human players,
414   with the total reward from the environment representing the score. For benchmarking, we selected
415   three RL algorithms - SPR (Schwarzer et al., 2020), DreamerV3 (Hafner et al., 2023), and DART
416   (Agarwal et al., 2024) - and obtained results after 100K sampling steps. The results are presented in
417   the table 1.

524   5   C ONCLUSION
525
526   This paper presents a method for multitask learning using LoRA adapter fusion methods. In contrast
527   to traditional model fusion approaches, we have developed a pipeline from fine-tuning to model
528
      merging. Through Routing Tuning, the model learns based on different losses for different data,
      reducing interference between the model’s capabilities and new tasks. Subsequently, multiple LoRA
529
      adapters can be combined to represent modifications to the base model, and more importantly, new
530
      task adapters can be iteratively added. A general model capable of handling various tasks can be
531   constructed by directly merging models and fused adapters. We run the experiments in Atari game
532   scenarios because each game can be seen as a separate task, unlike general tasks. The experiment
533   results have shown the remarkable performance of our methods and demonstrate the potential of
534   using LoRA adapters for fusion to enable lifelong learning capabilities in models.
