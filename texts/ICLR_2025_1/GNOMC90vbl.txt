011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The security and robustness of deep neural networks (DNNs) have become in-
014            creasingly concerning. This paper aims to provide both a theoretical foundation
015
               and a practical solution to ensure the reliability of DNNs. We explore the con-
               cept of Lipschitz continuity to certify the robustness of DNNs against adversarial
016
               attacks, which aim to mislead the network with adding imperceptible perturba-
017
               tions into inputs. We propose a novel algorithm that remaps the input domain
018            into a constrained range, reducing the Lipschitz constant and potentially enhanc-
019            ing robustness. Unlike existing adversarially trained models, where robustness
020            is enhanced by introducing additional examples from other datasets or genera-
021            tive models, our method is almost cost-free as it can be integrated with existing
022            models without requiring re-training. Experimental results demonstrate the gener-
023            alizability of our method, as it can be combined with various models and achieve
024            enhancements in robustness. Furthermore, our method achieves the best robust
025            accuracy for CIFAR10, CIFAR100, and ImageNet datasets on the RobustBench
026
               leaderboard.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Deep neural networks (DNNs) have demonstrated promising results across various tasks
031   (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Redmon et al., 2016), prompting concerns about AI security as these net-
032   works are increasingly deployed in our daily lives. A single erroneous prediction could lead to
033   catastrophic consequences. For example, the Overload attack can significantly inflate the inference
034   time of detecting objects for self-driving systems (Chen et al., 2023), while even minor typos in
035   input prompts can cause large language models to produce unexpected responses (Zhu et al., 2023).
036   The focus of this paper is to design robust DNNs that can defend against adversarial attacks, which
037   aim to create perturbations in inputs that are imperceptible to humans but can mislead DNNs. Pre-
038   vious studies have revealed the existence of adversarial examples in diverse domains, such as image
039   pixels (Szegedy et al., 2013), audio data (Carlini & Wagner, 2018), and textual content (Li et al.,
040   2018). Consequently, exploring the vulnerabilities of DNNs and developing theoretically grounded
041   explainable AI is crucial for ensuring the reliability of DNN-based applications.
042   Adversarial training (Madry et al., 2017) has proven to be an effective strategy for enhancing the
043   robustness of DNNs. It achieves this by generating adversarial examples on the fly during the train-
044   ing phase and optimizing the model’s weights to minimize the losses caused by these examples.
045   Recent studies have shown that robustness can be further improved by introducing additional ex-
046   amples from other datasets (Carmon et al., 2019) or using generative models (Gowal et al., 2021;
047   Wang et al., 2023) to cover low-frequency data. Despite the promising improvements in robustness,
048   training costs increase significantly due to the demand for additional data, which can be up to 20 to
049   100 times larger than the original dataset. This poses a trade-off between training cost and robust-
050
      ness. The concern over high computational costs becomes a significant obstacle in deploying robust
      DNN-based applications, especially in fields like medicine, autonomous driving systems, and other
051
      areas where human lives are at stake.
052
053   In this paper, we explore how robustness is certified by the theorem of Lipschitz continuity, which
      theoretically gauges how much outputs are amplified by the perturbations. However, we argue that

104   2.1   A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS
105
106   Adversarial attacks aim to inject tiny perturbations into inputs, causing victim DNNs to output in-
107   correct predictions with high confidence (Chen & Hsieh, 2022). These attacks have been observed
      in numerous vision applications (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022; Yin


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2022). Furthermore, these tiny perturbations can be embedded not only in image pixels but
109   also in textual contexts (Kumar et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023), audio space (Xie et al., 2021), and
110   other fields (Ilahi et al., 2021). Some research has shown how adversarial attacks threaten real appli-
111   cations (Xu et al., 2020; Komkov & Petiushko, 2021; Du et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022). Investigating
112   the vulnerability of DNNs and theoretically avoiding adversarial examples when optimizing model
113   weights or designing architecture is an ongoing challenge.
114
      Adversarial attacks can be classified into two types based on the amount of information the attacker
115   has access to: white-box attacks and black-box attacks. In the case of white-box attacks, the attacker
116   has full access to all information about the victim model. Methods such as the PGD attack (Madry
117   et al., 2017) and AutoAttack (Croce & Hein, 2020) generate adversarial examples by leveraging
118   the gradient direction of the model. Although this scenario is often unrealistic in practical settings,
119   research in this area is valuable for developing more robust models in the future.
120
      In contrast, black-box attacks, such as the square attack (Andriushchenko et al., 2020) and ZO-NGD
121   (Zhao et al., 2020), only provide the attacker with access to the model’s output predictions. In these
122   cases, adversarial examples can still be generated through transferability, where models with similar
123   architectures are used to create adversarial examples (Wang et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2024). The
124   goal of black-box attacks is to investigate the potential risks of adversarial attacks in real-world
125   application scenarios, where attackers may have limited access to model internals.
126
127   2.2   D EFENSIVE S TRATEGIES
128
129   Adversarial training is a defensive strategy that aims to find optimal weights against adversarial
130   attacks. It achieves this by generating adversarial examples on the fly during the training phase
131   and optimizing the model’s weights to minimize the losses caused by these examples. Despite the
132
      superior robustness achieved by adversarial training, the associated training costs of adversarially
      trained models are generally ten times more expensive than those of models trained utilizing a stan-
133
      dard policy. The concern over high computational costs becomes a significant obstacle in deploying
134
      DNN-based applications.
135
136   Balancing between training cost and robustness is a challenge for adversarial training. Fast adver-
137   sarial training has been proposed for applications pursuing higher robustness under a limited budget
138
      (Chen & Lee, 2020; Zhang et al., 2022). However, numerous adversarial examples cannot be drawn
      from these approaches, potentially leading to catastrophic overfitting, where robust accuracy signif-
139
      icantly decreases without warning signs (Rice et al., 2020). On the contrary, some studies attempted
140
      to refine robustness by introducing additional examples from other datasets (Carmon et al., 2019)
141   or using generative models (Gowal et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023). Alternatively, another line of
142   research has demonstrated that the removal of partial adversarial examples does not compromise ro-
143   bust accuracy, addressing the issue of unaffordable training costs (Zhang et al., 2020; Chen & Lee,
144   2024).
145
      Despite the potential of adversarial training to enhance model robustness, budgetary constraints
146
      often limit the scope of their crafting to one or two specific attack types during the training stage.
147   This restricted approach may inadvertently render adversarially trained models susceptible to novel,
148   unseen attacks. As an alternative, Lipschitz-based certified training offers a theoretical framework
149   for ensuring an upper bound on prediction errors (Gowal et al., 2018; Huang et al., 2021; Müller
150   et al., 2022). However, it is important to acknowledge that these training methods often suffer from
151   scalability issues.

490   4.4   D ISCUSSION
491
      A common pitfall is the misconception that minimizing the magnitude of the Lipschitz constant
492
      necessarily leads to improved robustness. However, in an extreme scenario, if the weights of the
493   linear layers are replaced with an identity matrix, the Lipschitz constant would equal 1. This change
494   would completely alter the output distribution, resulting in zero natural accuracy and, therefore, no
495   meaningful robustness to assess. This work demonstrates that by manipulating the input domain of
496   a linear system, it is possible to obtain an equivalent matrix that produces the same output while
497   having a Lipschitz constant that is equal to or less than the original.
498
      While it is true that the Lipschitz constant can be reduced through certified training (Mao et al.,
499   2024) or Lipschitz-constrained methods (Zühlke & Kudenko, 2024), these approaches often intro-
500   duce additional regularization terms or more complex objectives, complicating the training of robust
501   models. In contrast, this work is specifically designed for model optimization during inference time,
502   providing a cost-effective means to enhance robustness. Furthermore, this research offers valuable
503   insights into identifying which weights can be eliminated with minimal impact on performance.
504   This property could be integrated into existing training frameworks to further enhance robustness.
505   The overall procedure of the proposed algorithm shares many similarities with pruning techniques
506   (He & Xiao, 2023). However, we would like to emphasize that the proposals are distinctly different.
507   Pruning primarily aims to create a highly sparse model that accelerates inference times or reduces
508   the model size for deployment on edge devices, often without considering robustness. In particular,
509   when the pruned model exhibits extremely high sparsity without applying re-training or fine-tuning,
510   natural accuracy drops significantly, implicitly indicating that condition (11) does not hold and that
511   Lemma 2 is not applicable in this case. On the other hand, when the pruned model has low sparsity,
512   only a small proportion of weights with values close to zero are eliminated, resulting in insignificant
513
      adjustments to the center and radius of the disk. The proposed algorithm, on the other hand, can
      identify crucial elements with minimal cost. We believe that investigating the impact of various
514
      pruning techniques, such as iterative or post-training methods, on robustness, or combining these
515
      techniques with our proposed approach, represents a valuable direction for future research.
516

518   5     C ONCLUSION
519
520   In this paper, we recap how robustness is certified by the theorem of Lipschitz continuity. We
521   introduce the concept of the empirical Lipschitz constant, which minimizes the influence of the space
522   not drawn from real data, resulting in a precise estimation of the robustness of the corresponding
523   observed data. We prove that by remapping the input domain of a specific layer to a constrained
524   range, the Lipschitz constant can be shrunk, leading to better robustness. The proposed function
525   introduces only one parameter, the value of which can be determined by scanning the training data
526   once, without re-training or fine-tuning. Compared with adversarial training, the proposed method
527   is almost cost-free. The experimental results suggest that our method can be combined with various
528
      existing methods and achieve robustness improvements, and no gradient masking occurs in our
      algorithm. Furthermore, our method can achieve the best robust accuracy for CIFAR10, CIFAR100,
529
      and ImageNet datasets on the RobustBench leaderboard.
530
531   Numerous future directions merit exploration. Firstly, due to the property of maximization, the
532   proposed function might easily be influenced by outliers. Designing a better function is an interest-
533   ing research topic. Secondly, exploring the combination with various activation functions, different
534
      model architectures or large-scale datasets would be beneficial. Lastly, it is worth investigating to
      understand the theoretical reasons why our proposed function improves standard accuracy.
