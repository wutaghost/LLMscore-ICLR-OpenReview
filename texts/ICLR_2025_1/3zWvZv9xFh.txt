011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advancements in deep generative models have significantly facilitated
014            protein-ligand structure design, which is crucial in protein engineering. However,
015            recent generative approaches based on diffusion models in this field usually start
016
               sampling from a unified distribution, failing to capture the intricate biochemical
               differences between receptors. This may limits their capacity to generate reliable
017
               ligands for the corresponding receptors. Moreover, the current sampling process
018
               incurs a heavy computational burden and inefficiency, which further escalates the
019            training demands on the model. To this end, we introduce a novel diffusion model
020            with customized perturbing and sampling for the protein-ligand design targeting
021            the specific receptor, named as Receptor-Specific Diffusion Model (RSDM). In
022            particular, the receptor-specific information is used to tailor fine-grained sampling
023            distributions via changing the noise for customized perturbing. Meantime, we
024            refine the sampling process using a predefined schedule to perform stepwise de-
025            noising and gradually decrease the influence of the receptor’s guidence in the lig-
026            and generation for customized sampling. The experimental reaults indicate that
027
               RSDM is highly competitive with state-of-the-art learning-based models, includ-
               ing the latest models like ElliDock and DiffDock-PP. Additionally, RSDM stands
028
               out for its faster inference speed compared with all baseline methods, highlighting
029
               its potential for generating dependable protein-ligand.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Protein design is essential in biomedical research, particularly for targeting specific proteins, by
035
      facilitating the development of highly specific drugs and deepening our understanding of biological
036   mechanisms. Protein-ligand structure design complements protein design by providing insights into
037   how a designed protein will interact with its receptor, such as drugs or substrates. To accurately
038   predict protein-ligand structures for a given protein-receptor, researchers need to determine ligand-
039   bound conformations that are specific to the receptor while ensuring the stability and functionality
040   of the resulting complex. Traditional search-based methods (Chen et al., 2003; De Vries et al., 2010;
041   de Vries et al., 2015) employ a scoring function paired with search techniques to identify the most
042   plausible predicted pose of a ligand matching experimental data. While these methods can yield
043   satisfactory results, they are computationally intensive and time-consuming.
044   Recently powerful learning-based methods (Gainza et al., 2019; Ganea et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2024;
045   Ketata et al., 2023; Guan et al., 2024; Evans et al., 2021) aim to predict the final pose of the input
046   ligand directly, prioritizing an end-to-end, data-driven approach. Deep learning generation meth-
047   ods based on diffusion are gaining increasing attention due to their global 3D structure generation
048   capability and ability to rapidly produce multiple conformations simultaneously. These methods
049   formulate protein-ligand structure design as a generative problem: given an interacting protein pair,
050
      the goal is to estimate the distribution over all potential poses using a diffusion model. For example,
      DiffDock-PP (Ketata et al., 2023) aims to directly predict the structure of the protein-ligand while
051
      comprehensively considering both the ligand pose and the protein-receptor structure.
052
053   However, we have identified issues in the current diffusion process that may limit its performance
      for ligand structure design. In the forward process, applying noise sampled from a unified distri-

084
085   To optimize the diffusion process mentioned above, as shown in Figure 1, we propose a novel
086   receptor-specific diffusion model (RSDM) towards generating ligand structures with customized
087
      perturbing in the forward process and customized sampling in the reverse process. Specifically,
      RSDM refines the diffusion process using two targeted strategies to enhance both the accuracy and
088
      computational efficiency of diffusion-based approaches. In the forward process, personalized sam-
089
      pling distribution applies customized noise perturbation for each ligand by tailoring the noise ac-
090   cording to receptor-specific information. In the reverse process, the RSDM employs customized
091   sampling via step-by-step data purification to iteratively refine the model’s output based on a prede-
092   fined schedule that incorporates receptor-specific information. This schedule enables the model to
093   directly predict the noise-perturbed sample from the previous time step based on the current sam-
094   ple while gradually reducing the receptor’s influence on ligand generation. This refined diffusion
095   process offers two key benefits: First, customized perturbing ensures that the generated ligand is
096   strongly influenced by its corresponding receptor during the initial phases of the sampling process,
097   which is crucial for maintaining receptor-ligand specificity. Second, customized sampling prevents
098
      over-reliance on receptor guidance, allowing the model to generate a independent ligand structure
      that is more biologically accurate and functional. Our experimental results demonstrate that RSDM
099
      exhibits robust competitiveness against state-of-the-art learning-based models, while significantly
100
      reducing inference times compared to all baseline methods.

105   2.1   P ROTEIN -P ROTEIN D OCKING
106
107   The existing complex structures capture merely a fraction of the vast number of interactions believed
      to occur within living organisms. Manually collecting and labeling a sufficient amount of protein

108
      complexes data is impractical due to its time-consuming and labor-intensive property. Thus it is
109   highly necessary to discover effective and novel protein complexes to development protein-protein
110   docking experimental efforts with computational approaches. Traditional docking methods (Chen
111   et al., 2003; De Vries et al., 2010; de Vries et al., 2015; Yan et al., 2020) follow the scheme that
112   typically begins by sampling from the geometric space of the two interacting proteins, then use a
113   scoring function to assess binding affinity, and finally refine the structures obtained in earlier stages
114   using an energy model. Recently deep learning methods for protein-protein docking task can be
115   roughly classified into two groups, i.e., single-step and multi-step methods. The former (Ganea
116   et al., 2021; Sverrisson et al., 2022; Watson et al., 2023) predicts the complex structure directly in
117
      one step, while the latter (Evans et al., 2021; McPartlon & Xu, 2023; Guan et al., 2024) iteratively
      refines a set of proposed structures to produce its final predictions.

119
120   2.2   E QUIVARIANT G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS (EGNN S ).
121
      Due to any problems exhibit 3D translation and rotation symmetries, such as point clouds (Uy et al.,
122
      2019) and 3D molecular structures (Ramakrishnan et al., 2014), it is often desired that predictions
123
      on these tasks are either equivariant or invariant with respect to different coordinate transformation.
124   Recent works (Fuchs et al., 2020; Jiao et al., 2023; Jing et al., 2021; Satorras et al., 2021; Yim et al.,
125   2023) are proposed from geometric first-principles to improve the ability of traditional GNNs on
126   achieving equivariance from E(3) transformations. SE(3)-Transformers (Fuchs et al., 2020) employs
127   the equivariance constraints on the self-attention to ensure the output of model is invariant to global
128   rotations and translations. EGNN (Satorras et al., 2021) computes the weight coefficient via the
129   relative squared distance between particles to guarantee equivariance, without requiring the spherical
130   harmonics (Fuchs et al., 2020; Thomas et al., 2018). FrameDiff (Yim et al., 2023) implements the
131   proposed theory as a SE(3) invariant diffusion model for protein backbone generation.
132
133   2.3   D IFFUSION M ODELS
134
135   Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song & Ermon, 2019) are increas-
136   ingly powerful tools to generate novel and effective samples by iteratively denoising data points
137
      sampled from a prior noise distribution, which have shown unprecedented success in images (Dhari-
      wal & Nichol, 2021; Nichol et al., 2021) and texts (Ramesh et al., 2022). Considering the great
138
      potential of diffusion models in generating data, several recent works (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021;
139
      Ho & Salimans, 2022) have proposed expanding diffusion models to generate protein structures.
140   ProteinSGM (Lee et al., 2023) implements the diffusion process via learning inter-residue 6D co-
141   ordinates in an amino acid chain based on the idea of the score-based diffusion model (Song et al.,
142   2020). FoldingDiff (Wu et al., 2024) implements the diffusion model on the inter-residue angles
143   in protein backbones instead of 3D coordinates. Due to the primary objective of the initial dif-
144   fusion model is to understand the data distribution, some researchers incorporate classifier-based
145   guidance to implement controllable generation. DiffSBDD (Schneuing et al., 2022) employs the
146   diffusion model to design small-molecule ligands while keeping SE(3)-equivariance. DiffAb (Luo
147   et al., 2022) develops a deep learning model to generate antibodys explicitly by considering the 3D
148
      information from antigens.

      4.2   P ROTEIN D IFFUSION M ODEL IN 3D
191
192   Our proposed model is based on DDPM, which employs a Markov process to introduce random
193   noise to a sample x0 across T discrete time steps until it becomes indistinguishable denoted as xT .
194   Recent advancements in modeling 3D data have demonstrated that neural networks built to follow
195   geometric invariances can introduce meaningful biases, thereby enhancing model generalizability
196   and training efficiency (Batzner et al., 2022). Motivated by this insight, we incorporate an equiv-
197   ariant graph neural network (EGNN) (Satorras et al., 2021) into the diffusion model as fθ , which
198   demonstrates equivariance to transformations within the Euclidean group when handling 3D data.
199   Before generating ligand structures, we need to encode the input point cloud with atoms to cap-
200   ture the underlying structural dependencies between the ligand and the receptor. Specifically, we
201   construct a two-level encoder (Jin et al., 2022) to capture ligand-receptor interactions, including an
202   atom-level encoder and a residue-level encoder.
203
204   • The atom-level encoder takes atom types as model input and constructs a K nearest neighbor
205     graph for each atom. The edge embeddings between two atoms are derived from two perspec-

      ▷ Datasets. We evaluate RSDM on two datasets:
329
330   Docking benchmark version 5 (DB5.5). DB5.5 (Vreven et al., 2015) is recognized as a gold stan-
331   dard dataset for its high-quality data, encompassing 253 high-quality complex structures. Following
332
      the data partitioning approach of EquiDock (Ganea et al., 2021), DB5.5 is divided into training,
      validation, and test sets with sizes of 203, 25, and 25, respectively.
333
334   The Structural Antibody Database (SAbDab). SAbDab (Dunbar et al., 2014) is a specialized
335   database curated for ligand-receptor complexes. The data is partitioned based on sequence similarity
336   assessed by MMseqs2 (Steinegger & Söding, 2017), resulting in a training set and a validation set
337   with sizes of 1781 and 300, respectively. For performance evalution, we employ an independent
338
      test set with 54 ligand-receptor complexes curated from the RAbD (Adolf-Bryfogle et al., 2018)
      database. The setting of the evaluation tests on SAbDab in this work aligns with that of Ellidock (Yu
339
      et al., 2024).
340
341   ▷ Baselines. To verify the effectiveness of RSDM, we compare it with five state-of-the-art meth-
342   ods for protein-protein docking, including the alphafold-based protein complex prediction model
343   Alphafold-Multimer (Evans et al., 2021), the template-based docking server HDock (Yan et al.,
344
      2020), the regression-based docking model EquiDock (Ganea et al., 2021), the diffusion-based
      docking model DiffDock-PP (Ketata et al., 2023) and interface-fitting approach docking model
345
      Ellidock (Yu et al., 2024). The recommended hyperparameters of EquiDock, DiffDock-PP, and
346
      Ellidock are applied in our evaluation tests. The original pre-trained models are used for HDock and
347   Alphafold-Multimer.
348
349   ▷ Implementation. Our models are trained and tested on NVIDIA A40 GPUs, each with 48GB of
350
      memory. The hierarchical encoder consists of four message passing layers to update the target node
      embedding with a hidden dimension of 256. We utilize the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2015)
351
      with a learning rate of 1 × 10−3 . The dropout ratio is set to 0.1. The number of nearest neighbors K
352
      is set to 9. RSDM is trained with β1 = 1 × 10−4 , βT = 0.7, and T = 8 for 500 epochs. We save the
353   model with the lowest loss evaluated on the validation set. The ligand structure generated by RSDM
354   is refined using OpenMM (Eastman et al., 2017) and then be utilized for performance evaluation.
355

525   6     C ONCLUSION
526
527
      We develop a novel model for protein-ligand structures generation based on the diffusion model,
528   which is strongly competitive with state-of-the-art learning-based methods. Crucial to the success
529   of the proposed model is to tailor an customized sampling distribution for each ligand and sim-
530   plify model prediction of raw ligand data through stepwise denoising. RSDM outperforms existing
531   learning-based models and performs competitively against search-based methods at the inference
532   time level. Experimental results on two benchmark datasets and ablation study demonstrate the
533   effectiveness of our proposed model.
534   In the future, we look forward to explore more sophisticated strategies for incorporating more do-
535   main knowledge to refine the reverse process of the protein diffusion model via tailoring customized
536   sampling distributions or investigating additional contextual information. Meantime, the limitation
537   of our model is that RSDM only considers ligand generation without considering the variations in
538   the binding sites, which can affect the ligand generation and binding capabilities. We hope the
539   protein-protein docking paradigm can provide an insight to enhance the flexibility, adaptability, and
      robustness of our approach to better handle a wider range of receptor-ligand interaction scenarios.

748   C     E VALUATION METRICS

750   We evaluate all models via Complex Root Mean Squared Deviation (CRMSD), Interface Root Mean
751   Squared Deviation (IRMSD) and DockQ (Basu & Wallner, 2016). Specifically, when given both the
752   ground-truth and predicted complex structures, CRMSD is calculated by aligning them with the
753   Kabsch algorithm (Kabsch, 1976) and subsequently computing the CRMSD. Similarly, IRMSD is
754   determined by aligning their interface residues and calculating the RMSD over the interface. DockQ
755   serves as a common metric for protein-protein docking models, represented as a weighted average
      of three components: contact accuracy, interface RMSD, and ligand RMSD.

      CDR (Complementarity Determining Region) refers to specific regions within antibodies located in
759   the variable regions, primarily responsible for antigen binding. The CDR comprises six variable re-
760   gions: CDR-H1, CDR-H2, CDR-H3, CDR-L1, CDR-L2, and CDR-L3, where ”H” stands for heavy
761   chain and ”L” for light chain. While all CDR regions contribute to antigen binding, CDR-H3 is
762   often considered the most critical. This is because CDR-H3 exhibits the highest variability and ac-
763   counts for much of the specificity, while other CDRs are relatively conserved. In this subsection,
