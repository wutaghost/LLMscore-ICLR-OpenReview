009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Reward models have been increasingly critical for improving the reasoning capa-
012            bility of LLMs. Existing research has shown that a well-trained reward model can
013            substantially improve model performances at inference time via search or best-of-
014            N votes. However, the potential of reward models during RL training time still
015            remains largely under-explored. It is currently unclear whether these reward mod-
016
               els can provide additional training signals to RL training that uses sparse success
               rewards, which verify the correctness of solutions. In this work, we evaluate pop-
017
               ular reward models for RL training, including the Outcome-supervised Reward
018
               Model (ORM) and the Process-supervised Reward Model (PRM), and train a col-
019            lection of LLMs for math problems using RL by combining these learned rewards
020            with success rewards. Surprisingly, even though these learned reward models have
021            strong inference-time performances, they may only bring marginal improvements
022            or even hurt RL training, producing worse performances than LLMs trained with
023            the success reward only. We find that training collapse easily occurs in RL train-
024            ing when PRM simply serves as reward shaping in addition to the success rewards.
025            Our further analysis reveals two issues that may lead to the sub-optimal perfor-
026            mance. Therefore, we introduce two novel reward refinement techniques, includ-
027
               ing the Clip and the Delta mechanisms, to tackle the identified issues. We evaluate
               our techniques with multiple reward models over a set of 1.5B and 7B LLMs on
028
               MATH and GSM8K benchmarks, where both Clip and Delta consistently enhance
029
               RL training. Finally, we also demonstrate that with a carefully designed reward
030            function, pure RL training without any additional supervised tuning can further
031            improve all the evaluated LLMs, including the state-of-the-art 7B LLM Qwen2.5-
032            Math-7B-Instruct on MATH and GSM8K benchmarks.

035   1    INTRODUCTION
036
037   There is a recent trend to improve the reasoning ability of LLMs with learned reward models (Light-
038   man et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Yu et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024; Yang et al.,
039
      2024b; Luo et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024c; Havrilla et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024; Uesato et al.,
      2022). Recent research has been focusing on guiding search processes during inference (Lightman
040
      et al., 2024; Snell et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b), with two main categories of reward models:
041
      Outcome-supervised Reward Model (ORM) (Cobbe et al., 2021b; Yu et al., 2024a) and Process-
042   supervised Reward Model (PRM) (Lightman et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Luo et al., 2024).
043   ORM generates outcome rewards that estimate the success rewards, which evaluate the correctness
044   of generated answers, enabling the selection of the most reliable answer from a pool of generated
045   candidates. By contrast, PRM is trained to distinguish correct reasoning steps from incorrect ones
046   and can provide step-level process rewards for search algorithms like Monte-Carlo Tree Search
047   (Chen et al., 2024a) and beam search (Snell et al., 2024).
048   However, the potential of reward models in RL training for LLM reasoning is not yet fully explored.
049   The most straightforward method for RL training in reasoning tasks is to optimize the success re-
050   wards. Some prior works further try the integration of a reward model into RL training (Havrilla
051   et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Shao et al., 2024). Havrilla et al. (2024) finds that PPO training
052   with a reward model only results in performance degeneration. In addition, some powerful LLMs
053   that exhibit strong reasoning abilities such as the Qwen2.5-Math family (Yang et al., 2024b) and
      DeepseekMath-7B-RL (Shao et al., 2024) adopt RL training with reward models as a part of their


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      overall training process for mathematical reasoning. However, due to a lack of detailed analysis on
055   the reward models, it remains unclear whether the reward models can provide additional training
056   signals beyond what the success rewards offer for LLM reasoning.
057
058
      In this work, we evaluate popular reward models, including ORM and PRM, as RL rewards
      on the challenging mathematical reasoning benchmark MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021) and
059
      GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021a) by using PPO as the RL algorithm (Schulman et al., 2017). Sur-
060
      prisingly, we find that these reward models may only bring marginal benefits to RL training or even
061   lead to performance degradation, yielding even worse results than LLMs trained with a sparse suc-
062   cess reward only. We observe that outcome rewards consistently achieve similar training results as
063   success rewards. We hypothesize that outcome rewards may not provide additional training signals
064   since a more accurate success reward is accessible at training time. For PRM, we perform an in-
065   depth analysis of the RL training process and observe that RL training easily collapses when simply
066   using the PRM as reward shaping in addition to the success rewards. Through further case studies,
067   we identify two possible causes, including the intrinsic biases of the PRM and a reward misspecifi-
068   cation issue, which can be largely exploited by the training LLM to generate sub-optimal behavior
069
      patterns.
070   To tackle these challenges, we propose two novel techniques, i.e., Clip and Delta, which refine the
071   process rewards for effective RL training. In particular, the Clip mechanism mitigates the intrin-
072   sic biases of PRM. The Delta mechanism tackles the reward misspecification issue by optimizing
073   single-step PRM rewards. Evaluation of these two techniques on synthetic reasoning trajectories
074   demonstrates that they can effectively mitigate the tendency of training to favor the observed sub-
075
      optimal patterns. Finally, we conduct full RL training on a set of advanced 1.5B and 7B LLMs
      from the Qwen2 and Qwen2.5 families (Yang et al., 2024a;b) with different reward models. Our
076
      experiment results show that our proposed techniques effectively enhance RL training. Moreover,
077
      with a carefully crafted reward, RL training can improve all the evaluated LLMs, including the state-
078   of-the-art 7B LLM Qwen2.5-Math-7B-Instruct on the challenging MATH and GSM8K (Hendrycks
079   et al., 2021; Cobbe et al., 2021a) benchmarks.

      2   RELATED WORK
084
085   Reinforcement Learning for LLMs. In RLHF, Reinforcement learning algorithms can effectively
086   fine-tune LLMs to align with the preference of humans (Dong et al., 2023; Rafailov et al., 2024;
087   Ouyang et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2024; Schulman et al., 2017), to improve the reasoning ability (Shao
088   et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024b) and coding skills (Wang et al., 2024a; Guo et al., 2024). PPO is the
089   most widely used among the popular RL algorithms due to its robust performance across various do-
090   mains (Ouyang et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2024). Xu et al. (2024) investigates the implementation details
091   of PPO for dialogue tasks and coding tasks, revealing batch size as a critical factor for improving
092   PPO performance in reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF). Our work addresses the
093
      challenge of designing RL rewards for LLM reasoning.
094   Reward Learning for LLMs. Learned reward models are widely adopted in RLHF to align LLMs
095   with human preferences (Dong et al., 2023; Rafailov et al., 2024; Ouyang et al., 2022). In RLHF,
096   reward models are trained on binary preference datasets collected from human annotators, following
097   the Bradley-Terry model (Bradley & Terry, 1952). In reasoning tasks involving reliable solution
098   checkers, two main approaches are the Outcome-supervised Reward Model (ORM) (Cobbe et al.,
099
      2021b; Yu et al., 2024a) and the Process-supervised Reward Model (PRM) (Lightman et al., 2024;
      Wang et al., 2024b; Luo et al., 2024). An ORM predicts the correctness of the final answer. A
100
      PRM estimates whether the steps so far are correct. Despite the successful applications of reward
101
      models, reward hacking is a broadly observed issue in learned reward models (Skalse et al., 2022;
102   Singhal et al., 2023; Casper et al., 2023). Through RL training, the LLM may learn to generate
103   high-reward outputs that could not fulfill the intended objectives. Several approaches have been
104   proposed to tackle the reward hacking issue, including disentangling the length aspect of reward
105   modeling (Chen et al., 2024b; Shen et al., 2023), reward ensemble (Eisenstein et al., 2024; Rame
106   et al., 2024), length penalty (Singhal et al., 2023), length normalization (Meng et al., 2024), and
107   various PPO implementation tricks (Singhal et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023). In this work, we
      investigate how to effectively use PRM/ORM as rewards in RL training for LLM reasoning, and

111   Improving Reasoning Ability of LLMs. To improve the reasoning ability of LLMs, prior works

      have focused on several different aspects, including pre-training (Yang et al., 2024b; Achiam et al.,

115   Shao et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024b; Shah et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024b). Pre-
116   training methods focus on enriching the data distribution to cover a large amount of rationals and
117   pre-training the LLM over the dataset. The prompting methods elicit the reasoning ability of LLMs
118   through dedicated prompting strategies and automatic agent frameworks. Inference-time search
119   utilizes learned reward models to guide the selection of promising solutions. PRM and ORM could

123
      and optimizing the reasoning ability with reinforcement learning (Yang et al., 2024b; Shao et al.,
      2024; Wang et al., 2024b). In this work, we study how to effectively combine dense and sparse
124
      rewards in RL training for reasoning tasks.

127   3    PRELIMINARY
128
129   Language Model. An LLM is represented as a policy πθ (s|q) parameterized by θ. In reasoning
130   tasks, πθ generates a solution s given a question q. In addition to the question, q usually also
131
      contains a prompt to elicit chain-of-thought reasoning. The solution s is structured with a list of
      reasoning steps and thus can be viewed from two perspectives, including tokens and steps. From the
132
      perspective of tokens, s consists of T tokens, s = (s1 , s2 , · · · , sT ). From the perspective of steps,
133
      s consists of K reasoning steps, s = (s(1) , s(2) , · · · , s(K) ) where s(k) denotes the k-th reasoning
134
      step. For convenience, we use p(k) = (s(1) , s(2) , · · · , s(k) ) to denote the solution prefix up to the k-
135
      th step. In practice, reasoning steps can be parsed with rule-based detectors, enforcing strict output
136
      formats, or special tokens (Chen et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024b; Lightman et al., 2024).
137
138   Reward Modeling. In RLHF, the reward models are usually trained with binary prefer-
139   ences (Bradley & Terry, 1952). In reasoning tasks where the correctness of solutions is accessible,
140
      reward models can be trained under the supervision of such ground-truth correctness. In reasoning
      tasks, two primary methods for reward modeling are the Process-supervised Reward Model (PRM)
141
      and the Outcome-supervised Reward Model(ORM).
142

253   Case Study for PR. For PR with a large α (α ≥ 0.05), a case study of the generated samples
254   reveals that the LLM learns to obtain high rewards with some specific patterns without faithfully
255   optimizing the ground-truth correctness through RL training. In the generated solutions of PR, there
256   are many short reasoning steps, but these steps only contain unnecessary or meaningless information
257
      that does not contribute to problem-solving. As the generation length increases, the model outputs
      only a single word or even emoji.

260   Analysis for PR. The rewards of unnecessary reasoning steps are positive and could even be large,
261   as shown in the case study (Fig. 2). The LLM learns to exploit this phenomenon by generating more
262   reasoning steps, resulting in a higher return. We further confirm the behavior through some synthetic
263   reasoning trajectories (Fig. 4(a) and Fig. 4(b)), where PR demonstrates extremely larger returns. We
264   identify two key observations through further case studies,
265   Observation 1: PRM has intrinsic biases to output positive and even high rewards to sub-
266   optimal reasoning steps. In the left part of Fig. 3, we illustrate a case study of biases of PRM.
267   The PRM could assign high values to steps with simple patterns, such as unnecessary and repetitive
268   steps, even achieving higher rewards than the optimal correct steps. On the other hand, incorrect
269   and nonsense steps could also receive small positive rewards. Consequently RL training would
      encourage the LLM to generate more sub-optimal steps.

      Here are two key takeaways regarding applying ORM and PRM in RL training,
309
310   Takeway for ORM. For ORM, it only marginally improves the sample efficiency of RL training
311   compared with the sparse success reward. We hypothesize this is because, when a success reward is
312   available during training time, ORM does not provide additional supervision signal and should not
313
      be a preferred choice at RL training time. We also remark that ORM does not suffer from training
      collapse since OR only uses ORM to generate sparse rewards, and the ORM outputs a 0-1 value,
314
      naturally ensuring an upper-bounded objective.
315
316   Takeway for PRM. Simply adopting PRM rewards as dense rewards would easily lead to training
317   collapse during RL training. Although PRM provides useful training signals for intermediate steps,
318   issues including the intrinsic biases of the learned PRM and the reward misspecification issue could
319
      cause the LLM to generate sub-optimal behavior patterns through RL training.

398
399   Training Dataset. We conduct RL training on the MathInstruct (Yue et al., 2024) dataset. In par-
400   ticular, we only use the questions and the golden answers in the dataset while the provided solutions
401   are not used for training. We use Qwen2-7B-Instruct to sample 16 answers for each question in the
402
      training dataset and keep those questions that have both correct and wrong answers. To train an
      ORM, binary cross entropy loss is adopted. For PRM training, we follow Wang et al. (2024b) to
403
      generate automatic process annotations by using Qwen2-7B-Instruct as the completer. Specifically,
404
      for each step in the generated samples, we sample 8 solutions starting from the solution prefix. This
405   step is labeled as correct if any of these 8 solutions is correct.
406
407
      Benchmarks & Metrics. We carry out our evaluation on the GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021a) and
408   MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021) datasets. For evaluation metrics, we report the Greedy and Sam-
409   pling scores, which correspond to adopting greedy decoding and sampling with temperature of 1 as
410   generation strategies, respectively.
411
412   Base Models. Our experiments are taken over a series of large language models from the
413   Qwen2 (Yang et al., 2024a) family and the state-of-the-art LLMs for mathematical reasoning,
414   Qwen2.5 (Yang et al., 2024b) family. Specifically, we use various 1.5B and 7B LLMs, including gen-
415   eral and math-specific models. For general models, we consider Qwen2-1.5B-Instruct and Qwen2-
416   7B-Instruct. For math-specific models, we consider Qwen2-Math-1.5B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-Math-
417   1.5B-Instruct, Qwen2-Math-7B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-Math-7B-Instruct. Note that these LLMs al-
418
      ready equip sufficient instruction following ability and we do not perform any further supervised
      fine-tuning. Lastly, the PRM is trained with the same base model as the actor model.

      et al., 2024), which supports LLM fine-tuning with dense rewards. The detailed hyperparameters

      6    C ONCLUSION
524
525
      In this work, we investigate designing dense rewards with a process-supervised reward model in
526   RL training to improve the reasoning ability of LLMs. We examine some popular reward models
527   and identify the issue of reward hacking, which manifests as the generation of nonsensical texts
528   or unnecessary reasoning steps. The reward hacking issue can be mitigated with our proposed
529   techniques, using the Clip mechanism to prevent the LLM from exploiting the reward model and the
530   Delta mechanism to ensure a bounded RL objective. We show that the proposed techniques can be
531   utilized to apply Process-supervised Reward Models for improved RL training.
532
533   Limitations. Limited by computation resources, our experiments are conducted over 1.5B&7B
534   LLMs, while evaluations on larger LLMs could further help verify our proposed techniques. Also, it
535
      is an interesting direction to perform various inference-time search strategies with the LLMs trained
      with PPO, which could help further understand whether RL training can improve search perfor-
536
      mance. Furthermore, we believe that with the support of more powerful reward models, RL training
537
      can bring greater benefits to LLM reasoning.

1051   PPO Hyperparameters and Training Setup. Following prior practices (Shao et al., 2024; Xu
1052   et al., 2024), we adopt a large batch size and sample multiple solutions for each question within a
1053
       batch. We find a large batch size and multiple sampling critical to the overall performance of RL
       training. For 1.5B models, there are 1024 questions, and 8 solutions are sampled for each question
1054
       in a batch, leading to a batch size of 1024 × 8. For 7B models, the batch size is 4096 × 8. Each
1055
       training batch is split into 4 minibatches. We apply a KL penalty coefficient of 0.1, a coefficient
1056   of 1 for dense rewards, and a coefficient of 5 for successful rewards. For the reward threshold η in
1057   the Clip mechanism, by default the average value of PRM rewards of all reasoning steps related to
1058   one question in a training batch. The learning rates of 1B and 7B actor models are 1e-6 and 1e-5,
1059   respectively, while all critic models use a learning rate of 5e-6. We use Adam optimizer weight
1060   decay of 0.05. The 1.5B models are trained on a cluster of 4 machines, each with 8 Nvidia H100
1061   GPUs, for approximately 8 hours. The 7B models are trained on a cluster of 8 machines, each with
1062   8 Nvidia H100 GPUs, for approximately 20 hours.
1063   Implementation of Clip and Delta. Both the Clip and the Delta mechanisms are straightforward to
1064   integrate into existing workflows. For the Clip mechanism, its implementation involves computing
1065   the mean of the reward as a threshold after reward calculation, followed by applying the formula
1066   specified in Eq. 5. This additional step is computationally lightweight and seamlessly fits within
1067   the existing reward processing pipeline. The Delta mechanism requires computing the difference
1068   between rewards from two adjacent steps, a process that is both conceptually simple and computa-
1069   tionally efficient. As such, neither method introduces significant overhead, ensuring their ease of
1070
       adoption.

       E.2   PRM AS VALUE
1135
1136   In this section, we are going to show the connection between PRM training and Q learning. We will
1137   show that PRMs can be interpreted as Q functions or value functions.
1138
       Notation. To train a PRM, the following elements are required,
1139
1140
              • A PRM rprocess (q, p) where q is the question and p is a partial solution prefix.
1141
1142          • A prompt dataset of pairs of questions and partial solution prefices Dprompt =
1143            {(qi , pi )}i∈[N ] .
1144
              • A completer πc that is a policy used for generating the full solution s given a question q
1145            and a partial solution prefix p. The generated full solution s contains p as the prefix and
1146            contain necessary rationales that lead to the final answer. The completer is not necessary
1147            an LLM policy and can also be a perfect oracle policy, or an LLM-based search policy.
1148
1149
              • A labeled dataset constructed by using the completer πc to find solution for the prompt
                dataset Dprompt , i.e. Dlabel = {(qi , pi , si , yi )|si ∼ πc (·|qi , pi ), yi = Correct(qi , si )}
1150
                where si is a full solution generated by the completer πc and yi denotes the correctness
1151
                label. The labels thus depend on the strength of the completer πc .
1152
1153          • A loss function L for PRM training. By default, we use the logistic loss
1154
1155
                               L(rprocess , Dlabel ) =Eqi ,pi ,si ,yi ∼Dlabel [−yi log rprocess (qi , pi )    (16)
1156                                                  − (1 − yi ) log(1 − rprocess (qi , pi ))]               (17)
1157
1158
       We first list some possible options for the completer,
1159
              • A perfect oracle completer πorcale . We assume the existence of a perfect oracle completer
1160
                πoracle that is perfectly rational in the sense that, given a question q and a partial solution
1161
                prefix p, πorcale would make optimal reasoning towards the correct answer while also not
1162            driving any conclusions conflicting to the partial solution prefix when completing the rest
1163            reasoning trajectory. Therefore, if the solution prefix p contains any erroneous reasoning
1164            steps that conflict with the correct answer, πoracle can not identify the correct answer and
1165            thus the label y for this pair (q, p) would be zero.
1166
              • A base LLM completer πbase . πbase is essentially an LLM that samples the rest of the
1167
                solution given a question q and a partial solution prefix p. We assume a temperature of 1.0
1168
                and do not consider any sampling techniques such as top-p and top-k sampling. Therefore
1169            the label y is 1 with the probability of πbase sampling a correct solution.
1170
1171          • A LLM-based search completer πsearch . We specially consider the search approach for
1172            automatic process label generation in Wang et al. (2024b), which tries to find a correct
1173
                solution out of the sampled M solutions from a base LLM πbase . Clearly the search com-
                pleter πsearch is an augmented policy of πbase . The probability of πsearch generating a
1174
                correct solution is higher than the probability of πbase generating a correct solution.
