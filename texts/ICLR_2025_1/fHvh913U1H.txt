011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated re-
014            markable capabilities across a wide range of tasks. These models are typically pre-
015
               trained on extensive corpora and subsequently fine-tuned on task-specific datasets.
               However, during the fine-tuning process, LLMs often suffer from catastrophic
016
               forgetting, wherein previously acquired general knowledge is lost. Traditional
017
               approaches to mitigating forgetting often rely on data replay, which may not be
018            viable when the original training data is inaccessible. Additionally, methods that
019            alter the training process or the model architecture can increase complexity and
020            detract from the accuracy of downstream tasks, thus limiting their generalizabil-
021            ity. In this paper, we propose Forgetting-Aware Pruning Metric (FAPM), a novel
022            pruning-based approach to balance forgetting and downstream task performance.
023            Our investigation reveals that the degree to which task vectors (i.e., the subtraction
024            of pre-trained weights from the weights fine-tuned on downstream tasks) overlap
025            with pre-trained model parameters is a critical factor for forgetting. Motivated by
026
               this insight, FAPM employs the ratio of the task vector to pre-trained model pa-
               rameters as a metric to quantify forgetting, integrating this measure into the prun-
027
               ing criteria. Importantly, FAPM does not necessitate modifications to the training
028
               process or model architecture, nor does it require any auxiliary data. We con-
029            ducted extensive experiments across six datasets encompassing natural language
030            inference, question answering, reading comprehension, and cloze tests. The re-
031            sults demonstrate that FAPM limits forgetting to just 1% while maintaining 99%
032            accuracy on downstream tasks, rendering FAPM highly competitive relative to the
033            state-of-the-art methods that involve modifications to the training process.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Large language models have demonstrated impressive general capabilities in handling various tasks
039
      (Bubeck et al., 2023; Rafailov et al., 2024). Nevertheless, practical deployment frequently uncovers
      the necessity for augmenting domain-specific competencies (Touvron et al., 2023; Scialom et al.,
040
      2022). To this end, task-oriented datasets are harnessed to fine-tune these models, thereby enhanc-
041
      ing their efficacy in targeted downstream tasks (Zhou et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024b). Many studies
042   have found that while LLMs acquire specialized knowledge during instruction fine-tuning, they tend
043   to forget their general capabilities, especially in full fine-tuning, which is also known as Catastrophic
044   Forgetting (CF) (Luo et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023; Wu et al.). Consequently, devising methodolo-
045   gies to alleviate CF during the instruction fine-tuning phase has become a critical research direction
046   for LLMs.
047

048   Replay-based methods incorporate a portion of the pre-training data into the fine-tuning data for
049   training (Scialom et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2024). 2) Regularization-based methods introduce
050   additional penalty terms in the loss function, encouraging the fine-tuned model to remain close to
051   the pre-trained model (Lin et al., 2023; Panigrahi et al., 2023). 3) Weight-based methods introduce
052   parameter weight coefficients to regulate their updates (Ke et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024). 4)
053   Architecture-based methods design additional modules outside of the original model (Wang et al.,
      2023; Hu et al., 2021). Although these methods can alleviate the forgetting problem to a certain

084
085   Can we solve the problem of catastrophic forgetting without changing training process, without
086   any additional data, and without altering model structure?
087   Recent research has highlighted two key findings: 1) There are a significant number of redundant
088   parameters in large language models (Yadav et al., 2024). 2) The task vector specifies a direction
089   in the weight space of the pre-trained model and moving towards its direction can improve task
090   performance (Ilharco et al., 2022). These findings suggest that we can prune portions of the task
091   vector’s parameters and set them to zero. By doing so, the corresponding positions of the pre-
092   trained model’s parameters are exposed, potentially preserving the accuracy of downstream tasks
093
      while mitigating catastrophic forgetting to some extent. To this end, we first try to apply existing
      pruning methods to prune the task vector to alleviate catastrophic forgetting. Unfortunately, we
094
      find it challenging to strike an optimal balance between maintaining downstream task accuracy and
095
      mitigating catastrophic forgetting using existing pruning techniques alone (Han et al., 2015; Sun
096   et al., 2023). Specifically, pruning the task vector with a low sparsity ratio fails to effectively mitigate
097   catastrophic forgetting, whereas pruning with a high sparsity ratio results in poor downstream task
098   accuracy. We find that there are two main reasons for this problem: 1) The existing pruning criteria
099   only ensure the balance between downstream task accuracy and sparsity, while not considering
100   catastrophic forgetting. 2) The extent to which the values of task vectors overlap with pre-trained
101   model parameters is a critical factor contributing to catastrophic forgetting.
102   In this paper, we propose a new pruning method called Forgetting-Aware Pruning Metric (FAPM).
103   FAPM not only applies magnitude as the pruning criterion for task vectors but also uses the ratio of
104   task vectors to pre-trained model parameters as the criterion for mitigating CF. By adopting FAPM,
105   we aim to identify, during the pruning process, those parameters in the task vector whose values
106   are large (crucial for maintaining the accuracy of downstream tasks) and we concurrently intend
107   to penalize those parameters where the ratio of their magnitude in the task vector to that of the
      corresponding parameter in the pre-trained model is notably high (more likely to induce CF). This

140
141          Problem Setting. Given downstream data D and a pre-trained model like Llama3, we fine-tune the
142          model using D. Let the pre-trained model parameters be Wpre and the fine-tuned model parameters
143          be Wf t . In this paper, we perform a series of operations on the task vector. Following previous
144          work (Ilharco et al., 2022), for a task, the task vector ∆W ∈ Rd can be defined as Wf t − Wpre .
145          This operation allows us to focus on the changes that occur during the fine-tuning stage.
146          Pruning on the task vector. We first try to apply existing pruning methods to prune the task vector,
147          which prunes parameters in the task vector according to their magnitude (Han et al., 2015). The red
148          lines in Figure 2(a) and Figure 2(b) display how downstream performance changes with sparsity ratio
149          on RTE and MRPC datasets. For each sparsity ratio, the model task vectors are “trimmed” to retain
150          only the top-k% highest-magnitude values, with the remaining values reset to zero. The green lines
151          in Figure 2 illustrate the impact of the sparsity ratio of the task vector on general task performance,
152
             focusing particularly on the extent of CF, where higher accuracy indicates lesser forgetting.
153          From Figure 2, we find that numerous values in a given task vector are redundant, and their removal
154          does not compromise task accuracy. Remarkably, the downstream task accuracy remains stable
155          even when the sparsity ratio reaches 90%. This suggests that when the pruned ∆W and Wpre are
156          combined as the final fine-tuning parameters, 90% parameters in Wpre are exposed. Conversely,
157          when sparsity exceeds 90%, increasing sparsity can further alleviate CF effectively; however, this
158
             coincides with a marked deterioration in downstream task performance. This paradox highlights the
             inherent difficulty in striking an ideal balance between maintaining downstream task performance
159
             and mitigating CF solely through magnitude pruning on ∆W . This raises a pertinent question:
160
161          What additional factors, beyond ∆W itself, could also affect the balance between maintaining
             downstream task accuracy and mitigating CF?

198   Considering Wf t = Wpre + ∆W , we hypothesize that the relative magnitude between ∆W and
199   Wpre is also a crucial factor influencing the balance between downstream task accuracy and forget-
200   ting. Let’s consider a simple scenario: if the change in parameters during the fine-tuning process is
201
      zero, then the fine-tuned model equals the pre-trained model, making this model optimal for address-
      ing the forgetting problem, i.e., there is no forgetting. Conversely, if the change in the pre-trained
202
      model parameters during fine-tuning is substantial, it indicates a significant modification of the pre-
203
      trained model parameters. The greater the difference between the new model and the no-forgetting
204   model, the more likely it is to result in forgetting.

286
287   Models and Evaluation: We evaluate FAPM on two widely adopt LLMs: Llama3-8B (Dubey et al.,
288   2024) and Qwen2-7B (Yang et al., 2024a). Following previous studies (Yadav et al., 2024; Wu et al.,
289
      2024; Han et al., 2024), we evaluate FAPM’s specialized performance across four key tasks: natu-
      ral language inference, question answering, cloze tests, and reading comprehension. We utilize the
290
      MRPC (3.67k training samples) (Wang et al., 2019) and RTE (2.49k training samples) (Wang et al.,
291
      2019) datasets for natural language inference, with accuracy as the evaluation metric. For question
292   answering, we employ the WikiQA (20.4k training samples) (Yang et al., 2015) and QASC (8.13k
293   training samples) datasets (Khot et al., 2020), using ROUGE-L as the evaluation metric. We use the
294   Winogrande dataset (10.2k training samples) (Sakaguchi et al., 2021) for cloze tests, measuring per-
295   formance with accuracy. Lastly, we utilize the SQuAD dataset (87.6k training samples) (Rajpurkar
296   et al., 2016) for reading comprehension, with the F1-score as the evaluation metric.
297
      To evaluate the generality of LLMs, we integrate insights from previous studies (Dubey et al.,
298   2024; Yang et al., 2024a) and focus on four key aspects. We use MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021)
299   to assess the inherent world knowledge stored in the LLM, C-Eval (Huang et al., 2023) to evalu-
300   ate the model’s understanding of general knowledge in Chinese, GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) to
301   evaluate mathematical reasoning, and HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021) to assess the code generation
302   capabilities.
303
      Compared Methods We compared FAPM with the full-parameter SFT (Full SFT) and four CF
304   baselines, which are described in detail in Appendix B. These baselines are carefully categorized
305   into three groups: 1) Regularization-based: These methods introduce additional terms in the loss
306   function to constrain parameter changes. The selected comparison baselines are L1 regulariza-
307   tion (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017). 2) Weight-based: These methods design a coefficient for each weight
308   to control its update during training. The selected baselines include V-SoftMask (Ke et al., 2023)
309   and CoFiTune (Zhang et al., 2024). 3) Architecture-based: These methods introduce additional pa-
310   rameters to ensure the pre-trained model’s parameters remain frozen during training. The selected
311   baseline is LoRA (Hu et al., 2021).
312   Experimental Setting: During training, we set the learning rate to 5e-6 and the batch size to 2. Each
313   dataset was trained for 3 epochs. The AdamW optimizer was used for fine-tuning. We employed
314   LLaMA-Factory (Zheng et al., 2024) as the training platform and vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023) for
315   inference. When implementing the FAPM algorithm, we applied a 90% sparsity rate across all
316   models and datasets.

415
416
417
      One question that needs to be analyzed is why FAPM is improved based on Magnitude Pruning
      instead of the SOTA LLM pruning method. To elucidate this choice, this section examines the ap-
418
      plication of a straightforward and efficient pruning method, Wanda (Sun et al., 2023), to mitigate the
419
      issue of catastrophic forgetting. Wanda addresses pruning by removing weights with the smallest
420   magnitudes, as determined by the product of the weight magnitudes and the norms of the corre-
421   sponding input activations, thereby preventing the need for retraining or weight updates, which is
422   formulated as Sij = |Wij | · ∥Xj ∥2 . We prune ∆W according to this criterion in our experiments.
423
      Table 3 and Table 4 present the comparative results of FAPM and different pruning criteria methods,
424
      with all results using a 90% sparsity ratio. These tables reveal that while Wanda can somewhat miti-
425
      gate catastrophic forgetting, it significantly impairs performance on downstream tasks. For example,
426   in the Llama3-8B model, Wanda results in an average performance decline of 3.6% across six down-
427   stream datasets when compared to Full SFT, whereas Magnitude Pruning exhibits negligible impact
428   on downstream task accuracy. Given the necessity to preserve downstream task accuracy, we have
429   opted to use Magnitude Pruning as our foundational pruning criterion. Furthermore, Wanda requires
430   a small amount of calibration data while Magnitude Pruning does not necessitate any auxiliary data.
431   This further reinforces our decision to select Magnitude Pruning as the basis for our methodology.
      More experimental results on MRPC and QASC can be found in Appendix C.

490   6   R ELATED W ORK
491
492   Catastrophic Forgetting in LLMs. Fine-tuning LLMs with additional task-specific data, a com-
493   mon practice to enhance model specialization, often leads to catastrophic forgetting of previously
494
      acquired general capabilities (Luo et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023; Wu et al.). Existing approaches
      to mitigate catastrophic forgetting can be broadly categorized into four main categories: 1) Replay-
495
      based methods (Scialom et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2024) typically integrate some pre-training data
496
      into the fine-tuning dataset for training. However, the assumption of access to a certain amount of
497   pre-training data is often unrealistic in practice. 2) Regularization-based methods (Lin et al., 2023;
498   Panigrahi et al., 2023) introduce additional penalty terms in the loss function, encouraging the fine-
499   tuned model to maintain proximity to the pre-trained model. 3) Weight-based methods (Ke et al.,
500   2023; Zhang et al., 2024) introduce parameter weight coefficients to modulate their updates, thereby
501   ensuring controlled adjustments during the optimization process. However, both regularization-
502   based and weight-based methods require to modify the optimization process, which makes the train-
503   ing process more challenging. 4) Architecture-based methods (Wang et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2021;
504   Razdaibiedina et al., 2023) involve the design of additional modules external to the original model.
505
      These methods enhance models’ specialization without altering the core architecture but their effects
      on improving downstream task accuracy are limited.
506
507
      LLM Pruning. Network pruning (LeCun et al., 1989; Han et al., 2015), which shrinks network
508
      sizes by removing specific weights, is often considered a popular approach for compressing LLMs.
509
      Magnitude Pruning (Han et al., 2015) is a standard pruning technique to induce sparsity in models.
510   It removes individual weights based on their magnitudes, where weights with magnitudes below a
511   certain threshold are removed. Recent LLM pruning methods typically involve calculating pruning
512   metrics according to model weights and activations by using some additional data. SparseGPT
513   (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023) frames pruning as an extensive sparse regression problem and solves it
514   using an approximate sparse regression solver. Wanda (Sun et al., 2023) prunes weights with the
515   smallest magnitudes multiplied by the norm of the corresponding input activations, without the need
516   for retraining or weight updates. DSnoT (Zhang et al., 2023) minimizes the reconstruction error
517   between dense and sparse models through iterative weight pruning and growing. All these methods
518
      aim to increase the sparsity of the model as much as possible and reduce the model parameters
      while maintaining model performance. Different from this, in this paper, we intend to achieve a
519
      better balance mitigating CF and improving downstream accuracy by pruning task vectors in LLM
520
      fine-tuning.

523   7   C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this study, we present a straightforward and efficient method to tackle the issue of catastrophic
526   forgetting that emerges during the continuous fine-tuning of LLMs. Inspired by the magnitude-
527
      based pruning techniques employed in LLMs, we propose a new pruning criterion, known as the
      Forgetting-Aware Pruning Metric, which effectively addresses catastrophic forgetting while pre-
528
      serving the performance of the fine-tuning tasks. Our research reveals that the extent to which task
529
      vectors overlap with the pre-trained model parameters is a key factor influencing catastrophic for-
530   getting. Based on this insight, FAPM integrates the ratio of the task vector to the pre-trained model
531   parameters as a criterion, combining it with the magnitude-based pruning metric. Our FAPM does
532   not require any additional auxiliary data, nor does it necessitate alterations to the training process or
533   model structure. It operates solely during the inference phase, thereby enhancing its versatility. We
534   hope our work serves as a baseline for future research in this area and encourages further exploration
535   into understanding CF during the inference phase of LLMs.

      B     BASELINE D ESCRIPTIONS
723
      In this Section, we describe the baseline method in our setting in detail.
724
725   L1 regularization (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017) adds an L1 penalty term to the original loss function to
726   promote sparsity in the parameter updates. The modified loss function is L (θ) + λ1 ∥θ − θpre ∥1 ,
727   with the regularization hyperparameter set to 1e-6.
728   Ke et al. (Ke et al., 2023) proposed the Vanilla Soft-masking method to address the issue of catas-
729   trophic forgetting in language models during continual fine-tuning. Specifically, this method em-
730   ploys a gradient-based detection technique to calculate the importance of units within both the
731   attention and feed-forward network (FFN) modules across all transformer layers. The obtained
732   importance weights are then used to control the backpropagation of gradients.
733   Zhang et al. (Zhang et al., 2024) proposed the CoFiTune method to tackle the issue of catastrophic
734   forgetting. CoFiTune employs a two-stage approach. At the coarse-grained level, an empirical tree-
735   search algorithm is used to identify and update specific modules that are crucial for the fine-tuning
736   task, while keeping other parameters frozen. At the fine-grained level, a soft-masking mechanism is
737   employed to adjust the updates of the large model, thereby alleviating catastrophic forgetting.
738
      Inspired by the perspective that “pre-trained models have a lower intrinsic dimension when fine-
739   tuned on specific tasks,” Hu et al. (Hu et al., 2021) proposed a fine-tuning method called LoRA.
740   During the training process of LoRA, the pre-trained parameters are kept frozen to preserve their
741   general capabilities, while all the decomposition matrices within the low-rank matrix are trainable.
