009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Background. Protein language models (pLMs) are machine learning models
012            that learn high-dimensional representations of protein sequences. These models
013            have utility in biological settings; pLMs can convert between protein sequence
014            and structure (Heinzinger et al., 2023), determine evolutionary relationships be-
015            tween organisms (Bordin et al., 2023), and design protein sequences with de-
016            sired functions (Madani et al., 2023). Transfer learning with previously trained
017            pLMs offers a powerful, minimal resource strategy for performing diverse large-
018
               scale classification and prediction tasks. However, as pLMs proliferate in the re-
               search community with differences in training objectives, model structure(s) and
019
               training datasets, it is daunting for a less-experienced end user to decide which
020
               pLM to use for biological experiments and discovery. Consequently, it is essen-
021            tial to compare pLMs to determine their strengths and limitations in use-cases
022            relevant to biological researchers. Here, we present a comparison of the per-
023            formance of pre-trained pLMs in a difficult remote homology detection task for
024            phage proteins described previously in Flamholz et al. (2024). We make avail-
025            able our code and notebooks to facilitate other research scientists to use such
026            models via anonymous Github https://anonymous.4open.science/
027            r/plm-model-comparison-7733/README.md. Results. Variations in
028            model training resulted in significantly different performance in our biological
029
               task. We present an analysis that compares five recently published pLMs : (1)
               ProtT5, (2) ProstT5, (3) TMVec, (4) ESM-2, and (5) CARP. We observed that all
030
               models were able to capture information that could be used to annotate viral pro-
031
               teins. Model embeddings could be used to train functional classifiers that, when
032            tested using the large PHROG and EFAM databases of phage proteins, captured
033            meaningful biological information. Performances across models were noticeably
034            different for this task. Models trained on larger, more diverse databases of ge-
035            nomic sequences, such as Big Fantastic Database (BFD), performed better over-
036            all. Models with Transformer architectures performed better than those with the
037            convolutional neural network (CNN) architectures. Conclusion. The utility of
038            pLMs in areas of biological research is clear; we demonstrate such models are
039            useful for remote homology detection in phage genomes, an area of active interest
040
               in environmental and clinical biology. Our study provides a framework for how
               biological scientists can choose pLMs to incorporate into their experiments and
041
               analyses. Overall, while some models clearly performed better, on the whole, all
042
               pLMs achieved high scores for prediction. For end-users, the implication is that
043            many pLM models are useful, but domain knowledge coupled with specialized
044            model training paradigms may improve results when addressing specific biologi-
045            cal questions.

048   1   BACKGROUND
049
050   Natural language processing (NLP) algorithms are algorithms that model language by converting
051   text into numerical representations that capture information about the context and meaning of words.
052   Researchers have used NLP algorithms on protein sequences to learn representations of amino acid
053   (AA) sequences that capture biologically meaningful properties (Iuchi et al., 2021). The represen-
      tations embed information related to protein structure, function, and classification. Representations


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      have also been shown to carry information about the relationship between different sequences. Pro-
055   tein language models (pLMs) are a subset of biologic NLP models that biologists can use to cat-
056   egorize protein sequences (Flamholz et al.). Protein sequence annotation is an unsolved and key
057   problem for biological discovery and application, and pLMs may enable detection of relationships
058   between proteins that are outside the capacity of current state-of-the-art approaches. pLMs are
059   trained on large datasets of AA sequences and can capture the context provided by the position
060   of AAs (CARP) (Yang et al., 2024), predict interactions between protein residues (Foldseek) (van
061   Kempen et al., 2024), generate protein sequences (ProGen) (Madani et al., 2023), or taxonomize
062   protein sequences (Genomic Language Model) (Hwang et al., 2024). Biologists have begun using
063
      these generalized models to formulate experimental hypotheses (Hie et al., 2023); however, many
      biologists still train models on small datasets for specific tasks and these models have not been
064
      widely adopted in the biological sciences.
065
066   Within the past decade, a host of different pLMs were developed. These models were trained on
067   different sequence datasets with different model architectures, and were designed to perform a mul-
068   titude of different tasks. Deciding which model is best to use in an experiment, especially for
069
      domain-specific tasks useful to individual scientists, can be daunting for a non-expert (Flamholz
      et al.). The comparison experiment described here, applying pLMs to annotating viral proteins in
070
      large, diverse, metagenomic datasets, will make these models more accessible to biologists.
071
072   Viruses are abundant, fundamental players in shaping life on Earth. Present in every environment
073   from gut microbiomes to soil samples, viruses radically alter the genomes and populations of their
074   host organisms. Bacteriophages, or phages that target bacteria specifically, have significant impacts
075
      on the microbial communities. Phages shift the dynamics between bacterial organisms, driving how
      the ecosystem functions (Kauffman et al., 2022). In ecosystems such as the gut microbiome, phage-
076
      bacterial interactions can trigger disease in the host, or can protect the organism against pathogenic
077
      bacteria (Zhang et al., 2023). This impact is in part due to the ability of phages to evolve rapidly
078   alongside their bacterial hosts (Koskella et al., 2022).
079
080   Consequently, it is crucial to develop a robust taxonomy of phages in order to best understand and
081
      predict the impact of these interactions. However, due to the lack of conserved marker genes in
      viruses, thousands of viruses discovered in viral catalog studies go unclassified (Flamholz et al.,
082
      2024). The pace of viral genome discovery is also rising with environmental metagenomic sequenc-
083
      ing (Kuhn, 2021), (Camargo et al., 2023), reinforcing the importance of innovative, accessible,
084   solutions to this problem.
085
086   Current phage annotation methods include profile-profile Hidden Markov Model (HMM) and other
087
      sequence-based homology methods. However, these methods suffer from the limited amount of an-
      notated viral protein sequences, costliness of sequence-based annotation and rapid rate of phage
088
      evolution. It is difficult to construct statistical models from poorly annotated datasets. Due to
089
      rapid evolution, annotating phages based on immediate evolutionary relationships is unfeasible. We
090   showed previously that annotation of uncultivated phage genomes is aided by pre-trained pLMs
091   (Flamholz et al., 2024) but the proliferation of pLMs in the community prompts the question of
092   whether different training regimes influence the results, and if so, how.
093
      In this comparison experiment, we present five pLMs that each have unique elements related to
094
      their training, structure and dataset. We show that each can produce protein representations that are
095
      useful for classification-based transfer learning, but that differences in training corpus and model
096   architecture affect performance on our remote homology detection task. We test the performance
097   of these models on two, large viral sequence databases, PHROGs (Terzian et al., 2021) and EFAM
098   (Zayed et al., 2021).

106   using a novel method for remote homology detection. The protein sequences were first gathered
107   using similarity searches, and then clustered into protein families using HMM profiles. 5,134
      of the protein families were then annotated as belonging to one of nine functional categories us-

      3.1   P ROT T5
157
158   ProtT5-XL-Uniref50 is an example of one of the first pLMs. It was created as an example of how
159   machine learning models can capture meaningful biological information from protein sequences
160   alone, rather than evolutionary information, which is computationally costly and not always avail-
161   able. The model was trained on BFD100 (Jumper et al., 2021), and fine-tuned on Uniref50 (Suzek
      et al., 2015). It has an Encoder-Decoder Transformer structure. ProtT5 utilizes the same training ob-

162
      jective as BERT, where single tokens were corrupted and reconstructed with masking probabilities
163   of 15%.
164
165
      3.2   ESM-2
166
167   ESM-2 was trained to learn large amounts of information and representations from protein se-
168   quences. The same model was trained on multiple scales, ranging from 8 million parameters to
169   15 billion parameters, making ESM-2 the largest model at the time of its release (Lin et al., 2023).
170   The model was trained on the Uniref50 and Uniref90 databases, and has a Transformer architecture
171   with an attention mechanism to learn pairwise interactions between amino acid sequences. ESM-2
172   has a masked language modeling (MLM) training objective, where 15% of amino acid tokens were
173
      hidden, and the model was tasked with predicting them.
174
175
      3.3   CARP 640M
176
      CARP is an example of a convolutional neural network (CNN)-based model, and was provided
177   as an efficient alternative to the prevalent Transformer-based models in the market. The model
178   was trained on sequences from Uniref50. CARP models were trained using the masked language
179   modeling objective, where 15% of tokens from each sequence were randomly selected. 80% of these
180   tokens were replaced with a mask token, 10% were replaced with a random amino acid, and 10%
181   were unchanged.
182
183   3.4   TM-V EC CATH
184
185   TM-Vec was designed to predict the TM-score, a measure of structural similarity, between two
186
      protein sequences without the intermediate computation of their structures. The model was trained
      on sequences from the CATH and SwissModel structural databases. The training objective of TM-
187
      Vec was to reduce the L1 distance between the cosine similarity of the proteins’ function-reduced
188
      representations and their TM-scores.
189
190
      3.5   P ROST T5
191
192   ProstT5 was designed to translate between protein sequences and 3Di (structural) tokens. To create
193   ProstT5, ProtT5 was fine-tuned on the AlphaFold protein structure database. The model shares
194   the same structure and training objectives as ProtT5 (Encoder-Decoder Transformer and span-based
195   denoising).

      Scientists introducing novel models such as ESM-2 already compare different scales of their model
      (Lin et al., 2023), illustrating that larger model size has a positive impact on model performance.
412
      However, experiments comparing the impact of model structure or training dataset alone have not
413
      been conducted. Instead of using model scale as the independent variable when comparing multiple
414   models, we can use model structure (training multiple models on the same set of parameters, and
415   comparing their performances) or training dataset (training multiple models with the same structure
416   on similarly sized sets of different parameters).
417
      We hypothesize that for the remote homology prediction task here, performance is influenced by
418
      structure-based objectives due to the nature of viruses. Viruses evolve rapidly, however, certain
419
      structures such as the capsid protein are evolutionarily conserved. Will structure-based objectives
420   be as useful when applying pLMs to remote homology prediction tasks in Bacterial, Archaeal, or
421   Eukaryotic proteins? Will larger and more diverse training sets enable us to cover the large swaths
422   of protein sequence space that we still cannot annotate? Answering such questions may begin to
423   uncover fundamental rules of protein function across the domains of life.
424
      When answering other biological questions such as predicting a person’s susceptibility to disease
425
      or designing protein sequences for specific tasks, different training parameters may have greater
426   impacts on model performance. We encourage end-users to use these powerful pLMs on other
427   specific biological questions, testing their applicability and expanding domain knowledge.

      6.1   E MBEDDINGS
436
      The sequence information from the PHROGs fasta files were uploaded into a Virtual Machine (VM)
437
      hosted by Google Cloud. The models were directly hosted on the VMs via Python 3 scripts. For
438   each of the embedding experiments, a VM with a single NVIDIA L4 GPU under the G2 series, 16
439   vCPU, 8 cores and 64 GB of memory (g2-standard-16) was utilized. The operating system was Deep
440   Learning with Linux, and the version was Deep Learning VM with CUDA 11.8 M123. The boot
441   disks are balanced persistent, with sizes of 100 GB each. The average runtime for each experiment
442   was two to three days, and batch sizes of 1 were used.
443
      ProstT5, ProtT5 and ESM-2 were accessed via the Hugging Face Hub. CARP and TM-Vec were
444   accessed via the sequence-models and tm-vec packages respectively. Models were run using the
445   provided Python functions from their respective Github repositories. To create the averaged em-
446   beddings, the n-dimensional embeddings from each model were grouped based on their PHROG
447   families. The arithmetic mean was taken across each column to create a single n-dimensional vector
448   for each family.

      6.2   T RAINED M ODEL P ERFORMANCES ON PHROG S
451
452
      A multi-class classifier was trained on the embedded PHROGs database for each of the five model
453   embeddings using the methods described in (Flamholz et al., 2024). The classifier architecture is a
454   dense, feed-forward neural network. The models were trained using Tensorflow with the following
455   parameters: loss=categorical crossentropy, opt=Adam(0.0001), batch size=60. The networks of the
456   models had three hidden layers each, with the input layer the size of the embedding and the hidden
457   layers size 512, 256 and 128 respectively (with the exception of TM-Vec embeddings, where the
458   model had only three layers and an input layer size matching the size of the 512-dimension embed-
459   dings). The layers were trained with 20% dropout and ReLU activation. The output layer was the
460   same size as the number of functional categories being predicted and had a softmax activation.
461   These new models were used to make predictions on labeled sequences that were left out of the train-
462   ing sets. The number of correct predictions, or true positives (TP) was measured versus the false
463   positives (FP), true negatives (TN) and false negatives (FN). Evaluations for the classifiers were mea-
464   sured per functional category using area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC),
465   area under the precision-recall curve (AUPRC), and F1 scores (calculated using the following for-
466   mula). For the PHROGs five-fold cross validation, true labels were taken from the database. ROC,
467
      PRC, AUC and F1 scores were calculated using scikit-learn https://scikit-learn.org/
      stable/index.html methods roc curve, precision recall curve and auc. The F1, precision and
468
      recall scores were compared across models via boxplot.

471   6.3   T RAINED M ODEL P ERFORMANCES ON EFAM
472
473   A multi-class classifier was trained on the embedded EFAM database for each of the five model
474   embeddings, using the same model architectures and training parameters as the PHROGs classifiers.
475   Each of the models was used to embed the EFAM database. As ground truth for making predictions,
476   the HMM profiles in the EFAM database were annotated to the ten PHROGs functional categories
477   using profile-profile HMM matching from hhsearch. The profiles were taken from Flamholz et al.
478   An EFAM family was given a PHROGs family label assignment if the family matched a PHROGs
479   HMM with an e-value ¡ 1E-10. The annotated HMM profiles were taken from https://pubmed.
480   ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37205395/.
481   The models were used to make predictions on the EFAM clusters. The number of “correct” predic-
482   tions were counted and used to calculate the precision and recall scores for each model. Evaluations
483   for the classifiers were measured per functional category using AUROC, AUPRC and F1 scores.
484   The mean predicted value is the probability of a EFAM family matching its predicted functional
485   category, with 0 indicating no probability and 1 indicating a 100% probability. The number of
      predictions were counted and plotted in a histogram by their mean predicted value.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Using the test set from each of the models, a per-class calibration analysis was performed using
487   the scikit-learn calibration curve method. EFAM VPFs with matches to annotated PHROGs HMMs
488   were used to test the performance of the newly trained models. A perfectly trained model (where
489   the mean predicted value is equivalent to the number of positives) is represented by the line in the
490   middle of the plot. Models that are overconfident trend above the line, and models that are under
491   confident trend below the line. Then, the calibrated classifier was used to predict EFAM VPFs not
492   captured by PHROG HMMs.
493   To determine the biological relevance of these models, their F1 scores were plotted against their
494   false discovery rates (FDR). The FDR threshold was determined to be 10%, following with the FDR
495   threshold from Flamholz et al. (2024).
496
      To determine whether the five models could make accurate functional predictions of biological in-
497
      terest, the integration and excision category was closely examined. This category was also selected
498
      so that the results from the model in Flamholz et al. (2024). could be compared with the results from
499   the five models. The probability of a family that was predicted as “integration and excision” was
500   plotted against the average protein length in that family.
