011                                                     A BSTRACT
012
013    1            State-of-the-art computer vision and language models largely owe their success
014    2            to the ability to represent massive prior knowledge contained in multiple datasets
015
       3            by learning over multiple tasks. However, large-scale cross-dataset studies of
       4            deep probabilistic regression models are missing, presenting a significant research
016
       5            gap. To bridge this gap, in this paper we propose, analyze, and evaluate a novel
017
       6            probabilistic regression model, capable of solving multiple regression tasks rep-
018    7            resented by different datasets. To demonstrate the feasibility of such operation
019 8               and the efficacy of our model, we define a novel multi-dataset probabilistic re-
020 9               gression benchmark LPRM-101. Our results on this benchmark imply that the
021 10              proposed model is capable of solving a probabilistic regression problem jointly
022 11              over multiple datasets. The model, which we call NIAQUE, learns a meaningful
023 12              cross-dataset representation, scores favorably against strong tree-based baselines
024 13              and Transformer and exhibits positive transfer on unseen datasets after fine-tuning.

    14     1    I NTRODUCTION
028
    15     For decades, the ML community has focused on addressing tabular predictive modeling problems
029
    16     using advanced, non-linear models. Tree-based methods such as Random Forests (Breiman, 2001),
030
      17   XGBoost (Chen and Guestrin, 2016), LightGBM (Ke et al., 2017), and CatBoost (Prokhorenkova
031 18     et al., 2019) have traditionally been the preferred approaches for solving these tasks. The first
032 19     notable shift toward deep learning in large-scale dense tabular problems occurred in domains like
033 20     e-commerce, ads, and click-through rate modeling, where deep representation learning demonstrated
034 21     clear advantages (Guo et al., 2017), and TabNet (Arik and Pfister, 2021) emerged as the first
035 22     deep model built specifically for tabular data. Recent findings based on Transformer architectures
036 23     highlight that deep learning models typically require extensive upstream pre-training data to perform
037 24     effectively (Arik and Pfister, 2021; Levin et al., 2023; Hollmann et al., 2023). Our study complements
038 25     existing results by showing that deep learning models can be trained directly on a large collection
039
    26     of diverse downstream datasets to effectively solve the multi-task learning problem. We show
    27     that meaningful dataset-level representations emerge in this setting, and when compared to tree-
040
    28     based approaches under similar conditions, deep probabilistic models clearly outperform them.
041
      29   Additionally, we show that our model pretrained on a large set of datasets exhibits positive transfer
042 30     on a set of unseen datasets after fine-tuning. These new results establish the viability of cross-dataset
043 31     multi-task learning and transfer learning, with direct implications for model architecture design in
044 32     large enterprises. Currently, the common approach involves deploying isolated, disjoint models,
045 33     each requiring substantial scientific and engineering support. Our findings indicate that unified
046 34     models capable of concurrently addressing multiple probabilistic regression tasks represent a viable
047 35     alternative. On top of this, we show the feasibility of pretraining a probabilistic regression model that
048 36     can then be fine-tuned on the target problem of interest demonstrating positive transfer compared to
049 37     the model trained on the same target dataset from scratch.
050 38     The growing recognition of the importance of probabilistic and distributional modeling in predic-
051 39     tive scenarios is evident too, particularly in fields like medical applications, such as clinical trial
052 40     analysis (Heller et al., 2022). Moreover, representation of uncertainty is a general requirement for
053 41     any problem with incomplete knowledge (Taylor et al., 1994), and predictive distributions build an
      42   understanding of uncertainty. Hence, distributional modeling is a natural choice for overcoming

054
    43   barriers to ML adoption and enhancing system trustworthiness. A model that can flag its potential
055 44   failure cases is more trustworthy than the model that is randomly and unpredictably wrong. By
056 45   quantifying output distributions, probabilistic models can alert downstream users to high-uncertainty
057 46   cases (e.g., large posterior distribution spreads), where predictions should not be trusted in critical
058 47   decisions. Another dimension of trust, interpretability, is gaining importance for predictive models in
059 48   tabular data (Sahakyan et al., 2021). In this paper, we focus on global interpretability—identifying
060 49   independent variables that are key to solving a given problem. We show that probabilistic modeling
061 50   and feature importance assessment can work in tandem: the posterior distribution of individual
062 51   features helps highlight those that strongly impact prediction accuracy.
063 52   In this work, we identify and bridge several key research gaps. First, existing multi-dataset tabular
064 53   benchmarks are predominantly focused on classification problems, lacking a comprehensive bench-
065 54   mark for large-scale probabilistic regression tasks. We introduce a new multi-dataset regression
066 55   benchmark and train multiple baseline models across all its datasets in a multi-task fashion. This
067 56   benchmark comprises 101 diverse datasets from various domains, with varying sample sizes and
068 57   feature dimensions. Second, we propose NIAQUE, a novel probabilistic regression model capa-
069
    58   ble of solving multi-task learning problem across multiple diverse datasets, effectively developing
    59   meaningful dataset-level representations. NIAQUE compares favorably against strong tree-based
070
    60   baselines and Transformers, despite being trained solely on a collection of downstream regression
071
    61   tasks. Moreover, it demonstrates positive transfer when pretrained on a large collection of regression
072 62   datasets and later fine-tuned on unseen new datasets. Our contributions can be summarized as follows.
073
074 63      • We define a new probabilistic regression benchmark based on 101 diverse regression datasets
075 64        publicly available from UCI, PMLB, OpenML and Kaggle repositories
076
    65      • We introduce NIAQUE, a novel model designed to address probabilistic regression by learning
077
    66        to approximate the inverse of the posterior distribution during training.
078
079 67      • Our theoretical analysis provides strong methodological foundation for NIAQUE.
080 68      • We demonstrate that NIAQUE achieves superior accuracy compared to strong baselines
081 69      • We propose feature weights derived from NIAQUE’s marginal posterior distributions that
082 70        enhance interpretability by taking advantage of the model’s probabilistic nature.

217
218 156   NIAQUE, shown in Fig. 1, follows the encoder-decoder pattern. Encoder deals with N independent
219 157   variables, where N is variable. At inference time, for i-th observation sample, xi , with variable
220 158   dimensionality Ni it accepts a tensor of values of dimensionality 1 × Ni and a tensor of feature codes
221 159   of dimensionality 1 × Ni , transforms, embeds and concatenates them into tensor of size 1 × Ni × Ein .
222 160   The encoder then collapses the independent variable dimension using prototype approach, resulting
223 161   in output embedding of size 1 × E. Decoder modulates the quantile agnostic representation received
224
    162   from encoder with the vector of quantiles q ∈ RQ , again, of arbitrary dimensionality Q. This design
    163   is compute efficient with complexity O(Ni + Q) for a given xi , whereas processing quantiles and
225
    164   observations in encoder and decoder would imply complexity O(Ni Q).
226
227
    165   Inputs. For each element in the observation vector x, NIAQUE receives its value along with an
228 166   integer representing the independent variable ID, wrapped with a learnable embedding. The variable
229 167   ID is crucial for capturing the distinct statistical properties of each variable, the interactions between
230 168   independent variables, and their statistical relationship with the dependent variable. The embedded
231 169   variable ID is concatenated with its value, transformed into the log domain:

302
303 210     NIAQUE handles variable input combinations through the use of semantically encoded variables.
304 211     Thus, the model can be trained across multiple heterogeneous datasets by presenting to the model,
305 212     for each dataset, only the relevant variables. The combination of the variables informs the model of
306
    213     the dataset and task required for inference via their learnable semantic embeddings. When pretrained
      214   across multiple datasets, the model is expected to generalize effectively to each dataset. Additionally,
307
    215     the pretrained model is expected to generalize to new unseen datasets with appropriate additional
308
    216     fine-tuning. Our experimental results provide empirical validation to both these hypotheses.

455 310     Interpretability Analysis. Figure 3b depicts the empirical analysis of the feature importance
456 311     assessment mechanism proposed in Section 2.3. The procedure boils down to computing the
457 312     normalized inverse average confidence interval on the samples from the marginal distribution of
458 313     each feature drawn from the validation set. Then features are ordered by the importance weight, per
459 314     dataset. In Figure 3b, top-1 refers to the feature with highest weight, bot-1 refers to the feature with
460 315     lowest weight. Top-rated (most important) features contribute the most to the AAD metric decrease,
461 316     when removed. Unimportant features have much smaller effect on AAD. This shows the efficacy
462
    317     of the proposed feature importance assessment in that it produces scores predictive of the effect of
      318   features on accuracy. Note that this mechanism is tightly linked to the probabilistic nature of the
463
    319     model, it can be executed on a pre-trained model and it does not require ground truth labels.

466 321     pendix I, demonstrating the following important observations. First, applying the log-transform
467 322     to input values, as shown in eq. (8), enhances both training stability and prediction accuracy. Sec-
468
    323     ond, NIAQUE’s performance shows relatively low sensitivity to network width variations, but is
      324   more dependent on the number of blocks. Third, the training approach incorporating single-feature
469
    325     rows, which supports the interpretability mechanism discussed in Section 2.3, proves crucial. When
470
    326     single-feature rows are excluded from the training mix (Appendix I, Figure 7c), the model poorly dis-
471 327     tinguishes between high-importance and low-importance features. However, including single-feature
472 328     rows to NIAQUE’s training mix, creates a clear accuracy gap between the cases of top-importance
473 329     feature removal and the bottom-importance feature removal. Importantly, this training procedure
474 330     adjustment does not negatively impact prediction accuracy.

    332     We believe that our results applying NIAQUE to the multi-dataset benchmark LPRM-101 lay out the
479
      333   stepping stone for the development of probabilistic meta-models eventually possessing the following
480 334     key properties. Scalability: A unified model shares computational resources to address multiple
481 335     regression tasks, optimizing resource utilization and reducing the operational costs of maintaining
482 336     separate models. Data Efficiency: Training on diverse tasks introduces strong regularization effects,
483 337     and we expect existing datasets to be repurposed to solve emerging problems, promoting data reuse
484 338     and recycling. Representation and Generalization: A model trained across multiple datasets
485 339     uncovers generalizable representations of regression tasks and ways of solving them, acquiring the
      340   ability to apply this knowledge across datasets.

    341     Limitations. While we significantly expand the scope of cross-dataset probabilistic model training by
    342     applying our neural model to a 101-dataset benchmark, this remains a limited effort. It is still unclear
510
      343   how many datasets are required for a regression model to be considered foundational for solving, for
511
    344     instance, 80% of industry problems. What level of dataset diversity is necessary? Will millions or
512 345     billions of unrelated datasets be required, or would 10,000 overlapping datasets suffice? Defining and
513 346     evaluating global success in this context remains an open question, necessitating further research.
514
515
    347     Broader Impacts. Our findings have implications for designing machine learning deployments based
    348     on unified models that address multiple regression tasks. We expect that this will eventually lead to
516
      349   improved operational efficiency and accuracy of the models. However, this could also contribute
517
    350     to the centralization of power among a few large entities. In this context, risk mitigation strategies
518 351     include (i) improving model computational efficiency and (ii) publicly releasing data, model training
519 352     code and pretrained models. Additionally, multi-task learning on multiple datasets may introduce
520 353     new biases not present in locally trained models, making interpretability and fairness research critical.
521 354     We explore some interpretability aspects in this paper, and further research on interpretability and
522 355     fairness in large probabilistic regression models pretrained across multiple datasets seems to be an
523 356     important area for future work.

      358   In this paper we introduce NIAQUE, a novel probabilistic regression model, and LPRM-101, a novel
528
    359     multi-dataset large regression model benchmark. We show that learning a probabilistic regression
529 360     model across datasets is viable and that there exists a strong neural baseline model that compares
530 361     favorably against usual suspects in the domain of tabular learning: boosted trees and Transformer.
531 362     We also show that the probabilistic nature of the proposed model opens up a way for achieving
532 363     global model interpretability via feature importance defined through the average marginal posterior
533 364     confidence interval. Future work will focus on finding more effective ways of representing variable
534 365     relationships across datasets, increasing the volume of datasets and applying developed techniques to
535 366     wide array of application domains, such as multi-variate cross-dataset time series forecasting.

770 489     LPRM-101 is the multi-dataset benchmark for large probabilistic regression models (hence, LPRM)
771 490     consisting of 101 dataset (hence LPRM-101). The datasets, along with their sample count, number of
772
    491     variables and source information are listed in Table 3. To construct the benchmark, we first collect
    492     101 dataset publicly available from the following primary repositories: UCI (Kelly et al., 2017),
773
    493     Kaggle (Kaggle, 2024), PMLB (Romano et al., 2021; Olson et al., 2017), OpenML (Vanschoren
774
      494   et al., 2013), KEEL (Alcalá-Fdez et al., 2011). We focus specifically on the regression task in which
775 495     the dependent variable is continuous or, if it has limited number of levels, these are ordered such
776 496     as student exam scores or wine quality. The target variable in each dataset is normalized to the [0,
777 497     10] range and the independent variables are used as is, raw. The target variable scaling is applied
778 498     to equalize the contributions of the evaluation metrics from each dataset. Datasets have variable
779 499     number of samples, the lowest being just below 1000. For very large datasets we limit the number of
780 500     samples used in our benchmark to be 20,000 by subsampling uniformly at random. This allows us (i)
781 501     to model imbalance, and at the same time (ii) avoid the situation in which a few large datasets could
782 502     completely dominate the training and evaluation of the model. The distribution of datasets by source,
783
    503     number of samples and number of variables is shown in Figure 4.

          To train both NIAQUE and Transformer models we use       √ feature dropout defined as follows. Given
    547   dropout probability dp, we toss a coin with probability dp to determine if the dropout event is going
1429
    548   to happen at√all for a given batch. If this happens, we remove each feature from the batch, again with
1430
    549   probability dp. This√way each feature has probability dp of being removed from a given batch and
1431550   there is a probability dp that the model will see all features intact in a given batch. The intuition
1432551   behind this design is that we want to expose the model to all features most of the time, but we also
1433552   want to create many situations with some feature combinations missing.
1434
    553   Architecture and training ablations are reported in Table 13 shown that increasing the number of
1435
    554   blocks and width improves accuracy until saturation happens at 4 blocks and width 1024.

1437555   Input log transformation defined in eq. (8) is important to ensure the success of the training, as
1438556   follows both from Table 13 and Figure 6. The introduction of log-transform makes learning curves
1439557   well-behaved and smooth and translates into much better accuracy.
1440558   Adding samples containing only one of the features as input does not significantly affect accuracy.
1441559   At the same time, the addition of single-feature training rows has very strong effect on the effectiveness
1442560   of NIAQUE’s interpretability mechanism. When rows with single feature input are added (Figures 7a
1443561   and 7b), NIAQUE demonstrates very clear accuracy degradation when top features are removed and
1444562   insignificant degradation when bottom features are removed. When rows with single feature input are
1445563   not added (Figure 7c), the discrimination between strong and weak features is poor, with removal of
