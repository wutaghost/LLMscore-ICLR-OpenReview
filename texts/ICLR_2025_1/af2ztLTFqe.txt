009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Effectively mitigating the misalignment of large language models (LLMs) is cru-
012            cial for ensuring secure AI applications. Inference-Time Intervention (ITI) tech-
013            nique, which applies interventions to internal representations along the probed
014            alignment direction during inference, offers substantial alignment enhancements
015            with minimal cost. However, previous ITI methods adopt coarse sentence-level
016            analysis which neglects the misalignment discrepancy among varied tokens, re-
017            sulting in deviant alignment direction and inflexible intervention strength. In this
018
               work, we propose a Token-Aware Inference-Time Intervention (TA-ITI) approach
               to fully utilize token-level alignment information, therefore realizing superior
019
               post-intervention performance. TA-ITI primarily consists of Mutual Information-
020
               guided Token-level Graph Aggregation (MIG) and Misalignment-aware Adap-
021            tive Token-level Intervention (MAI). MIG develops a MI-guided graph to exploit
022            the tokens’ informative interaction for representation enrichment, thus improv-
023            ing alignment probing and facilitating subsequent intervention. MAI comprehen-
024            sively perceives the token-level misalignment degree from token representation
025            and prediction to guide the adaptive adjustment of intervention strength, thereby
026            enhancing final alignment performance. Extensive experiments on three align-
027            ment capabilities demonstrate the efficacy of TA-ITI, notably surpassing baseline
028            by 25.8% on the primary metric of truthfulness.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      With the rapid advancement of artificial intelligence, large language models (LLMs) (Touvron
      et al., 2023a; Brown et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2023) have profoundly changed daily production and
034
      lifestyle. However, LLMs continue to exhibit misalignment issues and generate erroneous (Li et al.,
035
      2024a), harmful (Li et al., 2024b), and other content that deviate from human expectations (Shen
036   et al., 2023). For example, unaligned LLMs may abet depressed people to commit suicide, causing
037   serious hazards. This misalignment significantly impedes the safe and reliable applications of LLMs
038   in real-world scenarios. Although training-based alignment techniques like supervised fine-tuning
039   (SFT) (Wang et al., 2022; Taori et al., 2023) and reinforcement learning from human feedback
040   (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2023) have gained considerable success, their practical
041   applications are constrained by excessive costs and unstable effects (Casper et al., 2023).
042   Benefiting from minimal resource consumption and notable alignment effectiveness, Inference-Time
043   Intervention (ITI) methods (Li et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024) based on representation editing have
044   gained significant attention. They first identify the alignment directions by training interpretable
045   probes on aligned and misaligned samples, and then intervene in the model’s internal representa-
046   tions along with the directions during inference. This reduces the need for extensive training data
047   and computational resources, lowering alignment costs and avoiding introducing new risks, such as
048   sycophancy (Perez et al., 2022). Consequently, ITI can effectively mitigate erroneous (Li et al.,
049   2024a) and harmful (Li et al., 2024b) contents, thereby holding substantial application value.
050   However, previous ITI methods (Li et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024) simply adopt coarse sentence-
051   level analysis, and neglect the fine-grained alignment contributions of individual tokens, leading to
052   deviant alignment direction and inflexible intervention strength. As shown in Figure 1, this coarse
053   sentence-level analysis brings negative impacts on both probe training and representation interven-
      tion stages: (1) Sentence-level probe training simply utilizes the last token as a surrogate for the

078   In this paper, we propose Token-Aware Inference-Time Intervention (TA-ITI), which comprises
079   MI-guided Token-level Graph Aggregation (MIG) and Misalignment-aware Adaptive Token-level
080   Intervention (MAI), to fully perceive and utilize multiple tokens for alignment improvement. Aim-

083   resentations, thereby refining the probed direction and facilitating subsequent alignment interven-
084

      significantly boosting the training representations when integrated with graph. To achieve adaptive
085
      intervention across distinct tokens, MAI adjusts the editing strength with the guidance of token-level
086
      misalignment awareness. Grounded in the motivation that both token representation and prediction
087   are closely associated with misalignment, MAI assesses the token representation using an estimator
088   trained on token-level misalignment dataset, and quantifies the prediction uncertainty with entropy,
089   thus accurately perceiving token misalignment to provide dependable guidance for intervention.
090
      To evaluate the effectiveness of TA-ITI, we conduct comprehensive experiments of three typical
091
      alignment capabilities, including truthfulness, harmlessness, and fairness. On the classic truthful-
092
      ness evaluation dataset TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022), our method especially achieves a remarkable
093   87.8% of primary True*Info metric, exceeding the sentence-level ITI by 18.8%. TA-ITI also signif-
094   icantly decreases the toxicity of baseline-generated content on RealToxicityPrompt (Gehman et al.,
095   2020), particularly achieving nearly 90% reduction on metric TP. Our performance on both fairness
096   evaluation metrics of StereoSet (Nadeem et al., 2021) also surpasses all debiasing methods. Exten-
097   sive experiments confirm the substantial improvement of TA-ITI in post-intervention alignment.

      2.1   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL A LIGNMENT
102
103   LLM alignment, which ensures that the behaviors or outputs of LLM systems are aligned with hu-
104   man expectations (Ji et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023), is crucial for guaranteeing the safe application
105   of LLM. The alignment necessitates that LLMs acquire various human-preferred capabilities, such
106   as truthfulness, harmlessness, fairness (Shen et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Ji et al.,
107   2023), etc. To meet these intricate alignment requirements, researches have investigated a range of
      approaches to establish a multifaceted technical alignment framework. Prominent methodologies


                                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      include Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) (Wang et al., 2022; Taori et al., 2023) and Reinforcement
109   Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2023). They acquire
110   alignment knowledge by training on the crafted datasets containing human expectations, demon-
111   strating considerable success. However, these methods are constrained by significant limitations
112   like high implementation costs and instability in performance (Casper et al., 2023).
113
114   2.2   I NFERENCE -T IME I NTERVENTION
115
116   Inference-Time Intervention (ITI) is an efficient alignment technique, which posits that the inter-
117   pretable internal structures related to model alignment can be utilized to reduce misaligned content.
118
      Li et al. (Li et al., 2024a) first proposed the ITI technique and demonstrated its capability to enhance
      the truthfulness of responses. Building on ITI, Chen et al. (Chen et al., 2024) utilized multiple or-
119
      thogonal probes to model truthful directions across various dimensions, and introduced a diversified
120
      sampling method to capture a broader range of features. Additionally, Li et al. proposed DESTEIN
121   (Li et al., 2024b) to use representation intervention for toxicity mitigation. TruthX (Zhang et al.,
122   2024) utilized auto-encoders to decouple internal representations into semantic and truthful spaces
123   and intervened in the truthful space to enhance truthfulness while maintaining semantic integrity.
124   However, these methods roughly adopt sentence-level analysis, neglecting the valuable information
125   and alignment degrees of individual tokens, leading to suboptimal intervention results.
126
127
      3     M ETHODOLOGY
128
129   Sentence-level ITI ignores the significance of individual tokens to alignment analysis, leading to
130   unsatisfactory intervention. To fully analyze and utilize all tokens for realizing optimal interven-
131   tion, we explore the implementation of ITI from a token-level perspective and introduce Token-
132   Aware Inference-Time Intervention (TA-ITI). Concretely, as shown in Figure 2, we devise tailored
133   token-level modules for both stages, i.e. MI-guided Token-level Graph Aggregation (MIG) and
134   Misalignment-aware Adaptive Token-level Intervention (MAI). In this section, we first review the
135   fundamental process of ITI during LLM inference in Section 3.1. We then elaborate on the proposed
136   MIG in Section 3.2 and MAI in Section 3.3, with detailed demonstration of their contributions.
137

      3.2     MI- GUIDED T OKEN - LEVEL G RAPH AGGREGATION
186   To mitigate the directional deviation in sentence-level probes, we aim to leverage all informative
187   tokens within a sentence to probe a universal alignment direction. However, directly averaging
188   inadvertently neglects the impacts of token interactions on the whole sentence, potentially misguid-
189   ing alignment probing. Therefore, we propose MI-guided Token-level Graph Aggregation (MIG)
190   to model and utilize the critical interactions among tokens, finally enhancing the discrimination of
191   alignment in training representations. It innovatively applies mutual information (MI) (Steuer et al.,
192   2002; Gabrié et al., 2018) for a global probabilistic analysis of information sharing among tokens
193   to promote the perception of their contributions, and subsequently boost the useful information in
194
      training representations by aggregating the augmented features after graph propagation.
195
196

225   3.3   M ISALIGNMENT- AWARE A DAPTIVE T OKEN - LEVEL I NTERVENTION
226
227   To address the imprecise edition of sentence-level intervention, we propose Misalignment-aware
228   Adaptive Token-level Intervention (MAI), which adjusts the intensity based on misalignment aware-
229   ness to achieve token-level adaptive intervention. Studies have revealed that LLM misalignment is
230   typically manifested in token representation (Ji et al., 2024) and prediction probabilities (Varshney
231
      et al., 2023). Therefore, MAI perceives token misalignment by combining representation misalign-
      ment estimation using an estimator trained on a token-level misalignment dataset, and prediction
232
      uncertainty quantification leveraging the predictive capability of LLM. They mutually compensate
233
      for ignored misalignment aspects, thereby providing reliable guidance for intervention.
234
235

236   sentation, we construct a token-level misalignment dataset assuming that specific misaligned tokens
237   contribute to the misalignment of sample s− (e.g. ‘can’ leads to the misalignment of ‘Pigs can fly’).
238   Consequently, we should label the preceding tokens (e.g. ‘Pigs’ preceding ‘can’) prone to generate
239   misaligned predictions as 1, while other unrelated tokens as 0. Given a selected pair of aligned
240   and misaligned samples (s+ , s− ), we specifically regard the different tokens between s+ and s− as
241   misaligned tokens. To clarify, we annotate the misalignment label yj of token xj in s− as follows:

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
298
      Benchmarks and Metrics We assess the truthfulness of LLMs using widely adopted TruthfulQA
299   (Lin et al., 2022), which encompasses open-ended generation task and multiple-choice task. The
300   open-ended generation task is measured through the primary metric True*Info rate. Three types of
301   multiple-choice accuracy metrics, specifically MC1, MC2, and MC3, are used for multiple-choice
302   task evaluation. For harmlessness, we follow (Li et al., 2024b) and use the RealToxicityPrompts
303   (Gehman et al., 2020) dataset to examine whether the LLMs can generate harmless content under
304   toxic prompts. We utilize the toxicity score from the Perspective API, which encompasses both
305   Expected Maximum Toxicity (EMT) and Toxicity Probability (TP), to measure the capability for
306   harmless alignment. To evaluate the alignment of fairness, we perform stereotype recognition task
307
      (Sun et al., 2024) based on benchmark StereoSet (Nadeem et al., 2021) with metrics Stereotype
      Score and Recognition Accuracy. More details can refer to Appendix C.
308
309
      Baseline and Comparative Methods We choose multiple open-source LLMs, including LLaMA
310
      (Touvron et al., 2023a), LLaMA-2 (Touvron et al., 2023b), LLaMA-3 (Meta, 2024), Alpaca (Taori
311
      et al., 2023), Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023), Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023), and Baichuan (Yang et al.,
312   2023) as our baselines. We focus on recently released LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct for our experiments.
313
314   We first compare the proposed TA-ITI with various ITI methods, including ITI (Li et al., 2024a),
315
      TrFr (Chen et al., 2024), TruthX (Zhang et al., 2024), LITO (Bayat et al., 2024), and DESTEIN
      (Li et al., 2024b). We also consider other effective methods that enhance various alignment ca-
316
      pabilities for comparison. Other comparative methods of truthfulness involve Supervised Fine-
317
      Tuning (SFT) performed by (Li et al., 2024a), Few-shot Prompting (FSP) achieved by (Bai et al.,
318   2022), and decoding-based methods including DoLa (Chuang et al., 2023) and SH2 (Kai et al.,
319   2024). Aimed at comparing the alignment effectiveness of harmlessness, additional selections in-
320   clude finetuning-based DAPT (Liu et al., 2021), prompt-based Self-Debiasing (SD) (Schick et al.,
321   2021), and decoding-based DEXPERTS (Liu et al., 2021). Regarding fairness, we further con-
322   sider widely recognized debiasing methods, including finetuning-based Counterfactual Data Aug-
323   mentation (CDA) (Dinan et al., 2020), prompt-based Debias-Prompt (DP) (Hida et al., 2024) and
      Self-Debiasing (SD) (Schick et al., 2021).

512   cates that mt and ut indeed compensate for each other’s neglected misalignment factors, resulting in

514   estimation relative to auxiliary uncertainty quantification emphasizes its essential role.

532
      In this paper, we delve into the significant fine-grained contribution of individual tokens to the
533
      overall alignment assessment and enhancement, and propose a Token-Aware Inference-Time In-
534   tervention approach. TA-ITI improves the alignment probes’ accuracy and universality by MIG,
535   and adaptively applies appropriate intervention to diverse tokens through MAI, therefore effectively
536   rectifying the misaligned generation. Extensive experiments on typical alignment capabilities of
537   truthfulness, harmlessness, and fairness have confirmed that the proposed TA-ITI achieves superior
538   alignment performance. Practical case studies also verify that TA-ITI can intervene in originally
539   misaligned outputs to achieve alignment. In the future, we will explore more efficient token-level
      ITI techniques and extend them to multimodal large language models.

      C.1       T RUTHFULNESS
925
926
      Benchmark Evaluation TruthfulQA is a benchmark specifically developed to challenge mod-
927   els to generate truthful responses. It consists of 817 questions, each paired with one best answer,
928   multiple correct answers, and several incorrect ones. The TruthfulQA benchmark includes both
929   open-ended generation and multiple-choice tasks.
930
      Open-ended generation tasks require the model to generate responses to questions directly using
931
      greedy decoding. Previous studies (Li et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024) evaluated
932
      the truthfulness and informativeness of responses using two fine-tuned GPT-3 models, ”GPT-judge”
933   and ”GPT-info,” based on OpenAI’s Curie engine, which performed binary classification on these
934   two criteria. However, as of February 8, 2024, OpenAI has discontinued the Curie engine, making
935   it unavailable for TruthfulQA evaluation. To address this, we utilize the LLaMA-2-7B model fine-
936   tuned by Allen AI (AI, 2024), which has comparable parameters and has been validated to achieve
937   similar performance to the original GPT-3 model 2 , enhancing the accessibility and reproducibility
938   of evaluations. Consistent with (Li et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024), we employ
939   the True (%), Info (%), and True*Info (%) metrics for open-ended generation tasks. True refers to
940   the percentage of truthful responses, Info to the percentage of responses providing useful informa-
941
      tion, and True*Info represents their product, serving as a comprehensive metric for evaluating both
      truthfulness and informativeness.
942
943   The multiple-choice task requires the model to select an answer from a set of correct and incorrect
944   options by comparing the conditional probabilities of the candidate answers given the question. It is
945   evaluated using multiple-choice accuracy (MC), which includes MC1 (%), MC2 (%), and MC3 (%)
946   metrics. MC1 measures the proportion of instances where the model assigns the highest probability
947
      to the best answer. MC2 represents the proportion of instances where the normalized probability
      mass for all correct answers exceeds that of incorrect answers. MC3 reflects the proportion of cases
948
      where all correct answers rank higher than all incorrect ones, where the probability of all correct
949
      answers precedes that of incorrect options.
950
951
952   Implementation Details Following (Li et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024), we
953   employ a 2-fold validation on the TruthfulQA benchmark. Specifically, half of the questions (408
954   samples) are allocated for training and validation of TA-ITI, while the remaining half is used for
955   testing. The training and validation sets are randomly split in a 3:1 ratio.
956   The constructed probe training samples from TruthfulQA by previous works (Li et al., 2024a; Chen
957   et al., 2024) typically consist of a question Q and an answer A. In our MIG implementation, MIG
958   specifically targets all tokens contained in the model-generated answer A for mutual information
959   propagation and aggregation. Consistent with (Li et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024), we consider
960   two choices of editing direction in MIG: the vector orthogonal to the separating hyperplane learned
961   by the probe (Probe Weight Direction) and the vector connecting the means of the alignment and
962   misalignment distributions (Mass Mean Shift). The extraction of misaligned and aligned tokens in
963
      the token-level misalignment dataset is both simple and rational. We use all samples from the probe
      training dataset as the data source. For a given untruthful sample containing a question Q and an
964
      untruthful answer A− , we first identify the most adversarial correct answer from all correct answers
965
      {A+ } to the same question Q. This adversarial correct answer has the fewest different tokens with
966   A− , indicating that these tokens are critical to why the answer is untruthful. Consequently, these
967   tokens are considered misaligned, while the rest are deemed aligned. In adaptive interventions via
968   MAI, we intervene only on the last token in each generation round during open-ended generation
969   task, and on every token within the given answers in multiple-choice tasks.

       C.2      H ARMLESSNESS
973
974    Benchmark Evaluation In the harmlessness experiment, we leverage the RealToxicityPrompts
975    dataset (Gehman et al., 2020) as the primary benchmark to assess the harmfulness levels of model-
976    generated content. It consists of carefully curated prompts designed to evaluate the potential toxicity
977    of generated text. For a comprehensive analysis, we randomly sampled 2100 prompts from the
978    dataset, ensuring the representativeness of the evaluation results.
979    The evaluation metrics assess both toxicity and fluency. Toxicity is measured using the Perspec-
980    tive API 3 , which provides two key indicators: Expected Maximum Toxicity (EMT), representing the
981    highest predicted toxicity across generated continuations, and Toxicity Probability (TP), indicating
982    the likelihood of generating toxic content. Fluency is evaluated by calculating the perplexity (PPL)
983    of the generated text, using a slightly larger model from the same family, where lower perplexity
984    scores correspond to more coherent and fluent outputs. Together, these metrics offer a comprehen-
985    sive assessment of the model’s performance.
986
987    Implementation Details We employed a training process similar to a 2-fold approach on the Re-
988
       alToxicityPrompts dataset. Specifically, we randomly sampled 2,000 instances during training, with
       1,000 being non-toxic and 1,000 toxic. The randomly selected 2,000 samples were shuffled, and half
989
       of them were used in each fold for TA-ITI training and validation, while testing was conducted on
990
       an additional 100 randomly sampled instances. The training and validation sets were split randomly
991    in a 3:1 ratio. To ensure a fair comparison, our experimental setup closely follows the practices es-
992    tablished by (Liu et al., 2021). Specifically, we employ the widely used nucleus sampling technique
993    to generate continuations. For each prompt, 25 continuations are generated, allowing for a thorough
994    examination of the model’s behavior under different sampling conditions. This approach effectively
995    captures the diversity of the model’s outputs while facilitating an in-depth analysis of the toxicity
996    levels. Within the MIG module, mutual information propagation and aggregation were applied to
997    all tokens contained in the model-generated continuations. We utilized all samples from the probing
998    training dataset as the token-level misalignment dataset. For a given toxic sample, which includes a
999
       prompt and a continuation exhibiting toxicity, we identified the most adversarial non-toxic prompt
       from all non-toxic samples. This adversarial non-toxic sample had the fewest differing tokens from
1000
       the given toxic prompt, indicating that these tokens strongly influenced the toxicity of the model’s re-
1001
       sponse. Thus, these tokens were considered misaligned, while others were deemed aligned. During
1002   adaptive intervention via MAI, we intervened on each token in the model-generated prompt.

1005
1006   Benchmark Evaluation In this study, we focus on evaluating the fairness of models using the
1007   StereoSet dataset (Nadeem et al., 2021), which is a widely recognized benchmark for assessing
1008   stereotypes in natural language processing models. The StereoSet dataset consists of sentence
1009   pairs designed to measure biases across various domains such as gender, race, religion, and pro-
1010   fession. Each pair includes one sentence reflecting a stereotype and another that contradicts it (anti-
1011
       stereotype), allowing for a comprehensive evaluation of model biases.
1012   Our primary evaluation metrics include the Stereotype Score, which represents the proportion of
1013   stereotypical responses generated by the model, and the accuracy (ACC) of the model in correctly
1014   identifying whether a sentence contains a stereotype. The Stereotype Score is crucial for under-
1015   standing the model’s predisposition toward stereotypical outputs. An ideal Stereotype Score is near
1016   50%, signifying that the model’s responses do not systematically favor either stereotypical or anti-
1017
       stereotypical answers, thus indicating minimal bias. The accuracy metric (ACC) is used to determine
       how well the model can distinguish between stereotypical and non-stereotypical content, which re-
1018
       flects its understanding and response to potentially biased contexts. A higher accuracy indicates a
1019
       model’s ability to correctly identify and handle biased inputs.

1022   preprocessing and sampling, the StereoSet dataset yielded 654 samples, each of which contains a
1023   prompt, type, label, sentence, and context, where the sentence may exhibit stereotypes. For all
1024   samples, half (327 samples) were used for the training and validation of TA-ITI, while the remaining

       D    M ORE D ETAILS OF ITI BACKGROUND
1041
1042   Inference-time intervention techniques posit that the interpretable internal structures related to model
1043   alignment requirements can be utilized to reduce misaligned content. Li et al. (Li et al., 2024a) first
1044   argued that language models contain interpretable structure related to real-world correctness that
1045   may potentially be useful in reducing incorrect answers, which is induced from many evidence that
1046
       LLMs sometimes ”know” more than they ”say” (Kadavath et al., 2022). Therefore, Li et al. (Li et al.,
       2024a) intervenes in the model’s internal representations generated by the interpretable structure
1047
       (typically the multi-head self-attention) to guide the model toward truthful directions, which are
1048
       indicated by trained probes. Furthermore, Li et al. (Li et al., 2024b) pointed out that the hypothesis,
1049   where the conceptual direction can be infered based on converse linear representations, holds for
1050   the majority of concepts in language models (Park et al., 2024). Thus, inference-time intervention
1051   techniques can be adopted to infer and intervene the alignment direction(e.g. toxicity-nontoxicity)
1052   within the model’s activation space, therefore improving the alignment performance.
