009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Machine unlearning aims to selectively remove specific knowledge from a model.
012            Current methods, such as task arithmetic, rely on fine-tuning models on the forget
013            set, generating a task vector, and subtracting it from the original model. However,
014            we argue the effectiveness of this approach is highly sensitive to hyperparameter
015            selection, necessitating careful validation to identify the best model among many
016            fine-tuned candidates. In this paper, we propose a novel method that leverages all
017            given fine-tuned models rather than selecting a single one. By constructing task
018            vectors from models trained with varied hyperparameters and merging only the
019
               components of the task vectors with consistent signs, we perform unlearning by
               negating the merged task vector from the original model. Given that existing meth-
020
               ods also utilize multiple fine-tuned models, our approach delivers more effective
021
               unlearning without incurring additional computational costs. We demonstrate the
022            effectiveness of our method on both vision-language models and standard image
023            classification models, showing improved unlearning performance with minimal
024            degradation on the retain set, outperforming state-of-the-art techniques.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Recent advances in pre-training (Devlin, 2018; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Radford et al., 2021; Oquab
030
      et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024) have achieved remarkable performance, primar-
031   ily driven by the use of large-scale datasets. However, the datasets often include underfiltered, un-
032   wanted, or sensitive private information, which raises critical concerns about privacy protection. The
033   Right to be Forgotten regulation (Hoofnagle et al., 2019) allows individuals to request the deletion
034   of their personal data. However, applying this concept to machine learning models is challenging
035   because the training process deeply embeds the data into the model’s parameters, making it difficult
036   to remove its influence. The most straightforward solution is to remove the data from the training set
037   and retrain the model from scratch, which requires enormous computational resources. As a result,
038   ensuring that models forget learned patterns becomes a challenging task. Machine unlearning (War-
039
      necke et al., 2021; Golatkar et al., 2020; Thudi et al., 2022; Koh & Liang, 2017; Jia et al., 2023;
      Chen et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2023) offers a solution by enabling models to erase specific knowledge
040
      without the need for full retraining.
041
042   Despite promising results, many existing methods struggle to remove only the target knowledge
043   while preserving the rest. This challenge arises because fine-tuning often disrupts knowledge in
044   the retain set (i.e., remaining data) during attempts to erase knowledge from the forget set (i.e.,
045
      data to be forgotten) (Chen et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2023). A known method robust to this issue
      is task arithmetic (Ilharco et al., 2022a), where direct fine-tuning of the model is avoided. In-
046
      stead, this method calculates a task vector – the parameter-wise difference between the original
047
      model and a model fine-tuned on the forget set. The task vector is then subtracted from the orig-
048   inal model through a negation operation. This process, referred to as forgetting by negation, has
049   demonstrated strong unlearning performance while preserving the model’s knowledge, similar to
050   continual learning researches (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Aljundi et al., 2018) addressing catastrophic
051   forgetting (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017). However, we argue that task arithmetic has limitations; not all
052   fine-tuned models are suitable for task vectors, and thus, unlearning performance is highly sensitive
053   to hyperparameter setups used for fine-tuning. As a result, searching for an optimal hyperparameter
      set for effective unlearning can be both time-consuming and computationally costly.

054
      To address these limitations, we propose a novel method, NegMerge, that improves the process of
055   forgetting by negation. We argue that relying on a single optimal model, as current methods (Ilharco
056   et al., 2022a; Ortiz-Jimenez et al., 2024) do, is not truly optimal. Hyperparameter tuning generates
057   multiple fine-tuned models, and instead of selecting just one, we suggest leveraging all of them.
058   Specifically, we compute the final task vector by merging multiple task vectors derived from the
059   fine-tuned models. This approach draws inspiration from model merging techniques (Wortsman
060   et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023; Jang et al., 2024), which similarly utilize multiple fine-tuned models
061   to enhance performance. By extending this concept to machine unlearning, we provide a more
062   effective solution. Specifically, unlike these existing techniques, we only combine elements with
063
      consistent signs across the task vectors while masking elements with inconsistent signs to zero.
064   We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach in two experimental settings. The first involves
065   unlearning specific knowledge from a vision-language model like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021). The
066   second focuses on unlearning knowledge from specific data points in a general image classification
067   network (Chen et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2023). We validate our method using the ViT (Dosovit-
068   skiy et al., 2021) and ResNet (He et al., 2016) architectures across nine datasets. In both settings,
069
      our approach achieves new state-of-the-art performance while using similar or fewer computational
      resources than existing methods.

072   2   R ELATED WORK
073
074   Machine Unlearning. Recent machine unlearning methods can be categorized into two main
075   groups; unlearning specific knowledge in vision-language pre-trained models (Ilharco et al., 2022a;
076   Ortiz-Jimenez et al., 2024) and unlearning data in standard classification networks (Chen et al.,
077   2023; Fan et al., 2023). Traditionally, these categories have been viewed as separate fields.
078   In the formal setup, the negation method in task arithmetic (Ilharco et al., 2022a) is commonly used
079   for unlearning specific knowledge. A recent advancement is the neural tangent kernel-based linear
080   negation method (Ortiz-Jimenez et al., 2024), which addresses weight disentanglement issues in
081   task arithmetic by linearizing models and fine-tuning them in their tangent space. Both techniques
082   depend on a single fine-tuned model to compute the task vector.
083
      On the other hand, unlearning with a standard image classifier usually involves fine-tuning the orig-
084   inal model. Fine-tuning (Warnecke et al., 2021) and ℓ1 -sparse (Jia et al., 2023) aim to overfit the
085   model only on the retain set to erase the knowledge of the forget set. Meanwhile, Influence (Koh
086   & Liang, 2017) and SalUn (Fan et al., 2023) utilize both the retain and forget sets to selectively
087   degrade performance on the forget set while maintaining it on the retain set.
088
      When the forget set is much smaller than the retain set, using retain set for unlearning can be ineffi-
089
      cient. This challenge has led to the development of methods that focus on unlearning using only the
090   forget set. Several approaches (Golatkar et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2023) attempt this by relabeling
091   the forget set to different classes and fine-tuning the model. However, these methods often suffer
092   from catastrophic forgetting of the retain set, as the retain set is not used during fine-tuning.
093
      This paper proposes a unified approach to tackle both classification tasks using vision-language
094
      models and standard models. Furthermore, our method also focuses on using only the forget set
095
      for unlearning. We recognize the inherent trade-off between unlearning performance and retaining
096   performance on the retain set. Relying on a single model to address this trade-off is inefficient. To
097   overcome this, we propose a new approach that utilizes multiple fine-tuned models. By building on
098   task arithmetic, our method computes a more effective task vector from these models, enhancing
099   unlearning performance.
100

101

106   capabilities. TIES-Merging (Yadav et al., 2024) refines the merging process by incorporating a
107   trimming step and, in cases of sign conflicts, selects one sign through a voting process, merging all
      task vectors corresponding to the chosen sign. AdaMerging (Yang et al., 2024) autonomously learns

       3.1                             BACKGROUND
136
137   Task Arithmetic. Task arithmetic (Ilharco et al., 2022a) defines a task vector τt = θft t − θpre .
138   Specifically, the vectors are the result of subtracting (negating) the weights of a pre-trained model
139   θpre from those of a model θft t fine-tuned on a target task t. We can adjust the model in the desired
                                                                            P
140   direction by adding or subtracting the sum of these task vectors τ = t τt from the original model’s
141   weights, according to the formula θnew = θpre + λτ . This approach is more computationally
142   efficient than fine-tuning, as it leverages pre-trained models from public repositories and eliminates
143   the need for additional training.
144   A key application of task arithmetic is to make a model forget certain capabilities (Ilharco et al.,
145   2022a). This can be achieved through the negation of task vectors from the original weight, which
146   decreases performance on a target task. For instance, task arithmetic can be applied to unlearning
147   in models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), which is a strong vision-language model. In the original
148   paper, the authors demonstrated that task vectors derived from a CLIP model fine-tuned on a specific
149   dataset (e.g., Cars) could reduce the model’s accuracy on the fine-tuning dataset while maintaining
150   overall accuracy on a general dataset (e.g., ImageNet). However, while task arithmetic has shown
151   promising results for machine unlearning, there has been little research on fine-tuning models and
152
      computing task vectors for more effective unlearning. Our research addresses this gap.
153   Motivation. Our pilot study identifies two major challenges. First, unlearning performance is highly
154   sensitive to the hyperparameters used for fine-tuning. Figures 1 (b) and (c) exhibit accuracy on both
155   the forget set and retain set, which can vary by up to 15 percentage points depending on the hyperpa-
156   rameters. Second, finding a balance between reducing accuracy on the forget set while maintaining
157   accuracy on the retain set is challenging. As shown in Figure 1 (a), improving performance on the
158
      retain set tends to result in a clear decrease in performance on the forget set, and vice versa.
159   We argue overfitting the fine-tuned model to the forget set greatly diminishes performance on the
160   retain set when unlearning is applied; conversely, underfitting the model to the forget set leads to
161   ineffective unlearning, where the forgetting performance does not decrease sufficiently. Empirical
      evidence supporting our claim is presented in Section 4.3. Additionally, for successful unlearning,

      Our Unlearning scenarios. In our study, we explore two distinct unlearning scenarios. The first
      scenario is the one described above, where a vision-language model like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)
202
      is made to forget the knowledge of a specific class. For this scenario, we adopt the evaluation
203

      3.2      N E G M E R G E : I MPROVED TASK A RITHMETIC F OR M ACHINE U NLEARNING
210
211   Given multiple models fine-tuned on the forget set, which applied various training configurations
212   to ensure diversity among the fine-tuned models, we propose a method that neatly aggregates the
213   model for effective unlearning. Our proposed method called NegMerge consists of the following
214   steps: 1) We calculate the task vectors using all the fine-tuned models, 2) We identify the elements
215   corresponding to the forget set in each vector, and 3) Finally, we compute the final task vector
      by using the identified elements, and perform machine unlearning by subtracting this final task

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      vector from the original model. We provide a detailed description of each step below, and Figure 2
217   illustrates the overview of our method.
218
219   Preparing Diverse Fine-Tuned Models. There are numerous methods for preparing diverse fine-
220
      tuned models on the forget set. A simple yet effective approach is just altering hyperparameters
      such as learning rate and the number of epochs or employing data augmentation techniques like
221
      RandAugment (Cubuk et al., 2020) and CutMix (Yun et al., 2019). In this work, we focus on making
222
      minimal adjustments to the existing training setup, either by modifying RandAugment parameters
223   or adjusting training configurations like the number of epochs. Further details on these adjustments
224   can be found in Section 4.1. While additional techniques could further enhance model diversity and
225   improve unlearning performance, these are left for future exploration.
226
      Identifying Elements in the Task Vector Corresponding to the Forget Set. We derive task vectors
227
      from the fine-tuned models and analyze them to determine which elements (in weights) correspond
228
      to the forget set. We conjecture that elements that consistently show the same sign across task vectors
229   are attributed to the forget set, as each model is trained to align with this set, regardless of the training
230   configurations. On the other hand, components that exhibit differing signs are considered less related
231   to the forget set, as their variations are more likely a result of different training configurations rather
232   than supervision from the forget set. Our conjecture regarding sign conflicts is supported by the
233   unlearning performance reported in Table 4 and qualitative results visualized in Figure 3.
234

      Computational Complexity. The standard setup for task negation-based methods (Ilharco
      et al., 2022a; Ortiz-Jimenez et al., 2024) typically involves conducting multiple evaluations
247
      (i.e., usually done 20 iterations for the merging coefficient to find the optimal coefficient λ ∈
248
      {0.0, 0.05, . . . , 1.0}). For vanilla task arithmetic, this requires evaluating the coefficients m times
249   (m = 20 in the papers (Ilharco et al., 2022a; Ortiz-Jimenez et al., 2024)) for n models, leading to
250   a significant computational cost of O(mn). In contrast, our merging method requires only m eval-
251   uations for a single merged task vector, making it a more computationally efficient approach with a
252   cost of O(m). Therefore, while Task Arithmetic uses a single model in its final stage, achieving op-
253   timal performance demands more computation than our approach indeed. We believe this highlights
254   the advantages of our method over the competing methods.
255   Relationship with TIES-merging. We highlight some explicit differences compared to a similar-
256   looking strong method TIES-merging (Yadav et al., 2024). Since TIES-merging also performs selec-
257   tive merging based on signs, unlike our method, it includes elements with inconsistent signs across
258   task vectors in the merging process. Specifically, it sums the values of these elements, checks the
259   sign of the result, and sets the values of elements that do not match this sign to zero. The final task
260   vector is obtained by merging these adjusted task vectors.
261   However, we argue that elements with inconsistent signs across task vectors are more closely related
262   to the retain set than the forget set. Therefore, by including these sign-inconsistent elements in the
263   merging process, TIES-merging may alter the knowledge of the retain set in the original model.
264   For effective unlearning, task arithmetic should minimally affect the original model’s knowledge of
265   the retain set, but TIES-merging could significantly change this knowledge, making it unsuitable
266   for machine unlearning. The results presented in Table 1 along with additional empirical evidence
267   provided in Section 4.3, support our claim,

      Datasets and Backbones. In the CLIP scenario (i.e., referred to as the scenario using a vision-
275   language model), we follow the training and evaluation protocols of Ilharco et al. (2022a). We
276   assess unlearning performance on eight datasets: SUN397 (Xiao et al., 2016), Cars (Krause et al.,
277   2013), RESISC45 (Cheng et al., 2017), EuroSAT (Helber et al., 2019), SVHN (Yuval, 2011), GT-
278   SRB (Stallkamp et al., 2011), MNIST (LeCun, 1998), and DTD (Cimpoi et al., 2014). We use the
279   pre-trained CLIP ViT-{B/32, B/16, L/14} models (Radford et al., 2021) for these experiments. In the
280   standard classifier scenario, we evaluate unlearning performance on CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al.,
281   2009) using a ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016) model.
282   Baselines and Metrics. For the CLIP scenario, we compare our method with five existing meth-
283   ods: Task Arithmetic (Ilharco et al., 2022a), Uniform Merge (Wortsman et al., 2022), Greedy
284   Merge (Wortsman et al., 2022), TIES-Merging (Yadav et al., 2024), and MagMax (Marczak et al.,
285   2024). For the Greedy Merge, we rank models by their loss on the retain set and merge them in a
286   direction that minimizes this loss. We evaluate performance by measuring accuracy on the forget set
287   Df and the retain set Dr .
288   In the standard classifier scenario, we follow Fan et al. (2023) to compare our method against
289   eight unlearning techniques: Fine-tuning (Warnecke et al., 2021), Random Labeling (Golatkar
290   et al., 2020), Gradient Ascent (Thudi et al., 2022), Influence Unlearning (Koh & Liang, 2017),
291   ℓ1-sparse (Jia et al., 2023), Boundary Shrink and Expand (Chen et al., 2023), and SalUn (Fan et al.,
292   2023). We also compare against Task Arithmetic (Ilharco et al., 2022a), Uniform Merge (Worts-
293   man et al., 2022), TIES-Merging (Yadav et al., 2024), and MagMax (Marczak et al., 2024). The
294   objective is to match the unlearned model’s performance to that of a fully retrained model. Greedy
295
      Merge (Wortsman et al., 2022) is infeasible for comparison in this scenario, only using the forget set.
      We use the accuracies of the retain set Dr , forget set Df , and test set Dtest to evaluate performance.
296
      To assess privacy protection, we employ the Membership Inference Attack (MIA) metric (Carlini
297
      et al., 2022), aiming to achieve similar results to the fully retrained model.
298
299   Implementation Details. In the CLIP scenario, for fine-tuning, we set the batch size to 128 and
300   use a learning rate of 1e-5 with a cosine annealing schedule. We utilize the AdamW optimizer,
301
      applying a weight decay of 0.1. We enhance the diversity of the fine-tuned models by adjusting
      the configurations of RandAugment. Specifically, we vary the number of sequential augmentation
302
      transformations (ranging from 1 to 3) and the magnitude of these transformations (ranging from 1 to
303
      10). A total of 30 models are fine-tuned. Unlike previous works, we incorporate data augmentation
304   directly into the fine-tuning process, which requires adjusting the number of training epochs to better
305   accommodate the augmented data. Consequently, the number of training epochs is set as follows:
306   70 epochs for Cars, 100 epochs for DTD, 40 epochs for EuroSAT, GTSRB, RESISC45, SUN397,
307   and 30 epochs for MNIST and SVHN.
308
      In the standard image classifier unlearning scenario, for the CIFAR-10 dataset, we set the batch size
309
      to 256 and the learning rate to 0.05. Since CIFAR-10 has relatively low image quality, instead of
310   applying data augmentation, we vary the training hyperparameters. We set the number of epochs
311   to 40, 50, and 60, the weight decay to 0.0001, 0.00005, and 0.00001, and the label smoothing to 0,
312   0.05, and 0.1 to enhance the diversity of the fine-tuned models. The total number of models used in
313   the model merge is 27.

      5    C ONCLUSION
526
527
      In this paper, we propose a novel machine unlearning technique, NegMerge, based on task arith-
528   metic and model merging. We hypothesize that multiple fine-tuned models are necessary for ef-
529   fective unlearning based on the observation of a trade-off between accuracy on the forget set and
530   the remain set. Building on the fact that existing techniques generate numerous fine-tuned models
531   through validation using various hyperparameters, we propose a method that utilizes all derived fine-
532   tuned models. Assuming that elements with consistent signs across task vectors obtained from the
533   fine-tuned models are related to the forget set, we merge only those elements. This approach enables
534   us to compute task vectors that fit the forget set more effectively while preserving the knowledge
535   in the retain set, thus overcoming the trade-off. We then perform forgetting by negation with the
536
      merged task vector. Our NegMerge is tested on the CLIP ViT models and the standard ResNet18
      classifier, achieving new state-of-the-art performance across nine datasets.
537
538   Limitations. Limitation of this work is its reliance on empirical approaches without formal theoret-
539   ical justification. In future research, we aim to validate our assumptions theoretically and develop
      an analytical solution informed by these insights.

952   C                                  R ATIO OF Z EROED E LEMENTS IN THE M ERGED V ECTOR
953
954   Tables C1 and C2 present the proportion of zeroed weight elements based on the number of merged
955   models. The results indicate that as the number of task vectors increases, the percentage of masked
956   weight elements (%) also increases. Notably, this increase is accompanied by consistent improve-
957   ments in unlearning performance (Df ). This suggests that leveraging more task vectors allows for
958   a more accurate identification of the specific weight elements that require modification for effec-
959   tive unlearning. Ultimately, the task vectors generated through our method lead to changes in only
960   5–10% of the weight elements in the original model via negation. These findings strongly support
961
      our argument that not all weight elements are critical for unlearning, and a small subset is sufficient
      to achieve strong unlearning performance.
962
963   Additionally, these experiments also highlight that our technique maintains the performance of the
964   retain set effectively. By selectively targeting only the weight elements essential for unlearning, our
965   method minimizes the impact on the retain set, resulting in better preservation of its performance
966   compared to other merging techniques.
967   Table C3 shows that larger zero-out values with uniformly merged sparsified task vectors lead to
968   improved unlearning results. TIES-merging and MagMax exhibit fewer zero-out values, and their
969   performance is expected to be outperformed by our method.

1101   E     M EMORY AND C OMPUTATIONAL E FFICIENCY
1102
1103
       We intend to address the computational cost from four perspectives: storage, runtime memory,
1104   merge time and inference time complexity. It is important to note that the merging methods we
1105   compare all share the same model pool, so there is no inherent computational overhead from using
1106   multiple models.
1107
       E.1   S TORAGE
1108
1109
       Although storing all models before merging would be costly in terms of memory, our method does
1110   not require keeping all fine-tuned models stored. During the process of training multiple models,
1111   we only need to check the sign consensus of each element and can store them by adding them to
1112   the existing model. This approach allows our technique to utilize the same amount of storage as the
1113   conventional single-model baseline while performing more effective unlearning. This is not feasible
1114   with other merging methods like TIES-Merging, highlighting the superiority of our approach.
1115
       E.2   RUNTIME M EMORY
1116
1117   Our method offers a distinct advantage in runtime memory usage. As shown in Table C1 and C2,
1118   during the merging process, a significant proportion of the weights in the task vector are zeroed
1119   out, leaving only 5–10% of the total weight elements active. This substantial reduction in active
1120   weights enables the adoption of lightweight storage techniques, such as weight lookup tables, to
1121   further minimize runtime memory requirements. By storing only the active weights, our method
1122   achieves greater efficiency compared to baseline approaches.
1123   E.3   M ERGE T IME
1124
1125   Our method requires checking the sign of each weight element, which takes longer than methods
1126   like MagMax, which only detects maximum values, or Uniform merge, which calculates averages.
1127   However, our method is significantly faster than those requiring more complex operations, such as
1128   TIES-Merging or Greedy Merge. Detailed estimation results can be found in Table 1.
1129   E.4   I NFERENCE T IME C OMPLEXITY
1130
1131   Achieving optimal performance in Task Arithmetic often requires multiple training rounds for hy-
1132   perparameter validation due to hyperparameter sensitivity shown in Figure 1. This sensitivity often
1133   necessitates n iterations of hyperparameter tuning during the validation process. Additionally, de-
       termining the scaling coefficient—an inference hyperparameter—requires 20 inferences per task

       vector. With n validation rounds, the total inference cost scales to 20 × n, resulting in a complexity
1135   of O(mn), where m represents the number of inferences per round.

1137   In contrast, our approach eliminates the need for repeated inferences per task vector. It requires

       only 20 inferences to determine the scaling coefficient, regardless of the number of task vectors or

1149
1150   Let θori and θf t denote the weights of the pre-trained model and a fine-tuned model, respectively.
1151   We have the formulation θunlearn = θori − λτmerged , where τmerged is from Eq. 1 - our consensually
1152   merged task vector. We argue that achieving larger zero-out values with sparsified consensus editing
1153   signals in τmerged could lead to unlearning performance improvements, based on the following fun-
1154
       damental claims: (1) Weight-wise unanimous consensus merging reduces non-zero values and gives
       a robust τmerged ; the larger zero-out values in τmerged contribute to stable merging. (2) There exists
1155                              ∗
       a stable merged point θunlearn    , which enjoys better unlearning results as k as the number of task
1156
       vector τk increases.
1157
1158
1159   F.2   I NFORMAL PROOF
1160
1161   As the number of task vectors τk in τmerged increases, the non-zero values decrease because our
1162   consensus operation performs like an AND operation. The robustness of τmerged increases upon
1163   merging, as sparse weights are merged uniformly, which enjoys inherently more robustness than an
1164   individual weight Pas revealed in (Wortsman et al., 2022; Jang et al., 2025), where the first term of
1165
       τmerged = 1/n ∗ k θf t − θori conducts uniform merge. θunlearn moves closer to θori , which likely
       stays lower loss regions due to weights generally holding linear mode connectivity (LMC) (Frankle
1166
       et al., 2020; Juneja et al., 2022; Entezari et al., 2021). It also mitigates issues that cause fluctuating
1167
       high losses (i.e., loss barriers) on certain loss surfaces, when weights deviate significantly from θori .
1168                                                  ∗
       Therefore, task negation at an improved θunlearn        would likely reside in lower loss regions which
1169                                                                             ∗
       leads to yielding a better result. While we do not specify the exact θunlearn    , increasing the number
1170   of merged task vectors allows the process to approach a closer-to-optimal point as more sparsified
1171   merged weights are merged uniformly (Jang et al., 2025).
