013                                               A BSTRACT
014
015
               Over the past decade, predictive modeling of neural responses in the primate visual
               system has advanced significantly, largely driven by various deep neural network
016
               approaches. These include models optimized directly for visual recognition, cross-
017
               modal alignment through contrastive objectives, neural response prediction from
018            scratch, and large language model embeddings. Likewise, different readout mecha-
019            nisms—ranging from fully linear to spatial-feature factorized methods—have been
020            explored for mapping network activations to neural responses. Despite the diver-
021            sity of these approaches, it remains unclear which method performs best across
022            different visual regions. In this study, we systematically compare these approaches
023            for modeling the human visual system and investigate alternative strategies to
024            improve response predictions. Our findings reveal that for early to mid-level visual
025            areas, response-optimized models with visual inputs offer superior prediction accu-
026
               racy, while for higher visual regions, embeddings from Large Language Models
               (LLMs) based on detailed contextual descriptions of images and task-optimized
027
               models pretrained on large vision datasets provide the best fit. Through compara-
028
               tive analysis of these modeling approaches, we identified three distinct regions in
029            the visual cortex: one sensitive primarily to perceptual features of the input that
030            are not captured by linguistic descriptions, another attuned to fine-grained visual
031            details representing semantic information, and a third responsive to abstract, global
032            meanings aligned with linguistic content. We also highlight the critical role of
033            readout mechanisms, proposing a novel scheme that modulates receptive fields and
034            feature maps based on semantic content, resulting in an accuracy boost of 3-23%
035            over existing SOTAs for all models and brain regions. Together, these findings
036            offer key insights into building more precise models of the visual system.

      1   I NTRODUCTION AND R ELATED W ORK
039
040
      The effort to build accurate predictive models of the visual system has been a longstanding goal
041
      in neuroscience. Early approaches primarily relied on handcrafted features, such as Gabor filters,
042   curvature models, and motion energy models, to predict responses in early to mid-level visual areas.
043   Similarly, word-based descriptions were often used to model responses in higher-level visual regions.
044   These models provided interpretability, as the features they employed were well understood and
045   linked to specific visual computations. However, they lacked quantitative precision in their ability
046   to predict neural responses (Hubel and Wiesel, 1962; Livingstone and Hubel, 1984; Albrecht and
047   Hamilton, 1982; Gallant et al., 1993; Hubel and Wiesel, 1968; Desimone et al., 1984; Tanaka et al.,
048   1991; Pasupathy and Connor, 2002; Yue et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2023; Pasupathy and Connor, 1999;
049   Tsunoda et al., 2001; Rust and DiCarlo, 2010; Brincat and Connor, 2004; Zeki, 1973; Pasupathy and
050
      Connor, 2001; Moran and Desimone, 1985; Kobatake and Tanaka, 1994; Kriegeskorte et al., 2008;
      Kobatake et al., 1998; Miyashita, 1988).
051
052   The advent of deep convolutional neural networks (DCNNs) marked a significant improvement in
053   predictive accuracy across the visual system (Yamins et al., 2014; Abdelhack and Kamitani, 2018;
      Wen et al., 2018; Horikawa and Kamitani, 2017; Eickenberg et al., 2017; Güçlü and Van Gerven,


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2015; Cichy et al., 2016; Khaligh-Razavi and Kriegeskorte, 2014; Schrimpf et al., 2020; Storrs et al.,
055   2021; Safarani et al., 2021; Schwartz et al., 2019; Seeliger et al., 2021). DCNNs trained on image
056   categorization tasks emerged as the first class of models capable of capturing neural activity in the
057   primate visual cortex with a reasonable degree of fidelity. This success spurred a wave of model-brain
058   comparisons, wherein variations in input data, architecture, and learning objectives were explored to
059   identify the most predictive models of brain responses in both non-human primates and humans.
060
      More recently, models trained using multimodal contrastive learning approaches, such as CLIP, or
061   image-caption embeddings from large language models (LLMs), have shown promise in predicting
062   neural responses in the visual cortex (Tang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2022; Doerig et al., 2024). These
063   findings suggest that visual brain responses may encode some linguistically learned structure or
064   semantics. In parallel, another class of models, optimized specifically for neural response prediction
065   (Khosla and Wehbe, 2022; Khosla et al., 2022; Federer et al., 2020; Dapello et al., 2022; St-Yves
066   et al., 2023) — either trained from scratch or fine-tuned to better align with primate visual representa-
067   tions—has achieved impressive predictive accuracy, particularly with the availability of large-scale
068   neural datasets.
069   Given the broad range of modeling approaches applied to different regions of the visual cortex, a
070   critical question remains: which approach offers the most quantitatively precise predictions of neural
071   responses across the various areas of the human visual system? This challenge underscores the
072   need for systematic comparisons to determine the optimal models for different visual processing
073   stages. While some recent studies have made strides in conducting large-scale comparative analyses,
074   they tend to focus primarily on specific pre-selected visual regions and largely compare different
075
      task-optimized vision networks (Conwell et al., 2022b). A more comprehensive comparison is
      needed to evaluate a broader set of approaches, including models based on response optimization and
076
      embeddings from language models trained on vision-aligned tasks or pure language data.
077
078   A related but less explored area in human visual research involves investigating different readout
079   mechanisms for predicting neural responses from neural representations. The predominant readout
080   in primate studies is the fully-connected affine readout, often used in regularized linear regression
081
      models. However, these linear ridge regression readouts require numerous parameters, especially in
      high-dimensional spaces, leading to significant computational and memory demands. To mitigate
082
      this, more efficient methods have been developed, such as factorized linear readouts by (Klindt et al.,
083
      2017), that decouple spatial from feature selectivity, reducing overhead and improving prediction
084   accuracy. The Gaussian2D readout (Lurz et al., 2020) further enhances parameter efficiency by
085   learning spatial readout locations using a bivariate Gaussian distribution informed by anatomical
086   retinotopy. Given this diversity, a key question is which readout method best predicts neural responses
087   across different regions of the human visual cortex, and how can these methods be refined for better
088   predictions? Addressing these questions is essential for improving neural response models and
089   computational modeling frameworks.
090   To address the above challenges, we make the following key contributions in this paper:
091
092
093
094
095
            1. Introduction of a novel readout mechanism: We introduce a novel readout method
               utilizing Spatial Transformers, which delivers significant improvements in accuracy (3-23%)
096
               compared to previously employed SOTA readouts.
097
            2. Identification of brain regions responsive to visual and semantic input: Through com-
098            parative analysis of models across various visual regions, we identify three distinct regions
099            in the human visual cortex that respond primarily to (a) perceptual characteristics of the
100            input, (b) localized visual semantics aligned with linguistic descriptions, and (c) global
101            semantic interpretations of the input, also aligned with language.
102         3. Comprehensive analysis of neural network models: We conduct an in-depth analysis
103            of various artificial neural network models, incorporating both vision and language inputs.
104            Additionally, we explore different readout mechanisms and examine which models perform
105            better in specific brain regions, while highlighting the unique advantages each provides.

      2.1   E NCODERS
165
166
      Task Optimised Models - We use encoders from pre-trained models like AlexNet and ResNet,
167   originally trained for object classification on the large-scale ImageNet dataset. The weights of
168   their intermediate layers are frozen, and only the readout layers (described later) are trained. Prior
169   research shows that early layers of neural networks align with lower visual cortex regions, while later
170   layers correspond to higher regions (Khaligh-Razavi and Kriegeskorte, 2014; Güçlü and Van Gerven,
171   2015; Cichy et al., 2016; Eickenberg et al., 2017; Horikawa and Kamitani, 2017; Wen et al., 2018;
172   Abdelhack and Kamitani, 2018; Yamins et al., 2014). Thus, we experimented with all layers of
173   task-optimized networks. For fair comparison, we selected the best-performing layers for each
174   cortical region (see Appendix Table 2 and summary in Table 1).
175   Response Optimised Models - Task-optimized models often rely heavily on established a priori
176   hypotheses, which may be biased towards pre-existing conclusions, limiting novel discoveries.
177   Further, these networks are typically optimized for specific tasks, such as object classification, which
178   may not capture the full range of visual processing in the cortex. Recently, (Khosla and Wehbe, 2022)
179   showed that training neural networks from scratch with stimulus images and fMRI data from the
180   NSD dataset (Allen et al., 2022) can achieve accuracy comparable to SOTA task-optimized models.
181
      By training directly on neural data, the network is not constrained by biases introduced from tasks
      like object recognition (as in predominant task-optimized networks), allowing it to learn more flexible
182
      or diverse representations that are more specific to the neural responses across different brain regions
183
      or stimuli.
184
185   We leverage the same architecture for response-optimized models as prior work (Khosla and Wehbe,
186   2022), which consists of a convolutional neural network (CNN) core that transforms raw input
187
      data into feature spaces characteristic of different brain regions, followed by a readout layer that
      maps these features to fMRI voxel responses (Figure 1-A). The core contains four convolutional
188
      blocks, where each convolutional block includes two convolutional layers, followed by internal batch
189
      normalization, nonlinear ReLU activations, and an anti-aliased average pooling operation. To ensure
190   equivariance under all isometries, we use E(2)-Equivariant Steerable Convolution layers (Weiler and
191   Cesa, 2019). Further analysis on the importance of network architecture for Response Optimized
192   models can be found in Supplementary Section A.6 and Table 7.
193
      Language Models - Recent studies show that higher visual regions converge toward representational
194
      formats similar to large language model (LLM) embeddings of scene descriptions. (Doerig et al.,
195
      2024) used MPNET (Song et al., 2020) to encode image captions and map them to fMRI responses
196   via ridge regression, finding it effectively modeled higher visual areas despite being trained on
197   language inputs alone. In contrast, (Tang et al., 2024) and (Wang et al., 2022) used multimodal
198   models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and BridgeTower (Yang et al., 2023), and showed that CLIP
199   outperforms vision-only models in capturing higher regions due to language feedback. The language
200   models preserve ROIs’ category selectivities, and were more effective at predicting responses to
201   visual data compared to the reverse. These insights motivated us to evaluate language models relative
202   to vision-only response-optimized and task-optimized models as detailed below (A more detailed
203   comparison on CLIP and MPNET embeddings can be found in Appendix A.2) -
204
205         1. Single Caption - Images in the NSD dataset are sourced from MS COCO (Lin et al., 2014)
206            and annotated by 4-5 human annotators. We encode these captions using CLIP or MPNET,
207
               average the encodings, and input them into a one-layer linear regressor to map them to fMRI
               voxel responses. Since the captions describe the image as a whole without offering spatial
208
               details (i.e., fine-grained delineations of features at different locations), we only use the
209
               ridge linear readout for single caption inputs as shown in Figure 1-A.
210         2. Dense Caption - An image of size 424 ∗ 424 is divided into grids of size 53 ∗ 53. For each
211            grid, a caption is generated using GPT-2, which is then encoded by either CLIP or MPNET.
212            Thus an image of shape 3 ∗ 424 ∗ 424 is transformed into a feature representation N ∗ 8 ∗ 8,
213            where N is the size of the embedding produced by CLIP or MPNET. The dense-caption
214            language encoders further process these feature maps through a single convolutional block
215            (as described earlier for the response-optimized vision encoders) before passing them to the
               readout model. More details can be found at Supplementary Section A.3 and Figure 9.

      2.2   R EADOUTS
217
218   The encoders discussed above are paired with a readout model (Figure 1-A) that maps the encoder
219   feature representations to voxel fMRI responses from various regions of the visual cortex.
220
      Linear Readout consists of a ridge regression model that takes in the flattened feature representations
221
      from the encoders as inputs and maps them to voxel fMRI representations. Specifically, for a given
222   stimuli i, the response vector Yi ∈ Rn (where n represents the number of voxels) is predicted as
223   Yi = Ŵ Ei , where Ei ∈ Re refers to the flattened feature representation from the encoder and
224
      Ŵ ∈ Rn∗e refers to the linear readout weights. The weights Ŵ are obtained by minimizing the
225   ridge regression objective function: f (W ) = minW ||Y − EW ||2F + λ||W ||2F , where λ is the
226   regularization parameter. We find the optimal λ through cross-validation.
227
228
      Gaussian 2D Readout (Lurz et al., 2020) models each neuron’s spatial sensitivity as a 2D Gaussian
      in the input feature space. Each voxel is assumed to respond to a specific region of the input, with
229
      sensitivity characterized by a bivariate normal distribution. The mean represents the neuron’s most
230
      sensitive location (receptive field center), while the covariance matrix determines the size, shape,
231   and orientation of the receptive field along the x and y axes. This Gaussian is applied uniformly
232   across all input channels, indicating shared spatial sensitivity. Neuron responses are computed by
233   bilinearly interpolating values from each channel based on the Gaussian distribution, weighting them
234   by learned channel-specific weights, and summing the contributions to get the final voxel response.
235
      For a voxel n with receptive field defined by Gn (x, y) ∼ N (µn , Σn ), the voxel response Yn
236
      is calculated as: Yn = Σc Wnc Vc (x, y), where Wnc is the learned weight that modulates the
237   contribution of channel c to voxel n, Vc (x, y) is the feature value sampled from channel c of the
238   encoder feature map E ∈ RC×W ×H at location (x, y), using bilinear interpolation based on the
239   receptive field defined by Gn (x, y), and C is the number of channels in the encoder output.
240
      Spatial-Feature Factorized Linear Readout factorizes the linear readout model into spatial (the
241
      portion of the input space a voxel is sensitive to) and feature (the specific features of the input space a
242
      voxel responds to) dimensions, as described in (Klindt et al., 2017). By separating spatial (where)
243   and feature (what) dimensions, the model mirrors the known structure of neural receptive fields in the
244   brain, where neurons exhibit sensitivity to specific spatial locations and particular feature types. This
245   approach not only significantly reduces the number of parameters but also aligns more closely with
246   the known characteristics of neural responses.

      Semantic Spatial Transformer Readout - We introduce a novel readout that modulates the encoder
257
      feature representation and spatial weights for every voxel depending on the stimulus using a learnable
258   module based on the Spatial Transformer Network (STN) (Jaderberg et al., 2015). The STN in essence
259   allows us to ‘zoom in’ on relevant portions of the image (e.g., via rotation, scaling, translation, or
260   affine transformations) (Figure 1-B) and these relevant regions are determined independently for
261   every voxel and for every channel. By learning to apply the right transformations, STNs adjust inputs
262   to a canonical form, effectively making the model invariant to geometric transformations.
263
      This module comprises four elements - (1) A localization network (a pretrained resnet50 model)
264   which takes in the image as input and returns a feature representation of the image (before the
265   adaptive average pooling operation), (2) Two linear deformation networks that take in the feature
266   representation from the localization network and return two sets of affine transformations θ1 ∈ RC∗6
267   and θ2 ∈ RN ∗6 as shown in Figure 1-A, where C is the number of channels of the encoder feature
268   representation and N is the number of voxels, (3) A parameterised sampling grid that generates
269   a transformed grid of the same shape as the encoder feature representation E using θ1 and of the
      same shape as the spatial weight matrix S using θ2 and (4) A sampler that transforms E and S

271   Spatial-Feature Factorized Linear Readout (Equations 1, 2), with E replaced by E ′ = AT (E, θ1 )

      Different channels in a feature map often encode distinct attributes, such as edges, textures, or
275
      shapes. By allowing channel-specific transformations, the STN can adapt to the unique geometric
276
      properties of the features represented in each channel. Unlike object classification tasks, where
277   we can employ augmentations tailored to known invariances (e.g. rotating an image won’t change
278   the category label) to boost predictive accuracy, the geometric invariances of voxel responses are
279   unknown. STN enables the network to learn these invariances directly from the data, providing a
280   crucial advantage in predicting voxel responses across diverse visual regions. Moreover, STN also
281   allows voxel-specific spatial modulation of receptive fields (RFs), inspired by studies demonstrating
282   the dynamic adaptability of RFs to stimulus properties - RF sizes can expand or contract based on
283   contrast Sceniak et al. (1999) and can also shift or reshape in response to contextual or attentional
284   influences Womelsdorf et al. (2006). Unlike fixed spatial masks used in the previous readouts, the
285
      STN employs affine transformations to capture stimulus-dependent spatial changes such as translation,
      scaling, rotation, and shearing. This flexibility enables more accurate modeling of neural responses
286
      that exhibit dynamic RF properties. Further analysis on this readout is expanded in Supplementary
287
      Table 5, Figure 10 and Section A.4.

      2.3   T RAINING AND DATASET
290
291   In this study, we utilized stimuli-response pairs from four subjects (Subjects 1, 2, 5, and 7) from the
292   Natural Scenes Dataset (More details in Appendix A.1). The experimental setup involved presenting
293   a total of 37,000 image stimuli from the MS COCO dataset (Lin et al., 2014) to these subjects. Out
294   of these, 1,000 images were shown to all four subjects, and these shared images were designated as
295   the test set for our analyses. The remaining 36,000 images were split into 35,000 for training and
296   1,000 for validation purposes. We trained separate models for each of the following brain regions:
297   the high-level ventral, dorsal and lateral streams, V4, V3v, V3d, V2v, V2d, V1v, and V1d. This
298   approach allowed us to tailor the models to the unique neural response patterns of each region, thereby
299
      providing a more precise understanding of how different parts of the visual cortex process information.
      Throughout the paper, the reported accuracy refers to the test-time performance, measured as the
300
      noise-normalized Pearson correlation between predicted and actual voxel responses.
301
302   All models were trained using an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 and NVIDIA A40 GPU. We employed
303   a batch size of 4 with gradient accumulation to achieve an effective batch size of 16, using a learning
304   rate of 0.0001. Training was performed using an equal-weighted combination of Mean Squared Error
305
      (MSE) and correlation loss between predicted and target voxel responses, with early stopping applied
      after 20 epochs without improvement in validation accuracy, measured by Pearson correlation.

310   3.1   P ERFORMANCE C OMPARISON OF R EADOUTS ACROSS V ISION AND L ANGUAGE M ODELS
311         IN THE V ISUAL C ORTEX
312
313   We first evaluated the performance of various readout mechanisms in predicting neural responses
314   across different brain regions. Our results showed that the Semantic Spatial Transformer readouts
315
      consistently outperform linear, 2D Gaussian, and Spatial-Feature Factorized Linear readouts across all
      regions of the visual cortex and for almost all encoder models (see Figure 2). This is due to the Spatial
316
      Transformer Network (STN) in the readout, which applies learnable transformations to encoder
317
      feature maps, converting them into canonical forms. This process removes irrelevant geometric
318   variations while emphasizing key feature characteristics. Additionally, the STN learns transformations
319   for spatial weights, enabling stimulus-dependent modulation of receptive field maps for each voxel,
320   providing greater flexibility in modeling complex inputs. This trend of superior performance is
321   especially evident in vision models (see Figure 2-A) and holds for other task-optimized encoders
322   processing visual input (details in Appendix Tables 2 and 3). Figure 2-B further illustrates the brain
323   voxels where each readout performs best, underscoring the dominant performance of the Semantic
      Spatial Transformer readout for vision models.


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      While the Semantic Spatial Transformer achieves the overall highest accuracy across all regions for
325   all models (Appendix Tables 2, 3, 4), its improvement is less pronounced with language embedding
326   inputs (Figure 2-B). This disparity arises because the Semantic Spatial Transformer readout uses a
327   pretrained ResNet50 encoder as the localization network to learn affine transformations that adjust
328   both vision and language encoder feature spaces. Vision encoder features are generally larger per
329   channel (e.g., 28×28) than language encoder features (e.g., 4×4). Consequently, the Semantic Spatial
330   Transformer readout has a greater capacity to leverage the rich spatial information available in
331   vision models. Larger spatial dimensions provide more granular information, allowing STNs to
332   learn transformations that account for variations in position, scale, and orientation of features more
333
      accurately. Further analysis on bias introduced by readouts can be found in Supplementary Section
      A.5 and Table 6.
334
335   Further, Spatial-Feature Factorized Linear Readouts outperform linear ridge regression readouts both
336   in terms of memory efficiency and prediction performance, as shown in Figure 2-A and Appendix
337   Tables 4, 3 and 2. This improvement is attributed to the readout’s capability to effectively disentangle
338   voxel response selectivity into spatial and feature dimensions. This approach aligns with established
339
      phenomena in neuroscience, where neurons exhibit selectivity not only for specific features but also
      for stimuli presented within their receptive field locations.

466   3.3         B RAIN REGIONS SENSITIVE TO V ISION V S L ANGUAGE M ODELS
467
468   Recent research shows that pure language models, like MPNET, can predict image-evoked brain
469   activity in the high-level visual cortex using only image captions (Doerig et al., 2024). This raises
470
      intriguing questions about the alignment between the human visual cortex and language. To explore
      this relationship further, we compare these language models with vision-only models.
471
472   When we assess language models that receive only image captions—without the images them-
473   selves—against response-optimized vision models, we find that the lower regions of the visual cortex
474   are better modeled by vision-based approaches. In contrast, higher regions are more effectively
475   captured by language models (see Figure 4-A, column 3 and Table 1). This pattern also holds
476
      when comparing language models to task-optimized vision models, although the distinction is less
      pronounced (first two columns of Figure 4-A).
477
478   Next, we differentiate between single-caption and dense-caption models. Single-caption models
479   convey only the overall semantic content of an image, whereas dense-caption models capture both
480   spatial and semantic details. Consequently, the lower regions of the visual cortex, which are sensitive
481   to fine-grained visual information, are better modeled by dense-caption models, as illustrated in
482
      Figure 4-B.
483   As we move from the lower to the higher regions of the visual cortex, there is a notable shift in
484   sensitivity from localized semantics to global semantics across all ventral, dorsal, and lateral streams.
485   Figure 4-B demonstrates that single-caption models dominate in the mid-to-higher regions of these
      streams, emphasizing the sensitivity of these areas to the overall meaning or interpretation of an entire


                                                                                                                  9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      image or scene. This trend is further corroborated in Figure 4-A, which compares vision models with
487
      both single-caption and dense-caption language models. Here, response-optimized vision models
488   outperform single-caption models in the lower regions of the ventral, dorsal, and lateral streams, but
489   do not maintain this advantage in the mid-to-higher regions.
490
491
      Thus, we can identify three distinct regions in the visual cortex that are sensitive to different types
      of stimulus (Figure 4-D): (1) the lower visual regions (V1, V2, V3, and V4) are most sensitive to
492
      perceptual features that are not fully captured by linguistic descriptions - region A; (2) the mid-level
493
      regions of the dorsal, ventral, and lateral streams are most sensitive to localized semantics (i.e.
494   detailed, specific information about particular parts or regions of an image) - region B; and (3) the
495   higher regions of the dorsal, ventral, and lateral streams are sensitive exclusively to global semantic
496   information - region C. Vision models outperform both single and dense caption language models in
497   region A (Figure 4-A and Table 1), thus proving its sensitivity to largely perceptual features. Dense
498   Caption language models outperform single caption language models (Figure 4-B) and response-
499   optimized vision models (Figure 4-A) in region B, thus proving it is most sensitive to nuanced,
500   localized semantic details. Vision models also outperform single caption models in region B (Figure
501   4-A), thus proving it is more sensitive to detailed visual information. Lastly, single caption language
502
      models outperform both dense caption models (Figure 4-B) and vision models (Figure 4-A) in region
      C, thus confirming its sensitivity to global semantics. Although this comparison was done extensively
503
      using Semantic Spatial Transformer readout, the trends hold true for other readouts as well, although
504
      to a much lesser extent (Appendix Figures 7, 6, 5).

507   4    D ISCUSSION
508
509   In this study, we leveraged the Natural Scenes Dataset to evaluate various neural network models
510
      in predicting neural responses across different brain regions. Our analysis focused on three key
      comparisons: task-optimized vs. response-optimized models, vision models vs. language models,
511
      and different readout methods for mapping model activations to brain signals.
512
513   First, we compared task-optimized models, pre-trained on specific visual tasks and thus biased toward
514   those tasks, with response-optimized models trained directly from brain response data. Our results
515   show that response-optimized models, which learn from raw visual inputs, significantly outperform
516
      task-optimized models in early visual regions. This suggests that brain-like processing in early-to-mid
      visual areas does not fully emerge in task-optimized models, and explicit alignment with neural
517
      data enhances prediction accuracy. However, in higher visual regions, both model types perform
518
      comparably, with task-optimized models showing a slight edge.
519
520   Next, we compared vision models with language models, including single-caption and dense-caption
521   models. Vision models outperformed language models in early visual regions, which are more
522
      attuned to perceptual features not captured by linguistic descriptions. In mid-level visual regions,
      sensitivity shifts toward semantic information, with dense-caption models excelling due to their
523
      ability to represent localized semantics. In higher visual regions, single-caption models perform
524
      better, indicating the importance of global scene understanding.
525
526   Finally, we evaluated different readout mechanisms for mapping activations to brain responses.
527   Factorized readouts significantly outperformed linear models, and incorporating a Semantic Spatial
528
      Transformer further improved performance, particularly in vision models.
529   Our work has several limitations. First, we focused on task-optimized models trained for object
530   categorization. A comprehensive comparison of models trained on other visual objectives and data
531   sets is outside the scope of this study. However, prior research suggests that variations in architecture,
532   objective, and data diet do not drastically impact response prediction accuracy, so we do not expect
533   our conclusions to change significantly with additional models. While we found that language models
534
      become more accurate in predicting responses in high-level visual regions, we did not explore what
      specifically drives this performance. It is still uncertain whether object category information (e.g.,
535
      nouns) or other elements such as actions, spatial relationships, or contextual details play a more
536
      significant role. Finally, while the Semantic Spatial Transformer led to better predictions, future
537   work should investigate how spatial and feature weights are modulated by different inputs. We also
538   only tested affine transformations; more constrained or nonlinear deformations may offer further
539   improvements.

950   A.3   T HE N ECESSITY OF S PATIAL S UBDIVISION IN D ENSE C APTIONING FOR E FFECTIVE
951         V ISUAL C ORTEX M ODELING
952
953
      We further investigated whether the observed differences between dense and global captioning are
954   due to (a) the spatial subdivision of the image (Hypothesis 1) or the increased semantic detail in
955   dense captions (Hypothesis 2). The original idea behind using dense captions was to provide spatial
956   information in addition to semantic information in the form of captions, and subdividing the image
957   into equal sized grids and getting captions for each grid was one of the easiest and most intuitive
958   ways to do that.
959
      We further tried generating more comprehensive single captions of the image using existing LLMs,
960   however none of them were able to provide more information than those already present in the
961   original MS-COCO dataset. In an attempt to densify the single captions, we thus adopted a different
962   approach: for each image, we took the embeddings of dense captions generated for individual grid
963   locations and averaged these embeddings to produce a single ”aggregate dense caption” embedding.
964
      On comparing single caption stimuli with ‘densified’ single caption stimuli (as opposed to the dense
965
      caption approach discussed in the paper) (Figure 9), we saw a similar trend where the higher regions of
966   the visual cortex were better modeled by single caption stimuli. However, the transition in sensitivity
967   from dense to single caption in the middle regions of the ventral, dorsal and lateral stream that is so
968   clearly pronounced when using dense captions is missing when using the above ‘densified’ single
969   captions. Further comparing ‘densified’ single captions to dense captions (as proposed in the paper),
970   we saw that the dense captions modeled the overall visual cortex better. Hence, we do feel that adding
971   spatial information to the dense caption is necessary for building more accurate models, be it by
      sub-dividing the image into grids or via any other way.

1052   Our study carefully controlled several factors to compare task-optimized and response-optimized
1053   neural network models for predicting brain responses. Specifically, we held constant both the
1054   stimulus set and readout layer, varying only the encoder architecture across models. The rationale for
1055   employing different architectures in our study was to leverage state-of-the-art approaches tailored to
1056   distinct modeling paradigms. A direct comparison between task-optimized and response-optimized
1057   models is inherently challenging due to differences in the available training stimulus sets. Specifically,
1058   the stimulus set for training response-optimized models is substantially smaller—approximately
1059
       0.03 times the size of the datasets used for task optimization. Incorporating structural biases into
       response-optimized models (e.g., rotation equivariance) enables them to learn effectively from smaller
1060
       datasets. This advantage of rotation-equivariant architectures in neural encoding contexts has been
1061
       demonstrated in prior studies Khosla and Wehbe (2022) and is a critical factor when designing models
1062   that align with the constraints of neural data.
1063
1064   While head-on comparisons using identical architectures for task and neural response optimization
1065
       could provide valuable insights into the specific contributions of these factors , the primary objective
       of our study was not to isolate these factors. Instead, we aimed to identify the most predictive models
1066
       for voxel responses across distinct regions of the visual system. Our findings reveal the current
1067
       best-performing models for this goal, emphasizing practical predictive utility rather than dissecting
1068   the contributions of task versus response optimization in isolation.

       In summary, task optimized models consist of a pretrained CNN core that has been pretrained
       extensively on large datasets such as ImageNet for very specific object oriented tasks. Only the final
1085
       layer of these models are finetuned to predict brain responses. While it would be insightful to explore
1086
       how different task optimized models pretrained on various tasks would perform (as already mentioned
1087   in discussion), such a comparison is beyond the scope of this study.Further, past research Conwell
1088   et al. (2022a) have shown that varying architectures (convolutional such as Mask-RCNN vs non-
1089   convolutional such as transformers) pretrained on different training objectives have minimal impact
1090   on brain predictivity. However, task-optimized models tend to be highly biased toward their specific
1091   training tasks, which limits their capacity for novel insights into brain function. This limitation led to
1092   the development of response-optimized models, which are trained from scratch on neural data with
1093   approximately 3% of the dataset size used for task-optimized models. The architecture needs to be
1094   very biologically intuitive to model the brain as closely as possible, especially since the training diet
1095
       is so small. Merely training any architecture (e.g., AlexNet, ResNet-50, or transformers) on neural
       data does not make it inherently suited for response optimization. Thus, the difference between
1096
       task- and response-optimized models goes beyond whether one is pretrained or not. Instead, it’s
1097
       about selecting the most effective pretrained model (with optimized architecture and dataset) for
1098   task-optimized applications versus the most suitable architecture for response-optimized models that
1099   aligns with neural data constraints.
