009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Weights play an essential role in determining the performance of deep net-
012            works. This paper introduces a new concept termed ”Weight Augmentation strat-
013            egy”(WAS), which emphasizes the exploration of weight spaces rather than tradi-
014            tional network structure design. The core of WAS is the utilization of randomly
015            transformed weight coefficients, referred to as Shadow Weights (SW), for deep
016            networks to calculate the loss function and update the parameters. Differently,
017            stochastic gradient descent is applied to Plain Weights (PW), which is referred to
018            as the original weight of the network before the random transformation. During
019
               training, numerous SW collectively form a high-dimensional space, while PW is
               directly learned from the distribution of SW. To maximize the benefits of WAS,
020
               we introduce two operational modes, i.e., the Accuracy-Priented Mode (AOM)
021
               and the Desire-Oriented Mode (DOM). To be concrete, AOM relies on PW, which
022            ensures that the network remains highly robust and accurate. Meanwhile, DOM
023            utilizes SW, which is determined by the specific objective of our proposed WAS,
024            such as reduced computational complexity or lower sensitivity to particular data.
025            These dual modes can be switched at any time as needed, thereby providing flex-
026            ibility and adaptability to different tasks. By extending the concept of augmenta-
027            tion from data to weights, our WAS offers an easy-to-understand and implement
028            technique that can significantly enhance almost all networks. Our experimen-
029            tal results demonstrate that convolutional neural networks, including VGG-16,
030
               ResNet-18, ResNet-34, GoogleNet, MobileNetV2, and EfficientNet-Lite, benefit
               substantially with little to no additional costs. On the CIFAR-100 and CIFAR-10
031
               datasets, model accuracy increases by an average of 7.32% and 9.28%, respec-
032
               tively, with the highest improvements reaching 13.42% and 18.93%. In addition,
033            DOM can reduce floating point operations (FLOPs) by up to 36.33%.

      Izmailov et al. Izmailov et al. (2018) proposed stochastic weight averaging, which integrates the

081   feature space mapping is challenging, particularly when both the weight space and the feature space
082   are high-dimensional. Figure 1(a) illustrates the traditional training method, where the relationship
083   between data and weights is many-to-one. Model training aims to find weights that correspond to
084   the data. The formula can be expressed as follows:
085

086
087   In this context, Φ represents the function that maps an m-dimensional feature space to the corre-
088   sponding weights. Specifically, Rm is defined as the m-dimensional Euclidean space representing
089   the feature vectors. Likewise, Rn denotes the n-dimensional space of potential weight vectors,
090   illustrating the multidimensional space where the weights reside. Figure 1(b) illustrates our innova-
091   tive strategy, where the relationship between data and weights is conceptualized as many-to-many.
092   Throughout the training process, the weight space becomes increasingly complete. With the ap-
093
      plication of the proposed WAS, the weight space will be further expanded. Instead of seeking the
      optimal weights directly, our approach involves learning the distribution of the entire weight space.
094
      The relationship is mathematically expressed as follows:
095
096                                               Ψ : Rm → R n                                            (2)
097
098   where Ψ denotes the transformation that maps the m-dimensional feature space to the n-dimensional
099
      weight space. To address the shortcomings of traditional methods like Dropout Srivastava et al.
      (2014), which combats overfitting by randomly dropping units during training but does not reduce
100
      computational complexity in inference, we introduce Weight Augmentation Strategy (WAS). This
101
      technology is influenced by both dropout and data augmentation strategies and takes a novel ap-
102   proach by randomly transforming weights during the training phase. These transformed weights,
103   referred to as SW, are employed to compute the loss function and facilitate the update of model
104   parameters. In contrast, during the inference phase, Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) is applied
105   to PW. This dual-weight strategy ensures that the model achieves optimal performance only when
106   PW aligns effectively with the majority of SW. As a result, WAS allows for a more robust system
107   that improves upon the limitations of dropout by considering the computational demands during
      inference.

143   2   R ELATED WORK
144
145
146   With the continuous development of deep learning, researchers have begun to seek more efficient
147
      and economical methods to obtain model weights. WiSE-FT Wortsman et al. (2022) enhances the
      robustness of fine-tuned pre-trained models by integrating the weights of zero-shot and fine-tuned
148
      models. This method can maintain high accuracy and adapts well to changes in data distribution.
149
      However, the performance of WiSE-FT largely depends on the pre-trained model and fine-tuning
150   data. Guo et al. Guo et al. (2020) introduced a collaborative knowledge distillation approach that
151   trains multiple student models simultaneously. This strategy enhances learning efficacy without
152   the need for a separate teacher model, though the selection and quantity of student models impact
153   the outcome and require substantial computational resources. Zhang et al. Zhang et al. (2019)
154   developed a self distillation technique that employs the network as both the student and the teacher,
155   facilitating internal knowledge transfer. It does not require an additional pre-trained teacher model
156   and can improve accuracy without increasing inference time. However, self distillation introduces
157   an additional shallow classifier, which prevents the model convergence and increases the complexity
158
      of training.
159   To overcome the limitations of these methods, we introduce the Weight Augmentation Strategy
160   (WAS). We randomly transform PW during training to obtain SW. PW is used to be compatible
161   with various SW, in order to enhance the robustness of the network and reduce the sensitivity of the
      model to noise.

182   (2002); Krawczyk et al. (2017); Huang et al. (2009) can integrate these seemingly useless weights
183   to form a more powerful and robust model. These conventional approaches of leveraging diverse
184   weights have not fully tapped into the potential of weight augmentation. A robust weight space,
185   where weights are learned from distributions rather than specific data points, can significantly im-
186   prove performance. The augmentation involves transforming weights through various methods such

414
415   WAS endows models with unique capabilities that can be tailored to specific needs during inference.
416
      These capabilities encompass a range of enhancements, including reduced computational complex-
      ity and decreased sensitivity to particular types of data. This functionality is achieved by switching
417
      to SW during inference, allowing for customizable adjustments according to specific requirements.
418
