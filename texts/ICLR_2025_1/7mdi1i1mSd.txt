009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Trajectory prediction aims to forecast an agent’s future trajectories based on its
012            historical observed trajectories, which is a critical task for various applications such
013            as autonomous driving, robotics, and surveillance systems. Most existing trajectory
014            prediction methods assume that the observed trajectories collected for forecasting
015            are clean. However, in real-world scenarios, noise is inevitably introduced into
016            the observations due to errors from sensors, detection, and tracking processes,
017            resulting in the collapse of the existing approaches. Therefore, it is essential to
018            perform robust trajectory prediction based on noisy observations, which is a more
019
               practical scenario. In this paper, we propose NoisyTraj, a noise-agnostic approach
               capable of tackling the problem of trajectory prediction with arbitrary types of noisy
020
               observations. Specifically, we put forward a mutual information-based mechanism
021
               to denoise the original noisy observations. This mechanism optimizes the produced
022            trajectories to exhibit a pattern that closely resembles the clean trajectory pattern
023            while deviating from the noisy one. Considering that the trajectory structure may
024            be destroyed through the only optimization of mutual information, we introduce an
025            additional reconstruction loss to preserve the structure information of the produced
026            observed trajectories. Moreover, we further propose a ranking loss based on the
027            intuitive idea that prediction performance using denoised trajectories should surpass
028            that using the original noisy observations, thereby further enhancing performance.
029            Because NoisyTraj does not rely on any specific module tailored to particular noise
030
               distributions, it can handle arbitrary types of noise in principle. Additionally, our
               proposed NoisyTraj can be easily integrated into existing trajectory prediction
031
               models. Extensive experiments conducted on the ETH/UCY and Stanford Drone
032
               datasets (SDD) demonstrate that NoisyTraj significantly improves the accuracy of
033            trajectory prediction with noisy observations, compared to the baselines.

      The objective of trajectory prediction is to anticipate the future trajectories for agents given their
      past observed trajectories, which is an essential and emerging task in numerous applications, such
040
      as autonomous driving (Phong et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023b; Zhou et al., 2023; 2022), drones
041
      (Corbetta et al., 2019), surveillance systems (Valera & Velastin, 2005), and robotics (Jetchev &
042   Toussaint, 2009; Rösmann et al., 2017). In recent years, trajectory prediction has garnered significant
043   attention in the computer vision and machine learning communities, with numerous methods proposed
044   (Bae et al., 2023; Choi et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b;a). Among these methods, they typically
045   assume the observed historical trajectories are clean, and leverage them to predict future trajectories.
046   Recent advances have demonstrated promising performance in trajectory prediction by learning from
047   such clean observed trajectory data.
048   However, in real-world scenarios, the acquisition of trajectory data is inevitably accompanied by the
049   introduction of noise. For instance, in an autonomous driving system’s trajectory acquisition pipeline,
050   object detection is initially performed to determine the positions and categories of objects. This
051   process is susceptible to noise stemming from sensor errors (e.g., cameras or LiDARs) or inaccuracies
052   in the detection algorithm. Subsequently, object tracking algorithms are employed to associate the
053   same object across multiple timestamps, thereby forming trajectories. At this stage, noise may be
      introduced due to occlusions and inherent errors in the tracking algorithms.

071   The presence of noise in the observed trajectories significantly hinders the performance of existing
072   trajectory prediction methods. To substantiate this point, we corrupt the input observed trajectories by
073   adding noise in both the training and testing stages. We then conduct experiments on the ETH/UCY
074   (Pellegrini et al., 2009; Leal-Taixé et al., 2014) and Stanford Drone datasets (SDD) (Robicquet et al.,
075   2016) using several recently proposed trajectory prediction approaches. We compare the performance
076
      before and after introducing noise to the observations. As shown in Figure 1, the presence of noise
      in the observed trajectories leads to a significant performance drop for various trajectory prediction
077
      methods on both the ETH/UCY and SDD datasets. Specifically, on the ETH/UCY dataset, the Final
078
      Displacement Error (FDE) increases from approximately 0.35-0.45 meters in the clean observation
079   setting to 0.60-0.75 meters in the noisy observation setting. Similarly, on the SDD dataset, the FDE
080   rises from around 13-15 pixels to 18-20 pixels This significant performance degradation highlights
081   the detrimental impact of noise on trajectory prediction accuracy, even for state-of-the-art models.
082   Therefore, it is crucial to devise a robust method for predicting future trajectories based on noisy
083   observations.
084
      In this paper, we propose NoisyTraj, a noise-agnostic method designed to address the challenge of
085   trajectory prediction with arbitrary types of noisy observations. Specifically, we first propose a mutual
086   information-based mechanism to filter noise from the original observations. This mechanism ensures
087   the produced trajectories closely resemble the patterns of noise-free trajectories while deviating
088   from the noisy patterns. To this end, we maximize the mutual information between the produced
089   trajectories and the clean future trajectories (i.e., ground-truth), while simultaneously minimizing
090   the mutual information between the produced trajectories and the original noisy observations. In
091   this way, the produced trajectories are forced to collate information from both the noisy trajectories
092   and clean future trajectories, thereby preserving the necessary information while filtering out noise.
093
      However, solely relying on optimizing mutual information for denoising may disrupt the structure
      of the trajectory. Therefore, we propose to randomly mask several observations and attempt to
094
      reconstruct the masked locations. By jointly optimizing the mutual information and reconstruction
095
      losses, the trajectory denoise model can effectively eliminate noise while preserving the structure
096   information of the trajectory. In the meantime, we design a ranking loss to facilitate the ability of the
097   trajectory prediction module based on an intuitive thought: predictions using the produced denoised
098   observations will be superior to those using noisy observations. It is noteworthy that the ranking loss
099   optimizes not only the trajectory prediction module but also the denoising module, which can further
100   assist in filtering noise to some extent. Since NoisyTraj does not rely on any specific module tailored
101   to a particular noise distribution, it can handle arbitrary noise in principle. Essentially, our proposed
102   NoisyTraj is a plug-and-play approach that is compatible with existing trajectory prediction models,
103   enabling them to gracefully handle cases with noisy observations.
104   Our main contributions are summarized as follows: 1) We investigate a new problem setting for
105   trajectory prediction with noisy observations, addressing a more practical scenario. To tackle this,
106   we propose a noise-agnostic, plug-and-play approach called NoisyTraj. 2) We design a denoising
107   module that incorporates a mutual information-based loss along with a reconstruction loss, effectively
      denoising observed trajectories while preserving their structural information. 3) We propose a ranking

      2.1   T RAJECTORY P REDICTION WITH C LEAN O BSERVATIONS
116
117   Trajectory prediction has been an active area of research in the computer vision and machine learning
118   communities. Early works employ physics-based methods to model the trajectories of agents (Luber
119   et al., 2010; Pellegrini et al., 2009). Subsequently, learning-based approaches are proposed, which
120   significantly enhance the performance of trajectory prediction (Zhu et al., 2023a; Rowe et al., 2023;
121   Wang et al., 2023a; Xu et al., 2023b). They model trajectory temporal information and the interaction
122   between agents (Alahi et al., 2016; Altché & de La Fortelle, 2017; Shi et al., 2022; Xue et al.,
123
      2018; Mohamed et al., 2020). One representative approach is social pooling, which aggregates
      hidden state information of neighbors within a spatial grid (Gupta et al., 2018; Sadeghian et al.,
124
      2019). Additionally, attention mechanisms (Fernando et al., 2018; Vemula et al., 2018), graph neural
125
      networks (Li et al., 2019; Kosaraju et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2020b) and transformers (Yuan et al., 2021;
126   Zhu et al., 2023b; Shi et al., 2023) have also been exploited to model interactions among agents. To
127   further enhance prediction performance, researchers delve into incorporating the map information.
128   Works such as (Shafiee et al., 2021; Dendorfer et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2020a; Mangalam et al., 2021;
129   Meng et al., 2022) encode RGB scene information, while (Ye et al., 2021; Gu et al., 2021; Zhao et al.,
130   2021; Kang et al., 2024) incorporate lane and road traffic information. Moreover, due to the inherent
131   uncertainty associated with agents, researchers have proposed a series of models to predict multiple
132   plausible future trajectories, including GANs (Liang et al., 2021; Li, 2019; Zhao et al., 2019), VAEs
133   (Lee et al., 2022; 2017; Sun et al., 2021), and diffusion models (Rempe et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b;
134
      Jiang et al., 2023). Recently, several new task settings have been introduced to address more practical
      trajectory prediction problems, including momentary trajectory prediction (Li et al., 2024a; Monti
135
      et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024b), long-tailed distribution in trajectory prediction (Zhang
136
      et al., 2024; Mercurius et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023c), and distribution shift in trajectory prediction
137   (Stoler et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2022c; Kong et al., 2024).
138
139   Despite these methods having shown promising performance, they rely on sufficiently clean observed
140
      trajectories. As aforementioned, when the observed trajectories are corrupted by noise, the model
      performance severely deteriorates. In contrast to these approaches, we attempt to tackle the problem
141
      of predicting future trajectories with noisy observed trajectories.

      2.2   T RAJECTORY A NOMALY D ETECTION
144
145   The goal of trajectory anomaly detection is to identify abnormal patterns in trajectories, such as
146   abnormal deviations, trajectory repetitions, and missing segments. Trajectory anomaly detection
147   methods can be categorized as supervised learning, semi-supervised learning, and unsupervised
148   learning approaches (Fan et al., 2009; Quispe-Torres et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2018; Sillito & Fisher,
149   2008; Chebiyyam et al., 2018; Jiao et al., 2023; Mondal et al., 2021; Liatsikou et al., 2021). Supervised
150   anomaly detection entails training a deep supervised binary or multi-class classifier using labeled data
151   of both normal and anomalous trajectories. For instance, the work in (Chebiyyam et al., 2018) extracts
152   statistical features from trajectories to train a multi-class SVM for classifying trajectories as normal
153
      or anomalous. Despite their promising performance, supervised methods require substantial effort to
      label trajectory data. To mitigate this labeling burden, researchers have explored semi-supervised
154
      anomaly detection, where only normal trajectory data are labeled. A common approach involves
155
      employing deep autoencoders trained in a semi-supervised manner (Minhas & Zelek, 2020; Song
156   et al., 2017). These methods assume that the autoencoder will accurately encode and decode normal
157   samples while failing to reconstruct anomalous data. Moreover, in scenarios where labeled data
158   is scarce or unavailable, researchers have proposed unsupervised methods for trajectory anomaly
159   detection by leveraging intrinsic data properties. Clustering is a popular unsupervised technique for
160   trajectory anomaly detection. For example. Hu et al. (2006) clusters trajectories based on spatial and
161   temporal information, and each motion pattern is represented with a chain of Gaussian distributions.
      Then, they detect anomalies based on these motion patterns. Fan et al. (Fan et al., 2009) propose

409
410   Evaluation Metrics. Following previous works (Mao et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2022; Sadeghian et al.,
411   2019; Shi et al., 2021), we employ Average Displacement Error (ADE) and Final Displacement Error
412   (FDE) to evaluate the predicted trajectories. ADE is the average L2 error between all future timesteps,
413   and FDE is the error at the final timestamp. We take the best out of K = 20 predictions to account
414
      for the multi-modality for trajectory prediction, as in (Salzmann et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2022a).
415   Backbones and Compared Baselines. To validate the efficacy of NoisyTraj, we integrate it into
416   two popular trajectory prediction backbones GraphTern (Bae & Jeon, 2023) and EqMotion (Xu
417   et al., 2023a). We first compare our method against five state-of-the-art trajectory prediction models,
418   including GraphTern, EqMotion, MID (Gu et al., 2022), SocialImplict (Mohamed et al., 2022)
419   and SocialVAE (Xu et al., 2022b). These methods take original noisy observations as input to
420
      predict future trajectories. Considering there are few works focusing on trajectory prediction with
      noisy observations, we establish two trajectory denoising baselines by integrating Wavelet and EMA
421
      with the trajectory prediction backbones, respectively, for a more comprehensive comparison. The
422
      Wavelet utilizes the wavelet transform to decompose the signal into multiple scales, obtaining wavelet
423   coefficients of different frequencies. Then the noise in high-frequency coefficients is removed by the
424   thresholding method. The EMA smooths the current trajectory location by taking an exponentially
425   weighted average of the current location and past locations.

      5        C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we investigated an extremely challenging task of trajectory prediction with noisy obser-
534   vations. We proposed NoisyTraj, a framework that simultaneously filters out noise and predicts future
535   trajectories, enabling them to benefit from each other. To remove the noise from the observations,
536   we designed a denoising mechanism by jointly optimizing a mutual information-based loss and a
537   reconstruction loss. Moreover, we devised a ranking loss that requires the prediction performance
538   using denoised observed trajectories to be superior to that using the original noisy observations,
539   thereby further improving the performance of the model. Extensive experiments demonstrated the
      effectiveness of NoisyTraj and its compatibility with various trajectory prediction models.

1055   6.5   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
1056
       The trajectory denoise model ΦTDM is implemented using a 3-layer Transformer with a feature
1057   dimension of 256 and the attention head is set to 4. The number of masked locations is set to 2 in
1058   our experiments. We empirically set the trade-off parameter β to 0.01 and the margin ∆ to 0.05.
1059   Additionally, we set the trade-off parameters α, δ, and γ to 0.01, 1 and 0.01, respectively. For the
1060   Wavelet denoising method, we utilize the Daubechies wavelet to decompose the signals, and the level
