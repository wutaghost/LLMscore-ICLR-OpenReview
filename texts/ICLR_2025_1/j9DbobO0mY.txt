011                                                              A BSTRACT
012
013                  In multimodal machine learning, effectively addressing the missing modality
014                  scenario is crucial for improving performance in downstream tasks such as in
015
                     medical contexts where data may be incomplete. Although some attempts have
                     been made to effectively retrieve embeddings for missing modalities, two main
016
                     bottlenecks remain: the consideration of both intra- and inter-modal context,
017
                     and the cost of embedding selection, where embeddings often lack modality-
018                  specific knowledge.. In response, we propose MoE-Retriever1 , a novel frame-
019                  work inspired by the design principles of Sparse Mixture of Experts (SMoE).
020                  First,MoE-Retriever samples the relevant data from modality combinations,
021                  using a so-called supporting group to construct intra-modal inputs while incorporat-
022                  ing inter-modal inputs. These inputs are then processed by Multi-Head Attention,
023                  after which the SMoE Router automatically selects the most relevant expert, i.e., the
024                  embedding candidate to be retrieved. Comprehensive experiments on both medical
025                  and general multimodal datasets demonstrate the robustness and generalizability
026
                     of MoE-Retriever, marking a significant step forward in embedding retrieval
                     methods for incomplete multimodal data.

051   become a pivotal challenge for enhancing downstream task performance (Baltrušaitis et al., 2018; Guo
052   et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2024a). In practical cases such as clinical and biological settings, modalities

      such as imaging, genetic, and clinical data often contain missing entries due to varying acquisition
055   times, costs, or patient-specific factors (Ma et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022a;b; Wang et al., 2023). To
056   address this, prior approaches primarily focus on two strategies: imputing missing features directly
057   within the input feature space or employing learnable embedding to represent missing features in the
058   latent space. The former often involves some rule-based prior, such as using the population mean
059   to perform imputation. This method does not scale with data, as the imputation method remain
060   fixed when the underlying distribution changes. In contrast, recent research has increasingly turned
061   toward the latter — leveraging learnable embedding to provide more adaptive and context-aware
062   representations for missing modalities (Zhang et al., 2022b; Wang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022a;

      Wu et al., 2024b; Han et al., 2024). However, despite their promise, these learnable embedding-based
      methods still face several critical limitations.
064
065   Intra- & Inter-Modal Context. As illustrated in Figure 1 (a), current methods inadequately address
066   both intra-modal and inter-modal contexts when supplementing missing modalities, often focusing
067   on one or the other. In intra-modal scenarios, the goal is to retrieve embeddings for the missing
068   (target) modality by identifying similar samples (Malitesta et al., 2024). However, existing works
069
      often choose unimodal approaches that primarily address intra-modal context, failing to personalize
      the sample’s heterogeneous context. Conversely, in inter-modal scenarios, it is assumed that modality-
070
      invariant and modality-specific information exists across input modalities, suggesting that missing
071
      modalities can be imputed from the sample’s specific observed modalities (Zhang et al., 2022b;
072   Wang et al., 2023). However, these works do not carefully consider intra-sample information while
073   proceeding with multi-modal fusion. As a result, focusing solely on either intra-modal or inter-modal
074   context leads to incomplete supplementation and limits the model’s ability to effectively leverage the
075   rich multimodal information available in real-world datasets. This highlights the need for a more
076   holistic approach that integrates both perspectives for more accurate and robust imputation of missing
077   modalities.
078   Embedding Selection. Figure 1 (b) illustrates the current state of embedding retrieval. Current
079   methods either treat the learnable or retrieved embeddings as a single embedding (Wang et al., 2023;
080   Han et al., 2024) or use diverse embeddings but require activating all candidates every time a retrieval
081   is performed, using operations like summation, averaging, or attention mechanisms. These methods
082   can incur a high computational cost as the number of samples or modalities grows, and they lack the
083   ability to adapt to diverse observed modality combinations, treating all potential scenarios equally
084   regardless of the specific context. This uniformity in handling observed modalities limits the capacity
085   for more nuanced and context-specific supplementation. For instance, specific knowledge may
086
      be required when certain input modality combinations are present, which is crucial for improving
      downstream task performance.
087
088   Our Approach. To address these challenges, we propose MoE-Retriever, a novel framework for
089   embedding retrieval given a incomplete multimodal data. The main idea of MoE-Retriever is
090   to borrow the desgin principle from the Sparse Mixture of Experts (SMoE), which activates most
091   relevant experts (i.e., embedding candidates) given a specific intra- and inter-modal context within in
092
      a router in a sparse manner. To achieve this, we first begin with genertaing supporting group which is
      based on given modality combination and aim for target (missing) modality, responsible for sampling
093
      intra-modal samples. Next by incoporating inter-modal samples and via Multi-head attention within
094
      this incoproated inputs and router with experts which both includes the shared and modality-specific
095   experts finally retreives the most relevant embedding for target modality. Extensive experiments on
096   two medical datasets (ADNI, MIMIC) and two general machine learning datasets (ENRICO, CMU-
097   MOSI) validate the efficacy and generalizability of our proposed method, consistently demonstrating
098   its robust performance across various multimodal settings.

110   Multimodal Learning with Missing Modality. Multimodal learning has garnered increasing
111   attention in the machine learning community, particularly in the medical domain, where clinical
112   data is inherently multimodal (Khader et al., 2023; Steyaert et al., 2023). However, in real-world
113   clinical practice, missing modalities are a common challenge (Zhou et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023).
114   To address this issue, one straightforward approach is to leverage generative models to impute the
115   missing modalities (Pan et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2024). Nonetheless, generative modeling of another
116   distribution is a ill-posed problem (Zhang et al., 2022a). In contrast, non-generative approaches have
117   emerged, utilizing techniques such as graph-based modeling (Wu et al., 2024b), and modality fusion
118
      (Zhang et al., 2022b; Wang et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2024). While these methods can harness both
      inter-patient and intra-patient information, they face challenges related to scalability and struggle to
119
      handle fleximodal scenarios (Han et al., 2024), where any combination of modalities may be present.
120
      To improve scalability, FuseMoE (Han et al., 2024) introduced a sparse Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)
121   model aims to be robust to any combination of missing modality scenario. However, despite its
122   scalability advantages, FuseMoE do not explicitly account both the inter-patient and intra-patient
123   relationships simultaneously, limiting its ability to fully utilize the multimodal context of clinical
124   data.
125
      Sparse Mixture-of-Experts (SMoE). SMoE (Shazeer et al., 2017) builds on the traditional Mixture-
126
      of-Experts (MoE) model (Jacobs et al., 1991; Jordan & Jacobs, 1994; Chen et al., 1999; Yuksel et al.,
127   2012) by introducing sparsity, which enhances both computational efficiency and model performance.
128   By selectively activating only the most relevant experts for a specific task, SMoE minimizes overhead
129   and improves scalability, making it particularly useful for complex, high-dimensional datasets across
130   various applications. It has been widely applied in both vision (Riquelme et al., 2021; Lou et al.,
131   2021; Ahmed et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2019; Abbas & Andreopoulos, 2020)
132   and language processing (Lepikhin et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
133   2021; Zuo et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2021). Its capacity to dynamically allocate different network
134   parts to specific tasks (Ma et al., 2018; Aoki et al., 2021; Hazimeh et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023)
135
      or data modalities (Kudugunta et al., 2021) has been explored for various applications (Mustafa
      et al., 2022). Research shows its effectiveness in areas like classification tasks for digital number
136
      recognition (Hazimeh et al., 2021) and medical signal processing (Aoki et al., 2021). However, the
137
      current use of SMoE is often biased toward its role as a backbone design, typically integrated into
138   Transformer architectures to improve embedding representations in fusion or prediction layers. Its
139   potential for more effective use, such as serving as a retriever or supplementing missing embeddings
140   to bridge the feature space and encoder space, remains underexplored.

146   Motivation behind bringing SMoE design. In the context of incomplete multimodal data, only the
147   observed features in the raw feature space can pass through the modality-specific encoder. This raises
148   a critical question: how can we effectively handle samples with missing modalities to provide robust
149   embeddings for the missing features? Ensuring that the embedding space, followed by the fusion and
150   prediction layers, remains trainable through continuous gradient flow is essential. It is important to
151   note that different samples exhibit varying combinations of observed modalities, which necessitates a
152
      personalized approach capable of handling each sample’s unique environment, such as its specific
      modality combination.
153
154   To address this challenge, we introduce the design principles of SMoE (Shazeer et al., 2017). Given a
155   pool of diverse experts (i.e., trainable feed-forward networks), the SMoE architecture enables the
156   automatic and sparse activation of different experts, each specializing in certain knowledge, based on
157   the input scenario. This dynamic routing mechanism effectively mitigates the limitations of static,
158
      one-size-fits-all designs, where learnable embeddings are constrained to a single expert or a fixed
      combination of embeddings without a router. In such static setups, embeddings for missing modalities
159
      are often selected at random, leading to suboptimal performance for downstream tasks.
160

      the total number of experts, and a router, R, which governs the routing mechanism, sparsely selecting

207   3.2     M O E-R E T R I E V E R
208
      The overall framework of MoE-Retriever, along with the detailed procedure, is illustrated in
209
      Figure 2. In essence, the key idea behind MoE-Retriever is to retrieve the most relevant embed-
210   ding for the missing modality by leveraging two contexts: (1) Intra-Modal Context, which samples
211   similar examples from a well-defined supporting group based on the observed modality combination
212   (Sec 3.2.1), and (2) Inter-Modal Context, which considers the sample-specific heterogeneous com-
213   bination of observed modalities (Sec 3.2.2). The next step is (3) Context-Aware Routing, where the
214   expert pool is designed modality-specifically, using both contexts to effectively supplement the target
215   (i.e., missing) modality. Finally, the selected experts and their linear combination with the inputs are
      integrated into a single embedding, which is regarded as the final retrieved embedding (Sec 3.2.3).

      3.2.1    I NTRA -M ODAL C ONTEXT
217
218   We begin with the intra-modal context (column-wise context in Figure 2), where intra-modal refers
219   to the homogeneous modality that matches the target modality we aim to supplement. The rationale
220   for incorporating this context is that, by forming a pool of similar samples, we can capture patterns
221   directly observed across patients, without requiring any additional preprocessing. The observed
222
      pattern can be represented as a modality combination, which reflects similar trends or patterns, i.e.,
      knowledge observed across the samples. Empirically, samples (e.g., patients) with similar observed
223
      modality combinations have shown exhibit analogous characteristics. For instance, patients who
224
      lack the image modality but possess both genetic and clinical modalities may be more likely to
225   display correlations with certain domain-specific traits, such as early-stage diagnosis, mild cognitive
226   impairment, or slower progression rates, often associated with genetic risk factors like the APOE ϵ4
227   allele (Dubois et al., 2023; Jack Jr et al., 2018; Lambert et al., 2013).
228
      To effectively sample from an intra-modal sample pool, we first need to define a modality combination-
229
      specific pool, which we denote as the supporting group. The core idea behind the supporting group
230
      is that, given an observed modality combination and a target (missing) modality, the corresponding
231   group must include the observed modalities as well as the target modality to support the patient’s
232   intra-modal pool. For example, if a sample contains the modalities ‘GC’ and we aim to impute the
233   modality ‘I’ (as illustrated in Figure 2), the supporting group should include samples with ‘GC’ as
234   well as the missing modality ‘I’. Consequently, the supporting group would comprise samples with
235   modality combinations such as ‘IGC’ or ‘IGCB’.
236

252   3.2.2    I NTER -M ODAL C ONTEXT
253
      Beyond intra-modal context, we now consider another critical dimension: inter-modal context
254   (illustrated row-wise in Figure 2). This approach allows us to incorporate personalized context
255   specific to a given sample that would be missed by only considering intra-modal context. As a
256   real-world example, this perspective is particularly meaningful in multimodal medical scenarios such
257   as Alzheimer’s diagnosis. When genetic (G) and clinical (C) data are available but imaging (I) is
258   missing (case of Figure 2), it may suggest the patient is in the early stages of the disease, where less
259   invasive and more accessible modalities are prioritized. Imaging, typically more expensive, may be
260   reserved for later stages when symptoms progress (Dubois et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022). Additionally,
261   genetic and clinical data alone can provide valuable early insights, guiding initial interventions before
262
      resorting to costly imaging techniques (Kim, 2023).
263   Formally, to consider inter-modal context, we directly focus on the observed modalities, i.e., mc (e.g.,
264   (G, C)) for a sample index, i. By doing so, we integrate these sample-specific heterogeneous modality
265   combinations, which will serve as input for the inter-modal examples in the final retrieval by SMoE,
266   referring to the personalized context within the heterogeneous modalities.

349   Multimodal Medical Datasets. We evaluate MoE-Retriever on two real-world multimodal
350   medical datasets. ADNI Dataset: The Alzheimer’s Disease Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI) is
351
      pivotal for Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) research, aggregating multimodal data on disease evolution and
      biomarkers including four modalities, image modality (MRI and PET scans), genetic profiles, clinical
352
      metrics, and biospecimen samples, with open access for research standardization (Weiner et al., 2010;
353
      2017). After preprocessing, we extract 2380 samples and target a three-tier classification task of AD
354   stages: Dementia, Cognitively Normal (CN), or Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). MIMIC Dataset:
355   MIMIC-IV (Medical Information Mart for Intensive Care IV) is sourced from critical care units,
356   offers both structured (demographics, vitals, labs, medications) and unstructured data (clinical notes).
357   For the experiments, we extracted labs results, clinical notes, and ICD 9 codes from 9,003 patient
358   records to predict a binary classification of one-year mortality prediction (Johnson et al., 2023). For
359   the detailed preprocessing for ADNI and MIMIC dataset, please refer to Appendix A.1.
360   Additional Multimodal Datasets. To demonstrate the generalizbility of MoE-Retriever toward
361   other real-world multimodal domain, we use two general multimodal datasets. CMU-MOSI Dataset:
362   The Multimodal Corpus of Sentiment Intensity (CMU-MOSI) dataset comprising 2,199 annotated
363   video clips, advances affect recognition through detailed sentiment analysis on a scale from -3
364   to +3, utilizing YouTube vlogs for real-world sentiment expression research (Zadeh et al., 2016).
365   ENRICO Dataset: The Enhanced Rico (ENRICO) (Leiva et al., 2020) dataset is a collection of
366   1,460 Android app screens, each comprising an image along with the set of apps and their respective
367   locations. This dataset is organized into 20 distinct design categories, which focuses on a classification
368
      tasks to identify different design motifs.

532   5     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this work, we propose MoE-Retriever , a novel framework inspired by the SMoE design that
535   uniquely integrates both intra-modal and inter-modal contexts. By utilizing a modality combination
536   based supporting group for intra-modal context and modality-specific expert which also include shared
537   experts, MoE-Retriever effectively selects the most relevant expertes, i.e., embeddings tailored
538   to specific missing modality scenarios. Our extensive experiments on both medical and general
539   machine learning datasets demonstrate that MoE-Retriever not only enhances accuracy and
      robustness in missing modality scenarios but also exhibits scalability and computational efficiency.

      A.1.1    D ETAILED DATA P REPROCESSING IN ADNI
750
751   Image Modality To preprocess the image data, we first applied a correction for magnetic field
752   intensity inhomogeneity to ensure consistency and reliability across MRI images. Next, we used
753   the MUSE (Multiatlas Region Segmentation Utilizing Ensembles of Registration Algorithms and
754   Parameters) method to segment gray matter tissue, the primary focus of this study (Doshi et al., 2016).
755   This technique involves utilizing multiple atlases to extract the most accurate region-of-interest
      values from the segmented gray matter. Afterward, voxel-wise volumetric maps of tissue regions

      were created by spatially aligning skull-stripped images to a template in the Montreal Neurological
757   Institute (MNI) space, using a registration method (Ou et al., 2011).
758
759   Genetic Modality We obtained SNP (single nucleotide polymorphisms) data from the ADNI 1,
760
      GO/2, and 3 studies, and pre-processed it as follows. First, SNP data from these studies were
      aligned to a unified reference build using Liftover https://liftover.broadinstitute.
761
      org/, converting all data to NCBI build 37 (UCSC hg19). Next, we aligned strands based on
762
      the 1000 Genome Project phase 3, using McCarthy Group Tools https://www.well.ox.ac.
763   uk/~wrayner/tools/. Linkage disequilibrium (LD) pruning was then applied with parameters
764   (50, 5, 0.1) to remove highly correlated SNPs, reducing the total SNPs from 565, 989 to 144, 746.
765   Imputation was performed on this pruned set using the Michigan Imputation Server https://
766   imputationserver.sph.umich.edu/index.html#!, and the resulting SNP data was
767   recoded as {0, 1, 2}.
768
      Biospecimen Modality Biospecimen data was extracted from several ADNI-provided csv files.
769   CSF Aβ1-42 and Aβ1-40 data were taken from ISOPROSTANE_09May2024.csv, Total Tau and
770   Phosphorylated Tau from UPENNBIOMK_ROCHE_ELECSYS_09May2024.csv, Plasma Neurofila-
771   ment Light Chain data from batemanlab_20221118_09May2024.csv, and ApoE genotype data from
772   APOERES_09May2024.csv. Numerical data was scaled using a MinMax scaler to a range of -1 to 1,
773   while categorical data was one-hot encoded. For missing values, we imputed the mean for numerical
774   fields and the mode for categorical fields.
775   Clinical Modality Clinical data was extracted from ADNI csv files, including MED-
776   HIST_09May2024.csv, NEUROEXM_09May2024.csv, PTDEMOG_09May2024.csv, REC-
777   CMEDS_09May2024.csv, and VITALS_09May2024.csv. During preprocessing, we excluded the
778   columns ’PTCOGBEG,’ ’PTADDX,’ and ’PTADBEG,’ which contain direct Alzheimer’s Disease
779   diagnosis information. Numerical data was scaled using a MinMax scaler (-1 to 1), while categorical
780   data was one-hot encoded. Missing values were imputed by using the mean for numerical columns
781   and the mode for categorical columns.

      A.6   G RADIENT C ONFLICT IN CMU-MOSI DATASET
865
866   In Table 4 of the main paper, we observe that when three modalities (V+A+T ) are used, the
867   performance for all models does not reach its peak, even compared to using two modalities (V+T
868   or A+T ). This highlights an intriguing phenomenon: adding more modalities does not always
869   guarantee improved performance. To investigate this from an optimization perspective, we analyze
870   the gradients when all modalities are provided. Specifically, we compute the derivative of the loss
871   with respect to each modality and measure the cosine similarities between modality pairs to detect
872
      potential gradient conflicts. Higher cosine similarity indicates positive correlation between gradients,
      while lower values suggest conflicts.
873
874   In Figure 5(a), where dense models such as ShaSpec (Wang et al., 2023) or mmFormer (Zhang
875   et al., 2022b) (leveraging fully connected layers or Transformers) are used in the fusion layer,
876   V+A shows positive synergy. However, other pairs, such as V+T and A+T , exhibit less positive
877   interactions, leading to challenges in simultaneously optimizing V+A+T and negatively impacting
878
      overall performance. In contrast, as shown in Figure 5(b), sparse models like FuseMoE (Han et al.,
      2024), which use SMoE in the fusion layer, demonstrate improved synergy due to SMoE’s ability to
879
      selectively activate the most relevant experts, thereby reducing interference between modalities.
880
881   Finally, in our approach (MoE-Retriever), as shown in Figure 5(c), SMoE is applied prior to the
882   fusion layer to retrieve missing modalities before the fusion step. This design further enhances the
883   synergy between modalities, resulting in better optimization compared to other baselines, achieving
884
      the best performance (65.21 for V+A+T ). However, it still does not outperform the model’s per-
      formance when using two modalities (66.13 for A+T ). These findings highlight the importance of
885
      addressing gradient conflicts and carefully synergizing modalities, suggesting a promising direction
886
      for future research in multi-modal learning.
