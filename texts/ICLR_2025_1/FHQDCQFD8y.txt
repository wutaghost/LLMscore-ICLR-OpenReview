011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The visualization and interpretability of electroencephalogram (EEG) decoding
014            significantly contribute to brain-computer interfaces (BCI) and cognitive neuro-
015            science. Although some existing research has attempted to map EEG features to
016            specific brain regions, these approaches fail to fully utilize raw signals and lack
017            extensibility to other Deep Learning (DL) models. In this work, Grad-TopoCAM
018            (Gradient-Based Topographic Class Activation Map) is proposed, which enhances
019            interpretability in DL models for EEG decoding adaptively. Grad-TopoCAM cal-
020
               culates the gradient of feature maps for the target class at the target layer. The
               weights of the feature maps are obtained through global average pooling of the
021
               gradients. The class activation map is generated by performing a linear com-
022
               bination of weights and feature maps, which is subsequently mapped to different
023            brain regions. Grad-TopoCAM is validated across eight DL models on four public
024            datasets. Experimental results indicate that Grad-TopoCAM effectively identifies
025            and visualizes brain regions that significantly influence decoding outcomes, while
026            also facilitating channel selection for different decoding tasks. The code and data
027            are open-source.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Electroencephalogram (EEG) decoding is the cornerstone of brain-computer interfaces (BCI) (Ji
034   et al., 2024). The introduction of interpretability and visualization methods not only enhances the
035   transparency and reliability of models but also promotes deeper exploration in neuroscience and
036   clinical applications (Miao et al., 2023). By employing such methods, researchers can gain insight
037   into which brain regions the model emphasizes during decision-making processes, thereby high-
038   lighting the critical roles of specific brain regions in brain activity and offering valuable guidance
039
      for future neuroscience research (Zong et al., 2024).
040   Despite the remarkable progress of Deep Learning (DL) as an end-to-end ”black-box” method in
041   various fields, (Phan-Trong et al., 2023) its inherent opacity poses significant challenges to inter-
042   pretability. While interpretability techniques such as Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017) and LIME
043   (Ribeiro et al., 2016) have been extensively applied in Computer Vision (CV) and Natural Lan-
044   guage Processing (NLP), their use in EEG signal decoding remains underexplored. Current EEG
045
      decoding research predominantly relies on complex, indirect approaches for interpretability anal-
      ysis (Sujatha Ravindran & Contreras-Vidal, 2023). In some studies, EEG signals are transformed
046
      into two-dimensional feature maps (Qian et al., 2024), (Ding et al., 2023), or multi-channel signals
047
      are mapped into two-dimensional matrices (Li et al., 2020), followed by visualization with Grad-
048   CAM. However, these methods struggle to reveal the specific brain regions that deep models focus
049   on during decision-making. Though some studies have designed specific algorithms for proposed
050   methods to visualize brain region features (Cai & Zeng, 2024), these approaches generally lack gen-
051   eralizability and are not easily adaptable for feature visualization across any target network layer.
052   Consequently, current interpretability and visualization methods of EEG decoding still lack a uni-
053   versal interpretability method that can directly map model-decision features to corresponding brain
      region activity.

054
      In this work, we propose a universal interpretability and visualization method, Grad-TopoCAM
055   (Gradient-Based Topographic Class Activation Map), which directly maps EEG features to brain
056   regions in EEG decoding. When the raw EEG signals are input into the DL model, Grad-TopoCAM
057   computes the gradients of the feature maps at the target layer for the predicted class. The gradients
058   are then globally averaged to calculate the feature map weights. By linearly combining the weights
059   with the feature maps, class activation maps are generated for the target class. The class activation
060   maps are subsequently mapped to different brain regions, illustrating the varying contributions of
061   each brain area in EEG decoding.
062   We summarize our contributions below.
063
064         1. We propose Grad-TopoCAM, a class-discriminative localization technique that generates
065            visualizations of salient brain region features from DL models without requiring modifica-
066
               tions to the architecture or retraining.
067         2. Grad-TopoCAM has been validated across eight different DL models and four publicly
068            available datasets, with the salient brain features aligning with established findings in cog-
069            nitive neuroscience.
070         3. Grad-TopoCAM is applied to the multi-layer convolutional structure of the EEGNet net-
071            work. As the convolutional layers of EEGNet deepen, Grad-TopoCAM reveals the feature
072            variations of different brain regions in the EEG decoding decision-making process.
073         4. The visualizations of salient brain region features generated by Grad-TopoCAM can be
074            utilized to identify key brain areas, facilitating EEG channel selection.
075
      The remainder of this article is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the related work. Section
076   3 describes the proposed method. Section 4 presents the datasets, DL models, and brain topogra-
077   phy. Section 5 describes the discussion. Finally, Section 6 presents the conclusion and future work
078   directions.

      2   R ELATED WORKS
081
082
      Early EEG visualization methods primarily rely on topographic maps (Vafaei et al., 2023), (Cline
083   et al., 2023) and time-frequency (Cai et al., 2022), (Kiselev et al., 2022) representations. Although
084   these methods display the characteristics of raw EEG signals (Ding et al., 2023), (Currey et al.,
085   2023), (Shi et al., 2024), they fail to reveal the brain regions that play a critical role in EEG decod-
086   ing. With the introduction of DL, researchers explore how to apply the interpretability of DL models
087   to EEG signal decoding. In the existing studies, Li et al. (2022) convert EEG signals into brain to-
088   pography images, train these images, and employ Grad-CAM for visualization. Nevertheless, this
089   method does not fully utilize the raw signals, resulting in limitations in interpretability. Moreover,
090   Qian et al. (2024) utilize Grad-CAM to visualize features on the time-frequency representation of
091
      EEG signals but are unable to accurately identify the brain regions that significantly contribute to the
      results. This shortcoming renders the interpretation of the model insufficient. To enhance the direct
092
      utilization of raw EEG signals, Li et al. (2020) and Cui et al. (2022) propose mapping multi-channel
093
      signals into two-dimensional matrices and inputting them into a two-dimensional convolutional neu-
094   ral network (CNN) for training. This approach aims to achieve image-like feature visualization of
095   the matrices, ultimately generating visualization results. Even so, while this method somewhat im-
096   proves interpretability, it remains limited by the necessity of employing a two-dimensional convo-
097   lutional structure within the neural network. To overcome this limitation, Cai & Zeng (2024), Song
098   et al. (2022) and Miao et al. (2023) propose various EEG decoding models that simultaneously en-
099   able feature visualization across specific network layers corresponding to different brain regions.
100   Despite these advancements, these feature visualization methods require additional specific design,
101   limiting their applicability for direct feature mapping across different target network layers and lack-
102
      ing generalizability and flexibility. Therefore, exploring universal interpretability and visualization
      methods that effectively link model features to brain region activity is essential.

      4.1   DATASETS AND PREPROCESS
174
      Dataset I: The BCI Competition IV Dataset 2a (Tangermann et al., 2012), provided by Graz Univer-
175   sity of Technology, contains EEG recordings from nine healthy participants. The EEG signals were
176   acquired using a 10–20 electrode system with 22 Ag/AgCl electrodes, sampled at 250 Hz. Each
177   participant was instructed to perform four distinct motor imagery tasks: imagining movements of
178   the left hand, right hand, both feet, and tongue. The data was filtered to [4, 40] Hz using a band-pass
179   filter.
180
      Dataset II: Nieto et al. (2022) developed an inner speech EEG dataset consisting of 10 native
181   Spanish-speaking participants. EEG recordings were acquired using the 10-20 system with 128
182   EEG channels and 8 external EOG/EMG channels at a sampling rate of 1024 Hz. Participants were
183   instructed to silently articulate four Spanish words: “arriba” (up), “abajo” (down), “derecha” (right),
184   and “izquierda” (left). During preprocessing, the data were re-referenced using earlobe channels and
185   filtered with a band-pass filter ranging from 0.5 to 100 Hz, along with a notch filter at 50 Hz. The
186   sampling rate was then downsampled to 254 Hz. Independent component analysis (ICA) was em-
187   ployed to remove artifacts, ensuring signal quality.
188   Dataset III and Dataset IV: Li et al. (2024) developed a silent reading EEG dataset, which includes
189   data from a single participant who is a native Mandarin speaker and proficient in English as a second
190   language. The EEG activity was recorded over 26 days while the participant silently read seven
191   Chinese words and nine English words. The Chinese words included: “你”, “去”, “天”, “头”,
192   “来”, “水”, and “说” , while the English words were: “apple”, “book”, “come”, “cup”, “go”,
193   “head”, “stand”, “water”, and “you”. EEG signals were collected using 64 electrodes (with 59 EEG
194   channels and 5 body function channels) based on the 10-20 system, at a sampling rate of 1000 Hz.
195
      Dataset III consists of Chinese words and Dataset IV consists of English words

197   4.2   OVERVIEW OF DL MODELS
198
199
      Eight different DL models are employed to train and test across four distinct datasets. The models
      are summarized below.
200
201   ConvNet (Schirrmeister et al., 2017) family includes multiple convolution and pooling layers. Shal-
202   lowConvNet uses a single convolutional layer, while DeepConvNet leverages multiple convolutional
203   layers to capture more complex features.
204   EEGNet (Lawhern et al., 2018) is a compact CNN model. It consists of three key components:
205   one-dimensional convolution for temporal feature extraction, depthwise separable convolution for
206   spatial feature learning, and a fully connected layer for classification.
207
      RACNN (Fang et al., 2020) is a novel regional attention convolutional neural network that extracts
208
      spectral-spatial-temporal features. It aggregates spectral-temporal features produced by a convolu-
209   tional neural network into fixed-length features.
210
211   EEG-ChannelNet (Palazzo et al., 2020) consists of a series of convolutional modules that initially
212
      extract temporal and spatial features using 1D convolutions. These features are refined through
      residual layers, with final predictions generated via convolutional and fully connected layers.
213
214   Conformer (Song et al., 2022) is a compact Convolutional Transformer model. It captures both local
215   and global features using convolutional layers and self-attention modules. A simple fully connected
      classifier is then employed to predict EEG categories.

356   5.2   C HANNEL SELECTION ON EFFICIENCY AND FERFORMANCE
357
      Through Grad-TopoCAM visualization, the contribution of different brain regions to classification
358
      tasks can be determined. Channel rankings for each label are calculated based on their individual
359
      significance, and these rankings are weighted and summed to derive the final channel sequence for
360   each subject. As shown in Table 4, “Full Channel Signals” refers to the use of all original signal
361   channels, while “Selected Channel Signals” represents the use of the top half of the channels with
362   the highest contributions.
363
      Channel selection significantly optimizes both the parameter and computational demands of the
364
      models. For example, in EEGNet, the parameter complexity decreases from 130.245M to 59.175M,
365
      and the computational count is reduced from 213.748K to 86.772K. This reduction substantially
366   lowers the computational burden while maintaining good performance, making real-time processing
367   and application more feasible. Similarly, other models such as LMDA-Net and ShallowConvNet
368   also show marked reductions in parameter and computation requirements, laying a foundation for
369   practical deployment.
370
      Channel selection not only improves computational efficiency but also enhances classification per-
371
      formance by focusing on brain regions that are most crucial for the task, as shown in Table 5.
372   For instance, ShallowConvNet’s accuracy for subject S06 increases by 20.0%, and DeepConvNet
373   demonstrates consistent performance across multiple tasks. This suggests that the channel selected
374   is beneficial for the classification performance. Additionally, D-FaST also achieves a balance be-
375   tween accuracy and computational efficiency in certain tasks. However, some models experience
376   a drop in classification performance after channel selection. For instance, EEGNet’s accuracy de-
