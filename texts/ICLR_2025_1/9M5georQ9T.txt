009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Attention mechanisms have revolutionized several domains of artificial intelligence,
012
               such as natural language processing and computer vision, by enabling models
               to selectively focus on relevant parts of the input data. While recent work has
013            characterized the optimization dynamics of gradient descent (GD) in attention-
014            based models and the structural properties of its preferred solutions, less is known
015            about more general optimization algorithms such as mirror descent (MD). In this
016            paper, we investigate the convergence properties and implicit biases of a family
017
               of MD algorithms tailored for softmax attention mechanisms, with the potential
               function chosen as the p-th power of the ‚Ąďp -norm. Specifically, we show that
018
               these algorithms converge in direction to a generalized hard-margin SVM with
019            an ‚Ąďp -norm objective when applied to a classification problem using a softmax
020            attention model. Notably, our theoretical results reveal that the convergence rate
021            is comparable to that of traditional GD in simpler models, despite the highly
022            nonlinear and nonconvex nature of the present problem. Additionally, we delve
               into the joint optimization dynamics of the key-query matrix and the decoder,
023
               establishing conditions under which this complex joint optimization converges to
024            their respective hard-margin SVM solutions. Lastly, our numerical experiments on
025            real data demonstrate that MD algorithms improve generalization over standard
026            GD and excel in optimal token selection.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
      Attention mechanisms (Bahdanau et al., 2014) have transformed natural language processing (NLP)
031   and large language models (LLMs). Initially developed for encoder-decoder recurrent neural networks
032   (RNNs), attention enables the decoder to focus on relevant input segments rather than relying solely on
033   a fixed-length hidden state. This approach became fundamental in transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017),
034   where attention layers‚ÄĒcomputing softmax similarities among input tokens‚ÄĒare the architecture‚Äôs
035   backbone. Transformers have driven rapid advancements in NLP with models like BERT (Devlin
      et al., 2019) and ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2023), and have become the preferred architecture for generative
036
      modeling (Chen et al., 2021b; Ramesh et al., 2021), computer vision (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021;
037   Radford et al., 2021), and reinforcement learning (Driess et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2021a). This has
038   led to increased exploration of the mathematical foundations of attention‚Äôs optimization.
039
      To understand optimization dynamics of attention mechanisms, Tarzanagh et al. (2024; 2023) studied
040   the implicit bias of gradient descent (GD) in a binary classification setting with a fixed linear decoder.
041   This bias refers to the tendency of GD to learn specific weight characteristics when multiple valid
042   solutions exist. For example, in linear logistic regression on separable data, GD favors solutions
043   aligned with the max-margin class separator (Soudry et al., 2018; Ji & Telgarsky, 2018). Similarly,
044
      Tarzanagh et al. (2023; 2024) propose a model resembling a hard-margin Support Vector Machine
      (SVM)‚ÄĒspecifically, (‚Ąďp -AttSVM) with p = 2‚ÄĒwhich maximizes the margin between optimal
045   and non-optimal input tokens based on their softmax logits. These studies show that as training
046   progresses, the combined key-query weights W (k) of the model increasingly align with the locally
                            őĪ
047   optimal solution Wmm     ‚ÄĒthe minimizer of (‚Ąďp -AttSVM) with p = 2. Expanding on these insights,
048   Vasudeva et al. (2024a) explores global directional convergence and the convergence rate of GD under
049   specific conditions. Sheen et al. (2024) further extends these findings by relaxing assumptions about
      the convergence of regularized paths for the (WK , WQ ) parameterization of the key-query matrix,
050
      showing that gradient flow minimizes the nuclear norm of the key-query weight W = WK WQ‚ä§ .
051

053   selection properties of attention mechanisms, their analyses are limited to simplistic GD models.

793   Transformers Optimization. Recently, the study of optimization dynamics of attention mechanisms
794   has garnered significant attention (Deora et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2023b; Fu
795   et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Tarzanagh et al., 2024; 2023; Vasudeva et al., 2024a; Sheen et al., 2024;
796   Deng et al., 2023; Makkuva et al., 2024; Jeon et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2023; Collins et al., 2024;
797
      Chen & Li, 2024; Li et al., 2023; Sheen et al., 2024; Ildiz et al., 2024; Vasudeva et al., 2024b;
      Bao et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a; Huang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024). We
798   discuss the works most closely related to this paper. Studies such as Sahiner et al. (2022); Ergen
799   et al. (2022) investigate the optimization of attention models through convex relaxations. Jelassi et al.
800   (2022) demonstrate that Vision Transformers (ViTs) identify spatial patterns in binary classification
801   via gradient methods. Li et al. (2023) provide sample complexity bounds and discuss attention
802
      sparsity in SGD for ViTs. Nguyen et al. (2024) provided static primal-dual formulations for attention
      mechanisms, connecting self-attention to support vector regression (SVR). Nguyen et al. (2022)
803
      connects self-attention to kernel methods to enhance Transformers. Chen et al. (2024b) provided a
804   novel attention mechanism that optimizes self-attention in Transformers using asymmetric Kernel
805   Singular Value Decomposition (KSVD) in the primal representation, achieving improved efficiency
806   and performance through low-rank approximations and regularization techniques. However, these
807   works do not examine optimization dynamics, the role of descent algorithms, or the implications of
      implicit bias in training, which are the main focus of this work.
808
809   Oymak et al. (2023) and Deora et al. (2023) explore optimization dynamics in prompt-attention and
      multi-head attention models, respectively. Tian et al. (2023a;b) study SGD dynamics and multi-


                                                        15
810
      layer transformer training. Tarzanagh et al. (2024; 2023) explored GD‚Äôs implicit bias for attention
811   optimization. Vasudeva et al. (2024a) discusses the global directional convergence and convergence
812   rate of GD for attention optimization under specific data conditions. Sheen et al. (2024) notes that
813   gradient flow not only achieves minimal loss but also minimizes the nuclear norm of the key-query
814   weight W . Thrampoulidis (2024); Li et al. (2024); Zhao et al. (2024) also studied the optimization
815
      dynamics of attention mechanisms and provided the implicit bias of GD for next token prediction.
      Our work extends these findings and those of Tarzanagh et al. (2024; 2023), focusing on the implicit
816   bias of the general class of MD algorithms for attention training.
817
818
      Implicit Bias of First Order Methods. In recent years, significant progress has been made in
      understanding the implicit bias of gradient descent on separable data, particularly highlighted by the
819   works of Soudry et al. (2018); Ji & Telgarsky (2018). For linear predictors, Nacson et al. (2019); Ji
820   & Telgarsky (2021); Ji et al. (2021) demonstrated that gradient descent methods rapidly converge
821   to the max-margin predictor. Extending these insights to MLPs, Ji & Telgarsky (2020); Lyu &
822   Li (2019); Chizat & Bach (2020) have examined the implicit bias of GD and gradient flow using
823   exponentially-tailed classification losses, and show convergence to the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT)
      points of the corresponding max-margin problem, both in finite Ji & Telgarsky (2020); Lyu & Li
824
      (2019) and infinite width scenarios Chizat & Bach (2020). Further, the implicit bias of GD for training
825   ReLU and Leaky-ReLU networks has been investigated, particularly on orthogonal data Phuong
826   & Lampert (2020); Frei et al. (2022). Additionally, the implicit bias towards rank minimization in
827   regression settings with square loss has been explored in Vardi & Shamir (2021); Arora et al. (2019);
828   Li et al. (2020).
829   Our work is closely related to the implicit bias of MD (Gunasekar et al., 2018; Azizan & Hassibi, 2018)
830   for regression and classification, respectively. Specifically, Sun et al. (2022) extended the findings of
831   Gunasekar et al. (2018); Azizan & Hassibi (2018) to classification problems, and developed a class
832
      of algorithms exhibiting an implicit bias towards a generalized SVM with ‚Ąďp norms that effectively
      separates samples based on their labels; for a survey, we refer to Vardi (2023).

       H.4    A RCHITECTURE D ETAILS FOR S TANFORD L ARGE M OVIE R EVIEW C LASSIFICATION
1945
1946   The architecture for the model used to perform the semantic analysis task on the Stanford Large
1947   Movie Review dataset follows the transformer encoder architecture from Vaswani et al. (2017) with a
1948   linear classifier as the last layer.
1949   The embedding layer has trainable token embedding E and position encoding P . The model‚Äôs
1950   vocabulary size is 30522 with maximum token length of 512 and embedding dimension 384, so
1951   E ‚ąą R30522√ó384 and P ‚ąą R512√ó384 . If a token t is in position i, its embedding will be Xi = Et + Pi ,
1952   where Et and Pi denote the tth and ith rows of E and P , respectively.
1953   Then, the token features get passed through the encoding blocks, each of which consists of a multi-
1954   head self-attention layer MultiHead, two layer-normalization layers LayerNorm1 and LayerNorm2 ,
1955   and a Multilayer Perceptron layer (MLP). If the sequence of input token features for the encoding
1956   block are X1 , . . . , XT , and if we denote MultiHead(X1 , . . . , XT )i as the ith token feature from the
1957   multi-head self-attention, the output of the encoding block for the ith token is LayerNorm2 (Xi‚Ä≤ +
1958   MLP(Xi‚Ä≤ )), where Xi‚Ä≤ = LayerNorm1 (Xi + MultiHead(X1 , . . . , XT )i ). When training the model,
1959
       we apply dropout of 0.2 for regularization.
1960   The MultiHead attention is a variant of what is known as the single-head attention that horizontally
1961   stacks several instances of single-head attention within the same layer. A single-head attention is
1962   equivalent to (2), but with the vector z replaced with the matrix X ‚ä§ , and the vector v replaced with a
1963
       matrix V .
1964   We experimented with having 3 encoding blocks with 3 attention heads each, 4 encoding blocks
1965   with 4 attention heads each, and 6 encoding blocks with 6 attention heads each. Finally, we pass the
1966
       feature vector of the first token from the last encoding layer into a linear classifier.
