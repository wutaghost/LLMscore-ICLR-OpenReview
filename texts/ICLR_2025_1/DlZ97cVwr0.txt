009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Most current benchmarks evaluate Generative Language Models based on the ac-
012            curacy of the generated output. However, in some scenarios, evaluating the recall
013            of the generations is more valuable, i.e., whether a model can generate all correct
014            outputs, such as all security vulnerabilities of a given codebase. There are two
015            challenges in evaluating the recall: the lack of complete sets of correct outputs for
016            any task and many distinct but similar outputs (e.g., two exploits that target the
017
               same vulnerability).
018            In this paper, we propose a benchmark from the domain of small organic
019
               molecules. We define several sets of molecules of varying complexity and fine-
               tune language models on subsets of those sets. We characterize set complexities
020
               via recall of model generations and show that we can predict the recall for a given
021
               number of generations in advance, using only perplexity on a held-out validation
022            set. This prediction method extends to several i.i.d. sampling generation meth-
023            ods. Subsequently, we propose a novel decoding method based on beam search
024            that maximizes recall by avoiding duplicates. Finally, we design a recall-aware
025            loss function that leverages intuition from prior experiments to improve model re-
026            call for small language models. We perform additional analyses on the impact of
027            molecular representation and model pretraining.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Evaluating the performance of generative models, particularly language models, is an important
033
      challenge in modern deep learning (Chang et al., 2024). Most of the current benchmarks evaluate
      the ability of models to produce correct output, e.g., correctly answer questions, translate a sentence,
034
      generate a coherent story on a given topic, etc.
035
036   An overlooked aspect of evaluation is the ability of generative models to generate all correct outputs
037   for the given input. This capability is crucial for security-focused applications. In software code
038   analysis, generating exploits that target all vulnerabilities (Liguori et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023) is
039
      a significant challenge. In language model security, one would like to find all “jailbreaks”, i.e., the
      prompts that would force the target model to produce undesired outputs (Samvelyan et al., 2024),
040
      which allows for patching language models in the post-training phase before making them publicly
041
      available. In healthcare, AI assistants can suggest causes for the given symptoms, and sometimes, it
042   is desirable to list not only the most probable, but all possible conditions that could produce those
043   symptoms. In scientific discovery, generating new molecules or materials with given characteristics
044   is a cornerstone problem. For example, in drug discovery, most of the correctly generated molecules
045   may prove useless in subsequent phases of drug development (e.g., in toxicity analysis), so gen-
046   erating a diverse and complete set of initial molecules is useful. Another related problem is the
047   exhaustive generation of all conformations (3D positions) for a given molecule. These conforma-
048   tions then become inputs for multiple downstream tools in drug discovery (Watts et al., 2010).
049   The ability of language models to cover all correct outputs without incorporating retrieval has yet
050   to be systematically evaluated. We believe there are two significant obstacles. First, developing a
051   benchmark that includes all correct outputs is hard. Second, the representations of the objects we are
052   trying to generate are typically not unique. For example, the same security exploit can be written in
053   multiple ways by changing variable names or refactoring; the same health condition can be described
      in different terms. More formally, the set of correct outputs is usually split into equivalence classes.

054
      An ideal evaluation benchmark should ignore multiple generations from the same equivalence class
055   and count the number of distinct classes a generative model can cover.
056
057   In this paper, we propose a benchmark that overcomes both obstacles and enables research into opti-
058
      mizing the recall of language models. We start from GDB-13 (Blum & Reymond, 2009), a complete
      database of molecules with specific characteristics and, most significantly, having at most 13 heavy
059
      atoms. We design four sets of molecules of varying complexity and train language models on small
060
      subsets of them. We represent molecules by SELFIES strings (Krenn et al., 2020), for which the
061   equivalence classes are well-defined and can be computed algorithmically. We use the models to
062   generate millions of molecules and evaluate the precision and recall of the generations by leverag-
063   ing information about equivalence classes. We show that we can predict recall in advance without
064   performing generation and devise novel recall-optimized generation methods and loss functions.
065   We hope that this system for LLM evaluation will prove to be a valuable tool in other domains, like
066   security, where the same software vulnerability can be exploited with similar attacks or the same
067   language model exploit can be triggered with different wording.

      2.1   E VALUATING M OLECULAR G ENERATION
072
073   A variety of methods have been successfully applied to molecular generation tasks, including
074   GFlowNets (Bengio et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2024), recurrent neural networks (Guo & Schwaller,
075   2023; Blaschke et al., 2020), and graph-based genetic algorithms (Jensen, 2019) among others.
076   Finally, LLMs have recently demonstrated strong performance on these tasks, especially when com-
077   bined with iterative training, prompt design, and reinforcement learning methods (Wang et al., 2024;
078   Guevorguian et al., 2024; Guo & Schwaller, 2024). In order to directly compare these methods,
079   benchmarks in molecular generation and molecule optimization emphasize measuring the number
080
      of molecules generated under constraints that satisfy pre-specified criteria. For instance, the Practi-
      cal Molecular Optimization benchmark involves a suite of tasks evaluated using the area under the
081
      curve of the scores of generations throughout the optimization process (Gao et al., 2022). However,
082
      this does not measure the diversity of generations, a critical criterion for generative models in indus-
083   trial settings. Similarly, benchmarks involving docking simulations count the number of molecules
084   generated that exceed certain property thresholds (Lee et al., 2024; Guo & Schwaller, 2023).
085
086
      2.2   R ECOVERING M OLECULAR DATASETS
087
088   Blum & Reymond (2009) introduced GDB-13, a complete set of small molecules with certain char-
089   acteristics. Arús-Pous et al. (2019) attempted to re-generate the GDB-13 by training a recurrent
090   neural network (RNN) on 1 million randomly sampled molecules. Arús-Pous et al. (2019) explored
091   the effect of SMILES randomization on the recall. This line of papers motivated our work to explore
092   the recall abilities of modern language models. However, the set of molecules these papers were
093   trying to recover are simple and do not require significant knowledge of chemistry, as they can be
094   described as all possible graphs with certain rules on node labels and graph properties. The sets we
095
      introduce in this paper have varying complexity and rely on more complex chemical features.
096
097   2.3   R ECALL OF G ENERATIVE M ODELS
098
099
      For generative image models, the need for and introduction of precision and recall-based evalua-
      tion methods is an extension of Fréchet Inception Distance (Heusel et al., 2017), which allow for
100
      a more comprehensive understanding of tradeoffs between the quality and diversity of generated
101
      images. In a seminal work, (Sajjadi et al., 2018) demonstrated a method to estimate these metrics by
102   comparing dataset and model distributions, with subsequent works making these estimations more
103   accurate (Kynkäänniemi et al., 2019; Park & Kim, 2023) and efficient to implement (Liang et al.,
104   2024). Further research has been done to provide stronger theoretical motivation and a unifying
105   framework for these methods (Sykes et al., 2024). Evaluating language models in terms of recall is
106   a comparatively new direction, with fewer existing application methods and theoretical background.
107   Several benchmark datasets evaluate models’ ability to recall information from a provided corpus of
      text (Amouyal et al., 2022; Akhtar et al., 2024). In order to characterize the diversity of generations,


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the MAUVE score describes the divergence criteria via a suite of comparison measures between
109   ground truth and generated texts (Pillutla et al., 2021) , which was expanded and improved upon in
110   (Pimentel et al., 2022) by performing more comprehensive ablations and operating on more granu-
111   lar textual features. The research closest to our research work continues in this direction and uses
112   a K-nearest neighbor estimator on text embeddings of generated text under reduced dimensionality
113   (Bronnec et al., 2024).
114
      For molecular generation tasks, the ability to precisely evaluate the recall of generations for a prede-
115   fined closed set of desired molecules provides more evidence for the effectiveness of a modeling ap-
116   proach compared to current threshold-based evaluatory frameworks. Methods proposed for general
117   recall evaluation of generative models typically focus on other generative architectures (Generative
118   Adversarial Networks, Variational Autoencoders, Gaussian Processes) or operate directly on image
119   data. Existing methods that target LLMs require retrieving data from a text corpus or using a K-
120   nearest neighbors (KNN) estimator to approximate model recall. By contrast, our problem setting
121   allows for the direct calculation of recall. We show that we can predict this recall from quantities al-
122   ready implemented in standard model evaluation pipelines (perplexity) without additional modeling
123
      considerations. Finally, this paper is the only work that proposes methods for improving language
      model recall derived from the recall problem formulation we define.

128   Let S be the set of all correct generations, i.e., strings in the language modeling context. Assume
129   there is an equivalence relation among the strings in S which divides S into M equivalence classes.
130   We denote the set of equivalence classes by Su (u stands for unique). Each equivalence class corre-
131   sponds to a single object. For any object m ∈ Su , the size of its equivalence class, i.e., the number
132   of distinct strings corresponding to that object, is denoted by ∥m∥.
133   The goal is to train a model that can generate strings from the maximum number of equivalence
134   classes, i.e., a maximum number of unique objects. To achieve that, we train a (potentially pre-
135   trained) language model on a subset of M objects. Suppose we also have a validation set, i.e., a
136   subset of V objects (V < M ) distinct from the training set. After training, we generate G strings by
137   sampling from the model. Let G denote all generated strings.
138   We evaluate the model using the following metrics. True positives, denoted by T P , are the gener-
139   ated strings that belong to S. Note that G can contain duplicate strings but may also contain dis-
140   tinct strings that belong to the same equivalence class. Hence we also define unique true positives,
141   T P u = |Gu |, as the number of equivalent classes represented in G. We track two metrics:

      generations). On the contrary, T P u does not scale indefinitely with G as it is upper bounded by
      M = |Su |. Hence, the recall increases with G and can ideally reach M and stay constant. Section

      4     E XPERIMENTS
217
218
      For pretraining, we utilize a large subset of 848 million molecules from GDB-13 that explicitly
219   excludes the four sets defined above. We split this data into a training set and a 10,000-instance
220   validation set. We adopt most of the pretraining settings and model architecture from the OPT
221   1.3B model (Zhang et al., 2022), with additional information in A.1. We represent molecules using
222   SELFIES strings (Krenn et al., 2020). Note that while it guarantees that the generated strings in its
223   alphabet correspond to some molecules, it is not necessarily helpful for the quality of the generated
224   molecules as shown by Skinnider (2024). We perform experiments demonstrating the impacts of
225   the SELFIES representation in the analysis section. Canonical SELFIES correspond to a specific,
226   uniform, rule-based molecular structure traversal. Similarly, we define randomized SELFIES as
227
      corresponding to random traversals of the molecule. Refer to the appendix for more details on
      definitions of canonical and random SELFIES.
228
229   Training is performed from scratch for one epoch over the entire pretraining dataset, ensuring each
230   training string is used exactly once. For fine-tuning, we take our pretrained models and fine-tune
231   them on the four sets, from which we randomly select 1 million molecules as training data. For the
232   baseline models, we utilize the same OPT 1.3B architecture and employ the finetuning procedure on
233
      the four subsets without pretraining.
234   We adopt the following methodology for each subset to construct evaluation sets encompassing
235   nearly all randomized versions of a given set of molecules. We randomly select 500 SELFIES
236   strings from each subset. For each selected SELFIES string, we loop 1 million times and ask the
237   RDKit library to generate an unrestricted randomized version. We finalized the set by removing all
238   duplicates to ensure a unique set of randomized versions for each molecule.

359   4.3   R ECALL - ORIENTED G ENERATION
360
361   The experiments so far used only random sampling. Over the past few years, many papers have
362   focused on improving the sampling procedures of language models. Here, we investigate how basic
363
      sampling strategies affect precision and recall. We then present a new generation method motivated
      by our findings to improve model recall. For the model pretrained on canonical SELFIES and fine-
364
      tuned on canonical ones from Sasp , we performed generation with temperature sampling. High
365
      temperature means high entropy and more diverse generations, simultaneously increasing the risk
366   of incorrect generations. Figure 4 shows how the true positive generations (i.e., precision) drop as
367   the temperature grows. It also shows that the optimal number of unique true positive molecules is
368   achieved with T = 0.8 temperature.
369
      All generation methods studied above suffer from duplicates in G. Beam search, another commonly
370
      used sampling strategy for language models, can be used to mitigate this issue. The regular beam
371
      search is an extension of the greedy algorithm and uses a relatively small beam size to keep the
372   best B generations at each token. At the end of the generation, the algorithm produces B distinct
373   sequences, and the top one is selected. We propose to keep all generations of the beam search
374   and use a beam size equal to G. We used beam search with B = 106 and B = 107 on one of
375   the language models fine-tuned on Sasp . The results are presented in Table 4, demonstrating a
376   significant improvement in recall using this approach. Furthermore, the beam size can be adjusted
377   to modulate precision, or the selection can be limited to the top-ranked generated sequences. We
      leave the prediction of the recall of the generations using beam search to future work.

      4.4   R ECALL - ORIENTED L OSS F UNCTION
412
413
      Our next target is the loss function in the fine-tuning phase. One potential problem is that the
414   models “waste efforts” on generating multiple SELFIES strings of the same molecules. We notice
415   from Tables 3 and 8 that a model trained on randomized SELFIES during both training phases has
416   18.75 percentage points higher precision than recall, which means 1.875 million generated strings
417   are correct (i.e., they belong to Sasp ), but represent the same molecules that are duplicated in the
418   56.40%. Some of these strings are duplicated SELFIES strings, but others are different SELFIES
419   representations of the same molecule. Notably, using canonical SELFIES for both training phases
420   does not improve this gap.
421   In this subsection, we perform an experiment explicitly forcing the network to focus on one SELF-
422   IES only. In contrast to earlier experiments, we enlarge the training set with 8 SELFIES for each
423   of the 1 million molecules. Then, we design the batches such that all variants of the same molecule
424   appear in the same batch. We use batch size 128, which covers 16 molecules with 8 variants each.
425   The loss function during fine-tuning first aggregates the loss over the 8 variants and then takes the
426   average across 16 distinct molecules.
427   We use three kinds of aggregation functions: mean, minimum, and maximum. Mean aggregation
428   is equivalent to the regular loss function; it puts equal weight on all SELFIES strings available to
429   the model. Minimum aggregation forces the model to optimize only the best SELFIES string, i.e.,
430   the one with the lowest loss. The hypothesis is that the model will direct its capacity on the easiest
431   string only. We hypothesize that the rest of its capacity will be allocated to covering more molecules
      (instead of more SELFIES of the “known” molecules), hence increasing the recall. Maximum ag-


                                                                        8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      gregation has the opposite effect, it forces the model to put efforts on optimizing even the hardest
433   SELFIES string.
434
435   Table 5 shows that our hypothesis did not hold generally. Mean aggregation performs the best both
436
      in terms of precision and recall. Diverging from the regular loss function in both directions does
      not help. We thought that the 1.3B parameter model might have too much capacity. Hence, there
437
      is no actual competition between storing information about diverse molecules vs. about various
438
      SELFIES strings of the same molecule. We tried the same experiment with smaller 125M and 800K
439   parameter versions of OPT (pretrained on the same set of canonical SELFIES). Although the same
440   result was observed for the 125M parameter model, the 800K model benefited from the minimum
441   aggregation loss. This discrepancy suggests that there may be a relationship between total model
442   capacity and the benefit gained from the proposed loss formulations. A more detailed investigation
443   of this relationship belongs to future work.

503   5.2.2   C ANONICAL VS R ANDOMIZED R EPRESENTATIONS
504
      In section 4, we use canonical SELFIES representations for pretraining and randomized SELFIES
505
      for fine-tuning. We define Canonical SELFIES of molecules as SELFIES generated via conversion
506
      from the canonical SMILES representation produced by the RDKit library. By assigning unique
507   numbers to each atom in a molecule, RDKit enables a consistent traversal of the molecular structure.
508   This process ensures that the SMILES string generated will always be the same for a given molecule.
509   Likewise, to achieve the random SELFIES representation, we randomize the SMILES strings and
510   then transform them into SELFIES. It is possible to obtain randomized SMILES by altering the
511   atom ordering, which does not change how the algorithm traverses the graph (e.g., depth-first in the
512   case of RDKit) but changes the starting point and the order in which branching paths are selected.
513   Arús-Pous et al. (2019) defines two versions of randomization: restricted and unrestricted. The
514   unrestricted version allows the graph to be traversed without any constraints, whereas the restricted
515
      version imposes certain restrictions, such as prioritizing sidechains when traversing a ring. We used
      restricted versions in the pretraining data, but we used the unrestricted version during fine-tuning.
516
517   We used random sampling with temperature 1.0 to generate 10 million molecules from each of the
518   four fine-tuned models to better understand the tradeoffs between training on canonical vs random-
519   ized SELFIES. Tables 3 and 2 show the key evaluation metrics. Surprisingly, there is little difference
520   between the models trained on randomized and canonical SELFIES. The largest difference in recall
521
      is less than 3 percentage points over 10 million samples. This result is in contrast with the find-
      ings of Arús-Pous et al. (2019) (note the sets of molecules are different). While the difference is
522
      small, pretraining on randomized SELFIES is better for precision and recall on three out of four
523
      sets, with Ssas being the only exception. For precision, fine-tuning only on canonical is better, while
524   fine-tuning on randomized is preferable for recall. Pretraining on canonical and fine-tuning on ran-
525   domized SELFIES performs the worst on all molecular sets. We do not have a clear understanding
526   of the causes of these patterns. The recall numbers from Table 8 are not directly comparable across
527   molecular sets because the size of the respective sets (M ) varies. This is also evident from the upper
528   bound numbers. To make the numbers comparable, Table 2 shows the recall when the number of
529   generations equals the number of molecules in the respective set.

      6    C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we presented a benchmark for evaluating the recall of language models. We showed
534   how different factors affect the recall and highlighted a path toward improving the recall through
535   sampling methods and loss functions. We also present a novel method to predict generative recall
536   without doing generation. Whether pretraining or fine-tuning strategies of language models can
537   significantly improve their recall is still an open question. We will publicly release the sets of
538   molecules and the pretrained models to foster further research on methods for improved recall of
539   language models. Future work should develop methods covering much smaller datasets with few to
      no training samples.
