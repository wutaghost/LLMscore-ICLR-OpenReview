011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            For aligning large language models (LLMs), prior work has leveraged reinforce-
014            ment learning via human feedback (RLHF) or variations of direct preference
015            optimization (DPO). While DPO offers a simpler framework based on maximum
016
               likelihood estimation, it compromises on the ability to tune language models to
               easily maximize non-differentiable objectives according to the LLM designer’s
017
               preferences (e.g., using simpler language or minimizing specific kinds of harmful
018
               content). These may neither align with user preferences nor even be able to be
019            captured tractably by binary preference data. To leverage the simplicity and per-
020            formance of DPO with the generalizability of RL, we propose a hybrid approach
021            between DPO and RLHF. With a simple augmentation to the implicit reward de-
022            composition of DPO, we allow for tuning LLMs to maximize a set of arbitrary
023            auxiliary rewards using offline RL. The proposed method, Hybrid Preference Opti-
024            mization (HPO), shows the ability to effectively generalize to both user preferences
025            and auxiliary designer objectives, while preserving alignment performance across
026            a range of challenging benchmarks and model sizes.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Language models (LMs) have shown capability to mimic language effectively across a variety of
032   datasets and tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2019; Touvron et al., 2023). Given a large
033
      corpus of text collected across a diverse set of sources, most successful generative LMs are trained
      on next-token prediction objectives. Consequently, they exhibit a variety of different skillsets, but
034
      mimicking text may not always exhibit desirable generation capabilities, e.g., producing intelligent
035
      responses to questions or high-quality code. In order to further refine the LM’s capabilities to
036   tailor responses to human preferences, we leverage human-labeled preference datasets and perform
037   task-specific fine-tuning and feedback alignment (Ouyang et al., 2022).
038
039
      Traditionally, alignment to human preferences has leveraged reinforcement learning via human
      feedback (RLHF) (Akrour et al., 2011; Christiano et al., 2017). Equipped with a relatively small
040
      dataset of feedback collected from a fine-tuned LM, we train one or more reward models using
041
      maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) (Ouyang et al., 2022). Using the trained reward models,
042   we apply a reinforcement learning (RL) algorithm to the LM to maximize those generated rewards.
043   Typically, the RL algorithm of choice is Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), developed in order to
044   promote training stability for policy gradient algorithms (Schulman et al., 2017). However, despite
045   this, RLHF often remains unstable during training (Rafailov et al., 2024), and especially for on-policy
046   techniques like PPO, the training time remains a concern due to generation through sampling from
047   the LM (Gao et al., 2024). While offline RL techniques have been attempted to mitigate the training
048   efficiency, they either incur additional training instability, requiring loss clipping or additional penalty
049   terms (Baheti et al., 2023; Snell et al., 2022).
050   Recent work has shown that an alternative approach to alignment, Direct Preference Optimization
051   (DPO), can yield a simple MLE objective that is more stable and often outperforms RLHF (Rafailov
052   et al., 2024). Through reframing and reparameterizing the standard RLHF objective with the
053   Bradley-Terry (Bradley & Terry, 1952) or Plackett-Luce (Plackett, 1975) preference models, it
      entirely bypasses training a reward model and trains significantly faster than on-policy RL techniques

054
      that sample from the LM. Extensions to DPO leverage different preference models, such as the
055   Kahneman-Tversky Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979; Ethayarajh et al., 2024), or offer
056   generalizations to arbitrary Ψ-preference optimization objectives (Azar et al., 2023). However, while
057   DPO and its extensions presents significant advantages, the aforementioned techniques lack the
058   ability to incorporate arbitrary non-differentiable objectives like RLHF. For instance, it lacks the
059   direct capability to minimize unsafe content or control the text reading level through an additional
060   objective in a sample-efficient manner; consequently, its direct applicability as a standalone, sample
061   and computationally-efficient alignment framework that is usable in the real world is diminished.
062   Concurrent techniques to handle auxiliary objectives such as multi-objective DPO (MODPO) (Zhou
063   et al., 2024) and REBEL (Gao et al., 2024) have proposed reward margin-based techniques to optimize
064   multiple objectives. However, in our pursuit of a standalone preference optimization framework,
065   there are several caveats to their methodology and experimentation. While REBEL improves upon
066   PPO’s stability, it still requires on-policy generation: hence, it incurs over 3x the computational cost
067   and training time and ∼30% more memory compared to DPO, reducing its practical applicability.
068   Methodologically, MODPO is multi-stage and tied to paired data through DPO (unclear whether
069
      unpaired data can be supported), limiting its practical usage. On the experimentation front, MODPO
      is only evaluated using one LLM, which is insufficient to make conclusions as there can be variance
070
      in performance across LLMs (Ethayarajh et al., 2024). Additionally, they exclude performant and
071
      popular baselines such as KTO (e.g., on real data, they only compare to SFT and RLHF).
072
073   To leverage the strengths of both RLHF and recent DPO-style techniques, we propose a hybrid
074   technique that leverages the simplicity of direct preference optimization techniques for maximizing
075
      preference alignment in feedback datasets, while allowing for arbitrary auxiliary objectives with stable
      and efficient offline RL. The highlights of our proposed approach, Hybrid Preference Optimization
076
      (HPO), are as follows:
077
078          • With roughly ten additional lines of model code on top of KTO, HPO shows a significantly
079            improved ability to optimize important auxiliary objectives, including various forms of
080            toxicity and readability, compared to prior multi-objective and single objective alignment
081            approaches (e.g., KTO, MODPO, DPO), while retaining or surpassing overall performance.
082
             • Despite using RL, HPO reduces the complexity of traditional RL objectives through a
083
               reduction to a simple weighted maximum likelihood objective, which removes the need for
084            on-policy generation, loss clipping, importance sampling, or bootstrapping. This results in:
085
086               – more stable, efficient, and easier to tune compared to prior work (e.g., using PPO)
087               – more theoretically principled than techniques using PPO for offline RL

      2    R ELATED W ORK
091
092   Traditionally, alignment methods have been based on reinforcement learning via human feedback
093
      (RLHF), which typically involves training reward models using maximum likelihood estimation
      (MLE) and applying an RL algorithm to tune the LM to maximize rewards (Akrour et al., 2011;
094
      Cheng et al., 2011; Christiano et al., 2017; Askell et al., 2021; Rame et al., 2024). RLHF is often
095
      performed with on-policy methods such as Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al.,
096   2017), but these have been shown to be computationally expensive and often unstable (Ouyang et al.,
097   2022).
098
099
      To mitigate these issues with RLHF, Baheti et al. (2023) propose an offline importance sampling-
      based approach, reducing training cost yet introducing instability into training that requires clipping.
100
      Snell et al. (2022) propose an offline approach that adapts Implicit Q-Learning (Kostrikov et al.,
101
      2021), but it requires sampling generations from an LM and many additional tricks for stability.
102   Ethayarajh et al. (2024) propose an offline-only variant of PPO to reduce training cost, but both PPO
103   and its predecessor, TRPO, require on-policy samples for their guarantees (Schulman et al., 2015). In
104   concurrent work, Gao et al. (2024) propose REBEL, an RL-based alternative which demonstrates
105   multi-objective capabilities and reduces instability; however, the training cost/time and memory usage
106   are significant and roughly comparable to PPO due to requiring on-policy generation. All in all, prior
107   work does not seem to indicate that there is a sufficiently flexible, computationally efficient, stable,
      and high-performance RL framework for LLMs.

108
      Direct preference optimization (DPO) style objectives reframe RLHF as a maximum likelihood
109   task by reparameterizing the reward function using a chosen preference model (e.g., Bradley &
110   Terry (1952), Plackett (1975), Kahneman & Tversky (1979)). They have shown improvements in
111   performance, stability, and efficiency (Rafailov et al., 2024; Ethayarajh et al., 2024; Azar et al., 2023).
112   Extensions have further improved these objectives through the addition of an offset (Amini et al.,
113   2024), rejection sampling (Liu et al., 2023; Khaki et al., 2024), diversification Wang et al. (2023),
114   and in-context learning (Song et al., 2024).
115   However, these aforementioned methods that optimize for preferences using MLE lack the capability
116   of maximizing arbitrary non-differentiable and non-binary objectives (e.g., empathy, readability,
117   or safety) without additional data or features, which limits their practical usage. Liu et al. (2024)
118   propose a technique for safe DPO, but it is quite limited in its scope. In concurrent work, Zhou
119   et al. (2024) explore multi-objective learning with DPO using a margin-based approach, but the
120   methodology is tied to availability of paired preference data and the experimentation is insufficient to
121   conclude generalization across different models and datasets (i.e., only evaluated on a single LLM,
122   LLAMA-7B, with only SFT and RLHF as a baseline on real data).

173   4.1   M ODELING AUXILIARY O BJECTIVES WITH R EWARDS
174
      To motivate the utility of auxiliary rewards over categorical preference datasets (e.g., as in DPO), we
175   demonstrate use cases wherein vanilla direct preference optimization style techniques are impractical.
176   For instance, consider the prompt and sample generations in Figure 1, where the LM has to consider
177   a tradeoff between helpfulness and safety. Should it respond with profanity, as the user requested,
178   or refuse to answer? How can we empirically control the acceptable margin of model toxicity? In
179   such cases, granular control for the LM designer over the way in which the model prioritizes or ranks
180   these objectives is critical.
181   In service of this, consider a fully ranked list of all possible generations y ∈ A for all prompts in
182   S, i.e., using a state-action ranking function R. Given an offline dataset D, we demonstrate that
183   it is impossible to learn a ranking R exactly within a binary preference dataset using DPO without
184   intractable sample complexity, i.e., requiring an intractable O(|S||A| log |A|) data samples, or losing
185   the ability to learn certain preference orderings. We include a proof in Appendix A.
186   Definition 1 (State-Action Ranking Function). Let R : S × A → N be a ranking of actions y ∈ A
187   for each state x ∈ S, such that for any two actions y1 and y2 for a given state x, R(x, y1 ) < R(x, y2 )
188   iff y1 is preferable to y2 given x.
189
190   However, given an RL framework, we can show that all such rankings can be trivially modeled by at
191   least one well-defined and bounded reward function (e.g., r(x, y) = 1/R(x, y)). Such an inclusion
192
      of arbitrary objectives through reward functions provides more granular ability to control and tune the
      preferred generations to arbitrary state-action rankings, ultimately beyond the practical capabilities of
193
      any methods that only leverage binary preference data.

260   Algorithm To apply Hybrid Preference Optimization, we initialize a small value function head on
261   top of the existing LM, detaching the gradient from the LM to prevent LV from backpropagating
262   through πϕ . Since we do not use on-policy RL, we simply sample from the preference dataset and
263   apply forward passes through the policy πϕ and reference model πref to compute log probabilities
264   from the logits. To align the model, we combine the ΨPO objective and the offline RL objectives, LV
265   and Lπ , amounting to an extra 10 lines of Python code on top of a ΨPO algorithm (Algorithm 1).
266   We note that this approach addresses prior issues with RLHF: training efficiency and stability. Stability
267   for maximum likelihood objectives has been examined in the context of both RL tasks and LM tasks,
268   and it has been found to be successful in both domains (Emmons et al., 2021; Rafailov et al., 2024).
269   Importantly, from an efficiency point of view, this adds no additional forward or backward passes or

282   In this section, we evaluate the proposed method, HPO, and compare it with prior alignment methods
283   in terms of producing user and designer-preference aligned generations. We choose a set of societally
284   relevant auxiliary evaluation objectives, then we demonstrate that HPO sufficiently optimizes these
285   compared to reasonable baselines, while retaining or surpassing the performance of prior methods.
286   Based on our experiments showing that neither prior multi-objective approaches that are purely
287   RL-based nor purely DPO-based can achieve similar performance with competitive sample and
288   computational efficiency, we show that the proposed hybrid objective surpasses prior approaches
289
      with a simple augmentation.

      6    D ISCUSSION
505
506
      In this study, we address the important tradeoff in language model alignment between performance,
      stability, and simplicity using direct preference objectives with granular, multi-objective prioritization
507
      using RLHF. To bridge this gap, we propose Hybrid Preference Optimization, based on a simple
508
      augmentation to direct preference-style methods that allows for optimizing auxiliary objectives.
509   With minimal additional computational cost compared to DPO-style methods and improved stability
510   compared to RLHF, HPO demonstrates significant improvements in optimization of auxiliary ob-
511   jectives (i.e., safety and readability) compared to its base method, KTO, and other multi-objective
512   approaches without compromising on the overall performance (as adjudged by GPT-4). Across all
513   of our evaluations and models, it equals or outperforms prior techniques, with greater consistency
514   than other techniques. Consequently, this work presents a pathway forward to a more granular and
515   multi-objective approach to DPO, given that different practical use cases demand different priorities.
516
517   Limitations and Future Work Despite HPO’s ability to optimize these objectives, many of its
518
      limitations are reminiscent of the limitations of multi-objective RL and similar methods, such as
      MODPO. For specific use cases, it may require experimentation to optimally weight the different
519
      auxiliary objectives and the preference objective to achieve satisfactory performance in all facets. To
520
      our knowledge, there exist no satisfactory techniques that determine a set of weights that guarantee
521   an optimal balance between performance and computational cost, which deters from the practical
522   applicability of such a tool. Another important avenue of exploration is to examine different hybrid
523   techniques, e.g., other combinations of preference optimization and auxiliary optimization schemes
524   such as DPO and Conservative Q-Learning (Kumar et al., 2020).

      A     M ODELING AUXILIARY O BJECTIVES WITH R EWARDS : P ROOF
703
704
      Theorem A.1. Given a binary preference dataset D of size n, representing a state-action ranking
705
      function R exactly requires O(|S||A| log |A|) data samples.
706
707
708   Proof. The proof relies on existing computational arguments for the minimum complexity of worst
709
      case sorting algorithms, given only pairwise comparisons. For simplicity, we will consider a fixed
      state x ∈ S and attempt to enumerate all possible rankings y ∈ A for x.
710
711   We can reduce this problem to sorting an unsorted list of actions y ∈ A. Given binary preference
712   data (i.e., a1 ≻ a2 ), which serve as our pairwise comparisons for sorting, we wish to arrange or sort
713   the actions in ascending order of preference. As stated in Sedgewick & Wayne (2011), the minimum
714   number of worst-case comparisons for an optimal algorithm is O(log |A|). Applying Sterling’s
715
      inequality yields O(|A| log |A|) as the time complexity for enumerating all rankings for x.
716   Across all possible x ∈ S, this requires O(|S||A| log |A|) comparisons. Hence, we require
717   O(|S||A| log |A|) binary preferences to learn a R exactly.

                exp_a = torch.exp((rewards - values.detach()) * self.temperature)
977
                actor_loss = -(exp_a * (all_logps / masks.sum(-1)))
978          else:
979             actor_loss = torch.zeros(1).to(rewards.device)
980          value_loss = expectile_loss(values, rewards)
981          losses = kto_loss + scaling * actor_loss + 0.1 * value_loss
             # ...
