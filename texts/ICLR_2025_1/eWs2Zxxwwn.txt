011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Predicting molecular properties plays an important role in both scientific research
014            and industrial applications. Given that different molecular properties are influ-
015            enced by specific atoms or functional groups, it is essential to incorporate both
016            types of information. Previous approaches either leverage subgraph informa-
017            tion in self-supervised learning to pre-train atom-based architectures or develop
018            subgraph-based architectures tailored to specific downstream tasks. However,
019            these methods often lack a thorough analysis or theoretical support concerning
020
               the expressive capabilities of these two types of representations. Moreover, they
               typically rely on fixed coupling representations, which cannot adaptively priori-
021
               tize more discriminative information for various downstream tasks. In this paper,
022
               we introduce a Route-guided Bilateral Compensation (RBC) architecture that ex-
023            plicitly extracts atom-wise and subgraph-wise information through two decoupled
024            branches and integrates them via a route module. Theoretically, we demonstrate
025            that our decomposition-polymerization subgraph-wise branch exhibits greater ex-
026            pressive power than the atom-wise branch, and that the integration process re-
027            duces the generalization error bound. Furthermore, we propose a coordinated
028            self-supervised learning strategy that incorporates node-level masked graph re-
029            construction tasks for atomic and lexicalized subgraph tokens, alongside a graph-
030            level contrastive learning task. For different downstream tasks, the route module
031
               facilitates dynamic integration, enhancing the discriminative power of the final
               representation. External experiments verify the effectiveness of our method.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037
038   Molecular properties prediction plays a fundamental role in many tasks like drug and material
039
      discovery (Feinberg et al., 2018). Previous methods typically model molecules as graphs, where
      atoms and chemical bonds are modeled as nodes and edges, respectively. Graph Neural Networks
040
      (GNNs) (Hamilton et al., 2017) have been widely applied to predict specific properties associated
041
      with atoms, such as solubility and reactivity (Zhang et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2022).
042   However, not all molecular properties are determined by individual atoms, and some chemical prop-
043   erties are closely related to functional groups (subgraphs) (Zhong et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Kong
044   et al., 2022), such as efficacy, and metabolic properties. Therefore, how to fuse atom-wise informa-
045   tion and subgraph-wise information is vital for molecular property prediction.
046
      Existing methods to employ both kinds of information for making final predictions can be broadly
047
      grouped into two categories. One category involves utilizing subgraph-wise knowledge to pre-train
048   atom-based architectures in the self-supervised learning stage. For instance, MGSSL (Zhang et al.,
049   2021) integrates atom-wise self-supervised tasks with subgraph-wise tasks, such as masking and
050   predicting subgraph tokens, achieved by transforming molecular graphs into tree structures. The
051   other category focuses on constructing joint architectures of atoms and subgraphs for downstream
052   tasks. For instance, in (Yang et al., 2022), the output representation is amalgamated using both
053   atom-wise and subgraph-wise information through an attention mechanism, which is designed for
      downstream tasks specifically.


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Achieving great success, the above two categories of methods lack theoretical analysis on the ex-
055   pression capacities (Wollschläger et al., 2024) of atom-wise and subgraph-wise representations, as
056   well as the benefit of integrating them. Besides, these methods generally obtain coupling integrated
057   representations, i.e., the mechanism by which the two types of information are integrated is fixed
058   and the two types of information are coupled together within the final representations. However, the
059   discriminative information for different downstream tasks is different. Transferring such statically
060   fused representations to different tasks in a unified manner will affect their discriminative ability.
061   In this paper, we propose a novel Route-guided Bilateral Compensation (RBC) architecture, which
062   consists of an atom-wise branch, and a subgraph-wise branch to explicitly decouple the two types of
063   information. For the subgraph-wise branch, we propose a decomposition-polymerization network
064   that interacts between atoms and subgraphs in decomposition layers and interacts among subgraph
065   nodes in polymerization layers. A Route-guided Module is introduced to enable the dynamic selec-
066   tion of atom-wise or subgraph-wise information, catering to the specific requirements of molecular
067   properties. In theory, we verify that subgraph-wise information can boost the expression of graphs,
068   which complements the shortcomings of atom-wise information. Moreover, we also provide theo-
069
      retical support for the RBC module and verify the generalization error bound.
070   Existing subgraph-aware self-supervised learning methods (Feng et al., 2019; Dash et al., 2021;
071   2022; Yang et al., 2022) cannot be directly applied to train our RBC since it is different from the
072   architectures they used. Therefore, we propose a coordinated self-supervised learning strategy for
073   RBC. For the atom branch, we employ the commonly used masked atom prediction task, i.e., pre-
074   dicting randomly masked atoms. For the subgraph branch, most existing self-supervised molecu-
075
      lar learning methods, which are mainly designed for atom-based architectures, cannot fully capture
      subgraph-wise information and the relations among substructures. To this end, we propose a Masked
076
      Subgraph-Token Modeling (MSTM) strategy for the subgraph-wise branch. MSTM first tokenizes
077
      a given molecule into pieces and forms a subgraph-token dictionary. Compared with atom tokens,
078   such subgraphs correspond to different functional groups, thus their semantics are more stable and
079   consistent. MSTM decomposes each molecule into subgraph tokens, masks a portion of them, and
080   learns the molecular representation by taking the prediction of masked token indexes in the dictio-
081   nary as the self-supervised task. Although the atom-wise branch and the subgraph-wise branch aim
082   to extract molecular features from different levels, the global graph representations for the same
083   molecule should be consistent. To build the synergistic interaction between the two branches for
084   joint pre-training, we perform contrastive learning to maximize the average invariance of the two
085   branches. Experimental results show the effectiveness of our method.
086   Our contributions can be summarized as:
087
088         1. We propose a novel architecture for the molecular property prediction task, which con-
089            sists of the atom-wise branch and the decomposition-polymerization subgraph-wise branch.
090            Moreover, a Route-guided Bilateral Compenstation (RBC) architecture is proposed to fuse
091
               the information of two branches dynamically. We verify that the two branches play a com-
               plementary role theoretically and give the generalization error bound of our RBC.
092
093         2. We propose a cooperative node-level and graph-level self-supervised learning method to
094            jointly train the two branches of our bilateral model. For the subgraph branch, we pro-
095            pose MSTM, a novel self-supervised molecular learning strategy, which uses the auto-
096
               discovered subgraphs as tokens and predicts the dictionary indexes of masked tokens. The
               subgraph tokens are more stable in function and have more consistent semantics. In this
097
               way, masked subgraph modeling can be performed in a principled manner. At the global
098
               graph level, we perform a contrastive learning strategy that imposes the interaction of the
099            two branches with the consistency constraint.
100
            3. We provide extensive empirical evaluations to show that the learned representation by our
101
               bilateral model and our self-supervised learning method has a stronger generalization abil-
102
               ity in various functional group-related molecular property prediction tasks.

105   2   R ELATED WORK
106
107   Molecular property prediction The prediction of molecular properties is an important research
      topic in the fields of chemistry, materials science, pharmacy, biology, physics, etc (Wang & Hou,


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2011). Since it is time-consuming and labor-intensive to measure properties via traditional wet ex-
109   periments, many recent works focus on designing end-to-end machine learning methods to directly
110   predict properties. These works can be divided into two categories: SMILES string-based meth-
111   ods (Butler et al., 2018; Dong et al., 2018) and graph-based methods (Gilmer et al., 2017; Yang
112   et al., 2019; Lu et al., 2019; Gasteiger et al., 2020). Compared with SMILES strings, it is more
113   natural to represent molecules as graphs and model them with Graph neural networks (GNNs).
114   However, the training of GNNs requires a large amount of labeled molecule data and supervised-
115   trained GNNs usually show limited generalization ability for newly synthesized molecules and new
116   properties. In order to tackle these issues, self-supervised representation pre-training techniques are
117
      explored (Rong et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Stärk et al., 2022) in molecular property prediction.
118
119   Self-supervised learning of graphs Based on how self-supervised tasks are constructed, previous
120
      works can be classified into two categories, contrastive models and predictive models. Contrastive
      models (Li et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2020; You et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2021; Subra-
121
      monian, 2021; Xia et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022b; Zhong et al., 2024) generate different views for each
122
      graph via data augmentation and learn representations by contrasting the similarities between views
123   of the same graph and different graphs. Predictive models (Hu et al., 2020; Rong et al., 2020; Hou
124   et al., 2022) generally mask a part of the graph and predict the masked parts. Most existing methods
125   focus on learning node-level or graph-level representations, with some work involving subgraph-
126   level feature that utilizes the rich semantic information contained in the subgraphs or motifs. For
127   instance, in (Zhang et al., 2021), the topology information of motifs is considered. In (Wu et al.,
128   2023), a Transformer architecture is proposed to incorporate motifs and construct 3D heterogeneous
129   molecular graphs for representation learning. Different from these works, we propose a bilateral fu-
130   sion model with a novel subgraph-aware GNN branch and propose a joint node-wise and graph-wise
131
      self-supervised training strategy so that the learned representation can capture both atom-wise and
      subgraph-wise information.

198   Subgraph embedding In this phase, we only focus on learning the embedding of each subgraph
199   by modeling the intra-subgraph interactions. For a molecule G = (V, E), we decompose it into
200   a set of non-overlapped subgraphs {Sπ1 , Sπ2 , · · · , SπT }, where T is the number of decomposed
201   subgraphs and πt is the corresponding index of the tth decomposed subgraph in the constructed
202   vocabulary V. For each subgraph Sπt = (V̂πt , Êπt ), we have V̂πt ⊂ V and Êπt ⊂ E. For each
203   edge (u, v) in E, we add it into the inter-subgraph edge set E if it satisfies that nodes u and v are in
204   different subgraphs. Therefore, we have V = ∪V̂πt and E = ∪Êπt ∪ E.
205   We apply a single GNN model with K1 layers to map each decomposed subgraph into an embedding.
206   GNN depends on the graph connectivity as well as node and edge features to learn an embedding
207   for each node v. We discard the inter-subgraph edge set E, any two subgraphs are disconnected and
208   the information will be detached among subgraphs. This is equivalent to feeding each subgraph Sπt
209   into the GNN model individually.
210   By feeding the molecular graph after discarding all inter-subgraph edges into the GNN model, the
211   embeddings of all atoms in the T decomposed subgraphs are updated in parallel and the embeddings
212   of all subgraphs can be obtained by adaptive pooling. Compared with previous strategies (Hu et al.,
213   2019; Zhang et al., 2021) that directly obtain molecular representations from the context-dependent
214   atom-wise embeddings with all edges, our strategy first extracts subgraph-level embeddings. When
215   a subgraph appears in different molecules, both its atom-wise embeddings and the subgraph embed-
      ding remain the same.

528   5   C ONCLUSION
529
530
531
      In this paper, we acknowledge that neither atoms nor subgraphs solely determine molecular proper-
      ties and introduce a novel approach termed Route-Guided Bilateral Compensation (RBC). The RBC
532
      model consists of two branches: one dedicated to modeling atom-wise information and the other
533
      focused on subgraph-wise information. Theoretically, we prove that the subgraph-wise branch is
534   more expressive than the atom-wise branch and integration can achieve lower generalization error
535   bound. Furthermore, recognizing that molecular properties are influenced differently by atoms and
536   subgraphs, we propose a route-guided mechanism for the automatic fusion of features from these
537   two branches. To enhance generalization, we introduce a node-level self-supervised learning method
538   called MSTM, specifically designed for the less-explored subgraph-wise branch. Additionally, we
539   implement a graph-level self-supervised learning strategy aimed at maximizing the average invari-
      ance across the two branches. Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach.

      B.1    I NTRODUCTION OF BRICS ALGORITHM
803
804   The BRICS algorithm is one of the molecular fragmentation methods, which is an improved and
805   optimized algorithm based on the RECAP algorithm. The BRICS algorithm introduces a better
806   set of fragmentation rules and a set of recombinant motifs rules to form a fragmentation space.
807   Specifically, the BRICS algorithm obtains active building blocks by segmenting active molecules.
808   It is known that some common chemical reactions form bonds, so when segmenting molecules,
809   BRICS segments these bonds. The algorithm splits into 16 pre-defined bonds. These 16 pre-defined
      bonds ensure that the split fragments are suitable for combination and applicable to combinatorial


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      chemistry, and these 16 pre-defined bonds are given in the form of fragment structures. When
811   segmenting molecules, all breakable bonds are cut off at the same time to avoid redundant fragments,
812   and if the fragments after cleavage only contain small functional groups (such as hydrogen, methyl,
813   ethyl, propyl, and butyl), the fragments will not be cleaved again to avoid generating useless small
814   fragments. In the process of splitting, the algorithm preserves the cyclic structure. After each
815   bond is broken, two breakpoints are formed. RECAP algorithm directly annotates ’isotope labels’
816   at the divided breakpoints, that is, the ids of breakable bonds, but BRICS divides firstly annotates
817   “isotope labels” at the breakpoints, and then replaces these isotope labels with link atoms. For the
818   RECAP algorithm, the ids of breakable bonds corresponding to the isotope labels annotated at the
819
      two breakpoints are the same, but for the BRICS algorithm, they are different, which also proves
      that the BRICS algorithm takes the chemical environment and surrounding substructures of each
820
      broken bond into account, and the partition effect is better. Finally, the molecules decomposed by
821
      the BRICS algorithm are a list composed of a one-step partitioned non-redundant fragment string.
822
823
824   B.2   T HE PRINCIPLE SUBGRAPH EXTRACTION
825
      Principal Subgraph Extraction is a molecular fragmentation technique proposed in (Kong et al.,
826
      2022), and it consists of three primary steps: initialization, merging, and updating. The process
827
      begins by predefining the number of subgraphs to be extracted, denoted as N .
828
829   In the initialization step, each unique atom in the molecule is considered as an individual subgraph.
830   Then, during the merging phase, two adjacent fragments are combined in each iteration to form a
831
      new set of subgraphs. In this context, adjacent fragments are defined as fragments containing at least
      one first-order neighboring node. After merging, the subgraph with the highest frequency is selected
832
      as the new subgraph for this iteration. This merging and selection process is repeated until the total
833
      number of subgraphs equals the preset value N .
834
835   By systematically refining subgraph selection, this method enables the extraction of meaningful and
836   frequent substructures, facilitating more efficient molecular representation for downstream tasks.
