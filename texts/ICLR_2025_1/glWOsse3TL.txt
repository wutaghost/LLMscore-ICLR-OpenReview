011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large-batch training has become a cornerstone in accelerating the training of deep
014            neural networks, yet it poses challenges in optimization and generalization. Ex-
015            isting optimizers like AdamW present performance degradation during language
016
               models’ large-batch training, due to the information bottleneck of attention layers
               caused by the sharp increase of max attention logit. While the LAMB optimizer
017
               partially addresses this issue, some attention layers still experience sharply in-
018
               creased maximum attention logits. The reason is that l2 -norm-based trust ratios
019            in LAMB are less effective in directly influencing extreme weight values. Fur-
020            thermore, the weight-wise trust ratio in LAMB is error-prone due to overlooking
021            relationships of weight values within rows or columns. Building on these obser-
022            vations, we propose a novel optimizer, MERIT, which leverages the max norm to
023            calculate the trust ratio to directly constrain the max attention logit. Moreover, we
024            further construct element-wise trust ratios to provide more robust update scaling
025            by focusing on local weight structures. Extensive experiments of large-batch train-
026            ing across various sizes of GPT-2 models demonstrate the superior performance
027
               of MERIT. Notably, during the training of GPT-2 Medium, MERIT enables the
               use of a 6k batch size without any performance degradation compared to the stan-
028
               dard batch size (480). This work highlights the importance of considering the max
029
               attention logit and finer granularity trust ratio calculation in large-batch training.
030            It successfully improves the training stability and paves the way for larger batch
031            usage, enabling faster development and iteration on large language models.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The advent of large language models has revolutionized natural language processing, achieving un-
037   precedented performance across a wide range of tasks (Dubey et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023;
038   OpenAI, 2024). However, the increasing size and complexity of language models always result in a
039   high time cost for the training. With the growing availability of powerful GPU clusters and special-
040   ized hardware accelerators, large-batch training can dramatically reduce the time required to train
041   state-of-the-art models, making it possible to iterate faster and explore more ambitious architectures
042   by processing more data in parallel.
043   While large-batch training offers the potential for increased parallelism and faster convergence, it
044   also introduces complex optimization dynamics that can impede model performance and stability
045   (Goyal et al., 2018; Keskar et al., 2017; Shallue et al., 2019). Training large language models with
046   large batches typically encounters two main issues. The first problem is that when a fixed number of
047   training tokens is available, using larger batch sizes reduces the total number of training iterations,
048   limiting its ability to learn fine-grained patterns in the data. Additionally, research has shown that
049   training with large batches often leads to models performing poorly on unseen data. When using
050
      AdamW optimizer with a large batch size, Figure 1 shows clear performance degradation, requiring
      additional training tokens to reach comparable generalization levels.
051
052   This paper identifies a crucial problem in large-batch training of language models: we observe the
053   sharp increase of max attention logit in attention layers during the training process using AdamW
      optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2017; Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019). The inflated max attention logit can

      Moreover, our analysis reveals that rows and columns in large-batch trained weights often share
086   similarities. The neglect of these relationships in the weight-wise ratio method proposed in LAMB
087   leads to training instability as it fails to mitigate the negative impact of extreme values from other
088   rows or columns. Given rows and columns exhibit high similarity, it allows for calculating element-
089   wise ratios by considering the weights within the same rows/columns, while eliminating influences
090   from other rows/columns. The proposed finer granularity ratios focus on local weight structures,
091   resulting in more stable large-batch training for language models.
092   Inspired by these insights, we propose a novel optimizer, MERIT, that specifically targets the control
093   of attention logit magnitudes while maintaining the benefits of efficient large-batch training. Our
094   approach builds upon the foundations laid by AdamW and LAMB, introducing max-norm-based
095   trust ratios to precisely modulate the impact of large weights on attention logit distributions and finer
096   granularity ratios for focusing on specific weight structures. We conduct extensive experiments to
097   evaluate the performance of MERIT compared to existing optimizers across various sizes of GPT-
098   2 models. Our findings demonstrate the potential of MERIT to enhance large-batch training by
099   improving convergence properties and generalization performance. This work contributes to the
100
      ongoing exploration of optimization strategies in large-batch training and highlights the significance
      of finer granularity ratio calculation in the design of effective optimizers.

105   2.1   L ARGE - BATCH T RAINING
106
107   Scaling up batch sizes during the training of deep neural networks has been an active area of re-
      search, as it allows for better parallelization across multiple GPUs and reduces time-to-train. How-


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      ever, naively increasing the batch size often leads to degraded model performance, a phenomenon
109   dubbed the “generalization gap”. Several techniques have been proposed to enable large-batch train-
110   ing without compromising accuracy. Goyal et al. (2018) showed that linear scaling of the learning
111   rate with respect to the batch size can maintain model quality for batch sizes up to 8K on ImageNet.
112   Other works proposed novel optimization algorithms like LARS (You et al., 2017) and LAMB (You
113   et al., 2020) that dynamically adapt layer-wise learning rates based on parameter norms and momen-
114   tum. Liu et al. (2022) introduced a more efficient SAM (Foret et al., 2021) variant for training Vision
115   Transformers (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) using large batches and Luo et al. (2023) explored memory-
116   efficient optimization techniques for large-batch training of language models. Overall, these meth-
117
      ods aim to maintain proper scaling of parameter updates and normalization statistics when scaling up
      batch sizes. However, LAMB still presents a large max attention logit and shows a weight-wise trust
118
      ratio calculation containing some error, leading to large-batch training performance degradation.
119
120
121   2.2   M AX ATTENTION L OGIT
122
      The relationship between max attention logit and training stability of transformers has been explored
123
      extensively. Researchers have previously documented that Transformer training fails when the at-
124   tention logits become large. Dehghani et al. (2023) addressed the challenge of uncontrolled growth
125   in attention logits in large-scale transformers by implementing query/key normalization, effectively
126   stabilizing the training process and preventing the near one-hot attention distributions typical in
127   models with parameters nearing 8 billion. Wortsman et al. (2024) observed the loss diverges and
128   training fails when the max attention logit exceeds approximately 104 . Zhai et al. (2023) identified
129   attention entropy collapse as a common issue in Transformer training across various domains and
130   tasks and proposed σReparam to reparameterize the weights of linear layers using spectral normal-
131   ization and a learned scalar. Nevertheless, the significant increase in the maximum attention logit
132
      value during large-batch training remains unexplored and leads to the poor performance of existing
      optimizers.

249
250   Algorithm 1 summarizes our proposed MERIT optimizer. The design of MERIT comes from two
251   parts: maximum-normalized trust ratio and element-wise refinement for faster convergence and bet-

253   ment an element-wise clipping mechanism that limits the maximum update magnitude to 1 across
254   all parameter dimensions, which mirrors the update strategy of stochastic Sign Momentum Gra-
255
      dient Descent (Bernstein et al., 2018), serving to enhance the overall stability of the large-batch
      optimization process. The designs incorporated in MERIT successfully address the issue of rapidly
256
      increasing max attention logits in the middle self-attention layers of language models, as illustrated

      in Figure 2(b).

      5     E XPERIMENTS
325
326   5.1   S ETUP
327
328   Language modeling. We conducted large-batch training experiments on OpenWebText (Gokaslan
329   & Cohen, 2019), training autoregressive models from scratch using settings derived from the Chin-
330   chilla scaling law (Hoffmann et al., 2022). Following standard protocol, we set the context length of
331   GPT-2 to 1024. Our experiments encompassed three model sizes: 125M (small), 355M (medium),
332   and 770M (large). Detailed specifications of the model configurations can be found in Appendix A.
333   Baselines. We compare MERIT with LAMB, the dominantly used optimizer on large-batch training
334   of language modeling tasks, Adam with decoupled weight decay (AdamW), Lion (Chen et al., 2023),
335   and Sophia-G (Liu et al., 2024). For all models, all learning rates are tuned with grid search. The
336   weight decay is set to 0.1 for all optimizers for a fair comparison. We follow (Liu et al., 2024) for
337   the settings of β values: For AdamW: β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.95. For Lion: β1 = 0.95 and β2 = 0.98.
338   For Sophia-G: β1 = 0.92 and β2 = 0.99.
339   Chinchilla Scaling Law. The Chinchilla scaling law suggests an optimal training regime of approx-
340   imately 20 tokens per parameter for large language models. This principle, derived from Hoffmann
341   et al. (2022)’s comprehensive analysis, proposes that model performance is maximized when the
342   number of training tokens scales proportionally with the number of parameters under a fixed com-
343   pute budget. While the exact ratio may vary slightly depending on specific circumstances, the “20x
344   rule” has been widely adopted as a practical guideline for efficient model scaling in the field of
345
      natural language processing (Anil et al., 2023; Muennighoff et al., 2023).
346   Implementation. Following the Chinchilla scaling law, we use batch size 1K for GPT-2 small with
347   2B training tokens, 4K for GPT-2 medium with 8B tokens, and 8K for GPT-2 large with 16B tokens
348   for the large-batch training setting. Our learning rate (LR) follows a cosine schedule, with the final
349   LR set to 0.1 of the peak LR. We maintain a constant LR warm-up ratio of 0.02 and apply standard
350   gradient clipping (norm) with a threshold of 1.0. In the case of Sophia-G, we select 240 examples
351
      from each minibatch to compute the diagonal Gauss-Newton and update the diagonal Hessian every
      10 steps. We implement the algorithms in PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and train all the models in
352
      bfloat16. All models are trained on H100 GPUs.
353
354   Technical details. We apply the previously described baseline optimizers and MERIT for three
355   token counts: 2B (Small), 8B (Medium), and 16B (Large). We mainly evaluate GPT-2 models with
356   their log perplexity and plot the validation loss curves. The results from SuperGLUE (Wang et al.,
357
      2019), LAMBADA (Paperno et al., 2016), and WikiText (Merity et al., 2017) evaluations are also
      included in our experiments.

      equal 37.231 and 12994.91 respectively, and eigenvalues of MERIT are predominantly confined to
      the range [−5, 5]. This reduced spread of eigenvalues suggests that MERIT converges to an overall
465
      flatter region in the optimization landscape. Such flat regions, characterized by small eigenvalues
466
      and trace, are frequently associated with improved generalization capabilities in language models.

522   of language models. As illustrated in Figure 9(a), the application of this lower bound significantly
523   enhances generalization performance, highlighting the importance of balanced updates across dif-
524   ferent elements. Figure 9(b) further demonstrates that this lower bound becomes particularly crucial
525   in the latter stages of training. This observation indicates that maintaining a minimum update mag-
526   nitude grows increasingly important as the model nears convergence. Such a strategy likely enables
527   the model to continue refining its parameters effectively in later training phases, potentially circum-
528
      venting premature convergence to sub-optimal solutions.
529   Ablation 3: Element-wise Ratio. Element-wise trust ratio calculations enhance the generaliza-
530   tion capability of language models by providing more robust ratio estimates focusing on local
531   weight structures for individual weight elements. Figure 9(a) demonstrates the advancement of
532   using element-wise ratio.

536   Accelerating the pre-training of language models heavily relies on large batch techniques. In this
537   study, we present the MERIT optimizer, which integrates maximum norm and local weight infor-

539   enables larger batch size usage than LAMB and AdamW, while maintaining comparable generaliza-
      tion performance.

      Comprehensive downstream task assessment. We evaluate large-batch pre-trained models on 7

      Scaling up to larger language models and datasets. MERIT has shown promising scalability up

      scaling further, our comparison with AdamW and LAMB on more extensive models and datasets is

799   pre-training stability, we anticipate MERIT to outperform AdamW and LAMB in large-batch train-

801   future work with access to greater computational resources.

851
852   Figure 12 presents the element-wise clipping ratio in GPT-2 Small training setting (2B tokens) for
853   the 1st, 6th, and 12th layers.
854   The analysis of clipping effects across different layers reveals distinct patterns in gradient update
855   behavior during training. The input layer (Layer 1) maintains near-zero clipping ratios throughout,
856   suggesting that early-layer gradient updates rarely require adjustment. In contrast, the middle layer
857   (Layer 6) experiences more substantial clipping, with ratios peaking at 12% during later training
858   stages. The output layer (Layer 12) shows minimal clipping, with ratios reaching only 0.25% at
859   maximum.
860   This layered pattern demonstrates that the clipping mechanism primarily influences the middle lay-
861   ers, leaving input and output layers unaffected. Such behavior indicates that the clipping mechanism
862   functions as a targeted stabilizer rather than a uniform constraint across the network. Most gradient
863   updates maintain their original direction, with the most significant stabilization occurring in middle
      layers where feature representations undergo refinement.
