009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural 3D representations such as Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF), excel at pro-
012            ducing photo-realistic rendering results but lack the flexibility for manipulation
013            and editing which is crucial for content creation. Previous works have attempted
014            to address this issue by deforming a NeRF in canonical space or manipulating
015            the radiance field based on an explicit mesh. However, manipulating NeRF is
016            not highly controllable and requires a long training and inference time. With the
017            emergence of 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS), extremely high-fidelity novel view
018
               synthesis can be achieved using an explicit point-based 3D representation with
               much faster training and rendering speed. However, there is still a lack of effec-
019
               tive means to manipulate 3DGS freely while maintaining rendering quality. In
020
               this work, we aim to tackle the challenge of achieving manipulable photo-realistic
021            rendering. We propose to utilize a triangular mesh to manipulate 3DGS directly
022            with self-adaptation. This approach reduces the need to design various algorithms
023            for different types of Gaussian manipulation. By utilizing a triangle shape-aware
024            Gaussian binding and adapting method, we can achieve 3DGS manipulation and
025            preserve high-fidelity rendering. Our approach is capable of handling large de-
026            formations, local manipulations, and soft body simulations while keeping high-
027            quality rendering. Furthermore, we demonstrate that our method is also effective
028            with inaccurate meshes extracted from 3DGS. Experiments conducted demon-
029
               strate the effectiveness of our method and its superiority over baseline approaches.
               Project page here: https://mani3dgs.github.io/

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Manipulating and editing 3D content is essential for content creation and has various applications in
035
      movies, gaming, and virtual/augmented reality. 3D model editing enables users to create and modify
036   models flexibly, thereby enhancing production efficiency. The traditional pipeline for modeling and
037   editing a 3D asset with photo-realistic rendering involves a process with geometry modeling, tex-
038   turing, UV mapping, lighting, and rendering, which is a tedious and time-consuming flow requiring
039   lots of manual work.
040
      Over the past few years, the neural radiance field (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2021) has been widely
041
      studied due to its high capability and simple reconstruction process in 3D representation. However,
042   the implicit representation poses challenges for editing.
043
044   To address this, some methods are proposed to edit this implicit neural radiance field (Mildenhall
045
      et al., 2021). NeRF-Editing (Yuan et al., 2022) is the first to utilize the triangular mesh to help edit the
      implicit radiance field. They train a canonical NeuS (Wang et al., 2021) and extract the triangular
046
      mesh from NeuS (Wang et al., 2021). A tetrahedra grid is then constructed to contain the object
047
      mesh. To render the deformed object by editing the triangular mesh, a volume rendering is conducted
048   in the deformed space. The sampling points in deformed space are mapped to canonical space based
049   on the constructed tetrahedra grid, where the points in deformed space have the same tetrahedron
050   barycentric coordinate in the same tetrahedron with their corresponding points in canonical space.
051   Moreover, (Jambon et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) demonstrate similar ideas by employing tetrahedra
052   to deform sampling points and achieve editable nerf-based scenes. Instead, Neu-Mesh (Yang et al.,
053   2022) and SERF (Zhou et al., 2023a) define the neural radiance field by associating each mesh vertex
      with radiance and geometry features. Then they can conduct volume rendering like Point-NeRF (Xu

072   on implicit neural radiance fields still suffer from inconvenient manipulation, suboptimal rendering
073   results, and long training and rendering times.
074   Meanwhile, 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023) has gained significant attention in differential rendering due
075   to its high-fidelity and fast rendering proficiency. However, despite being an explicit 3D repre-
076   sentation, it still lacks an effective method for manipulating 3DGS while maintaining high-quality
077   rendering. SuGaR (Guédon & Lepetit, 2023), the work most closely related to our objective, de-
078   velops a novel algorithm to extract a triangular mesh from 3DGS. Although their main goal is not
079   to facilitate editable photorealistic rendering, they bind the 3DGS to the extracted mesh, enabling
080
      model animation as demonstrated in their demo.
081   This work proposes a method that enables 3DGS manipulation, achieving high-quality and photo-
082   realistic rendering. Our key insight is to manipulate 3DGS using a triangular mesh as the proxy,
083   which allows for direct transfer of mesh manipulation to 3DGS with 3DGS self-adaptation. With
084   our methods, we can achieve large deformation, local manipulation, and soft body simulaton with
085   high-quality results as shown in Figure 1, which also avoid the need to design various algorithms for
086
      different types of manipulation.
087   To achieve controllable 3DGS manipulation through the mesh, an intuitive approach is to bind the
088   GS to lay perfectly on the triangle and enforce the GS to be thin enough. After mesh manipulation,
089   the GS will automatically adapt its rotation and position with the attached triangle, as employed
090   in SuGaR (Guédon & Lepetit, 2023). However, SuGaR heavily relies on the accuracy of mesh
091   geometry, inheriting the defects of mesh rendering. Specifically, for inaccurate parts, SuGaR cannot
092
      inpaint the missing parts or remove the redundant parts in the final rendering.
093   Adding an offset to the position of attached Gaussians during the reconstruction may seem like
094   a reasonable solution to compensate for mesh inaccuracy. However, this fixed offset cannot be
095   generalized well to the deformed space after novel manipulation. Our proposed solution is to define
096   a local coordinate system for each triangle, which we refer to as local triangle space. We then bind
097   Gaussians to each triangle and optimize the Gaussian attributes, including rotation, position, and
098
      scaling, in the attached local triangle space.
099   During mesh manipulation, the attributes in the local triangle space remain unchanged, while the
100   global Gaussian position, scaling, and rotation will be self-adaptively adjusted according to our
101   proposed formula. As a result, our proposed approach enables us to manipulate 3DGS using a
102   triangular mesh while maintaining rendering quality. Since our Gaussians are set to be free outside
103   the triangle, we can also support high-fidelity manipulation even when the Gaussians are bound to
104
      an inaccurate mesh, exhibiting a high tolerance for mesh accuracy.
105   GaMeS (Gao et al., 2024) and Mesh-GS (Waczyńska et al., 2024) are two concurrent works that also
106   employ triangular meshes for Gaussian Splatting manipulation. In particular, GaMeS (Gao et al.,
107   2024) constrains the Gaussians on the surface exactly; Mesh-GS (Waczyńska et al., 2024) permits
      an offset along the normal direction without adapting the Gaussian scale when the triangle shape

113          • We propose a 3DGS manipulation method that can effectively transfer the triangular mesh

123   2.1   N E RF E DITING
124
      Recently, neural radiance field (Mildenhall et al., 2021) (NeRF) has garnered significant attention
125
      due to its high-quality and photo-realistic rendering results for novel view synthesis. NeRF rep-
126   resents the scene as a continuous function that maps a spatial location and viewing direction to a
127   volume density and color, which is parameterized by a multilayer perceptron (MLP). Owing to the
128   implicit representation that encodes the scene within the network parameters, editing and deforming
129   the geometry of the NeRF scene explicitly like mesh can be challenging. To enable user editing of
130   NeRF, (Liu et al., 2021) introduce editing conditional radiance fields trained on a shape category.
131   However, it only supports basic editing operations, such as removing/adding object parts or shape
132   transfer. CLIP-NeRF (Wang et al., 2022) achieves NeRF editing with text or images by leveraging
133   CLIP model (Radford et al., 2021) but still can not edit the geometry locally. Some other work (Xi-
134
      ang et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023b; Bao et al., 2023; Zhan et al., 2023) edit the NeRF in texture
      level which is not the focus of this paper.
135
136   To edit and deform NeRF locally, (Jambon et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023) construct
137   a tetrahedra grid based on the underlying 3D shape. After explicitly deforming the tetrahedra into
138   the posed space for editing, the sampled 3D points are mapped from the posed space to the canonical
139   space through the unaltered tetrahedron, which means the canonical position can be calculated from
140
      the shared barycentric coordinate for both deformed and canonical tetrahedron. The density and
      radiance in the posed space can be calculated for the mapped sampling points in canonical space.
141
      On the other hand, (Wang et al., 2023a; Zhou et al., 2023a; Yang et al., 2022) employ mesh as the
142
      guidance for deformation. NeuMesh (Yang et al., 2022) presents a novel representation to encode
143   neural implicit field on a mesh-based scaffold for geometry and texture editing. (Wang et al., 2023a)
144   achieves the manipulation of both the geometry and color of neural implicit fields through differen-
145   tiable colored meshes. Furthermore, there are several methods (Sun et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2022;
146   Zhang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023a; Zheng et al., 2022) that particularly focus on editing NeRF
147   for avatar. In this work, we introduce an editing method based on 3DGS for general objects.
148
149   2.2   M ESH - BASED N E RF R ENDERING
150
151   NeRFs have shown impressive rendering results, however, rendering one pixel using NeRF repre-
152   sentation necessitates a volumetric rendering algorithm that involves inferring MLP hundreds of
153
      times to estimate their radiance and density. This process is significantly slower than traditional
      mesh rendering. To accelerate NeRF rendering, several methods (Chen et al., 2023b; Rakotosaona
154
      et al., 2023; Yariv et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b; Yao et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2023b) have been
155
      proposed to combine NeRF representation with mesh reconstruction. By converting this implicit
156   representation to an explicit mesh, these methods may also facilitate applications like editing. In
157   particular, MobileNeRF (Chen et al., 2023b) represents NeRF as a collection of polygons with deep
158   feature textures, which can be rendered using the classic polygon rasterization pipeline, generating
159   a feature vector for each pixel and passing it to an MLP to decode the color. MobileNeRF is capable
160   of achieving rendering even on standard mobile devices. Additionally, NeRFMeshing (Rakotosaona
161   et al., 2023) distills the reconstructed NeRF into a signed surface approximation network to extract
      3D mesh and shows the simulation results for editing. BakedSDF (Yariv et al., 2023) proposes a


                                                       3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      neural surface-volume representation to extract the mesh and supports editing like material decom-
163   position, appearance editing, and physics simulation. However, the editing results of these methods
164   heavily rely on the accuracy of the extracted mesh. Artifacts present in the mesh will directly influ-
165   ence the editing results. In contrast, our method demonstrates robustness to the reconstructed mesh
166   and can still yield promising results even with the inaccurate mesh.

168   2.3   G AUSSIAN S PLATTING E DITING , S IMULATION AND A NIMATION
169
170   3D Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023) presents an innovative 3D Gaussian scene representation,
171   accompanied by a differentiable renderer that attains real-time rendering of radiance fields while
172   maintaining high quality. Initially, 3D Gaussian Splatting focuses solely on static scenes, which has
173
      been extended to model dynamic scenes (Wu et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Lin
      et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023b), human avatars (Zielonka et al., 2023; Qian et al., 2023; Yuan et al.,
174
      2023; Hu et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Kirschstein et al., 2023), Gaussian Splatting simulation and
175
      animation (Guédon & Lepetit, 2023; Xie et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024). Specif-
176   ically, SuGaR (Guédon & Lepetit, 2023) proposes a method to extract meshes from 3D Gaussian
177   Splatting with additional regularization, in which they bind 3DGS on extracted mesh and animated
178   with Gaussian Splatting rendering. GSP (Feng et al., 2024) incorporates physically-based fluid
179   dynamics in 3DGS and PhysGaussian (Xie et al., 2023) introduces a unified simulation-rendering
180   pipeline that generates physics-based dynamics with photorealistic renderings. VR-GS (Jiang et al.,
181   2024) achieves interactive physics-based editing in Virtual Reality. In this work, we also introduce
182   a Gaussian Splatting manipulation method that binds Gaussian Splatting to the mesh, achieving
183   state-of-the-art results.

185   3     M ETHOD
186
187   Recently, due to its exceptional high-fidelity rendering capabilities and fast rendering speed,
188   3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023) has emerged as a popular 3D representation in the differential render-
189   ing field. However, despite being an explicit 3D representation, it still lacks a way for manipulating
190   this 3D representation for editing while maintaining high-quality rendering after the manipulation.
191   In this work, giving multi-view RGB images of an object as input, we introduce a method for object
192
      manipulation that can achieve photorealistic editable rendering by employing Gaussian Splatting.
193   The pipeline of our method is illustrated in Figure 2 and consists of three main stages. First, we
194   extract a mesh from 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) or a neural surface field for subsequent 3D
195   Gaussian binding (Sec. 3.2). Next, we devise a novel Mesh-Gaussian binding method dedicated
196   to manipulating Gaussian Splatting while maintaining photo-realistic rendering quality (Sec. 3.3).
197   Finally, we describe the types of Gaussian manipulation we support(Sec. 3.4).

199   3.1   P RELIMINARY
200
      Thanks to its superior capability for high-fidelity and rapid rendering, 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS)
201
      (Kerbl et al., 2023) has recently emerged as a popular 3D representation in differential rendering.
202   3DGS utilizes explicit 3D Gaussians as its primary rendering primitives. A 3D Gaussian point is
203   mathematically defined as:
204
205
                                     G(\boldsymbol {x}) = exp(-\frac {1}{2}(\boldsymbol {x}-\boldsymbol {\mu })^\top \Sigma ^{-1}(\boldsymbol {x}-\boldsymbol {\mu })) . \label {eq:3d_gaussian}    (1)
206
207
      Each 3D Gaussian point is characterized by a 3D mean position coordinate µ and a covariance
208
      matrix Σ. Additionally, each Gaussian has an opacity o and a view-dependent color c represented
209   by a set of spherical harmonics (SH). To ensure that the covariance matrix Σ retains its meaning-
210   ful interpretation, it is parameterized as a unit quaternion q and a 3D scaling vector s, defined as
211   Σ = RSS ⊤ R⊤ .
212
      To render an image from a specific viewpoint, 3D Gaussians are projected onto the image plane,
213
      resulting in 2D Gaussians. The 2D covariance matrix is approximated as:
214
215
                                                                           \Sigma '=\boldsymbol {J}\boldsymbol {W}\Sigma \boldsymbol {W}^\top \boldsymbol {J}^\top ,                                (2)

216
      where W and J denote the viewing transformation and the Jacobian of the affine approximation
217   of perspective projection transformation (Zwicker et al., 2002), respectively. The 2D means are
218   calculated through the projection matrix. After this, the pixel color is composited through the alpha
219   blending of N ordered 2D Gaussians:
220

224   from Σ′ and pixel coordinate on the image space.

226   3.2   M ESH E XTRACTION
227
228   Our method can achieve high-quality editing using guided meshes obtained from various methods.
229   In this section, we investigate different mesh extraction and reconstruction techniques with different
230   mesh accuracy and processing time to guide the 3D Gaussians in our approach.
231   Marching Cube for Gaussian Splatting. In DreamGaussian (Tang et al., 2023a), the method at-
232   tempts to summarize the alpha values of neighboring Gaussian points as the composite density value
233   of marching cube sampling points. However, We found that this method often ignores the thin and
234   small structures. With our Mesh-Gaussian binding strategy, we can achieve high-fidelity rendering
235   with the inaccurate mesh and support smooth Gaussian manipulation.
236
      Screened Poisson Reconstruction. 3D Gaussian Splatting could be considered a type of point
237   cloud, making it intuitive to extract the mesh using the Poisson-reconstruction algorithm. However,
238   the 3D Gaussians do not have normal vectors for reconstruction. Inspired by recent 3DGS inverse
239   rendering methods (Gao et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2023), we allocate an additional gaussian attribute,
240   normal n, for 3D Gaussians, which is supervised by the pseudo normal derived from depth map.
241   After training 3DGS with normal attributes, we can extract the mesh using the Screened poisson
242   surface reconstruction (Kazhdan & Hoppe, 2013) algorithm.
243   Neural Implicit Surfaces. In this work, we also try to extract high-quality surfaces from the implicit
244   representation utilizing the method proposed in NeuS (Wang et al., 2021). NeuS mesh has a large
245   number of triangles, which negatively affects both training and inference speeds. We utilize mesh
246   decimation techniques to reduce the count of triangles to approximately 300K.

338   3.4   M ANIPULATE G AUSSIAN S PLATTING THROUGH M ESH
339
340   Utilizing our triangle shape aware gaussian binding and adapting strategy, upon the completion of
341   model training and mesh manipulation, the 3DGS is instantly manipulated and adapted. During
342   mesh manipulation, the attributes in the local triangle space remain unchanged. The triangle rota-
343
      tion, position, and edge length can be calculated instantly. Therefore, the global Gaussian position,
      scaling, and rotation can be self-adaptively adjusted following our proposed formula. In this paper,
344
      we exhibit the 3DGS manipulation rendering outcomes, such as large-scale deformation, local ma-
345
      nipulation, and soft-body simulation, which are driven by the manipulated mesh. Many 3D design
346   software applications possess the capability to execute mesh manipulation. In our experiments, we
347   employ Blender to manipulate the mesh.

353
      The first stage of our methods includes a mesh extracting stage, during which we extract triangular
354
      mesh from NeuS (Wang et al., 2021) or 3DGS (Screened Poisson or Marching Cube). However, the

357   With the extracted mesh, we conduct the triangle shape aware Gaussian binding and adapting
358
      strategy on the mesh. For each triangle, we bind N = 3 Gaussian on the surface initially. The

359
      the second stage. We train our model for 30K iterations in the initial stage to extract mesh and 20K
360
      iterations in the second stage. All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU.

527   5   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
528
529   In this paper, we introduce a triangle shape aware Gaussian binding strategy with self-adaptation,
530   which supports various 3DGS manipulations, maintains rendering quality, and has a high toler-
531   ance for mesh accuracy. We evaluate our methods on the NeRF synthetic dataset and demonstrate
532   state-of-the-art results, showcasing various 3DGS manipulations, including large deformations, lo-
533   cal manipulations, and soft body simulations.
534   During our experiments, we noticed that some results still exhibit distortions. When the local region
535   of the manipulated mesh contains highly non-rigid deformations, it can result in rendering distor-
536   tions. Additionally, during our simulation demos, we found that conducting physics simulations on
537   meshes with more than 35K triangles can take hours. It would be a novel direction to explore fast
538   simulation methods for 3DGS. Finally, we found that our results may not have accurate boundary
539   rendering when the extracted mesh has a significant discrepancy from the ground truth mesh, such
      as unconnected regions.
