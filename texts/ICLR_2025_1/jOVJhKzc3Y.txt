011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Pre-trained vision-language models (VLMs), such as CLIP, have shown remark-
014            able success in the text-video retrieval task due to their strong vision-language
015            representations learned from large-scale paired image-text samples. However,
016            compared to videos, text is often short and concise, making it difficult to fully
017
               capture the rich and redundant semantics present in a video with thousands of
               frames. Recent advances have focused on utilizing text features to extract key in-
018
               formation from these redundant video frames. However, text representation gen-
019
               erated without considering video information can suffer from bias and lack the
020            expressiveness needed to capture key words that could enhance retrieval perfor-
021            mance. In this study, we first conduct preliminary experiments to demonstrate
022            the importance of enhancing text representations. These experiments reveal that
023            text representation only generated from text input often misinterpret critical infor-
024            mation. To address this, we propose a simple yet efficient method, VICTER, i.e.,
025            video-conditioned text representation refinement, to enrich text representation us-
026            ing a versatile module. Specifically, we introduce a video abstraction module that
027            extracts representative features from multiple video frames. This is followed by
028
               a video-conditioned text enhancement module that refines the original text fea-
               tures by reassessing individual word features and extracting key words using the
029
               generated video features. Empirical evidence shows that VICTER not only ef-
030
               fectively captures relevant key words from the input text but also complements
031            various existing frameworks. Our experimental results demonstrate a significant
032            improvement of VICTER over several baseline frameworks (with 0.4% ∼ 1.0%
033            improvements on R@1). Furthermore, VICTER achieves state-of-the-art perfor-
034            mance on three benchmark datasets, including MSRVTT, DiDeMo, and LSMDC.
035            Code will be made available.

038   1   I NTRODUCTION
039
040   Text-video retrieval aims to find the most relevant video or text based on a given query (Gabeur
041   et al., 2020; Luo et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023a; Ging et al., 2020). With the exponential growth
042   of online video content on platforms like YouTube, Netflix, and TikTok, text-video retrieval has
043   gained increasing attention and plays a vital role in modern applications, such as search engines and
044   recommendation systems (Davidson et al., 2010; Gomez-Uribe & Hunt, 2015).
045   The emergence of vision-language models (VLMs) like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), which are
046   pretrained on large-scale image-text datasets, offers a powerful solution to this problem. These
047   models have demonstrated remarkable cross-modal representation capabilities, making it feasible to
048   transfer their knowledge from images to videos (Luo et al., 2022; Gan et al., 2023). Specifically,
049   CLIP uses a dual-branch structure, comprising a text encoder and an image encoder, to learn cross-
050   modal alignment. However, CLIP’s image encoder can only generate frame-level features, which
051   fail to capture the temporal information inherent in videos. As a result, one research direction has
052   been to explore better ways to leverage this temporal information (Bain et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022).
053   A video typically contains hundreds of frames, while text descriptions—such as captions or subti-
      tles—are often much shorter and consist of only a few words (Lin et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024).

086

089   trieval tasks. By efficiently refining the text representation using video content, VICTER bridges the

091   with minimal additional computation. We summarize the contributions of this work as follows:

106
107   Pretrained Vision-Language Model. Vision-language pre-training aims to understand and process
      the relationship between image and text modalities. Early research works utilized sequence en-

108
      coders, such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) (Graves & Graves, 2012) and Gated Recurrent
109   Units (GRU) (Chung et al., 2014), to learn language representations. With the success of BERT (De-
110   vlin, 2018) in learning contextual text representations, many vision-language works (Gabeur et al.,
111   2020; Sun et al., 2019; Zhu & Yang, 2020; Wang et al., 2021b) began leveraging pre-trained BERT
112   features to enhance language representation capabilities. More recently, contrastive image-text pre-
113   training (Tan & Bansal, 2019; Radford et al., 2021) on large-scale web data has significantly ad-
114   vanced performance in vision-language tasks. CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), one of the most promi-
115   nent pre-trained models, has demonstrated powerful zero-shot capabilities (Luo et al., 2022; Deng
116   et al., 2023) for video-language understanding and has become a de facto baseline for text-video
117
      retrieval tasks.
118   Text-to-video Retrieval. The goal of the retrieval task is to retrieve relevant videos from a database
119   of video clips based on a text query (Rohrbach et al., 2015; Xu et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2019;
120   Anne Hendricks et al., 2017). Previous studies (Chen et al., 2020; Mithun et al., 2018) primarily
121   focused on developing fusion strategies to align pre-extracted text and video features. With the
122   introduction of CLIP, more recent approaches have concentrated on enhancing video and text rep-
123
      resentations (Croitoru et al., 2021; Lei et al., 2021; Bain et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Li et al.,
      2023b). For example, X-Pool (Gorti et al., 2022) utilizes text-conditioned feature fusion across
124
      video frames, while PIDRo (Guan et al., 2023) models fine-grained semantic clues between video
125
      and text. UATVR (Fang et al., 2023) addresses the inherent uncertainties in both text and image
126   modalities. DiffusionRet (Jin et al., 2023b) advances retrieval by integrating diffusion models into
127   the text-video retrieval pipeline, and T-MASS (Wang et al., 2024) enriches text embeddings by treat-
128   ing them as stochastic embeddings. In contrast to previous methods focused on carefully selecting
129   video features, our VICTER takes a complementary approach by refining the text features to address
130   the inherent limitations of the CLIP’s text encoder.
131
      Video-Language Post-Pretraining. To better leverage the temporal information in video data and
132   overcome the limitations of CLIP’s image-text pretraining, several works (Wang et al., 2023; Cheng
133   et al., 2023) re-pretrain a unified backbone architecture to directly output video-level features in-
134   stead of frame-level ones. For instance, CLIP-ViP (Xue et al., 2022b) introduces a proxy-guided
135   video attention mechanism and re-pretrains the entire framework on large datasets like WebVid-
136   2.5M (Bain et al., 2021) and HDVILA-100M (Xue et al., 2022a) to generate richer video represen-
137   tations. Cap4Video (Wu et al., 2023), on the other hand, enhances video representations by incorpo-
138   rating auxiliary captions. Our VICTER requires no large-scale post-pretraining overhead. Instead,
139   it serves as a plug-and-play module that can be seamlessly integrated into existing frameworks.
140   Fine-grained Interaction between Words and Frames. There is a research trend that closely
141   relates to our VICTER, focusing on semantic alignments through fine-grained interactions (Wang
142   et al., 2021a;b; Zhu & Yang, 2020; Ma et al., 2022) between word and frame features. However,
143   many of these approaches introduce complex cross-modal fusion modules that emphasize specific
144   entities within the language (e.g., words and phrases) and video (e.g., frames and regions) (Chen
145   et al., 2020). While these methods have demonstrated significant performance improvements, they
146
      often come with prohibitive computational costs. In this paper, we propose a lightweight and ver-
      satile video-conditioned text enhancement module that refines the global text representation by ad-
147
      dressing the attention bias introduced by the text encoder’s emphasis on certain words.

      4.4   OVERALL A RCHITECTURE
389
      Building on the foundation of adapting pre-trained VLMs, we present the detailed framework of
390   VICTER built on CLIP and X-Pool models (Gorti et al., 2022) in Figure 7 in appendix. However,
391   VICTER is versatile and can be seen as a general module for video-conditioned text representa-
392   tion refinement. For example, if we replace X-Pool’s vision encoder with CLIP-VIP’s vision en-
393   coder (Xue et al., 2022b), which directly uses the first video proxy token as the video representation,
394   our video-conditioned text enhancement module can be easily integrated into their network without
395   major adjustments. During the testing phase, the extra cost is from the cross-attention mechanism
396   added during the text representation enhancement stage between each text-video pair, resulting in
397   negligible additional computational overhead.

399   5     E XPERIMENT
400
401   5.1   E XPERIMENT S ETUP
402
403   Datasets. We evaluate our method on three widely used text-video retrieval benchmarks:
404   • MSR-VTT (Xu et al., 2016) contains 10,000 video clips, each annotated with 20 sentences. We
405     follow the 1K testing split used in (Gorti et al., 2022).
406   • DiDeMo (Anne Hendricks et al., 2017) comprises 10,642 video clips paired with a total of 40,543
407     captions. We adopt the train-test split from (Bain et al., 2021), where all sentence descriptions for
408     a video are concatenated into a single query.
409   • LSMDC (Rohrbach et al., 2015) consists of 118,081 short clips from 202 movies, with each video
410     typically paired with one caption. We follow the split defined by Gorti et al. (2022), using 109,673
411     videos for training, 7,408 for validation, and 1,000 for testing.
412
413   Evaluation Metrics. We assess model performance using standard retrieval metrics, including
414
      R@K (Recall at Rank K=1,5,10, higher is better), Median Rank (MdR, lower is better), and Mean
      Rank (MnR, lower is better), following the protocols from (Luo et al., 2022; Gorti et al., 2022).
415
416   Implementation Details. We initialize both the text and image encoders using CLIP checkpoints
417   (ViT-B/32 and ViT-B/16). All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA A100 80GB GPU
418   with PyTorch library. Following (Gorti et al., 2022), new FC layers are initialized with identity
419   matrices, and biases are set to zero. Our models are fine-tuned end-to-end on each dataset, with
420
      12 frames uniformly sampled from each video and resized to 224×224. We use a batch size of
      32 for all experiments, setting the learning rate to 1e-6 for CLIP-initialized weights and 1e-5 for
421
      other parameters. The models are trained for 5 epochs (10 epochs for DiDeMo), optimized using
422
      the AdamW optimizer with a weight decay of 0.2 and a cosine learning rate schedule.

478
      abstraction module, we compare it with several variants, including mean pooling, random selection,

483   to poorer performance. In contrast, average pooling outperforms random selection, indicating that
484   most frames can accurately represent the video’s content. Our abstraction method achieves the best
485   results, supporting our hypothesis that emphasizing the content that occupies more of the video is a
      more effective strategy.

531
532
      This work focuses on refining text representation by addressing the inherent limitations of adapt-
533
      ing pretrained image-text models for text-video retrieval tasks. We observed that directly using a
534   pretrained text encoder often results in suboptimal bias, where the model tends to overemphasize
535   certain nouns, leading to misinterpretations of the description. Moreover, key elements in the video,
536   which typically occupy a significant portion of the frames, can serve as useful prior knowledge for
537   extracting video feature. Based on these insights, we propose a versatile solution with two com-
538   ponents: the video abstraction module and the video-conditioned text enhancement module. These
539   modules can be seamlessly integrated into existing retrieval frameworks to significantly improve
      performance with minimal additional computational cost.
