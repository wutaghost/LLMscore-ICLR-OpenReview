011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we revisit the image restoration (IR) task and propose a new train-
014            ing strategy that models the IR problem as a distribution mapping challenge from
015            two perspectives, i.e., (1) the intra-pixel regression and (2) the inter-pixel interac-
016
               tion. At the beginning of optimization, due to the pattern distribution involving a
               group of pixels within a neighborhood, it is not very easy for the model to capture
017
               such multi-pixel distribution mapping. A more optimal solution would be firstly
018
               teaching the model to learn a relatively simple yet important distribution w.r.t the
019            pixel-by-pixel mapping between the degraded/clean pixels, as warming up. By
020            doing so, the learned distribution is served as a prior, regarded as an injection of
021            a kind of inductive bias into the model’s whole optimization procedure. Subse-
022            quently, as conventional, the model is shifted to focus on the mapping distribution
023            of the cross-pixel patterns, which ensures the consistency and fidelity of the im-
024            age patterns. The final learned mapping is a joint distribution, which transfers the
025            knowledge from the pixel distributions to the pattern ones. Experimental results
026            indicate that under the compact and elegant training paradigm, the newly learned
027
               joint distribution is closer to the ideal one and yields a stronger representation
               ability, to circumvent the dilemma of the difficulty for existing methods to learn
028
               the patterns mapping distribution between degraded/clean images right off the bat.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Image restoration (IR), aims to remove degradations (e.g., blur and noise) from a low-quality de-
034
      graded image (LQ) to generate a high-quality counterpart (HQ). Recent years have witnessed great
035
      success in IR tasks with deep learning techniques (Nah et al., 2017; Vincent et al., 2008). Fur-
036   thermore, many existing methods face challenges either in designing complex network architec-
037   tures (Liang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2023b) or in incorporating auxiliary structures
038   like (Liu et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2024). These approaches aim to enhance the
039   models’ representational power and generalization ability but often introduce increased complexity
040   and external dependencies. Some approaches (Wang et al., 2022; Zamir et al., 2022a; Li et al., 2021)
041   propose new training strategies also to improve the average performance on benchmark datasets.
042   The essence of the IR problem is to learn the pixel-to-pixel regression mapping from the degraded
043   image space to the clean one. However, as illustrated in Fig. 1, rather than learning an easier pixel-
044   by-pixel distribution mapping, current IR methods generally apply an across-the-board training strat-
045   egy to their optimization process that makes the model directly learn a distribution mapping involv-
046   ing a set of pixels within a neighborhood from stem to stern. They consistently overlook the fact
047   that networks struggle to effectively model the cross-pixel contents and patterns distribution map-
048   ping, which need to take multi-pixels into account at a time in the early training stage. Therefore,
049   this increases the overall complexity of training the IR model and further results in the optimization
050
      being trapped in a sub-optimal geometry landscape.
051   In this paper, we identify the nature of pixel-to-pixel regression for IR tasks and model it as an
052   optimization problem from two perspectives: (1) the intra-pixel regression and (2) the inter-pixel
053   interaction. With such observations, we propose the InTRA-patch Pixel-Shuffle training strategy,
      dubbed TRAPS. Specifically, from the intra-pixel perspective, at the early stage in training, the

      2.1   I MAGE R ESTORATION
112
      Image restoration aims to recover images degraded by factors like noise and blur. Driven largely by
113
      the capabilities of various backbone neural networks architecture (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Dosovit-
114   skiy et al., 2021) with the development of deep learning, significant advancements have been made
115   in sub-fields such as image denoising (Zhang et al., 2017; 2019; Zamir et al., 2022b) and image
116   deblurring (Nah et al., 2017; Xu et al., 2014). Generally, IR methods like (Liang et al., 2021; Zamir
117   et al., 2022a; Wang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023b) use a data-driven approach after the network struc-
118   ture is designed and train directly on pairs of data until the network converges. These methods do
119   not pay attention to the fact that IR tasks should have different preferences when dealing with inputs
120   with different content distributions. However, even if some methods (Gu et al., 2024; Jiang et al.,
121   2023a) pay attention to this issue, they still do not recognize the inherent property of IR task pixel-
122
      to-pixel regression. This entangles the intra- and inter-processes and leads to sub-optimal training
      strategies.
123
124
125   2.2   T RAINING STRATEGY ON I MAGE R ESTORATION
126
127   Recently, researchers have explored various deep learning-based training strategies for IR tasks. RL-
128   Restore (Yu et al., 2018) introduces reinforcement learning into IR. Huang et al. presents a complex
129   training strategy, BayerEnsemble (Huang et al., 2022), which addresses raw image denoising based
130
      on the Bayer filter. Liu et al. (Liu et al., 2022) and Chen et al. (Chen et al., 2024) leverage pre-
      training strategies to learn generalized natural image priors for IR tasks. DIL (Li et al., 2023a)
131
      proposes a causality-based training strategy to improve the generalization ability of IR methods for
132
      unknown degradations. Chen et al. introduce MaskedDenoising (Chen et al., 2023a) to explicitly
133   learn representations for content reconstruction in image denoising from a masked image modeling
134   perspective. Jiang et al. introduce a few-shot learning paradigm (Jiang et al., 2023b) to eliminate
135   the reliance on clean-noisy image pairs for the denoising task. Self-supervised training is introduced
136   to DCD-Net (Zou et al., 2023) to progressively achieve improved denoising results by an iterative
137   optimization strategy. Huang et al. propose WaveDM (Huang et al., 2024) to learn the low-/high-
138   frequency spectrums in distinct modules in the transform domain for IR tasks by introducing the
139   Diffusion Model (Ho et al., 2020). The performance gains from such training strategies come at the
140   cost of introducing complex computational mechanisms or additional auxiliary structures that bring
141
      an additional burden into the training process.

      3.1   M OTIVATION : L EARNING D ISTRIBUTIONS FROM P IXELS TO PATTERNS
147
      Recently, there has been a proliferation of IR methods based on deep learning, which have achieved
148
      considerable performance on various benchmark datasets. However, these previous approaches have
149
      consistently ignored one of the fundamental properties of the IR task, i.e., pixel-by-pixel regression,
150   instead choosing to optimize it from a more complicated perspective - learning the cross-pixel pat-
151   tern distribution. Therefore, we re-visit the IR task from this perspective and raise the following
152   scientific question: on the basis of the existing network structure and the available data, is it possible
153   to take advantage of the pixel-by-pixel regression and re-model the IR optimization problem to find
154   a concise and elegant training strategy to achieve better representation ability?
155
      To this end, we seek the probabilistic distribution modeling for help. The essence of the IR problem
156   is to learn a mapping whereby the model is expected to map images from one distribution (degraded
157   image space) into another image space (clean image space). Formally, hypothesizing that the ideal
158   clean image Yideal has the distribution qideal , i.e., Yideal ∼ qideal . The original IR methods sample
159   training data points from the degraded image space Xin ∼ qdegraded and try to directly learn the
160   mapping relation from Xin to the clean image distribution qideal . This approach removes degrada-
161   tion by mapping limited degradation from Xin to the clean image space partially. Due to the large
      gap between the two distributions, however, direct modeling qdegraded to qideal is more difficult, and

196   3.2     T ECHNICAL D ETAILS : THE I N TRA- PATCH P IXEL -S HUFFLE STRATEGY
197
198   In this section, we detail the specifics of our proposed solution and answer the research question
199   posed in the motivation above. In response to that, we propose the InTRA-patch Pixel-Shuffle
200   training strategy, dubbed TRAPS. The comprehensive framework is elucidated in Fig. 3.
201   Specifically, considering the high difficulty of directly learning the distribution q(Xout |Xin ) - the
202   model’s limited ability to capture cross-pixel content and pattern representations at the early stages
203   of training, we prioritize a simpler yet crucial mapping relationship. That is, we first enable the
204   model to learn a priori distribution q(Yp2p |Xp2p ) on how to map individual pixels from the LQ im-
205   age distribution into the pixel space of the corresponding GT image distribution. To implement this,
206   before each iteration in the early stages of training, we randomly shuffle the pixels of each image
207
      batch - taking the input LQ as an example, we first reshape the LQ from B, C, H, W to B, C, H ∗ W
      along its pixel dimensionality and further generate a corresponding randomly-indexed tensor with
208
      the same shape, shuf f le indices, where each slice of shuf f le indices is populated by a random
209
      permutation consisting of 0 to HW−1, for a total of B∗C such slices. Once the shuf f le indices are
210   obtained, we then shuffle the pixels of each image in the LQ according to the random indices in the
211   tensor to obtain Shuf f led LQ. Similarly, by doing the above for GT we obtain Shuf f led GT .
212   The gathered Shuf f led LQ will be subsequently fed to the IR network for iterative optimization
213   under the supervision of the signal Shuf f led GT to allow the model to capture the priori distri-
214   bution q(Yp2p |Xp2p ) until it is asked to stop early. At this point, we consider the network’s warm
215   up period complete, i.e., the priori distribution has been learned, having developed the ability to
      learn cross-pixel content and pattern distributions. The network then naturally transitions to the

      6   C ONCLUSION
530
531
532
      In this paper, we propose a concise and elegant training strategy for the IR task, which adheres
533
      to the idea of ‘Haste makes waste’ and abandons the paradigm that existing IR methods directly
534   learn the pattern distribution between degraded/clean images. The new paradigm decomposes the
535   IR optimization process into a distribution mapping problem from two perspectives. We propose
536   TRAPS to guide the model to first learn an a priori distribution for intra-pixel mapping. Conditioned
537   on this prior, the model is allowed to learn the mapping distribution among inter-pixel patterns. The
538   newly learned distribution can be regarded as a joint distribution, which smoothly transfers the final
539   distribution from the pixel level to the pattern one. Compared to the original method, the learned
      joint distribution is closer to the ideal one, yielding a stronger representation capability.

      A.2   T HE PSEUDOCODE OF TRAPS
719
      The pseudocode for TRAPS is as follows:
720
721
      Algorithm 1 InTRA-Patch Pixel Shuffle (TRAPS)
722
      Input: Two semantic preserved image batches, images1/images2, with the shape of (B, C, H, W).
723
          # B: the batch size, C: the number of channels, H/W: the dimensions of the image.
724    1: Flatten each image along the space dimension into 2D tensors of size (C, (HW)).
725    2: Generate random shuffle indices for each image in the batch, with the size (B, C, (HW)).
726    3: Use the random indices to shuffle the flattened image data.
727    4: Reshape the shuffled images back into their original shape (B, C, H, W).
728   Output: Two semantic stripped image batches, shuffled images1/shuffled images2, with the shape
729       of (B, C, H, W).
730
731
732
      Notably, since the shuffle indices are generated randomly each time, this ensures the randomness of
      pixel spatial position changes in the resulting semantically stripped images. Consequently, the priori
733
      distribution q(Yp2p |Xp2p ) learns from more independent mappings of individual pixels.
