                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               End-to-end automated driving behavior models require extensive training data
012
               from machine or human driver experts or interacting with the environment to learn
013
               a driving policy. Not all human driver expert data represent safe driving that the
014            end-to-end model is learning to imitate, and similarly, neither are some of the be-
015            haviors learned during exploration while learning by trial and error. However, the
016            models should learn from such data without being negatively affected during the
017            learning process. We aim to provide a learning framework to incorporate formal
018            verification methods to improve the robustness and safety of the learned models in
019            the presence of training data that contain unsafe behaviors, dubbed as STL-Drive.
020            We are particularly interested in utilizing this framework to enhance the safety of
021            end-to-end automated driving models. In this work, we incorporate Signal Tempo-
022
               ral Logic (STL) as the formal method to impose safety constraints. In addition, we
               utilize the Responsibility-Sensitive Safety (RSS) framework to define the safety
023
               constraints. We designed a loss function that combines the task objectives and
024
               the STL robustness score to balance the learned policy’s performance and safety.
025            We demonstrate that encoding safety constraints using STL and utilizing the ro-
026            bustness score during training improves the performance and safety of the driving
027            policy. We validate our framework using open-loop predictive simulator NAVSIM
028            and real-world data from OpenScene. The results of this study suggest a promis-
029            ing research direction where formal methods can enhance the safety and resilience
030            of deep learning models. Formal verification of safety constraints for automated
031            driving will further increase the public’s trust in automated vehicles.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   In recent years, automated driving systems have seen remarkable advancements, transforming how
037   we envision the future of transportation. These systems, whether semi-automated or fully automated,
038   are designed to enhance human mobility by making travel safer, more efficient, and more convenient.
039
      However, their value lies in meeting these mobility needs and ensuring the highest safety standards
      for all road users, including human drivers and pedestrians. Automated vehicles must prioritize
040
      accident prevention in critical scenarios, even compensating for other drivers’ mistakes, to create a
041
      safer, more reliable transportation ecosystem. For example, in Figure- 1, we observe different agents
042   reacting to the same scenario. The green dots represent the human driver’s trajectory, while the red
043   dots represent the learned model’s trajectory. The automated policy on the left mimics the expert
044   trajectory as closely as possible. At the same time, the robustness-aware RSS-Trained model on
045   the right adopts a more conservative approach by slowing down since there is a vehicle traveling in
046   the same lane that the ego-vehicle is attempting to merge with. In this case, the Transfuser model’s
047   prediction of future waypoints closely matches the expert trajectory. However, it is not necessarily
048   safe since the human driver can drive unsafely.
049   The modern approach to automated driving is formulating the task as a learning problem where the
050   agent has to learn a driving policy from data. Imitation Learning and Reinforcement Learning are
051   the two most popular learning methods in automated driving literature. Imitation learning (IL) (Chen
052   et al., 2020a; Chen & Krähenbühl, 2022; Prakash et al., 2021) is a form of supervised learning where
053   the agent learns a policy to mimic the behavior of an expert. Expert demonstrations from Human
      drivers or an automated vehicle with access to the states of other agents in a simulator are collected.

054
      IL methods suffer from distribution shift, i.e., if the agent encounters an unseen situation not present
055   in the training data, the agent will fail to take appropriate action and continue to do so without
056   recovering.
057
058
      On the other hand, Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) (Toromanoff et al., 2020; Chen et al.,
      2020a; Chekroun et al., 2021) methods are more robust to distribution shift than IL by allowing the
059
      agent to interact with the environment to learn a policy by trial-and-error. At every step, the agent
060
      takes an action and receives a reward from the environment, which signifies how good the action is.
061   The agent’s goal is to maximize the sum of the accumulated rewards, thereby learning a sequence
062   of actions that achieve the highest reward. Since the agent learns independently by exploring the
063   environment using only the reward function, DRL methods require more data than IL methods to
064   converge to an optimal policy. When collecting data through trial and error is expensive, researchers
065   use offline RL methods such as (Kumar et al., 2020) to learn a policy from collected data. These
066   methods are akin to supervised learning, where the agent learns to map input states to action pairs.

      To validate the safety of vehicle interactions on the road, researchers have proposed driving safety
090   assessment metrics (Wishart et al., 2020), which provide formal definitions to measure the safety
091   of driving behavior. The Responsibility-Sensitive Safety (RSS) framework (Shalev-Shwartz et al.,
092   2017) also calculates the minimum safe distance a pair of vehicles should maintain for a safe inter-
093   action in both lateral and longitudinal cases. When a violation of this minimum safe distance occurs,
094   the RSS framework provides a proper response action for the ego-vehicle to restore the minimum
095   distance to a safe distance again. RSS is quickly becoming a safety standard and has been used to
096   improve adaptive cruise control (ACC) (Brosowsky et al., 2021), lane change behaviors (Naumann
097   et al., 2019) for automated driving tasks. RSS has also been used for safety testing and validation
098
      of Automated Vehicle (AV) behaviors using falsification techniques in (Hekmatnejad et al., 2020)
      to identify the scenarios where an AV might become unsafe. Despite these efforts, research has
099
      been lacking in utilizing RSS to learn a driving policy for end-to-end automated driving. Our work
100
      STL-Drive fills this critical gap.
101

             • Using RSS framework to train a robust and safe policy rather than using it as a real-time

               (reactive) safety monitor;

             • Using Signal Temporal Logic Robustness score as a loss term in imitation learning;
107          • Finally, we formulate STL-Drive, which adopts Imitation learning to combine the robust-
               ness loss to improve the safety and robustness of a learned automated driving policy.

      2     M ETHODOLOGY
109
110
      In this work, we formulate AV decision-making as a waypoint prediction problem using Imitation
111   learning. Instead of predicting low-level control signals, we predict the waypoints the AV will take
112   in the future. To improve the robustness and safety of the automated driving policy, we use the RSS
113   model as safety constraints to enforce the minimum safe distance measure. Using STL, we encode
114   the RSS minimum safe distance rules and compute the robustness score to verify whether the vehicle
115   maintains the minimum safe distance. We use the robustness score as an additional loss term with
116   IL task loss to improve the performance and safety of the automated driving policy. We test and
117   validate our hypothesis on the NAVSIM benchmark.

      Signal Temporal Logic: (STL) (Maler & Ničković, 2004) is a formalism used for specifying
131   and reasoning about temporal properties of signals in cyber-physical systems. STL extends classi-
132   cal temporal logic by introducing quantitative predicates over real-valued signals, allowing for the
133   expression of timing constraints and conditions. STL is particularly useful for specifying behav-
134   iors such as safety, liveness, and performance requirements in systems where time plays a critical
135   role. The robustness score evaluates how well a system adheres to the specified temporal properties.
136   Unlike traditional Boolean evaluations that provide binary outcomes (true/false), robustness scores
137   offer a scalar value indicating the degree of satisfaction or violation of the STL specifications. This
138   not only helps with the testing of the system capabilities using falsifying techniques but also for
139
      the real-time monitoring of systems, which is valuable in applications requiring high precision and
      reliability, such as automated control systems and safety-critical monitoring.
140
141   Responsibility-Sensitive Safety: The RSS (Shalev-Shwartz et al., 2017) model is a formal frame-
142   work designed to ensure the safety of automated driving systems. It provides a set of guidelines and
143   rules that define what constitutes safe driving behavior under various traffic scenarios. RSS model
144   ensures that vehicles maintain a safe distance by considering speed and reaction time, dictates ap-
145
      propriate responses to other vehicles’ actions like braking or changing lanes, and outlines appropri-
      ate actions for handling dangerous situations to avoid collisions. It uses mathematical and logical
146
      formulations to provide formal safety guarantees, ensuring predictable system behavior. Safety dis-
147
      tance formulation by the framework considers worst-case scenarios to establish safety boundaries
148   that account for human error and unpredictable events. In this work, we encode the Minimum Safe

364   4    R ELATED W ORK AND BACKGROUND
365
366   End-to-End Automated Driving with Imitation Learning: Since RL relies on trial and error,
367   it is unsafe, costly, and data-inefficient to train automated driving vehicles in the physical world.
368   Hence, most of the research related to automated driving is studied in simulation. For example,
369
      CARLA (Dosovitskiy et al., 2017) is a popular choice for simulating realistic urban traffic interac-
      tions that include intersections, multiple vehicles, pedestrians, traffic lights, and stop signs, along
370
      with weather conditions affecting visibility. In CARLA, the task is to learn a goal-based navigation
371
      policy given a list of waypoints and high-level commands. Since there is a gap between simulated
372   and real-world data, some methods utilize recorded expert human driver logs to learn a policy using
373   Imitation Learning. In (Chekroun et al., 2021), the authors propose combining Imitation Learning
374   and Reinforcement Learning to utilize expert demonstrations and exploration to solve the distribu-
375   tion shift of general IL methods and sample inefficiency of RL methods. (Chitta et al., 2023) predict
376   waypoints using Imitation Learning by using sensor fusion and a transformer to gather spatial and
377   temporal context. (Chen & Krähenbühl, 2022) similarly predicts waypoints by learning to predict
      the trajectories of other agents in the scene.


                                                                                 7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Safe Imitation Learning: Constrained Imitation Learning, focuses on replicating expert driving
379   behavior while adhering to safety constraints. In Constrained Behavioral Cloning, the vehicle learns
380   to imitate expert trajectories but avoids unsafe actions like speeding or tailgating, ensuring a balance
381   between imitation and safety (Bojarski et al., 2016). Enhancements like Safe DAgger improve upon
382   this by incorporating safety checks during the training process, preventing the model from learning
383   unsafe behaviors even as it adapts and refines its policy (Ross et al., 2011). Additionally, real-
384   time mechanisms such as Model Predictive Control (MPC) Shields monitor the vehicle’s predicted
385   trajectory, overriding any potentially dangerous actions to prevent accidents or collisions (Chen
386   et al., 2020b). In contrast, we use the safety constraints offline to learn a robust policy without the
387
      need to constrain the policy in real time.
388   Safe Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL) methods solve this issue by inferring the reward func-
389   tions that drive human behavior, integrating safety into the learning process. Safe IRL helps vehi-
390   cles infer typical driving objectives, such as efficiency and safety while penalizing unsafe actions
391   like running red lights or following too closely (Ziebart et al., 2008). Risk-sensitive IRL introduces
392   additional caution in more high-risk environments, allowing autonomous systems to make conser-
393
      vative decisions in complex scenarios like dense urban traffic or interactions with vulnerable road
      users, such as pedestrians (Levine et al., 2011). However, inferring the reward function preferred
394
      by a human driver is a complex task and would need a lot of feature engineering to search for the
395
      optimal reward function that can improve the policy. Our method does not require such a task of
396   finding an appropriate reward function.
397
398   End-to-end learning with Safety Enhancements focuses on directly mapping sensor inputs, such as
399
      camera or LiDAR data, to driving control actions. These systems require careful regularization to
      avoid unsafe behavior. For example, barrier function-based safety regularization penalizes unsafe
400
      trajectories, keeping the vehicle within safe zones (Gurriet et al., 2018). Learning from safe demon-
401
      strations ensures that models only learn from optimal, expert-level behavior by filtering out unsafe
402   or sub-optimal human driving data (Zhang & Cho, 2016). Additionally, interactive imitation learn-
403   ing allows real-time expert intervention to correct unsafe actions during training (Ross et al., 2011).
404   Hybrid approaches like Safe RL, on top of Imitation, combine imitation learning with reinforcement
405   learning, allowing the vehicle to learn from demonstrations and then refine its behavior through RL,
406   ensuring both performance and safety in complex scenarios like merging onto highways or navi-
407   gating intersections (Kahn et al., 2017). Our work follows a similar approach by using the RSS
408   framework to guide and train the automated policy.

413   5   C ONCLUSION
414
415
416
417
      In this work, we propose a framework to combine task-based loss function with robustness loss
      function to learn a safe and robust automated driving policy. RSS rules are encoded using Signal
418
      Temporal Logic, and given a trace of the vehicle behavior, we compute the robustness score and
419
      utilize this robustness score in a loss function. We trained an imitation learning agent using the new
420   loss function that was shown to outperform a similar driving policy without the robustness loss.
421
422   The rapid evolution of artificial intelligence, especially in the realm of generative models, mirrors
423
      the multifaceted nature of human intelligence. While verbal intelligence has driven early advances
      through large language models (LLMs) and text-prompted generative AI, the future of AI lies in
424
      developing spatial intelligence. This shift is critical in fields such as automated driving, where
425
      understanding and interacting with the physical world is paramount. In the context of automated
426   driving, as discussed in this work, spatial intelligence manifests in the ability of AI models to reason
427   about and respond to complex, real-world scenarios involving dynamic environments and safety
428   constraints. The integration of Signal Temporal Logic (STL) and the Responsibility-Sensitive Safety
429   (RSS) framework enhances the spatial reasoning capabilities of AI, enabling safer and more reliable
430   decision-making. As we move forward, developing AI systems with deeper spatial understanding
431   will be the key to unlocking the next phase of human-machine collaboration, allowing us to build
      smarter vehicles and better, safer environments.

      6   L IMITATIONS
433
434
      While our framework STL-Drive demonstrates advancements in integrating formal verification
435   methods, such as STL and the RSS framework, to enhance the safety of end-to-end automated
436   driving systems, it is not without limitations. The first limitation lies in the dependency on the
437   quality and quantity of the training data. The scope of the training scenarios directly influences the
438   performance of our model, and although we incorporated diverse datasets like OpenScene, there
439   may still be unaccounted edge cases in real-world environments. Additionally, while incorporating
440   STL-based safety constraints offers a rigorous approach to improving robustness, the reliance on
441   predefined logical safety rules may limit flexibility in novel, unpredictable driving scenarios. Our
442   model has been designed to handle various situations, yet unforeseen conditions not covered by
443
      the defined temporal logic could challenge the framework’s adaptability. Another limitation is the
      computational cost associated with calculating robustness scores during training. While using the
444
      RSS model for enforcing safety has improved results, these computations can be resource-intensive,
445
      making it challenging to scale for real-time applications in highly dynamic environments. It is
446   worth highlighting that this is a trade-off between the depth of safety verification and the speed of
447   decision-making, which could impact the model’s deployment in real-world systems.
448
449
      Despite these constraints, we believe STL-Drive’s contributions to the field are substantial. By in-
      corporating formal verification into the training process, we have introduced a new pathway for en-
450
      hancing the safety and reliability of automated driving policies (and other safety-critical real-world
451
      applications, such as manufacturing procedures and tactical logistics), laying the groundwork for
452   further exploration in combining machine learning algorithms (system 1 thnking) with formal safety
453   guarantees (system 2 thinking). STL-Drive represents a promising step toward safer, more robust
454   automated driving systems that can inspire continued research and development in this domain.
