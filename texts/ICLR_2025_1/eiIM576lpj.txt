011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Despite their considerable achievements across a range of domains, deep learning
014            models have been demonstrated to be susceptible to adversarial attacks. In order
015            to mitigate this vulnerability, adversarial training has become a prevalent defense
016
               strategy. In this context, we propose Bernoulli Rectified Linear Units (BReLU),
               an activation function designed to further enhance the effectiveness of adversarial
017
               training. In contrast to conventional activation functions, BReLU modulates ac-
018
               tivation probabilities in accordance with input values, thereby introducing input-
019            dependent randomness into the model. The experimental results demonstrate that
020            the incorporation of BReLU into adversarial training significantly enhances the
021            robustness of the model against adversarial attacks. Specifically, on the CIFAR-
022            10 dataset using the ResNet-18 model, BReLU improved robustness by 15% un-
023            der FGSM, by 8% under PGD-20, and by 54% under the CW attack compared
024            to ReLU. Our findings indicate that BReLU represents a promising addition to
025            adversarial training techniques for strengthening deep learning models against ad-
026            versarial attacks.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Randomness plays a significant role in deep learning models. Weight initialization, dropout, and
032   stochastic gradient descent all involve the random selection of values during the training phase.
033
      However, once the model is trained, it becomes deterministic. In other words, if the inputs are
      the same, the model will always produce the same output. This means that the decision boundary
034
      is fixed in the input space, which can make it easier to generate adversarial examples by creating
035
      inputs that lie just outside the decision boundary.
036
037   White-box adversarial attacks exploit detailed knowledge of the model, such as its architecture and
038   weights, to generate adversarial examples that are both effective and difficult to detect. These at-
039
      tacks aim to find subtle perturbations in the input that push the example just beyond the decision
      boundary, causing the model to misclassify it. The Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) works by
040
      perturbing the input data in the direction of the gradient of the loss function, scaled by a small factor
041
      (Goodfellow et al., 2015). Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) improves upon FGSM by applying an
042   iterative approach, refining the perturbations over multiple steps to craft stronger adversarial exam-
043   ples (Madry et al., 2018). At each iteration, the perturbation is projected back into a predefined range
044   to ensure it remains valid. Finally, the Carlini and Wagner (CW) attack diverges from gradient-based
045   approaches by formulating the adversarial example generation as an optimization problem (Carlini
046   & Wagner, 2017). Instead of relying purely on gradients, the CW attack minimizes a combination of
047   two terms—one ensuring the adversarial example closely resembles the original input and another
048   maximizing the attack’s success by maximizing the logits of the target class.
049   To enhance the robustness of deep learning models against adversarial attacks, several defense mech-
050   anisms have been proposed. Lecuyer et al. introduced a method called randomized smoothing,
051   which incorporates Gaussian noise between layers to extend the decision boundary of the original
052   models, thereby improving their robustness (Lécuyer et al., 2019; Cohen et al., 2019). Cohen et
053   al. theoretically demonstrated that the addition of noise effectively smooths decision boundaries,
      increasing the model’s resistance to adversarial examples (Cohen et al., 2019). Another adopted de-

      2     BACKGROUND
109
110
      2.1     A DVERSARIAL ATTACK
111
112   In this work, we focus on three major white-box attacks: FGSM, PGD, and CW, as they represent
113   a range of adversarial techniques with distinct characteristics. FGSM is computationally efficient
114   and offers a fast method for generating adversarial examples with a single-step approach. PGD,
115   on the other hand, is a multi-step variant that is more robust, offering stronger attacks through
116   iterative updates, and is commonly used as a benchmark in adversarial training. CW, being a more
117
      sophisticated attack, minimizes perturbations while bypassing defenses such as adversarial training,
      making it valuable for evaluating the limits of model robustness under targeted attacks.
118
119
      2.1.1    FAST G RADIENT S IGN M ETHOD
120
121   FGSM is a relatively simple yet fundamental approach for generating adversarial examples. It per-
122   turbs the input data by taking the sign of the gradient of the loss with respect to the input, scaled by a
123   small constant, denoted as ϵ. Adversarial examples are created as x∗ = x + ϵ · SIGN(∇x L(θ, x, y)),
124   where x∗ is the adversarial example, x is the original input data, ϵ is the magnitude of the perturba-
125   tion, L is the loss function, θ represents the model parameters, and y is the label. The perturbation
126   ϵ is designed to be imperceptible to humans, making the attack subtle yet effective.
127
128   2.1.2    P ROJECTED G RADIENT D ESCENT
129   PGD is frequently regarded as a more robust variation ofQFGSM, employing an iterative approach to
130   the attack. The PGD formula can be expressed as xt = x+S (xt−1 + α · SIGN(∇x L(θ, xt−1 , y))),
131   where x is the original         t
                         Q input, x is the adversarial example at the t-th step, and α represents the step
132   size. The symbol x+S denotes the projection of the perturbation back into the valid input space
133   S, ensuring that the adversarial example remains within a certain allowable range. By repeating this
134   process over multiple steps, PGD can generate adversarial examples that are often more effective
135   than those generated by a single-step method like FGSM.
136
137   2.1.3    CW ATTACK
138
      CW attack is a white-box attack that optimizes a adversarial example using the model’s output
139
      logits, differing from gradient-based models like FGSM and PGD. The attack is formulated as an
140
      optimization problem, which seeks to create adversarial examples by minimizing two key terms.
141   The optimization objective is to minimize || 21 (tanh(w) + 1) − x||22 + c · f ( 21 (tanh(w) + 1), where
142   w represents intermediate results used to create an adversarial example. In this equation, the first
143   term ensures that the adversarial example remains similar to the original input x by minimizing
144   the L2 norm between them. The second term controls the success of the attack, where f (x′ ) =
145   max(max{Z(x′ )i : i ̸= t} − Z(x′ )t , −κ). Here, Z(x′ ) represents the logits of the model for input
146   x′ , and t is the target class. This term ensures that the logits of the target class t become larger than
147   those of any other class by at least κ, thus increasing the likelihood of the model misclassifying x′
148   as the target class. While the CW attack requires more computational time compared to FGSM and
149
      PGD due to its iterative optimization process, it is often more successful in generating adversarial
      examples that closely resemble the original input while maintaining higher attack efficacy.
150

      2.2     A DVERSARIAL T RAINING
153   Adversarial training (Goodfellow et al., 2015) was introduced as a method to enhance the robustness
154   of models against adversarial attacks. In contrast to the conventional approach of training with only
155   clean samples, adversarial training incorporates adversarial examples generated through adversarial
156   attacks into the training process. The modified loss function utilized in adversarial training can be
157   expressed as follows:
158
                                 L̃(θ, x, y) = αL(θ, x, y) + (1 − α)L(θ, x∗ , y),                           (1)
159
160   where L is the loss function, θ represents the model parameters, x is the input data, y is the corre-
161   sponding label, x∗ is the adversarial example, and α is the coefficient determining the ratio between
      the the vanilla loss and the loss from the adversarial examples.

      In the initial stages of adversarial training research (Goodfellow et al., 2015), FGSM was employed
163   as a single-step attack to rapidly generate adversarial examples. However, models trained with
164   FGSM were found to be susceptible to more sophisticated multi-step attacks, such as PGD. To ad-
165   dress this vulnerability, subsequent research (Madry et al., 2018; Lamb et al., 2022) has adopted
166   PGD as the preferred method for generating adversarial examples during adversarial training, re-
167   sulting in more robust models.
168
169
      3     B ERNOULLI R ECTIFIED L INEAR U NITS
170
171   We propose Bernoulli Rectified Linear Units (BReLU), an activation function that incorporates a
172   Bernoulli sampling based on the input data. While the BReLU methodology can be extended to
173   any activation function, such as ReLU (Nair & Hinton, 2010), Leaky ReLU (LReLU) (Maas et al.,
174   2013), and PReLU (He et al., 2015), this paper focuses on ReLU.
175
      The operation of BReLU involves three steps as shown in Figure 1. The first step is probability
176
      calculation. We compute the activation probability using a sigmoid function parameterized by α.
177
                                                                   eαx
178                                         f (α, x) = σ(α, x) = αx       ,                                (2)
179                                                              e +1
180   where x is the input data, and α controls the slope of the sigmoid curve. In the standard BReLU,
181   we set α = 1. The sigmoid function was selected for its advantageous characteristics, namely its
182
      capacity to output values between 0 and 1 while effectively mapping input values to probabilities.
      As the absolute value of the input increases, the sigmoid function converges more sharply toward
183
      its limits of 0 or 1, effectively distinguishing between inactive and active states. Additionally, other
184
      functions were tested, including periodic functions, but the sigmoid function was found to yield
185   superior performance. This led to the conclusion that the sigmoid function is the optimal choice
186   for probability calculation in BReLU. The second step is Bernoulli sampling. Using the calculated
187   probabilities, we perform Bernoulli sampling to obtain binary values (0 or 1). 0 means deactiva-
188   tion and 1 means activation. Finally, the input of BReLU and the result of Bernoulli sampling are
189   multiplied element-wise.
190
      In BReLU, the activation probability is directly determined by the input value, introducing input-
191   dependent randomness. The sigmoid function ensures probabilities in the range [0, 1], smoothly
192   transitioning between inactive and active states based on the input. The parameter α allows adjust-
193   ment of the sigmoid curve’s steepness. Increasing α sharpens the transition, reducing the range of
194   moderate probabilities (around 0.5), while decreasing α broadens this range. Although we use the
195   sigmoid function for probability calculation, other suitable functions can also be employed depend-
196   ing on the desired activation characteristics.
197   By applying the reparameterization trick (Kingma & Welling, 2014), BReLU maintains differen-
198   tiability, ensuring that backpropagation is not distrupted during training. This allows BReLU to be
199   seamlessly integrated into existing models, potentially enhancing their robustness by simply replac-
200   ing the activation function.

      4.4.3   W HY BR E LU IS ROBUST AGAINST A DVERSARIAL ATTACK
500
501
      To investigate why BReLU improves model robustness, we analyzed the proportion of negative
502   units before and after applying various activation functions in a ResNet-18 model using the CIFAR-
503   10 validation set. Table 6 presents the average ratios of negative values, where the layer number
504   indicates the specific layers in the model. For the sake of brevity, we include the entire table into the
505   appendix.
506
      In the case of ReLU, both pre- and post-activation negative ratios are zero, indicating that the net-
507
      work evolves to produce non-negative pre-activation values, effectively making ReLU act as an iden-
508   tity function in those layers. In the case of PReLU, we observed that some layers behave like ReLU
509   with zero negative activations post-activation, suggesting that the learnable parameter adjusts to sup-
510   press negative outputs when advantageous. Other activation functions, including LReLU, GELU,
511   ELU, and SiLU maintain negative values after activation. However, the proportion of negative acti-
512   vations tends to increase significantly in deeper layers, at times exceeding 90%. The accumulation
513   of negative values may impede effective learning and gradient propagation.
514   In contrast, BReLU maintains a balanced and stable ratio of negative activations throughout the
515   network. Before activation, the negative ratio ranges between 40% and 60%; after activation, it
516   consistently remains around 20% across all layers. This stability suggests that BReLU provides
517   a consistent activation distribution, potentially facilitating better learning dynamics. We hypothe-
518   size that this balanced maintenance of negative activations contributes to the enhanced robustness
519   observed with BReLU. By preventing the excessive accumulation or suppression of negative activa-
520   tions, BReLU contributes to the maintenance of a healthier activation landscape, thereby rendering
521   the model less susceptible to adversarial perturbations.
522   Furthermore, the input-dependent randomness introduced by BReLU disrupts the ability of attack-
523   ers to craft effective adversarial examples by rendering the model’s responses less predictable. This
524   effect is particularly significant against optimization-based attacks, such as the CW attack, which
525   relies on precise gradient information. In our experiments, BReLU-trained models exhibited ex-
526   ceptional robustness against CW attacks, with up to a 60% performance improvement over models
527   using standard activation functions. An example illustrating the changes in logits is provided in the
528
      Appendix.
