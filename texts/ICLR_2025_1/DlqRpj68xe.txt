011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Q-shaping is an extension of Q-value initialization and serves as an alternative to
014            reward shaping for incorporating domain knowledge to accelerate agent training,
015            thereby improving sample efficiency by directly shaping Q-values. This approach
016            is both general and robust across diverse tasks, allowing for immediate impact
017
               assessment while guaranteeing optimality. We evaluated Q-shaping across 20 dif-
               ferent environments using a large language model (LLM) as the heuristic provider.
018
               The results demonstrate that Q-shaping significantly enhances sample efficiency,
019
               achieving an 16.87% average improvement across the 20 tasks compared to the
020            best baseline, and a 226.67% improvement compared to LLM-based reward shap-
021            ing methods. These findings establish Q-shaping as an effective and unbiased
022            alternative to conventional reward shaping in reinforcement learning.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Reinforcement learning (RL) can solve complex tasks but often faces sample inefficiency. For
028   example, AlphaGo (Silver et al., 2016) required approximately 4 weeks of training on 50 GPUs,
029   learning from 30 million expert Go game positions to reach a 57% accuracy. Similarly, training a real
030   bipedal soccer robot required 9.0 × 108 environment steps, amounting to 68 hours of wall-clock time
031   for the full 1v1 agent (Haarnoja et al., 2024). These cases demonstrate the significant computational
032   demands of RL.

      et al.; Ma et al., 2023) is the most practical approach, as it avoids the need for expert trajectories or
055   predefined controllers.
056
057   Reward shaping methods fall into two main categories: (1) potential-based reward shaping
058
      (PBRS) (Ng et al., 1999) and (2) non-potential-based reward shaping (NPBRS) (Ng et al., 1999).
      PBRS provides state-based heuristic rewards and ensures that optimality is preserved by following
059
      the potential function rule, as defined by . NPBRS, on the other hand, refers to reward shaping
060
      methods that do not adhere to the potential function rule, and as a result, the learned policy does not
061   guarantee optimality. Additionally, reward shaping methods often suffer from a slow verification
062   process, requiring completion of training to assess the impact of the heuristic reward, which limits
063   their development, as noted by Ma et al. (2023). Lastly, designing high-quality reward functions
064   remains a challenging and often frustrating task for researchers, hindering the adoption of these
065   methods (Ma et al., 2023).
066
      With the growing popularity of large language models (LLMs), LLM-guided reinforcement learning
067   (RL) has emerged as a promising field. This approach leverages the strong understanding capabilities
068   of LLMs to guide RL agents in exploration or policy updates. Existing research has focused on two
069   main areas: LLM-based policy generation and LLM-guided reward design. For example, Chen et al.
070   (2021); Micheli et al. (2022) utilize LLMs to enhance policy decisions, while Kwon et al. (2023);
071   Carta et al. (2023); Ma et al. (2023) employ LLMs to design reward structures. Although these works
072   have improved task success rates, the challenges associated with reward shaping remain unresolved.
073   In this work, we introduce a novel framework, Q-shaping, which leverages domain knowledge from
074   large language models (LLMs) to guide agent exploration. Q-shaping offers two key advantages over
075   reward shaping:
076
077
            1. Remain Optimality: Q-shaping inspires exploration by modifying Q-values during training
               while ensuring that the agent’s optimality remains unaffected upon convergence.
078
079
            2. Efficient Heuristic Verification: Unlike reward shaping methods, which require waiting
               until the end of training to observe the impact of the reward heuristic, Q-shaping enables
080
               experimenters to verify and refine heuristic guidance rapidly during training.

087   LLM-guided reward shaping methods like T2R (Xie et al.) and Eureka (Ma et al., 2023), Q-shaping
088   achieves up to 226.67% improvement in episodic total rewards while enhancing task success rates.

094   2.1   H EURISTIC R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
095
096   There are four common approaches to incorporating domain knowledge into reinforcement learning
097   to enhance sample efficiency: (1) Imitation Learning, (2) Residual Policy, (3) Reward Shaping, and
098
      (4) Q-value Initialization.
099   Imitation Learning requires access to expert trajectories, as demonstrated by works such as GAIL (Ho
100   & Ermon, 2016), where agents learn by mimicking expert behavior. However, the reliance on high-
101   quality expert data limits its applicability in complex tasks. Residual Policy (Johannink et al., 2019)
102   methods involve designing a controller to guide agent actions, but this manual design process restricts
103   their scalability and generality.
104   Q-value initialization, although promising, often requires precise Q-value estimates to derive an
105   effective policy. For instance, Cal-QL (Nakamoto et al., 2024) employs calibrated Q-values to
106   enhance agent exploration, but these calibrated values still rely on expert knowledge, making Q-value
107   design more challenging than reward shaping. Consequently, few studies have pursued this direction
      due to the inherent difficulty in obtaining accurate Q-values compared to reward shaping.

108
      Reward shaping directly modifies the reward function to influence agent behavior, improving training
109   efficiency without requiring expert trajectories or manual controller design. This approach has
110   been refined to address diverse learning scenarios, such as in Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL)
111   (Ziebart et al., 2008; Wulfmeier et al., 2015; Finn et al., 2016) and Preference-based RL (Christiano
112   et al., 2017; Ibarz et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2021; Park et al., 2022). Additionally, various heuristic
113   techniques have been introduced, including unsupervised auxiliary task rewards (Jaderberg et al.,
114   2016), count-based reward heuristics (Bellemare et al., 2016; Ostrovski et al., 2017), and self-
115   supervised prediction error heuristics (Pathak et al., 2017; Stadie et al., 2015; Oudeyer & Kaplan,
116   2007).
117   However, reward shaping often suffers from inaccuracies in the heuristic functions and a slow
118   verification process, which limits its effectiveness in certain applications.

130   correction (Shinn et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024), and observation summarization (Sridhar et al.,
131   2023).

133   2.3   LLM- ENHANCED RL
134
135   Relying on the understanding and generation capabilities of large models, LLM-enhanced RL has
136   become a popular field (Du et al., 2023; Carta et al., 2023). Researchers have investigated the diverse
137   roles of large models within reinforcement learning (RL) architectures, including their application
138   in reward design (Kwon et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Carta et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2023; Yu et al.,
139   2023; Ma et al., 2023), information processing (Paischer et al., 2022; 2024; Radford et al., 2021), as a
140   policy generator, and as a generator within large language models (LLMs) (Chen et al., 2021; Micheli
141
      et al., 2022; Robine et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2022). While LLM-assisted reward design has improved
      task success rates (Ma et al., 2023; Xie et al.), it often introduces bias into the original Markov
142
      Decision Process (MDP) or fails to provide sufficient guidance for complex tasks. Additionally, the
143
      verification process is time-consuming, which slows down the pace of iterative improvements.

      Markov Decision Processes. We represent the environment as a Markov Decision Process (MDP)
149
      in the standard form: M := ⟨S, A, R, P, γ, ρ⟩. Here, S and A denote the discrete state and action
150   spaces, respectively. We use Z := S × A as shorthand for the joint state-action space. The reward
151   function R : Z → Dist([0, 1]) maps state-action pairs to distributions over the unit interval, while
152   the transition function P : Z → Dist(S) maps state-action pairs to distributions over subsequent
153   states. Lastly, ρ ∈ Dist(S) represents the distribution over initial states. We denote rM and PM as
154   the true reward and transition functions of the environment.
155
      For policy definition, the space of all possible policies is denoted as Π. A policy π : S → ∆(A)
156   defines a conditional distribution over actions given states. A deterministic policy µ : S → A is
157   a special case of π, where one action is selected per state with a probability of 1. We define an
158   “activity matrix” Aπ ∈ RS×Z for each policy, encoding π’s state-conditional state-action distribution.
159   Specifically, Aπ (s, ⟨ṡ, a⟩) := π(a|s) if s = ṡ, otherwise Aπ (s, ⟨ṡ, a⟩) := 0. The value function is
160   defined as v : Π → S → R or q : Π → S × A → R, both with bounded outputs. The terms q and v
161   represent discrete matrix representations, where v(s) and q(s, a) specifically denote the outputs of
      an arbitrary value function for a given policy at a particular state or state-action pair.

209   The Q-value represents a high-level abstraction of an agent’s interaction with the environment. It
210   encapsulates the expected cumulative reward by integrating critical elements such as rewards r,
211   transition probabilities P , states s, actions a, and the policy π. Changes in any of these components
212
      directly affect the Q-values.

      the optimal Q-values of the local MDP.

234
      At the early training stage, the Q-values for different actions are nearly identical, leading the policy
235
      to execute actions randomly.To address this, we leverage the LLM’s domain knowledge to provide
236   positive Q-values for actions that contribute to task success and negative Q-values for actions that do
237   not. The imprecise Q-values provided by the LLM can be categorized into two types: overestimations
238   and underestimations.
239
240   Underestimation of Non-Optimal Actions An agent does not need to fully traverse the entire
241   state-action space to identify the optimal trajectory that leads to task success. Therefore, imprecise
242   Q-value estimation can be effectively utilized to guide the agent’s exploration.
243
      For instance, consider a scenario where the agent is required to control a robot arm to operate on
244   a drawer located in front of it. In this case, actions such as moving the arm backward or upward
245   are evidently unhelpful in finding the optimal trajectory. Assigning very low Q-values to these
246   non-contributory actions discourages the agent from exploring them, thereby enhancing sample
247   efficiency.

      Policy-Network Shaping In most reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms, the policy is derived
325   from the Q-function, where the policy is optimized to execute actions that maximize the Q-value
326   given a state. The policy update is expressed as: µ(s) = arg maxa q̂(s, ·)
327
328
      While introducing a learning rate and target policy can help stabilize the training process and prevent
      fluctuations in the policy network, this approach often slows down the convergence speed. To
329
      accelerate this adaptation, we introduce a "Policy-Network Shaping" phase designed to allow the
330
      policy to quickly align with the good actions and avoid the bad actions provided by the LLM.
331
332   The shaping loss function is defined as:
333          Lpolicy−shaping = λ1 E(s,a)∼GLLM ∥µ(s) − a∥2 − λ2 E(s,a)∼BLLM ∥µ(s) − a∥2

                                                                                                            (4)
334
335   , where (s, a) ∼ GLLM and (s, a) ∼ BLLM represent state-action pairs sampled from the LLM-
336   provided goodQ and badQ sets, respectively, and λ1 and λ2 are hyperparameters controlling the
337   influence of the LLM-guided shaping.
338   With this "Policy-Network Shaping" phase, researchers can quickly observe the impact of heuristic
339   values, facilitating the rapid evolution of heuristic quality, ultimately leading to a more efficient
340   exploration process and faster convergence to optimal behavior.
341
342   High-Performance Agent Selection With Q-network shaping and policy-network shaping, the
343   policy is initialized to perform actions suggested by the LLM. However, due to the randomness of
344   initial states and the learning process, the shaped agent may still perform poorly and have a chance of
345   failing to learn effectively. To address this, each agent is allowed 10,000 steps to test its performance
346
      after the Q-network and policy network initialization. Following this evaluation, weaker agents are
      removed, and only the top-performing agent continues with the training process.

      7    C ONCLUSION
529
      We propose Q-shaping, an alternative framework that integrates domain knowledge to enhance sample
530
      efficiency in reinforcement learning. In contrast to traditional reward shaping, Q-shaping offers two
531
      key advantages: (1) it preserves optimality, and (2) it allows for rapid verification of the agent’s
532   behavior. These features enable experimenters or LLMs to iteratively refine the quality of heuristic
533   values without concern for the potential negative impact of poorly designed heuristics. Experimental
534   results demonstrate that Q-shaping significantly improves sample efficiency and outperforms LLM-
535   guided reward shaping methods across various tasks.
536
      We hope this work encourages further research into advanced techniques that leverage LLM outputs
537
      to guide and enhance the search process in reinforcement learning.

1233
       The Meta-World benchmark is a suite of 50 diverse robotic manipulation tasks designed to evaluate
1234
       reinforcement learning (RL) and meta-reinforcement learning (meta-RL) algorithms. In Yu et al.
1235
       (2020), the authors introduce a simulated Sawyer robotic arm and provide detailed definitions of the
1236   observation space, action space, and evaluation metrics.
1237
1238   For the purpose of this paper, we focus on Section 4.1 Actions, Observations, and
1239
       Rewards from Yu et al. (2020), which outlines the design of the state space, action space, and
       reward functions. These details are critical for understanding how to guide large language models
1240
       (LLMs) to generate high-quality (s, a, Q) pairs.
1241



                                                         23
            Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
                  Environment Description
1243
1244
                  4.1 Actions, Observations, and Rewards In order to represent policies for multiple
1245              tasks with one model, the observation and action spaces must contain significant shared
1246              structure across tasks. All of our tasks are performed by a simulated Sawyer robot.
1247              The action space is a 2-tuple consisting of the change in 3D space of the end-effector
1248              followed by a normalized torque that the gripper fingers should apply. The actions in
1249              this space range between −1 and 1. For all tasks, the robot must either manipulate one
1250              object with a variable goal position, or manipulate two objects with a fixed goal position.
1251              The observation space is represented as a 6-tuple of the 3D Cartesian positions of the
1252              end-effector, a normalized measurement of how open the gripper is, the 3D position of
1253
                  the first object, the quaternion of the first object, the 3D position of the second object, the
                  quaternion of the second object, all of the previous measurements in the environment,
1254
                  and finally the 3D position of the goal. If there is no second object or the goal is not
1255
                  meant to be included in the observation, then the quantities corresponding to them are
1256              zeroed out. The observation space is always 39 dimensional.
1257
1258              Designing reward functions for Meta-World requires two major considerations. First, to
1259              guarantee that our tasks are within the reach of current single-task reinforcement learning
1260              algorithms, which is a prerequisite for evaluating multi-task and meta-RL algorithms,
1261              we design well-shaped reward functions for each task that make each of the tasks at least
1262              individually solvable.
1263
1264
                  More importantly, the reward functions must exhibit shared structure across tasks.
                  Critically, even if the reward function admits the same optimal policy for multiple tasks,
1265
                  varying reward scales or structures can make the tasks appear completely distinct for the
1266
                  learning algorithm, masking their shared structure and leading to preferences for tasks
1267              with high-magnitude rewards.
1268
1269              Accordingly, we adopt a structured, multi-component reward function for all tasks, which
1270              leads to effective policy learning for each of the task components. For instance, in a task
1271              that involves a combination of reaching, grasping, and placing an object, let o ∈ R3 be
1272              the object position, where o = (ox , oy , oz ), h ∈ R3 be the position of the robot’s gripper,
1273              ztarget ∈ R be the target height of lifting the object, and g ∈ R3 be goal position. With
1274              the above definition, the multi-component reward function R is the combination of a
1275              reaching reward, a grasping reward, and a placing reward or subsets thereof for simpler
1276
                  tasks that only involve reaching and/or pushing. With this design, the reward functions
                  across all tasks have a similar magnitude that ranges between 0 and 10, where 10 always
1277
                  corresponds to the reward-function being solved, and conform to similar structure, as
1278
                  desired. The full form of the reward function and a list of all task rewards is provided in
1279              Appendix.

            D    L IMITATIONS AND D ISCUSSION
1681
            In this section, we discuss several limitations of our work and propose promising directions for future
1682
            research to address these challenges.
1683
1684        One limitation of the Q-shaping framework is its dependence on the understanding capabilities of
1685        large language models (LLMs). For tasks where the LLM cannot fully comprehend the dynamics of
1686        movement or the control-specific requirements of the task, the framework fails to deliver meaningful
1687
            improvement. This limitation constrains the applicability of Q-shaping to domains where task
            requirements can be effectively interpreted by the LLM. Models with stronger reasoning capabilities
1688
            may be needed to generate valid state-action pairs.
1689
1690        Another limitation is the difficulty of scaling Q-shaping to visual or real-world settings. The frame-
1691        work requires models capable of generating states, but current technology lacks models that can
1692        simultaneously process textual and visual inputs and output comprehensive state-action descriptions.
1693
            This gap restricts the ability of Q-shaping to operate effectively in environments where visual data is
            a critical component. Future progress in multimodal modeling, such as vision-language models that
1694
            integrate text and images, could alleviate this challenge by enabling richer state representations.
1695
1696        By addressing these limitations, Q-shaping has the potential to evolve into a more versatile framework
1697        capable of operating across diverse tasks and environments, ultimately advancing its impact on
1698        reinforcement learning research.
