011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            FlashAttention series has been widely applied in the inference of large language
014            models (LLMs). However, FlashAttention series only supports the high-level GPU
015
               architectures, e.g., Ampere and Hopper. At present, FlashAttention series is not
               easily transferrable to NPUs and low-resource GPUs. Moreover, FlashAttention
016
               series is inefficient for multi- NPUs or GPUs inference scenarios. In this work,
017
               we propose FastAttention which pioneers the adaptation of FlashAttention series
018            for NPUs and low-resource GPUs to boost LLM inference efficiency. Specifically,
019            we take Ascend NPUs and Volta-based GPUs as representatives for designing our
020            FastAttention. We migrate FlashAttention series to Ascend NPUs by proposing
021            a novel two-level tiling strategy for runtime speedup, tiling-mask strategy for
022            memory saving and the tiling-AllReduce strategy for reducing communication
023            overhead, respectively. Besides, we adapt FlashAttention for Volta-based GPUs
024            by redesigning the operands layout in shared memory and introducing a simple
025            yet effective CPU-GPU cooperative strategy for efficient memory utilization. On
026
               Ascend NPUs, our FastAttention can achieve a 10.7× speedup compared to the
               standard attention implementation. Llama-7B within FastAttention reaches up to
027
               5.16× higher throughput than within the standard attention. On Volta architec-
028
               ture GPUs, FastAttention yields 1.43× speedup compared to its equivalents in
029            xformers. Pangu-38B within FastAttention brings 1.46× end-to-end speedup
030            using FasterTransformer. Coupled with the propose CPU-GPU cooperative strategy,
031            FastAttention supports a maximal input length of 256K on 8 V100 GPUs. All the
032            codes will be made available soon.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Recent years have witnessed the impressive performance of transformer-based large language models
038   (LLMs) (Zhao et al., 2023; Vaswani et al., 2017; Kaplan et al., 2020) in understanding and generative
039
      tasks (Ainslie et al., 2023; Viggiato & Bezemer, 2023; Peng et al., 2023; Beltagy et al., 2020). It’s
      noteworthy that the runtime and memory requirements of Transformer-based LLMs scale quadratically
040
      with the input sequence length. Dao et al. (2022) proposed FlashAttention series algorithms to reduce
041
      the runtime requirements and decrease memory usage from quadratic to linear complexity with regard
042   to sequence lengths, of which FlashAttention2/3 (Dao, 2023; Shah et al., 2024) is widely used in the
043   domain (Aminabadi et al., 2022; Shoeybi et al., 2020; NVIDIA, 2023b; Wu et al., 2023).
044
045
      However, the FlashAttention series is typically designed for resource-rich Graphics Processing Units
      (GPUs) featuring high-level architectures, e.g., FlashAttention2 for Ampere and FlashAttention3 for
046
      Hopper (Jia & Van Sandt, 2021; Choquette, 2022). We identify three limitations of FlashAttention:
047
      1) For architectures with lower capabilities, e.g., Volta (Choquette et al., 2018), and non-CUDA
048   architectures, e.g., the architectures of Neural network Processing Units (NPUs), the existing
049   FlashAttention project is not applicable. Additionally, xFormers implements the memory-efficient
050   attention for V100 GPUs with suboptimal computational efficiency (Lefaudeux et al., 2022; Rabe &
051   Staats, 2021). Moreover, a dedicated version of FlashAttention has been developed to support AMD
052   GPUs (AMD, 2024). 2) The FlashAttention series exhibits inefficiency in distributed inference
053   across multiple devices, arising from the communication overhead incurred by the AllReduce
      operations. 3) Under the constraints of limited device memory, FlashAttention can not enable

054
      inference with ultra-long sequences on a single node within multi-NPUs and multiple low-resource
055   GPUs. The limitations are caused by the significant differences in architectures and instruction sets,
056   which pose challenges to adapting the existing FlashAttention for NPUs and low-resource GPUs, as
057   detailed in § 3. In particular, directly transferring the workflow of the FlashAttention series to NPUs
058   is inefficient, which means the techniques used in FlashAttention, such as tiling and work partitioning,
059   typically can only exploit partial capabilities of non-CUDA architectures. As shown in Table 2.
060
      Given the numerous inference systems that rely on low-resource GPUs and economical NPUs, failing
061   to deploy FlashAttention in these systems could have significant adverse impacts. To address the
062   issues mentioned above, we propose FastAttention, a pioneering adaptation of FlashAttention series
063   for NPUs and low-resource GPUs with more efficiency. Without loss of generality, we design
064   FastAttention for Ascend NPUs, e.g., Ascend 910B, and Volta-based GPUs, e.g., V100, serving as
065   examples of the FlashAttention extension for NPUs and low-resource GPUs. Our contributions are
066   summarized as follows:
067
068          • On NPUs, We propose a generalizable two-level tiling strategy, tiling-mask strategy and
069            tiling-AllReduce strategy to save memory and improve runtime speedup for the adaption
070
               of FlashAttention. Remarkably, to the best of our knowledge, we are the first to map
               FlashAttention series on NPUs.
071
072          • We provide the implementation of FlashAttention tailored for low-resource GPUs, alongside
073            a fine-grained CPU-GPU cooperative strategy to scale up the maximum input sequence
074            length.
075
             • Experimental results demonstrate that FastAttention achieves a 10.7× speedup over the
076
               standalone implementation and provides a 5.16× higher throughput compared to not using it
077
               on Ascend NPUs. On Volta-based GPUs, FastAttention achieves up to 1.43× speedup when
078            compared to its equivalents in xformers, enabling a 1.46× lower latency and supporting
079            a maximal input length of 256K when using FasterTransformer on a single node.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
083
084   Large Transformers: Large Transformers, characterized by their extensive parameters and layers,
085   are primarily employed for complex tasks such as natural language processing (NLP) and computer
086   vision (Voulodimos et al., 2018). In these models, particularly in LLMs like GPT (Achiam et al.,
087
      2023), the attention mechanism plays a pivotal role, consuming a significant portion of computational
      resources. Although models such as Vision Transformers (ViT) and Diffusion Transformers (Spolaore
088
      & Wacziarg, 2009; Yuan et al., 2021) also incorporate attention mechanisms, the proportion of com-
089
      putation dedicated to attention in these models is relatively small. Consequently, the FlashAttention
090   series is more specifically tailored to large transformer models, where attention computations are
091   more prominent.
092
093
      FlashAttention series algorithms: FlashAttention employs tiling and recomputation to minimize the
      number of memory access between the on-chip SRAM (a.k.a shared memory) and high bandwidth
094
      memory (HBM). It introduces frequent data flow via SRAM between Tensor Core and Cube Core
095
      (Choquette et al., 2021; Markidis et al., 2018). FlashAttention2 further optimizes the workflow
096   of FlashAttention, exhibiting better parallelism and work partitioning. Based on the characters
097   of newer GPU architectures, such as Hopper and Blackwell, FlashAttention3 hides the the non-
098   GEMM operations under asynchronous General Matrix Multiplication (GEMM) with asynchronous
099   instructions to further improve performance. Appendix A provides a more detailed description.
100   However, FlashAttention2/3 only supports the resource-rich GPUs, neglecting low-resource GPUs
101   and powerful NPUs. What’s more, FlashAttention series lacks the capability to reduce the memory
102   occupied by attention mask and decrease the communication overhead introduced by AllReduce.
103   Ultra-long sequence inference: Limited device memory poses a significant constraint, rendering
104   FlashAttention series incapable of supporting inference with ultra-long sequences (e.g., 256K) on
105   a single node. Notably, the offloading is generally coupled with attention optimization for efficient
106   memory utilization. For instance, both FlexGen (Sheng et al., 2023) and DeepSpeed-Inference
107   (Aminabadi et al., 2022) design a classical offloading strategies that schedules data among GPUs,
      CPUs, and disks but lacks fine-grained pipeline design.

      3   NPU S AND LOW- RESOURCE GPU S
109
110   Similarity between NPUs and GPUs: Most of the NPUs, such as Ascend NPUs, Hanguang NPUs,
111   and Cambricon-series NPUs (Liao et al., 2021; Jiao et al., 2020; Song et al., 2023), are designed
112   for high throughput and energy efficiency (Chen et al., 2020; Han & Yoo, 2023; Ahn et al., 2022).
113   Taking Ascend NPUs as a representative, as shown in Figure 1, Ascend NPUs share similar design
114   principles with GPUs, such as AI Cores corresponding to SMs in GPUs, Vector units corresponding
115
      to Cuda Cores and Cube units corresponding to Tensor Cores. Specifically, Cube units handle matrix
      computations while Vector units manage element-wise computations.

129   Differences between NPUs and GPUs: 1) Decoupled architecture. Each AI Core in Ascend
130
      NPUs integrates Cube, Vector, and Scalar units. The Cube units are decoupled from the Vector units,
      facilitating data exchange through the L2 buffer and global memory (GM), while Tensor Cores in
131
      GPUs interface with CUDA Cores via the shared memory. Consequently, the frequent data flow
132
      between Tensor Cores and CUDA Cores can limit the benefits of the decoupled architecture due to the
133   synchronization overhead between the Cube and Vector units. In contrast, the decoupled architecture
134   in Ascend NPUs enables seamless pipelining between Cube and Vector units, suggesting that the
135   optimal programming model for Ascend NPUs follows a pipelined approach. This design allows
136   element-wise computations by the Vector unit to overlap with matrix computations by the Cube unit,
137   requiring a redesign of efficient attention mechanisms on NPUs from an overlapping perspective.
138   2) Memory hierarchy. In GPUs, Tensor Cores and Cuda Cores share access to the L2 cache, L1
139   cache, and register files. In contrast, the Cube units in NPUs are equipped with L0 buffers, which are
140   absent in the Vector units, and feature a larger L1 buffer compared to the latter. The tiling method
141
      employed in FlashAttention fails to fully leverage the L1 buffer. To maximize the performance of
      NPUs, a meticulously designed data flow is essential. 3) SDMA. Ascend NPUs support System
142
      Direct Memory Access (SDMA) (Huawei, 2023b), which enables them to execute computation and
143
      communication in parallel. It’s imperative to redesign FlashAttention algorithm to fully capitalize on
144   SDMA, thereby reducing communication overhead and enhancing overall efficiency during inference.
145
146   Low-resource GPUs versus high-end GPUs: Low-resource GPUs exhibit similar architectures
147
      and memory hierarchies (HBM and SRAM) while different Tensor Cores for matrix computations
      compared to the resource-rich GPUs, resulting in distinct requirements for data layout in SRAM.
148
      This variation in data layout presents significant challenges when extending FlashAttention series
149
      to low-resource GPUs. Notably, high-level architectures like Ampere and Hopper already feature
150   efficient attention implementations, i.e., FlashAttention series. GPUs with lower-level architectures
151   than Volta do not possess Tensor Cores, which are essential for implementing various efficient
152   attention mechanisms. For this reason, we take the Volta-based GPUs as representatives in our work.
153
      Why we refer to FastAttention as an extension of FlashAttention: Due to the similarities between
154
      GPUs and NPUs, some basic ideas inside FlashAttention series, such as fused blocked GEMM
155
      and online softmax, can be also employed on NPUs with non-trivial implementation. However,
156   significant differences in architecture, memory hierarchy, and SDMA necessitate a tailored redesign
157   to fully leverage NPU capabilities. FlashAttention series is also not applicable for low-resource
158   GPUs, as they require distinct data layouts and block partitioning strategies to utilize Tensor Cores.
159   Moreover, FlashAttention is not optimized for multi-NPU or multi-low-resource GPU scenarios. In
160   contrast, FastAttention incorporates only a few basic ideas from FlashAttention while introducing
161   substantial novel techniques as detailed below, extending FlashAttention in both design techniques
      and applicability.

      4     M ETHODOLOGY

      We considers different application scenarios to design FastAttention: 1) In single-NPU scenarios,
165
      FastAttention features a two-level tiling strategy for the NPU’s computational power utilization and an
166
      architecture-independent tiling-mask strategy for memory saving. 2) In multi-NPU scenarios, building
167   upon the prior method, FastAttention further integrates the tiling-AllReduce strategy to minimize
168   the communication overhead. 3) For low-resource GPUs such as those with Volta architecture,
169   FastAttention applies the standard FlashAttention2 kernel and redesigns the shared memory layout of
170   operands in FlashAttention2 to adapt the instructions of Volta architectures. 4) In case GPU memory
171   is insufficient for inference, FastAttention equips a fine-grained CPU-GPU cooperative strategy with
172   the prior standard kernel to fully utilize the CPU’s computing power and memory.

      In this section, we delve into the redesign of the FlashAttention2 operator for Ascend NPUs. Ini-
188
      tially, drawing upon the standard FlashAttention2 implementation for GPUs, we develop a standard
189
      FlashAttention2 kernel for Ascend NPUs, which employs the unified tiling strategy illustrated in
190   the left of Figure 2. Specifically, considering the L1 buffer sizes in Ascend NPUs, we distribute the
191   Q matrix across the Ascend NPU’s AI Core units and split the input matrices K and V along the
192   sequence length (S dimension) into small blocks.
193
      Pipeline: Each of these small tiling blocks follows computations sequentially executed by Cube and
194
      Vector units. This design allows Vector and Cube units to work in tandem, achieving a better pipeline
195
      for efficient parallel computation. For instance, when the block0 performs the Exp calculation by the
196
      Vector unit, the block1 will perform the matrix multiplication of Q ∗ K T by the Cube unit.

198   NPU-specific two-level tiling strategy: The unified tiling strategy employs small block size, leading
199   to the frequent data flow between Cube unit and Vector unit via L2 buffer, which in turn introduces
200   significant synchronization overhead. Additionally, the distinct computational characteristics and
201
      discrepancy in L1 buffer size between Cube and Vector units result in underutilization of the Cube’s
      L1 buffer. To address the issues, we propose a novel two-level tiling strategy. The two-level tiling
202
      strategy is depicted in the right of Figure 2. In the first level, we adopt larger block sizes than the
203
      former implementation for Cube unit, which can effectively decrease the number of synchronizations.
204   Additionally, we optimize the pipeline parallelism of Vector and Cube by utilizing the double-
205   buffering technique on GM. Furthermore, this design allows the Cube unit to load larger continuous
206   blocks for the utilization of memory bandwidth. Considering the limited L0 and L1 size, we split the
207   large blocks into several small blocks along row dimension for Vector unit. This design provides a
208   more refined pipeline over the multi-level memory of each computing unit. What’s more, we also
209   apply the double-buffering technique to overlap the data transfer and computation in the second level.
210   The more detailed mathematical equations for the two-level tiling are provided in Appendix B.

271   Tensor Core instructions, while the V100 supports only m8n8k4 one. As shown in Figure 5, Volta

533   6 C ONCLUSION
534   In this paper, we propose FastAttention, an extension of FlashAttention2 for both NPUs and low-
535   resource GPUs, enabling longer input sequence lengths and lower inference latency. FastAttention
536   contains a series of insightful strategies and optimizations that are theoretically generalizable to
537   all of the NPUs and low-resource GPUs with similar architectures. Extensive experiments have
538   demonstrated the excellent efficiency and generalization of FastAttention on multiple LLMs. In
539   future work, we will apply our FastAttention to other NPUs and low-resource GPUs if accessible.
      We will also focus on compilation to facilitate easy invocation of our methods by users.

705   FlashAttention: As shown in Figure 12, FlashAttention employs the unified tiling strategy to split
706   Query (Q), Key (K), and Value (V ) into multiple blocks with small block size along the batch
707   and heads dimension. Then, FlashAttention utilize online softmax (Milakov & Gimelshein, 2018;
708   Rabe & Staats, 2021) and fused block GEMM to complete the attention calculation. To succinctly
709
      summarize, each of these Q tiling blocks sequentially executed the following four stages: matrix
710
      multiplication of Q ∗ K T , Exp calculation for softmax, multiplication of Exp and V , and updates
      of the output. During the process, the martrix multiplication, i.e., GEMM, is handled by Tensor cores
711
      while non-GEMM operations like softmax are performed by CUDA Cores. This design minimizes
712
      memory access between SRAM and HBM while introduces frequent data flow between Tensor Cores
713   and CUDA Cores due to the small block size imposed by SRAM limitations.

735

736

745   instructions for GEMM execution. FlashAttention2 is regarded as the state-of-the-art (SOTA) method
746   for the Ampere architecture, while FlashAttention3 represents the SOTA for architectures above

789   C    T HE DETAILED MECHANISM OF THE DATA LAYOUT REDESIGN
790
791   We here provide a detailed explanation of the data layout redesign for the Volta architecture. Fun-
792   damentally, a layout maps coordinate spaces to an index space. Layouts can be combined and
793   manipulated to construct more complicated layouts and to tile layouts across other layouts. This
794
      can help users do things like partition layouts of data over layouts of threads. In the Cutlass library,
      a layout is a tuple of (Shape, Stride). Semantically, it implements a mapping from any coordinate
795
      within the Shape to an index via the Stride. For instance, Shape:(4,2) and Stride: (1,4) is a 4x2
796
      column-major layout with stride-1 down the columns and stride-4 across the rows. And the more
797   detailed introduction of layout can be found in the Cutlass documents.
798
799   Furthermore, high-end GPUs feature resource-rich architectures, such as Ampere and Hopper, which
800
      differ from Volta in terms of MMA instructions and thread-data layouts. The Cutlass documentation
      provides a comprehensive description of thread-data layouts for Volta, Ampere, and Hopper architec-
801
      tures. For the implementation of FastAttention, it is crucial to utilize the MMA instructions of the
802
      Volta architecture and redesign the data layout to support these instructions.
803
804   Specifically, there are two matrix multiplications in the workflow of attention. To clarify the chal-
805   lenges associated with adding support for Volta’s m8n8k4 MMA instruction, we consider the con-
806
      vert layout acc Aregs function, which converts the layout of the output argument C from the first
      matrix multiplication into the layout of the input argument A for the second multiplication.
807
808   When executing a single MMA instruction for matrices A, B, and C, the data is distributed across
809   the threads. For Ampere’s m16n8k16, as illustrated in Figure 13, thread 0 contains 8 elements of
      matrix A (V0-V7), 4 elements of matrix B (V0-V3), and produces 4 elements of matrix C (V0-V3).
