011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Language models trained on large amounts of data are known to produce inap-
014            propriate content in some cases and require careful tuning to be used in the real
015
               world. We revisit the reward augmented decoding (RAD) approach to control the
               generation from a language model using the scores from a task-specific reward
016
               model. We investigate the training objective of RAD, and reformulate it as a task
017
               of learning a reward matrix. We show that RAD is designed to support high flexi-
018            bility when representing the reward matrices, which leads to higher computational
019            costs during decoding. However, we demonstrate that RAD does not use its full
020            flexibility. Motivated by this, we propose a simpler but more efficient low-rank
021            parametrization of the reward model enabling fast and effective guided decoding.
022            For the detoxification and sentiment control tasks, we show that our low-rank
023            reward model performs on par with the more flexible RAD parametrization, while
024            requiring only a single reward model call per generated token.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Generative large language models (LLMs) have gained a lot of popularity in recent years and shown
029
      impressive results in zero-shot and few-shot scenarios on numerous downstream tasks (Touvron et al.,
030   2023; OpenAI, 2024; Jiang et al., 2023). These large-scale models are pretrained on large amounts
031   of data, and are known to inherit and memorize underlying biases (Sheng et al., 2019) as well as to
032   provide unsafe responses (Wallace et al., 2019; Ganguli et al., 2022), necessitating further tuning for
033   safer deployment and control (Ouyang et al., 2022).
034
      Control over LLMs can be roughly divided into methods which modify the original model via
035
      finetuning (Ouyang et al., 2022; Rafailov et al., 2023), and decoding-time solutions, which do
036   not modify the parameters of the original model. As models increase in size, finetuning becomes
037   prohibitive with limited computational resources. In this work, we focus on a more modular approach
038   of decoding-time guidance, and assume we have access to top-k logits of a black-box base language
039   model (see §2.1 for details). In this line of work, a discriminator model is trained to modify or rerank
040   the logits of the base model during decoding in order to satisfy the desired constraint (Yang & Klein,
041   2021), while preserving the distribution of the language model as much as possible.
042   Recently Deng & Raffel (2023) proposed the reward augmented decoding (RAD), an approach to train
043   an autoregressive reward model as the discriminator. While RAD demonstrates high effectiveness for
044   controlled generation, it scales poorly when the number of next token candidates grows, requiring a
045   separate forward pass through the backbone of the reward model for each token candidate. In this
046   aspect, RAD diverges from previous work of Liu et al. (2021) and Krause et al. (2021): the latter
047   propose more efficient approaches using external attribute-conditioned language models, where each
048   expert model only performs a single forward pass to predict the scores for all next token candidates.
049   In §3.1, we analyze RAD and reformulate its training objective in terms of approximating an
050   incomplete reward matrix. We highlight that the RAD approach is flexible enough to represent a
051   large space of reward matrices including those of high rank. However, when we empirically measure
052   the rank of the reward matrix learned by RAD, it appears to be low-rank. This observation suggests
053   that RAD might not use its full flexibility, which motivates us to reconsider the trade-off between
      efficiency and expressivity of reward models. By analyzing the incomplete reward matrix constructed

      from the training data, we observe that it is enough to have a low-rank approximation to this matrix
055   in order to reconstruct its observed values.
056
057   In light of this observation, we propose the autoregressive reward model (ARM), a low-rank reward
058
      model which combines the strengths of two paradigms: fast inference with language modeling
      prediction style and high quality of generations following the reward augmented decoding (RAD)
059
      approach (Deng & Raffel, 2023). We propose a simple strategy for how to transform a pretrained
060
      language model into an efficient autoregressive reward model. In the evaluation, we show that guided
061   decoding with our model results in a comparable attribute control/fluency to the more flexible but
062   more computationally intensive RAD approach.

      2.1   G UIDED DECODING WITH EXTERNAL EXPERTS
067
068   In this section, we outline the approach of guiding a base language model with external token-level
069   discriminators. At each step of decoding, both the base model and the discriminator observe an
070   already generated prefix x, and cooperate to score the next token candidates v ∈ V . A language
071   model predicts the logits zLM (·|x) ∈ R|V | and the goal of discriminator is to augment these logits
072   with reward scores r̂(·|x) ∈ R|V | . A standard practice is to consider only likely tokens V ′ ⊆ V
073   at each decoding step e.g. via top-k (Fan et al., 2018; Deng & Raffel, 2023) or nucleus sampling
074   (Holtzman et al., 2020):
075

                                            {︃

                                               −∞,                   otherwise

078   and the next token is sampled from the categorical distribution:
079

080
081   While some language models might have a restrictive application programming interface (API) for
082   safety reasons, this line of work makes a reasonable assumption that we have access to the top-k
083   logits of a language model either directly or via API for a relatively small k ≪ |V |.
084
      To define reward scores, GeDi (Liu et al., 2021) and DExperts (Krause et al., 2021) use attribute-
085
      conditioned unidirectional language models (undesired attribute in GeDi or two LM experts for
086
      desired and undesired attribute in DExperts), trained via the standard language modeling objective on
087   class-conditioned data: r̂y (v|x) = zt (v|x, y), where y ∈ {0; 1} is the attribute (e.g. positive/negative
088   sentiment). Given a prefix x they only pass it once through the external language model backbone,
089   relying on the linear output layer to obtain the scores for each of the next token candidates.
090
      Alternatively, RAD (Deng & Raffel, 2023) trains a unidirectional reward model to predict the attribute
091
      of interest for a prefix concatenated with a next token candidate r̂RAD ([x, v]), where [·, ·] denotes the
092
      concatenation of a prefix and a next token candidate. This approach requires passing each next token
093   candidate as input to the model, thus, to obtain the scores for k next token candidates v for top-k
094   decoding RAD would need k forward calls of the reward model, which can slow down inference
095   significantly and constrains them to limit the number of next token candidates. Despite being less
096   efficient, RAD outperforms the approach with attribute-conditioned language models in terms of
097   controlled generation quality.

120   3.1.1          R EWARD MODELING AS MATRIX COMPLETION

122   To better understand the training objective of RAD, we start by looking at the optimization prob-
123

124
      enumerate all the contexts x′ in the training data and all possible next tokens v, we task a reward
125
      model to predict the values of R ∈ RN ×|V | , which we dub the reward matrix.
126
127   If each context would be observed only once, R would have a single observed reward in each row. For
128   short and common contexts we can observe more continuations per row, and also for some contexts
129   there can be ambiguities: {(x, y1 , λ1 ), ...(x, ym , λm )}. From a mean squared error point of view, it
130
      is equivalent to compress these ambiguities by taking the weighted average of their y (Appendix A):

      3.1.2          RAD CAN BE HIGH - RANK , BUT IS NOT IN PRACTICE
157
158   Given a prefix x, RAD accepts a token candidate v as an additional input to the model R̂RAD [x′ , v] =
159   r̂RAD ([x′ , v]), passing v through the layers of the reward model. For this reason, we expect RAD
160   to have the capacity to represent a large space of reward matrices including matrices with higher
161   rank. In Appendix C.1, we empirically verify that RAD is capable to approximate PΩ (R) matrix
      with high rank: rank(PΩ (R)) > d, where d is the dimensionality of the model . This flexibility

162
      does come at the cost: to score many next token candidates during top-k decoding, RAD needs to do
163   a forward pass through all layers of the model for each of the k next token candidates. Hence, an
164   important question is do we need this flexibility at the cost of slower decoding?
165
166   In Figure 1, we aim to measure the rank of R̂RAD for RAD trained on two datasets: for detoxification
167   and sentiment control tasks (discussed in detail in §5). To numerically estimate the rank, we follow
168
      Finlayson et al. (2024) and first sample N random prefixes x from the dataset Df to calculate N
169   full rows of R̂RAD (requiring N · |V | calls to the RAD reward model). Then we use singular value
170
      decomposition with the standard singular value cutoff to compute the rank (Appendix C.4). We
      observe that the reward matrix learned by RAD tends to be low-rank, suggesting that it is possible
171
      to use less flexible but faster reward models to improve the efficiency of reward models.

      3.1.3    I S THE TRAINING DATA FULL RANK ?
174
175   Note that the presence of a low-rank solution compatible with Ω does not imply that the true reward,
176   if it could be fully observed, is necessarily low rank. We argue that low-rank predictions can partly be
177   explained by the specifics of the training objective. Particularly, the incompleteness of PΩ (R) makes
178   it easier for a reward model to learn a low rank approximation. To understand why this is the case,
179   consider a simple scenario, when all prefixes x appear only once in the dataset. For this case, there
180   exists a rank-1 R̂ compatible with PΩ (R) (Appendix B.1).
181   To better understand this phenomenon, we would like to understand whether the PΩ (R) can be
182
      fit with the low-rank model. We define the minimum rank of a partially observed matrix R̂ as
183
      min rank(PΩ (R)) = min{rank(R̂) : PΩ (R) = PΩ (R̂)}. We claim that the data has low minimal
184
      rank. Note that empirically calculating the minimal rank of the data is challenging due to the very
185   large number of prefixes. We use a combination of theoretical and empirical approaches listed in
186   Appendix B.2 to demonstrate that incomplete PΩ (R) matrix can be fit with the low-rank matrix
187   factorization with a small error (with the rank less than the model dimension).

189   3.2     L OW-R ANK AUTOREGRESSIVE R EWARD M ODEL
190
191   Motivated to reduce the decoding costs of RAD, we propose ARM (Figure 2), a low-rank autoregres-
192   sive reward model suitable for guided decoding, designed for efficient modeling of rewards scores
193   for next token candidates. To ensure prediction efficiency of the reward model, we aim to revisit
194   the language modeling style of prediction (Liu et al., 2021; Krause et al., 2021) and aim to predict
195   the scores for all next token candidates with just a single forward pass through the backbone of a
196
      language model.
197   In contrast to RAD, ARM predicts the representation vector h(x) ∈ Rd given a prefix x and uses
198   output embeddings e(v) ∈ Rd to get the scores for all next token candidates. We propose the
199   following ARM parametrization, similar to the how Dueling Network (Wang et al., 2016; Tang et al.,
200   2023; Han et al., 2024) parametrizes the scores for the next tokens given the prefix:
201
                                   r̂ARM (v|x) = r̂b (h(x)) + ∆r̂(e(v)|h(x)),                             (6)
202                                                 baseline
203
      where the baseline predicts the score for the prefix x and ∆r̂ predicts how observing a next token v
204
      changes the score. Particularly, we use a linear parametrization:
205
206                      r̂b (v|x) := ⟨h(x), w⟩      ∆r̂(e(v)|h(x)) := ⟨h(x), W e(v)⟩.                    (7)
207
      Here, we introduced two attribute-specific parameters: w ∈ Rd for modeling the baseline reward
208
      score of the prefix, and W ∈ Rd×d to model marginal rewards for each next token candidate. Now it
209

260   4     R ELATED W ORK
261
262   There are multiple approaches that investigate how to finetune a language model using attribute-
263   conditioned data (desired/undesired examples). Keskar et al. (2019) finetunes a language model using
264   control prompts. More recent approaches (Schulman et al., 2017; Stiennon et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2022)
265   perform finetuning while regularizing the weights of the model to stay close to pretrained weights.
266
      Despite the efficiency of decoding, these methods might require more resources for finetuning if the
      language model is large, or might even be unusable if we only have access to the top-k logits of base
267
      language model via an API.
268
269   Unlike finetuning, alternative approaches keep the language model untouched and use external
      models to guide the decoding from the base language model. Dathathri et al. (2019) use the gradients


                                                           5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      from a discriminator to modify the prefix activations of the base model during decoding. However,
271   gradient-based methods are costly to use during decoding since they require backpropagating through
272   the large base model.
273
274
      Closest to our work are gradient-free guided decoding methods, where we have access to the frozen
      base language model and use external models to guide the sampling process from the base model.
275
      GeDi (Krause et al., 2021) proposes to use class-conditioned language models as discriminators to
276
      augment the decoding and efficiently compute the scores for next token candidates. DExperts (Liu
277   et al., 2021) improves the quality of GeDi introducing an ensemble of two class-conditioned language
278   models finetuned on desired and undesired data. We look closer into parametrization of the output
279   layer and propose to decouple the prefix score from marginal scores of the next token candidates.
280   Additionally, for our parametrization, we propose a regularization, which makes it easier for the
281   model to abstain.
282
      More recently, Deng & Raffel (2023) and Sitdikov et al. (2022) argue to use discriminator models
283   Yang & Klein (2021) to guide the decoding. Sitdikov et al. (2022); Dekoninck et al. (2023) use
284   available bidirectional Transformers to guide the base language model, which, however, requires to
285   recompute all prefix tokens at each decoding step. To tackle this issue, RAD (Deng & Raffel, 2023)
286   proposes a unidirectional model suitable for caching of prefix activations. They train a reward model
287   on partial prefixes to predict the expected future attribute and demonstrate high quality of controlled
288   generation.
289   In our work, we focus on the analysis of the RAD, while an alternative but related direction follows
290   reinforcement learning (RL) approach. Particularly, RAD uses value-function style parameterization,
291   while ARM is Q-function style. Among RL-based approaches, Mudgal et al. (2024), Chakraborty
292   et al. (2024) parametrize the value function, which results in higher decoding complexity; Cao
293   et al. (2022) parametrize the Q-function, resulting in similar efficiency to ARM. To the best of
294   our knowledge, there is little attention to the implied efficiency-quality trade-off that we study in
295   our work. The closest to our analysis is the recent work of Han et al. (2024), where they compare
296   both parametrizations in relation to language modeling, however they observe that value function
297
      parametrization outperforms Q-function parametrization, which disagrees with our work.
298   To summarize, we complement the previous work, by zooming in into the parametrization of an
299   autoregressive reward model. We highlight the trade-off between efficiency and expressiveness of a
300   reward model, and showcase that, for tasks and datasets we consider, higher rank-expressiveness can
301   be traded for higher efficiency without quality drop. We hope our analysis will inform future work on
302   the design choices of autoregressive reward models.

      For the detoxification evaluation, we follow previous work (Deng & Raffel, 2023; Liu et al., 2021)
353   and evaluate samples from guided decoding given a 10k subset (Liu et al., 2021) of prompts from the
354   RealToxicityPrompts dataset (Gehman et al., 2020). We follow Deng & Raffel (2023) and Liu et al.
355   (2021) and finetune our model on 2M pairs of text and continuous ‘toxicity’ responses between 0 and
356   1 from the Jigsaw Unintended Bias in Toxicity Classification challenge (cjadams et al., 2019). Like
357   previous work, we train our model on 7 independent responses (‘toxicity’, ‘severe toxicity’, ‘obscene’,
358   ‘identity attack’, ‘insult’, ‘threat’, ‘sexual explicit’) with different head parameters wi , Wi , i ∈
359   {1, ..., 7} for each sub-task. During decoding, we only use the ‘toxicity’ predictor. For the distillation
360   experiment, we use the same dataset, and the released toxicity discriminator from Deng & Raffel
361
      (2023) as a teacher.
362   During decoding, we sample 25 continuations generating at most 20 new tokens. To evaluate toxicity,
363   we use an external closed-source toxicity classifier Perspective API (Lees et al., 2022), and following
364   previous work (Deng & Raffel, 2023; Liu et al., 2021), we rely on the Maximal Average Toxicity
365   metric, which is the maximal toxicity score value over 25 samples for a given prompt, averaged
366   over the set of 10k prompts. We also report Toxic Rate, which is calculated as the probability that at
367
      least one out of 25 continuations is toxic according to Perspective API (toxicity score > 0.5); and
      Diversity score, which is the average number of distinct n-grams normalized by the length of text
368
      (Li et al., 2018). To evaluate the fluency of model generations, we follow previous work (Liu et al.,
369

452
453   To compare RAD and ARM, we rely on the methodology of Deng & Raffel (2023) and Liu et al.
454   (2021), and visualize the trade-off plots for both models varying the control parameter β. Namely,
455
      each point in the figure will represent two metrics: toxicity/sentiment along the vertical axis and
      fluency along the horizontal axis. From this plot, we can read e.g. what fluency (perplexity/mauve)
456
      can be achieved for a given ‘target’ toxicity. To compare two models, we compare their curves (in the
457
      same plot). Our hypothesis is that ARM will perform similar to the more flexible RAD approach,
458   meaning that the trade-off plots for these models will be close to each other.
459
460
      Detoxification. For the detoxification task (Figure 3), our efficient student (ARM) closely follows
461   the RAD teacher for toxicity control/fluency trade-off. We observe that ARM trained on responses
462   only shows slightly worse fluency w.r.t. average perplexity for lower levels of toxicity. For complete-
463   ness, in Figure 12, we include the results for other baselines from Deng & Raffel (2023) computed
464   for an older version of Perspective API. For guided decoding from the LLaMa-2-(7b/13b), we ob-
465   serve that again ARM closely follows RAD in terms of toxicity/fluency trade-off (see Figure 14 in
466   Appendix F.1.1).
467
468   Sentiment control. From the results on the sentiment control task in Figure 4, we observe that the
469   ARM student model shows slightly better trade-off than the RAD teacher model, closely following
470   approaches that require training using feedback from the evaluation pipeline (Lu et al., 2022, Quark),
471   (Stiennon et al., 2020, PPO). Again, ARM trained on original responses slightly lags behind but still
472
      performs competitively compared to other guided decoding baselines.
473
474   Summary. Our empirical results suggest that ARM can match the quality of more flexible but
475
      less efficient RAD. We observe that distilling the RAD teacher into the ARM student results in
      slightly higher quality compared to training ARM on original responses. One difference is that when
476
      training from data, we will see short contexts multiple times with different reward responses and
477
      must implicitly converge to their average, while in distillation, the teacher already performs this
478   compression and provides a single deterministic target r̂(v|x) for every context (x, v). We conjecture
479   that this may lead to better-trained distilled models.

508   5.6   E FFICIENCY
509
510   We consider using a reward model to compute the scores for k candidate tokens at each of L steps of
511
      decoding. Similar to RAD (Deng & Raffel, 2023), ARM is based on the unidirectional Transformer
      architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017), which means that we can cache the prefix activations during
512
      decoding. To compute the prediction for k next token candidates v given a prefix x, RAD needs to
513
      pass k next tokens as input to the Transformer model, thus RAD processes O(Lk) tokens during
514   decoding. In contrast, ARM only processes O(L) tokens as input to the Transformer model and relies
515   on the output layer to efficiently compute the scores for all next token candidates. In Table 1, we
516   summarize how many tokens external expert models process during top-k decoding. In Figure 6, we
517   measure the time per generated token when running the decoding for the toxicity task with ARM and
518   RAD (Deng & Raffel, 2023) on a single RTX A6000 GPU.

      6     C ONCLUSION
521
522
      We review the recently proposed RAD approach of training a reward model for the guided decoding,
523   and we reformulate it as the incomplete reward matrix learning problem. In the light of the rank
524   analysis of the reward matrix, we observe that the high flexibility of RAD might not overweight
525   its lower efficiency during decoding. We present the low-rank ARM, an efficient approach to
526   parameterize the reward model, suitable for autoregressive decoding, caching of prefix activations,
527   and prediction of next token scores with a single call of a backbone model. We bridge the gap
528   between two paradigms of training external expert models, demonstrating that we can have both
529   efficient and effective controlled generation.
530
531   L IMITATIONS
532
533   The models discussed in this work can only reduce the probability of generating the toxic responses,
534    not prevent it. Moreover, evaluation of toxicity is far from perfect, and even a very low toxicity
535    score from automatic evaluation such as Perspective API does not necessary mean that the sample is
536   ‘safe’. Furthermore, we should not exclusively rely on toxicity when evaluating the safety of samples
537    from language models due to the complexity and variability of language. It is also not clear that
538    by reducing toxicity, we are not introducing other harms. Furthermore, both RAD and our models
539    represent low-rank R̂ and further qualitative research is needed to investigate whether certain toxicity
       patterns require high rank to represent them.

775   B     FACTORIZATION OF PΩ (R)
776
777   Any matrix R ∈ RN ×|V | can be factored as R = U V T with U, V of dimensions N × q; |V | × q. If
778   R is incomplete, then there are in general multiple possible factorizations of PΩ (R) compatible with
779   the observed values.
780
781   B.1    R ANK -1 CASE
782
      To get better intuition why the incompleteness of PΩ (R) allows to find a compatible factorization
783
      with lower minimal rank, consider a simple example. If we only know 1 element per row of R, then
784
      minimal rank of PΩ (R) is equal to 1. To prove this, consider completing PΩ (R) such that each row
785   is filled with the same element (the only one known for this row):

      B.2    E STIMATING THE MINIMAL RANK OF THE DATA
792
793   Empirically calculating minimal rank is challenging due to the very large number of prefixes (row of
794   the matrix), particularly, the large portion of the prefixes have unique continuation. We show how we
795   simplify the minimal rank estimation by considering only the prefixes with two or more continuations,
796   and demonstrate that partially observed R̂ can be fit with a low rank matrix factorization.
797
          Lemma 1. Consider a partially observed reward (sub-)matrix PΩ (R), such that for every
798
          row, only one element of R is observed. Then there exists R̂, such that rank(R̂) = 1 and
799
800
          ∥PΩ (R) − PΩ (R̂)∥2F = 0.
801
802   Proof. Let vi be the observed value in R at row i, and let R̂ = v1⊤ ; i.e., we complete the whole row
803   with the same value. This rank-1 matrix achieves zero loss.
804
805   Given a training dataset of responses and text utterances, there will be many unique prefixes, for
806   which the Lemma 1 is applicable.
807
          Lemma 2. Let PΩ (R) be a partially observed matrix and [PΩ (R)]2 correspond to the
808
809
          submatrix formed only with the rows of PΩ (R̂) with at least two observed indices. Then,
          min rank(PΩ (R)) ≤ 1 + min rank([PΩ (R)]2 )

810
      Next, we demonstrate that [PΩ (R)]2 can be fitted by a model that produces low rank R̂:
811   min rank([PΩ (R)]2 ) ≤ d − 1 for our specific dataset, and using Lemmas 1 and 2, we conclude that
812   min rank(PΩ (R)) < d. This implies that the training dataset Df can be fit by a reward model that
813   produces low-rank R̂, regardless of the specifics of said model.
814
815   Here we check that there exists a factorization of [PΩ (R)]2 with rank q at most 512 (q is less than the
816
      model dimension d = 764). In general, finding minimal rank factorization of incomplete matrices
      is known to be NP-hard, and usually convex relaxation such as minimization of the nuclear norm
817
      is considered (see Nan (2009)). To factorize PΩ (R), we use the alternating least squares algorithm
818
      (Mazumder et al., 2010; Hastie et al., 2015).4 To accelerate convergence to a good solution, we first
819   optimize for 50 iterations with a trace norm penalty of λ = 0.01 (i.e we start by solving a non-convex
820   approximation of a convex problem) followed by an additional 50 iterations with no trace penalty. At
821   the end, the mean squared error (MSE) over the observed entries is 0.00056. Given the large scale of
822   PΩ (R), it is possible that a better fit could be found nevertheless we find this sufficient evidence to
823   claim that low-rank models could indeed fit the training data.
