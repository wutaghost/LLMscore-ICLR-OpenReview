009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose a novel technique for training Energy-based Models (EBMs), which
012            are neural network-based models capable of modeling complex probability distri-
013            butions. The standard approach to EBM training relies on samples generated from
014            the modeled distribution using Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD).
015            However, this training method is known to be unstable, as SGLD may fail to
016            provide reliable samples. Compared to other popular generative models, EBMs
017            can directly evaluate unnormalized log-likelihoods for input observations. Unfortu-
018            nately, trained EBMs typically fail to robustly estimate the likelihoods for distant
019
               input observations, as the training procedure only considers the gradients of the
               log-likelihood with respect to the observations and not the actual log-likelihood
020
               values. This paper proposes a generalization of the standard training objective that
021
               addresses both issues. The proposed objective explicitly incorporates estimated un-
022            scaled log-likelihoods, allowing the EBM to estimate the likelihoods more reliably.
023            Notably, EBMs do not need to (and as we point out, cannot) correctly estimate
024            log-likelihoods to be effective for sampling using the non-convergent SGLD pro-
025            cedure. The proposed objective is controlled by a single hyper-parameter, which
026            balances the trade-off between the quality of the estimated log-likelihoods and
027            the generated samples. A specific setting of this parameter recovers the standard
028            EBM training objective. Moreover, the proposed objective enhances robustness to
029            unreliable SGLD samples by de-weighting contributions from samples that appear
030
               inconsistent with the modeled distribution, i.e., samples with very low estimated
               likelihoods compared to other generated samples or real training data. We demon-
031
               strate the improvement in log-likelihood modeling on toy datasets and enhanced
032
               stability in a real data scenario, where this stability leads to better performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
      Unrestricted probabilistic Energy-based Models (EBMs) (Du & Mordatch, 2019; Nijkamp et al.,
037   2019; Xie et al., 2016) are powerful generative models. Theoretically, trained EBMs can generate
038   new data following the Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) iterative procedure. However, two
039   closely related approaches, score-based generative models (Hyvärinen & Dayan, 2005; Vincent, 2011;
040   Song & Ermon, 2019; 2020; Song et al., 2021) and diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho
041   et al., 2020), have shown superior quality in the tasks of generating new data, typically shown on
042   image datasets. Nevertheless, EBMs stand out as they can directly provide unnormalized likelihood
043   values for each input, unlike the alternative approaches. The promise of having reliable unscaled
044   likelihood values could give rise to new approaches requiring them (Du et al., 2023) or improve the
045
      performance of existing ones that rely on likelihood from alternative generative models (Kingma
      & Welling, 2013; Dinh et al., 2016; Kingma & Dhariwal, 2018). The most widely used approach
046
      of training EBMs via Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) is based on generated approximate
047
      samples from the EBM that are treated as true samples. This EBM training is unstable in most cases,
048   which is considered as currently one of the biggest issues related to EBMs (Grathwohl et al., 2019).
049   An expert can remedy some cases by fine-tuning all the necessary hyperparameters, but usually at
050   the cost of restricting the architecture or causing a potential drop in performance. This makes it not
051   just time-consuming but also impractical as the whole process might need to be repeated when a
052   change in data, model architecture, or hyperparameters is required, let alone joint training, where
053   EBM would represent only a part of the whole system. Unfortunately, except for the stability issues,
      trained EBMs typically suffer from poor estimates of unscaled likelihood values when comparing the

      likelihoods of two distant input observations. Instead, what is considered informative and utilized
055   after training are the local changes in log-likelihood values, also known as score ∇x log pθ (x).
056
057   The standard approach to training EBMs does not directly utilize the actual likelihood values; instead,
058
      it relies solely on the score used during the generation of model samples required for training. Our
      method is motivated by the fact that in real scenarios, only approximate and often unreliable model
059
      samples will be generated. To better handle these cases, we propose a generalized training approach
060
      (loss) that has a single hyperparameter β, whose specific setting β = 0 recovers the standard training.
061   All other settings result in training that explicitly requires values of unscaled log-likelihoods, so
062   log-likelihoods directly play a role in the optimization process. The parameter β controls the dynamic
063   range of these weights. We can trade off sample quality for a more credible estimate of likelihood
064   values and improved training stability based on the setting of β. We demonstrate this effect on the
065   toy dataset. Additionally, we show increased training stability on a real dataset; however, we were
066   unable to train models that could benefit from better-estimated likelihoods because the utilized SGLD
067   sampler was ineffective.

117   2.2   S OURCE OF P OTENTIALLY U NRELIABLE L IKELIHOOD VALUES
118
      The described training procedure does not directly incorporate likelihood or log-likelihood values, as
119
      they are absent in Equation 3, and the negative examples3 are generated solely based on the score
120   ∇x fθ (x) (Equation 4). In theory, likelihoods do not need to be evaluated to train the model because
121   it is assumed that we have access to true samples from pθ (x). Nijkamp et al. (2019) demonstrated
122   that even when negative examples are not true samples from pθ (x), the training can still converge. In
123   such cases, we can generate samples distributed similarly to pd (x), but the learned distribution pθ (x)
124   may differ significantly from pd (x). We can interpret SGLD as a procedure that transforms the initial
125   distribution p(x0 ) into the distribution SGLD(pθ (x))4 , using the gradient of the negative energy of
126   pθ (x), thereby generating samples from this distribution. If the SGLD procedure converges, then
127   SGLD(pθ (x)) will be equivalent to pθ (x). However, when the initial distribution is far from pθ (x),
128
      using a limited number of SGLD steps may not produce samples from pθ (x) utilizing the gradient of
      its true negative energy. Nonetheless, a different EBM might exist, potentially far from the desired
129
      pθ (x), that models pd (x) Its negative energy gradient in the non-convergent SGLD procedure can
130
      transform the initial distribution into pd (x). This scenario is illustrated in the first three columns
131   of Figure 2, which show the true distribution pd (x), the incorrectly learned distribution pθ (x),
132   and the distribution of generated examples SGLD(pθ (x)). If this occurs, the training converges
133   as pd (x) = SGLD(pθ (x)). While this approach offers the advantage of constructing an implicit
134   generative model (sampler) of pd (x), we must avoid relying on unnormalized likelihood values, as
135   fθ (x) may correspond to a completely different EBM. Additionally, the ability to generate realistic
136   data is sensitive to the specific setting of hyperparameters for the SGLD procedure. Modifications
137   such as increasing the number of steps can significantly degrade the sample quality.

      2.3   F ORCE A NALOGY OF EBM T RAINING
139
140   We liken EBM training via Equation 3 to shaping the surface of the probability density function
141   by balancing two forces as it can provide an intuitive explanation and motivation for our technique.
142   The first (positive) force increases the log-likelihood (via fθ (x)) at the locations of training data
143
      x ∼ pd (x), as illustrated in Figure 1a. The locations of the second (negative) force, which decreases
      the log-likelihood values, should be determined by pθ (x) (Figure 1b). The force strength emitted
144
      by all positive and negative examples is equivalent. When negative examples are true samples from
145
      pθ (x), both positive and negative forces will be balanced everywhere as the training progresses, and
146   pθ (x) → pd (x). However, from a force balancing point of view, using the same approach when an
147   excessive number of negative examples originate from regions of low likelihood, positive examples
148   keep increasing fθ (x), but negative examples decrease fθ (x) at different locations. This discrepancy
149   can lead to an unconstrained increase or decrease of fθ (x), possibly causing training divergence. Our
150   approach tries to address this issue by mitigating the damage caused by negative examples with low
151   likelihood values.

      3.2   A NALYSIS
268
269   The particular choice of including positive examples among negative ones, which resulted in Equa-
      tion 8, might seem arbitrary. However, this decision is motivated by the fact that Equation 8 can


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      alternatively be viewed as a form of discriminative training. Further insight into this interpretation
271   is provided in Appendix C. Unlike Equation 6, which can replace Equation 2 without affecting the
272   optimized objective, using Equation 8 instead is not equivalent. Importantly, the resulting objective
273   being maximized is a lower bound of the objective that was optimized before including this additional
274   negative example. The proof is provided in Appendix D. We also study the effect of including a
275   positive example among negative ones from a mechanistic point of view. This reveals two effects.
276   First, it rescales the gradient for each batch, depending on the difference between the negative
277   energy of positive and negative examples. As fθ (x+ ) − fθ (x– ) increases, the scale of the gradient
278   becomes smaller, allowing for the possibility of ignoring all negative examples from the batch that do
279
      not have competitive likelihoods. Similar to a rescaling of gradient contributions among negative
      examples introduced by β, the second effect resulting from replacing Equation 6 with Equation 8
280
      leads the rescaling of the gradient contributions among positive examples. This rescaling effectively
281
      slows down the growth of the log-likelihood value for positive examples that have relatively high
282   likelihoods among other positive examples. This effect is magnified as the performance of the SGLD
283   sampler decreases, i.e., when fθ (x+ ) − fθ (x– ) increases. Derivations and more details are provided
284   in Appendix E. We expect these effects to positively influence training. However, as mentioned, they
285   result in a biased objective that is a lower bound of the original objective, which may be undesirable
286   in certain cases. To address this, it is possible to isolate the effect of rescaling the overall gradient in
287   each batch based on how inaccurate the negative examples are, resulting in the optimization of the
288   original unbiased objective. These variants also serve as ablations, and all details and derivations are
289   described in Appendix E.1.
290   Our proposed modification of EBM training involves two main components. First, we introduced
291   the β-parameterized objective in Section 3. Second, we incorporated positive examples among
292   negative ones in Section 3.1. Isolating the effect of including a positive example without introducing
293   β may seem intriguing; however, this approach is not useful since it corresponds to β = 0. In
294   this case, including a positive example results in only a slight modification to the learning rate, as
295   demonstrated in Appendix F. Section 3 discusses sampling based on negative energy 1 − β and
296
      subsequent reweighting according to β. Notably, these two values do not necessarily need to sum
      up to 1; arbitrary values can be employed instead, corresponding to a different parameterization of
297
      the EBM. We derive this in Appendix G, based on an analysis of key aspects of the practical SGLD
298
      sampler, including its extension to β ̸= 0 settings.
299
300   Section 2.2 explains that when β = 0, the learned EBM pθ (x) might be significantly different from
301   pd (x). With some simplifications, we expect that if training converges for β ̸= 0, pθ (x) must partially
302
      reflect pd (x), but at the same time, it must compensate for the differences between SGLD(pθ (x))
      and pd (x). As a result, for values of x where SGLD(pθ (x)) > pd (x), we expect pθ (x) < pd (x).
303
      We explain our reasoning in Appendix I and discuss additional trade-offs related to the different
304
      settings of β in Appendix I.1.
305
306   If, during many subsequent updates, we fail to generate any reasonable negative examples, the
307   proposed method will not assist with training stabilization when combined with optimizers that
308
      apply an adaptive learning rate. We provide more details in Appendix H. Although encountering this
      failure state necessitates modifying the approach for generating negative examples, we aim to raise
309
      awareness about why we cannot prevent instability in this case.

      4.1           E VALUATION ON T OY DATASETS
335
336   To verify whether the expected behavior holds in practice, we focus on experiments using toy
337   datasets, where we can visually compare the learned densities. Specifically, we adopt the 2D toy
338
      dataset setup from Nijkamp et al. (2020), which initializes the SGLD procedure with a Gaussian
      distribution and employs Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) as the optimizer. For evaluation, we consider
339
      the standard circles dataset and modify the standard Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) dataset by
340
      assigning different weights to each component. This modification is useful for examining the effects
341   of the proposed method. We follow the non-convergent setup described in Nijkamp et al. (2020),
342   where only a limited number of SGLD steps with a constant step size are used to generate negative

      5                       D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
483
484   We proposed a new training approach, parameterized by β, for training Energy-based Models using
485   negative examples generated by SGLD. This method generalizes the standard approach, which
      corresponds to setting β = 0. This work focuses on establishing a theoretical foundation for


                                                                                                                  9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      the proposed approach and includes a non-trivial analysis. Due to space limitations, most of the
487   theoretical details are presented in the Appendix. The motivation behind this method is to mitigate
488   the negative influence of inaccurate negative examples that are not true samples from pθ (x), which
489   should, in turn, improve likelihoods and reduce training instabilities. However, this approach may
490   lead to a decrease in the quality of generated examples when using a practical non-convergent SGLD
491   sampler. Fortunately, we can balance these effects by tuning β. Our method extends standard EBM
492   training by effectively deweighting unreliable training samples as analyzed in Appendix E. This effect
493   can be split into three components: the weight that rescales the contribution of negative examples,
494   preventing fθ (x– ) from shrinking to large negative values; the weight that adjusts the contribution
495
      of positive examples, preventing fθ (x+ ) from growing excessively; and the weight that scales the
      overall contribution of a single batch based on the energy difference between positive and negative
496
      examples, reducing the importance of batches with a significant gap between fθ (x+ ) and fθ (x– ).
497
498   On a toy dataset, we confirmed our theoretical hypothesis regarding the behavior of β and demon-
499   strated that the learned density improves as β increases. However, with the real dataset, we were
500   unable to fully realize this potential, successfully training models only with extremely small values
501
      of β, such us β = 10−6. This limitation arose because, with small values of β such as β = 0.01,
      the SGLD sampler struggled to produce negative examples x– with energies comparable to those
502
      of positive examples. In contrast, when β = 0, both negative and positive examples exhibited
503
      comparable energies; however, this gap gradually widened as β increased. To explain this behavior,
504   we propose a hypothesis in Appendix J, suggesting that training with β = 0 follows a more general
505   approach of “attractor-repeller training”. Fortunately, we demonstrated that we can mitigate training
506   instabilities by using very small values of β. This was illustrated in the training of JEM, where setting
507   β = 0 can lead to divergence, which may occur even within the first few epochs.
508
      Even though training stabilization is an important aspect, we believe that improving likelihood could
509
      potentially be even more valuable. Unfortunately, achieving this requires a different sampling method
510   that produces negative examples with higher fθ (x– ), enabling the training of models with values in
511   the range 0.1 < β < 0.5. It is unclear whether existing approaches could be adapted for this purpose,
512   whether a new method needs to be developed, or if the issue could be resolved through alternative
513   strategies, such as better initialization of the SGLD or a specialized architecture.
514
      Our method is applicable in any context where SGLD samples are used to train EBMs with the
515
      standard loss function (β = 0). This integration is straightforward and can potentially enhance
516   performance or address stability issues. However, since this work primarily focuses on building
517   a theoretical foundation, our method introduces additional possibilities. By efficiently filtering
518   out negative examples with low fθ (x– ), we shift from the theoretical requirement that all negative
519   examples must be valid to a more flexible condition—that at least one negative example is good. This
520   flexibility allows for the combination of multiple techniques for generating negative examples, such
521   as using both SGLD and HMC or deploying multiple SGLD samplers with varying hyperparameters.
522   Furthermore, in the case of JEM, we demonstrated that the proposed method could overcome phases of
523   training where most, if not all, generated examples were unreliable. This flexibility is a powerful tool,
524   as it enables adaptive HMC or SGLD sampling that can optimize parameters during training, which
525   has been reported to be challenging for β = 0 due to instabilities limiting exploration. Additionally,
526   the introduced weights can also serve as effective monitoring tools for EBM training.
527
      Our approach can also help assess how much a system relies on the introduced attractor-repeller
528   scheme by comparing systems trained with β = 0 to those trained with, for example, β = 0.01.
529   In this work, we addressed the issue of samplers frequently producing samples with low fθ (x).
530   Similarly, if a sampler tends to produce samples with excessively high fθ (x– ) values (e.g., sampling
531   from ∝ pθ (x– )4 ), the proposed approach could be adapted by using negative values of β. Lastly, all
532   values of β correspond to EBM training, allowing for dynamic adjustments of β during training or
533   the simultaneous optimization of objectives with varying β values.

628   A     SGLD I SSUES
629
630   A.1   A REAS OF C ONSTANT OR U NDEFINED L IKELIHOOD
631
      SGLD (MCMC chain) can travel through areas of constant likelihood only based on diffusion, i.e.
632
      Gaussian noise added at each step. It requires a large step size or a large number of steps to escape
633
      these areas. Practical applications with a limited number of SGLD steps will likely result in an SGLD
634   sample originating in this area, which artificially increases the probability of producing samples from
635   these areas, therefore, resulting in sampling from a modified distribution.
636
637
      According to the manifold hypothesis, in many real applications, the support of the data distribution
      forms a low-dimensional manifold in the observed space. In other words, the true data distribution
638
      pd (x) = 0 for most x. It can be interpreted as the score ∇x fθ (x) being either 0 or undefined. The
639
      training objective minimizes KLD between pd (x) and pθ (x). When pθ (x) starts converging to pd (x),
640   the importance of this flaw increases. Convolving the data distribution with a Gaussian filter (adding
641   Gaussian noise to each x) can alleviate it, but it would require a large amount of noise to remove that
642   issue entirely. This is not suitable as the resulting distribution would be very noisy.
643
644   A.2   M IXING R ATE
645
646   SGLD has a slow mixing rate. Once the MCMC chain imposed by SGLD enters a particular mode
647   of multimodal distribution, it will likely stay in that mode when only a limited number of steps
      with suitable step size is performed. Within a single distribution mode, Langevin Dynamics are not


                                                       12
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


648
      affected by the weight (probability mass) of that mode, and it is believed that SGLD might provide
649   genuine samples from the underlying distribution mode. However, in a real scenario, it results in the
650   probability of obtaining a sample from each    mode m not being affected by the weight of each mode,
                                               R
651   i.e. the mode mixture weight pθ (m) = x pθ (x, m). Instead, we believe that the weightRwill roughly
652   correspond to a probability of the MCMC chain being initialized in that mode p0 (m) = x0 p(x0 , m),
653   where p(x0 ) is the distribution used to initialize Markov chain of SGLD.
654
655   A.3    SGLD S TEP S IZE
656
657   As training progresses, the optimal SGLD step size can vary. Since we perform only a limited number
658   of steps, typically with a fixed size, too small step sizes will not cause enough movement, while too
659   large step sizes will only cause uninformative jumping over the space under the exploration. Too-large
660   step sizes could be avoided or at least detected by incorporating Metropolis-Hastings acceptance
661   probability, which gives rise to Metropolis-adjusted Langevin algorithm (Besag, 1994), but it is
662
      typically not applied during training.

941   When all generated negative examples have low likelihood values, and for sufficiently large β, the
942   parameter (θ) updates during training are effectively disregarded due to the presence of the additional
943                                                                               +

944   When a certain θ is reached during training and then suddenly all negative examples stop being
945   proper, the proposed updates should almost not modify θ, thus preserving the currently achieved
946   solution θ. Conversely, the standard training approach (β = 0) continues to modify θ, essentially
947   erasing previously learned information.

949   If negative examples are consistently inadequate for many iterations, we should observe very low
950                           +
      gradient values as (1 − w(x+ )) ≈ 0. Despite this, certain optimizers like AdaGrad (Duchi et al., 2011)

953   current θ, but it would not prevent it. Nevertheless, if no competitive negative example is generated
954   for an extended duration, proactive measures should be taken. This involves adjusting the procedure
955   for generating negative examples to ensure that at least some have comparable likelihood values.

988    e0fθ (x) as shown in Figure 1c. In the context of the manifold hypothesis, most (practically all) of
989    these examples would reside in low-likelihood regions carrying little information about the desired
990    expectation and we would like them to be filtered out. Increasing β makes SGLD steps more driven
991
       by sampled noise than ∇x fθ (x). This will have an unwanted effect on the number of required SGLD
       steps needed to travel from low-likelihood to high-likelihood regions. On the other hand, the mixing
992
       rate (traveling across distribution modes) should improve. The effectiveness of filtering out unwanted
993
       negative examples (sensitivity to detect outliers) increases with increasing value of β as the weight is
994
       determined based on eβfθ (x) .
995
996    The automatic decrease of gradient magnitude is based on the likelihood comparison between a single
997    positive and the sum of all negative examples. However, especially when considering a small number
998
       of negative examples M and a large β, the likelihood of a positive example can be much higher,
       even with a proper sampler of negative examples. This is because a positive example is drawn from
999                                       1−β
1000
       pd (x), while negative from pθ (x)      rather than pθ (x), but the effect can be partially counteracted
       by adjusting the learning rate.
1001
1002   These arguments demonstrate a trade-off between larger and smaller values of β. However, we want
1003   to emphasize that as an extension of this work, it is also possible to arbitrarily change the value of β
1004   during training as long as it is fixed for all negative examples within each batch.

1006   J     ATTRACTOR -R EPELLER DYNAMICS : A N EBM T RAINING H YPOTHESIS
1007
1008   We initially expected that the difference between EBM training with β = 0 and β < 0.01 would
1009   be imperceptible or, at most, result in minimal differences. However, when applied to real data, the
1010   difference is pronounced. While the SGLD sampler is capable of providing negative examples x–
1011   with fθ (x– ) close to fθ (x+ ) for β = 0, it fails for β = 0.01, resulting in inefficient training. From a
1012   theoretical point of view, updating parameters based on calculations with β = 0.01 should be more
1013   precise. However, we propose a hypothesis that explains why this less precise update can help the
1014   SGLD sampler be more effective. This hypothesis is based on the concepts of attractors and repellers,
1015   which we refer to as “attractor-repeller training”.
1016   As β increases, the weight of negative examples with lower likelihood values decreases to the point
1017   where they are effectively ignored. This approach is in line with the principles of EBM training, as
1018   these negative examples should not be produced in the first place. However, as a consequence, fθ (x– )
1019   remains unaffected around this x– , and the SGLD procedure also remains unchanged. Consequently,
1020   repeating the SGLD procedure with the same initialization is likely to yield a similar negative example.
1021   In contrast, when β = 0, such negative examples are treated as true samples, further incorrectly
1022
       decreasing already small fθ (x– ), which causes the trained EBM to deviate from modeling the correct
       distribution. Therefore, this process reduces the probability of generating the same negative example
1023
       again.
1024
1025   We can interpret this by conceptualizing the practical SGLD procedure as an implicit multi-step gen-
       erator that iteratively explores the domain of x. As fθ (x+ ) increases for positive examples x+ , local


                                                          19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       maxima may form, serving as attractors for the SGLD procedure. Simultaneously, negative examples
1027   function as repellers, as updates can create local minima at those locations. The establishment of a
1028   local minimum forces SGLD to search for another negative example in the subsequent iteration. This
1029   explains why the effectiveness of the SGLD procedure is significantly greater when β = 0 compared
1030   to β = 0.01. The setting β = 0 produces an effective implicit sampler, resembling GAN (Goodfellow
1031   et al., 2020) training; however, as we have shown, it does not correspond to the training of EBM
1032   modeling the true data distribution.
1033   Moreover, since increasing β eliminates the divergent behavior, the attractor-repeller training appears
1034   to be responsible for training instabilities. It tends to decrease fθ (x– ) too rapidly in regions where
1035   fθ (x– ) is already small, which consequently leads to unrestricted growth of fθ (x+ )—a phenomenon
1036   that would not occur in true EBM training. While true EBM training can also be viewed as an
1037   attractor-repeller scheme, we use this term to describe a more general training method that does not
1038   require negative examples to be sampled from the model. Instead, it only requires that increasing
1039   fθ (x) raises the probability of x being sampled, while decreasing fθ (x) lowers this probability.

1585   We selected this work to demonstrate the usefulness of our method for two key reasons. First, it
1586
       uses a standard architecture, Wide Residual Networks (Zagoruyko & Komodakis, 2016), rather
       than a specialized one designed for generative performance. This choice leads to frequent training
1587
       instabilities, and the authors report that the only remedy is restarting training from the last checkpoint
1588
       and resetting the random seed. A further increase in the number of SGLD steps becomes necessary if
1589   that proves ineffective. These instabilities worsen with any changes to the hyperparameters, making
1590   model development particularly challenging. We demonstrate that an appropriate choice of β can
1591   mitigate these training divergences. Second, demonstrating the model’s utility becomes challenging
1592   when the SGLD procedure ceases to provide good samples. To address this, we leverage JEM’s
1593   ability to function as a classifier, which allows us to evaluate its classification accuracy.
