009                                                                                              A BSTRACT
010
011                                           Scaling up neural networks has been a key recipe to the success of large language
012                                           and vision models. However, in practice, up-scaled models can be disproportion-
013                                           ately costly in terms of computations, providing only marginal improvements in
014                                           performance; for example, EfficientViT-L3-384 achieves <2% improvement on
015                                           ImageNet-1K accuracy over the base L1-224 model, while requiring 14× more
016                                           multiply–accumulate operations (MACs). In this paper, we investigate scaling
017                                           properties of popular families of neural networks for image classification, and
018
                                              find that scaled-up models mostly help with “difficult” samples. Decomposing
                                              the samples by difficulty, we develop an embarrassingly simple model-agnostic
019
                                              two-pass Little-Big algorithm that first uses a light-weight “little” model to make
020
                                              predictions of all samples, and only passes the difficult ones for the “big” model to
021                                           solve. Good little companions achieve drastic MACs reduction for a wide variety
022                                           of model families and scales. Without loss of accuracy or modification of existing
023                                           models, our Little-Big models achieve MACs reductions of 76% for EfficientViT-
024                                           L3-384, 81% for EfficientNet-B7-600, 71% for DeiT3-L-384 on ImageNet-1K.
025                                           Little-Big also speeds up the InternImage-G-512 model by 62% while achieving
026                                           90% ImageNet-1K top-1 accuracy, serving both as a strong baseline and as a simple
027                                           practical method for large model compression.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      Advances in parallel computing hardware, such as GPUs, have made end-to-end single-pass parallel
057   processing standard in computer vision models. Large vision datasets like ImageNet-1K (Deng
058   et al., 2009) made it possible for such deep vision models (e.g.Alexnet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012),
059   ResNet (He et al., 2016) and ViT Dosovitskiy et al. (2020)) to learn general visual features at
060   scale. While vision models surpassed human performance on ImageNet-1K a decade ago (Szegedy
061   et al., 2015), researchers are in the perpetual pursuit of achieving improved performance by using a
062   combination of two strategies: 1) developing more performant models and training techniques for
063   a given compute budget, and 2) scaling up the models. While improved models from the former
064   approach are often preferred in compute or memory-constrained applications, the latter has become
065
      increasingly popular, thanks to its success in large language models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023).
      However, despite architectural improvements, scaling up models remains expensive; we are often
066
      trading exponential compute cost for marginal gains in model accuracy (Table 1).
067
068   In this work, we show that much of the inefficiency comes from our implicit preference for single-pass
069   models and propose an embarrassingly simple two-pass algorithm to drastically speed up models
070   with little companions. We summarize our work in response to two critical questions around model
071
      scaling and compression.
072   〈Q1〉 Given a pair of Little-Big models in the same model family, which incorrect predictions made
073        by the Little model are fixed using the Big model?
074
075   • Using ImageNet-1K as the test bed and binning the Little model’s predictions by confidence, we
076     find that, very often, mistakes made by the Little model correspond to low confidence (measured
077     via maximum softmax probability)
078   〈Q2〉 Without compromising accuracy, can we speed up a Big model by using a Little model to
079        preprocess a proportion of samples in a distribution?
080
081   • We propose an embarrassingly simple two-pass Little-Big protocol where a light-weight Little
082     model is used to make predictions (class and confidence) on all samples in the first pass, and a
083     Big model performs a second pass on samples with low confidence from the first pass, achieving
084     significant reduction in inference compute costs without compromising accuracy.
085
086   • Without any modification to existing models, we prescribe Little-Big pairs that significantly reduce
087
        compute costs of models across types and scales, while not compromising accuracy: Little-Big
        models achieves MACs reductions of 76% for EfficientViT-L3-384, 81% for EfficientNet-B7-600,
088
        71% for DeiT3-L-384 on ImageNet-1K. Little-Big also speeds up the very large InternImage-G-512
089
        model by 62% while achieving 90% ImageNet-1K top-1 accuracy, serving both as a strong baseline
090     and as a practical approach for efficient deployment of large models.

110   2.1   C OMPUTER V ISION M ODELS
111
112   Since Alexnet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), single-pass neural classifiers trained end-to-end have
113   dominated leaderboards of various vision tasks from image classification to video segmentation
114   (Beyer et al., 2020; Deng et al., 2009; He et al., 2016; Szegedy et al., 2015; Xu et al., 2018). Most
115   neural models originates from two families of neural architectures: convolutional neural networks
116   (CNNs) and transformers. Core to CNNs are “convolutions” which apply the same compute across
117
      locations on a feature map. On the other hand, transformers, which first found success in sequence-
      to-sequence language models (Vaswani et al., 2017) and subsequently in vision (Dosovitskiy et al.,
118
      2020), embed a sequence of tokens (e.g., image patches) and utilize attention mechanism to model
119
      intra- and inter-token interactions. Finally, hybrid models like Swin (Liu et al., 2021) combine CNN
120   priors and attention mechanisms to achieve good performance.
121

      Complementing innovations in model architecture F that make models more compute efficient, a
      straightforward way of improving performance is to scale up the model. Thanks to architectural
142
      improvements like the skip connections (He et al., 2016), normalization layers Szegedy et al. (2015),
143

144   of magnitude has been made feasible. Furthermore, efficient scaling strategies such as compound
145   scaling in model width and depth, as well as input resolution (Tan & Le, 2019) have also emerged.
146   However, Equation 2 imposes a fundamental limitation on the prohibitive cost of scaling: doubling in
147   H, w, and l leads to 25 − 1 = 31× increase in model compute cost. The marginal accuracy gains
148   associated with model scaling shown in Table 1 further make it unappealing for many practical use
149   cases with limited compute budget.

151   2.3   M ODEL C OMPRESSION
152
153   It is well known that modern neural networks have significant redundancy, thus impacting both
154
      the model size (often measured by the number of parameters) and inference compute cost (often
      measured by MACs). This has motivated the design of compression strategies to reduce redundancy
155
      in the model. This has motivated the development of various compression strategies to reduce
156
      redundancy. Popular approaches include:
157
158          • Pruning: Ablating weights or neurons deemed less important for predictions. For example,
159            WDPruning (Yu et al., 2022a) reduces width based on saliency scores, while X-Pruner (Yu
160            & Xiang, 2023) measures a unit’s importance by its contribution to predicting each target
161            class. The underlying ideas of these models is that the redundancy in size and compute are
               coupled, and one reduces compute by removing the units.


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
                                   • Adaptive Computation: Employing mechanisms that dynamically adjust the computation
163                                  based on input complexity (Kag & Fedorov, 2023; Rao et al., 2021; Yin et al., 2022). These
164                                  methods often adopt some form of “early exit” mechanisms that reduce l in Equation 2. For
165                                  instance, techniques like DynamicViT (Rao et al., 2021) downsamples the number of tokens
166                                  adaptively to reduce compute cost, while A-ViT (Yin et al., 2022) introduces learned token
167                                  halting for ViT models so that not all tokens are processed by the full depth of the model,
168                                  thus effectively reducing the average depth of the compute graph during inference.
169
                                   • Quantization: This is a widely adopted technique that reduces the precision of model
170
                                     weights and activations, thereby decreasing memory usage and computational require-
171
                                     ments (Rokh et al., 2023). While quantization is not the focus of Little-Big, it is worth
172                                  noting that it can complement our framework to further reduce inference costs. For instance,
173                                  quantized versions of both Little and Big models could be used within the Little-Big frame-
174                                  work, offering compounded efficiency gains. However, it is also important to highlight
175                                  that the benefits of quantization are often hardware-dependent, relying on specific accel-
176                                  erators or processors that support low-precision computations. In contrast, Little-Big is
177                                  entirely hardware-independent, making it applicable across a wide range of deployment
178                                  environments without requiring specialized hardware.

180   2.4                         H UMAN V ISION
181
182   While parallel processing plays an essential role in making it possible to ingest gigabits/s of raw
183   visual information and compress it to tens of bits/s to guide our behavior (Soto et al., 2020; Zheng &
184   Meister, 2024), human vision is not a single-pass process. Human eyes have two distinct information
185   processing pathways that originate from two types of photoreceptors called rods and cones. While
186   the visual acuity in the cone-rich fovea is ∼ 1 arcmin, it only covers ∼ 2 degrees, or ∼ 0.01% of the
187   visual field (Rosenholtz, 2016). The rest of the ∼ 99.9% of the visual field is mostly dominated by
188   rods which provides much lower visual acuity (∼ 10 arcmin). A given small patch in the visual field
189
      either only gets processed in a single-pass by the low-acuity rod pathway, or followed by additional
      passes with high-acuity foveal vision if needed as directed by the saccade. Studies (Tang et al., 2018;
190
      Torralba, 2009) have shown that human visual classification performance adapts to variable compute
191
      budget.

321    G(x, T ) achieve very little accuracy drop or even slight accuracy boost across a wide range of T .

323    might immediately notice that any point on the curves is a valid G(x, T ) model, and adjusting T
       allows traversal along the full curve. Many points on certain curves, such as GB4,B7 (x, T ), achieves

351   families including CNNs, transformers, and hybrid models, across scales from 1 to 2700 GMACs.
352   Thanks to the model agnostic nature of Little-Big , it allows pairing up models of different families
353   (models in blue in Table 2). Large modes such as EfficientVit-L3-384 (Cai et al., 2023), DeiT3-L3-
354   384 (Touvron et al., 2022), ViT-H-14-518 (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) can be compressed without any
355   loss of accuracy by 70% to 80%.
356   It is worth noting that the roles of Big and Little in the Little-Big pair are relative. For example,
357   DeiT3-L-384 performs well as Little model, efficiently speeding up the 3B-parameter InternImage-
358   G-512 (Wang et al., 2022b) and 600M-parameter ViT-H-14-518 by 62% and 80%, respectively.
359   However, it does not imply that DeiT3-L-384 itself cannot act as the Big model and be sped up by an
360   even smaller model. In fact, Table 2 shows that it can be sped up by the EfficientViT-L2-288 by 71%.
361   Similarly, EfficientNet-B4 can serve as a strong Little model for compressing EfficientNet-B7, as well
362   as a Big model to be compressed by the smaller EfficientNet-B2. This enables the generalization of
363   Little-Big to a K-pass framework where K > 1 with additional performance gain (Appendix Section
364
      A.2). Another important observation is that the speed up depends on the distribution D: Relative
      MACs reduction are consistently lower on the slightly harder ImageNet-V2 than on ImageNet-1K.
365
      This suggests that one may measure distribution shift by measuring average MACs with Little-Big.
366
      Furthermore in Appendix A.3, we investigate a more sophisticated variant of the Little-Big approach,
367   termed Little-Big Ensemble, where predictions from the Little and Big models are combined using a
368   weighted average for hard samples. Our findings reveal that despite the added memory overhead of
369   storing the Little model’s predictions, Little-Big Ensemble does not yield significant improvements
370   in achieving lossless accuracy compared to the simpler Little-Big method.

462   While many multi-pass test-time augmentations (TTAs) (Shanmugam et al., 2021) that aggregate
463
      predictions of several augmented views of the same sample have been developed as a post-hoc add-on
      to improve performance, these methods go in the opposite direction of Little-Big . Fundamentally,
464
      K-pass TTAs are multiplicative methods that trades K× inference cost for slight improvement of
465
      accuracy. Little-Big is a subtractive multi-pass algorithm that relies on a good decomposition of
466   problems and solve each part with the least compute, not unlike Speculative Decoding in languange
467   modeling (Leviathan et al., 2023). What TTA and Little-Big share in common is that both are post-hoc
468   methods that do not require any re-training of the original models. It is actually possible to combine
469   both in the same inference pipeline much like human vision to make predictions adaptively.
470
      A potentially important contributing factor to the sub-optimal performance of popular adaptive
471
      compute models such as DynamicViT and A-ViT lies in their end-to-end training protocol. As the
472   number of tokens decrease over depth, the deeper layers effectively “see” less pixels during training.
473   This implicit coupling between the need to reduce compute cost at inference time and at training time
474   due to the end-to-end training protocol may hinder the potential of adaptive methods. In contrast,
475   Little-Big decouples inference and training compute costs, and uses models individually trained on
476   all pixels on full datasets.
477
      In language modeling, recent work such as Hybrid-LLM (Ding et al., 2024) shares the same principle
478   as Little-Big . Hybrid-LLM involves training a probabilistic router that connects a little transformer
479   and a big transformer. Like many other model compression methods, Hybrid-LLM trades accuracy
480   for inference speed. In contrast, the embarrassingly simple routing mechanism of Little-Big involves
481   choosing only one hyperparameter T , and achieves lossless speedup without losing accuracy.
482
483   Limitations It is worth noting that, while Little-Big achieves drastic MACs reduction across model
484   families and scales, the speed up is not free: since an additional Little model is needed to make the
485   Little-Big pair, the storage overhead is usually a small fraction of the storage requirement of the Big
      model. However, the storage overhead does not necessarily translate to memory overhead. Although


                                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      the example Pytorch pseudo code keeps both Big and Little models in memory at the same time to
487   minimize latency while increasing maximum memory usage, one can alternatively only load one
488   model into the memory at a time so that there is no overhead on maximum memory usage. Batching
489   predictions can further reduce the memory I/O overhead per sample.
490
491   Extensions It is possible to extend the same principles of Little-Big to other tasks by re-examining
492   and modifying Equation 1. For example, consider the task of video classification, where the input x
493   is updated as a sequence of images with an added time dimension t, x ∈ RC×W ×H×t . The inference
494   cost of a neural video classifier is given by:
495                                   M ACs[F (x)] ≈ CF ∗ H 2 ∗ t ∗ w2 ∗ l,                             (6)
496   which makes brute-force upscaling more costly because of the additional t dimension. However,
497   decomposition based on Equations 4 and 5 generalize to this task provided video classifiers are well
498   calibrated and video samples are decomposable by confidence. To demonstrate the generalizability of
499   Little-Big , we apply it to the Swin3D-B video classification model on Kinetics-400. The Swin3D-T +
500   Swin3D-B pair achieves 41% MACs reduction of Swin3D-B while maintaining 79.4% Top-1 accuracy
501   on Kinetics-400. For dense image prediction tasks like semantic segmentation, the prediction y
502   becomes a map, where y ∈ [0, 1]W ×H×N . In principle, one can continue perform decomposition
503
      on a per sample basis, but it may be more efficient to perform decomposition on a per pixel or
      per patch level. In Appendix A.1 we also demonstrate how Little-Big could also be extended to
504
      zero-shot multimodal classification, semantic segmentation, multi-label segmentation as well as for
505
      text classification
506
507
      Additional contextualization of novelty As the ML community continues to produce increasingly
508   large models with massive parameter counts, efficiently deploying them has become a significant
509   challenge. Current inference optimization strategies, such as quantization or distillation, often require
510   training new, smaller models or performing operations that result in performance drops. Additionally,
511   these techniques fail to fully leverage the extensive ecosystem of models that is readily available (e.g.,
512   the EfficientNet family or user-submitted models on platforms like Torch or HuggingFace Hub).
513
      In this context, we believe that our study on speeding up large models using smaller models is an
514   important direction of work. Though the proposed protocol is simple to implement, our work holds
515   significant practical value since (i) our approach completely post-hoc, requiring no re-training; (ii)
516   our approach is entirely model- and architecture-agnostic, allowing seamless integration with a
517   variety of models; and ours is the first work to systematically study and benchmark its utility in
518   improving inference efficiency across a variety of tasks and model architectures.
519
      By leveraging the growing diversity of pre-existing models across frameworks, Little-Big enables
520   users to mix and match architectures (e.g., pairing models from different families such as EfficientNet
521   and ViTs, BERTs and T5s). This flexibility ensures that one can adopt state-of-the-art models
522   without the burden of additional training or specialized pipelines, and obtain significant compute
523   savings and latency reductions without sacrificing accuracy. We argue that the framework’s simplicity,
524   adaptability, and compatibility with existing models make it a highly practical solution for real-world
525   use cases.
526
527   6    C ONCLUSION
528
529   In summary, we investigate how scaled-up models help base models correct their mistakes, show
530   that the former preferentially help with samples with low confidence predictions. Inspired by that,
531   we propose a simple two-pass Little-Big algorithm that selectively pass “difficult” samples to large
532   models, achieving drastic MACs reduction of up to 80% without sacrificing accuracy for a wide
533   range of model families and sizes.
534   The inefficiency in model scale-up and the effectiveness of Little-Big in compressing the scaled-up
535   models are two sides of the same coin, with effective decomposition of samples in a dataset being
536   the key to connecting the two sides. Despite being embarrassingly simple and surprisingly effective,
537   Little-Big is considered as a lower bound on how much a model can be compressed without losing
538   accuracy. More sophisticated ways to decompose the data distribution and better ways to use the
539   Little models output are promising directions to further improve the performance of such subtractive
      multi-pass algorithms.

788
789   To evaluate the applicability of the Little-Big framework in multi-label classification, we conducted
790   experiments using the CelebA dataset (Liu et al., 2015), which contains 40 attributes (classes). In this
791   setup, we used Vision Transformers (ViTs) pretrained on ImageNet: with ViT-B-32 as the "Little"
792
      model, and ViT-L-14 as the "Big" model. With the backbones frozen, we trained a classification
      head— comprising of two linear layers with ReLU activation and dropout on the CelebA dataset.
793
794   Since the multi-label classification is typically posed as k binary predictions for each sample, where
795   k is the number of classes, we obtain k logits per sample. A sigmoid function is then applied to obtain
796   the probabilities for each class. We compute the confidence for a class as confidence = |prob − 0.5|
797   measuring the distance from the decision boundary. To obtain an aggregated confidence for an image,
798
      the confidences across all k classes are averaged.
799   Using this aggregated confidence from the predictions of the Little model, we determine a threshold
800   T on a validation subset and pass the images whose aggregated confidence is below T to the big
801   model. Through the results, reported in Table 5, we demonstrate the effectiveness of the Little-Big
802   framework for multi-label classification, with minimal loss in F1-score while significantly reducing
803   computational costs.

      A.1.3    E XTENDING L ITTLE -B IG TO S EMANTIC S EGMENTATION
811
812   To evaluate the Little-Big framework in the context of semantic segmentation, we conducted exper-
813   iments using DeepLabv3 (Chen, 2017) with MobileNet (Howard, 2017) as the "Little" model and
814   FCN (Long et al., 2015) with ResNet-101 as the "Big" model. Both models were sourced from the
815   PyTorch repository and pretrained on a subset of the COCO dataset (Lin et al., 2014) that includes
816
      class names overlapping with Pascal VOC (Hoiem et al., 2009). These models were not fine-tuned
      further and evaluation was performed on the validation set.
817
818   In this setup, we first generated superpixels for each input image using the SLIC algorithm (Achanta
819   et al., 2012), producing compact regions that preserve spatial information. The image was then
820   passed through the Little model to obtain class-wise probabilities for each pixel. For each SLIC
821   region, we computed a region-level confidence by averaging the maximum softmax probability across
822
      all pixels in the region. These region-level confidences were further aggregated across the image
      to compute an image-level aggregated confidence, summarizing the overall certainty of the Little
823
      model’s predictions.
824
825   Based on the image-level aggregated confidence, a threshold T was applied and images with con-
826   fidence below T were passed to the Big model for further evaluation. This two-stage approach
827   leverages the efficiency of the little model while ensuring high-quality predictions via the big model
828
      when necessary. The results, detailed in Table 6, demonstrate that the Little-Big framework effectively
      balances computational efficiency with segmentation accuracy in this setting.
