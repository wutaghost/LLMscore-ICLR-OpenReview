011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The advent of large-scale foundation models has revolutionized EEG analysis;
014            however, their ability to generalize to Out-of-Distribution (OoD) brain signals re-
015
               mains limited due to the inherent variability in physiological states, individual
               differences, and experimental setups. To address these challenges, we introduce
016
               Beatrix, a novel spectral EEG foundation model that achieves state-of-the-art OoD
017
               generalization across diverse brain activity tasks. Beatrix leverages a unique ana-
018            lytic wavelet-based spectral tokenization that captures the intricate non-stationary
019            dynamics of EEG signals, and employs a semi-causal generative modeling ap-
020            proach during pre-training, enabling it to learn expressive latent representations
021            capable of both interpolation and extrapolation across temporal and frequency
022            domains. For fine-tuning, we propose an innovative Contrastive Invariant Fine-
023            Tuning (CIFT) method that enhances domain-invariant learning without the need
024            for explicit environment labels, thus significantly improving OoD generalizability
025            in a parameter-efficient manner. Our multi-view Transformer architecture further
026
               integrates both spectral and temporal information, allowing Beatrix to compre-
               hensively model EEG signals across channels. Extensive experiments demon-
027
               strate that Beatrix consistently outperforms existing EEG models in tasks such as
028
               seizure detection and forecasting, auditory neural decoding, motor imagery, and
029            sleep staging, showcasing its robustness and broad applicability. By achieving su-
030            perior performance with reduced fine-tuning costs, Beatrix represents a significant
031            advancement in the field of EEG foundation models.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The advent of modern neuroelectrophysiological techniques such as electroencephalography (EEG)
037   has revolutionized our capacity to monitor neural functions with high precision, offering unprece-
038   dented insights into brain activity. These advances are particularly crucial for diagnosing neuro-
039
      logical disorders and deepening our comprehension of brain function. Inspired by the success of
      foundation models in other domains, we have witnessed a surge of interest in developing analogous
040
      models for EEG analysis (Jiang et al.; Zhang et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024a;b; Wang et al., 2023;
041
      Yang et al., 2023). These EEG Foundation Models (EFMs), developed on time or time-frequency
042   (spectral) domain representation of raw brain records, exhibit the potential to markedly enhance
043   EEG-based applications such as neural decoding and brain-computer interfaces, and to refine diag-
044   nosis and treatment strategies for neurological conditions like epilepsy.
045
      Despite their promise, the development of general-purpose and domain-specific EFMs faces sig-
046
      nificant challenges due to the inherent complexity and variability of EEG signals. These signals
047
      are affected by a wide range of factors, such as age, cognitive state, eye movements, etc.. (Croce
048   et al., 2020). This variability is further amplified by differences in electrode setups and experi-
049   mental conditions across different institutions, making it difficult for EFMs to generalize well to
050   unseen data. Although efforts have been taken to promote robustness to distribution shifts in EEG
051   records, it is difficult to define and partition domains or environments, a necessity explicitly or
052   implicitly assumed for many OoD generalization techniques (Lai & Wang, 2024; Creager et al.,
053   2021), as the EEG signal is inherently nonstationary. This challenge is especially pronounced in
      epilepsy-related tasks, where the diversity of seizures and the high inter- and intra-subject variabil-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      ity in EEG recordings—spanning interictal, pre-ictal, and ictal phases—complicate generalization
055   effortss (Yuan et al., 2024b; Guerrini et al., 2023; Assogba et al., 2010). Furthermore, most exist-
056   ing EFMs rely primarily on temporal domain representation, with few models leveraging time-
057   frequency or spectral one. This potentially limits their ability to fully capture the complexity of
058   EEG data, particularly in tasks where pure time-domain approaches may fall short (Ma et al., 2021).
059
      While numerous large-scale, publicly accessible datasets support seizure detection research, a
060
      scarcity exists for the intricate cases of rare epilepsy types and seizure forecasting due to privacy
061   concerns. Consequently, many existing EFMs (Yuan et al., 2024a; Jiang et al.; Yuan et al., 2024b)
062   have relied on private data for pre-training and/or evaluation, complicating the advancement of fur-
063   ther research.
064
      To address these challenges, we present Beatrix, a pioneering spectral EFM designed for robust out-
065
      of-distribution (OoD) generalization. Beatrix is subjected to a two-stage pre-training process using
066
      the most extensive open-source EEG corpus to date, spectrally tokenizing EEG signals and disen-
067   tangling time-frequency and channel interactions to capture comprehensive spatiotemporal patterns.
068   Furthermore, we introduce a novel environment-aware fine-tuning method known as Contrastive
069   Invariant Fine-Tuning (CIFT). This method learns domain-invariant features without the need for
070   explicit environment information, thereby bolstering the downstream performance over challenging
071   benchmarks out-of-disease and out-of-institute seizure-related asks. CIFT achieves this by lever-
072   aging new information from the interaction between spectrally and temporally encoded prompts,
073   enhancing the model’s robustness to distribution shifts. Remarkably, our approach substantially
074   reduces computational costs compared to traditional full-parameter tuning. This improvement in
075
      generalization is also observed across a variety of OoD generalization baselines.

      OoD Generalization in EEG-Based Applications Out-of-Distribution (OoD) generalization in
149
      EEG-based biomedical applications is a significant challenge, particularly when dealing with hetero-
150   geneous data from diverse domains. The focus is on extracting consistent features across domains
151   while discarding misleading ones. Wang et al. propose data augmentation techniques to address
152   OoD scenarios (Wang et al., 2024b), while others enhance domain generalization through mutual
153   reconstruction strategies (Wang et al., 2022b) and mutual information-based methods (Jeon et al.,
154   2021). Yuan et al. present preprocessing techniques to improve the OoD generalizability of pre-
155   trained models (Yuan et al., 2024b).
156   EEG Foundation Models The development of foundational models for EEG has gained momen-
157   tum. Models like LaBraM (Jiang et al.), designed for general EEG analysis, and Brant (Zhang
158   et al., 2024), tailored for intracranial signals, have shown promise in seizure detection and fore-
159   casting. PPi (Yuan et al., 2024b), pre-trained on a large SEEG corpus, demonstrates robustness to
160   domain shifts and achieves state-of-the-art results in subject-independent seizure detection. Brant-
161   2, building on this, incorporates both stereo- and scalep-electroencephalography modalities during
      pre-training (Yuan et al., 2024a).

      Analytic Wavelet Spectral Analysis For effective EEG signal analysis, accurately capturing the
169
      complex, non-stationary dynamics of brain activity is essential, with time-frequency representation
170
      being key. Techniques like Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) and Continuous Wavelet Trans-
171   form (CWT) are utilized to achieve this balance. However, STFT faces limitations due to the fixed
172   trade-off between temporal and spectral resolutions, which can result in spectrogram leakage when
173   the window length is set, impacting the accuracy of the analysis (Wang et al., 2023). The CWT
174   addresses these limitations by decomposing the signal into a set of dilated and translated versions of
175   a predefined mother wavelet. This approach allows for a more favorable balance between temporal
176   and spectral resolutions compared to STFT (Arts & van den Broek, 2022).
177   In this work, we employ the Analytic Wavelet Transform (AWT) Lilly & Olhede (2010), a complex-
178   valued extension of CWT, to extract both magnitude and phase information from the time-scale or
179   time-frequency domain. This approach is especially beneficial for non-stationary signals, where
180   frequency content fluctuates over time. The AWT provides a more accurate estimation of instanta-
181   neous frequency and superior frequency reassignment properties than real-valued CWT and STFT.
182   Formally, the AWT of a signal f (t) with respect to a mother wavelet ψ(t) is defined as

      Spectral Tokenizer Previous studies (Jiang et al.; Cai et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024b) have in-
      vestigated time-domain EEG tokenization using techniques such as Vector-Quantized Variational
200
      Autoencoders (VQVAEs) and linear projections. We propose a spectral tokenizer that transforms
201
      raw EEG signals into a rich time-frequency representation using a Vector-Quantized Generative
202   Adversarial Network (VQGAN). This model employs a linear patch embedding layer followed by
203   a Transformer and convolutional downsampling layers, which efficiently reduce the input spectro-
204   grams into a lower-resolution latent space. A decoder is then trained to reconstruct the input from
205   the quantized latent vectors. The VQGAN encoder is used as the tokenizer. Further details are
206   provided in Appendix C.1.
207
      Temporal Tokenizer During the fine-tuning phase, we employ a convolutional neural network
208   to encode temporal features from raw EEG signals, which complements the spectral domain fea-
209   tures in our proposed contrastive fine-tuning approach. Unlike spectral tokenizer, this network is
210   uniquely trained-from-scratch for each specific downstream dataset. Further details are provided in
211   Appendix C.3.

213   3.2   M AIN A RCHITECTURE
214
215   Following recent work on foundation models, our network is based on Transformer. Previous EEG
      foundation models (Zhang et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024a; Jiang et al.) have primarily focused on


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      processing a single or fixed number of EEG channels. By contrast, Beatrix employs two distinct
217   Transformers. The first, termed the spectral Transformer, captures interactions among tokens
218   within the same channel. The second, known as the multiview Transformer, captures interactions
219   among tokens across different channels. Additionally, inspired by previous work such as (Shazeer,
220   2020; Nguyen et al., 2024), we introduce several minor modifications: 1) SwiGLU. We replace
221   the feedforward layer with SwiGLU (Shazeer, 2020), a gated linear unit with Swish nonlinearity to
222   improve network capacity and expressivity. 2) Feature Scaling. Transformer’s expressivity may
223   deteriorate due to the low-pass nature of attention, which causes oversmoothing issue where token
224   become identical as the depth grows (Nguyen et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2022a; Shi et al., 2022).
225
      Therefore, we adds a feature scaling layer (Nguyen et al., 2024) after multi-head self-attention.
      After being trained on the curated pre-training data corpus, the tokenizer is frozen and the main
226
      part of Beatrix is pretrained on the tokenized data. The tokenizer extract latent embeddings for
227
      each channel independently and leave modeling of the interchannel correlations to the main part of
228   Beatrix. More details about the main architecture can be found in Appendix C.2.

269   Contrastive Invariant Fine-Tuning We design Contrastive Invariant Fine-Tuning (CIFT) to adapt
      our spectrally pre-trained foundation model to downstream datasets with minimal additional train-


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      able parameters. CIFT leverages parameter-efficient low-rank adapters to reduce computational cost
271   while facilitating out-of-distribution (OoD) generalization.
272
273   At the core of CIFT is an automated prompt generation system powered by cross-attention mecha-
274
      nisms. This system devises continuous prompts based on token embeddings from both the spectral
      tokenizer and a complementary temporal tokenizer. The temporal tokenizer is equipped with induc-
275
      tive biases optimized for handling multiscale time-series data, ensuring that the prompts effectively
276
      capture the nuances of EEG signals across both spectral and temporal domains. These prompts are
277   instrumental in guiding the main tokens, which are derived from the time-frequency representation
278   of EEG data by the spectral tokenizer. The resulting embeddings are then utilized to meet the spe-
279   cific objectives of the downstream tasks. Furthermore, CIFT includes an automatic environment
280   partitioner that segments the training data into a predefined number of virtual environments. This
281   partitioning is achieved without relying on costly or privacy-sensitive domain annotation informa-
282   tion, which is commonly required by many OoD generalization algorithms.
283   The technical details of CIFT are provided as follows.
284
285   Parameter-efficient adaptors CIFT incorporates three distinct parameter-efficient adapters, draw-
286
      ing inspiration from parameter-efficient fine-tuning strategies employed in large language models.
      These adapters enable the model to maintain its pretrained parameters θ fixed while introducing only
287
      a few adaptable, low-rank parameters ∆θ into the existing architecture.
288
289   As shown in Figure 3 (a), our approach employs the following trainable modules: 1) Bottleneck
290   adapter (Houlsby et al., 2019): A low-rank multilayer perceptron (MLP) with ReLU activation is
291   integrated sequentially over the SwiGLU and attention modules. 2) LoRA adapter (Hu et al., 2021):
292
      It is implemented by inserting low-rank decomposition linear projection layers into the key-value
      projection layer of the attention modules parallelly. 3) Layer Normalization: The parameters of
293
      the normalization layers are made trainable to enhance adaptability during fine-tuning. Our empir-
294
      ical findings show that these adapters reduce the memory footprint and improve OoD performance
295   compared to full-parameter tuning while offering greater adaptability than linear probings (Kumar
296   et al., 2022; Alain & Bengio, 2018) for assessing large pre-trained models. The rank of bottleneck
297   and LoRA adapters, denoted by r, is a tunable hyperparameter.

323   yielded promising results through various fine-tuning strategies, we can further enhance it by ad-
      dressing potential biases in the training environments. As illustrated in Figure 3 (b), EEG data is

329

330

409   EnD (Ghaddar et al., 2021), DFA (Wang et al., 2024a), RUBI (Tian et al., 2022), and LfF (Nam
410   et al., 2020). Unlike our approach, these methods explicitly use subject identities as environment
411   labels to enhance domain generalization. We also incorporate contrastive algorithms known to
412   benefit OoD generalization that, which can perform spectrotemporal alignment like CIFT, includ-
413   ing InfoNCE (Harary et al., 2022), HSIC (Galstyan et al., 2022), SelfReg (Kim et al., 2021), and
414   RELIC (Mitrovic et al., 2020)
415   Evaluation metrics To comprehensively evaluate the experimental results, we use precision, recall,
416   F1- and F2-score as evaluation metrics. F2-score is adopted in critical applications that value in-
417   formation retrieval more than accuracy (i.e., accepting a relatively large number of false positives
418   but virtually guaranteeing that all the true positives are found). In the biomedical scenario, F2-
419   score is more valued than F1-score, since ignoring any seizure is costly in diagnosis. Other metrics,
420   including accuracy, AUCROC and AUCPR, are also reported. For tasks involving non-epileptic sub-
421
      jects, we employ metrics that are most sensitive to performance nuances and align with established
      research practices, which are detailed in their respective sections.

      5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
541
542
      In this paper, we introduce Beatrix, a pioneering EEG foundation model that demonstrates superior
543   out-of-distribution (OoD) generalization over current state-of-the-art (SOTA) models for a range of
544   seizure-related and non-seizure tasks, all with a significantly reduced fine-tuning cost. To bolster
545   Beatrix’s OoD generalization capabilities, we have developed CIFT, a novel contrastive invariance
546   learning technique. CIFT delivers substantial performance improvements by effectively inferring
547   environmental contexts and seamlessly integrating spectrotemporal information.
548
      While Beatrix is currently optimized for domain-specific applications, such as epilepsy monitor-
549   ing and forecasting, it has yielded promising results for both healthy individuals and those with
550   non-epileptic neurological conditions. Looking ahead, our future research will be directed towards
551   expanding the versatility of our approach. We aim to embrace an even broader array of downstream
552   tasks and to incorporate a variety of brain modalities, including MEG and fMRI, into a unified,
553   comprehensive generative multimodal foundation model. This advancement will not only amplify
554   its predictability and generalizability in real-world clinical settings.

1199   coding methods. s The model operates at a dimension of 768, utilizing convolution and transposed
1200   convolution layers with a kernel size of 4x4 and a stride of 2x2 for downsampling and upsampling,
1201   respectively. The embedding layer transforms 16x16 non-overlapping spectrogram patches using a
1202   linear layer.

1271   Beatrix is architected with a spectral Transformer composed of M layers of Transformer encoders
1272   and a multiview Transformer comprising N layers. The generative reconstruction of spectrograms
1273   is handled by a decoder consisting of P layers of Transformer blocks. The spectral Transformer op-
1274

       Transformer facilitates attention across tokens that share the same time-frequency location across
1275
       various channels. This design efficiently reduces computational expenses while capturing interchan-
1276
       nel correlations. It is important to note that the decoder operates independently for each channel and
1277   is not engaged during the fine-tuning phase. In addition, we use rotary positional encoding (Heo
1278   et al., 2024), which is more flexible than absolute positional encoding methods

       Further discussion about semi-causal generative pre-training Mainstream generative pre-
1284   training currently relies on the sequence modeling paradigm, wherein data from diverse modalities
1285   undergo tokenization to form sequential data. Within this framework, various sequence modeling
1286   methods have been developed.
1287
       Causal sequence modeling stands out for its robust capabilities in zero-shot generalization and in-
1288
       context learning, attributed to its high sample efficiency. This approach leverages the participation
1289
       of all tokens in prediction, thereby providing comprehensive supervision information. It has demon-
1290   strated state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance across domains such as general-purpose natural lan-
1291   guage generation, reasoning, decision-making, and has found applications in vision and audio mod-
1292   eling. On the other hand, non-causal sequence modeling, based on an encoder-decoder architecture,
1293   fosters transferability across tasks and modalities while achieving enhanced fine-tuning efficiency.
1294   Widely employed in language understanding, sentiment analysis in natural language processing, as
1295   well as image and video classification, segmentation, and reconstruction in vision tasks, this method
       has been the cornerstone of previous EEG foundation models. Semi-causal sequence modeling rep-


                                                        24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       resents a hybrid approach that combines elements of both causal and non-causal methods. Here, the
1297   model is tasked with predicting each token outside the prefix given all preceding tokens.
1298
1299   Prior work on EEG foundation models, exemplified by references such as (Jiang et al.; Zhang et al.,
1300
       2024; Yuan et al., 2024b), predominantly adopts non-causal sequence modeling. However, it is
       worth noting that the bidirectional attention mechanism utilized in non-causal modeling suffers from
1301
       a rank degeneration issue, where representations with degenerated ranks become indistinguishable
1302
       due to the absence of informative components. Conversely, unidirectional attention, while less ef-
1303   fective than its bidirectional counterpart in capturing global context information, offers a solution to
1304   the rank degeneration problem. Thus, we advocate for the adoption of semi-causal sequence mod-
1305   eling, which encompasses both autoregressive and non-autoregressive prediction of masked and
1306   subsequent tokens.

1308   C.3   T EMPORAL T OKENIZER
1309
1310   The temporal tokenizer used in CIFT is a one-dimensional convolutional neural network of 5 layers,
1311   each convolutional layer has a kernel size of 3 and a stride size of 2. The temporal tokenizer, similar
1312   to the spectral tokenizer, process different EEG channels independently. Through a linear projection
1313   layer, all the output feature embeddings are simply concatenated sequentially, which will be used to
1314   calculate temporal prompts through cross attention mechanism.

1316   D     T RAINING D ETAILS
1317
1318   To make the model adaptive to variable input signals, we select the context length of Beatrix uni-
1319   formly from L = {2s, 4s, 6s, 8s, 10s, 16s}. Within non-causal spans we mask 75% of the total
1320   tokens. For a sequence we sample M ∈ [0, L − 1] uniformly, L is the total length, and M is the
1321   length of the non-causal span. To make the training process simple, we designate the first M tokens
1322   for non-causal modeling, and the rest are used for causal modeling. The channel number of EEG
1323   samples is uniformly chosen between 1 and C, C is the maximal number of utilizable channels for
1324   each record. The model is pre-trained for a total of 100,000 steps on 4 GPUs (NVIDIA Tesla A100
1325
       40G). The optimizer is AdamW with β1 = 0.99, β2 = 0.999 and a weight decay rate of 1e-4 and
       initial learning rate of 1e-6. A cosine annealing scheduler is used with a total of 5,000 warm-up
1326
       steps and maximum learning rate of 1e-4. At fine-tuning stage, we use 100 warmup steps and a
1327
       maximum learning rate of 1e-5 on one GPU.

1340   E.1   DATA C OLLECTION AND C LEANSING
1341
1342   The diversity and quality of the data utilized significantly influence the training process and subse-
1343   quent performance on downstream tasks. Therefore, it is crucial to curate a comprehensive collection
1344   of data to train the foundation model on a wide array of datasets.
1345   During the pre-training stage, we gather a substantial amount of openly available neuroelectro-
1346   physiological signals, with a particular focus on EEG data. While EEG datasets are increasingly
1347   available online, large-scale datasets are still relatively scarce due to the costs associated with data
1348   recording and privacy concerns. To ensure a diverse and representative corpus that encompasses
1349   various subjects, diseases, and tasks, we carefully curate the collected datasets. Unlike previous
       works that predominantly rely on clinical resting state EEG records of patients and normal controls,


                                                         25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       we extensively gather data from a range of diseases and normal states, including resting state and
1351   task/event-evoked brain activities. Some datasets are allowed to be downloaded freely, others need
1352   registration.
1353
1354
       Uncensored pre-training corpora often contain numerous corrupted data records due to discrepancies
       in data recording and preprocessing conditions. To address this, we strictly exclude unusable or
1355
       corrupted data and harness about 32,900 hours of EEG data for pre-training. We checked the data
1356
       using EDFBrowser and MATLAB EEGLAB and excluded the unusable sessions manually before
1357   preprocessing. Sinc interpolation is used for resampling and Notch filter with a quality factor of 50
1358   and an order of 2 is used at 50 or 60 Hz.
1359
1360
       We ensure all data are anonymized and de-identified and store them in FIF data format using Python
       MNE to avoid loss of floating-point precision.
1361
1362   Given the lack of consensus on the sampling and segmentation of EEG data, during training, we load
1363   each data record into memory as a whole and randomly segment it into epochs of varying lengths.
1364   Furthermore, we resample the data to 256Hz and apply a 50 and/or 60 Notch filter to filter out utility
1365   frequency noise.
1366   To alleviate distributional bias in the pre-trained model, we rebalance the data distribution of the
1367   train dataloader based on the total number of subjects in each dataset.

1540   OoD Generalization and Fine-Tuning Various fine-tuning methods have been developed to ef-
1541   ficiently adapt pre-trained models to new tasks with minimal parameter adjustments. However,
1542   standard fine-tuning can compromise OoD generalizability (Li et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2023; Kumar
1543
       et al., 2022; Wortsman et al., 2022; Andreassen et al., 2021). Salman et al. demonstrate that fine-
       tuning can degrade pre-trained features, adversely affecting OoD performance (Salman et al., 2020).
1544
       New techniques have emerged to counteract these effects (Lee et al., 2022; Wortsman et al., 2022;
1545
       Kumar et al., 2022). Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) strategies, such as those illustrated by
1546   Lee et al. and Kim et al., effectively mitigate distribution shift issues (Lee et al., 2023; Kim et al.).
1547   Empirical evidence suggests that parameter-efficient fine-tuning, often employing adaptors (Sung
1548   et al., 2022; He et al., 2021; Houlsby et al., 2019), enhances OoD generalization, especially when
1549   downstream data is limited (Chen et al., 2024; Goyal et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2022). Prompt tun-
1550   ing, a variant of PEFT, introduces flexible trainable prompts for multi-modal extensions (Li et al.,
1551   2024; Samadh et al., 2023; Shu et al., 2022).
