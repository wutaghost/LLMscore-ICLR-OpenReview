009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have become indispensable in numerous real-
012            world applications. Unfortunately, fine-tuning these models at scale, especially in
013            federated settings where data privacy and communication efficiency are critical,
014            presents significant challenges. Existing methods often resort to parameter-efficient
015            fine-tuning (PEFT) to mitigate communication overhead, but this typically comes
016            at the cost of model accuracy. To address these limitations, we propose federated
017            full-parameter tuning at scale for LLMs (Ferret), the first first-order method with
018            shared randomness to enable scalable full-parameter tuning of LLMs across decen-
019
               tralized data sources while maintaining competitive model accuracy. Ferret accom-
               plishes this through three aspects: (1) it employs widely applied first-order methods
020
               for efficient local updates; (2) it projects these updates into a low-dimensional
021
               space to considerably reduce communication overhead; and (3) it reconstructs
022            local updates from this low-dimensional space with shared randomness to facilitate
023            effective full-parameter global aggregation, ensuring fast convergence and compet-
024            itive final performance. Our rigorous theoretical analyses and insights along with
025            extensive experiments, show that Ferret significantly enhances the scalability of
026            existing federated full-parameter tuning approaches by achieving high computa-
027            tional efficiency, reduced communication overhead, and fast convergence, all while
028            maintaining competitive model accuracy.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Recently, Large Language Models (LLMs) have become indispensable tools across a wide range of
034   real-world applications, from natural language processing tasks like translation (Xu et al., 2024) and
035   summarization (Van Veen et al., 2024) to more complex tasks such as code generation (Liu et al.,
036   2024) and decision-making systems (Shao et al., 2023). The immense scale and versatility of LLMs
037   make them highly valuable in practice, but they also introduce significant challenges, particularly
038   when they are fine-tuned in federated settings. Federated Learning (FL) offers a decentralized
039
      approach to fine-tuning LLMs while retaining data on local clients to ensure privacy. However, while
      this approach effectively addresses privacy concerns, it also results in prohibitive communication
040
      overhead when the model parameters of LLMs scale to billions.
041
042   One of the straightforward strategies to mitigate the prohibitive communication costs in the federated
043   tuning of LLMs is parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT). PEFT methods (Hu et al., 2022; Wei et al.,
044   2024) focus on fine-tuning only a subset of model parameters, which is able to significantly reduce
045
      the communication overhead between clients and a central server (Che et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023;
      Kuang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b). Despite the effectiveness in reducing bandwidth usage, this
046
      type of approach often compromises model accuracy (Pu et al., 2023), as fine-tuning a subset of
047
      model parameters may fail to fully capture the nuances of local data distributions. Thus, recent efforts,
048   e.g., FedKSeed (Qin et al., 2024), have been devoted to utilizing zeroth-order optimization (ZOO)
049   (Nesterov & Spokoiny, 2017; Berahas et al., 2022) in federated full-parameter tuning of LLMs, aiming
050   to maintain competitive model accuracy while reducing the communication overhead by transmitting
051   only thousands of scalar gradients per round between clients and a central server. Unfortunately, this
052   approach often suffers from its poor scalability, including increased computational cost per round
053   and a larger number of communication rounds required for convergence, compared to FL methods
      that use first-order optimization (FOO), e.g., FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017).

054
      To this end, we propose federated full-parameter tuning at scale for LLMs (Ferret), the first first-order
055
      FL method with shared randomness to enable scalable federated full-parameter tuning of LLMs with
056
      compelling computational efficiency, reduced communication overhead, and fast convergence speed,
057   all while maintaining competitive model accuracy. Ferret achieves this through three aspects: First, it
058   employs widely applied first-order methods to perform computationally efficient local updates on each
059   client, which typically requires fewer iterations to achieve the same local update process compared to
060   existing ZOO-based FL. Next, Ferret projects these updates into a low-dimensional space, leading
061   to a significantly reduced communication cost compared to existing FOO-based FL. Finally, Ferret
062   reconstructs local updates from the low-dimensional space with shared randomness for effective full-
063   parameter global aggregation, ensuring fast convergence and competitive model accuracy compared
064   to existing ZOO-based FL. We further complement Ferret with rigorous theoretical analyses and
065
      principled insights, showing the theoretical advantages of Ferret over other baselines and guiding the
      best practices for its implementation. Finally, through extensive experiments, we verify that Ferret
066
      significantly outperforms existing methods with superior scalability and competitive model accuracy,
067
      making it a desirable solution for deploying LLMs in large-scale federated environments.
068
069   To summarize, our contributions in this work include:
070    • We propose Ferret, the first first-order FL approach with shared randomness (to the best of our
071      knowledge), which significantly enhances the scalability of federated full-parameter tuning of
072      LLMs while maintaining competitive model accuracy.
073    • We present rigorous theoretical analyses and insights to support the effectiveness of our Ferret,
074      demonstrating its theoretical advantages over other baselines and guiding its best practices.
075    • Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate that Ferret consistently improves over existing
076      methods in practice, offering both superior scalability and competitive model accuracy.

133     3.1     OVERVIEW OF F ERRET
134
        To achieve scalable LLM federated full-parameter tuning, our Ferret algorithm combines the strengths
135
        of both first-order FL, which offers efficient computation and fast convergence, and zeroth-order
136
        FL, which reduces communication overhead. Specifically, Ferret (a) follows first-order FL to apply
137     first-order optimization methods for local updates on clients, ensuring both computational efficiency
138     and fast convergence, and (b) draws inspiration from zeroth-order FL by projecting updates into a
139     low-dimensional space using random bases that can be regenerated using shared randomness among
140     clients for the reconstruction of these updates, thereby reducing communication overhead.
141

      With the theoretical results above, we summarize the scalability of Ferret and compare it to existing

361
362   Computation Per Round. Of note, Ferret enjoys a computational complexity of O(τ1 T ) for any
363   client i ∈ [N ] per round, where τ1 is the per-iteration complexity of the first-order update (including
364   forward and backward passes) in (3), and T is the number of local iterations. This is comparable to
365
      the well-established FedAvg. In contrast, both FedZO and FedKSeed incur a complexity of O(τ0 K),
      with τ0 being the per-iteration complexity of the zeroth-order update (i.e., forward pass) and K
366
      representing the number of forward passes. As first-order updates use more accurate gradients, T will
367
      be smaller than K (i.e., T ≪ K) to attain the same local update progress. Although τ1 can be at most
368   twice τ0 , our Ferret is still more computationally efficient than FedZO and FedKSeed (see Sec. 5).

373   FedAvg and FedZO, which have a communication complexity of O(d) due to their need to transmit

      A PPENDIX A         R ELATED W ORK
703
704
705   Federated PEFT for LLMs. The field of federated learning (FL) has gained significant traction
706   in its application to the fine-tuning of large language models (LLMs). Traditional FL approaches
707   in this domain (Zhang et al., 2023; Kuang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a; Kuang et al., 2023)
708
      have predominantly focused on parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) techniques (Hu et al., 2022;
      Wei et al., 2024; Lester et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024), which reduce the number of
709
      trainable parameters in LLMs to mitigate the extensive communication overheads in FL scenarios.
710
      Unfortunately, while PEFT methods such as those proposed in (Kuang et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
711   2024a) have shown promise, they often fall short in achieving the accuracy levels possible with
712   full-parameter tuning (Pu et al., 2023), particularly in non-IID (non-independent and identically
713   distributed) data settings commonly encountered in FL. In contrast, this paper focuses on federated
714   full-parameter tuning of LLMs, aiming to achieve significantly reduced communication overhead
715   while maintaining competitive model accuracy.
716
      Federated Learning with Shared Randomness. Several approaches leveraging shared randomness
717   have been proposed to enhance communication efficiency in FL. Methods including (Qin et al., 2024;
718   Xu et al., 2023; Maritan et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023; Dorfman et al., 2023; Zelikman et al., 2023;
719   Rahimi et al., 2024) demonstrate that by transmitting only a limited set of random seeds and scalar
720   gradients, communication overhead can be drastically reduced. However, these methods rely on
721   zeroth-order optimization (ZOO) for their local updates on each client. This reliance often results in
722   poor scalability, as these methods require substantial computational costs per round to achieve the
723   same local update progress and a larger number of communication rounds to converge compared
724   with their first-order counterparts, such as FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017) and FedProx (Li et al.,
725
      2020). This limitation therefore becomes a bottleneck in large-scale federated environments. In
      contrast, our paper introduces the use of shared randomness within first-order FL, aiming to improve
726
      both computational and communication-round efficiency of zeroth-order FL. To the best of our
727
      knowledge, this is the first time that shared randomness has been introduced in first-order FL to
728   reduce communication overhead.
