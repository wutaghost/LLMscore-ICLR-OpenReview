009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Transformer-based LLMs achieve great success on a variety of NLP tasks, in-
012            cluding machine translation, text summarization, and text generation. However, it
013            requires huge amount of computation and data to train such a powerful LLM. Re-
014            searchers have proposed transformer-based conditional computation algorithms
015            that significantly reduce redundant computations on certain tokens. By skipping
016            dense attention and feed forward computations, these approaches yield sparse
017            LLMs. However, these sparse LLMs are trained from scratch, requiring sub-
018
               stantial computation and data. Therefore in this paper, we proposed a training
               paradigm that can effectively transform a dense transformer-based LLM to its
019
               sparse variant with very limited computation resources and merely millions of
020
               tokens. We conducted thorough investigations into the key factors that may influ-
021            ence the dense-to-sparse transformation through numerous empirical experiments.
022            In addition, we conducted a case study on the how the tokens skip layers and ana-
023            lyzed their Part-of-Speech tags, gaining valuable insights.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Transformer-based large language models (LLMs) have been widely used in various application do-
029   mains due to their great performances. In the near future, LLMs are expected to be deployed on
030   mobile devices, enhancing the lives of more people. However, a vanilla transformer-based LLM
031   requires huge amount of data during the training process and its inference is also computation-
032   intensive and expensive. To this end, researchers investigated a number of transformer variants Ki-
033
      taev et al. (2020); Zhu et al. (2021); Tay et al. (2022); Pope et al. (2023); Cui et al. (2024) to reduce
      the training computation and accelerate the inference.
034
035   One of the widely used method is conditional computation, which is presented by Bengio (Bengio,
036   2013). The primary goal of this method is to reduce unnecessary computation. In fact, the hypothesis
037   behind this method is that not all tokens contribute equally to the generation process. This aligns
038   with how humans use language. In many languages, people first consider core words and then add
039
      auxiliary words while speaking. The main challenge lies in identifying which token computations
      are necessary and which are not. To this end, many algorithms are developed to determine when
040
      and how much computation should be used in the training and inference (Bengio et al., 2015; Jernite
041
      et al., 2016). Instead of processing a token from the input layer all the way to the output layer, an
042   advanced algorithm is used to determine if a token can exit the computation early in the middle
043   layers (Elbayad et al., 2020; Elhoushi et al., 2024). Recently, another algorithm MoD (Raposo et al.,
044   2024) is provided to dynamically allocate the computation across different tokens. In particular, at
045   a certain layer, a router is employed to decide whether a token can bypass the dense attention and
046   feed forward computation.
047
      The MoD-driven model skipped 87.5% of tokens in its optimal configuration while maintaining
048   nearly the same loss as its dense counterpart. However, The MoD models are trained from scratch,
049   which still requires huge amount of computation. Is there a method to obtain a sparse variant of a
050   dense transformer-based LLM without requiring such heavy computation? In this paper, we answer
051   this question by studying a different training paradigm, i.e., transforming a dense LLM into a sparse
052   LLM using very limited data and computational resources, rather than training a sparse LLM from
053   scratch. This paradigm relies on the existing LLM weights, significantly reducing the need for
      training data and computational resources.

054
      Specifically, we leverage LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) to perform continual pre-training on a small pro-
055   portion of parameters, transforming an existing dense transformer-based LLM to a relatively sparse
056   LLM with only a few hours of training on merely millions of tokens. Unlike MoD that pre-defines a
057   ratio of how much tokens to skip the layers per sentence, our paradigm skip the layers adaptively so
058   that a pre-defined KL divergence (Hershey & Olsen, 2007) to the base model can be maintained. We
059   also apply binary gates on the hidden states instead of weights, as the latter may cause distortion.
060   The binary gates are specially designed to allow gradients to pass through.
061   The empirical results show that we can skip the layers of an LLM at a relative low performance cost.
062   In addition, we performed a thorough analysis for the proposed training paradigm, examining the
063   factors that may affect the layer skip rate and model performance. Furthermore, we explored how
064   tokens skip the layers in an example and gained insights from their Part-of-Speech (POS) tags.
065
      To sum up, the main contributions of this paper are as follows:
066
067          • We presented a layer skip algorithm that dynamically allocate computation on different
068            tokens.
069
             • We proposed a training paradigm transforming a dense transformer-based LLM to its sparse
070
               variant with limited computation on merely millions of tokens.
071
072          • We performed a thorough analysis on the proposed paradigm and investigate the factors
073            that influence the dense-to-sparse transformation.

075   2   R ELATED W ORK
076
077
      Transformer-based architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) has been the cornerstone of LLMs. Accord-
078   ing to Guo (2024), LLM performance scales linearly with the log scale of compute. Hence, training
079   a sophisticated LLM requires a huge amount of FLOPs. This has inspired researchers to explore
080   more efficient transformer architectures (Tay et al., 2022; So et al., 2021). In 2013, Bengio (2013)
081   introduced a concept conditional computation which refers to a learned and optimized mechanism
082   that drops out unnecessary compute on some parameters. The concept is explored in depth after-
083   wards (Bengio et al., 2015; Graves, 2016; Jernite et al., 2016).
084   In recent years, a number of conditional computation methods have been developed for the trans-
085   former architecture, that unevenly allocate computation across tokens, thereby saving computation
086   on certain tokens. These methods can be roughly categorized into three groups: 1) “early exit” (El-
087   bayad et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021; Schuster et al., 2022; Elhoushi et al., 2024), which dynamically
088   ends the computation for tokens and skips the remaining transformer layers, 2) “layer skip” (Raposo
089   et al., 2024), which allows a token to skip continuous or discontinuous middle layers, thereby accel-
090   erating forward pass, and 3) “layer branching” (Ainslie et al., 2023), which enables tokens to switch
091   between a light and a heavy branch. Lei et al. (2023) proposed a conditional layer that includes two
092
      primary components: a parallel adaptor, as detailed by He et al. (2021), which processes all input
      tokens, and a pre-trained transformer layer that is specifically designed to process a selected subset
093
      of k tokens. The conditional layer enables the conversion of a dense pre-trained model into a sparse
094
      model by training on a few parameters.
095
096   However, it might require training, either in “pre-training from scratch” mode or “continual pre-
097   training” mode, on billions of tokens to enable the “early exit” mechanism while maintaining the
098
      utility of LLMs. The “layer skip” mechanism (Raposo et al., 2024) is investigated solely in the
      “pre-training from scratch”mode, which is both time-consuming and expensive. “layer branch-
099
      ing” requires a well-designed light-weight branch. These inspire us to investigate a mechanism for
100
      achieving “layer skip” LLMs with continual pre-training on merely million of tokens via sequential
101   adaptor LoRA (Hu et al., 2021).

      3.1   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
130
131   In transformer, a sequence of tokens are first converted to the corresponding word embeddings
132   h0 ∈ RC×d by the embedding layer, where C is the number of tokens in the context and d is the
133   hidden dimension. These embeddings are then passed through a stack of N layers {L1 , L2 , ..., LN },
134   where each layer Li produces its output hi with the output hi−1 from the previous layer as input:
135
                                                 hi = Li (hi−1 )                                           (1)
136
137   Finally, the last layer hidden states hN are passed to a task-specific head for down-stream tasks.
138   Inside each layer Li , there is an attention layer followed by a feed forward layer, each accompanied
139   with a normalization layer and a residual connection. Since our work focus on pre-norm transform-
140   ers, where the normalization occurs before the residual addition, we can use Ai to represent the
141   sub-block of the attention layer with its preceding normalization and Fi to represent the sub-block
142   of the feed forward layer with its preceding normalization:
143
144
                            Li (hi−1 ) = hi−1 + Ai (hi−1 ) + Fi (hi−1 + Ai (hi−1 ))                        (2)
145
146   To make the transformer relatively sparse, a layer specific router Ri is introduced to decide whether
147   tokens can skip the computation of layer Li . Binary gates gi ∈ {0, 1}C are generated for each token
148
      in the sequence based on their hidden states hi−1 from the previous layer:
149                                              gi = Ri (hi−1 )                                           (3)
150
151
      With binary gates, the hidden states will not be distorted by the weights.
152   Unlike in MoD, where the weight generated by the router is applied to the whole layer block output,
153   gates here are applied to each of the individual attention sub-block and the feed forward sub-block
154   as shown in Figure 1. Suppose a value of 0 for gik means the k-th token will be skipped for the
155   computation in layer Li . The gated output of the attention sub-block is:
156                                 gi · (hi−1 + Ai (hi−1 )) + (1 − gi ) · hi−1                            (4)
157
158   which can be simplified to:
159                                           hi−1 + gi · Ai (hi−1 )                                       (5)
160   Similarly, the gated output of the feed forward sub-block, which is also the output of the layer:
161
                      Li (hi−1 ) = hi−1 + gi · Ai (hi−1 ) + gi · Fi (hi−1 + gi · Ai (hi−1 ))               (6)

      4.2   S ETUP
217
218
      We selected the instruction-tuned version of Gemma 2B (Team et al., 2024) from Google as the
219   subject for continual pre-training in the experiment. In particular, we train for 1 epoch and set the
220   batch size to 4, learning rate to 1e-5 and threshold to 1e-4. The maximum sequence length is set to
221   288, 576 and 1,152 in different setups.
222
      We apply LoRA on all linear layers of the transformer, including the router. The rank is set to 64,
223
      and the trainable parameters take around 3% of the total parameters. The frozen base transformer
224
      is quantized to 4-bit as in QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023). The pre-training takes about 6 hours for
225   every 1M tokens on one A100 GPU.

245   4.4   M AIN R ESULTS
246
247   We first trained the gemma-1.1-2b-it using sampled datasets with varying number of rows and max-
248   imum context length to explore whether it is possible to transform a dense transformer-based LLM
249   to a more sparse and efficient variant. The results are shown in figure 2, where the x-axis represents
250   the training data size and the y-axis indicates the MC score.
251
      We observed that it is feasible to perform such transformation with limited data and computation as
252   MC scores can be narrowed down to a low interval given optimal hyper-parameter configurations.
253   For example, the MC scores of the second green point and third yellow point are around 0.2, which
254   means we only lose 20% of a performance unit to gain a full unit of skip. Conversely, poor hyper-
255   parameter configurations can result in low MC scores, making it cost-ineffective to perform such
256   transformation.
257
      We also noted that the 90K training data yielded the lowest MC scores in different context settings.
258   However, merely increasing the size of the training data does not always result in good transfor-
259   mations. The optimal training data size varies across different lines. This does not align well with
260   the empirical scaling law (Kaplan et al., 2020). One possible reason for this is that our continual
261   pre-training aims to approximate the distribution of the base model gemma-1.1-2b-it, whereas the
262   pre-training focuses on learning the distribution in the pre-training data. The distribution of the
263   SlimPajama subset may differ from that of learned distribution of the base model. In this case, it is
264   difficult to fit more data given the LKL restriction.
265   The results revealed that context length might slightly influence the MC scores. In particular, the
266   average MC scores of the three lines context-288, context-576, and context-1152 are 0.768, 0.804,
267   and 0.794, respectively. The short context-288 achieved the best average MC scores while the middle
268   context-576 achieve the best optimal MC score. The long context-1152 did not further decrease the
269   MC score. The likely reason is that the attention calculation for long samples is more affected by
      token skipping than for short samples.

472   We inspect which layers are skipped for the tokens generated from a MT-Bench question and have
473   some interesting findings. The left of Figure 7 shows a small sample from the generation. We
474   find that the skips are most likely to occur on tokens that do not contain much information, e.g.,
475   punctuation marks, space and articles. We also find that if a token start skipping a certain layer, it
476
      is likely to skip for more than one layer, and in many cases it will skip adjacent layers. Based on
      the analysis in Section 4.9, the hidden states might contain some information to indicate its non-
477
      significance that is recognizable by several routers, causing the token to skip more than one layer.
478
479   The right of Figure 7 shows how tokens of each POS tag skip each layer. Numbers are more likely
480   to skip layers than any other POS tags, which indicates for Gemma 2B we are studying, they will
481   not have much influence on the generation. This explains why Gemma 2B is performing badly on
482
      math as it is not sensitive to numbers. In contrast, conjunctions are most unlikely to skip layers. The
      reason is that they affect the trend of the generation, a small error could lead to significant deviation.
483
484   Figure 8 shows the per layer percentage of tokens skipping a layer out of all the tokens. We can see
485   the middle layers have the highest percentage, while none of the tokens skip layer 0, 1, 2, 16 and
      17. A possible reason could be that the first and last few layers are operating on hidden states that

524   5            C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this paper, we propose a training paradigm that effectively transforms a dense transformer-based
527   large language model (LLM) into a relatively sparse LLM. This approach avoids the massive com-
528   putation and data requirements needed to train a sparse LLM from scratch. Additionally, we utilize
529
      the parameter-efficient training method LoRA to significantly reduce trainable parameters and thus
      accelerate the continual pre-training process. The empirical experiments demonstrate the feasibility
530
      and effectiveness of the proposed transformation paradigm. We also investigate different configu-
531
      rations for dense-to-sparse transformation, benefiting the transformation on other LLMs. The case
532   study offers a visual analysis of tokens that are often skipped by routers. Moreover, POS tag analysis
533   of skipped tokens provides deeper insights into the types of tokens that are likely to be skipped.
