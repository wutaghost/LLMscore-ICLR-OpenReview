                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Deep clustering – joint representation learning and latent space clustering – is a
010
               well studied problem especially in computer vision and text processing under the
011            deep learning framework. While the representation learning is generally differ-
012            entiable, clustering is an inherently discrete optimization task, requiring various
013            approximations and regularizations to fit in a standard differentiable pipeline. This
014            leads to a somewhat disjointed representation learning and clustering. Recently,
015            Associative Memories were utilized in the end-to-end differentiable ClAM cluster-
016            ing scheme (Saha et al., 2023). In this work, we show how Associative Memories
017            enable a novel take on deep clustering, DClAM, simplifying the whole pipeline and
018            tying together the representation learning and clustering more intricately. Our exper-
019
               iments showcase the advantage of DClAM, producing improved clustering quality
               regardless of the architecture choice (convolutional, residual or fully-connected) or
020
               data modality (images or text).

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   The goal of clustering is to find coherent groups in a dataset. It is an important unsupervised learning
026   task, and given the generality of the task, many different methods have been proposed for effective
027   clustering (Xu & Tian, 2015; Zaki & Meira Jr, 2020). At a technical level, clustering critically
028   relies on a notion of (pairwise) distance (or similarity) to distinguish pairs of data samples as being
029   “similar” or “different”, and the insights from clustering can be unintuitive or misleading without
030   such a meaningful distance. When dealing with numerical data S ⊂ Rd with d dimensions, metrics
031   such as Euclidean distance are commonly used. Nevertheless, even with numerical data and an
032   appropriate notion of distance, increasing data dimensionality (that is, increasing d) makes clustering
033
      computationally hard as well as conceptually difficult since the separation between similar pairs and
      dissimilar ones can start to vanish (Verleysen & François, 2005; Steinbach et al., 2004; Assent, 2012).
034
035   In various domains, both these problems manifest – first, the raw representation of samples can be
036   extremely high dimensional (consider the number of pixels in an image, or the number of words in
037   a vocabulary for a bag-of-words representation of documents); second, while we have an ambient
038   representation, standard notions of vector distances (such as Euclidean) do not necessarily make
039
      sense – for example, Euclidean distance based on pixels can be large between an image and a slightly
      shifted version of it, which can be problematic if the content of an image is translation or rotation
040
      invariant.
041
042   One effective approach to handle these challenges is through deep clustering (Zhou et al., 2024),
043   where the goal is to both learning a low dimensional latent space where standard distance metrics
044   are meaningful, and to cluster or group the points at the same time. For the latent representations to
045
      be faithful to the original samples, deep clustering ensures that there is no significant information
      loss in the latent space, leading to the common use of autoencoders (AEs) (Rumelhart et al., 1985;
046
      Baldi, 2012; Bank et al., 2023) that learn latent representations (via an encoder) which can be
047
      used to reconstruct the original samples (via a decoder). The goal of deep clustering is to discover
048   the cluster structure in the latent space while ensuring low reconstruction loss. This is a widely
049   studied problem, especially in image datasets (Caron et al., 2018; Chang et al., 2017). While an
050   autoencoder is usually differentiable, standard clustering schemes (such as k-means (MacQueen,
051   1967) or agglomerative (Johnson, 1967)) are inherently discrete methods, since hard clustering (where
052   each sample is only assigned to a single cluster) is a discrete optimization problem. To incorporate it
053   in a differentiable deep learning pipeline, clustering is often “softened” by allowing samples to be
      partially assigned to multiple clusters, although various “regularizations” push the soft assignments to

054
      match hard assignments approximately (Xie et al., 2016; Guo et al., 2017a). The recent ClAM (Saha
055   et al., 2023) algorithm handles the dichotomy between hard assignments and differentiability via the
056   use of associative memories, yielding an end-to-end differentiable clustering approach. Nevertheless,
057   ClAM works only in the ambient d-dimensional data space, and is not designed to learn effective
058   lower dimensional latent representations, which poses challenges when clustering high-dimensional
059   data.
060
      As noted above, deep clustering tackles the joint objective of learning a good latent representation
061   where the points also cluster well. Whereas minimizing reconstruction loss is a prerequisite for deep
062   representation learning, one option for clustering in latent space is to first pretrain an autoencoder
063   to minimize the reconstruction loss, and then to freeze this latent space. Next, one can apply some
064   clustering scheme to group the points in that (frozen) space. Many AE based existing deep learning
065   methods adopt this scheme by either freezing both the encoder and decoder, or freezing only the
066   decoder (Xie et al., 2016; Guo et al., 2017b; 2021; Chazan et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2023).
067   In this paper, we consider a new approach that fine-tunes the autoencoder (i.e., both the encoder
068   and decoder) so that it modifies the latent space along with the task of finding clusters, while also
069   ensuring end-to-end differentiabilty via the use of associative memories (significantly extending
070   (Saha et al., 2023)). Our key insight and contribution is that we seamlessly combine the clustering
071   and reconstruction loss objectives into one expression that tackles the task of clustering-guided
072   latent representations, whereas previous deep clustering methods considered these separately. Our
073   work makes the following contributions:
074   • We propose DClAM, which uses associative memories to formulate a novel joint loss function that
075     simultaneously learns effective representations and clusters in the latent space, resulting in our
076     simplified deep clustering formulation.
077   • We conduct a thorough evaluation on image and text datasets, demonstrating that DClAM signifi-
078     cantly improves the clustering quality over both traditional (in ambient space) and deep clustering
079     (in latent space) baselines.
080   • We show that DClAM retains superior representation quality as measured by the reconstruction
081     loss; it is also agnostic to the encoder/decoder architecture choice.

083   2    R ELATED W ORK
084
085   Clustering is a long-studied and well-reviewed problem in computer science, with various formula-
086   tions and several applications (Kaufman & Rousseeuw, 2009; Zaki & Meira Jr, 2020). Given the
087   success of deep learning, deep clustering has also attracted attention over the past decade (Ren et al.,
088   2024; Aljalbout et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2024). Inspired by t-SNE (Van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008),
089   Xie et al. (2016) introduced DEC, enhancing clustering and feature representation by minimizing
090   the Kullback-Leibler Divergence (KLD) to an auxiliary target distribution. However, a drawback
091   is abandoning the decoder layer after pre-training, impacting the embedded space and clustering
092   performance. Guo et al. (2017a) showed that keeping the decoder layer improves clustering (IDEC),
093
      and Guo et al. (2017b) proposed DCEC using convolutional autoencoders (CAE). Chazan et al. (2019)
      proposed DAMIC, a mixture of autoencoders for clustering, determined by minimizing the recon-
094
      struction loss without needing a regularization term. However, they leverage multiple AEs in their
095
      model, while we focus on schemes using a single AE. Huang et al. (2023) introduced an innovative
096   embedded autoencoder architecture by incorporating it into both the encoding and decoding units of
097   the outer autoencoder. Guo et al. (2021) proposed DEKM which works on the embedding space (after
098   pretraining) and transforms it to a new cluster-friendly space using an orthonormal transformation
099   matrix. However, discarding the decoder after pretraining for both of these methods may lead to the
100   distortion of the embedded space, consequently hurting clustering performance. In addressing the
101   automatic inference of the number of clusters in a dataset, Ronen et al. (2022) introduced DeepDPM.
102   They proposed a novel loss inspired by EM in the Bayesian Gaussian Mixture Model framework,
103   facilitating a new amortized inference in mixture models. It is worth noting that DeepDPM diverges
104
      from the typical encoder-decoder architecture, opting instead for a multilayer perceptron model.
105   While many deep clustering methods utilize KLD as a clustering objective, it falls short in preserving
106   the global data structure (which implies that only within-cluster distances are prioritized, leaving
107   uncertainties regarding between-cluster similarities), leading Oskouei et al. (2023) (EDCWRN) to
      advocate for cross-entropy over KLD. They incorporate feature weighting to emphasize essential


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      features for clustering and employ a neighborhood technique to encourage similar representations for
109   samples within the same cluster. Addressing another challenge with KLD regarding the presence of
110   hard, misclassified samples, Cai et al. (2022) introduced focal loss to enhance label assignment in
111   deep clustering methods and improved the representation learning module with a contractive penalty
112   term, capturing more discriminative representations. However, it could lead to unintentional bias
113   in the optimization focus between the representation learning and clustering modules. Dang et al.
114   (2021) introduce a novel deep clustering framework (NNM) based on a two-level nearest neighbors
115   matching approach. Distinguishing itself from prior methods (Van Gansbeke et al., 2020), NNM
116   incorporates matching at both local and global levels, resulting in a notable enhancement in clustering
117
      performance. It also leverages SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) to pretrain a representation learning
      model using the state-of-the-art contrastive learning loss. In our work, we rethink the deep clustering
118
      problem at a architecture agnostic level by leveraging the capabilities of associative memories. Thus,
119
      various architectural and pretraining advancements would also benefit our proposed scheme.
120
121   Recently, Saha et al. (2023) introduced ClAM, an end-to-end differentiable clustering approach,
122   utilizing Dense Associative Memories (AMs) for clustering. AMs adeptly store multidimensional
123
      vectors as fixed point attractor states in a recurrent dynamical system. AMs form associations between
      the initial state and a final state (memory), creating disjoint basins of attractions which are crucial
124
      for clustering. A prominent example of AM is the classical Hopfield Network (Hopfield, 1982). It
125
      exhibits limited memory capacity, approximately storing only ≈ 0.14d arbitrary memories in a d
126   dimensional data domain (McEliece et al., 1987; Amit et al., 1985). Subsequently, Krotov & Hopfield
127   (2016) proposed Dense Associative Memory (Dense AM) or Modern Hopfield Network introducing
128   rapidly growing non-linearities (activation functions) into the system. This innovation allows for
129   a denser arrangement of memories and achieves super-linear (in d) memory capacity (Demircigil
130   et al., 2017; Lucibello & Mézard, 2023). With softmax activation, Dense AMs are closely related
131   to the attention mechanism used in transformers (Ramsauer et al., 2020; Krotov & Hopfield, 2021;
132   Hoover et al., 2024). Further, Schaeffer et al. (2023) demonstrates that the energy function of ClAM’s
133   AM network is equal to a scaled negative log-likelihood of a Gaussian mixture model. In our work,
134
      we study the joint task of learning effective latent representations and clustering in the latent space.
      We continuously refine both the encoder and decoder networks and at the same time integrate the
135
      AM learning dynamics to cluster the points into k groups. This bears semblance to vector-quantized
136
      variational AEs (van den Oord et al., 2017), where the task is to learn a discrete vector code for each
137   point. Nevertheless, this assignment is non-differentiable, requiring gradient approximation, and
138   there is no clustering objective considered. Also related is the task of deep metric learning (Kaya
139   & Bilge, 2019), where the task is to learn a distance function between samples in latent space.
140   Nevertheless, this requires the use of labeled data for full or weak supervision. As such, the coupling
141   of deep clustering with AMs as done in DClAM for cluster-guided latent space learning has not been
142   considered in the literature before.

149   3.1   D EEP C LUSTERING BASICS
150
151   Deep clustering is an unsupervised task, where we have to learn (usually lower dimensional) repre-
152   sentations such that (i) no (critical) information is lost in the latent lower dimensional representations,
153   and (ii) the data in the latent space forms well-separated clusters. To ensure that no information is lost
154   in the latent space, we learn an encoder e : Rd → Rm (m < d) that maps the input x ∈ Rd to a latent
155   space (that is, e(x) ∈ Rm ), along with a decoder d : Rm → Rd that maps the latent representation
156
      back to the original ambient space. Encoder e and decoder d together give us an autoencoder, and
      the loss of information is often measured as the reconstruction loss:

      Our DClAM deep clustering provides various advantages: (i) First, it does not involve any balancing
      hyperparameter γ since the loss involves all parameters in a single term in the per-sample loss
297
      ℓ̄(x, e, d, ρ). (ii) Second, the updates for all the parameters in the pipeline are more explicitly tied
298
      together with the d ◦ ATρ ◦ e composition in the d(ATρ (e(x))) term. This ties the representation
299   learning and clustering objectives more intricately. (iii) Third, it continues to have all the advantages
300   of traditional deep clustering, being end-to-end differentiable since all operators in the above compo-
301   sition are differentiable, and performing a discrete cluster center assignment with T recursions of the
302   attractor dynamics operator Aρ . (iv) Forth, this deep clustering is completely architecture agnostic
303   – we can select a problem dependent encoder and decoder (for example, convolutional or residual
304   networks for images or fully-connected feed-forward networks for text or tabular data). (v) Fifth, it
305   does not involve any additional entropy regularization based hyperparameters as with existing deep
306   clustering algorithms.
307   On a less technical level, Fig. 1 clearly highlights how the overall information flow in the deep
308   clustering pipeline is simplified. The AM plays a critical role in this pipeline with the ability to obtain
309   the actual closest center ATρ (e(x)) in a differentiable manner. It is also worth emphasizing that there
310   are fundamental difference between how DClAM uses AMs versus ClAM. In the latter, AM is utilized
311   to act as a differentiable argmin solver for the k-means objective whereas in DClAM, which involves
312   representation learning, AM recursion actually has a more elaborate effect. The AM augmented
313
      encoder (ATρ ◦ e) explicitly creates basins of attraction in the latent space, and moves/pushes the
      latent representations of the points into these basins, thereby explicitly inducing a clustered data
314
      distribution in the latent space. While the encoder is moving points into basins of attraction, the
315
      DClAM loss tries to minimize the information loss in the latent representations by having the decoder
316   reconstruct these relocated latent representations.

360   Evaluation of deep clustering. A common metric to evaluate and benchmark deep clustering
361   algorithms is by computing the overlap between the obtained clusters in the latent space (thus,
362   partitions) and a semantic partitioning of the data (for example, using some ground-truth labels
363
      of the data that were not used for solving Eq. (3)) with metrics such as the Normalized Mutual
      Information (NMI) (Vinh et al., 2009). While this is a fair metric to compare methods on, it is critical
364
      to ensure that NMI (or any other label-dependent metric) is not utilized for hyperparameter selection
365
      since that is leaking supervision into the unsupervised task of deep clustering, making the overall
366   process a supervised learning pipeline. Unfortunately, for many of reported results, it is not clear how
367   hyperparameters are selected without being influenced at NMI (since they simply report results with
368   the highest NMI). Even for the sole purpose of evaluation, NMI like metrics might only tell us how
369   the learned clusters in the latent space match some semantic partitioning (often manual) of the data,
370   and do not provide any information regarding the reconstruction quality (and thus the information
371   loss in the latent space). Thus, it is easily possible to have high NMI with poor reconstruction loss,
372   which may not align with the primary goals of deep clustering. Existing literature typically report
373   NMI without explicitly discussing reconstruction loss.

      6    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
512
513
      In this paper, we introduce a fresh integration of associative memories in a deep neural network
      module to create the innovative deep clustering algorithms DClAM that leverages the AM attractor
514
      dynamics. Our findings demonstrate that DClAM significantly surpasses standard prototype-based
515
      clustering and existing deep clustering methods. However, it is worth noting that DClAM is still
516   sensitive to hyperparameters and requires pretraining to avoid latent space collapse. Inspired by
517   DClAM’s outstanding performance, our future work aims to extend it to multimodal deep clustering.
518   We plan to explore new energy functions and update dynamics to enhance spectral and semantic
519   clustering. Having flexibility to add other encoder/decoder frameworks with DClAM, we aim to
520   explore transformer-based AE approaches in the future. Additionally, leveraging DClAM’s flexibility,
521   we intend to automate the estimation of the number of clusters directly from the data.

      A.1    M ETRICS USED
705
706   To assess the performance of DClAM, we utilize the Silhouette Coefficient (SC) (Rousseeuw, 1987)
707   as an unsupervised metric for measuring clustering quality. SC scores range from −1 to 1, where 1
708   indicates perfect clustering and −1 indicates completely incorrect labels. A score close to 0 suggests
709   the existence of overlapping clusters. We also employ Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) (Vinh
710   et al., 2009) to evaluate the alignment between the partition obtained by DClAM and the ground
711   truth clustering labels. NMI scores range from 0 (completely incorrect) to 1 (perfect clustering).
712
      Additionally, we compute Reconstruction Loss (RL), representing the mean squared error between
      original and reconstructed points, where lower is better. Entropy (ETP) (Bein, 2006) and Cluster
713
      Size (CS) are computed to assess cluster balance. In clustering, higher entropy (the highest value is
714
      log2 (k) for each dataset, where k is the number of true clusters) indicates more balanced clusters,
715   while lower values suggest potential imbalance, possibly involving singleton or very small clusters.
716   Entropy (H(X)) is calculated based on the distribution of data points across clusters:
717
                                                     k
718                                                  X
719
                                        H(X) = −           P (Ci ) log2 P (Ci )
                                                     i=1
720
721   where, P (Ci ) is the proportion of data points in cluster Ci relative to the total number of data points.
722   Cluster Size (CS) indicates the largest and smallest clusters (in terms of the number of data points)
723   identified in the dataset (more balanced clustering is better).
724
725   A.2    PARAMETER SETTING
726
727
      For Convolutional AE or CAE, for k-means, Agglomerative, ClAM, DCEC, DEKM, and DClAM, we
      adopt an architecture identical to DCEC. The encoder network structure follows conv532 → conv564
728
      → conv3128 → FCd , where convkn represents a convolutional layer with n filters and a kernel size of
729
      k × k, and FCd denotes a fully connected layer of dimension d. Here, d is the number of true clusters
730   in the dataset, and serves as the latent dimension. The decoder mirrors the encoder.
731
732   The ResNet AE or RAE approach draws inspiration from the standard ResNet block described
733
      by Wickramasinghe et al. (2021). For DCEC, DEKM, and DClAM a streamlined configuration
      is employed using two filters with sizes 32 and 64. The size of the embedded representation is
734
      maintained at d, corresponding to the number of clusters in the dataset, as in the previous setup. In
735
      this experiment, the number of repeating layers in the ResNet block is set to 2. To enhance model
736   performance, batch normalization and leakyReLU are incorporated. For a given number of repeats
737   (f ), the total number of hidden layers is calculated as 2 + (f * number of filters), resulting in 6 layers
738   in our case.
739
      The EDCWRN AE or EAE, is that from Oskouei et al. (2023), so for both EDC and DClAM, we
740
      follow the proposed architecture, where the encoder network is configured as a fully connected
741
      multilayer perceptron (MLP) with dimensions i-500-500-2000-d for all datasets, where i represents
742   the dimension of the input space (features), and d is the number of clusters in the dataset. Similarly,
743   the decoder network mirrors the encoder, constituting an MLP with dimensions d-2000-500-500-i.
744   All internal layers, except for the input, output, and embedding layers, use the ReLU activation
745   function.
746
      All three architectures described above are pretrained end-to-end for 100 epochs using Adam (Kingma
747
      & Ba, 2014) with default parameters. The number of clusters k is not a hyperparameter, but rather
748   is taken as the true number of classes in each dataset. Also, as noted above, we set the latent
749   dimensionality d (or m) the same as the number of true classes k in the dataset, i.e., m = d = k.

      We extensively tune all hyperparameters (Table 5) for the optimal results in DClAM. We found that
788
      the inverse temperature β serves as the most critical hyperparameter, which we explore in the range of
