011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Text-to-motion generation is a crucial task in computer vision, which generates
014            the target 3D motion by the given text. The existing annotated datasets are lim-
015            ited in scale, resulting in most existing methods overfitting to the small datasets
016
               and unable to generalize to the motions of the open domain. Some methods at-
               tempt to solve the open-vocabulary motion generation problem by aligning to the
017
               CLIP space or using the Pretrain-then-Finetuning paradigm. However, the current
018
               annotated dataset’s limited scale only allows them to achieve mapping from sub-
019            text-space to sub-motion-space, instead of mapping between full-text-space and
020            full-motion-space (full mapping), which is the key to attaining open-vocabulary
021            motion generation. To this end, this paper proposes to leverage the atomic motion
022            (simple body part motions over a short time period) as an intermediate represen-
023            tation, and leverage two orderly coupled steps, i.e., Textual Decomposition and
024            Sub-motion-space Scattering, to address the full mapping problem. For Textual
025            Decomposition, we design a fine-grained description conversion algorithm, and
026            combine it with the generalization ability of a large language model to convert any
027
               given motion text into atomic texts. Sub-motion-space Scattering learns the com-
               positional process from atomic motions to the target motions, to make the learned
028
               sub-motion-space scattered to form the full-motion-space. For a given motion of
029
               the open domain, it transforms the extrapolation into interpolation and thereby
030            significantly improves generalization. Our network, DSO-Net, combines textual
031            decomposition and sub-motion-space scattering to solve the open-vocabulary mo-
032            tion generation. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our DSO-Net achieves
033            significant improvements over the state-of-the-art methods on open-vocabulary
034            motion generation.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   Text-to-motion (T2M) generation, aiming at generating the 3D target motion described by the given
040   text, is an important task in computer vision and has garnered significant attention in recent research.
041   It plays a crucial role in various applications, such as robotics, animations, and film production.
042   Benefiting from advancements in GPT-style (e.g., LlamaGen (Sun et al., 2024)) and diffusion-style
043   generative paradigm in text-to-image and text-to-video domains, some studies (Zhang et al., 2023a;
044   Jiang et al., 2023; Tevet et al., 2022b; Shafir et al., 2023) have started using these technologies
045   to address the T2M generation task. During the training process, paired text-motion data are uti-
046   lized to align the text space with the motion space. However, the open-vocabulary text-to-motion
047   generation remains a challenging problem, requiring good motion generation quality for unseen
048   open-vocaulary text at inference. Due to the limited scale of recent high-quality annotated datasets
049   (e.g., KIT-ML (Plappert et al., 2016) and HumanML3D (Guo et al., 2022)), as illustrated in Fig. 1
050
      top-left, the Simple Mapping paradigm only learns a mapping between a limited sub-text-space and a
      sub-motion-space, rather than the mapping from full-text-space to full-motion-space. Consequently,
051
      generalization to unseen open-vocabulary text is almost impossible.
052
053   To enhance the model’s generalization capabilities, two main strategies have been explored. As
      shown in Fig. 1 top-right, the first paradigm is CLIP-based Alignment (e.g., MotionCLIP (Tevet

      Consequently, we conclude that the problem of insufficient generalization ability in current meth-
      ods arises from the limited amount of high-quality labeled data and the inadequate utilization of
087
      large-scale unsupervised motion data for pretraining. Existing methods can only establish overfitted

089   to establish a mapping from the full-text-space to the full-motion-space.
090
091   We observe that when understanding a motion, human beings tend to partition it into the combi-
092
      nation of several simple body part motions (such as ”spine bend forward”, ”left hand up”) over a
      short time period, which we define as atomic motions. All these atomic motions are combined
093
      spatially and temporally to form the raw motion.This observation motivates us to decompose a raw
094
      motion into atomic motions and leverage atomic motions as an intermediate representation. Since
095   raw motion texts often contain abstract and high-level semantic meanings, directly using raw texts
096   to guide motion generation can hinder the model’s ability to understand open-vocabulary motion
097   texts. In contrast, atomic motion texts provide a low-level and concrete description of different limb
098   movements, which are shared across different domains.
099
      Leveraging the atomic motions as an intermediate representation, we propose to address the full-
100
      mapping problem through two orderly coupled steps: (1) Textual Decomposition. To enhance
101   generalization ability, we first design a textual decomposition process that converts a raw motion
102   text into several atomic motion texts, subsequently generating motions from these atomic texts. To
103   prepare training data, we develop a fine-grained description conversion algorithm to establish
104   atomic texts and motion pairs. Specifically, we partition the input motion into several time periods,
105   and describe the movements of each joint and spatial relationships from the aspects of velocity
106   (e.g., fast), magnitude (e.g., significant), and low-level behaviors (e.g., bending) for each period.
107   The fine-grained descriptions and the raw text are then input into a large language model (LLM) to
      summarize the atomic motion texts. Each raw motion text is decomposed into atomic motion texts of

      six body parts: the spine, left/right-upper/lower limbs, and trajectory. By this process, we guarantee
109   the converted atomic motion texts are consistent with the actual motion behavior. During inference,
110   for any given text, we employ the LLM to split the whole motion into several periods and describe
111   each period using atomic motion texts. In this way, we establish a mapping from the full-text-
112   space to the full-atomic-text-space.(2) Sub-motion-space Scattering. After obtaining the full-text-
113   space to the full-atomic-text-space mapping in the first step, we aim to further achieve alignment
114   from the full-atomic-text-space to the full-motion-space, through the Sub-motion-space Scattering
115   step, thereby establishing the mapping from the full-text-space to the full-motion-space. Given the
116   limited labeled data, it is difficult for the Pretrain-then-Finetuning paradigm to learn the full-motion-
117
      space, because its trivial alignment process only learns a mapping from text to a condensed subspace
      (which we refer to as sub-motion-space), requiring extrapolation for out-of-domain motions. In
118
      contrast, our approach scatters the sub-motion-space to form the full-motion-space, as shown in
119
      the bottom right of Fig. 1, transforming extrapolation into interpolation and significantly improving
120   generalization. The sub-motion-space scattering is achieved by learning the combinational process
121   of atomic motions to generate target motions, with a text-motion alignment (TMA) module to extract
122   features for atomic motion texts, and a compositional feature fusion (CFF) module to fuse atomic
123   text features into motion features and learn the the combinational process from atomic motions to
124   target motions. As shown in Fig. 1, Interpolating an out-domain motion is essentially a combination
125   of several nearest clusters of scattered sub-motion-space, which is highly consistent with the process
126   of CFF we design. Therefore, the CFF ensures for scattering sub-motion-space we learned.
127   Overall, we adopt the discrete generative mask modeling and follow the pretrain-then-finetuning
128   pipeline for open-vocabulary motion generation. First, we pretrain a residual VQ-VAE (Martinez
129   et al., 2014) network using a pre-processed large-scale unlabeled motion dataset, to enable the net-
130   work to have prior knowledge of large-scale motion. For the fine-tuning stages, we first leverage
131   our textual decomposition module to convert the raw motion text into atomic texts. Then, we utilize
132   both raw text and the atomic texts with our proposed TMA and CFF modules to train a text-to-
133   model generative model. Our network, abbreviated as DSO-Net, combines textual decomposition
134
      and sub-motion-space scattering to solve the open-vocabulary motion generation. We conduct ex-
      tensive experiments comparing our approach with previous state-of-the-art approaches on various
135
      open-vocabulary datasets and achieve a significant improvement quantitatively and qualitatively.
136
137   In summary, our main contributions include: (1) we propose to leverage atomic motions as an inter-
138   mediate representation, and design textual decomposition and sub-motion-space scattering frame-
139   work to solve open-vocabulary motion generation. (2) For textual decomposition, we design a rule-
140
      based fine-grained description conversion algorithm and combine it with the large language model
      to obtain the atomic motion texts for a given motion. (3) For sub-motion-space scattering, we pro-
141
      pose to leverage a text-motion alignment (TMA) module and a compositional feature fusion (CFF)
142
      module to learn the generative combination of atomic motions, thereby significantly improving the
143   model’s generalization ability.

148   2.1   T EXT- TO -M OTION G ENERATION
149
150   Text-to-Motion has been a long-standing concern. previous works (Guo et al., 2020; Petrovich
151   et al., 2021) usually generate a motion based on the given action categories. Action2Motion (Guo
152   et al., 2020) uses a recurrent conditional variational autoencoder (VAE) for motion generation. It
153
      uses historical data to predict subsequent postures and follows the constraints of action categories.
      Subsequently, ACTOR (Petrovich et al., 2021) encodes the entire motion sequence into the latent
154
      space, which significantly reduces the accumulated error. Using only action labels is not flexible
155
      enough. Therefore, some works began to explore generating motion under text (i.e., natural lan-
156   guage). TEMOS (Petrovich et al., 2022) uses a variational autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma et al., 2019)
157   architecture to establish a shared latent space for motion and text. This model aligns the two distri-
158   butions by minimizing the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the motion distribution and
159   the text distribution. Therefore, in the inference stage, only text input is needed as a condition to
160   generate the corresponding motion. T2M (Guo et al., 2022) further learns a text-to-length estimator,
161   enabling the network to give the generated motion length automatically. T2M-GPT (Zhang et al.,
      2023a) first introduces the VQ-VAE technique into text-to-motion tasks and leverages the autore-


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      gressive paradigm to generate motions. MotionGPT (Jiang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b) further
163   improves the motion quality under the autoregressive paradigm from the aspect of text encoder.
164   MDM (Tevet et al., 2022b) and MotionDiffuse (Zhang et al., 2022) are the first works to solve the
165   motion synthesis task by using diffusion models. Subsequent works (Chen et al., 2023; Zou et al.,
166   2024; Zhang et al., 2023b; Dai et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a; Karunratanakul et al., 2023; Xie
167   et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2024) further improve the controllability and quality of the generation results
168   through some techniques such as database retrieval, spatial control, and fine-grained description.
169   However, all these methods essentially overfit the limited training data, thereby can not achieve
170   open-vocabulary motion generations.

173   2.2   O PEN - VOCABULARY G ENERATION
174
175   Compared to the previous method of training and testing on the same dataset, the open-vocabulary
176   generation task expects to train on one dataset and test on the other (out-domain) dataset. CLIP (Rad-
177   ford et al., 2021) is pre-trained on 400 million image-text pairs using the contrastive learning method
178   and has strong zero-shot generalization ability. By calculating feature similarity with a given image
179   and candidate texts in a list, it realizes open-vocabulary image-text tasks. Therefore, on this basis,
180   many methods in the field of text-to-image generation, a series of Diffusion-style-based and GPT-
181   style-based methods (Rombach et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2024) are proposed to use CLIP to extract
182   features to improve the generalization ability of the model. Based on this, MotionCLIP (Tevet et al.,
183
      2022a) proposes to fit the motion data of the training set to the mesh of the human SMPL (Loper
      et al., 2023) model in the preprocessing stage, thereby rendering multi-view static poses. In the
184
      training stage, the motion features are aligned with the text and image features extracted by CLIP
185
      simultaneously, thereby aligning the motion features to the CLIP space and enhancing the general-
186   ization ability of the model. AvatarCLIP (Hong et al., 2022) first synthesizes a key pose and then
187   matches from the database and finally optimizes to the target motion. OOHGM (Lin et al., 2023),
188   Make-An-Animation (Azadi et al., 2023), and PRO-Motion (Liu et al., 2023) first train a genera-
189   tive text-to-pose model with diverse text-pose pairs. Then, OOHGM further learns to reconstruct
190   full motion from the masked motion. Inspired by the success of AnimateDiff (Guo et al., 2023),
191   Make-An-Animation inserts and finetunes the temporal adaptor to achieve motion generation. PRO-
192   Motion leverages the large language model to give the key-pose descriptions and synthesize motion
193   by a trained interpolation network. However, all these methods, aligning the static poses with the
194
      image space, lose the temporal information during the learning and finally result in the generation
      of unrealistic motion. Recently, OMG (Liang et al., 2024a) try to use the successful paradigm,
195
      pretrained-then-finetuning, in the LLM to achieve open-vocabulary. Therefore, it first pretrained a
196
      un-condiditonal diffusion model with unannotated motion data, then finetunes on the annotated text-
197   motion pairs by a ControlNet and MoE structure. However, due to the extremely limited labeled
198   data, this type of method can ultimately only achieve the effective mapping from sub-text-space to
199   sub-motion-space, which is still far from sufficient for achieving open-vocabulary tasks.

      2.3   G ENERATIVE M ASK MODELING
203
204
205   BERT (Devlin, 2018) as a very representative work in the field of natural language processing,
206   pretrains a text encoder by randomly masking words and predicting these masked words. Numerous
207
      subsequent methods in the generative fields have borrowed this idea to achieve text-to-image or
      video generation, such as MAGVIT (Yu et al., 2023), MAGE (Li et al., 2023), and Muse (Chang
208
      et al., 2023). Compared to autoregressive modeling, generative masked modeling has the advantage
209
      of faster inference speed. In the motion field, MoMask (Guo et al., 2024) first introduced generative
210   masked modeling into the field of motion generation. It adopts a residual VQ-VAE (RVQ-VAE)
211   and represents human motion as multi-layer discrete motion tokens with high-fidelity details. In the
212   training stage, the motion tokens of the base layer and the residual layers are randomly masked by a
213   masking transformer and predicted according to the text input. In the generation stage, the masking
214   transformer starts from an empty sequence and iteratively fills in the missing tokens. In this paper,
215   our overall architecture also adopts the similar generative masked modeling as MoMask (Guo et al.,
      2024) to implement our pretrain-then-finetuning strategy.

247   We propose a novel DSO-Net for open-vocabulary motion generation, aiming to generate a 3D hu-
248   man motion x from an open-vocabulary textual description d that is unseen in the training dataset. As
249   shown in Fig. 2, our framework takes the pretrain-then-finetuning paradigm, which is first pretrained
250   on a large-scale unannotated motion dataset, and then finetuned on a small dataset of text-motion
251   pairs. All those motions are processed in a unified format by UniMocap (Chen & UniMocap, 2023).
252   As analyzed before, the key to achieving open-vocabulary motion generation is to establish the
253   alignment between the full-text-space and the full-motion-space, which we call full-mapping. To
254
      this end, we leverage atomic motions as intermediate representations and convert the full-mapping
      process into two orderly coupled stages: 1) Textual Decomposition, and 2) Sub-motion-space Scat-
255
      tering. The Textual Decomposition stage aims at converting any given motion text into several
256
      atomic motion texts, each describing the motion of a simple body part over a short time period,
257   thereby mapping the full-text-space to the full-atomic-text-space. The Sub-motion-space Scattering
258   stage is designed to learn the combinational process from atomic motions to the target motions,
259   which scatters the sub-motion-space learned from limited paired data to form the full-motion-space,
260   via a text-motion alignment (TMA) module and a compositional feature fusion (CFF) module. The
261   scattered sub-motion-space eventually improves the generalization of our motion generation ability.
262

      3.1      T EXTUAL D ECOMPOSITION
264
265   The textual decomposition is designed to convert any given motion text into several atomic mo-
266   tion texts (each describing the motion of a simple body part in a short time period). Different
267   from the raw motion texts that contain abstract and high-level semantic meanings, which hinder the
268   model’s ability to understand open-vocabulary motion texts, atomic motion texts provide a low-level
269   and concrete description of different limb movements, which are shared across different domains.
      Therefore, we use the text of atomic motions as an intermediate representation, first converting raw

      motion texts into atomic motion texts, and then learning the process of combining atomic motions
271   to generate target motions.
272
273   First, we construct atomic motion and text pairs from a limited raw motion-text paired dataset. Al-
274
      though Large Language Models (LLM) have the generalization ability to describe any given motion
      as an atomic motion, directly inputting a raw motion text into a large model will lead to a mis-
275
      match between the generated description and the real motion behavior, where some works also said
276
      before He et al. (2023); Shi et al. (2023). For this reason, we design a Fine-grained Description
277   Conversion algorithm to describe the movement of each joint and the relative movement relation-
278   ship between joints in a fine-grained way for a given 3D motion. Then, this fine-grained description
279   and the raw motion text are input into the LLM to summarize it into the final atomic motion de-
280   scription. The entire algorithm describes the movement of body parts in the input motion from three
281   aspects: speed, amplitude, and specific behavior; and divide the entire movement into at most P
282   time periods. Specifically, this fine-grained description conversion consists of four steps:
283   Pose Extraction. For each frame in a given 3D motion xi , we compute different pose descriptors,
284   including the angle, orientation, and position of a single joint, and the distance between any two
285   joints. Taking the angle as an example, we can use three joint coordinates, Jshoulder , Jelbow , and
286   Jwrist , to compute the bending magnitude of the upper limb, which is formulated as:

341
      atomic motion texts describing simple body part movements.

      3.2   S UB - MOTION - SPACE S CATTERING
345
      As mentioned before, given the limited paired data, we can only learn a sub-motion-space, i.e., a
346   condensed subspace of the full-motion-space. To enhance the open-vocabulary generalization abil-
347   ity, we propose to learn the combination process from atomic motions to the target motion, thereby
348   rearranging the sub-motion-space we learned in a much more scattered form. Although some tar-
349   get motions are out-of-distribution for the sub-motion-space, we scatter the subspace to form the
350   full-motion-space, and convert the generative process from extrapolation into interpolation, thereby
351   significantly enhancing the generative generalization ability of our model.
352   Specifically, to enable our network to learn a scattering sub-motion-space, we propose to establish
353   the combination process of atomic motions instead of learning the target motion directly, which con-
354   sists of two main parts: (1) Text-Motion Alignment (TMA) and (2) Compositional Feature Fusion
355   (CFF).
356
      Text-Motion Alignment (TMA). Previous T2M generation methods usually leverage the CLIP
357
      model to extract text features, and then align the text feature into the motion space through some
358   linear layers or attention layers during training. However, the CLIP method is trained on large-
359   scale text-image pair data, where the text is a description given for a static image, which has a
360   huge gap with the description of motion (including dynamic information over a time period). As
361   a result, learning the alignment during training brings an extra burden and seriously interferes with
362   our network’s focus on learning the combination process from atomic motions to the target motion.
363   Therefore, inspired by the text-motion retrieval method TMR (Petrovich et al., 2023), we first use
364   the contrastive learning method to pretrain a text feature extractor TMA on text-motion pair data.
365   Compared to the CLIP encoder trained on text-image pair data, our TMA is trained on text-motion
366
      pair data, which better aligns the text features to the motion space. Specifically, we use the InfoNCE
      loss function to pull the positive pair (x+ , d+ ) (a motion and its corresponding textual description)
367
      closer, and push the negative pair (x+ , d− ) (a motion and another textual description) away, to
368
      ensure that the text features are aligned with the motion space. For M positive pairs, the loss
369   function is defined as follows:
370                                                                                       !
371                                  1 X             exp Aii /τ             exp Aii /τ
                        LNCE = −               log P               + log P                  ,             (2)
372                                2M i               j exp Aij /τ           j exp Aji /τ
373
374   where Aij = (m+ , d+ ), and τ is the temperature hyperparameter. Subsequently, as shown in Fig. 2,
375   we use TMA as our text feature extractor for both the raw texts and decomposed atomic texts.
376   All these atomic text features are input to our next compositional feature fusion (CFF) module to
377   guide the motion generation. In this way, we greatly reduce the interference caused by text-motion
      misalignment during the atomic motion combination learning process.

      Overall Architecture of Motion Generative Model. Our motion generative network contains two
379   different generative models, one corresponds to the base layer of Residual VQ-VAE (RVQ), and the
380   other corresponds to the residual layers. As detailed in Fig. 2, each generative model, either base-
381   layer or residual-layer, contains sequentially stacked transformer layer and Compositional Feature
382   Fusion (CFF), i.e., 1 transformer layer followed by 1 CFF module, repeated for K times. Different
383   residual layers share the same parameters, but have different input indicators, i.e., V residual layers
384   correspond to indicator 1 to V . Since the indicators are the minor difference between the base and
385   the residual layer generative model, we omit it for convenience.
386   Given a motion x of F motion frames, we first sample it with ratio r, and encode each of Fr down-
387   sampled frames with the motion encoder in RVQ. We then perform quantization by mapping the
388   encoded features to the code indices of their nearest codes in the codebook of base/residual layers,
389   denoted as I = [I1 , I2 , ...IN ], where N = Fr . Then, the code indices I is first converted into a
390   one-hot embedding, and then mapped into a motion embedding m = [m1 , m2 , ...mN ] ∈ RN ×Dm
391   by linear layers, which is taken as the initial input to the generative models, where Dm is the channel
392   dimension.
393   Given a text, and the atomic description (a L×P text matrix), we encode the raw text and the atomic
394   texts by our TMA text encoder, and the outputs are: 1) raw text feature Tr ∈ RDT , where DT is the
395   channel dimension; and 2) atomic text feature W ∈ RL×P ×DW , where L is the number of atomic,
396   P is the number of motion periods, and DW is the channel dimension.
397
      The motion embedding m is first randomly masked out with a varying ratio, by replacing some to-
398
      kens with a special [MASK] token. Subsequently,the masked embedding m̃ = [m̃1 , m̃2 , ...m̃N ] is
399   combined with the raw text feature Tr , which is then input into a transformer layer FT ransf ormer .
400   The outputs are refined raw text feature and refined motion feature, denoted as Tro and m̃1 =
401   [m̃11 , m̃12 , ...m̃1N ] ∈ RN ×Dm , which is formulated as:
402
403                                     Tro , m̃1 = FT ransf ormer (Tr ; m̃).                            (3)
404
      The transformer layer enables the output m̃1 to integrate both global information and the temporal
405   relationship.
406
407   Compositional Feature Fusion (CFF). The CFF module is designed to fuse atomic text features
408
      into motion features, and guide the model to learn the combinational process from atomic motions to
      the target motions. As shown in Fig. 3, we utilize the cross-attention mechanism to fuse the atomic
409
      motion text feature W into the motion feature in a spatial combination manner. Specifically, we split
410
      the refined motion feature m̃1 into L parts along the channel dimension (L is the number of body
411   parts), and reshape the splitted motion feature and input it into a linear layer to obtain the updated
412   motion embedding m̃2 ∈ RL×N ×DW . Then, the m̃2 is taken as the Query, and the the atomic text
413   feature W is taken as the Key and the Value to conduct the cross-attention calculation. Since the
414   atomic text feature W is extracted by our TMA model, which has aligned the text space with the
415   motion space, the output motion feature of the cross-attention m̃3 ∈ RL×N ×DW could composite
416   the atomic motions explicitly and directly. The overall process of CFF module is formulated as:
417                                          m̃3 = FCF F (m̃2 ; W ).                                     (4)
418
                        3
419   Eventually, the m̃ goes through a linear layer and is reshaped to the final output motion feature
420   m̃o ∈ RN ×Dm . The m̃o is then combined with the refined raw text feature Tro and input to the next
421
      transformer layer and the CFF module. The transformer layer and the CFF module are sequentially
      stacked for K times. The output motion feature of the final CFF module is input into a classification
422
      head and punished by a cross-entropy loss.

      3.3   I NFERENCE P ROCESS
425
426   During the inference stage, for generative models of the base layer and R residual layers, we initial-
427   ize all motion tokens as [MASK] tokens. During each inference step, we simultaneously predict all
428   masked motion tokens, conditioned on both the raw motion text and the atomic motion texts using
429   in-context learning. Our generative models first predict the probability distribution of tokens at the
430   masked locations, and sample motion tokens according to the probability. Then the sampled tokens
431   with the lower confidences are masked again for the next iteration. Finally, all the predicted tokens
      are decoded back to motion sequences by the RVQ decoder.

      5    C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we propose our DSO-Net, i.e. Texutal Decomposition then Sub-motion-space
534   Scattering, to solve the open-vocabulary motion generation problem. Textual decomposition is first
535   leveraged to convert the input raw text into atomic motion descriptions, which serve as our interme-
536   diate representations. Then, we learned the combination process from intermediate atomic motion to
537   the target motion, which subsequently scattering the sub-motion-space we learned and transforming
538   extrapolation into interpolation and significantly improve generalization. Numerous experiments
539   are conducted to compare our approach with previous state-of-the-art approaches on various open-
      vocabulary datasets and achieve a significant improvement quantitatively and qualitatively.
