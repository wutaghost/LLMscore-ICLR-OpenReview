009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This empirical study aims at improving the effectiveness of the standard 2-layer
012            MLP projection head g(·) featured in the SimCLR framework through the use
013            of pretrained autoencoder embeddings. Given a contrastive learning task with a
014            largely unlabeled image classification dataset, we first train a shallow autoencoder
015            architecture and extract its compressed representations contained in the encoder’s
016
               embedding layer. After freezing the weights within this pretrained layer, we use it
               as a drop-in replacement for the input layer of SimCLR’s default projector. Addi-
017
               tionally, we also apply further architectural changes to the projector by decreasing
018
               its width and changing its activation function. The different projection heads are
019            then used to contrastively train and evaluate a feature extractor f (·) following the
020            SimCLR protocol, while also examining the performance impact of Z-score nor-
021            malized datasets. Our experiments indicate that using a pretrained autoencoder
022            embedding in the projector can not only increase classification accuracy by up
023            to 2.9 % or 1.7 % on average but can also significantly decrease the dimension-
024            ality of the projection space. Our results also suggest, that using the sigmoid
025            and tanh activation functions within the projector can outperform ReLU in terms
026            of peak and average classification accuracy. When applying our presented pro-
027
               jectors, then not applying Z-score normalization to datasets often increases peak
               performance. In contrast, the default projection head can benefit more from nor-
028
               malization. All experiments involving our pretrained projectors are conducted
029
               with frozen embeddings, since our test results indicate an advantage compared to
030            using their non-frozen counterparts.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Contrastive learning is a self supervised machine learning paradigm that has proven itself to be use-
036   ful for Big Data applications, which can capture nearly unlimited amounts of unlabeled data at high
037   speeds and low costs. In such situations, classical supervised learning approaches become infeasible
038   due to missing labels and because they can neither be generated in time nor in sufficient quality. For
039
      this reason, despite the groundbreaking success of supervision in the field of machine learning, the
      research community will continue to investigate new and existing methods for learning representa-
040
      tion with a limited amount of labeled data. This paper combines the well understood fundamentals
041
      of unsupervised learning using autoencoders with more recent approaches of contrastive learning
042   as offered by the SimCLR framework (Chen et al., 2020). Both autoencoders and SimCLR are
043   designed to be trained without any labeled data, although SimCLR’s evaluation protocol requires
044   some amount of labeled samples. This is due to the fact that SimCLR is usually used for train-
045   ing image classification models, whereas autoencoders are often utilized for image generation and
046   reconstruction tasks.
047
      In order to understand how the concepts behind SimCLR and plain autoencoders are combined in
048   this paper, one first has to understand the abstract structure of SimCLR. At its most basic level, Sim-
049   CLR is a composition of two artificial neural networks, the backbone f (·) followed by the projection
050   head g(·). Instead of focusing on the backbone, which is a deep convolutional neural network re-
051   sponsible for feature extraction, we direct our attention to the projection head, a shallow multilayer
052   perceptron (MLP) consisting of two ReLU activated linear layers. The main role of this architec-
053   tural building block is to project the features generated by the backbone into a lower dimensional
      projection space. This latent space and its embeddings can significantly influence the backbone’s

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      ability to learn meaningful representations as the contrastive loss is applied to the outputs of the pro-
055   jector. This highlights the importance of the projection head within the SimCLR framework since
056   backpropagation will always adapt the backbone’s weights based on the loss values generated by
057   the compressed projections. Also, compressing certain inputs into more efficient representations
058   can generally be seen as the central task of autoencoders. In fact, we find regular autoencoders to
059   be a special type of MLP that can be trained to reconstruct inputs by optimizing a hidden embed-
060   ding layer using some reconstruction loss that captures the difference between the encoder’s input
061   and the decoder’s output. Based on these observations, we expect to increase the quality of con-
062   trastively learned representations by exchanging the randomly initialized input layer of SimCLR’s
063
      default projector with a pretrained autoencoder embedding.
064   From a practical point of view, it is well known that deeper and wider backbones usually improve
065   representation quality and downstream classification accuracy. In contrast, it is generally not well
066   understood, why the backbone benefits from using a nonlinear projection head. Chen et al. (2020)
067   introduce the projector after empirically observing significant increases in model accuracy by more
068   than 10 %. From this the authors conclude that the hidden layer before the projection head learns
069
      better representations than the layer after. The authors also conjecture that including a projection
      head increases the backbone’s ability to aggregate and retain information that is valuable for down-
070
      stream tasks. However, the rational given for this phenomenon only explains why the backbone’s
071
      learned representations are of higher quality but not how the projector is able to generate them in the
072   first place. This lack of reasoning becomes obvious when comparing the proposed end-to-end opti-
073   mization of both the backbone and the projector to only training the backbone without any projection
074   head. Despite some open questions about the projector’s precise mode of operation, its contribution
075   to the success of SimCLR and related methods remains undeniable. We therefore conduct exper-
076   iments as part of this paper to investigate the effectiveness of pretrained autoencoder embeddings
077   within SimCLR’s default projector. The main contributions of our paper are summarized as follows:
078
            1. We demonstrate on five well-known image classification datasets that exchanging the in-
079
               put layer of SimCLR’s default projector with a pretrained autoencoder embedding reliably
080
               increases linear classification accuracy.
081
            2. We show that activating the projector with the sigmoid or tanh functions can outperform
082
               ReLU in terms of peak and average accuracy.
083
084
            3. We observe that pretrained autoencoder embeddings can drastically reduce the width of the
               projector as well as the dimensionality of the projection space while increasing average
085
               accuracy.
086
087
            4. We find that freezing the weights within the pretrained embedding layers almost always
               increases classification accuracy.
088
089         5. We note that Z-score normalization of datasets drastically impairs the training of autoen-
090            coder embeddings.
091         6. To ensure reproducibility of our results, we publish the training and evaluation code
092            together with CSV listings of all training runs on GitHub: https://anonymous.
093            4open.science/r/simclr-ae

      2   R ELATED W ORK
101
102   Literature review does not reveal any references to publications dealing explicitly with pretraining
103   the nonlinear projection head used in the SimCLR framework. A few papers aim at explaining
104   the role and the effects of the projector in more detail. These include the study conducted by Jing
105   et al. (2022) who are trying to understand dimensional collapse in contrastive self-supervised learn-
106   ing (SSL). The authors first show that dimensional collapse can also happen in contrastive learning
107   despite the use of positive and negative samples. For SimCLR, dimensional collapse of the repre-
      sentation space usually occurs when the backbone f (·) is trained without a projection head (g = id).


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Instead of improving the projection head itself, the authors propose an alternative to SimCLR called
109   DirectCLR, a contrastive learning method that does not rely on a trainable projection head but uses
110   a fixed low rank diagonal projector instead. DirectCLR does not try to prevent dimensional collapse
111   by using a trainable projector but rather through direct optimization of the backbone. The proposed
112   approach outperforms SimCLR when using a 1-layer linear projector by 1.6 % but fails to match the
113   performance of SimCLR’s default 2-layer nonlinear projection head by 3.8 %.
114
      In contrast to Jing et al. (2022), the work of Gupta et al. (2022) aims at understanding and improving
115   the role of the projection head in self-supervised learning. The authors conjecture that the projection
116   head implicitly learns to choose a subspace of features on which the contrastive loss is applied. This
117   subspace selection is believed to address the disadvantages of the contrastive loss function which
118   are mainly introduced by sub-optimal data augmentations. It is also assumed that implicit subspace
119   selection can enable the projection head to generate embeddings which minimize contrastive loss,
120   while allowing the backbone to learn meaningful representations. Based on these observations, the
121   authors argue that data-dependent subspace selection should be part of the loss function used in
122   self-supervised learning. This is why the authors reformulate SSL as a bilevel optimization prob-
123
      lem where at each training step, the first optimization problem is to select the best subspace for the
      contrastive loss by optimizing the projection head while the second optimization performs gradient
124
      descent on the backbone. As a result, a contrastive loss function is proposed which treats the pro-
125
      jector as part of the criterion and not only as an architectural component. The authors continue to
126   empirically demonstrate that a trainable projector generally increases performance and can therefore
127   be viewed as an improved optimization scheme for SSL. This result contradicts the general notion of
128   DirectCLR (Jing et al., 2022), where backbone features are optimized directly using a non-trainable
129   projector.
130
      Since contrastive learning is not only limited to SimCLR and its nonlinear projection head, Gui et al.
131
      (2023) address the question why linear projectors affect the generalization properties of learned rep-
132   resentations. The authors find the heuristics in the existing literature to be insufficient for answering
133   this question, especially in the case of linear projection heads. Since discarding the projection head
134   after training is a common approach in contrastive learning, the authors assume projectors to act
135   as a buffer component that protects the representation space from distortions induced by loss mini-
136   mization carried out on the outputs of the projector. For nonlinear projection heads, this assumption
137   shows significant overlap with the conjecture made by Chen et al. (2020) w. r. t. the superiority of the
138   layer before the projector. This may be rooted in information loss triggered by contrastive loss: i. e. ,
139   statistical shrinkage and expansion, i. e. a reduction or increase in the effects of sampling variation
140
      induced by the contrastive loss on the projector, are highly correlated with downstream accuracy.
      Expansion decreases test error, while shrinkage reduces signals and increase test error.
141
142   Xue et al. (2024) theoretically investigate the benefits of a projection head for representation learn-
143   ing. The authors also observe that linear models progressively assign weights to features as they
144   propagate through the layers of the backbone. Based on this observation, the authors state that fea-
145   tures in deeper layers of the backbone are represented more unequally. Furthermore, these represen-
146
      tations not only turn out to be more specialized towards the pretraining objective but are believed to
      be additionally distorted through the use of nonlinear activations within the layers of the backbone.
147
      The authors argue that this allows deeper layers to learn features which are entirely absent in the
148
      outputs of the projector. As a major result, it is stated that “projection heads provably improve the
149   robustness and generalizability of representations, when data augmentation harms useful features of
150   the pretraining data, or when features relevant to the downstream task are too weak or too strong
151   in the pretraining data”. The authors show that these findings also apply to supervised contrastive
152   learning and regular supervised learning where lower layers can also learn subclass-level features
153   that are not represented in the final layer. Finally, it is noted that these findings “demonstrate how
154   representations before the final representation layer can significantly reduce class/neural collapse”.
155   In current literature, this effect is well observed for both nonlinear and linear projection heads (Chen
156   et al., 2020), although DirectCLR (Jing et al., 2022) suggests linear projectors to be outdated.
157   In summary, much of the current literature focuses on understanding the usefulness of projection
158   heads for contrastive learning applications mainly from a theoretical point of view. We conclude
159   from our literature review, that projection heads do increase the quality of contrastively learned rep-
160   resentations in the backbone. Here, linear projection heads are significantly inferior to nonlinear
161   projectors and that the advantage gained by linear projectors can also be experienced by directly
      optimizing the backbone. In contrast to linear projectors, there is no compelling evidence to sug-

      gest that nonlinear projection heads are ineffective in contrastive learning applications. In fact,
163   research indicates that nonlinear projectors continue to provide an easy and effective building block
164   for achieving peak downstream performance while reducing the impact of model collapse.

169   3.1   T HE S IM CLR F RAMEWORK
170
171
      SimCLR is a contrastive learning framework by Chen et al. (2020). It allows for learning repre-
      sentations from input images without the use of labeled data. The SimCLR framework builds upon
172
      extensively researched and carefully parameterized data augmentation pipelines used to create a
173
      positive contrastive image pair (x̃i = t(x), x̃j = t′ (x)) for each dataset sample x. Here t and
174   t′ are two transformations which are randomly sampled from the space T of particularly effective
175   transformations. These transformations include random cropping and flipping as well as the random
176   application of color jitter, grayscaling and dynamic Gaussian blurring where the kernel size adapts
177   to changes in image resolution.
178
      The composite and easy to implement architecture of SimCLR features an interchangeable feature
179
      extractor f (backbone), followed by a ReLU activated 2-layer nonlinear projection head g (projec-
180   tor). The backbone can be any neural network, although in practice, convolutional architectures like
181   ResNet are widely used, especially for image classification tasks. Given an augmented pair of im-
182   ages (x̃i , x̃j ), SimCLR first generates two separate intermediate representations hi , hj by applying
183   the feature extractor to both x̃i and x̃j . These representations are then mapped via the projector into
184   a lower dimensional projection space:
185
186
                                             zi = g(hi ), zj = g(hj )                                     (1)
187
188   The SimCLR framework is trained using the Normalized Temperature-scaled Cross Entropy Loss
189   (NT-Xent),

                            ℓi,j = − log P2N                                                 (2)
                                            k=1 1[k̸=i] exp(sim(zi , zk )/τ )

192
193   a variant of the InfoNCE loss (van den Oord et al., 2018), which does not require explicit sampling
194   of negative examples for a given positive pair. Instead, given a batch of N samples, all 2N − 2
195   augmented samples (excluding the positive pair) are treated as negative examples. Research by
196   Chen et al. (2020) suggests, that this approach requires large batch sizes of up to 4096 to reach
197   maximum efficacy. The NT-Xent loss shown in Equation 2 is calculated by taking the negative
198
      natural logarithm of the normalized exponential function (softmax), which is applied to the cosine
      similarities sim(·) of the projections zi , zj and scaled by a temperature parameter τ ∈ [0, 1].
199
200   In summary, the main idea behind SimCLR’s image augmentations and its use of a contrastive loss
201   function is to force the backbone f to learn fundamental representations that are invariant to a wide
202   range of transformations. This is achieved by minimizing the distance between the projections zi , zj
203   of positive pairs while at the same time increasing it to the projections of negative examples from
204
      the same batch.

206   3.2   AUTOENCODERS
207
208   An autoencoder (Hinton & Salakhutdinov, 2006) is a type of multilayer perceptron consisting of an
209
      encoder and a decoder. The encoder can be formalized as a mapping E from an input space X into
      the embedding space Z, where both X = Rm and Z = Rn are often euclidean spaces with m ≥ n.
210
      For an input vector x ∈ X the output E(x) of the encoder is called its embedding or latent vector
211
      z ∈ Z. The decoder D is usually the mirror image of the encoder. It maps the encoder’s outputs z
212   from the embedding space Z back into the space of decoded messages X . The autoencoder A can
213   therefore be written as a function composition of E followed by D, mapping input x ∈ X via some
214   z ∈ Z to its reconstructed output x′ ∈ X :
215
                                       A : X → X , x 7→ D(E(x)) = x′                                      (3)

      The objective of an autoencoder is to learn compressed representations from a training dataset that
217   allow the decoder to reconstruct new inputs with as little error as possible. In doing so, the autoen-
218   coder learns efficient codings for inputs which are embedded into a lower dimensional latent space.
219   Autoencoders are traditionally trained without labeled data in a purely unsupervised manner.
220
      A basic autoencoder embedding is a single hidden layer whose weights store the compressed rep-
221
      resentations of the encoder’s inputs. This embedding layer is trained by minimizing some recon-
222
      struction loss, i. e. a quality function d : X × X → R≥0 that measures the reconstruction quality
223   d(x, x′ ) of the encoder’s input x ∈ X compared to the decoder’s output x′ ∈ X . A widely used
224   quality function for optimizing autoencoders is the squared L2 norm, which is also known as the
225   mean squared error (MSE) of the input x and its reconstruction x′ :

233   3.3   P RETRAINING AUTOENCODER E MBEDDINGS
234
235
      The pretrained autoencoder embeddings used in this paper are generated by training a basic 4-layer
      autoencoder on five well-known image classification datasets (see Table 1) using gradient descent
236
      and MSE loss. For this part of our research, we use the base implementation of the autoencoder
237
      provided by Chadebec et al. (2022) through the pythae Python library.
238
239   For each of the five datasets, we train three embeddings in a 70/10/20 train, validation and test split
240   using varying latent dimensions of 128, 256 and 512. This equates to a total of 15 different pretrained
241
      autoencoder embeddings. Dataset samples are either used in their native resolution or scaled down
      to a maximum of 128 × 128 px in order to reduce both the number of trainable parameters and
242
      GPU memory consumption. We do not apply Z-score normalization to the datasets as previous
243
      experiments with normalization resulted in validation loss becoming unresponsive.
244
245   All autoencoder embeddings are trained in parallel on three NVIDIA V100 GPUs with a fixed
246   batch size of 100 and training times ranging from 25 to 50 minutes each, depending on dataset
247
      and embedding size. Each training run is configured analogous to Chadebec et al. (Chadebec et al.,
      2022, p. 25) to last for 100 epochs while using the Adam optimizer with an initial learning rate of
248
      10−4 and no weight decay. We parameterized the ReduceLROnPlateau learning rate scheduler
249
      to monitor the validation loss with a patience of 10 epochs and a reduction factor of 0.5. At the
250   end of each training run, only the best performing autoencoder checkpoint is saved based on its
251   validation loss.

253   3.4   B UILDING P ROJECTORS FROM AUTOENCODER E MBEDDINGS
254
255   Using the trained autoencoders, we replace the input layer of SimCLR’s default 2-layer MLP projec-
256   tor with the encoder’s pretrained embedding layer. This is achieved by matching the number of input
257   features of the pretrained embedding layer to the number of input features of the projector. Both in-
258   put dimensions depend solely on the number of output features generated by the backbone. The
259   weights within each pretrained embedding layer are frozen due to previous experiments showing an
260
      improvement in the backbone’s downstream performance.
261   We continue to modify the standard projector by replacing its ReLU activation with SiLU (Sigmoid
262   Linear Unit), sigmoid and tanh. As with SimCLR, our modified projector is activated using the
263   output features generated by its linear input layer. For this reason, the number of input features
264   in the projector’s output layer must match the number of output features produced by the input
265   layer. In order to avoid mismatches in layer dimensions during our experiments, we choose to
266
      programmatically apply these modifications to the projector’s architecture.
267   Finally, we also change the dimensionality of the projector’s embedding space by varying the num-
268   ber of output features in the output layer. Analogous to SimCLR, we limit the maximum number
269   of features present in the final projections zi , zj to 128. We additionally examine the influence of
      projecting the intermediate representations hi , hj into lower dimensional latent spaces with 64 and

      3.6    E VALUATING L EARNED R EPRESENTATIONS
327
328   For each trained backbone f , we evaluate the quality of its contrastively learned representations
329   by training a logistic regression model on the intermediate representations h using a labeled test
330   split. This approach follows the standard evaluation protocol of SimCLR and related methods.
331   The regression model is represented by a single linear layer whose number of input features equals
332   the dimensionality of h. The number of its output features is dynamically determined by the the
333   number of classes present in the test split. We train the logistic regression model for 50 epochs with
334   no weight decay, a learning rate of 10−3 and a minibatch size of 32. We apply standard softmax to
335
      the model’s logits to obtain class probabilities.
336   In order to train and evaluate the logistic regression model, we generate feature datasets for each
337   SimCLR model by first discarding the projection head and then predicting representations h = f (x)
338   for samples x from the test split using only the trained backbone. We then split each feature dataset
339   via a 70/10/20 scheme into new train, validation and test splits. After training and evaluating the
340   regression model on these datasets, we finally report its peak classification accuracy (Acc@1) while
341
      assuming linear feature separability to be a valid proxy for the quality of learned representations.

      5      D ISCUSSION
458
459
      Our experiments demonstrate that the SimCLR framework can benefit from using pretrained au-
      toencoder embeddings over a randomly initialized input layer to its nonlinear projection head. This
460
      finding does therefore confirm our initial hypothesis outlined in Section 1. We explain the success
461
      of our approach by the fact that pretraining even a simple autoencoder on a given dataset can cap-
462   ture meaningful representations in its embedding layer. These features can then be exploited within
463   the SimCLR framework by offering a priori knowledge about the high-level structure of the training
464   data. By including these predetermined features into the projector, subsequent training iterations can
465   benefit immediately from these representations. This effect cannot be achieved using the standard
466   2-layer MLP as the weights of its input layer are randomly initialized. Due to the NT-Xent loss being
467   applied only to the projections zi , zj , it is reasonable to assume that using meaningful high-level
468   representations within the projector can guide gradient descent more effectively, especially in the
469   early stages of training.
470   Considering the architectural simplicity of our autoencoder, we find the absolute and average im-
471   provements in classification accuracy to be satisfactory, especially in the case of datasets containing
472   a large number of classes. It should also be noted, that in our experiments we neither adapt the archi-
473   tecture of the backbone nor the projection head to compensate for such datasets or different sample
474   resolutions. Regardless of dataset complexity, we assume absolute improvements in classification
475   accuracy to be achievable through deeper and wider backbones trained for longer periods of time.
476
      We also suspect that pretraining more advanced autoencoder architectures could further increase the
      projector’s overall performance.
477
478   Considering the main purpose of autoencoders, we conjecture that the same features which are suit-
479   able for image reconstruction tasks, can also enhance contrastive representation learning as demon-
480   strated in this paper. Additional benefits of our approach can be observed in architectural efficiency
481   based on the decreased number of output features required for the pretrained projector to surpass
482
      the standard MLP in average classification accuracy (see Table 4). This finding suggests that the
      implicit training of the standard MLP projector within the SimCLR framework is often less efficient
483
      than the independent pretraining of an autoencoder embedding.
484
485   The outstanding performance of the sigmoid activation function in the projector may be enabled
      by its shallow architecture, which does not offer enough hidden layers for the vanishing gradient


                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      problem to arise. Due to the fact that the comparatively deep ResNet34 backbone is only using
487   ReLU as its activation function, the backwards pass is unable to generate enough derivatives in the
488   range [0, 1), which multiplied together could cause the gradient to vanish. By using the sigmoid
489   activation in the projector, the calculated gradient response at the beginning of each backward pass
490   is likely dampened by its derivative being always less than one. This however is unlikely to trigger
491   a vanishing gradient on its own.

      6   C ONCLUSIONS
494
495
      In this paper we investigate the hypothesis whether pretrained autoencoder embeddings are able to
496   improve the performance of the standard 2-layer MLP projection head used in the SimCLR frame-
497   work. Since, from a theoretical point of view, the exact role of the classical nonlinear projector
498   remains unclear, we decide to empirically validate our hypothesis on five well-known image classi-
499   fication datasets. After conducting our experiments, we are able to draw the following conclusions:
500
501         • Increase in accuracy: Using a pretrained and frozen autoencoder embedding in SimCLR’s
502           default projector can increase classification accuracy by up to 2.9 % or 1.7 % on average
503           (see Table 2).
504         • Architectural efficiency: On average, a pretrained autoencoder embedding reliably de-
505           creases projector width and the dimensionality of the associated projection space (see Ta-
506           ble 4).
507         • Projector activation: On datasets with ten classes, both projector types achieve their high-
508           est classification accuracies when trained with the sigmoid or tanh activation function (see
509           Tables 2, 5).
510         • Projector stability: By freezing the weights within the pretrained embeddings, we demon-
511           strate the long lasting efficacy of our projector across multiple training epochs. If we allow
512           the weights to be changed during training, a noticeable decrease in test accuracy is observed
513           (see Table 6).
514
515   The results summarized above open up the possibility for a wide range of future research activities.
516
      A particularly interesting research question would be whether more advanced autoencoder architec-
      tures can further improve test accuracy for datasets with a large number of classes. Other research
517
      ideas include exploring different ways of pretraining the projector’s input layer or even the entire
518
      projector itself. It might even be worthwhile to completely redesign the projector’s architecture by
519   increasing its depth and including modern concepts like attention and transformers. Especially vi-
520   sion transformers are known to benefit disproportionately from large amounts of training data, which
521   is usually readily available in the context of contrastive learning.
