                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            How can we compress large language models without compromising accuracy?
012            Quantization, which reduces the number of bits for representing weights, is an
013            essential technique to utilize large language models (LLMs) in real-world appli-
014            cations. Specifically, binary-coding quantization (BCQ) is a promising approach
015            since it has extensive representation space, which encompasses the representation
016            space of uniform quantization (UQ), and fast inference speed. However, because
017            of the lack of accurate optimization techniques, BCQ shows inferior performance
018            compared to UQ algorithms, failing to leverage their powerful expressive power.
019
               In this paper, we propose F LEX BCQ (FLEXible Binary-Coding Quantization), an
               accurate optimization algorithm for BCQ. We leverage the sophisticated optimiza-
020
               tion techniques of UQ by decomposing the quantization process of BCQ into the
021
               composition of a UQ and an inner BCQ. As a result, we take advantage of both
022            the sophisticated optimizing techniques of UQ, specifically the flexible mapping
023            technique, and the powerful expressive capability of BCQ. Through extensive ex-
024            periments, we find that F LEX BCQ provides 3.24%p higher accuracy than existing
025            UQ and BCQ algorithms on MMLU 5-shot benchmark when quantizing a Llama-
026            3 70B model into 3 bits.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
      How can we compress large language models without compromising accuracy? Reducing the size
031   of large language models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024)
032   is crucial for deploying LLMs in real-world applications, as their gigantic size makes deployment
033   challenging. Quantization (Xu et al., 2018; Kwon et al., 2022; Dettmers et al., 2022; Xiao et al.,
034   2023; Lee et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024) is a technique used to compress LLMs by reducing the
035   number of bits needed to represent their weights. It reduces the bit count by representing the model’s
036   weights as a smaller set of values, namely, quantization levels. For example, 3-bit quantization uses
037   23 distinct values to represent all the weights in the model. It is essential to use a quantization
038   scheme that aligns well with the distribution of the model’s weights, such as uniform quantization
039
      (UQ) (Lee et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024) and binary-coding quantization (BCQ) (Xu
      et al., 2018; Kwon et al., 2022), to maximize the accuracy of a quantized model.
040
041   Uniform quantization (UQ) is a scheme that has evenly-spaced quantization levels. Figure 1(a) illus-
042   trates the quantization process of Round-to-Nearest (RTN) which maps a weight w0 to the nearest
043   quantization level q3 . RTN provides an optimal mapping for approximating weight itself, but if
044   we consider the distribution of inputs, other mapping strategies encompassing farther quantization
045
      levels exhibit better accuracy (Nagel et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2023). FlexRound (b) achieves out-
      standing performance through its flexible mapping which allows weights to explore diverse quanti-
046
      zation levels flexibly and map the weights to the quantization levels that maximize the accuracy of
047
      quantized models. On the other hand, binary-coding quantization (BCQ) is a scheme that has non-
048   uniform quantization levels. Figure 1(c) illustrates the quantization process of Alternating update
049   approach (Xu et al., 2018), a representative BCQ algorithm. As shown in the figure, BCQ adapts
050   its non-uniform quantization levels close to the weight and reduces quantization errors which rep-
051   resents the distance between the weight and the mapped quantization level. Additionally, a recent
052   study (Park et al., 2024) has introduced a fast inference kernel that supports BCQ and UQ at the
053   same speed, providing faster inference speed than conventional UQ kernels (Frantar et al., 2023;
      Lin et al., 2024). This advancement positions BCQ as a promising quantization scheme, excelling

      In this paper, we propose F LEX BCQ, an accurate optimization algorithm for BCQ. We decompose
068   BCQ’s quantization process as the composition of UQ and inner BCQ to leverage the advanced
069   optimization techniques of UQ. This approach enables flexible mapping through its UQ compo-
070   nent and adaptive quantization levels via inner BCQ, combining the strengths of both schemes, as
071   shown in Figure 1 (d). We propose Unified Initialization technique which integrates the initializa-
072   tion methods of UQ and BCQ to initialize the quantization parameters of F LEX BCQ. We optimize
073   the quantization parameters of F LEX BCQ on a small sample dataset by reconstructing blockwise
074   outputs of quantized models. We propose novel optimization techniques such as Gradient Filtering
075   and Periodic Remapping to maximize the accuracy of F LEX BCQ. Finally, through Composition
076
      Theorem (Theorem 1), we prove that the decomposed quantization process is able to merge back
      into a single BCQ after optimization, showing that F LEX BCQ maintains the fast inference speed
077
      of BCQ. Through extensive experiments with diverse models on various benchmarks, we find that
078
      F LEX BCQ shows up to 3.24%p higher accuracy than existing UQ and BCQ algorithms on MMLU
079   5-shot benchmark when quantizing a Llama-3 70B model into 3 bits.
080
081   We summarize the main contributions of this paper as follows:
082          • Algorithm. We propose F LEX BCQ, an accurate BCQ algorithm which exploits both so-
083            phisticated optimization techniques of UQ algorithms and the powerful expressive capa-
084            bility of BCQ algorithms. We propose useful techniques for optimizing BCQ models that
085            effectively enhance the accuracy of quantized models. To the best of our knowledge, this is
086
               the first work that transfers UQ’s useful optimization technique to optimize BCQ.
             • Experiments. We conduct exhaustive experiments to verify the performance of
087
               F LEX BCQ. F LEX BCQ shows 3.24%p higher accuracy than existing UQ and BCQ al-
088
               gorithms on MMLU 5-shot benchmark when quantizing a Llama-3 70B model into 3 bits.
089          • Analysis. We analyze the quantized models generated by F LEX BCQ and demonstrate
090            that F LEX BCQ successfully takes advantage of FlexRound’s flexible mapping and binary-
091            coding quantization’s adaptive quantization levels, as we intended.
092
      The rest of this paper is organized as follows. We formally define LLM quantization problem and
093   provide preliminaries in Section 2. We propose F LEX BCQ in Section 3 and show our experimental
094   results in Section 4. After introducing related works in Section 5, we conclude.

191   3.1     OVERVIEW
192
193
      In this section, we propose F LEX BCQ, an accurate optimization algorithm for BCQ. The motiva-
      tion behind F LEX BCQ is to leverage advanced optimization techniques designed for UQ to optimize
194
      BCQ while retaining BCQ’s strong expressive capability. Before presenting our main idea, we out-
195
      line key challenges that must be tackled.
196
197
           C1. Formulation. How can we modify the quantizer of BCQ to leverage the useful optimization
                techniques designed for UQ without losing the strong expressive capability of BCQ?
198
           C2. Initialization. How can we effectively initialize quantization parameters to accurately cap-
199
                ture the distribution of the weights in the model?
200        C3. Optimization. How can we optimize quantization parameters on the sample dataset to
201             maximize the accuracy of quantized models?
202
      We address these challenges with the following main ideas:
203
           I1. F LEX BCQ (Section 3.2). We decompose the quantization process of conventional BCQ
204
                into the composition of UQ and inner BCQ to utilize the optimization techniques from UQ.
205
                Decomposed BCQ takes advantage of both UQ’s advanced optimization techniques and
206             BCQ’s strong expressive capability simultaneously.
207        I2. Unified Initialization (Section 3.3). We integrate the clipping range search algorithm of
208             UQ and the alternating update algorithm of BCQ to initialize the quantization parameters
209             of F LEX BCQ. Our initialization process is accurate by considering both clipping range and
210             adaptive quantization levels at the same time.
211        I3. Blockwise output reconstruction (Section 3.4). We optimize the quantization parame-
212             ters of F LEX BCQ by minimizing blockwise reconstruction errors. We propose novel opti-
213             mization techniques including Gradient Filtering and Periodic Remapping to maximize the
214
                accuracy of the quantized models.
215   We carefully analyze the quantization processes of UQ and BCQ, and design F LEX BCQ to com-
      bine the strengths of both quantization schemes. Figure 3 illustrates the calibration and deployment

298   3.3   U NIFIED I NITALIZATION
299
      After formulation, we need to optimize F LEX BCQ’s quantization parameters in ΘF to maximize
300
      the accuracy of the quantized models. We need an accurate algorithm to initialize the quantiza-
301
      tion parameters before optimization. We unify the initialization processes of UQ and BCQ since
302   F LEX BCQ has the quantization parameters of both quantization schemes. We initialize s and sr
303   as 1 to make the flexible mapping mimic the traditional rounding-to-nearest (RTN) method at the
304   beginning, following FlexRound (Lee et al., 2023). We initialize zB,(k) = (2k − 1)/2 and fix it
305   since the transformed space, in which the inner BCQ is defined, is designed for mapping weights to
306   the range [0, 2k − 1]. We propose Unified Initialization for initializing the remaining quantization
307   parameters ∆(k) , zU,(k) , and α(k) as in Algorithm 1.
308   We precisely initialize quantization parameters through an iterative process that integrates a grid
309   search-based clipping range search algorithm with the alternating quantization level adaptation (Al-
310   gorithm 2 in (Xu et al., 2018)). In each iteration, we adjust the length γ(wM − wm ) of the clipping
311   range by modifying the ratio γ fixing the minimum value wm . Within the adjusted clipping range,

      Gradient Filtering. Gradient filtering is a technique that filters the gradients of the weights that
      have large mapping errors, i.e., |w̄(k) − we(k) |, to stabilize the optimization process of F LEX BCQ.
336
      STE hypothesizes that the gradient of the transformed weight (w̄(k) ) and mapped weight (w    e(k) ) have

340   gradients of a weight w if |w̄(k) − w

341
      Periodic Remapping. Periodic remapping is a technique designed to reduce the errors induced by
342
      the excessive change of mapping between weights and quantization levels. During an optimization
343
      process, α(k) is updated according to the mapping between weights and quantization levels, and the
344   mapping between weights and quantization levels is changed by the update of α(k) . The renewed
345   mapping induces error since α(k) is updated according to the previous mapping and BCQ’s mapping
346   function MB does not guarantee the reduction of the output reconstruction error. Therefore, we set
347   a hyperparameter p and update mapping in every p steps, rather than every step.

      5   R ELATED W ORK
515
      Despite BCQ’s (Kwon et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2018) strong expressive power, it has been under-
516   investigated to uniform quantization (Lin et al., 2024; Ashkboos et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024),
517   which has a weaker expressive capability, mainly due to the lack of accelerated kernels for BCQ.
518   To the best of our knowledge, there has been no research on accurately compressing LLMs using
519   the BCQ scheme. In this context, the most important research related to BCQ is the development
520   of the LUT-GEMM kernel (Park et al., 2024), which enables BCQ and UQ to be accelerated at the
521   same speed. LUT-GEMM leverages the fact that BCQ’s binary code is composed of only 1s and -1s,
522   resulting in repetitive operations regardless of the weight values. LUT-GEMM enables fast general
523   matrix multiplication (GEMM) by referencing the look-up table (LUT) constructed based on this
524
      observation. LUT-GEMM has demonstrated that it performs inference with the OPT-175B (Zhang
      et al., 2022) model using BCQ faster than OPTQ (Frantar et al., 2023) and AWQ (Lin et al., 2024),
525
      which support the acceleration of UQ, under small batch size constraints. Although LUT-GEMM
526
      has demonstrated excellent performance under constrained environments, subsequent works, such
527   as You et al. (2024), indicate that fast kernels supporting BCQ are evolving. Precise BCQ algorithms,
528   such as F LEX BCQ, are crucial since they boost the studies of fast inference kernels for BCQ.

      6   C ONCLUSION
532
      In this paper, we propose F LEX BCQ, an accurate optimization algorithm for binary-coding quan-
533   tization (BCQ). Our motivation is to integrate uniform quantization (UQ)’s advanced optimization
534   technique into BCQ’s powerful expressive capability. We find that the UQ’s advanced optimization
535   techniques stem from its transformation process and propose a novel formulation that utilizes the
536   UQ’s transformation process for BCQ without latency overhead. Combined with our effective ini-
537   tialization and optimization techniques, F LEX BCQ shows 3.24%p higher accuracy than existing UQ
538   and BCQ algorithms on MMLU 5-shot benchmark when quantizing Llama-3 70B into 3 bits.

784
785   One critical implementation detail is about the computational speed of the mapping function MB
786   of BCQ. In Xu et al.’s Algorithm 1 (Xu et al., 2018), this mapping function is implemented using
787   a binary search tree (BST), but because of its sequential nature, it is significantly slow and makes
788   training infeasible within a reasonable time. Therefore, in this paper, we replace this operation with
789   the following GPU-friendly process: (1) calculate the values of all quantization levels, (2) compute
790   the distance between each weight and every quantization level in parallel, (3) return the index of the
791   closest quantization level for each weight, (4) mapping the weight to the corresponding quantization
792   level found in (3). We named this process as Direct BCQ Mapping process. Table 7 compares the
793

795

      In F LEX BCQ, the mapping process is performed in the space of transformed weights and we map
839   each weight to its nearest quantization level. If integers in the range [0, 2k − 1] are used as quanti-
840   zation levels, as shown in Figure 2(a), this corresponds to uniform quantization (UQ). Alternatively,
841   if a binary tree is used to represent non-uniform quantization levels, as shown in Figure 2(b), it
842   corresponds to F LEX BCQ.
843
      For simplicity, we first consider the case of uniform quantization. Weights within the clipping range
844
      [wm,c , wM,c ] are transformed to real values within [0, 2k − 1], while weights outside this range take
845   on values beyond, e.g. wm and wM . These out-of-range weights cause substantial mapping errors.
846   In the transformed space, the interval between quantization levels in UQ is 1, and each level has a
847   range of 0.5 on either side. Thus, it is reasonable to allow a margin of 0.5 even for the extreme levels
848   0 and 2k−1 .
849
      For F LEX BCQ, the value of 0.5 is replaced by the smallest scale factor min(α(k) ) in BCQ. There-
850
      fore, τ is assigned as the smallest scale factor in each weight group, automatically providing a
851   threshold suited to the group’s characteristics.
