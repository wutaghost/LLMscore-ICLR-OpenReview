009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Object-centric architectures can learn to extract distinct object representations
012            from visual scenes, enabling downstream applications on the object level. Sim-
013            ilarly to autoencoder-based image models, object-centric approaches have been
014            trained on the unsupervised reconstruction loss of images encoded by RGB color
015            spaces. In our work, we challenge the common assumption that RGB images are
016
               the optimal color space for unsupervised learning in computer vision. We discuss
               conceptually and empirically that other color spaces, such as HSV, bear essential
017
               characteristics for object-centric representation learning, like robustness to light-
018
               ing conditions. We further show that models improve when requiring them to
019            predict additional color channels. Specifically, we propose to transform the pre-
020            dicted targets to the RGB-S space, which extends RGB with HSV’s saturation
021            component and leads to markedly better reconstruction and disentanglement for
022            five common evaluation datasets. The use of composite color spaces can be im-
023            plemented with basically no computational overhead, is agnostic of the models’
024            architecture, and is universally applicable across a wide range of visual comput-
025            ing tasks and training types. The findings of our approach encourage additional
026            investigations in computer vision tasks beyond object-centric learning.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      The ability to form abstract, structured representations of the physical world is a cornerstone of
031   human intelligence (Lake et al., 2017; Fodor & Pylyshyn, 1988). It enables us to excel in dealing
032   with out-of-distribution situations, reasoning, analogy-making, and causal inference. Similarly, ma-
033   chine learning models that adapt the structural knowledge of an environment are more efficient and
034   generalizable (Schölkopf et al., 2021). Modeling language as a sequence of structured tokens was a
035   significant step toward the applicability and generalization of LLMs (Sennrich et al., 2016; Devlin
036   et al., 2019). Similarly, many computer vision applications might also profit from segmenting visual
037   scenes into semantic tokens, but inducing those is less self-evident. Thus, state-of-the-art models
038   primarily represent images as a whole or as fixed-sized grids, consequently failing at simple reason-
039
      ing that requires structural knowledge of physical entities such as counting objects (Radford et al.,
      2021).
040
041   Addressing this issue, object-centric representation learning (OCRL) aims to infer a set of slots,
042   each representing a distinct object - allowing for causal inference at the object level (Ding et al.,
043   2021). Although binding an object to a slot from raw perceptual input is challenging (Greff et al.,
044   2020a), recent advancements in OCRL, such as the Slot Attention (SA) module (Locatello et al.,
045
      2020), enabled unsupervised learning for simple synthetic datasets. However, scaling these methods
      to complex datasets proves challenging - slots attend to features considered irrelevant (Kipf et al.,
046
      2020) such as static backgrounds, represent spatial areas instead of objects (Seitzer et al., 2023), and
047
      are highly entangled (Singh et al., 2023). Subsequently, researchers investigated modified architec-
048   tures utilizing the compositional nature of objects (Biza et al., 2023; Singh et al., 2023), optimiza-
049   tion methods respecting the generative process of visual scenes (Brady et al., 2023; Wiedemer et al.,
050   2023), and (semi-)supervised objectives (Elsayed et al., 2022b; Seitzer et al., 2023). Substantial
051   performance increases of the latter highlighted that a pure RGB reconstruction loss may be a signal
052   that is too weak for slots to segment scenes (Seitzer et al., 2023). Our work follows this assumption
053   - However, we provide additional unsupervised signals by augmenting the RGB color space with
      color channels from other color spaces. We reason about our research in the following.

054
      As in many vision-based approaches, the training data for unsupervised OCRL are color images,
055   represented as red, green, and blue components, i.e., the classical RGB color space. To the best
056   of our knowledge, all previous work for OCRL trains models on such RGB scenes. The RGB
057   representation can be a sensible choice for images, primarily due to convenience in vector format
058   mapping, its byte size, and its similarity to the human eye apparatus (Podpora et al., 2014). However,
059   we argue that the RGB space is insufficient for unsupervised OCRL due to its color channels’ high
060   correlation, sensitivity to lightness, and non-uniformity. Other color spaces (which are non-linear
061   and non-continuous transformations from RGB), such as HSV, do not suffer from these problems.
062   Furthermore, they include highly discriminative features for object detection (see Figure 1) However,
063
      they introduce other drawbacks detailed in section 2. Thus, the question arises of how OCRL models
      can profit from the strengths of various color spaces while not inheriting their weaknesses - we
064
      propose to solve this problem by developing novel composite color spaces that combine expressive
065
      color channels from multiple spaces. We argue and show empirically that those color channels
066   that are robust to some effects, such as lighting or change in saturation, significantly improve the
067   performance of OCRL models.

101   2   BACKGROUND : S LOT ATTENTION
102
103   In the following, we introduce the technical background for Slot Attention (SA) (Locatello et al.,
104
      2020). We use SA for all our experiments, but our composite color spaces are transferable to other
      OCRL implementations.
105
106   Traditional encoder-decoder architectures for computer vision generate a single, fixed-size (high-
107   dimensional) latent vector z for an observation x. The latent space should accurately represent the
      underlying data distribution and capture the generative factors (Bengio et al., 2013; Locatello et al.,


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2019). In OCRL, we assume data consists of individual objects. Thus, object-centric architectures
109   aim to induce a latent space decomposed into distinct objects.
110
111   For example, SA is an iterative algorithm employed on the learned features of an encoder. Each iter-
112
      ation starts with K slots ∈ RK×Dslots and inputs ∈ RN ×Denc , the position-embedded outputs
      of an encoder. K must be user-defined or estimated. First, attention values attni,j are computed
113
      between each slot and the inputs using dot-product attention with learnable linear queries and
114
      keys (Vaswani et al., 2017). Crucially, SA introduces competition by softmax normalizing the atten-
115   tion coefficients over the slots, distributing inputs information among the slots.
116
117   After introducing competition, attention weights attn and a learnable function v embed the values
118
      to update the slots. Finally, a Gated Recurrent Unit and another MLP merge the updates into
      the slots. For the mathematical details and formulas, we refer to Locatello et al. (2020).
119
120   The SA algorithm delivers slots that distributedly store the input information. Depending on the
121   task and data, learned slot representations can be used in different downstream architectures. For
122   unsupervised object discovery, which we examine in our work, slots are spatially broadcasted and
123   decoded separately with a weight-shared decoder - the decoder usually predicts a four-dimensional
124
      output, capturing the RGB representations and masks. Masks are softmax normalized to merge
      individual reconstructions into a combined output (Greff et al., 2020b; Locatello et al., 2020).
125
126   Because RGB images strongly correlate with the lightness (see ”value” in Table 7) and the models
127   are optimized with a simple pixel-wise reconstruction loss, models predominantly focus on pre-
128   dicting the lightness accurately. Consequently, the binding mechanism discriminates spatial areas
129   primarily by a single dimension. Other perceptual variables, such as the hue and saturation (Smith,
130
      1978; Schwarz et al., 1987) that may be important to discriminate between objects are thus under-
      represented. We argue that requiring models to additionally decode complementary color channels,
131
      such as hue and saturation, provides a stronger unsupervised signal for segmenting semantic objects.

134   3     C OLOR S PACES FOR UNSUPERVISED O BJECT C ENTRIC R EPRESENTATION
135         L EARNING
136
137   In this section, we highlight the impact of color choices on unsupervised OCRL. Outgoing from the
138   common RGB color space, we discuss requirements for sensible color representations and arrive at
139   composite color spaces that combine channels with complementary information of multiple spaces.
140
141   3.1   W HY IS RGB A SUBOPTIMAL CHOICE FOR NATURAL SCENES IN OCRL?
142
143   We argue that RGB is a suboptimal color space for unsupervised OCRL, as RGB is
144
145          • strongly correlated: The RGB color space consists of the additive ”Red,” ”Green,” and
146            ”Blue” color channels. Those are all strongly correlated in natural images. We provide
147
               empirical evidence for that in Table 7, where we calculated the Pearson Correlation Coef-
               ficient between each color channel for 1000 images, respectively sampled from ClevrTex
148
               (Karazija et al., 2021), MS Coco (Lin et al., 2014), and ImageNet (ILSVRC2012) (Deng
149
               et al., 2009). The correlation not only introduces redundancy into machine learning models
150            but clashes with the perceptual variables of humans (Smith, 1978; Schwarz et al., 1987):
151            As the RGB space is optimized with a reconstruction loss, models primarily focus on pre-
152            dicting the value (or lightness). Contrarily, humans’ color vision assimilates incoming light
153            sources differently — while RGB cones produce the first physical stimulus, the stimuli are
154            transformed into the sensations of hue, colorfulness (”saturation”), and brightness (”value”)
155            (Müller & , Lipsk; Kim et al., 2009). Our brain finally uses those sensations to discriminate
156            between objects. Consequently, objects that are similar in the perception of humans (but
157            differ in some properties, e.g., their hue) are scattered by the RGB space.
158          • sensitive to lighting: The RGB channels are not only strongly correlated with each other
159            but also with the value/lightness (see Table 7). In natural scenes, the same objects might
160            be represented by entirely different RGB color histograms due to rapid changes in lighting.
161            We elaborate on this effect in subsection D.4 and even show that the slot representations in
               the HSV space are less dependent on the lighting conditions.


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
             • non-uniform in the perceived color space: Distances in uniform color spaces reflect the
163            perceived distance of humans (Paschos, 2001). The RGB space is non-uniform, leading
164            to distorted representations of visually similar features. As slots attend to similar features
165            (based on color representations), the binding mechanism might be negatively influenced.

167   3.2   A RE OTHER COLOR SPACES BETTER SUITED FOR UNSUPERVISED O BJECT C ENTRIC
168         R EPRESENTATION L EARNING ?
169
170   Other color spaces, such as HSV and CIE-LAB solve some of the problems discussed for the RGB
171   space. HSV is a cylindrical-coordinate representation of RGB, modeling the ”Hue,” ”Saturation,”
172   and ”Value.” The value strongly correlates with the RGB channels (see Table 7). Contrarily, the hue
173
      and saturation are not correlated to the RGB channels and are only slightly influenced by lighting.
      Similarly, it aligns with the perceptual variables of humans (Smith, 1978; Schwarz et al., 1987).
174
      Furthermore, it is almost perceptually uniform. CIELAB models the luminosity along with the
175
      two color dimensions. Due to the explicit modeling of luminosity, it is perceptually uniform, non-
176   correlated, and robust to light influences. HSV and CIE-LAB can be losslessly transformed from
177   the RGB space. Although those transformations are non-continuous, they can be approximated by
178   machine learning algorithms. However, we argue and empirically verify that transformations in
179   the target representations significantly influence OCRL models’ performance. As the models are
180   trained on the pixel-wise reconstruction error, models may use their predictive capacity differently
181   depending on the color space. For example, if we use RGB space, we rely on the OCRL model
182   to implicitly learn objects’ invariances due to lightness effects while we model them in HSV with
183   the hue and saturation component. We verify in subsection D.4 that the robustness of the HSV
184
      channels to different lighting conditions transfers to the slot representations - objects that share the
      same underlying factors remain more similar when confronted with different lighting.
185
186   On the contrary, HSV and CIE-LAB introduce other problems. For example, HSV’s hue is discontin-
187   uous and thus not well modeled by differentiable machine learning algorithms, and transformations
188   from CIE-LAB to RGB are not lossless. Thus, our work doesn’t rely on a single color space but
189   later creates composite color spaces. To identify which color channels are suitable for composite
190
      spaces, we first assess which color spaces impact OCRL’s performance. Therefore, we trained SA
      models with the same architecture and hyperparameters predicting RGB, CIE-LAB, or HSV scenes
191
      on the Clevr and Clevrtex datasets. The input scenes are always represented as RGB scenes, and
192
      we trained on six random seeds. Machine learning algorithms can approximate transformations of
193   the inputs and thus have less impact on the performance (Mishkin et al., 2017). We further discuss
194   input transformations in subsection D.3. We report the Foreground Adjusted Rand Index in Table 1,
195   measuring object discovery in scenes.

232   3.3   C OMBINING C OLOR S PACES
233
234   While we previously gauged between individual color spaces, we will now argue about combining
235   complementary color channels into a composite target space. Combined color spaces have been
236
      initially considered in the literature but have yet to be focused on OCRL (Du et al., 2024). Models
      trained on composite color spaces are less dependent on inherent biases of the color spaces. For
237
      example, differences in hue and saturation are subtle in the RGB space (due to the channels high
238
      correlation with the lightness) but explicitly incorporated in the HSV space. For the following pa-
239   per, we mainly use the stability and reliability of the RGB space and enrich it with complementary
240   channels of the HSV space - especially the saturation bears interesting characteristics for unsuper-
241   vised OCRL due to its non-correlation to the RGB channels, its continuity, and robustness against
242   lightness. We show that this single dimension already significantly improves the performance of ob-
243   ject detection and intra-object disentanglement (see Table 5.1.1 and Figure 4). Moreover, we tested
244   further combinations of RGB and HSV, but their improvements were less significant or unstable.
245   The SA model requires minimal change in architecture for the enhanced color spaces. We solely
246   alter the spatial broadcast decoder to predict a five-dimensional output for the RGB-S space (instead
247   of four) consisting of the RGB channels, the saturation, and the alpha masks. The parity of the
248   alpha masks among the four color channels reflects the consistency of the color channels for these
249   objects: Slots are required to model two independent but semantically relevant targets that are
250   spatially compact. Similar to the minor adjustments in the architecture, the loss function remains
251   an unsupervised reconstruction loss but includes saturation (or any other channel) as a target. For
252   our experiments, the input to the networks remains RGB. Although we argue that complementary
253
      color channels are helpful as signals in the target, we estimate that their influence is less substantial
      for inputs: The RGB color space already contains all information, and the encoder can learn the
254
      transformations. However, we consider the exploration of the input space an interesting future work,
255
      although we report mixed initial results (see Table 8).

258   4     R ELATED W ORK
259
260   To our knowledge, combined color spaces have not yet been explored for object-centric representa-
261
      tion, although they have shown promising success in computer vision tasks (Shin et al., 2002; Gowda
      & Yuan, 2019; Li et al., 2011). However, they focus on transformations on the input instead of the
262
      targets. In particular, using different color spaces such as HSV or YCbCr were investigated for chal-
263
      lenging segmentation and detection tasks (Shin et al., 2002; Vandenbroucke et al., 2003; Taipalmaa
264   et al., 2020). The choice of input color space might have been a typical approach in feature engi-
265   neering. However, with the advent of deep learning-based methods, such feature engineering has
266   lost its importance in favor of simple and general methods (Mishkin et al., 2017). Existing work has
267   focused on general color transformations Miao & Wang (2023), the usage of a single alternate color
268   space (Lim et al., 2020), or colorspace conversion within autoencoders (Akbarinia & Gil-Rodrı́guez,
269   2021). All have shown mixed results, primarily due to the usage of whole color spaces. Only very
      recently, the use of composite color spaces (Tan et al., 2023) has been investigated in deep learning,


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      albeit for image colorization (Du et al., 2024). We revisit color space transformations for unsuper-
271   vised OCRL. The challenging nature of this task and the transformation of the output (instead of the
272   input) makes our work conceptually different from all presented work.
273
274
      The observation that the RGB pixel-wise reconstruction loss does not suffice to bind semantic ob-
      jects in natural scenes is discussed in the literature (Seitzer et al., 2023; Kipf et al., 2022). Conse-
275
      quently, Elsayed et al. (2022a) enhance the RGB output space with a supervised depth signal for
276
      object discovery in videos. They build their work on Kipf et al. (2022) which find that SA for videos
277   strongly improves when conditioning slots on objects in the first frame. Similarly, Kim et al. (2023)
278   utilize point-based descriptors to prohibit slots to focus on backgrounds. While all of the above
279   works utilize pixel-wise reconstruction losses, Seitzer et al. (2023); Qian et al. (2023); Wen et al.
280   (2024); Aydemir et al. (2023) abstain from training slots on a reconstruction error and instead predict
281   semantic semi-supervised signals. Similarly, Xu et al. (2022) leverage embedded text descriptions
282   as supervision for semantic segmentation of images. Another unsupervised alternative is discussed
283   by Brady et al. (2023) and Wiedemer et al. (2023) - they leverage derivatives in the loss function
284   to explicitly model the compositionality of OCRL scenes. In our evaluation, we also test whether
285
      the additional color channels influence the representation of underlying generating factors in slots.
      Biza et al. (2023) explicitly disentangle position, size, and pose information by invariant position
286
      embeddings. Majellaro et al. (2024) extend this framework to disentangle the shape and texture of
287
      objects. Singh et al. (2023) introduce the concept of blocks that bind different factors of objects, and
288   Stammer et al. (2024) discretize those blocks. Combined color spaces can be used complementary
289   to those approaches.

294
      For our experiments, we investigate the impact of five color spaces on unsupervised Slot Attention.
      Our baselines are RGB and HSV, and we utilize RGB-S, RGB-SV, and RGB-HSV as combined
295
      color spaces. For comparability, all models use the same architecture; only the final output layer
296
      is adjusted to match the dimension of the color spaces. As motivated in subsection 3.2, we only
297   transform the targets, and the input remains RGB.
298
299   We mainly evaluate object discovery, measuring whether slots segment scenes into distinct objects.
300
      In line with previous work, we report the Foreground-Adjusted Rand Index (FG-ARI) and the mean
      Intersection over Union (mIoU). Additionally, we report the RGB-MSE for scene reconstruction
301
      quality. Note that if a network predicts additional color channels, they are not considered for the
302
      MSE measurement, and HSV is algorithmically transformed to RGB for this to work. Furthermore,
303   we evaluate how well the slot representations generalize to the underlying generating factors of
304   objects. We utilize the DCI framework (Eastwood & Williams, 2018) in line with Singh et al.
305   (2022). Previous work investigated that unsupervised SA trained on RGB mainly focuses on low-
306   level features such as color statistics (Seitzer et al., 2023) - we show that one additional, uncorrelated
307   color channel already suffices to achieve a significantly higher generalization of slots. We evaluate
308   the color spaces on five multi-object datasets, namely Clevr, MultiShapeNet (4 and 24), Clevrtex,
309   and Movi-C (Johnson et al., 2016; Stelzner et al., 2021; Karazija et al., 2021; Greff et al., 2022). We
310   treat Movi-C as an image dataset. Model and optimization parameters are detailed in Appendix A.
311
      Furthermore, we provide qualitative results in Appendix D.

447
448   We tested how well the slots represent the objects they binded. We, therefore, run two tests to
449   predict their underlying generating factors (Locatello et al., 2019). The first test is to train a linear
450   and shallow non-linear predictor that should map from the slot representations to the factors of
451   variation of the Clevrtex objects: Those are uniformly sampled from four shapes, three sizes, and 59
452   materials. We matched objects to slots based on the maximal mask overlap to construct the dataset to
453   train and evaluate the predictor. Average precision is shown in Figure 4 (left and middle). Although
454
      the quality of slot representations was shown to correlate with the FG-ARI (Wenzel et al., 2022), the
      quality of slot representations differs strongly, especially for the materials: The HSV and RGB-S
455
      model at least double the precision of the RGB model. While the additional color dimensions do
456
      not directly influence the size and shape, the material is mainly determined by hue, saturation, and
457   value. We suspect that the explicit representation helps slots generalize to underlying factors.

494   6   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
495
496   By only requiring a different output representation, our approach is generally applicable to all visual
497   scenes; nonetheless, there are several limitations in our approach and evaluation: We only consid-
498   ered preexisting color channels of the most common color spaces, and there may be better com-
499   posite channels suited for object detection. Analyzing whether a non-correlated RGB space, e.g.,
500   by applying PCA, already improves the scene segmentation is a promising starting point. Further-
501   more, we base our argument on the non-correlation to detect suitable complementary color channels.
502   However, more elaborate techniques considering the data distribution of pixels may be needed to
503   filter for complementary dimensions efficiently. Furthermore, we only evaluated our enhanced color
504
      spaces for OCRL tasks, but they might generally apply to a broader field of computer vision. At the
      same time, we based our discussion only on natural images. Finally, we only tested and compared
505
      the enhanced color spaces on SA - however, recent approaches based on self-supervised learning
506
      and pre-trained encoders have shown promising results - using composite color spaces (e.g., in the
507   pretraining method) might lead to further performance improvement, but is impossible to assess a
508   priori. An interesting direction for future research is if the performance leaps are achieved by using
509   composite color space transfer to other unsupervised networks for visual computing. We started the
510   elaboration of this paper by investigating the influence of the visual scene data representation on the
511   performance of unsupervised OCRL. Our work is a starting point, showing promising performance
512   without negative effects in OCRL, bearing the potential for numerous research directions: A first
513   step is to evaluate the design space of combined color spaces - other channels than saturation may
514   bear similar potentials, providing a consistent target to discriminate between objects. Furthermore,
515
      we designed the color space for OCRL, but combining color spaces got less attention in computer
      vision in general. However, a combination of color spaces still needs to be explored.
516
517
518   7   C ONCLUSION
519
520   We challenge the common assumption that RGB images are the optimal color space for unsuper-
521   vised learning in computer vision (Caron et al., 2021; Radford et al., 2021; Locatello et al., 2020)
522   and demonstrate this for OCRL. We highlighted that the RGB space is not only highly correlated,
523   effectively reducing the learned target to one dimension, but that composite color spaces offer non-
524
      correlated dimensions while upholding expressive features for OCRL. In a evaluation, we tested
      the LAB and the HSV color space as complimentary targets and found that HSV shows interesting
525
      properties due to its non-correlatedness and similarity to human perception. However, while HSV
526
      improves models on some datasets, it leads to degeneration for other datasets due to the disconti-
527   nuity in the Hue channel. We thus combined the stability of the RGB space with the uncorrelated,
528   expressive channels of HSV, constructing combined color spaces. Training models on the combined
529   color spaces leaves the main architecture unchanged, is broadly applicable, virtually cost-free, and
530   significantly improves performance on object detection. Especially the RGB-S space, combining
531   the RGB space with the Saturation channel (significantly) enhances performance on object discov-
532   ery and scene segmentation across all five datasets. Most significantly, on the challenging Clevrtex
533   dataset, we increase the FG-ARI from 75.6 to 92.7. We further show evidence that the combined
534   color spaces also improve performance on photo-realistic datasets such as Movi-C and improve the
535
      mIoU from 21.3 to 27.2. Similarly, we highlight that training on the pure HSV color space can
      sometimes improve the FG-ARI further but lacks stability on other datasets. Moreover, we eval-
536
      uated the extracted slots’ capability to bind object property and noticed significant improvements
537
      from 27.1 % to 57.8 % average precision. To summarize, the composite RGB-S and RGB-SV color
538   space (significantly) outperforms RGB in any experiment without negative side effects or additional
539   cost.

898
      While the metrics for object discovery are well established for OCRL (Locatello et al., 2020; Biza
899   et al., 2023; Seitzer et al., 2023), we provide more details about our experiments of underlying
900   generating factors. There we use the DCI metrics of Eastwood & Williams (2018). According to
901   Eastwood & Williams (2018), those metrics measure:
902
903         • Disentanglement: ”The degree to which a representation factorizes or disentangles the
904           underlying factors of variation, with each variable (or dimension) capturing at most one
905           generative factor”
906         • Completeness: ”The degree to which a single code variable captures each underlying fac-
907           tor”
908         • Informativeness: ”The amount of information that a representation captures about the
909           underlying factors of variation”
910
911   The classifier and DCI metrics require slots to be matched to a ground truth object. We match
912   pairs by the largest overlap of the ground truth mask with the decoded alpha mask of slots. For the
913
      property prediction experiments, we trained two classifiers. The linear one directly maps from the
      slot representations to one-hot encoded property predictions, while the non-linear one additionally
914
      includes a ReLu-activated intermediate layer with 128 dimensions. We utilize an external library
915
      available at Loc for the DCI experiments. We use a decision tree classifier with a batch size of 32.

950   A.4   I MPLEMENTATION OF C OLOR SPACES
951
952   In the main text, we regularly discuss RGB, CIELAB, and HSV as color spaces. However, while the
953   term ”color spaces” is regularly used, it is imprecise: For example, RGB comprises a collection of
954   additive color spaces, and arguably its most important representative is the standard RGB (sRGB)
955   space - images in the web are usually represented by the sRGB space if not tagged otherwise. For
956
      our paper, we do not discriminate between the different implementations, and our reasoning is not
      based on a specific implementation. Contrarily, we argue that adding additional color channels leads
957
      to output representations that are less statistics-based but lead to more semantic segmentations. We
958
      utilize the Tensorflow implementation (tfi) to convert images to other color spaces.

1005   C     C OLOR S PACES AND THE BIOLOGICAL VISUAL SYSTEM
1006
1007   We predominantly used the RGB and HSV color space in the main text for our experimental eval-
1008   uation. We motivate their usage in subsection 3.2 with analogies to the human visual system. This
1009   section serves as a basic introduction to the color perception of humans. We refer to Müller &
1010   (Lipsk) and Kim et al. (2009) for details.
1011
       Almost all theories of human color vision are based on the zone model of Müller & (Lipsk); Kim
1012   et al. (2009). Physical light waves stimulate three types of cones (short-, middle-, and long-wave)
1013   and rods in the pupil. Neurons combine those physical signals, yielding achromatic brightness, hue,
1014   and colorfulness sensations. Those are finally propagated with nerve fibers to the visual cortex.
1015   While the RGB color space is designed in analogy to the rudimentary physical activations of the
1016   cones, the HSV space is closely related to the sensations propagated to our visual cortex (Smith,
1017   1978; Schwarz et al., 1987). In our work, we report that using additional color channels closely
1018   related to how humans perceive the world significantly improves the object detection capabilities of
1019   unsupervised OCRL networks.

       D.5   S LOT ATTENTION M APS
1159
       We extend our analysis of learned slot parameters from subsection 5.2 by inspecting the attention
1160   maps for each slot. To get an overview of the whole model, we aim to visualize not only single
1161   scenes but the whole Clevrtex test set. For each scene, we compute the α mask for every slot and
1162   derive the weighted (x,y)-means of the mask per slot. The mean (x,y)-values (per slot) are presented
1163   in Figure 6 for six models trained on RGB, RGB-S, and HSV.
1164
       While all slots, independent of the color space, occupy a spatial area, the distributions of the center
1165
       points of the attention maps vary: For four models of the RGB space, the attention maps centers
1166   differ only slightly - those represent degenerated slots, binding fixed-size grids instead of objects.
1167   In our experiments, this behavior appears regularly for models trained on RGB and is also observed
1168   in the related literature(Seitzer et al., 2023; Locatello et al., 2020). This behavior does not happen
1169   as frequently with composite color spaces. We suppose that the complementary information of
1170   the additional color channels provides targets that pressure models to discriminate between objects
1171   and backgrounds - those might be similar in the RGB space due to their strong correlation with
1172   lighting effects but strongly differ in the uncorrelated HSV space. Those features (e.g., in color and
1173   saturation) must be represented by the underlying slot representations.

1175   D.6   C OMPARISON TO S ELF -S UPERVISED M ETHODS
1176
       Although self-supervised targets are not the main focus of our research, recent related work (Seitzer
1177
       et al., 2023) has highlighted their remarkable performance on challenging real-world datasets. Thus,
1178
       we compare our composite color spaces to the recent Dinosaur (Seitzer et al., 2023) architecture.
1179   We run experiments on the Clevrtex and Movi-C datasets using the same hyperparameter setting
1180   as described in (Seitzer et al., 2023) for Movi-C. However, we exchange the Vit-s8 backbone for a
1181   ResNet34 variant for comparability to our networks. We utilize training signals from a DINO (Caron
1182   et al., 2021) Vit-b16. The Dinosaur models with the ResNet variant show superior performance over
1183   the composite color spaces on Movi-C (FG-ARI 53.1 ± 1.3, mIoU 22.7 ± 0.6) but lack performance
1184   on Clevrtex (FG-ARI 22.1 ± 10.7, mIoU 11.2 ± 4.8). Related work Zhao et al. (2024) optimized a
1185   Dinosaur model for Clevrtex that does not degenerate, but its performance (FG-ARI ≈ 80.0) is still
1186   far behind the composite color spaces.
