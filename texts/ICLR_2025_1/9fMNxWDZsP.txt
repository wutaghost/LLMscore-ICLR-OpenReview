009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Concept-based explanations have become a popular choice for explaining deep
012            neural networks post-hoc because, unlike most other explainable AI techniques,
013            they can be used to test high-level visual “concepts” that are not directly related
014            to feature attributes. For instance, the concept of “stripes” is important to classify
015            an image as a zebra. Concept-based explanation methods, however, require prac-
016            titioners to guess and collect multiple candidate concept image sets, which can
017            often be imprecise and labor-intensive. Addressing this limitation, in this paper,
018            we frame concept image set creation as an image generation problem. However,
019
               since naively using a generative model does not result in meaningful concepts, we
               devise a reinforcement learning-based preference optimization (RLPO) algorithm
020
               that fine-tunes the vision-language generative model from approximate textual
021
               descriptions of concepts. Through a series of experiments, we demonstrate the
022            capability of our method to articulate complex and abstract concepts which aligns
023            with the test class that are otherwise challenging to craft manually. In addition to
024            showing the efficacy and reliability of our method, we show how our method can
025            be used as a diagnostic tool for analyzing neural networks.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In an era where black box deep neural networks (DNNs) are becoming seemingly capable of
031   performing general enough tasks, our ability to explain their decisions post-hoc has become even
032   more important before deploying them in the real world. Among many use cases, explaining the
033   behavior of DNNs enable engineers and regulatory bodies to assess the correctness of a DNN’s
034   decision-making process and characterize the limits of what the DNN knows. Thus, an explainable
035   AI (XAI) method’s ability to correctly communicate information in a human-centric way is essential
036
      for its usefulness.
037   Humans utilize high-level concepts as a medium for providing and perceiving explanations. In
038   this light, post-hoc concept-based explanation techniques, such as Testing with Concept Activation
039   Vectors (TCAV) (Kim et al., 2018), have gained great popularity in recent years. Their ability to
040   use abstractions that are not necessarily feature attributes or some pixels in test images helps with
041   communicating these high-level concepts with humans. For instance, as demonstrated in TCAV, the
042
      concept of stripes is important to explain why an image is classified as a zebra, whereas the concept of
      spots is important to explain why an image is classified as a jaguar. Given 1) a set of such high-level
043
      concepts, represented as sample images (e.g., a collection of stripe images and a collection of spot
044
      images) and 2) test images of the class (e.g., zebra images), TCAV assigns a score to each concept on
045   how well the concept explains the class decision (i.e., zebra).
046
047   Although concept-based XAI methods are a good representation, their requirement to create col-
048
      lections of candidate concept sets necessitate the human to know which concepts to test for. This
      is typically done by guessing what concepts might matter and manually extracting such candidate
049
      concept tests from existing datasets. While the stripe-zebra analogy is attractive as an example, where
050
      it is obvious that stripes is important to predict zebras, in most applications, we cannot guess what
051   concepts to test for, limiting the usefulness of concept-based methods in testing real-world systems.
052   Additionally, even if a human can guess a few concepts, it does not encompass most concepts a DNN
053   has learned because the DNN was trained without any human intervention. Therefore, it is important
      to automatically find human-centric concepts that matter to the DNN’s decision-making process.

      Testing with Concept Activation Vectors (TCAV): The TCAV score quantifies the importance of
112   a “concept” for a specific class in a DNN classifier (Kim et al., 2018). Here, a concept is defined
113   broadly as a high-level, human-interpretable idea such as stripes, sad faces, etc. A concept (e.g.,
114   stripes), c, is represented by sample images, Xc (e.g., images of stripes). In TCAV, a human has
115   manually collected these sample concept images based on educated guesses, whereas our objective is
116   to automatically generate them. For a given set of test images, Xm (e.g., zebra images), that belongs
117   to the same decision class (e.g., zebra), m, TCAV is defined as the fraction of test images for which
118   the model’s prediction increases in the “direction of the concept.” By decomposing the DNN under
119   test as f (x) = f2 (f1 (x)), where f1 (x) is the activation at layer l, TCAV score is computed as,

129   ACE (Ghorbani et al., 2019) introduced a way to automatically find concepts by extracting relevant
130   concepts from the input class. It used segmentation over different resolution to get a pool of segments
131   and then grouped them based on similarity to compute TCAV scores. Though the ACE concepts
132   are human understandable, they are noisy because of the segmentation and clustering errors. As a
133   different method, EAC (Sun et al., 2024) extracts concepts through segmentation. CRAFT (Fel et al.,
134   2023) introduced a recursive strategy to detect and decompose concepts across layers. Lens (Fel
135   et al., 2024) elegantly unified concept extraction and importance estimation as a dictionary learning
136   problem. However, since all these methods obtain concepts from test images, the concepts they
137
      generate tend to be very similar to the actual class (e.g., a patch of zebra as a concept to explain the
      zebra instead of stripes as a concept), making it challenging to maintain the “high-level abstractness”
138
      of concepts. In contrast, we generate concepts from a generative model. Under generative models,
139
      LCDA (Yan et al., 2023) simply queries an LLM to get attributes but does not generate concepts.

      Preference Optimization: Directly optimizing generative models with preference data was first
154
      introduced in Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024). It is a technique used
155
      to ensure models, such as large language models, learn to align its outputs with human preference
156   by asking a human which of its generated output is preferred. This technique was later extended to
157   diffusion models in Diffusion-DPO (Wallace et al., 2023), where they updated Stable Diffusion XL
158   model using Pick-a-Pic dataset (human preferred generated image dataset). Unlike traditional image
159   or text generation tasks, where the dataset for human preferred outputs are readily available, it is
160   hard to have a general enough dataset for XAI tasks. To counter this problem, we provide preference
161   information by using the TCAV score instead of a human, and use it to align the text-to-image
      generative model to generate concept images that matters for the neural network under test.

209   3.1      T HE R ATIONALES B EHIND D ESIGN C HOICES
210
211
      Before presenting the algorithm in detail, we provide the rationale for design choices, which are
      validated through ablation studies in Section 4.1-4.2, and comparisons in the Section 4.3-4.4.
212
213   Rationale 1: Why concept generation is a better idea. Let us denote the set of human-interpretable
214   concepts that the f (·) has learned be CN . If we use concept-based explanation the traditional
215   way (Kim et al., 2018; Schut et al., 2023), then the end users need to manually guess what concepts
      to test for. Automatically retrieving the concept set by segmenting test images (Sun et al., 2024)


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      also results in a limited concept set. In contrast, a SOTA generative model can generate high quality
217   images. We provide more theoretical insights in Appendix B.
218
219   Rationale 2: Why a deep RL-controlled VLM fine-tuning for generating concepts is a better
220
      idea. “A picture is worth a thousand words but words flow easier than paint.”
221   As the saying goes, “a picture is worth a thousand words,” it is much easier for people to explain and
222   understand high-level concepts when images are used instead of language. For instance, we need a
223   long textual description such as “The circles are centered around a common point, with alternating
224   red and white colors creating a pattern” to describe a simple image of a dart board (i.e., Target Co.
225   logo). Therefore, we keep our ultimate concept representation as images. However, controlling a
226
      generative model from visual inputs is much harder. However, since human language can be used as
      a directed and easier way to seed our thought process, as the saying goes, “words flow easier than
227
      paint,” we control the use of text prompts. Since the vastness of the search space cannot be handled
228
      by most traditional search strategies, we resort to a DQN for controlling text. Since simple text alone
229   cannot generate complex, high-level visual concepts, in each DQN update step, we use preference
230   optimization to further guide the search process towards more preferred outcome, allowing the DQN
231   to focus on states similar to the target. This approach improves our starting points for each DQN
232   episode, enabling more efficient search and incremental progress towards the desired target.

236   Since a generative model can generate arbitrary images, if we provide good starting point for
237   optimization then the convergence to explainable states would be faster. In this paper, to extract
238   seed prompts for a particular class we make use of the off-the-shelf VQA model followed by several
239   preprocessing steps, as described in Appendix C.3. We also explore how random gibberish prompts
240   can be used as seed prompts in Appendix C.4 which did not yield useful concepts.

272   At time t, the policy picks the seed prompt kt , which is then used by the generative model, g(kt ; wt ),
273   with model weights w, to generate 2Z number of images. We randomly divide the generated images
274   into two groups: Xc1 ,t = {xc1 ,t,i }Z                              Z
                                              i=1 and Xc2 ,t = {xc2 ,t,i }i=1 . Let the TCAV scores of each
275   group be T Sc1 ,m,t and T Sc2 ,m,t . Since our objective is to find concepts that generate a higher
276   TCAV score, concept images that have a higher score is preferred. Note that, unlike in the classical
277   preference optimization setting with a human to rank, RLPO preference comes from the TCAV
278   scores (e.g., T Xc1 ,t ≻ T Xc2 ,t ). We call this notion RLPO-XAI in some ablation studies below. If
279   the generative model at time t is not capable of generating concepts that are in an explainable state,
280
      max(T Sc1 ,m,t , T Sc2 ,m,t ) ≤ η, we then perform preference update on SD’s weights (more details in
      Appendix C.5). Following Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021), we only learn auxiliary
281
      weights a and b at each time step, and update the weights as wt+1 ← wt + λab.
282
283   As the deep RL agent progresses over time, the states become more relevant as it approaches
284   explainable states (Fig. 1), thus the same action yields increasing rewards over time. To accommodate
285   this, with reference to the rewards defined in Section 3.3, we introduce a parameter ξ, which starts at
286
      0.1 and incrementally rises up to 1 as the preference threshold, η, is approached. Different actions
      may result in different explainable states, reflecting various high-level concepts inherent to f (·).
287
      Some actions might take longer to reach an explainable state. Also, it is possible for different actions
288
      to lead to the same explainable state. As the goal is to optimize all states to achieve a common
289   target, DQN progressively improves action selection to expedite reaching these states. Thus, deep
290   RL becomes relevant as it optimizes over time to choose the actions that are most likely to reach an
291   explainable state more efficiently.

       D.2   H UMAN AND LLM- BASED AI F EEDBACK M ECHANISMS
1027
1028   We test other feedback mechanism in RL by replacing the XAI-TCAV feedback with AI and Human
1029   feedback’s. Herer we discuss the experiental setup and configuration for both experiements.
1030
1031
       AI feedback: GPT-4 is leveraged to evaluate the explanatory power of image sets by focusing
       on concepts related to a target class, a method that aligns with the growing trend of incorporating
1032
       AI-driven feedback Bai et al. (2022). This approach involves sending a structured prompt to an LLM,
1033
       asking it to score how well two sets of images explain a target class using a specified concept. The
1034   process involves the following.
1035
1036         1. Image Encoding: The images from two sets (concept1 and concept2) are first converted into
1037            a base64 format to ensure they can be transmitted via the request as encoded strings.
1038
1039         2. Structured Prompt: A detailed and specific prompt is crafted for the LLM. It asks the model
1040            to assess the quality of explanation each image set provides for a particular class through the
1041
                lens of a specific concept. The prompt used is “Please evaluate each of the following sets of
                images for how well they explain the class {class_name} via the concept {concept_name}.
1042
                For each set, provide a numerical score between 0 and 1 (to two decimal places)” The
1043
                prompt clearly defines how the model should respond, asking for a numerical score between
1044            0 and 1, where:
1045
1046             (a) 0 indicates that the image set does not explain the class at all via the concept.
1047             (b) 1 indicates that the image set perfectly explains the class via the concept.
1048
1049         3. LLM-Based Scoring: Once the prompt is sent to the LLM, it evaluates the image sets and
1050
                provides scores based on its learned knowledge and understanding. The response is parsed
                to extract the scores for each set of images.
1051
1052
1053
       Human feedback: In this experiment, 7 computer science majors provided live feedback after
       each step of a reinforcement learning process, leveraging their prior knowledge of reinforcement
1054
       learning with human feedback (RLHF) mechanisms Christiano et al. (2017). The feedback from all
1055
       participants was averaged to serve as the reward for each step in the RL process. Given the abstract
1056   nature of the initial concepts, participants needed to take time to thoughtfully assess each step, which
1057   contributed to a lengthier feedback cycle.

1283   D.5      E FFECTS OF F INE - TUNING
1284
1285   When fine-tuning a model, the optimization process updates its weights through gradient-based
1286   methods, causing shifts in the concepts (Fig. 9) it learns. These weight adjustments modify how the
1287   model attends to different regions or patterns in an image, leading to changes in the internal activation
1288   maps and the conceptual understanding of the input. As the model learns new concepts or refines
1289   existing ones, it adjusts its feature extraction and decision-making processes to better align with the
1290   specific objectives of the fine-tuning task, thereby altering the way it interprets and generates outputs.
1291   To demonstrate this experiment, a ResNet50 model, defined in Appendix C.1, was used. We fine-
1292   tuned only the final layer of the neural network by setting the learning rate to 0.001 and momentum
1293   to 0.9 using the Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) optimizer. Maintaining a low learning rate was
1294   crucial to preserving high accuracy. RLPO was then applied to the fine-tuned ResNet50, and a shift in
1295   the learned concepts was observed, as shown in Fig. 9. This process highlights the model’s sensitivity
       to fine-tuning and how training on a subset can shift its conceptual interpretation of images.
