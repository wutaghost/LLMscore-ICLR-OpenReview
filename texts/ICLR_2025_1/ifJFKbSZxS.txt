009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP) is a powerful framework used to address a
012            wide range of NP-hard combinatorial optimization problems, often solved by Branch and
013            bound (B&B). A key factor influencing the performance of B&B solvers is the variable
014            selection heuristic governing branching decisions. Recent contributions have sought to
015            adapt reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms to the B&B setting to learn optimal branch-
016            ing policies, through Markov Decision Processes (MDP) inspired formulations, and ad
017
               hoc convergence theorems and algorithms. In this work, we introduce B&B MDPs, a
               principled vanilla MDP formulation for variable selection in B&B, allowing to leverage a
018
               broad range of RL algorithms for the purpose of learning optimal B&B heuristics. Compu-
019
               tational experiments validate our model empirically, as our branching agent outperforms
020            prior state-of-the-art RL agents on four standard MILP benchmarks.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP) is a subfield of combinatorial optimization (CO), a discipline
026   that aims at finding solutions to optimization problems with large but finite sets of feasible solutions.
027   Specifically, mixed-integer linear programming addresses CO problems that are NP-hard, meaning that no
028
      polynomial-time resolution algorithm has yet been discovered to solve them. Mixed-integer linear programs
      are used to solve efficiently a vast range of high-dimensional combinatorial problems, spanning from op-
029
      erations research (Hillier & Lieberman, 2015) to the fields of deep learning (Tjeng et al., 2019), finance
030
      (Mansini et al., 2015), computational biology (Gusfield, 2019), and fundamental physics (Barahona, 1982).
031   MILPs are traditionally solved using Branch and bound (B&B) (Land & Doig, 1960), an algorithm which
032   methodically explores the space of solutions by dividing the original problem into smaller sub-problems,
033   while ensuring the optimality of the final returned solution. Intensively developed since the 1980s (Bixby,
034   2012), MILP solvers based on the B&B algorithm are high-performing tools. In particular, they rely on com-
035   plex heuristics fine-tuned by experts on large heterogeneous benchmarks (Gleixner et al., 2021). Hence, in
036   the context of real-world applications, in which similar instances with slightly varying inputs are solved on
037   a regular basis, there is a huge incentive to reduce B&B total solving time by learning efficient tailor-made
038   heuristics. The branching heuristic, or variable selection heuristic, which determines how to iteratively par-
039
      tition the space of solutions, has been found to be critical to B&B computational performance (Achterberg
      & Wunderling, 2013). Over the last decade, many contributions have sought to harness the predictive power
040
      of machine learning (ML) to learn better-performing B&B heuristics (Bengio et al., 2021; Scavuzzo et al.,
041
      2024). By using imitation learning (IL) to replicate the behaviour of a greedy branching expert at lower
042   computational cost, Gasse et al. (2019) established a landmark result as they first managed to outperform a
043   solver relying on human-expert heuristics. Building on the works of Gasse et al. (2019) and He et al. (2014),
044   who proposed a Markov decision process (MDP) formulation for node selection in B&B, several contribu-
045   tions succeeded in learning efficient branching strategies by reinforcement (Etheve et al., 2020; Scavuzzo
046   et al., 2022; Parsonson et al., 2022), without surpassing the performance achieved by the IL approach. Yet,

151   2.3   R ELATED WORK
152
153   Following the seminal work by Gasse et al. (2019), few contributions have proposed to build more complex
154   neural network architectures based on transformers (Lin et al., 2022) and recurrence mechanisms (Seyfi et al.,
155
      2023) to improve the performance of IL branching agents, with moderate success. In parallel, theoretical and
      computational analysis (Bestuzheva et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2022) have shown that neural networks trained
156
      by imitation could not rival the tree size performance achieved by strong branching (SB), the branching
157
      expert used in Gasse et al. (2019). In fact, low tree sizes associated with SB turn out to be primarily due to
158   the formulation improvements resulting from the massive number of LPs solved in SB, not to the intrinsic
159   quality of the branching decisions themselves.
160
161
      Since branching decisions are made sequentially, reinforcement learning appears as a natural candidate to
      learn good branching policies. Etheve et al. (2020) and Scavuzzo et al. (2022) proposed the model of
162
      TreeMDP, in which state si = (Pi , x∗LP,i , x̄i ) consists in the MILP associated with node vi along with
163
      the solution of its linear relaxation and the incumbent solution at vi . The actions available at si is the set
164   of fractional variables in x∗LP,i . Given (si , ai ) the tree Markov transition function produces two child node
165
      states (s−    +
               i , si ) that can be visited in any order. Crucially, when the B&B tree is explored in depth-first-search
166
      (DFS), TreeMDP trajectories can be divided in independent subtrees, allowing to learn policies minimizing
167   the size of each subtree independently. This helps mitigate credit assignment issues that arise owing to the
168   length of episode trajectories. Subsequently, Parsonson et al. (2022) found the DFS node selection policy
169   to be highly detrimental to the computational performance of RL branching strategies. Assuming that RL
170   branching agents trained following advanced node selection strategies would perform better despite the lack
171   of theoretical guarantee, they proposed to learn from retrospective trajectories, diving trajectories built from
172   original TreeMDP episodes. In fact, Parsonson et al. (2022) found retrospective trajectories to alleviate the
173   partial observability induced by the “disordered” exploration of the tree and outperform prior RL agents.
174   A large body of work has proposed to learn, either by imitation or reinforcement, better-performing B&B
175   heuristics outside of variable selection (Nair et al., 2021; Paulus et al., 2022). RL contributions in primal
176   search (Sonnerat et al., 2022; Wu & Lisser, 2023) node selection (He et al., 2014; Etheve, 2021) and cut se-
177   lection (Tang et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2023) have all relied on the TreeMDP framework to
178   train their agents, simply adapting the action set to the task at hand. Finally, machine learning applications
179   in combinatorial optimization are not limited to B&B. For example, in the context of routing or schedul-
180   ing problems where exact resolution rapidly becomes prohibitive, agents are trained to learn direct search
181
      heuristics (Kool et al., 2019; Grinsztajn et al., 2022; Chalumeau et al., 2023) yielding high-quality feasible
      solutions.

184   3     B RANCH AND BOUND M ARKOV DECISION PROCESS
185
186   By using the current B&B node as the observable state, prior attempts to learn optimal branching strategies
187   have relied on the TreeMDP formalism to train RL agents. However, TreeMDPs are not MDPs, as they


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


188
      do not define a Markov process on the state random variable (for instance, a transition yields two states
189
      and is hence not a stochastic process on the state variables). As a result, this forces Etheve et al. (2020)
190   and Scavuzzo et al. (2022) to redefine Bellman updates and derive ad hoc convergence theorems for TD(0),
191   value iteration, and policy gradient algorithms. In order to leverage broader theoretical results from the
192   reinforcement learning literature, we propose a description of variable selection in B&B as a proper Markov
193   decision process.
194
195   3.1   D EFINITION
196
197   The problem of finding an optimal branching strategy according to Equation (1) can be described as a
198   regular deterministic Markov decision process. To this end, we introduce Branch and bound Markov decision
199
      processes (BBMDP) by making the tuple (S, A, T , p0 , R) explicit, taking γ = 1 since episodes horizons
      are bounded by the (finite) largest possible number of nodes:
200
201          • State space. S is the set of all B&B trees st = (Vt , Et , x̄t ). Note that this includes intermediate
202            B&B trees, whose incumbent solutions x̄t are yet to be proven optimal.
203
             • Action space. A is the set of all integer variables indices I.
204
205
             • Transition function: The Markov transition function is defined as T = κρ with κρ the branching
               operation described in Section 2.1. Note that if the variable associated with at is not fractional in
206
               x∗LP,t , then st+1 = T (st , at ) = st as relaxing a variable that is not fractional has no impact on the
207
               LP relaxation. Importantly, all states for which O = ∅ are terminal states.
208
209          • Starting states. Initial states are single node trees, where the root node is associated to a MILP
210
               P0 drawn according to the distribution p0 defined in Section 2.1 (hence the use of p0 for both the
               initial problem P0 and the MDP’s initial state s0 ).
211
212          • Reward model. We define R(s, a) = −2 for all transitions until episode termination. Since each
213            transition results in the addition of two B&B nodes, the overall value of a trajectory is the opposite
214
               of the number of node added to the B&B tree from the root node, which is inline with the definition
               of Equation (1).
215
216   Unlike in TreeMDP, the current state is defined as the state of the entire B&B tree, rather than merely the
217   current B&B node. The transition function returns a B&B tree whose open nodes are sorted according to
218   the node selection policy ρ, thus reflecting the true dynamics of the B&B algorithm, instead of a couple
219   of pseudo-states associated with former current node’s child nodes. Note that the definition above sets
220   BBMDPs among the specific class of MDPs called stochastic shortest path problems (Puterman, 2014).

      3.4      BBMDP VS T REE MDP
320
321   As it evacuates the core MDP notions of temporality and sequentiality, TreeMDP fails to describe variable
322   selection in B&B accurately in the general case. This is illustrated in Figure 3: although the TreeMDP
323   model is a valid approximation of BBMDP when training an RL agent to minimize the one-step temporal
324   difference, it produces inconsistent learning schemes when considering a multi-step temporal difference
325   loss. In fact, applying Etheve (2021) tree Bellman operator repeatedly yields trees that cannot be produced
326
      by a B&B algorithm explored in DFS.
327   Hence, BBMDP leverages the results established in Etheve et al. (2020) and Scavuzzo et al. (2022)–in
328   DFS, acting according to a policy minimizing the size of the subtree rooted in the current B&B node is


                                                                                      7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


329
      equivalent to acting according to a global optimal policy–all while preserving MDP properties. Crucially,
330
      BBMDP allows to harness RL algorithms that are not compatible with the TreeMDP framework, such as k-
331   step temporal difference, TD(λ), or any RL algorithms using MCTS as policy improvement operator (Grill
332   et al., 2020). In the same fashion, BBMDP can be applied to augment the pool of RL algorithms available
333   for learning improved cut selection and primal search heuristics, simply by adapting the action set and the
334   reward model to the task at hand.

      4.4   A BLATION STUDY
444
      We perform an ablation study on easy set covering instances to separate the performance gain associated
445   with BBMDP and the HL-Gauss classification loss. Since BBMDP and TreeMDP yield strictly equivalent
446   learning schemes when minimizing one-step temporal difference, see Figure 3, we evaluate the performance
447   gap between one-step and k-step TD learning for both DQN-BBMDP and DQN-TreeMDP.
448
      As shown in Table 2, we find that the bulk of the performance gain is brought by the use of a cross-entropy
449
      loss. Nonetheless, we find that the use of a multi-step TD loss improves the performance of DQN-BBMDP,
450
      while it undermines the performance of DQN-TreeMDP. This further supports the adoption of BBMDP
451   for learning branching strategies by reinforcement in the future. Following Parsonson et al. (2022), we
452   also evaluate the cost of opting for depth-first search instead of best estimate search, SCIP’s default node
453   selection policy, when learning branching strategies. Contrary to their work, we find DFS not to be restrictive
454   in practice in terms of performance. We further investigate theses discrepancies in Appendix F.

457   5     C ONCLUSION AND PERSPECTIVES
458
459   Combinatorial optimization has proven to be a challenging setting for RL algorithms, including beyond the
460   field of mixed-integer linear programming (Berto et al., 2023). Not only are RL agents rather consistently
461   outperformed by human-expert CO heuristics or IL agents trained to mimic these experts, but their applica-
462
      tion has also been limited so far to fairly easy problem instances. In this work, we showed the theoretical
      and practical limits of the concept of TreeMDP for learning optimal branching strategies in MILP. Intro-
463
      ducing BBMDP, we proposed a rigorous description of variable selection in B&B which we found to yield
464
      better performance than prior RL agents on the Ecole benchmark. We believe that building on a robust
465   MDP formulation of variable selection in B&B is key to achieve substantial acceleration of solving time for
466   higher-dimensional MILPs in the future. In fact, agents trained by reinforcement have proven in the past to
467   be able to defeat human knowledge in combinatorial settings such as board games (Silver et al., 2017; Schrit-
468   twieser et al., 2020). Through our contribution, we built a theoretical framework that enables the adaptation
469   of model-based MCTS RL algorithms for the purpose of learning optimal branching strategies.

      State representation Following the works of Gasse et al. (2019), MILPs are best represented by bipartite
746
      graphs G = (VG , CG , EG ) where VG denotes the set of variable nodes, CG denotes the set of constraint nodes,
747   and EG denotes the set of edges linking variable and constraints nodes. Nodes vG ∈ VG and cG ∈ CG are
748   connected if the variable associated with vG appears in the constraint associated with cG . Given a MILP P ,
749   defined as in section 2.1, its associate bipartite representation G has |G| = |VG | + |CG | = n + m nodes.
750   We use bipartite graphs to represent M ∈ M as described in Section 3.2. In our experiments, IL and
751   PG-tMDP agents use the list of features of Gasse et al. (2019) to represent variable nodes, constraint nodes

780
781   As they investigated the uneven success met by complex neural network architectures such as Transform-
782   ers in supervised versus reinforcement learning, Farebrother et al. (2024) found that training agents using a
783
      cross-entropy classification objective significantly improved the performance and scalability of value-based
      RL methods. However, replacing mean squared error regression with cross-entropy classification requires
784
      methods to transform scalars into distributions and distributions into scalars. Farebrother et al. (2024) found
785
      the Histogram Gaussian loss (HL-Gauss) (Imani & White, 2018), which exploits the ordinal structure struc-
786   ture of the regression task by distributing probability mass on multiple neighboring histogram bins, to be a
787   reliable solution across multiple RL benchmarks. Concretely, in HL-Gauss, the support of the value func-
788   tion Z ⊂ R is divided in mb bins of equal width forming a partition of Z. Bins are centered at zi ∈ Z for
789   1 ≤ i ≤ mb , we use η = (zmax − zmin )/mb to denote their width. Given a scalar z ∈ Z, we define the
790   random variable Yz ∼ N (µ = z, σ 2 ) and note respectively ϕYz and ΦYz its associate probability density
791   and cumulative distribution function. z can then be encoded into a histogram distribution on Z using the
792   function phist : R → [0, 1]mb . Explicitly, phist computes the aggregated mass of ϕYz on each bin:

809   F    BBMDP VS R ETRO BRANCHING
810
811   In their work, Parsonson et al. (2022) proposed to train RL agents on retrospective trajectories built from
812   TreeMDP episodes, in order to leverage the state-of-the art node selection policies implemented in MILP
813   solvers. When reproducing their work, we found several discrepancies with the results they stated. First,
814   the performance gap between DQN-Retro and DQN-TreeMDP (Etheve et al., 2020) turned out to be much
815   narrower than expected. On easy set covering instances, the only benchmark on which the two agents are
816   compared in Parsonson et al. (2022), we even found DQN-TreeMDP to perform better. Second, Parsonson
817   et al. (2022) found that adopting a best-first-search (BeFS) node selection strategy at evaluation time greatly
818   improved the performance of DQN-TreeMDP on easy set covering instances, indicating that abandoning
819
      DFS during training could be beneficial. However, in our experiments, we observed a 20% performance
      drop when replacing DFS by BeFS at evaluation time. After thorough examination of both Parsonson et al.
820
      (2022)’s article and implementation, we found that the baseline labeled as DQN-TreeMDP (FMSTS-DFS in
821
      their article) was quite distant from the branching agent originally described in Etheve et al. (2020). In fact,
822   in Parsonson et al. (2022), the Etheve et al. (2020) branching agent is not trained on TreeMDP trajectories,
823   but on retrospective trajectories built from TreeMDP episodes, using a DFS construction heuristic. There-
824   fore, Parsonson et al. (2022) could not conclude on the superiority of retro branching over TreeMDP, nor
825   could they assess the limitations of DFS-based RL agents. In contrast, our contribution provides compelling
826   evidence that, while DFS is generally expected to hinder the training performance of RL agents due to its
827   reputation as a suboptimal node selection policy, the theoretical guarantees brought by DFS in BBMDP
828   enable to surpass prior state-of-the-art non-DFS agents. We believe this is because optimizing the node
829   selection policy has less influence on tree size performance compared to optimizing the variable selection
830
      policy, as evidenced by Etheve (2021).

897   are solved within a time limit of 10 minutes. Since most instances cannot be solved within the time limit,
898   we report final gaps and primal dual integrals averaged over 100 instances. The gap g is defined as the
899   normalized difference between (primal) global upper bound GUB and (dual) global lower bound GLB such

901                                                      |GUB − GLB|
                                                g=                          ,
902                                                  min(|GUB|, |GLB|)
903   while the primal-dual integral is defined as the integral of g with respect to running time. Among RL agents,
904   DQN-Retro achieves the best performance across the four hard instance benchmarks, despite exhibiting the
905   worst performance across the four medium instance benchmarks, as show in Table 1. We believe this to be
906   due to the use of final gap and primal-dual integral as performance metrics. In fact, contrary to other RL
907   agents, DQN-Retro is not trained to minimize B&B tree size. Instead, DQN-Retro learns variable selection
908
      strategies yielding the shortest diving trajectories, hence strategies favoring primal-dual gap reduction over
      tree size minimization. As a result, the DQN-Retro agent manages to achieve the best final gap and primal
909
      dual integral performance in average, all while solving fewer instances to optimality than other RL agents
910
      on hard instance benchmarks, as illustrated in Table 6. More importantly, we observe that while the IL
911   expert demonstrates reasonable generalization capability, achieving performance comparable to SCIP on
912   hard instance benchmarks, all RL baselines perform poorly, with none managing to outperform the random
913   agent across the four benchmarks. This underscores the limited generalization capacity of current model-free
914   RL agents to higher-dimensional instances, and highlights the need to adapt model-based RL approaches to
915   the B&B setting, by leveraging the BBMDP framework. Indeed, model-based MCTS RL approaches have
916   previously demonstrated the ability to surpass human expertise in combinatorial tasks such as board games
917   (Schrittwieser et al., 2020).
