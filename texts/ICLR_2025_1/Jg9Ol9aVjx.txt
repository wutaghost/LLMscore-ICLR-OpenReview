009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             Typical contrastive self-supervised learning methods apply inter-image contrast to
012             post-projector embeddings, thereby indirectly encouraging the pre-projector repre-
013             sentations’ invariance to several augmentation operators.1 While effective, these
014             methods do not account for the inherent difference between semantics-altering
015             (such as cropping and cutout2 ) and semantics-preserving augmentation opera-
016             tors (such as resizing, flipping and color distortion), and thereby lack an explicit
017
                mechanism to encourage distinguishable representations for semantically differ-
                ent contents within the same image. We explain, both in reason and in practice,
018
                that these issues can harm the generalizability of the representations in down-
019
                stream tasks. To address these issues, we propose Direct Intra-image Contrastive
020             Regularization (DICR), a plug-and-play regularization method that directly applies
021             intra-image contrast to pre-projector representations. Empirical results show that
022             DICR can significantly enhance the generalizability of existing methods in down-
023             stream tasks, and validate the crucial role of semantic content distinguishability in
024             the generalizable performance of contrastive learning.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Recently, contrastive self-supervised learning has emerged as a powerful paradigm for learning
      generic representations from unlabeled datasets (He et al., 2020; Grill et al., 2020; Chen & He,
030
      2021; Caron et al., 2021; Bardes et al., 2022; Geiping et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Gui et al.,
031
      2024). These approaches primarily focus on inter-image contrast, which aims to attract views of the
032   same image while repulsing views from different images (Wu et al., 2018; Oord et al., 2018; Hjelm
033   et al., 2018; Bachman et al., 2019; Tian et al., 2020; Yeh et al., 2022). Contrastive learning have
034   demonstrated remarkable results in downstream tasks by indirectly encouraging the representations’
035   invariance to a handful of different augmentation operators indiscriminately (Chen et al., 2020).
036
      However, these augmentation operators are born different. Loosely speaking, some augmentation
037
      operators such as resizing, flipping, and color distortion do not alter the semantics of an input image,
038   and thus it is desirable to enforce their invariance in (semantic) representations; other operators such
039   as cropping and cutout do change semantic content, and thereby a mechanism is expected to prevent
040   them from collapsing into a single identical representation. To better understand this intuitive idea,
041   we first explicitly explain it in reason (Section 2.1), and then motivate it from empirical observation
042   perspective (Section 2.2).
043
      Dealing with fundamentally different things indiscriminately can lead to serious problems. In the
044   context of contrastive learning, it will confuse correlation with identity between augmented views of
045   an input image. Concretely, this problem may occur in two cases: when one view is from foreground
046   object and the other is from background (as illustrated in Figure 1a), or when two views show different
047   parts of the foreground object (as illustrated in Figure 1b). This confusion in representation learning
048   will probably lead to performance degeneration on downstream tasks where the correlation does not
049   necessarily hold (for example in Figure 2).

074   To tackle this problem, we develop a regularization method, called Direct Intra-image Contrastive
075
      Regularization (DICR, pronounced duiker3 ). It explicitly tries to guarantee the distinguishability of
      different but co-occurring contents via intra-image contrast on representations, and also preserve their
076
      correlation via inter-image contrast on embeddings. Our contribution is threefold:
077
078              • Firstly, we identify that treating semantics-altering and semantics-preserving augmentation
079                operators indiscriminately can be suboptimal for downstream tasks, as it confuses correlation
080                with identity, and does not prevent representations of different yet co-occurring contents
081                from collapsing into a single identical representation.
082              • Secondly, as existing methods lack a mechanism to address this issue, we propose DICR,
083                which adds no extra parameters and can efficiently regularizes existing methods.
084
                 • Finally, our empirical results demonstrate DICR’s capacity to differentiate between different
085
                   semantic contents within an image, and its effectiveness in improving the generalizable
086
                   downstream performance of existing methods.

089   2       M OTIVATION

091   2.1       E XPLICIT EXPLANATION IN REASON
092
093   Typical inter-image contrastive learning methods attract the post-projector embeddings between views
094
      from the same image to model the invariance to certain augmentation operators. Such direct attraction
      on post-projector embeddings can lead to an indirect attraction on the pre-projector representations,
095
      thus encouraging, though not guaranteeing, invariance of the pre-projector representations to these
096
      augmentation operators.
097
098   However, augmentation operators are inherently different, and encouraging invariance indiscrimi-
099   nately to all the operators can be suboptimal. On the one hand, some augmentation operators are
100
      semantics-preserving, such as resizing, flipping, and color distortion. Modeling invariance to these
      operators is tantamount to modeling semantic identity, which can help downstream models focus on
101
      core semantic features instead of spurious style features (Mitrovic et al., 2020). On the other hand,
102
      other augmentation operators, such as cropping and cutout, are semantics-altering. Modeling invari-
103   ance to them involves modeling the correlation between different yet co-occurring semantic contents
104   through attracting their representations. Although the correlation between co-occurring contents
105   does exist in the real world, the co-occurrence does not always hold true under all circumstances.
106   For instance, camels typically roam in the sandy deserts, and dogs usually frolic in the green grass.
107

      However, as illustrated in Figure 1a, when camels appear amidst a grass background and dogs appear
109   amidst a desert background in the downstream task, attracting the foreground and the background to
110   an identical representation during pretraining can lead to confusion in downstream models. Another
111   example to consider involves the correlation between object parts (Figure 1b). During pretraining,
112   many images of zebra duikers are included, while in the downstream tasks, images of mice and
113   tigers are included. Similar features exist between the head of a mouse and the head of a duiker, as
114   well as between the back of a tiger and the back of a duiker. Therefore, indistinguishable pretrained
115   representations of the duiker’s head and its striped back can mislead downstream models when
116   classifying mice and tigers.
117   Based on the above explicit analysis, in order to obtain more generalizable representations, it is
118   crucial to develop an explicit mechanism to decouple identity and correlation, thereby ensuring
119   distinguishable representations for different semantic contents.

259   3.2   D IRECT I NTRA - IMAGE C ONTRASTIVE R EGULARIZATION
260
261
      We propose DICR to decouple identity and correlation. The basic idea behind DICR is that the
      similarity in representation space should reflect the identity between contents, while the similarity
262
      in embedding space should reflect the correlation between contents. Therefore, besides the typical
263
      inter-image contrastive loss on embeddings, there should be a mechanism to ensure different yet
264   co-occurring contents have different representations. A straightforward implementation of this
265   idea would be to sample some semantically identical views as positive views and different yet co-
266   occurring views as negative views, then apply a contrastive loss to their representations as a form
267   of regularization. However, this implementation can be computationally slow, as it does additional
268   forward and backward propagation to optimize the regularization term. In fact, as discussed in
269   Section 3.1, there are ready-made regions with semantically identical and different yet co-occurring
      contents in views generated by the typical view generation process, and their representations can

      Pretraining. We conduct experiments on CIFAR10/CIFAR100 (Krizhevsky, 2009), TinyIma-

350
      Following Chen et al. (2020), we set the augmentation strategy to resized cropping, flipping, and color
351   distortion. We adopt ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016) as the backbone for all the experiments, modifying
352   it by removing the first max pooling operation and replacing the first 7x7 convolutional layer of stride
353   2 with a 3x3 convolutional layer of stride 1, to accommodate the smaller image sizes in our selected
354   datasets compared to ImageNet. For optimization, we use the SGD optimizer with momentum 0.9
355   and weight decay 1 × 10−4 , and perform cosine-annealing learning rate scheduling. For CIFAR10
356   and CIFAR100, we initialize the learning rate as 0.5 and pretrain the models for 500 epochs with
357   batch size 512. For TinyImagenet and STL10, we initialize the learning rate as 0.25 and pretrain the
358   models for 250 epochs with batch size 256.
359   Regarding our approach, we adjust the hyperparameters for each baseline individually, due to their
360   distinct loss functions. For SimCLR, we assign a final weight λ of 40 and a temperature τ of 0.05 for
361   all datasets. In the case of MoCo, we set a final weight λ of 10 and a temperature τ of 0.02 across
362   all datasets. For SimSiam, we determine a final weight λ of 2 and a temperature τ of 0.005 for all
363   datasets. In the case of Matrix-SSL, we set a final weight λ of 10 and a temperature τ of 0.02 across
364   all datasets. We configure the threshold ϵ to 2 × 2 pixels for CIFAR10 and CIFAR100, and to 3 × 3
365
      pixels for TinyImagenet and STL10.5
366
367   Evaluation protocol. We follow the typical linear readout protocol (He et al., 2020), training a
368
      linear classifier on top of the frozen backbone for 100 epochs using the SGD optimizer. We evaluate
      the representations on in-distribution and out-of-distribution datasets. For each dataset, we evaluate
369
      the in-distribution on the pretraining dataset itself, and the out-of-distribution on the other three

      datasets.

500   5    R ELATED W ORK
501
502
      Contrastive self-supervised learning. Contrastive learning is a widely adopted self-supervised
503   learning paradigm that aims to learn a generic representation from unlabeled pretraining datasets (He
504   et al., 2020; Grill et al., 2020; Chen & He, 2021; Caron et al., 2021; Bardes et al., 2022; Geiping
505   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Gui et al., 2024). The key idea of contrastive learning is to perform
506   inter-image contrast, which is to attract views generated from the same images and to repulse views
507   generated from different images (Wu et al., 2018; Oord et al., 2018; Hjelm et al., 2018; Bachman
508   et al., 2019; Tian et al., 2020; Yeh et al., 2022). Different from inter-image contrast adopted by
509   the above mentioned methods, DICR applies intra-image contrast to existing contrastive learning
510   methods to enhance their generalizability on downstream tasks.
511
512   Learning object-level representations through intra-image contrast. Some works involve intra-
513   image contrast (Hénaff et al., 2021; Xiao et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2022) to better
514   align with pixel-wise tasks. The main difference between these methods and DICR is how they
515   contruct positive and negative pairs. Hénaff et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022) rely on external tools
516   (external segmentation algorithms in Hénaff et al. (2021) and copy-paste in Wang et al. (2022)) to
517   generate positive and negative pairs. The positive view pairs generated by these methods can be
518   different parts of the same object, and thereby do not decouple identity from correlation, which differs
519   from DICR. The work by Yan et al. (2022) adapts contrastive learning for pretraining on anatomical
520
      images. It applies global and local pixel-level contrast, involving intra-image pixels as negatives. The
      correlation between different pixels within single images is somewhat overlooked in Yan et al. (2022),
521
      since different pixels are never treated as positive pairs. However, in contrastive learning for regular
522
      images, the correlation between different pixels is misleading but can be useful for downstream tasks,
523   so DICR is designed to preserve both the identity and correlation. Xiao et al. (2021) involves both
524   intra-image contrast that attract the same contents with different styles and inter-image contrast that
525   attract different contents from the same image. However, the intra-image contrast in Xiao et al. (2021)
526   is not directly applied on representations, which has been shown to be essential in decoupling identity
527   from correlation in Section 4.2. Additionally, the method by Xiao et al. (2021) handle the edge case
528   where the two views are completely non-overlapping by simply ignoring the repulsion between them,
529   which is different from DICR.
530
531
532   6    C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this work, we identify the importance of decoupling identity and correlation in contrastive learning
535   to enhance the generalizability of downstream performance. We propose DICR, a regularization
536   method that can decouple identity and correlation in existing contrastive learning methods. It
537   apply intra-image contrast on representations, and also preserve their correlation via inter-image
538   contrast on embeddings. Our empirical evidence shows that DICR substantially improves the
539   generalizability of downstream performance in existing methods, underscoring the pivotal role that
      content distinguishability plays in the robust performance of contrastive learning.
