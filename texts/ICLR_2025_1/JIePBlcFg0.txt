011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Optimal resource scheduling aims to cover resource demand with minimum eco-
014            nomic cost, which is far from model-based constraint optimization or data-driven
015            prediction based scheduling process. To address this model-free constraint opti-
016            mization issue, a sparse system identification framework with quantile regression
017
               and QR orthogonal decomposition (iQR) is proposed for complex systems ranging
               from small to large scales. It leverages quantile optimization with L1 -norm to
018
               construct a business-driven strategy to reach the proportion of meeting resource
019
               demand. It also involves a complete-mapping Fourier Transformation process and
020            an orthogonal least squares technique to select basis vectors in advance to achieve
021            fast regression with sparse mathematical expression, which reduces the number
022            of basis functions from thousands to hundreds and to dozens. iQR represents a
023            specific expectation for single time series prediction, which only achieves predic-
024            tions that deviate from the true values as little as possible, but aims for predictions
025            consistently higher than the actual values of real demand. Numerical experiments
026            was conducted on eight datasets, including commonly used time series and real-
027            world CPU resource data. The results indicate that most neural network-based
028
               methods fail to balance both resource demands and prediction accuracy effectively.
               In contrast, iQR can achieve optimal scheduling with the minimum economic cost
029
               and it is easier to satisfy the business constraints with quantile tuning. Notably,
030
               iQR is lightweight with a training speed in seconds and does not rely on the support
031            of computing power of GPU resources. This study may provide new insight into
032            investigations on resource scheduling optimization issues.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Optimal resource scheduling seeks to meet demand with minimal cost, which is essential across
038   various industries such as services, energy, and communications due to its substantial economic
039   benefits (He et al., 2012; Zhang et al., 2018). Such constraint optimization problems often lack explicit
040   formulas and is typically solved by either model-based planning methods or data-driven prediction
041   based approaches. However, both of them have significant shortcomings with little consideration of
042   empirical modeling errors or business constraints. Recently, numerous methods have been proposed
043   to tackle this issue. Firstly, model-based optimization techniques are applied in resource scheduling,
044   such as NFS (Jones & Peet, 2021), STS (Wang et al., 2021), and DD-TMPC (Zhao et al., 2024).
045
      Despite their utility, these methods struggle to capture the complexities of dynamic environments and
      incur significant computational costs for model maintenance and updates. Secondly, prediction-based
046
      scheduling solutions are also prevalent. Contemporary neural network approaches for time series
047
      forecasting include models like iTransformer (Liu et al., 2023), FITS (Xu et al., 2024), PatchTST (Nie
048   et al., 2023), and TimesNet (Wu et al., 2023). These models are capable of providing long-term
049   forecasts for a variety of multivariate data types, including electricity, exchange rates, weather, and
050   disease data (Zhou et al., 2022; Zhang & Yan, 2022; Zeng et al., 2023; Bi et al., 2023; Zhou et al.,
051   2024). However, these methods fall short of adequately meeting business requirements, even when
052   the objective function is simply modified to quantile regression to provide a confidence of forecasting.
053   Additionally, they necessitate GPU resources, which are not accessible to some smaller laboratories.
      Thirdly, direct application of quantile regression for scheduling is another approach. These methods

054
      provide good interpretability and aligns well with business requirements (Zarnani et al., 2019; Zhang
055   et al., 2018). Nonetheless, due to the inherent complexity of time series data, these methods often
056   require extensive running time, which exceeds practical tolerance limits. In light of these challenges,
057   we aim to develop a lightweight, business-oriented, more interpretable (with explicit formulas)
058   framework for resource scheduling problems. This framework will be specifically designed to meet
059   business requirements and deliver accurate and rapid predictions without relying on complex neural
060   network models or extensive pre-training process.
061   Thus, we propose a sparse system identification framework with quantile regression and QR orthog-
062   onal decomposition (iQR) for optimal resource scheduling. Specifically, iQR applies the Fourier
063   transform to time series, selecting the most influential basis vectors to build a basis vectors library.
064   Additionally, we apply Seasonal-Trend decomposition using LOESS (STL) decomposition (Cleve-
065   land et al., 1990) to the input sequence, allowing individual identification for different time series
066   and improving the precision of long-term scheduling. To enhance efficiency, we initially screen
067   the basis vectors before incorporating business constraints. We design the Stepwise Orthogonal
068   Regression (SOR) algorithm to retain only the most important basis vectors for training. Subsequently,
069
      we employ L1 -norm optimization with quantile regression to solve the business-driven problem. The
      main contributions of iQR can be summarized as follows:
070
071          • A novel business-driven identification-prediction framework has been introduced, offering
072            remarkable flexibility by enabling the customization of the objective function according
073            to specific business satisfaction levels. It enables rapid, cost-effective scheduling across
074            various scenarios, offering new insights for future resource scheduling applications.
075          • By incorporating global information in the form of Fourier bases into local identifications
076            and leveraging STL decomposition to differentiate identifications, iQR enables long-term
077            and comprehensive identifications for time series. This innovative approach combines data-
078            driven techniques with explicit mathematical expressions, enhancing both interpretability
079            and scheduling capabilities.
080          • The specially designed SOR algorithm for the further selection of basis vectors and the
081            utilization of quantile optimization accelerate the optimization process with sparse mathe-
082            matical expression, which reduces the number of basis functions from thousands to hundreds
083            and even to dozens. This contributes to the investigation of regression acceleration, and
084            enables the iQR framework to be swiftly applied in business-driven scheduling scenarios.
085          • Impressive scheduling accuracy was achieved on eight benchmark datasets, surpassing
086            current state-of-the-art (SOTA) outcomes. Our method is more effective in satisfying
087            scheduling demands, and when constraints are met, it incurs significantly lower economic
088            costs. Notably, iQR is lightweight and does not rely on GPU computing power support
089
               during the training process compared with neural network based methods.

091   2    R ELATED W ORK
092
093   Model-based Optimization for Scheduling Model-based scheduling optimization methods utilize
094   explicit empirical models of the environment to optimize schedules. Based on the well-designed
095   model, many works have been proposed to address the resource scheduling issues with convex or
096   non-convex optimization solving approaches, including NFS (Jones & Peet, 2021), STS (Wang et al.,
097
      2021), DD-TMPC (Zhao et al., 2024). However, several drawbacks exist in these methods, e.g., the
      difficulty of accurately modelling complex dynamics and the high computational cost on updating
098
      models. Model-free methods are increasingly gaining attention due to their enhanced adaptability
099
      and capability of handling complex dynamics behind the data. Latest works develop the model-free
100   scheduling policy for optimization, involving Zhao et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024). Model-free
101   optimization strategies directly learn optimal policies from interactions without explicit environmental
102   models, and are more robust to changes in the dynamical process.
103
104   Prediction-based Scheduling Recently, methods for time series prediction using neural networks
105   have gained widespread attention. Transformer-based methods aim to continuously reduce the time
106   complexity of the model while improving accuracy. These methods include Timemixer (Wang et al.,
107   2024), TimesNet (Wu et al., 2023), Basisformer (Ni et al., 2023), iTansformer (Liu et al., 2023),
      PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023), FEDformer (Zhou et al., 2022), FITS (Xu et al., 2024), and others.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Despite the significant advancements of Transformer-based methods, their accuracy and training
109   speed can still be unsatisfactory in some cases. As a result, two optimization approaches have
110   emerged. The first approach involves leveraging larger models (Zhou et al., 2024; Cao et al., 2024)
111   to further enhance accuracy, but this leads to slower total times. The second approach involves
112   abandoning the Transformer-based framework and instead implementing plain neural networks (Zeng
113   et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Das et al., 2023) for time series prediction. Unfortunately, these deep
114   learning methods often excel at solving a specific tasks, and their performance may decline when
115   prediction requirements change or business constraints are introduced.
116
117   Quantile Regression-based scheduling Common quantile regression include FLM (Zarnani et al.,
118   2019), NWPs (Zarnani et al., 2019) or traditional model TRMF (Yu et al., 2016), SINDy (Brunton
119   et al., 2016), TSM (Gao & Yan, 2022) with quantile regression. These models can achieve quantile
120
      regression by utilizing their own basis function library without relying on the backpropagation
      process of the support of GPU. Moreover, there are existing methods that can handle business-driven
121
      problems (Dai, 2023; Wang & He, 2024; Li & Zhu, 2008). However, most of these methods lack open-
122
      source code, which limits their accessibility. Compared to data-driven approaches, these methods may
123   face challenges in making long-term scheduling, and the prediction accuracy may not be as high as
124   that of neural network-based methods. Besides, when incorporating business-driven constraints into
125   them, although they can be solved in a CPU environment, the execution speed becomes significantly
126   slower, especially when dealing with large basis function libraries. This poses a challenge in meeting
127   the speed requirements of resource scheduling.

265   3.4   T HE K EY M ODULE OF I QR F RAMEWORK : SOR
266
      To identify significant basis vectors and further eliminate irrelevant ones, the key module of iQR,
267
      Stepwise Orthogonal Regression (SOR), has been proposed. The detail theory is shown as follows.
268
269   Given the initial set of basis vectors Θ = {θ1 , θ2 , . . . , θp }, we employ the following Lemma 1 to
      ensure orthogonality among basis vectors.


                                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Lemma 1. (QR Decomposition (Golub & Van Loan, 2013)) Given a real n × n square matrix A,
271   there exist n × n orthogonal matrix Q and upper triangular matrix R such that A = QR.
272
273   Then, the Lemma 1 is extended to a general scenario, that is, matrices of arbitrary dimensions.
274
      Corollary 1. Any real m × n matrix A can be decomposed into the product of an m × n orthogonal
275   matrix Q, whose columns are mutually orthogonal, and an n × n upper triangular matrix R.
276
277
      Corollary 2. Any real m × n matrix A can be factorized into the product A = V R, where V is an
      orthogonal matrix with its columns being pairwise orthogonal, and R is an upper triangular matrix
278
      with diagonal entries equal to 1.
279
280   According to Corollary 2, the basis vectors Θ is orthogonalised to filter the insignificant basis vectors,
281   and sbsequently serve for the later lemma. The following lemmas show a stepwise regression strategy
282   to efficiently select the optimal subset of basis vectors.
283
      Lemma 2. (Orthogonality Principle) For an unbiased optimal estimator (i.e., the least squares
284   estimation), if the basis vectors are orthogonal, then the error is orthogonal to any basis vector.
285
      Lemma 3. (Orthogonal Greedy Optimality) If the basis vectors are orthogonal, then a greedy
286
      stepwise optimal selection of basis vectors will yield the same result as the global optimal solution.
287
288   Remark 1. Since the basis vectors are orthogonal, the contribution of each function to the model
289   can be evaluated independently of the others. At each step of the greedy algorithm, the basis vector
290
      that minimizes the sum of squares of the residuals is selected. Due to orthogonality, this incremental
      selection is equivalent to evaluating the contributions of all possible subsets of vectors at the same
291
      time, ensuring that the greedy selection is consistent with the global optimum. Then, we have the
292
      following the key theorem of SOR, which plays the role of the second filtering strategy of basis vectors,
293   and ensuring obtaining the optimal basis vectors.
294
      Theorem 1. (Stepwise Orthogonal Regression) If the set of basis vectors {θ1∗ , θ2∗ , . . . , θn∗ } is orthog-
295
      onal, then the greedy stepwise optimal selection of basis vectors will yield the same result as the
296
      global optimal solution.
297
298   Remark 2. SOR obtains the same result as the global optimum by sequentially selecting orthogonal
299
      basis vectors. It independently evaluate the contribution of each basis vectors to the model at each
      step of the greedy algorithm, selecting the basis vectors that minimizes the sum of squared residuals.
300
      The orthogonality ensures that this incremental selection is equivalent to simultaneously evaluating
301
      the contributions of all possible subsets of vectors, thus guaranteeing consistency with the global
302   optimum. The insight is to use Stepwise Orthogonal Regression to filter out significant basis vectors
303   from the initial set and eliminate irrelevant ones, ultimately obtaining the optimal basis vectors.
304
305   The proof of Lemma 2, Lemma 3, and Theorem 1 are present in Appendix F.1, Appendix F.2, and
306   Appendix F.3, separately. Thus, the stepwise optimal basis vectors selection is feasible.

470   5      C ONCLUSION R EMARKS
471
472   In this study, we address the crucial issue of resource scheduling across diverse industrial contexts by
473   introducing a novel framework, termed iQR, which leverages sparse system identification techniques.
474   This framework deviates significantly from conventional model-based or prediction-based scheduling
475   approaches, adopting instead a global-local coordination strategy to bolster identification perfor-
476   mance. Specifically, we first utilize Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) to comprehensively extract vital
477
      features from a frequency domain perspective. Furthermore, iQR incorporates Stepwise Orthogonal
      Regression (SOR) to derive more prominent basis vectors and employs L1 -norm Quantile Regres-
478
      sion (L1 QR) to ensure precise scheduling across various quantile levels. The deployment of L1 QR
479
      for resource scheduling not only fulfills resource demands with high confidence but also minimizes
480   resource allocation costs. Rigorous experiments conducted on eight datasets demonstrate that iQR
481   attains state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance, characterized by a higher satisfaction rate and reduced
482   economic cost, compared to recent deep learning-based prediction methods incorporating quantile
483   regression. Notably, iQR is lightweight, with training durations measured in seconds, and operates
484   efficiently without the need for GPU resources. Looking ahead, we aim to broaden the applicability
485   of iQR to a wider range of scheduling scenarios and explore more potent and efficient solutions for
      time series analysis in resource-constrained environments.

      Sparse System Identification Sparse System Identification (SSI) (Brunton et al., 2016; Wilms
705
      et al., 2023; Fasel et al., 2022) infers the underlying dynamics and the algebraic representation of a
706   control system from observed data. This methodology is rooted in the principle of parsimony, seeking
707   to disentangle the most concise and informative representation of system behavior by selecting a
708   minimal set of relevant basis vectors from a predefined library.
709
      SSI is inherently characterized by the basis function library, which commonly encompasses polyno-
710
      mial series, trigonometric functions, and other suitable functional forms. Given this library, we are
711
      able to identify a sparse combination of basis vectors that best approximates the system dynamics.
712   This is achieved by minimizing a sparsity-promoting penalty term within the optimization framework.
713   SSI eliminates superfluous terms, thereby yielding compact and interpretable dynamical models.
714
715
      SSI has demonstrated remarkable progress in revealing hidden system dynamics within data, resulting
      in highly accurate predictions in various contexts (Liu et al., 2024). However, the efficacy of SSI for
716
      business-driven identification prediction is not without challenges. Direct application of the method
717
      without tailored strategies for basis vectors design may lead to accumulation of errors over extended
718   prediction horizons, compromising its suitability for long-range predictions.
719
720
      L1 -norm Quantile Regression Traditional regression analysis focuses on the relationship between
721   covariates and the conditional expectation of the response variable. Models derived from such
722   analyses, such as linear or generalized linear regressions, aim to construct a function that predicts the
723   conditional mean of the response given observed covariate values. While these methods effectively
724   capture the central tendency of the response distribution in many instances, they provide a single-
725   dimensional view and can be susceptible to outliers or data heterogeneity. To address these limitations,
726   Quantile Regression (QR) has emerged as an alternative approach, particularly when interest lies in
727   other aspects of the response distribution.
728   Quantile regression extends the scope of conventional regression by investigating the association
729   between covariates and conditional quantiles of the response variable rather than solely its condi-
730   tional expectation. Specifically, quantile regression models estimate the conditional quantile of the
731   dependent variable given the value of the independent variable. QR offers a comprehensive portrayal
732   of the response distribution across different quantiles, encompassing tail behavior, asymmetry, and
733   potential non-linear effects.
734   Recently, L1 -norm Quantile Regression (L1 QR) has been proposed as a robust and flexible approach
735   for modeling conditional quantiles (Li & Zhu, 2008; Shang & Kong, 2021). L1 QR employs a
736   penalty term based on the absolute values of the regression coefficients, thereby promoting sparsity
737   in the model. Unlike the L2 -norm, some regression coefficients derived by the L1 -norm likely to be
738   estimated as zero. This facilitates variable selection, particularly in high-dimensional settings, by
739   identifying a subset of key covariates with significant influence on specific quantiles. For a given
740   quantile τ , the objective function of L1 QR is formulated as:

777   Stepwise Orthogonal Regression Stepwise Orthogonal Regression (SOR) is a sequential feature
778   selection algorithm specifically tailored for model building in the context of time series analysis
779
      and forecasting. SOR combines the principles of forward selection and orthogonal least squares to
      efficiently identify and incorporate the most influential variables into a model.
780
781   The SOR algorithm proceeds in a stepwise manner, iteratively adding one predictor at a time to an
782   initially empty model. At each iteration, the algorithm selects the variable that maximally improves
783   the model fit while minimizing collinearity issues through the use of orthogonal projections. The
784   improvement in model fit is typically assessed via a goodness-of-fit criterion, such as the reduction
785
      in the sum of squared errors or the increase in the coefficient of determination. Once a variable is
      added, its contribution is made orthogonal to the existing model terms, ensuring that subsequent
786
      selections are independent and do not merely replicate previously captured information. This process
787
      continues until a stopping criterion is met, which could be based on a predefined maximum number
788   of predictors, a threshold for the improvement in model fit, or a desired level of explained variance.
789
790   In the realm of quantile regression for time series forecasting, SOR can be employed to select the most
791
      relevant predictors for each quantile level of interest. Given the potential heterogeneity in the effects
      of different variables across different quantiles, SOR enables the construction of separate, tailored
792
      models for each quantile, ensuring that each model includes only the most influential predictors for
793
      that specific quantile. As a result, SOR-enhanced quantile regression models provide more accurate
794   and reliable forecasts across the entire conditional distribution of the time series.
795

      Seasonal-Trend decomposition using LOESS Seasonal-Trend decomposition using LOESS (STL)
797   is a nonparametric filtering procedure, proposed by Cleveland (Cleveland et al., 1990), with LOcally
798   Estimated Scatterplot Smoothing (LOESS) used for estimating nonlinear relationships. STL has
799   gained popularity due to its ability to handle diverse types of time series data, including those
800   exhibiting irregular seasonal patterns and time-varying trends. The simplicity of STL allows fast
801   computation for long sequence. STL decomposes time series X into three additive components,
802   namely trend T , seasonal S, and residual R.
803
                                                  X = T + S + R,
804
805   in which the trend component T captures the long-term behavior of a time series, embodying its
806   overall direction and pattern over time. Seasonality S refers to recurring patterns or cycles occurring
807   within fixed time frames, such as daily, weekly, or annual patterns. The residual component R,
808   also known as the remainder or irregular component, represents unexplained time series variations
809   after removing the trend and seasonal components. It encapsulates random fluctuations, noise, or
      irregularities not accounted for by the trend and seasonal patterns.

      STL decomposition serves as a critical preprocessing step in time series forecasting models. By
811   decomposing the raw time series into its constituent parts, we gain deeper insights into the underlying
812   structure and identify potential patterns and anomalies. This decomposition facilitates the develop-
813   ment of more accurate prediction models by modeling each component separately and incorporate
814   relevant features into the forecasting framework.

865
866   Time series forecasting modeling is a long-standing research problem. From early statistical models
867   to later deep models, many new models have been proposed for sequence modeling and time series
868   forecasting. However, long-term forecasting still suffers from a lack of accuracy. Considering the
869   challenges of traditional statistical methods in long-term forecasting, the baselines we compare
870   are mainly neural network methods. Specifically, we consider the latest time series forecasting
871   methods, including TimeMixer (Wang et al., 2024), TimesNet (Wu et al., 2023), Basisformer (Ni
872
      et al., 2023), iTransformer (Liu et al., 2023), PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023), Dlinear (Zeng et al., 2023),
      and FEDformer (Zhou et al., 2022). The details are introduced as follows
873
874
      TimeMixer TimeMixer is an architecture based entirely on multilayer perceptual machines. It
875
      consists of past decoupled mixing and future multi-predictor mixing modules, which decompose multi-
876
      scale sequences and integrate multiple predictors for prediction, respectively.TimeMixer provides
877   insights, that is, time series exhibit different patterns at different sampling scales. Its scheduling
878   implementation is public available at https://github.com/kwuking/TimeMixer.
879
880   TimesNet TimesNet is an innovative time series analysis method that converts a one-dimensional
881   series into a multi-periodic two-dimensional tensor, which maps the time series into a two-
882   dimensional space and enhances the representation of periodic features. It decomposes com-
883   plex temporal dynamics into intra-periodic fluctuations and inter-periodic trends, and thus per-
884   forms better on datasets with significant periodic structure. Code is available at the repository
885   https://github.com/thuml/Time-Series-Library.
886
887   Basisformer BasisFormer is an end-to-end time series forecasting architecture that designs learn-
888   able and interpretable substrates for forecasting. It consists of three core components: acquiring the
889
      substrate through adaptive self-supervised learning; computing the similarity coefficients between the
      time series and the substrate in historical perspective through a two-way cross-attention mechanism;
890
      and selecting the substrate based on the similarity coefficients to generate accurate future forecasts.
891
      The source code can be found at https://github.com/nzl5116190/Basisformer.
892
893
      iTransformer iTransformer is an advanced model designed for time series forecasting. Utilizing an
894   “inverted Transformer” architecture, iTransformer has demonstrated great effectiveness in forecasting
895   time series data, especially for long term forecasting problems with complex periodicity, trends, and
896   non-stationarity. The source code is available at the repository https://github.com/thuml/
897   iTransformer.
898
899   PatchTST PatchTST is a powerful Transformer-based model specialized in multivariate time
900   series prediction and self-supervised representation learning. There are two core components: (i)
901   segmentation of the time series into subsequence patches, which are then provided to the Transformer;
902   and (ii) channel independence, where each channel represents a univariate time series with shared
903   embeddings and Transformer weights for all series.The components in PatchTST greatly improve the
904
      accuracy of long-term forecasts. Code of PatchTST is present at the repositry https://github.
      com/yuqinie98/PatchTST.
905
906
      Dlinear DLinear represents a straightforward neural network prediction model that ingeniously
907
      integrates time series decomposition with linear layers, unlike Tranformer-based models. The
908
      essence of DLinear lies in its direct multi-step forecasting approach, decomposing historical time
909   series data into trend and remainder components, each modeled through separate single-layer linear
910   networks to achieve predictive outputs. The source code for DLinear is openly accessible at https:
911   //github.com/cure-lab/LTSF-Linear.
912
913   FEDformer FEDformer is a Transformer-based model that is designed to tackle the long-term time
914   series prediction issue by incorporating Fourier transforms and wavelet basis vectors to enhance the
915   effectiveness of the model. The time complexity of FEDformer is O(L), where L is the length of
916   the sequence, which means that FEDformer can significantly improve the computational efficiency
917   when dealing with long sequences compared to traditional methods. Its implementation is provided
      in https://github.com/MAZiqing/FEDformer.
