009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language process-
012            ing, albeit at the cost of immense memory and computation requirements. Post-
013            training quantization (PTQ) is becoming the de facto method to reduce the mem-
014            ory footprint and improve the inference throughput of LLMs. In this work, we aim
015
               to push the upper limit of LLM PTQ by optimizing the weight rounding param-
               eters with the block reconstruction technique, a predominant method in previous
016
               vision models. We propose TesseraQ, a new state-of-the-art PTQ technique, to
017
               quantize the weights of LLMs to ultra-low bits. To effectively optimize the round-
018            ing in LLMs and stabilize the reconstruction process, we introduce progressive
019            adaptive rounding. This approach iteratively transits the soft rounding variables
020            to hard variables during the reconstruction process. Additionally, we optimize the
021            dequantization scale parameters to fully leverage the block reconstruction tech-
022            nique. We demonstrate that TesseraQ can be seamlessly integrated with existing
023            scaling or clipping-based PTQ algorithms such as AWQ and OmniQuant, signif-
024            icantly enhancing their performance and establishing a new state-of-the-art. For
025            instance, when compared to AWQ, TesseraQ improves the wikitext2 perplexity
026
               from 14.65 to 6.82 and average downstream accuracy from 50.52 to 59.27 with
               2-bit weight-only quantization of LLaMA-2-7B. Across a range of quantization
027
               schemes, including W2A16, W3A16, W3A3, and W4A4, TesseraQ consistently
028
               exhibits superior performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing with their remark-
      able capabilities. LLMs such as, GPT-4 (Bubeck et al., 2023) and LLaMA-3 (Meta, 2024), contain
034
      hundreds of billions of parameters. While this scale enables their impressive performance, it also
035
      poses significant deployment challenges (Zhou et al., 2024). LLMs require substantial memory and
036   computational resources, making them impractical for many real-world applications, especially on
037   consumer devices or in resource-limited environments (Dettmers et al., 2022). Quantization ad-
038   dresses this issue by reducing the precision of the model’s parameters and activations, typically
039   from 32-bit floating-point (FP32) to lower bit-width representations such as 8-bit or 4-bit integer
040   (INT8, INT4). This technique significantly decreases the model’s memory footprint to increase the
041   I/O throughput, often with marginal performance loss.
042   Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) (Gholami et al., 2022) has perhaps become the most widespread
043   and the easiest way to compress the LLM by reducing the bitwidth of the pretrained model’s param-
044   eters. For example, with a single GPU and a small number of input sequences, GPTQ (Frantar et al.,
045   2022) can compress an FP16 LLM into INT4 format by deriving the exact solution for quantization
046   error minimization. Recent works like AWQ (Lin et al., 2023), QuaRot (Ashkboos et al., 2024) and
047   OmniQuant (Shao et al., 2023) have pushed the compression limit further with INT3 weight-only
048   quantization achieving a small performance gap with respect to the FP16 baseline. However, in a
049   more challenging scenario like INT2 weight-only quantization and weight-and-activation quantiza-
050
      tion, these methods still incur a large performance gap compared to the original FP16 model.
051   We conjecture that the major reason for the low performance on ultra low-bit PTQ is limited opti-
052   mization space. Most works only focus on optimizing distribution transformation or weight clipping
053   ranges (Lin et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2023). While being straightforward, they prove
      inadequate for extremely low-bit scenarios due to the constrained optimization space. For instance,

137   3.1       P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
138
139   Element-wise weight adjustments were also studied in GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2022), in which the
140   weights are computed using closed-form solutions using the inverse Hessian matrix. However, this
141   technique makes it hard to improve scale-transformed models like AWQ1 . Gong et al. (2024) also
142   report similar observations with GPTQ. We hypothesize that the reason for the failed improvement of
143   GPTQ+AWQ could be the layer-wise reconstruction objective and its approximation for the Hessian
144
      matrix, for example, to compute the inverse Hessian they dampen the matrix by λI.
145   In this work, we select a different weight optimization framework, the rounding optimization (Nagel
146   et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021), which is a different optimization space compared to GPTQ, given by

153   able. Note that we omit the block index for simplicity. This rounding optimization framework shares
154

177   3.2   P ROGRESSIVE A DAPTIVE ROUNDING
178
179   To optimize α, we introduce a differentiable rounding optimization framework called progressive
180   adaptive rounding (PAR) that does not rely on regularization loss or the straight-through estimator
181   in contrast to previous works (Nagel et al., 2020; Hubara et al., 2020).
182   To start with, we relax the rounding variable into a continuous variable by using the Sigmoid repa-
183   rameterization α = σ(ν). Therefore, ν can be initialized as ν = σ −1 (θ/s − ⌊θ/s⌋), resulting in
184   θ̂ = θ. The PAR algorithm divides all rounding variables into two sets: SHard and SSoft , standing for
185   the hard and soft rounding of the variable ν. Formally, we define the rounding function as
186                                      (
                                                           1
187                                        σ(νi ) = 1+exp(−ν)    if i ∈ SSoft
                                    αi =                                      .                          (5)
188                                        σ ′ (νi ) = 1νi >0    if i ∈ SHard
189
190   The σ ′ (νi ) is a hard rounding function that returns 1 if νi is larger than 0, otherwise it returns 0.
191   Starting from an empty hard rounding set, we iteratively put variables from SSoft into SHard (called
192   Harden Phase), and optimize the remaining soft rounding variables to compensate for the hard
193   rounding loss (called Soften Phase). We elaborate on them in the following two subsections.
194   Harden Phase. Intuitively, after setting rounding variables to hard ones, we would expect minimum
195   loss change in the block output error. Therefore, we define a score metric
196
197
                                                 HS(ν) = |σ(ν) − 0.5|.                                    (6)
198   Essentially, the lower the score, the closer the soft rounding variable (σ(ν)) is to 0.5, implying that
199   rounding these variables to binary values will result in a larger increase in reconstruction loss. As a
200   result, in the Harden Phase, we sort the parameter indices based on their HS and select the lowest
201   P % of them to SHard . The hyper-parameter P should increase from 0 to near 100 during block
202   reconstruction. During the early stage of the reconstruction, P can be increased rapidly, however, in
203   the later stage, we slowly increase P since the learnable soft variables are becoming fewer in each
204   iteration. In our experiments, we find that TesseraQ is not sensitive to any specific decay schedule
205   for P , as long as we progressively slow down the increasing rate of P . We conduct an ablation study
206
      of how to schedule the change of P in Sec. 4.3.
207   Soften Phase. For this stage, we employ the gradient-descent optimization to optimize the soft

223   3.3   D EQUANTIZATION S CALE T UNING
224
225
      During the PAR process, the quantized tensor θq undergoes continuous changes. To accommodate
      these dynamic adjustments, we propose a method that optimizes the dequantization scale concur-
226
      rently with the rounding variable. Specifically, for the dequantization step, we introduce an addi-
227
      tional parameter v and represent it as
228
229                                        θ̂ = 2σ(v) × s × (θq − z).                                     (9)
230   By initializing v = 0, we initialize the dequantization scale factor (2σ(v)) to 1 and subsequently
231   adjust it to a value within the range (0, 2). The sigmoid reparameterization can smooth the training
232   process and reduce the efforts to adjust learning rate hyper-parameter. Note that we avoid optimizing
233   the scale s in the quantization step (Eq. (1)) since, (1) any change in s would result in a change of
234   the rounding mechanism (Nagel et al., 2020), (2) the optimization requires straight-through estima-
235   tion (Shao et al., 2023) which leads to biased gradient calculation. Experiments in Sec. 4.3 demon-
236
      strate that dequantization scale tuning can benefit the final quantization performance of TesseraQ by
      a large margin.

501

      Quantization has been a primary method to compress and accelerate off-the-shelf large models.
      Survey papers by Gholami et al. (2022) and Nagel et al. (2021) have systematically summarized the
503
      progress of quantization. Here, we list several major quantization works, especially for LLMs.
504
505   Post-Training Quantization for LLMs. While Quantization aware Training (QAT) guarantees
506   better task performance in low-bit quantization, PTQ is more suitable for LLM due to its less
507   reliance on computing resources and training data. PTQ methods like Frantar et al. (2022); Lin
508
      et al. (2023); Wei et al. (2022; 2023); Shao et al. (2023); Chee et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023a)
      improve the uniform quantization performance by optimizing weights, transformation scales, and
509
      clipping ranges. Our method continues improving the uniform quantization effect by incorporating
510
      rounding optimization. Other works try to improve PTQ in LLMs in different ways. For example,
511   AQLM and GPTVQ (Egiazarian et al., 2024; van Baalen et al., 2024) explore non-uniform quanti-
512   zation schemes for weight-only quantization, which may better match the distribution of weights.
513   LLM.int8 (Dettmers et al., 2022), BiLLM (Huang et al., 2024a), SiLLM (Huang et al., 2024b) apply
514   mixed-precision quantization to keep salient weights in high precision and maintain the accuracy.
515   However, these methods cannot be applied to quantize activations and thus cannot support integer
516   MatMul. QuaRot (Ashkboos et al., 2024), SpinQuant (Liu et al., 2024) target activation outliers
517   and eliminate them through the rotation matrix. We have demonstrated that our method can also be
518   combined with them.
519   QAT for LLM. Recent works also explore QAT-based quantization for LLMs. To reduce data ac-
520   cess, LLM-QAT (Liu et al., 2023b) generates language data for data-free QAT. To prevent massive
521   weight memory usage, Q-LoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023) applies quantization-aware low-rank adap-
522   tation for finetuning. Recently, BitNet and BitNet b.158 (Wang et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024) trained
523   a 1-bit and 1.58-bit model from scratch, enabling multiplication-free LLM. However, these methods
524   are hard to scale up due to the massive memory and computation requirements, especially for more
525
      than 70B models. As a result, they only focus on 1B∼3B-scale models.
526
527   6      C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we have proposed TesseraQ, a PTQ method for effectively calibrating large language
530   models. Based on block reconstruction, TesseraQ optimizes weight rounding through a progressive
531   approach that iteratively hardens and softens the rounding variables. Together with dequantization
532   scale tuning, TesseraQ can be seamlessly combined with other PTQ methods like transformation,
533   clipping, and rotation, to reach new state-of-the-art performance. We demonstarte TesseraQ’s su-
534   periority on open source LLaMA models. TesseraQ establishes a new state-of-the-art for quantized
535   LLMs, in terms of perplexity, downstream accuracy and hardware performance.
536   Limitations. TesseraQ shares some limitations in terms of algorithm runtime, which may require
537   longer processing time than existing baselines. For example, the LLaMA-2-7B takes 3∼6 hours
538   to finish the calibration process, while for AWQ/GPTQ, the calibration time is around 0.5 hours.
539   Nevertheless, compared to QAT, our method still exhibits remarkable resource efficiency in required
      data and GPU memory. We leave how to accelerate rounding optimization in our future directions.
