009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            One of the most significant and longstanding problems in computer vision is in-
012            variance - the ability to robustly handle changes in real-world transformations such
013            as rotation, viewpoint, and lighting. Unfortunately, popular foundation models re-
014            main brittle under such transformations. While existing solutions towards invari-
015            ance have shown promise, they all fundamentally require some model training,
016            limiting their ability to adapt broadly to new tasks, transformations, and datasets.
017            Our key insight is that foundation model priors can be used to reason about trans-
018            formations. We thus propose Foundation Model Canonicalization (FMC), an ap-
019
               proach that can undo nuisance transformations in images without any model train-
               ing. With a single core approach, FMC can make models like CLIP and SAM
020
               invariant to different transformations without any training or fine-tuning. Our
021
               approach FMC flexibly adapts to new foundation models and tasks, making it
022            significantly easier for newer and larger models to achieve invariance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      One of the oldest and most important problems in vision is invariance, and one of the oldest and most
028
      promising solutions to invariance is canonicalization (Pitts & McCulloch, 1947; Marr & Nishihara,
029   1978; Hinton, 1981; Tarr & Pinker, 1989; Olshausen et al., 1993). No object appears the same
030   way twice due to rotation, lighting, and viewpoint changes. Yet, the brain can flexibly handle these
031   variations. One leading hypothesis for this impressive ability is that the brain transforms the input
032   into a canonical version, thus eliminating the nuisance variations (Shepard & Metzler, 1971).
033
      In contrast, recent foundation models are brittle against such transformations despite their large-scale
034
      training (Bommasani et al., 2021). This limitation hurts their applicability to real-world settings be-
035   cause mobile agents frequently encounter objects in unfamiliar contexts (e.g., a robot seeing a chair
036   from an unusual viewpoint). While these models show impressive generalization in-distribution,
037   they are unable to handle out-of-distribution inputs.
038
      Achieving invariance in foundation models is especially challenging due to their scale. Re-training
039
      or fine-tuning such models can be prohibitively costly or even impossible in many domains. Thus,
040
      popular invariance approaches such as data augmentation (Bouchacourt et al., 2021), equivariant
041   architectures (Weiler & Cesa, 2019), or learned invariance (Benton et al., 2020) can be impractical
042   since these approaches rely on re-training or fine-tuning. Even if such a model could be fine-tuned,
043   this procedure reduces the out-of-the-box flexibility and generality of the target foundation model.
044
      Thus, we ask: Could a single invariance method work for different tasks, models, and transforms?
045
046   Canonicalization is a promising approach because it decouples the invariance method from the
047   downstream model. Once an input has been made “upright”, any downstream model can use it. A
048   recent line of work (Mondal et al., 2023; Kaba et al., 2022) shows impressive results across domains
049   through learned canonicalization. Specifically, PRLC (Mondal et al., 2023) trains transformation
050
      and model-specific canonicalization networks, relying on fine-tuning or dataset-dependent priors for
      alignment with the downstream model. Still, a key limitation remains: PRLC still requires training
051
      specialist networks for each dataset and transformation pair.
052
053   We thus propose Foundation Model Canonicalization (FMC), a general canonicalization approach
      that provides invariance without any model training (Figure 1). Our key insight is that foundation

097   canonicalize other transformations such as color chrominance and 3D viewpoints (Section 4.3).

106   assume a group of transformations G acting on the input. Specifically, we denote the transformation
107   as Tg : X → X where g ∈ G is a group element. Please note that while Kaba et al. (2022) consider
      linear symmetries, i.e., T ∈ GL(X ), we consider any transforms Tg parameterized by a group.

178   We start with our key insight and describe the overall framework of FMC (Section 3.1). We then
179   define the energy functions based on each foundation model (CLIP, Stable Diffusion, SAM) and
180   how to combine them (Section 3.2). Finally, we then describe how we use Bayesian Optimization
181   to efficiently search the continuous space of transformations (Section 3.3).

183   3.1   K EY INSIGHT AND FRAMEWORK
184
      Key insight: An image x, along with all of its transformed versions Tg (x), defines a slice through
185
      the overall distribution of natural images (Figure 2). In this slice, the canonical version of the image,
186
      x∗ is likely to be encountered the most often in training data (similar to the upright assumption of
187   PRLC (Mondal et al., 2023)). Formally:
188
189                                      pdata (x∗ ) ≥ pdata (Tg (x∗ )) ∀g ∈ G
190   Maximizing this probability over all transformations is sufficient to find the canonical x∗ . A general
191   model of the natural image distribution might thus be used to canonicalize images across a large
192   range of transformations.
193   Framework: We define an energy function EFMC ∝ − log pdata (x) estimated by foundation models
194   to be used as the canonicalizer in Equation (1). Minimizing this energy over transformations thus
195   maximizes the probability of the resulting image, thus canonicalizing it:
196
                             y = f (Tĝ (x)) where ĝ = arg min EFMC Tg (x)                         (3)
197                                                                   g∈G
198
      Our framework is thus divided into three components: (1) A parametrized transformation Tg (e.g.,
199
      rotation), (2) An energy function EFMC derived from various foundation models, and (3) A down-
200
      stream model f that performs the desired task.
201
202   Please note that this framework is fully modular, with the necessary equivariance/invariance emerg-
203   ing from the system design rather than an inherent property of any single component.

303   show that FMC generalizes better than PRLC Mondal et al. (2023) across datasets, extending to new

469    5               D ISCUSSION
470
471    Limitations: As an unsupervised technique, we do not outperform SOTA on pose estimation bench-
472    marks or color correction methods such as Hernandez-Juarez et al. (2020). For 3D, FMC’s ability
473    to classify good viewpoints is weakened for modest gains at worse viewpoints. Further improve-
474    ments and analysis on how to configure FMC with Zero123 could improve performance. Finally,
475    our technique is slow at inference time due to repeated evaluations of large models. This cost could
476
       be further reduced by only using canonicalization for OOD inputs.
477    Choice of Foundation Models: The core principle behind FMC is based on the ability of foundation
478    models to reason about the natural distribution of transformations. We now discuss the requirements
479    for suitable foundation models to provide accurate priors.
480    In theory, the probability distribution represented by our energy functions (Section 2) must approx-
481    imate the data distribution along the submanifold defined by the transformation to work well. The
482    suitable foundation models must then: 1) see enough natural data to be able to model the data
483    distribution across multiple settings, 2) have been trained on realistic data that reflects the natural
484    distribution of images rather than augmented data which may distort the learned prior, and 3) employ
485    a training loss that can model the data distribution either explicitly (e.g., flow models) or implicitly
       (e.g., classifiers through JEM (Grathwohl et al., 2019)). In essence, if the foundation model is a use-


                                                                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      ful out-of-distribution detector for the desired transformations, it is likely to work for FMC. Once
487   foundation models have been selected, the balance between them can be tuned with hyperparameter
488   selection via Bayesian Optimization.

491   6   R ELATED W ORK
492
493
494   Data Augmentation: Data augmentation during training is the simplest and most popular way to
495   achieve invariance, but it requires fixing the transformations ahead-of-time. Some recent work such
496   as VIAT (Ruan et al. (2023)), ViewFool (Dong et al. (2022)), and Omniview Tuning (Ruan et al.
497   (2024)) use adversarial viewpoints as augmentations during training/fine-tuning. However, adapting
498   an existing model to new transformations thus requires expensive re-training or fine-tuning. Ad-
499   ditionally, the range of augmentations (e.g., rotation degrees) is unknown and artificially chosen,
500
      which can hurt accuracy for some classes (Bouchacourt et al., 2021; Kirichenko et al., 2024). Ad-
      ditionally, the resulting model is not as robust for classes with fewer training examples (Zhou et al.,
501
      2022), making data augmentation unsuitable for tasks with imbalanced data.
502
503   Equivariant Networks: Another line of work (Bronstein et al., 2021) aims to design neural net-
504   works with the necessary equivariance hardcoded into the architecture itself. This approach led to
505   Convolutional Neural Networks (LeCun et al., 1999; Fukushima, 1988), and more recently, group
506
      equivariant networks for various transforms Cohen et al. (2019); Esteves et al. (2017); Kondor &
      Trivedi (2018). This elegant approach is useful when the group of transformations is known and
507
      fixed (e.g., 2D rotations on images or 3D rotations of point clouds). However, this approach severely
508
      limits the choice of architecture. In contrast, our approach does not restrict the underlying models.
509
510   Learned Invariance: Augerino (Benton et al. (2020)) invariances from the dataset, learning the aug-
511   mentation range for each transformation independently. LILA (Immer et al. (2022)) improves upon
512
      Augerino by using marginal likelihood methods. InstaAug (Miao et al. (2022)) learns per-instance
      invariances but still struggles with multi-model distributions as it can only model transformation
513
      parameters independently. Singhal et al. (2023) generalize Augerino, LILA, and InstaAug by train-
514
      ing a normalizing flow to jointly predict transformation distributions for all transformations. This
515   flow-based approach generalizes better and adapts to long-tailed data but still requires training. In
516   contrast, our approach does not require any dataset-specific training. Our key insight is that the
517   knowledge needed to achieve invariance already exists in foundation models, and we propose a way
518   to extract these priors. Our approach thus generalizes better across datasets and can be plug-and-play
519   with classifier dataset pairs without further retraining.
520
      Learned Canonicalization: Learned canonicalization has its early roots in mental rotation Shepard
521   & Metzler (1971). Hock & Tromley (1978) found that the response time in humans to recognize ro-
522   tated objects increased linearly with rotation. Tarr & Pinker (1989) drew further ties between mental
523   rotation and invariant object recognition. These works suggest that canonicalizing can robustly align
524   neural networks to the adaptable nature of the human brain.
525
      Kaba et al. (2022) propose a learned canonicalization (LC) approach via a learned energy function.
526
      At test time, it minimizes this function to canonicalize the inputs before passing them through the
527   downstream model, enabling better generalization. EquiAdapt (Mondal et al. (2023)) enforces a
528   regularization prior to align the training set distributions with the predicted canonicalization distri-
529   butions, improving this approach for downstream models.
530
      However, LC and EquiAdapt still require dataset, transform, and model-specific training and do not
531
      generalize well beyond their trained settings (Section 4). In contrast, our approach makes no such
532
      assumptions, instead leveraging energy functions of pre-trained foundation models.
533
534   We discuss more related work in the Appendix.
535   Conclusion: FMC is a training-free method for canonicalization. We note that foundation model
536   priors can be used to reason about transformations. We can outperform PRLC (Mondal et al., 2023)
537   on their specific settings, generalize better to new datasets and models like ImageNet (Deng et al.,
538   2009) and CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), and extend to transformations other than 2D rotations without
539   any training or fine-tuning. FMC is designed to adapt to new models and tasks, enabling equivari-
      ance without the burden of model training.

860
861   All hyperparameters were found using Bayesian Optimization with the same kernel and acquisition
862   function mentioned in Section 3 and performed using the Bayesian Optimization Toolbox (Nogueira,
863   2014) for 300 time steps. Each energy hyperparameter was tuned on a small training or validation
      set by recording logits and finding the combination of energy functions that maximized accuracy.

      For experiments on ImageNet, CIFAR10, CIFAR100, and STL10, we only used the classification
865   energy for computational efficiency. This setting can be reduced to a single free parameter, which
866   we denote αlogit . The coefficient for mean logit is thus αlogit and the coefficient for max logit is
867   (1 − αlogit ).

      Specifically, the αlogit coefficients we found were: 0.59 for CIFAR10, CIFAR100, and ImageNet,

870
      αlogit is 0.74 with a diffusion energy factor of 0.94 and a segmentation energy factor of 1.12. For
871   ImageNet, we also found it helpful to include the mean of top-5 logits with a factor of 0.08.

873   For diffusion energy, we subsample the time steps to range from 500 to 1000 with a stride of 20.

876   A.4   3D
877
878   For our CO3D experiments, we take 10 random videos from each class, and sample 50 random
879   frames from each video. We crop and preprocess the view following the pipeline in (Liu et al.,
880   2023). Like Appendix A.3, we tune the energy hyperparameter with Bayesian Optimization using
881   a 10% subset of the data. The metric optimized is the difference in mean accuracy of the best five
882
      ranks and the worst five ranks. We sort the frames by energy and bin them by their respective video
      frame ranks, and then compute the accuracy of each rank over the videos. The αlogit coefficient
883
      found was 1.31.
884
885   For Chrominance invariance results, we use Bayesian Optimization to make the search more ef-
886   ficient. We initialize the GP with 10 random samples and then iteratively search over 20 more
887   samples.
888   For Objaverse-LVIS (Deitke et al., 2022), we render 400 objects at 36 views, corresponding to
889   the upper hemisphere of azimuth and elevation angles at an interval of 30 degrees. Due to some
890   Objaverse-LVIS containing similar labels which confused the models (e.g., orange vs. mandarin
891   orange vs. tangerine, ring vs. wedding ring, etc.), we filtered the dataset. Specifically, we only
892   kept objects with (1) more than 10% of renders classified correctly and (2) a clear winner class
893   (i.e., the frequency of the most common class should be at least 33% more than 2nd most common
894
      class). This selects roughly 30% of the objects.. This generates the test set of images with different
      viewpoints to evaluate on.
895
896   Then, to evaluate FMC, we start at each Objaverse render, simulate Zero123 (Liu et al., 2023)
897   generated images at azimuth circles of an interval of 30 degrees at elevation angles of [-60, -30,
898   0, 30, 60], taking the minimum energy (best) Zero123 generation as the canonical form. Like for
899   CO3D, we rank the frames, sort and bin them, and compute the accuracy for each rank. However, to
900
      isolate the effects of Zero123 on FMC, we rank both the baseline and FMC curves by the probability
      of the ground truth mask as a proxy for the true ranking of viewpoint. We use the same Bayesian
901
      Optimization setting as CO3D. The αlogit coefficient found was 0.79.

906
907   3D Robustness and pose estimation: Existing approaches for 3D robustness combine multiple
908   views by pooling features across them Fan et al. (2024); Su et al. (2015); Yang & Wang (2019);
909   Wei et al. (2020; 2022); Hamdi et al. (2021); Kanezaki et al. (2018). However, in many vision
910   settings, multiple views or 3D models may not be available. In contrast, our technique only requires
911   one view at test time—we simulate alternate views with a generative model instead. Chen et al.
912   (2020) learns category-level pose estimation using analysis-by-synthesis. This approach is closely
913   related to our approach; however, it is category-specific, whereas our model is category-agnostic.
914
      ImageNet3D (Ma et al., 2024) annotates a large dataset of 3D objects with poses and trains NNs for
      open-set pose estimation in a supervised manner. In contrast, our method is unsupervised.
915
916   OOD detection: A variety of approaches have been proposed for OOD detection, including energy
917   functions and generative models (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016; Liu et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2018;
      Liang et al., 2017; Graham et al., 2023), but this capability has yet to be harnessed for invariance.
