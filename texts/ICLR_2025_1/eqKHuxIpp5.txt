009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The application of machine learning is becoming more widespread, with a grow-
012            ing number of use cases. The development of centralized data training and the
013            exponential growth of data generation raise significant privacy and security con-
014            cerns. On-device training offers a solution by enhancing privacy and reducing
015            the need for communication between the cloud and the device. Furthermore, on-
016            device transfer learning (TL) can leverage the knowledge gained from pre-trained
017            models, hence, accelerating the training process. However, backpropagation, es-
018            pecially in embedded systems, requires more memory than running inference,
019            which becomes a challenge for devices with limited resources. This paper aims
020
               to improve the efficiency and performance of on-device TL. We propose an open
               source mixed-precision partitioning framework that identifies optimal partitioning
021
               layers for retraining, combining quantized and bfloat16 layers to enhance per-
022
               formance and energy efficiency. Our approach is validated through experiments
023            on ResNet-18 and SqueezeNetV1.1 models using Flowers-102, STL-10, and Ox-
024            fordIIITPet datasets. The partitioned mixed-precision model is able to transfer the
025            knowledge from the pre-trained model to new datasets without losing accuracy
026            compared to the baseline bfloat16 model. These results illustrate the potential for
027            resource-constrained devices to perform TL locally.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
033
      Machine Learning (ML) in the past decade has been applied in various fields from healthcare (Javaid
      et al., 2022) to autonomous driving (Bachute & Subhedar, 2021), due to its well-known ability to
034
      derive patterns and make predictions from vast amounts of data. Traditional ML approaches often
035
      involve centralizing data in cloud servers in order to train a model. The following factors, such as
036   the centralized training approach, the growth of generated data, and the continued adoption of ML
037   algorithms, could pose critical concerns about the privacy and security of user data (Xu et al., 2021).
038
039
      Addressing these privacy concerns is one of the reasons to use Transfer Learning (TL). This tech-
      nique allows models to leverage the knowledge of the pre-trained models from another domain (Pan
040
      & Yang, 2010). TL is also applicable when limited data is available, reducing the need for exten-
041
      sive data collection. Many research papers have explored the potential of TL, for example, in the
042   autonomous driving (Chen et al., 2024), and robotics (Zhu et al., 2023). However, to fully imple-
043   ment TL solutions into real-world scenarios, it is essential to bring the training process closer to the
044   data source. This can be achieved through on-device training, where models are trained directly on
045   devices in the deployed environment (Zhu et al., 2022).
046
      On-device training offers several advantages, including improved data privacy, real-time model up-
047
      dates, and reduced latency in cloud-to-device communication, which is very important for an au-
048   tonomous driving use case. Despite these benefits, performing training on a device is a challenging
049   task due to the limited computational resources and memory constraints of embedded devices (Dhar
050   et al., 2019), which are required for the inference and backpropagation on device. To overcome
051   the limitations of hardware, common approach in TL is to freeze weights and biases of the feature
052   extractor layers and only retrain the classifier. This technique enables the network adaptation to new
053   data with less computational resources, but at the same time leads to the accuracy degradation of the
      adapted model. Moreover, current studies rarely address the problem of the partitioning point selec-

      tion, before which all layers of the network are frozen. Hence, our paper fills this gap by answering
055   the following research question:
056
057          • How can we identify a partitioning layer to freeze the preceding layers and retrain the

070   2   M OTIVATION
071
072   The influential paper of Yosinski et al. (2014) proved the possibility of Deep Neural Networks
073   (DNNs) to transfer the learned features from one dataset to another. One of the main results demon-
074   strated in that paper was the performance degradation of the model when only the top layers were
075   retrained. As they concluded, the closer we get to the final layer, the less a model can relearn for a
076   new dataset. Despite of their contribution, many scientific works, e.g. Chiang et al. (2023), still split
077
      models between the feature extraction and the classification layers during transfer learning. Table 1
      shows the accuracy of the ResNet-18 model by splitting at the three feature extraction layers (first,
078
      penultimate, and the last one) and at the classification layer. After splitting, the upper layers were
079
      retrained on three datasets (more details in section 5). The model weights were initially pre-trained
080   on the ImageNet dataset. As expected, splitting a model even one layer before the classifier signifi-
081   cantly improves the model performance. Hence, our first motivation is partitioning the model before
082   the classifier will increase the accuracy.
083
      The mentioned work of Chiang et al. (2023) targeted a challenging task - transfer learning on em-
084
      bedded devices, such as NVIDIA Jetson Nano and Raspberry Pi 4. Due to the limited memory and
085
      computational resources of these devices, the backward pass computation should be highly opti-
086   mized to achieve a lower memory footprint as well as lower latency of the forward and backward
087   passes. It is thus apparent that the bottom layers cannot be considered viable candidates for use as a
088   partitioning point in order to enable on-device transfer learning in such embedded systems. More-
089   over, the reduction in the number of layers undergoing retraining will result in enhanced memory
090   efficiency with regard to backpropagation. As a result, this serves as a second key motivation for
091   our work.
092   Finally, the work of Xiao et al. (2023) demonstrates the significant memory reduction by using int8
093   precision instead of fp16. As stated, quantization is an effective method for reducing the model
094   size and accelerating inference. Other works, such as Rossi et al. (2022), also showed the increased
095   efficiency and performance of using integer rather than single-precision floating-point format for
096   the presented Internet-of-Thing endnode system on chip. The bfloat16 format seems to be the
097   best trade-off between the training performance of a DNN and energy efficiency. As stated by Norrie
098   et al. (2021), bfloat16 works seamlessly for almost all ML training, while reducing hardware and
099   energy costs. They estimated that bfloat16 has approximately a 1.5 x energy advantage over the
100
      IEEE 16-bit float for the more recent 7 nm processors. This leads to our third key motivation, that
      the combination of integer and bfloat16 will significantly increase performance and energy
101
      efficiency.

155

158

159
      existence of numerous potential topological orderings for such models.

      a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG). Non-recurrent DNNs are acyclic and can therefore be represented

      In general, we assume that the sensitivity factor of each layer indicates its impact on the model’s
      accuracy. This allows us to reduce the search space for partitioning to O(N ), where N is the num-
187
      ber of layers. To conduct the in-depth analysis of the impact of each layer in the forward pass, the
188
      current state of the art primarily calculates the sensitivity of each individual parameter (Dash et al.,
189   2022). Nevertheless, this approach entails a considerable runtime overhead, with the analysis requir-
190   ing approximately an hour per eigenvector on two NVIDIA GTX1080 Ti GPUs for a ResNet-18. In
191   contrast, we use the robustness of each layer to quantization as an effective and expedient indicator
192   for identifying sensitive layers. Given the vast number of potential integer precision combinations
193   within the search space, two simplifications based on characteristics of typical hardware architec-
194   tures are applied in our framework to further reduce the runtime. Firstly, the activations and weights
195   are quantized to the same integer precision. Secondly, only the relevant integer computational preci-
196   sion that can be implemented in the system is selected, i.e. 4-, 6-, 8-, and 16-bit integer. Remaining
197
      combinations are efficiently explored with the NSGA-II (Deb et al., 2002), similar to the method-
      ology proposed by Hotfilter et al. (2023). As a result, the exploration algorithm can be defined as
198
      follows.

207   to find Pareto-optimal quantization schemes s ∈ S of the DNN that provide an accuracy a ≥ ath .

209

212   framework assesses 20 generations to identify non-dominated solutions, as described in Definition 5.

263
      The main idea of TL is to utilize pre-trained models on large datasets to derive learned features,
264   and then apply them to improve the learning performance on a new dataset. In our case, we used
265   image classification as a TL task. The ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016) and SqueezeNetV1.1 (Iandola

268   order to demonstrate our approach on the TL example, we used three additional image classification
269   datasets, i.e. the Flowers-102 (Nilsback & Zisserman, 2008), the STL10 (Coates et al., 2011), and
      the OxfordIIITPet (Parkhi et al., 2012).

      5    R ESULTS
340
341
342   In this section, we demonstrate the results of the described experimental setup, in order to validate
343   our mixed-precision partitioning algorithm. Figure 3 shows the results of the proposed partitioning
344   scheme for the ResNet-18 and SqueezeNetV1 1 models to perform TL locally for three datasets.
345   As described in subsection 4.2, in order to validate our approach, we identified the partitioning layer
346   by iteratively freezing the layers and retraining the subsequent ones. Blue dots represent the retrained
347   model in float32, while the orange ones represent the baseline bfloat16 model. Each plot has
348   a red vertical line that illustrates the partitioning layer to retrain the model. The preceding layers are
349   frozen and int8 quantized. The layers after this partition are in bfloat16. The green dots show
350   the test accuracy of the mixed-precision models with this layer configuration.
351   The results yield the following conclusions. Firstly, all plots have an additional vertical line (dotted,
352   blue) that indicates the accuracy of the DNN if it was split before the classifier. In all cases, the
353   accuracy of the model that was retrained at a feature extractor layer was higher than that of the
354   model that was split before the classifier. This highlights the need to consider the upper layers of
355   feature extractor as potential layers of adaptation for transfer learning tasks.
356   Secondly, all plots demonstrate that our proposed mixed-precision algorithm successfully identified
357   the uppermost partitioning layer without losing accuracy compared to the baseline accuracy of mod-
358   els in bfloat16. Moreover, the partitioning scheme was found without the necessity of retraining
359   the model on a number of times equivalent to the total number of layers in the model. Hence, our
360   algorithm significantly reduces the computational overhead.
361   Finally, the partitioned mixed-precision model achieves the same model performance as the full
362   bfloat16 model on new datasets during the TL tasks. As a result, the approach presented in this
363   work successfully identifies the partitioning layer using layer robustness analysis. We verified that
364   a model with mostly all quantized layers can leverage the knowledge from the pre-trained model
365   and transfer it to new datasets. We believe that our approach has the potential to contribute to the
366   realization of on-device TL in embedded devices.

372   presented in this work and the obtained results can be potentially applied to another ML tasks.
373   We also focused on a clear separation between the fully frozen or quantized bottom layers and the

      7   R ELATED W ORK
428
429   It is quite common practice in TL on edge devices to freeze feature extractor layers and train only
430   classifier layers, often using only dense layers of the classifier part (Chiang et al., 2023), (Reguero
431   et al., 2025), (Kang et al., 2024), (Valery et al., 2018). These approaches allow efficient TL, since
      only weights of the classifier are updated, which in turn requires less computational resources and


                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      memory. However, the expressiveness of TL in this case is limited and the accuracy of the adopted
433   model degrades, as it is demonstrated in section 2 or by Cai et al. (2020).
434
435   An alternative approach to TL on the edge that is superior to the aforementioned methods is to
436
      gradually freeze layers based on the per-layer convergence (Li et al., 2024), (Wang et al., 2023).
      The aforementioned methods determine the layers to be frozen during runtime, thereby reducing
437
      the time required for TL. However, in contrast to our approach, the initial model must fit into the
438
      device memory in order to perform the necessary inference and backpropagation at the early training
439   stages. Consequently, since our approach limits the number of layers in backpropagation from the
440   outset, we can significantly reduce the memory footprint in comparison to such methods achieving
441   similar results.
442
      Finally, the idea of partial updates of weights and biases in the backpropagation pass has been
443
      introduced and explored by a few studies such as Lin et al. (2022). Similar to our approach, these
444
      methods allow to reduce memory requirements for on-device training, enabling TL on edge devices.
445   As an example, Cai et al. (2020) freeze the memory-heavy modules (weights of the feature extractor)
446   and only update memory-efficient modules (bias, lite residual, classifier head) during TL, regardless
447   of the position number of the layer. This methodology achieves memory saving compared to fine-
448   tuning the full network. In our work, we used the fully quantized layers along with fully bfloat16
449   layers split by the partition. Nevertheless, a combination of these two approaches to the upper layers
450   of a network will be considered in the future, as they are complementary to each other.
451
452
453   8    C ONCLUSION
454
455   In this paper, we presented our mixed-precision partitioning approach for transfer learning of DNNs
456   in embedded systems. We emphasize that partitioning the model before the classifier improves the
457   model performance. The partitioning algorithm identifies the potential partitioning layer through a
458   process of layer robustness analysis. In order to allow resource-constrained devices to train locally,
459   the algorithm maximizes the number of quantized layers and the top-1 accuracy while minimizing
460   the computational precision. Investigating the best trade-off, we identified the partitioning scheme
461   for a model. It consists of the int8 quantized bottom layers and the bfloat16 upper layers. We
462
      demonstrated our approach on the TL example for the image classification task, using pre-trained
      models and three additional datasets. We showed that the mixed-precision model can be retrained
463
      without losing accuracy compared to the baseline accuracy of the models in bfloat16. This leads
464
      to the conclusion that the mixed-precision model is able to leverage the knowledge from the pre-
465   trained model to new datasets, and retrain locally on a device. Overall, our work can improve the
466   efficiency and performance of on-device transfer learning in embedded devices.
