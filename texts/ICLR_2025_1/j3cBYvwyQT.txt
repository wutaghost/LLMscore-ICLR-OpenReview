009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large-scale general domain pretraining followed by downstream-specific finetun-
012            ing has become a predominant paradigm in machine learning. However, dis-
013            crepancies between the pretraining and target domains can still lead to perfor-
014            mance degradation in certain cases, underscoring the need for task-adaptive con-
015            tinued pretraining (TAP). TAP methods typically involve continued pretraining
016
               on task-specific unlabeled datasets or introducing additional unsupervised learn-
               ing objectives to enhance model capabilities. While many TAP methods perform
017
               continued pretraining with multiple pretraining objectives, they often determine
018
               the tradeoff parameters between objectives manually, resulting in suboptimal out-
019            comes and higher computational costs. In this paper, we propose TapWeight, a
020            task-adaptive pretraining framework which automatically determines the optimal
021            importance of each pretraining objective based on downstream feedback. Tap-
022            Weight reweights each pretraining objective by solving a multi-level optimization
023            problem. We applied TapWeight to both molecular property prediction and nat-
024            ural language processing tasks, significantly surpassing baseline methods. Ex-
025            perimental results validate the effectiveness and generalizability of TapWeight.
026            Our code is publicly available at https://anonymous.4open.science/
027
               r/TapWeight-9A2E.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Foundation models pretrained on large-scale general domain corpora have achieved state-of-the-
032   art performance across a wide range of tasks (He et al., 2021; Devlin et al., 2019; Brown et al.,
033   2020). These models, which capture general knowledge for specific modalities such as text or im-
034   ages through unsupervised learning, are typically adapted to downstream tasks via finetuning. How-
035   ever, when there is a domain discrepancy between the pretraining corpus and the target task, direct
036   finetuning of the pretrained model often fails to deliver optimal results (Lee et al., 2020; Chen et al.,
037   2023; Xie et al., 2024). To address this challenge, downstream task-adaptive continued pretraining,
038   or task-adaptive pretraining (TAP), has been introduced. TAP bridges this gap by introducing an
039
      additional continued pretraining stage between general domain pretraining and task specific fine-
      tuning. For example, Gururangan et al. (2020) conducts task-adaptive pretraining by performing
040
      unsupervised learning on the unlabeled data of the downstream task. Wu et al. (2021) introduces
041
      an additional perturbation masking objective during continued pretraining of a BERT model (Devlin
042   et al., 2019), enhancing its performance on dialogue understanding tasks.
043
044   Among these, many existing task-adaptive pretraining methods consist of multiple pretraining ob-
045
      jectives (Wu et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2021; Cui et al., 2023), making it challenging to determine
      the relative importance of each objective. Some TAP methods assign equal weight to each pretrain-
046
      ing objective (Lee et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021), disregarding their varying impact on downstream
047
      performance. For instance, Gao et al. (2021) shows that pretraining BERT with a contrastive learn-
048   ing (CL) objective results in better downstream performance on semantic textual similarity (STS)
049   datasets than using masked language modeling (MLM) loss, indicating that the CL objective is more
050   important than the MLM objective for these tasks. Other approaches attempt to manually tune the
051   importance ratios through hyperparameter search (Gao et al., 2021), which often results in subopti-
052   mal performance and increased computational costs. This issue becomes particularly severe when
053   the number of pretraining objectives is large, such as with the task-adaptive pretraining of a popular
      molecular model Imagemol, which involves 5 distinct pretraining objectives (Zeng et al., 2022).

076
      To address the aforementioned challenges, we propose a novel framework, TapWeight, designed
      to learn the optimal tradeoff parameters between various pretraining objectives during task-adaptive
077
      pretraining. The goal is to learn these optimal tradeoff parameters such that the pretrained model, af-
078
      ter finetuning on a downstream task, achieves the best downstream task performance. Our approach
079   involves a three-level optimization framework to learn these parameters. In the first stage, we per-
080   form task-adaptive pretraining using initial tradeoff parameters, denoted as λ. These parameters
081   are kept fixed during this stage and will be updated in subsequent stages. The resulting pretrained
082   model is thus a function of λ. In the second stage, the pretrained model from above stage is fine-
083   tuned on the training split of the downstream dataset. Consequently, the finetuned model becomes
084   an implicit function of the tradeoff parameters. In the third stage, the finetuned model is evaluated
085   on the validation split of the downstream dataset, and the tradeoff parameters λ are optimized by
086   minimizing the validation loss. This end-to-end process allows the three stages to dynamically influ-
087
      ence one another, forming an integrated framework that optimizes task-adaptive pretraining process
      and enhances downstream task performance. Moreover, TapWeight is broadly applicable to pre-
088
      trained models with multiple pretraining objectives across various data modalities and downstream
089
      task types, demonstrating superior generalizability compared to existing task-adaptive pretraining
090   methods (Nishida et al., 2021; Cui et al., 2023). Figure 1 illustrates the complete framework of
091   TapWeight.
092
093
      We apply TapWeight for task-adaptive pretraining of both a molecule representation model, Im-
      agemol (Zeng et al., 2022), and a language model, RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019b). Evaluating its
094
      performance across 13 molecular property prediction datasets and 11 natural language processing
095
      tasks, TapWeight significantly outperforms baseline methods. The superior performance of Tap-
096   Weight highlights its effectiveness and generalizability. Our contribution can be summarized as
097   follows:
098
099            • We propose TapWeight, an approach that automatically searches for the tradeoff parameters
100              across multiple pretraining objectives and performs reweighted task-adaptive pretraining.
101              TapWeight is formulated within a multi-level optimization (MLO) framework. We employ
102              an efficient gradient descent algorithm to solve the MLO problem, obtaining the optimal
103              tradeoff parameters for multiple pretraining objectives. Our implementation of TapWeight
104              is publicly available.
105            • We apply TapWeight for task-adaptive pretraining of a molecule representation model and
106              a language model. Extensive experiments on 13 downstream datasets in molecular property
107              prediction and 11 datasets in natural language processing underscore its effectiveness and
                 generalizability.

111   2.1   D OMAIN / TASK A DAPTIVE P RETRAINING
112
113   To bridge the gap between general domain pretraining and downstream tasks in a specific domain,
114   domain-adaptive pretraining (DAP) and task-adaptive pretraining (TAP) have been introduced (Gu-
115   rurangan et al., 2020). DAP performs continued pretraining on a large, unlabeled corpus from a sim-
116   ilar domain as the downstream task. For example, BioBERT continues to pretrain a BERT model on
117   a large-scale biomedical corpus, enhancing its performance on a variety of biomedical text mining
118   tasks (Lee et al., 2020). Similarly, LegalBERT continues to pretrain a BERT model on legal docu-
119   ments to improve performance on legal NLP tasks (Chalkidis et al., 2020), while SciBERT leverages
120
      a large multi-domain corpus of scientific publications for further pretraining, enhancing its effective-
      ness on scientific NLP tasks (Beltagy et al., 2019). More recently, MEDITRON performs continued
121
      pretraining of a Llama-2 model with 80 billion parameters on text in medical domain, showing sig-
122
      nificant performance gains on major medical benchmarks (Chen et al., 2023). U-PaLM (Tay et al.,
123   2023) performs continued pretraining on PaLM (Chowdhery et al., 2024) model with 540 billion
124   parameters using UL2’s mixture-of-denoiser pretraining objective (Tay et al., 2022), achieving per-
125   formance improvement on many few-shot tasks, such as MMLU and GSM8K.
126
      Although DAP significantly improves model performance on downstream tasks, it needs a large cor-
127
      pus of unlabeled data in a specific domain, which is not always available. To address this limitation,
128
      multiple task-adaptive pretraining (TAP) methods have emerged, which do not rely on additional
129   domain-specific corpora beyond the downstream dataset itself. TAP methods can also be viewed as
130   a novel finetuning process, where standard finetuning is preceded by low-cost continued pretrain-
131   ing. For instance, TAPT performs continued pretraining directly on the unlabeled training split of
132   the downstream dataset (Gururangan et al., 2020). TAPTER first trains new word embeddings using
133   the unlabeled training split of the downstream dataset, and then use these embeddings for continued
134   pretraining of the model (Nishida et al., 2021). SimCSE introduces an additional constrastive learn-
135   ing loss in addition to the original masked language modelling loss to further pretrain a RoBERTa
136   model, specifically enhancing its capability on standard semantic textual similarity tasks (Gao et al.,
137
      2021). PCP combines the idea of instruction tuning with conventional continued pre-training, con-
      sistently improving the performance of state-of-the-art prompt-based finetuning approaches on 21
138
      benchmarks (Shi & Lipani, 2023). While existing TAP methods are effective, they are typically tai-
139
      lored to specific downstream tasks or data modalities (Wu et al., 2021; Cui et al., 2023). In contrast,
140   TapWeight is applicable to any pretrained model with multiple pretraining objectives, underscoring
141   its broad generalizability.

      2.2   M ULTI - LEVEL O PTIMIZATION
145
146
147
      Many machine learning tasks can be formulated as multi-level optimization (MLO) problems, such
      as neural architecture search (Liu et al., 2019a; Chen et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2020), meta learn-
148
      ing (Finn et al., 2017; Rajeswaran et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2024), and hyperparameter optimiza-
149
      tion (Lorraine et al., 2020; Lorraine & Duvenaud, 2018; Mackay et al., 2019). MLO problems con-
150   sist of multiple levels of optimization problems that are mutually dependent, making it challenging
151   for common automatic differentiation algorithms to handle them. To tackle this challenge, multiple
152   algorithms (Lorraine et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2019a; Rajeswaran et al., 2019) and libraries (Choe
153   et al., 2023c;a) have been proposed to efficiently compute gradients in MLO problems.
154
      Recently, MLO techniques have been widely adopted in data reweighting and task reweighting. In
155
      these methods, the weights of data or tasks are often treated as hyperparameters and optimized in the
156   upper levels of MLO problems. For example, MetaWeightNet learns an explicit weighting function
157   for each data point to maximize the performance on a small amount of unbiased meta-data (Shu
158   et al., 2019). DoGE optimizes weights for each data domain using a small proxy model to guide
159   the pretraining of larger models (Fan et al., 2024). MetaWeighting learns tradeoff parameters for
160   each task in multi-task learning to minimize generalization loss (Mao et al., 2022). Our method also
161   falls within this category, with a specific focus on reweighting pretraining objectives for downstream
      task-adaptive continued pretraining.

      3.1   OVERVIEW
165
166   Given n continued pretraining objectives T1 , T2 , ...Tn and their corresponding training losses
167   L1 , L2 , ...Ln , we formulate the multi-objective continued pretraining loss Lpt as:
168
169                                                         n
                                                            X
170                                    Lpt (θ, λ, Dpt ) =         λi Li (θ, Dpt )                         (1)
171                                                         i=1
172
173   where Dpt is the unsupervised pretraining dataset, θ denotes the pretraining model parameters, and
174   λi is the tradeoff parameter for each pretraining objective. We denote the target downstream task as
175   Df t and split it into Dtr , Dval and Dts , which are training, validation and test splits respectively.
176   In our framework, TapWeight, we aim to automatically search for the optimal tradeoff weights
177   λ = {λ1 , ..., λn }, so that the pretrained model will achieve highest performance on Dval after
178   finetuned on a downstream dataset Df t . To achieve this, our method consists of three end-to-end
179   stages. In the first stage, we perform continued pretraining of the model, with tradeoff weights
180   tentatively fixed. In the second stage, we conduct finetuning of the pretrained model on the training
181   split of the downstream dataset. In the third stage, we compute a loss by applying the finetuned
182   model on the validation split of the downstream dataset, and optimize the tradeoff parameters by
183
      minimizing this loss. We next formally define these three stages under a multi-level optimization
      framework.
184
185
186   3.2   TAP W EIGHT F RAMEWORK
187
      Level I In the first level, we aim to perform continued pretraining for the model. Formally, the
188
      optimization problem (OP) is to optimize the model weights θ to minimize the multi-objective pre-
189
      training loss Lpt on a unlabeled dataset Dpt :
190
191
192
                                         θ∗ (λ) = arg min Lpt (θ, λ, Dpt )                                (2)
193                                                   θ
194
195   Since the optimal solution θ∗ to this problem depends on the value of the tradeoff parameter, it is an
196   implicit function of λ, denoted as θ∗ (λ).
197
198   Level II In the second level, we aim to finetune the pretrained model with optimal parameters θ∗
199   obtained from previous level on the downstream dataset. However, formulating the optimization
200   problem here with the same set of parameters θ as the lower level imposes high computation and
201   memory burdens, as it requires differentiating through the whole gradient update trajectory in the
202   lower level (Rajeswaran et al., 2019). Optimizing distinct sets of parameters at different levels
203   enables the use of implicit differentiation methods, which significantly reduces computational costs,
204   as detailed in Section 3.3. Therefore, we create a model with new parameters ω that are different
205
      from those in the pretrained model, but with a regularization loss R between ω and θ to encourage
      them to be close. This proximal constraint casts strong dependence between ω ∗ and θ∗ , closely
206
      resembling the real finetuning process. Formally, the OP in this level is to optimize ω by minimizing
207
      the weighted summation of finetuning loss Ltr and the proximal regularization loss R:
208
209
210
                               ω ∗ (θ∗ (λ)) = arg min Ltr (ω, Dtr ) + γR(ω, θ∗ (λ))                       (3)
211                                              ω
212
213   where Dtr is the training split of the downstream dataset, and γ is a tradeoff hyperparameter to
214   balance the finetuning loss and regularization loss. In practice, we select the mean squared error
215   (MSE) loss as the regularization loss R. The optimal solution of ω in this level is a function of θ∗
      due to the loss term R, which is in turn a function of λ, denoted as ω ∗ (θ∗ (λ)).

238   3.3     O PTIMIZATION A LGORITHM
239
240   In this section, we illustrate the algorithm we use to efficiently approximate the gradient of loss Lval
241   in the third level with respect to the tradeoff parameter λ. This full derivative dLdλval can be computed
242   with the following equation using chain rule:
243
244
                                          dLval   ∂Lval   ∂ω ∗   ∂θ∗
245                                             =       ×      ×                                           (6)
                                           dλ      ∂ω ∗   ∂θ∗    ∂λ
246
247   In the right hand side of Equation 6, the green term, a partial derivative vector, can be directly
248   computed with popular automatic differentiation libraries, such as Pytorch (Paszke et al., 2019).
249   However, directly computing the two red terms, which are best-response Jacobian matrices, can be
250   computationally prohibitive due to the lack of analytical solutions to these optimization problems.
251   Inspired by previous works (Lorraine et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021), we use Implicit Function
252
      Theorem (IFT) based methods to approximate the best-response Jacobian matrices. We include
      more details of IFT based gradient computation method in Appendix A. In this way, we are able to
253
      compute both red terms in Equation 6 efficiently, thereby obtaining the gradient of Lval with respect
254
      to λ. We then optimize the tradeoff parameter λ with gradient descent. The complete algorithm is
255   implemented using the Betty library (Choe et al., 2023c;b).

346   4.2     NATURAL L ANGUAGE P ROCESSING
347
348   In this section, we validate the effectiveness of TapWeight for continued pretraining of a masked
349   language model (MLM) with its application to natural language processing tasks.
350
351   4.2.1    P RELIMIMARY
352
      Given a large-scale raw-text dataset D = {xi }1≤i≤n consisting of millions of sentences, we define
353
      the following continued pretraining loss:
354
355
356                              L(x) = λ1 Lmlm (x) + λ2 Lcl (x) + λ3 Lsop (x)                               (8)
357
358   where x is a sentence, and λ = {λi }1≤i≤3 are tradeoff parameters. Lmlm represents the masked
359   language model loss, which involves randomly masking tokens in the input sentences and predicting
360   these masked tokens (Devlin et al., 2019). Lcl denotes the contrastive learning loss, where an input
361   sentence is used to predict itself with standard dropout applied as noise (Gao et al., 2021). Lsop
362   is the sequence ordering prediction loss, which emphasizes inter-sentence conherence (Lan et al.,
363   2020). We include details of these losses in Appendix C.1.
364   The multi-objective loss L is optimized on the raw text dataset D for continued pretraining of a
365   Transformer encoder. The learnt encoder can then be finetuned on downstream NLP datasets. In ex-
366   isting works (Gao et al., 2021), the tradeoff parameters λ for different pretraining objectives require
367   manual hyperparameter tuning, which is time-consuming and often leads to suboptimal results. We
368   address this challenge by applying TapWeight for the continued pretraining of a Transformer en-
369   coder, enabling the automatic determination of the importance for each objective.
370
371
      4.2.2    E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS

488   In this section, we present the evolution trend of the pretraining objective weights along the training
489   trajectory using our method. As shown in Figure 2, we plot the value of λ for 3 regression tasks

491   training step. Our observations reveal that different downstream datasets require varying importance
492   for each pretraining objective. For example, the JPP pretraining objective, Ljpp , plays an key role in
493   Lipo and Toxcast datasets, whereas the MG3 pretraining objective, Lmg3 , is more critical for Esol,
494   Freesolv and Tox21 datasets. The diverse requirements of pretraining objectives across downstream
495   datasets emphasize the need for a reweighting method like TapWeight, providing a clear explana-
496
      tion for why our method outperforms baseline approaches. Furthermore, similar downstream tasks
      exhibit some degree of similarity in the weights assigned to pretraining tasks. For instance, the Esol
497
      and Freesolv datasets, both focused on predicting physical chemistry properties of molecules, as-
498
      sign large weights to the MG3 pretraining objective. In contrast, the ToxCast and ClinTox datasets,
499   which involve predicting molecular toxicity, assign smaller weights to the MG3 objective.

      5     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
516
517   In this paper, we propose a task-adaptive continued pretraining method that dynamically reweights
518   each pretraining objective within a multi-level optimization framework. Unlike previous approaches
519   that use a fixed ratio between pretraining objectives, our method adjusts the importance of each
520
      objective based on feedback from downstream datasets. Experiments in both molecule property
      prediction and natural language processing validate the effectiveness and generalizability of our
521
      method.
522
523   Given the success of TapWeight, several promising future research directions emerge. For instance,
524   large multimodal pretrained models have recently gained popularity (Liu et al., 2023; Zhu et al.,
525   2024). The combination of multiple modalities introduces a greater number of potential continued
526
      pretraining objectives, presenting necessities of applying TapWeight in this context. Additionally,
      exploring objective reweighting strategies for general pretraining, rather than task-adaptive pretrain-
527
      ing (TAP), is another promising direction. Unlike TAP, pretraining objective reweighting in general
528
      domain presents greater challenges for algorithm efficiency, as general pretraining typically incurs
529   significantly higher computational costs.

      Attribute Masking Attribute masking (AttrMask) based pretraining captures domain knowledge
907
      by learning the regularities of the node/edge attributes distributed over graph structure (Hu et al.,

913   tures (Hu et al., 2020). It pretrains a GNN so that it maps nodes appearing in similar structural
914   contexts to nearby embeddings. Specifically, the method first encodes the context into a fixed vector
915   using an auxiliary GNN, and then trains the GNN encoder with negative sampling.

       ship between a premise and a hypothesis. Question-answering Natural Language Inference (QNLI)
987
       involves question-paragraph pairs, with the task of determining whether the paragraph contains the
988    answer to the question. The Recognizing Textual Entailment (RTE) datasets consist of sentence
989    pairs from news and Wikipedia, where the task is to predict the entailment between two sentences.

991    C.3    BASELINE M ETHODS
992
993    RoBERTa The Robustly Optimized BERT Pretraining (RoBERTa) paper (Liu et al., 2019b) thor-
994    oughly evaluates the impact of key hyperparameters and training data size in BERT. RoBERTa
995    uses the same architecture as BERT but is pretrained with an optimized strategy, leading to signifi-
996    cant improvements in performance across various downstream tasks. The main differences between
997
       RoBERTa and BERT are: (1) training for a longer duration with larger batches and more data; (2) re-
       moving the next sentence prediction objective; (3) training on longer sequences; and (4) dynamically
998
       adjusting the masking patterns applied to the training data.

1000
       SimCSE The Simple Contrastive Learning of Sentence Embeddings (SimCSE) framework in-
1001
       cludes both unsupervised and supervised approaches. In the unsupervised approach, SimCSE takes
1002   an input sentence and predicts the same sentence using a contrastive objective, where standard
1003   dropout serves as the noise. In the supervised approach, it integrates annotated pairs from natu-
1004   ral language inference datasets into the contrastive framework, using human-labeled ”entailment”
1005   pairs as positive examples and ”contradiction” pairs as hard negatives.
