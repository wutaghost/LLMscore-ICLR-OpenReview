009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We test the robustness of debate as a method of scalable oversight by training mod-
012            els to debate with data generated via self-play. In a long-context reading compre-
013            hension task, we find that language model based evaluators answer questions more
014            accurately when judging models optimized to win debates. By contrast, we find
015            no such relationship for consultancy models trained to persuade a judge without
016            an opposing debater present. In quantitative and qualitative comparisons between
017
               our debate models and novel consultancy baselines, we find evidence that debate
               training encourages stronger and more informative arguments, showing promise
018
               that it can help provide high-quality supervision for tasks that are difficult to di-
019
               rectly evaluate.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   As AI systems tackle increasingly challenging problems, it will become correspondingly more dif-
025   ficult for humans to verify their answers as safe, useful, and accurate. For example, confirming the
026   solution to a graduate-level physics problem requires domain expertise, evaluating a literature re-
027   view requires considerable time, and identifying a race condition in code requires careful reasoning,
028   all of which a human may struggle with under practical time and resource constraints. As existing
029   AI alignment and oversight approaches depend on reliable human supervision, we will need new
030   interaction mechanisms and training protocols for scalable oversight (Amodei et al., 2016; Bowman
031
      et al., 2022), i.e., ones which scale with the increased complexity of the tasks being performed by
      state-of-the-art AI models.
032
033   Debate, proposed as a scalable oversight method by Irving et al. (2018), works by having two copies
034   of a model argue against each other in defense of alternative responses to a question. A judge,
035   who can be either a human or a weaker, trusted model, tries to discern which debater is defending
036   the correct answer. In principle, debate should simplify evaluation by incentivizing the competing
037
      models to discover and explain the subtle flaws that a human or weaker model may not notice due
      to a lack of expertise, care, or time. As long as the refutational abilities of models scale alongside
038
      their general argumentation skills, we would expect that debates between more proficient models
039
      will yield more accurate judgments.
040
041   Validating debate as an oversight paradigm requires showing this empirically. Existing work has pro-
042   duced promising results for debate in human experiments (Michael et al., 2023) and with inference-
043
      time optimization of frontier models (Khan et al., 2024; Kenton et al., 2024), but prior work training
      models to debate has failed to show significant increases in evaluator accuracy (Radhakrishnan,
044
      2023).
045
046   In this work, we show for the first time that training language models to win debates can pro-
047   duce more accurate evaluator judgments, taking another crucial step in implementing and validating
048   debate as a practical scalable oversight method. 1 To do so, we train a calibrated judge model
049
      and develop a variant of Direct Preference Optimization (DPO; Rafailov et al., 2023) for multi-
      turn debate training (Section 3). For our experiments, following Michael et al. (2023), we study
050
      information-asymmetric debates on reading comprehension questions from the QuALITY dataset
051
      (Pang et al., 2022), where the judge cannot see the underlying short story except through quotes
052

092   2.1   TASK D ESIGN
093
094   Following Parrish et al. (2022b), our debates center on questions from the QuALITY dataset (Pang
095   et al., 2022) of multiple choice reading comprehension questions over short stories. We exclusively
096   sample questions from the QuALITY-HARD subset, with one debater defending the correct answer
097   and the other defending the answer marked as the best distractor by annotators.
098   To ensure that the judge needs the debate transcript to answer each question, we follow Michael
099   et al. (2023) in using a hidden information setup where the debaters have access to the short story
100   text while the judge can only read quotes from the story selected by the debaters. This setup models
101   a scalable oversight setting by artificially elevating the capabilities of the debaters relative to that of
102   the judge: in this case, the debater’s expertise comes from superior access to information.

135   2.3      BASELINES
136
137   For our baselines, we extend the consultancy setup introduced by Michael et al. (2023) into three
138
      variants.
139
140
                • Under single consultancy, an individual debater (the “consultant”) introduces arguments
                  in favor of their assigned side with no opposing debater present, with the consultant being
141
                  assigned the correct side half of the time. For their second speech in the round, the con-
142
                  sultant generates additional arguments to support the claims they advanced in their initial
143               speech. This setup represents a simplified version of the oversight process in Reinforcement
144               Learning from Human Feedback (Christiano et al., 2017), testing whether a supervisor can
145               properly evaluate arguments without the aid of AI critiques or competing claims. It also
146               matches the consultancy setup of Michael et al. (2023), with the exception that we include
147               no judge interaction.
148
                • Ensembled consultancy averages together the results of two separate single consultancies
149               in which the consultant argues for each of the two possible answers to a question. This
150               kind of pairwise comparison should, in theory, improve the judge’s accuracy by correcting
151               for calibration errors in which the judge systematically gives scores that are either too
152               high or too low (Zheng et al., 2023; Liusie et al., 2024). These errors can arise from
153               sycophancy bias, where the judge is overly inclined to agree with its interlocutor(Perez
154               et al., 2022), or from the model learning to misleadingly convince its evaluator (Wen et al.,
155               2024). Ensembled consultancy may also increase accuracy if the judge is often uncertain
156               except for the occasional case where the correct answer has ironclad arguments in its favor.
157             • Double consultancy is similar to ensembled consultancy except that both sets of speeches
158               are presented to the judge in one context, allowing the judge to explicitly compare the
159               arguments to produce a single judgment. It differs from debate in that the debaters never get
160               to see the claims advanced by their opponent. The difference between the debate and double
161               consultancy results lets us measure the strength of the debaters’ capacity for refutation and
                  the importance of refutation in the judge’s decision-making process.

      2.4   E VALUATION
167
168   For each debater and consultant model, we compute its win rate compared to other models, which
169   tracks how well it optimizes its training objective, and the judge accuracy when evaluating tran-
170   scripts, which tracks how well the training and evaluation protocol produce truth-seeking behavior.
171
      Following Khan et al. (2024), we also investigate how judge accuracy changes with optimization—
172   if the trend is positive, this provides evidence that the oversight protocol will continue to produce
173   truth-seeking behavior with more advanced AI systems.
174
175          • Judge accuracy is measured using self-play where each model is pit against a copy of
176            itself. The judge is considered to be correct if it assigns greater than 50% probability to the
177            correct answer. For single consultancy, where there is no opposing model, judge accuracy
178            is averaged equally between cases where the consultant is advocating for the correct and
179            incorrect answer.
180          • Debater win rate is measured using a round-robin tournament where each model debates
181            every other model. Since some positions are easier to defend than others, each question
182            gets debated twice, with the debaters flipping sides between rounds, and a debater wins if
183            it receives an average judge confidence over 50% across both rounds. The results of the
184            round-robin tournament are then used to construct Elo scores for each model. These Elo
185            scores yield an implied probability that a given model will defeat an average debater, which
186
               we report as the final reported win rate.
187          • Consultant win rate is the frequency with which the judge assigns a greater than 50%
188            probability to the position being defended by the consultant in single consultancy. Although
189            ensembled and double consultancy allow for head-to-head matchups, we use the single
190            consultancy win rate when tracking the relationship between consultant skill and judge
191
               accuracy because the models are trained to win at single consultancy,

195   3.1   J UDGE
196
197   We use a finetuned version of GPT-4-Turbo (GPT-4T) as our judge. Although Khan et al. (2024)
198   found that the publicly vended version could judge rounds with high accuracy, two weaknesses limit
199   its utility for our purposes:
200
201
            1. Calibration: We are interested not only in choosing a winner, but also in assigning a
               probability to each answer in order to construct a high-quality reward signal. We found it
202
               difficult to extract calibrated probabilities from GPT-4T, as asking directly for probabilities
203
               as tokens produced overconfident and uncalibrated results (Figure 3).
204
205         2. Sycophancy: Many RLHF-trained models default to agreeing with their user (Perez et al.,
206
               2022). Although this is not necessarily a problem for debate, where the judge sees argu-
               ments for both sides, it makes the judge more exploitable by a one-sided consultant. In our
207
               experiments, an untrained GPT-4-Turbo judge agreed with the consultant 72% of the time,
208
               even before consultancy training. For a tougher baseline, we want a judge that is calibrated
209            to the 50/50 prior probability that the consultant is correct.
210
211   To circumvent these issues, we finetune GPT-4T using the OpenAI finetuning API. As training data,
212   we use the human judgments on debate and consultancy transcripts from Michael et al. (2023) and
213   Khan et al. (2024). Although we trained the model to output its confidence as tokens, we found that
214   we obtained the most calibrated results by using the token-level probabilities associated with each
215   debater’s name, which were no longer clustered at the boundaries as they were prior to finetuning
      (Figure 3).

249   3.2.2    S ELF -P LAY DPO T RAINING

251   After supervised finetuning, we further finetune our models with multiple iterations of a novel,
252   modified version of Direct Preference Optimization (DPO; Rafailov et al., 2023). We choose DPO
253   over standard RL methods like PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) because of ease of implementation
254
      and tuning. However, the standard formulation of DPO assumes access only to discrete preference
      judgments. Since we have access to the AI judge’s output probabilities, this means throwing away
255
      information about the exact reward. We modify DPO to take advantage of this information.

342   Sampling Method We generate our soft preference dataset for DPO using self-play, using branch-
343   ing rollouts to get two completions for each prompt. We begin by sampling self-play debate rollouts
344   with one of the two sides (i.e., a debater defending answer A or B) randomly designated as the target.
345   Each time the target model takes a turn, we sample two speeches from the model instead of one and
346   bifurcate the game tree. We then recurse through each subtree until we reach the end of the debate
347   (in our case, after two turns), where we use the judge’s decision to compute the final reward for the
348   target model. The expected reward for each of the target model’s speeches is estimated using the av-
349
      erage reward at the leaves in its subtree. These estimated rewards are used to produce the weighted
      preferences that comprise our modified DPO training dataset. For more details, see Appendix E.
350
351
352   Training Procedure We train the debater and consultant using multiple iterations of our variant of
353   DPO (Xiong et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024), starting from the SFT model. During each iteration, we
354   sample branching rollouts from the current model (as described above) to produce a new preference
355   dataset. We then combine this with the preference data from previous iterations to form a shuffled,
356   aggregate dataset which is used to run another round of modified DPO training initialized from the
357   SFT model. All of our analysis results are reported on different checkpoints from the final iteration
358   that is trained on the full, aggregated dataset.
359
360
      Implementation Details We run two iterations of DPO training, where, in each iteration, we add
361   7,512 preference pairs drawn from both sides of 1,252 unique questions in the QuALITY training
362   split (three pairs per round, two rounds per question). To save memory, all models are trained with
363   low rank adapters (Hu et al., 2021) on the attention and MLP projection matrices with a rank of 128.
364   We train with a mini-batch size of 32, a learning rate of 10−5 , and a β (KL penalty) value of 0.5.
365   Exclusively for the second round of debate training, we use a lower learning rate of 5−5 as that was
366   found to produce a more performant model in head-to-head debates (we ran a similar hyperparameter
367   sweep for consultancy, but a lower learning rate did not improve the win rate). Based on the results
368   of a brief hyperparameter sweep, we set γ = 7 for debate and γ = 10 for consultancy, and weigh
369
      the SFT loss at α = 0.005.

      4.3   E XPOSURE TO I NFORMATION I NFLUENCES J UDGE ACCURACY M ORE T HAN
            R EFUTATION
418
419
      Results for double and ensembled consultancy provide further insight into why debate can help
420   judges answer questions with greater accuracy than single-consultancy–like alternatives. The
421   strength of ensembled consultancy (72% accuracy across all checkpoints) relative to its single coun-
422   terpart (68% accuracy) suggests that a sizeable portion of the gap between debate and consultancy
423   is due to asymmetric strength of evidence, where considering only the strength of the evidence for
424   each side allows for a better decision.
425
      Similarly, double consultancy (75% accuracy) is more likely to yield correct answers than ensembled
426   consultancy, implying that the judge benefits from being able to see speeches from both sides in
427   context at the same time. That judge accuracy in double consultancy is close to that of debate
428   (77% accuracy) despite the former precluding each side from seeing the other’s arguments suggests
429   that either (a) the debaters are failing to engage in meaningful refutation, or (b) the judge does not
430   benefit from reading the models’ refutations. We also run experiments with single-turn debate and
431   consultancy which yield a similar conclusion, as the one-turn debates where no explicit refutation
      could occur are judged just as accurately as two-turn debates (see Appendix G).

      4.4   D EBATERS L EARN M ORE H ELPFUL P OLICIES T HAN C ONSULTANTS
433
434   We track two different metrics to explain how the models are able to become more convincing over
435   the course of training.
436
437         1. Use of evidence (Figure 6, left): The speeches of the fully-trained debate model contain
438            96% more quoted words than the initial SFT model. The story is the reverse for consul-
439            tancy: the final model uses 70% fewer quotes than the initial SFT model, although this trend
440            is barely significant due to high variance. The consultant also becomes more repetitive over
441            time, with 98% of quoted material in the final model’s second speech being repeated from
442
               its initial speech.
443         2. Transfer to other judges (Figure 6, right): We also evaluate the debaters and consultants
444            using an untrained version of GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024). We find a Pearson correlation of
445            0.98 between the win rates of the debaters according to the trained GPT-4T judge and
446            untrained GPT-4o judge but only a 0.51 correlation for consultancy. This suggests that
447
               the debater may be learning more generally useful or informative argumentation strategies
               while the consultant model is learning features that are idiosyncratically convincing to the
448
               particular judge it was optimized against.
449
450
451   5     D ISCUSSION
452
453   5.1   A NALYSIS
454
455   DPO training can help current open-source 8B-sized models be more convincing to a GPT-4–
456   level judge. The fully trained debate model wins 67% of the time against the average debater in
457   the sample and 82.5% against its SFT-trained base model. Similarly, the fully-trained consultant
458
      convinces the judge of its position 52% of the time, up from 36% with just supervised finetuning.
      These results suggest it may be feasible to train much stronger persuasive models with larger LMs
459
      and more compute.
460
461
      Models trained to debate are more likely to learn helpful policies than models trained for
462
      consultancy. As the debate models grow stronger during training, they use more evidence from
463
      the underlying text. Not only does this behavior fail to arise in the consultant models, we instead
464   observe an increase in repetition and the adoption of argumentative strategies that convince our judge
465   model but do not convince other models.
466
467
      It seems plausible that the presence of competing arguments at training time — as is true in debate
      — should help prevent this behavior. For example, it might be more obvious that a debater is making
468
      assertions without supporting evidence if the speech is juxtaposed against another, better evidenced
469
      one. In general, if the persuasiveness of a cheap argumentative strategy, like repetition or baseless
470   claims, is independent of the truth value of the claim being defended, then incentivizing the adoption
471   of such a strategy should fail to improve judge accuracy.
472
473
      Explicit refutation does not yet seem to play a role in judge decision making in our setting.
474   When first proposing debate as a means of scalable oversight, Irving et al. (2018) cited refutation
475   as a key mechanism behind why debate might succeed. The idea is that each of the debaters could
476   identify flaws in their opponent’s facts and reasoning, which would be easier for the judge to evaluate
477   than if they had to personally originate the various counter-considerations. Although a surface-level
478   reading of the transcripts does find cases of apparent refutation (Figure 2), we find little evidence
479   that this refutation materially affects the judge’s decision making.
480   Instead, our results support the idea that debate outperforms consultancy due to a combination of
481   several factors:
482
483         1. The presentation of two different sides gives the judge more opportunities to settle the ques-
484            tion on the basis of strong arguments, taking advantage of cases with asymmetric strength
485            of evidence for either side. This would explain why the judge is more accurate when eval-
               uating ensembled consultancies than single consultancies.


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
            2. The presence of two different sides in one context allows the judge to directly weigh ar-
487            guments against each other, as we observe in the success of double consultancy relative to
488            ensembled consultancy.
489
            3. The presence of two different sides in one context at training time also discourages the
490
               exploitation of weaknesses in the judge model, which we see evidence of in our analysis
491            of the learned policies in Section 4.4. The difference in judge accuracy between double
492            consultancy and debate, at least for the fully-trained models, may be attributable to this
493            feature.
494
495   5.2   R ELATED W ORK AND L IMITATIONS
496
497   Previous literature has mixed results on the question of whether debate helps evaluators discern truth,
498   with several negative results using humans as debaters and judges (Barnes & Christiano, 2020; Par-
499   rish et al., 2022b;a). On the other hand, Michael et al. (2023) find a positive result for human debate
500   relative to consultancy, citing the length, flexibility, and interactivity of their debates as reasons for
501
      the difference from prior findings. Research on debate between language models has shown more
      optimistic results, with some caveats. Khan et al. (2024) find that debate outperforms a baseline sim-
502
      ilar to our single consultancy, and that this effect grows alongside the abilities of the debaters. How-
503
      ever, the parity of this skill-accuracy relationship is ambiguous for stronger, GPT-4–level models,
504   and their consultancy results optimize against a GPT-4T judge which is overly sycophantic (agree-
505   ing with their strongest consultants over 90% of the time). Similarly, while Kenton et al. (2024)
506   record similar findings for debates on reading comprehension tasks, they are unable to replicate the
507   findings on other kinds of tasks.
508
      In this work, we show that the positive judge accuracy trend observed for inference-time opti-
509
      mization of debate (Khan et al., 2024; Kenton et al., 2024) persists with debate training, a result
510   which Radhakrishnan (2023)—the only prior work to train models to debate in a scalable oversight
511   context—failed to observe. On top of this, we show that this effect persists even after mitigating
512   sycophancy bias with a trained judge, and our two novel consultancy baselines help explain debate’s
513   stronger performance.
514
      Nonetheless, debate’s mixed record in the literature suggests that our results should be interpreted
515
      with caution. First, we do not foreclose the possibility that even stronger models might find strategies
516   that perplex the judge and draw out debates, like Barnes (2020)’s obfuscated arguments. Second,
517   our judge–debater expertise gap, relying on asymmetric access to textual information, may not be
518   the best proxy for expertise gaps in, e.g., reasoning abilities (Kirchner et al., 2024), that we will need
519   to supervise across in the future. Third, we focus only on reading comprehension questions. In their
520   experiments, Kenton et al. (2024) find that debate is more helpful for these kinds of questions than
521   for other reasoning-related tasks. However, more recently, George et al. (2024) document affirmative
522   evidence that GPT-3.5 can supervise GPT-4–level debaters on knowledge-based multiple-choice
523   questions, providing preliminary evidence that the debate procedure can succeed in other domains.
524
525   6     C ONCLUSION
526
527   We explore whether training models to win debates can also help judges determine the correct an-
528   swer to reading comprehension questions where the judge does not have access to the text of the
529   story being discussed. We find that there is indeed a small but significant positive relationship be-
530   tween the ability of the model to win a debate and the usefulness of that model’s debate transcripts
531   in discovering true answers.
532   Non-adversarial alternatives, in which a single model argues for an assigned answer, are compar-
533   atively less productive. We trace this weakness to three sources: one-sided information (the judge
534   is unaware of the strength of the alternative answer), lack of explicit comparison (the judge cannot
535   see arguments side-by-side), and the rewarding of non-truth-seeking strategies (where the lack of an
536   adversary makes the judge easier to exploit).
537   Although our conclusions are limited to one particular domain and set of model capabilities, these
538   results nonetheless suggest that debate training has unique properties that make it well suited for
539   supervising more sophisticated models.

      A.1   D EBATE FOR S CALABLE OVERSIGHT
813
814   Debate fits within the broader paradigm of scalable oversight, which attempts to empower a less
815   capable evaluator to oversee a more capable model (Amodei et al., 2016; Bowman et al., 2022).
816   Our approach is a variant of sandwiching, where the outputs of the oversight protocol are compared
817   against experts more capable than the supervisor (the weakest participant) and models that are not
818   robustly aligned (Cotra, 2021). In our case, we engineer the capability gap using information asym-
819
      metry (where the story the question is fully visible only to the debaters), and engineer the models’
      misalignment by forcing models to argue for the right answer exactly 50% of the time).
820
821   Irving et al. (2018) introduced the concept of AI safety via debate, arguing via analogy to compu-
822   tational complexity theory that debate should should simplify the supervisor’s job, allowing a poly-
823   nomial judge to correctly answer questions in PSPACE under the assumption of optimal debaters.
824   Recent work by Brown-Cohen et al. (2023) develops this theory further.
825   However, other work has also identified problems with certain debate protocols. Barnes (2020) and
826   Barnes & Christiano (2020) identify an obfuscated arguments problem, where the debater advocat-
827   ing for the incorrect position are able to make lengthy, complicated argument chains against which
828   the correct debater was unable to mount a simple and concise rebuttal.
829   Subsequent work with humans taking the place of models has also reached mixed conclusions.
830
831         • Parrish et al. (2022b) and Parrish et al. (2022a) found that debate did not improve the
832           accuracy of judges in practice. Like us, they used the QuALITY dataset from Pang et al.
833           (2022) and experimented on one- to two-round debates. Unlike us, they limited the judge’s
834           access to the underlying short story to a narrow time window, rather than obfuscating it
835           entirely.
836         • In contrast, Michael et al. (2023) found that debate improves judge accuracy, evaluating
837           on the same QuALITY questions. They attribute their divergent conclusion to the length
838           of their debates (the round only ended when the judge chose to end it), the capability gap
839           between the debaters and judge (unlike Parrish et al. (2022a), the judge could not read the
840           story at all), and interactivity (the judge was allowed to ask questions of the debaters).
841
      More recently, there has also been work that has tested how well debate has performed with language
842
      models as the debaters.
843
844         • Also looking at questions from the QuALITY dataset, Khan et al. (2024) tested different
845           API-based models and found that the accuracy of the judges (both human and model-based)
846           improved as the debaters got stronger. They varied the model type and used Best-of-N
847           decoding and critique-and-refinement to generate models of varying strength.
848         • Concurrently with our work, Kenton et al. (2024) evaluated debate across a suite of different
849           tasks, also using Best-of-N and varying model size to generate debaters of various skill
850           levels. They found positive results for reading comprehension, but more muted results in
851           other settings.
852
853   The most similar work to ours is Radhakrishnan (2023), who used reinforcement learning to train
854
      Claude to participate in single-turn debates. We differ from their work by using open-source models,
      public training details, and validating against a baseline. We also use multi-turn debates, affording
855
      the debaters the opportunity to respond to their opponents.
856
857
      A.2   D EBATE AS C APABILITY E LICITATION
858
859   Outside of the scalable oversight literature, debate and multi-agent discussion have been explored
860   as methods to unlock new capabilities from language models at decoding time. Work in this area
861   generally falls into one of two categories:
862
863         • Viewpoint Diversity: Many works prompt models to mimic the behavior of different kinds
              of people, in order to produce a final output that represents a wider variety of perspectives

                                                       16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
               (Cheng et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Chan et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024a; Lu et al., 2024;
865            Pang et al., 2024; Mao et al., 2024).
866
             • Extra Computation: Other works use debate as a means of eliciting additional computa-
867
               tional steps in order to improve models’ reasoning ability (Moniri et al., 2024; Du et al.,
868
               2023; Chern et al., 2024). In this sense, it is similar to more popular methods like chain-of-
869            thought reasoning (Kojima et al., 2023) or self-refinement (Madaan et al., 2023).
870
871   Although many of these works use a similar debating format, their purposes are very different,
872   as testing debate as a scalable oversight protocol requires showing that a judge can successfully
873   adjudicate debates between models that are stronger than itself in relevant ways.
874
875   A.3    L ANGUAGE M ODELS AS E VALUATORS
876
877   Although not the core of our contribution, our work is related to the literature on language models
878   as evaluators. Most works in this specialty focus on devising techniques to enable language models
879
      to score the quality of other language model completions. Some of these take the form of prompting
      (Liu et al., 2023) while others take the form of specially-trained models (Kim et al., 2024b; Vu
880
      et al., 2024). Automated judges have also been used as scorers on widely-cited benchmarks (Li
881
      et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024). These works serve a similar purpose as reward
882   modeling (Christiano et al., 2017), with the distinction being that the latter uses a classification rather
883   than language modeling head in their final layer.
884
885
      A few works have also specifically designed language models to judge debates, including Rescala
      et al. (2024) and Liang et al. (2024).
886
887   Although many of these works attempt to address known biases such as self-preference (Panickssery
888   et al., 2024; Koo et al., 2023), length (Dubois et al., 2024), position order (Koo et al., 2023), and
889   sycophancy biases (Perez et al., 2022), we have the additional constraint in that our judge needs to
890   be robust to adversarial optimization pressure.

1046   E    T RAINING ROLLOUT P ROCEDURE
1047
1048   A prerequisite for DPO training is to assemble a dataset of preference pairs. Specifically, we need
1049   pairs of completions (speeches) that are in response to the same prompt (defending the same side),
1050   along with a reward estimate for each speech. To generate these pairs, we use branching rollouts
1051   (Figure 8).
1052   For each round, we designate one of the debaters as the target, which will generate two speeches
1053   instead of one each time they are called upon. At the end of the first turn then, there will be two
1054   speeches from the target debater and one from the other. We then package those three speeches
1055   into two separate, alternate versions of the first turn, with the speech from the non-branched debater
1056   being shared across the two transcripts. This process repeats in the second turn for each of the two
1057   transcripts generated so far, resulting in three total preference pairs and four total transcripts for the
1058   judge to score.
1059   Speech strength is computed by averaging over all of the judge confidences in the transcripts in
1060   which that speech is present. Each second turn speech appears in only a single transcript, so its
1061   score is set directly to the judge confidence for that transcript. Each first turn speech appears in
1062   two different transcripts, so computing its score requires averaging across two different judge confi-
1063   dences.
