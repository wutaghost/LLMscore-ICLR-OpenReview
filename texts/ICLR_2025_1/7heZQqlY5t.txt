009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Generalized Additive Models (GAMs) are widely recognized for their ability to
012            create fully interpretable machine learning models for tabular data. Traditionally,
013            training GAMs involves iterative learning algorithms, such as splines, boosted trees,
014            or neural networks, which refine the additive components through repeated error
015            reduction. In this paper, we introduce GAMformer, the first method to leverage
016            in-context learning to estimate shape functions of a GAM in a single forward pass,
017            representing a significant departure from the conventional iterative approaches to
018
               GAM fitting. Building on previous research applying in-context learning to tabular
               data, we exclusively use complex, synthetic data to train GAMformer, yet find
019
               it extrapolates well to real-world data. Our experiments show that GAMformer
020
               performs on par with other leading GAMs across various classification benchmarks
021            while generating highly interpretable shape functions.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   The growing importance of interpretability in machine learning is evident, especially in areas where
027   transparency, fairness, and accountability are critical (Barocas and Selbst, 2016; Rudin et al., 2022).
028   Interpretable models are essential for building trust between humans and AI systems by allowing
029   users to understand the reasoning behind the modelвАЩs predictions and decisions (Ribeiro et al., 2016).
030   This is crucial in safety-critical fields like healthcare, where incorrect or biased decisions can have
031   severe consequences (Caruana et al., 2015). Additionally, interpretability is vital for regulatory
032   compliance in sectors like finance and hiring, where explaining and justifying model outcomes is
033
      necessary (Arun et al., 2016; Dattner et al., 2019). Interpretable models also help detect and mitigate
      bias by revealing the factors influencing predictions, ensuring fair and unbiased decisions across
034
      different population groups (Mehrabi et al., 2021).
035
036   Generalized Additive Models (GAMs) have proven a popular choice for interpretable modeling due
037   to their high accuracy and interpretability. In GAMs, the target variable is expressed as a sum of
038   non-linearly transformed features. This approach strikes a balance between the interpretability of
039
      linear models and the flexibility of capturing non-linear relationships between features and the target
      variable (Hastie and Tibshirani, 1987). A wide variety of GAMs exist, differing in the non-linear
040
      functions used to transform features and the methods employed to fit these functions to training data.
041
      Traditionally, GAMs have used splines in conjunction with the backfitting algorithm (Hastie and
042   Tibshirani, 1987), while Explainable Boosting Machines (EBMs) utilize decision trees and cyclic
043   gradient boosting (Lou et al., 2012; 2013; Caruana et al., 2015). More recently, Neural Additive
044   Models (NAMs) have employed multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) optimized via gradient descent
045   (Agarwal et al., 2021). All existing GAM variants share the need for an iterative optimization
046   algorithm to fit the shape functions, which introduces additional hyperparameters for optimization
047   and regularization that require tuning (Siems et al., 2023; Kov√°cs, 2022).
048   Recently, in-context learning (ICL) has emerged as a powerful paradigm for eliminating explicit
049   optimization in models. This breakthrough was first observed in large language models (Brown et al.,
050   2020a), where a model trained in an unsupervised manner on vast amounts of unlabeled data can learn
051   to execute a new task when presented with examples, without any further optimization or updates
052   to its parameters. Since then, ICL has been applied to various domains, including multi-modal
053   foundation models (Li et al., 2023) and time-series forecasting (Dooley et al., 2024). Of particular
      relevance to our work is TabPFN (Hollmann et al., 2023; M√Љller et al., 2022), a transformer model

      We introduce GAMformer (see Figure 1), the first GAM method to estimate shape functions using
085
      ICL in a single forward pass. GAMformer distinguishes itself from existing GAM methods by
086   employing a non-parametric, binned representation of shape functions, thus eliminating the need
087   to impose a specific model class. Similar to TabPFN, our model is trained exclusively on large-
088   scale synthetic datasets, yet demonstrates robust performance on real-world data. During training,
089   GAMformer estimates shape functions for each feature based on the training dataвАЩs features and labels.
090   These estimated functions are then utilized to generate predictions for test data points by summing
091   the shape function values across features. The model is trained end-to-end based on the GAMвАЩs
092   predictions, ensuring that it learns to accurately construct shape functions for reliable predictions.
093   Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
094
095            вАҐ We introduce GAMformer, the first method to utilize in-context learning with sequence-to-
096              sequence models to form shape functions in a single forward pass, eliminating the need for
097              iterative learning and hyperparameter tuning.
098            вАҐ Our experimental results demonstrate GAMformerвАЩs capacity to match the accuracy of
099              leading GAMs on various classification benchmarks.
100            вАҐ Our case study on MIMIC-II demonstrates how GAMformer can be applied to real-world
101              data to generate interpretable models and insights of that data.

      2.1   G ENERALIZED A DDITIVE M ODELS .
109
110   Generalized Additive Models (GAMs) (Hastie and Tibshirani, 1987) emerged as a generalization of
111   Generalized Linear Models (Nelder and Wedderburn, 1972) which include non-linear transformations
112   of the input features. The structure of a GAM is given by:
113                                                       Xp

117   variable , and fi : R вЖТ R are univariate functions termed shape functions that capture the individual
118   contributions of each feature. The intercept ќ≤ вИИ R is a learnable bias term, and g : R вЖТ R is the
119   link function that connects the expected outcome to the linear predictor, examples of which include
120   the logit or softmax function for binary or multiclass classification or the identity function for linear
121
      regression. The shape functions fi in GAMs, also sometimes called partial dependence plots, allow
      for an interpretable representation of each featureвАЩs effect, akin to the role of coefficients in linear
122
      regression, thus enabling practitioners to inspect the learned potentially non-linear relationships.
123
124   Traditional GAMs often use splines and backfitting (Hastie and Tibshirani, 1987), enhanced by
125   penalized regression splines (Wood, 2003) and fast fitting algorithms (Wood, 2001). Spline-based
126   GAMs use the backfitting algorithm, iteratively updating each shape function to fit the residuals of
127
      others until convergence. More recent advances include Explainable Boosting Machines (EBMs) (Lou
      et al., 2012; 2013; Caruana et al., 2015), which use decision trees to model shape functions via
128
      cyclic gradient boosting. This approach learns each featureвАЩs contribution iteratively in a round-robin
129
      manner, mitigating collinearity effects and accurately modeling steps in the data, which is crucial for
130   capturing discontinuities like treatment effects in medical data. On the other hand, Neural Additive
131   Models (NAMs) (Agarwal et al., 2021) and follow up works (Chang et al., 2021; Dubey et al., 2022;
132   Radenovic et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2022; Enouen and Liu, 2022; Bouchiat et al., 2024) use multilayer
133   perceptrons (MLPs) as non-linear transformations to model the shape functions fi . As a result,
134   NAMs can be optimized using variants of gradient descent by leveraging automatic differentiation
135   frameworks. Finally, GAMs have also found applications in time-series forecasting, with models
136   such as Prophet (Taylor and Letham, 2018) and NeuralProphet (Triebe et al., 2021). For a more
137   comprehensive related work refer to Appendix A.

139   2.2   I N -C ONTEXT L EARNING & P RIOR -DATA F ITTED N ETWORKS
140
141   In-Context Learning (ICL) was first demonstrated alongside the introduction of GPT-3 (Brown
142   et al., 2020b), where the authors showed that Transformer models (Vaswani et al., 2017) could
143   learn to perform tasks solely from input examples, without explicit training or fine-tuning, after
144
      self-supervised pre-training. This capability marks a significant paradigm shift from the traditional
      machine learning paradigm of in-weights learning, where the parameters of a model are adjusted
145
      in order to learn a new task. The discovery of ICL has led to numerous investigations into the
146
      mechanisms used by trained transformers that enable ICL. Olsson et al. (2022) found that a two-layer
147   attention-only network can develop вАЬinduction headsвАЭ, a mechanism that outputs the token succeeding
148   a previous instance of the current token, precisely when its ICL performance increases. Chan et al.
149   (2022) investigated the properties of the data distribution that contribute to the emergence of ICL
150   abilities, while Reddy (2024) identified factors responsible for the abrupt emergence of induction
151   heads.
152

153   Hollmann et al., 2023), which showed that a transformer trained on complex synthetic data generated
154   using random causal graphs can be used for tabular classification. From a Bayesian perspective,
155   such causal graphs ѕХ sampled from a hypothesis space ќ¶ (the prior), define a mechanism that
156   describes the relationship between the input and output variables. In TabPFNs (Hollmann et al.,

179   3       GAM FORMER
180
181   We first provide a high-level overview of how GAMformer works before delving into the details of
182   each of its components. GAMformer follows a two-step approach that first fits a GAM on training
183   data Dtrain and then predicts on test data xtest , as illustrated in Figure 1. Initially, a transformer
184   estimates shape functions using ICL on the training dataset Dtrain . Next, predictions are computed by
185
      aggregating the shape function values for each test data point xtest . This methodology replaces the
      traditional data fitting process of GAM variants with a single forward pass of a pre-trained transformer
186
      model, eliminating the need for optimization and regularization hyperparameters. We now describe
187
      each model component in more detail.
188

203   3.2       M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
204
205   Feature Preprocessing. Prior to being passed through the transformer, all features of each data
206   point are binned, one-hot encoded, and finally embedded using an MLP. We use nbins = 64 bins for
207   each feature, allocating bins based on the quantiles of the feature in the training dataset. Similarly
208   to TabPFN, we embed the label of each datapoint and add it to the embedding of each feature.
209
      Categorical features are equally distributed across the 64 bins according to their ratios.
210
211
      Representation of the shape functions. To accurately represent the shape functions, we chose
      to predict a discrete representation for each feature by discretizing it into 64 bins. An alternative
212
      approach would have been to predict the weights of a Neural Additive Model (NAM), similar to the
213
      method employed by Mothernet (M√Љller et al., 2023b). However, we decided against this approach to
214   more naturally represent sudden discontinuities in the shape functions1 .

      Transformer. The preprocessed training datapoints are processed by a transformer architecture
217   consisting of 12 layers, each with a dual-module design that sequentially applies self-attentionвАФfirst
218   over the features and then over the data points. This design, inspired by (Lorch et al., 2022), ensures
219   that our model is agnostic to the number of features and data points, and is equivariant with respect to
220   the order of both. As a result, unlike TabPFN (Hollmann et al., 2023), our approach does not require
221   padding to a fixed maximum number of features.
222
      After the transformer layers, we compute the average embeddings for each class based on training
223   labels enabling multi-class classification (limited to 10 classes in our experiments). This averaging
224   yields one embedding per class per feature which we denote h вИИ Rp√Чd√Чm where d denotes the
225   embedding dimension of the transformer2 . Each embedding is then passed through a shared decoder
226   MLP to produce the binned shape functions fЋЬ вИИ Rp√Чnbins √Чm . This architecture is parameter-efficient
227   as it allows sharing of parameters across features and classes. The model comprises 40k parameters
228   in the encoder layer, 50.5M parameters in the transformer layers, and 0.3M parameters in the decoder,
229   resulting in a total of 50.8M parameters. Note that while the shape function estimation scales
230   quadratically in the number of features and datapoints, the inference only scales linearly in both.

239   refer to Appendix D. During training, the synthetic data is randomly split into train and test datasets.

      GAMformerвАЩs core contribution is the substitution of the data fitting process of traditional GAM
246   variants with a single forward pass of a pre-trained transformer model, which is presented with
247   data through in-context examples. Consequently, GAMformer replaces the manually crafted fitting
248   procedures used in methods like EBMs (Caruana et al., 2015), where the boosting procedure is
249   restricted to one feature at a time in a round-robin manner, or the joint optimization of all shape
250   functions in NAMs (Agarwal et al., 2021) using SGD. Note that in both traditional GAM fitting and
251   GAMformer, the output of the processes remains the same; a main effects GAM fitted to a given
252   dataset represented by its shape functions.

254   3.4    H IGHER - ORDER EFFECTS
255
256
      We now describe how GAMformer can be extended to handle higher-orders effects. We extend
      GAMformer to model higher-order effects, specifically pairwise interactions, by incorporating
257
      feature products, resulting in up to O(p2 ) potential features. GAMformer can accommodate this by
258
      performing ICL on concatenated original data and higher-order effects, represented as feature vectors
259   in Rp+P , where P denotes the number of pair interactions. However, increasing feature dimensions
260   beyond the 10 used in pretraining is problematic and adds complexity to shape function estimation.
261   To mitigate this, we rank the most informative pairs via the FAST method (Lou et al., 2013) and the
262   optimal number of pairs is determined as a hyperparameter through cross-validation during inference.

445   5    L IMITATIONS & B ROADER I MPACT
446
447   Limitations. While GAMformer introduces a novel approach to estimating Generalized Additive
448   Models (GAMs), it is important to acknowledge its current limitations. This work primarily focuses
449   on main and second-order effect GAMs and does not account for higher-order interactions, which
450   are addressed in other GAM implementations, such as EBMs (Lou et al., 2013; Nori et al., 2019;
451   Chang et al., 2021). Future research could explore incorporating these interactions to enhance the
452
      modelвАЩs expressiveness and predictive capabilities. Another limitation of the current GAMformer
      model is its difficulty in improving predictions when presented with datasets that exceed twice the
453
      size of the data it saw during training (c.f. Figure 8). This issue is related to the well-known challenge
454
      of length extrapolation in sequence-to-sequence models, including transformers (Grazzi et al., 2024;
455   Zhou et al., 2024). Addressing this limitation may require exposing the model to a larger variety of
456   number of examples during training. However, due to computational constraints, the experiments in
457   this work were limited to a maximum of 500 datapoints during training. Future studies with increased
458   computational resources could investigate the modelвАЩs performance on larger datasets and develop
459   strategies to mitigate the length extrapolation problem. The GAMformer modelвАЩs transformer-based
460   architecture scales quadratically with both the number of training data points and features, posing
461   a similar challenge to handling large datasets as faced by TabPFN (Hollmann et al., 2023). Novel,
462   scalable transformer alternatives, such as the recently proposed Mamba (Gu and Dao, 2023) or Gated
463
      Linear Attention (Yang et al., 2024), may prove useful in overcoming this issue.
464   Broader Impact. As a versatile machine learning model for tabular data, GAMformer offers both
465   positive and negative societal impacts. Positively, it can generate novel insights in fields like medicine,
466   enhancing disease diagnosis and treatment. However, it can also be misused to not mitigate but
467   exploit biases, such as adjusting insurance premiums based on ethnicity, leading to discrimination.
468
469   6    C ONCLUSION
470
471   In this paper, we introduce GAMformer, a novel approach to creating GAMs using in-context learning
472   with transformer models. By leveraging a single forward pass to form shape functions, GAMformer
473   overcomes the limitations of traditional GAM algorithms that require iterative learning processes
474   and hence hyperparameter tuning. Our approach uses non-parametric, binned representations of
475   shape functions, resulting in significant improvements in efficiency and accuracy across various
476   classification benchmarks. Extensive experiments demonstrate that GAMformer approaches the
477   accuracy of leading GAM variants while exhibiting robustness to label noise and class imbalance.
478
      The modelвАЩs ability to generalize beyond the number of examples seen during training highlights its
      adaptability and potential for practical applications.
479
480   GAMformer is fundamentally different from the iterative optimization methods traditionally used,
481   and offers a new research direction for interpretable models on tabular data. Further, our case study on
482   the MIMIC-II dataset showcases that interpreting GAMformerвАЩs shape functions can yield qualitative
483   insights and uncover flaws in datasets similar to state of the art GAM methods. This work contributes
484
      to the development of more transparent and explainable AI systems, with potential applications in
      various domains where interpretability is crucial. Future research can expand on this initial new
485
      paradigm, and explore scalable alternatives to transformers to handle larger datasets.

      A     G ENERALIZED A DDITIVE M ODELS : E XTENDED R ELATED W ORK
703
704   As with many families of machine learning algorithms, the differences among GAM algorithms lie in
705   (a) the functional form of the shape functions fi , (b) the learning algorithm used for their estimation
706   and (c) regularity assumptions and regularization. Two important properties that all GAMs share are
707   (1) the ability to learn non-linear transformations for each feature and (2) additively combining these
708   shape functions (prior to applying the link function) to create modularity that aids interpretability by
709   allowing users to examine shape functions one-at-a-time.
710   Typically, GAMs have relied on splines and backfitting algorithms for estimation (Hastie and Tibshi-
711   rani, 1987), with subsequent works focusing on improving efficiency and stability through penalized
712   regression splines (Wood, 2003) and fast, stable fitting algorithms (Wood, 2001). Spline-based
713   GAMs are typically fitted using the backfitting algorithm, an iterative procedure that starts with
714   initial estimates of the smooth functions for each predictor variable. The algorithm then repeatedly
715   updates each function by fitting a weighted additive model to the residuals of the other functions until
716   convergence is achieved. The weights are determined by the current estimates of the other functions
717   and the link function in the case of generalized additive models.
718   Modern approaches leverage machine learning advances. Explainable Boosting Machines (EBMs)
719   (Lou et al., 2012; 2013; Caruana et al., 2015) model the shape functions using decision trees, which
720   are fitted using a variant of gradient boosting called cyclic gradient boosting. The model iteratively
721   learns the contribution of each feature and interaction term in a round-robin fashion, using a low
722   learning rate to ensure that the order of features does not affect the final model. This cyclic training
723   procedure helps mitigate the effects of colinearity among predictors by providing opportunity for
724
      data-driven credit attribution among the features while preventing multiple counting of evidence.
      EBMs are also popular because they can accurately capture steps in the shape functions, which is
725
      important for modeling discontinuities in data, such as treatment effects in medical data.
726
727   More recently, Neural Additive Models (NAMs) (Agarwal et al., 2021) and follow up works (Chang
728   et al., 2021; Dubey et al., 2022; Radenovic et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2022; Enouen and Liu, 2022;
729   Bouchiat et al., 2024) use multilayer perceptrons (MLPs), as non-linear transformations, to model
730
      the shape functions fi . As a result, NAMs can be optimized using variants of gradient descent by
      leveraging automatic differentiation frameworks.
731
732   Finally, GAMs have also found applications in time-series forecasting, with models such as
733   Prophet (Taylor and Letham, 2018) and NeuralProphet (Triebe et al., 2021). Interestingly, the
734   1-layer versions of the recently proposed Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KANs) (Liu et al., 2024)
735   may be viewed as GAMs with spline based shape functions.

      D           S YNTHETIC DATA P RIORS
859
860
      We use the same synthetic data generation process proposed in Prior-Data-Fitted Networks
861
      (PFNs) (Hollmann et al., 2023; M√Љller et al., 2022) and provide a brief summary of the process.
862
863   TabPFN is trained on two synthetic data priors, which are mixed during training.TabPFN introduced
      a synthetic data prior based on Structural Causal Models (SCMs). SCMs are particularly suitable for


                                                                                            16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      modeling tabular data as they capture causal relationships between columns, a strong prior in human
865   reasoning. An SCM comprises a set of structural assignments (mechanisms) where each mechanism
866   is defined by a deterministic function and a noise variable, structured within a Directed Acyclic Graph
867   (DAG). The causal relationships are represented by directed edges from causes to effects, facilitating
868   the modeling of complex dependencies within the data. To instantiate a PFN prior based on SCMs,
869   one defines a sampling procedure to create supervised learning tasks. Each dataset is generated from
870   a randomly sampled SCM, including its DAG structure and deterministic functions. Nodes in the
871   causal graph are selected to represent features and targets, and samples are generated by propagating
872   noise variables through the graph. This process results in features and targets that are conditionally
873
      dependent through the DAG structure, capturing both forward and backward causation (Hollmann
      et al., 2023). This allows for the generation of diverse datasets.
874
875   The second prior samples of synthetic data using Gaussian Processes (GPs) (Rasmussen and Williams,
876   2006) with a constant mean function and a radial basis function (RBF) kernel to define the covariance
877   structure. Hyperparameters such as noise level, output scale, and length scale are sampled from
878   predefined distributions to introduce variability. Depending on the configuration, input data points
879
      can be sampled uniformly, normally, or as equidistant points and the target column is generated
      by passing the input data through the GP. This prior gives the model the ability to learn smoother
880
      functions.
881
882   For multi-class prediction, scalar labels are transformed into discrete class labels by partitioning the
883   scalar values into intervals corresponding to different classes, ensuring the synthetic data is suitable
884   for imbalanced multi-class classification tasks.
885   Finally, both priors are combined by sampling batches of data from each prior with different prob-
886   abilities during training. In all of our experiments we sampled from the SCM and GP prior with
887   probability 0.96 and 0.04, respectively.

      AdamW (Loshchilov and Hutter, 2019) optimizer (ќ≤1 = 0.9) and cosine learning rate schedule with

907   To handle higher-order effects, we compute the best pairs with the FAST algorithm (Lou et al., 2013)
908   and evaluate GAMformer on the top pairs using the following ratios of features:
909
910                              P = [0.01p, 0.05p, 0.1p, 0.2p, 0.4p, 0.8p, 0.9p]

912   where we recall that p denotes the number of features. We round off each ratio to determine the
913   number of target pair features, evaluate performance on hold-out validation data from the training set,
914   and select the number of pairs with the best validation performance. The model is then fitted on the
915   entire training dataset. This involves doing |P| + 1 forward passes, which is unproblematic as doing
916   one forward pass is very fast, even on a CPU. One could also vectorialize all computations which we
917   do not do given the low fitting time.
