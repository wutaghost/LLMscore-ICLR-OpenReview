011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Symbolic regression plays a critical role in uncovering interpretable expressions
014            that elucidate complex phenomena by revealing the underlying mathematical and
015            physical relationships within data. In this paper, we present an advanced sym-
016            bolic regression method that incorporates symbol priors from diverse scientific
017            domains—such as physics, biology, chemistry, and engineering—into the regres-
018            sion process. By systematically organizing and analyzing domain-specific expres-
019
               sions, we identify the probability distributions of symbols across these fields. We
               propose novel tree-structured recurrent neural networks (RNNs) armed with sym-
020
               bol priors to generate expressions, allowing the learning process to be guided by
021
               domain knowledge. Additionally, we introduce a new hierarchical tree structure
022            to represent expressions, where unary and binary operators are arranged hierar-
023            chically to facilitate more efficient learning. By analyzing symbol combinations
024            at different hierarchical levels, our method captures the structural information of
025            expressions, enriching the regression process. Furthermore, we compile character-
026            istic expression blocks from each domain and include them in the operator dictio-
027            nary during training, accelerating learning by providing relevant building blocks.
028            Experimental results show that leveraging symbol priors from domain knowledge
029            significantly improves the performance of symbolic regression, leading to faster
030
               convergence and greater accuracy.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Symbolic regression is a powerful regression analysis technique that searches the space of mathe-
036
      matical expressions to find the one that best fits a given dataset. Unlike traditional regression models
037   that fit data to complex models that are difficult to interpret, symbolic regression can discover the in-
038   terpretable equations or relationships between variables. This ability leads to a deeper understanding
039   of the inherent structure and dynamics of the data. It is particularly important in fields where the rela-
040   tionships between variables are complex and not well understood. In the physical sciences(Angelis
041   et al., 2023; Miles et al., 2021; Neumann et al., 2020), it has been used to derive fundamental
042   equations and understand intricate phenomena. In materials science, symbolic regression aids in
043   predicting material properties and uncovering underlying mechanisms (Wang et al., 2019; 2022). In
044   the chemical sciences, symbolic regression models physico-chemical laws from experimental data
045
      (Neumann et al., 2020) and aids in understanding molecular adsorption processes on surfaces, which
      is crucial for catalyst design and atmospheric chemistry (Xie & Zhang, 2022; Hu & Zhang, 2023).
046
      In climate science, symbolic regression helps forecast and model atmospheric phenomena (Feng
047
      et al., 2016). In neuroscience, it analyzes dynamic time series data to understand neural dynamics
048   (Nascimento et al., 2020). In ecological science, it reveals complex ecological dynamics and mod-
049   els ecosystem behaviors, providing valuable tools for conservation and environmental management
050   (Chen et al., 2019; Martin et al., 2018; Cardoso et al., 2020). In financial markets, symbolic regres-
051   sion assists in strategy inference and market prediction, extracting meaningful models from large
052   datasets for investment strategies (Duffy & Engle-Warnick, 2002; Jin et al., 2019). These exam-
053   ples highlight symbolic regression’s vast potential and adaptability across scientific and engineering
      fields, emphasizing its importance as a tool for discovery and analysis across disciplines.

      Symbolic regression methods are typically divided into two main approaches. The first approach
055   involves a two-step process: first, generating a “skeleton” of the equation using a parametric function
056   constructed from a predefined set of operators, such as basic arithmetic operations and elementary
057   functions (e.g., square roots, exponentials, trigonometric functions). This step defines the overall
058   structure of the equation. The second step uses optimization techniques like the Broyden-Fletcher-
059   Goldfarb-Shanno (BFGS) algorithm to estimate the constants within this skeleton.
060
      Earlier methods like (Blkadek & Krawiec, 2019; Schmidt & Lipson, 2009) often employed genetic
061   algorithms (Mirjalili & Mirjalili, 2019) and genetic programming (Langdon & Poli, 2013). These
062   evolutionary techniques generate populations of candidate expressions, selecting and evolving the
063   fittest individuals based on their data-fitting performance. Recent improvements to genetic program-
064   ming include integrating neural networks to identify qualitative patterns and reduce search space
065   (Mundhenk et al., 2021).
066
      Alternatively, reinforcement learning (RL)-based methods use reward signals during the search pro-
067   cess. For example, Deep Symbolic Regression (Petersen et al., 2019) uses a recurrent neural network
068   (RNN) to generate expression skeletons, optimizing constants through stochastic gradient descent
069   (SGD) and updating the RNN using risk-seeking policy gradients. Deep Symbolic Optimization
070   (Mundhenk et al., 2021) combines RNNs with genetic programming to create an enhanced initial
071   population for the algorithm. FEX (Liang & Yang, 2022) identifies governing equations by exploring
072   mathematical expressions from binary expression trees with a fixed operator set. Symbolic Physics
073   Learner (Sun et al., 2022) frames symbolic regression within the Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)
074   framework, allowing an agent to generate expressions containing both operators and operands, and
075
      updating the agent with expressions that yield higher rewards.
076   Inspired by recent advances in language models, a second approach to symbolic regression has
077   emerged, often referred to as Neural Symbolic Regression (NSR). This line of work treats symbolic
078   regression as a natural language processing (NLP) task, leveraging large-scale pre-trained models
079   to map data directly to expressions in an end-to-end fashion, similar to how machine translation
080   converts text from one language to another (Bendinelli et al., 2023; Kamienny et al., 2022; Vastl
081
      et al., 2024; Shojaee et al., 2024; Li et al., 2022; Merler et al., 2024). These neural approaches are
      trained end-to-end, with sampled data points as input and the symbolic representation of the formula
082
      as output, effectively learning to generate mathematical expressions that fit the data.
083
084   Symbolic Prior for Symbolic Regression: When we use symbolic regression to learn expressions
085   that describe dynamical systems across various domains—such as physics, biology, and chem-
086   istry—we encounter differences in the frequencies of symbols, operators or combinations of op-
087
      erators used in these expressions. Each scientific field tends to employ a unique set of mathematical
      symbols and functions due to the underlying principles and commonly used formulations specific to
088
      that domain. For instance, trigonometric functions like sine and cosine are prevalent in physics for
089
      modeling oscillatory systems, while exponential and logistic functions are common in biology for
090   modeling population growth and decay processes. This intuition leads us to a pivotal question:
091
092   How can we extract these symbol priors? Furthermore, how can we efficiently incorporate this
093
      symbol prior knowledge to improve current symbolic regression methods?

165   Related works Related Works
166   Bastiani et al. (2024) employs the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) to balance interpretabil-
167   ity and data fitness, relying solely on general complexity-based constraints. Similarly, Jin et al.
168   (2019) sets priors for tree structures and operators based on uniform distributions or user prefer-
169   ences, but lacks a method for deriving domain-specific priors. In contrast, our approach leverages
170   domain-specific symbol priors, enhancing both the accuracy and interpretability of the models. By
171   incorporating KL divergence regularization, we align the learned categorical distribution with the
172
      domain-specific prior, achieving better control and faster convergence than MCMC-based methods.
173   Other works, such as (Gupta et al., 2016; Bezerra et al., 2019; Kronberger et al., 2022), incorporate
174   properties like monotonicity, convexity, and symmetry to limit the search space, thus increasing
175   efficiency. However, these properties are often difficult to determine solely from data and may not
176   always hold in practice. Similarly, (Ashok et al., 2021; Kubalik et al., 2021) constrain the search
177   to equations that adhere to fundamental physical laws, like conservation principles, significantly
178
      narrowing the search space. However, this requires detailed prior knowledge of the system, which
      may not always be available.
179
180   Blkadek & Krawiec (2022) use genetic algorithms to validate candidate solutions against struc-
181   tural constraints (e.g., symmetry, monotonicity, convexity) and knowledge constraints (e.g., logical
182   ranges, slopes, boundary conditions). The main limitation of these methods is their reliance on data
183   to define and verify constraints, which can be challenging in practice. (Petersen et al., 2019; 2021)
184
      address this by eliminating the probability of certain tokens based on the expression tree’s context,
      thereby reducing the generation of invalid symbol combinations and improving search efficiency.
185
186   Tenachi et al. (2023) incorporate physical units as priors, masking the categorical distribution gen-
187   erated by the RNN based on local unit constraints to prevent unphysical expressions. In our work,
188   we extend this idea by treating certain priors as ”hard constraints,” excluding symbol combinations
189   that have never appeared in a specific topic. Additionally, we incorporate the probabilities of spe-
190
      cific token combinations to bias the search toward more meaningful expressions, further refining the
      search space.

      In this section, we outline our approach to integrating symbol priors into symbolic regression. We
196
      begin by introducing a tree-structured representation for mathematical expressions, designed to sup-
197   port the systematic collection and effective utilization of symbol priors. This representation enables
198   a more compact and organized encoding of expressions, facilitating analysis across scientific do-
199   mains. Subsequently, we detail the extraction procedure of these symbol priors from mathematical
200   expressions sourced from domain-specific papers on arXiv. Through this method, we aim to capture
201   and leverage the unique symbol distributions and operator preferences inherent to each field, thus
202   refining the symbolic regression model by guiding it with structured domain knowledge.
203
204   2.1   R EPRESENTATION M ETHOD
205
206   Our proposed structure addresses the limitations of traditional binary expression trees by enabling
207   a single binary operator to link multiple sequences of unary operators. This approach allows for
208   a more flexible and expressive hierarchical representation of mathematical expressions. Through
209   comprehensive analysis of collected expressions, we find that most physically meaningful expres-
210   sions—particularly those that characterize specific dynamical systems—can be effectively repre-
211   sented within a two-level tree structure, with some even reducible to a single layer. This observation
212
      indicates that our representation closely aligns with the inherent structure of many real-world ex-
      pressions, enhancing both the interpretability and efficiency of symbolic regression tasks.
213
214   For instance, in cases of consecutive additions, the addition operator connects multiple child nodes,
215   which are treated as equivalent without the strict parent-child hierarchy typically enforced in tradi-
      tional binary expression trees. This approach contrasts with the conventional binary representation

444           bility, as identified in prior research. These combinations are strictly prohibited and are
445           excluded from the sampling process.
446         □ HC2 : Represents combinations that rarely occur. Although they are not commonly ob-
447           served, we assign them a very small probability, ϵ, and include them in the set of soft
448           constraints. This design promotes model exploration, enabling the potential discovery of
449           novel physical laws.
450
451   For the operators combinations in hard constraint, we simply use the method in (Petersen et al.,
452   2019), zero-out the probability during sampling.
453   By categorizing constraints in this way, we ensure that our model adheres to known rules while
454   still allowing flexibility for exploration. This approach balances enforcing known constraints with
455   maintaining a level of uncertainty, enabling the model to explore new combinations that might reveal
456   novel insights.

532
533   We found that combining domain-specific priors with our tree-structured RNN agent quickly results
534   in an effective policy. Learning from expressions across various fields has provided valuable insights
535   for future research. However, our approach is sensitive to the prior categorical distribution, making
536
      bias a challenge despite careful data collection.
537   The prior for each domain consists of two parts: a ”behavior prior” shared across all fields, and a
538   domain-specific component. This is similar to the multitask problem in reinforcement learning. In
539   future work, we plan to optimize both the domain-specific and ”behavior” priors during training,
      aiming to uncover intriguing and interesting results.
