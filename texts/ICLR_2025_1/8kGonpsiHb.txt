010                                                     A BSTRACT
011
012                Despite the growing global demand for large language models (LLMs) that serve
013                users from diverse linguistic backgrounds, most cutting-edge LLMs remain pre-
014
                   dominantly English-centric. This creates a performance gap across languages, re-
                   stricting access to advanced AI services for non-English speakers. Current meth-
015
                   ods to enhance multilingual capabilities largely rely on data-driven post-training
016
                   techniques, such as multilingual instruction tuning or continual pre-training. How-
017                ever, these approaches encounter significant challenges, including the scarcity of
018                high-quality multilingual datasets and the limited enhancement of multilingual ca-
019                pabilities. They often suffer from off-target issues and catastrophic forgetting of
020                central language abilities. To this end, we propose L ENS, a novel approach to
021                enhance multilingual capabilities of LLMs by leveraging their internal language
022                representation spaces. Specially, L ENS operates by manipulating the hidden rep-
023                resentations within the language-agnostic and language-specific subspaces from
024                top layers of LLMs. Using the central language as a pivot, the target language
025
                   is drawn closer to it within the language-agnostic subspace, allowing it to inherit
                   well-established semantic representations. Meanwhile, in the language-specific
026
                   subspace, the representations of the target and central languages are pushed apart,
027
                   enabling the target language to express itself distinctly. Extensive experiments
028                on one English-centric and two multilingual LLMs demonstrate that L ENS effec-
029                tively improves multilingual performance without sacrificing the model’s original
030                central language capabilities, achieving superior results with much fewer compu-
031                tational resources compared to existing post-training approaches.1

034   1       I NTRODUCTION
035
036   In an increasingly interconnected world, large language models (LLMs) are expected to cater to a
037
      diverse range of users across various linguistic backgrounds (Ouyang et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024a;
      Zheng et al., 2024). However, despite this global trend, most state-of-the-art LLMs remain predom-
038
      inantly English-centric (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Jiang et al., 2023; AI@Meta,
039
      2024). These models exhibit significantly better performance in English than in other languages,
040   leading to an imbalance in user experience and potentially excluding large segments of the global
041   population from accessing advanced AI services (Wang et al., 2024a; Zhu et al., 2024b).
042
043
      This disparity has directly spurred research efforts to enhance the multilingual capabilities of LLMs,
      aiming to provide more equitable access and performance across various linguistic communities.
044
      Current approaches are predominantly based on data-driven post-training paradigm, such as mul-
045
      tilingual instruction tuning (Zhang et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Üstün et al., 2024) or continual
046   pre-training (Cui et al., 2023; Kuulmets et al., 2024; Jaavid et al., 2024), which primarily seeks to
047   inject or elicit multilingual knowledge with the supervision signals from external datasets.
048
049
      While this paradigm is widely embraced and demonstrates certain successes, it faces several sig-
      nificant challenges. (1) The efficacy of multilingual enhancement heavily depend on large-scale
050
      and high-quality multilingual datasets (Zhou et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b), which are both time-
051
      consuming and labor-intensive to obtain for each language. (2) It favors improving multilingual un-
052   derstanding over generation capabilities, which leaves the off-target issue inadequately addressed.
053

054
      As a result, the model often struggles to generate accurate responses in the intended language when
055   prompted (Zhang et al., 2020; Lai et al., 2024; Sennrich et al., 2024). (3) The model’s performance
056   in languages it previously handled well is risking at catastrophic forgetting (McCloskey & Cohen,
057   1989), such as English in the LLaMA family (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; AI@Meta, 2024).
058
      In this work, we seek to provide a new perspective on addressing the aforementioned challenges by
059
      exploring and manipulating the internal representation within the language-related latent spaces of
060
      LLMs (Zou et al., 2023; Park et al., 2024). Taking the enhancement of multilingual capabilities for
061   English-centric LLMs as an example. This is based on the intuitive idea that the well-established En-
062   glish representations in existing English-centric LLMs can act as a pivot to improve the performance
063   of other languages. More specifically, for the target language to be extended, its language-agnostic
064   semantic representations should be pulled close to those of English, enabling it to quickly inherit
065   general abilities in English without the need for supervision signals from external multilingual train-
066   ing data. Conversely, the language-specific linguistic representations of the target language should
067   be pushed away from English to avoid off-target issues, ensuring accurate responses in the target
068   language. Also, during this process, it is crucial to ensure that the English pivot representation
069
      remains unchanged to effectively prevent catastrophic forgetting.
070   To achieve this, we propose L ENS, a novel multiLingual Enhancement method based on the hidden
071   represeNtations within language Space of LLMs. To be more specific, L ENS comprises two stages:
072   Language Subspace Probing (LSP) and Language Subspace Manipulation (LSM). During LSP, the
073   multilingual hidden space within a single layer of the backbone are decoupled into two orthogonal
074   components: a language-agnostic subspace and a language-specific subspace. These subspaces are
075
      efficiently derived using singular value decomposition. Then in LSM, we align the parallel multilin-
      gual input representations of the target language and the central language in the language-agnostic
076
      subspace. This allows the target language to directly inherit the well-established semantic represen-
077
      tations of the central language. Simultaneously, the projection components of the target language
078   within the language-specific space are pushed away from those of the central language, guiding the
079   target language toward its distinct linguistic expression and ensuring the target language is properly
080   expressed thereby mitigating the off-target issue. Finally, we align the central language’s current
081   representations with its original ones to preserve its proficiency during multilingual enhancement.
082
      We conduct extensive experiments under bilingual and multilingual enhancement setups. Results
083
      on one English-centric (LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct) and 2 multilingual LLMs (LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct
084   and Phi-3.5-mini-Instruct), demonstrate that L ENS succeed to improve target languages on both
085   multilingual comprehension and generation tasks without sacrificing the strong capability of central
086   language, showing the efficacy and scalability of our method. Deeper analysis highlights the signifi-
087   cance of steering the target language towards its unique expressions within its own language-specific
088   subspace to fully enhance both comprehension and generation capabilities. This is overlooked by
089   most existing approaches, which primarily focus on aligning representations across different lan-
090   guages to boost multilingual performance. It is crucial to note that, building on recent findings that
091   language-related parameters are primarily concentrated in the top layers of LLMs (Wendler et al.,
092
      2024), L ENS achieves high resource efficiency compared to baselines, with much fewer computa-
      tional costs by only updating the model’s higher layers using just a few hundred data points.
093
094   The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
095
096
      • We provide a novel perspective for the multilingual enhancement of large language models with
        their internal language representation space leveraged.
097
098   • We propose L ENS, an efficient and effective multilingual enhancement method that operates
099
        within the language representation space of large language models.
100   • Extensive experiments on one English-centric and two multilingual LLMs demonstrate the ef-
101     fectiveness, efficiency, scalability of our method to obtain truly multilingual enhanced chat-style
102
        backbones without sacrificing original central language performance.

104   2   R ELATED W ORKS
105
106   Multilingual Large Language Model With the acceleration of globalization, multilingual large
107   language models (MLLMs) are gaining significant attention for their ability to handle multiple lan-
      guages comprehensively (Qin et al., 2024). Pretraining on multilingual data is a common approach


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      to gain the multilingual capabilities (Conneau & Lample, 2019; Xue et al., 2020; Lin et al., 2022;
109   Shliazhko et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2023; Xue et al., 2022; Le Scao et al., 2023; Blevins et al., 2024).
110   However, due to the uneven distribution of data in pretraining corpora, current LLMs or MLLMs
111   exhibit uneven language capabilities, with most state-of-the-art models heavily biased towards En-
112   glish (Jiang et al., 2023; AI@Meta, 2024; Abdin et al., 2024). Moreover, pretraining from scratch
113   is computationally intensive. These limitations have directly sparked research into expanding or
114   enhancing the language capabilities of current LLMs or MLLMs.
115
116   Multilingual Enhancement for LLMs Current methods for multilingual enhancement of LLMs
117   can be categorized into two types: 1) prompt-based methods and 2) post-training-based methods.
118
      The former focuses on leveraging the LLMs’ own translation capabilities to translate low-resource
119
      language inputs into the central language, and then generating a response (Shi et al., 2023; Huang
120
      et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2023; Etxaniz et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b). For example, Huang et al.
121   (2023) introduce cross-lingual-thought prompting to minimize language disparities. However, Liu
122   et al. (2024a) reveal the limitations of these methods, showing they are not optimal for real-world
123   scenarios and highlighting the necessity of more comprehensive multilingual enhancement.
124
      The latter aims to conduct further multilingual post-training to inject or elicit extensive language
125
      knowledge for specific languages, including ways of continual pre-training (Zhang et al., 2021b;
126
      Cui et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b; Lin et al., 2024; Kuulmets et al., 2024; Jaavid et al., 2024) and
127   instruction tuning (Muennighoff et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023c; Indurthi et al., 2024; Ahuja et al.,
128   2024; Lai & Nissim, 2024; Zhang et al., 2024c; Zhu et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024d).
129   For example, Cui et al. (2023) attempt to inject Chinese knowledge into LLaMA by conducting con-
130   tinual pre-training on a large-scale Chinese corpus, while Zhu et al. (2023) focus more on building
131   language alignment through cross-lingual instruction tuning and translation training.
132
      Our proposed L ENS stands out from existing methods in that we seek multilingual supervision sig-
133   nals from the internal language representation space of the LLMs, rather than relying primarily on
134   external multilingual datasets as in the above methods, which offers fresh insights and new oppor-
135   tunities for enhancing the multilingual capabilities of LLMs both efficiently and effectively.
136
137
      Representation Engineering Editing or manipulating representation within LLMs has garnered
138   increasing attention due to its transparency and lightweight properties (Zou et al., 2023). This is
139   theoritically rooted from Linear Representation Hypothesis (Mikolov et al., 2013; Nanda et al.,
140   2023; Park et al., 2024), which posits that various human-interpretable concepts are encoded in linear
141   subspaces of model representations. Building upon this, exist works attempt to edit representations
142   at inference time to develop models that are more truthful (Li et al., 2023b; Campbell et al., 2023;
143   Zhang et al., 2024a), and harmless (Lee et al., 2024; Uppaal et al., 2024). We expand and implement
144   this paradigm for the multilingual enhancement of LLMs by focusing on representations during the
145   training phase, ensuring that the efficiency of LLMs remains unaffected during the inference phase.

212   3.3     L ANGUAGE S UBSPACE M ANIPULATION
213
214   To eliminate the heavy reliance on hard-to-access high-quality multilingual datasets, we leverage the
215   well-trained hidden representations of the central language in LLMs as a pivot to derive supervision
      signals for multilingual enhancement within the model’s internal language space.

217   agnostic subspace Ma . This allows us to directly inherit the well-established general capabilities of
218   the central language. Formally, this goal is accomplished by projecting multilingual representations
219   (at the position of the last token) onto the subspace Ma , with the optimization objective defined as:

248   4.1      E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
249
250   Models We select one representative English-centric LLMs: LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct (AI@Meta,
251   2024) and 2 MLLMs: LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct2 and Phi-3.5-mini-instruct (Abdin et al., 2024), to
252   fully validate the effectiveness and generality of our L ENS in enhancing multilingual performance.
253
254   Languages to be Enhanced We conduct experiments in both bilingual and multilingual settings
255
      to address various multilingual enhancement needs.
256   In the bilingual setting, English (En) serves as the central language, while Chinese (Zh) is chosen as
257   the target language for expansion. Chinese is selected due to its growing prominence in the academic
258   focus on multilingual enhancement for LLMs.
259   In the multilingual setting, we select six target languages for enhancement based on the availabil-
260   ity of language resources. The high-resource languages are Chinese (Zh) and Japanese (Jp); the
261   medium-resource languages are Korean (Ko) and Arabic (Ar); and the low-resource languages are
262   Bengali (Bn) and Swahili (Sw), with English (En) continuing to serve as the central language.
263
      It is important to note that these target languages are classified as out-of-scope in the official model
264
      card of the above LLMs and MLLMs, which further underscores their relevance for enhancement.
265
266
      Training Data The multilingual data used for the language subspace probing stage is sourced

268   fine-tuning dataset with 204,000 instances in 65 languages. For the language subspace manipulation

      benchmarks, showing a marked increase in language fidelity during multilingual generation. This
      effectively mitigates the off-target issue. Moreover, L ENS is the only approach that enhances multi-
349
      lingual performance across all languages. In contrast, baseline methods primarily focus on boosting
350
      multilingual understanding with little to no improvement in generation tasks. Additionally, methods
351   like QAlign and SDRRL, which rely on translation-based training for multilingual alignment, fall
352   short in effectively enhancing large models’ overall multilingual performance. This suggests that
353   aligning multilingual representations alone is insufficient for fully optimizing multilingual capabil-
354   ities (Hua et al., 2024). Finally, L ENS safeguards the central language from catastrophic forgetting,
355   allowing the resulting model to effectively serve users from diverse linguistic backgrounds.
356
      Using the central language representations within the backbone as a supervision signal proves
357   more effective than relying on external data for supervision. The key distinction between L ENS
358   and baseline methods lies in how multilingual performance is enhanced: L ENS relies on the model’s
359   internal representation of the central language, while baseline methods depend on external data.
360   This difference make baselines not only fail to improve the target languages but also lead to per-
361   formance degradation. This phenomenon becomes more pronounced in xSFT-Full when trained
362   with more data. However, the Aya Dataset and Bactrain-X datasets we used are already considered
363   high-quality multilingual resources, widely employed and proven effective in boosting multilingual
364   capabilities in previous models such as mT5 and LLaMA-2 (Li et al., 2023a; Üstün et al., 2024).
365
      This highlights that for current extensively trained LLMs such as LLaMA-3 (which has been trained
      on over 15T data), an over-reliance on external supervision signals may fall short of scalability needs
366
      (Cao et al., 2024). We hope L ENS could inspire further research to explore more efficient, scalable,
367
      and automated supervision signals for multilingual enhancement of the most advanced LLMs.

448   5.2     I MPACT OF VARYING THE N UMBER OF M ANIPULATED L AYERS
449
450
      Recent studies on the interpretability of LLMs has sought to reveal the mechanisms underlying their
      multilingual capabilities (Zhao et al., 2024c; Zhong et al., 2024). A growing consensus suggests
451
      that language-specific parameters or neurons are primarily concentrated in the upper layers of these
452
      models, while the middle layers tend to process inputs from various languages using a shared and
453   language-agnostic mechanism (Chen et al., 2023a; Wendler et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024; Kojima
454   et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024d). Drawing inspiration from this, our main experiments focus on per-
455   forming updates solely within the upper layers of the backbone, resulting in a notable improvement
456   in multilingual performance. In Figure 4, we explore the effect of increasing the number of layers
457   involved on the model’s multilingual enhancement. The horizontal axis represents the starting point
458   of the layers where manipulation is applied, with the default endpoint being the final layer. This
459   experiment is performed under the bilingual enhancement with LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct.
460   “Thinking” in English at the intermediate layers is more favorable for improving multilingual under-
461   standing. If we partition representations of target language into the language-specific subspace too
462   early at the middle layers, it may impair its multilingual understanding capability. On the contrary,
463   inheriting more from the shared representations at the middle layers, while emphasizing language-
464   specific representations only at the higher layers (where most language-specific parameters and neu-
465   rons are concentrated), is more beneficial for enhancing multilingual performance.
466   It is important to note that modifying only the final layer does not significantly improve either multi-
467   lingual understanding or generation. This is because language-specific information is not sufficiently
468   enhanced, causing the model to suffer from off-target issues and struggle to represent specific lan-
469   guages accurately. The lack of improvement in multilingual understanding aligns with the findings
470   in Section 5.1, which highlight the critical role of supervision provided by the Push loss (L2 ).
471   Our proposed L ENS further validates the conclusions of existing works on LLM interpretability and
472   applies these findings to multilingual enhancement of LLMs.

      6        C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this paper, we introduce L ENS, a novel method designed for the effective, efficient and com-
532
      prehensive multilingual enhancement of large language models (LLMs). L ENS first decouple the
      multilingual hidden spaces of the backbone into two orthogonal components: a language-agnostic
533
      subspace and a language-specific subspace. Then taking well-established representations of the cen-
534
      tral language as a pivot, representations of target languages are pulled closer and pushed away from
535   them in language-agnostic subspace and language-specific subspace, respectively. Experimental re-
536   sults on 3 representative cutting-edge LLMs demonstrate that L ENS outperforms baseline methods
537   with much lower training costs, underscoring its efficacy, efficiency and scalability.

       A       L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
933
       Despite our L ENS achieving comprehensive and efficient multilingual enhancement, there are still
934
       limitations and future directions worth exploring.
935
936    First, due to limited computational resources, our experiments are not conducted on larger-scale
937    models (larger than 8B). This remains a valuable direction to apply L ENS on larger LLMs.
938    Second, our current operations on language representation are still relatively coarse-grained. Future
939    work could delve into more specific parameter areas for finer operations.
940
       Finally, as we find that relying too much on external datasets to enhance multilingual capabilities
941
       may be limited, we instead seek higher quality supervision signals from within the model itself. Fu-
942
       ture work could consider combining these two paradigms by incorporating data selection strategies
943    (Albalak et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b), thereby providing higher quality multilingual supervision
944    signals to the model from both internal and external sources.

1164   F     A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORKS
1165
1166   Here we provide additional discussion on the theoretical foundation of language-agnostic and
1167   language-specific subspaces, dividing it into two aspects: linguistic theory and LLM interpretability.
1168
1169
1170
       Linguistic Theory From a linguistic standpoint, the idea of separating representations into
1171
       language-agnostic and language-specific spaces is grounded in established theories of language uni-
1172
       versals and typology. Language-agnostic features align with universal linguistic structures, such as
1173   shared syntactic patterns or semantic primitives (Greenberg, 1963; Comrie, 1989), while language-
1174   specific features capture unique aspects like phonology, morphology, or syntax (Croft, 2002; Cot-
1175   terell et al., 2016). These distinctions have also been studied in computational linguistics, such as
1176   in multilingual embeddings (Artetxe et al., 2018) and cross-lingual representation learning (Ruder
1177   et al., 2019), supporting the conceptual basis in L ENS.
1178
1179
1180
       LLM Interpretability Recent interpretability studies have provided compelling evidence that
1181
       LLMs internally encode language-agnostic and language-specific subspaces. For example, specific
1182   neurons or groups of neurons have been identified as responsible for mapping multilingual input rep-
1183   resentations into either a shared language-agnostic space (Chen et al., 2023a; Starace et al., 2023;
1184   Wang et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024; Wendler et al., 2024) that different languages share the com-
1185   mon knowledge or distinct language-specific spaces (Tang et al., 2024; Kojima et al., 2024; Zhang
1186   et al., 2024d) that are crucial for the accurate expression for specific languages. These findings sup-
1187   port our assumption that LLMs naturally exhibit such separable structures, and our work leverages
       this inductive bias to improve multilingual performance.

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
       Building upon such two theoretical foundations, particularly from linguistic theory, most previous
1189   works regarding multilingual enhancement have focused on aligning representations in the language-
1190   agnostic space (Hu et al., 2024; Berend, 2020; Cao et al., 2020; Karthikeyan et al., 2020; Alaux et al.,
1191   2019; Wang et al., 2019) or aligning gradients during optimization (Lee et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
1192   2021) to leverage shared features across languages. However, few works in multilingual machine
1193   translation have considered language-specific characteristics, primarily to implement routing mech-
1194   anisms or modular designs to improve performance (Zhao et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2021a)
1195   In contrast, our proposed L ENS goes a step further that it leverages both language-agnostic and
1196   language-specific subspaces to comprehensively enhance multilingual performance both inheriting
1197   the theoretical soundness and demonstrating practical utility of our approach.
