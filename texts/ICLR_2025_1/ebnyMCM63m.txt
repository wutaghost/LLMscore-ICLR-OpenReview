009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               We introduce RE-A DAPT, an approach to fine-tuning large language models on
012
               new domains without degrading any pre-existing instruction-tuning. We reverse
013            engineer an adapter which isolates what an instruction-tuned model has learned
014            beyond its corresponding pretrained base model. Importantly, this requires no
015            additional data or training. We can then fine-tune the base model on a new domain
016            and readapt it to instruction following with the reverse engineered adapter. RE-
017            A DAPT and our low-rank variant L O RE-A DAPT both outperform other methods of
018            fine-tuning, across multiple popular LLMs and datasets, even when the models are
019            used in conjunction with retrieval-augmented generation.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024
025   Large Language Models (LLMs) require a significant investment to develop and train, requiring
026   resources available to only a limited number of organizations. For instance, Meta’s Llama-3 family
027
      of models was trained using two custom-built compute clusters, each containing 24,000 high-end
      GPUs (Meta, 2024). Parameter Efficient Fine Tuning (PEFT) enables resource-efficient downstream
028
      customization of existing LLMs for new domains by adjusting a relatively small number of parameters
029
      while keeping the majority unchanged. However, an important distinction exists between the types
030   of model used for further fine-tuning. It is common for LLM producers to release two versions of a
031   model, one which is pretrained on a general task such as next-token prediction and an instruct version
032   which is then continued trained on annotated data to learn how to follow instructions or respond to
033   queries in a preferential manner (Touvron et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Almazrouei et al., 2023;
034   Banks & Warkentin, 2024). The availability of both versions introduces a choice for organizations
035   wanting to adapt a model to their custom task or domain.
036   While an instruction-tuned model is generally more capable for popular tasks, the majority of data
037   available for additional fine-tuning is unlabeled, lacking the annotations expected from instruct
038   models. This poses a significant problem as annotation by the downstream organization can be too
039   difficult, expensive, or error-prone (Fredriksson et al., 2020; Desmond et al., 2021). Additional
040   fine-tuning can also degrade the performance of the instruction-tuned model outside of the new
041   fine-tuning distribution (Kotha et al., 2024). On the other hand, pretrained models can be easily
042   fine-tuned with unlabeled text but lack the additional capabilities of their instruct counterparts.
043   To address this dilemma, we seek the ability to fine-tune existing LLMs on unlabeled text while
044   retaining the capabilities from pre-existing instruction-tuning. We draw inspiration from adapters,
045   sets of learnable parameters added to an existing model for fine-tuning (Bapna & Firat, 2019; Houlsby
046   et al., 2019). We make the key observation that the difference in weights between an instruction-
047   tuned and corresponding pretrained model is effectively an adapter. Isolating the information
048   learned from instruction-tuning into this Reverse Engineered (RE)-Adapter enables fine-tuning of the
049   pretrained model without directly impacting the instruction-tuning. The fine-tuned model can then be
050
      readapted with the instruction following capabilities (Figure 1).
051   We apply our RE-A DAPT approach to fine-tune several popular LLM variants with unlabeled data
052   from new domains. We use both closed- and open-book question-answering to measure their ability
053   to incorporate the new domain-specific information without decreasing the previous capabilities
      out-of-domain. Specifically, we:

080   2     BACKGROUND
081
082   2.1   A DAPTERS
083
084   Adapters (Bapna & Firat, 2019; Houlsby et al., 2019) have played an important role in the context of
085   transfer learning for language models in recent years, particularly for fine-tuning pretrained models
086   which are too large to fully train on commodity hardware. The concept introduced by Houlsby et al.
087
      (2019) provides a lightweight alternative to full fine-tuning through the augmentation of models
      with small modular sets of trainable parameters. Adapters have been useful for enabling the use of
088
      pretrained models on new tasks (Pfeiffer et al., 2021; Karimi Mahabadi et al., 2021; Rücklé et al.,
089
      2021), new domains (Malik et al., 2023; Schopf et al., 2023; Diao et al., 2023), and adapting to
090   multiple languages (Chronopoulou et al., 2023b; Üstün et al., 2022; Parovic et al., 2023).
091
092   Low-Rank Adapters (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) are a particularly parameter efficient adaptation
093
      technique which adds a low-rank matrix to the weights of existing layers. Because the adapter is
      low-rank it can be represented as the product of two much smaller matrices, significantly lowering the
094
      number of trainable parameters. Weight-Decomposed Low-Rank Adaptation (DoRA) is an extension
095
      to LoRA with superior performance and similar efficiency (Liu et al., 2024). Liu et al. (2024) achieve
096   this by decomposing the pretrained weights into both magnitude and direction components, applying
097   LoRA for directional fine-tuning only. Important to this work, adapters learned with either LoRA or
098   DoRA can be represented as a single matrix which captures the information learned during fine-tuning.
099   The pretrained model is then adapted by simply adding the new matrix to the existing weights. We
100   leverage DoRA to fine-tune our models on a new domain, and take inspiration from the additive
101   nature of these techniques to derive our reverse engineered adapters.
102   Several approaches have been developed which utilize the mixing or combination of adapters to
103   benefit from diverse tasks or domains Pfeiffer et al. (2021); Rücklé et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022);
104   Chronopoulou et al. (2023a); Fleshman et al. (2024); Zadouri et al. (2024) or for parameter efficient
105   federated learning (Babakniya et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024). One method to categorize these
106   approaches is by the mechanism used for combining the adapters. Either a weighted combination of
107   adapters is applied to the base model (Chronopoulou et al., 2023a; Fleshman et al., 2024; Babakniya
      et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024) or another set of parameters are used to learn adapter interactions


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025



108
      (Pfeiffer et al., 2021; Rücklé et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Zadouri et al., 2024). We focus on the
109
      former, as we reframe instruction-tuned models as the summation of a pretrained model with an
110   instruction adapter. We add new knowledge by combining domain-specific and instruction adapters
111   via linear combination. As highlighted by Sun et al. (2024), separate adapters can be incompatible
112   when averaged. Chronopoulou et al. (2023a) and Fleshman et al. (2024) try to mitigate this by
113   initializing adapters with the same random weights, and Sun et al. (2024) by doing the same through
114   a data driven approach. Neither option is applicable here, as we have no control over the instruction
115   adapter. This motivates our new approach for partial adaptation which we introduce in Section 3.

119   2.2   I NSTRUCT M ODELS
120
121   Some of the most capable LLMs are instruct variants, pretrained on massive amounts of unannotated
122   text and further trained on curated datasets with a combination of instruction-tuning (Mishra et al.,
123   2022; Wei et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022b; Sanh et al., 2022) and Reinforcement Learning from
124   Human Feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Stiennon et al., 2020). For example, Llama-3
125   was pretrained on 15T tokens and the instruct version continued training with a combination of
126   supervised fine tuning (SFT), rejection sampling, proximal policy optimization (PPO), and direct
127
      preference optimization (DPO) (Meta, 2024). Open-source LLM producers generally release both the
      instruct versions as well as the pretrained models from which they were derived (Jiang et al., 2023;
128
      Almazrouei et al., 2023; Banks & Warkentin, 2024; Meta, 2024). Access to the pretrained LLM
129
      allows users to customize the model to a new task or domain while taking advantage of the large
130   investment required for pretraining. Fine-tuning the instruct model directly is generally avoided due to
131   catastrophic-forgetting, a phenomenon where models lose previous abilities with subsequent rounds
132   of continued training (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989; Kotha et al., 2024). This is unfortunate, as few
133   organizations have the resources to conduct fine-tuning at the scale of the original instruction-tuned
134   models. In this work, we explore methods of fine-tuning LLMs which take advantage of both the
135   pretraining and instruction-tuning of existing LLMs. We specifically design our approach to minimize
136   forgetting while fine-tuning instruction-capable models with unlabeled text.

      2.3   M ODEL A RITHMETIC
141
142   Previous works have looked at the ability to arithmetically manipulate models to isolate certain
143   behaviors (Ilharco et al., 2023; Mitchell et al., 2024). Ilharco et al. (2023) constructed task vectors by
144   differencing weights between a pretrained model and several corresponding models each fine-tuned
145   for a particular task. They observed for their models that task vectors are almost orthogonal to
146   each other, preventing interference and allowing combinations of the vectors for negating certain
147
      behaviors, improving multi-task performance, or performing well on new tasks via more complicated
      task analogies (Ilharco et al., 2023). We similarly solve for our reverse engineered adapter with
148
      a simple differencing, but using a single LLM fine-tuned for multi-task instruction-following. By
149
      effectively isolating instruction-tuning into an adapter, we allow further fine-tuning of pretrained
150   models, maximizing knowledge acquisition before readapting their ability to follow instructions. We
151   introduce an optional step for reducing the rank of our RE-Adapter, lowering memory requirements
152   while maintaining or improving performance in some scenarios. Unlike task vectors, our multi-
153   purpose RE-Adapters are not assumed to be orthogonal to new training domains. We introduce a
154   technique for mitigating potential interference in Section 3 by controlling the adaptation strength.
155
      Mitchell et al. (2024) developed an alternative approach for isolating pretraining knowledge from
156   fine-tuned behaviors which they call emulated fine-tuning. Instead of differencing model weights,
157   emulated fine-tuning considers the difference in outputs between pretrained and fine-tuned versions
158   of a model. By combining this difference with the output of a larger pretrained model, Mitchell et al.
159   (2024) found that they could benefit from the additional pretraining knowledge while still solving
160   the task of the smaller model. Their technique could be extended to meet our goal but requires the
161   storage and forward pass of multiple models for inference. Our approach isolates new knowledge
      and instruction-following into separate adapters, merged into a single model at no extra cost.

      3     PARTIAL A DAPTATION
163
164
      We detail our main methods in Section 4, but first we introduce a technique for controlling the strength
165
      of adaptation. Consider a model with weights W and an adapter A used to fine-tune the model on a
166   new domain. Using additive adapters such as LoRA or DoRA, the combined weights:
167
168                                               Ŵ = W + A                                               (1)
169
      are then used for inference (Hu et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024). We make the observation that the
170
      resulting model assigns equal weight to the original parameters and the new adapter, which is
171
      generally trained with significantly less data than the original weights. This potentially leads to
172   overfitting in the new domain and degradation of performance in the general setting. These issues
173   compound in situations where multiple adapters are combined. Both Chronopoulou et al. (2023a)
174   and Fleshman et al. (2024) discuss complications arising from mixing adapters, especially if they
175   were not initialized with the same values to encourage compatibility.
176
      To mitigate these challenges we propose a technique for partial adaptation which introduces a
177
      post-hoc scaling factor for each fine-tuned adapter. Importantly, Equation 1 is still used during
178   fine-tuning, but inference becomes:
179
                                               Ŵ = W + λA                                           (2)
180
181   where 0 ≤ λ ≤ 1 is used to scale the strength of adaptation. In our experiments, we find that partial
182   adaptation improves performance when using either single or multiple combined adapters.

184   4     R EVERSE E NGINEERED A DAPTATION
185
186   Here we describe Reverse Engineered Adaptation (RE-A DAPT), our approach to solve the challenge
187   of updating an instruction-tuned model with unlabeled text without degrading the ability of the model
188   to follow instructions. In Section 5, we demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach for closed-book
189   and retrieval-augmented question answering over new domains.
190
191   4.1   RE-A DAPTERS
192
193   First consider two language models: TΦ , which has been pretrained with parameters Φ; and TΘ ,
194   having the same architecture as TΦ but with parameters Θ updated from the pretrained parameters
195
      Φ via instruction-tuning. Given these models, we can solve for the RE-Adapter parameters ∆ using:
196                                                ∆=Θ−Φ                                                   (3)
197
198   to isolate the information learned during instruction-tuning. Next, we augment the pretrained model
199   TΦ with a learnable adapter Ψ and fit TΦ+Ψ on a new domain by only updating the adapter weights
200
      Ψ. We refer to Ψ as the knowledge adapter. We utilize DoRA to fit Ψ in our experiments, but any
      fine-tuning approach is applicable. We construct our final model TΩ with parameters:
201
202                                           Ω = Φ + αΨ + β∆                                              (4)

206

      5     E XPERIMENTS
253
254
      We quantify the effectiveness of RE-A DAPT using question-answering (QA), a task suitable for
255
      measuring knowledge acquisition from unlabeled data for which instruction-tuned models perform
256
      better than their pretrained counterparts due to significant portions of instruction-following datasets
257   being QA-based (Ouyang et al., 2022a; Wang et al., 2023; Taori et al., 2023). Specifically, we want
258   to see if RE-A DAPT is better than alternatives for adding knowledge from data not annotated with
259   question-answer pairs. We would like the resulting model to do well answering questions about the
260   new domains, while maintaining the level of performance of the original instruction-tuned model
261   when answering unrelated questions.
262

267   using the HuggingFace API (Wolf et al., 2020). We utilize the parameter efficient fine-tuning library
268   (Mangrulkar et al., 2022) for adding DoRA (Liu et al., 2024) knowledge adapters to each of these
269   models. We perform all fine-tuning and inference with a single 80GB A100 GPU. We include
      hyper-parameters and other details of our fine-tuning in Appendix A.

288   5.2    DATA
289
      Kotha et al. (2024) showed that fine-tuning degrades performance outside of the fine-tuning distribu-
290
      tion. We hypothesize that our approach mitigates this issue by isolating existing instruction-tuning
291   from additional fine-tuning. We test this by measuring the changes in question-answering perfor-
292   mance when various fine-tuning strategies are used to update models with unlabeled data. An optimal
293   approach would benefit from the new knowledge when asked related questions, without losing the
294   ability to answer unrelated questions.
295
      We explore this hypothesis by fine-tuning models in two different settings. We use English WMT
296
      News Crawl (Kocmi et al., 2022) articles published in the year 2020 as our first fine-tuning distribu-
297   tion.1 These articles provide non-annotated information which we capture through DoRA adapters
298   trained for next-token-prediction. We evaluate how well this knowledge is acquired by using the
299   resulting models to answer related questions from the StreamingQA dataset (Liška et al., 2022),
300   which contains 21,681 QA pairs derived from our subset of articles.2
301
      We use the evidence passages from RetrievalQA (Zhang et al., 2024) as our second fine-tuning
302
      distribution and measure performance on the corresponding questions from the same dataset.3 Zhang
303   et al. (2024) curated the dataset by compiling the subset of questions from five other QA benchmarks
304   for which GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024) is unable to answer without access to external knowledge. The
305   questions were selected with the goal of having the corresponding knowledge absent from current
306   LLMs, making this dataset especially challenging in the closed-book setting.
307
      To measure any performance degradation from fine-tuning, we also evaluate our models using a
308
      short-answer subset of the Natural Questions dataset (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019) which is unrelated to
309   either fine-tuning distribution.4 We use these questions as a control to measure performance before
310   and after fine-tuning our models on the other domains. We would like our approach to result in
311   improved performance when answering questions related to the fine-tuning data without a reduction
312   in performance on the unrelated Natural Questions Figure 4.

456   6   D ISCUSSION
457
458
      Combined, our results demonstrate RE-A DAPT’s effectiveness at incorporating new knowledge
459   into existing LLMs without having to discard previous instruction-tuning. Our methods increase
460   QA performance by a greater amount when compared to traditional fine-tuning strategies. We also
461   find that our approach improves RAG based systems, even in the most optimistic case of perfect
462   retrieval. Our improved results outside of the fine-tuning distribution suggest that we can recover
463   additional pretraining knowledge by reducing the strength of instruction-tuning through partial
464   adaptation. Importantly, an improvement is seen without any additional fine-tuning of the underlying
465   models. These results encourage additional future research into controlling the competing priorities
466   of knowledge acquisition and general problem solving capability.
467
468
      7   C ONCLUSION
469
470
      In this work, we presented RE-A DAPT, a new approach for adding knowledge to existing instruction-
471
      tuned models. RE-A DAPT isolates the differences between an instruction-tuned model and its
472
      pretrained counterpart in order to preserve instruction-following capabilities during additional fine-
473   tuning on unlabeled data. We demonstrated that our approach outperforms fine-tuning pretrained or
474   instruction-tuned models directly, which otherwise causes performance to degrade outside of the new
475   fine-tuning domain. Our findings are robust across three state of the art large language models.
476
      We achieved our best performance using partial adaptation, a new method for controlling the strength
477
      of adaptation at inference time when using single or combined adapters. We found that partially
478
      adapting instruction-tuned models improved QA performance without any additional fine-tuning.
479
480   We also analyzed the spectrum of RE-A DAPT’s weight matrices, constructing a low-rank variant
481   of our approach, L O RE-A DAPT, which captures the majority of variation in the instruction-tuning
482
      weights at a much lower rank. L O RE-A DAPT performed similarly to RE-A DAPT with occasional
      out-performance, while decreasing the number of parameters by as much as 5x in our experiments.
483
484   Finally, we demonstrated that RE-A DAPT improves performance even when the information required
485   to answer questions is available via retrieval augmented generation. Combined, our results suggest
      RE-A DAPT is an effective approach for infusing new knowledge into instruction-tuned LLMs.
