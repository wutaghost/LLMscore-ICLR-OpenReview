011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We present a simple yet effective method to improve the robustness of both Convo-
014            lutional and attention-based Neural Networks against adversarial examples by post-
015
               processing an adversarially trained model. Our technique, M EAN S PARSE, cascades
               the activation functions of a trained model with novel operators that sparsify mean-
016
               centered feature vectors. This is equivalent to reducing feature variations around the
017
               mean, and we show that such reduced variations merely affect the model’s utility,
018            yet they strongly attenuate the adversarial perturbations and decrease the attacker’s
019            success rate. Our experiments show that, when applied to the top models in the Ro-
020            bustBench leaderboard, M EAN S PARSE achieves a new robustness record of 75.28%
021            (from 73.71%), 44.78% (from 42.67%) and 62.12% (from 59.56%) on CIFAR-10,
022            CIFAR-100 and ImageNet, respectively, in terms of AutoAttack accuracy. Code:
023            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/MeanSparse-84B0/

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Adversarial Training (AT) Madry et al. (2018) has become a popular method to defend deep neural
028
      networks (DNNs) against adversarial examples Szegedy et al. (2013); Goodfellow et al. (2014). The
029
      core idea involves generating adversarial examples during the training phase and incorporating them
030   into the training dataset. The mere existence of such adversarial examples are due to features that
031   are non-robust to imperceptible changes Ilyas et al. (2019). AT attenuates the significance of these
032   non-robust features on model’s output, therefore improving robustness.
033
      While AT has demonstrated effectiveness in enhancing model robustness, it faces limitations in
034
      generalizability across various methods for generating adversarial examples and suffers from low
035
      training efficiency. Various directions have been taken Xing et al. (2022); Gowal et al. (2021);
036   Wong et al. (2019) to overcome these challenges in order to enhance the robustness provided by
037   AT. In this work, we explore a complementary direction towards improving AT’s robustness: The
038   design of activation functions presents a promising, yet underexplored, avenue for improving AT.
039   Smooth Adversarial Training (SAT) has shown that replacing ReLU with a smooth approximation
040   can enhance robustness Xie et al. (2020), and activation functions with learnable parameters have
041   also demonstrated improved robustness in adversarially trained models Dai et al. (2022); Salimi et al.
042   (2023a). This paper aims to further investigate the potential of activation functions in enhancing AT,
043   providing new insights and approaches to bolster model robustness.
044   In this work, we explore the impact of sparsifying features to enhance robustness against adversarial
045   examples. We introduce the M EAN S PARSE technique, which integrates sparsity into models trained
046   using AT. Our findings indicate that non-robust features persist in the model, albeit with reduced
047   values, even after applying AT. This insight inspired our approach to partially block these remaining
048   non-robust features. To achieve this, we propose imposing sparsity over mean-centered features,
049   denoted by Mean-based Sparsification, inspired by sparsity-promoting regularizers commonly used
050
      for feature denoising Elad & Aharon (2006); Aharon et al. (2006) and feature selection Tibshirani
      (1996); Mairal et al. (2010).
051
052   The M EAN S PARSE operator selectively suppresses variations around the mean of feature representa-
053   tions, effectively filtering out non-robust features. For a given feature channel, we compute the mean
      (µ) and standard deviation (σ) over the training set. Using a tunable threshold (T h = ασ), we block

147   2.2   R ELATED W ORK
148
      Adversarial training, a method to enhance model robustness, encounters high complexity because of
149
      the adversarial attack design incorporated during training. The concept of using adversarial samples
150   during training was first introduced in Szegedy et al. (2013) and then AT was introduced in Madry
151   et al. (2018). Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) is a low-complexity attack design used for AT
152   Tramèr et al. (2018); Liu et al. (2021); Wong et al. (2019); Vivek & Babu (2020). This approach while
153   reducing the complexity of AT, cannot generalize to more complex adversarial attacks. On the other
154   hand using Projected Gradient Descent with AT, although increasing the attack design complexity
155   due to its iterative nature, can better generalize to other adversarial attacks Madry et al. (2018);
156   Tramèr et al. (2018); Wang et al. (2019); Cheng et al. (2022). Curriculum Adversarial Training
157   gradually increases the complexity of adversarial samples during AT Cai et al. (2018). Other types of
158
      adversarial attacks also have been proposed to be used for AT including Jacobian-based Saliency
      Map Attack Qin et al. (2019), Carlini and Wagner attack Carlini & Wagner (2017); Wen et al. (2018)
159
      and attacking Ensemble of methods during AT Pang et al. (2019), among others.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
196
197
      From the concept of non-robust features, it is understood that through adversarial training, these
198
      features become less informative about the output label Ilyas et al. (2019). From an information-
199   theoretic point-of-view, we also recognize that as the occurrence probability of a random vector
200   increases, its informational value decreases Cover & Thomas (2006). Consequently, to identify the
201   less informative features, it is useful to examine the regions of high probability within the feature
202   space. One easily accessible high-probability point is the feature mean. Following this reasoning,
203   we can block minor variations around the mean to eliminate the less informative or equivalently,
204   the non-robust features. We begin this section by designing a regularized optimization objective to
205   block non-robust features. Although this optimization problem is not used in the final M EAN S PARSE
206   technique, its parameter update rule provides valuable insight into the design of the sparsification
207
      operator introduced later. Finally, we demonstrate the complete M EAN S PARSE technique.
208

265   3.3   M EAN S PARSE T ECHNIQUE
266
267   Now we have a mean-centered sparsification operator that can be integrated into the model to
268   block non-robust variations around the mean value. However, there are several challenges and
269   improvements to address, which will be discussed in this section. By applying these enhancements,
      the final technique is formed.

      S PARSIFICATION IN P OST- PROCESSING
271
272   As a general input-output mapping relation, mean-centered feature sparsification can be added to any
273   part of deep learning architecture without changing the dimension. The back-propagation of training
274   signals through this sparsification operator will be zero for inputs in the T h-vicinity of the feature
275   mean, which avoids model training since the mean is the place where the highest rate of features lies.
276   Two options can be applied to solve this problem.
277   The first approach is to gradually increase the T h value through iterations. At the start of training,
278   T h is set to zero. Thus, sparsification reduces to an identity transformation, which solves the problem
279   of backpropagation. Then, throughout the iterations, T h is increased until it reaches a predefined
280   maximum value. The most challenging problem in this scenario is the selection of a T h scheduler as
281   the model output is not differentiable with respect to T h and T h cannot be updated using gradient.
282   In this scenario, we need to calculate the mean vector for the input feature representation inside the
283   sparsification operator in a similar way to batch normalization layers.
284   The second solution is to apply sparsification as a post-training step to an adversarially trained model.
285   In this scenario, first, we freeze the model weights and add the mean-centered feature sparsification
286   operator to the model in the predefined positions. Then we need to pass through the complete training
287   set once to calculate the feature mean value. Next, we decide on the value of T h. Similar to the
288   previous scenario, this step cannot be done using gradient-based methods as the operator is not
289   differentiable with respect to T h. Thus, we can search over a range of T h values and find the best
290
      one that results in the highest robustness while maintaining the model’s clean accuracy.
291   The two approaches differ significantly: while training with MeanSparse can improve robustness
292   by influencing learned representations, it requires a carefully designed threshold scheduler to avoid
293   issues like gradient zeroing or instability, particularly in large models, with early experiments
294   showing unstable training and failed convergence. In contrast, the post-training approach integrates
295   more easily, leveraging established model statistics and requiring only a search over alpha values,
296
      making it scalable to large models. It has been successfully applied to architectures like Swin-L,
      achieving a +2.56% robustness improvement without destabilization. Given the challenges of training
297
      MeanSparse in large models and the success of post-training integration, we chose the latter. This
298
      involves freezing the model, applying sparsification over mean-centered features before activation
299   functions, calculating the mean value over the training set, and determining the optimal threshold
300   (T h) to maximize robustness while maintaining clean accuracy.
301
302
      A DAPTIVE S PARSIFICATION U SING F EATURE S TANDARD D EVIATION
303
304   As we need to apply sparsification before all activations in the model, searching over a simple space
305   and finding a suitable set of T h for different activation functions becomes intractable, even in small
306   models. Thus, we design to select the threshold as T h = α × σa . While we pass through the
307   training set to find the mean value µa , we can also determine the variance value σa2 in parallel and
308   use T h = α × σa as the threshold. As a result, for a fixed value of α, the blocking vicinity around
309   the mean increases as the variance of the feature increases.
310
311   P ER - CHANNEL S PARSIFICATION
312
      Throughout the neural network, the input to the activation functions can be considered as 4-
313
      dimensional, represented as N × C × H × W (N , C, H and W represents the batch size, channels,
314
      height and width, respectively). To better capture the statistics in the representation, we use per-
315   channel mean µch and variance σch in sparsification operator. As a result, the mean and variance
316   vectors are C-dimensional vectors. The sparsification operates on each feature channel separately,
317   using the mean and variance of the corresponding channel.

475   4.3   I NSIGHTS FROM A BLATION S TUDY
476
477
      We conducted several experiments to explore different aspects of M EAN S PARSE integration into
      trained models. These experiments examine the effects of activation functions A.1, the type of
478
      adversarial attack (either ℓ∞ or ℓ2 ) A.2, the robustness of M EAN S PARSE integrated models against
479
      black-box attacks A.3, the effect of adaptive attack with access to M EAN S PARSE modules location
480   and specifications (mean and variance vectors) A.4, the types of adversarial examples used during
481   training with AT A.5, the effect of integrating a standard trained model with M EAN S PARSE A.6,
482   the impact of centering M EAN S PARSE around the mean value A.7, the influence of attack power on
483   M EAN S PARSE integration performance A.8, the effects of integrating M EAN S PARSE with different
484   patterns, such as isolated integration with a single activation A.9, and the robustness improvement
485   resulting from M EAN S PARSE integration in terms of PGD-50 attack A.10. The main takeaways from
      our ablation study can be summarized as:


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
             • M EAN S PARSE effectively enhances robustness across various activation functions. In other
487            words, the activation function alone does not effectively eliminate non-robust features, which
488            are previously reduced during training.
489          • By calculating the sparsity using other well-known adversarial attacks such as PGD-50
490            Madry et al. (2018), we observe a similar improvement in robustness, consistent with the
491            AA and APGD accuracy values.
492          • Although M EAN S PARSE masks the gradient, which can explain the robustness improvement
493            against white-box attacks, it also demonstrates enhanced robustness when evaluated using
494            black-box attacks.
495
             • A straightforward attack aiming to minimize the impact of M EAN S PARSE in affected regions
               is generally ineffective at significantly compromising the robustness of M EAN S PARSE-
496
               integrated models. However, as noted in the limitations, alternative strategies, such as
497
               treating M EAN S PARSE as an identity function during backpropagation, can reduce the
498            robustness provided by M EAN S PARSE (see Section A.4 for details on adaptive attacks).
499          • M EAN S PARSE is effective against both ℓ∞ and ℓ2 attacks. Specifically, if a model is fortified
500            against ℓ∞ attacks using AT, adding M EAN S PARSE enhances robustness against both ℓ∞ and
501            ℓ2 attacks. Also, M EAN S PARSE is not sensitive to the method used to generate adversarial
502            samples during AT.
503          • Using dedicated mean and variance for each channel in the M EAN S PARSE technique is
504            critical to its performance. Otherwise, we cannot expect the observed improvement in
505            robustness.
506
             • Integrating M EAN S PARSE in isolated activation functions remains effective for robustifica-
               tion, although the improvement is limited.
507
508
509   4.4   L IMITATIONS
510
      The M EAN S PARSE technique proposed in this paper is a post-training method that is useful on top of
511   adversarially trained models. Using M EAN S PARSE for models trained in a standard way does not lead
512   to robustness improvement as we expect based on our intuition introduced in this paper. Additionally,
513   while integrating a model with M EAN S PARSE shows clear improvements against both white-box and
514   black-box non-adaptive attacks, white-box attacks that ignore the MeanSparse transformation during
515   backpropagation and use the identity function can impact M EAN S PARSE’s effectiveness.
516
517
      5     C ONCLUSIONS
518
519
      Adversarial training, a widely accepted defense against evasion attacks, attenuates non-robust feature
520
      extractors in a model. Although adversarial training (AT) is effective, this paper demonstrates that
521   there is still an easily accessible capacity for attackers to exploit. We introduce the M EAN S PARSE
522   technique, which partially blocks this capacity in the model available to attackers and enhances
523   adversarial robustness. This technique, easily integrable after training, improves robustness without
524   compromising clean accuracy. By integrating this technique into trained models, we achieve a new
525   record in adversarial robustness on CIFAR-10 for both ℓ∞ and ℓ2 untargeted attacks, as well as on
526   CIFAR-100 and ImageNet for ℓ∞ untargeted attacks.

      A.4   A DAPTIVE ATTACK
830
831   In this experiment, we investigate the potential for the attacker to enhance the attack by utilizing the
832   knowledge that M EAN S PARSE is employed to robustify the model. We propose a formulation to adapt
833   the original PGD attack, incorporating the integration of M EAN S PARSE to create a more powerful
834   attack. In this scenario, we assume the attacker has complete access to the locations, mean, and
835   variance vectors of the integrated M EAN S PARSE operator throughout the model. Let x represent the
836
      input image and {zi } the corresponding representations at the input of the M EAN S PARSE modules
      in the model. When a perturbation δ is added to the input (x + δ), it results in new representations
837
      {ziδ }. In the adaptive PGD attack, we solve the following optimization problem to determine the
838
      perturbation δ:
839                                                             X
840                         arg max CE(f (x + δ), y) − λ            ∥mi ⊙ (zi − ziδ )∥qp
841                            δ                                i
842                        subject to ∥δ∥∞ ≤ ϵ,       x + δ ∈ [0, 1]D                                      (8)
843
844
      where CE(·, ·) is the cross-entropy loss, f is the model, y is the true label, λ is a hyper-parameter, i
      indexes different M EAN S PARSE modules in the model, ⊙ is the element-wise multiplication operator,
845
      ϵ controls the perturbation imperceptibility via the ℓ∞ norm, and D is the dimension of the input
846
      image. mi represents a mask with the same dimensions as zi , identifying the locations in zi affected
847   by the M EAN S PARSE module. For a given z, the corresponding m can be computed element-wise
848   as:
849
850
                                1 if µch − ασch ≤ zj ≤ µch + ασch
                        mj =                                              , j = 1, 2, . . . , K
851                             0 Otherwise
852   where µch and σch are the statistics of the M EAN S PARSE module, and K represents the representation
853   dimension. Thus, whenever a feature in the input of M EAN S PARSE is affected by this module for the
854   input image x, the corresponding mask value becomes 1; otherwise, it is 0. As a result, the second
855   term in the objective function of the adaptive PGD attack equation 8 penalizes perturbations that lead
856   to changes in the features affected by M EAN S PARSE. In the ideal case, the perturbation is designed
857   such that none of the M EAN S PARSE modules can block its effect in their input (we know that the
858   changes in the input of the i-th M EAN S PARSE module are zi − zid ). Thus, the attacker can bypass
859   the M EAN S PARSE, which we denote as the Adaptive-(p, q) attack.
860   To test the adaptive attack, we solve the optimization problem equation 8 using gradient ascent with a
                         1                                            8

961
      where the channel mean is used as the reference. The main takeaway from the table is the importance

1028   The M EAN S PARSE technique is designed to sparsify mean-centered inputs to all activation functions
1029   in a model. In this experiment, we will focus on applying sparsification over a selected subset of
1030   activation functions. For this purpose, we index the activation functions in the ResNet-18 model.
1031   This model has 8 residual blocks, each with 2 activation functions: one in the Main Path and one
1032   after the result of the main path is added to the residual connection, totaling 16 activation functions.
1033   Additionally, this architecture has one activation function before the residual block. So, in total, we
1034
       have 17 indices. Index 0 is the initial activation function. The odd indices are related to the activation
       function in the main path, and the even indices correspond to activation functions after the addition of
1035
       the residual connection to the main path output. We select two scenarios. In the first scenario, denoted
1036
       by Single Activation, we present the results when M EAN S PARSE is applied to only the corresponding
1037   row-indexed activation function, while Cumulative depicts the result when M EAN S PARSE is applied
1038   to the activation functions with indices less than or equal to the index represented in each row.

1040
       Table 10 represents the results for both Single Activation and Cumulative scenarios. One shared
       remark is that the starting layers are more effective in improving robustness. We can attribute this to
1041
       the abstraction level in different layers of the ResNet-18 model. In the case of Single Activation, when
1042
       we apply the M EAN S PARSE technique to larger indices, we observe that not only does the robust
1043   accuracy decrease, but the clean accuracy increases. This indicates that for even better performance,
1044   we can use different α values for activation functions based on their depth level. Note that for the
1045   current version of the M EAN S PARSE technique, we use a shared α for all activation functions.
1046
       Another aspect worth inspecting in this experiment is how the application of the M EAN S PARSE
1047
       technique to activation functions in the main path and after the addition of residual data to the main
1048
       path result affects robustness in terms of APGD accuracy. So we consider two cases in Table 11. In
1049   the first case, M EAN S PARSE is applied to all the activation functions in the main path of the ResNet
1050   block, denoted by Main Path. In the second case, M EAN S PARSE is applied to all the activation
1051   functions after the addition of residual data to the output of the main path, denoted by After Addition.
