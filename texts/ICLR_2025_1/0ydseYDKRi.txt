011                                   Rainbow Deep Q-Network (DQN) demonstrated combining multiple independent
012                                   enhancements could significantly boost a reinforcement learning (RL) agent’s per-
013                                   formance. In this paper, we present “Beyond The Rainbow” (BTR), a novel al-
014                                   gorithm that integrates six improvements from across the RL literature to Rain-
015                                   bow DQN, establishing a new state-of-the-art for RL using a desktop PC, with a

019
                                      agents can be trained using a high-end desktop PC on 200 million Atari frames

046       1                        I NTRODUCTION
047
048       Deep Reinforcement Learning (RL) has achieved numerous successes in complex sequential
049       decision-making tasks, most rapidly since Mnih et al. (2015) proposed Deep Q-Learning (DQN).
050       With this success, RL has become increasingly popular among smaller research labs, the hobbyist
051       community, and even the general public. However, recent state-of-the-art approaches (Schrittwieser
052       et al., 2020; Badia et al., 2020a; Hessel et al., 2021; Kapturowski et al., 2022) are increasingly out of
053       reach for those with more limited compute resources, either in terms of the required hardware or the
          walltime necessary to train a single agent. This is a unique issue in RL compared to natural language


                                                                             1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      processing or image recognition which have foundation models that can be efficiently fine-tuned for
055   a new task or problem (Lv et al., 2023). Meanwhile, RL agents must be trained afresh for each
056   environment. Therefore, the development of powerful RL algorithms that can be trained quickly on
057   inexpensive hardware is crucial for smaller research labs and the hobbyist community.
058
      These concerns are not new. Ceron & Castro (2021) highlighted that Rainbow DQN (Hessel et al.,
059
      2018) required 34,200 GPU hours (equivalent to 1435 days) of training, making the research impos-
060
      sible for anyone except a few research labs, with more recent algorithms exacerbating this problem.
061   Recurrent network architectures (Horgan et al., 2018), high update to sample ratio (D’Oro et al.,
062   2022), and the use of world-models and search-based techniques (Schrittwieser et al., 2020) all in-
063   crease the computational resources necessary to train agents, many using distributed approaches
064   requiring multiple CPUs and GPUs (or TPUs), or requiring numerous days and weeks to train a
065   single agent. These features have dramatically decreased RL’s accessibility.
066
      For this purpose, we develop “Beyond the Rainbow” (BTR), taking the same principle as Rain-
067   bow DQN Hessel et al. (2018), selecting 6 previously independently evaluated improvements and
068   combining them into a singular algorithm (Section 3). These components were chosen for their
069   performance qualities or to reduce the computational requirements for training an agent. As a re-
070   sult, BTR sets a new state-of-the-art score for Atari-60 (Bellemare et al., 2013) (excluding recurrent
071   approaches) with an Interquartile Mean (IQM) of 7.61 using a single desktop machine in less than
072   12 hours, and outperforms Rainbow DQN on Procgen (Cobbe et al., 2020) in less than a fifth of the
073   walltime (Section 4.1). Further, we demonstrate BTR’s potential by training agents to solve three
074   modern 3D games for the first time, Mario Kart Wii, Super Mario Galaxy and Mortal Combat, that
075
      each contain complex mechanics and graphics (Section 4.2). To verify the effectiveness and effect
      of the six improvements to BTR, in Section 5.1, we conduct a thorough ablation of each component,
076
      plotting their impact on the Atari-5 environments and in Section 5.2, we utilise seven different mea-
077
      sures to analyse the component’s impact on the agent’s policy and network weights. This allows us
078   to more precisely understand how the components impact BTR beyond performance or walltime.
079
080
          In summary, we make the following contributions to state-of-the-art RL.
081
082              • High Performance (Section 4.1) - BTR outperforms the state-of-the-art for non-
083                recurrent RL on the Atari-60 benchmark, with an IQM of 7.6 (compared to Rain-
084
                   bow DQN’s 1.9), outperforming humans on 52/60 games. Furthermore, BTR out-
                   performs Rainbow DQN with Impala on the Procgen benchmark despite using a
085
                   smaller model and 80% less walltime.
086
087              • Modern Environments (Section 4.2) - Testing beyond Atari, we demonstrate BTR
088
                   can train agents for 3 modern games: Super Mario Galaxy (final stage), Mario Kart
                   Wii (Rainbow Road), and Mortal Combat (Endurance mode). These environments
089
                   contain 3D graphics and complex physics and have never been solved using RL.
090
091              • Computationally Accessible (Figure 5) - Using a high-end desktop PC, BTR
092
                   trains Atari agents for 200 million frames in under 12 hours, significantly faster than
                   Rainbow DQN’s 35 hours. This increases RL research’s accessibility for smaller
093
                   research labs and hobbists without the need for GPU clusters or excessive walltime.
094
095              • Component Impact Analysis (Section 5) - We conduct thorough ablations inves-
096
                   tigating BTR without each component in terms of performance and other measures.
                   We discover that BTR widens action gaps (reducing the effects of approximation
097
                   errors), is robust to observation noise, and reduces neuron dormancy and weight
098
                   matrix norm (shown to improve plasticity throughout training).

      2.1   RL P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
109
110   We adopt the standard formulation of RL (Sutton & Barto, 2018), described as a Markov Decision
111   Process (MDP) defined by the tuple (S, A, P, R), where S is the set of states, A is the set of actions,
112   P : S × A →∆(S) is the stochastic transition function, and R : S × A →R is the reward function.
113   The agent’s objective isPto learn a policy π : S → ∆(A) that maximizes the expected sum of
                                ∞
114   discounted rewards Eπ [ t=0 γ t r(st , at )], where γ ∈ [0, 1) is the discount rate.
115
116   2.2   D EEP Q-L EARNING (DQN)
117
118
      One popular method for solving MDPs is Q-Learning (Watkins & Dayan, 1992) where an agent
      learns to predict the expected sum of discounted future rewards for a given state-action pair. To
119
      allow agents to generalize over states and thus be applied to problems with larger state spaces, Mnih
120
      et al. (2013) successfully combined Q-Learning with neural networks. To do this, training minimizes
121   the error between the predictions from a parameterized network Qθ and a target defined by
122
123                                        rt + γ max Qθ′ (st+1 , a) ,                                   (1)
                                                   a∈A

125   where Qθ′ is an earlier version of the network referred to as the target network, which is periodically
126
      updated from the online network Qθ . The data used to perform updates is gathered by sampling
      from an Experience Replay Buffer (Lin, 1992), which stores states, actions, rewards, and next states
127
      experienced by the agent while interacting with the environment.

      2.3   R AINBOW DQN AND I MPROVEMENTS TO DQN
130
131   In collecting 6 different improvements to DQN, Rainbow DQN (Hessel et al., 2018) proved cumula-
132   tively that these improvements could achieve a greater performance than any individually. We briefly
133   explain the individual improvements, ordered by performance impact, most of which are preserved
134   within BTR (see Table 1), for more detail, we refer readers to the extension’s respective papers:
135
136         1. Prioritized Experience Replay - To select training examples, DQN sampled uniformly
137            from an Experience Replay Buffer, assuming that all examples are equally important to
138            train with. Schaul et al. (2015) proposed sampling training examples proportionally to
139
               their last seen absolute temporal difference error, encouraging more training on samples
               for which the network most inaccurately predicts their future rewards.
140
141         2. N-Step - Q-learning utilizes bootstrapping to minimize the difference between the pre-
142            dicted value and the resultant reward plus the maximum value of the next state (Eq. 1).
143            N-step (Sutton et al., 1998) reduces the reliance on this bootstrapped next value by consid-
144
               ering the next n rewards and observation in n timesteps (Rainbow DQN used n = 3).
145         3. Distributional RL - Due to the stochastic nature of RL environments and agent’s policies,
146            Bellemare et al. (2017) proposed learning the return distribution rather than scalar expecta-
147            tion; this was done through modelling the return distributions using probability masses and
148
               the Kullbeck-Leibler divergence loss function.
149         4. Noisy Networks - Agents can often insufficiently explore their environment resulting
150            in sub-optimal policies. Fortunato et al. (2017) added parametric noise to the network
151            weights, causing the model’s outputs to be randomly perturbed, increasing exploration dur-
152
               ing training, particularly for states where the agent has less confidence.
153         5. Dueling DQN - The agent’s Q-value can be rewritten as the sum of state-value and ad-
154            vantage (Q(s, a) = V (s) + A(s, a)). Looking to improve action generalisation, Wang
155            et al. (2016) split the hidden layers into two separate streams
                                                                            P for the value and advantage,

156
               recombining them with Q(s, a) = V (s) + (A(s, a) − |A|         a′ A(s, a )).
157         6. Double DQN - In selecting the next observation’s maximum Q-value (Eq. 1), this can
158            frequently overestimate the target’s Q-value, negatively affecting the agent’s performance.
159            To reduce this overestimation, Van Hasselt et al. (2016) propose utilising the online network
160            rather than the target network to select the next action when forming targets, defined as:

181   3.1     E XTENSIONS
182
183   Impala Architecture + Adaptive Maxpooling - Espeholt et al. (2018) proposed a convolutional
184   residual neural network architecture based on He et al. (2016) featuring three residual blocks3 , sub-
185   stantially increasing performance over DQN’s three-layer convolutional network. Following Cobbe
186   et al. (2020), we scale the width of the convolutional layers by 2 to enhance its capabilities. We
187
      include an additional 6x6 adaptive max pooling layer after the convolutional layers (Schmidt &
      Schmied, 2021) found to speed up learning and support different input resolutions. Our adaptive
188
      maxpooling is identical to that of a standard 2D maxpooling layer, but can be used with any input
189
      resolution as it automatically adjusts the stride and kernel size to fit the specified output size (6x6).
190
191   Spectral Normalisation (SN) - To help stabilize the training of discriminators in Generative Adver-
192   sarial Networks (GANs), Miyato et al. (2018) proposed Spectral Normalisation to help control the
193
      Lipschitz constant of convolutional layers. SN works to normalize the weight matrices of each layer
      in the network by their largest singular value, ensuring that the transformation applied by the weights
194
      does not distort the input data excessively, which can lead to instability during training. Bjorck et al.
195
      (2021) and Gogianu et al. (2021) found that SN could improve performance in RL, especially for
196   larger networks and Schmidt & Schmied (2021) found SN reduced the number of updates required
197   before initial progress is made.
198
199
      Implicit Quantile Networks (IQN) - Dabney et al. (2018) improved upon Bellemare et al. (2017),
      used in Rainbow DQN, learning the return distribution over the probability space rather than prob-
200
      ability distribution over return values. This removes the limit on the range of Q-values that can be
201
      expressed, and enables learning the expected return at every probability.
202

      Vectorization - RL agents typically take multiple steps in a single environment, followed by a
217   gradient update with a small batch size (Rainbow DQN took 4 environment steps, followed by a
218   batch of 32). However, taking multiple steps in parallel and performing updates on larger batches
219   can significantly reduce walltime. We follow Schmidt & Schmied (2021), taking 1 step in 64 parallel
220   environments with one gradient update with batch size 256 (Schmidt & Schmied (2021) took two
221   gradient updates rather than the one we take). This results in a replay ratio (ratio of gradient updates
                                1
222   to environment steps) of 64 . Higher replay ratios have been shown to improve performance (D’Oro
223   et al., 2022), however we opt to keep this value low to reduce walltime.
224
225   3.2                   H YPERPARAMETERS
226
227
      Hyperparameters have repeatedly shown to have a very large impact on performance in RL (Ceron
      et al., 2024), thus we perform a small amount of tuning to improve performance. Firstly, how
228
      frequently the target network is updated is closely intertwined with batch size and replay ratio.
229
      We found that updating the target network every 500 gradient steps4 performed best. Given our
230   high batch size, we additionally performed minor hyperparameter tests using different learning rates
231   finding that a slightly higher learning rate of 1 × 10−4 performed best, compared to 6.25 × 10−5 in
232   Rainbow DQN. In Appendix C.2, we clarify the meaning of the terms frames, steps and transitions.
233
      For many years, RL algorithms have used a discount rate of 0.99, however, when reaching high
234
      performance, lower discount rates alter the optimal policy, causing even optimally performing agents
235
      to not collect the maximum cumulative rewards. To prevent this, we follow MuZero Reanalyse
236   (Schrittwieser et al., 2021) using γ = 0.997. For our Prioritized Experience Replay, we use the lower
237   value of α = 0.2, the parameter used to determine sample priority, recommended by Toromanoff
238   et al. (2019) when using IQN. Lastly, many previous experiments used only noisy networks or ϵ-
239   greedy exploration, however, we opt to use both until 100M frames, then set ϵ to zero, effectively
240   disabling it. We elaborate on this decision in Appendix G.

425   6   R ELATED W ORK
426
427   The most similar work to BTR, developing a computationally-limited non-distributed RL algorithm,
428   is “Fast and Efficient Rainbow” (Schmidt & Schmied, 2021). They optimised Rainbow DQN to
429   maximise performance for 10 million frames through parallelizing the environments and dropping
430   C51 along with hyperparameter optimisations. This differs from our goals of producing an algorithm
431   that scales across training regimes (up to 200 million frames) and domains (Atari, Procgen, Super
      Mario Galaxy, Mario Kart and Mortal Combat), resulting in different design decisions.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      For less computation-limited approaches, Ape-X (Horgan et al., 2018) was the first to explore highly
433   distributed training, allowing agents to be trained on a billion frames in 120 hours through using
434   100 CPUs. Following this, Kapturowski et al. (2018) proposed R2D2 using a recurrent neural net-
435   work, increasing sample efficiency but slowing down gradient updates by 38%. Agent57 (Badia
436   et al., 2020a) was the first RL agent to achieve superhuman performance across 57 Atari games,
437   though required 90 billion frames. MEME (Kapturowski et al., 2022), Agent57’s successor, focused
438   on achieving superhuman performance within the standard 200 million frames limit, achieved by
439   used a significantly higher replay ratio and larger network architecture. Most recently, Dreamer-v3
440   (Hafner et al., 2023) used a 200 million parameter model requiring over a week of training, achiev-
441
      ing similar results as MEME. We detail some of the key differences between BTR, MEME and
      Dreamer-v3 in Table 3. While these approaches perform equally to or better than BTR, all are in-
442
      accessible to smaller research labs or hobbyists due to their required computational resources and
443
      walltime. Therefore, while these algorithms have important research value demonstrating the possi-
444   ble performance of RL agents, performative algorithms with a lower cost of entry, like BTR, are a
445   necessary component for RL to become widely applicable and accessible.

460   7   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE WORK
461
462   We have demonstrated that, once again, independent improvements from across Deep Reinforce-
463   ment Learning can be combined into a single algorithm capable of pushing the state-of-the-art far
464   beyond what any single improvement is capable of. Importantly, we find that this can be accom-
465   plished on desktop PCs, increasing the accessibility of RL for smaller research labs and hobbyists.
466   We acknowledge that there are many more promising improvements we were not able to include in
467   BTR, leaving room for more future work in a few years to create even stronger integrated agents.
468   For example, BTR does not add an explicitly exploration component, resulting in it struggling in
469   hard-exploration tasks such as Montezuma’s Revenge; therefore, mechanisms used in Never Give
470   Up (Badia et al., 2020b) or other components may prove useful. Section 5.1 found that the neural
471   network’s core architecture, Impala, had the largest impact on performance, an area we believe
472   is generally underappreciated in RL. Previous work (Kapturowski et al., 2018) has incorporated
473
      recurrent models enhancing performance, though we are uncertain how this can be incorporated into
      BTR without affecting its computational accessibility.

       C.3    C LARITY OF THE TERMS F RAMES , S TEPS AND T RANSITIONS
987
988    Throughout the Arcade Learning Environment’s history (ALE) (Bellemare et al., 2013; Machado
989    et al., 2018), there have been many ambiguities around the terms: frames, steps and transitions,
990    which are sometimes used interchangeably. Frames refer to the number of individual frames the
991    agent plays, including those within repeated actions (also called frame skipping). This is notably
992    different from the number of steps the agent takes, which does not include these skipped frames.
993    When using the standard Atari wrapper, training for 200M frames is equivalent to training for 50M
994    steps. Lastly, transitions refer to the standard tuple (st , at , rt , st+1 ), where the timestep t refers to
995    a steps, not frames. We encourage researchers to make this clear when publishing work, including
996
       when mentioning values of different hyperparameters.
997
998
999
       D     B EYOND T HE R AINBOW A RCHITECTURE & L OSS F UNCTION
1000
1001   D.1    A RCHITECTURE
1002
1003   Figure D6 shows the the neural network architecture of the BTR algorithm. The architecture is
1004   highly similar to the Impala architecture (Espeholt et al., 2018), with notable exceptions:
1005
1006   • Spectral Normalization Within each Impala CNN blocks, each residual layer (containing two
1007           Conv 3x3 + ReLu) has spectral normalization applied, as discussed in Section 3.1.
1008
       • Maxpooling Following the CNN blocks, a 6x6 adaptive maxpooling layer is added.
1009
1010   • IQN In order to use IQN, it is required to draw Tau samples which are multiplied by the output
1011          of the CNN layers, as shown by the section ‘IQN Samples’ in figure D6.
1012
1013   • Dueling Dueling (as included in the original Rainbow DQN) splits the fully connected layers into
1014
               value and advantage streams, where the advantage stream output has a mean of 0, and is
               then added to the value stream.
1015
1016   • Noisy Networks As included in Rainbow DQN, Noisy Networks replace the linear layers with
1017           noisy layers.
1018
1019
       Lastly, the sizes of many of the layers given in Figure D6 are dependant upon the Impala width scale,
1020   of which we use the value 2. For example, the Impala CNN blocks have [16×width, 32×width,
1021   32×width] channels respectively. The output size of the convolutional layers (including the max-
1022   pooling layer) is 6×6×32×width, as a 6x6 maxpooling layer is used. Lastly, the cos embedding
1023   layer after generating IQN samples requires the same size as the output of the convolutional layers,
1024   hence the size is selected accordingly. Another benefit of the 6x6 maxpooling layer is following the
1025   product of the convolutional layers and IQN samples, the number of parameters is fixed, regardless
       of the input size. Figure D7 shows the numbers of parameters the ablated versions of BTR have.

       Throughout the development of the BTR algorithm, we experimented with many different compo-
       nents and hyperparameters. A brief list of ideas we tried that performed worse or equivalent to the
1324
       final algorithm includes:
1325
1326   Using Exponential Moving Average networks rather than using fixed target networks (this was both
1327   computationally slower and performed worse), varying the frequency of updating the target network,
1328   changing the size of maxpool layer following the convolutional layers (we tried 4 and 8, however
1329
       6 performed significantly better) and decaying the learning rate over the course of training. Using
       a linearly decaying learning rate from 1 × 10−4 to 0 over the course of training gave showed no
1330
       significant difference. We also experimented with some different learning rates (with and without
1331
       decay), and found 1 × 10−4 to perform best, however 5 × 10−5 also performed similarly as was
1332   used in Implicit Quantile Networks (IQN). We also tried replacing all ReLu activation units with
1333   GeLu, however this lead to dramatically worse performance. Some other ideas which we performed
1334   a single-game analysis of included annealing the discount rate from 0.97 to 0.997 (no significant
1335   difference on performance), applying spectral normaliation to the linear layers (dramatically worse
1336   performance), increasing the number of cos’ from IQN (no significant difference on performance)
1337   and using Dopamine’s Prioritized Experience Replay buffer which doesn’t include a α value (mod-
1338   erately worse performance). As discussed in G, we also tried not using ϵ-greedy when using noisy
1339   nets.
1340   Recently in RL, it has been shown that Neural Networks have a severe problem with under-
1341   parameterisation (Kumar et al., 2020) and neurons becoming dormant (Sokar et al., 2023), pre-
1342   venting larger models from seeing the success evident in other areas of Deep Learning. One method
1343   used to remedy this is weight decay through the use of the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter,
1344   2017). As this is far simpler than many other the other techniques to prevent under-parameterisation
1345   we decided to test this approach, however no significant differences in performance were observed.
1346
       We tested this approach using the decay parameter 1e − 4, however potentially using a higher value
       may results in significant changes in performance.

       ence Replay and Noisy Networks both proved beneficial, so were kept in the algorithm. Dueling did

1474   BTR interfaces with different Wii Games via the Dolphin Emulator. Specifically, we use a forked
1475   repository of to allow Python scripts to interact with the emulator. This includes loading savestates
1476   (used to reset episodes), grabbing the screen as a PIL (Clark, 2015) image at the Wii’s internal
1477   resolution of 480p (downsampled to 140x114 and grey-scaled, used for all observations), reading
1478   the Wii’s RAM (used for reward functions and termination conditions) and allows programmatic
1479   input into the emulator (used for actions). Using Dolphin’s portable setting, we are able to run
1480   multiple Dolphin Emulators simultaneously on the same machine. Each instance runs as a unique
1481   process, and communicates with the agent via Python’s multiprocessing library. Similarly to the
1482
       Atari benchmark, for all games we used a frameskip of 4.

       K.1    S UPER M ARIO G ALAXY
1485
       This environment used Super Mario Galaxy’s final level, The Center of the Universe, and had to
1486   make it to the final fight at the end of the game. The agent had 6 actions, including None, moving in
1487   each direction and jumping. Additionally, if the jump action was performed following a movement,
1488   the agent would continue to move in that direction.
1489
       Rewards were given via finding many values in the Wii’s memory that resembled progress in the
1490
       level. The agent was then rewarded for this progress value increasing from the last frame. If the
1491
       agent’s position entered a set region, the progress variable would be moved. Additionally, the game
1492   uses a life system, where the player has a maximum of 3 lives and can lose or gain lives in many
1493   different ways. The agent was given a reward of +1 for gaining a life, and -1 for losing a life. Lastly,
1494   episode termination occurred if the agent reached 0 lives, or if the agent made it to the end of the
1495   level. For this task, we also allowed the agent to start episodes at many points throughout the level,
1496   which rapidly sped up training since the agent could easily experience different areas of the level.
1497
       Whilst a difficult task, once the agent first completed the level, it did not take long to start consis-
1498   tently completing it due to the deterministic nature of the game.

1501
1502   The Mario Kart Wii environment had the agent play against the game’s internal opponents (on hard
1503   mode, with 12 racers including the agent), on the course Rainbow Road (with items on the 150cc
1504   speed setting). The agent had to complete 4 laps of the course to finish the race. The agent had just
1505   4 action, including accelerate, drifting left or right, and using its item. While this limited the agent’s
1506   potential actions substantially, we found using fewer actions to dramatically accelerate training.
1507   Rewards of +1 were given via reaching checkpoints that were scattered throughout the course (100
1508   in total per lap). Additionally, if the agent’s speed dropped below a set threshold (65 km/h), the
1509   agent would receive a reward of -0.01 per frame. The agent would be terminated with a reward of
1510   -10 if its speed dropped below the threshold for over 80 frames, or with a reward of +10 for finishing
1511   the race, with a bonus based on the position the agent finished in. Lastly, the agent was rewarded
       with a +1 for using its item. Without this reward, we found the agent to often neglect using its item,

       K.3    M ORTAL KOMBAT
1522
1523   The Mortal Kombat environment put the agent in the game’s endurance mode, where the agent
1524   would sequentially fight 15 different opponents, but keep retain its health between fights, and only
1525   gain health after defeating every 3 opponents. We provided the agent with 14 actions, including:
1526   None, Left, Right, Up, Down, Axe Kick, Punch, Snap Kick, Grab, Block, Toggle Weapon, Jump
1527   Left, Jump Right, and Crouch. These actions were far from the game’s total action space, and limited
1528   the agent’s ability to perform some of the combos within the game. We limited the agent’s actions
1529
       as the full action space is extremely large.
1530   The agent was positively rewarded for damaging the opponent, and negatively rewarded for taking
1531   damage, with one taking one tenth of the health bar equating to +1 reward respectively. The episode
