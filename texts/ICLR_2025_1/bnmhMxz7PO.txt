009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The adaptation of large language models (LLMs) to time series forecasting poses
012            unique challenges, as time series data is continuous in nature, while LLMs op-
013            erate on discrete tokens. Despite the success of LLMs in natural language
014            processing (NLP) and other structured domains, aligning time series data with
015            language-based representations while maintaining both predictive accuracy and
016            interpretability remains a significant hurdle. Existing methods have attempted
017            to reprogram time series data into text-based forms, but these often fall short in
018
               delivering meaningful, interpretable results. In this paper, we propose a multi-
               level text alignment framework for time series forecasting using LLMs that not
019
               only improves prediction accuracy but also enhances the interpretability of time
020
               series representations. Our method decomposes time series into trend, seasonal,
021            and residual components, which are then reprogrammed into component-specific
022            text representations. We introduce a multi-level alignment mechanism, where
023            component-specific embeddings are aligned with pre-trained word tokens, en-
024            abling more interpretable forecasts. Experiments on multiple datasets demonstrate
025            that our method outperforms state-of-the-art models in accuracy while providing
026            good interpretability.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Time series forecasting, which involves predicting future values based on historical data, has nu-
032   merous practical applications, such as demand planning, inventory optimization, energy load fore-
033
      casting, and climate modeling (Gao et al., 2020; Li et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023a; Dimri et al.,
      2020). Traditionally, these tasks demand substantial domain expertise and carefully designed mod-
034
      els tailored to specific datasets. However, recent advancements in pre-trained large language models
035
      (LLMs), such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) and LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023), have achieved
036   remarkable success in natural language processing (NLP) and demonstrated potential in handling
037   complex, structured domains. This raises a compelling question: how can these powerful pre-trained
038   LLMs be effectively adapted for time series forecasting?
039
      LLMs, trained on vast and diverse text corpora, provide a powerful foundation for various down-
040
      stream tasks, requiring only minimal task-specific prompt engineering or fine-tuning. This flexibility
041
      has sparked a growing interest in leveraging LLMs for time series analysis. For example, methods
042   like Promptcast (Xue & Salim, 2023) and LLMTime (Gruver et al., 2024) reformulate numerical in-
043   puts and outputs into prompts, treating time series forecasting as a sentence-to-sentence task, which
044   enables the direct application of LLMs. Meanwhile, approaches like TEMPO (Cao et al., 2024) and
045   GPT4TS (Zhou et al., 2023) take a different route by fine-tuning pre-trained LLMs, modifying com-
046   ponents such as the Add&Norm layers and positional embeddings, further demonstrating LLMs’
047   adaptability for time series forecasting.
048   Despite their potential, the benefits of LLMs in time series forecasting depend on the effective
049   alignment between time series data and natural language modalities. For instance, TEST (Sun et al.,
050   2023) developed an encoder that aligns time series data to word embedding space through instance-
051   wise, feature-wise, and text-prototype-aligned contrast. TimeLLM (Jin et al., 2024a) introduced a
052   reprogramming framework that aligns time series patches with text prototypes, while S2 IP-LLM
053   (Pan et al., 2024) employed a semantically informed prompt to bridge time series embeddings and
      semantic space. These approaches, however, primarily achieve a “time series→pattern→text” trans-

068   For example, the embedding of a subsequence with an upward trend may be misaligned with a word
069   representing a decline or with a word that doesn’t capture the trend at all. As a result, the challenge
070   remains to fully unlock LLMs’ capabilities for general time series forecasting in a way that is both
071   accurate and interpretable.
072
      In this paper, we address the challenge of interpretability in LLM-based time series forecasting
073   by developing an interpretable multi-level text alignment framework while preserving the back-
074   bone model. Our approach consists of two key principles for effective time series representation
075   learning: (a) modeling specific time series components such as trend, seasonality, and residuals,
076   and (b) deriving interpretable explanations from the inherent properties of time series data through
077   multi-level text alignment. Specifically, we decompose the time series input into three additive com-
078   ponents—trend, seasonality, and residuals—using locally weighted scatterplot smoothing (LOESS)
079   (Cleveland et al., 1990). These components are then reprogrammed into component-specific text
080   representations that better align with the language capabilities of LLMs. Additionally, we employ
081
      component-specific prompts to guide the generation of learnable continuous vector representations
      that encode temporal knowledge of each component.
082
083   In summary, the main contributions of this paper are as follows: (1) We propose an interpretable
084   multi-level text alignment framework for time series forecasting using LLMs, while keeping the
085   backbone model unchanged. (2) Our method leverages this multi-level alignment to map decom-
086   posed time series components—trend, seasonality, and residuals—into distinctive, informative joint
087
      representations. The aligned trend-specific anchors enhance the interpretability of LLMs, while
      the aligned seasonality and residual prototypes improve the overall representation of the input time
088
      series. (3) Experimental results on multiple datasets validate the superiority of our model over state-
089
      of-the-art approaches, highlighting the effectiveness of interpretable multi-level text alignment.

095
096   The recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have opened up new opportunities
097   for time series modeling. LLMs like T5 (Raffel et al., 2020), GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019), GPT-4
098   (Achiam et al., 2023), and LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023) have demonstrated impressive capabilities
099
      in understanding complex dependencies in heterogeneous textual data and generating meaningful
      outputs. Recently, there has been growing interest in exploring how to transfer the knowledge em-
100
      bedded in these pre-trained LLMs to the time series domain (Jin et al., 2024b; Jiang et al., 2024).
101
      For instance, (Xue & Salim, 2023) converts time series data into text sequences, achieving promis-
102   ing results. Other works, such as (Zhou et al., 2023; Gruver et al., 2024), tokenize time series data
103   into overlapping patches and strategically fine-tune LLMs for time series forecasting tasks. Simi-
104   larly, recent works such as (Cao et al., 2024; Pan et al., 2024) decompose time series data and use
105   retrieval-based prompts to enhance fine-tuning of pre-trained LLMs. However, these approaches
106   often fall short of delivering interpretable results and tend to treat time series as mere sequences of
107   tokens, overlooking their inherent temporal structures. Converting numerical data to text without
      sufficient alignment to temporal dynamics can lead to inaccurate predictions and a lack of trans-

136   2.2                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                         T IME S ERIES A LIGNED E MBEDDINGS
137
138   A key challenge in adapting LLMs for time series forecasting lies in aligning the continuous nature
139   of time series data with the discrete token-based embeddings used in language models. Inspired
140   by prototype-level contrast methods (Caron et al., 2020), (Sun et al., 2023) select certain text em-
141   beddings as basic prototypes to guide and constrain the learning of time series token embeddings.
142
      Similarly, (Jin et al., 2024a) reprogram time series data using the source data modality alongside
      prompts without modifying the input time series directly or fine-tuning the backbone LLM. These
143
      methods essentially follow a “time series→pattern→text” paradigm to activate LLMs for time series
144
      forecasting. However, the selection of text prototypes in these approaches is often arbitrary, and the
145   chosen prototypes may not accurately reflect the underlying characteristics of the time series data
146   (Sun et al., 2023). To address this limitation and enhance the interpretability of LLMs for time series,
147   our approach selects time series-specific anchors to guide and constrain the learning of time series
148   token embeddings. By aligning these embeddings with time series-related prototypes, we improve
149   both the interpretability and performance of LLMs for time series forecasting.

210
211   Here we reprogram patch embeddings into the LLMs’ pre-training data representation space to align
212   the modalities of time series and natural language to activate the backbone’s time series understand-
213   ing and reasoning capabilities. Naively, we can align the token embedding of time series and text
214   using similarity estimation. Although time series tokens lack text annotation, we can place their em-
215   bedding near typical text descriptions of time series. Thus, it is intuitively expected that various time
      series tokens can represent various descriptive words such as up, down, stable, and so on. However,

250   3.4   C OMPONENT- SPECIFIC PROMPTS
251
252   Prompting techniques have proven highly effective across various tasks by leveraging task-specific
253   knowledge encoded in prompts. This success stems from prompts providing a structured framework
254
      that aligns the model’s output with desired objectives, improving accuracy and coherence. However,
      directly translating time series into natural language poses challenges, complicating the creation of
255
      instruction-following datasets and effective on-the-fly prompting (Xue & Salim, 2022). Recent ad-
256
      vances show that prompts can enrich input context and guide the transformation of reprogrammed
257   time series patches (Jin et al., 2024a). To leverage the semantic information in time series compo-
258   nents, we propose a component-specific prefix prompting strategy. This includes three elements:
259   dataset context, input statistical features, and component-specific task instructions for trend, sea-
260   sonal, and residual components. For instance, the task description ’forecast the next 96 steps given
261   the previous 512 steps [trend, seasonal, residual]’ serves as a template for our task instructions,
262   which are then concatenated with the corresponding component data.

      5   C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
476
477
      We propose a multi-level text alignment framework for time series forecasting utilizing pre-trained
478
      language models. Our multi-level aligned embeddings enhance the LLM’s interpretability and fore-
479
      casting performance by aligning time series components with anchors and text prototypes. Due to
480   the impractical interpretability of the text prototypes aligned with the seasonal and residual com-
481   ponents of the time series, and the sparsity of text prototypes describing the time series trend, we
482   align the time series trend with selected anchors from the pre-trained word embeddings. Our results
483   demonstrate that time series tokens aligned with anchors provide a clearer and more intuitive in-
484   terpretation of similar time series trends. Future research should focus on optimizing the alignment
485   module for selected anchors and time series tokens, and work toward developing multimodal models
      capable of joint reasoning across time series, natural language, and other modalities.
