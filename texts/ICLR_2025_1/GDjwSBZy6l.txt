009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011           Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) has emerged as a prominent technique for fine-
012           tuning large language models (LLMs) with limited computational resources.
013           However, by injecting low-rank adapters with a rank identical across all layers,
014           standard LoRA overlooks the varying importance of the weight matrices, often
015           leading to suboptimal performance. Therefore, discovering an optimal rank con-
016           figuration that efficiently utilizes limited training resources remains an open ques-
017           tion. Existing solutions typically compromises computational constraints for per-
018           formance gains, limiting their practical usage in resource-constrained scenarios.
019
              To address these issues, in this paper, we propose a novel method named ROLoRA
              to efficiently discover an effective rank configuration for low-rank adaptation,
020
              while strictly adhering to a constrained computational budget during training. In
021
              particular, our method iteratively prunes saturated adapters and expands under-
022           fitted ones to increase their capacity until they converge to a highly optimized
023           configuration. Our approach is delicately designed within the Frank-Wolfe algo-
024           rithmic framework, which offers potential theoretical guarantees. Experimentally,
025           we demonstrate that ROLoRA outperforms standard LoRA on common natural
026           language processing tasks, including the GLUE and SQuAD benchmarks. Addi-
027           tionally, we provide a comprehensive analysis to explain why ROLoRA surpasses
028           competing state-of-the-arts.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
      Fine-tuning pre-trained large language models (LLMs) for downstream tasks has become a common
033
      practice to meet customized and domain-specific demands, especially after the recent prevalence
034
      of these models. However, the computational cost during training remains a significant concern,
035   particularly due to the increasing number of parameters in these models. Naively fine-tuning the
036   entire models often requires as much computational capacity as training the model from scratch,
037   which limits the feasibility of fine-tuning LLMs on resource-constrained edge devices.
038
      To fully exploit the advantages of model fine-tuning, recent works have departed from full fine-
039
      tuning towards more light-weighted approaches, also known as Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning
040
      methods (or PEFT) (Houlsby et al., 2019; Karimi Mahabadi et al., 2021; Mao et al., 2022). PEFT
041   methods aim at reducing the number of trainable parameters during the fine-tuning process, which
042   brings about simultaneously two benefits. First, PEFT methods reduce the memory required to store
043   trained parameters, enabling the deployment of fine-tuned models for multiple tasks on the same
044   device. Second, the reduced number of trainable parameters typically lowers the optimization cost
045   during training, particularly by minimizing the memory required to store optimizer’s states, which
046   facilitates on-device fine-tuning for these models.
047   Among many recently developed PEFT methods, Low-Rank Adaptation, or LoRA, proposed by Hu
048   et al. (2022) is among the most important. In principle, LoRA freezes the base pre-trained model
049   and attach updates to each weight matrix of the base model as separate modules, known as adapters,
050   in form of a product of two matrices of significantly smaller size. Concretely,
051

055   number of trainable parameters to possibly only 0.5% compared with full fine-tuning while achieves

058
      As simple and efficient as it may seem, LoRA still has its limitations. In particular, it is unclear how
      to optimally choose the rank r for each adapter in LoRA. The standard LoRA sets an identical rank
059
      r for all adapters. This strategy ignores the fact that different layers in the model carry different
060
      amount of knowledge (Chen et al., 2023a) and thus require different level of tuning. In a recent
061   work, Zhang et al. (2023) demonstrate that varying the ranks r according to the importance of the
062   weight matrices can improve the performance of LoRA-style methods. However, existing solutions
063   often require a greater memory budget during training to provide more buffer to explore a high-
064   performing adapter configuration. This is especially undesirable for fine-tuning the model on small
065   devices where the memory allowed during training is strictly limited.
066
      In this work, we study the problem of determining the ranks of the adapters that can improve the
067   performance of LoRA while imposing a strict memory constraint during training, reflected by a con-
068   straint on the number of trainable parameters. Towards answering this question, our contributions
069   can be summarized as follows.
070
      We propose a general framework, named ROLoRA, that iteratively sparsifies and grows the ranks
071
      of the adapters. Specifically, at each iteration, our algorithm performs a flexible rank sparsification
072
      step to determine the importance of the adapted weight matrices. Our framework allows to use any
073   rank sparsification algorithm as a blackbox. Next, we expand the rank configuration to increase
074   the capacity of the adapters, while strictly adhering to memory budget, before updating the ranks
075   following the direction suggested this configuration. Our approach aims to imitate a first order
076   gradient method (specifically Frank-Wolfe algorithm), which is search-free and highly efficient.
077
      To demonstrate its effectiveness, we show in experiment that our algorithm improves the perfor-
078
      mance of LoRA on common NLP benchmarks, including GLUE (Wang et al., 2019) and SQUAD
079   datasets (Rajpurkar et al., 2016; 2018). We show that our algorithm even outperforms AdaLoRA
080   which requires more memory during training. We show at the same time that the output adapters
081   can be smaller in size, reducing the memory storage compared with the standard LoRA.
082
      Finally, to explain the effectiveness of our method, we conduct a comprehensive ablation study. We
083
      demonstrate that a certain type of weight matrices in the transformer architecture, such as value
084
      matrices, plays a more important role than the others (key and query matrices) in fine-tuning. Mean-
085   while, our approaches could effectively discover the varying saliency pattern and assign proper ranks
086   accordingly. These advantages contribute to the superior performance we achieved.

      Most related to our work is DyLoRA (Valipour et al., 2023), AdaLoRA Zhang et al. (2023) and
109   SoRA (Ding et al., 2023). DyLoRA focuses on determining an optimal constant for setting the rank
110   of all adapters in LoRA. This is in contrast to our work which aims at finding ranks for adapters that
111   can vary across layers when given a budget on the number of trainable parameters. AdaLoRA and
112   SoRA use different techniques to reduce (sparsify) the ranks initialized by LoRA, which in effect,
113   gives us a rank configuration that reflects the importances of the adapted weight matrices. However,
114   both methods violate the budget constraint when starting with a rank higher than the one initialized
115   by LoRA. Our solution to the problem uses a sparsification method such as AdaLoRA and SoRA as
116   a subroutine in an iterative framework, while strictly guarantees the budget constraint is satisfied.

118   3     BACKGROUND AND P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
119
120   3.1   L OW-R ANK A DAPTATION
121
122   Low-rank adaptation, or LoRA, was introduced by Hu et al. (2022), as a Parameter-Efficient Fine-
123   Tuning method, based on the assumption that the incremental update to the pretrained model has
124
      a low intrinsic rank. Specifically, given the pretrained weight W0 ∈ Rn×d , LoRA considers only
      updates in the form of a product of two small matrices ∆W = BA, where B ∈ Rn×r and A ∈ Rr×d
125
      with the rank r ≪ min{n, d}. We will refer to r as the rank of the adapter, although r is only an
126
      upper bound on the rank of A or B. For h = W0 x, the forward pass becomes
127
128                                    h = (W0 + ∆W )x = W0 x + BAx.
129   In this way, the number of trainable parameters is significantly reduced from nd to r(n + d). For ex-
130   ample, when the model is trained with commonly used adaptive optimizers such as Adam (Kingma,
131   2014), the memory required to store the optimizer’s states, e.g., momentums, is proportional to the
132   number of trainable parameters. By reducing the number of trainable parameters, LoRA saves on
133   the memory required to fine-tune the model.
134
      In this work, we study a more generalized variation of LoRA by introducing a diagonal matrix to the
135   update process. Let g ∈ Rr be an r-dimensional vector and diag(g) ∈ Rr×r be a diagonal matrix
136   formed by g. We consider an update of the form ∆W = B diag(g) A. Remark here that when g is
137   set to 1, this variant becomes numerically equivalent to the standard LoRA. When g is made train-
138   able, the number of trainable parameters increases marginally by r, which is negligible compared to
139   the total number of trainable variables in LoRA, i.e., r(n+d). This generic adjustment offers greater
140   flexibility during training and can often lead to a more favorable optimization landscape, potentially
141   resulting in improved performance.

      In this section, we describe our algorithm ROLoRA to solve Problem (4) shown in Algorithm 1.
232
      ROLoRA is an iterative method to automatically discover an effective rank configuration. After
233
      proper initialization, ROLoRA begins with a given adapter rank configuration, then iteratively identi-
234   fies saturated and underfitting weights. It adjusts the adapter rank assignment by performing pruning
235   and growth operations, and is delicately structured within the Frank-Wolfe algorithmic framework
236   to leverage potential theoretical advantages. Each main step is elaborated as follows, which is also
237   demonstrated in Figure 1.
238
      Sparsifying / pruning the ranks (Step (a) in Figure 1). Given a rank configuration R =
239
      (r1 , . . . , rK ), certain weight matrices may become saturated during further fine-tuning, meaning
240   they might not require as many trainable parameters. To address this, we introduce a sparsification
241   or pruning operator to identify redundant ranks across the adapter sets. In Algorithm 1, we flexi-
242   bly employ any rank sparsification algorithm for the S PARSIFY operator, such as AdaLoRA (Zhang
243   et al., 2023) and SoRA (Ding et al., 2023) or pruning approaches based on saliency scores (Chen
244   et al., 2023b; 2024). Without loss of generality, we assume that S PARSIFY takes in a pruning aggres-
245   siveness hyper-parameter µ to produce a new trial rank configuration S = (s1 , · · · , sK ). S reflects
246   more accurately than R the (relative) importance of the corresponding weight matrices (Line 5).
247   Reassign and grow the ranks (Step (b) in Figure 1). In addition to certain adapters requiring fewer
248   ranks due to saturation, some adapters may need their capacity increased instead to learn more
249   effectively. Therefore, it is essential to identify which adapters should grow to meet this demand.
250   To achieve this, we use the signals from the pruned rank configuration. Typically, the ranks that
251   remain unchanged in S indicate that these adapters require more parameters, as no redundancy is
252   detected. However, because we operate under a strict memory budget, the amount of growth must
253   strictly adhere to this constraint. Taking these factors into account, we scale up S to align with the
254
      target average rank R (Line 6).
255   Intuitive connection to gradient estimation to f . The sparsification and growth steps in our algorithm
256   effectively identify adapters that require more capacity or exhibit redundancy. Consequently, it
257   builds on the intuition that the new rank configuration output S suggests a favorable search direction
258   that can enhance the performance measure f (R) of the current rank configuration R.
259   Update the ranks and adapters (Step (c-d) in Figure 1). Once obtaining the new trial configuration
260   S, we update the current configuration R by moving it towards S, i.e., taking an interpolation
261   step with step size γ (Step (c)). This step is a reminiscence of the update step in the Frank-Wolfe
262   algorithm (Frank et al., 1956) for solving constrained optimization problems. To update the adapter
263   weights, we can carry over the trained adapters obtained from the S PARSIFY algorithm (Step (d)).
264   Since the some of trial ranks in S are increased during the rank growth step, additional rows and
265   columns will be added to the up and down projection matrices, as well as additional entries to the
266
      diagonal matrix, requiring only the initialization of these extended parts. These extended parts are
      initialized in the same way as LoRA adapters so that their product is 0 to ensure computational
267
      invariance after the adapter growth.
268
269   Scheduling the sparsification. After each sparsification-growth-update step, we update the sparsi-
      fication parameter of the S PARSIFY operator via S CHEDULE. One approach is to keep the sparsifi-

271   reduces the pruning aggressiveness over time. Intuitively, as the model converges to a better rank
272   configuration over time, it requires fewer adjustments to determine an update direction.

277   In this section, we present the outline for the convergence analysis of ROLoRA to solve the target
278
      problem (4). Towards this end, we first show that under certain assumptions on the S PARSIFY
      operator, Algorithm 1 will improve the performance measure f over iterations.
279
280   Assumption 1. The S PARSIFY(R; θ; µ) operator has the following properties:
                                                                                P 1. During the course
281   of the algorithm, S PARSIFY satisfies the memory budget, indicated by i ri . 2. For any rank
282   configuration R and initial weight θ, there exists a sparsification parameter µ such that if S, θ ←
283
      S PARSIFY(R; θ; µ) then f (S) ≤ f (R).
284
      We interpret this assumption as follows. As a rank configuration can contain redundant information,
285   the S PARSIFY operator with a well chosen sparsification parameter can remove this redundancy
286   without sacrificing the model performance. In the trivial case, we can choose µ so that no sparsifica-
287   tion happens, thus the loss function is not increased (yet in which case the algorithm will terminate
288   without updating the rank configuration). In practice, if the model has saturated weights, keeping
289   fine-tuning these weights can easily result in overfitting problem, which further degrades the model
290   performance. Hence, yielding proper sparsity over the adapters can promote the model performance
291   instead, i.e., f (S) ≤ f (R).
292   We then show the following Proposition.
293
294

295
      after iteration t. For all t ≥ 1, f (R(t) ) ≤ f (R(0) ).

      Connection to Frank-Wolfe algorithm and Convergence rate. We now show the connection
303
      between Algorithm 1 and Frank-Wolfe algorithm Frank et al. (1956). To minimize a function f over
304
      a convex domain X , in iteration t, Frank-Wolfe algorithm finds a solution in s ∈ X that minimizes
305
      ⟨s, ∇f (x(t) )⟩, with x(t) being the current solution, and update x(t+1) ← (1 − γ)x(t) + γs with some
306   step size γ. Jaggi (2013) shows that Frank-Wolfe with appropriate step sizes converges with rate
307   O( T1 ) for convex smooth functions; later Lacoste-Julien (2016) shows a O( √1T ) convergence rate of
308   the duality gap for nonconvex smooth functions. Our algorithm is driven by this Frank-Wolfe type
309   of update. Our problem (4) while being a discrete problem can be relaxed to a continuous convex
310   domain1 . Although we do not have access to the exact gradient ∇f (R), we approximate the update
311   direction s using the dedicated designed joint sparsification and growth step as the proxy described in
312   Section 4. Consequently, we could incorporate with the existing theoretical framework of Lacoste-
313   Julien (2016) to indicate that within a finite number of iterations, the proposed ROLoRA could find
314   a high-performing rank configuration, which is further numerically demonstrated in Section 6.

465   6.3   A BLATION S TUDY
466
      We conduct an in-depth study to show why ROLoRA outperforms LoRA and AdaLoRA.
467
468   To understand how ROLoRA assigns ranks, we plot the rank distribution of the output adapters for
469   DeBERTa-v3-base on CoLA and QNLI datasets in Figure 2(a) (same experimental results in Table
470   2). We compare these distributions with the configurations discovered by AdaLoRA in Figure 2(b),
471   which also serves as the base S PARSIFY operator in our algorithm. We observe the following:
472   (1) ROLoRA finds a configuration that allows ranks to go beyond the upper limit set by AdaLoRA.
473   In particular, in the plot for CoLA in Figure 2(a), some adapters have ranks higher than the initial
474   rank 12 set by AdaLoRA in the same experiment in Figure 2(b). This means, ROLoRA can go be-
475   yond the search space set by a vanilla sparsification algorithm, and thereby achieves improvements.
476   (2) ROLoRA focuses significantly on the value matrices. The overall ranks assigned to the
477   value matrices are significantly higher than those to the key and query matrices. Compared with
478   AdaLoRA, which shows a similar pattern, ROLoRA further accentuates this difference.
479
      We further investigate this latter point to see the role each type of matrices plays in fine-tuning.
480
481   In Figure 3 we plot the gradient norm of the pretrained DeBERTA-v3-base model by making one
482   backward pass over the corresponding datasets (CoLA and QNLI). We can see that in each layer,
483   the value matrices have much larger gradient norms than the others. Intuitively, this implies that
484   these matrices are much more sensitive and thus require higher level of tuning. Therefore, one could
485
      expect that the value matrices play a more important role in fine-tuning and by assigning higher ranks
      to the adapters for them relatively to the other matrices, we could achieve a better performance.

534     7                  F UTURE WORK AND C ONCLUSION
535
536    In this work, we propose a novel iterative algorithm that optimizes rank configurations to enhance
537    the performance of the popular fine-tuning algorithm LoRA. Our approach leverages a new insight
538    that value matrices in the transformer architecture are more critical for fine-tuning on downstream
539    tasks than key and value matrices. We leave further investigation on the applications of the our
       method and its theoretical convergence guarantee to future work.
