011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            During the pretraining phase, large language models (LLMs) acquire vast amounts
014            of knowledge from extensive text corpora. Nevertheless, in later stages such as
015
               fine-tuning and inference, the model may encounter knowledge not covered in
               the initial training, which can lead to hallucinations and degraded performance.
016
               This issue has a profound impact on the model’s capabilities, as it will inevitably
017
               face out-of-scope knowledge after pretraining. Furthermore, fine-tuning is of-
018            ten required to adapt LLMs to domain-specific tasks, necessitating the acqui-
019            sition of new knowledge. However, this phenomenon limits the model’s abil-
020            ity to learn and integrate new information during fine-tuning. The effectiveness
021            of fine-tuning largely depends on the type of knowledge involved. Existing re-
022            search suggests that fine-tuning the model on partially mastered knowledge—for
023            instance, question-answer pairs where the model has a chance of providing cor-
024            rect responses under non-greedy decoding—can enable the model to acquire new
025            knowledge while mitigating the forgetting of previously learned information. No-
026
               tably, this approach can still lead to the forgetting of fully mastered knowledge,
               constraining the fine-tuning dataset to a narrower range and limiting the model’s
027
               overall potential for improvement. Given the model’s intrinsic reasoning abilities
028
               and the interconnectedness of different knowledge areas, it is likely that as the
029            model’s capacity to utilize existing knowledge improves during fine-tuning, pre-
030            viously unmastered knowledge may become more understandable. To explore this
031            hypothesis, we conducted experiments and, based on the results, proposed a two-
032            stage fine-tuning strategy. This approach not only improves the model’s overall
033            test accuracy and knowledge retention but also preserves its accuracy on previ-
034            ously mastered content. When fine-tuning on the WikiQA dataset, our method
035            increases the amount of knowledge acquired by the model in this stage by 24%.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      In recent years, large language models (LLMs) have advanced rapidly, demonstrating exceptional
040
      performance across a variety of tasksDevlin et al. (2019); Achiam et al. (2023); Dubey et al. (2024);
041
      Yang et al. (2024). These models are pretrained on vast amounts of text data, allowing them to
042   encode substantial knowledge within their parametersPetroni et al. (2019); Roberts et al. (2020);
043   AlKhamissi et al. (2022). Following pretraining, LLMs undergo supervised fine-tuning to improve
044   their ability to follow instructionsZhao et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023) and tackle domain-specific
045   tasksHe et al. (2023); Cui et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023).
046
      However, unlike the pretraining phase, LLMs face challenges when encountering new knowledge
047
      during other stages, including fine-tuning and inference. When fine-tuning data includes knowl-
048   edge unrelated to the data seen during the pretraining phase, LLMs struggle to learn this new in-
049   formation, making them prone to overfitting and generating hallucinations.Gekhman et al. (2024);
050   Gudibande et al. (2023). Additionally, even when external knowledge is integrated into prompts
051   during inference, such as through KG-enhanced LLMsPan et al. (2024) or Retrieval-Augmented
052   Generation (RAG)Zhao et al. (2023), these models often struggle to manage unfamiliar knowledge
053   effectivelyKang et al. (2024); Lee et al. (2023); Yin et al. (2023). These characteristics impose the
      following constraints on the design of LLMs:


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
             • Constraint 1: During the fine-tuning stage, the knowledge in the data should be associated
055            with the knowledge from the pretraining phase to mitigate overfitting and reduce halluci-
056            nations.
057
             • Constraint 2: During the inference stage, if external knowledge is to be provided in
058            prompts, it must be related to the knowledge the model has already learned.
059
060   In this data-driven eraBai et al. (2024), these constraints fundamentally limit the data utilization dur-
061   ing fine-tuning and inference, thereby significantly restricting the capabilities of LLMs. To mitigate
062   the impact of these constraints on model performance, we propose a novel two-stage fine-tuning
063
      method based on the interconnections between knowledge and the model’s reasoning ability. This
      approach aims to broaden the pool of data suitable for training during the fine-tuning stage.
064
065   In comparison to smaller models, LLMs exhibit surprising emergent abilities, such as reasoningWei
066   et al. (2022). For instance, we can harness the knowledge reasoning capability of LLMs by utilizing
067   KG to enhance the inputs of these models, thereby improving their performancePan et al. (2024).
068   Additionally, the knowledge within these models is not isolated but rather interconnectedJi et al.
069
      (2021). Based on these observations, we hypothesize that if an LLM acquires knowledge that was
      not fully mastered—knowledge the model is likely to answer correctly without fine-tuning, dur-
070
      ing self-supervised fine-tuning, it could potentially leverage its existing knowledge to comprehend
071
      additional concepts that were neither previously mastered nor present in the training dataset. To
072   test this hypothesis, we conducted experiments using mainstream LLMs on a closed-book question-
073   answering task and validated our conjecture. Building on these findings, we designed a two-stage
074   fine-tuning framework: in the first stage, the model is fine-tuned using knowledge that it partially
075   understands; in the second stage, we monitor changes in the types of knowledge within the train-
076   ing set and use the data with improved mastery as augmented training data for a second stage of
077   fine-tuning. The experimental results illustrate that our approach substantially improves the model’s
078   accuracy on test data and its mastery of knowledge within the training dataset.
079   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
080
081          • We propose that fine-tuning can enhance a LLM’s mastery of knowledge that it previously
082            did not fully comprehend and that was not present in the original training dataset. We
083            conducted experiments to validate this hypothesis.
084          • Building on this, we utilized the data with improved mastery to augment the training dataset
085            and performed a subsequent round of fine-tuning on the LLM, which can significantly
086            enhance knowledge acquisition and lead to increased accuracy on the test set. This method
087
               broadens the range of data available for fine-tuning LLMs, offering a new perspective from
               a data-driven standpoint to enhance the performance.

091
092   2.1   I MPACT OF N EW K NOWLEDGE ON H ALLUCINATION
093
      LLMs can acquire substantial knowledge during the pretraining phase, enabling them to function
094
      as knowledge bases for downstream tasksAlKhamissi et al. (2022). However, these models are
095
      prone to a phenomenon known as hallucination, where the generated content, while syntactically
096   correct and seemingly coherent, conflicts with contextual information or real-world knowledge. The
097   causes of hallucinations are varied, including the use of low-quality datasets and suboptimal data
098   utilizationHuang et al. (2023).
099
      It is widely argued that LLMs primarily acquire knowledge during the pretraining phase, while pro-
100
      cesses such as supervised fine-tuning and in-context learning are more focused on the extraction and
101
      application of existing knowledge, with limited capacity for learning new knowledge. Gudibande et
102   al.Gudibande et al. (2023) found that during supervised fine-tuning, LLMs predominantly learn the
103   stylistic aspects of the fine-tuning dataset rather than factual knowledge. This can lead the model
104   to confidently generate content that is not factually accurate. Research by Kang et al.Kang et al.
105   (2024) suggests that unfamiliar queries can trigger hallucinations in LLMs and that fine-tuning the
106   model on data not covered during pretraining can influence the type of hallucinations produced.
107   Experiments by Yin et al.Yin et al. (2023) and Lee et al.Lee et al. (2023) indicate that using unfa-
      miliar knowledge as a template in in-context learning can degrade the performance of LLMs in such


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      tasks. Gekhman et al.Gekhman et al. (2024) categorized knowledge into four distinct types based
109   on the model’s probability of correctly answering corresponding questions under different decoding
110   strategies. Their experiments unveiled that fine-tuning the model on knowledge that the model has
111   not yet mastered-knowledge that the model cannot correctly answer under a greedy decoding strat-
112   egy—results in overfitting and notably diminishes the model’s accuracy in responding to questions
113   related to mastered knowledge—knowledge that the model can correctly answer under a greedy
114   decoding strategy. Their research indicates that fine-tuning on knowledge that the model is likely
115   to answer correctly enhances test set accuracy, mitigates overfitting, and sustains performance on
116   previously acquired knowledge, as opposed to fine-tuning on all data or a specific type of data. Our
117
      methods followed the knowledge classification proposed by Gekhman et al.
118   Overall, encountering new knowledge after the pretraining phase poses a significant challenge for
119   LLMs, making it difficult both to learn this knowledge and to utilize it effectively during fine-tuning
120   and inference.
121
122   2.2   Q UANTIFYING LLM K NOWLEDGE M ASTERY
123
124   Assessing the degree to which LLMs have mastered specific knowledge is crucial for both under-
125   standing the internal mechanisms of these models and constructing better ones. In scenarios where
126   the ground truth is known, such as in closed-book question-answering tasks, the model’s knowledge
127
      can be evaluated by generating multiple responses and measuring accuracyKadavath et al. (2022);
      Gekhman et al. (2024). However, in cases where ground truth is unavailable, such as in real-world
128
      applications of LLMs, alternative evaluation methods are required. The confidence score is often
129
      used to assess a model’s certainty in its specific outputs (such as words, phrases, or sentences) and
130   can serve as an indicator of the model’s knowledge mastery. Ideally, a confident score should be
131   well-calibrated, meaning it can accurately reflect the probability that an answer is correct in the real
132   world. Nevertheless, since LLMs are often trained with Reinforcement Learning from Human Feed-
133   back (RLHF)Ouyang et al. (2022), they may confidently produce incorrect information, leading to
134   conditional probabilities that are not well-calibrated. Researchers have proposed various methods
135   to address this issue, such as calculating the degree of agreement among different generated out-
136   putsLyu et al. (2024), training models to compute confidence scores based on activation statesAzaria
137   & Mitchell (2023), or directly querying the LLM about its confidence in its knowledgeTian et al.
138
      (2023). Notably, Kadavath et al.Kadavath et al. (2022) pointed out that the accuracy of repeated
      responses is a well-calibrated metric for assessing the model’s knowledge, which is also the method
139
      we adopted to gauge the extent of knowledge mastery.

      2.3   C ONTINUE F INE - TUNING
143
      Our approach involves the issue of continual fine-tuning, where after fine-tuning the model, further
144
      fine-tuning is conducted using new data. Continual learning is crucial for models that need to adapt
145   over time, learning from a continuous stream of data without forgetting previously acquired knowl-
146   edge. However, one of the most significant challenges in this area is the phenomenon known as
147   catastrophic forgetting. This occurs when a model, after being exposed to new data, experiences a
148   significant degradation in performance on tasks it had previously learned and mastered. As the model
149   adjusts its parameters to accommodate new information, it inadvertently overwrites the information
150   and skills it had previously acquired, leading to a loss of accuracy and effectiveness in those earlier
151   tasks. To mitigate this issue, researchers have proposed various methods, broadly categorized into
152   four typesZheng et al. (2024): Replay-BasedShin et al. (2017); Ren et al. (2024), Regularization-
153
      BasedMi et al. (2020), Gradient-BasedLee et al. (2021), and Architecture-BasedGeng et al. (2021)
      approaches. In our experiment, we employed basic experience replay, appropriately lowered the
154
      initial learning rate, and set weight decay parameters to prevent overfitting to the new data.

180   3.1         D ETECT K NOWLEDGE T YPE C HANGE
181
182   Knowledge is interconnected, and it is possible to infer new knowledge from existing information.
183   We propose that during the fine-tuning process, the model may acquire knowledge that it originally
184   did not possess and that is not present in the training dataset for the following reasons:
185
186                • Some knowledge not included in the training dataset and initially unknown to the model
187
                     can be inferred from the knowledge that is present within the training dataset.
188                • Even if certain knowledge in the training dataset cannot directly infer other knowledge, it
189                  can be combined with information acquired by the model during the pretraining phase to
190                  infer knowledge that is absent from the training dataset and was originally unknown to the
191                  model.
192
193   To validate our hypothesis, we first classified the knowledge into four categories—‘Highly Known’,
194   ‘Maybe Known’, ‘Weakly Known’, and ‘Unknown’—based on the work of Gekhman et al.Gekhman
195   et al. (2024), with the specific criteria outlined in Table 1. We then fine-tuned the model using data
196   classified as ‘Maybe Known’. At the end of each epoch, we evaluated the model’s accuracy on the
197
      test set. Once the accuracy reached its maximum, we re-evaluated the knowledge types.
198
199   3.2         T WO - STAGE F INE - TUNING
200
201   Based on the experimental results presented in Section 3.1, which will be detailed in Section 4.1,
202   we observed that following fine-tuning, a significant portion of knowledge showed improved mas-
203
      tery, while some knowledge exhibited a decline in mastery. Building on these observations, we
      proposed a two-stage fine-tuning strategy. In the second stage of fine-tuning, we used data catego-
204
      rized as ‘Maybe Known’ at the end of the first stage, which includes data originally classified as
205
      ‘Weakly Known’ and ‘Unknown’ that were excluded from the initial training dataset. We continued
206   fine-tuning with this dataset while implementing a replay strategy with a 0.2 replay ratio for data
207   categorized as ‘Highly Known’ after fine-tuning. Specifically, at the start of each epoch, we sam-
208   pled a portion of the ‘Highly Known’ data according to the replay ratio, mixed it with the rest of the
209   training data, and shuffled it for training. This strategy not only expands the training data but also
210   helps prevent the forgetting of knowledge that the model has already mastered.

      4.1.1    E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
241
242   At this stage, we broadly followed the experimental methodology of Gekhman et al. We conducted
243   a closed-book question-answering task using the WikiQA datasetYang et al. (2015). The only ad-
244   justment we made to the dataset was to train on question-answer pairs with a single answer. During
245   testing, for questions with multiple answers, we recorded a response as correct if the model accu-
246   rately generated the first answer. For each data point in the training set, we randomly selected four
247   other questions of the same type to create a prompt template and performed multiple generations
248   to assess accuracy. The generation parameters were consistent with those outlined in Table 1. To
249   account for cases where the model generated extra content, we considered an answer correct if the
250
      correct response appeared as a substring in the model’s output. We used VLLMKwon et al. (2023)
      to accelerate inference when determining the types of knowledge.
251
252   During fine-tuning, we employed LoRAHu et al. (2021) with a rank setting of 64. We used the
253   AdamW optimizer, a cosine scheduler, and set the initial learning rate to 3e-4. All training was
254   conducted on four A100 GPUs, with inference performed on a single A100 GPU, each with 80GB
255   of memory. The batch size was set to 32.
256   We primarily used the Qwen2 modelYang et al. (2024), a leading open-source model, for our ex-
257   periments. When the model name is not explicitly mentioned, the model being used is Qwen2-7B.
258   To further validate our conclusions, we also conducted trials using the LLaMA3 modelDubey et al.
259   (2024).

      the first stage as well as knowledge classified as ‘Highly Known’, we aimed to determine whether

518   5   D ISCUSSION
519
520   We proposed that fine-tuning can alter the classification of knowledge that was not directly involved
521   in the fine-tuning process, and we conducted experiments to validate this hypothesis. Based on
522   this insight, we refined the fine-tuning process, which led to improvements in both model accuracy
523   on the test set and the model’s mastery of the knowledge in the fine-tuned dataset. However, our
524
      experiments remain largely qualitative, focusing primarily on demonstrating the feasibility of this
      approach.
525
526   Compared to the specific domain datasets that might be used for fine-tuning large language models
527   in real-world applications, the WikiQA dataset we used is relatively loosely structured. This dataset
528   consists of simple question-answer pairs with a limited set of question formats, and the questions
529   lack the strong internal coherence typically found in domain-specific datasets. In such special-
530
      ized datasets, data is often more focused within a particular domain, where the tighter connections
      between knowledge might lead to more significant changes in knowledge classification during fine-
531
      tuning.
532
533   Moreover, for knowledge that falls outside the scope of closed-book question-answering tasks, de-
534   termining whether the knowledge is present in the model is more challenging. These differences
535   indicate that our approach has broader potential applications but also faces new challenges.
536   Furthermore, when conducting continual learning, effectively addressing catastrophic forgetting re-
537   quires a balanced approach between new and old data. Better application of continual fine-tuning
538   methods is necessary to achieve this balance. In our experiments, we only utilized basic data replay
539   and moderately reduced the learning rate, indicating that there is still significant room for improve-
      ment.
