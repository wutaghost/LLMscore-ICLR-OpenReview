009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities
012            across textual and visual domains but often generate outputs that violate physical
013            laws, revealing a gap in their understanding of the physical world. Inspired by hu-
014            man cognition—where perception is fundamental to reasoning—we explore aug-
015            menting LLMs with enhanced perception abilities using Internet of Things (IoT)
016
               sensor data and pertinent knowledge for IoT task reasoning in the physical world.
               In this work, we systematically study LLMs’ capability to address real-world IoT
017
               tasks by augmenting their perception and knowledge base, and then propose a uni-
018
               fied framework, IoT-LLM, to enhance such capability. In IoT-LLM, we customize
019            three steps for LLMs: preprocessing IoT data into formats amenable to LLMs, ac-
020            tivating their commonsense knowledge through chain-of-thought prompting and
021            specialized role definitions, and expanding their understanding via IoT-oriented
022            retrieval-augmented generation based on in-context learning. To evaluate the per-
023            formance, We design a new benchmark with five real-world IoT tasks with dif-
024            ferent data types and reasoning difficulties and provide the benchmarking results
025            on six open-source and close-source LLMs. Experimental results demonstrate the
026            limitations of existing LLMs with naive textual inputs that cannot perform these
027
               tasks effectively. We show that IoT-LLM significantly enhances the performance
               of IoT tasks reasoning of LLM, such as GPT-4, achieving an average improvement
028
               of 65% across various tasks against previous methods. The results also showcase
029
               LLMs’ ability to comprehend IoT data and the physical law behind data by pro-
030            viding a reasoning process. Limitations of our work are claimed to inspire future
031            research in this new era.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Recent advancements in large generative models have showcased their exceptional performance and
037   versatility in handling complex tasks across textual and visual domains, as evidenced by the GPT
038   series (Radford et al., 2018; 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023) and
039
      visual generation models (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021; Ho et al., 2020; Peebles &
      Xie, 2023; Blattmann et al., 2023). However, these models could occasionally generate outputs
040
      that are physically implausible, often referred to as “hallucinations” (Alkaissi & McFarlane, 2023;
041
      Huang et al., 2023). Even advanced video generation models, e.g., Sora (Brooks et al., 2024), are
042   susceptible to producing animations that contravene fundamental physical laws, such as a video
043   clip containing a tipping water glass that appears to defy gravity. These observations suggest that
044   generative models may not really comprehend and apply physical laws of the physical world as
045   accurately as humans when acting as world simulators. This has renewed interest in research on
046   the World Model that focuses on understanding and modeling the physical world in a brain-like
047   manner (Dawid & LeCun, 2023; Garrido et al., 2024; Mendonca et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024).
048   Unlike Large Language Models (LLMs) that map descriptions of the physical world to a latent
049   space and perform reasoning by predicting the text sequence according to the probability, research
050   on human cognitive science illustrates a different mechanism. The human brain comprises multiple
051   mutually-functional areas, of which the important components include the temporal and occipital
052   lobes for perception, and the frontal cortex for reasoning (Churchland & Sejnowski, 1988; Saxe
053   et al., 2009; Hobeika et al., 2016; Grèzes et al., 2001). Notably, perception is the primary mecha-
      nism through which information about the physical world is acquired, and then effective reasoning

084   We draw inspiration from how humans understand the physical world: perception to acquire in-
085   formation and reasoning with relevant domain knowledge. Firstly, humans perceive the world via
086   a multitude of sensory organs, such as eyes for sight and ears for hearing. To empower machine
087
      perception, Internet of Things (IoT) sensors are developed. Since the first IoT sensor was designed
      for Coke machines to count the number of bottles in the 1980s (Madakam et al., 2015), IoT sensors
088
      become the “sensory organs” of machines, modeling the physical world for machine automation.
089
      Secondly, humans understand the world via the perception data with domain knowledge gained
090   from experience and education. Similarly, LLMs can learn domain knowledge of both the physical
091   world and sensors from the context to have stronger reasoning capabilities by in-context learning.
092   In this manner, as shown in Fig. 1, we believe perception data with pertinent knowledge can enable
093   LLMs to address complex problems with IoT-enabled perception in the real world. In this work, we
094   aim to explore the following questions: (1) What types of real-world tasks can LLMs perform via
095   the IoT perception of the physical world? (2) How can we enhance the LLM capability to deal with
096   real-world tasks? (3) Do LLMs truly understand perception data and apply knowledge to realize
097   real-world tasks?
098   Previous studies have primarily shown the viability of using LLMs for IoT task reasoning Xu et al.
099   (2024b); Ji et al. (2024), but we find that these studies are not carefully scrutinized. (1) These studies
100   only focus on specific tasks, such as R-peak identification and action recognition. The choices of
101   tasks are not comprehensive, and thus they lack a benchmark to evaluate the performances of the
102   methods. (2) They directly input raw IoT data into LLMs for reasoning, but LLMs are not good at
103   dense numerical data and calculation (Zhou et al., 2024; Gruver et al., 2024). (3) They only evaluate
104
      their effectiveness on close-source LLMs, and lack a comprehensive study of benchmarking open-
      source LLMs with different parameter size.
105
106   To bridge this gap and answer the questions we proposed, we conduct an in-depth investigation of
107   how to utilize LLMs to perform various tasks in the physical world using IoT data. Firstly, we
      explore whether LLMs can solve IoT classification and regression problems by setting a new bench-

136   2   R ELATED WORK
137
138   ML/DL methods in IoT tasks. The Internet of Things (IoT) sensors gather diverse data from
139   the real world, such as tri-axial acceleration, electrocardiogram readings, WiFi signals, and pres-
140   sure (Sehrawat & Gill, 2019). These data have empowered various human sensing tasks, including
141   Human Activity Recognition (HAR) (Lara & Labrador, 2013), health monitoring like heartbeat and
142   respiration anomaly detection (Mousavi & Afghah, 2018; Aytekin et al., 2022), and industrial appli-
143   cations such as machine operational state monitoring (Kong et al., 2023). Currently, these IoT data
144   are primarily processed using traditional machine learning techniques, such as Support Vector Ma-
145   chines (SVM) and K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) Algorithm (Alam et al., 2016; Luo et al., 2021), or
146
      deep learning methods (Li et al., 2021; Njima et al., 2019). These approaches build black-box pre-
      dictors for specific tasks, yet each predictor only supports one task, and the task cannot be addressed
147
      with reasoning analysis, which motivates us to explore LLM for IoT tasks.
148
149
150   LLMs in IoT tasks. Existing literature on Large Language Models (LLMs) in IoT mainly regards
151   LLM as a user interface or as coordinators in smart machines (Li et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2023;
152   Du et al., 2023). However, in these studies, LLMs function as intermediaries and do not directly
153
      interpret IoT data to perform real-world tasks. Recent studies, such as Penetrative AI (Xu et al.,
      2024a) and HarGPT (Ji et al., 2024), have begun integrating IoT data into LLMs for specific tasks,
154
      leveraging their inherent knowledge bases. Despite these advancements, the exploration of LLMs
155
      processing IoT data remains nascent. Penetrative AI converts IoT data into textual and numerical
156   formats for basic tasks like R-peak identification in ECG data, heavily relying on manually crafted
157   expert knowledge, which limits automation and scalability. Similarly, HarGPT processes raw IMU
158   data to recognize human activities using a chain of thought technique but is restricted to this specific
159   data type and task, not demonstrating the broader applicability of LLMs. While these studies provide
160   initial insights into using LLMs in the IoT domain, they do not offer a comprehensive framework
161   that fully exploits LLM capabilities or systematically explores the interaction between LLMs and
      the physical world through IoT devices, which is the primary focus of our work.

199   In this section, we define the problem for IoT task reasoning with LLM and introduce our research
200   methodology. The formulated research problem is how to leverage LLM and in-context learning for
201
      task reasoning for IoT data, termed as IoT task reasoning, e.g., using accelerators data for activity
      recognition or machine sensor for anomaly detection. The prompt for LLM should include two
202
      parts: data, as a way to perceive the physical world, and the task description, such as “Is it a Normal
203
      heartbeat (N) or Premature ventricular contraction beat (V)?”, serves as the query. To evaluate
204   the performance of IoT reasoning task, we build a new benchmark including 5 real-world tasks
205   with different IoT data types and difficulty levels, encompassing both classification and regression
206   problems.
207
      At first, we employ LLMs to execute IoT tasks in a basic setting, similar to the existing ap-
208
      proaches (Ji et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024b), where the prompt provided to the LLMs includes only
209
      raw IoT data and the associated query. However, the performance of LLMs remains suboptimal. As
210   shown by the baseline results in Table 2, even GPT-4 only achieves an accuracy of 43% for 3-way
211   activity recognition and 50% for machine diagnosis based on their approach. These preliminary
212   results akin to near-random guessing suggest a lack of comprehension of IoT data and tasks by
213   this naive prompting way. Upon analyzing the characteristics of IoT data and real-world tasks, we
214   identify that the challenges stem from the abstraction of dense numeric data and the lack of domain
215   knowledge within LLMs. To address these challenges, we propose a unified framework (Fig. 2) con-
      sisting of three key stages: (1) IoT data simplification and enrichment, (2) IoT-oriented knowledge

      3.1   I OT DATA SIMPLIFICATION AND ENRICHMENT
220
221   Unlike textual human tasks that have been learned by LLMs, IoT data for IoT task reasoning presents
222   unique challenges that hinder LLMs’ comprehension. Firstly, IoT data encompasses a diverse range
223   of types and forms, many of which are complex time-series data (e.g., electrocardiogram read-
224   ings) (Goldberger et al., 2000) or multi-variant data (e.g., WiFi CSI) (Yang et al., 2024). LLMs
225   often struggle with accurately interpreting dense numerical data, especially when it involves long-
226   sequence time-series data (Zhang et al., 2024b). Secondly, IoT data is typically composed of raw
227   numerical values. This data often lacks essential textual annotations, such as units of measurement
228   and metadata about the data collection process, which are critical for LLMs to interpret effectively
229
      in real-world applications. In summary, raw IoT data requires (1) appropriate simplification and
      (2) information enrichment. Previous studies (Xu et al., 2024b) have employed down-sampling
230
      techniques for time-series data but they only achieve coarse-grained simplification at a length level
231
      without enhancing the informational content of the IoT data. In contrast, we not only simplify IoT
232   data at the token level but also enrich the IoT data by providing additional information to facilitate
233   better understanding by LLMs (as illustrated in Fig. 10 in Appendix B). In this way, we transform
234   complex raw IoT data into an LLM-friendly format for IoT task reasoning.
235
236   IoT data simplification. To achieve effective simplification, it is crucial to understand why LLMs
237   struggle with dense numeric data. Firstly, according to recent research (Gruver et al., 2024; Spathis
238   & Kawsar, 2023), tokenization methods, such as Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) often fragment numbers
239   into tokens that do not align with their digits, resulting in inconsistent tokenization of floating-
240   point numbers and complicating arithmetic operations. Therefore, in addition to down-sampling
241   and keeping fixed precision (e.g., two digits of precision) to efficiently manage context length, we
242   propose to insert spaces between digits to ensure distinct tokenization of each digit and use a comma
243
      (“,”) to separate each time step in a time series. Secondly, the complexity of long-sequence IoT data
      poses significant challenges for LLMs in analysis. To assist LLMs in processing this data, we extract
244
      essential statistical features, e.g., mean, variance, and FFT mean, utilizing external tools, such as
245
      Python scripts. We find that these fundamental features are strong enough for IoT task reasoning in
246   classic IoT tasks. By doing so, we not only simplify IoT data at both length and token levels but also
247   transform it into a format that is more suitable for tokenization and processing by LLMs.
248
249   IoT data enrichment. As previously noted, IoT data alone is insufficient for LLMs to effectively
250   perform real-world tasks. To address this, we enrich the data by incorporating contextual information
251   about the physical world. Specifically, we provide a comprehensive overview of IoT data collection
252   and the integration of physical information. For instance, in human activity recognition (HAR) tasks
253   where we employ inertial measurement unit (IMU) data including triaxial acceleration and angular
254   velocity from accelerometers and gyroscopes, we meticulously outline the data collection process,
255   incorporating the metadata such as sampling frequency (e.g., 10 Hz), device placement on the body,
256   and units of measurement (e.g., gravitational acceleration and radians per second). This approach
257
      enables LLMs to not only align the three-axis IMU data with the corresponding three-dimensional
      spatial orientations of the human body but also to understand the physical significance of these
258
      numerical values, thereby enhancing the comprehension of LLMs for the task in the physical world.
259

      3.2   I OT- ORIENTED K NOWLEDGE R ETRIEVAL AUGMENTATION
262
      In IoT task reasoning, the knowledge of LLMs to perform IoT tasks is significant. For example, de-
263
      tecting abnormal heartbeats from electrocardiogram (ECG) data requires interpreting ECG signals
264   and associating them with specific heartbeat states (e.g., normal, premature ventricular contraction),
265   necessitating specialized domain knowledge. Although previous research (Xu et al., 2024b) pro-
266   poses to include specific expert knowledge for specific tasks, the augmentation is task-specific and
267   added manually, which is time-consuming and not scalable. To address this, we enable LLMs with
268   IoT knowledge in an automatic fashion. Inspired by the in-context learning capability of LLMs, we
269   also retrieve task-specific demonstrations, such as question-answer pairs, to guide LLMs in effec-
      tively utilizing IoT data for analyzing IoT tasks.


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      We first construct an IoT domain knowledge base and a demonstration knowledge base, which will
271   be utilized for retrieving domain knowledge and task-specific demonstrations. To ensure compre-
272   hensive coverage of knowledge about IoT data and tasks within the IoT domain knowledge base, we
273   gather relevant documents (e.g., Wikipedia articles, research papers) through web searches encom-
274   passing the following themes: (1) IoT data domain knowledge, (2) IoT task domain knowledge, and
275   (3) expert insights on leveraging IoT data for task execution. For the demonstration knowledge base,
276   we create task-specific demonstrations (i.e., question-answer pairs) authored by human or AI models
277   (e.g., ChatGPT). We then employ an embedding model (e.g., text-embedding-ada-0021 by OpenAI)
278   to embed texts into vectors and store the text chunks and corresponding embeddings as key-value
279
      pairs, which allows for efficient and scalable search capabilities. To improve the quality of retrieved
      contents, we also store metadata (e.g., IoT data type for IoT domain knowledge base and task type
280
      for demonstration knowledge base) alongside the vector embeddings within the vector database.
281
      This approach allows for advanced post-processing techniques, such as metadata filtering (Poliakov
282   & Shvai, 2024), to refine search results and improve task-specific retrieval accuracy. Secondly, we
283   retrieve relevant knowledge using both IoT data description and task description as query. We adopt
284   a hybrid search method, which means utilizing both keyword-based retrievers and embedding-based
285   retrievers to harness their unique strengths, ensuring the consistent retrieval of highly relevant and
286   context-rich information. Finally, after applying a re-ranking technique to recalibrate the similarity
287   between the query and retrieved texts using ranker models (e.g. bge-reranker-base2 ), we filter out
288   the top-m most relevant pieces, thus obtaining pertinent knowledge, encompassing documents with
289   specific domain knowledge and task demonstrations relevant to the task at hand.
290
291   3.3      P ROMPT C ONFIGURATION
292
      In addition to augmenting LLMs’ knowledge by providing external documents in the context uti-
293
      lizing the in-context learning capability of LLMs, we further invoke LLMs’ internal knowledge by
294
      carefully configuring the prompt. Recent studies demonstrate that LLMs possess strong role-playing
295   capabilities (Park et al., 2023). To leverage this, we assign specific roles to LLMs for particular
296   tasks. For instance, we have LLMs assume the role of a professional doctor when performing heart-
297   beat anomaly detection, thereby activating their internal domain knowledge. What’s more, since
298   LLMs’ reasoning capability can be improved a lot by decomposing the whole problem into several
299   parts (Wei et al., 2022), we decompose the reasoning procedure into two steps, prompting LLMs to
300   analyze the IoT data and task first, and then provide the final answer based on this analysis. By do-
301   ing so, we can also evaluate the extent to which the LLM understands IoT data and its capability to
302   perform IoT tasks through the generated analysis. In the end, we employ a prompt template (refer to
303
      Fig. 8 in Appendix B) to structure the content discussed previously. The ultimate prompt is crafted
      based on the template and subsequently fed into a downstream LLM. The LLM then produces the
304
      final output, encompassing both analysis and answer to the specified task.

      4.1.2     I OT DATASETS .
379
380   In our benchmark, we choose public IoT datasets on the five tasks to ensure fairness. Since some
381   datasets are too challenging for LLMs with many classes, we simplify some datasets by only using
382   a subset, which is also employed in previous works (Ji et al., 2024).
383
384   Human Activity Recognition. We employ the Smartphone-Based Recognition of Human Activ-
385   ities and Postural Transitions Dataset (Reyes-Ortiz et al., 2015). This dataset comprises raw IMU
386   data, specifically 3-axial linear acceleration, and 3-axial angular velocity, captured at a sampling rate
387
      of 50Hz by the smartphone’s accelerometer and gyroscope. The data encompasses twelve distinct
      activities. To reduce both the sequence length and data complexity, we down-sample the data to
388
      10Hz. Given the challenges associated with multi-class classification for LLM, instead of utilizing
389
      all twelve activity categories, we conduct a binary classification task involving the WALKING and
390   STANDING labels, and a ternary classification task with the LYING, WALKING UPSTAIRS, and
391   LIE TO SIT labels.
392
393   Industrial anomaly detection. We employ the Condition Monitoring of Hydraulic Systems
394   Dataset (Helwig et al., 2018), which facilitates the assessment of a hydraulic test rig’s condition
395   using multi-sensor data, including temperature, cooling power, and efficiency factor series, all ex-
396   perimentally derived from the rig. The dataset categorizes cooler conditions into three severity
397   grades: (1) close to failure; (2) reduced efficiency; and (3) full efficiency. For simplicity, we focus
398   on a binary classification task using only “close to failure”and “full efficiency”categories.
399
400   Heartbeat anomaly detection. We employ the MIT-BIH Arrhythmia Database (Goldberger et al.,
401   2000). This dataset comprises ECG recordings from 48 subjects, each sampled at 360Hz, and cat-
402   egorizes heartbeats into several types, including Normal beat (N), Atrial premature beat (A), and
403
      Premature ventricular contraction (V), among others. To reduce the difficulty of the task, we down-
      sample the signals to 72Hz and focus on a binary classification task using only the Normal beat (N)
404
      and Premature ventricular contraction (V) categories.
405
406
      Human sensing task. We utilize a dataset collected using a TP-Link TL-WDR4300 WiFi router
407
      operating at 5 GHz with a 40 MHz bandwidth (Zhuravchak et al., 2022). The dataset specifically
408
      captures the absence of human presence across three different rooms. Each room’s environment is
409   carefully monitored to record Channel State Information (CSI) that reflects the presence or absence
410   of occupants, providing a robust basis for occupancy detection tasks.
411
412   Indoor localization task. We utilize a dataset collected in a laboratory environment using an IoT
413   system developed in (Huang et al., 2022). The dataset consists of RSSI signals, the basis for
414   determining human positions within the space. By collecting RSS fingerprints at various reference
415   points, a signal radio map is constructed using a modified Gaussian Process Regression (GPR)
416   method. This approach allows us to estimate the RSS distribution at any given location, providing a
417   reliable means of localizing human presence in the environment.

      5     CONCLUSION
528
529
      LLMs often struggle with tasks requiring an understanding of physical laws. To address this, we pro-
      pose IoT-LLM, a framework that integrates IoT sensor data with LLMs to enhance their perception
530
      and reasoning abilities in the physical world. Evaluated on tasks like human activity recognition and
531
      industrial anomaly detection, IoT-LLM improves LLM performance by approximately 65%, though
532   challenges remain in specialized domains. This approach systematically enhances LLM capabilities
533   for real-world applications by leveraging IoT data.
534
535   Limitations. While LLMs can manage low-dimensional time-series data, they face significant
536   challenges with higher-dimensional data, such as audio and 3D point cloud data, due to their ex-
537   tensive length and complexity. Integrating such data into the context of LLMs is both difficult and
538   impractical. Instead, directly fine-tuning LLMs with data specific to these modalities may be a more
539   effective approach. This strategy could be explored in future research to extend the capabilities of
      LLMs to handle more complex data types within the IoT domain.
