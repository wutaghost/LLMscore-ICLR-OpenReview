                                                    A BSTRACT
013
014
               Large language model development relies on the pre-train-then-align paradigm,
015
               in which the model is typically pre-trained on a large text corpus and undergoes a
016
               tuning stage to align the model with human preference or downstream tasks. We
017            investigate the relationship between pre-training and fine-tuning by fine-tuning
018            multiple intermediate pre-trained model checkpoints to understand how models
019            develop as they train. Our results on 18 datasets suggest that i) continual pre-
020            training improves the model in a latent way that manifests after fine-tuning; ii)
021            fine-tuning most benefits datasets where the model does not show capability during
022            pre-training; iii) although the model benefits significantly through supervised fine-
023            tuning, it may forget previously known domain knowledge and tasks not seen
024            during fine-tuning; iv) the model exhibits high sensitivity to evaluation prompts
025
               after supervised fine-tuning, but this sensitivity can be alleviated through more
               pre-training. 1

038   1    I NTRODUCTION
039
040   The rise of large language models (LLMs) as a general-purpose tool for a diverse range of natural
041   language processing tasks has dramatically transformed the field, introducing new paradigms for data
042   collection and model training (Brown et al., 2020, Biderman et al., 2023, Touvron et al., 2023, Jiang
043
      et al., 2023, Chowdhery et al., 2023, Groeneveld et al., 2024, Wang et al., 2024, inter alia). Numerous
      models, training methods, datasets, and evaluation methods continue to be developed on an ongoing
044
      basis. Nevertheless, a unified paradigm has emerged for training LLMs: pre-train on an enormous
045
      corpus of diverse documents, ranging from 250B (Biderman et al., 2023) to 15T (AI@Meta, 2024)
046   tokens, followed by an alignment stage to make the model more useful and performative for various
047   tasks.
048
049
      Based on this paradigm, work has focused on improving these two stages. Work to improve pre-
      trained models includes larger training sets (Hoffmann et al., 2022; AI@Meta, 2024; Touvron et al.,
050
      2023), different data selection mechanisms (Xia et al., 2024), higher quality data (Zhou et al., 2024),

      and various model architectures (Su et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023). Meanwhile, research on model
055   alignment includes different training objectives (Rafailov et al., 2024; Schulman et al., 2017), new
056   datasets (Narayanan & Aepli, 2024), more efficient training (Hu et al., 2021; Dettmers et al., 2024)
057   and safety tuning (Bianchi et al., 2023). The alignment stage usually involves either supervised fine-
058   tuning for specific tasks or instruction fine-tuning for general-purpose usage. Regardless, fine-tuning
059   (almost always) comes at the end of pre-training and yields remarkable improvements on downstream
060   tasks (Touvron et al., 2023; Groeneveld et al., 2024). Consequently, the benefits of each stage are
061   largely explored independently, with improvements to pretraining being orthogonal to benefits from
062   model alignment.
063   Rather than exploring these two training regimes independently, we ask: How do pretraining
064   and fine-tuning interact to produce the resulting model? Does more pre-training hinder better
065   fine-tuning results? What does the model learn and forget during pre-training and fine-tuning?
066   Answering these questions requires us to examine how models learn during pre-training and how this
067   affects fine-tuning. Therefore, we fine-tune multiple pre-training checkpoints of a large language
068   model (Figure 1), evaluating each checkpoint and its fine-tuned variant on downstream evaluation
069
      sets. We track model abilities during pre-training and compare them to improvements achieved
      after fine-tuning at the corresponding pre-training step. We explore both supervised and instruction
070
      fine-tuning, testing the models’ memorization and forgetting when learning specific tasks and serving
071
      as general-purpose language-AI tools. We believe that we are the first to explore the relationship
072   between fine-tuning and pre-training by fine-tuning intermediate model checkpoints.
073
074   Our experiments yield the following insights into LLM training: we find that (1) continued pre-
075
      training can improve a model in ways that are only revealed after fine-tuning (§5); (2) tasks for which
      the model already performs well during pre-training benefit much less from fine-tuning than those
076
      where the model does not demonstrate capabilities (§4, §5); (3) although supervised fine-tuning can
077
      improve performance on in-distribution tasks, it can also cause the model to forget domain knowledge
078   or tasks that it was previously capable of solving (§6); (4) fine-tuned models show high sensitivity to
079   evaluation prompts, but this sensitivity can be alleviated by more pre-training (§6).
080
081
      Our findings provide insights into model training and can inform methods for both pre-training and
      fine-tuning. Furthermore, our work shows the value of analyzing the training dynamics, in addition to
082
      analyzing the final checkpoint of an LLM, as an aspect of interpretability, and we encourage model
083
      developers to release these checkpoints to aid future studies.

086   2   BACKGROUND : M ODEL T RAINING
087
088
      We begin with a brief survey of the core components of LLM training: pre-training, fine-tuning, and
089
      instruction fine-tuning. We also discuss the related topic of in-context learning as well as different
090   efficient fine-tuning strategies. We use “model alignment” as a general term for techniques that align
091   a model with a desired behavior, which can be accomplished by fine-tuning models after pretraining.
092   The term is also associated with other definitions (Shen et al., 2024). We also note several related
093   studies that explore training dynamics to understand model behavior (Tirumala et al., 2022; Chen
094   et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2023). With this in mind, we conduct an empirical study on how the amount
095   of pre-training affects the effectiveness of fine-tuning.
096   Pre-training. The first step of training a LLM is pre-training on a massive text corpus (Achiam
097   et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Groeneveld et al., 2024). For decoder-only models in the GPT
098   family, the subject of our paper, work since the introduction of GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019) has
099   focused on scaling up model training. Initial work increased model size to hundreds of billions of
100   parameters (Brown et al., 2020; Rae et al., 2021; Chowdhery et al., 2023), along with explorations in
101   model size, training corpus size, and training data characteristics (Hoffmann et al., 2022; Gururangan
102   et al., 2020). Since the push towards large models, work has shifted to increasing the amount of
103   pre-training data (Computer, 2023; Soldaini et al., 2024), with new models now reaching 15 trillion
104
      tokens (AI@Meta, 2024). Studies of model performance on various tasks at different model sizes
      introduced the idea of emergent model abilities (Wei et al., 2022), with new model abilities being
105
      revealed as model training grows.
106
107   We also recognize a particularly important trend for this paper: model openness. Early LLMs were
      proprietary models accessible only through an API. The first large open model, Bloom (Bloom Ström


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2023), allowed widespread LLM evaluation. Subsequent open models, such as OPT (Zhang
109   et al., 2022), LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023; Keles & Bayraklı, 2024) and others (Biderman et al.,
110   2023; Gururangan et al., 2023b; Almazrouei et al., 2023), have become the norm. In this paper, we
111   study OLMo (Groeneveld et al., 2024), one of the only models to release individual pre-training
112   checkpoints.
113
      Fine-Tuning. Early work on instruction fine-tuning using reinforcement learning with human
114
      feedback (RLHF) (Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022) demonstrates the
115   dramatic effect that model alignment could have on a pre-training model. When a specific task of
116   interest has been identified, supervised fine-tuning can improve a pre-trained model. Task-agnostic
117   tuning became popularized with the advent of T5 models (Raffel et al., 2020), where a pre-trained
118   LLM is tuned using a general text-to-text solution. When multiple tasks are given to the model, the
119   model is commonly given a task-specific prefix or an instruction along with the task input, leading to
120   the development of various methods of prefix tuning (Li & Liang, 2021) and instruction tuning (Wei
121   et al., 2021; Mishra et al., 2022; Victor et al., 2022).
122   Instruction Fine-Tuning. Instruction fine-tuning is preferred when more general model behaviors
123   are desired. Popularized through reinforcement-learning with human feedback (RLHF) (Christiano
124   et al., 2017; Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022) and reinforcement-
125   learning with AI feedback (RLAIF) (Lee et al., 2023), these methods utilize a reward model to
126   simulate human feedback. Others explore human preference tuning without a reward model (Rafailov
127   et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024), or study the effects of these tuning methods (Shen
128   et al., 2024; Perez et al., 2023). Sharma et al. (2024) show that supervised fine-tuning can lead to
129
      similar performance as RLAIF.
130   In-Context Learning. ICL, also called few-shot learning, is also used as an evaluation strategy
131   where the model is given a prompt composed of examples of tasks expected to be solved. The
132   underlying model is evaluated based on its response to the input. ICL can benefit from a larger context
133   window that adds more examples, which can spur work on the development of model quantization
134   techniques (Dettmers et al., 2022) and the alleviation of hardware constraints (Brown et al., 2020;
135
      Xie et al., 2021; Min et al., 2022). While not the subject of this paper since it does not make changes
      to model parameters, in-context learning utilizes a small amount of supervised data to improve model
136
      performance.
137
138   Fine-Tuning Techniques. While model pre-training can be done by a few groups with large
139   resources interested in developing new models, fine-tuning depends on the task and is of broad
140   interest. Therefore, many techniques facilitate time-, memory-, and data-efficient model training
141
      through parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) (Hu et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; 2023), quantization
      (Jacob et al., 2018; Dettmers et al., 2022; 2024), and specialized data filtering (Xia et al., 2024;
142
      Zhou et al., 2024; Attendu & Corbeil, 2023). This paper focuses specifically on full-parameter
143
      fine-tuning, while our findings suggest the potential for data-efficient and budget-friendly training
144   by understanding the critical turning point of model training. Our findings are closely related to the
145   recent study on phase transition of model training (Olsson et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022; Chen et al.,
146   2023).

247   3.3                 E VALUATION
248
249   The evaluation challenge is to select a representative number of datasets for different types of tasks
250   to test model abilities, recognizing that each dataset requires evaluating each model checkpoint
251   and its fine-tuned counterparts. We also select datasets based on the availability of in-domain and
252   out-of-domain samples.
253   Datasets. The datasets are summarized in Table 1. We evaluate the model with an in-domain test
254   set and one or two out-of-domain test sets for each of the supervised fine-tuning tasks. We conduct
255   experiments on the tasks of summary generation (Narayan et al., 2018; Hasan et al., 2021; Hermann
256   et al., 2015), question generation (Sap et al., 2019; Xiong et al., 2019; Welbl et al., 2017), natural
257   language inference (Williams et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2018; Dagan et al., 2006; Bar Haim et al.,
258   2006; Giampiccolo et al., 2007; Bentivogli et al., 2009), and paraphrase detection (Zhang et al.,
259   2019; Wang et al., 2018; Agirre et al., 2007). Each training set is sub-sampled to a size of 6,000 for a
260
      fair comparison.
261   In instruction fine-tuning, we base our downstream evaluation settings on Groeneveld et al. (2024),
262   as OLMo is found to have stable performance on these datasets. The instruction-tuned models
263   are evaluated on ARC (both arc easy and arc challenge) (Clark et al., 2018), OpenbookQA
264   (Mihaylov et al., 2018), Hellaswag (Zellers et al., 2019), BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019), and SciQ (Welbl
265   et al., 2017).
266   Metrics. We use accuracy (Pedregosa et al., 2011) for classification tasks and ROUGE -L (Lin, 2004)
267   for generation tasks. The maximum amount of newly generated tokens is set to 5 for classification
268   tasks and 60 for generation tasks. Outputs are generated with greedy decoding. For classification
269   tasks, we experiment with both constrained decoding and logit-based predictions. We find the best
      performance by selecting the label with the highest logit of its first subtoken (Appendix B).

305   5   D OES MORE PRE - TRAINING YIELD BETTER FINE - TUNING RESULTS ?
306
307   Groeneveld et al. (2024) compares OLMo’s performance on several tasks before and after fine-tuning
308   the final checkpoint and finds that fine-tuning enables the model to do well on tasks for which the
309   unaligned model does poorly. We observe (§4) that while some datasets improved during pre-training,
310   there is a group of datasets for which a pre-trained model does poorly. Does the model learn anything
311   that helps solve these tasks, and is fine-tuning required to do well on them? Alternatively, does the
312   model learn useful information for these tasks but cannot express it without fine-tuning? In this
313   section, we further explore this dataset dichotomy by examining fine-tuned checkpoints for each of
314
      the datasets.
315   Our results appear in Figure 3 and Figure 4. First, we consider those datasets where the pre-trained
316   models do well (Figure 2a). These datasets do not improve with fine-tuning, suggesting whatever is
317   learned during fine-tuning, which we discuss below, the model already gains the knowledge during
318   pre-training. This effect is observed at all checkpoints; fine-tuning simply does not help.
319   However, a different story is observed for datasets that are not learned during pre-training. For these,
320   fine-tuning yields significant improvements at every model checkpoint, with Figure 4 showing the
321   magnitude of improvement on these datasets compared to no improvement to the datasets already
322   learned during pre-training. Moreover, earlier checkpoints obtain more substantial gains from fine-
323   tuning than later checkpoints. The benefit of fine-tuning continues to increase until a certain threshold
      in pre-training steps is reached (approximately 424,000).

353

      fine-tuning models on models with less pre-training may be a reasonable practical choice for obtaining
354
      a high-quality model.

357   6     S UPERVISED F INE -T UNING : W HAT DOES THE MODEL LEARN AND FORGET ?
358
359   What exactly is the model learning during fine-tuning such that it shows abilities in pre-trained models
360   for some tasks but provides no benefit for other tasks? We analyze the supervised fine-tuning process
361
      to understand what is learned and what is forgotten. Specifically, we explore three dimensions: task
      format, task transfer, and domain knowledge.
362
363
364
      6.1   TASK F ORMAT
365
      Several works show that LLMs are extremely sensitive to prompt perturbation in few-shot settings
366   (Sclar et al., 2023; Leidinger et al., 2023; Salinas & Morstatter, 2024; Wahle et al., 2024). We
367   hypothesize that fine-tuning fits the model to a specific task format, resulting in higher performance
368   when the evaluation set matches this format. To test this hypothesis, we vary the task format to either
369   match the training format, use a different format, or rely on instructions. We carefully construct three
370   different prompt formats for the following settings. 1) Default is the same format used for training,
371   where we expect the model to benefit from learning the task format; 2) In contrast, IO format reflects
372   a common way of performing supervised fine-tuning by incorporating only unprocessed input and
373   output; 3) Instruct uses a human-readable instruction template to format the input. Table 4 shows
374
      an example of each format. Checkpoint performance on OLMo before and after fine-tuning is shown

      Some datasets can be learned without fine-tuning. We discover a dichotomy between datasets.
468   Some are learned during model pre-training, while others show no improvements during pre-training.
469   Furthermore, the datasets learned during pre-training do not benefit from fine-tuning. This observation,
470   combined with our study about what is learned during fine-tuning (§6) suggests that some tasks are
471   presented in a manner that aligns with what the model sees during pre-training, and thus fine-tuning
472   provides no additional information. It may be possible to modify tasks to better align with pre-training
473   and thus make them learnable.
474   Pre-training can improve models in unseen ways. Some datasets are not learned during pre-training
475   but benefit significantly from fine-tuning (§4). However, these datasets still benefited from additional
476   pre-training, even though those benefits were not revealed without fine-tuning (§5). The model is
477   learning important information about the task, even though it cannot express that information. Future
478   work may identify ways to detect these improvements during pre-training, which can better guide
479   pre-training choices to produce models that perform better post-fine-tuning. Perhaps there is a way
480   in which information about these tasks can be included in pre-training, allowing the model to better
481   utilize the massive amount of pre-training data. For example, early stopping during pre-training could
482
      lead to better utilization of limited training resources if we know when to stop. Our results suggest
      that early stopping in pre-training and starting fine-tuning is an efficient way of utilizing the resource
483
      when the downstream datasets are never picked up by the model during pre-training.
484
485   Fine-tuning teaches task format but leads to forgetting unused abilities. Our results show that fine-
      tuning guides the model to understand the format and complete a given task. As this information

      diminishes, the model’s overall ability improves. However, fine-tuning comes at the expense of other
487   model abilities, such as the capability of solving tasks or domains that are unrelated or weakly related
488   to the fine-tuning task. This insight can be helpful in our understanding of the multitask abilities of
489   LLMs, where certain tasks can introduce conflicts during multi-task training (Mueller et al., 2022).

492   8   L IMITATIONS
493
494   While our insights suggest directions for future work, we note important limitations inherent in our
495   experiments. We discuss the weaknesses and limitations in the following section.
496
      Computing Resource. Due to computational constraints, we can only conduct experiments on a 1B
497
      model and a limited amount of datasets. We supply the final checkpoint of an 8B model to further
498   verify the findings that are shared across checkpoints. The amount of GPU hours spent for each
499   experiment in this study is listed in Table 3.
500
501
      Model Size and Variant. For the analysis with intermediate checkpoints, this study considered
      a single, relatively small LLM, which may, therefore, conceal the emergent capability brought by
502
      larger models (Wei et al., 2022). To combat this, we include the final checkpoint of an 8B model
503
      from a different model family. Although this study is conducted on less than a dozen datasets, it
504   still consumes thousands of hours of GPU training time at significant expense. Future work needs to
505   confront these issues on larger models and more datasets.
506
507
      Availbility of Pre-training Checkpoints. This study would benefit significantly from including a
      broader spectrum of models, but the public pre-training checkpoint releases are limited. Open-source
508
      LLMs with intermediate checkpoint release include OLMo (Groeneveld et al., 2024), TinyLLAMA
509
      (Zhang et al., 2024), RedPajama-Incite (TOGETHER, 2023), OpenLM (Gururangan et al., 2023a),
510   and Pythia (Biderman et al., 2023). After a series of preliminary experiments, we select these models’
511   best-performing and robust families.
512
513
      Analysis Protocol. Wu et al. (2023) show that the evaluation result may be affected by samples that
      have been memorized by the model during training instead of revealing the reasoning capability. The
514
      only analysis protocol used is the downstream performance of a trained model. More investigation
515
      should be done into model internals during pre-training dynamics and how they relate to the effects
516   of fine-tuning.
517
518   Training Paradigm. Although multiple tuning strategies exist, to create a fair comparison en-
519
      vironment where checkpoints receive the same amount of training, models are fine-tuned with a
      fixed amount of epochs in this work. On different pre-training stages, the model may converge at a
520
      different speed. Further study can be done to study the effect of pre-training on different fine-tuning
521
      methods or fine-tuning dynamics in different pre-training stages. We only explored the scenario of
522   full-parameter fine-tuning. Whether parameter-efficient fine-tuning or human preference tuning will
523   lead to a different conclusion also remains an open question.
524
525
      Randomness. In this study, we only assess uncertainty with Bootstrap during evaluation. However,
      uncertainty may emerge during training, which poses optimizer initialization and data ordering, the
526
      study of which requires an extensive amount of computing resources.
527
528
529   9   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      We explore the relationship between fine-tuning and pre-training LLMs through fine-tuning multiple
532
      pre-training checkpoints of large language models. Our results on 18 datasets provide insights into
533
      LLM training. We find that continual pre-training improves the model in a latent way that is only
534   observable after fine-tuning. The model may excel at some tasks without fine-tuning. However, the
535   model can rapidly learn datasets that it does not demonstrate capabilities during pre-training with
536   only a small amount of supervision. In the meantime, we identify the aspects that LLM learns and
537   forgets during supervised fine-tuning: task format, task solution, and domain knowledge. Overall,
538   our study highlights the value of analyzing language model training dynamics, in line with Chen et al.
539   (2023). We would like to advocate for the release of pre-training checkpoints, the release of which
      will enable the broader research community to conduct more in-depth future research.

995    B     P REDICTION G ENERATION M ETHOD
996
997    For classification tasks, we examine three different prediction generation methods: Free Generation
998    (Free), Constrained Generation (Constrained), and Token Probability (TokenProb), the results
999    are shown in Table 2. In Constrained, we force the output to include at least one label in the
1000   acceptable label set. In TokenProb, we compare the logits of acceptable labels and select the label
1001   with the highest score as the final output. This ablation study is conducted only on the BASE
1002   and fine-tuned versions of the final checkpoint of the pre-trained model. We find that, although
1003   prediction generation methods have less effect on the evaluation result of a fine-tuned model, BASE
1004   variants suffer much more from not knowing the desired output. Therefore, we proceed with all the
1005
       classification experiments with TokenProb.

1008

1010   unreliability. For example, in paraphrase detection, the model could assign probability on both “yes"
1011
       and “ yes" (the same label with a prefix space). This behavior is reported and explored in various
       related work (Sun et al., 2023; Batsuren et al., 2024; Singh & Strouse, 2024). In this study, we
1012
       leniently regard all individual tokens that contain the whole label or part of the label along with some
1013
       special characters that do not affect the semantics as an acceptable target label.
