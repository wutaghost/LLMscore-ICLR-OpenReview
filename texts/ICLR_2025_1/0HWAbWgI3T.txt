011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Personalized item recommendation typically suffers from data sparsity, which is
014            most often addressed by learning vector representations of users and items via
015
               low-rank matrix factorization. While this effectively densifies the matrix by assum-
               ing users and movies can be represented by linearly dependent latent features, it
016
               does not capture more complicated interactions. For example, vector representa-
017
               tions struggle with set-theoretic relationships, such as negation and intersection,
018            e.g. recommending a movie that is “comedy and action, but not romance”. In
019            this work, we formulate the problem of personalized item recommendation as
020            matrix completion where rows are set-theoretically dependent. To capture this
021            set-theoretic dependence we represent each user and attribute by a hyper-rectangle
022            or box (i.e. a Cartesian product of intervals). Box embeddings can intuitively be
023            understood as trainable Venn diagrams, and thus not only inherently represent
024            similarity (via the Jaccard index), but also naturally and faithfully support arbi-
025            trary set-theoretic relationships. Queries involving set-theoretic constraints can be
026
               efficiently computed directly on the embedding space by performing geometric
               operations on the representations. We empirically demonstrate the superiority of
027
               box embeddings over vector-based neural methods on both simple and complex
028
               item recommendation queries by up to 30% overall.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Recommendation systems are a standard component of most online platforms, providing personalized
034   suggestions for products, movies, articles, and more. In addition to generic recommendation, these
035   platforms often present the option for the user to search for items, either via natural language or
036   structured queries. While collaborative filtering methods like matrix factorization have proven
037   successful in addressing data sparsity for unconditional generation, they often fall short when
038
      attempting to combine them with more complicated queries. This is not unexpected, as vector
      embeddings, while effectively capturing linear relationships, are ill-equipped to handle the complex
039
      set-theoretic relationships. Even advanced neural network-based approaches, which are designed to
040
      capture intricate relationships, have been shown to struggle with set-theoretic compositionally that
041   underlie many real-world preferences.
042
043   Let us consider an example where a user named Bob wants to watch a comedy which is not a romantic
044
      comedy. Assuming we have a prior watch history for users, standard collaborative filtering techniques
      (e.g. low-rank matrix factorization) would yield a learned score function score(m, Bob) for each
045
      movie m. If we also have movie-attribute annotations, we could form the set of comedies C and
046
      set of romance movies R and simply filter to those movies in C \ R, however this assumes that the
047   movie-attribute annotations are complete, which is rarely the case.
048
049   A standard approach in a setting with sparse data is to learn a low-rank approximation for the attribute
050   × movie matrix A, yielding a dense matrix Â. We can then form sets of movies based on this
051   dense matrix using an (attribute-specific) threshold, e.g. Ĉ := {m | Âcomedy,m > τcomedy } and
052   R̂ := {m | Âromance,m > τromance }, and then rank movies m ∈ Ĉ \ R̂ according to score(m, Bob).
053   While this approach does allow for performing the sort of queries we are after, it suffers from three
      fundamental issues:

087         1. Limited user-attribute interaction: Since the attribute classification is done independently

095
096   In this paper, we formulate the problem of attribute-specific recommendation as matrix completion
097   where rows are not necessarily linear combinations of each other but, rather, are set-theoretic
098   combinations of each other. More precisely, given some user × movie interaction matrix U and
099   attribute × movie matrix A, the queries we are considering are set-theoretic combinations of these
100   rows (see Figure 1a). For example, the ground-truth data for comedies which are not romance movies
101   which Bob likes would be the vector x ∈ {0, 1}|M | , where xm = 1 if and only if UBob,m = 1 and
102   Acomedy,m = 1 and Aromance,m = 0. Note that this is not a linear combination of the previous
103   rows, and so while the inductive bias of low-rank factorization has proven immensely effective for
104
      collaborative filtering we should not expect it to be directly applicable in this setting.
105   Instead, we propose to learn representations for the users and attributes that are consistent with
106   specific set-theoretic axioms. These representations must also be compactly parameterizable in a
107   lower-dimensional space, differentiable with respect to some appropriate score function, and allow for
      efficient computation of various set operations. Box Embeddings (Vilnis et al., 2018; Dasgupta et al.,


                                                                2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2020), which are axis-parallel n-dimensional hyperrectangles, meet these criteria (see Figure 1b). The
109   volume of a box is easily calculated as the product of its side-lengths. Furthermore, box embeddings
110   are closed under intersection (i.e. the intersection of two boxes is another box). Inclusion-exclusion
111   thus allows us to calculate the volume of arbitrary set-theoretic combinations of boxes.
112
      The contributions of our paper are as follows -
113
114         1. We model the problem of attribute-specific query recommendation as “set-theoretic matrix
115            completion”, where attributes and users are treated as sets of items. We discuss the challenges
116            faced by existing machine-learning approaches for this problem setup.
117         2. We demonstrate the inconsistency of existing vector embedding models for this task. Addi-
118            tionally, we establish box embeddings as a suitable embedding method for addressing such
119            set-theoretic problems.
120
            3. We conduct an extensive empirical study comparing various vector and box embedding
121            models for the task of set-theoretic query recommendation.
122
123   Box embeddings, with their geometric set operations, significantly outperform all vector-based
124   methods. We also evaluate score multiplication and threshold-based prediction for both vector and
125   box embedding models, and find that performing set operations directly on the box embeddings
126   performs best, solidifying our claim that the inductive bias of box embeddings provides the necessary
127
      generalization capabilities to address set-theoretic queries.

129   2     TASK F ORMULATION

131   2.1   BACKGROUND
132
133
      Matrix completion is a fundamental problem in machine learning, and arises in a wide array of tasks,
      from recommender systems to image reconstruction. Formally, this problem is typically modeled
134
      as follows: Given a matrix X ∈ Rm×n where only a subset of the entries are observed, find a
135
      complete matrix X̂ ∈ Rm×n which closely approximates X on the observed entries. For the task
136
      of recommendation, this involves predicting user interactions with items they have not previously
137
      interacted with, and a common assumption is that the preferences of users and characteristics of the
138   items can be expressed by a small number of latent factors, with the alignment of these latent factors
139
      captured via dot-product. This justifies the search for a low-rank approximation X̂ = BC, where
140   B ∈ Rm×D and C ∈ RD×n . In the case where the original matrix is binary, X ∈ {0, 1}m×n , it is
141   common to perform logistic matrix factorization, where an elementwise sigmoid is applied after the
142   dot-product of latent factors, which we denote (with slight abuse of notation) as X̂ = σ(BC).
143   Many recent advanced methods for this matrix completion task utilize sophisticated neural networks
144   (He et al., 2017; 2020; Liang et al., 2018), that capture more expressive interactions among users
145   and items. However, they are not particularly recognized for their set-theoretic compositionality, as
146   previously described.
147
148   2.2   S ET-T HEORETIC M ATRIX C OMPLETION
149
150   We will describe the task of set-theoretic matrix completion on the setting of movies, users, and
151
      attributes, though the formulation and our proposed model can be generalized to arbitrary domains.
      We are given a set DU ⊆ U ×M of user-movie interactions, and a set DA ⊆ A×M of attribute-movie
152
      pairs. We assume both of these sets are incomplete.
153
154   Our goal is to eventually be able to recommend movies based on some query, for example “comedy
155   and not romance”. Such a query for a particular user can be represented as u ∧ a1 ∧ ¬a2 , where u is
156   the user, a1 = comedy and a2 = romance. We let Q be the set of all queries of interest, which
157
      depends on which queries we anticipate evaluating at inference time. In this work, we will take Q to
      be queries of the form u, a1 , u ∧ a1 , u ∧ a1 ∧ a2 , and u ∧ a1 ∧ ¬a2 , where u ∈ U and a1 , a2 ∈ A.
158
159   With this formulation, we can view our task as matrix completion for a matrix X ∈ {0, 1}|Q|×|M | ,
160   where the rows are derived by applying bitwise operators on the rows of user and attribute data. While
161   we could, in theory, proceed directly with logistic matrix factorization on this matrix, there are both
      practical and theoretical reasons to search for an alternative. First, the number of rows of this matrix


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      is very large relative to the original data - in our case we have |Q| = O(|U ||A|2 ), but in general
163
      |Q| = O(3|U ||A| ). This poses practical issues, both at training time (as there are an exponential
164
      number of elements of X to traverse) and inference time (storing the low-rank approximations
165   requires O(|Q|) memory, which is much larger than |U | + |A|). There are also theoretical issues with
166   the underlying assumption, as it is no longer reasonable to assume the rows of σ −1 (X) are linear
167   combinations of some latent factors.

      3     M ETHOD
171
172
      Our proposed solution to address these issues starts by defining the sets of movies which comprise
      the queries of interest. Let, P(M ) be the power set of movies M . Specifically, for each user u
173
      we can define the set Mu = {m | (u, m) ∈ DU }, and for each attribute a we can define the set
174
      Ma = {m | (a, m) ∈ DA }. If we let M ⊆ P(M ) be the collection of all such sets, then the set of
175   movies corresponding to a given query q are direct set-theoretic combinations of elements in M.
176   Hence, the reasonable underlying assumption, in this case, is to model the elements of M as sets
177   via a map f : M → R where R is also a set of sets, and the map f respects set-theoretic operations,
178   i.e. f (S ∩ T ) = f (S) ∩ f (T ) and f (S \ T ) = f (S) \ f (T ), etc. Such a map is referred to as a
179   homomorphism of Boolean algebras, and the problem of learning such a function was explored in
180   general in Boratko et al. (2022), wherein the authors proposed the use of box embeddings as the
181   function f which is then trained to obey the homomorphism constraints.

359   not all combinations make sense. For instance, ’Sci-Fi’ & ’Documentary’ might not be a meaningful
360   combination, whereas ’Sci-Fi’ & ’Time-Travel’ is. Similarly, ’Sci-Fi’ ¬’ Fiction’ doesn’t make sense,
361   but ’Fiction’ ¬ ’Sci-Fi’ does. Sometimes, even if the intersection is valid, it could be trivial and
362   non-interesting, e.g., ’Fiction’ & ’Sci-Fi’.
363   Intuitively, for two attributes a1 & a2 , their intersection is interesting if |a1 ∩ a2 | is greater than
364
      combining any two random items set. Also, for their intersection to be non-trivial the size of the
      intersection |a1 ∩ a2 | must be less than the individual sizes of the attributes i.e., α|a1 | and α|a2 |.
365
      Here,|.| denotes the size of the item set corresponding to the attributes. α ∈ [0, 1] is a design parame-
366
      ter, dedicated after manual inspection of the quality of the item sets for the combinations . In case of
367   difference queries such as a1 ∩ ¬a2 , we consider ¬a2 to be the second attribute and carry out the
368   same filtering strategy as done for the intersection queries.
369   We denote the set of non-trivial and viable attribute pairs for the intersection to be A∩ =
370   {(a1 , a2 )||a1 ∩ a2 | > ϵ, |a1 ∩ a2 | < α|a1 |, |a1 ∩ a2 | < α|a2 |}, and for the difference to be
371   A\ = {(a1 , a2 )||a1 ∩ ¬a2 | > ϵ, |a1 ∩ ¬a2 | < α|a1 |, |a1 ∩ ¬a2 | < α|¬a2 |}. Using the above formu-
372   lation, we generate the test set for the personalized complex queries QU ∩A1 ∩A2 and QU ∩A1 ∩¬A2
373   using algorithm 2. Please refer to Table 2 for the detailed statistics.

392   4.4       BASELINES
393
394
      The recommendation systems literature offers a wide range of methods that represent users, and
      items in Rd . These methods then propose a compatibility score function between the user and item,
395
      ϕ : Rd × Rd → R. A common and effective choice for ϕ is the dot product, which underpins matrix
396
      factorization (Rendle et al., 2020; Koren & Bell, 2015). To capture more complex interactions among
397   users, items, and attributes, He et al. (2017) extend matrix factorization by replacing the dot product
398   with a neural network-based similarity function. This method, called Neural Matrix Factorization
399   (N EU MF), combines the dot product with an MLP. Similarly, He et al. (2020) propose LightGCN
400   (LGCN) to captures the user, items, and attribute interaction using Graph Convolution Network Kipf
401   & Welling (2017) over a joint graph of user-item-attribute. We use MF, and, N EU MF LGCN as our
402   baselines.
403   For a personalized query, be it simple or complex, we need to devise a method to combine the
404   individual scores of the user and the attributes involved in the query. In this work, we compare three
405   approaches to obtain an aggregated score:
406
407   1. F ILTER: In this approach, we retrieve a list of items corresponding to the attributes based on
408      the scores provided by the embedding models. The list is generated by thresholding the scores,
409      where the threshold is optimized by minimizing the F1 score between the training data and
410      predicted scores. We refer to the methods using this aggregation technique as B OX -F ILTER for
411      box embeddings and MF-F ILTER, N EU MF-F ILTER, LGCN-F ILTERfor vector-based methods.
412   2. P RODUCT: In this method, the compositional score is computed by multiplying the scores for the
413      individual queries. For vector-based embeddings, the scores for each movie related to a user or
414      attribute are normalized using the sigmoid function. For box embeddings, the energy function is
415      normalized by conditioning on the movie box volume (see Section 3.3). The score for negation is
416      calculated by subtracting the normalized score from 1. The three methods using this technique are
417      referred to as B OX -P RODUCT, MF-P RODUCT, N EU MF-P RODUCT, and LGCN-P RODUCT.
418
      3. G EOMETRIC: This approach leverages the geometry of the embedding space. For vector-based
419
         embeddings, learned through Matrix Factorization, addition, and subtraction are often used for
420      query composition (Mikolov et al., 2013). Box embeddings, on the other hand, naturally represent
421      intersection operations, allowing us to compute scores for any set-theoretic combination using box
422      intersection and inclusion-exclusion principles. We refer to these methods as B OX -G EOMETRIC
423      and MF-G EOMETRIC.

532   6     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this work we presented the task of personalized recommendation with set-theoretic queries. We
535   discussed how this problem can be viewed as set-theoretic matrix completion, and why the common
536   approach of logistic matrix factorization is not aligned with the set-theoretic operations we wish to
537   perform at inference time. We observed substantial improvements over the vector/neural baselines
538   when using box embeddings as the representation, validating our intuition regarding the necessary
539   set-theoretic bias. Our empirical results confirm that box embeddings are ideally suited to the task of
      recommendation with set-theoretic queries.

705   A.1    B OX E MBEDDINGS
706
707   Vilnis et al. (2018) also proposed a probabilistic interpretation of box embeddings, where the volume
708   of a box could be trained to represent the probability of an associated binary random variable, and
709
      intersections of boxes would be trained to capture the joint probabilities. Some of the recent works
      have tried to incorporate box embeddings in a recommendation systems setup.Xu et al. (2024); Wu
710
      et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2021) use the side-length of the box embeddings as a preference range to
711
      obtain diverse set recommendations for users, Mei et al. (2022a) utilizes the axis parallel nature of
712   the box embeddings for faster retrieval for recommendation systems. Sun et al. (2020a;b); Ren et al.
713   (2020) are some of the recent works that focus on logical query over knowledge bases (KB).Liang
714   et al. (2023) uses Ren et al. (2020) and add a volumetric contrastive loss as a regularizer. However, in
715   this work, we frame collaborative filtering as a set-theoretic matrix completion problem, which helps
716   us to achieve better generalization for the composition of personalized queries.
717

719   A.2    C ONTEXT AWARE R ECOMMENDATION
720
721   The concept of context-aware recommendation, as introduced in Adomavicius et al. (2011), provides
722   a general framework where “context” is broadly defined as any auxiliary information. This framework
723
      emphasizes that user preferences for items can vary based on the context in which interactions occur,
      reflecting a user-centric view of contextual information.
724
      Building on this foundation, recent works have explored specific instances of context-aware recom-
725
      mendation, such as “attribute-aware recommendation.” These approaches often leverage item or
726   user attributes as contextual information to address various goals, including improving user profiling
727   (Adomavicius et al., 2011), predicting missing item attributes (Wu et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2022), en-
728   hancing recommendations for cold-start scenarios(Deldjoo et al., 2019), or providing attribute-based
729   explanations for recommendations (Xian et al., 2021).
730
      Our work differs significantly in its focus and objectives. we term “attribute-constrained recommen-
731
      dation,” which involves generating recommendations explicitly constrained by logical combinations
732   of attributes. Unlike attribute-aware approaches, which aim to improve recommendation quality by
733   incorporating attribute information as auxiliary data, our work directly targets the task of satisfying
734   explicit attribute-based constraints posed by users.
735

737   A.3    C OMPOSITIONAL Q UERIES WITH V ECTOR E MBEDDINGS
738
739   It is common in machine learning to represent discrete entities such as items or attributes by vec-
740   tors Bengio et al. (2013) and to learn them by fitting the training data. Besides semantic similarity,
741   some have claimed that learned vectors have compositional properties through vector arithmetic,
742   for example in the empirical analysis of word2vec Mikolov et al. (2013) and GLOVE Pennington
743
      et al. (2014), and some theoretical analysis Levy & Goldberg (2014); Arora et al. (2018). However,
      anecdotally, many have found that the compositional behavior of vectors is far from reliable Rogers
744
      et al. (2017). Our paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of vector embeddings on compositional
745
      queries and compares the results to a region-based alternative.

748   A.4    S ET- BASED QUERIES IN S EARCH AND GROUP RECOMMENDATION SYSTEMS .
749
750   While set-theoretic queries are commonplace in search, popular question-answering (QA) benchmarks
751   often do not include them. We found QUEST (Malaviya et al., 2023) the most closely related study,
752   introducing a benchmark for entity-seeking queries with implicit set-based semantics. However,
753   QUEST does not focus on explicit constraints or personalization, which are central to our work.
754   Additionally, related studies in group recommendation systems (Amer-Yahia et al., 2009) touch on
755   explicit constraint-based personalization, where preferences of multiple users are explicitly aggregated
      into a coherent recommendation.
