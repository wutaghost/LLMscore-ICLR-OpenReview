009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Recently, State Space Models (SSMs) with efficient hardware-aware designs, i.e.,
012                Mamba, have demonstrated significant potential in computer vision tasks due to
013                their linear computational complexity with respect to token length and their global
014                receptive field. However, Mamba’s performance on dense prediction tasks, in-
015                cluding human pose estimation and semantic segmentation, has been constrained
016                by three key challenges: insufficient inductive bias, long-range forgetting, and
017
                   low-resolution output representation. To address these challenges, we introduce
                   the Dynamic Visual State Space (DVSS) block, which utilizes multi-scale con-
018
                   volutional kernels to extract local features across different scales and enhance in-
019
                   ductive bias, and employs deformable convolution to mitigate the long-range for-
020                getting problem while enabling adaptive spatial aggregation based on input and
021                task-specific information. By leveraging the multi-resolution parallel design pro-
022                posed in HRNet (Wang et al., 2020), we introduce High-Resolution Visual State
023                Space Model (HRVMamba) based on the DVSS block, which preserves high-
024                resolution representations throughout the entire process while promoting effec-
025                tive multi-scale feature learning. Extensive experiments highlight HRVMamba’s
026                impressive performance on dense prediction tasks, achieving competitive results
027                against existing benchmark models without bells and whistles. We will make the
028
                   source code publicly accessible.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)(He et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023;
033
      2024a;b) and Vision Transformers (ViTs)(Yuan et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Shaker et al., 2023;
034   Yun & Ro, 2024) have driven significant progress in tasks like image classification, human pose es-
035   timation, and semantic segmentation. While CNNs excel at local feature extraction with linear com-
036   putational complexity, they lack global context modeling. ViTs, despite capturing global receptive
037   fields via self-attention, face quadratic complexity and lack inductive bias, especially with large in-
038   puts. Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) introduces the S6 structure, improving the efficiency of State Space
039   Models (SSMs) for long-range feature extraction. By using input-dependent state-space parameters,
040   Mamba enables better context modeling with linear complexity. This led to many follow-up visual
041   Mamba models like ViM (Zhu et al., 2024), VMamba (Liu et al., 2024b), LocalVMamba (Huang
042
      et al., 2024), GroupMamba (Shaker et al., 2024), and MambaVision (Hatamizadeh & Kautz, 2024).
043   However, visual Mamba models have not achieved optimal performance on dense prediction tasks,
044   including human pose estimation (Xu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024d) and semantic segmenta-
045   tion (Wang et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2021), due to three key challenges. Firstly, like ViT, visual
046   Mamba splits images into sequences of patches (tokens) and employs either a bidirectional (Zhu
047   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024) or four-way scanning mechanism (Liu et al., 2024b; Huang et al., 2024)
048
      to traverse these tokens, constructing a global receptive field. While this approach is effective for
      handling long sequences, it disrupts the natural 2D spatial dependencies of images and lacks the in-
049
      ductive bias crucial for effective local representation learning. Secondly, Mamba’s token processing
050
      leads to the decay of the previous hidden state, resulting in long-range forgetting. Consequently, it
051   may lose high-level, task-specific features relevant to the query patch and instead focus on low-level
052   edge features, as shown in Fig. 1, column 21 . Lastly, current visual Mamba models usually generate

104   2   R ELATED WORK
105
106   Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Vision Transformers (ViTs). CNNs have long been
107   the cornerstone of computer vision, evolving from early models like AlexNet (Xiong et al., 2024)
      and ResNet (He et al., 2016) to more recent architectures such as ConvNeXt (Liu et al., 2022),


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      SCGNet (Zhang et al., 2023), FlashInternImage (Xiong et al., 2024), and FMGNet (Zhang et al.,
109   2024b). These models excel in local feature extraction, achieving remarkable performance across
110   tasks such as image classification, semantic segmentation, and human pose estimation. ViTs in-
111   troduce self-attention mechanisms from natural language processing (NLP), segmenting images
112   into patches to capture global dependencies, forming the foundation of Large Vision-Language
113   Models (Zhang et al., 2024c; Ying et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a). Various methods, including
114   DeiT’s (Touvron et al., 2021) distillation strategies, Swin Transformer’s (Liu et al., 2021) hierar-
115   chical structures, and SwiftFormer’s (Shaker et al., 2023) efficient attention mechanisms, have been
116   developed to broaden the adoption of ViTs in vision tasks. Recently, hybrid architectures (Yun
117
      & Ro, 2024; Ma et al., 2024) that combine the strengths of CNNs and Transformers have gained
      attention. These models leverage the inductive biases of CNNs for local feature extraction while
118
      incorporating the global attention capabilities of ViTs, marking a significant direction in backbone
119
      network research.
120
121   State Space Models (SSMs). SSMs are a mathematical framework for modeling dynamic systems
122   with linear computational complexity, making them efficient for long sequences. Optimizations
123
      in models like S4 (Gu et al., 2021), S5 (Smith et al., 2022), and H3 (Fu et al., 2022) have en-
      hanced SSMs performance through structure optimization, parallel scanning and hardware improve-
124
      ments. Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) introduces input-specific parameterization and parallel scanning
125
      (S6), positioning SSMs as a compelling alternative to Transformers. Since then, SSMs have been
126   widely adopted in vision tasks, with S4ND (Nguyen et al., 2022) being one of the first to process
127   visual data as continuous signals. Building on Mamba, models like ViM (Zhu et al., 2024) and
128   VMamba (Liu et al., 2024b) address the direction-sensitivity of Mamba with bidirectional or four-
129   way scanning. LocalVMamba (Huang et al., 2024) captures local details with windowed scanning,
130   while PlainMamba (Yang et al., 2024) refines 2D scanning for sequential processing. MambaVi-
131   sion (Hatamizadeh & Kautz, 2024) integrates SSMs with Transformers, and GroupMamba (Shaker
132   et al., 2024) improves training stability with a distillation-based approach. However, these visual
133   Mamba models often produce single-scale, low-resolution features, limiting their ability to capture
134
      the fine-grained details and multi-scale variations required for dense prediction tasks.
135   High-Resolution Networks for Dense Prediction. The High-Resolution network was first intro-
136   duced in HRNet (Wang et al., 2020), demonstrating strong performance in tasks such as human pose
137   estimation and semantic segmentation. Its multi-resolution parallel branches combine information at
138   various scales by exchanging multi-resolution features through a multi-scale fusion module. Build-
139   ing on this, HRFormer (Yuan et al., 2021) integrates the local-window self-attention mechanism (Liu
140
      et al., 2021) with the high-resolution structure, achieving excellent results in dense prediction tasks.
      Further advancements, including Lite-HRNet (Yu et al., 2021), Dite-HRNet (Li et al., 2022), and HF-
141
      HRNet (Zhang et al., 2024a), introduce lightweight CNNs through techniques such as depthwise and
142
      dynamic convolutions, enabling high-resolution networks to be more efficiently deployed on mobile
143   devices. However, whether Mamba can perform optimally within high-resolution structures and
144   how to mitigate Mamba’s lack of inductive bias and long-range forgetting in dense prediction tasks
145   remain open areas for further investigation.

      4.1      M ULTI - RESOLUTION PARALLEL VM AMBA
195
196   Current visual Mamba models typically generate single-scale, low-resolution features, resulting in
197   significant information loss and difficulty in capturing the fine details and multi-scale variations
198   needed for dense prediction tasks. To address this, we adopt HRNet’s multi-resolution parallel
199   design (Wang et al., 2020), using parallel branches to develop the High-Resolution Visual State
200   Space Model (HRVMamba). We illustrate the overall architecture of HRVMamba in Fig. 3 (a).
201   Given an input image X ∈ RH×W ×3 , HRVMamba begins with a downsampling stem using two
202   3×3 convolutions with a stride of 2, reducing the feature resolution to H      W
                                                                               4 × 4 . It then progresses
203
      through four stages, where the streams in later stages include the previous stage’s resolutions and
      an additional lower one, with the final four branches having feature dimensions of H4 × W  4 × C1 ,
204   H     W         H    W            H     W
205    8 × 8 × C2 , 16 × 16 × C3 , and 32 × 32 × C4 . Previous studies (Yun & Ro, 2024; Ma et al., 2024)
      have shown that convolution operations on larger feature maps in early stages are more effective for
206
      visual feature extraction, so we adopt the BottleNeck structure in the first stage like HRNet. The
207
      remaining stages use our proposed Dynamic Visual State Space (DVSS) block (Fig. 3 (c)) as the
208   basic unit. The multi-scale fusion method, following HRNet, incorporates a series of upsampling
209   and downsampling blocks to merge features from different parallel branches.

457   despite long-range decay (long-range forgetting issue).

462   improves the AP to 74.2. Yet, replacing the multi-scale convolutional kernels with 3 × 3 convolution
463   does not result in any performance improvement (line 5 vs. line 3). This indicates that the multi-scale
464   convolutional kernels are effective in capturing local features at various scales, thus strengthening
465   the inductive bias for features.

      Our experimental results show that pretraining on ImageNet improves the performance of the smaller
470   model, HRVMamba-S, in pose estimation. However, for the larger model, HRVMamba-B, it can
471   even lead to a performance drop. This suggests that the pretraining strategy for SSM models might

      7     C ONCLUSION
477
478
      Visual Mamba’s performance on dense prediction tasks faces challenges such as insufficient induc-
479
      tive bias, long-range forgetting, and low-resolution output representations. To address these issues,
480   we introduce the Dynamic Visual State Space (DVSS) block, which employs multi-scale convo-
481   lutional kernels to enhance inductive bias and utilizes deformable convolutions to mitigate long-
482   range forgetting. By leveraging the multi-resolution parallel design from HRNet, we present the
483   High-Resolution Visual State Space Model (HRVMamba), which maintains high-resolution repre-
484   sentations throughout the network, ensuring effective multi-scale feature learning. Extensive exper-
485   iments demonstrate that HRVMamba achieves competitive results across various dense prediction
      benchmarks compared to CNNs, ViTs, and SSMs.
