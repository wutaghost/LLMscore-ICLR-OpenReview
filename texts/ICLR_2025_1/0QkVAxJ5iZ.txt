009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite advancements in large language models (LLMs), non-factual responses
012            remain prevalent. Unlike extensive studies on post-hoc detection of such responses,
013            this work studies non-factuality prediction (NFP), aiming to predict whether an
014            LLM will generate a non-factual response to a question before the generation
015            process. Previous efforts on NFP have demonstrated LLMs’ awareness of their
016            internal knowledge, but they still face challenges in efficiency and transferabil-
017            ity. In this work, we propose a lightweight NFP model named Factuality Lens
018
               (FacLens), which effectively probes hidden representations of questions for the
               NFP task. Besides, we discover that hidden question representations sourced from
019
               different LLMs exhibit similar NFP patterns, which enables the transferability
020
               of FacLens across LLMs to reduce development costs. Extensive experiments
021            highlight FacLens’s superiority in both effectiveness and efficiency.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Large language models (LLMs) have shown impressive abilities in understanding and generating
027   coherent text (OpenAI, 2023; Meta, 2024; Jiang et al., 2023), yet they usually struggle to provide
028   accurate facts, leading to the generation of non-factual responses (Zhang et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2024).
029   Extensive studies have devoted to detecting the non-factual responses, a task we name non-factuality
030   detection (NFD) (Manakul et al., 2023; Azaria & Mitchell, 2023; Chen et al., 2024a; 2023; Min et al.,
031   2023b). However, these post-hoc methods require response generation, which incurs computational
032   overhead. Therefore, this paper studies non-factuality prediction (NFP), which predicts the likelihood
033
      of an LLM generating a non-factual response before the response generation. Figure 1 (a) illustrates
      the difference between NFD and NFP.
034
035   To address the NFP problem, researchers have proposed to extract and analyze specific tokens within
036   a question (Mallen et al., 2023; Yüksekgönül et al., 2024), making their methods applicable to specific
037   questions. For more general questions, some methods employ multi-round conversations with the
038   LLM or fine-tune the entire LLM for NFP (Luo et al., 2023; Kadavath et al., 2022). While they have
039
      highlighted LLMs’ awareness of whether they possess certain knowledge, two limitations remain: (1)
      current NFP models lack a more efficient way to leverage such knowledge awareness, and (2) they
040
      are trained for individual LLMs, lacking transferability for rapid adaptation to new LLMs.
041
042   Inspired by recent studies on monitoring and manipulating hidden representations to improve LLM
043   performance (Zou et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b), we assume that the knowledge
044   awareness has been embedded in the hidden representation of a question. Based on this, we design
045
      a lightweight model, Factuality Lens (FacLens), which effectively probes hidden representations
      of input questions for NFP. Figure 1 (b) illustrates the workflow of FacLens. With its lightweight
046
      structure and rapid acquisition of hidden question representations, FacLens achieves high efficiency
047
      in both training and prediction (see Table 2). To obtain the training data of FacLens, we prompt the
048   target LLM to produce responses to questions from high-quality question-answering (QA) datasets.
049   We then compare the LLM-generated answers with the golden answers, assigning binary factual/non-
050   factual labels to the responses. Nevertheless, extending FacLens to support multiple LLMs becomes
051   resource-intensive and time-consuming, because each LLM must conduct response generation for the
052   training data construction. Fortunately, we discover the transferability of FacLens, allowing us to
053   assign the binary labels on just one LLM and apply unsupervised domain adaptation to rapidly apply
      FacLens to other LLMs without collecting new labels, thereby improving the development efficiency.

072
      The transferability of FacLens is inspired by research on human cognition, which shows individuals
073
      with similar cognitive styles and encoding strategies exhibit similar brain activity when performing
074   the same task (Miller et al., 2012). We thus hypothesize that different LLMs have similar cognitive
075   patterns in terms of knowledge awareness (i.e., similar NFP patterns), as current LLMs generally
076   follow the Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) architecture and share overlapping training corpora. To
077   validate this hypothesis, we define a collection of hidden question representations sourced from a
078   certain LLM as a data domain. Our experimental results show that a FacLens trained on data from
079   multiple domains performs similarly to the one trained on a single domain, suggesting that different
080   individual domains do not have a significant concept shift (Moreno-Torres et al., 2012).1 Building
081   on this insight, we can quickly apply FacLens to support a new LLM through unsupervised domain
082
      adaptation (Smola et al., 2007; Liu et al., 2022). To enhance the adaptation process, we introduce a
      question-aligned strategy to improve the efficacy of mini-batch training.
083
084   Overall, this paper makes the following contributions:
085
086        • Findings. We verify that hidden question representations in LLMs contain valuable information
087
             for NFP, i.e., LLMs’ activation during question-thinking mostly reveal whether they know the
             factual answers. Furthermore, we show that different LLMs exhibit similar NFP patterns to
088
             enable transfer learning of cross-LLM FacLens, which can align with human cognitive processes.
089
090        • Method. We propose a NFP model named FacLens, which features efficient development and
091
             application. Furthermore, we enable effective unsupervised domain adaptation of FacLens with
             a question-aligned mini-batch training strategy. To the best of our knowledge, this is a pioneer
092
             work to train a transferable NFP model for multiple LLMs.
093
094        • Experiments. All analyses are based on extensive experiments involving four popular open-
095
             source LLMs and three widely used factual QA datasets. The results show that FacLens
             outperforms the baselines in terms of both AUC metric and runtime. Human evaluation also
096
             demonstrates the effectiveness of FacLens.

135   3.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
136
      In this section, we formally define the concept of non-factual response and the problem of NFP for
137
      LLMs. Subsequently, we define the problem of transferable NFP across LLMs, which has rarely been
138
      discussed before.
139
140   Definition 1 Non-Factual Response. Given an LLM m ∈ M and a question q ∈ Q, m generates an
141   answer s to the question. If the answer s fails to convey the queried fact, it is a non-factual response.
142
143   Problem 1 Non-Factuality Prediction in an LLM (NFP). Given an LLM m ∈ M and a question
144   q ∈ Q, the objective is to learn a function f (m, q) → y, where y = 1 if m will generate a non-factual
145   response to q and y = 0 otherwise.
146
      Problem 2 Transferable Non-Factuality Prediction Across LLMs. Given an LLM m1 ∈ M, an
147
      LLM m2 ∈ M, and a question set Q, NFP labels have been collected based on Qtrain ⊂ Q for
148   m1 , resulting in a training set {((m1 , qi ) , y1,i )}qi ∈Qtrain . The goal is to utilize the training set and
149   m2 ∈ M to learn a function f (m, q) → y, where m ∈ {m1 , m2 } and q ∈ Q.
150
151   3.2   N ON -FACTUALITY P REDICTION (NFP) DATASETS
152
153   NFP Dataset Construction. Given an LLM m and a QA dataset, for each question q ∈ Q, we
154   assign a binary label y to the (m, q) pair, where y = 1 if m fails to generate the golden answer
155   for q, and y = 0 otherwise. The goal of NFP is to predict the labels prior to answer generation.
156   Specifically, we follow previous work (Mallen et al., 2023) to adopt QA datasets with short answers
157   like entity mentions, and mark an LLM’s response as non-factual (i.e., y = 1) if no sub-string of the
158
      response matches any of the gold answers.2 To ensure the experimental reproducibility, we set the
      LLM’s decoding strategy to greedy search rather than top-p or top-k sampling. We have also run the

      4     M ETHODOLOGY
175
176
      In this section, we propose a lightweight and transferable NFP model named FacLens. Our findings
177   show that the hidden representations of users’ input questions in LLMs contain valuable patterns that
178   are useful for NFP. Additionally, we show that hidden question representations derived from different
179   LLMs share similar NFP patterns, facilitating the transfer learning of FacLens across multiple LLMs.
180   Finally, we implement the cross-LLM FacLens using unsupervised domain adaptation.
181
182   4.1   FAC L ENS
183
184   Inspired by representation engineering (Zou et al., 2023; Chuang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023; Azaria &
185   Mitchell, 2023; Su et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Yüksekgönül et al., 2024), we posit that before an
186   LLM generates a response, the hidden question representation in the LLM contain potential patterns
187
      for predicting whether the LLM can generate the requested facts. In this section, we implement
      FacLens to verify this hypothesis.
188
189   Factuality Lens (FacLens). We introduce FacLens, a learnable lightweight network to extract useful
190   patterns from hidden question representations for NFP. Given an LLM m and an input question q, we
191   can quickly acquire the hidden states corresponding to input tokens. In a certain layer, we use the
192   hidden states corresponding to the last input token as the question’s hidden representation x. Then
193
      we use an encoder genc to transform the question’s hidden representation into a latent feature space,
      where we presume that the NFP patterns are represented. Afterwards, a linear classifier gclf is set
194
      upon genc for classification. Formally, based on the ℓ-th hidden layer of m, FacLens predicts by,
195
196                         p = f (m≤ℓ , q) = gclf (genc (m≤ℓ (q))) = gclf (genc (x))                           (1)
197   where m≤ℓ (·) denotes the function composed of the ℓ-th transformer layer and its preceding lay-
198   ers, genc is implemented by a lightweight multi-layer perceptron (MLP)3 , gclf is implemented
199   by a linear layer with the Softmax function, and p is a two-dimensional vector revealing the
200   probability of (not) producing non-factual responses. Based on a set of labeled NFP instances
201   {(m≤ℓ (qi ) , yi )}qi ∈Qtrain ∪Qval , where Qtrain and Qval denote question sets used for training and
202   validation, respectively, we can train a FacLens for m with the classic cross-entropy (CE) loss.
203   Hidden Question Representation vs. Hidden Response Representation. In order to explore
204   the feasibility of using only hidden representations of users’ input questions for non-factuality
205   identification, we compare FacLens with SAPLMA (Azaria & Mitchell, 2023) and INSIDE (Chen
206   et al., 2024a). They are two typical post-hoc methods that employ hidden representations of LLM-

239   4.2      T RANSFERABILITY OF FAC L ENS
240
      Why Explore the Transferability of FacLens Across LLMs. When it comes to multiple LLMs, the
241
      process of training data construction becomes resource-intensive and time-consuming, because each
242
      LLM needs to conduct costly response generation for the training data construction (see Section 3.2).
243   In this subsection, we discover the transferability of FacLens, which allows us to label training data
244   on just one LLM and transfer the FacLens to support other LLMs. Appendix B illustrates the more
245   efficient labeling process enabled by the transferability of FacLens.
246
      Notably, the transferability of FacLens is inspired by insights from human cognition. Research has
247
      shown that individuals with similar cognitive styles and encoding strategies exhibit similar brain
248
      activity when performing specific tasks (Miller et al., 2012). In this analogy, we consider LLMs as
249   individuals with similar cognitive styles and encoding strategies, given their common reliance on
250   the Transformer architecture and overlapping training datasets. Accordingly, we assume that LLMs
251   display similar cognitive patterns used by NFP.
252
      Why Domain Adaptation is Effective for Transferring FacLens Across LLMs. Domain adaptation
253
      (DA) is an approach in transfer learning that transfers information from a source domain to improve
254
      performance in a target domain (Ben-David et al., 2006; Liu et al., 2022; Moreno-Torres et al.,
255   2012). The premise of DA is that the source and target domains have distinct marginal probability
256   distributions P (X), but share similar conditional probability distributions P (y|X) (i.e., no significant
257   concept shift) (Liu et al., 2022; Moreno-Torres et al., 2012). In this paper, we refer to the domain as,
258
259   Remark 1 Let the variable X represent the hidden question representation in an LLM. A data
260   domain D refers to a collection of hidden question representations sourced from a certain LLM.
261

262   can say that similar NFP patterns exist in the hidden question representations sourced from different
263   LLMs, and thus we can conduct unsupervised domain adaptation to derive a cross-LLM FacLens.

368   Question-Aligned Strategy for Mini-Batch Training. MMD loss involves multiplications of feature
369   matrices, which can lead to out-of-memory on GPUs when the number of training instances exceeds a
370   certain threshold. To address this issue, mini-batch training is used by cross-LLM domain adaptation
371   of FacLens. Specifically, in each mini-batch, two sets of questions QS and QT are sampled from
372   Qtrain to empirically approximate the population distributions of the source and target domains. This
373   raises a question: should QS and QT be identical?
374   Given a range of questions, the distribution P (Z) is determined by the LLM. In a mini-batch, the
375   number of sampled questions is limited, so the estimation of PS (Z) and PT (Z) within the mini-batch
376   is likely to be affected by the sampling process. Hence, we propose to use the same set of questions in
377   each mini-batch, i.e., QS = QT , to alleviate the influence of sampling process in estimating the true
      distance between PS (Z) and PT (Z). We name the strategy question-aligned mini-batch training.

404   5.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
405
406   We compare FacLens with existing NFP methods, which have been introduced in Section 2, in-
407   cluding Entity-Popularity (Mallen et al., 2023), SAT Probe (Yüksekgönül et al., 2024), Self-
408
      Familiarity (Luo et al., 2023), and Self-Evaluation (Kadavath et al., 2022). As Self-Evaluation fully
      fine-tunes the LLM for NFP, we adopt LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) as a baseline to conduct parameter-
409
      efficient fine-tuning for NFP. We additionally consider a Prompting-based method, which directly
410
      asks the LLM whether the LLM knows the factual answer to the given question. Moreover, inspired
411   by using the perplexity to evaluate the factual precision of responses (Min et al., 2023a), we consider
412   perplexity (PPL) on the input question as a baseline (see Appendix C).4 PPL measures how well
413   a language model (LM) predicts a given text. As a low PPL value suggests that an LM has likely
414   learned relevant texts, we regard PPL on the input question as a non-factuality predictor. Due to
415   space limitation, we provide the hyper-parameter settings in Appendix D. As the number of positive
416   samples is larger than that of negative samples (see Table 3), we adopt AUC, a widely used metric for
417   imbalanced binary classification, as the evaluation metric.

535   In this paper, we find that the hidden representations of users’ input questions contain valuable
536   information for identifying potential non-factual responses (i.e., NFP). We also discover that similar
537   NFP patterns emerge in hidden question representations sourced from different LLMs. These findings
538   support our lightweight and transferable NFP model, FacLens, which offers a more efficient approach
539   to leveraging LLMs’ knowledge awareness for NFP. Extensive experiments show the superiority of
      FacLens, and we hope this work can inspire future research on improving LLMs’ factuality.

      A    S TATISTICS OF QA DATASETS
737
738   During the NFP dataset construction, to reduce the false negative samples, we exclude multiple-choice
739   questions because their LLM-generated responses are likely to mention both correct and incorrect
740   answers. Additionally, we exclude questions where the golden answers are three characters or fewer,
741   as such short strings are likely to appear as substrings within unrelated words. PQ initially contains
742   14,267 questions. After eliminating duplicates and removing the above special questions, 11,360
743   unique questions are remained. EQ contains 100K questions. We randomly sample 7,200 questions
744   from EQ, ensuring uniform coverage across all question topics. After eliminating duplicates and
745   removing special questions, we retain 7,159 questions from EQ. The full NQ dataset is 42Gb, so we
746
      download a simplified development set of NQ. In this paper, we focus on the case of short answers,
      so we select questions whose answers are comprised of 30 characters or less. Consequently, our
747
      dataset comprises 1,244 questions sourced from NQ. Table 3 shows the ratios of positive and negative
748
      samples in each NFP dataset, where a pair of QA dataset and LLM corresponds to an NFP dataset.

      D    H YPER -PARAMETER S ETTINGS
770
      Our experiments are conducted based on 4 * 80G NVIDIA Tesla A800 GPUs. We implement the
771
      encoder genc of FacLens by a 3-layer MLP, setting the dimension of each MLP layer to 256. We use
772
      the Adam optimizer with weight decay 1e-4. The hyper-parameters determined on the validation set
773   include: the training epochs (set the maximum epochs to 100), the learning rate ∈ {1e-3, 1e-4} for
774   single-LLM FacLens. Considering that the training questions from NQ is relatively small, we set
775   the learning rate of FacLens to 1e-4 on NFP datasets derived from NQ. The default learning rate of
776   cross-LLM FacLens is set to 1e-5. Due to the memory limitation, we minimize the MMD loss via
777   mini-batch training with batch size of 64.
778
      In terms of baselines, we adopt hyper-parameter settings recommended by their authors. Since we
779   extend PPL to be calculated in each hidden layer, we determine the specific layer according to PPL’s
780   performance on the labeled data. We introduce the Prompting-based method, which encourages an
781   LLM to answer whether it knows the factual responses via prompt “Question: {question}\Can you
782   provide a factual response to the above question? If you can, please reply yes or Yes. If you can not,
783   please reply no or No.\nAnswer: {label}\n”. The probabilities of predicting tokens “yes”, “Yes”, “no”
784   and “No” are normalized for prediction. For the Self-Evaluation (Fully FT), we train the model on
785   4*80G A800 GPUs, with learning rate of 1e-6, batch size of 32, epochs of 12, and we also determine
786   the training epochs based on the performance on the validation set. Self-Evaluation (Fully FT) needs
787
      to fully fine-tune an LLM. Therefore, to mitigate overfitting, the learning rate scheduler employs
      a cosine decay strategy with 5% of the training steps dedicated to linear warm-up. Additionally,
788
      the final learning rate is set to one-tenth of its initial value. For LoRA, we integrate adapters on
789
      all “q_proj”, “k_proj”, “v_proj”, and “o_proj” layers, while maintaining the original weights of the
790   language model unchanged. The configuration is as follows: we specify a rank of 128 and an alpha of
791   256, with a learning rate of 1e-4, a batch size of 32, and the training is conducted over 32 epochs. We
792   employ the same learning rate scheduler as used in Fully SFT. Because LoRA is a parameter-efficient
793   fine-tuning technique, the training process requires only a single 80G A800 GPU.

      E    A PPLICATION L IMITATIONS OF C ERTAIN BASELINES
796
797
      In Table 1, Entity-Popularity and SAT Probe are not suitable for certain datasets. Here, we explain the
798   reasons. Entity-Popularity uses Wikipedia page views to approximate the entity popularity. However,
799   EQ and NQ datasets do not provide the relevant Wikipedia page views, and not every subject entity
800   in the two datasets can be uniquely matched to a Wikidata entity. As a result, Entity-Popularity is
801   unsuitable for EQ and NQ. For the baseline SAT Probe, each question is assumed to contain constraint
802   tokens, and the model extracts LLMs’ attention to the constraint tokens to probe factuality. The
803   authors of SAT Probe have restricted the formats of questions to directly identify the constraint tokens.
804   However, extracting constraint tokens from free-form questions can be challenging. For PQ and EQ,
805   which are template-based, obtaining constraint tokens is relatively straightforward. However, SAT
806
      Probe is not suitable for NQ, as questions in NQ come from real users and exhibit diverse structures.
807   The core of SAT Probe is using an LLM’s attention weights to constraint tokens within a question to
808   reflect the LLM’s factual accuracy. In the original paper, the SAT probe is implemented by a linear
809   layer, optimized by logistic regression. To compare the effectiveness of hidden representations and
      attention weights, we employ the same MLP structure and CE loss for both SAT Probe and FacLens.

898   User Interface of the Demo. We have implemented a demo of FacLens, whose web user interface is
899   shown in Figure 11. In the demo, a user can choose a specific LLM, and then enters a question in
900   the text box. After submitting the question, FacLens will return whether the LLM knows the factual
901   answer. Then the user can decide whether to call the LLM to generate the response. If the user
902   decides to query the LLM, the demo will provide the response generated by the LLM. According to
903   the prediction of FacLens and the LLM’s response, the user can score the performance of FacLens.
904   FacLens in the Demo. Taking LLaMA2-7B-Chat (abbreviated as LLaMA2) as the example, we
905   integrate its NFP datasets, i.e., LLaMA2-PQ, LLaMA2-EQ, and LLaMA2-NQ, to train a FacLens.
906   Specifically, we use instances from LLaMA2-PQ, LLaMA2-EQ for training, and use instances from
907   LLaMA2-NQ for validation. That is because NQ, released by Google, consists of questions posed
908   by real users. We set the learning rate to 1e-3, and determine the training epochs according to the
909   performance on the validation set. On the validation set, we use FacLens to predict the probability
910   p (y = 1|x) for each instance. Then we calculate the averaged probability p (y = 1|x)pos based on
911   positive instances in the validation set, as well as the averaged probability p (y = 1|x)neg based on
912   negative instances in the validation set. p (y = 1|x)pos and p (y = 1|x)neg are used as the thresholds
913   for predicting whether the LLM knows the factual answers. Clearly, given a question, FacLens
914   predicts the probability of LLaMA2 generating a non-factual response. If the probability is larger than
915   p (y = 1|x)pos , the demo outputs “The LLM does not know the factual answer”; If the probability is
916   smaller than p (y = 1|x)neg , the demo outputs “The LLM knows the factual answer”; otherwise, the
917   demo outputs “I am not sure if the LLM knows the factual answer”. Similarly, we train FacLens for
      LLaMA3-8B-Instruct, Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2, and Qwen2-1.5B-Instruct, respectively.
