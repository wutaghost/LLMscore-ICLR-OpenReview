                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Selection bias is a prevalent challenge in real-world data analysis, often stemming
011
               from biased historical censoring policies. While there is a growing body of literature
               on fairness in mitigating accuracy disparities, few studies have considered the
012
               potential impact of selection bias in training data. Depending on the selection
013
               mechanism, significant differences can arise between the population distribution
014            and the training data distribution. Therefore, the training fairness metric can be
015            heavily biased, leading to unfair learning. To address this issue under the fair
016            regression problem, we propose weighting adjustments in the fairness constraint,
017            which results in a novel fair regression estimator. Despite non-convexity, we derive
018            an efficient algorithm to obtain a globally optimal solution. This work pioneers the
019            integration of weighting adjustments into the fair regression problem, introducing
020            a novel methodology to constrain accuracy disparities under arbitrary thresholds.
021

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   The use of machine learning methods has become increasingly popular in critical sectors such as
026   employment, education, healthcare, and criminal justice. However, bias and unfairness arising from
027   prediction algorithms have led to growing concerns. Notably, accuracy disparities across different
028   demographic groups have been observed in various scenarios (Barocas & Selbst, 2016). For example,
029   Seyyed-Kalantari et al. (2021) showed AI systems consistently underdiagnosed historically under-
030   served patient populations, such as female patients and black patients. In another case, analysis
031   conducted by Julia Angwin & Kirchner (2016) highlights that the recidivism risk scale of COMPAS
032   software exhibits a higher false positive rate for black defenders compared to their white counterparts.
033   While recent efforts have been devoted to mitigating algorithmic accuracy disparity (Agarwal et al.,
034   2019; Donini et al., 2018; Chi et al., 2021), most of them presume the training data unbiasedly
035   represents the population, which is a usually violated assumption in the real world. In practice,
036   datasets may suffer from selection bias, originating from historically biased decision-making, thereby
037   distorting their representativeness of the population. Machine learning algorithms trained on such
038   datasets induce bias on population-level estimands, posing a remaining violation of fairness, some-
039
      times referred to as residual unfairness (Kallus & Zhou, 2018). Many sectors grappling with AI
      fairness concerns have encountered such biases in their data collection procedures. For instance,
040
      data on loan default is typically collected only from applicants who were historically approved. This
041
      dataset is then used to develop loan approval policies applicable to all applicants, introducing an
042   inherent bias (Kallus & Zhou, 2018).
043
044   In this paper, we introduce weighting adjustments to the model trained on a dataset under selection
045
      bias, thereby extending the model accuracy parity to the population level. Our primary focus is
      on regression problems with continuous outcomes (Y ) and a binary protected attribute (A). Some
046
      prior works (e.g., Chi et al., 2021; Zhao, 2021; Oneto et al., 2019) have made strides in mitigating
047
      regressor accuracy disparity without considering selection bias. Chi et al. (2021) reduced the accuracy
048   disparity of a regressor through adversarially training a fair representation that minimizes the distance
049   between two group distributions while maximizing the representation’s predictive power. Similarly,
050   Zhao (2021) showed for Lipschitz continuous predictors, the accuracy disparity can be reduced by
051   training the model on a fair representation that minimizes the Wasserstein distance between two
052   group distributions. Oneto et al. (2019) converted the regression problem to binary classifications
053   by discretizing the continuous label and adopted the same technique as in Donini et al. (2018),
      which regulates the disparity of true positive rate across the sensitive groups. However, research in


                                                         1
054
      mitigating accuracy disparity in regression under selection bias remains relatively scarce. We present
055   a few related works below.
056
057
      Related work Many studies addressing the impact of selection bias on algorithmic fairness focus
058   on scenarios where the training data consists of samples from the population of interest pT , with their
059   covariates (X) always observed and outcomes (Y ) selectively observed (Kallus & Zhou, 2018; Du
060   et al., 2022; Coston et al., 2021b). Kallus & Zhou (2018) used an estimated propensity score ratio
061   between the target and training distribution to address the covariate shift caused by selection. Du
062   et al. (2022) considered a special sample selection process and used the Inverse Mills Ratio derived
063   from the Heckman Model to correct the selection bias. Coston et al. (2021b) used extrapolation to
064   infer the unobserved labels and de-biased the fairness estimation over a Rashomon Set of the model.
065   In practice, however, data subject to selection may also exhibit incompleteness in their covariates
066
      (X). We provide a recruitment example to illustrate this scenario in Example 1 below. As far as we
      know, only Zhang & Long (2021) considered this more general setting of selection bias characterized
067
      by incomplete covariates (X) and/or outcome (Y ) in fairness-related literature. They utilize inverse
068
      probability weighting (IPW) to assess the bias in fairness metric estimands while not aiming to
069   control the algorithmic unfairness. Among all these works, the most relevant one we could find is Du
070   et al. (2022), which addresses the selection bias issue in the regression problem in order to construct
071   a fair regressor. However, the method in Du et al. (2022) is developed under a more restrictive
072   setting than ours, assuming that the propensity score follows the probit model of a linear function of
073   the covariates, which is not required in our assumption. Moreover, Du et al. (2022) formulates the
074   constrained optimization with a Lagrangian form that is similar to ours, but controls the trade-off
075   by manually tuning the dual variable, while we develop an algorithm that constrains the accuracy
076   disparities under any predetermined thresholds.
077
078   Our contributions: This work offers a few novel contributions. Firstly, we introduce weighting
079
      techniques to address selection bias in fair regression problems, a topic that has received limited
      attention in the literature. Our method is developed under a more general setting than previous studies
080
      in this area. Secondly, there is a lack of research on constraining accuracy disparity in regression
081
      problems, and none have considered constraining accuracy disparity below arbitrary thresholds. In
082   this work, we propose a computationally efficient algorithm that limits training accuracy disparity
083   below arbitrary thresholds and provides a globally optimal solution despite the non-convexity of the
084   constrained optimization. Thirdly, we propose a novel parameter tuning method by approximating
085   leave-one-out cross-validation in constrained regression problems, significantly reducing computation
086   time compared to commonly adopted K-fold cross-validation.

113   Training data distribution under selection bias We assume the training samples are collected
114   under potential selection bias. We use Si ∈ {0, 1} to indicate whether the i-th datum remains fully
115   observed after selection. When Si = 1, we obtain Zi . Conversely, when Si = 0, part of Zi is not
116   observed. The conditional distribution of Zi given Si = 1 is denoted by pD , which is also referred to
117   as training distribution.
118   Due to the selection process, the training distribution pD is not necessarily the same as the population
119   distribution pT , and the empirical group risk can be biased for estimating the conditional risk. As such,
120   adjustment will be needed to correct the selection bias and its effect on the estimated (conditional)
121   risk. The issue has been discussed in Zhang & Long (2021); Kallus & Zhou (2018); Du et al. (2022);
122   Coston et al. (2021a). We present an illustrative example in Example 1.
123   Example 1 (Recruitment pre-screening). Consider a recruitment system where the success of a job
124   interview (Y ) is predicted based on a candidate’s major (X1 ), undergraduate GPA (X2 ), and an
125   assessment score (X3 ), with gender as the protected attribute A. A pre-screening process is first
126   applied to filter candidates based on X1 and X2 . If a candidate fails this pre-screening (S = 0),
127   their X3 and Y will become unrecorded (i.e., not selected). Therefore, if the pre-screening policy
128   favors majors predominantly chosen by one gender group (A = 1), the other group (A = 0) may be
129
      underrepresented in the training data (pD ). Consequently, models trained on such data can propagate
      biased estimates of risk RT and fairness metrics in the population pT .
