009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            It is common practice to combine deep neural networks to ensembles. These deep
012            ensembles can profit from the cancellation of errors effect: Errors by ensemble
013            members may average out and the ensemble achieves better generalization perfor-
014            mance than each individual network. Bayesian neural networks learn a posterior
015            distribution over model parameters, and sampling and weighting networks accord-
016
               ing to this posterior yields an ensemble model referred to as Bayes ensemble.
               In this study, we stress that neither the sampling nor the weighting in a Bayes
017
               ensemble are particularly well-suited for increasing generalization performance,
018
               as they do not support the cancellation of errors effect, which is evident in the
019            limit from the Bernstein-von Mises theorem for misspecified models. In contrast,
020            a weighted average of models, where the weights are optimized by minimizing
021            a PAC-Bayesian generalization bound, can improve generalization performance.
022            This requires that the optimization takes correlations between models into account,
023            which can be achieved by minimizing the tandem loss at the cost that hold-out data
024            for estimating error correlations need to be available. The PAC-Bayesian weighting
025            increases the robustness against correlated models and models with lower perfor-
026            mance in an ensemble. This allows us to safely add several models from the same
027
               learning process to an ensemble, instead of using early-stopping for selecting a
               single weight configuration. Our study presents empirical results supporting these
028
               conceptual considerations on four different classification datasets. We show that
029
               state-of-the-art Bayes ensembles from the literature, despite being computationally
030            demanding, do not improve over simple uniformly weighted deep ensembles and
031            cannot match the performance of deep ensembles weighted by optimizing the
032            tandem loss, which additionally come with non-vacuous generalization guarantees.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Combining different deep neural networks to an ensemble model is a common way to increase
038   generalization performance (Bishop and Bishop, 2023; Goodfellow et al., 2016). Such deep ensembles
039
      can profit from the cancellation of errors effect (Eckhardt and Lee, 1985): When the individual
      networks perform better than random guessing and make independent errors, the errors in an additive
040
      ensemble (or committee) average out and the ensemble tends to outperform the individual models
041
      (Hansen and Salamon, 1990; Perrone and Cooper, 1993; Dietterich, 2000). Many strategies to create
042   the networks for an ensemble have been explored (e.g. Buschjäger et al., 2020; D’Angelo and Fortuin,
043   2021; Dziugaite and Roy, 2017; Garipov et al., 2018; Huang et al., 2017; Jiang et al., 2017; Lee
044   et al., 2015; Liu and Yao, 1999; Masegosa et al., 2020; Monteith et al., 2011; Ortega et al., 2022;
045   Pérez-Ortiz et al., 2021), however, simple deep ensembles, which solely rely on repeating the neural
046   network training with random weight initializations and stochastic training to generate ensemble
047   members, often already improve generalization (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017).
048   One way to build a neural network ensemble is based on the predictive posterior of Bayesian neural
049   networks (Adlam et al., 2020; Aitchison, 2021; Chen et al., 2014; Farquhar et al., 2020; Gal and
050   Ghahramani, 2016; Grünwald, 2012; Izmailov et al., 2021; Kapoor et al., 2022; Kendall and Gal, 2017;
051   MacKay, 1992b; Maddox et al., 2019; Monteith et al., 2011; Nabarro et al., 2022; Neal, 1992; 1996;
052   2011; Pearce et al., 2020; Ritter et al., 2018; Welling and Teh, 2011; Wenzel et al., 2020a;b; Wilson
053   and Izmailov, 2020; Zhang et al., 2020), and this approach has gained attention recently (Bachmann
      et al., 2022; Fortuin et al., 2022; Rudner et al., 2022; Wiese et al., 2023). A Bayesian neural network

054
      learns a posterior distribution over the network weights (MacKay, 1992a; Neal, 1996). Averaging
055   networks sampled from this posterior yields an approximation of the Bayesian model average (BMA)
056   and is referred to as Bayes ensemble. This approach comes with great expectations, for example,
057   Domingos (2000) stated ‘Given the “correct” model space and prior distribution, Bayesian model
058   averaging is the optimal method for making predictions; in other words, no other approach can
059   consistently achieve lower error rates than it does.’ We argue that neither the sampling nor the
060   weighting in a Bayes ensemble are particularly well-suited for increasing generalization performance
061   in theory and practice (see also Ortega et al., 2022). The Bernstein-von Mises theorem (assuming
062   identifiability of the model) shows that the BMA converges towards the maximum likelihood point
063
      estimate with growing dataset size (Kleijn and Van der Vaart, 2012). In the limit, it will eventually
      concentrate on a single model without exploiting ensemble diversity. Thus, the Bayes ensemble
064
      typically does not leverage the cancellation of errors effect. This is particularly problematic because
065
      the considered model space in the BMA setting is typically not ‘correct’: The Bayes ensemble itself
066   is in general outside the model space from which its members are sampled – and in the space of
067   ensemble models the Bayes ensemble cannot be expected to be optimal.
068
069
      PAC-Bayesian methods (McAllester, 1998) provide an alternative approach to incorporate prior
      information about models and to improve the weighting of ensemble members. We bring forward
070
      optimizing the weighting of deep ensemble members using a PAC-generalization bound based on
071
      the tandem loss, which accounts for pairwise correlations between networks (Masegosa et al., 2020).
072   This allows us to maintain diversity and increase generalization performance of the deep ensemble.
073   The PAC-Bayesian weight optimization is especially useful when intermediate weight configurations
074   (snapshots) from model training are considered, in which case the optimization performs model
075   selection taking both individual network performance and ensemble diversity into account. The price
076   to pay for the improved weighting is additional hold-out data for the optimization of the weighs,
077   however, then the resulting PAC-Bayesian bound provides a rigorous performance guarantee.
078   Our study transfers existing, but scattered and often partly neglected knowledge on the (1) behavior
079   of Bayesian model averaging (BMA), in particular its suboptimality in terms of generalization
080   performance in the (typical) misspecified setting, (2) cancellation of errors, (3) and second order
081   PAC-Bayesian bounds to ensembling of deep neural networks (DNNs). The main contributions can
082   be summarized as:
083
084
             • We discuss the conceptual differences between simple, Bayes and PAC-Bayesian deep
               ensembles, stressing that Bayes ensembles do not consider the cancellation of errors effect,
085
               which explain some observations in the current literature (e.g., on “cold posteriors”).
086
087
             • We support our conceptual arguments through unbiased experiments on DNN benchmark
               problems that were used to promote BMA. Our results on four datasets show that complex
088
               Bayesian approximate inference methods can often be surpassed by more efficient simple
089
               deep ensembles. Bagging decreased predictive performance, showing the trade-off between
090            the degree of randomization and single-network performance given limited training data.
091
             • We show that the optimization of a PAC-Bayesian generalization bound using the tandem
092
               loss can improve the predictive performance of deep ensembles and provides non-vacuous
093            generalization guarantees. This is in contrast to what could have been expected from the
094            results by Ortega et al. (2022). Our results stress the importance of second order PAC-
095            Bayesian analysis for ensembles.
096
             • We demonstrate that the inclusion of intermediate checkpoints from the same training run
097            in a neural network ensemble can increase its predictive performance, especially when the
098            PAC-Bayesian bound optimization weights their contributions.
099

149   Deep ensembles. Deep ensembles (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017) are ensembles with deep neural
150   networks as members. More specifically, we refer to a deep ensemble if the networks all share
151
      the same structure. These networks are typically the result of independent training processes. The
      diversity is a result of random initialization and the stochastic optimization. This randomness alone
152
      can yield well-performing ensembles, while additional randomization of the training data by bootstrap
153
      aggregation (bagging) bears the risk of the ensemble being worse than a single network trained on
154   all data (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2015). Other ways to create deep ensemble
155   members include the use of a cyclical learning rate schedule and taking the intermediate checkpoints
156   as snapshot ensembles (SSEs) (Huang et al., 2017) as well as searching for other ensemble members
157   in neighborhood of a single pre-trained network (fast geometric ensembling (Garipov et al., 2018)).
158   While being introduced as an alternative to Bayesian approaches, Bayesian interpretations of deep
159   ensembles can be found in recent work (D’Angelo and Fortuin, 2021; Wilson and Izmailov, 2020).

195   Bayes ensembles and cancellation of errors. As outlined above, general ensemble methods and
196   Bayesian neural networks have different motivations. In a weighted additive ensemble, a finite set of
197   hypotheses from H are combined to a new model hρ ∈ HE from the hypothesis class
198
199                       HE = hρ (x|h1 , . . . , hM ) M ∈ N, ρ ∈ RM , hi ∈ H .                         (4)
200
201   As discussed in Section 2, hρ can generalize better than the individual h1 , . . . , hM if the ensemble
202   members are diverse in the sense that their errors are not strongly correlated. Typically, H ⊂ HE , and
203   for most ensemble methods this is key. The ensemble members selected from H are often referred to
204
      as weak learners and are not expected to have a very low risk (generalization performance) as long as
      they are better than random guessing: The desired model is expected to be in HE \ H. Boosting with
205
      decision stumps is a prime example (Freund et al., 1996).
206

210

211
      that ensures identifiability, that is, different parameters generate different hypotheses. With increasing
212
      training data set size n = |D| the uncertainty about the desired model decreases, and p(w | D) should
213   concentrate on w∗ (i.e., the weights of the desired model h∗ = hw∗ ). This is formally shown by the

233   Thus, as already argued by Masegosa (2020, sec. 4), from the perspective of general ensemble
234
      learning and the goal of finding a model with minimum risk, a sample from the posterior p(w | D)
      does not appear to be a particularly good choice for ensemble members. As the sample will be
235
      distributed around the maximum likelihood estimate hw∗ ∈ H, the ensemble members cannot be
236
      expected to be very diverse, and the lack of diversity reduces the cancellation of errors effect. The
237   Bayes ensemble will tend to the model with the highest likelihood in H – and not to the model with
238   the highest likelihood in HE . One may well argue that the presented asymptotic argument is of little
239   practical relevance if H already has a high capacity (e.g., consists of overparameterized deep neural
240   networks), because then hw∗ ∈ H may be close to the optimal solution. However, the important
241   point is that even for finite n, the M ensemble members can be expected to be quite similar in their
242   predictive behavior. Thus, we formulate the following hypothesis:
243
244
      Hypothesis 1 The Bayes ensemble is not a particularly good way of selecting and weighting networks
      in a deep ensemble.
245
246   While this hypothesis appears to be evident given the theoretical considerations above, many studies
247   evaluate the performance of the Bayes ensemble by measuring generalization performance on a test
248   set and provide favorable comparisons to baselines suggesting the posterior predictive as a way to
249   maximize generalization performance. This study presents unbiased comparisons of Bayes ensembles
250   with simple deep ensemble approaches to test Hypothesis 1.
251   A Bayesian way to finding the best generalizing ensemble is to lift the Bayesian reasoning from H
252   to HE . This has been demonstrated by Monteith et al. (2011), who apply BMA not to hw ∈ H, but
253   to hρ ∈ HE , where HE are all ensembles of a constant size M . In such an approach, the posterior
254   distribution contracts around the maximum likelihood estimate of the best weighting of ensemble
255   members. Thus, we go from a distribution over the weights of a single neural network to a distribution
256   over all parameters of the M networks and the ensemble weights. This growth in the number of
257   dimensions renders the approach computationally infeasible in larger settings, however.
258
259   PAC-Bayesian deep ensembles. If Hypothesis 1 holds true, this does neither mean that we cannot
260   do better than uniform averaging given M models from H (as already pointed out in early works
261
      on ensembles, e.g., Krogh and Sollich, 1997) nor that we cannot make use of priors and Bayesian
      reasoning to improve ensembles. For example, there is no need to put weight on an ensemble member
262
      that is worse than random guessing or always errs when another ensemble member errs (i.e., it can
263
      never correct an error). Lorenzen et al. (2019); Masegosa et al. (2020); Wu et al. (2021) Given the
264   results by Masegosa et al. (2020); Wu et al. (2021), it appears to be promising to weight ensemble
265   members based on PAC-Bayesian generalization bounds optimized using the tandem loss. It is
266   crucial to consider a second order bound that takes interactions between ensemble members – error
267   cancellation – into account, otherwise the approach suffers from the same problem as BMA and
268   concentrates all weight on a single ensemble member in the limit. However, the bounds require for
269   each ensemble member hi that there are data points Di not used for training hi and that for each
      pair of models hi and hj the overlap Di ∩ Dj is large enough for a good enough estimate of the

277   Hypothesis 2 PAC-Bayesian weighting optimized using the tandem loss can improve the general-
278
      ization performance of a deep ensemble, in particular if extra data for computing the bound are
      available, and provide non-vacuous performance guarantees.
279
280   In most deep ensemble approaches, each ensemble member is the result of an independent training
281   process, where the weight configuration is chosen by early-stopping (requiring some hold-out data) or
282   simply after a predefined number of learning iterations (which is then also a crucial hyperparameter).
283   In contrast, Wenzel et al. (2020a) sample from the posterior using a single stochastic gradient Markov
284   chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) sequence and SSEs combine networks from a single learning
285   process (Huang et al., 2017). That is, one process creates all ensemble members. If the latter part of
286   Hypothesis 2 is true, one need not worry about adding models to the ensemble that do not perform
287   particularly well and/or are correlated. The PAC-Bayesian weighting performs a model selection
288
      taking both individual performance and diversity into account, which should provide protection
      from networks not contributing positively to a ensemble. This makes it safe to add several weight
289
      configurations (checkpoints) from a single learning process to an ensemble. It also renders using
290
      a hold-out validation dataset for early-stopping the neural network training unnecessary: It should
291   neither be harmful to add underfitted nor overfitted models. The early-stopping data can instead be
292   used to optimize the PAC-Bayesian weighting:
293
294   Hypothesis 3 Optimizing the weighting using the tandem loss allows inclusion of several models from
295   a training run in a way that efficiently improves performance and makes early-stopping unnecessary.

478   5        D ISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS
479
480   Bayesian model averaging (BMA) is not meant to maximize predictive performance but to get a
481   better idea about the maximum likelihood estimate of the parameters of a single model. Still, the
482   Bayes ensemble is often – implicitly and explicitly – brought forward as an approach to improve
483   generalization performance. Several lines of research study the generalization performance of the
484   Bayes ensemble and try to improve it, where performance is measured in terms of accuracy on a
485   test set. Here we argued conceptually in the line of Ortega et al. (2022) and showed empirically
      that the predictive posterior is not a particularly good basis for selecting and weighting the networks

507   in a deep ensemble. A proper Bayesian way to build a deep ensemble would be to apply Bayesian
508
      inference to the space of ensembles (Monteith et al., 2011), but this is computationally expensive and
      – also for that reason – is not what is typically proposed in the research directions our study addresses.
509
      A simple uniformly weighted deep ensemble can be expected to perform on par with BMA based
510
      approaches and it can be created more efficiently. It does not require sampling from the posterior and
511   training is embarrassingly parallel.
512
513   Our proposed optimization of the weighting of DNN ensemble members using the tandem loss
514
      and additional data can improve uniform deep ensembles. Using a second order bound to derive
      the optimization objective is crucial, because minimizing a first order bound will lead to a lack
515
      of diversity similar to BMA (see also Lorenzen et al., 2019). To our knowledge, the only study
516
      applying PAC-Bayesian methods to DNN ensembles is the work by Ortega et al. (2022), who present
517   a PAC-Bayesian generalization bound including a diversity term for different losses. They empirically
518   compared uniform DNN ensembles with ensembles of DNNs that are jointly trained with the newly
519   proposed loss function, directly optimizing the PAC-Bayesian bound during training. This procedure
520   implies that the generalization bounds are not valid anymore after training, as they are not based on
521   independent data. For larger DNNs like ResNet20, the authors find that the direct optimization of
522   their bound leads to ensembles without higher diversity and with similar or even worse performance
523   than uniform deep ensembles with individually trained members – both is in contrast to our results.
524   The PAC-Bayesian weighting allows us to include several models from a training run, which can
525   improve performance efficiently and make early-stopping unnecessary. It has already been argued by
526   Sollich and Krogh (1995) that having models overfitted to a subset of the training data in a properly
527   weighted ensemble need not be harmful. While no hold-out data for early-stopping is needed, it is a
528   limitation of the weighting approach that additional hold-out data is required. For the rather small data
529   sets and the models considered in this study, experiments with canonical bagging showed that leaving
530   out training data impaired the performance of the ensemble (as also observed by Lakshminarayanan
531
      et al., 2017, and Lee et al., 2015), even though bagging increases diversity. However, the data used
      for optimization of the weighting provide at the same time a rigorous, non-trivial upper bound on
532
      the generalization performance – and getting such formal guarantees generally requires independent
533
      data. The PAC-Bayesian bounds still hold after optimization. In our case, this optimization was
534   necessary to elevate some of the bounds from being trivial to non-vacuous guarantees on the ensemble
535   performance. The bound presumes model combination by majority voting as prediction method,
536   however, in our experiments we did not observe a big difference in generalization performance
537   between majority voting and averaging predictive distributions. Thus, PAC-Bayesian optimization
538   of the ensemble weights is highly recommended if not all data available for model construction
539   is needed for training so that a subset can be employed for optimization and getting performance
      guarantees.

836   A.2     T EST- TIME CROSS - VALIDATION
837
838   Ashukha et al. (2020) describe the problem of requiring a validation set (in their case for scaling the
839
      logit outputs of neural networks with a temperature parameter), while the benchmark datasets only
      feature a training and test dataset (e.g. CIFAR). In that case, when splitting the training set into a
840
      training and validation subset, the performance may drop compared to methods that make use of the
841
      full training data because the reduced training data set is a worse description of the task. In contrast,
842   when splitting off a validation set from the test data, one still obtains an unbiased estimate of the
843   generalization error, but with higher variance due to smaller test dataset size. In order to reduce the
844   variance, Ashukha et al. (2020) propose test-time cross-validation: splitting the test dataset randomly
845   and averaging the results of the generalization estimates to reduce variance. We follow this approach
846   and divide the test dataset randomly in half. One half is used for the tandem loss bound optimization,
847   while the other serves as a generalization estimate. Afterwards, the datasets’ roles are switched, and
848   the risk estimates are averaged. In our case, this setting does not result in a fair comparison. All
849   methods should make use of the same amount of data, and algorithms that do not need a validation
850
      set could use the additional data for training, which can improve performance, in particular if data
      are scarce. However, one can envision a scenario where the additional data are not available during
851
      training but only later when deploying the model (e.g., local fine-tuning of centrally trained models).

886   A.4.1    BAGGING
887
      In the bagging experiments, we used different training data for each ensemble member to increase
888
      diversity. We drew bootstrap samples from the training data uniformly at random with replacement.
889
      The sample size was equal to the size of the training data. The experiments illustrate the effect of
890   decreasing the training data volume (e.g., to use the data for bound optimization).
891
892   The individual network performance suffered from omitting unique training data points, and although
893
      the networks are assumed to be more diverse, the resulting ensembles performed worse performance
      across all experiments. The results are visualized in Figure A.6, which shows that none of the
894
      ensembles, neither uniform nor optimized in their weighting, could match the Bayesian reference.
895
      This is in line with the literature (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2015), which finds the
896   same decrease in performance for neural network ensembles. PAC-Bayesian optimization is therefore
897   best suited for large datasets with additional data that is not essential for training. On a positive note,
898   random initialization and stochastic training appears to be enough randomization for creating diverse
899   ensembles.

901   A.4.2    T RAINING TIME COMPARISON
902
903
      It is not straight-forward to compare the computational resources required by the different approaches
      due to their different training and inference procedures. The BMA based approaches require training
904
      of the neural network and sampling the posterior, where the latter can be time consuming. In contrast,
905
      minimization of the PAC-Bayesian bound is highly efficient and its computational costs can be
906   neglected. Simple deep ensembles are embarrassingly parallel, while methods that consider ensemble
907   members from one process cannot be fully parallelized.
908
909
      But even for simple ensembles, there can be a trade-off between number of networks and the number
      of training iterations spent on each network. To explore this trade-off, we considered the worst
910
      case scenario for deep ensemble training and assume that there is no parallelization. Furthermore,
911
      we assumed that the sampling from the posterior does not take any time. That is, for methods
912   based on BMA only the training epochs for optimizing the network are counted, not the sampling.
913   In this sequential setting, we asked, given an overall budget of B training epochs, how does the
914   performance of our deep ensembles depend on the number M of networks when each network is
915   trained for ⌊B/M ⌋ epochs. For the experiments taken from Wenzel et al. (2020a), we set B = 1500
916   for CIFAR-10 and B = 500 for IMDB, corresponding to the number of training epochs used for the
917   Bayes ensemble. The results are shown in Figure A.7. Even in this biased comparison, there were
      choices of M for which the simple deep ensemble outperformed the Bayesian approach.
