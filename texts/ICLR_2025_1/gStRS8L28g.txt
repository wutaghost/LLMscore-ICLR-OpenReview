009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Distribution shifts between training and testing datasets significantly impair the
012            model performance on graph learning. A commonly-taken causal view in graph
013            invariant learning suggests that stable predictive features of graphs are causally
014            associated with labels, whereas varying environmental features lead to distribu-
015            tion shifts. In particular, covariate shifts caused by unseen environments in test
016            graphs underscore the critical need for out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization.
017            Existing graph augmentation methods designed to address the covariate shift often
018
               disentangle the stable and environmental features in the input space, and selec-
               tively perturb or mixup the environmental features. However, such perturbation-
019
               based methods heavily rely on an accurate separation of stable and environmental
020
               features, and their exploration ability is confined to existing environmental fea-
021            tures in the training distribution. To overcome these limitations, we introduce a
022            novel approach using score-based graph generation strategies that synthesize un-
023            seen environmental features while preserving the validity and stable features of
024            overall graph patterns. Our comprehensive empirical evaluations demonstrate the
025            enhanced effectiveness of our method in improving graph OOD generalization.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Deep learning algorithms have become predominant in the analysis of graph-structured data. How-
031   ever, a common limitation of existing methods is the assumption that both training and testing graphs
032   are independently and identically distributed (i.i.d.). This assumption often falls short in real-world
033
      scenarios, where shifts in data distribution frequently occur, leading to significant degradation in
      model performance. As a result, there has been considerable progress in improving graph out-of-
034
      distribution (OOD) generalization, evidenced by advancements in invariant graph learning (Chen
035
      et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2024) and graph data augmentation (Rong et al., 2019;
036   Wang et al., 2021; Han et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2022; Sui et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024).
037
038   Recent studies (Gui et al., 2022; Sui et al., 2024) have identified two primary types of distribution
039
      shifts. Correlation shifts occur when the statistical relationships between environments and labels
      differ between the training and testing datasets, assuming that the test environments are represented
040
      within the training dataset. Covariate shifts, on the other hand, arise when the test environments are
041
      not present in the training dataset. A prevalent causal perspective in graph invariant learning (Chen
042   et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2022) suggests that stable features of graphs, which causally determine labels,
043   remain invariant across different environments, whereas varying environmental features contribute
044   to distribution shifts. Consequently, previous studies (Miao et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022; Wu
045   et al., 2022) have primarily focused on correlation shifts by isolating invariant stable graph patterns
046   from the environmental features. In this work, we focus on the relatively neglected but challenging
047   problem of covariate shift in graph learning.
048   While different graph data augmentation techniques have been proposed to generate new data across
049   various domains (Rong et al., 2019; Han et al., 2022; Sui et al., 2024), these methods mainly mod-
050   ify existing data within the training set by mixing or dropping edges, which either have limited
051   environmental exploration ability or could result in invalid data samples (e.g., molecular graphs vio-
052   lating chemical rules). Moreover, indiscriminate augmentations (Rong et al., 2019) can distort stable
053   patterns, resulting in uncontrollable augmented distributions. While controlled augmentations (Sui
      et al., 2024) have shown promising outcomes, they heavily depend on the accurate separation of


                                                         1

054
      stable and environmental patterns, which remains a nontrivial challenge and could be inherently
055   infeasible. These observations prompt an essential inquiry: ”Is it possible to explore the training
056   distribution under control, such that the exploration extends beyond the confines of the training
057   environments while still preserving essential stable patterns?”
058
      In response, this work introduces an innovative score-based graph augmentation strategy that mit-
059
      igates graph covariate shift by improving the exploration of training distribution while preserving
060
      stable predictive patterns on the generated graphs. In high level, we formulate the problem of OOD
061   data augmentation as graph generation simultaneously conditioned on graph labels and exploration
062   variables, based on the graph generation hypothesis widely used in prior studies (Wu et al., 2022;
063   Yang et al., 2022; Gui et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022). Specifically, we employ a score-based diffu-
064   sion probabilistic model, commonly known as a diffusion model (Song et al., 2020), to effectively
065   capture the data distribution of unlabeled graphs. During the generation phase, we introduce a novel
066   guidance scheme that generates augmented graphs, concurrently retaining predictive stable patterns
067   and incorporating explored environments. Our proposed Out-of-Distribution Diffusion Augmenta-
068   tion (OODA) framework utilizes graph labels to guide the sampling process toward graphs that are
069
      highly likely to contain stable patterns. The exploration parameter facilitates exploration beyond
      the training graph space by flexibly adjusting the discrepancy from the training distribution. The
070
      robustness of the score-based diffusion model ensures the validity of the generated graphs, prevent-
071
      ing the formation of invalid structures (e.g., non-viable molecules) that could impair downstream
072   classification performance. Furthermore, our guidance scheme eliminates the need to explicitly split
073   graphs into stable and environmental subgraphs. We experimentally validate our method on both
074   synthetic and real-world graph classification tasks under diverse covariate shift settings. Our results
075   demonstrate that OODA outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, including invariant graph learning
076   and graph data augmentation, highlighting its effectiveness in exploring environments under control
077   while preserving stable patterns.
078   Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
079
080   • We propose a novel graph generation-based environment augmentation approach to address co-
081     variate distribution shifts in graph learning. Our method enables controlled exploration of envi-
082     ronmental patterns while preserving stable patterns, without the need to explicitly separate them.
083   • Our approach can simultaneously generate out-of-distribution (OOD) graph structures, node fea-
084     tures, and edge features, making it uniquely capable of handling covariate shifts in both feature
085     and structural distributions, as well as when these shifts occur simultaneously.
086   • Extensive empirical evaluations demonstrate that our framework outperforms state-of-the-art
087     graph OOD generalization methods across diverse tasks, including synthetic, semi-artificial, real-
088     world molecular, and natural language sentiment analysis datasets.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
092
093
      Graph-structured data are inherently complex, characterized by the intricate challenges of irregu-
      larity and nuanced structural information. This complexity gives rise to graph out-of-distribution
094
      (OOD) problems that not only necessitate addressing shifts in feature distributions but also demand
095
      attention to variations of structural distributions. In this context, we summarize two principal cate-
096   gories of algorithms for graph OOD robustness: (i) invariant graph learning strategies, which aim
097   to ensure model stability across varying distributions; and (ii) graph data augmentation techniques,
098   designed to enhance model generalizability by simulating diverse distribution scenarios.
099
      Invariant Graph Learning. The concept of invariant graph learning draws inspiration from semi-
100
      nal works such as those by (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Rosenfeld et al., 2020; Ahuja et al., 2021). This
101
      approach aims at identifying stable graph structures (e.g., subgraphs) or representations (predictors)
102   that remain consistent across different environments, thereby enhancing out-of-distribution (OOD)
103   generalization. This is achieved by capturing salient graph features and minimizing empirical risks
104   across varying conditions. In scenarios where establishing causality is complex or where strong as-
105   sumptions may not hold, the task can be approximated by identifying features that demonstrate in-
106   variance under distributional shifts, thereby facilitating OOD generalization (Li et al., 2022). Effec-
107   tive OOD generalization is achieved by basing predictions solely on invariant information (Li et al.,
      2022). For example, DIR (Wu et al., 2022) distinguishes between invariant and environment-specific

                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      subgraphs by creating varied interventional distributions on the training distribution. CIGA (Chen
109   et al., 2022) further explores this domain by employing synthetic environments and the graph gen-
110   eration process to identify stable features under various distribution shifts. However, this line of
111   research assumes access to test environments during training, which is an unrealistic assumption
112   given the impracticality of covering all possible test scenarios. Training in limited environments
113   reduces spurious correlations but fails to generalize to new, unseen environments. DISGEN (Huang
114   et al., 2024) gains promising results in disentangling the size factors from graph representations by
115   minimizing the shared information between size- and task-related information, however, the tech-
116   nique is constrained to handle size generalization. In this work, we propose a framework capable of
117
      generalizing to unseen environments characterized by differences not only in graph size but also in
      graph structure, node features, and edge features.
118
119   Graph Data Augmentation. Beyond invariant graph learning, graph data augmentation aims to
120   diversify the training distribution, thereby enhancing the out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization
121   of models. DropEdge (Rong et al., 2019) introduces randomness by selectively removing edges,
122   thus varying the training data’s structure. M-Mixup (Wang et al., 2021) enriches the dataset by
123
      interpolating diverse and irregular graphs within semantic space. G-Mixup (Han et al., 2022) ex-
      tends this concept to graph classification, interpolating across different graph generators (graphons)
124
      to produce augmented graphs. Adversarial augmentation techniques, such as FLAG (Kong et al.,
125
      2022), apply gradient-based perturbations to node features, and AIA (Sui et al., 2024) generates
126   adversarial masks on graphs, both aimed at probing environmental discrepancies. Despite these ad-
127   vancements, overcoming the limitations in environmental exploration caused by modifying graphs
128   within the original training set remains a challenge, indicating ongoing opportunities for innovation
129   in graph data augmentation strategies. Recently, environment-aware augmentation frameworks (Li
130   et al., 2024) have utilized environment information to linearly explore training graph structures and
131   node features, however, they depend heavily on high-quality and sufficiently diverse environment in-
132   formation. In practice, annotating environment labels or capturing diverse environment information
133   is costly and often infeasible. In this work, we introduce a generation-based augmentation method
134
      that eliminates the need for accessing environment information.

138   Notations. We represent a graph with n nodes as G = (A, X, E), where A ∈ Rn×n is the
139   adjacency matrix, X ∈ Rn×a denotes a-dimensional node features and E ∈ Rn×n×b encodes
140   b-dimensional edge features. Without the loss of generality, we focus on the graph classification
141   task where each graph G is associated with a label Y ∈ Y, determined by a predefined labelling
142   rule G → Y. Following invariant learning (Ahuja et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022), we denote the
143
      graph dataset as D = {(Gei , Yie )}e∈Eall , where (Gei , Yie ) ∼ Pe (G, Y ) is an i.i.d. draw in the
      environment e sampled from all possible environments Eall . The complete dataset can be partitioned
144
      into a training set Dtr = {(Gei , Yie )}e∈Etr and a test set Dte = {(Gei , Yie )}e∈Ete , where Etr and Ete
145
      index the training and testing environments, respectively. In practice, environment information may
146   not be explicitly given, and we further denote the training distribution as Ptr (G, Y ) and the testing
147   distribution as Pte (G, Y ).

191   In this section, we present the novel score-based graph augmentation framework, OODA, designed
192   to generate augmented graphs that retain predictable stable features while also exploring new envi-
193   ronments. We begin by discussing the score-based generative model for unlabeled graphs and then
194
      extend the model to handle out-of-distribution scenarios with controlled adaptation. Thereafter, we
      illustrate the working principles and implementation details of OODA.
195
196   Motivation From the perspective of graph generation, the goal of exploring the training distribution
197   Ptr (G, Y ) is to generate OOD graph samples from the conditional distribution Ptr (G, Y | yood )
198   where yood represents the OOD exploration condition. We assume an exploration variable λ controls
199   the extent of exploration within the training distribution Ptr (G, Y ). The augmented distribution
200   P̃tr (G, Y ) is then modelled by the conditional graph distribution Ptr (G, Y | yood = λ), which can
201   be decomposed as follows:
202
                         Ptr (G, Y | yood = λ) ∝ p (G) p (Y | G) p (yood = λ | G, Y )                      (3)

344
345   Intuitively, at the early stages of the reverse-time SDEs, the graphs are highly perturbed, resembling
346   the prior noise distribution. Therefore, the classifier cannot accurately approximate the target class
347   probability. Consequently, in the initial denoising steps, we focus more on guiding the reverse-time
348
      SDEs towards the OOD distribution. As the reverse-time SDEs progressively denoise the graphs,
      we introduce guidance to direct the reverse-time SDEs towards regions exhibiting the desired stable
349
      patterns and the explored OOD environmental patterns.

527   6   C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this work, we proposed OODA, an out-of-distribution graph generation framework based on a
530   score-based diffusion probabilistic model, designed to address covariate shifts in graph learning.
531   Our approach generates OOD graph samples that integrate both explored environmental and stable
532   features, eliminating the need to separate them during training. Furthermore, OODA can simul-
533   taneously explore new environments in graph structure, node features, and edge features. While
534
      score-based diffusion models demonstrate significant potential in handling diverse covariate shifts,
      they present scalability challenges when applied to large-scale graphs. Additionally, generating
535
      OOD graphs may require careful tuning of hyperparameters in the guidance scheme to balance
536
      exploration quality, particularly across different datasets. In this study, we applied minimal hyper-
537   parameter tuning to achieve competitive results. Future work could focus on developing scalable
538   diffusion models and exploring parameter-efficient tuning strategies to further enhance OOD graph
539   generation.

725   A.2      G RAPH COVARIATE SHIFT
726
727
      In this work, we address the challenge of OOD graph classification, where the goal is to de-
      velop models trained on a dataset Dtr that can generalize effectively to a dataset Dte . Consider-
728
      ing invariant perspectives under covariate shifts (Gui et al., 2022; Sui et al., 2024), we note while
729
      Ptr (Y | G) = Pte (Y | G) holds, the marginal distributions of graphs differ, i.e., Ptr (G) ̸= Pte (G).
730   This discrepancy may stem from the training set’s limited size or diversity and the unpredictable
731   conditions in test environments. For instance, acquiring sufficient samples of molecules for prop-
732   erty prediction can be costly and challenging. Moreover, geometric deep learning models are fre-
733   quently applied to predict properties of molecules with unseen scaffolds. Prior study (Li et al., 2024)
734   identifies two primary types of covariate distribution discrepancies: (1) Ptr (X) ̸= Pte (X) while
735   Ptr (A) = Pte (A) and Ptr (E) = Pte (E), exemplified by the GOOD-CMNIST dataset (Gui et al.,
736   2022), where digits of different colors indicate different environments that correspond to dataset
737   splits. (2) Ptr (A, E) ̸= Pte (A, E) or Ptr (A, X, E) ̸= Pte (A, X, E), as seen in the GOOD-Motif
738
      dataset (Gui et al., 2022) where training and testing environments differ in graph size and bases. In
      the GOOD-HIV benchmark (Gui et al., 2022), the molecular scaffolds differ between the training
739
      and testing datasets.
740
741   The covariate shift, whether in graph structure, node features or eddge features, poses significant
742   challenges to OOD generalization. This work aims to provide a general framework to address all
743   three types of covariate shifts.
744

753

755   et al., 2022), the MMD RBF scalar is also one of the most reliable metrics for measuring distribu-

764   A.4.1    DATASET D ETAILS
765
      We utilize six datasets from the GOOD benchmark (Gui et al., 2022), including GOOD-Motif-base,
766
      GOOD-Motif-size, GOOD-CMNIST-color, GOOD-HIV-scaffold, GOOD-HIV-size, and GOOD-
767
      SST2-length. The GOOD benchmark (Gui et al., 2022) is the state-of-the-art framework for sys-
768   tematically evaluating graph OOD generalization. It carefully designs data environments to induce
769   reliable and valid distribution shifts. The selected datasets span a diverse range of domains, cov-
770   ering covariate shifts in general graphs, image-transformed graphs, molecular graphs, and natural
771   language sentiment analysis graphs. The dataset details are as follows:
772
773           • GOOD-Motif: GOOD-Motif is a synthetic dataset from Spurious-Motif (Wu et al., 2022)
774             specifically designed to investigate structure shifts. Each graph consists of an environmen-
775             tal base graph connected to a label-determining motif. The two primary covariate shift
776             domains are the base graph type and graph size. For base covariate shift, the training distri-
777
                bution includes graphs with wheel, tree, and ladder base structures, while the validation set
                features star base graphs, and the test set contains path base graphs. For size covariate shift,
778
                the training distribution consists of graphs with sizes ranging from 6 to 45 nodes, the vali-
779
                dation set contains graphs with sizes between 20 and 75 nodes, and the test set comprises
780             graphs with sizes ranging from 68 to 155 nodes.
781
              • GOOD-CMNIST: GOOD-CMNIST is a semi-synthetic dataset designed to investigate
782
                node feature shifts. It consists of graphs transformed from MNIST handwritten digit images
783
                using superpixel techniques (Monti et al., 2017). Node color features are manually applied,
784             making the color shift environment independent of the underlying structure. Specifically,
785             for covariate shift, digits are colored using seven different colors. The training distribution
786             includes digits colored with the first five colors, while the validation and test distributions
787             contain digits with the remaining two colors, respectively.
788           • GOOD-HIV: GOOD-HIV is a small-scale, real-world molecular dataset sourced from
789             MoleculeNet (Wu et al., 2018). The nodes in these molecular graphs represent atoms,
790             and the edges represent chemical bonds. This dataset is designed to study node feature
791             shifts, edge feature shifts, and structure shifts. The two covariate shift domains are scaffold
792             graph type and molecular graph size. For the scaffold covariate shift, environments are par-
793             titioned based on the Bemis-Murcko scaffold (Bemis & Murcko, 1996), a two-dimensional
794             structural base that does not determine a molecule’s ability to inhibit HIV replication. For
795             the size covariate shift, the training distribution consists of molecular graphs ranging in size
796
                from 17 to 222 atoms. The validation set contains molecules with sizes between 15 and 16
                atoms, while the test set includes molecules with sizes from 2 to 14 atoms.
797
798           • GOOD-SST2: GOOD-SST2 is a real-world natural language sentimental analysis dataset
799             from (Yuan et al., 2022), designed to investigate node feature shifts and structure shifts.
800             Each graph is derived from a sentence, transformed into a grammar tree, where nodes
801
                represent words, and node features are corresponding word embeddings. The task is to
                predict the sentiment polarity of each sentence. Sentence length is chosen as the covariate
802
                shift environment, as sentence length should not inherently affect sentiment polarity. For
803
                the length covariate shift, the training distribution consists of grammar graphs with sizes
804             ranging from 1 to 7 nodes, the validation distribution includes graphs with sizes from 8 to
805             14 nodes, and the test distribution contains graphs with sizes from 15 to 56 nodes.
806

939   A.8   T IME AND MEMORY COMPLEXITY
940
      Our pipeline consists of two stages: data augmentation, and trainng of the GNN classifier on aug-
941
      mented graphs. The second stage of classification follows general GNN training setup, without
942
      introducing additional complexity: the time/memory complexity per layer of using Graph Isomor-
943   phism Networks (GIN) as backbone is Θ(n+e), where n is the number of nodes and e is the number
944   of edges. For the data augmentation stage, we introduce graph transformer whose memory and time
945   complexity per layer is Θ(n2 ). This arises from the computation of attention scores and predictions
946   across each edge.
