009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Video and audio are closely correlated modalities that humans naturally perceive
012            together. While recent advancements have enabled the generation of audio or video
013            from text, producing both modalities simultaneously still typically relies on either a
014            cascaded process or multi-modal contrastive encoders. These approaches, however,
015            often lead to suboptimal results due to inherent information losses during inference
016            and conditioning. In this paper, we introduce SyncFlow, a system that is capable of
017            simultaneously generating temporally synchronized audio and video from text. The
018
               core of SyncFlow is the proposed dual-diffusion-transformer (d-DiT) architecture,
               which enables joint video and audio modelling with proper information fusion.
019
               To efficiently manage the computational cost of joint audio and video modelling,
020
               SyncFlow utilizes a multi-stage training strategy that separates video and audio
021            learning before joint fine-tuning. Our empirical evaluations demonstrate that
022            SyncFlow produces audio and video outputs that are more correlated than baseline
023            methods with significantly enhanced audio quality and audio-visual correspondence.
024            Moreover, we demonstrate strong zero-shot capabilities of SyncFlow, including
025            zero-shot video-to-audio generation and adaptation to novel video resolutions
026            without further training.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Humans experience the world multimodally, where audio and video are naturally related, providing
032   complementary information that enhances perception and understanding. This natural synchronization
033
      is reflected in most media content we consume, such as movies, virtual reality content, and human-
      computer interfaces. With the advancement of artificial intelligence generated content (AIGC), there
034
      has been substantial progress in generating audio or video from textual descriptions. State-of-the-art
035
      models have achieved impressive results in tasks such as image generation (Rombach et al., 2022;
036   Ramesh et al., 2021), audio generation (Yang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a; 2024a; Kreuk et al.,
037   2022), video generation (Singer et al., 2022b; Ho et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2024b), and audio-visual
038   cross-modal generation (Iashin & Rahtu, 2021; Luo et al., 2024; Mei et al., 2023; Mo et al., 2024),
039   showcasing the potential of AIGC in creating realistic and engaging content.
040
      Despite the strong correlation between audio and video, most existing AIGC research treats audio and
041
      video generation as isolated tasks, generating each modality independently (Żelaszczyk & Mańdziuk,
042   2022; Park et al., 2022; Yariv et al., 2024). For instance, diffusion models have recently shown
043   potential as real-time game engines by predicting frames sequentially (Valevski et al., 2024), but
044   audio is still not incorporated into the generation process despite the crucial role of audio in enhancing
045   the immersive and engaging experience in gaming. While there are a few studies that explore joint
046   audio-video generation, such as MMDiffusion (Ruan et al., 2023) and the more recent AV-DiT (Wang
047   et al., 2024), these approaches are primarily designed for unconditional generation and are often
048   domain-specific, such as focusing on dancing video (Li et al., 2021) or natural scenes (Lee et al.,
049   2022). Notably, MMDiffusion offers examples of open-domain, unconditional joint audio-video
050
      generation but lacks evaluation metrics or comparative results in its publication, leaving a gap in
      assessing its effectiveness. Whether audio and video can be generated simultaneously from text using
051
      a unified approach has received limited attention.
052
053   Two main approaches have emerged that bring us closer to joint text-to-audio-video (T2AV) gen-
      eration, though each comes with its limitations. One approach involves employing two separate


                                                          1

054
      systems, such as concatenating a text-to-video (T2V) model with a video-to-audio (V2A) model
055   or equipping a video understanding model with a text-to-audio (T2A) model (Chen et al., 2024a).
056   While these cascaded systems can generate both modalities, they introduce additional latency and the
057   risk of error propagation during the cascaded processing. Moreover, the lack of direct interaction
058   between the three modalities in such systems can potentially lead to sub-optimal results. Another
059   approach leverages a contrastively aligned latent space to generate audio and video jointly. For
060   instance, models like composable diffusion (CoDi) (Tang et al., 2024b) align visual, audio, and
061   textual representations in a shared latent space for T2AV generation. Similarly, the model proposed
062   by Xing et al. (2024) adopts pretrained Imagebind (Girdhar et al., 2023), a model that aligns six
063
      modalities with contrastive learning to guide the generation of audio and video. However, these
      methods are limited by using a one-dimensional contrastive representation, which contains limited
064
      temporal information. Some previous work (Tang et al., 2024b) even targeted audio and video
065
      with different durations, resulting in poor temporal alignment between the modalities. Recently,
066   TVGBench (Mao et al., 2024) addresses a text-to-audible-video generation task, which marks the
067   first attempt on the text conditioning audio-video joint generation.
068
069
      This paper introduces SyncFlow, a model capable of generating temporally synchronized audio and
      video from text. We propose a dual-diffusion-transformer (d-DiT) architecture to handle the synchro-
070
      nized generation of video and audio. The d-DiT builds upon the Diffusion Transformer (DiT) (Peebles
071
      & Xie, 2023a) architecture, which has demonstrated strong performance in both video and image
072   generation (Esser et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2024b). To address the challenges of computational cost and
073   the scarcity of paired audio-video data, we propose a modality-decoupled multi-stage training strategy.
074   Specifically, we decouple the model training on video and audio before joint audio-video fine tuning.
075   Starting with a pre-trained text-to-video model, we freeze the video generation component and adapt
076   it to audio generation by leveraging intermediate features from the video model as conditioning inputs
077   for audio synthesis. This decoupled approach allows the video generation related parameters to be
078   trained using widely available text-video datasets, while the audio component can be adapted with a
079   relatively small amount of paired data. Given the high computational demands of video generation,
080
      particularly for high-resolution and high-frame-rate outputs, our strategy significantly reduces the
      computational overhead of joint training and mitigates the need for large-scale text-video-audio
081
      datasets. Finally, the entire d-DiT model is finetuned end-to-end on both video and audio modalities
082
      to enhance the generation quality. Both the audio and video generation components of SyncFlow are
083   built using a flow-matching latent generative model (Lipman et al., 2022). Our experiment shows
084   SyncFlow not only achieves temporally synchronized T2AV but also achieves strong performance
085   compared with cascaded systems and systems built with contrastive encoders. In summary, our
086   contributions are as follows:
087
088          • We introduce SyncFlow for synchronized joint video-audio generation from text (T2AV).
089            SyncFlow can jointly generate temporally synchronized 16 FPS video and 48kHz sampling
090
               rate audio with open-domain text conditions.
091          • We empirically show that SyncFlow performs better than other T2AV systems based on
092            cascaded processing and multi-modal contrastive encoders.
093          • The pretrained SyncFlow model demonstrates strong zero-shot performance on video-
094            to-audio generation and a zero-shot adaptation ability to new video resolutions for joint
095            audio-video generation.

      Rectifier Flow Matching Flow matching (FM) (Lipman et al., 2022) is a powerful method for gener-
100
      ative modelling that enables efficient training of continuous normalizing flows (CNFs) (Papamakarios
101
      et al., 2021) by directly predicting vector fields along fixed conditional probability paths. Building
102   on FM, rectified flow matching (RFM) (Liu et al., 2023b) enforces straight sampling trajectories
103   between prior and target data distributions. This process also shares a similar intuition as optimal-
104   transport-flow (Onken et al., 2021). Compared with diffusion-based methods (Ho et al., 2020), RFM
105   demonstrates improved sample quality on image generation while reducing the number of sampling
106   steps (Lipman et al., 2022). Subsequent works have expanded the use of RFM to various applications,
107   such as text-to-image generation (Esser et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b), point cloud generation (Wu
      et al., 2023a), text-to-speech synthesis (Guo et al., 2024; Mehta et al., 2024), source separation (Yuan


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2024) and sound generation (Vyas et al., 2023; Prajwal et al., 2024), highlighting the versatility
109   of RFM across different domains.
110
111   Text-conditioned Generative Modeling Recent years have witnessed remarkable progress in text-
112
      conditioned generative modelling. For text-to-image generation, models such as DALL-E 2 (Ramesh
      et al., 2022) and Stable Diffusion Series (Rombach et al., 2022) demonstrated strong performance
113
      by producing high-quality images aligned with the textual inputs. In the audio domain, there
114
      has been substantial advancement in generating speech, music, and environmental sounds from
115   text or transcriptions (Tan et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024b; Ye et al., 2024; Li
116   et al., 2024; Agostinelli et al., 2023; Copet et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023). In video generation,
117   CogVideo (Hong et al., 2023) and Make-a-Video (Singer et al., 2022a) have demonstrated early
118   success by effectively adapting text-to-image methodologies to video through language models and
119   diffusion-based approaches, respectively. Later, the diffusion-transformer architecture (Peebles & Xie,
120   2023b) has further enhanced video generation capabilities, as showcased by the OpenAI release of
121   Sora (OpenAI, 2024b). The recently proposed CogVideoX (Yang et al., 2024) scales the open-source
122   video generation model to 5-billion parameters, achieving state-of-the-art performance. SyncFlow
123
      differs from prior work in that it focuses on the joint generation of synchronized audio and video,
      posing challenges in both computational efficiency and coordination between modalities.
124
125   Joint Audio-visual Generation While significant progress has been made in generating audio,
126   video, and images independently, the task of simultaneously generating audio and video from
127   text remains underexplored. Although CoDi-2 (Tang et al., 2024a) demonstrates the ability to
128   generate video frames and sound from text instructions, it does not directly address the T2AV
129
      task. MMDiffusion (Ruan et al., 2023), AV-DiT (Wang et al., 2024), TAVDiffusion (Mao et al.,
      2024) and Hayakawa et al. (2024) have demonstrated success in the joint generation of videos with
130
      accompanying audio. Some approaches perform text-conditioned joint audio-video generation by
131
      relying on contrastive modality encoders, as seen in CoDi (Tang et al., 2024b) and (Xing et al.,
132   2024), where a shared video-audio-text contrastive-aligned one-dimensional representation is used to
133   condition both audio and video generation. While this allows for joint generation, the one-dimensional
134   contrastive representation lacks sufficient temporal information, limiting the performance of the
135   model on temporal synchronization. In fact, the audio and video samples generated by CoDi1 exhibit
136   mismatched duration, falling short of achieving true synchronization.

159   Preliminary: Rectifier Flow Matching (RFM) The training of SyncFlow is based on rectifier flow
160   matching (Liu et al., 2023b), which improves upon flow matching (Lipman et al., 2022) by optimizing

      the transport between the prior distribution p0 and the target distribution p1 . Given a training data
163   sample x1 ∼ p1 from the target distribution and a sample from the prior distribution x0 ∼ p0 , the
164   target velocity field v of RFM is calculated as v = x1 − x0 . This velocity field represents the optimal
165   direction for transporting the sample x0 to the sample x1 along a straight trajectory. We follow Tong
166   et al. (2024) to perform mini-batch optimal transport within the batch of x0 and x1 during training to
167   find an approximate solution to the dynamic optimal transport.
168
      To ensure that the transport follows a straight path between the prior and the target distributions,
169   RFM enforces that each point on this trajectory predicts the same velocity field. The intermediate
170   points along the trajectory are determined by the forward process of the RFM, where the noisy latent
171   variable at time t ∈ [0, 1] is given by xt = (1 − t)x0 + tx1 . At each time step t, given the latent
172   sample xt , the RFM model u(xt , t; θ) is optimized toward predicting the velocity field that minimizes
173   the deviations with the target velocity field v = x1 − x0 , in which θ are the trainable parameters for
174   RFM. With a pretrained velocity field prediction model u(xt , t; θ), the sampling process of RFM is
175   obtained by solving the ordinary differential equations (ODE) dxdt = u(xt , t; θ), where the generative

186   Latent Representation for Video and Audio Raw video and audio data often have extremely large
187   dimensionality. This results in high computational complexity during model training and inference,
188   particularly when dealing with high video frame rates (FPS) and audio sampling rates. To efficiently
189
      model the high-dimensional y V and y A , we adopt a latent modelling approach inspired by the latent
      diffusion model (Rombach et al., 2022). On both video and audio modalities, we train variational
190
      autoencoders (VAE) (Kingma & Welling, 2014) with a latent space with compressed dimensions
191
      compared with the original video or audio. The latent encodings for video and audio are formulated
192                               ′      ′   ′
      as z V = Evideo (y V ) ∈ RF ×C×H ×W , and z A = Eaudio (y A ) ∈ RT ×DA , where Evideo and Eaudio are
193
      pre-trained VAE encoders for video and audio, respectively. The video encoder Evideo , based on
194   a video spatial-temporal VAE proposed by Zheng et al. (2024), compresses the high-dimensional
195   frames y V into a latent representation z V with reduced dimension on both spatial and temporal axes.
196   The audio encoder Eaudio is derived from the Encodec (Défossez et al., 2023), which was originally
197   designed for learning discrete audio latent representation. We adapt Encodec by removing vector
198   quantization layers and adding a kullback–leibler (KL) divergence loss to regularize the variance of
199   the latent space following the training losses used in a standard VAE models (Kingma & Welling,
200   2014).
201   Both the video VAE and the audio VAE are paired with corresponding decoders that map the latent
202   representations back to their original high-dimensional spaces. Specifically, the video decoder DV
203   reconstructs the video frames from the latent space z V , while the audio decoder DA reconstructs
204   the audio samples from the latent space z A . The decoding processes can be described as ŷ V =
205   DV (z V ), ŷ A = DA (z A ). This ensures that the compressed latent variables can be converted back
206   to full-resolution video and audio outputs after generation in the latent space.

301   Modality-decoupled Multi-stage Learning Generative modelling of video and audio data is compu-
302
      tationally intensive. To address this, we propose a modality-decoupled training strategy consisting of
      three stages: (1) Pretraining the video tower on text-video paired data; (2) Adapting the pretrained
303
      video tower for audio generation, where the audio tower is trained while the video tower remains
304
      frozen; (3) Jointly fine-tuning both the video and audio towers on the full training set. This approach
305   offers two main advantages. Due to the scarcity of text-video-audio data, our method is data-efficient
306   as it allows the video tower to be pretrained separately. Second, this method is computationally
307   efficient. Since the video tower is frozen during the second stage, the audio tower can be trained with
308   larger batch size, reducing computational overhead while improving performance. Experiments on
309   audio generation can be conducted more efficiently without retraining the video tower.

314   Dataset We conduct experiments using the curated VGGSound (Chen et al., 2020), and the Greatest
315
      Hits dataset (Owens et al., 2016). VGGSound was initially built using a specifically designed pipeline
      to ensure strong audio-video correspondence and to filter out samples with significant ambient noise.
316
      The Greatest Hits dataset (Owens et al., 2016) contains 977 videos of various objects being hit,
317
      scratched, or poked with a drumstick, capturing interactions with materials such as metal, plastic,
318   cloth, and gravel. Each video includes both visual and audio data, making it ideal for studying the
319   correspondence between physical interactions and their resulting sounds. We split each video in the
320   Greatest Hits dataset into 10-second segments, resulting in a total of 2, 995 segments, from which we
321   select 744 segments as the test set, ensuring that no test samples originate from the same videos used
322   for training. For both the VGGSound and the Greatest Hits dataset, we use the VideoOFA (Chen
323   et al., 2023) to generate video captions automatically. We primarily use the Greatest Hits dataset for
      ablation studies as it is smaller in scale.

      Evaluation Metrics To evaluate the quality of the generated video and audio, we use Fréchet
325   video distance (FVD) and Fréchet audio distance (FAD). FVD measures the similarity between
326   the distribution of generated and real videos by comparing feature representations extracted from
327   a pre-trained I3D model (Carreira & Zisserman, 2017), while FAD compares generated and real
328   audio using features from the VGGish model (Hershey et al., 2017). To assess the similarity between
329   the generated audio and target audio, we use KL divergence (Kreuk et al., 2022), which measures
330   the divergence between the VGGish classification output of paired audio samples. Additionally,
331   we use the CLAP score (Wu et al., 2023b) to measure the alignment between the generated audio
332   and the input text caption. To further examine the relationship between video, audio, and text, we
333
      employ ImageBind (Girdhar et al., 2023) to extract contrastive embeddings from each modality and
      calculate cosine similarity, referred to as the ImageBind Score (IB) (Mei et al., 2023). For instance,
334
      IB (Gen-A&Gen-V) denotes the IB score between the generated audio and video, which is similar to
335
      the AVHScore metrics proposed in Mao et al. (2024).
336
337   Setup Details We randomly sample two-second video-audio segments from the training dataset,
338   using 16 FPS video data with centre cropping and resizing to a resolution of 256 × 256. The audio
339
      data are sampled at 48kHz. The Video VAE downsamples the temporal dimension by a factor of 4
      and the spatial dimensions by a factor of 8. We flatten the output of Pre-Conv3D (see Figure 1) into a
340
      sequence of tensors by 2 × 2 patch splitting on the spatial dimension. Building upon the Encodec, the
341
      audio VAE downsamples the temporal dimension by a factor of 960, resulting in an audio latent with
342   a temporal resolution of 50Hz and an embedding dimension of 1142. The video generation tower
343   utilizes a pretrained text-to-video generation model OpenSora 2 . Both video and audio generation
344   towers in d-DiT have 28 layers and a transformer feature dimension of 1142. The video VAE and
345   audio VAE are pre-trained independently and remain frozen during the SyncFlow training. For the
346   VGGSound dataset, we train the audio generation tower with a batch size of 16 per GPU for 150, 000
347   steps on 32 H100 GPUs, taking about 140 hours. Joint fine-tuning of the audio and video towers is
348   done with a batch size of 2 per GPU for 20, 000 steps. On the smaller Greatest Hits dataset, we train
349   for 25, 000 steps with a batch size of 16 on 8 H100 GPUs. We set the CFG weight in Equation (3) to
350
      6.0 by default and use 50 sampling steps during generation. Additionally, we randomly drop the text
      conditioning with a 10% probability during training to enable CFG.
351
352   Baselines For the cascaded model baselines, we combine the OpenSora model with three publicly
353   available T2A models: AudioLDM (Liu et al., 2023a), AudioLDM 2 (Liu et al., 2024a), and
354   AudioGen (Kreuk et al., 2022), two publicly available V2A models: SpecVQGAN (Iashin & Rahtu,
355   2021), Diff-Foley (Luo et al., 2024), and our reproduction of another V2A model FoleyGen (Mei et al.,
356
      2023). The latter three V2A models are also compared against SyncFlow in video-to-audio generation
      tasks. The FoleyGen we used is our reproduced version following the original paper (Mei et al., 2023).
357
      AudioLDM is a latent diffusion model designed for generating audio from text, while AudioLDM 2
358
      improves upon this by incorporating self-supervised pretraining for better audio quality and diversity.
359   AudioGen frames audio generation as a conditional language modelling task, using transformer
360   architectures to produce audio from textual descriptions. SpecVQGAN utilizes a vector-quantized
361   autoencoder to learn compact and meaningful audio representations, combined with a transformer
362   decoder for V2A generative modeling. Diff-Foley leverages diffusion models to create realistic sound
363   effects for videos. As the work that perform T2AV generation using contrastively pretrained encoders,
364   we reproduce the result of CoDi for comparison. In the original CoDi training, the video duration is 2
365   seconds while the audio duration is 10 seconds. For evaluation, we trim the CoDi audio generation
366   output to the first 2 seconds to match our setup.

      Zero-shot Video-to-Audio Generation Diffusion and flow-matching-based generative models have
      proven effective in tasks like in-filling and out-painting (Liu et al., 2023a; Rombach et al., 2022),
468
      where part of the target data is known. In these cases, noise is added to the known information,
469
      replacing the predicted part of the model, so that each denoising step incorporates the noisy version
470   of the ground truth. This process, often referred to as latent inversion (Lan et al., 2024), can also be
471   applied to editing tasks, where denoising begins with partially noisy data, and the process is guided
472   by specific editing instructions. Similarly, SyncFlow can perform V2A generation by replacing the
473   predicted video latent ẑtV with the ground truth latent ztV , ensuring that the model receives accurate
474   guidance from the ground truth video at each denoising step.

      6   C ONCLUSIONS
541
542
      In this paper, we introduced SyncFlow, a model for joint audio and video generation from text,
543   addressing the limitations of existing cascaded and contrastive encoder-based methods. By leveraging
544   the dual-diffusion-transformer (d-DiT) architecture and a modality-decoupled training strategy,
545   SyncFlow efficiently generates temporally synchronized audio and video with improved quality and
546   alignment. Our experiments demonstrated strong performance on multiple benchmarks, including
547   VGGSound and Greatest Hits, showcasing the ability of SyncFlow to achieve strong audio-visual
548   correspondence and zero-shot adaptability to new video resolutions. Additionally, our ablation studies
549   highlighted the importance of the modality adaptor in enhancing synchronization between modalities.

781   A.1   L IMITATIONS
782
      The model is trained on a video sub-clip randomly sampled from a video in the dataset, while the text
783
      caption from VideoOFA is based on the full-length video. This means the caption we used for the
784   model training is not optimal. Nevertheless, most videos have consistent semantics, so our model
785   generally works fine. Improving caption quality could be a way to improve the proposed method.
786
787
      Despite carefully curating the VGGSound dataset, we still observe videos with static frames and
      ambient sounds, such as videos with static album covers or food-sizzling sounds. There are also a lot
788
      of off-screen sounds in the data, such as narration, environmental sound, etc. Future work can be
789
      done to address the data quality issue, such as filtering the data based on audio-visual correspondence.
790
791   The samples generated by the text-to-video model can sometimes lack clear and coherent movement,
792   leading to potential ambiguities and mismatches during training and inference. Future work could
793
      focus on enhancing the performance of the video generation tower to address these issues.
