011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Tabular data, as the most fundamental structure of many real-world applications,
014            has been a spotlight of machine learning since the last decade. Regardless of the
015            adopted approaches, e.g., decision trees or neural networks, Categorical Encoding
016            is an essential operation for processing raw data into a numeric format so that ma-
017            chine learning algorithms can accept it. One fatal limitation of popular categorical
018            encodings is that they cannot extrapolate to unseen categories for machine learning
019            models without re-training. However, it is common to observe new categories in
020
               industry, while re-training is not always possible, e.g., during the cold-start stage
               with no target examples. In this work, we propose Contemporary Continuous
021
               Aggregation (CCA), a novel and theoretically sound categorical encoding which
022
               can automatically extrapolate to unseen categories without any training. CCA
023            only relies on statistics from raw input that can be maintained at low time and
024            memory costs, thus it is scalable to heavy workloads in real-time. Besides, we also
025            empirically showcase that CCA outperforms existing encodings on unsupervised
026            unseen category extrapolation, and achieves similar or even better performance in
027            normal situations without extrapolation, promising CCA to be a powerful toolkit
028            for tabular learning.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Tabular data, consisting of a collection of the same combinations of categorical and continuous values,
035   is the most basic and common data type in real-world domains including fraud prevention (Cartella
036   et al., 2021; Khatri et al., 2020), medical profiling (Ogunleye & Wang, 2019; Zhang et al., 2020),
037   molecular analysis (Babajide Mustapha & Saeed, 2016; Bi et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020), advertising
038   recommendation (Zhang et al., 2019), and anomaly detection (Pang et al., 2021). With the great suc-
039
      cess of artificial intelligence in recent years, many machine learning approaches have been proposed
      and adopted to automatically learn to predict from a giant amount of samples (observations). Repre-
040
      sentative state-of-the-art works include XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016), CatBoost (Prokhorenkova
041
      et al., 2018), and LightGBM (Ke et al., 2017) from the conventional Gradient Boosted Decision Tree
042   (GBDT) family, along with deep neural network competitors such as TabTransformer (Huang et al.,
043   2020), FT-Transformer (Gorishniy et al., 2021), VIME (Yoon et al., 2020), SAINT (Somepalli et al.,
044   2021), and many other recent works (Arik & Pfister, 2020; Popov et al., 2019) that achieve similar or
045   slightly worse performance.
046
      One fundamental requirement of all the aforementioned state-of-the-art approaches, regardless of
047
      GBDTs or neural networks, is that the input tabular data must be preprocessed into continuous values.
048   However, real-world tabular data often contains categorical values, thus it is necessary to convert
049   categorical values into continuous values, and such conversion techniques are called Categorical
050   Encodings (Hancock & Khoshgoftaar, 2020). The most popular categorical encoding in industry is
051   CatBoost (target) encoding for GBDTs, which utilizes correlation information between categories
052   and learning targets, e.g., target label distribution w.r.t. each category; and parametric encoding for
053   neural networks, which tunes category encodings as a group of learnable parameters through gradient
      descent optimization.

054
      For most existing categorical encodings, they assume that all categories are known during learning,
055   and when an unseen category appears at inference time, machine learning approaches will treat it as
056   an "Unknown" category, i.e., a preset category for unseen categories (Pargent et al., 2019). While this
057   trick works well for stable systems whose unseen categories are rare, it will not work well for highly
058   dynamic systems. For instance, in social media or video streaming, “tags” is an important categorical
059   variable for recommendation, whose cardinality increases frequently over time. Simply treating all
060   new tags as an unknown category may lead to poor service quality since up-to-date recommendations
061   are often related to those new tags. The most common industrial solution is to periodically re-train
062   new category encodings and machine learning models, thus covering new categories (Bifet & Gavalda,
063
      2007; Gama et al., 2014). However, training and deploying new models, especially for large service
      systems, will cost a lot of resources (Le & Hua, 2021), and new categories will still be weakly handled
064
      between two training periods.
065
066   Another critical industry challenge is that learning targets for training new models are generated and
067   collected with huge delays. For example, in a fraud detection system, it may take investigators weeks
068   or months to identify only tens of fraudsters, while hundreds or thousands of labeled samples are at
069
      least required for re-training. Thus, it is impossible to train new models to keep pace with category
      increments. Another challenge is Category Shifting in transfer learning: Suppose we already have a
070
      machine learning model tuned by data from one region, and plan to initialize the service in a new
071
      region. The machine learning model should work directly since the task stays the same. However,
072   categories can possibly be expressed in different text, merged into new categories, or split into sub-
073   categories in a different region. Therefore, the machine learning model fails drastically in practice
074   since its encoding system cannot handle such shifting and treats most categories as "Unknown".
075
      Contributions. In this work, we propose Contemporary Continuous Aggregation (CCA), a novel
076
      categorical encoding to address the zero-shot transfer learning challenge with category shifting and
077
      increasing category cardinality for highly dynamic applications:
078
079   • We first formalize the requirements and expressivity of categorical encodings, providing a theoreti-
080
        cal analysis toolkit for researchers to justify the power of any categorical encoding design.
081   • We then implement CCA, a scalable and theoretically sound categorical encoding with the ability to
082     extrapolate to unseen categories. Our experiments show that CCA outperforms other encodings on
083     unsupervised extrapolation, and performs similarly or even better on tasks without extrapolation.
084

124   3   R ELATED W ORKS
125
126   Supervised Encodings. As far as we know, supervised encodings are the most popular encoding
127   techniques used in tabular learning when targets y are available. The most representative work
128   is Target Encoding (Hayashi, 2011; Micci-Barreca, 2001), which uses the mean of all targets cor-
129   responding to the same category as the encoding. Other popular supervised variants follow the
130
      same schema but adopt different target statistics or smoothing techniques. For example, CatBoost
      Encoding (Prokhorenkova et al., 2018) quantizes continuous targets into buckets; Quantile Encod-
131
      ing (Mougan et al., 2021) uses target quantiles instead of means; James-Stein Estimator (James &
132
      Stein, 1992; Said, 2017) uses a biased mean estimation to collect target means of multiple categories
133   jointly; GLMM (Gelman & Hill, 2007) simply learns the linear coefficient between the category and
134   targets as the encodings. Another trend from deep learning is to make categorical encodings part of
135   the learnable weights of neural networks (Mikolov et al., 2013), and the variants in this trend differ in
136   their regularization techniques.
137
      Unsupervised Encodings. On the other hand, in many real-world applications, target labels are not
138
      available, e.g., anomaly detection, thus unsupervised encodings are preferred. The most common
139   unsupervised encodings are Ordinal Encoding and One-hot Encoding (Hancock & Khoshgoftaar,
140   2020; Potdar et al., 2017). However, they both have fatal risks: Ordinal Encoding introduces
141   unexpected ordering between categories, which may confuse machine learning models and cause
142   over-fitting; One-hot Encoding has a dimensionality equal to the number of categories, thus suffering
143   from the curse of dimensionality (Hancock & Khoshgoftaar, 2020; Pargent et al., 2019; Potdar et al.,
144   2017; Verleysen & François, 2005). To control the dimensionality, Hash Encoding (Weinberger et al.,
145   2009) projects categories into hash buckets, and Random Encoding assigns each category a random
146   vector of short length (Ahlswede & Zhang, 2006; Hutchinson, 1989), but they all have randomness in
147
      encoding generation, thus extrapolating poorly to unseen categories. CESAMMO (Valdez-Valenzuela
      et al., 2022) proposes a meaningful pruning over Random Encoding, which preserves the correlation
148
      of each category with other features; however, it needs to compute polynomial approximations
149
      between every encoding dimension and every feature, which does not scale well. Similar concepts
150   are shared by (Kuri-Morales, 2018; 2015) but with even more severe scalability issues. As far as we
151   know, Count Encoding, which replaces each category by its frequency statistics, is the only scalable
152   unsupervised categorical encoding (Pargent et al., 2019). The representative work is SDV (Patki et al.,
153   2016), which uses the cumulative distribution function as the frequency statistics and adds Gaussian
154   noise for better inference generalization.
155
      Semantic Encodings. With the recent rise of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Floridi & Chiriatti,
156   2020), utilizing word embeddings from LLMs to construct categorical encodings has gained increasing
157   interest in tabular learning (Hegselmann et al., 2023; Onishi et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Zhang
158   et al., 2023). For example, TabLLM (Hegselmann et al., 2023) describes each tabular sample x in
159   text and directly uses LLMs to make predictions from the text description. Similar concepts can also
160   be found in conventional categorical encoding techniques, such as Similarity Encoding (Cerda et al.,
161   2018), Min-Hash and Gamma-Poisson Encoding (Cerda & Varoquaux, 2020), or Word Embedding
      Encoding (Mikolov et al., 2013). They all ensure that encodings with similar text in the corpus Vi


                                                         3

162
      are close enough to preserve semantic information. However, while these ideas have the benefit of
163   borrowing external knowledge, they may not be suitable for highly abstracted or privacy-protected
164   categories, e.g., anonymous categories (such as "Type-A").

169   In this section, we introduce our unsupervised categorical encoding proposal: Contemporary Contin-
170
      uous Aggregation (CCA), which effectively and efficiently extrapolates to unseen categories without
      any learning. We first provide the theoretical inspiration of CCA in Section 4.1, then formally define
171
      the design and practical implementation of CCA in Section 4.2.

      4.1   T HEORETICAL I NSPIRATION
174
175   As claimed in (Kuri-Morales, 2018; 2015; Valdez-Valenzuela et al., 2022), the fundamental require-
176   ment of categorical encoding is to assign each unique category value c of arbitrary feature i with a
177   group of continuous values Ei (c) which preserves the interdependency (correlation) of the category
178   with all the other values. To be more instantiated, we introduce Definitions 4.1 and 4.2 for better
179   clarity.
180

197   The simplest categorical encoding is a Self Pair Pattern Distinguishable encoding, which can
198   distinguish any self tabular pair patterns Pi,i (c) for any categorical feature i. Such encodings are
199   constructed by bijections between the original categories and their encodings, e.g., Ordinal and One-
200   hot Encodings. They are also considered Perfect Pair Pattern Distinguishable encodings, as they
201
      can distinguish all tabular patterns due to their bijectivities. However, since these encodings require
      bijections between the original categories and encodings, they will suffer from an out-of-distribution
202
      issue when encountering unseen categories, thus extrapolating poorly on evolving systems. Another
203
      issue with self pair pattern distinguishable encodings is that they are extremely sensitive to different
204   categories, even when those categories exhibit similar relationships with other features. For instance,
205   if Pi,j (c1 ) and Pi,j (c2 ) are similar, we would expect their encodings to be more similar to reflect such
206   property than any c3 whose Pi,j (c3 ) differs significantly. However, self pair pattern distinguishable
207   encoding can not guarantee this expectation, e.g., c1 , c2 , c3 will have same encoding distance with
208   each other for One-hot Encoding, and c3 may even be closer to c1 for Ordinal Encoding.
209
      To overcome this sensitivity, Target Pair Pattern Distinguishable encoding which can distinguish
210   any target tabular pair pattern Pi,C+N +1 (c), e.g., Target Encoding, is proposed. Since such kind
211   of encodings utilizes the interdependency between categories and targets as the encoding, they
212   reduce the encoding sensitivity to category difference, thus are more robust and beneficial for target
213   predictions. While target pair pattern distinguishable encoding has successfully proved their power in
214   many studies, they necessarily requires targets to be provided for all categories during development.
215   However, as introduced in Section 1, many real-world scenarios do not ideally have available targets
      and may frequently encounter unseen categories at inference time.

      CCA encoding may introduce too many redundant dimensions, which can easily cause over-fitting.
345   In such cases, we do not expect CCA to be the best encoding.
346
347   In the supervised ablation study, where we have available target labels during training, we also
348
      consider CatBoost Encoding, a supervised target encoding embedded in the CatBoost algorithm that
      is widely adopted in industry, as an auxiliary baseline. The purpose of this baseline is to show that
349
      CCA can achieve similar or even better performance without relying on target statistics.
350
351   For tabular learning frameworks, we adopt the representative state-of-the-art GBDT methods including
352   XGBoost, CatBoost and LightGBM for zero-shot transfer learning experiment and supervised ablation
353   study. We do not consider deep neural networks since they lack explainability, scale poorly and
354
      only achieve similar performance as GBDTs with more costs (Grinsztajn et al., 2022; Shwartz-Ziv
      & Armon, 2022), thus are not widely adopted in industry. In unsupervised ablation study where no
355
      targets are presented, we consider unsupervised learning approaches instead including One-Class
356
      SVM (Schölkopf et al., 2001; Ruff et al., 2018) 1 , Isolation Forest (Liu et al., 2008) and Local Outlier
357   Factor (Breunig et al., 2000).
358
359   In all considered datasets, positive labels are (mostly far) less than negative labels. Thus, we select
360
      Precision-Recall Area Under Curve (PR-AUC) as the evaluation metric for all experiments which is
      insensitive to label imbalance. Furthermore, we sort all datasets by the descending order of categorical
361
      feature importance (the mean difference from other encodings to Discard Encoding). Consequently,
362
      earlier datasets rely more on categorical encodings, and are more valuable for encoding comparison.
363   For the test performance comparison, we select the best model and hyperparameter configuration
364   based on validation PR-AUC for each encoding separately. Hyperparameter details are provided in
365   Appendix A.

      6    C ONCLUSION
463
464   This work proposes Contemporary Continuous Ag-
465   gregation (CCA), a novel categorical encoding that can effectively and efficiently extrapolate to
466   unseen categories for tabular learning. We first proved the expressivity of CCA’s inspiration and
467   implementation through representation theory. Then, we empirically showed that CCA outperforms
468   preceding encodings in a zero-shot transfer learning challenge from a real-world application and its
469   simulation on public datasets. Finally, we compared CCA’s performance on regular supervised and
470   unsupervised tasks, showcasing that it achieves similar or even better performance when compared to
471   other encodings. Thereby, we demonstrate that CCA is a worthy addition with extrapolation power to
472
      the categorical encoding toolbox for tabular learning.
