009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The Pinyin Input Method Engine (IME) is widely used for inputting Chinese
012            characters, but effectively integrating it with powerful large language models
013            (LLMs) remains a challenge due to issues such as semantic discontinuity and
014            inefficient training. This paper presents AttnInput, a novel approach that lever-
015            ages the strengths of the RWKV language model, specifically its linear compu-
016            tational complexity and ”infinite” context length, to enhance Pinyin IME. Our
017            method integrates Pinyin information directly into the internal state of RWKV
018            through a lightweight side network, effectively addressing the semantic disconti-
019
               nuity issue faced by previous LLM-based IMEs. Furthermore, AttnInput utilizes a
               pre-training strategy, significantly reducing training data and computational costs
020
               compared to previous methods. Experimental results demonstrate that AttnInput
021
               achieves state-of-the-art performance on abbreviated Pinyin input, especially as
022            the Pinyin sequence length increases. This efficient design allows us to scale up
023            to larger models and incorporate longer contexts, further improving accuracy and
024            user experience.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
045
046
047   Pinyin Input Method Engine (IME) allows users to input Chinese characters using a standard key-
048   board. Pinyin is the official romanization system for Chinese, which represents the pronunciation of
049   Chinese characters using the Latin alphabet.
050   The advent of GPT models has spurred research into applying large language models to input method
051   engines. As illustrated in Figure 1(b), most of the previous research that achieve state-of-the-art
052   performance like PinyinGPT-Concat (Tan et al., 2022) and GeneInput (Ding et al., 2023) simply
053   concatenate the context and the pinyin sequence to form the prompt for the language model. How-
      ever, inserting pinyin sequences disrupts the semantic flow between the prompt and target text, and


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      poses challenges for effectively leveraging pretrained large language models, as their training ob-
055   jective primarily focuses on predicting the next token. Furthermore, these models are trained in an
056   SFT manner, indicating that only a small number of pinyin information in each training sample is
057   learned, leading to a need for extensive training resources and difficulty in increasing context length.
058   Our work confirms that concat-based method disrupts semantic consistency and leads to inefficient
059   training. As illustrated in Figure 1(c), pinyinGPT-embed (Tan et al., 2022) demonstrates superior
060   training efficiency, however, its performance remains suboptimal due to its inability to fully utilize
061   the pinyin information in the input during inference.
062   We explored the direct use of pinyin-constrained beam search outputs from large language models as
063   candidate word lists, resulting in substantial performance improvement. Nevertheless, this method
064   abandons pinyin information, which leads to a higher probability of prematurely pruning the correct
065   answer during the initial stages of beam search, particularly when the target’s prefix tokens are
066   infrequent. This presents opportunities for further improvement.
067   Therefore, we propose a novel approach named AttnInput to leverage large language models for
068   input method engine. It addresses the semantic discontinuity of previous methods by integrating
069   Pinyin information directly into the RWKV’s internal state through a lightweight side network. This
070   side network uses ladder side-tuning, attaching to the main model without requiring backpropaga-
071   tion through it, thus saving computational resources. The model is pre-trained, unlike many pre-
072   vious approaches which use fine-tuning, leading to more efficient use of training data and lower
073   computational cost. During inference, the model receives both the context and a sequence of abbre-
074   viated Pinyin, processing them together to predict the corresponding Chinese characters. The use
075
      of RWKV allows for efficient handling of long contexts and Pinyin sequences. Pinyin-constrained
      training and beam search are employed to further improve accuracy by restricting predictions to
076
      characters matching the given Pinyin. AttnInput offers the following advantages:
077
078
             • To the best of our knowledge, it achieves state-of-the-art performance on abbreviated
079            pinyin.
080
081          • In the training stage, it requires significantly less computational resources and training data
082            compared to previous work.
083
             • It is based on RWKV6(Peng et al., 2024), a linear attention large language model, which is
084
               more suitable for input method engine due to its ”infinite” context length1 and efficiency in
085
               inference.

088   2    TASK
089
090
      The input of pinyin input method includes a sequence of Chinese characters W = {w1 , ..., wn }
091
      representing the context and a sequence of abbreviated pinyin P = {p1 , ..., pm }. Each abbreviated
092   pinyin is a single English letter, ranging from a to z. The output is a sequence of Chinese characters
093   O = {wn+1 , ..., wn+m }. The output sequence follows the input sequence semantically, and the
094   pronunciation corresponds to the abbreviated pinyin.

097   3    M ODELS
098
099   In this section, we first introduce standard RWKV6 large language model. The vanilla RWKV6
100   model exhibits competitive performance compared to existing state-of-the-art models in IME tasks,
101   even when ignoring pinyin information during inference. Afterward, we will introduce the new
102   model named AttnInput, which can leverage enriched pinyin information during inference while
103   maintaining efficient training and inference performance.

156
157   As illustrated in Figure 2(b), we introduce the new model named AttnInput. We use the RWKV6

      model as the backbone model and attach a relatively small side network to the backbone model to
      extract the pinyin feature and integrate it with information from the context.

160   We integrate pinyin feature with context information by mapping the former to a fixed-size vector
161   through a linear layer and multiplying it with the internal state of the RWKV6 model. The formula

      3.3   L ADDER S IDE -T UNING
168
169   As illustrated in Figure 2(b), we employ ladder side-tuning (Sung et al., 2022) to attach side net-
170   works for mixing pinyin and context information. This approach avoids backpropagating updated
171   parameters through the backbone network.
172
      Due to the significantly fewer parameters in the side network compared to the backbone network, it
173
      can save a large amount of computation and memory usage for storing activation values, gradients
174   and optimizer states. See Appendix A for the detailed cost analysis.
175
176
      3.4   E NCODING PINYIN SEQUENCE
177
178   As illustrated in Figure 1(a), for a certain position i in the pinyin sequence, we select this position and
179   subsequent pinyin Pi = {pi , ..., pm } as the pinyin information input to the model at this position.
180   Therefore, there is no information interaction between the pinyin information at different positions in
181   the input. The output Oi at each position i is only related to the text context Wi = {w1 , ..., wn+i−1 }
182   and the pinyin information Pi . This ensures the efficiency of training, as each character’s pinyin
183   information is trained, while also maintaining consistency in the data input during both training and
184
      inference.
185   To encode the pinyin sequence, we employ a concatenation operation to combine all pinyin embed-
186   ding vectors into a unified representation. We pad pinyin sequences with zeros to a fixed length,
187   which is 16 in our experiments. Sequences exceeding this length are truncated. We tokenize pinyin
188   sequence by mapping each letter to its position in the alphabet.
189
190   3.5   E FFICIENT T RAINING
191
      As illustrated in Figure 1(e), the AttnInput model is trained in a pre-training manner, which is similar
192
      to the one used in the large language models. The pinyin sequences at each position are independent,
193
      with no information interaction between them, to ensure consistency during training and inference.
194   This method potentially enables the model to leverage pinyin information from a greater proportion
195   of tokens within the training data.
196
197
      However, for previous concat-based models like PinyinGPT-Concat and GeneInput, the design that
      connects pinyin to the context makes it necessary to train them using the SFT method, as shown
198
      in Figure 1(f). Assuming that the length of the context in the training data is n and the length of
199
      the pinyin is m, with n being much larger than m, only the pinyin information of m tokens will be
200                                                                n
      learned. This suggests that AttnInput potentially exhibits a m times improvement in training data
201   utilization compared to prior approaches.

      4.1.1      DATASET
225
226   We use SkyPile-150B (Wei et al., 2023) to generate training and evaluation dataset, which is a large-
227   scale and comprehensive Chinese dataset including 150 billion tokens and 620 gigabytes of text data.
228   SkyPile-150B is not included in the training datasets of the RWKV6 models. The corresponding
229   abbreviated pinyin sequences are automatically generated using the public Python library, pypinyin2 .
230   The evaluation data is derived from SkyPile-150B, with pinyin lengths ranging from 1 to 16 and
231   context lengths of 64 ,512 and 1536. Each evaluation set contains 500 context-pinyin pairs, which
232   are strictly separated from the training data.
233
234   4.1.2      T RAINING
235
236   We use RWKV6-1.6B, a pretrained RWKV6 model with 1.6B parameters, as the backbone model,
237   which is fixed during training. AttnInput have a side network with 500M trainable parameters. The
238
      loss function is cross-entropy loss. The max learning rate is 3e-4. The learning rate is decayed by
      cosine annealing with a warmup period of 300 steps. The optimizer is AdamW with a weight decay
239
      of 0.01. The batch size is 8. The context length is 1024. The length of pinyin sequence at each
240
      position is randomly selected from [0, 16]. The model is trained for 40K steps on a single RTX
241   4090D GPU.
242
243   To ensure a fair comparison with previous concat-based methods, we also trained a concat-based
244
      model with RWKV6-1.6B, labeled as RWKV6-concat-lora. This model was fine-tuned with LoRA
      (Hu et al., 2021) and includes 500M trainable parameters. The training data is the same as the
245
      AttnInput model.
246
247
      4.1.3      E VALUATION M ETRIC
248
249   We use the precision at top-K as the evaluation metric, which measures if the ground-truth Chinese
250   character sequence is among the top-K predicted sequences. K is set to 1, 5, 10, and 15.

311   4.3   A NALYSIS AND D ISCUSSION
312
313   We noticed that AttnInput performs slightly worse than vanilla RWKV6 in Top-1 accuracy. This
314   phenomenon is also observed in previous works (Tan et al., 2022). Our hypothesis is that the train-
315   ing procedure led to a slight degradation in the original model’s performance. We analyzed instances
316   where the vanilla RWKV6 model provided the correct answer, while AttnInput failed to prioritize
317   the target. Our investigation revealed that in these specific instances, the abbreviated pinyin corre-
318   sponded to numerous contextually appropriate Chinese character sequences, causing AttnInput to
319   encounter difficulties in accurately ranking them based on probability. This observation supports
320
      our initial hypothesis.
321   The performance gains observed in other metrics are hypothesized to be a consequence of AttnInput
322   boosting the scores of the initial target tokens based on pinyin information. This mechanism effec-
323   tively prevents the early elimination of potential target sequences during beam search, especially
      when the initial tokens are relatively rare.

399   5     R ELATED W ORKS
400
401   5.1   C LASSICAL P INYIN IME S
402
403   Pinyin Input Method Engines (IMEs) have been extensively studied for decades, with a focus on
404   improving accuracy and efficiency. Early methods relied heavily on statistical language models,
405   such as N-gram models (Chen & Lee, 2000), statistical machine translation (Yang et al., 2012) and
406   Conditional Random Fields (Xia & Cheung, 2016). These approaches often struggled with data
407
      sparsity and lacked the ability to capture long-range dependencies in language.
408
409   5.2   N EURAL P INYIN IME S
410
      Recent years have witnessed the successful application of neural networks to Pinyin IMEs. Long
411
      Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks (Zhang et al., 2019; Huang & Zhao, 2018) and attention-
412
      based neural networks (Huang et al., 2018) have achieved promising results by modeling sequential
413   data effectively. However, these models face limitations in capturing long-term dependencies and
414   parallelization during training.
415
416
      5.3   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS FOR P INYIN IME S
417
418   The emergence of large language models (LLMs) like GPT has opened up new possibilities for
419   Pinyin IMEs. Recent work has explored the use of LLMs for generating candidate characters based
420   on Pinyin input (Tan et al., 2022; Ding et al., 2023). However, directly applying LLMs to Pinyin
421   IMEs presents challenges, including semantic discontinuity caused by inserting Pinyin sequences
422   and the need for large amounts of training data and computational resources. Our work differs from
423   previous works in that we are the first one to fully leverage the power of large language models and
424
      train the models to learn pinyin-context relationships efficiently in a pre-training manner, achieving
      state-of-the-art performance with minimal training data and computational resources.
425

427   6     C ONCLUSION
428
429   This paper introduces AttnInput, a novel approach for Pinyin IME that effectively integrates Pinyin
430   information with a large language model, RWKV, for accurate and efficient Chinese character pre-
431   diction. By addressing semantic discontinuity and reducing computational overhead, AttnInput
      achieves state-of-the-art performance on abbreviated Pinyin input. Moreover, the efficient design of

435   user experience.

530   B    A B RIEF I NTRODUCTION TO H ANYU P INYIN AND I TS ROLE IN C HINESE
531        T EXT I NPUT
532
533   Hanyu Pinyin, or Pinyin, is the standard romanization system for Standard Mandarin Chinese. It
534   employs the Latin alphabet to represent the sounds of Mandarin, aiding in pronunciation and lan-
535   guage learning. Importantly, Pinyin is not a replacement for Chinese characters, which are the core
536
      written units conveying meaning in the language.
537   The relationship between Pinyin and Chinese characters can be summarized as:
538
539         • Characters as Semantic Units: Chinese characters are primarily logographic, with each
              character representing a morpheme or word and carrying meaning.


                                                       10
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


540
            • Pinyin as Phonetic Representation: Pinyin indicates the pronunciation of characters but
541           does not convey meaning directly.
542
            • Homophony and Context: A single Pinyin spelling can correspond to multiple charac-
543
              ters with different meanings due to homophones (same pronunciation, different meanings).
544
              Context is crucial for disambiguation. For example, the abbreviated pinyin ”JDGB” in Fig-
545           ure 1 can match multiple Chinese phrases, such as ”鸡蛋灌饼” (egg pancake) and ”见到
546           过吧” (have you seen it before).
547
            • Tones: Pinyin uses diacritical marks to denote the four main tones in Mandarin, which are
548
              essential for distinguishing meaning.
549
550   The advent of computers and mobile devices has made Pinyin indispensable for Chinese text input.
551   Pinyin input methods allow users to type Pinyin on a standard keyboard and then select the corre-
552   sponding Chinese characters from a list of suggestions. This technology significantly bridges the
553   gap between the phonetic representation of Pinyin and the character-based writing system.
