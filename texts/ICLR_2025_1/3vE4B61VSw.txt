                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009            Noise injection is applied in Split Learning to address privacy concerns about data
010            leakage. Previous works protect Split Learning by adding noise to the intermediate
011            results during the forward pass. Unfortunately, noisy signals significantly degrade
012            the accuracy of Split Learning training. This paper focuses on improving the
013            training accuracy of Split Learning over noisy signals while protecting training
014            data from reconstruction attacks. We propose two denoising techniques, namely
015            scaling and random masking. Our theoretical results show that both of our denoising
016            techniques accurately estimate the intermediate variables during the forward pass of
017
               Split Learning. Moreover, our experiments with deep neural networks demonstrate
               that the proposed denoising approaches allow Split Learning to tolerate high
018
               noise levels while achieving almost the same accuracy as the noise-free baseline.
019
               Interestingly, we show that after applying our denoising techniques, the resultant
020            network is more resilient against a state-of-the-art attack compared to the simple
021            noise injection approach.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Privacy concerns in many application domains, such as finance, healthcare, and online commerce,
027
      constrain the sharing of raw data that are necessary to train accurate deep neural networks (DNNs).
      Split Learning Gupta & Raskar (2018); Vepakomma et al. (2018a) has recently emerged as a solution
028
      that allows different parties to learn a model collaboratively, without explicitly sharing raw input data.
029
      Typically, in two-party Split Learning, the Split Neural Network (SplitNN) is divided between the
030   data owner, a.k.a. the client, and the label owner, a.k.a. the server, as shown in Figure 1 (a). During
031   training, in the forward pass, the client forwards the intermediate results (IRs) (i.e., the neurons of the
032   cut layer, the last layer in the client’s part of the DNN) to the server. The server completes the forward
033   pass and during the backpropagation, it returns the gradients of the IRs to the client. Consequently,
034   the client can train the joint model without revealing the private training data to the server.
035
      Unfortunately, sharing only the IRs does not protect the raw data. The shared IRs contain considerable
036   latent information about the data and can be used to stage powerful attacks, such as model inversion
037   attack He et al. (2019a); Zhang et al. (2020); Erdogan et al. (2021), label inference attack Erdogan
038   et al. (2021); Kariyappa & Qureshi (2021); Li et al. (2021) and hijacking attack Pasquini et al. (2021).
039   Several works Titcombe et al. (2021); Abuadbba et al. (2020); Mireshghallah et al. (2020); Wang
040   et al. (2018) attempt to mitigate that risk through adding a certain amount of noise to the IRs before
041   sharing with the other party. However, one of the fundamental issues of adding noise is the trade-off
042   between the trained model quality and its susceptibility toward an external attacker. While high levels
043   of noise are favorable in making the training data private, the noise inevitably impacts the quality
044
      of the trained the model Abuadbba et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2020a; 2021), that is the accuracy;
      see Figures 1 (b) and (c) for an example. Thus, a fundamental question is: Can we improve the
045
      training accuracy of Split Learning under noise injection, without affecting the making of the model
046
      vulnerable to data leakage?
047
048   We answer the above question affirmatively by applying a post-processing denoising layer on top of
049   noise-injected IRs in the Split Learning process. Our intuition is that the injected noise introduces
050
      an error during the forward pass, which is dominated by variance when the noise level is high. As
      long as we can reduce the variance by denoising techniques, the training quality should be improved.
051
      Such a post-processing layer will not impose any additional private information leakage as long as it
052
      does not interact with the original private data.

      Federated Learning (FL) with noise injection. Applying noise injection in vertical FL shares some
086
      similarities with noise-injected SplitNN since in both cases, the noise is injected on intermediate
087
      results during the forward pass. Existing works Wang et al. (2020b); Chen et al. (2020) propose to add
088   Gaussian/Laplacian noise on participants’ IRs to protect private training data or labels. Unfortunately,
089   Chen et al. (2020) only demonstrates the impact on training accuracy when some applications have a
090   relatively low noise scale. The other framework Wang et al. (2020b) proposes a similar noise injection
091   technique only for linear model collaborative learning, which is not directly applicable to general
092   DNNs.
093
      Split Learning with noise injection. Due to the vulnerability of SplitNN against model inversion
094
      attacks, Titcombe et al. (2021) proposed to apply differentially private noise injection on IRs during
095
      the inference time to prevent data reconstruction by the attacker. Shredder, proposed by Mireshghallah
096   et al. (2020), adaptively generates a noise mask to minimize mutual information between input and
097   intermediate data. However, these two methods only introduce noise injection during the inference
098   time; thus, the privacy of training data is not preserved. Abuadbba et al. (2020) successfully applies
099   noise to the IRs during the training to defend against model inversion attacks on one-dimensional
100   ECG data; also, see Wu et al. (2023) for SpiltNN with differential privacy for integrated terrestrial and
101   non-terrestrial Networks. It turns out that the noise has dramatically impacted the model’s accuracy.
102   Unlike previous works that only focus on the attack defense efficacy, we target to improve the training
103   accuracy with a significant noise level.
104   In this paper, we provide an answer to split learning denoising by proposing two post-processing
105   techniques (i.e., scaling and masking) to improve the accuracy and stability of the splitNN training
106   when Gaussian noise is injected into the IRs. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to propose
107   denoising techniques on the Gaussian noise-injected IRs to improve the training accuracy of SplitNN
      and theoretically show the privacy guarantee in this setup. In the following, for completeness, we

108
      mention some differential privacy (DP) techniques used in federated learning (FL) and related settings
109   for completeness.
110
111   Gaussian noise injections (GNIs) are a family of regularization methods for DNN training through
112   adding Gaussian noise on the activations or weights during the forward pass. It is similar to the noise
113   injection in SplitNN except for the following two aspects: (i) There is no requirement of bounded
114
      sensitivity in injection objects. (ii) The noise scale is usually set small to avoid negative impacts on
      training accuracy. The explicit regularization effects of GNIs are well investigated in Camuto et al.
115
      (2020); Li & Liu (2020); Lim et al. (2021), demonstrating better generalization for trained models
116
      over unseen data. In addition, GNIs can improve the robustness of DNNs against adversarial attacks
117   or data perturbations Lim et al. (2021); He et al. (2019b). However, Camuto et al. (2021) also found
118   that GNIs can also introduce some implicit bias on gradient updates, which inevitably degrades the
119   training accuracy.
120
121   Denoising. Adding the Gaussian noise and the denoising mechanisms in our work shares many simi-
122
      larities with the differential privacy (DP) and their post-processing that maintains the DP guarantee
      and often improves accuracy Zhu et al. (2022; 2021). Denoising for DP has been well adopted in the
123
      statistical estimation Hay et al. (2009; 2010); Nikolov et al. (2013); Bernstein et al. (2017), where
124
      they exploit some prior knowledge to design data release mechanism with better DP utility. Recently,
125   Balle & Wang (2018)   proposed an optimal denoising technique for Gaussian mechanism, where given
126   y ∼ N f (x), σ 2 I and their target is to find a postprocessing function g such that g(y) is closer to
127   f (x) than y. This is substantially different from SplitNN as there are subsequent layers on top of
128   the Gaussian mechanism in the training process. Nasr & Shokri (2020) has also investigated using
129   scaling as a denoising technique to improve the DP utility for DP-SGD Abadi et al. (2016). However,
130   the authors scale up/down the noisy gradients based on the "usefulness" of gradients while we utilize
131   scaling to minimize the estimation error of the noisy neural network outputs. Wang et al. (2020a)
132   showed that adding Laplacian smoothing on Gaussian noise-injected gradients can improve the utility
133   of DP-SGD. While Ligett et al. (2017) proposed a general noise reduction framework, Lecuyer et al.
134
      (2019) proposed PiXel-DP, an adversarial defense mechanism, scalable to diverse large networks and
      datasets.

259   3.3   D IFFERENTIAL PRIVACY (DP) PRESERVATION
260
      Recent works show that Split Learning with Laplacian or Gaussian noise injection at the cut layer is
261
      resilient to attacks Abuadbba et al. (2020); Wu et al. (2023). In particular, DP (see Definition in §A.4)
262
      could be used to describe the privacy guarantee. In this sub-section, we explain how our modification
263   (scaling or masking) to Split Learning with noise injection (such as GNI) preserves DP. The key
264   to our argument is to view our modification as a post-processing of a DP mechanism. Then by the
265   immunity of DP to post-processing, we can conclude that DP will be preserved.

516   5   C ONCLUSION
517
518   We propose scaling and masking as denoising techniques to achieve accurate Split Learning on
519   noisy signals. We show theoretically and empirically that denoising helps achieve more accurate
520
      intermediate outputs in DNN training under noise injection that significantly improves the stability
      and accuracy of Split Learning. Additionally, we show that the masking technique can provide better
521
      security enhancement than scaling against powerful attacks. Although in theory, scaling has better
522
      denoising efficacy, masking is likely to show better accuracy improvement due to its easier parameter
523   tuning. Finally, we demonstrate the possibility of co-optimization of denoising and attack defense.

1138   private if for all adjacent inputs, D, D′ ∈ D, with Hamming distance, d(D, D′ ) = 1, and all possible
1139   output, O ∈ Rm ∈ B(Rm ) such that

       In the FSHA Pasquini et al. (2021) attack, the attacker (e.g. the server) can hijack the client’s learning
1440
       process and learn an inverse version of the client’s model. During the inference, the attacker can
1441
       recover the client’s raw data by using the output of the client. In this work, we showed that the
1442   masking operator simultaneously improves the SplitNN training and in the meantime, decreases
1443   the efficacy of the FSHA attack. Our intuition is that the denoising effect depends on the specific
1444   application, which is a function applied to the noisy input, X + ∆. If the function’s goal is to identify
1445   each value of X, such as reconstructing an image in an FSHA attack, then denoising cannot help
1446   too much. However, if the goal is to get a more accurate estimation on some statistical metrics of X,
1447   such as the mean, norm of X, then it is possible to have an evident denoising improvement.
