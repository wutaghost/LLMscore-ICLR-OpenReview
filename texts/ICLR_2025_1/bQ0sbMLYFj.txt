011                                                    A BSTRACT
012
013             Due to their multimodal capabilities, Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have
014             found numerous impactful applications in real-world scenarios. However, recent
015             studies have revealed that VLMs are vulnerable to image-based adversarial at-
016
                tacks, particularly targeted adversarial images that manipulate the model to gen-
                erate harmful content specified by the adversary. Current attack methods rely on
017
                predefined target labels to create targeted adversarial attacks, which limits their
018
                scalability and applicability for large-scale robustness evaluations. In this pa-
019             per, we propose AnyAttack, a self-supervised framework that generates targeted
020             adversarial images for VLMs without label supervision, allowing any image to
021             serve as a target for the attack. To address the limitation of existing methods
022             that require label supervision, we introduce a contrastive loss that trains a gener-
023             ator on a large-scale unlabeled image dataset, LAION-400M dataset, for generat-
024             ing targeted adversarial noise. This large-scale pre-training endows our method
025             with powerful transferability across a wide range of VLMs. Extensive experi-
026             ments on five mainstream open-source VLMs (CLIP, BLIP, BLIP2, InstructBLIP,
027
                and MiniGPT-4) across three multimodal tasks (image-text retrieval, multimodal
                classification, and image captioning) demonstrate the effectiveness of our attack.
028
                Additionally, we successfully transfer AnyAttack to multiple commercial VLMs,
029
                including Google’s Gemini, Claude’s Sonnet, and Microsoft’s Copilot. These re-
030             sults reveal an unprecedented risk to VLMs, highlighting the need for effective
031             countermeasures. Upon publication, we will release the pre-trained generator to
032             support further research in addressing this challenge.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have exhibited remarkable performance across a diverse array
038   of tasks, primarily attributed to the scale of training data and model size (Radford et al., 2021;
039   Li et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024). Despite their remarkable performance, these models, heavily
040   reliant on visual inputs, remain vulnerable to image-based adversarial attacks1 , which are carefully
041   crafted input images designed to mislead the model into making incorrect predictions (Szegedy et al.,
042
      2013). While general, untargeted adversarial attacks only aim to induce incorrect outputs, targeted
      adversarial attacks present a more insidious threat, manipulating the model’s output to yield an
043
      adversary-specified, predetermined response. For instance, a benign image such as a landscape
044
      could be subtly altered to elicit harmful text descriptions such as “violence” or “explicit content”
045   from the model. Such manipulation could have severe implications for content moderation systems,
046   potentially leading to the removal of legitimate content or the inappropriate distribution of harmful
047   material.
048
      Both targeted adversarial attacks and jailbreak techniques (Zou et al., 2023; Bagdasaryan et al., 2023;
049
      Carlini et al., 2024) aim to induce harmful responses from models. However, the key distinction lies
050
      in their outputs: targeted adversarial attacks produce adversary-specified, predetermined responses,
051   whereas jailbreak attacks elicit non-predetermined responses. We will further elaborate on this dis-

      To address this limitation, we propose a novel self-supervised learning framework called AnyAt-
074   tack, which utilizes the original image itself as supervision, allowing any image to serve as a target
075   for the deployment of targeted adversarial attacks. Figure 1 illustrates the differences between
076   our framework and the existing methods. Drawing inspiration from popular contrastive learning
077   techniques (Chen et al., 2020; He et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023), we introduce
078   a contrastive loss to allow training a generator on the large-scale dataset to generate targeted ad-
079   versarial noise. This encourages the generated noise to emulate the original image itself (positive
080   pairs) in the embedding space while differentiating from other images (negative pairs). The unsuper-
081   vised nature of our method enables large-scale pre-training of our generator on the LAION-400M
082
      dataset (Schuhmann et al., 2021), allowing for the generation of effective adversarial noise. Bene-
      fiting from this large-scale pre-training and exposure to a broader spectrum of images, our method
083
      shows powerful transferability across a wide range of VLMs. To the best of our knowledge, this is
084
      the first time such a large-scale dataset has been employed to train a generator for the generation
085   of targeted adversarial noise. Notably, the generator can produce adversarial images with any given
086   image, imposing no restrictions on the image itself, thereby introducing an unprecedented security
087   risk to the entire community.
088
      Furthermore, to enable adaptation to specific domains and multimodal tasks, we fine-tune the
089
      pre-trained generator on downstream datasets for adapting downstream vision-language tasks. To
090
      demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach, we conduct extensive experiments on 5 target VLMs
091   (CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), BLIP (Li et al., 2022), BLIP2 (Li et al., 2023), InstructBLIP (Dai et al.,
092   2023), and MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2024)), across 3 multimodal tasks (image-text retrieval, multi-
093   modal classification, and image captioning). We also evaluate our method on commercial VLMs,
094   including Google’s Gemini, Claude’s Sonnet, and Microsoft’s Copilot.
095
      In summary, our main contributions are:
096

098                 sarial attacks on VLMs without the need for target labels, allowing any image to be used
099                 as a target for the attack.
100
101               • We introduce a contrastive loss that facilitates the training of the generator for adversarial
102                 noise using unlabeled, large-scale datasets. This makes our AnyAttack framework scal-
103                 able, effectively overcoming the limitations of previous methods.
104               • We demonstrate the effectiveness of our AnyAttack on five mainstream open-source VLMs
105                 across three multimodal tasks. Additionally, we successfully transfer our attack to three
106                 commercial VLMs. These results offer valuable insights into the vulnerabilities of state-
107                 of-the-art models in real-world applications.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Targeted Adversarial Attacks A number of works have been proposed to enhance the effective-
111
      ness and transferability of targeted adversarial attacks against vision models. Input augmentation
112   techniques like image translation (Dong et al., 2019), cropping (Wei et al., 2023), mixup (Wang
113   et al., 2021; Liu & Lyu, 2024), and resizing (Xie et al., 2019), have been employed to increase the
114   diversity of adversarial inputs, thereby improving their transferability across different target mod-
115   els. Additionally, adversarial fine-tuning and model enhancement techniques have been explored
116   to bolster the attack capabilities of surrogate models (Springer et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Wu
117   et al., 2024). These methods typically involve retraining the surrogate models with a mix of clean
118   and adversarial examples to improve their robustness against future attacks. Furthermore, optimiza-
119   tion techniques have evolved to stabilize the update processes during adversarial training. Methods
120
      such as adaptive learning rates and gradient clipping have been integrated to ensure more consistent
      updates and enhance the overall performance of the adversarial attacks (Dong et al., 2018; Wang &
121
      He, 2021; Lin et al., 2023). These advancements collectively contribute to the development of more
122
      effective and transferable adversarial attacks in the realm of vision models.
123
124
      Jailbreak Attacks on VLMs VLMs have revolutionized DNNs by leveraging large-scale pre-
125
      training on diverse image-text datasets. These models learn to integrate visual and textual informa-
126   tion effectively, enabling superb performance across a wide range of tasks. Broadly, VLMs can be
127   categorized into two types: the first offers multimodal functionalities built on large language models
128   (LLMs), complemented by visual models, such as BLIP2 (Li et al., 2023), InstructBLIP (Dai et al.,
129   2023), and MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2024). The second type provides a more balanced approach,
130   bridging textual and visual modalities efficiently, as seen in models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021),
131   ALIGN (Jia et al., 2021), and BLIP (Li et al., 2022). Recent advancements in VLMs have spurred
132   parallel research into their vulnerabilities, with jailbreaks and adversarial attacks emerging as dis-
133   tinct threat vectors. Multimodal jailbreaks primarily exploit cross-modal interaction vulnerabilities
134
      in VLMs, with the intention of influencing LLMs (Zou et al., 2023). These attacks manipulate in-
      puts of text (Wu et al., 2023), images (Carlini et al., 2024; Gong et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2024; Niu
135
      et al., 2024), or both simultaneously (Ying et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024), aiming to elicit harmful
136
      but non-predefined responses. In contrast, image-based adversarial attacks focus on manipulating
137   the image encoder of VLMs, typically leaving the LLM component largely undisturbed. The objec-
138   tive is to induce adversary-specified, predetermined responses through precise visual manipulations.
139   Understanding these differences is crucial for explaining our methodology.
140
141   Adversarial Attacks on VLMs Adversarial research on VLMs is relatively limited compared to
142   the extensive studies on vision models, with the majority of existing attacks focusing primarily on
143   untargeted attacks. Co-Attack (Zhang et al., 2022) was among the first to perform white-box untar-
144   geted attacks on several VLMs. Following this, more approaches have been proposed to enhance
145   adversarial transferability for black-box untargeted attacks (Lu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Yin
146   et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024). Cross-Prompt Attack (Luo et al., 2024) investigates a novel setup
147   for adversarial transferability based on the prompts of language models. AttackVLM (Zhao et al.,
148
      2024) is the most closely related work, using a combination of text inputs and popular text-to-image
      models to generate guided images for creating targeted adversarial images. Although their approach
149
      shares a similar objective with our work, our method distinguishes itself by being self-supervised
150
      and independent of any text-based guidance.

174   We begin by formulating the problem of targeted adversarial attacks. Let fs represent a pre-trained
175   surrogate model, and D = {(x, y)} denote the image dataset, where x is the original image and y
176   is the corresponding label (description). The attacker’s objective is to craft an adversarial example
177   x′ = x + δ that misleads the target model ft into predicting a predefined target label yt . In the
178   context of VLMs, this objective requires that x′ aligns with yt as a valid image-text pair. The
179
      process of generating targeted adversarial images typically involves finding a perturbation δ using
      the surrogate model fs . Existing strategies can be approached through two primary strategies: target
180
      label supervision and target image supervision.
181
182   The first approach utilizes the target label yt as supervision, directing the embedding of the adver-
183   sarial image x′ to align with that of yt , as demonstrated in AttackVLM-it Zhao et al. (2024). The
184   second approach employs the target image xt , which corresponds to yt , as supervision to encourage
185
      the embedding of x′ to replicate that of xt . This is illustrated in AttackVLM-ii (Zhao et al., 2024)
      and certain image-based attacks (Wei et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024). Both methods depend on ex-
186
      plicit target supervision, as summarized in Table 1. In these approaches, L denotes a distance-based
187
      loss function, such as Euclidean distance or cosine similarity. In contrast, our method, AnyAttack,
188   employs the input image itself to guide the attack and thus is unsupervised. In this context, xr is a
189   random image that is unrelated to x, while the adversarial noise δ is designed to align with the origi-
190   nal image x within the surrogate model’s embedding space. In summary, existing methods generate
191   adversarial noise that mimics other images, whereas our approach produces adversarial noise that
192   closely resembles the original image itself.

195   3.2   A NYATTACK
196
197   Framework Overview Our proposed framework, AnyAttack, employs a two-stage training
198   paradigm: pre-training and fine-tuning. Figure 2 provides a framework overview of AnyAttack.
199
      In the pre-training stage, we train a decoder F , to produce adversarial noise δ on large-scale datasets
200
      Dp . Given a batch of images x, we extract their embeddings using a frozen image encoder E. These
201   normalized embeddings z are then fed into the decoder F , which generates adversarial noise δ cor-
202   responding to the images x. To enhance generalization and computational efficiency, we introduce a
203   K-augmentation strategy that creates multiple shuffled versions of the original images within each
204   mini-batch. During this process, adversarial noise is added to the shuffled original images (unre-
205   lated images) to produce the adversarial images. After passing through E, we employ a contrastive
206   loss to maximize the cosine similarity between positive sample pairs (the i-th elements of the ad-
207   versarial and original embeddings) while minimizing the similarity between negative sample pairs
208   (the remaining elements). This approach trains the decoder F to ensure that the perturbed images
209
      resemble the original images in the embedding space of encoder E, while distinguishing them from
      the shuffled versions.
210
211   In the fine-tuning stage, we adapt the pre-trained decoder F to a specific downstream dataset Df .
212   The frozen encoder E continues to provide embeddings that guide the generation of adversarial
213   noise δ. We use an unrelated random image xr from an external dataset De as the clean image to
214   synthesize the adversarial image xr + δ. Unlike the pre-training stage, where only the encoder is
215
      utilized as a surrogate model, we introduce auxiliary models during the fine-tuning stage to enhance
      transferability. We tailor various fine-tuning strategies based on the requirements of each task.

236   Pre-training Stage The pre-training phase of AnyAttack aims to train the generator on large-scale
237   datasets, enabling it to handle a diverse array of input images as potential targets. Given a batch of
238
      n images x ∈ Rn×H×W ×3 from the large-scale training dataset Dp , we employ the CLIP ViT-
      B/32 image encoder, which is frozen during training, as the encoder E, to obtain the normalized
239
      embeddings E(x) = z ∈ Rn×d corresponding to the original images x, where d represents the
240
      embedding dimension (i.e., 512 for CLIP ViT-B/32). Subsequently, we deploy an initialized decoder
241   F , which maps the embeddings z to adversarial noise D(z) = δ ∈ Rn×H×W ×3 corresponding to
242   the original images x. We expect the generated noises δ to serve as adversarial noise representative
243   of the original images x. Our goal is for the generated noises δ, when added to random images xr ,
244   to be interpreted by the encoder E as the original images x, i.e., E(xr + δ) = E(x).
245
      However, when the number of random images is smaller than the training dataset, the generated
246
      noises δ may overfit to this limited set, leading to poor generalization for F . To address this, we
247   propose the K-augmentation strategy, which expands the set of random images to match the size
248   of the training dataset Dp . This strategy increases the number of sample pairs within each batch by
249   a factor of K, thereby improving computational efficiency. Specially, K-augmentation duplicates
250   both adversarial noises δ and the original images x K times, forming K mini-batches. For each
251   mini-batch, the order of the adversarial noises remain consistent, while the order of the original
252   images is shuffled within the mini-batch, referred to as shuffled images. These shuffled images
253   are then added to the corresponding adversarial noise, resulting in adversarial images x′ . Next,
254   the adversarial images are fed into F to produce adversarial embeddings z(adv) , which are then
255   used for subsequent calculations against the original embeddings z. We introduce a contrastive loss
256
      that maximizes the cosine similarity between positive sample pairs, defined by the i-th elements of
      adversarial and original embeddings in each mini-batch, while minimizing the similarity between the
257
      negative pairs, which consist of all other elements. This setup creates n positive pairs and n(n − 1)

      3.2.1    F INE - TUNING S TAGE
271
272   In the fine-tuning stage, we refine the pre-trained decoder F on downstream vision-language datasets
273   using task-specific objective functions, facilitating its adaptation to particular domains and multi-
274   modal tasks. The motivation for fine-tuning arises from scenarios where well-defined multimodal
275   tasks and in-domain images are available. Given a batch of n images x ∈ Rn×H×W ×3 from the
276
      downstream dataset Df , the encoder E remains frozen and outputs the embeddings z, which are then
      fed into the decoder F to generate the noise δ. We randomly select images from an external dataset
277
      De as unrelated images xr , which are then added to the generated noise δ to create adversarial im-
278
      ages. To improve transferability, we incorporate auxiliary models alongside the encoder E, forming
279   an ensemble surrogate. Drawing on research in ensemble learning for adversarial attacks (Liu et al.,
280   2017), we select auxiliary models based on model diversity, as greater differences between mod-
281   els are known to improve complementarity. This ensures that both adversarial and original images
282   maintain consistency across the embedding spaces of multiple models.
283
      Depending on the downstream tasks, we employ two different fine-tuning objectives. The first
284
      strategy is tailored for the image-text retrieval task, which imposes stricter requirements for distin-
285   guishing between similar samples. It demands robust retrieval performance in both directions: from
286   z(adv) to z and from z to z(adv) . This motivates the adoption of a bidirectional InfoNCE loss:

304
      Baselines We first employed the state-of-the-art (SOTA) targeted adversarial attack for VLMs,
305   referred to as AttackVLM (Zhao et al., 2024). This method includes two variations: AttackVLM-ii
306   and AttackVLM-it, which are based on different attack objectives. Both methods utilize the CLIP
307   ViT-B/32 image encoder as the surrogate model, consistent with our approach. Additionally, we
308   incorporated two targeted adversarial attacks designed for visual classification models: SU (Wei
309   et al., 2023) and SASD-WS (Wu et al., 2024). Since the original cross-entropy loss used in these
310   methods is not suitable for vision-language tasks, we modified them to employ cosine loss and mean
311   squared error (MSE) loss to match targeted images. These modified methods are denoted as SU-
312   Cos/SASD-WS-Cos and SU-MSE/SASD-WS-MSE, respectively. For the SU attack, the surrogate
313
      model is configurable, and we set it to align with our proposed method, namely, CLIP ViT-B/32. For
      the SASD-WS attack, we utilized the officially released weights, as its surrogate model includes a
314
      self-enhancement component. We denote our proposed methods as AnyAttack-Cos, AnyAttack-Bi,
315
      AnyAttack-Cos w/ Aux, and AnyAttack-Bi w/ Aux. These represent AnyAttack fine-tuned with
316   LCos , fine-tuned with LBi , fine-tuned with LCos using auxiliary models, and fine-tuned with LBi
317   using auxiliary models, respectively.

361   Metric In this work, we examine perturbations constrained by the ℓ∞ norm, ensuring that the per-
362   turbation δ satisfies the condition ∥δ∥∞ ≤ ϵ, where ϵ defines the maximum allowable magnitude of
363   the perturbation. We use the attack success rate (ASR) as the primary evaluation metric to assess
364   the performance of targeted adversarial attacks. The calculation of ASR varies slightly depend-
365
      ing on the specific task. For instance, in image-text retrieval tasks, ASR represents the recall rate
      between adversarial images and their corresponding ground-truth text descriptions. In multimodal
366
      classification tasks, ASR refers to the accuracy of correctly classifying pairs of ”adversarial image
367
      and ground-truth description.” Essentially, ASR is calculated by replacing clean images with their
368   adversarial counterparts and then computing the relevant task-specific evaluation metric.

526   5     C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we introduced AnyAttack, a novel self-supervised framework for generating targeted
529   adversarial attacks on VLMs. Our approach overcomes the scalability limitations of previous meth-
530   ods by enabling the use of any image to serve as a target for attack target without label supervision.
531   Through extensive experiments, we demonstrated the effectiveness of AnyAttack across multiple
532   VLMs and vision-language tasks, revealing significant vulnerabilities in state-of-the-art models.
533   Notably, our method showed considerable transferability, even to commercial VLMs, highlighting
534   the broad implications of our findings.
535   These results underscore the urgent need for robust defense mechanisms in VLM systems. As VLMs
536   become increasingly prevalent in real-world applications, our work opens new avenues for research
537   in VLM security, particularly considering that this is the first time pre-training has been conducted
538   on a large-scale dataset like LAION-400M. This emphasizes the critical importance of addressing
539   these challenges. Future work should focus on developing resilient VLMs and exploring potential
      mitigation strategies against such targeted attacks.
