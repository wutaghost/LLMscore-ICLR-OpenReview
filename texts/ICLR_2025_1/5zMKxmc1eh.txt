011                                                              A BSTRACT
012
013                     We investigate oscillation phenomena observed in inversion methods applied to
014                     large text-to-image diffusion models, particularly the “Flux” model. Using a
015                     fixed-point-inspired iteration method to invert real-world images, we find that the
016
                        solution does not converge but instead oscillates between distinct clusters. Our
                        results, validated both on real diffusion models and toy experiments, show that
017
                        these oscillated clusters exhibit significant semantic coherence. We propose that
018
                        this phenomenon arises from oscillatory solutions in dynamic systems, linking it
019                     to the structure of rectified flow models. The oscillated clusters serve as local la-
020                     tent distributions that allow for effective semantic-based image optimization.We
021                     provide theoretical insights, linking these oscillations to fixed-point dynamics and
022                     proving conditions for stable cluster formation and differentiation in flow models.

042   1        I NTRODUCTION
043
044   Very recently, large text-to-image diffusion models utilizing rectified flow ( Wang et al. (2024)), like
045   the Flux model from Black Forest Labs, have demonstrated exceptional performance in generating
046   high-quality images with rapid sampling. However, the underlying latent structure of rectified flow-
047   based models presents unique challenges, as it differs fundamentally from the layered manifold
048   structure of DDPMs ( Ho et al. (2020)) shaped by a parameterized Markov chain. This distinction
049   makes previous inversion techniques, such as DDIM inversion ( Song et al. (2020a)), and editing
050
      methods like SDEdit ( Meng et al. (2021)), less viable. Therefore, adopting a new perspective for
      understanding and navigating the latent space of these flow-based models is essential for enabling
051
      more effective inversion and image manipulation strategies.
052
053   When attempting to invert real-world images using fix-point iteration methods in rectified flow-
      based models, we observe that the sequence of iterates does not converge to a single point but


                                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      instead oscillates between several clusters. These clusters are semantically meaningful and can be
055   leveraged for image optimization and editing tasks. This behavior contrasts with the fixed-point
056   methods used in DDIM, which are primarily designed to mitigate numerical accumulation errors at
057   each step, ensuring smooth convergence to a single, stable solution. The oscillatory nature observed
058   in rectified flow-based models, however, opens up new possibilities for iterative refinement and
059   enhanced flexibility in image inversion tasks.
060
      To investigate this, we first propose Oscillation Inversion, a method that uses fixed-point iteration
061   to directly establish a one-to-one mapping between noisy latents at a given timestep and the cor-
062   responding encoded image latent. The inverted latents oscillate among several clusters, which can
063   serve as local latent distributions, facilitating effective semantic-based image optimization. Addi-
064   tionally, we generalize this fixed-point method in three ways for broader downstream applications:
065   1) Group Inversion: We invert a group of images simultaneously, rather than a single image, en-
066   abling semantic guidance and blending across images. 2) Finetuned Inversion: By controlling the
067   oscillation direction, we provide a mechanism for customized, user-driven manipulation of the im-
068   ages. 3) Post-Inversion Optimization: After inversion, we perform optimization, and analyze the
069
      differentiable structure induced by the separated sub-distributions created by oscillation inversion.
      These extensions make Oscillation Inversion a versatile tool for various image manipulation tasks.
070
071   The main contributions of this work are as follows:
072
073         • We propose Oscillation Inversion to facilitate one-step inversion to any timestep in rectified
074           flow-based diffusion models for the semantic manipulation of latents. Additionally, we
075
              present three extensions that enable diverse user inputs for real-world applications.
076         • Extensive experiments on various downstream tasks, such as image restoration and en-
077           hancement, stroke based make up transfer, validate our theoretical findings and demonstrate
078           the effectiveness of our method on both perceptual quality and data fidelity.

147

148

149   and the latent variable is progressively refined through a sequence of transformations. The forward

349   In this section, we refer to our proposed method, Oscillation Inversion, as OInv. Our experiments
350   consist of three parts: stroke-based human makeup synthesis to demonstrate the effectiveness of
351   OInv-Finetune, human face post-enhancement to refine industry-standard enhancement results and

      Experiment Settings All of our experiments are based on the ‘black-forest-labs/FLUX.1-schnell’
355
      checkpoint. We run the experiments on a single A6000 GPU with 48GB of memory. All images are
356
      cropped and resized to 512×512 pixels. The oscillation inversion consistently runs for 30 iterations,

362   4.1   I MAGE R ESTORATION AND E NHANCEMENT
363
364   Image restoration and enhancement can be seen as a specialized editing task that aims to recover an
365   underlying clean image with high fidelity and detail from degraded measurements. Existing inver-
366   sion methods, such as BlindDPS Chihaoui et al. (2024), often achieve good perceptual quality but
367   tend to compromise on fidelity to the original image. Meanwhile, current image restoration tech-
368   niques and enhancers, like ILVR Choi et al. (2021), struggle with real-world blind scenarios where
369
      the type of degradation is unknown or undefined. Image enhancement is a challenging problem, as
      it involves transferring a degenerated distribution to a natural image distribution. To demonstrate
370
      the effectiveness of our method in discovering high-quality distributions, as discussed in Section ??,
371
      we perform both qualitative and quantitative evaluations. We use real-world degraded (low-quality)
372   images for the qualitative assessment and apply simulated noise, blur, and low-resolution degrada-
373   tion to the CelebA validation dataset ( Liu et al. (2015)) for the quantitative analysis. For the latter,
374   we follow the protocols of previous studies, using metrics like PSNR and LPIPS to measure per-
375   formance. We compare our approach against existing image restoration and enhancement methods,
376   including BlindDPS, DIP, GDP, and BIRD ( Chung et al. (2023); Ulyanov et al. (2018); Fei et al.
377   (2023); Chihaoui et al. (2024)). Since our approach is positioned as a post-processing image en-
      hancement technique, we also select an image enhancement baseline. We chose Piscart due to its

524   6    C ONCLUSIONS
525
526   In this work, we introduced Oscillation Inversion, a novel method for image manipulation within
527   rectified flow-based diffusion models, addressing the challenges posed by the unique latent struc-
528   tures of these models. Our approach leverages oscillatory behavior in fixed-point iteration to enable
529   semantic-based image optimization, while extensions like Group Inversion, Finetuned Inversion, and
530   Post-Inversion Optimization provide flexibility for diverse image editing tasks. Theoretical analy-
531   sis and extensive experiments across various applications, including image restoration, relighting,
532
      and watermark removal, validate the effectiveness of our method, showcasing improvements in both
      perceptual quality and fidelity.
