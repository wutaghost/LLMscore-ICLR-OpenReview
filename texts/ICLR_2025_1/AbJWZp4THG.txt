                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Adaptive optimization plays a pivotal role in federated learning, where simultane-
010
               ous server and client-side adaptivity have been shown to be essential for maximiz-
011            ing its performance. However, the scalability of jointly adaptive systems is often
012            constrained by limited resources in communication and memory. In this paper,
013            we introduce a class of efficient adaptive algorithms, named FedAda2 , designed
014            specifically for large-scale, cross-device federated environments. FedAda2 op-
015            timizes communication efficiency by avoiding the transfer of preconditioners be-
016            tween the server and clients. At the same time, it leverages memory-efficient
017            adaptive optimizers on the client-side to reduce on-device memory consumption.
018            Theoretically, we demonstrate that FedAda2 achieves the same convergence rates
019
               for general, non-convex objectives as its more resource-intensive counterparts that
               directly integrate joint adaptivity. Empirically, we showcase the benefits of joint
020
               adaptivity and the effectiveness of FedAda2 on both image and text datasets.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Federated learning is a distributed learning paradigm which aims to train statistical models across
026   multiple clients while minimizing raw data exposure (McMahan et al., 2017; Li et al., 2020a; Wang
027   et al., 2021a). In vanilla federated learning, a central server orchestrates the training process by dis-
028   tributing the global model to a subsample of thousands or even millions of clients. These clients col-
029   laboratively perform local stochastic gradient descent while drawing from their private data streams.
030   After several epochs have elapsed, each client communicates their aggregate updates to the server,
031   which averages this information to make an informed adjustment to the global model. This algo-
032   rithm, using non-adaptive weight updates, is called FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017). A recent trend
033
      is to investigate utilizing adaptive optimizers to support federated learning (Reddi et al., 2021).
      Adaptivity can be employed in either the server-side or the client-side, where joint adaptivity (con-
034
      sisting of global and local adaptive updates) has been shown to play a pivotal role in accelerating
035
      convergence and enhancing accuracy (Wang et al., 2021b).
036
037   Nevertheless, efficiency challenges remain for the successful deployment of jointly adaptive algo-
038   rithms in practice, especially in cross-device federated settings (Kairouz et al., 2021). The server,
039
      which collects pseudogradients pushed by participating clients, consolidates a global approximation
      of the preconditioners for adaptive model updates. Typically, the server sends the preconditioners
040
      back to the clients to precondition local adaptive updates. However, this can lead to significant com-
041
      munication overhead that detracts from the advantages offered by adaptivity (Wang et al., 2022).
042   Furthermore, dynamically varying client resource limitations restrict the reliability of client-side
043   adaptive optimizers in practice, especially when additional memory is required for handling local
044   preconditioners during each client model update.
045
      In this work, we propose a class of efficient jointly adaptive distributed training algorithms, called
046
      FedAda2 , to mitigate the aforementioned communication and memory restrictions while retaining
047
      the benefits of adaptivity. FedAda2 maintains an identical communication complexity as the vanilla
048   FedAvg algorithm. Instead of transmitting global server-side preconditioners from the server to the
049   selected clients, we propose the simple strategy of allowing each client to initialize local precon-
050   ditioners from constants (such as zero), without any extra communication of preconditioners. In
051   addition, when running local updates, we adopt existing memory-efficient optimizers that factorize
052   the gradient statistics to reduced dimensions to save on-device memory. We prove that for the gen-
053   eral, non-convex setting, FedAda2 achieves the same convergence rate as prior adaptive federated
      optimizers (e.g., Reddi et al. (2021)). In this paper, we demonstrate that jointly adaptive federated


                                                         1

054
      learning, as well as adaptive client-side optimization, are practicable in real-world settings while
055   sidestepping localized memory restrictions and communication bottlenecks.
056
057   Contributions. Our contributions are summarized as follows.
058   • Motivated by the importance of joint server- and client-side adaptivity both empirically and the-
059     oretically, we propose a framework FedAda2 to avoid extra communication cost and reduce on-
060     device memory while retaining the benefits of joint adaptive optimization (Section 4).
061   • We provide convergence analyses for a class of FedAda2 algorithms instantiated with different
062     server- and client-side adaptive methods and memory-efficient local optimizers (Section 5). To the
063     very best of our knowledge, there are no known convergence results on joint adaptive federated
064
        optimization in the general convex or non-convex settings.
065   • Empirically, we show that FedAda2 , without transmitting preconditioners and employing on-
066
        device preconditioner compression, matches the performance of its more expensive counterparts,
        and outperforms baselines without joint adaptivity on both image and text datasets (Section 6).

      We now provide a brief overview of related work in adaptive federated learning and memory-
072

073
074   Adaptive Federated Optimization. Adaptive optimization preconditions the gradients to enhance
075   optimization efficacy, dynamically adjusting the learning rate for each model parameter (e.g., Duchi
076   et al., 2011; Kingma & Ba, 2015; Reddi et al., 2018). Recent developments in federated learning
077   have leveraged adaptive methods for server and client model parameter updates. Frameworks such
078   as FedAdam (Reddi et al., 2021) and FederatedAGM (Tong et al., 2020) focus primarily on server-
079
      side adaptivity while using a constant learning rate for client updates. Additionally, FedCAMS
      (Wang et al., 2022) delves into communication-efficient adaptive optimization by implementing er-
080
      ror feedback compression to manage client updates while maintaining adaptivity solely on the server
081
      side. Conversely, methodologies such as FedLALR (Sun et al., 2023), Local AdaAlter (Xie et al.,
082   2019), and Local AMSGrad (Chen et al., 2020) have adopted client-side adaptivity exclusively.
083   These approaches involve transmitting both client preconditioners and model parameters for global
084   aggregation in the server. Moreover, some frameworks have embraced joint adaptivity. Local Adap-
085   tive FedOPT (Wang et al., 2021b) implements joint adaptivity while incorporating an additional
086   client correction term. These terms, along with transmitted client pseudogradients, are aggregated
087   on the server to construct a global preconditioner used to synthesize the subsequent model update.
088   Alternatively, frameworks such as MIME (Karimireddy et al., 2021; Jin et al., 2022) transmit addi-
089   tional optimizer state information aggregated in the server to mimic adaptive updates in centralized
090
      settings, while maintaining frozen-state optimizers on the client-side. In contrast with all these
      approaches, FedAda2 avoids the transmission of any local/global preconditioners and optimizer
091
      states entirely, maintaining precisely identical communication complexity as vanilla FedAvg despite
092
      leveraging joint adaptivity. We include further discussions in Appendix G.5.
093
094
      Memory-Efficient Adaptive Optimizers. The implementation of local adaptive methods sub-
095
      stantially increases client memory requirements, as it necessitates the maintenance of local precon-
096   ditioners. For some models, it has been noted that the gradients combined with optimizer states con-
097   sume significantly more memory than the actual model parameters themselves (Raffel et al., 2020).
098   Memory-efficient adaptive optimizers have been extensively studied in prior literature. Algorithms
099   such as Adafactor (Shazeer & Stern, 2018) address memory reduction by tracking moving averages
100   of the reduction sums of squared gradients along a singular tensor axis, attaining a low-rank projec-
101   tion of the exponentially smoothed preconditioners. GaLore (Zhao et al., 2024) targets the low-rank
102   assumption of the gradient tensor, which reduces memory of both gradients and preconditioners.
103   Shampoo (Gupta et al., 2018) collapses gradient statistics into separate preconditioning matrices for

      3.1   D EEP R EMORSE OF F EDAVG AND SGD
156
157   So far, we have examined toy problems in which heavy-tailed gradient noise is guaranteed to destabi-
158   lize distributed training in expectation. We now prove that this is an instantiation of a more general
159   phenomenon in federated learning where a family of online µ-strongly convex global objectives
160   collapses to the identical failure mode. To our knowledge, this provable limitation of distributed
161   training resultant from the heavy-tailed noise of a singular client has not previously been established
      within the literature. The proofs of all results are given in the appendix.

      Definition 3. A learning algorithm A is deeply remorseful if it incurs infinite or undefined regret
163   in expectation. If A is guaranteed to instantly incur such regret due to sampling even a single client
164   with a heavy-tailed gradient noise distribution, then we say A is resentful of heavy-tailed noise.
165
      Theorem 4. Let the global objectives ft (x) of a distributed training problem satisfy µ-strong con-
166   vexity for t = 1, . . . , T . Assume that the participation probability of a client with a heavy-tailed
167   stochastic gradient noise distribution is non-zero. Then, FedAvg becomes a deeply remorseful al-
168   gorithm and is resentful of heavy-tailed noise. Furthermore, if the probability of the heavy-tailed
169   client being sampled at step t is nontrivial, then the variance of the global objective at t + 1 satisfies
170   E∥ft+1 (xt+1 )∥2 = ∞.
171
      In federated learning, we typically have ft (x) ≡ f (x) for all t = 1, . . . , T (i.e., the objective func-
172
      tions are the same across all rounds). Proposition 2 intuits that inserting local adaptivity successfully
173
      breaks the generality of remorse and heavy-tailed resent for FedAvg. A high-level overview is that
174   client-side AdaGrad clips the local updates of each iteration, which mollifies the impact of stochas-
175   ticity in perturbing the weight updates. This gives Proposition 5, which is formulated loosely without
176   utilizing any advantages provided by local adaptivity except for clipping. Given that adaptive meth-
177   ods inherently include an implicit soft clipping mechanism due to the effects of preconditioning, we
178   consider them to be preferable to clipped SGD for large-scale applications as they also offer the ben-
179   efits of adaptivity. This preference holds, provided that the memory and computational constraints
180   of the clients can be adequately managed.
181   Proposition 5. Introducing client-side adaptivity via AdaGrad for the setting in Theorem 4 produces
182   a non-remorseful and a non-resentful algorithm.
183
      The benefits of client-side adaptivity have also been shown in previous works (e.g., Zhou et al.
184
      (2024); Wang et al. (2021b)). We note that Proposition 5 can be straightforwardly extended to jointly
185   adaptive methods as well as for ft ∈ C(Rd ) not necessarily convex. An advantage of federated
186   learning is that when done tactfully, the large supply of clients enable the trainer to draw from a
187   virtually unlimited stream of computational power. The downside is that the global model may be
188   strongly influenced by the various gradient distributions induced by the private client data shards.
189   In this paper, we focus specifically on joint adaptive optimization as a countermeasure to stabilize
190   learning. In Section 4, we propose FedAda2 , which utilizes joint adaptivity in an efficient and
191   scalable manner for distributed or federated training.

208

215   based on the local data introduces memory overheads. Next, we discuss two main techniques we

330   5.1   D ISCUSSION OF C ONVERGENCE B OUND
331
332   There have been several recent works exploring adaptivity and communication efficiency in fed-
333   erated learning. The convergence rate in Corollary 8 matches the state of the art for federated
334   non-convex optimization methods (Reddi et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Tong et al., 2020; Sun
335
      et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020). However, to the best of our knowledge, there are no
      known convergence results of jointly adaptive federated optimization that explicitly support several
336
      popular methods including Adam and AdaGrad.
337
338   Generality of FedAda2 : Federated Blended Optimization. The gradient descent setting used
339   in the analysis of Theorem 6 is conceptually equivalent to accessing oracle client workers capable
340   of drawing their entire localized empirical data stream. While this constraint is a limitation of
341
      our theory, it enables us to derive stronger results and induce additional adaptive frameworks for
      which our analysis generalizes. For instance, our bound deterministically guarantees asymptotic
342
      stabilization of the minimum gradient, regardless of initialization or client subsampling procedure.
343
      In Appendix D, we present the FedAda2 framework under its most general, technical form, which
344   we also call Federated Blended Optimization (Algorithm 5).
345
346   Blended optimization distributes local optimizer strategies during the subsampling process, which
347
      are formalized as functions that take as input the availability of client resources and outputs hyper-
      parameters such as delay step size z or choice of optimizer (Adam, AdaGrad, SGD, etc). These may
348
      be chosen to streamline model training based on a variety of factors, such as straggler mitigation or
349
      low availability of local resources. In particular, this framework permits the deployment of differ-
350   ent adaptive optimizers per device for each round, enhancing the utility of communication-efficient
351   frameworks that do not retain preconditioners between clients or between the server and client. This
352   flexibility is especially beneficial in scenarios where there are inconsistencies between server and
353   client adaptive optimizer choices.

505   Summary. For DP StackOverflow and CIFAR-100 experiments, a natural yet expensive imple-
506   mentation of joint client- and server-side adaptivity with transmitted global preconditioners sur-
507   passes the performance of FedAvg and server-only adaptivity. However, full preconditioner trans-
508   mission incurs significant communication costs, as noted in Section 1. Additionally, the adaptive op-
509
      timizer substantially increases the memory demand on the client due to the maintenance of auxiliary
      second-order statistics used to synthesize model updates in every local iteration, which motivates the
510
      development of efficient adaptive frameworks. In our empirical evaluations, we consistently found
511
      that initializing local preconditioners from zero did not underperform direct joint adaptivity (full
512   server-side preconditioner transmission) after optimal hyperparameter tuning. The performance of
513   joint adaptivity under differential privacy is notable, where this compromise to reduce communica-
514   tion cost even achieved better test performance than the more expensive baseline with full precondi-
515   tioner transmission. In addition, when evaluating convergence in terms of the actual communicated
516   bits (communication rounds times number of bits per round), FedAda2 significantly outperforms di-
517   rect joint adaptivity (Figure 2), saving significant communication bandwidth. In general, we observe
518   that FedAda2 retains the competitive advantage of joint adaptivity while being communication- and
519   memory-efficient. Empirically, avoiding preconditioner transmission and leveraging client-side pre-
520
      conditioner approximations (i.e., FedAda2 ) does not substantively harm the performance of its
      more expensive variants, and can even surpass the performance of direct joint adaptivity in certain
521
      settings (e.g., StackOverflow and GLD-23K under constrained client resources).

524   7   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
525
526   In this work, we introduce FedAda2 , a class of jointly adaptive algorithms designed to enhance
527   scalability and performance in large-scale, cross-device federated environments. FedAda2 is con-
528
      ceptually simple and straightforward to implement. In particular, we show that joint adaptivity is
      practicable while sidestepping communication bottlenecks and localized memory restrictions. By
529
      optimizing communication efficiency and employing localized memory-efficient adaptive optimiz-
530
      ers, FedAda2 significantly reduces the overhead associated with transferring preconditioners and
531   extra on-device memory cost without degrading model performance. Our empirical results demon-
532   strate the practical benefits of FedAda2 in real-world federated learning scenarios. Future research
533   could explore extensions of FedAda2 (Section 5.1, Appendix D) to study the training dynamics
534   under alternative, potentially client-specific local optimizer instantiations.

       C.1   P RECOMPACT C ONVERGENCE A NALYSIS
1459
1460
1461
       We aim to analyze the convergence of learning algorithms under the general, non-convex setting.
1462
       However, extremely popular and well known adaptive optimizers such as Adam whose efficacy
1463
       is strongly supported by empirical evidence have been shown to fail to converge even for convex
1464   settings (Reddi et al., 2018). Therefore, recent works have investigated the asymptotic stabilization
1465   of gradients, instead of requiring strict convergence to local or global optima of the objective (Reddi
1466   et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Tong et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020;
1467   Zhang et al., 2020). Such convergence bounds are of the form mint ∥∇f (xt )∥ ≤ O(T −α ), and are
1468   interpreted via the following lemma:
1469   Lemma 18. For xt the t-step parameters of any objective f (x) learned by an algorithm, let
1470   min1≤t≤T ∥∇f (xt )∥ ≤ O(T −α ) for α > 0. Then, there exists a learning algorithm which out-
1471   puts parameters {e      e2 , . . .} such that ∥∇f (x̃t )∥ → 0 as t → ∞.
                          x1 , x
1472
1473   Proof. Assuming otherwise gives that ∥∇f (xt )∥ is ε-bounded away from 0 for some ε > 0, for
1474   any parameter xt realized by the algorithm. Clearly, min1≤t≤T ∥∇F (xt )∥ → 0 as T → ∞ gives
1475   a contradiction. More constructively, note that ∀ε > 0, ∃ Te(ε) ∈ N such that T ≥ Te(ε) =⇒
1476   min1≤t≤T ∥∇f (xt )∥ < ε. Letting ε = 1/n for n ∈ N and Tn := Te(1/n), we have that there
1477   exists tn ∈ [Tn ] such that ∥∇f (xtn )∥ < 1/n. Letting xei := xti extracts the desired parameter
1478   sequence.
1479   This notion of convergence can be formalized as precompact convergence which is consistent with
1480   sequence properties of precompact normed sets. In this paper, we explicitly formalize the conven-
1481   tions used in prior works, and take the term convergence to mean precompact convergence unless
1482   stated otherwise.
1483   Definition 19 (Precompact convergence). A sequence {yn }n∈N in a normed space Y is said to
1484   converge precompactly to y ∈ Y if there exists φ : N → N such that yφ(n) → y.
1485
1486   Our goal is to develop principled federated algorithms whose global gradients are guaranteed to
1487
       converge precompactly to 0 regardless of parameter initialization, in the general, non-convex setting.

       In federated blended optimization, we distribute local optimizer strategies during the subsampling
1985
       process which may be formalized as functions that take as input the availability of client resources,
1986   and outputs the number of local epochs, K(Oli ), as well as additional hyperparameters such as delay
1987   step size z or preconditioner initialization. These may be chosen to streamline model training based
1988   on a variety of factors, such as straggler mitigation or dynamically restricted availability of local
1989   resources.
1990
       In the general formulation of FedAda2 , blended optimization allows the trainer to utilize the unique
1991
       strengths of each individual optimizer, balancing resource constraints and client noise. Each client
1992   has the option to run different optimizer strategies as the training rounds progress, depending on
1993   varying individual resource constraints or distribution shift in the local data stream. This faithfully
1994   corresponds to real-world settings where the availability of local resources are actively dynamic.
1995   Future work will provide empirical results on the performance of blended optimization, includ-
1996   ing identifying the settings in which mixing optimizer strategies are advantageous for distributed
1997   learning. The following theorem shows that under certain non-restrictive conditions, blended opti-
       mization still allows for convergence of the global gradient objective.

2691
         In the original FedAvg algorithm introduced by McMahan et al. (2017), the server-side aggrega-
2692
         tion is performed without any additional momentum, relying solely on simple averaging. On the
2693
         other hand, algorithms like FedAdaGrad and FedAdam represent examples of server-only adaptive
2694     approaches (Reddi et al., 2021), where the server employs adaptive optimizers such as AdaGrad or
2695     Adam instead of vanilla averaging. We note that server-only adaptive frameworks such as FedAdam
2696     and FedAdaGrad are optimizer-specific instantiations of FedOpt (Reddi et al., 2021), a competitive
2697     framework that has been utilized in recent works to develop leading applications (e.g., by Google
2698     Deepmind to develop DiLoCo (Douillard et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Jaghouar et al., 2024)). The
2699     concept of ‘Direct Joint Adaptivity’ (Direct Joint Adap.) refers to a training paradigm where the
         server’s adaptive preconditioners are shared with clients during each communication round. An


                                                                                                                                                              50
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2700
       example of this is the AdaGrad-AdaGrad setup used as a differential privacy baseline in the Stack-
2701   Overflow task, where the server-side AdaGrad preconditioners are applied to client-side AdaGrad
2702   optimizers, guiding client model updates.
2703
2704
       Alternatively, by eliminating the transmission of server-side preconditioners and initializing client-
       side preconditioners to zero, we derive the ’Joint Adaptivity without Preconditioner Communica-
2705
       tion’ (Joint Adap. w/o Precond. Commu.) baseline, which is more communication-efficient. Fur-
2706
       ther, compressing local preconditioners to align with client memory constraints leads to the develop-
2707   ment of FedAda2 . Thus, FedAda2 and the various baselines can be viewed as logically motivated
2708   extensions, incorporating adaptive updates and memory-efficient strategies. We provide compre-
2709   hensive evaluations of all 15 algorithms (including 12 jointly adaptive methods tailored to each
2710   adaptive optimizer, 2 server-only adaptive methods, and 1 non-adaptive method) in Section 6 and in
2711   the Appendix G, I.
