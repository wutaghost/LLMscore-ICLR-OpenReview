009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We present DirectTriGS, a novel framework designed for 3D object generation
012            with Gaussian Splatting (GS). GS-based rendering for 3D content has gained con-
013            siderable attention recently. However, there has been limited exploration in directly
014            generating 3D Gaussians compared to traditional generative modeling approaches.
015            The main challenge lies in the complex data structure of GS represented by discrete
016            point clouds with multiple channels. To overcome this challenge, we propose
017            employing the triplane representation, which allows us to represent Gaussian Splat-
018
               ting as an image-like continuous field. This representation effectively encodes
               both the geometry and texture information, enabling smooth transformation back
019
               to Gaussian point clouds and rendering into images by a TriRenderer, with only
020
               2D supervisions. The proposed TriRenderer is fully differentiable, so that the
021            rendering loss can supervise both texture and geometry encoding. Furthermore, the
022            triplane representation can be compressed using a Variational Autoencoder (VAE),
023            which can subsequently be utilized in latent diffusion to generate 3D objects. The
024            experiments demonstrate that the proposed generation framework can produce
025            high-quality 3D object geometry and rendering results.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Neural rendering has grown to a focal point in rendering techniques in recent years, as it achieves
031   more realistic rendering effects by leveraging the great expressiveness of neural network. The
032   representatives are neural radience field (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2021) and the newly emerged
033
      Gaussian Splatting (GS) (Kerbl et al., 2023). However, when applied to the field of 3D generation,
      the slow rendering and training speed become a strong limitation for NeRF. While GS is more flexible
034
      with greater rendering efficiency and editability, few works have addressed the challenge of direct
035
      GS generation in 3D due to its complex data structure. In this paper, we propose a novel approach
036   called DirectTriGS, which encodes GS as triplane representation, and introduce its corresponding
037   renderer, TriRenderer. Subsequently, we apply latent diffusion on the Triplane representation to
038   generate high-quality GS objects.
039
      Gaussian splatting uses multi-channel point cloud of “splats” to describe the 3D contents. With a
040
      differentiable splats rasterizer, GS has fast rendering speed. However, the sparsity, multiple channels,
041
      and uneven distributed density of 3D GS bring great difficulty for 3D generation. We propose to
042   encode GS contents as multi-channel triplane representations, which have been shown to possess
043   favorable properties for representing 3D geometry or NeRF, as demonstrated in previous works Wu
044   et al. (2024); Shue et al. (2023). In our work, we leverage the triplane representation to encode both
045   the geometry information and other GS attributes in two separate groups of channels. By training on
046   a dataset of 3D objects, we obtain a shared TriRenderer that is capable of decoding different triplane
047   representations to GS and then render it to image. TriRenderer is fully differentiable, enabling the
048   use of only 2D rendering loss to supervise both the texture and geometry of 3D GS. The advantage
049   of using Triplane to represent GS are two folds. First, it leads to high memory efficiency compared
050
      with dense voxels and is expressive enough for generating various 3D GS. Second, Triplane is more
      compatible with the convolution-based encoders compared with the original sparse GS point cloud,
051
      which require specifically designed networks on processing sparse point clouds.
052
053   We follow stable diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) to train DirectTriGS on the proposed triplane
      representation. Specifically, a VAE is designed to further convert the Triplanes into latent code. We


                                                         1

054
      use two separate decoders to decouple the decoding of geometry and GS attributes. Second, we roll
055   out the triplane latent to an expanded multi-channel image, then exploit staged latent diffusion to do
056   generation. Two-stage diffusion is employed to generate the geometry and the corresponding GS
057   appearance. Finally, score distillation sampling (SDS) (Poole et al., 2022) is adopted as a optional
058   post-processing to refine or restyle the generated 3D objects.
059
      Our contribution can summarized as follows. 1) We propose a Triplane representation for direct 3D
060
      GS generation, which is memory efficient to derive GS point clouds with vivid rendering. 2) We
061   design a fully differentiable TriRenderer to enable the end-to-end training of triplane representations,
062   with only 2D supervisions. 3) We develop a Triplane-based GS generation framework DirectTriGS
063   that incorporates a specially designed VAE, latent diffusion module, and a SDS based refiner. 4)
064   Experiments demonstrate our method produces competitive performance in both 3D geometry and
065   multi-view rendering quality.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
068
069
      Gaussian Splatting. Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023) exploits pointcloud of splats to describe
070
      the 3D content, and every splat is a 3D ball with a shape of Gaussian distribution, and it has other
071   properties like opacity, color or SH parameters. The GS can be rendered like mesh using a special
072   designed rasteraization, which allows real-time rendering of photorealistic scenes. As a newly
073   developed rendering technique, there emerges so many research focusing on its reconstruction quality
074   enhancement and algorithm optimization (Yu et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2023; Lu
075   et al., 2023). Another related active area includes 4D dynamic GS modeling (Wu et al., 2023a;
076   Yang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023c) , scene editing (Fang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b) and its
077   applications like SLAM (Matsuki et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2023).
078   3D Object Generation. Recently, there emerges many works aiming to solve the geometry generation
079   and rendering in combination, which can be roughly divide into 2 groups. First is the route of multi-
080   view image to 3D, which generate multi-view colored 3D image and then reconstruct the 3D shape
081   and project image to texture, such as Poole et al. (2022); Shi et al. (2023); Höllein et al. (2023);
082   Liu et al. (2023); Chung et al. (2023); Tang et al. (2023). Since these approaches are actually 2D
083   generator, it is difficult to maintain 3D consistency, which may lead to Janus problem. And the second
084   group mainly operate in 3D, which can be clustered by different 3D representation. For example,
085
      Gupta et al. (2023); Gao et al. (2022) are able to generate high-quality 3D textured meshes using
      differentiable rendering; Nichol et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2023c) generate colored pointcloud; Chen
086
      et al. (2023a); Metzer et al. (2023) generate NeRF volumes; Ju et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2024)generate
087
      SDF volumes.
088
089   As for GS generation, some works exploit multi-view image based route such as Chung et al. (2023);
090   Tang et al. (2023), where the image generator output image from required views to enhance the GS
091
      rendering in a recursive manner. Rare works explore the GS generation directly in 3D space, such as
      Zou et al. (2023) generates a point cloud using a Transformer structure with 2D image as input, and
092
      build a mapping of image to tri-plane encoding of GS attribute; GaussianCube (Zhang et al., 2024)
093
      proposes to use optimal transport to model the 3D GS for text-to-3D generation.

136   4         M ETHODOLOGY
137
138   Our motivation is to directly generate 3D GS with fast speed by utilizing the proposed triplane-based
139   GS representation. While 2D-lifting methods like SDS-based approaches yield intricate results,
140   they are time-intensive and susceptible to Janus problem. Direct generation, notably with fast
141   sampling, can complete 3D generation in under a minute, contrasting with SDS methods that take
142   over 10 minutes or longer, such as Chen et al. (2024)Wang et al. (2024), posing challenges for users.
143   Therefore, direct 3D generation is more practical, with 2D-lifting reserved for enhancing texture
144
      details when needed.
145   The procedures of our generation framework can be divide into 4 stages as shown in Fig.1 (a). Firstly,
146   we encode the 3D objects into triplanes using the TriRenderer. The triplane encoding and TriRenderer
147   training can be concurrently executed with only the multi-view RGBA image and camera poses as
148   supervision. Secondly, a VAE is trained to compress the triplanes to latent space, enabling effective
149   capture of high-level information for subsequent modules. Thirdly, a diffusion model is trained on
150
      the triplane latent code. During inference, the diffusion model produces the latent code, which can
      be decoded back to its corresponding triplane by the VAE decoder. Subsequently, the TriRenderer
151
      decodes the triplane into the standard GS point cloud. Finally, an SDS-based refiner can be utilized
152
      as an optional post-processing step to enhance or restyle the generated 3D object. Leveraging the
153   pretrained 2D diffusion model within the SDS, the generated GS can be further improved to achieve
154   a more detailed appearance.

      4.1         T RIPLANE - BASED GS F IELD
157
158   We employ triplanes to convert the discrete multi-channel GS point cloud into a continuous field,
159   tailored for more efficient encoding in the subsequent generative model. Triplanes are 3 planes formed
160   by every 2 axes of x, y, z axes in 3D space, where every 3D point p can query these planes to get
161   corresponding features Fp = (Fxy , Fxz , Fyz ) using orthogonal projection. In real implementation,
      triplane is a tensor with the size of 3 × H × H × C, where H denote the resolution along every axis


                                                                                                  3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      and C is the feature channel. The query of point with arbitrary continuous coordinate should be a
163   bi-linear intepolation in the triplane grids. Considering GS is a set of points with multiple channels
164   of attributes, it is natural to use triplane for GS encoding. If the triplane feature and GS attributes of a
165   point can be transformed to each other, the sparse GS pointcloud of every object can be represented as
166   continuous triplane GS field for further encoding. We use separate channels to encode 3D geometry
167   and other GS attributes as described in Eq. 1, which is an experimental setting for better convergence
168   in the training.
169
170   4.2   T RI R ENDERER
171
172   The TriRenderer serves as the crucial component for converting a 3D object into a triplane-based
173   GS field. It acts as a fully differentiable bridge connecting the GS field with rendering, enabling the
174
      optimization from 2D images to geometry and appearance encoding.
175   As depicted in Fig. 1 (b), the TriRenderer comprises a geometry branch and a GS attribute branch,
176   each equipped with independent decoders to decode the triplane. The geometry branch is responsible
177   for retrieve the geometry as triangular mesh from triplane, with a surface sampler for GS pointcloud
178   sampling. Then GS attribute branch uses the obtained pointcloud to query the triplane to obtain the
179   GS attribute corresponding to each point. In this way, the GS pointcloud in the original format is
180
      retrieved, and can be rendered using the original GS renderer. It is worth mentioning that all triplanes
      of different object share a common TriRenderer, which ensures the features on different triplanes
181
      subject to a similar distribution.
182
183   Geometry Branch. We use signed distance function (SDF) to represent the geometry, and allow
184   every vertex deform as Shen et al. (2021), so that the geometry branch decodes Fp to a SDF volume
185   and its vertice deformation. By query all grid coordinates of the designated resolution L × L × L, the
186
      deformable SDF volume is reconstructed. Then we exploit a differentiable Marching Cube algorithm
      FlexiCubes (Shen et al., 2023) to extract triangular mesh from the SDF volume.
187
188   Surface Sampling. Considering that GS pointcloud is generally gathered on the surface of objects,
189   we randomly sample GS points on the faces of triangular mesh with barycentric coordinates. We
190   expect that every splat is flat and with a normal consistent with its source triangular face. Assume
191   a face is formed by vertices < va , vb , vc >, and vector v1 = (va − vb ), v2 = (vb − vc ), the face
192
      normal can be calculated as n = v1 × v2 . Then the rotation matrix of every splat drawn from this
      face can be formulated as
193
                                               R = [v1 , n × v1 , n] ,                                  (2)
194
195   which can be transform to q in Eq.1 by standard matrix-to-quaternion algorithm. To make the splats
196   flat, s3 in Eq.1 is fixed to an infinite small value.
197   To further reduce the computational load in the subsequent GS rendering process, only faces oriented
198   toward the camera are sampled. These face indices can be obtained through fast rasterization of
199   the mesh faces. Note this rasterization is not required to be differentiable, and we use the library
200   Nvdiffrast (Laine et al., 2020) for implementation.
201
      GS Attribute Branch. By querying the triplane GS channels with the sampled pointcloud, we can
202   obtain the GS feature and use the GS attribute branch to transform them to the rest GS attributes.
203   Then the GS renderer can render GS pointcloud to multi-view images. Since different GS attributes
204   have different numerical scales and distributions, we customize individual headers to decode them
205   respectively, which is similar to Zou et al. (2023).
206
      Training. The training consists of 2 stages. In the first stage, a small batch of data is used to train
207
      the triplanes and the TriRenderer together. The second stage involves the whole dataset to train all
208   triplanes with the parameters of TriRenderer frozen. In this way, we can handle large dataset by
209   distributed training in the second stage, and the same TriRenderer can be shared by all triplanes.
210   There is no need to pre-train the GS pointcloud using original GS training as Kerbl et al. (2023).
211

254   4.3      T RIPLANE VAE
255
256   We employ an UNet-like structure of VAE to compress the triplane to latent space. The training
257   pipeline is demonstrated in Fig. 2 (a). Considering that the 3 planes of triplanes encodes features
258   from 3 orthogonal view direction, these 3 planes should be homogeneous data, so that We reshape the
259   triplanes of batchsize B to a new batch as B × 3 × H × H × C to 3B × H × H × C for the VAE
260
      training. We use decoupled encoders and decoders for geometry and GS channels of triplanes, for
      experiments prove that a mixed encoding may lead to blur of the rendering results. After the triplane
261
      is reconstructed, the TriRenderer trained in Section.4.1 can be used to retrieve the GS pointclouds
262
      and render it to images.
263
264   In the training process, same loss functions as Eq. 3 and Eq. 5 are used, along with an L1 loss between
265   the input and reconstructed triplanes, denoted as LTri . Additionally, a Kullback–Leibler divergence
266

      4.4   T WO - STAGE L ATENT D IFFUSION FOR T EXT- TO -3D G ENERATION
278
279   Taking into account the significant relevance of texture appearance to the underlying geometry, we
280   proposed to utilize staged diffusion to generate geometry and GS attribute successively as shown in
281   Fig. 2 (b). It is easy to implement it because the latent codes for geometry and other GS attributes
282   are totally decoupled by VAE, as mentioned earlier . In this way, the geometry code can be a new
283   condition for the second stage.
284   We follow DDPM Ho et al. (2020); Karras et al. (2022) to implement the latent diffusion conditioned
285   on the text description. To better capture the relation between different planes, we roll out the triplane
286   latent of B × 3 × h × h × c to a image-like B × 3h × h × c as the input of diffusion model, and the
287   generated result will be transformed back to the original shape for TriRenderer decoding.
288
289   4.5   SDS- BASED T EXTURE B OOSTER
290
291   To refine or restyle the generated GS pointcloud, we utilize a pretrained 2D diffusion model for the
292   SDS training process (Poole et al., 2022) as shown in Fig. 3. As the generated 3D GS is constrained
293   by the mesh reconstructed from SDF, detailed in Section 4.2, we maintain the GS splats’ adherence
294   as previously established. However, we enable the mesh vertices to shift within a limited radius to
295   enhance the stability of the SDS process. Additionally, throughout the entire SDS procedure, there
296   is no need for densification or pruning operations. This ensures that the number of points can be
297
      controlled to align with the number of mesh faces.
298   With the RGB image rendered from the initial GS pointcloud, the 2D diffusion model can produce
299   images with better 2D/3D consistence in different views, so that the optimization process can converge
300   rapidly without Janus problem.

      6     C ONCLUSION
473
474   We have presented a novel framework DirectTriGS for Gaussian Splatting Field generation. Direct-
475   TriGS mainly consists of 3 parts: 1) a light-weight triplane representation for 3D object with the
476   format of Gaussian Splatting; 2) a fully differentiable TriRenderer which can decode triplane to
477
      orginal GS and render it to multi-view images; 3) the triplane VAE and staged diffusion model for
      the whole generation process. By utilizing our DirectTriGS, the intricate GS data can be generated
478
      directly and efficiently. 4) Additionally, we incorporate a SDS refiner to further improve the texture
479
      and details of generated objects.

      A.1   DATASET I NFORMATION .
705
706   Objaverse Deitke et al. (2023) is the main dataset for our experiment, which contains over 800K 3D
707   objects. As the rendering process on such a massive dataset is very time-consuming, we adopt the
708   pre-processed version sourced from the repository of Qiu et al. (2023), which pre-filters over 260K
709   samples. In this processed dataset, every object is normalized to the voxel range of [±0.5, ±0.5, ±0.5],
710   and rendered to RGBA images in a resolution of 512 ∗ 512 ∗ 4, with 40 views in total. Our training
711
      data only comprises multi-view images and their corresponding camera poses, without any kind of
      original 3D data.
712
713
714   A.2   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS .
715
      Triplane. The triplane resolution is configured as 3×128×128×16, where 16 represents the channels
716   within each grid. The first half of the channels is designated for encoding geometry information,
717   while the remaining half is allocated for encoding GS appearance details. Each triplane is initialized
718   to random Gaussian noise with a standard deviation of 0.01. This random initialization allows the
719   triplane to be decoded into random SDF values, subsequently leading to the generation of diverse
720   fragmented mesh faces. Upon rasterization of these faces onto the screen, the geometry loss facilitates
721   swift removal of undesired faces. During our experiments, we observed that this initialization method
722   enables faster convergence compared to zero initialization.
723   As for loss configuration, we configure w1 = 5.0, w2 = 1.0, w3 = 1.2, β = 0.2, γ1 = 0.2, γ2 = 0.1,
724   γ3 = 0.01, γ4 = 1.0 by experiments, corresponding to the loss function described in Eq. 3, Eq. 4 and
725   Eq. 5.
726
      TriRenderer. As for the TriRenderer instroduced in Fig.1, both the geometry decoder and the GS
727
      attribute decoder inside it are composed of linear blocks. In the GS attribute decoder, there are 3
728   headers for GS splats scaling, opacity and SH prediction, and the rotation is fixed by the mesh face
729   normal as introduced in Section 4.2. All the GS attribute headers are linear layers. We set SH degree
730   to 1 in all experiments, which is enough to obtain satisfying results on Objaverse.
731
732   A.3   C OMPARISON WITH G AUSSIAN C UBE .
733
734   GaussianCube (Zhang et al., 2024) is the most recent paper aiming to solve a similar task of ours,
735   which can generate 3D GS directly without SDS or reconstruction from images. As for now, the
736   authors of GaussianCube have not release their pre-trained models for text-to-3D task on Objaverse
737   dataset. Therefore, we just use the images provided in their paper for a qualitative comparison. The
738
      generated samples are shown in Fig. 10. Our method produces more diverse and detailing generation
      results.
