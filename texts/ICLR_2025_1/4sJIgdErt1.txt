011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Non-stationary data is prevalent in various real-world domains such as climate
014            science, economics, and neuroscience, presenting significant challenges for tasks
015            like forecasting and causal discovery from observational data. Existing approaches
016
               often operate under the assumption that the data is stationary. In this work, we
               introduce a unified framework that combines long-term forecasting and causal
017
               discovery with non-linear relations in a non-stationary setting. Specifically, we
018
               assume that the nonlinear causal relations in the observed space can be transformed
019            into linear relations in the latent space via projections. In addition, we model
020            the non-stationarity in the system as arising from time-varying causal relations.
021            The proposed model demonstrates that adopting a causal perspective for long-term
022            forecasting not only addresses the limitations of each individual task but also makes
023            the causal process identifiable, enhances interpretability, and provides more reliable
024            predictions. Moreover, our approach reformulates causal discovery into a scalable,
025            non-parametric deep learning problem. Through experiments on both synthetic and
026            real-world datasets, we show that our framework outperforms baseline methods in
027
               both forecasting and causal discovery, underscoring the benefits of this integrated
               approach.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Causal discovery over observational time series data is a crucial task, with far reaching applications
      across various real-world domains such as climate science, economics, and neuroscience. Causal
034
      discovery methods aim to uncover contemporaneous and/or time-lagged dependencies, from the
035
      observed data. These dependencies not only provide useful insights into the underlying processes gov-
036   erning the system but also can be extremely beneficial in tasks such as making informed predictions,
037   identifying key drivers of change, and designing effective interventions.
038
039
      Most widely-used causal discovery methods are constraint based approaches. They aim to recover
      a directed acyclic graph (DAG) representing the causal structure among the observed variables by
040
      using conditional independence tests. Under certain assumptions and and given enough data points,
041
      constraint based methods, provide a theoretical guarantee to be able to recover the true causal graph.
042   Notable examples include approaches like PCMCI (Runge (2022) )and tsFCI (Entner & Hoyer (2010))
043   which extend the classic PC and the FCI algorithms respectively(Spirtes et al. (1993), Spirtes (2001))
044   for time series data. Additionally, many approaches also utilize traditional statistical methods such as
045   Granger causality and vector auto-regression (VAR). A variable xi Granger causes xj if past values
046   of xi provide unique and statistically significant information about future values of xj . However, all
047   these methods share a critical limitation: they assume stationarity, meaning the causal relationships
048   between variables remain constant over time. This assumption often fails in real-world scenarios
049   where relationships can evolve due to changing system dynamics. Furthermore, these approaches
050
      tend to struggle with non-linearities and could face scalability issues when applied to large datasets.
051   Long-term forecasting for non-stationary time series is equally challenging. In recent years, this
052   problem has been widely studied, and numerous deep learning-based approaches have been proposed.
053   Transformer based models ( Wu et al. (2022), Zhou et al. (2022), Zhou et al. (2021) ) have gained
      popularity due to their exceptional performance in forecasting based tasks. However, recent works


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      (Zeng et al. (2022) Das et al. (2024) )have questioned the necessity of such complex architectures,
055   advocating for simpler MLP-based models that can achieve competitive performanc. Despite their
056   simplicity, linear models are inherently limited in their ability to capture the non-linearities com-
057   monly found in real-world data. Transformer-based models, on the other hand can be sensitive to
058   distributional shifts over time. They may struggle when faced with abrupt changes, evolving trends,
059   or time-varying dependencies typical of non-stationary systems. It is also important to note that
060   the majority of datasets benchmark the performance of these models are often stationary, with clear
061   seasonal and trend dynamics, simplifying the forecasting task Wang et al. (2023).
062   Given a non-stationary time series, with non-linear causal relations, we present a unified framework
063   that combines the tasks of causal discovery and long-term forecasting over the observed data.
064   Specifically, we aim to find a projection of the observables to a latent space such that the non-linear
065   causal relations in the observed space are transformed into linear relations in the latent space. Further,
066   we assume the non-stationarity in the data arises from time varying causal weights. As a result, the
067   causal model in the latent space can be represented as a time-varying coefficients regression model.
068   This formulation guarantees identifiability of the parameters of the causal model in the latent space,
069
      and by extension, in the observation space. In our current approach we only assume time-lagged
      causal relations.
070
071   We demonstrate that incorporating a causal perspective into long-term forecasting effectively ad-
072   dresses the limitations of both tasks. Discovery of the changing causal relations enables us to capture
073   and leverage the uncertainty in the system, to enhance interpretability and provide more reliable
074   predictions over a longer time periods. Additionally, our approach reformulates causal discovery
075
      into a scalable, non-parametric, deep-learning based task. Through experiments on both synthetic
      and real-world datasets, we show that our proposed method outperforms baseline approaches in both
076
      causal discovery and long-term forecasting.
077
078   To summarize, the proposed approach has the following features:
079
080          • It accommodates non-linear causal relations among the observed variables by learning a
081
               projection to a latent space where these relations become linear.
082          • It models non-stationary time series by allowing causal mechanisms to change over time.
083
             • It imposes minimal constraints on the underlying causal structure while ensuring identifia-
084            bility in both the latent and observation spaces.
085
086          • We provide experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating
087
               the efficacy of our model.

089   2    R ELATED W ORK
090
091   Causal discovery for time series Causal discovery in time series, particularly in non-stationary
092   environments, has been explored through various modeling approaches. One notable work Huang
093   et al. (2019) uses state-space models to represent non-stationary processes, treating the problem as
094   a nonlinear state-space model that captures changes in both causal strengths and noise variances.
095   The underlying hypothesis is that non-stationarity, often seen as a challenge, can actually aid in
096   identifying causal structures, and that forecasting accuracy naturally improves when informed by
097   these learned causal relationships. While effective, this approach has limitations: it relies on linear
098   assumptions, and focuses mainly on the next-step prediction. Building upon this work, our goal
099
      is to relax these conditions by accommodating non-linear relationships between observed causal
      variables and dynamically changing causal weights, while extending the framework to support
100
      long-term prediction on both synthetic and real-world datasets. PCMCI−Ω (Gao et al. (2024b))
101
      builds upon the PCMCI (Runge (2022)) framework to present a constraint-based, non-parametric
102   algorithm designed for semi-stationary environments. In these settings, the data is characterized
103   by a finite number of causal mechanisms that occur periodically. Another prominent method is
104   CDNOD (Gao et al. (2024a)), a non-parametric, constraint-based causal discovery technique tailored
105   for heterogeneous and nonstationary data. CDNOD focuses on detecting variables with changing
106   causal mechanisms and models these changes using a surrogate variable. It orients the causal edges
107   from the surrogate variable to the mechanisms that exhibit variability, effectively leveraging data
      heterogeneity and distributional shifts to identify both causal structures and their directionalities.


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      DYNOTEARS (Pamfil et al. (2020)) represents another significant contribution, adapting the well-
109   known NOTEARS framework to time series data. DYNOTEARS formulates causal discovery as
110   a continuous optimization problem, where the graph search is expressed through a differentiable
111   objective function that quantifies the "DAG-ness" (Directed Acyclic Graph property) of the causal
112   graph. A comprehensive overview of these methods, among others, is provided in the survey paper
113   Causal Discovery in Temporal Data (Gong et al. (2023)), which summarizes and categorizes various
114   techniques used in causal discovery for temporal settings.
115   Long-term forecasting Transformer-based models have seen significant advancements in time
116   series forecasting, with various extensions enhancing their capability to capture complex temporal
117   patterns. Among these, Autoformer (Wu et al. (2022)) is notable for its series decomposition
118   approach, where the input is divided into trend and seasonal components using a specialized series
119   decomposition block. A key component in Autoformer is the use of an auto-correlation mechanism,
120   which identifies period-based dependencies by measuring the similarity between the input and lagged
121   inputs in the Fourier domain. FEDFormer (Zhou et al. (2022)) takes a different approach with its
122   Frequency Enhanced Transformer architecture, which operates in the frequency domain to extract
123
      relevant features. Spacetimeformer (Grigsby et al. (2023)) introduces a novel modification in temporal
      encoding by flattening the feature vector, to encode both spatial and temporal dimensions.
124
125   Linear-based models have gained an increasing popularity as an alternative to more complex architec-
126   tures, particularly challenging the effectiveness of attention mechanisms in time series forecasting.
127   DLinear (Zeng et al. (2022)) argues that attention mechanisms, being permutation invariant, often
128   fail to capture the intricate dependencies inherent in time series data and primarily serve to reduce
129
      computational complexity rather than enhance predictive power. Instead, DLinear proposes a straight-
      forward linear model augmented with a seasonal decomposition block, demonstrating that such
130
      a simple approach can achieve performance comparable to, or even surpass, more sophisticated
131
      models that rely heavily on attention. Time Series Dense Encoder (TiDE Das et al. (2024)) further
132   emphasizes the potential of simpler architectures, proposing a method based solely on multi-layer
133   perceptrons (MLPs). State space models represent another category of alternatives, with S4 (Gu et al.
134   (2022))standing out for its capability to model long sequences effectively. S4 addresses the traditional
135   bottlenecks associated with state-space models and is able to capture long-range dependencies with
136   remarkable efficiency.

      Time-varying coefficient models provide a powerful framework to address non-stationarity by al-
      lowing the coefficients that govern the relationships between variables, to change over time. Instead
144
      of assuming fixed relationships, time-varying coefficient models adapt to the dynamic nature of the
145
      data, enabling the modeling of evolving dependencies and interactions. We can describe the model in
146   terms of a regression relation between the observables and a state equation describing the evolution
147   of the coefficients over time.
148

151   x and y represent the observables, β are the time varying coefficients, A is the matrix governing the
152   transitions of β and et , ϵt are independent zero mean noise terms. The identification of the system is
153   quite challenging as the identification of βt depends on identification of the transition matrix A, the
154   covariance matrix (Q) of the noise term ϵt and the initial conditions of the process β0 .
155
      Wall (1987), provides the identifiability conditions for such a system. Further, Huang et al. (2019)
156   reformulates the problem as a causal discovery task where β constitutes the time varying causal
157   weights. The work provides a theoretical baseline for causal discovery and forecasting in a non-
158   stationary time series characterized by a time varying linear causal model. The core result can be
159   summarized as follows - Given a state space model describing the linear causal relations between
160   observed variables, the causal weights and their associated noise variance are allowed to change in an
161   auto-regressive manner. This system is identifiable under both contemporaneous and time-lagged
      causal relations.

      Our hypothesis builds upon this proof. Given a non-stationary time series data, with observed casual
163   variables, we assume that the causal relations are nonlinear in the observation space. Non-stationarity
164   in the system arises due to smoothly changing casual relations between the observed variables (as
165   a function of time). We aim to find an alternative mapping or projection of the observations in a
166   latent space such that the causal relations between the projections in the latent space are linear. The
167   causal relations in the latent space (similar to the observation space) also change as a function of time.
168   We assume that the causal weights change smoothly over time and at least one of the causal weight
169   matrices is full rank.
170   Given no other constraints, and following the proof from Huang et al. (2019), we claim that in this
171   projection space the system is identifiable. The projection is a learnt one-to-one mapping, therefore
172   the identifiability claim can be extended to the causal mechanisms in the observed space.

522
523   In this work, we propose a unified framework for causal discovery and long-term forecasting on
524   non-stationary time series data. By leveraging a causal perspective, our approach enhances long-term
525   forecasting accuracy, as demonstrated by comparative results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
526   The framework also transforms causal discovery into a scalable, non-parametric objective, effectively
527
      addressing many limitations commonly associated with this task. Future directions include extending
      the framework to incorporate contemporaneous causal relations and conducting experiments with
528
      different modalities such as video.
