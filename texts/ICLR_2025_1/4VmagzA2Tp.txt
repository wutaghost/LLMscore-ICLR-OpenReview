009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently there has been a surge of interest in extending the success of large
012            language models (LLMs) to graph modality, such as molecules. As LLMs are
013            predominantly trained with 1D text data, most existing approaches adopt a graph
014            neural network to represent a molecule as a series of node tokens and feed these
015            tokens to LLMs for molecule-language alignment. Despite achieving some suc-
016
               cesses, existing approaches have overlooked the hierarchical structures that are
               inherent in molecules. Specifically, in molecular graphs, the high-order structural
017
               information contains rich semantics of molecular functional groups, which en-
018
               code crucial biochemical functionalities of the molecules. We establish a simple
019            benchmark showing that neglecting the hierarchical information in graph tokeniza-
020            tion will lead to subpar molecule-language alignment and severe hallucination in
021            generated outputs. To address this problem, we propose a novel strategy called
022            HIerarchical GrapH Tokenization (HIGHT). HIGHT employs a hierarchical graph
023            tokenizer that extracts and encodes the hierarchy of node, motif, and graph levels
024            of informative tokens to improve the graph perception of LLMs. HIGHT also
025            adopts an augmented molecule-language supervised fine-tuning dataset, enriched
026            with the hierarchical graph information, to further enhance the molecule-language
027
               alignment. Extensive experiments on 14 molecule-centric benchmarks confirm the
               effectiveness of HIGHT in reducing hallucination by 40%, as well as significant
028
               improvements in various molecule-language downstream tasks.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive capabilities in understanding and
034   processing natural languages (Radford et al., 2019; OpenAI, 2022; Touvron et al., 2023a; Bubeck
035   et al., 2023). Recently, there has been a surge of interest in extending the capabilities of LLMs to
036   graph modality (Jin et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023d; Wei et al., 2024; Mao et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2024)
037   such as social networks (Tang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024) and molecular graphs (Liu et al., 2023d;
038   Zhao et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024). Inspired by the success of large vision-language
039
      models (Zhang et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a), existing large graph-language models
      (LGLMs) predominantly adopt a graph neural network (GNN) (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Hamilton
040
      et al., 2017; Xu et al., 2019) to tokenize graph information as a series of node embeddings (or node
041
      tokens), and then leverage an adapter such as a Multi-layer perceptron (MLP) or a Q-former (Li et al.,
042   2023b) to transform the node tokens into those compatible with LLMs (Fan et al., 2024). To facilitate
043   the alignment of graph and language modalities, LGLMs will undergo a graph-language instruction
044   tuning stage with the graph and the corresponding caption data, so as to realize the graph-language
045   alignment (Jin et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023d; Fan et al., 2024).
046
      Despite achieving certain success, the graph tokenization in existing LGLMs neglects the essential
047
      hierarchical structures that are inherent in graph data (Ying et al., 2018). Especially, in molecular
048   graphs, the high-order structural information, such as motifs or functional groups, contains rich
049   semantics of the biochemical functionalities of the molecules (Milo et al., 2002; Bohacek et al., 1996;
050   Sterling & Irwin, 2015). For example, the existence of the hydroxide functional group (“-OH”) in
051   small molecules often indicates a higher water solubility. Therefore, the perception of functional
052   groups in a molecule is essential for LLMs to understand the molecules. Intuitively, feeding LLMs
053   with only node tokens makes the molecule understanding harder as LLMs have to learn to combine
      atoms in a functional group during the instruction tuning phase. However, atoms are usually treated

083   In this paper, we study the problem with a focus on molecular data, and introduce a new graph-
084   language alignment strategy called HIerarchical GrapH Tokenization (HIGHT). As shown in Fig. 1,
085   HIGHT includes a hierarchical graph tokenizer, as well as a hierarchical molecular instruction tuning

088

089   AutoEncoder (VQVAE) to obtain atom-level, motif-level, and graph-level tokens separately with
090   the self-supervised tasks (Zang et al., 2023). To retain more original structural information, we
091   further attach Laplacian positional encodings to the tokens. After that, we adopt a multi-level adapter
092   consisting of three adapters processing atom-level, motif-level, and graph-level tokens, respectively,
093   before feeding them into the LLMs. In addition, to facilitate the use of hierarchical information
094   encoded by the tokens, we augment the original molecular instruction tuning dataset with motif
095   descriptions. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
096
097
      • To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to propose incorporating the hierarchical graph
        information into LGLMs with new architectures and instruction tuning dataset HiPubChem.
098
099   • To facilitate the graph-language alignment study on molecular graphs, we also propose the first
100     hallucination benchmark MotifHallu based on the existence of common functional groups.
101   • We conduct extensive experiments with 14 real-world molecular and reaction comprehension bench-
102     marks. The results show that HIGHT significantly reduces the hallucination on MotifHallu by
103     up to 40% and consistently improves the downstream molecule-language performances.

      3.2      M OTIF H ALLUCINATION
230   To understand the issue of node-centric tokenization more clearly, we construct a simple benchmark
231   called MotifHallu, which measures the hallucination of common functional groups by LGLMs.
232   Specifically, we consider the 38 common functional groups in RDKit1 and leverage RDKit (Landrum,
233   2016) to detect the existence of the functional groups within a molecule. We leverage 3, 300
234   molecules from ChEBI-20 test split (Edwards et al., 2021), and adopt the query style as for large
235   vision-language models (Li et al., 2023c), which queries the existence of the specific functional group
236   in the molecule:
237
238
                       Is there a <functional group name> in the molecule?
239
      Then, we detect whether the LGLM gives outputs meaning “Yes” or “No” following the practice
240   in (Li et al., 2023c). For each molecule, we construct questions with positive answers for all kinds of
241   functional groups detected in the molecule, and questions with negative answers for randomly sampled
242   6 functional groups from the 38 common functional groups in RDKit. Therefore, MotifHallu
243   consists of 23, 924 query answer pairs. While it is easy to scale up MotifHallu by automatically
244   considering more molecules and a broader scope of functional groups, we find that the current scale
245   is already sufficient to demonstrate the hallucination phenomena in LGLMs.

286   4.2   H IERARCHICAL G RAPH I NSTRUCTION T UNING DATASET
287
      Although HIGHT tokenizer properly extracts the hierarchical information from the input graph
288
      modality, it remains challenging to properly align the language information to the corresponding
289
      graph information, without the appearance of the respective captions in the texts. For example, if
290   the caption does not contain any information about the water solubility of the hydroxide functional
291   group (“-OH”), LGLMs will never know that “-OH” motif corresponds to the water solubility of
292   the molecule, despite that HIGHT tokenizer extracts the “-OH” token. In fact, the commonly used
293   molecular instruction tuning curated from PubChem (Kim et al., 2022) in existing LGLMs (Liu et al.,
294   2023d; Cao et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024), contains surprisingly little information about motifs. Some
295   samples are given in Appendix B.2.
296   To this end, we propose HiPubChem, which augments the molecular instruction tuning dataset
297   with captions of the functional groups. We consider both the positive and negative appearances of
298   motifs when augmenting the instructions. For the positive case, we directly append the caption of all
299   functional groups detected with RDKit:
300
301             This molecule has <#> of <functional group name> groups.
302
      where <#> refers to the detected number of the functional group in the molecule, and <functional
303
      group name> refers to the name of the functional group as listed in Appendix B.2. In addition,
304
      we also include a brief introduction of the corresponding functional groups to provide fine-grained
305   information for molecule-language alignment. For the negative case, we randomly sample kneg that
306   do not appear in the molecule:
307
308                This molecule has no <functional group name> groups.
309
310   Despite the simple augmentation strategy, we find that HiPubChem significantly reduces the halluci-
311   nation issue and improves the molecule-language alignment performance.

313   4.3   H IERARCHICAL G RAPH I NSTRUCTION T UNING
314
      For the training of HIGHT, we use a two-stage instruction tuning as (Cao et al., 2023).
315
316   Stage 1 Alignment Pretraining. We curate a new molecule-text paired dataset from PubChem
317   following the pipeline of (Liu et al., 2023b). We set the cutoff date by Jan. 2024, and filter out
318   unmatched pairs and low-quality data, which results in 295k molecule-text pairs. Furthermore, we
319   construct the HiPubChem-295k dataset based on the curated PubChem-295k dataset. The alignment
320
      pretraining stage mainly warms up the adapter to properly project the graph tokens with the LLM
      embedding space. To avoid feature distortion, both the LLM and the GNN encoder are frozen.
321
322   Stage 2 Task-specific Instruction Tunning. With a properly trained adapter, we further leverage the
323   task-specific instruction tuning datasets from MoleculeNet (Wu et al., 2017), ChEBI-20 (Mendez
      et al., 2019), and Mol-Instructions (Fang et al., 2024). More details of the instruction tuning

325   both the adapter and the LLM. The LLM is tuned using low-rank adaptation (i.e., LoRA) (Hu et al.,

      For chemical reaction prediction tasks, we incorporate three tasks from Mol-Instructions (Fang
473
      et al., 2024), i.e., reagent prediction, forward reaction prediction, and retrosynthesis prediction, which
474   are crucial for AI-aided drug discovery. Reagent prediction aims to predict the suitable reagents for a
475   particular chemical reaction. Forward reaction prediction aims to predict the products of a chemical
476   reaction, given the reactants and the reagents. Retrosynthesis prediction aims to predict the suitable
477   reactants given a target product. The inputs and outputs for chemical reaction related tasks adopt the
478   SELFIES (Krenn et al., 2019) as recommended by (Fang et al., 2024). In terms of the evaluation
479   metrics, we incorporate both linguistic distance metrics such as BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) and
480   Levenshtein (Yujian & Bo, 2007), as well as molecular similarity measures such as similarity of the
481   molecular fingerprints by RDKit (Landrum, 2016).
482   The results are given in Table 5. It can be found that across all tasks in chemical reaction prediction,
483   LGLMs with HIGHT consistently and significantly improve the performances compared to the node-
484   centric tokenization. Meanwhile, LGLMs with HIGHT achieve state-of-the-art results in several tasks
485   and metrics, compared to other generalist models that even incorporate a stronger LLM backbone
      such as Mol-Instruction, and additional information of SELFIES.

529   6     C ONCLUSIONS
530
531   This paper presents HIGHT, a novel hierarchical graph tokenization technique, which enhances the
532   synergy between molecules and language. By incorporating the hierarchical graph information,
533   HIGHT improves the graph-language alignment performance, reducing hallucinations and boosting
534   accuracy in molecular tasks, as validated through comprehensive benchmarking. Nevertheless, the
535
      current concentration on molecular graphs requires further verification for wider applicability to
      other forms of graph data, such as that originated from social networks. Despite the limitation,
536
      HIGHT represents a significant step forward in advancing LLMs’ graph comprehension capability,
537
      and highlighting paths for future research in this direction.

       B.6     D ETAILS OF M O T I F H A L L U DATASET
1135
1136   The MotifHallu is mainly used to measure the hallucination of common functional groups by
1137   LGLMs. For the construction of MotifHallu, we consider the common functional groups in
1138   RDKit6 as shown in Table 14. There are 39 common functional groups, while we neglect the one
1139   with the name of “???”.
1140   Then, we leverage RDKit (Landrum, 2016) to detect the existence of the left 38 valid functional
1141   groups within a molecule. We consider 3, 300 molecules from ChEBI-20 test split (Edwards et al.,
1142   2021), and adopt the query style as for large vision-language models (Li et al., 2023c) that queries
1143   the existence of specific functional group one by one:
1144
1145                 Is there a <functional group name> in the molecule?
1146
1147   Examples of MotifHallu are given in Table 15.
1148   During the evaluation, we detect whether the LGLM gives outputs meaning “Yes” or “No” following
1149   the practice in (Li et al., 2023c). For each molecule, we construct questions with positive answers
1150   for all kinds of functional groups detected in the molecule, and questions with negative answers
1151   for randomly sampled 6 functional groups from the 38 common functional groups in RDKit. The
1152   construction finally yields 23, 924 query answer pairs about the existence of functional groups in the
1153   molecule. While it is easy to scale up MotifHallu by automatically considering more molecules
1154   and a broader scope of functional groups, we find that the current scale is already sufficient to
1155
       demonstrate the hallucination phenomena in LGLMs.

1157   C       D ETAILS OF E XPERIMENTS
1158
1159   Implementation of graph tokenizer. We implement the GNN tokenizer/encoder based on the
1160   same GNN backbone, which is a 5-layer GIN (Xu et al., 2019). The hidden dimension is 300. For
1161   the node-centric tokenization, we employ the VQVAE GNN tokenizer from Mole-BERT (Xia et al.,
1162   2023) and adopt self-supervised learning tasks from the official Mole-BERT implementation.7 For
1163   HIGHT, we train the VQVAE with the self-supervised learning tasks from (Zang et al., 2023) based
1164   on the official implementation.8 Meanwhile, we set the hyperparameters of GNN tokenizer training
1165   the same as those recommended by (Xia et al., 2023; Zang et al., 2023).
1166   After training the tokenizer, we adopt the GNN encoder within the tokenizer instead of the codebook
1167   embeddings as we empirically find that the GNN embeddings perform better than that using the
1168   VQVAE codebook embeddings.
1169
1170   Implementation of LGLMs. For the cross-modal adapters, we implement it as a single-layer MLP
1171   with an input dimension of 300 as our main focus is the tokenization. For HIGHT, we adopt three
1172   distinct adapters to handle the node-level, motif-level and graph-level embeddings. Meanwhile, we
1173   also adopt a Laplacian position encodings with respect to the supernode-augmented graphs. The
1174   dimension of the Laplacian position encoding is set to 8, therefore the input dimensions of the
1175   adapters in HIGHT will be 308.
1176   For the LoRA adapters, we use a LoRA rank of 128 and a scaling value α of 256 (Hu et al., 2022) for
1177   all methods and tasks.
1178
       For the base LLM, we mainly adopt vicuna-v-1.3-7B (Chiang et al., 2023). The overall scale
1179
       of parameters is around 6.9B.
1180
1181
       Implementation of instruction tuning. In stage 1 instruction tuning, we train all methods based on
1182
       PubChem-295k dataset. The training goes 5 epochs, with a batch size of 64 (distributed to 4 GPUs)
1183   by default. If there is an OOM issue, we will decrease the batch size a little bit to 40. The learning
1184   rate is set to 2 × 10−3 for all methods.
