009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) are crucial in modern natural language processing
012            and artificial intelligence. However, they face challenges in managing their signifi-
013            cant memory requirements. Although quantization-aware training (QAT) offers a
014            solution by reducing memory consumption through low-bit representations with
015            minimal accuracy loss, it is impractical due to substantial training resources. To
016            address this, we propose Efficient Quantization-Aware Training (EfficientQAT),
017            a more feasible QAT algorithm. EfficientQAT involves two consecutive phases:
018
               Block-wise training of all parameters (Block-AP) and end-to-end training of quan-
               tization parameters (E2E-QP). To the best of our knowledge, Block-AP is the first
019
               method to enable direct training of all parameters in a block-wise manner, reducing
020
               accuracy loss in low-bit scenarios by enhancing the solution space during optimiza-
021            tion. E2E-QP then trains only the quantization parameters (step sizes) end-to-end,
022            further improving the performance of quantized models by considering interactions
023            among all sub-modules. Extensive experiments demonstrate that EfficientQAT
024            outperforms previous quantization methods across a range of models, including
025            base LLMs, instruction-tuned LLMs, and multimodal LLMs, with scales from 7B
026            to 70B parameters at various quantization bits. For instance, EfficientQAT obtains
027            a 2-bit Llama-2-70B model on a single A100-80GB GPU in 41 hours, with less
028            than 3 points accuracy degradation compared to the full precision (69.48 vs. 72.41).

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023; Bubeck et al., 2023;
      Chiang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023a; Ying et al., 2024) have demonstrated impressive capabilities in
034
      diverse language tasks such as reasoning (Clark et al., 2018; 2019; Zellers et al., 2019), cognitive
035
      processing (Fu et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023a), and agent-based applications (Qin et al., 2023a;b).
036   However, these models are characterized by their extensive parameters, which pose significant
037   challenges for memory footprint and bandwidth (Kim et al., 2023b; Xu et al., 2024a).
038
039
      Quantization-aware training (QAT) is a highly effective quantization technique that minimizes
      quantization errors by incorporating quantization constraints during training. For example, BitNet
040
      b1.58 (Ma et al., 2024) can achieve nearly lossless ternary quantization. The precision of QAT is
041
      due to two main factors: 1) Fully trainable parameters allow for enough optimized space for gradient
042   descent optimization; 2) End-to-end training accounts for interactions among all sub-modules in
043   the models. Despite its performance benefits, QAT demands significant training resources, such as
044   time and GPUs, as well as extensive training data. For instance, BitNet b1.58 requires retraining
045   LLMs from scratch using the entire pre-trained dataset. Therefore, this approach is impractical for
046   extremely large models and has only been verified on 3B models with 100B training tokens.
047
      In optimizing quantization for LLMs, current methods emphasize either fine-grained reconstruction or
048   reducing trainable parameters. While these approaches improve efficiency, they significantly degrade
049   accuracy in low-bit scenarios. Mainstream post-training quantization (PTQ) methods (Lin et al.,
050   2023; Frantar et al., 2022; Shao et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2023b) focus on block-wise reconstruction
051   (Li et al., 2021). They also restrict the optimization space to alleviate overfitting risk by only training
052   rounding parameters (Nagel et al., 2020; Cheng et al., 2023b), clipping thresholds (Shao et al., 2023),
053   or step sizes (Esser et al., 2019; Ding et al., 2023). However, these methods not only limit optimizable
      parameters but also overlook cross-block interactions, leading to notable accuracy degeneration

075

077   block-wise training of all parameters (Block-AP) to enhance the optimizable space and mitigate
078   quantization accuracy loss. Block-AP sequentially trains all parameters, including original full-
079   precision weights and quantization parameters (step sizes and zero points), within each transformer
080   block. Several works have been developed based on block-wise reconstruction. However, previous
081   approaches focus on designing additional trainable parameters, such as clipping thresholds for
082   OmniQuant (Shao et al., 2023), weight rounding for AutoRound (Cheng et al., 2023b) and BRECQ (Li
083   et al., 2021), or LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) parameters for CBQ (Ding et al., 2023). Our Block-AP
084

      performance compared to previous methods (see Table 6). Block-AP successfully demonstrates that
085

086

087   account for inter-block interactions. E2E-QP keeps the quantized weights fixed and trains only the
088   quantization parameters (step sizes) end-to-end.
089
090
      Thanks to the integration of the proposed Block-AP and E2E-QP, EfficientQAT characterizes itself
      as a fast-converging, memory-efficient, and high-performing quantization technique. For instance,
091
      EfficientQAT can obtain a 2-bit Llama-2-70B model on a single A100-80GB GPU in just 41 hours,
092
      with less than 3 points accuracy degradation on 5 zero-shot common-sense tasks compared to
093   its full-precision counterpart (69.48 vs. 72.41). We also evaluate EfficientQAT across scenarios
094   involving model compression and instruction-tuning. In model compression, as illustrated in Figure
095   1a, EfficientQAT significantly outperforms existing uniform quantization methods by approximately
096   5 points on accuracy in the challenging 2-bit quantization setting. It also matches the performance of
097   vector quantization methods (Egiazarian et al., 2024; Tseng et al., 2024) in this scenario. In terms of
098   instruction tuning, as shown in Figure 1b, EfficientQAT consistently outperforms existing Q-PEFT
099   methods, including QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023a), QA-LoRA (Xu et al., 2023b), and PEQA (Kim
100   et al., 2023a). For instance, EfficientQAT surpasses PEQA (Kim et al., 2023a) with 4.5 points MMLU
101

104
105   Post-Training Quantization of LLMs. PTQ is a pivotal technique for accelerating and deploying
106   LLMs. Quantization approaches generally fall into two categories: weight-only quantization (Frantar
107   et al., 2022; Dettmers et al., 2023b; Lee et al., 2023a; Kim et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2023a; Cheng
      et al., 2023a) and weight-activation quantization (Xiao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c; Wei et al., 2022;


                                                                                                                                                                                                                               2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2023; Yuan et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Ashkboos et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Ashkboos et al.,
109   2024). Weight-only quantization focuses on compressing weights into low-bit formats, reducing
110   memory demands and enhancing the efficiency of memory-bounded computations in LLMs (Lin
111   et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024). Conversely, weight-activation quantization compresses both weights
112   and activations, thus further decreasing the overhead associated with matrix multiplications (Lin
113   et al., 2024). Recent advancements in weight-only quantization include the introduction of vector
114   quantization methods by QUIP#Tseng et al. (2024) and AQLMEgiazarian et al. (2024). These
115   methods have shown promising performance but also introduce significant overhead (Gong et al.,
116   2024). Our research continues to explore uniform quantization, which is preferred for its compatibility
117
      with hardware implementations.
118   Quantization-Aware Training of LLMs. QAT can enhance the performance of quantized models
119   beyond what PTQ offers. However, QAT has been less explored in LLMs due to the significant
120   training costs involved. Studies such as LLM-QAT (Liu et al., 2023e) and BitDistiller (Du et al.,
121   2024) investigate the application of knowledge distillation within QAT contexts. Techniques like
122   BitNet b1.58 (Ma et al., 2024) and OneBit (Xu et al., 2024b) employ QAT to achieve extreme binary
123
      or ternary quantization levels. Although BitNet b1.58 demonstrates near-lossless performance on
      models up to 3 billion parameters and 100 billion training tokens with ternary quantization, its
124
      applicability to larger models or datasets remains uncertain due to prohibitive training expenses.
125
126   Quantized Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning of LLMs. Techniques like QLoRA (Dettmers et al.,
127   2023a), INT2.1 (Chai et al., 2023), LQ-LoRA (Guo et al., 2023), and LoftQ (Li et al., 2023c) quantize
128   model parameters to low-bit representations followed by the addition of LoRA (Hu et al., 2021)
129
      modules for fine-tuning. However, these methods require merging the LoRA modules into quantized
      weights, resulting in the model reverting to the FP16 format. Addressing this issue, QA-LoRA (Xu
130
      et al., 2023b) redesigns the LoRA module to merge seamlessly into the zero points. The approach
131
      most similar to ours is PEQA (Kim et al., 2023a), which uses a round-to-nearest (RTN) method
132   for low-bit quantization and fine-tunes step sizes for task adaptation. However, PEQA experiences
133   significant performance degradation due to limited trainable parameters, which hinders recovery from
134   quantization information loss.

138
139   3.1   M ETHOD OVERVIEW
140
      In this section, we introduce EfficientQAT, a novel quantization-aware training framework for
141
      LLMs that enhances memory efficiency. As illustrated in Figure 2, traditional QAT approaches
142   train the full-precision weights W and quantization parameters s (step sizes) and z (zero points)
143   simultaneously in an end-to-end manner, which significantly increases the memory requirements due
144   to the large number of parameters involved. To address this issue, EfficientQAT adopts a two-stage
145   strategy: block-wise training of all parameters (Block-AP) and end-to-end training of quantization
146   parameters (E2E-QP). In the Block-AP phase, model parameters and quantization parameters are
147   trained block-by-block using reconstruction loss, which not only allows for precise calibration with
148   full training but also reduces memory consumption (Li et al., 2021; Shao et al., 2023) by block-wise
149   training. Following this, the E2E-QP phase fixes the quantized weights and trains the step sizes
150
      exclusively on target datasets, thus achieving inter-block interaction in a memory-efficient way.
      Details on Block-AP and E2E-QP are further described in Sections 3.2 and 3.3, respectively.
151

185   Here we aim to enhance the training efficiency of QAT. Previous studies, such as BRECQ (Li et al.,
186   2021), have demonstrated that block-wise training achieves faster convergence and requires less
187   training time, data, and memory than end-to-end training given a pre-trained model. Following the
188   methodologies in BRECQ (Li et al., 2021) and OmniQuant (Shao et al., 2023), Block-AP sequentially
189   conducts quantization-aware training within one transformer block before moving on to the next
190   under a block-wise reconstruction framework.
191   Full Training of Model Weights and Quantization Parameters. Unlike previous methods which
192   optimize several quantization parameters such as rounding parameters (Nagel et al., 2020; Cheng
193   et al., 2023b; Lee et al., 2023b), clipping parameters (Shao et al., 2023), and step sizes (Esser et al.,
194   2019; Ding et al., 2023), Block-AP behaves like QAT, training all inherent parameters from Eq.(1)
195   and Eq.(2), including scaling factor s, zero point z, and model weights W.
196   In our Block-AP approach, a straightforward full-training regimen outperforms existing partial-
197   training variants (Nagel et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Ding et al., 2023) with intricate designs.
198   Traditional training methods involving rounding parameters (Nagel et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Ding
199   et al., 2023) serve as regularization techniques, constraining the update range of integral weights
200   to (−1, +1) to mitigate overfitting. However, this approach limits the solution space, potentially
201   hindering the final performance of quantized models. Our empirical findings demonstrate the
202   superiority of full training within our Block-AP over existing partial-training variants (Nagel et al.,
203   2020; Li et al., 2021; Ding et al., 2023), as shown in Table 6.
204   Following block-wise training, we obtain the quantized model which includes quantized weights Wq ,
205   step sizes s, and zero points z for each quantization group. The weights Wq and zero points z are
206   stored in a low-bit format, while step sizes s are stored in FP16. Note that s and z are shared within
207   their respective quantization groups and constitute only a small fraction of the model’s parameters,
208   approximately 1.6% for a group size of 64. Moreover, the model’s memory footprint is substantially
209   reduced by transitioning from full-precision 16-bit weights to 2/3/4-bit quantized weights.

      Training and Evaluation. Following existing works (Xu et al., 2023b; Qin et al., 2024), we train

355   et al., 2023a), we adjust the source context length to 384 and the target context length to 128, training
356   for 10,000 steps with a batch size of 16.
357   Baseline. We benchmark EfficientQAT against several leading methods, including QLoRA (Dettmers
358   et al., 2023a), QA-LoRA (Xu et al., 2023b), PEQA (Kim et al., 2023a), and IR-QLoRA (Qin et al.,

360   we also employ GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2022) to quantize the fine-tuned QLoRA models into a low-bit
361   format without FP16 LoRA for equitable comparison.
362
      Results. Both Table 4 and Figure 1b indicate that EfficientQAT significantly outperforms existing
363   Q-PEFT methods. For instance, in channel-wise quantization (group size of -1), EfficientQAT
364   achieves more than 3% higher accuracy than PEQA (Kim et al., 2023a). In the 2-bit quantization
365   scenario, the superiority of EfficientQAT is even more pronounced, surpassing QA-LoRA (Xu et al.,
366   2023b) by 5.1% and 4.0% in 7B and 13B models, respectively, and outperforming PEQA by 4.5%
367   and 8.7% in the same models. Moreover, Table 4 also demonstrates that EfficientQAT outperforms
368   both QA-LoRA and QLoRA with GPTQ in smaller model memory footprint (larger group size).

404   Trainable parameters of Block-AP. Block-AP trains all parameters, including original weights and
405   quantization parameters. Previous methods have introduced various training strategies to mitigate
406   overfitting, such as trained rounding (Nagel et al., 2020; Cheng et al., 2023b), clipping thresh-
407
      olds (Shao et al., 2023), and step sizes (Esser et al., 2019; Ding et al., 2023). We compare Block-AP
      with these methods by modifying only the trainable parameters of Block-AP. As shown in Table 6,
408
      Block-AP (training s, z, W) performs best with an acceptable training cost. Additionally, the memory
409
      footprint of directly training W is even smaller than that of training the rounding operation, which
410   requires an additional copy of rounding parameters.
411
412   Trainable parameters of E2E-QP. We further examine the trainable parameters within E2E-QP.
413
      Table 7 shows that training s, z, or both yields similar performance. However, given that converting z
      from an original low-bit representation to a trainable FP16 format increases the average bit count, we
414
      opt to train only s by default.
415
416   Samples number of Block-AP. We assess the number of training samples for Block-AP, noting
417   that E2E-QP trains all parameters, which may lead to overfitting. To address this, we introduce an
418   additional 64 unseen samples from ReadPajama to evaluate the overfitting issue. We adjust the training
419
      epochs to ensure a similar total training time, allowing for fair comparisons across different sample
      sizes. As illustrated in Figure 3, increasing the number of training samples significantly reduces the
420
      gap between training loss and validation loss from 1.07 to 0.06. This reduction corresponds to an
421
      increase in the average accuracy for zero-shot tasks from 57.14% to 58.99%. Consequently, we set
422   the default number of training samples for E2E-QP at 4096, as this maintains a minimal gap between
423   training and validation losses.
424
425
      Samples number of E2E-QP. In the E2E-QP, we train the model for 1 epoch to avoid over-fitting.
      Our examination of the training sample sizes for E2E-QP, detailed in Table 4, reveals that average
426
      perplexity consistently improves as sample sizes increase from 128 to 32,674. However, there is no
427
      significant improvement in average accuracy beyond 4096 samples. Therefore, we set the training
428   sample size for E2E-QP at 4096 by default to balance efficiency and performance. Nonetheless, it is
429   possible to further enhance the performance of EfficientQAT by increasing the sample size.

479
480   In this study, we introduce EfficientQAT, a novel method that completes QAT with improved efficiency
481   in both memory usage and training time. Through comprehensive testing, EfficientQAT proves
482   superior to existing PTQ, QAT, and Q-PEFT methods in terms of versatility and performance across
483   various models and quantization levels. Additionally, EfficientQAT leverages a standard uniform
484   quantization, which simplifies deployment using popular toolboxes. We anticipate that EfficientQAT
485   will stimulate further research and improve the compression of Large Language Models (LLMs),
      making them more efficient and widely accessible.

      F       A DDITIONAL A BLATION A NALYSIS
919
920
       Quantization Group Size. The group size is a crucial hyperparameter in weight-only quantization.
921   A smaller group size offers more granular compression and reduces quantization loss but increases
922   the number of quantization parameters required. As indicated in Table 12, a group size of 64 strikes
923   an optimal balance for 2-bit quantization using EfficientQAT. It outperforms a group size of 128
924   by achieving a 0.31 lower perplexity and a 0.64% higher accuracy, yet it slightly underperforms
925   compared to a group size of 32, with a marginal difference of 0.09 in perplexity and 0.14% in
926   accuracy.
927   Training Dataset. More trainable parameters can increase the risk of overfitting. Previous
928   works (Gong et al., 2024) show that a similar distribution between the calibration dataset and
929   the test dataset can improve test accuracy. RedPajama and C4 datasets are diverse, while WikiText2 is
930   simpler and sourced from Wikipedia. The close distribution of training and test datasets for WikiText2
931   results in significantly lower WikiText2 perplexity when using it as a calibration dataset. However,
932   the average accuracy of zero-shot tasks in Table R7 shows that Block-AP’s generation ability is
933   excellent, with only 0.26% and 1.28% accuracy declines when changing the calibration dataset from
934   RedPajama to WikiText2 for w3g128 and w2g64, respectively. Additionally, using C4 as a calibration
935
      dataset can even increase the average accuracy by 0.2-0.3 points. Overall, we recommend using
      Block-AP with more diverse calibration datasets like C4 or RedPajama.

962   Traditional Q-PEFT methods only do experiments on the language models. In this section, we further
963   extend proposed EfficientQAT into Large vision-Language models (LVLMs) such as LLaVA (Liu
964   et al., 2023b).
965
      Training and Evaluation. For the fine-tuning of large vision-language models (LVLMs), we
966
      largely align with LLaVA1.5 (Liu et al., 2023a), which encompass the training model, datasets, and
967
      hyperparameters8 . Unlike LLaVA1.5, which begins fine-tuning with full-precision Vicuna models
968   using either full fine-tuning or LoRA-based methods (Hu et al., 2021), EfficientQAT starts with
969   Vicuna models already quantized using our Block-AP method and continues with our E2E-QP fine-
970   tuning approach. The training process involves two steps: initially freezing the LLM and pre-training
971
          8
              For comprehensive details, please consult the official repository at https://github.com/haotian-liu/LLaVA.

972
       a projector to align features with a Vision Transformer (ViT), followed by end-to-end fine-tuning of
973    both the LLM and the projector. For EfficientQAT, we modify the learning rates in the second step to
974    2 × 10−5 for 4-bit and 3 × 10−5 for 2-bit and 3-bit.
975

       Evaluation. Evaluation of the fine-tuned LVLMs are conducted across four benchmarks: MME (Fu
       et al., 2023), MM-Vet (Yu et al., 2023), MMBench (Liu et al., 2023d), and ScienceQA (Lu et al.,

       2022).

979    Baseline. We compare our results with those of QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023a), applying our
980    Block-AP method to quantize the QLoRA fine-tuned models to low bits for fair comparison.
981    Results. As shown in Table 14, EfficientQAT outperforms QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023a) in
982    low-bit settings for both LLaVA-1.5-7B and LLaVA-1.5-13B models, consistent with previous results
983    in LMMs. Remarkably, the 2-bit LLaVA-1.5-13B model trained with EfficientQAT achieves an
984    average score of 59.9, surpassing the 59.6 of the FP16 LLaVA-1.5-7B model trained with LoRA.

986    compared to the 16-bit LoRA, indicating that further research is needed to optimize Q-PEFT within
