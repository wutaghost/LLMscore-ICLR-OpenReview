011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Capturing and recovering from hardware failures is important in fault-tolerant dis-
014            tributed training to guarantee system efﬁciency. However, some hardware-related
015            silent data corruption errors during gradient aggregation like bit corruptions or
016
               communication noise, are difﬁcult to capture and address, leading to slow or
               failed convergence. To understand and mitigate these errors, we ﬁrst mathemat-
017
               ically formulate and generalize them as gradient inconsistency. Then, we theo-
018
               retically analyze how it leads to model divergence accumulated during training
019            and the failed convergence. Based on the analytical study, we design PAFT, a
020            fault-tolerant distributed training system with dynamic and asynchronous parame-
021            ter synchronization. PAFT includes two parts: (1) PAFT-Sync, which mitigates
022            model divergence by periodically synchronizing parameters, and (2) PAFT-Dyn,
023            which minimizes synchronization overhead through dynamic training overlap and
024            synchronization frequency scheduling based on proﬁled error degrees. Together,
025            they ensure efﬁcient model convergence at scale. The fault-tolerant synchroniza-
026            tion in PAFT is optimized to support commonly used optimizers, e.g., Stochastic
027
               Gradient Descent (SGD), SGD momentum, and Adam. We implement PAFT on
               PyTorch Distributed and train ResNet, GPT-2, and LLaMA-2 on 4∼ 32 GPUs.
028
               Experimental results show that PAFT efﬁciently defends against gradient aggrega-
029
               tion error degrees while maintaining training performance.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      To efﬁciently train deep learning (DL) models (He et al., 2016) and large language models
035
      (LLMs) (Radford et al., 2018; Chung et al., 2022), high-performance and large-scale distributed
036   training frameworks have been proposed (Rasley et al., 2020; Narayanan et al., 2021; 2019; Tang
037   et al., 2023). Frequent system failures suspend training and require manual recovery from check-
038   points, signiﬁcantly reducing system efﬁciency and GPU utilization (up to 43%) (Maeng et al., 2021;
039   Wang et al., 2023b). Approximately 178,000 GPU hours were wasted during the OPT-175B train-
040   ing (Zhang et al., 2022) due to various failures like MPI and CUDA errors (Humbatova et al., 2020),
041   and hardware failures such as GPU malfunctions (Hu et al., 2024), electronic breakdowns, and node
042   failures (Wang et al., 2023b; Hu et al., 2024). Many existing studies focus on improving the robust-
043   ness and efﬁciency of the system through fast recovery (Wang et al., 2023b; 2024; Narayanan et al.,
044
      2021) or elastic training (Thorpe et al., 2022; Harlap et al.; He et al., 2023a).
045   However, unlike system failures, silent data corruption (SDC) errors (Wang et al., 2023a; Fiala
046   et al., 2012; Bacon, 2022; He et al., 2023b), which do not directly interrupt training, are increasingly
047   affecting model quality and convergence. As reported in LLaMA-3 pretraining cluster and Fire-Flyer
048   cluster, SDC errors have become the main cause of LLM convergence issues, and the secondary cost
049   of fault tolerance during pretraining (Dubey et al., 2024; An et al., 2024), harming the reliability and
050
      efﬁciency of GPU clusters at extensive scale. (We provide more real-world error types and frequency
      during LLM pretraining in Appendix B).
051
052   In this work, we consider the errors happen during gradient aggregation (GA), which are caused by
053   hardware failures like bit corruptions (Jeon et al., 2019; Tiwari et al., 2015; Gao et al., 2023; Hu et al.,
      2024) and communication noise on network links (Hu et al., 2024; Gill et al., 2011; Tan et al., 2019;

      6   R ELATED W ORKS
490
491   Due to the limited space, we introduce the concise related works here, and leave detailed discussions
492   in Appendix A.
493
494   Parallelism at Scale Distributed large model (LM) training (Narayanan et al., 2021) employs
495   hybrid parallelism techniques, including data parallelism (DP), tensor model parallelism (TP), and
496   pipeline parallelism (PP). DP (Krizhevsky et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2016; Cui et al., 2016; Zhang
497   et al., 2017; Tang et al., 2020; 2022), which replicates models for parallel training, is central in
498   hybrid parallelism. It scales the training effectively by increasing the batch size to accelerate model
499
      convergence. TP (Or et al., 2020; Narayanan et al., 2021) and PP (Narayanan et al., 2019; Rasley
      et al., 2020; Tang et al., 2023) complement DP by addressing memory limitations when models
500
      exceed a single device’s memory capacity. PAFT tackles GA errors and has been generalized to
501
      hybrid parallel training frameworks like DeepSpeed (Rasley et al., 2020) and Megatron (Narayanan
502   et al., 2021) towards large-scale LLM training.
503
504
      Safety and Reliability of Distributed Training Many studies focus on system reliability concern-
505   ing node failures, using checkpointing (Wang et al., 2023b; 2024; Narayanan et al., 2021) and elas-
506   ticity (Thorpe et al., 2022; Harlap et al.; He et al., 2023a) optimizations for rapid recovery. These
507   optimizations enhance system robustness and enable quick restarts. Also, there are many efforts
508   against Byzantine faults (El-Mhamdi et al., 2020; Damaskinos et al., 2018; Guerraoui et al., 2024)
509   by malicious node behavior. However, silent errors, represented by GA errors in the scope of this
510   paper, arise from unintentional issues like hardware errors or communication errors, leading to inac-
511   curacies in gradient updates. Unlike the other types of errors, GA errors are particularly challenging
512   due to their subtlety and variability, making them more difﬁcult and resource-intensive to detect and
513
      mitigate. To the best of our knowledge, PAFT is the ﬁrst effort to improve system reliability against
      GA errors at scale.

      7   L IMITATIONS
517   Performance gap between PAFT and the oracle. In this work, as illustrated in the experiments 5,
518   we do not completely close the performance gap when the noise degree is large.Future works should
519   consider combining both parameter synchronization and voting mechanisms like the Byzantine
520   Fault-tolerance problem (Guerraoui et al., 2024) to address this problem.
521
      Extra communication overheads. PAFT introduces extra communication overheads due to the
522   parameter synchronization. And the synchronizing optimizer states also introduce extra overheads.
523   While we have shown that the overheads are acceptable in the experiments, the overheads may be
524   signiﬁcant in some scenarios like the low-bandwidth environments. Future works should consider
525   optimizing the synchronization frequency to reduce the overheads.

527   8   C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this work, we address GA errors in distributed training caused by hardware issues like bit corrup-
530   tions and communication noise, which are challenging to capture and mitigate for fault tolerance.
531
      We ﬁrst mathematically formulate and generalize these errors as gradient inconsistency. Then, we
      theoretically analyze how they lead to accumulated model divergence and failed convergence. To ad-
532
      dress this issue, we propose PAFT, a fault-tolerant distributed training system incorporating dynamic
533
      and asynchronous parameter synchronization optimizations. The two components of PAFT-Sync
534   and PAFT-Dyn work synergistically to mitigate the negative impact of GA errors. PAFT-Sync
535   maintains model convergence by periodically synchronizing parameters, while PAFT-Dyn mini-
536   mizes overhead by adjusting synchronization frequency based on the proﬁled error degrees. Our
537   implementation of PAFT on PyTorch Distributed, evaluated on ResNet-18, ResNet-50, GPT-2, and
538   LLaMA-2 models across 32 GPUs, demonstrates the systems robustness against a wide range of
539   GA errors. The evaluation results indicate that, unlike vanilla distributed training, PAFT effectively
      maintains fault tolerance without compromising training throughput.

      A     M ORE R ELATED W ORKS
811
812
813   A.1   PARALLELISM AT S CALE
814
815   Distributed large model (LM) training (Narayanan et al., 2021) employs hybrid parallelism tech-
816   niques, including data parallelism, tensor model parallelism, and pipeline parallelism.
817   Data parallelism (DP) (Krizhevsky et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2016; Cui et al., 2016; Zhang et al.,
818   2017; Tang et al., 2020; 2022), which replicates models for parallel training, is central in hybrid
819   parallelism. It scales the training effectively by increasing the batch size to accelerate model conver-
820   gence. However, DP is limited by memory capacity and communication overheads, especially for
821   large-scale LM training. This paper focuses on the GA erros in DP training.
822
      Tensor model parallelism (TP) (Or et al., 2020; Narayanan et al., 2021) complement DP by ad-
823   dressing memory limitations when models exceed a single device’s memory capacity. PAFT tackles
824   GA errors and has been generalized to hybrid parallel training frameworks like DeepSpeed (Rasley
825   et al., 2020) and Megatron (Narayanan et al., 2021) towards large-scale LM training. The TP training
826   may also have communicatin errors, which is out of the scope of this paper. And the communication
827   errors in concating tensors in TP more like the computational SDC errors, which is different from
828   the GA errors in DP.
829   Pipeline parallelism (PP) (Narayanan et al., 2019; Rasley et al., 2020; Tang et al., 2023) splits the
830   whole model into different stages and processes them in a pipelined manner. The PP can reduce the
831   memory consumption and communication overheads. The communication errors happen in PP are
832   more like the quantization or compression errors, which is different from the GA errors either.
833
834
835   A.2   S AFETY AND R ELIABILITY OF D ISTRIBUTED T RAINING
836
837   Active Failures. Many studies focus on system reliability concerning node failures, which may
838   directly interrupt training processes. These studies propose fault-tolerant mechanisms using check-
839   pointing (Wang et al., 2023b; 2024; Narayanan et al., 2021) and elasticity (Thorpe et al., 2022; Har-
840   lap et al.; He et al., 2023a) optimizations for rapid recovery. These optimizations enhance system
841   robustness and enable quick restarts.
842   Silent Failures. There are other soft failures like the communication noise happen in GA, or the
843   workers upload the wrong gradients to the server. The typical methods to handle these failures
844   include gradient clip, or considering them as the Byzantine faults by malicious node behavior (El-
845   Mhamdi et al., 2020; Damaskinos et al., 2018; Guerraoui et al., 2024). However, the silent errors
846   in GA errors in the scope of this paper, arise from unintentional issues like hardware errors or
847   communication errors, leading to inaccuracies in gradient updates. And we mainly focus on the GA
848   errors happen during the broadcasting in DP training, which is different from the other types of soft
849
      failures.
850
851
852
      A.3   A SYNCHRONOUS O PTIMIZATIONS
853
854   To accelerate distributed training, asynchronous optimization techniques have been proposed to re-
855
      duce the synchronization overheads (Tsitsiklis et al., 1986; Zheng et al., 2017; Damaskinos et al.,
      2018). These techniques allow workers to update model parameters independently, reducing the
856
      waiting time for synchronization. To consider accelerating synchronizing checkpoints, many works
857
      utilize the asynchronous and heterogeneous capabilities of hardware resources for parallel process-
858   ing of different tasks. For example, in checkpointing optimizations, asynchronous parameter snap-
859   shotting can compete for memory bandwidth with training processes, potentially slowing down the
860   training speed (Mohan et al.; team, 2022; Wang et al., 2024). Additionally, inter-node communi-
861   cations asynchronous to training can introduce communication overheads (Shi et al., 2020; 2021b).
862   In PAFT-Sync, we also observe unavoidable asynchronous overheads during training. However,
863   the dynamic synchronization frequency effectively reduces the overall asynchronous overhead in the
      fault-tolerant system.

868
      Silent data corruption (SDC) errors are particularly insidious in high-performance computing
869   (HPC) (Wang et al., 2023a; He et al., 2023b), database (Bacon, 2022) and communication systems
870   because they can go undetected and lead to incorrect results. These errors can occur due to vari-
871   ous reasons, including hardware faults, software bugs, or cosmic radiation. In the context of HPC,
872   SDC errors can signiﬁcantly impact the reliability and accuracy of computations, especially in large-
873   scale simulations and data-intensive applications. The large-scale distributed deep learning might
874   be severely inﬂuenced by the SDC errors (He et al., 2023b).
875   In communication systems, SDC errors can be introduced during data transmission between nodes
876   in a distributed computing environment (Fiala et al., 2012; gorzata Steinder & Sethi, 2004). These
877   errors can be caused by issues such as faulty network hardware, electromagnetic interference, or sig-
878   nal degradation over long distances. The impact of SDC errors in communication can be severe, as
879   they can lead to incorrect data being propagated through the system, potentially causing widespread
880   computational errors.
