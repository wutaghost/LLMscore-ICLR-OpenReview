009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               As its name suggests, the convolution operator is the basis and an essential compo-
012
               nent in Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). At the moment, modern CNN
013
               architectures rely heavily on parallel computation using GPUs and CPUs to perform
014            many convolutions as fast as possible. However, the performance of computing
015            CNNs is reaching its limit as the scaling of transistors approaches its size lim-
016            its. The convolutional theorem suggests the possibility of using acoustic waves
017            to efficiently perform the convolution operations through Fourier transforms in
018            analog. This promises hardware that would be several orders of magnitude faster
019            than existing silicon-based approaches. However, to date, nobody has shown
020            the practical feasibility of such an approach. In this paper, we describe the first
021            physics-based simulator for Ultrasonic Fourier Transform Convolutions (UFTC).
022
               By exploiting the diffraction nature of the waves, the Fourier transforms can be
               computed in the time it takes to propagate an ultrasonic wavefront. Our results
023
               show that ultrasonic computation could drastically improve the performance of
024
               CNNs by 12-458× FLOPS reduction and 1.3-4× computation speedup without
025            loss of prediction accuracy.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Computer vision, image processing, and pattern recognition have undergone several breakthroughs
032   due to innovations in deep neural networks, computer architecture, and transistor scaling. These
033   image-processing architectures are largely based on CNNs, which consists of many convolutional
034   layers that extract features from images using trained kernels Li et al. (2022); Aloysius & Geetha
035   (2017); Liu et al. (2017); Krizhevsky et al. (2012); Lecun et al. (1998); LeCun et al. (1989). One of
036   the key reasons convolutional layers are so effective in these architectures is their inherent ability to
037   be parallelized. Since convolutional layers operate by applying filters to small patches of an image
038   independently, these operations can be distributed across multiple processing units, such as GPUs,
039
      CPUs, and FPGAs, to maximize computational efficiency.
040   Graphics Processing Units (GPUs) are one of the most widely used hardware for CNNs Dally et al.
041   (2021); Ridnik et al. (2021); Zhu et al. (2020); Min et al. (2021). This is due to the number of
042   cores and architecture that are suitable for convolutions and matrix multiplications, leading to deep
043   learning libraries that support parallel processing through GPUs. However, it has become increasingly
044   challenging to make advancements in speed and power for these processing units as the size of the
045
      transistor approaches nanometer scales. Furthermore, lowering power consumption and managing
      thermal dissipation have become more challenging, which are critical issues for some applications,
046
      such as Edge and mobile devices. Field Programmable Gate Arrays (FPGA) are another processing
047
      technology that is increasingly being used for CNN models and machine learning Ma et al. (2018);
048   Mohammad & Agaian (2009); Bai et al. (2018); Ahmad & Pasha (2020); Bekkerman et al. (2012);
049   Zhang et al. (2015). FPGAs are flexible and can be highly customized for parallel processing,
050   enabling improved performance for CNN models compared to traditional GPUs and CPUs Russo
051   et al. (2012); Li et al. (2016); Zhou & Jiang (2015); Wang et al. (2017); Podili et al. (2017). However,
052   designing and optimizing FPGA systems can be time-consuming, complex, and costly. Furthermore,
053   FPGAs have limited resources, such as memory and logic cells, to run massive CNN models, making
      it difficult to scale for larger CNN models.

      One method to accelerate the convolution operation is to use the convolution theorem. Based on this
055   theorem, the Fourier transforms of the input and the kernel can be point-wise multiplied, and the
056   inverse Fourier transform can be applied to compute the convolution. When using the convolution
057   theorem, the computation time for convolutions depends on the speed of the Fourier transform,
058   the piece-wise multiplication, and the inverse Fourier transform. For the direct calculation of 2D
059   Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT), the computation complexity is O((M N )2 ) for an input of MxN
060   images. The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) is an efficient algorithm to compute the DFT and, using
061   the Radix-2 FFT, the computation complexity becomes O(N M log(N M )). However, the FFT has
062   a limited throughput for large data sets, even with parallelization. For this reason, this method is
063
      comparably faster only for larger kernels in modern GPUs.
064   An alternate method of using the convolution theorem is to use the wave phenomenon of diffraction
065   to compute Fourier transforms. Optical wave transforms, for example, have been used in the past
066   to compute the Fourier transform. An image is propagated in space and focused on a focal plane,
067   generating the Fourier transform of the input image. Recently, ultrasonic waves have been used to
068   realize Fourier transforms Hwang et al. (2022); Rippel et al. (2015); Liu et al. (2018b); Mathieu et al.
069
      (2014); Hwang et al. (2024), which can lead to a much more compact system owing to the lower
      speed of sound compared to the speed of light. The Ultrasonic Fourier Transform (UFT) hardware
070
      consists of Complementary Metal-Oxide Semiconductor (CMOS) components and AlN piezoelectric
071
      transmitters and receivers Nair et al. (2020); Chi et al. (2020); Pratt et al. (2017); Highlander &
072   Rodriguez (2016). The ultrasonic Fourier hardware requires a phase shift module for the incoming
073   waves to compute the Fourier transform and to reduce the device’s size. One approach is to add a
074   phase shifter on the transmitter and receiver sides. In another approach, a microfabricated convex
075   acoustic lens is used as a phase shifter.

      Based on the recent success in realizing the hardware components of the UFT, this paper explores the
102   use of the UFT simulation to compute convolutions for CNNs. We use an UFT simulator based on
103   the Huygens-Fresnel wave propagation to demonstrate and optimize the UFT hardware for CNNs.
104   The Huygens-Fresnel is a fundamental principle used as a framework to analyze and predict the
105   propagation of waves through space, including sound and light. The principle states that each point on
106   a wavefront is the source of the secondary wavelet, which is combined to predict the propagation of
107   the wave at any given time. Using this theorem, the simulation predicts the propagation of ultrasonic
      waves through different mediums, including silicon wafers. In order to simulate the ultrasonic

      Contributions With any new hardware research, it can be extremely costly to develop a fully scaled
145   device. As a result, it is crucial to validate these theoretical frameworks through realistic simulations
146   and models. Our current proposed hardware is still under development; however, several critical
147   components of the technology were demonstrated in previous works, such as 2D arrays of transmitters
148   and receivers of 1-2GHz, ultrasonic lenses, and working subsystems. For the first time, this paper
149   presents a comprehensive, realistic simulation model in which an ultrasonic wave convolution tool
150   has been used in practical CNN models. To our knowledge, we are one the first to demonstrate a
151   physics-based wave simulation that can train various CNN models to accuracies similar to that of
152   traditional CNNs while increasing its efficiency as determined by the speed of the ultrasonic waves.

      Just as the GPU technology has allowed various machine learning models to be accelerated, we hope
      our paper will excite researchers about the potential that this hardware may have in accelerating
154
      machine learning models. In short, the Deep Neural Network (DNN) community may benefit from
155
      having a new hardware component to optimize and perhaps create new architectures.

180   3     U LTRASONIC F OURIER T RANSFORM
181
182   When used with an ultrasonic lens, the ultrasonic wave generates the Fourier transform at the focal
183   plane of the lens. This can be shown by ultrasonic wave simulation based on the Huygens-Fresnel
184   principle. This simulation framework is important for several reasons. Firstly, the ultrasonic tool
185   can verify and validate the acoustic Fourier transform hardware architecture. Finite Element Method
186   (FEM) and Finite Difference Time Domain (FDTD) are alternate choices that can simulate the
187   acoustic wave for both the longitudinal and shear waves using 3D meshes. However, it is nearly
188   impossible to scale and simulate larger devices of 1920x1080 Full-HD pixels while maintaining a
189
      mesh size of a few µm or below, which is required by components such as acoustic lenses. In order to
      overcome these limitations, we have made a C++ and Python simulation framework to compute the
190
      ultrasonic wave at GHz frequencies. Secondly, the ultrasonic simulation can be used to optimize the
191
      structure of the device and fine-tune physical parameters such as the focal length, pixel size, acoustic
192   wavelength, and input image width. These are important factors when designing the hardware to
193   compute Fourier transform close to the exact FFT value. Thirdly, the program can easily be scaled
194   and combined with machine learning libraries for training computer vision models.

279   In convolutional neural networks, the computation cost scales linearly with the size of both inputs.
280
      For efficiency concerns, input images are usually rapidly downsampled by the first few convolu-
      tional/pooling layers, and convolutional kernels are often limited to 1 × 1 or 3 × 3 He et al. (2016);
281
      Huang et al. (2017). Larger convolutional kernels have been studied Ding et al. (2022), but scal-
282
      ability remains a non-neglectable concern and these large kernels have to be sparsified to reduce
283   computation cost. Now, with ultrasonic Fourier transform, the computation time of convolutional
284   operations is no longer dependent on kernel size. To convolve a N × N image with a k × k kernel,
285   the computation cost can be drastically reduced from O(N 2 k 2 ) to O(N ), enabling future research
286   on gigantic convolutional kernels.

      3.5   U LTRASONIC F OURIER O PTIMIZATION
317
      The dimension of the ultrasonic hardware needs to be optimized to generate a Fourier transform
318   close to the exact FFT value. This is critical when using ultrasonic computation for CNN models
319   to generate high-accuracy results. In order to avoid over and under-sampling, the dimension of the
320   ultrasonic device needs to meet the critical sampling condition given by L∆x = λz , where L is the
321   full width of the input, ∆x is the pixel size of the transmitter, λ is the wavelength of the acoustic wave,
322   and z is the focal length, respectively Voelz (2011). In order to optimize the device, we first define
323   the wavelength. Each pixel size should be designed to be substantially larger than the wavelength.
      Therefore, higher frequency and smaller wavelength are preferable to reduce the device’s size. In

      this work, we use frequencies between 1.0 and 2.0GHz, resulting in wavelengths between 8.4µm
325   and 4.2µm for a longitudinal acoustic speed of 8,433 m/s in silicon. Secondly, the total number of
326   pixels and the width of each pixel are defined. The pixel width is determined by the IC design and
327   size of the CMOS technology. Pixel widths of 10-100µm are used in this simulation. Furthermore, if
328   the total number of input pixels is 1000x1000 and the pixel width is 10µm, the entire width of the
329   input, L, is 1mm. Finally, the focal length, z, is defined by the critical sampling equation. Using this

524   7      C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this work, we presented a simulation framework for an ultrasonic device that can compute
527   convolutions in CNN models with 12-458× FLOPS reduction and 1.3-4× speedup through the
528   Fourier transform using the intrinsic characteristics of ultrasonic waves in CMOS/piezoelectric arrays.
529   A simulation model was first used to optimize the model’s dimensions, including the wavelength,
530   focal length, pixel size, and width of the input image. Then, the optimized hardware simulation
531   model was used to train CNN models, which consistently showed high accuracy in predictions for
532
      several DNN models. This model demonstrates that there is a pathway to greatly reduce the time and
      energy consumption of CNNs using ultrasonic physical convolution accelerator systems.
