009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Reward Models (RMs) play a crucial role in aligning large language models
012            (LLMs) with human preferences, enhancing their performance by ranking out-
013            puts during inference or iterative training. However, the degree to which an RM
014            generalizes to new tasks is often not known a priori. For instance, some RMs
015            may excel at scoring creative writing, while others specialize in evaluating math
016            reasoning. Therefore, using only one fixed RM while training LLMs can be sub-
017            optimal. Moreover, optimizing LLMs with multiple RMs simultaneously can be
018
               prohibitively computationally-intensive and challenging due to conflicting signals
               from different RMs, potentially degrading performance. To address these chal-
019
               lenges, we introduce LAS E R (Learning to Adaptively Select Rewards), which
020
               iteratively trains LLMs using multiple RMs, selecting and utilizing the most well-
021            suited RM for each instance to rank outputs and generate preference data, framed
022            as a multi-armed bandit problem. Our empirical results on commonsense and
023            math reasoning tasks demonstrate that LAS E R can boost iterative LLM optimiza-
024            tion by optimizing for multiple RMs, improving the absolute average accuracy of
025            Llama-3-8B over three datasets by 2.67% over training with ensemble RM scores
026            while also showing superior training efficiency (e.g., a 2× speedup). Moreover,
027            on WildChat, a benchmark of instruction-following prompts in open-form gen-
028            eration, we find that using Llama-3-8B LAS E R leads to a 71.45% AlpacaEval
029
               win rate over sequentially optimizing multiple RMs. Extending to long-context
               generation tasks, we find that on Llama-3-8B, LAS E R achieves an average im-
030
               provement of 2.64 F1 points on single-document QA tasks and 2.42 F1 points on
031
               multi-document QA over random RM selection when used with best-of-n sam-
032            pling. Our analysis shows that LAS E R is robust to noisy rewards and generalizes
033            to multiple settings. Finally, we demonstrate that LAS E R’s RM selection changes
034            depending on the underlying task or instance, and we verify the presence of con-
035            flicting preferences from multiple RMs, which can be mitigated using LAS E R.

038   1   I NTRODUCTION
039
040   When comparing two responses, human preferences often differ depending on factors like the under-
041   lying task, who the annotators are (Santurkar et al., 2023; Ahmadian et al., 2024), and how prefer-
042   ences are elicited (Bansal et al., 2024). Therefore, models of preference data are also likely to differ
043   and might include noise as well as any biases contained in the preference data used to train them.
044   This can pose a problem when using such models as “reward models” (RMs) to align large language
045
      models (LLMs) to human preferences using reinforcement learning with human feedback (Chris-
      tiano et al., 2017; Ziegler et al., 2019; Ouyang et al., 2022). Recent work has focused on aligning
046
      LLMs through iterative training, using reward models as proxies for human judgment (Gulcehre
047
      et al., 2023), leveraging the LLM to act as an implicit RM or judge (Yuan et al., 2024b; Chen et al.,
048   2024b), or using the gold answer to compute a reward (Pang et al., 2024). Under this paradigm, there
049   are three stages to training LLMs: (i) generating multiple responses to a query from an LLM; (ii)
050   scoring the responses with an RM to create preference data with better and worse responses; and (iii)
051   using model-generated preference data to further train the LLM. Note that for most domains, the
052   gold reward is not readily available, making the quality of the RM or the degree to which it reflects
053   human preferences (i.e., the gold reward) crucial to improving LLM performance. Indeed, several
      prior efforts aim to train new RMs that better reflect human preferences (Lambert et al., 2024).

054
      However, selecting one reward model to guide LLM training can be suboptimal for three main rea-
055   sons: (1) A single RM may not be generalized to heterogeneous sets of examples. RMs are typically
056   designed to reflect specific objectives and may be trained on offline preference datasets. Thus, an
057   RM that performs well on one dataset or domain may not generalize effectively to others, leading to
058   misaligned outputs across different tasks or domains (Kirk et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024a; Casper
059   et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023). For instance, creativity plays a key role in evaluating the quality of a
060   story, whereas correctness is more important in scoring math solutions. (2) RM performance leader-
061   boards (e.g., Lambert et al. (2024)) that rely on human-annotated preferences can have unreliable
062   rankings due to the presence of incorrect and ambiguous preferences (Yu et al., 2024; Hejna et al.,
063
      2023). (3) Lastly, over-optimization on a particular RM can lead to reward hacking (Skalse et al.,
      2022; Rafailov et al., 2024a), resulting in minimal gain or even drops in downstream performance.
064
065   To mitigate these issues, a prevalent approach is to ensemble multiple reward models (Coste et al.,
066   2023; Eisenstein et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Ramé et al., 2024). However, these methods also
067   come with significant challenges: as RMs are typically based on LLMs, training with multiple RMs
068   often requires loading and managing several large models simultaneously, which can be computa-
069
      tionally expensive, becoming infeasible as models increase in size. Moreover, aggregating multiple
      RM scores together is susceptible to noisy rewards or conflicting preferences from RMs, especially
070
      RMs that are not well-suited for the specific task (Rita et al., 2024). This, in turn, can degrade the
071
      quality of the preference data, leading to low-quality updates during training (Wang et al., 2024a).
072   Finally, manually selecting a subset of RMs to combine is a labor-intensive process that involves
073   training many different variants on a combinatorially large set of RM groupings. This underscores
074   the need for more efficient methods that efficiently and robustly optimize LLMs using multiple RMs.
075
      In this work, we introduce Learning to Adaptively Select Rewards (LAS E R), that, given a set of
076
      RMs, adaptively and efficiently selects a suitable RM for each instance by casting selection as a
077
      multi-armed bandit problem (Vermorel & Mohri, 2005; Audibert et al., 2009). Specifically, during
078   training, the RM (arm) is chosen dynamically based on contextual information about the model’s
079   performance and past interactions. The LLM is then fine-tuned based on the RM-annotated data,
080   and the bandit’s parameters are updated accordingly to reflect the performance of the LLM after
081   training on preference data annotated using selected RM (see Fig. 1). By design, LAS E R’s adaptive
082   instance-level RM selection (c.f. Sec. 3) addresses the three shortcomings of choosing one reward
083   model (lack of generalization, unreliable rankings, and over-optimization) and outperforms using
084   the same RM across all instances, yielding higher downstream performance and better generaliza-
085   tion. Moreover, unlike previous multi-RM methods that require simultaneously loading and running
086
      multiple RMs (Ramé et al., 2024; Coste et al., 2023), our method selects one reward model at a time
      (Sec. 4). This makes the training more efficient and improves performance by allowing the model
087
      to adaptively focus on the most suitable RM for each specific instance or phase of training.
088
089   Empirically, we demonstrate the effectiveness of LAS E R for iteratively training LLMs using mul-
090   tiple RMs on three broad domains: reasoning, instruction-following in text generation, and long-
091   context understanding (Sec. 4.2). We show that on reasoning benchmarks such as StrategyQA (Geva
092
      et al., 2021) (testing commonsense reasoning) and GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) (testing math rea-
      soning), LAS E R with Llama-3-8B improves absolute accuracy (averaged across 3 datasets) by
093
      1.45% over the best single RM and 2.67% over an ensemble of RM scores. With Mistral-7B,
094
      LAS E R outperforms RM agreement ensemble baseline by 1.65% in absolute accuracy. LAS E R
095   is also effective on general instruction-following: we show that using LAS E R with four strong
096   7B RMs from RewardBench to finetune Llama-3-8B on a subset of WildChat (Zhao et al., 2024)
097   beats LLMs trained with the best RM in the RM score ensemble and a sequential baseline, with
098   56.34% and 71.45% win rates (respectively) on length-controlled AlpacaEval (Dubois et al., 2024).
099   LAS E R also beats the RM score ensemble, with 72.69% and 73.27% win rates using Llama-3-8B
100   and Mistral-7B. Moreover, our results show the effectiveness of LAS E R’s RM selection strategy at
101   inference-time on long-form generation tasks; on LongBench (Bai et al., 2022), we find LAS E R
102   beats random RM selection baseline by 2.64 F1 points on single-document QA tasks and 2.42 F1
103
      points on multi-document QA when using best-of-n sampling for Llama-3-8B. Our analysis reveals
      that LAS E R is more efficient than sequential multi-RM and RM ensemble baselines in terms of
104
      training time (wall-clock hours) by a factor of 3×, and 2×, respectively while being more robust
105
      to noisy rewards and conflicting preferences from multiple RMs (Sec. 5). Finally, we demonstrate
106   that LAS E R effectively selects RMs based on the underlying instance and generalizes to multiple
107   settings, including out-of-distribution datasets, different training loss functions, etc.

      2     R ELATED WORK
109
110   Multiple Reward Ensembles. Training large language models (LLMs) with multiple reward func-
111   tions is an emerging research area focused on aligning model outputs with complex objectives that
112   require diverse evaluation metrics. One prior approach involves using ensembles of multiple re-
113   wards (Ramé et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024; Coste et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b;
114   Jang et al., 2023; Eisenstein et al., 2023). Unlike these methods, which train multiple RMs or use
115
      multiple RMs during LLM training, LAS E R selects only a single pretrained RM at each LLM train-
      ing step. Not only is optimizing for one RM at a time more efficient, but it also avoids the problem
116
      of having conflicting rewards (Rita et al., 2024). In Sec. 4.2, we show that LAS E R outperforms
117
      multiple variants of optimizing LLMs with an ensemble of RMs aggregating scores (consistent with
118   Coste et al. (2023)) and based on the agreement between preferences from different RMs (similar to
119   Wang et al. (2024d)). Another line of work uses mixture-of-experts (MoE) techniques for training an
120   RM from multiple interpretable objectives (Wang et al., 2024c) or jointly training task-specific RMs
121   and a sparse MoE router (Quan, 2024). Instead of relying on static datasets with human-annotated
122   attributes for RM training (as Wang et al. (2024c) does), we employ existing RMs to train LLMs on
123   its own generations, a strategy that has been shown to be more effective (Ivison et al., 2024). Unlike
124   Quan (2024), who jointly train multiple RMs and sparse MoE router (requiring loading all models
125   at once), LAS E R implicitly and efficiently learns a sparse router and does not need to train RMs.
126
      LAS E R performs RM selection via multi-armed bandits over existing off-the-shelf RMs with strong
      performance on leaderboards (Lambert et al., 2024).
127
128   Iterative LLM Training. Recent works on training LLMs incorporate reinforcement learning with
129   human feedback (RLHF) due to its effectiveness in improving instruction following the ability of
130   LLMs over their pretrained counterparts (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023).
131   The standard RLHF training pipeline, which comprises finetuning the LLM on static datasets with
132   human-annotated preferences, is bottlenecked by the size and quality of annotated preference data as
133   well as the limited effectiveness of off-policy optimization (Xu et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2024; Yuan
134
      et al., 2024b; Guo et al., 2024). To remedy this, recent work focuses on training LLMs iteratively,
      scoring the LLM’s generations to create feedback data for RLHF. This line of work obtains scores
135
      either from gold labels (Singh et al., 2023; Pang et al., 2024), from a single RM (Gulcehre et al.,
136
      2023), or from the generating LLM (Yuan et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024b). In contrast, we take
137   advantage of the abundance of publicly-available RMs and growing interest in developing RMs
138   (Lambert et al., 2024) by using multiple RMs. This has several advantages over past work: it
139   works in cases where gold labels are not available (e.g., generation tasks), deals with the three
140   issues associated with using a single RM (unreliable rankings, lack of generalizability, and over-
141   optimization), reduces the burden on the user to pick the right RM, and avoids problems stemming
142   from the inability of LLMs to judge their own responses for certain domains (Huang et al., 2023).

      Background: Multi-Armed Bandits. The multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem addresses the
217   challenge of balancing exploration and exploitation in sequential decision-making (Vermorel &
218   Mohri, 2005; Audibert et al., 2009). The goal is to maximize cumulative MAB rewards over time
219   by selecting arms that yield the highest MAB rewards.2 A decision-making agent faces a trade-off:
220   whether to exploit the arm with the highest known MAB reward based on past observations or ex-
221   plore other, less familiar arms to gather more information that might lead to even better rewards in
222   the future. In a contextual MAB setting, the agent is also provided with additional information in the
223   form of a context, such as current state and input, to help inform arm selection accordingly. LAS E R
224   uses MABs to dynamically identify the most suitable RM for each query xi through exploration
225
      while simultaneously training the LLM. Pulling a previously un(der)-explored arm allows the MAB
      to update its information about the relevance and quality of preference pairs built using that RM via
226
      the MAB reward (discussed below).

      Reward models. We select K = 4 strong 7B RMs from RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024),

292   lowing the pipeline outlined in Lambert et al. (2024), for these models, we compute the reward for
293   each response as the log likelihood of the RM for that response (details in Appendix A.1).
294
295   Datasets and Metrics. Our experiments cover a range of tasks and datasets (see Appendix A.1):
296
      • Reasoning: Evaluating reasoning abilities is crucial for testing the model’s capacity to handle
297
        complex, multi-step tasks and has presented a challenge to iterative preference optimization meth-
298
        ods (Yuan et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024b). We train and evaluate on StrategyQA (Geva et al.,
299     2021), MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021b;a), and GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021).
300   • Instruction-Following: We further evaluate our method on heterogeneous tasks without gold
301     labels. We use user prompts from WildChat dataset (Zhao et al., 2024), which contains a collec-
302     tion of natural user-chatbot interactions. This dataset has five primary categories of instruction-
303     following prompts: creative writing, analysis, coding, factual information, and math reasoning.
304     Due to computational constraints, we randomly subsample 5K prompts from each category for
305     model training. We compare models trained with LAS E R against baselines (described below)
306     using length-controlled AlpacaEval (Dubois et al., 2024) that pairs responses from two different

350

354     Appendix A.1). Specifically, for each query and RM, we compute the RM’s score of the annotated
355     preferred and disprefered response. The RM that assigns the correct preference ordering with the
356     highest difference between the scores of the preferred and dispreferred responses is chosen as the
357     RM label and used to train the classifier. While training the LLM, for each training input xi ∈ D,
358     we select the RM for building preference pairs based on this trained classifier.

529   6   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      We present LAS E R, an adaptive method for selecting RMs and iteratively training LLMs using mul-
      tiple RMs. We formulate the problem as a contextual multi-armed bandit problem, learning to select
532
      the RM that most improves the LLM conditioned on the given input or query. We test LAS E R across
533
      diverse settings, showing its utility on reasoning tasks, instruction-following tasks, and long-context
534   generation. Across domains, we show that LAS E R consistently results in superior performance,
535   whereas multi-RM baselines that select RMs using random or fixed strategies or ensemble multiple
536   RMs uniformly have lower and more variable performance. In our analysis, we show that LAS E R
537   is robust to noisy RMs, and flexibly uses different RMs depending on the domain, and generalizes to
538   multiple settings. Lastly, by selecting one RM at a time, LAS E R provides the best of both worlds:
539   consistently outperforming all baselines while still maintaining efficiency by only optimizing for
      one model at a time.

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      LLMs have been shown to reflect stereotypes, biases, and other negative traits contained in their pre-
543   training data (Weidinger et al., 2021). Consequently, finetuned LLMs (including those trained with
544   LAS E R) may also exhibit such undesirable traits in their generations during inference or training
545   and exhibit the same potential for misuse as any other finetuned model. While prior work have made
546   some headway in detecting such harmful content generated by LLMs (Inan et al., 2023), consider-
547   able research effort is needed in mitigating bias in LLMs. Conceptually, classifiers that detect risky,
548   harmful, or biased content in the text can also be used as an additional RM in LAS E R’s training to
549   reinforce avoiding bias via preference optimization. However, we do not study this in our work and
550   leave it to future work to explore these directions.

849   normalization since we use this log-likelihood to rank the responses. Specifically, on GSM8K, we

1092   Related Work: Multi-Armed Bandits (MABs). There is a long-standing history of using multi-
1093   armed bandit algorithms for diverse applications in machine learning spanning online advertis-
1094   ing (Chen et al., 2013), recommendation systems (Li et al., 2010), hyperparameter optimization (Li
1095   et al., 2018), curriculum learning (Graves et al., 2017), with some recent work at the intersection
1096   of MABs and language models (Pasunuru et al., 2020; Krishnamurthy et al., 2024; Dwaracherla
1097
       et al., 2024, inter alia). In the realm of RLHF specifically, Dwaracherla et al. (2024) use double
       Thomson Sampling (Wu & Liu, 2016) to select which of the sampled responses should be annotated
1098
       and paired using a single fixed RM, improving LLM performance. In contrast, LAS E R first selects
1099
       which RM (i.e., model of preferences) should be used to annotate LLM’s responses to a query and
1100   then creates multiple preference pairs from these responses. Pasunuru et al. (2020) optimize text
1101   generation models for different evaluation metrics such as ROUGE-L and BLEU via policy gradi-
1102   ents (Williams, 1992) over existing (static) question and data-to-text generation datasets. In contrast,
1103   LAS E R adopts an iterative training recipe and improves downstream generation performance across
1104   a wide range of tasks from instruction-following to math and commonsense reasoning by selecting
1105   relevant RMs and scoring the LLM’s own generated responses without access to true rewards, i.e.,
1106   gold labels, and without optimizing for the downstream evaluation metric.
1107
       LAS E R with different “kinds” of RMs. In Sec. 4.2, we show that LAS E R can choose from a
1108   set of candidate RMs, and our analysis in Fig. 3 highlights the fact that LAS E R is robust to noisy
1109   RMs. In Appendix C, we show that LAS E R can also be used with metric-based rewards (Golovneva
1110   et al., 2022). These experiments reflect a conceptual split between the generator (the LLM) and the
1111   scorer (the RM or metric). Thus, LAS E R is applicable to other settings that follow this paradigm,
1112   e.g., using an LLM-as-a-judge (Zheng et al., 2023), where LAS E R could be used to choose between
1113   different judge models, prompts, or different combinations of RMs and metrics. However, consistent
1114   with the “self-preference” bias of LLMs (Panickssery et al., 2024), we caution that using an RM
1115   that is based on the same model as the LLM used for generating responses could lead to the MAB
1116
       spuriously favoring certain RMs. We leave further study on extending LAS E R to future work.
1117   Quality of RMs used with LAS E R. Methods that rely on RMs for scoring generally assume that
1118   these RMs have a strong correlation with human judgments. LAS E R tempers this assumption in
1119   a number of ways: first, by ensembling multiple RMs, LAS E R weakens the effect of noisy RMs;
1120   this can be seen in Fig. 3, where LAS E R mitigates the negative impact of a noisy RM even as the
1121   level of noise is increased. Moreover, the fact that LAS E R can select RMs at an instance-level
1122   means that there need not be a single RM that always correlates well across all instances. However,
1123   LAS E R does require at least one RM to be positively correlated with human judgments on each
1124   instance. If this assumption is not met (i.e., all RMs are poorly-correlated across all instances), then
1125
       optimizing for the RMs will yield poor results. Note that this holds true for any method optimizing
       for RMs. Because LAS E R selects from multiple RMs, its contributions are complementary to
1126
       developments in RMs, which can easily be integrated into LAS E R, as well as improvements to
1127
       preference optimization loss functions (see Appendix C). Such RM improvements are likely to be
1128   necessary as LLMs are deployed in domains that are out of scope for existing systems and domains
1129   with heterogeneous requirements (e.g., our generation domains in Sec. 4.2). In these cases, there
1130   will be no single “perfect” existing RM, and successful solutions will likely involve mixing multiple
1131   RMs. A core benefit of LAS E R is its the ability to automatically filter RMs; in Fig. 6 we see
1132   that utilization differs across domains. This allows users to avoid expensive experimentation with
1133   subsets of RMs: they can simply offload this task to LAS E R, which will automatically select the
       more useful RM(s).
