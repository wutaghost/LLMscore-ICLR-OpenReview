009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Accurate interpretation of Electrocardiogram (ECG) signals is pivotal for diag-
012            nosing cardiovascular diseases. Integrating ECG signals with their accompanying
013            textual reports holds immense potential to enhance clinical diagnostics through
014            the combination of physiological data and qualitative insights. However, this in-
015            tegration faces significant challenges due to inherent modality disparities and the
016            scarcity of labeled data for robust cross-modal learning. To address these ob-
017
               stacles, we propose C-MELT, a novel framework that pre-trains ECG and text
               data using a contrastive masked auto-encoder architecture. C-MELT uniquely
018
               combines the strengths of generative with enhanced discriminative capabilities to
019
               achieve robust cross-modal representations. This is accomplished through masked
020            modality modeling, specialized loss functions, and an improved negative sampling
021            strategy tailored for cross-modal alignment. Extensive experiments on five public
022            datasets across diverse downstream tasks demonstrate that C-MELT significantly
023            outperforms existing methods, achieving an average AUC improvement of 15% in
024            linear probing with only one percent of training data and 2% in zero-shot perfor-
025            mance without requiring training data over state-of-the-art models. These results
026            highlight the effectiveness of C-MELT, underscoring its potential to advance au-
027            tomated clinical diagnostics through multi-modal representations.
028
029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Electrocardiograms (ECGs), obtained through non-invasive electrode placement, provide a critical
032   window into the heart’s electrical activity by measuring voltage differences across specific anatomi-
033   cal regions. The standard 12-lead ECG, which captures unique electrical potential differences from
034   each lead, plays a vital role in diagnosing a wide spectrum of cardiac conditions, like arrhyth-
035   mias. In recent years, significant progress has been made in leveraging deep learning techniques
036   for automated ECG interpretation (Yan et al., 2019; Ebrahimi et al., 2020; Siontis et al., 2021).
037   However, these supervised deep learning approaches often necessitate large volumes of expertly an-
038   notated data, which are frequently scarce and expensive to acquire. Self-supervised learning (SSL)
039
      has emerged as a compelling alternative, offering the potential to learn robust representations from
      abundant unlabeled ECG data. These learned representations can be effectively utilized for zero-shot
040
      learning on novel tasks and adapted via fine-tuning to specific downstream applications, thereby mit-
041
      igating the reliance on extensive labeled datasets.
042
043   Numerous studies have explored the potential of SSL in the ECG domain, demonstrating its effi-
044   cacy in learning representations from vast quantities of unlabeled data. These efforts generally fall
045
      into two main tracks: contrastive and generative approaches. Contrastive methods, exemplified by
      works such as (Chen et al., 2020; 2021; Chen & He, 2021; Grill et al., 2020; Kiyasseh et al., 2021;
046
      Oh et al., 2022; McKeen et al., 2024), aim to learn discriminative representations by maximizing
047
      the similarity between positive pairs (e.g., different augmentations of the same ECG signal) and
048   minimizing the similarity between negative pairs (e.g., ECGs from different patients) within the em-
049   bedding space. Conversely, generative approaches (Hu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022a; 2023) focus
050   on reconstructing the input data, typically by predicting masked or missing segments of the ECG
051   signal, thereby learning to capture the underlying data distribution. Therefore, integrating both con-
052   trastive and generative approaches within a unified framework could leverage their complementary
053   strengths, leading to a more powerful method for learning robust representations (Kim et al., 2021;
      Li et al., 2022b; Song et al., 2024).

054
      Despite advancements, existing ECG-based SSL approaches have largely overlooked the valuable
055   information embedded within clinical text reports, which offer key insights into underlying cardiac
056   conditions and have the potential to significantly enhance a model’s diagnostic accuracy (Zhang
057   et al., 2022c; Chen et al., 2022). This oversight highlights a critical gap in the field: the lack of
058   emphasis on jointly learning ECG-text cross-modal representations. While some recent efforts (Liu
059   et al., 2024; Lalam et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024) have attempted to bridge this gap by integrating ECG
060   signals and clinical reports through cross-modal contrastive learning, the potential of learning unified
061   representations that capture the intricate interplay between ECG signals and their corresponding
062   textual descriptions shown in generative approaches remains largely unexplored. Moreover, the
063
      prevailing reliance on these contrastive methods presents inherent limitations. They depend on the
      availability of negative samples and often struggle to capture cross-modal relationships effectively
064
      due to difficulties in defining appropriate negative pairings across different modalities.
065
066   In this work, we depart from the reliance on either solely contrastive learning or stand-alone gen-
067   erative approaches for cross-modal representation learning. We introduce C-MELT, a novel hybrid
068   framework that synergistically integrates both learning paradigms to effectively capture fine-grained
069
      input details and discriminative ECG-text features. Our approach employs a transformer-based en-
      coder specifically for ECG signals and a well-pre-trained language model for clinical text encoder
070
      in a masked auto-encoder architecture, together with tailored loss functions that promote the joint
071
      learning of robust cross-modal representations. Additionally, we introduce a nearest-neighbor neg-
072   ative sampling strategy, a crucial refinement often overlooked in previous methods, to ensure that
073   negative samples are contextually selected and thereby, enhance the discriminative capability of the
074   learned representations. To rigorously evaluate the efficacy of C-MELT, we conduct extensive exper-
075   iments on various public ECG datasets and demonstrate that our method significantly outperforms
076   recent state-of-the-art baselines across all evaluation settings and datasets.

082   ECG Self-supervised Learning. Self-supervised learning (SSL) has been shown to work effec-
083   tively across various modalities, including vision (Li et al., 2022a; Han et al., 2021), language (De-
084   vlin, 2018; He et al., 2020; Chung et al., 2024), and time-series data (Tonekaboni et al., 2021; Zhang
085   et al., 2022b; Saeed et al., 2019). Particularly, recent advances in applying SSL to ECG signals have
086   demonstrated that models can learn meaningful representations from large amounts of unlabeled
087
      data, which is crucial in medical domains where labeled datasets are often limited and expensive to
      acquire. Here, we mainly discuss two common SSL approaches: generative and contrastive, which
088
      have seen notable progress in ECG representation learning in recent years.
089
090   Early contrastive methods such as SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020), MoCo (Chen et al., 2021), Sim-
091   Siam (Chen & He, 2021), and BYOL (Grill et al., 2020) introduced the concept of maximizing
092   agreement between augmented views of the same data sample by employing augmentation strategies
093
      to create challenging positive and negative pairs. In the context of ECG signals, recent approaches
      like 3KG (Gopal et al., 2021) apply physiologically inspired spatial and temporal augmentations, us-
094
      ing vectorcardiogram (VCG) transformations to capture the three-dimensional spatiotemporal char-
095
      acteristics of the heart’s electrical activity. Similarly, CLOCS (Kiyasseh et al., 2021) developed
096   Contrastive Multi-Segment Coding (CMSC), which enhances the model’s ability to handle varying
097   ECG signal characteristics across different axes—space, time, and patients. Building on this, (Oh
098   et al., 2022) incorporates Wav2vec 2.0 (Baevski et al., 2020), CMSC, and random lead masking
099   to simulate different global and local lead configurations during training, thereby improving model
100   robustness and achieving impressive results on ECG downstream tasks.
101
      On the other hand, generative approaches (Hu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022a; Na et al., 2024) are
102   less prevalent, but play a crucial role in ECG SSL. These methods focus on capturing the underly-
103   ing structure of the data by training auto-encoder models to generate or reconstruct masked input
104   data, enabling the model to understand and represent key features and patterns. For instance, ST-
105   MEM (Na et al., 2024) utilizes a masked auto-encoder with a spatio-temporal patchifying technique
106   to model relationships in 12-lead ECG signals. Additionally, the Cross-Reconstruction Transformer
107   (CRT) (Zhang et al., 2023) employs frequency-domain and temporal masking to reconstruct missing
      ECG segments, demonstrating the innovative use of generative SSL in ECG analysis.

      ECG-Text Multi-modal Representation Learning. Multi-modal representation learning com-
122
      bines information from different data types, shown to effectively improve model performance (Lin
123
      et al., 2024; Du et al., 2023). Particularly, pioneering works like CLIP-based models (Radford
124   et al., 2021; Rasheed et al., 2023; Zhai et al., 2023) have proven the power of contrastive learning

127   and uni-modal alignment techniques to generalize ECG and text-based medical classification tasks.

132   3      M ETHOD
133
134   We propose C-MELT, a framework designed to learn generalizable cross-modal representations
135   by aligning electrocardiogram (ECG) signals and corresponding medical text reports. C-MELT
136
      leverages masked language modeling (MLM) and masked ECG modeling (MEM) to reconstruct
      randomly masked segments within the input text and ECG signals, respectively. This encourages
137
      the model to learn fine-grained features within each modality. Furthermore, we introduce Siglep
138
      (Sigmoid language ECG pre-training) loss, which is based on SigLIP Zhai et al. (2023), and a
139   nearest-neighbor negative sampling strategy. These directly promote discriminative representation
140   learning and enhance cross-modal alignment, besides the ECG-text matching (ETM) learning task.
141
142
      Figure 1 depicts the overall architecture of C-MELT, which comprises two main branches: an ECG
      encoder and a text encoder. The ECG encoder utilizes a transformer-based architecture (Vaswani
143
      et al., 2023) to process the input ECG signals and generate corresponding representations, denoted
144
      as Hx ∈ RLx ×d , where Lx represents the sequence length of the ECG signal and d represents the
145   embedding dimension. The text encoder utilizes the recent pre-trained Flan-T5 model (Chung et al.,
146   2024) which, to our knowledge, has not been previously applied to this task, to extract high-level se-
147   mantic embeddings from the clinical text, denoted as Ht ∈ RLt ×d , where Lt represents the sequence
148   length of the text. These encoder outputs are then passed through a fusion module, which employs a
149   cross-attention mechanism to integrate information from both modalities, generating fused represen-
150   tations denoted as Hf ∈ R(Lx +Lt )×d . The model subsequently employs three distinct heads: two
151   decoders, responsible for reconstructing the masked portions of the ECG signal (X̂) and text (Tm ),
152   respectively, and a contrastive prediction head for ECG-text matching. Additionally, we introduce
153   two projection heads, gx and gt , following the ECG and text encoders, respectively. These pro-
154   jection heads, along with the Siglep loss, facilitate learning discriminative representation between
155   these modalities. The model is trained by jointly optimizing four loss functions: masked language
156
      modeling loss (LM LM ), masked ECG modeling loss (LM EM ), ECG-text matching loss (LET M ),
      and the Siglep loss (LSiglep ). The subsequent subsections provide a detailed description of each
157
      component within the C-MELT framework.
158
159   3.1       M ULTI -M ODAL MASKED AUTO - ENCODERS .
160
161   ECG Encoder. We implement the ECG encoder (denoted as Fx ) based on a transformer architec-
      ture, which was originally developed for efficiently processing sequential data in parallel (Vaswani

162
      et al., 2023). We first follow (Oh et al., 2022) to apply a masking strategy to the ECG input
163   X ∈ RL×C to encourage robust feature learning, where L is the length of the signal and C is the
164   number of channels. Specifically, we leverage random lead masking as an on-the-fly augmentation
165   where each lead randomly masked with a probability of p = 0.5 during pre-training. Furthermore,
166   we use a dropout layer on the input with p = 0.1 to enable masking modeling. We then pass the
167   masked input into a series of convolutional layers, each followed by GELU activation functions
168   and group normalization. The extracted features are subsequently projected into a 768-dimensional
169   space. Following that, we add a convolutional positional encoding layer to preserve the temporal
170   order of the ECG sequence. Next, we employ eight transformer encoder layers, each including a
171
      multi-head self-attention mechanism that allows the model to attend to different parts of the input
      sequence simultaneously. We conduct an experiment exploring the effects of different numbers of
172
      transformer layers in Section 4.
173
174   Language (Text) Encoder. For our text encoder, we utilize the Flan-T5-base encoder (denoted as
175   Ft ), which outputs 768-dimensional embeddings. The input to the encoder consists of token indices
176   generated by the Flan-T5 tokenizer, represented as T ∈ ZM , where M is the maximum sequence
177
      length. Flan-T5 is an advanced version of the T5 model (Raffel et al., 2023), which has been pre-
      trained on a massive and diverse text dataset covering numerous tasks, such as summarization and
178
      question answering. Note that our text encoder is fine-tuned during the pre-training stage. We also
179
      conduct an ablation with various text encoders in Section 4 to support our choice of Flan-T5.
180
181   Fusion Module. The fusion module begins with linear projections that map the outputs of the
182   ECG and language encoders to a 768-dimensional space. We apply modality-specific embeddings
183
      to the projected features to distinguish between ECG and text data. Importantly, we employ cross-
      attention to integrate the ECG and textual information, allowing each modality to inform the other
184
      by learning the relevant features. This cross-attention mechanism is crucial as it enables the model
185
      to leverage the complementary strengths of both ECG and text data more effectively.
186
187   Decoders and Loss Functions. After the fusion module, three distinct network heads are intro-
188   duced, each associated with a specific loss function: masked language modeling (MLM), masked
189
      ECG modeling (MEM), and ECG-text matching (ETM). MLM and MEM are designed for recon-
      struction tasks, while ETM adopts a contrastive learning approach to align the different modalities.
190

      Siglep Loss Function. In multi-modal masked auto-encoder architectures such as (Chen et al.,
235
      2022), contrastive learning’s effectiveness can be limited by the inherent tension between the
236   reconstruction-focused generative tasks of autoencoders and the discriminative nature of contrastive
237   learning. They are more biased for learning to reconstruct masked inputs in generative manners.
238   This can hinder the model’s capability to learn discriminative features useful for downstream tasks,
239   such as zero-shot inference or linear probing. Furthermore, although the ETM loss in such archi-
240   tectures can serve as a form of contrastive loss, it may not be sufficient for building a robust ECG
241   encoder. Specifically, the ETM module is primarily designed for binary classification based on
242   fused features rather than directly enhancing the discriminative power of individual encoders. This
243   limitation can restrict the model’s ability to produce high-quality multimodal embeddings.
244   Therefore, we propose strengthening contrastive learning in multi-modal masked auto-encoder ar-
245   chitectures using Siglep loss function. Specifically, we adapt the SigLIP implementation Zhai et al.
246   (2023), originally proposed for text-image pairs, to the text-ECG domain (Formula 4). This ap-
247   proach avoids the costly global normalization of softmax-based contrastive losses by operating in-
248   dependently on each ECG-text pair, improving memory efficiency and scalability. We introduce two
249   additional network heads to the ECG and text encoders, respectively. Each head consists of a pool-
250
      ing layer, a Tanh activation function, and a dense layer, enabling them to output 768-dimensional
      embeddings (denoted as x′ i ∈ R768 for the ith ECG sample and t′ j ∈ R768 for the j th text report).

257   Nearest-neighbor-based Negative Sampling (N3S). In contrastive learning, the selection of neg-
258   ative samples significantly impacts the training process (Xu et al., 2022). Conventional methods
259   often employ random sampling, where negative text reports are chosen randomly to replace positive
260   texts. However, this approach may lead to false negative selection, especially in medical datasets,
261   where randomly chosen reports might share substantial similarities with the positive reports, hinder-
262   ing effective contrastive learning. This is discussed more in the Appendix A.2.
263   Therefore, we propose nearest-neighbor negative sampling (N3S), which selects negative samples
264   based on their dissimilarity in the Flan-T5’s feature space, ensuring they are sufficiently distinct
265   from the positive samples while remaining semantically related to the domain. Specifically, we first
266   utilize pre-trained Flan-T5 (small) to generate vector representations, denoted as vt ∈ R512 , for
267   each text report t in the training dataset Dtrain . These embeddings capture the semantic meaning
268   of the reports. During training, for a given ECG and its corresponding positive text report (xk , t+ k)
269   in half of the training batch B, the negative report t−

300   4.1.1      P RE - TRAINING TASK .
301
      Pre-train Dataset. In the pre-training stage, we utilize the MIMIC-IV-ECG v1.0 database (Gow
302   et al., 2023), which includes 800,035 paired samples derived from 161,352 unique subjects. This
303   dataset contains numerous 10-second ECG recordings sampled at 500 Hz and the corresponding text
304   reports. Each ECG recording will have several reports, and we simply merge them into one single
305   report (diagnosis). We apply some necessary processing steps to prepare the custom dataset for
306   training (e.g., remove empty or containing NaN ECG recordings and clean text by using lowercase,
307   strip, and punctuation removal), which eventually yields a training size of 779891 samples. We
308   provide representative examples of ECG-text pairs in Appendix A.1.
309   Experimental Configurations. Our proposed model is developed based on the fairseq-signals *
310   framework in our work. We select the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 5 × 10−5 and use a
311   tri-stage scheduler with ratios of 0.1, 0.4, and 0.5 for learning rate adjustments. The optimizer is
312   configured with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.98, an epsilon value of 1 × 10−6 , and a weight decay of 0.01. We
313   pre-train the proposed model for 300000 steps, maintaining a batch size of 128. The quantitative
314   experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA H100-80GB GPU.

      5    C ONCLUSION
479
480   We propose C-MELT to pre-train a model on ECG signals and corresponding texts, utilizing a
481   novel contrastive masked transformer-based architecture. Our approach is generative self-supervised
482   learning, enhanced with Siglep loss, and nearest-neighbor negative sampling to support contrastive
483   aspects. Experimental results demonstrate that our method outperforms previous approaches across
484   multiple datasets and on a range of downstream tasks, including under full fine-tuning, linear prob-
485   ing, and zero-shot classification. C-MELT shows promise in advancing ECG-based diagnostic mod-
      els, paving the way for more accurate, efficient, and personalized cardiac care.

740   A.2   C ONTRASTIVE LEARNING DISCUSSION .
741
742   Why Using ETM Only Is Not A True Way To Zero-shot Learning. As mentioned in the Method
743   section, ETM functions as a contrastive learning technique in the masked auto-encoder architecture.
744   However, it heavily relies on binary classification tasks with explicit ECG-text pairs to learn cross-
745   modal correspondences. It is not designed for zero-shot learning which strongly requires the model
746   to generalize to unseen tasks or classes without the need for such supervised pairings or fused infor-
747   mation during training. This motivates us to use Siglep, boosting the model’s zero-shot ability.
748
      Why N3S Can Enhance The Performance. In medical datasets, particularly the MIMIC-IV ECG
749
      dataset (Gow et al., 2023), we observe a significant amount of duplicate or highly similar text sam-
750   ples: among nearly 800,000 records, only approximately 180,000 are unique. For instance, over
751   100,000 samples share an identical text report, which is ”sinus rhythm normal ecg”. Randomly
752   selecting negative samples for contrastive loss training is not a suitable approach in this scenario.
753   Therefore, we propose using the N3S technique to more effectively differentiate between similar
754   and dissimilar samples, improving contrastive learning by selecting more meaningful negatives.
755   Notably, we observe that during training, the ETM accuracy without N3S stagnates around 75%
      while with N3S, it exceeds 96%, demonstrating the significant impact of this approach.

809   Why Using LLMs But Not As MERL. In zero-shot learning, models typically rely on category
      names alone to make predictions. However, by incorporating Large Language Models (LLMs), we

810
      can enhance the context by generating richer, clinically relevant descriptions of the categories, as
811   discussed in MERL (Liu et al., 2024). However, we observe two main drawbacks in their enhanced
812   text reports, as shown in Figure 6: 1) MERL’s performance heavily depends on their sub-types and
813   attributes searching prompt and additional database. This leads to a limitation when testing detailed
814   analysis with labels that are different sub-types themselves. Moreover, this also raises suspicion
815   about the performance when new tasks require labels that are not able to search sub-types and
816   attributes in the database; 2) Following that point, MERL’s enhanced texts might be uncontrollable to
817   the outputs where the LLMs provide wrong sub-types or unnecessary context. For example, ”Atrial
818   Fibrillation” is already in ”AFIB” type but shown to misleadingly be in ”AF”-”Atrial Flutter”.
819   How Our Work Leverages LLM’s Strength. We address these points using a straightforward
820   prompt strategy with explicit instructions. Specifically, we employ a prompt: ”You are an experi-
821   enced cardiologist. For a given clinical term such as ’normal ECG’, your job is to describe each
822   term clinically and apply your medical domain knowledge to include other relevant explanations
823   that will help a text encoder like Flan-T5 fully understand medical concepts. Do not include any
824   recommendations in the description.” This makes the LLM generate clinically accurate and more
825   focused explainable descriptions, enhancing the text encoding without introducing irrelevant or re-
826
      dundant information. For example, with the code ”AFIB”, our prompt on GPT-4o can output: ”Atrial
      Fibrillation (AFIB). Irregular and often rapid heart rate due to uncoordinated atrial activity.”.
