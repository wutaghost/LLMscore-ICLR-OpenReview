009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Full Waveform Inversion (FWI) is a vital technique for reconstructing high-
012            resolution subsurface velocity maps from seismic waveform data, governed by
013            partial differential equations (PDEs) that model wave propagation. Traditional
014            machine learning approaches typically map seismic data to velocity maps by en-
015            coding seismic waveforms into latent embeddings and decoding them into veloc-
016            ity maps. In this paper, we introduce a novel framework that reframes FWI as a
017            joint diffusion process in a shared latent space, bridging seismic waveform data
018
               and velocity maps. Our approach has two key components: first, we merge the
               bottlenecks of two separate autoencoders—one for seismic data and one for ve-
019
               locity maps—into a unified latent space using VQ to establish a shared codebook.
020
               Second, we train a diffusion model in this latent space, enabling the simultane-
021            ous generation of seismic and velocity map pairs by sampling and denoising the
022            latent representations, followed by decoding each modality with its respective de-
023            coder. Remarkably, our jointly generated seismic-velocity pairs approximately
024            satisfy the governing PDE without any additional constraint, offering a new geo-
025            metric interpretation of FWI. The diffusion process learns to score the latent space
026            according to its deviation from the PDE, with higher scores representing smaller
027            deviations from the true solutions. By following this diffusion process, the model
028            traces a path from random initialization to a valid solution of the governing PDE.
029
               Our experiments on the OpenFWI dataset demonstrate that the generated seismic
               and velocity map pairs not only exhibit high fidelity and diversity but also adhere
030
               to the physical constraints imposed by the governing PDE.

053   Subsurface imaging is crucial for addressing many scientific and industrial challenges, such as un-
      derstanding global earthquakes (Virieux et al., 2017; Tromp, 2020), monitoring greenhouse gas

      storage (Li et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023b), improving ultrasound medical imaging (Guasch et al.,
055   2020; Lozenski et al., 2024), and aiding oil and gas exploration (Virieux & Operto, 2009; Wang &
056   Alkhalifah, 2018). Subsurface structure is typically represented by acoustic wave velocity, which
057   can be inferred from seismic data due to the physical relationship between them governed by a
058   partial differential equation (PDE)—specifically, the acoustic wave equation (Sheriff & Geldart,
059   1995; Shearer, 2019). Full waveform inversion (FWI) is a powerful technique for reconstructing
060   high-resolution subsurface acoustic velocity maps from seismic data. It is framed as a non-linear
061   optimization problem: given seismic data s, the task is to solve for the velocity map v according to
062   the governing wave equation (Tarantola, 1984; Warner & Guasch, 2016).
063   Recently, machine learning-based approaches have been introduced to solve FWI (Wu & Lin, 2019;
064   Sun & Demanet, 2020; Deng et al., 2022; Mousavi & Beroza, 2022). These methods typically use
065   neural networks, particularly encoder-decoder architectures, to directly map seismic data to subsur-
066   face velocity maps, treating the FWI task as an image-to-image translation problem (Richardson,
067   2018; Feng et al., 2021; Jin et al., 2024). A more recent approach introduced generative diffusion
068   models to regularize FWI by generating prior distributions for plausible velocity models, which
069
      guide the inversion process (Wang et al., 2023a). This approach treats FWI as a conditional genera-
      tion problem, i.e. generating velocity maps using seismic data as condition.
070
071   In this paper, we explore a new direction by considering FWI as a joint generative process. In
072   contrast to prior works that treat FWI as a conditional generation problem (where the velocity map
073   is generated for a given seismic waveform), we are curious whether the two modalities—seismic
074   waveform data and velocity map—can be generated simultaneously from a common latent space.
075
      Interestingly, we discovered that not only can these two modalities be jointly generated, but the
      generated seismic data and velocity maps also naturally satisfy the governing PDE without requiring
076
      any additional constraints. To achieve this, we propose a method with two key steps: first, we use a
077
      dual autoencoder architecture where both seismic data and velocity maps are encoded into a shared
078   latent space, capturing the essential relationships between the two modalities. This shared latent
079   space provides a coarse approximation of the wave equation solution space. Second, we apply
080   a diffusion process in their common latent space, progressively refining the latent representations
081   from random initializations. The corresponding decoders then generate seismic data and velocity
082   maps from the refined latent representations. Our experiments on the OpenFWI dataset (Deng et al.,
083   2022) empirically confirm that the jointly generated pairs satisfy the governing PDE.
084   This approach provides a new perspective on solving the governing wave equation. Unlike tra-
085   ditional FWI, which solves for one modality given the other, our method demonstrates that both
086   seismic data and velocity maps can be solved simultaneously. This is achieved through a diffusion
087   process within a shared latent space. Each point in this space corresponds to a seismic-velocity pair
088   (after decoding), though not all points inherently satisfy the governing PDE. The diffusion model,
089   however, learns to score each point based on its deviation from the PDE. By following the iterative
090   denoising process, we trace a path from random initialization (with a lower score) to a valid solution
091   (with a higher score) that satisfies both modalities of the PDE, as shown in Figure 1. With each
092
      denoising step, the deviation from the PDE decreases, leading to a more accurate solution.
093   It’s important to note that our goal is not to push the boundaries of FWI performance but to offer
094   a new perspective by extending FWI from a conditional generation problem to a joint generation
095   problem. We hope that this approach will inspire deeper exploration and understanding within the
096   research community, paving the way for new insights in computational imaging.

127   problem. These methods avoid the need for iterative PDE solvers by training neural networks to map
128   seismic data directly to velocity maps. Approaches like InversionNet (Wu & Lin, 2019) treat the FWI
129   problem as an image-to-image translation task, where convolutional encoder-decoder architectures
130
      are employed to generate velocity maps from seismic data in a single forward pass. This dramatically
      reduces computational costs compared to traditional methods.
131
132   However, while these machine learning-based models provide computational efficiency, they lack
133   the physical grounding of traditional methods and often consider FWI as a conditional generation
134   problem. As a result, they do not guarantee that the generated velocity maps satisfy the governing
135   wave equation, potentially leading to physically inconsistent solutions.

137   3     WAVE D IFFUSION : O UR M ETHOD
138
139   In this section, we introduce WAVE D IFFUSION, an extension of FWI that moves beyond solving for
140   velocity given seismic data, enabling the simultaneous solution of both modalities. Our approach
141   involves two key steps: (1) a dual autoencoder architecture with vector quantization (VQ) that maps
142   seismic data and velocity maps into a common latent space, and (2) a diffusion model applied in this
143   common latent space, followed by two decoders that generate both modalities simultaneously. This
144
      method provides a new perspective on addressing the underlying PDE.
145
146   3.1   M OTIVATION FOR J OINT G ENERATION
147
      Existing machine learning-based approaches typically focus on translating one modality (seismic
148   data) into another (velocity map) using encoder-decoder architectures. Mathematically, these meth-
149   ods provide only a partial solution to the governing PDE, as they rely on the availability of one
150   modality. In contrast, our method tackles the more challenging problem: can both modalities be
151   solved simultaneously?
152
      Inspired by the success of diffusion models in generating multimodal outputs, such as images, audio,
153
      and video, we demonstrate that the joint distribution of seismic data and velocity maps can be mod-
154   eled using a diffusion framework. This allows the two modalities to be generated simultaneously.
155   Following the structure of latent diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022), our approach involves
156   two main steps: (1) a dual autoencoder with vector quantization and (2) a joint diffusion process for
157   refining the latent representations.
158
159   3.2   P REPARING A S HARED L ATENT S PACE WITH A D UAL AUTOENCODER
160

      a shared latent space as a preparatory step for the joint diffusion process. As illustrated in Figure 3,

192   3.3   J OINT D IFFUSION P ROCESS
193
      Latent Diffusion: The joint diffusion process refines the coarse approximations produced by the
194
      autoencoder model by operating on the shared latent space z of the two modalities. Gaussian noise
195
      is added during the forward process, progressively perturbing the latent vector. During the backward
196   process, the noise is removed, guiding the model toward physically valid solutions (Figure 4). This
197   iterative refinement ensures that the generated seismic-velocity pairs satisfy the wave equation.
198
199         1. Forward Process: Gaussian noise is added to the latent vector z, creating a noisy repre-
200            sentation: zt = z + ϵt , where ϵt is the noise applied at step t.
201         2. Backward Process: The noisy latent vector is progressively denoised through zt−1 =
202            L(zt ). Each backward step removes noise and refines the latent vector toward a valid
203            solution that satisfies the wave equation.
204
205   Sampling New Solutions: After learning the backward denoising steps, new seismic-velocity pairs
206   can be generated that satisfy the wave equation by sampling latent vectors from a Gaussian distribu-
207   tion and refining them using the learned backward steps.
208
            1. Sample a latent vector zt from a standard Gaussian distribution: zt ∼ N (0, I).
209
210         2. Pass zt through the backward denoising steps: zt−1 = L(zt ),         zt−2 = L(zt−1 ), . . . , z0 .
211         3. Decode z0 back into seismic data and velocity maps: x̂s = Ds (z0 ), x̂v = Dv (z0 ).

474   5.1   T RADITIONAL P HYSICS - BASED FWI
475
476   Traditional FWI methods aim to reconstruct subsurface velocity models by iteratively minimizing
477
      the difference between observed and simulated seismic data, typically using gradient-based opti-
      mization methods. The key challenge lies in solving the wave equation, which governs wave prop-
478
      agation through the Earth. While effective, these methods are computationally expensive and sensi-
479
      tive to factors such as the quality of the initial velocity model, noise in the data, and cycle-skipping
480   issues—where the inversion algorithm converges to incorrect solutions due to poor starting models
481   or insufficient low-frequency data (Tarantola, 1984; Virieux & Operto, 2009). Techniques such as
482   adaptive waveform inversion (Warner & Guasch, 2016) and multiscale FWI (Bunks et al., 1995)
483   have been developed to reduce the risk of cycle-skipping and improve convergence by progressively
484   introducing higher-frequency data. These techniques remain the treatment of FWI as a conditional
485   generation problem using physical equations as the engine (Virieux et al., 2017; Warner & Guasch,
      2016).


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      5.2   DATA -D RIVEN A PPROACHES TO FWI
487
488   In recent years, machine learning approaches have been increasingly explored for FWI. Convolu-
489   tional Neural Networks (CNNs) have shown promise in learning image-to-image mappings from
490   seismic data to velocity models, bypassing the need for iterative solvers. Encoder-decoder archi-
491   tectures, such as those used in InversionNet (Wu & Lin, 2019) and VelocityGAN (Zhang et al.,
492   2019), have demonstrated the ability to predict velocity maps from seismic data while reduces com-
493   putational costs by learning implicit relationships between the two modalities. Richardson’s work
494
      (Richardson, 2018) further illustrated that deep learning models could predict velocity models effi-
      ciently. However, these approaches are still treating the FWI problem as an image-to-image trans-
495
      lation task or a conditional generation problem (Zhu et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2023a). Recent work
496
      on neural operators (Li et al., 2020; 2023) offers a more flexible approach by learning operators that
497   map between the two modalities in a revered direction, i.e. predicting seismic data given velocity
498   maps. Though these neural operator methods have shown powerful capacity in mapping the two
499   modalities, they are still constrained by their image-to-image mappings setup and cannot solve for
500   multiple modalities simultaneously that satisfy the governing PDE.
501
502   5.3   G ENERATIVE M ODELS IN FWI
503
504   Generative models, particularly Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) and their variants, have
505   emerged as alternatives to traditional CNN-based methods for FWI. These models aim to learn
506
      the latent representations of seismic data and velocity models, enabling the generation of synthetic
      training data or even direct inversion (Goodfellow et al., 2020). Vector Quantized GANs (VQ-
507
      GANs) (Esser et al., 2021), in particular, have been explored for their ability to generate high-quality
508
      modalities, such as images, audios, videos, etc. Such models can be tuned for imaging one physical
509   modality (e.g. velocity map) given another (e.g. seismic data) (Zhang et al., 2019).
510
511   Recent work has focused on Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) (Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol,
512
      2021; Rombach et al., 2022), which refine latent space representations through a diffusion process.
      LDMs iteratively denoise latent variables, progressively improving the quality of generated samples.
513
      While these models can produce realistic-looking data, they often generate new samples of one
514
      single modality at a time. Thus, it is difficult for them to generate multiple modalities using one
515   generative model as they lack the physical consistency to the governing PDEs that describe the
516   relationship between these modalities. Diffusion models have been applied to FWI by Wang et al.
517   (Wang et al., 2023a), who used them to generate prior distributions for plausible velocity models as
518   a regularization term. Their method still treats seismic data and velocity maps separately, limiting
519   its ability to generate physically consistent seismic-velocity pairs.
520
521
      6     C ONCLUSION
522
523
      In this paper, we introduced WAVE D IFFUSION, a novel framework that redefines FWI as a joint gen-
524   erative process for seismic-velocity pairs within a shared latent space. Unlike existing approaches
525   that either solve for one modality given the other or treat FWI as a conditional generation problem,
526   our method simultaneously solves for both modalities. This is achieved through a joint diffusion
527   process on a shared latent space, established via a dual autoencoder, which progressively refines
528   latent representations to satisfy the governing PDE. By tracing a path from random initializations to
529   valid PDE solutions, WAVE D IFFUSION offers a new geometric perspective on the FWI problem.
530   Experiments on the OpenFWI dataset validate the effectiveness of WAVE D IFFUSION, demonstrating
531   its ability to generate high-quality seismic-velocity pairs that exhibit strong fidelity and adhere to
532   the physical constraints of the wave equation. Moreover, our framework can be directly applied to
533   conventional FWI tasks by using a one-in-two-out configuration, where only seismic data is input
534   to the encoder. Additionally, training an existing FWI model, such as InversionNet, on WAVE D IF -
535   FUSION -generated samples improves performance, particularly in scenarios with limited real data
536   availability. These results highlight the versatility and practicality of WAVE D IFFUSION in advancing
537   FWI research.

659   blocks are set to [1, 2, 2, 4, 4] for velocity maps and [1, 2, 2, 4, 4, 4, 4, 8, 8] for seismic data. The
660   resolution for velocity maps is 64, while for seismic data it is [1024, 64]. The latent dimension z is
661
      [16, 16], and the number of residual blocks is set to 3. The model was trained with a base learning
      rate of 4.5 × 10−4 . It uses an embedding dimension of 32 and an embedding codebook size of 8192.
662
      The autoencoder employs a perceptual loss combined with a discriminator. The discriminator starts
663
      training at step 50001 with a discriminator weight of 0.5 and a perceptual weight of 0.5.

665
      A.1.2    J OINT D IFFUSION M ODEL
666
667   The Joint Diffusion model is based on the LatentDiffusion architecture. The backbone net-
668   work in the Joint Diffusion model is a UNet-based architecture. The UNet takes 32 input and output
669   channels, and the model channels are set to 128. The attention resolutions are [1, 2, 4, 4], corre-
670   sponding to spatial resolutions of 32, 16, 8, and 4. The model uses 2 residual blocks and channel
671   multipliers of [1, 2, 2, 4, 4]. It also employs 8 attention heads with scale-shift normalization enabled
672   and residual blocks that support upsampling and downsampling. The model is trained with a base
673   learning rate of 5.0 × 10−5 and uses 1000 diffusion timesteps. The loss function applied is L1 .
674
      The diffusion process is configured with a linear noise schedule, starting from 0.0015 and ending at
      0.0155.

676   A LambdaLinearScheduler is used to control the learning rate, with 10000 warmup steps. The initial
677   learning rate is set to 1.0 × 10−6 , which increases to a maximum of 1.0 over the course of training.

       A.8   P ERFORMANCE C OMPARISON OF FWI WITH N OISY S EISMIC DATA
993
       This experiment evaluates the robustness of WAVE D IFFUSION in inverting noisy seismic data com-
994    pared to InversionNet and VelocityGAN. By introducing Gaussian noise (mean=0, std=0.05) to clean
995    seismic data, we simulate a realistic scenario that reflects challenges in field data acquisition.
996
997
       Our WAVE D IFFUSION model was tasked with recovering both velocity maps and noise-free seismic
       data from the noisy seismic inputs. In contrast, InversionNet and VelocityGAN were tested only on
998
       recovering velocity maps. Importantly, none of the models were trained on noisy data, making this
999
       a stringent test of robustness.
1000
1001   The results in Table 8 and Figure 18 highlight the significant advantage of WAVE D IFFUSION over the
1002   baselines. WAVE D IFFUSION achieves the lowest MAE and RMSE and the highest SSIM, demon-
1003
       strating its ability to effectively handle noisy data.
1004
1005   Table 8: Performance Metrics for Noisy Seismic Data Inversion. Evaluation based on Gaussian
1006   noisy seismic data as input and velocity maps as output on the CVB dataset.
1007                           Model                    MAE RMSE SSIM
1008                           InversionNet             0.9069 1.0701 0.2604
1009                           VelocityGAN              0.4913 0.6916 0.3231
1010                           WaveDiffusion (Ours) 0.2227 0.3776 0.6142
1011
1012   Despite being trained only on clean data, WAVE D IFFUSION demonstrates exceptional robustness
1013   to noisy inputs. Its joint diffusion process effectively denoises the seismic data during inversion,
1014   recovering high-fidelity velocity maps and seismic data that align with the physical constraints of
1015   the wave equation.
1016   In contrast, both InversionNet and VelocityGAN fail to handle noisy seismic data, as evidenced
1017   by significantly higher errors (MAE and RMSE) and lower structural similarity (SSIM). This lim-
1018   itation stems from their image-to-image mapping architectures, which lack an inherent denoising
1019   mechanism.
1020   These results underline the practical utility of WAVE D IFFUSION in real-world scenarios where data
1021   noise is inevitable. By refining noisy latent representations through diffusion, our model not only
1022   recovers accurate velocity maps but also reconstructs noise-free seismic data, setting it apart from
1023   traditional mapping-based methods.
