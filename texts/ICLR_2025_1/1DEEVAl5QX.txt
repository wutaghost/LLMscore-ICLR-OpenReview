                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010       We present the first mini-batch algorithm for maximizing a non-negative monotone decompos-
                                           PN
011       able submodular function, F = i=1 f i , under a set of constraints. We consider two sampling
012       approaches: uniform and weighted. We first show that mini-batch with weighted sampling
013       improves over the state of the art sparsifier based approach both in theory and in practice.
014
          Surprisingly, our experimental results show that uniform sampling is superior to weighted
          sampling. However, it is impossible to explain this using worst-case analysis. Our main
015
          contribution is using smoothed analysis to provide a theoretical foundation for our experimental
016
          results. We show that, under very mild assumptions, uniform sampling is superior for both the
017       mini-batch and the sparsifier approaches. We empirically verify that these assumptions hold for
018       our datasets. Uniform sampling is simple to implement and has complexity independent of N ,
019       making it the perfect candidate to tackle massive real-world datasets.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Submodular functions capture the natural property of diminishing returns which often arises in
026   machine learning, graph theory and economics. For example, imagine you are given a large set of
027   images, and your goal is to extract a small subset of images, that best represent the original set (e.g.,
028   creating thumbnails for a Youtube video). Intuitively, this is a submodular optimization problem, as
029
      the more thumbnails we have, the less we gain by adding an additional thumbnail.
030   Formally, a set function F : 2E → R+ , on a ground set E, is submodular if for any subsets
031   S ⊆ T ⊆ E and e ∈ E \ T , it holds that F (S + e) − F (S) ≥ F (T + e) − F (T ).
032
      Decomposable submodular functions In many natural scenarios F is decomposable: F =

035   f i is represented by an evaluation oracle that when queried with S ⊆ E returns the value f i (S). For
036   ease of notation we assume that f i (∅) = 0 (our results hold even if this not the case). Our  goal is to
037   maximize F under some set of constraints while minimizing the number of oracle calls to f i . An
038   excellent survey of the importance of decomposable functions is given in (Rafiey & Yoshida, 2022),
039   which we summarize below.
040
      Decomposable submodular functions are prevalent in both machine learning and economics. In
041
      economics, they play a pivotal role in welfare optimization during combinatorial auctions (Dobzinski
042   & Schapira, 2006; Feige, 2009; Feige & Vondrák, 2006; Papadimitriou et al., 2008; Vondrák, 2008).
043   In machine learning, these functions are instrumental in tasks like data summarization, aiming to
044   select a concise yet representative subset of elements. Their utility spans various domains, from
045   exemplar-based clustering by (Dueck & Frey, 2007) to image summarization (Tschiatschek et al.,
046   2014), recommender systems (Parambath et al., 2016) and document summarization (Lin & Bilmes,
047   2011). The optimization of these functions, especially under specific constraints (e.g., cardinality,
048   matroid) has been studied in various data summarization settings (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2016a;b;c)
049   and differential privacy (Chaturvedi et al., 2021; Mitrovic et al., 2017; Rafiey & Yoshida, 2020). In
050
      many of the above applications N (the number of underlying submodular functions) is extremely
      large, making the evaluation of F prohibitively slow. We illustrate this with a simple example.
051
052   Example (Welfare maximization) Imagine you are tasked with deciding on a meal menu for a
053   large group of N people (e.g., all students in a university, all high school students in a country). You
      need to choose k ingredients to use from a predetermined set (chicken, fish, beef, etc.) of size n.

      steps: First, identify the element e′ not already in Sj that maximizes the function FSj (e). Add this
065
      element e′ to set Sj to form the new set Sj+1 . After completing all iterations, return the set Sk+1 .

067   When F is decomposable, and each evaluation of f i is counted as an oracle call, the above algorithm
068   requires O(N nk) oracle calls. This can be prohibitively expensive if N ≫ n. Looking back at our
069   example, Greedy will require asking every student k questions. That is, we would start by asking
070   all students: “Rate how much you would like to see food x on the menu” for n possible options. We
071
      would then need to wait for all of their replies, and continue with “Given that chicken is on the menu,
      rate how much you would like to see food y on the menu” for n − 1 possible options and so on. Even
072
      if we do an online poll, this is still very time-consuming (as we must wait for everyone to reply in
073
      each of the k steps). If we could sample a representative subset of the students, we could greatly
074   speed up the above process. Specifically, we would like to eliminate the dependence on N .

      Weighted mini-batch Mini-batch methods are at the heart of several fundamental machine learning
      algorithms (e.g., mini-batch k-means, SGD). Surprisingly, a mini-batch approach for this problem
086
      was not considered. We present the first mini-batch algorithm for this problem, and show that it is
087
      superior to the sparsifier based approach both in theory and in practice. Roughly speaking, we show
088   that sampling a new batch every iteration of the algorithm is “more stable” compared to the sparsifier
089   approach. This allows us to sample less elements in total and reduce the overall complexity. The
090   main novelty in our analysis is using the greedy nature of the algorithm and carefully balancing an
091   additive and a multiplicative error term. Our sampling probabilities are the same as those of (Kenneth
092   & Krauthgamer, 2023), and therefore, this algorithm pays the expensive O(N n) preprocessing time.
093
      Beyond worst-case analysis When conducting our experiments, we added a simple baseline for
094   both the sparsifier and the mini-batch algorithm – instead of using weighted sampling we used uniform
095   sampling (previous results neglected this baseline (Rafiey & Yoshida, 2022)). Surprisingly, we observe
096   that it outperforms weighted sampling both for the sparsifier and mini-batch algorithms. This is
097   remarkable, as uniform sampling is extremely simple to implement, and requires no preprocessing
098   which removes the dependence on N altogether.
099
      Unfortunately, we cannot get worst-case theoretical guarantees for uniform sampling. Consider the
100   case where only a single f j takes non-zero values (all other f i ’s are always 0). Clearly, uniform
101   sampling will almost surely miss f j . However, this is a pathological case that is very unlikely to
102   occur in practice. To bridge this gap we go beyond worst-case analysis and consider the smoothed
103   complexity of this problem.

144   1.1   T HE MINI - BATCH ALGORITHM
145
146   We focus on the greedy algorithm for constrained submodular maximization. We show that instead of
147   sparsifying F , better results can be achieved by using mini-batches during the execution of the greedy
148   algorithm. That is, rather than sampling a large sparsifier F̂ and performing the optimization process,
149   we show that if we sample a much smaller sparsifier (a mini-batch), F̂ j , for the j-th step of the greedy
150   algorithm, we can achieve improved results both in theory (Table 1) and in practice (Section 3). Most
151   notably, we observe that the mini-batch approach is superior to the sparsifier approach for small batch
152
      sizes on various real world datasets. This is also the case when we combine our approach with the
      popular stochastic-greedy algorithm (Buchbinder et al., 2015; Mirzasoleiman et al., 2015).
153
154   While the mini-batch approach results in an improvement in performance, the sparsifier approach
155   has the benefit of being independent of the algorithm. That is, while any approximation algorithm
156   executed on a sparsifier immediately achieves (nearly) the same guarantees for the original function,
157   we need to re-establish the approximation ratio of our mini-batch algorithm for different constraints.
158
      Although these proofs are often straightforward, compiling an exhaustive list of where the mini-batch
      method is applicable is both laborious and offers limited insights.
159
160   We focus on two widely researched constraints: the cardinality constraint and the p-system constraint.
161   The cardinality constraint was chosen for its simplicity, while the p-system constraint was chosen for
      its broad applicability.

300
301   Approximate oracles Apart from the results of (Goundan & Schulz, 2007; Călinescu et al., 2011)
302   there are works that use different notions of an approximate oracle. Several works consider an
303   approximate oracle F̂ , such that ∀S ⊆ E, F̂ (S) − F (S) < ϵF (S) (Crawford et al., 2019; Horel &
304   Singer, 2016; Qian et al., 2017). The main difference of these models to our work is the fact that they
305   do not assume the surrogate function, F̂ , to be submodular. This adds a significant complication to
306   the analysis and degrades the performance guarantees.
307
      Mini-batch methods The closest result resembling our mini-batch approach is the stochastic-
308
      greedy algorithm (Buchbinder et al., 2015; Mirzasoleiman et al., 2015). They improve the expected
309
      query complexity of the greedy algorithm under a cardinality constraint by only considering a small
310   random subset of E \ Sj at the j-th iteration. We note that their approach can be combined into our
311
      mini-batch algorithm, reducing our query complexity by a Θ̃(k) factor, resulting in an approximation
312   guarantee in expectation instead of w.h.p.
313
314   Smoothed analysis To the best of our knowledge, (Rubinstein & Zhao, 2022) is the only result
315
      that considers smoothed analysis in the context submodular maximization. They consider submod-
      ular maximization under a cardinality constraint, where the cardinality parameter k undergoes a
316
      perturbation according to some known distribution.

692   A distinguishing characteristic of a p-system is that for every E ′ ⊆ E, the ratio of the sizes of the
