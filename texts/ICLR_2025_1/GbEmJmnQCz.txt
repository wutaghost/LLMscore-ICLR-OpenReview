009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Memorization is the ability of deep models to associate training data with seem-
012            ingly random labels. Even though memorization may not align with a model’s
013            ability to generalize, recent work by Feldman & Zhang (2020) has demonstrated
014            that memorization is in fact necessary for generalization. However, upon closer
015            inspection, we find that their methodology has three limitations. First, the def-
016            inition of memorization is imprecise, leading to contradictory results. Second,
017            their proposed algorithm used for approximating the leave-one-out test (the gold
018
               standard for calculating memorization scores) suffers from a high approximation
               error. Three, the authors induce a distribution shift when calculating marginal util-
019
               ity, leading to flawed results. Having accounted for these errors, we re-evaluate
020
               the role of memorization on generalization. To do so, we track how memoriza-
021            tion changes at different levels of generalization (test accuracy). We control model
022            generalization by training 19 different combinations of models, datasets, and train-
023            ing optimizations. We find that memorization and generalization are strongly neg-
024            atively correlated (Pearson -0.997): As one decreases, the other increases. This
025            shows that memorization is not necessary for generalization, as otherwise, the cor-
026            relation would have been positive. In light of these findings, future researchers are
027            encouraged to design techniques that can accurately approximate memorization
028            scores.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032   One of the most interesting properties of deep learning models is their ability to fit outliers (i.e.,
033   samples that are not part of the data distribution) (Zhang et al., 2017a; Arpit et al., 2017; Stephenson
034   et al., 2021). Specifically, deep models can output arbitrary ground-truth labels to inputs in the data
035   set. For example, if a picture of Gaussian noise is mislabeled as a cat, then the model will output
036   this label, even though the label is incorrect (Zhang et al., 2017a). This is only possible due to the
037   model’s ability to memorize point-label pairs.
038   Intuitively, the ability to generalize (i.e., correctly label previously unseen points) should be at odds
039   with memorization. This is because generalization requires identifying the underlying patterns and
040   then subsequently applying them to unseen points. On the other hand, memorization simply retrieves
041   the labels of the previously observed inputs and consequently, should not help in correctly classify-
042   ing new unseen points. However, recent work from Feldman & Zhang (2020) has shown that this is
043   not true for deep models. Their work demonstrated that “memorization is necessary for achieving
044
      close-to-optimal generalization error”. They do so using a three-step process. First, they provide
      a definition that quantifies a given point’s memorization score (i.e., the likelihood that the point is
045
      memorized). Second, they propose an approximation method to calculate memorization scores for
046
      each point in the data set. Third, they calculate the marginal utility of memorization (i.e., its impact
047   on test set accuracy). They report an accuracy degradation of 2.54 ± 0.20%, thereby concluding that
048   memorization is necessary for generalization. However, their work has three core issues:
049
050         1. Imprecise Definition: We find that the definition of memorization, which also forms the
051            basis of their theoretical work, is imprecise. It does not provide any standard threshold
052            to identify memorized points: any point above an arbitrary threshold is considered mem-
053            orized. We find that different threshold values capture different sets of points, with high
               thresholds only capturing the singleton outliers, and lower thresholds capturing entire or


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
                   partial sub-populations. As a result, two different thresholds can lead to contradictory con-
055                clusions. In other words, experiments under this definition are not falsifiable, a basic tenant
056                of scientific research. To mitigate this issue, we evaluate marginal utility over multiple
057                thresholds and base our conclusions on consistent behavior over most threshold values.
058
                2. High Approximation Error: We find that Feldman (2020)’s approximation algorithm
059                suffers from a high error rate, significantly overestimating the memorization scores. When
060                we compare its scores against the baseline leave-one-out experiment, we found that: 1)
061                only a small fraction of the points (less than 6%) were within 5% error, 2) the remaining
062                points have an exceptionally high error, as much as, 50%, 3) resulting in a high rate of false
063                positives, with as many as 93% of the points being incorrectly marked as memorized. Upon
064                closer inspection, we find that small sub-populations are most vulnerable to these errors.
065                As a result, the approximation algorithm incorrectly groups them with the actual memo-
066                rized points, even though the small sub-populations are not memorized. To ameliorate this
067
                   limitation, we identify and remove the points with incorrect scores from the memorized set.
068             3. Flawed Marginal Utility: The authors removed the memorized points from the data to
069                calculate their marginal utility. However, in doing so, the authors accidentally purge entire
070                sub-populations, wholesale. This leads to a distribution shift (i.e., loss of sub-population),
071                a common oversight in ML research. As a result, we find the drop in accuracy was not a
072
                   product of the marginal utility of memorized points, but due to common ML oversight.
073
      Re-evaluating Memorization vs Generalization: One simple way to fix these issues to take a
074
      different approach to study memorization and generalization. Instead of measuring the drop in ac-
075   curacy after removing memorized points, we track how memorization changes at different levels
076   of generalization (test accuracy). We control model generalization by training 19 different com-
077   binations of models, datasets, and training optimizations. Specifically, we vary test accuracy by:
078   1) changing model complexity, (number of trainable weights), 2) changing training optimizations
079   (With and without weight decay and data augmentation), 3) across different models (VGG, ResNet,
080   and ViT) and 4) across datasets (Cifar-10/100 and Tiny ImageNet).
081   We then plot the corresponding rates of memorization and generalization. We find that there is a
082   strong negative correlation between memorization and generalization (Pearson -0.997). This means
083   that as generalization improves, memorization decreases (and vice versa). From the results of our
084   experiments, we disprove the conclusion of the original work and show that memorization is not
085   necessary for generalization.

      2.2   I NFLUENCE OF DATA S ET P OINTS
134
135   Influence is the ability of a training point (xi , yi ) to impact the prediction of some other point
136   (xj , yj ) (Koh & Liang, 2017). Hereinafter, xi denotes the data point and yi denotes the label of xi .
137   As an illustrative example for influence, if including xi in the training set increases the probability of
138   point xj being classified correctly by the resulting model, then xi is said to have a positive influence
139   on xj (Feldman & Zhang, 2020). The higher the influence value, the stronger the impact.
140
      Self-influence is a special case of influence. It is a measure of how well the model predicts the label
141   for xi when the point itself is present in the training data set in comparison to when xi is absent. If
142   a point has positive self-influence, it has a higher probability of being classified correctly when it
143   is present in the training data set. Therefore, when the point is removed from the training data set,
144   the likelihood of correct prediction goes down as well. Conversely, negative self-influence means a
145   higher likelihood of being classified correctly only if it is not present in the training data set (e.g.,
146   mislabeled points).
147   According to Feldman & Zhang (2020), higher self-influence means a higher risk of memorization.
148   The high self-influence points usually belong to the tail end of the data distribution. The tail usually
149   consists of atypical points (e.g., outliers and mislabeled points) or small-sized sub-populations (e.g.,
150   five black cats in a data set of all white cats). Therefore, these points have the highest risk of
151   memorization across the entire distribution.
152
      Furthermore, if the point has high self-influence and has a duplicate in the test set, then removing
153   this point from the training data will result in the wrong prediction on itself, but also its duplicate
154   (or near duplicate) in the test set.

      3.1   D EFINING M EMORIZATION
163
164   Feldman & Zhang (2020) define a memorized point as one having high self-influence (i.e., a point
165   that is predicted correctly only when present in the training data).
166
      Specifically, consider a training set S = ((x1 , y1 )...(xn , yn )) and a point xi in the training set S.
167
      The memorization score is the difference in prediction accuracy between when the point xi is present
168
      in the training data (h ← A(S)) and when xi is absent (h ← A(S \i )). Here, (h ← A(S)) means
169   that models h were trained on dataset S using algorithm A:
170
                                Prh←A(S) [h(xi ) = yi ] − Prh←A(S \i ) [h(xi ) = yi ]                       (1)
171
172   The definition captures the intuition that a point xi has been memorized if its prediction changes
173
      significantly when it is removed from the dataset. We include Table 1 for reference on the symbols
      used throughout the paper.
174
175   For example, consider training 1000 instances each of the models h ← A(S) and h ← A(S \i ). If
176   the correct classification rate for xi when it h ← A(S) is around 90% (i.e., 900 out of the 1000
177   instances classified the point correctly). However, it falls significantly when h ← A(S \i ) to 25%
178   (i.e., 250 out of the 1000 instances classified the point correctly). Due to the significant drop in self
179   accuracy, this point has a high self-influence, and therefore, a high memorization score, specifically
180   of 90%−25% = 65%. This means that xi is far more likely be classified correctly when it is present
181
      in the training data. In contrast, if there is no significant change in the classification rate, then it has a
      low memorization score. In this case, xi will likely be classified correctly, whether or not it is present
182
      in the train set. As a result, the memorization score of a given point will be inversely proportional to
183
      its sub-population size: the larger the sub-population the smaller the memorization score. In the case
184   of our hypothetical cat dataset, the pink cat (singleton) will have the highest memorization score,
185   followed by the black cats (small-subpopulation) and the white cats (large sub-population).

187   3.2   C ALCULATING M EMORIZATION S CORES
188
189   Precise Algorithm: Having defined memorization, the next step is to develop a methodology to
190   identify memorized points from a dataset. A point is considered memorized based on its memo-
191   rization score, calculated using Equation 1. The most precise way to compute this score is via the
192   classic leave-one-out experiment. Here, we remove a single point from the training dataset, retrain
193
      the model on the remaining data, and test to see if the removed point is correctly classified. We
      have to run this experiment on all the points in the dataset to get the memorization score for each.
194
      Additionally, we have to repeat this model training process, for each point, multiple times to account
195
      for randomness introduced during training (e.g., the varying initialization, GPU randomness, etc.).
196   Specifically, this would require training hundreds models for every point in the data. Considering
197   data sets contain tens of thousands of points, this would require training millions of models. There-
198   fore, running this experiment over a large dataset and model will require a large amount of resources
199   and is therefore, computationally intractable.
200
      Approximation Algorithm: To overcome this limitation, Feldman & Zhang (2020) propose a
201
      method to approximate the memorization scores. Instead of removing one point at a time, the
202   authors randomly sample a fraction r of the points from the training set (originally of size n) and
203   leave the remaining points out of training. The number of points used in training is then m = r · n,
204   0 ≤ r ≤ 1. In Feldman & Zhang (2020) the authors use r = 0.7 for their experiments. The authors
205   repeat this k times. The exact value of k depends on the dataset but is typically on the order of a few
206   thousand models. As a result, a random point xi will be present in approximately k · r of the total
207   trained models and will be absent from k · (1 − r) of them. By aggregating the results over both
208   sets of models, the authors can approximate the memorization score for xi . All the points that have
209   a higher memorization score than some predetermined threshold are said to be memorized.
210

212
      Having identified the memorized points, the authors now calculate their marginal utility (i.e., their
213
      impact on test accuracy). This is done using a two-step process:
214
215   Step 1: Training Models without the memorized points The authors train two sets of models: one
      on the full training data (that includes the memorized points), and another on the reduced dataset

228   4     F ELDMAN & Z HANG (2020) L IMITATIONS
229
230   In the previous section we discuss how Feldman & Zhang (2020) define point memorization, ap-
231   proximate scores, and evaluate marginal utility. In this section, we explore critical limitations within
232   each step and propose potential fixes.
233
234   4.1   I MPRECISE D EFINITION FOR M EMORIZATION :
235
236   The findings of Feldman & Zhang (2020), and their earlier theoretical work Feldman (2020), are
237   based on the premise that Equation 1 provides a precise representation of memorized points (Sec-
238   tion 3.1). We argue that the key limitation is not in their derived proofs, but in the use of an imprecise
239   definition as the cornerstone of their work. Specifically, Equation 1 does not provide any threshold
240   on how to differentiate memorized points from non-memorized ones. To further exacerbate the mat-
241   ter, points with high memorization scores (outliers) can behave in contradictory ways to ones with
242
      low memorization scores (large sub-populations). As a result, conclusions derived from one thresh-
      old can easily be disproved with points from a different threshold. The experiments based on this
243
      definition fail the falsifiability test; there simply is no way to prove your theory right or wrong.
244
245   Such contradictory results can be seen in the main result of the original work (and also corroborated
246   by our own findings in the next section). The authors show that removing memorized points with
247   high scores (i.e., using a high memorization threshold) does not impact test accuracy. However,
248
      removing memorized points with low scores does impact test accuracy. A natural question arises:
      which one of the two thresholds really represents the memorized data? Do we base our conclusion
249
      on the high thresholds and claim that memorization does not impact accuracy? Or based it on the
250
      low thresholds and argue the opposite?
251
252
253   4.2   H IGH A PPROXIMATION E RROR :
254
255
      To estimate the memorization scores, the authors provide an approximation algorithm (Section 3.2).
      In this subsection, we compare this approximation algorithm against the ideal LOO baseline. Specif-
256
      ically, we hypothesize that the approximation algorithm over-estimates the memorization scores of
257
      small sub-populations, making them indiscernible from singleton outliers. We find that this is caused
258   by the approximation algorithm’s sampling method producing biased partitions (i.e., ones that are
259   dissimilar to the partitions created by LOO). As a result, the approximation algorithm has a high
260   error for small populations, incorrectly marks them as memorized, resulting in many false positives.
261   As a consequence, the original work reached an inaccurate conclusion about the role of memoriza-
262   tion on model utility.
263
      To illustrate this idea, consider our earlier hypothetical cat dataset containing 96 white, three black,
264   and a single pink one. The approximation algorithm creates data shards by dropping a fraction of
265   the points. This results in three types of dataset partitions. Partitions that contain 1) all three black
266   cats, 2) some black cats, or 3) no black cats. On the other hand, LOO will only drop a single point
267   at a time, and therefore, it’s partitions will contain exactly two of the three cats every time. This
268   means the approximation algorithm aggregates over partitions with fewer than two or no black cats,
269   resulting in an overestimation of memorization scores. As a result, small sub-populations are most
      vulnerable to over-estimation.

      4.3   F LAWED M ARGINAL U TILITY:
314
315
      After identifying the memorized points, the authors finally calculate their marginal utility (Sec-
316
      tion 3.3). This is where we observe a distribution shift error, a common oversight in machine learn-
317
      ing research. Specifically, when calculating the utility of memorized points, the authors accidentally
318   remove all the points above a specified threshold, thereby dropping entire sub-populations from the
319   training data. This causes a complete or near complete sub-population purge from the training data,
320   resulting in a distribution shift. This approach has shown to be sub-optimal Zhang et al. (2017b)
321   because it results in a biased classifier. This is because the training data will no longer possess cer-
322   tain sub-populations that exist in the test data. This prevents the model from effectively learning
323   the features of the removed sub-populations, and consequently, leads to poor model performance
      on the corresponding test points. Therefore, the drop in model accuracy observed by Feldman &


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Zhang (2020) was not due to the marginal utility of the memorized points but due to the induced
325   distribution shift (because entire sub-populations had been removed).
326
327   To make the contradiction apparent, if the authors had removed all the points below a specific thresh-
328
      old (i.e., remove all the points with low memorization scores belonging to large sub-population), we
      would also observe a drop in test accuracy. This would not mean that non-memorized points are
329
      necessary for generalization, but is a result of an induced distribution shift.
330
331   This idea can be understood using our earlier cat dataset example, presented in Section 2.1. If we
332   remove all the black cats (three of them in total) from the dataset that contains another 94 white
333   ones, the corresponding model will likely perform poorly on black cats in the test set. Similarly,
334
      if we remove all 94 white cats, the model will perform poorly on the white cats in the test set.
      Unfortunately, as was in the case of approximation error, small sub-populations are most vulnerable
335
      here as well.

339
340   Having identified the issue of over-estimation, we now re-examine the relationship between marginal
341   utility and memorization. Since the existing method of studying the relationship (Section 3.3) will
342   exacerbate the above mentioned issues, we take a different approach. We study how memorization
343   changes at different levels of generalization. We train 19 different combinations of models, datasets,
344
      and training parameters. We find that the memorization and generalization have a strong negative
      correlation (Pearson score 0.997) i.e., as one increases, the other decreases, thereby showing that
345
      memorization is not necessary for generalization.

      5.1       S ETUP
349   To expose the relationship between memorization and generalization, we train a series of models
350   with an varying number of 1) trainable weights, 2) datasets, 3) architectures, and 4) training opti-
351   mizations. By changing these variables, we are able to manipulate test accuracy and observe the
352   change in memorization. Specifically, we designed four models using VGG blocks (VGG-0.5M2 ,
353   VGG-1M, VGG-8M, VGG-20M), two model Resnet models (Resnet-18 and Resnet-50), and two
354   ViT models (Tiny and Small). We run our experiments over three datasets (CIFAR-10/100, Tiny
355   ImageNet). Lastly, to isolate the impact of training procedure (i.e., weight decay and data augmen-
356   tation), we repeat our CIFAR-10 experiments without these optimizations. Running such exhaustive
357
      experiments exposes the relationship between test accuracy and memorization. In all, this provides
      us with 19 data points of experiments to understand memorization and generalization.
358
359   We identify the memorized points using Equation 1. Similar to Feldman & Zhang (2020), we train
360   the models for 100 epochs, using a batch size of 512, with a triangular learning rate of 0.4, weight
361   decay of 10−5 for all models. We train 2,000 models for each of the models. In the case of ViT, we
362   train 500 models using a learning rate of 0.1.
363   Results Figure 3 shows the results of our experiments. We can clearly observe the strong negative
364   correlation (Pearson 0.997) between memorization and generalization (test accuracy): This means
365   that as model utility increases, memorization decreases. We this trend hold across all models and
366   datasets (i.e., both CIFAR-10/100 and Tiny ImageNet).
367   Similarly, we can observe that this observation holds even for models that trained with or without
368   data-augmentation and weight decay. Even though these are common in modern training pipelines,
369   we remove them to isolate their impact on memorization. In both cases (with and without these
370   optimizations) we find that as model accuracy increases, memorization decreases.

      While Feldman & Zhang (2020) made valuable contributions, in the previous section, we showed
402
      that, after having accounted for the three limitations, memorization and generalization are strongly
403   negatively correlated. The original incorrect results were a result of the high approximation errors
404   (Section 4.2), distribution shifts (Section 4.3) and an imprecise definition (Section 4.1). As a result,
405   the original authors incorrectly marked a significant number of points as memorized. However,
406   by varying test set accuracy by iterating over different model architecutres, datasets, and training
407   parameters, we find not only that memroization is not necessary for generalization, but infact is
408   strongly negatively correlated.
409   Memorization has a direct implication for privacy research. This is because memorized points are
410   vulnerable to membership-inference attacks (Carlini et al., 2022a). Feldman & Zhang (2020) cre-
411   ated a tension between generalization and privacy. This is because they claimed that memorization
412   was needed for generalization while other works demonstrated that memorization was harmful to
413   privacy (Carlini et al., 2022a; Leino & Fredrikson, 2020; Carlini et al., 2019; Li et al., 2022). In
414   other words, generalization and privacy can not be simultaneously achieved. While this might have
415   dissuaded researchers in the community, our work shows that this tension does not exist. This is be-
416   cause memorization is not necessary for generalization. In light of these results, future researchers
417
      are encouraged to explore methods to build models that both generalize and are private.
418
419   7   R ELATED W ORK
420
421   One of the first papers to discover memorization deep learning models was Zhang et al. (2017a).
422   They showed that models can fit completely unstructured images even if these consist of random
423   Gaussian noise. Since then, there has been a tension between memorization and generalization and
424   how they impact model performance (Chatterjee, 2018). Earlier works focused on limiting model
425   memorization, thereby forcing the model to learn patterns instead. This was partly motivated by
426   the fact that memorization exposed models to privacy risks (e.g., membership inference) (Carlini
427   et al., 2019; 2022b). As a result, different methods were developed to counter memorization, which
428
      included using regularization (Arpit et al., 2017), filtering weak gradients (Zielinski et al., 2020;
      Chatterjee, 2020), adjusting model size (Arpit et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2019). While these methods
429
      did reduce model memorization, they did so at the cost of model accuracy.
430
431   However, the true impact of memorization on model behavior was yet unknown. This first and
      foremost required methods to identify memorized points. A number of post-hoc methods were


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      developed to identify them. These included clustering (Stephenson et al., 2021), repurposed mem-
433   bership inference attacks (Carlini et al., 2022b), pseudo leave-one-out method (Feldman & Zhang,
434   2020). Having developed the ability to identify these points, the authors were now able to study
435   their impact on model efficacy. As we describe in Section 3, Feldman & Zhang (2020) demon-
436   strated that memorization was in fact necessary for model memorization. However, this conclusion
437   was incorrect and was a by-product of a number of methodological errors. By accounting for these
438   errors and rerunning their experiments, our results show that memorization has minimal impact on
439   generalization.

441   8   C ONCLUSION
442
443   Memorization is the ability of the model to fit labels to seemingly random samples. Recent work
444   from Feldman & Zhang (2020) demonstrated that memorization is necessary for generalization. We
445   show that the original work suffered from a number of crucial errors. These include the use of
446   an imprecise definition, high approximation error, and distribution shift. In order to study the real
447   impact of memorization, we train 19 different combinations of models, datasets, and training pa-
448   rameters. Having done so, we track memorization across generalization and find that memorization
449   has a strong negative correlation with generalization. And therefore, memorization is not necessary
450   for generalization.
