009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011                Current state-of-the-art methods for video inpainting typically rely on optical flow
012                or attention-based approaches to inpaint masked regions by propagating visual in-
013                formation across frames. While such approaches have led to significant progress
014                on standard benchmarks, they struggle with tasks that require the synthesis of
015                novel content that is not present in other frames. In this paper, we reframe video
016                inpainting as a conditional generative modeling problem and present a framework
017                for solving such problems with conditional video diffusion models. We introduce
018
                   inpainting-specific sampling schemes which capture crucial long-range dependen-
                   cies in the context, and devise a novel method for conditioning on the known
019
                   pixels in incomplete frames. We highlight the advantages of using a generative
020
                   approach for this task, showing that our method is capable of generating diverse,
021                high-quality inpaintings and synthesizing new content that is spatially, temporally,
022                and semantically consistent with the provided context.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   Video inpainting is the task of filling in missing pixels in a video with plausible values. It has
058
      practical applications in video editing and visual effects, including video restoration (Tang et al.,
      2011), object or watermark removal (Patwardhan et al., 2005), and video stabilization (Matsushita
059
      et al., 2006). While significant progress has been made on image inpainting in recent years (Saharia
060
      et al., 2022; Lugmayr et al., 2022; Suvorov et al., 2021), video inpainting remains a challenging task
061   due to the added time dimension, which drastically increases computational complexity and leads
062   to a stricter notion of what it means for an inpainting to be plausible. Specifically, inpainted regions
063   require not only per-frame spatial and semantic consistency with the context as in image inpainting,
064   but also temporal consistency between frames and realistic motion of objects in the scene.
065
      Current state-of-the-art video inpainting methods explicitly attempt to inpaint masked regions by
066
      exploiting visual information present in other frames, typically by using optical flow estimates (Li
067   et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2020) or attention-based approaches (Zeng et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021)
068   to determine how this information should be propagated across frames. Such methods, which we
069   refer to as “content propagation” methods, implicitly assume that the content in unmasked regions
070   is sufficient to fill in the missing values, and tend to fail when this assumption is violated. In the
071   presence of large occlusions, objects may be partially or fully occluded for long durations, and in
072   these cases an inpainting method may need to complete an object’s appearance (as in Fig. 1) or infer
073   its behaviour (as in Fig. 2) to produce a plausible result, requiring the generation of novel content.
074

086   We argue that a more sensible approach to video inpainting is to learn a conditional distribution
087   over possible inpaintings given the observed context. Conditional generative models such as GANs
088   (Suvorov et al., 2021), autoregressive models (Peng et al., 2021), and diffusion models (Saharia
089   et al., 2022; Lugmayr et al., 2022) have long dominated image inpainting, a task that inherently
090   requires content synthesis as there is no additional information to draw upon. Learning such a
091
      distribution naturally accounts ill-posedness of the video inpainting problem: for instance, given the
      observed entry and exit points of the car in Fig. 2 there exists a diversity of plausible trajectories
092
      it could take. Further, generative approaches for image inpainting have a demonstrated ability to
093
      produce results that are semantically consistent with the context, a quality which is particularly
094   important for video inpainting tasks that require inferring an object’s behaviour as in Fig. 2 – without
095   a strong prior, there is insufficient information in the context to determine what such a trajectory
096   might look like; the model must have some notion of what makes a vehicle trajectory plausible at a
097   semantic level.
098
      In this work we aim to overcome the inherent limitations of content propagation by advancing gen-
099
      erative inpainting, making the following key contributions:
100
101
             • We reframe video inpainting as a conditional generative modeling problem, and present a
102            framework for solving this problem using conditional video diffusion models.
103
104          • We demonstrate how to use long-range temporal attention to generate semantically consis-
105            tent behaviour of inpainted objects over long time horizons, even when our model cannot
106            jointly model all frames due to memory constraints. We can do this even for inpainted
107            objects that have limited or no visibility in the context, a quality not present in the current
               literature.

117   2    R ELATED W ORK
118
119   Video Inpainting. Recent advances in video inpainting have largely been driven by methods which
120   fill in masked regions by borrowing content from the unmasked regions of other frames, which we
121   refer to as content propagation methods. These methods typically use optical flow estimates (Huang
122   et al., 2016; Kim et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2019; Gao et al., 2020), self-attention (Zeng et al., 2020; Liu
123
      et al., 2021; Oh et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2019) or a combination of both (Zhou et al., 2023; Zhang
      et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022) to determine how to propagate pixel values or learned features across
124
      frames. Such methods often produce visually compelling results, particularly on tasks where the
125
      mask-occluded region is visible in nearby frames such as foreground object removal with a near-
126   static background. They struggle, however, in cases where this does not hold, for instance in the
127   presence of heavy camera motion, large masks, or tasks where semantic understanding of the video
128   content is required to produce a convincing result.
129
      More recent work has utilized diffusion models for video inpainting. Gu et al. (2023) proposes
130
      a method for video inpainting that combines a video diffusion model with optical flow guidance,
131
      following a similar “content propagation” approach to the methods listed above. Chang et al. (2023)
132   uses a latent diffusion model (Rombach et al., 2022; Vahdat et al., 2021) to remove the agent’s view
133   of itself from egocentric videos for applications in robotics. Notably, this is framed as an image
134   inpainting task, where the goal is to remove the agent (with a mask provided by a segmentation
135   model) from a single video frame conditioned on h previous frames. Consequently, the results lack
136   temporal consistency when viewed as videos, and the model is evaluated using image inpainting
137   metrics only. Zhang et al. (2023b) proposes a method for the related task of text-conditional video
138   inpainting, which produces impressive results but requires user intervention.
139   Image Inpainting with Diffusion Models. This work takes inspiration from the recent success
140   of diffusion models for image inpainting. These methods can be split into two groups: those that
141   inpaint using an unconditional diffusion model by making heuristic adjustments to the sampling
142   procedure (Lugmayr et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023a), and those that explicitly train a conditional
143   diffusion model which, if sufficiently expressive and trained to optimality, enables exact sampling
144   from the conditional distribution (Saharia et al., 2022). In this work we extend the latter approach
145   to video data.

174   Video Diffusion Models. A number of recent papers have proposed diffusion-based approaches to
175   generative modeling of video data (Höppe et al., 2022; Harvey et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022; Yang
176   et al., 2022; Voleti et al., 2022). We follow the majority of these approaches in using a 4-D U-Net
177   (Ronneberger et al., 2015) architecture to parameterize ϵθ (. . .). Alternating spatial and temporal
178   attention blocks within the U-Net capture dependencies within and across frames respectively, with
179   relative positional encodings (Shaw et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2021) providing information about each
180   frame’s position within the video. Due to computational constraints, video diffusion models are
181
      inherently limited to conditioning on and generating a small number of frames at a time, which we
      denote as K.
182
183   Flexible Video Diffusion Models. Generating long videos with frame count N ≫ K then requires
184   sampling from the diffusion model multiple times. A typical approach is to generate frames in
185   multiple stages in a block-autoregressive fashion, in each stage sampling K/2 frames conditioned
186   on the previous K/2 frames. We depict this approach in Fig. 3a, with each row representing one
187
      stage. A problem with this strategy is that it fails to capture dependencies on frames more than K/2

188
      schemes”) are possible, as depicted in Figs. 3b and 3c. To avoid iteratively retraining to identify an
189
      optimal sampling scheme, Harvey et al. (2022) proposes training a single model that can perform
190   well using any sampling scheme, referred to as a Flexible Diffusion Model (FDM). An FDM is
191   trained to generate any subset of video frames conditioned on any other subset with the objective

206   We consider the problem of creating an N -frame video V conditioned on some subset of known
207   pixel values specified by a pixel-level mask M. Entries of M take value 1 where the corresponding
208   pixel in V is known and take 0 elsewhere. Unlike content propagation methods which determinis-
209   tically generate one possible completion, we aim to model the posterior distribution over possible
210   inpaintings as pθ (V|V ⊙ M) ≈ pdata (V|V ⊙ M) with a conditional video diffusion model, where
211   ⊙ is defined such that a ⊙ M returns the elements in a for which the corresponding value in M is 1.

213   ing on or generating at most K frames at a time. In the video inpainting problem we are predicting
214   and conditioning on pixels rather than frames, but our network architecture imposes an analogous
215   constraint: we can only predict or condition on pixels from at most K different frames at a time. We

241
242   Architecture. We generalize the FDM architecture to use pixel-level masks rather than frame-level
243   masks, as in the image inpainting approach proposed by Saharia et al. (2022). Concretely, every
244   input frame is concatenated with a mask which takes value 1 where the corresponding pixel is
245   observed and 0 elsewhere. The input values are clean (without added noise) for observed pixels and
246   noisy otherwise.
247   Model Inputs. Recall that we wish to train a model that can generate plausible values for unknown
248   pixels in frames indexed by X , conditioned on known pixel values in frames indexed by either X or
249   Y. We simulate such tasks by first sampling a video V and a mask M from our dataset, and then
250   sampling frame indices X and Y from a “frame index distribution” similar to that of FDM.1 The
251   distribution over masks M can be understood as reflecting the variability in the types of masks we
252   will encounter at test-time, and the frame index distribution can be understood as reflecting our desire
253
      to be able to sample from the model using arbitrary sampling schemes. Given M, X , and Y, we
      create a combined list of frames X ⊕ Y, where ⊕ denotes concatenation, and a corresponding mask
254
      MX ,Y := M[X ] ⊕ 1[Y], where 1[Y] is a mask of all 1’s for each frame indexed in Y. This masks
255
      only the missing pixels in frames X while treating all pixels in frames Y as observed (visualized in
256   Fig. 4). We then extract our training targets from the video as x := V[X ⊕ Y] ⊙ (1 − MX ,Y ), and
257   our observations as y := V[X ⊕ Y] ⊙ MX ,Y .

305   4.3   C ONDITIONING ON I NCOMPLETE F RAMES
306
      Given our proposed architecture and training procedure, we can sample from the resulting models
307
      using the FDM sampling schemes shown in Fig. 3 without further complication. A downside of
308   these sampling schemes, however, is that they do not enable us to condition on frames with unknown
309   pixel values. That is, we are unable to condition on the known pixels in a frame unless we either (a)
310   have previously inpainted it and know all of its pixel values already, or (b) are inpainting it as we
311   condition on it. We show in our experiments that this often leaves us unable to account for important
312   dependencies in the context.
313
      We therefore propose a method for conditioning on incomplete frames. This enables the sampling
314   schemes shown in Fig. 6, where we condition on the known pixels in incomplete frames, marked in
315   red. Recall that x denotes “unknown pixels in frames indexed by X ” and y denotes “known pixels
316   in frames indexed by X or Y”. If any frames indexed by Y are incomplete then we have a third
317   category, which we’ll call z: “unknown pixels in frames indexed by Y”.
318
      We then wish to approximately sample x ∼ pdata (·|y) without requiring values of z. We do not have
319
      a way to sample directly from an approximation of this distribution, as the diffusion model is not
320   trained to condition on “incomplete” frames. We note, however, that this desired distribution is the
321   marginal of a distribution that our diffusion model can approximate:

509   5.5   A BLATIONS
510
511   Effect of Sampling Schemes. We compare selected sampling schemes on the Traffic-Scenes dataset
512   in Table 2, before providing more thorough qualitative and quantitative comparisons in the appendix.
513   We find Lookahead-AR++ performs best on all datasets in terms of video realism (VFID) and tem-
514   poral consistency (warp error), demonstrating the importance of our method’s ability to condition
515   on incomplete frames. AR tends to do poorly on all metrics on both the Traffic-Scenes and BDD-
516
      Inpainting datasets; this is likely due to its inability to account for observed pixel values in frames
      coming after the ones being generated at each stage, causing divergence from the ground-truth and
517
      then artifacts when producing later frames conditioned on incompatible values. Hierarchy-2 does
518
      well on some metrics of reconstruction and frame-wise perceptual quality. It does less well in terms
519   of video realism, as the inpainting of the “keyframes” in the first stage is done without conditioning
520   on the known pixels of frames in their immediate vicinity, potentially leading to incompatibility with
521   the local context.
522
      Samplers and Number of Sampling Steps. The use of diffusion models allows for a trade-off
523
      between computation time and inpainting quality by varying the sampler and number of steps used
524
      in the generative process. In Table 3 we report the results of our model on the BDD-Inpainting test
525   set with the AR sampling scheme using different samplers and numbers of sampling steps. Aligning
526   with expectations and qualitative observations, performance on metrics degrades as the number of
527   sampling steps decreases for LPIPS, PVCS, FID and VFID. We note, however, that performance
528   on Ewarp , PSNR, and SSIM improves, suggesting that these metrics may not correlate well with
529   perceived quality, as has been previously suggested for the latter two metrics in Zhang et al. (2018b).

532   6     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this work, we have presented a framework for video inpainting by conditioning video diffusion
535   models. This framework allows for flexible conditioning on the context, enabling post-hoc experi-
536   mentation with sampling schemes which can greatly improve results. We introduce four challenging
537   tasks for video inpainting methods and demonstrate our model’s ability to use semantic information
538   in the context to solve these tasks effectively. Our experiments demonstrate a clear improvement
539   on quantitative metrics and, contrary to existing methods, our approach can generate semantically
      meaningful completions based on minimal information in the context.

      A    L IMITATIONS
703
704
705
706   The most notable limitation of our method is its computational cost relative to competing methods.
707
      The wall-clock time for our method is highly dependent on a number of factors, such as the number
      of sampler steps used and the number of frames generated in each stage of the sampling scheme used.
708
      See Section C for further details. Improvements in few-step generation for diffusion models and
709
      increasing the size of our model such that more frames can be processed at a time would both help
710   to mitigate these concerns. Additionally, our method requires that a model be trained on a specific
711   dataset which is reasonably close to the data distribution to be inpainted at test time, requiring large
712   datasets. The development of large scale, general purpose video generative models would likely
713   help to make generative approaches such as ours more robust to out-of-distribution tasks.

      F.1.1    BDD-I NPAINTING
857
      From the original BDD100K dataset we take a random subset of videos, using 48,886 for a training
858   set and 100 for each held-out test set. Videos are downsampled spatially by a factor of 2.5, center-
859   cropped to 256×256, and the frame rate is reduced by a factor of three to 10 fps. We truncate videos
860   to 400 frames, corresponding to a 40 second length for each. We randomly generate a set of 49,970
861   masks of four types: grids, horizontal or vertical lines, boxes, and blobs. Our mask generation
862   procedure first picks a mask type, and then randomly selects mask parameters such as size and
863   direction of motion (which includes stationary masks). Generated masks contain 400 frames. During
      training both videos and masks are sampled uniformly and independently, giving a distribution over


                                                      16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      nearly 2.5 billion video-mask pairs. Our test sets, as well as code for regenerating our training set,
865   will be made public upon publication.
866
867
      F.1.2         I NPAINTING -C ARS
868
869   We use an in-house dataset of overhead drone footage of vehicle traffic, for which we have tracker-
870   generated bounding boxes for each vehicle. The videos are spatially downsampled by a factor of two
871   from their original 4k resolution, and 256 × 256 sub-videos centred on vehicles are extracted using
872   the vehicle-specific tracks. The length of these videos is variable as it depends on the amount of
873
      time a given vehicle was visible in the source video, but typically ranges from 10 to 60 seconds at 10
      fps. Bounding boxes are dilated by a factor of two to ensure the entirety of the vehicle is contained
874
      within them, and these dilated bounding boxes are then used as masks. This dataset contains 2973
875
      training examples and 100 held-out test examples.

882   highway on-ramps, etc. We generate masks using the same mask generation procedure as in BDD-

       In this section we highlight the qualitative impact that different sampling schemes can have on the
1018
       quality of inpainted videos. We select a video from the test set where conditioning on the appropriate
1019
       frames is crucial for our method to succeed. Figure 13 shows the beginning of this video, where a
1020   car is occluded in the right-hand lane for the first few seconds of the video (see input frames in the
1021   first row). The Autoregressive scheme (second row) shows a distinct “pop-in” effect, as when the
1022   initial frames were generated the model was not able to condition on future frames where the cars
1023   existence and appearance are revealed. Both Reverse-Autoregressive and AR w/ Far Future (third
1024   and fourth rows) do condition on future frames that contain the car; Reverse-Autoregressive because
1025   the model is able to propagate the car backwards through time, and AR w/ Far Future because the
       model conditions on frames far out into the future where the car has been revealed. Figure 14 shows

       the end of the video, where the cars in the right-hand lane are occluded and remain occluded for
1027   the rest of the video (first row). The Autoregressive scheme keeps these cars visible, as it is able to
1028   propagate them forward through time (second row). Reverse-Autoreg (third row) fails for the same
1029   reason that Autoreg did at the beginning: when the final frames were generated, the model was not
1030   able to condition on frames where the vehicles were visible. AR w/ Far Future (fourth row) is again

1034   I     D IFFUSION M ODEL S AMPLING D ETAILS
1035
1036   As alluded to in the main paper, we train a diffusion model that uses ϵ-prediction to parameterize
1037   the score of a distribution over xt at each time t. This can be used to parameterize a stochastic dif-
1038   ferential equation (or ordinary differential equation) that morphs samples from a unit Gaussian into
1039   approximate samples from the conditional distribution of interest pdata (x|y) Song et al. (2020). We
1040   use the Heun sampler proposed by Karras et al. (2022) to integrate this SDE. Our hyperparameters
1041   are σmax = 1000, σmin = 0.002, ρ = 7, Schurn = 80, Smax = ∞, S0 = 0, and Snoise = 1. We use
1042   100 sampling steps (involving 199 network function evaluations) for each experiment except where
1043   specified otherwise.
