                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Quadratic programs (QPs) arise in various domains such as machine learning,
012            finance, and control. Recently, learning-enhanced primal-dual hybrid gradient
013            (PDHG) methods have shown great potential in addressing large-scale linear pro-
014            grams; however, this approach has not been extended to QPs. In this work, we
015            focus on unrolling “PDQP”, a PDHG algorithm specialized for convex QPs. Specif-
016            ically, we propose a neural network model called “PDQP-net” to learn optimal
017            QP solutions. Theoretically, we demonstrate that a PDQP-net of polynomial size
018            can align with the PDQP algorithm, returning optimal primal-dual solution pairs.
019            We propose an unsupervised method that incorporates KKT conditions into the
020
               loss function. Unlike the standard learning-to-optimize framework that requires
               optimization solutions generated by solvers, our unsupervised method adjusts the
021
               network weights directly from the evaluation of the primal-dual gap. This method
022
               has two benefits over supervised learning: first, it helps generate better primal-dual
023            gap since the primal-dual gap is in the objective function; second, it does not
024            require solvers. We show that PDQP-net trained in this unsupervised manner can
025            effectively approximate optimal QP solutions. Extensive numerical experiments
026            confirm our findings, indicating that using PDQP-net predictions to warm-start
027            PDQP can achieve up to 45% acceleration on QP instances. Moreover, it achieves
028            14% to 31% acceleration on out-of-distribution instances.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Convex quadratic programs (QPs) involve identifying a solution within the feasible region defined
      by linear constraints, aiming to minimize a convex quadratic objective function. This type of
034
      problem arises in various domains, including machine learning (Cortes, 1995; Candes et al., 2008),
035
      finance (Markowitz, 1952; Boyd et al., 2017), and control engineering (Garcia et al., 1989).
036
037   Extensive efforts have been devoted to developing efficient algorithms for convex QPs, with the most
038   classic approaches being the simplex (Dantzig, 2016) and barrier (Andersen et al., 2003) algorithms.
039
      While both methods have shown robust performance in solving QPs, their scalability is often hindered
      by the computational overhead associated with matrix factorization, particularly in large-scale
040
      scenarios (Wright, 1997). To address this challenge, there has been growing interest in leveraging
041
      matrix-free first-order methods (FOMs) for optimizing convex QPs, such as SCS (O’Donoghue,
042   2021), OSQP (Stellato et al., 2020), and PDQP (Lu & Yang, 2023). Both OSQP and SCS are
043   operator-splitting approaches that still require at least one iteration of matrix factorization, whereas
044   PDQP is a truly matrix-free method that solves QPs by alternately utilizing primal and dual gradients.
045   Consequently, PDQP shows greater potential for solving large-scale QPs. However, even with PDQP,
046   tackling these problems typically necessitates thousands of iterations.
047
      Recently, machine learning has been extensively utilized to expedite optimization algorithms (Bengio
048   et al., 2021; Gasse et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024a). Many works have trained graph neural networks
049   (GNNs) to predict key problem properties, while theoretical research by Chen et al. (2024b) has
050   shown that GNNs can reliably predict essential features of convex QP problems, such as feasibility,
051   optimal objective value, and optimal solution. In practice, Li et al. (2024) introduces an unrolling
052   approach that integrates GNNs with a primal-dual hybrid gradient (PDHG) solver called “PDLP”,
053   achieving efficient solution mappings for large-scale LPs. To our knowledge, no existing works have
      extended this line of research to address convex QPs. Furthermore, the commonly used end-to-end

      While PDQP offers strong computational efficiency, the inherent long-tail behavior of FOMs often
      requires a large number of iterations for convergence. To mitigate this issue, we propose enhancing
143
      its efficiency by incorporating machine learning techniques. Specifically, our goal is to develop
144
      a deep learning framework capable of predicting the solutions generated by PDQP. Motivated by
145   this, we introduce an unsupervised deep unrolling framework to solve convex QPs more efficiently.
146   This section outlines the framework in three stages: the algorithm-unrolled PDQP-Net (Section 3.1),
147   challenges of learning optimal QP solutions through supervised learning (Section 3.2), and the
148   unsupervised learning framework we propose to overcome these challenges (Section 3.3).
149
150   3.1   U NROLLING THE PDQP A LGORITHM
151
152   In this part, we discuss the design of PDQP-Net by unrolling the PDQP algorithm to ensure alignment
153   between the two. Previous unrolling approaches have primarily focused on specific real-world
154   applications, such as compressive sensing (Zhang & Ghanem, 2018; Xiang et al., 2021) and image
155
      processing (Liu et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020). These methods demonstrate considerable performance
      improvements over conventional “black-box” neural networks and provide enhanced interpretability.
156

157

160   incorporate a channel expansion technique. Specifically, at iteration k, the primal and dual variables,

      Remark 3.1. We note that PDQP-Net falls within the broader class of message-passing GNNs. In
219
      recent years, GNNs have gained considerable attention for their success in learning the solutions
220   of optimization problems (Nair et al., 2020; Li et al., 2024). Despite the empirical success, Chen
221   et al. (2022) and Chen et al. (2024b) take an initial step towards a theoretical understanding of the
222   power of GNNs. They demonstrated that given sufficient parameters, GNNs can approximate the
223   solution mappings of LPs and QPs to arbitrary precision. Their theoretical framework is based on the
224   universal approximation theorem for MLP and Lusin’s theorem, which guarantees the approximability

227   approximation accuracy may still need an impractically large number of parameters.
228   In contrast, PDQP-Net is built on a more refined theoretical foundation. By algorithm unrolling,
229   we prove that PDQP-Net can approximate the optimal solution of QP to an ϵ-accuracy using only
230   O(log 1ϵ ) neurons. This result provides insight into how efficient and reliable convergence can be
231   achieved with a given network size, thereby advancing our understanding of GNNs’ representational
232   power in solving QP problems.

234   3.2   S UPERVISED L EARNING AND P RIMAL -D UAL G APS
235
      In the realm of end-to-end learning for solving optimization problems, models are typically trained
236
      using a supervised learning approach, as emphasized in recent studies (Li et al., 2024; Chen et al.,
237
      2024b). In such settings, the associated loss function L is often designed to quantify the discrepancy
238   between the predicted solution and the optimal one. However, in addition to the high costs associated
239   with label collection, the supervised framework faces another significant issue: the predicted primal-
240   dual solution may deviate slightly from the optimal one while resulting in a substantial duality
241   gap.
242
      To illustrate this issue, we present the following proposition.
243
244
      Proposition 3.2. Let (x∗ , y ∗ ) denote the optimal primal-dual solution to a given convex QP, and let
      (x0 , y0 ) represent any primal-dual solution. The normalized primal-dual gap rgap (x0 , y0 ) is bounded
245
      as follows:
246
247
                    rgap (x0 , y0 ) ≤ ∥Q∥ · ∥x0 − x∗ ∥2 + ∥c∥ · ∥x0 − x∗ ∥ + ∥b∥ · ∥y0 − y ∗ ∥ + R,
248   where R epresents the difference in the reduced-cost contribution between (x0 , y0 ) and the optimal
249   solution (x∗ , y ∗ ).
250
      Proposition 3.2 indicates that the upper bound on the duality gap at (x0 , y0 ) depends not only on the
251   solution discrepancy but also on problem characteristics such as ∥Q∥, ∥c∥, and ∥b∥. Depending on
252   the characteristics of Q, c, and b, even when the predicted primal-dual solution (x0 , y0 ) is close to
253   the optimal one, the duality gap can still be significant. This issue is particularly pronounced for QPs,
254   as the discrepancy can be amplified by the quadratic matrix Q.
255
256   3.3   KKT- INFORMED U NSUPERVISED L EARNING
257
258   To address the limitations identified in Section 3.2, it is crucial to integrate the primal-dual gap into
259   the design of learning-based optimization algorithms. However, directly incorporating the primal-dual
260   gap term into the existing supervised learning framework presents challenges, primarily because
261
      it does not align naturally with the numerical scale of the distance to the optimal solution. As a
      result, effectively embedding the primal-dual gap into the loss function necessitates a comprehensive
262
      redesign. Given the overarching objective of predicting solutions that demonstrate both optimality
263
      and feasibility, we propose substituting the distance to the optimal solution with a combination of
264   metrics that simultaneously assess both aspects. One promising approach is to adhere to the optimality
265   conditions outlined in Lu & Yang (2024), which incorporate a KKT-based metric that evaluates
266   solution quality by accounting for both feasibility violations and the primal-dual gap. This method
267   not only aligns with our objectives but also offers theoretical guarantees based on KKT conditions.
268   Drawing inspiration from this, we derive a KKT-informed loss function that integrates considerations
269   of both feasibility and optimality. Specifically, for a QP problem (1), we introduce its KKT conditions
      as follows:

270
             • Primal Feasibility: Ax − b ≥ 0, l ≤ x ≤ u
271
272          • Dual Feasibility: y, λ, µ ≥ 0
273          • Stationary Condition: Qx + c − A⊤ y − λ + µ = 0
274
             • Complementarity slackness: y ⊤ (Ax − b) + λ⊤ (x − l) + µ⊤ (u − x) = 0
275
276   where λ and µ denote the dual variables associated with lower and upper bounds for x, respectively.
277
278
      Since the QPs under consideration are convex, the KKT conditions are both necessary and sufficient
      for optimality. Minimizing the violation quantities associated with x and y will lead to the optimal
279
      solution. Building on the approach proposed by Lu & Yang (2024), we employ the primal-dual
280
      gap (as shown in Equation 3) as a measure, rather than applying complementarity slackness directly.
281   This choice is well-justified, as the primal-dual gap becomes equivalent to the complementarity
282   slackness when the stationary condition is met.
283
284                                rgap := x⊤ Qx + c⊤ y − y ⊤ b − λ⊤ l + µ⊤ u                              (3)
285
286   Based on this, we propose an unsupervised learning loss comprising three components: (i) primal
287   residual (rprimal ), which quantifies the violation of constraints and primal variable bounds; (ii) dual
288   residual (rdual ), which assesses the violation of stationary conditions and dual variable bounds; and
289
      (iii) primal-dual gap (rgap ), which evaluates the optimality gap. Note that all three quantities are
      normalized to relative values, ensuring they are on the same numerical scale, which allows for direct
290
      summation. For the ease of distinguishing, we denote normalized quantities with ˆ·. The input to this
291
      loss function comprises the predicted primal solution x∗ , the dual solution y ∗ , and the QP instance
292   M. Denoting the learnable parameters of involved MLPs as ϑ, the loss function seeks to minimize
293   the total residual with respect to the parameters {Θ, ϑ}. Specifically, for each QP instance M, the
294   unsupervised learning loss can be expressed as:
295
296                              L{Θ,ϑ} (M, x∗ , y ∗ ) := rbprimal + rbdual + rbgap .
297   A detailed derivation procedure of this loss function can be found in Appendix H, along with the
298   actual implementation in Appendix G.
299
300
      According to Proposition 3.2, the supervised learning approach can potentially yield solutions with
      large primal-dual gaps, as it is rarely possible for a model to produce exact predictions in practice.
301
      As a result, employing this loss function is expected to yield higher-quality solutions compared to the
302
      supervised method. Additionally, utilizing an unsupervised learning approach eliminates the need for
303   collecting optimal solutions for training—an extremely time-consuming task for complex QPs—thus
304   enhancing sample complexity.

309   In this section, we present comprehensive numerical results to evaluate the performance of our

526   5    C ONCLUSIONS
527
528   This work presents a unsupervised learning framework for obtaining optimal solutions to QPs.
529   Central to this framework is a KKT-condition-based loss function that effectively integrates optimality
530   conditions to improve solution predictions. We also introduce the PDQP-Net architecture, which
531   unrolls the original PDQP algorithm, providing a compelling alternative to traditional GNN backbones.
532   Our theoretical analysis shows that the unsupervised framework effectively addresses the challenges
533
      of supervised learning, particularly the issue of large primal-dual gaps even when the distance to the
      optimal solution is minimal. Furthermore, our proposed PDQP-Net can replicate the original PDQP
534
      algorithm, inheriting its convergence properties while also enhancing expressiveness through channel
535
      expansion. Comprehensive numerical experiments further validate our approach, showing substantial
536   improvements in prediction quality and empirical acceleration of the PDQP algorithm. These findings
537   highlight the potential of integrating optimization theory into the L2O paradigm, suggesting that such
538   integration can substantially enhance the performance of existing optimization algorithms.
