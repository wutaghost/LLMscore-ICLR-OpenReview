                                                                     A BSTRACT
033
                    We have made significant progress towards building foundational video diffusion
034
                    models. As these models are trained using large-scale unsupervised data, it has
035                 become crucial to adapt these models to specific downstream tasks. Adapting these
036                 models via supervised fine-tuning requires collecting target datasets of videos,
037                 which is challenging and tedious. In this work, we utilize pre-trained reward
038                 models that are learned via preferences on top of powerful vision discriminative
039                 models to adapt video diffusion models. These models contain dense gradient
040                 information with respect to generated RGB pixels, which is critical to efficient
041                 learning in complex search spaces, such as videos. We show that backpropagating
042                 gradients from these reward models to a video diffusion model can allow for
043
                    compute and sample efficient alignment. We show results across a variety of
                    reward models and video diffusion models, demonstrating that our approach can
044
                    learn much more efficiently in terms of reward queries and computation than
045
                    prior gradient-free approaches. More visualization are available at https://
046                 vader-anonymous.github.io/

049   1       I NTRODUCTION
050
051   We would like to build systems capable of generating videos for a wide array of applications, ranging
052   from movie production, creative story-boarding, on-demand entertainment, AR/VR content genera-
053   tion, and planning for robotics. The most common current approach involves training foundational
      video diffusion models on extensive web-scale datasets. However, this strategy, while crucial, mainly


                                                                               1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      produces videos that resemble typical online content, featuring dull colors, suboptimal camera angles,
055   and inadequate alignment between text and video content.
056
057   Contrast this with the needs of an animator who wishes to bring a storyboard to life based on a
058
      script and a few preliminary sketches. Such creators are looking for output that not only adheres
      closely to the provided text but also maintains temporal consistency and showcases desirable camera
059
      perspectives. Relying on general-purpose generative models may not suffice to meet these specific
060
      requirements. This discrepancy stems from the fact that large-scale diffusion models are generally
061   trained on a broad spectrum of internet videos, which does not guarantee their efficacy for particular
062   applications. Training these models to maximize likelihood across a vast dataset does not necessarily
063   translate to high-quality performance for specialized tasks. Moreover, the internet is a mixed bag
064   when it comes to content quality, and models trained to maximize likelihood might inadvertently
065   replicate lower-quality aspects of the data. This leads us to the question: How can we tailor diffusion
066   models to produce videos that excel in task-specific objectives, ensuring they are well-aligned with
067   the desired outcomes?
068   The conventional approach to aligning generative models in the language and image domains begins
069   with supervised fine-tuning (Rafailov et al., 2024; Brooks et al., 2023). This involves collecting
070   a target dataset that contains expected behaviors, followed by fine-tuning the generative model on
071   this dataset. Applying this strategy to video generation, however, presents a significantly greater
072   challenge. It requires obtaining a dataset of target videos, a task that is not only more costly and
073   laborious than similar endeavors in language or image domains, but also significantly more complex.
074   Furthermore, even if we were able to collect a video target dataset, the process would have to be
075
      repeated for every new video task, making it prohibitively expensive. Is there a different source of
      signal we can use for aligning video diffusion, instead of collecting a target dataset of desired videos?
076
077   Reward models play a crucial role in aligning image and text generations (Schuhmann, 2022; Wu
078   et al., 2023; Lambert et al., 2024). These models are generally built on top of powerful image or
079   text discriminative models such as CLIP or BERT (Radford et al., 2021; Bardes et al., 2023; Tong
080   et al., 2022). To use them as reward models, people either fine-tune them via small amounts of human
081
      preferences data (Schuhmann, 2022) or use them directly without any fine-tuning; for instance, CLIP
      can be used to improve image-text alignment or object detectors can be used to remove or add objects
082
      in the images (Prabhudesai et al., 2023).
083
084   This begs the question, how should reward models be used to adapt the generation pipeline of
085   diffusion models? There are two broad categories of approaches, those that utilize reward gradients
086   (Prabhudesai et al., 2023; Clark et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023), and others that use the reward only as a
087
      scalar feedback and instead rely on estimated policy gradients (Black et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023).
      It has been previously found that utilizing the reward gradient directly to update the model can be
088
      much more efficient in terms of the number of reward queries, since the reward gradient contains
089
      rich information of how the reward function is affected by the diffusion generation (Prabhudesai
090   et al., 2023; Clark et al., 2023). However, in text-to-image generation space, reward gradient-free
091   approaches are still dominant (Sauer et al., 2024), since these methods can be easily trained within 24
092   hours and the efficiency gains of leveraging reward gradients are not significant.
093
      In this work, we find that as we increase the dimensionality of generation i.e transition from image to
094
      video, the gap between the reward gradient and policy gradient based approaches increases. This is
095
      because of the additional amount and increased specificity of feedback that is backpropagated to the
096   model. For reward gradient based approaches, the feedback gradients linearly scale with respect to
097   the generated resolution, as it yields distinct scalar feedback for each spatial and temporal dimension.
098   In contrast, policy gradient methods receive a single scalar feedback for the entire video output. We
099   test this hypothesis as shown in Figure 3, where we find that the gap between reward gradient and
100   policy gradient approaches increases as we increase the generated video resolution. We believe this
101   makes it crucial to backpropagate reward gradient information for video diffusion alignment.
102   We propose VADER, an approach to adapt foundational video diffusion models using the gradients
103   of reward models. VADER aligns various video diffusion models using a broad range of pre-trained
104   vision models. Specifically, we show results of aligning text-to-video (VideoCrafter, OpenSora, and
105   ModelScope) and image-to-video (Stable Video Diffusion) diffusion models, while using reward
106   models that were trained on tasks such as image aesthetics, image-text alignment, object detection,
107   video-action-classification, and video masked autoencoding. Further, we suggest various tricks to

      improve memory usage which allow us to train VADER on a single GPU with 16GB of VRAM.
109   We include qualitative visualizations that show VADER significantly improves upon the base model
110   generations across various tasks. We also show that VADER achieves much higher performance than
111   alternative alignment methods that do not utilize reward gradients, such as DPO or DDPO. Finally,
112   we show that alignment using VADER can easily generalize to prompts that were not seen during
113   training.

      Denoising diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020) have made significant
150   progress in generative capabilities across various modalities such as images, videos and 3D shapes
151   (Ho et al., 2022a;b; Liu et al., 2023). These models are trained using large-scale unsupervised or
152   weakly supervised datasets. This form of training results in them having capabilities that are very
153   general; however, most end use-cases of these models have specific requirements, such as high-fidelity
154   generation (Schuhmann, 2022) or better text alignment (Wu et al., 2023).
155
      To be suitable for these use-cases, models are often fine-tuned using likelihood (Blattmann et al.,
156   2023; Brooks et al., 2023) or reward-based objectives (Black et al., 2023; Prabhudesai et al., 2023;
157   Clark et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023). Likelihood
158   objectives are often difficult to scale, as they require access to the preferred behaviour datasets.
159   Reward or preference based datasets on the other hand are much easier to collect as they require a
160   human to simply provide preference or reward for the data generated by the generative model. Further,
161   widely available pre-trained vision models can also be used as reward models, thus making it much
      easier to do reward fine-tuning (Black et al., 2023; Prabhudesai et al., 2023). The standard approach


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      for reward or preference based fine-tuning is to do reinforcement learning via policy gradients (Black
163   et al., 2023; Wallace et al., 2023). For instance, the work of Lee et al. (2023) does reward-weighted
164   likelihood and the work of Black et al. (2023) applies PPO (Schulman et al., 2017). Recent works of
165   Prabhudesai et al. (2023) and Clark et al. (2023) find that instead of using policy gradients, directly
166   backpropagating gradients from the reward model to diffusion process helps significantly with sample
167   efficiency.
168
      A recent method, DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024; Wallace et al., 2023), does not train an explicit reward
169   model but instead directly optimizes on the human preference data. While this makes the pipeline
170   much simpler, it doesn’t solve the sample inefficiency issue of policy gradient methods, as it still
171   backpropagates a single scalar feedback for the entire video output.
172
      While we have made significant progress in aligning image diffusion models, this has remained
173
      challenging for video diffusion models (Blattmann et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). In this work, we
174
      take up this challenging task. We find that naively applying prior techniques of image alignment
175   (Prabhudesai et al., 2023; Clark et al., 2023) to video diffusion can result in significant memory
176   overheads. Further, we demonstrate how widely available image or video discriminative models can
177   be used to align video diffusion models. Concurrent to our work, InstructVideo (Yuan et al., 2023)
178   also aligns video diffusion models via human preference; however, this method requires access to a
179   dataset of videos. Such a dataset is difficult to obtain for each different task, and becomes difficult
180   to scale especially to large numbers of tasks. In this work, we show that one can easily align video
181   diffusion models using pre-trained reward models while not assuming access to any video dataset.

186   Diffusion models have emerged as a powerful paradigm in generative modeling. These models
187   operate by modeling a data distribution through a sequential process of adding and removing noise.
188   The forward diffusion process transforms a data sample x into a completely noised state over a series
189   of steps T . This process is defined by the following equation:

194   the cumulative product of αi = 1 − βi , which indicates the proportion of the original data’s signal

196   The reverse diffusion process reconstructs the original data sample from its noised version by
197   progressively denoising it through a learned model. This model is represented by ϵθ (xt ; t) and

253   take advantage of popular, large-scale image-text models such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), we can
254   take the following approach. For the entire video to be well aligned, each of the individual frames of
255   the video likely need to have high similarity with the context c. Given an image-context similarity
256   model gimg , we have:

268   to evaluate the similarity between the whole video and the text. This would allow the model to
269   generate videos where certain frames deviate from the context, allowing for richer, more diverse
      expressive generations. This also allows generating videos with more motion and movement, which

330
      Reducing Memory Overhead: Training video diffusion models is very memory intensive, as the
331   amount of memory linearly scales with respect to the number of generated frames. While VADER
332   significantly improves the sample efficiency of fine-tuning these models, it comes at the cost of
333   increased memory. This is because the differentiable reward is computed on the generated frame,
334   which is a result of sequential de-noising steps.
335
      Standard Tricks - To reduce the memory usage we use LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) that only updates a
336
      subset of the model parameters, further we use mixed precision that stores non-trainable parameters
337   in fp16. Also, gradient checkpointing is applied. For the long horizon tasks, offload the storage of the
338   backward computation graph from the GPU memory to the CPU memory.
339
340
      Truncated Backprop - Additionally, In our experiments we only backpropagate through the diffusion
      model for one timestep, instead of backpropagating through multiple timesteps (Prabhudesai et al.,
341
      2023), and have found this approach to obtain competitive results while requiring much less memory.
342
343   Subsampling Frames - Since all the video diffusion models we consider are latent diffusion models,
344   we further reduce memory usage by not decoding all the frames to RGB pixels. Instead, we randomly
345   subsample the frames and only decode and apply loss on the subsampled ones.
346   We conduct our experiments on 2 A6000 GPUS (48GB VRAM), and our model takes an average of
347   12 hours to train. However, our codebase supports training on a single GPU with 16GB VRAM.

      5    R ESULTS
350
351
      In this work, we focus on fine-tuning various conditional video diffusion models, including
352
      VideoCrafter (Chen et al., 2024) , Open-Sora (Zheng et al., 2024) , Stable Video Diffusion (Blattmann
353   et al., 2023) and ModelScope (Wang et al., 2023), through a comprehensive set of reward models
354   tailored for images and videos. These include the Aesthetic model for images (Schuhmann, 2022),
355   HPSv2 (Wu et al., 2023) and PickScore (Kirstain et al., 2023) for image-text alignment, YOLOS
356   (Fang et al., 2021) for object removal, VideoMAE for action classification (Tong et al., 2022), and
357   V-JEPA (Bardes et al., 2023) self-supervised loss for temporal consistency. Our experiments aim to
358   answer the following questions:
359
360          • How does VADER compare against gradient-free techniques such as DDPO or DPO regard-
361
               ing sample efficiency and computational demand?
362          • To what extent can the model generalize to prompts that are not seen during training?
363          • How do the fine-tuned models compare against one another, as judged by human evaluators?
364          • How does VADER perform across a variety of image and video reward models?
365
366   This evaluation framework assesses the effectiveness of VADER in creating high-quality, aligned
367   video content from a range of input conditioning.
368
369   Baselines. We compare VADER against the following methods:
370
371          • VideoCrafter (Chen et al., 2024), Open-Sora 1.2 (Zheng et al., 2024), and ModelScope
372
               (Wang et al., 2023) are current state-of-the-art (publicly available) text-to-video diffusion
               models. We serve them as base models for fine-tuning in text-to-video space.
373
374          • Stable Video Diffusion (Blattmann et al., 2023) is the current state-of-art publicly image-
375            to-video diffusion model. We use it for all our experiments in image-to-video space.
376          • DDPO (Black et al., 2023) is a recent image diffusion alignment method that uses policy
377            gradients to adapt diffusion model weights. Specifically, it applies PPO algorithm (Schulman
               et al., 2017) to the diffusion denoising process. We extend it to adapt video diffusion models.


                                                         7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
             • Diffusion-DPO (Wallace et al., 2023) extends the recent development of Direct Preference
379            Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) in the LLM space to image diffusion models.
380            They show that directly modeling the likelihood using the preference data can alleviate the
381            need for a reward model. We extend their implementation to align video diffusion models,
382            where we use the reward model to obtain the required preference data.
383
384   Reward models.      We use the following reward models to fine-tune the video diffusion model.
385
             • Aesthetic Reward Model: We use the LAION aesthetic predictor V2 (Schuhmann, 2022),
386
               which takes an image as input and outputs its aesthetic score in the range of 1-10. The model
387
               is trained on top of CLIP image embeddings.
388
389
             • Human Preference Reward Models: We use HPSv2 (Wu et al., 2023) and PickScore
               (Kirstain et al., 2023), which take as input an image-text pair and predict human preference.
390
391          • Object Removal: We design a reward model based on YOLOS (Fang et al., 2021), an object
392
               detection model, from which a video model learns to remove the target object category.
393          • Video Action Classification: We employ a reward model based on VideoMAE (Tong et al.,
394            2022). Our reward is the probability predicted by the action classifier given a video as input.
395          • Temporal Consistency via V-JEPA: We also use V-JEPA (Bardes et al., 2023) as our reward
396            model to improve temporal consistency, where the reward is the negative of the masked
397            autoencoding loss in the V-JEPA feature space.

      5.1   S AMPLE AND C OMPUTATIONAL E FFICIENCY
402
403   Training of large-scale video diffusion models is done by a small set of entities with access to a large
404   amount of computing; however, fine-tuning of these models is done by a large set of entities with
405   access to a small amount of computing. Thus, it becomes imperative to have fine-tuning approaches
406   that boost both sample and computational efficiency.
407
      In this section, we compare VADER’s sample and compute efficiency with other reinforcement
408
      learning methods such as DDPO and DPO. In Figure 5, we visualize the reward curves during
409   training, where the x-axis in the upper half of the figure is the number of reward queries and the one in
410   the bottom half is the GPU-hours. As can be seen, VADER is significantly more sample and compute
411   efficient than DDPO or DPO. This is mainly due to the fact that we send dense gradients from the
412   reward model to the diffusion weights, while the baselines only backpropagate scalar feedback.

      A.3   BASELINES
811
812   DDPO: We implement the approach in (Black et al., 2023), by closely following the code from
813   kvablack/ddpo-pytorch. We adopt the same code structure, which computes the log probability for
814   each ddim denoising step, for the video diffusion model. This log probability is used to compute
815   policy gradient, following the method in (Black et al., 2023). We use the PPO version of the method
816   as opposed to only using policy gradient, since this is reported to give slightly better performance.
817   We use the same LoRA and gradient checkpointing approach that is used in VADER for updating the
818
      video diffusion model.
819   DiffusionDPO: Our implementation for the approach in (Wallace et al., 2023) builds on the code
820   from SalesforceAIResearch/DiffusionDPO. We alternate between sampling from the diffusion model,
821   and training the model via the DPO objective, similar to the process in DDPO. Samples are added to
822   a replay buffer (since offline samples can be used for DPO training), and we use batches sampled
823   from the buffer for training. Given pairs of video generations, we assign them as V w and V l based
824
      on rewards from the reward model, where V w obtains higher reward. We then use the exact same
      loss function as them. We set β, the KL penalty in DPO to be 5000 following the standard. Just as
825
      for DDPO, the model is updated using same LoRA training and gradient checkpointing approach as
826
      in VADER.
