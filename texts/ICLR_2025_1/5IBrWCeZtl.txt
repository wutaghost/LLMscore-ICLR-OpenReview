                                                        A BSTRACT
008
009             Generative and representation models, whether trained independently or evolved
010             separately, require high-quality, diverse training data, imposing limitations on their
011             advancement. Specifically, self-supervised learning, as a popular paradigm for
012             representation learning, decreases the reliance on labeled data in representation
013             models. However, it still necessitates large datasets, specialized data augmenta-
014             tion techniques, and tailored training strategies. While generative models have
015             shown promise in generating diverse data, ensuring semantic consistency is still a
016             challenge. This paper introduces a novel co-evolution framework (referred to as
017
                CORE) designed to address these challenges through the mutual enhancement of
                generative and representation models. Without incurring additional, unacceptable
018
                training overhead compared to independent training, the generative model utilizes
019
                semantic information from the representation model to enhance the quality and
020             semantic consistency of generated data. Simultaneously, the representation model
021             gains from the diverse data produced by the generative model, leading to richer
022             and more generalized representations. By iteratively applying this co-evolution
023             framework, both models can be continuously enhanced. Experiments demonstrate
024             the effectiveness of the co-evolution framework across datasets of varying scales
025             and resolutions. For example, implementing our framework in LDM can reduce the
026             FID from 43.40 to 20.13 in unconditional generation tasks over the ImageNet-1K
027             dataset. In more challenging scenarios, such as tasks with limited data, this frame-
028
                work significantly outperforms independent training of generative or representation
                model. Furthermore, employing the framework in a self-consuming loop effectively
029
                mitigates model collapse. Our code will be publicly released.

      extract semantic information from real-world data, have made substantial advances across a range of

055   Ma et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2024; Lai et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Yin et al., 2023; Deitke et al., 2024;

061
      However, both generative models and representation models yet face several real-world challenges. In
062
      the challenge (i) of generative models: the SoTA generative models such as diffusion models (Song
063   et al., 2020; Ho et al., 2020) requires training on highly diverse and high-quality data (Rombach et al.,
064   2022), where a lack of diversity or quality in the data can prevent the models from accurately capturing
065   the underlying distribution, resulting in issues such as mode collapse (Shumailov et al., 2024). Such
066   extensive training datasets together with the large neural model architectures involved (Yang et al.,
067   2023) therefore require significant computational resources, yet the trained model often generates sam-
068   ples with incorrect semantic information, contradicting real-world physical observations. Similarly, in
069   the challenge (ii) of representation models, a diverse and high-quality labeled data is the key. Though
070   self-supervised learning (Chen et al., 2020; Oquab et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2021) reduces the need for
071
      labeled datasets, it requires significantly larger and diverse datasets along with carefully designed data
      augmentation and training strategies (Chen et al., 2020), encountering significant computational costs.
072
073   Building on previous studies (Huh et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024) that as model sizes expand and
074   training tasks diversify, distinct models tend to converge on similar representations, we observe a
075   consistent trend toward a shared statistical model capable of capturing the fundamental structure
076   of real-world phenomena. We further extend this insight and argue that deep representation and
077   generative models essentially perform the same task: capturing the underlying distribution and
078   structural attributes of real-world data, whether for generation or representation.
079
      To this end, we draw inspiration from the double helical structure of DNA and propose a helical
080   co-evolution strategy for generation and representation. This approach simultaneously addresses
081   challenge (i) and challenge (ii): it leverages representation models to enhance generative ability
082   models to capture the distribution of real-world data, and employs generative models to produce
083   high-quality yet diverse data to improve the training of representation models. Specifically, we
084   propose three technical frameworks: (1) R2G Framework leverages a representation model to
085   generate semantic features z for each input sample x. These features are used as additional guidance
086   to train a generative model by maximizing p(x|θ, z). (2) G2R Framework employs a generative
087   model to generate diverse samples x′ from the original input x. The generated samples are utilized
088
      to improve the representation model by optimizing p(z′ |ϕ, x′ ). (3) CORE Framework built on
      frameworks (1) and (2), the co-evolution framework iteratively strengthens both the generative
089
      and representation models through a feedback loop, progressively enhancing their performance.
090
      We present the following five key contributions below, laying the groundwork for a co-evolution
091   paradigm in representation and generative learning.
092
093   (a) Intriguingly, we find that even a lightly trained representation model effectively triggers our R2G
094       framework. To illustrate, employing a representation model trained on CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky
095       et al., 2009) requiring less than 16 GPU-minutes, we can significantly improve the training of
096
          ADM (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021), leading to a 30% reduction in the FID score. For further
          details, refer to Section 4.1 .
097
      (b) By employing a pre-trained generative model from R2G, we can generate diverse and realistic
098
          samples that improve the training and recognition performance of existing representation models.
099   (c) By identifying the efficacy of mutual assistance between representation and generative models,
100       we introduce the task of co-evolution between generative and representation models and propose a
101       straightforward framework (see CORE in Section 3 ) to facilitate this. Notably, there is minimal
102       additional training overhead in each iteration of the co-evolution training loop.
103   (d) Our extensive experiments indicate that applying (a) R2G, (b) G2R, or (c) CORE within the
104       training of generative or representation models effectively enhances their performance. These
105       experiments are conducted across various datasets and tasks (see Section 4.2 and Section 4.3 ).
106   (e) Additionally, we apply this co-evolution task in both data-scarce and self-consuming loop
107
          scenarios, demonstrating the significant potential of this method to address real-world challenges
          and provide novel insights to the community (see Section 4.5 and Section 4.6 ).

      2    R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Existing research has extensively investigated the unidirectional support between representation
111   models and generative models. Our co-evolutionary framework, however, introduces two fundamental
112   distinctions: (a) It functions as a multi-round, iterative process rather than a single-round one. (b) It
113   alleviates the pressure on individual model design, enabling the inclusion of suboptimal models and
114   incorporating the training process into the co-evolutionary framework.
115   In contrast, prior uni-directional approaches often necessitate a robust, well-trained model as a basis
116   (Li et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b), typically requiring significant data and computational resources
117   that might be difficult to access.
118   Our framework is constructed on advanced generative and representation models. Specifically, we
119   emphasize diffusion models for generative tasks (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song &
120   Ermon, 2019), which are now the dominant paradigm in image generation. Below, we examine how
121   representation models support generative models and vice versa.
122
123   Generative models. Generative models have become pivotal in artificial intelligence, enabling
124
      the creation of synthetic data with impressive realism and diversity. Deep learning has notably
      advanced this field through the introduction of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow
125
      et al., 2014) and Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma, 2013), significantly enhancing image
126
      generation capabilities. However, GANs encounter several architectural challenges (Dhariwal &
127   Nichol, 2021), primarily arising from the unstable nature of training the generator and discriminator
128   simultaneously. This instability often leads to mode collapse, where the generator produces uniform
129   samples. Diffusion models, inspired by non-equilibrium thermodynamics, present an alternative by
130   increasing system entropy over time to enhance randomness. Recent advancements by OpenAI have
131   substantially improved the practicality of diffusion models in real-world applications (Song et al.,
132   2023; Yang et al., 2023).
133
134   Generative model training/sampling with representation models. Using an external representa-
135
      tion model to assist a generative model enables more controllable generative processes (Ramesh et al.,
      2022; Nichol et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022). By leveraging a pre-trained representation model,
136
      control information can be extracted and used as a condition during the training of the generative
137
      model. Additionally, gradient information from the discriminative model can guide category-related
138   sampling, further enhancing control over the generation process (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021).
139
      To obtain a representation to assist in training a conditioned generative model, a self-supervised model
140
      like SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) and DINO (Caron et al., 2021), or supervised model like CLIP (Rad-
141   ford et al., 2021) can be directly used, where the model outputs an embedding vector that serves as the
142   conditioning information. These representation vectors can be further clustered to obtain cluster-level
143   information (Adaloglou et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2023), or multiple neighboring representations can
144   be leveraged for enhanced control (Blattmann et al., 2022). Regarding the granularity of the condi-
145   tional information obtained, finer-grained conditional signals are also possible beyond image-level
146   representations, such as bounding boxes and segmentation maps (Hu et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2024).
147   Recently, the Representation Diffusion Model (RDM) proposed by RCG (Li et al., 2023) has been
148   introduced to model the representation space effectively. In the training of generative models,
149   particularly pixel generators, the output from a well-trained self-supervised encoder is utilized as the
150   conditioning input. This approach by RCG shows superior performance in non-label-conditioned
151   generation, improving training on unlabeled datasets. However, the complexity of the framework
152   requires additional training for the RDM. In contrast, our proposed R2G method eliminates the need
153
      for a well-trained representation model and avoids the additional training required for the RDM.
154
      Training representation models with generated data. Generative models can produce virtually
155
      limitless samples for downstream model training. Leveraging advanced Diffusion Models to assist
156   in the training of representation models has led to significant performance gains across various
157   tasks (Wang et al., 2024b; Tian et al., 2024a;b; Jahanian et al., 2021; Afkanpour et al., 2024).
158   Existing studies have demonstrated the potential of synthesizing data through generative models
159   in supervised learning (Azizi et al., 2023; He et al., 2022; Sarıyıldız et al., 2023), self-supervised
160   learning (Chen et al., 2024b), and adversarial training (Wang et al., 2023). By using label information
161   from reference datasets to generate image data, or by combining large language models (LLMs)
      with image generation models to produce vast amounts of image-text paired data (Hammoud et al.,


                                                         3

162
      2024), it is possible to achieve results that match or even surpass those of models trained on real data.
163   AdaInf (Wang et al., 2024b) exemplifies how generative models can enhance model characterization.
164   It utilizes 1 million images generated by a generative model to augment the original training data.
165   Through data inflation, reweighting, and weak augmentation techniques, AdaInf achieves improved
166   self-supervised learning performance by effectively blending synthetic and original data.
167   To generate diverse and realistic samples for training, AdaInf requires a well-trained and powerful
168   generative model. However, accessing such a model is typically challenging, especially for complex
169   datasets like ImageNet-1K (Deng et al., 2009).

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
172
173   Our research introduces a novel approach to facilitate the Co-evolution of Representation models
174   and Generative models (CORE), as illustrated in Figure 1 . It aims to establish a viable evolutionary
175   paradigm for different types of models, using data as the bridging element. Specifically, CORE
176   is systematically divided into two stages: R2G and G2R. These stages enhance the training of
177   generative and representation models by leveraging their interactions. Notably, these stages can form
178   a feedback loop, allowing continuous evolution over time.
179   Additionally, by setting the maximization of the log-likelihood of the data log p(x) as our objective,
180   we conduct a theoretical analysis (see Appendix A ). This analysis reveals that the Evidence Lower
181   Bound (ELBO (Kingma, 2013)) of this objective can be decomposed into two distinct components:
182   one targeting the learning of representation models and the other focusing on generative models.
183   This decomposition further substantiates our position that both generative and representation models
184   collaboratively learn the distribution of real-world data and can naturally assist each other.
185

      3.1   R2G: FACILITATING DATA G ENERATION T HROUGH R EPRESENTATION M ODELS
187   Modern generative models are capable of producing diverse samples; however, they continue to
188   face challenges with unrealistic and semantically inconsistent data generation (Abdollahzadeh et al.,
189   2023; Yang et al., 2023). Prior research (Sun et al., 2024; Zimmermann et al., 2021) indicates that
190   features generated by representation models retain substantial information from the original samples,
191   suggesting a high mutual information (Shannon, 1948) between features DZ and samples DX . This
192   leads us to conjecture that representation models are naturally deconstructing complex samples into
193   fundamental, informative, and compact “seeds” (features) that encapsulate the essential information
194
      of the original samples. We posit that these “seeds” can be reconstructed with high fidelity into
      their original samples, preserving high semantic information through a generative process. The
195
      comprehensive technical approach is detailed as follows.

      Usage of representation models in the R2G framework. While our R2G does not impose
217   constraints on the representation models used, different representation models vary in their ability to
218   capture image information, leading to variations in the performance of conditional generative models
219   trained with these representations. In this study, we employ our R2G approach using self-supervised
220   learnt representation models as the core components for dataset DXZ , following the intuitions below:
221   (a) Building on prior studies (Bordes et al., 2022; Zimmermann et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2024), self-
222       supervised learnt models capture semantic information more comprehensively than supervised
223       learning models. This aligns with our requirements as outlined in Section 3.1 .
224   (b) These models are particularly advantageous for application to unlabeled datasets, enabling
225       training of f ϕ solely on samples DX when pre-trained models are not accessible from external
226       sources, such as public internet repositories.
227   Nonetheless, we rigorously evaluate the effectiveness of different representation models within our
228   R2G framework to enhance the training of diffusion models (see Section 4 ).

      4     E XPERIMENTS
299
      To assess the effectiveness of our approach, we begin by examining R2G (see Section 4.1 ) and
300
      G2R (see Section 4.2 ) individually. This initial analysis reveals how improvements in either the
301   generative model or the representation model can positively impact the training of the other. Building
302   on these findings, we then apply the CORE framework (see Section 4.3 ), evaluating its performance
303   across datasets with varying scales and resolutions. Furthermore, to showcase the versatility of this
304   framework, we expand our evaluation by examining its effectiveness with various self-supervised
305   representation learning methods and different generative models (see Section 4.4 ).
306   Beyond standard experimental settings, we assess the effectiveness of CORE under unconventional
307   and extreme conditions. One scenario involves training with limited data (see Section 4.6 ), which
308   poses a substantial challenge for any model (Abdollahzadeh et al., 2023). Another critical scenario is
309   the self-consuming loop (Shumailov et al., 2024) (see Section 4.5 ), where the training data for the
310   next generation is derived from samples produced by the generative model of the previous generation.
311   Such a loop often leads to model collapse issues in modern generative models.

      Implementations on CIFAR-10/CIFAR-100. In our experiments on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-

409   generative model and a ResNet-50 trained with the SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) method as the

411

412   a straightforward technique shown to enhance performance as indicated in the guided diffusion
413   literature (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Song et al., 2021). All other settings are maintained in alignment
414   with the original ADM model, including 1,000 diffusion steps, a base network width of 128, and
415   attention mechanisms at resolutions of 16 and 8.
416   In the initial phase, we train ResNet-50 exclusively with the original training dataset, omitting any
417   synthetic data. During subsequent iterations of the co-evolution process with G2R, we incorporate
418   synthetic data produced by the generative model and retrain the representation model from scratch.
419   The projector’s hidden dimension is set to 2,048, with an output dimension of 128. This output is
420   then utilized by the generative model for R2G. During each G2R phase of the co-evolution loop, the
421   generative model produces 100,000 samples.

425

476   4.5         U SE CORE TO H ELP S ELF - CONSUMING L OOP
477
      Self-Consuming Loop (Alemohammad et al., 2024) training refers to the cyclic process in generative
478
      model training where synthetic data generated by earlier generations is used as training data for
479
      subsequent generations. Studying this phenomenon is crucial, as the growing prevalence of synthetic
480   data on the internet and within standard datasets suggests that future models will likely rely, at
481   least in part, on a mix of real and synthetic data, thereby forming a self-consuming loop. In the
482   absence of sufficient fresh real data, such a loop can lead to a degradation in the quality (precision)
483   or diversity (recall) of generative models across generations, which is known as Model Autophagy
484   Disorder (MAD) (Alemohammad et al., 2024; Bertrand et al., 2024). Self-consuming loop training
485   can be categorized into several types (Alemohammad et al., 2024): Fully Synthetic Loop, Synthetic
      Augmentation Loop, and Fresh Data Loop.

      5     L IMITATION AND C ONCLUSION
529   We present CORE, a preliminary attempt to explore the co-evolution learning of generative and
530   representation models. It consists of two interdependent frameworks, R2G and G2R, which together
531   create a closed-loop system. The semantic information extracted by the representation model
532   facilitates the training of the generative model, while the generative model, in turn, enhances the
533   representation model by synthesizing new data. We empirically validate the effectiveness of both G2R
534   and R2G, and evaluate the performance of CORE across various datasets. We further demonstrate
535   its potential through two scenarios: the self-consuming loop and data-scarce learning. However, the
536   conditions necessary for effectively launching CORE and how to efficiently approach its performance
537
      limits with minimal training costs require more in-depth theoretical research and potentially larger-
      scale experimental exploration. Nonetheless, we hope that our current progress can inspire researchers
538
      to investigate the potential of co-evolution between two or even more models, and perhaps provide
539
      valuable insights.

813
814
      (a) Dataset: X = {x(1) , x(2) , . . . , x(N ) }, where each x(i) is generated from a joint latent variable

816   (b) Latent variables: z: A representation distribution to be learned from the data. ϵ: A latent
817       variable with a known conditional distribution p(ϵ|z) given z.
818   (c) Objective: Develop an ELBO that enables learning both a good representation p(z) and a robust
819       generative model p(x|z, ϵ) by maximizing this ELBO.

853   Simplifying the ELBO. To make the ELBO tractable and implementable, we make specific
854   assumptions about the structure of the variational posterior q(z, ϵ|x). A common and practical choice
855   is to assume that ϵ is conditionally independent of z given x, or to model certain dependencies to
856   reflect the generative process. However, to align closely with the generative model structure p(ϵ|z), a
857   sensible assumption is:
858                                        q(z, ϵ|x) = q(ϵ|z, x)q(z|x)                                  (17)

860   model’s dependency, leading to:
861
                                             q(z, ϵ|x) = q(ϵ|z)q(z|x)                                      (18)
862
863   This approximation assumes that given z, the posterior over ϵ does not directly depend on x, which
      aligns with p(ϵ|z) being known and independent of x.

866   maintain tractability. Given the factorization q(z, ϵ|x) = q(ϵ|z)q(z|x), the KL divergence becomes:
867

883                           L(x) = Eq(z|x)p(ϵ|z) [log p(x|z, ϵ)] − KL(q(z|x)|p(z))                    (24)

889   Optimization and learning. By maximizing the ELBO across all data points, we achieve:
890
      (a) Representation Learning (p(z)): The term KL(q(z|x)∥p(z)) guides the learning of p(z) to be
891
          a meaningful prior that captures the underlying structure of the data.
892
893
      (b) Generative Modeling (p(x|z, ϵ)): The expected log-likelihood E[log p(x|z, ϵ)] ensures that the
          generative model can accurately produce/reconstruct data points from the latent variables.
894
895   (c) Prior Alignment (p(ϵ|z)): The expectation E[KL(q(ϵ|z)∥p(ϵ|z))] ensures that the inferred ϵ
896       given z follows the known conditional distribution, integrating prior knowledge into the model.

1196   our self-consuming loop, which requires that the data for the next round of generative model training
