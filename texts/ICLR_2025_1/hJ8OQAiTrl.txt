009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Effective reasoning on real-world graphs necessitates a thorough understanding
012            and optimal utilization of structural information from graph structure and textual
013            information corresponding to nodes and edges. Recent research has primarily
014            focused on two paradigms: employing graph neural networks to capture struc-
015            tural features and utilizing language models to process textual information, re-
016            spectively. While these approaches have shown impressive performance, inte-
017            grating structural and textual information presents significant challenges. To be
018
               more specific, concurrently training graph neural networks and language models
               is particularly challenging, primarily due to the scale of real-world graphs. Ad-
019
               ditionally, the dynamic set of answer nodes poses a difficulty to the design of
020
               joint optimization objectives. This paper introduces a novel framework, named
021            CoST, tailored for graph reasoning tasks. The proposed optimization objective
022            enables alternating training of the GNN and PLM, leading to the generation of
023            effective text representations by the PLM model, thereby enhancing the reasoning
024            capabilities of the GNN model. Empirical results demonstrate that CoST achieves
025            state-of-the-art performance across representative benchmark datasets.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Significant progress has been achieved through the utilization of deep learning methods to enhance
031   reasoning tasks in recent years. The realm of reasoning encompasses a diverse array of tasks, in-
032   cluding natural language reasoning (Clark et al., 2020; Talmor et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2022; Suzgun
033
      et al., 2023), visual reasoning (Battaglia et al., 2016; Weston et al., 2016; Hu et al., 2017), multi-
      modal reasoning (Johnson et al., 2017; Nam et al., 2017; Cadène et al., 2019; Lu et al., 2022; Yang
034
      et al., 2023b), mathematical reasoning (Saxton et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2019a; Lu et al., 2023;
035
      Yang et al., 2023a; Gou et al., 2024), and sophisticated hard science reasoning (Fragkiadaki et al.,
036   2016; Bongini et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2022).
037
038   Graph-structured data plays a pivotal role in modeling complex and dynamic systems and the inter-
039
      connections among system components (Zartman & Gordon, 1981; Pan et al., 2021; Das & Soylu,
      2023), making it a crucial data type in reasoning tasks (Chen et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2019b; 2021).
040
      Reasoning on graphs typically involves a query and aims to predict a set of nodes as answers. In
041
      recent years, a considerable direction of research has delved into enhancing reasoning capabilities
042   on graphs. Traditional methods (Katz, 1953; Page, 1999; Borgatti & Everett, 2006) often rely on
043   statistical data and heuristic metrics to extract graph data features for reasoning purposes. More
044   recently, graph neural networks (GNNs) have demonstrated remarkable performance across various
045   graph-related tasks, including graph reasoning. GNNs (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Velickovic et al.,
046   2018; Xu et al., 2019) employ a message-passing mechanism that enables them to capture trans-
047   ferable invariances of structural nature among graphs, enhancing their ability to conduct reasoning
048   tasks effectively. However, these studies still encounter the issue: of solely focusing on graph struc-
049   ture while overlooking non-structural information associated with nodes and edges in graphs. In this
050
      paper, we focus on the textual information embedded in the text descriptions corresponding to nodes
      and edges.
051

053   sociated teams, suppose the goal is to answer queries such as (Kylian Mbappé, belong, ?).

078   Despite the promising application, discrete text information presents a challenge for neural networks
079   relying on stochastic gradient descent. An intuitive solution is to utilize a pre-trained language
080   model (Devlin et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2019; Dong et al., 2019; Bao et al., 2020;
081   He et al., 2021) (PLM) to convert textual data into continuous embeddings for reasoning or inte-
082   gration into graph reasoning models. Static text representations fall short in capturing contextual
083   nuances (Peters et al., 2018), highlighting the need for fine-tuning the pre-trained language model
084   for effective reasoning. However, the scale of graph data presents substantial challenges when at-
085
      tempting to fine-tune pre-trained language models in conjunction with reasoning GNN models (Zhao
      et al., 2023). In addition, for graph reasoning tasks, the probability of all candidate nodes on the
086
      graph needs to be modeled using pairwise representations under different query contexts. This re-
087
      quirement drives GNN-based models and PLM-based models to adopt different reasoning pipelines,
088   thereby posing significant challenges to the interaction and co-optimization of these models.
089
090   To overcome the aforementioned challenges, we propose an assembled graph reasoning framework
091
      that effectively Combines Structural and Textual information, named CoST. Specifically, we first
      introduce an objective to integrate both structural and textual elements into a GNN-based reason-
092
      ing model coupled with a PLM model, utilized for producing text embeddings. Additionally, we
093
      formulate an alternative objective function enabling us to optimize the GNN and PLM models in a
094   cyclical manner. Each iteration involves training both models using observed data from the training
095   graph and pseudo target generated by a separate fixed model. We conduct extensive experiments
096   on several benchmark datasets to demonstrate the superior performance of CoST. Significantly, our
097   framework enhances the performance of the GNN model by offering effective text representations
098   from the PLM model, leading to CoST achieving state-of-the-art performance on benchmarks.

494   4    R ELATED W ORK
495
496   Structure-based Methods.          Traditional methods for graph reasoning (Katz, 1953; Page, 1999;
497
      Borgatti & Everett, 2006) historically relied on statistical data and heuristic metrics to extract fea-
      tures from graph data for reasoning purposes. Recently, the widespread adoption of graph neural
498
      networks (GNNs) has enabled the learning of structural representations by encoding graph topolo-
499
      gies. Prominent frameworks (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Schlichtkrull et al., 2018; Davidson et al., 2018;
500   Vashishth et al., 2020) have embraced an auto-encoder approach, utilizing GNNs to encode node rep-
501   resentations and decode edges based on relationships between node pairs. Concurrently, alternative
502   frameworks (Zhang & Chen, 2018; Teru et al., 2020) explicitly encode the subgraph surrounding
503   each node pair for enhanced link prediction accuracy. While these methods have demonstrated su-
504   perior performance to traditional auto-encoder approaches (Zhang et al., 2020) and offer inductive
505   learning capabilities, their requirement to materialize subgraphs for each link hinders scalability, es-
506   pecially with large graphs. Subsequent advancements (Zhu et al., 2021b; 2023; Zhang & Yao, 2022;
507   Zhang et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2024) have focused on learning path-based representations for pairs,
508
      with innovative labeling strategies yielding state-of-the-art performance.
509
510
      Text-based Methods.         Text-based methods utilize pre-trained language models (Devlin et al.,
      2019; Liu et al., 2019; Lan et al., 2020; Dong et al., 2019; Bao et al., 2020) to derive text repre-
511
      sentations of nodes and relations, upon which reasoning models are developed. KG-BERT (Yao
512
      et al., 2019) applies BERT for knowledge graph reasoning, treating triples as textual sequences
513   to calculate a scoring function effectively for entities and relations by inputting their descriptions.
514   StAR (Wang et al., 2021a) combines textual encoding and graph embedding techniques for hetero-
515   geneous graph reasoning, utilizing a siamese-style textual encoder for knowledge graph triple pro-
516   cessing and incorporating structure learning for enhanced efficiency and performance. HittER (Chen
517   et al., 2021) introduces a hierarchical Transformer model that learns entity and relation representa-
518   tions by considering local graph neighborhoods. It comprises two Transformer blocks for capturing
519   entity-relation interactions and aggregating relational context, with a masked entity prediction task
520   for balancing contextual information and original entity features. SimKGC (Wang et al., 2022)
521
      proposes an efficient contrastive learning technique using pre-trained language models for knowl-
      edge graph completion, employing various types of negative samples and the InfoNCE loss function
522
      to enhance performance. In contrast, KGT5 (Saxena et al., 2022) adopts a sequence-to-sequence
523
      approach, treating the graph reasoning task as a text generation problem to streamline the model
524   architecture, achieving state-of-the-art results with a notably reduced model size.
525
526
527   5    C ONCLUSION
528
529
      This paper presents CoST, an optimized framework designed for graph reasoning tasks. While ex-
      isting methods have demonstrated remarkable performance, the integration of structural and textual
530
      data through simultaneous training of graph neural networks and language models poses notable
531
      challenges, especially with respect to the complexity of real-world graph structures. To address
532   these challenges related to training on extensive graph datasets, we employ an alternating training
533   strategy for the graph neural network (GNN) model and pre-trained language model (PLM) with a
534   variational objective. Empirical assessments validate the efficacy of our proposed model.

      A      D ISCUSSION AND C OMPARISON
865
866   In the recent research community, a subset of researchers have studied how to combine structural
867   information and textual information. The subsequent paragraphs delineate our motivation and dis-
868   tinguish our work from existing research works.
869
870   Focusing on Graph Reasoning Task. In this paper, we focus on the graph reasoning task, while
871   most previous studies (Zhu et al., 2021a; Yang et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2023)
872   attempt to exploit both textual information and graph structures to address node classification tasks.
873   Contrasting with node-level tasks like node classification, graph reasoning necessitates the model to
874   make predictions based on pairwise representations, presenting a substantial challenge in designing
875   GNN-based and PLM-based architectures, as well as in handling the interaction between these two
876
      models. Moreover, the graph reasoning task necessitates predicting across the entire node set of a
      specific graph, presenting a scalability challenge in contrast to classification tasks with predefined
877
      categories.
878
879
      Learning Effective Embedding. There are also partial works that try to combine textual infor-
880   mation and structural information to solve reasoning tasks such as knowledge graph completion (He
881   et al., 2024a). However their emphasis lies in enhancing the performance of two isolated models,
882   particularly in sparse graph scenarios, our model goes beyond solely improving performance to en-
883   compass learning effective text representation. By employing text representations as a connective
884   layer between GNN-based models and PLM-based models, our approach enhances the optimization
885   of diverse models and enhances the efficacy of text representations.

953
      relation test leakage arising from the inversion of triples in the training set, the WN18RR dataset

      was specifically formulated for evaluation purposes.

      Wikidata5M. Wikidata5M is a large-scale knowledge graph dataset accompanied by an aligned
