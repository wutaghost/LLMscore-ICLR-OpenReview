009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we introduce a novel approach for optimizing neural network training
012            by adjusting learning rates across weights of different components in Transformer
013            models. Traditional methods often apply a uniform learning rate across all network
014            layers, potentially overlooking the unique dynamics of each part. Remarkably,
015            our introduced Relative Learning Rate Schedules (RLRS) method accelerates the
016            training process by 13.6%, particularly in complex models such as the Mixture
017            of Experts (MoE). Hyperparameters of RLRS can be efficiently tuned on smaller
018
               models and then extrapolated to 27× larger ones. This simple and effective method
               results in a substantial reduction in training time and computational resources,
019
               offering a practical and scalable solution for optimizing large-scale neural networks.
020
021
022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   The learning rate is a crucial hyperparameter in Deep Learning, determining the size of the steps that
025   the optimization algorithm takes when updating model parameters during training. In the context of
026   Transformers, widely used for tasks in Natural Language Processing and other areas, the learning
027   rate significantly impacts the model’s convergence and overall performance. While higher learning
028   rates, with larger updates to the model, may generally converge faster, the training also becomes less
029   stable. Therefore, the learning rate must be carefully chosen to balance the speed and stability of the
030   training process.
031   At the same time, modern Deep Learning architectures are not homogeneous, with different parts
032   having distinct structures, serving varied purposes, and exhibiting unique behaviors. Importantly,
033   they also have individual training dynamics. As seen in Figure 1, gradient updates for different parts
034   follow different paths. This phenomenon also leads to components behaving differently depending
035   on the training phase, which can be problematic in some cases. For example, in Mixture of Experts
036   (MoE) models, the Router often stabilizes early in training, leading to deterministic routing to the
037   Experts (Xue et al., 2024).
038   Given the diversity of layers within a model, it is reasonable to expect that their requirements would
039   vary, particularly when balancing training speed and stability. Despite this, a uniform learning rate
040   is often applied across all modules. A common practice, for example, is to reduce the learning rate
041   for the whole model after the introduction of an MoE layer due to instabilities (Rajbhandari et al.,
042   2022). As a result, hyperparameters are typically tuned for the entire network, even if, plausibly, the
043   instabilities originate from a single layer. In this work, we relax the implicit assumption of a global
044
      learning rate. Since each layer serves a different purpose at different stages of the training process,
      can we improve it by tailoring the learning rate schedules accordingly?
045
046   To answer this question, we decouple learning rates in Transformers and tune them separately for
047   different model components, including Embedding and Unembedding, Attention, Feed-Forward, or,
048   in the case of Mixture of Experts architecture, Router and Experts. By tailoring the learning rate to
049   meet the specific needs of each component, we enhance the model’s overall performance and stability.
050   Furthermore, we propose a simple scheme to adjust relative learning rate values that can be effectively
051   scaled to models larger by orders of magnitude. This approach eliminates the need for extensive
052   hyperparameter searches for larger models, resulting in significant computational savings and en-
053   hancing its practical applicability. In essence, we propose the following approach: first, relative
      LRs should be tuned on a small model; later, the same relative LRs can be reused when training

         We define a decoupled learning rate as a separate learning rate schedule for different layer types (also
086
         called parts, modules, or components). Decoupled learning rate schedules enable the learning proce-
087
         dure to focus on different components during different phases of a model’s pretraining, facilitating
088      more targeted and efficient optimization process.
089
090      We specify decoupled learning rates following the structure of the cosine learning rate sched-
091
         uler (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2016), widely used for training Large Language Models (LLMs) (Touvron
         et al., 2023; Hoffmann et al., 2024). The cosine scheduler adjusts the learning rate over time according
092
         to a cosine function, starting with a high learning rate that gradually decays to a minimum value in a
093
         smooth, nonlinear manner.
094
095      The parameters we introduce are:
096       • Base LR (ηbase ) – the reference learning rate for the entire model. In a typical cosine schedule, it is
097         the initial (or maximum) learning rate, ηstart , representing the peak value during the training cycle
098         (after a possible period of warm-up).
099
          • Base LR Final Fraction (λbase ) – the fraction of the base learning rate that represents the final
100         learning rate at the end of training. The final (or minimum) learning rate is the lowest learning rate
101         value at the end of the training cycle and is given by ηend = λbase × ηbase .
102

127   2.1   P RESERVING R ELATIVE VALUES
128
129   In this section, we show that decoupled learning rates remain stable across various models, with their
130   values staying approximately constant relative to the base learning rate.
131   Tuning relative learning rate values directly on large models may be impractical due to significant
132   computational costs. To address this, we propose a method that fine-tunes these values on a smaller
133   proxy model and then transfers them to a larger model. This approach significantly reduces the need
134   for costly tuning on large models, offering substantial computational savings.
135   Our method, described in Algorithm 1, involves conducting a search for optimal values on smaller
136   models under the assumption that these relative values extrapolate effectively to larger models. This
137   search consumes only a fraction of the training time required for large models.
138
139   Algorithm 1 Relative LR Adjustment Algorithm
140    1: Find ηbase for a small model.
141    2: For each module m, find relative values λm              m
                                                       start and λend on a small model.
142    3: Find base learning rate ηbase for the large model.
143    4: Apply relative learning rates λm           m
                                          start and λend from the small model.
144
145   While we do not claim that λm             m
                                     start and λend values are optimal for larger models, they are easy to
146   use and yield substantial improvements, as shown in the next section. We leave the investigation of
147   optimal extrapolation as future work.
148   Algorithm 1 presents a simple way to find relative learning rates independently of the base learning
149   rate and apply them to both the small model and the large model. This is particularly desirable if we
150   are given a model with a base learning rate that has already been tuned. However, if this is not the
151   case, alternatively to Algorithm 1, we propose Algorithm 2, where the base learning rate is adjusted
152   again after the relative learning rates.
153
154   Algorithm 2 Relative LR Adjustment Algorithm
155    1: For a small model, find ηbase and for each module m, find relative values λm          m
                                                                                     start and λend .
156    2: Apply relative learning rates λmstart and λm
                                                     end from the small model.
157    3: Adjust base learning rate ηbase for the large model.
158
159   Algorithm 2 may perform slightly better than Algorithm 1. However, for practical purposes, applying
160   relative rates to an already tuned base model offers substantial gains, and we focus on this setting in
161   the next section. Additionally, the implementation of Step 1 of Algorithm 2 can be further expanded.
      We provide the details of our implementation in Section 3.2 and Algorithm 3.

      4.1   I NTERPRETING R ELATIVE L EARNING R ATES
273
274
      In this section, we present the numerical results and trends for relative learning rates (RLRS) and
275   analyze them with respect to each layer module. We prioritize MoE models, as RLRS yields more
276   substantial gains in this setting. Although the values have been determined experimentally, they are
277   often interpretable and aligned to counteract the existing issues of each component.
278
279
      Embedding. The relative learning rate λstart starts high (3.3 for MoE and 5 for dense) and decays
280
      to 0.6. This aggressive early training helps the Embedding stabilize quickly, as it influences the entire
281   network. Later in the training process, the learning rate is reduced to prevent drastic changes in the
282   Embeddings, ensuring the rest of the model can adjust accordingly. As seen in Section 4.3, this is the
283   only layer that prefers adjustment of relative learning rate when increasing model size; that is, while
284   other relative learning rates transfer without change, the Embedding’s rate should also be increased.
285
286   Unembedding. Unembedding handles the conversion of the model output into a probability dis-
287   tribution over the tokens in its vocabulary. We observe that, similar to the Embedding, the relative
288   learning rate gradually decreases toward the end of training. This behavior aligns with observations
289   in the literature that weights in the Unembedding may diverge, potentially causing instabilities later
290   in the training process (Chowdhery et al., 2022; Zoph et al., 2022), which would require reducing
291   gradient values.
292
293   Router. Router (or gating network) plays a crucial role in determining which Expert networks are
294   trained during the learning process. However, it has been observed that the model often learns its
295   routing decisions early in the pre-training phase, and these decisions remain largely fixed throughout
296   training (Xue et al., 2024). Once a token is assigned to an Expert, it is rarely reassigned, making it
297   difficult for the model to adapt to new or unseen data during later stages of training. Moreover, the
298   Router tends to be unstable at the early stages of training. Starting the relative learning rate with a
299
      lower value of 0.6 and then ending with 1 might help mitigate these problems.
300
301   Experts. The relative learning rate of an Expert layer increases from the smallest value of 0.3 to
302   aid stability when the Router is essentially random, and increases to the highest relative value at the
303   end, allowing the Experts to fine-tune while the Router remains largely fixed.
304
305   Attention. In Attention projection layers, the learning rate remains unchanged in the MoE model,
306   making it unique in not benefiting from relative rates.

406   5      R ELATED AND F UTURE W ORK
407
408   The literature on learning rates in Machine Learning, particularly for Transformers, highlights the
409   importance of adaptive learning rate schedules. Stochastic Weight Averaging (SWA) (Izmailov et al.,
410   2018) utilizes a modified learning rate schedule that applies a decaying learning rate during the initial
411   phase of training, followed by a constant rate for the remainder. In Sun et al. (2019), the authors
412   introduce layer-wise learning rate decay, which applies higher learning rates to top layers and lower
413   rates to bottom layers. A related concept, discriminative fine-tuning, is discussed in Howard & Ruder
414   (2018). Additionally, Everett et al. (2024) explores how various parameterizations and optimizers
415
      impact the learning process in large-scale models and proposes a per-layer learning rate strategy.

      5.1    C OMBINATION WITH T ENSOR P ROGRAMS

419
      Our method explores the transfer of relative learning rates; however, the base learning rate must still
420   be independently tuned for the extrapolated model. Approaches such as Tensor Programs (Yang,
421   2020; Yang et al., 2022) propose parameterizations that facilitate the transfer of the base learning rate.
422   By combining these two approaches, it may be possible to achieve a zero-shot transfer of RLRS.
423
      While our methods share similarities with Tensor Programs and draw inspiration from them, our
424
      project has a distinct goal. We aim to identify implicit assumptions in the tuning process and decouple
425
      parameters to devise a scheme that enables Large Language Models (LLMs) to converge in fewer
426   steps. Our extrapolations demonstrate that our optimization scheme depends on the architecture rather
427   than the model size. This scheme is defined relative to the base learning rate, which must be tuned
428   individually for each model size. Our method does not aim to facilitate learning rate transfer between
429   different model sizes and is supported by experimental evidence. We do not mathematically examine
430   the limits of parameter updates in a gradient descent step. A key difference is that our relative values
431   change dynamically during training, and our goal is to enable the model to focus on different aspects
      during pretraining.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      5.2   F INE -T UNING
433
434   Fine-tuning allows users to adapt pre-trained Large Language Models (LLMs) to more specialized
435   tasks. In traditional fine-tuning, certain model components are often “frozen” (effectively setting
436   their relative learning rates to zero) to preserve learned knowledge while adapting other parts. Our
437   proposed method introduces a more flexible approach, serving as a continuous alternative to freezing
438   parameters. This enables fine-grained control over information transfer within specific components
439   of the model. Consequently, our method could be particularly applicable to fine-tuning scenarios
440
      and complement existing methods that involve freezing parameters. Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning
      (PEFT) techniques, such as LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), address this by updating only a subset of
441
      parameters while freezing the rest. Our work aligns with more advanced methods like LoRA+ (Hayou
442
      et al., 2024), which select different learning rates for the adapter matrices, and AdaLoRA (Zhang
443   et al., 2023), which adapts the rank of the LoRA matrices, providing enhanced flexibility in the
444   fine-tuning process.
445
446
447
      6     C ONCLUSION
448
      We have presented a method for decoupling learning rate schedules across different neural network
449
      components, removing the implicit assumption of homogeneity among them, and achieving better
450
      training speed and stability as a result. This method applies to any Transformer-based model and
451   significantly enhances performance in Mixture of Experts (MoE) models. By tuning relative learning
452   rates on smaller models, this approach can be used to economically achieve significant improvements
453   in the training of order-of-magnitude larger models.
