011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013           Multimodal language models (MLLMs) are increasingly being applied in real-
014           world environments, necessitating their ability to interpret 3D spaces and compre-
015           hend temporal dynamics. Current methods often rely on specialized architectural
016
              designs or task-specific fine-tuning to achieve this. We introduce C OARSE C ORRE -
              SPONDENCES, a simple lightweight method which enhances MLLMs’ understand-
017
              ing of 3D and temporal concepts using only 2D images, without modifying the
018
              architecture or task-specific fine-tuning. Our method uses a lightweight tracking
019           model to identify primary object correspondences between frames in a video or
020           across different image viewpoints, and then conveys this information to MLLMs
021           through visual prompting. We demonstrate that this simple training-free approach
022           brings substantial gains to GPT4-V/O consistently on four benchmarks that require
023           3D and temporal understanding, including +20.5% improvement on ScanQA,
024           +9.7% on OpenEQA’s episodic memory subset, +6.0% on the long-form video
025           benchmark EgoSchema, and +11% on the R2R navigation benchmark. Addition-
026           ally, we show that C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES can also enhance open-source
027
              MLLMs’ understanding of 3D space (by +6.9% on ScanQA) when applied in both
              training and inference and that the improvement can generalize to unseen datasets
028
              such as SQA3D (+3.1%). Taken together, we show that C OARSE C ORRESPON -
029
              DENCES effectively and efficiently boosts models’ performance on downstream
030           tasks requiring 3D and/or temporal understanding.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Intelligence is multi-faceted. While multi-modal large language models (OpenAI, 2024) have shown
036   remarkable linguistic, logical and even mathematical intelligence, many remain doubtful about their
037   visual and spatial intelligence. Despite their excellent performance on visual-lingusitic tasks, many
038   recent works (Majumdar et al., 2024; Singh et al., 2024) demonstrate that state-of-the-art MLLMs
039   still struggle at 3D and long video benchmarks, performing only marginally better than blind text-only
040   baselines. These results suggest that 3D and temporal understanding are two major bottlenecks on
041   MLLMs’ path to general visual intelligence.
042   To enhance MLLMs’ 3D understanding, researchers have mainly explored three approaches: provid-
043   ing MLLMs with 3D data as input (Hong et al., 2024), designing specialized architectures for 3D
044   tasks (Hong et al., 2024), or employing supervised fine-tuning with 3D data (Chen et al., 2024). Simi-
045   larly, to boost MLLMs’ temporal understanding, prior works have proposed new model architectures
046   designed for long video understanding (Papalampidi et al., 2023; Balažević et al., 2024), or adopted
047   Socratic-based methods (Zhang et al., 2024; Kahatapitiya et al., 2024) (i.e., converting each frame of
048   a video into text using a caption model, and then using text-only LLMs to summarize).
049   In contrast to prior works, we propose a simple but effective training-free visual prompting method
050   C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES to boost 3D and temporal understanding in MLLMs. C OARSE
051   C ORRESPONDENCES uses a tracking model to extract object-level correspondences across multiple
052   images, and then represent the most salient correspondence relationships on the images through
053   visual prompting. Our method significantly boosts MLLMs’ understanding of 3D spacetime with
      only 2D image inputs and without any specialized architectural design or task-specific fine-tuning.

078
      understanding, we show that our method significantly surpasses state-of-the-art models by 20.5% and

080   for long video understanding, our method leads to a 6% gain in performance on the EgoSchema
081   benchmark (Mangalam et al., 2023). Notably, our method uses much fewer input images and, in a

083   EgoSchema, C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES surpasses state-of-the-art results with just 8 uniformly
084   sampled frames from a 3-minute video, greatly reducing the computational costs of MLLMs compared
085   to existing methods. In addition to 3D and video QA tasks, we further demonstrate that our method
086   enhances models’ performance on embodied tasks such as navigation (Krantz et al., 2020), which
087
      require strong spatial and temporal understanding, by 11% in success rate on R2R. These results
      suggest that C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES boosts MLLMs’ spatial and temporal understanding
088
      both effectively and efficiently. Last but not least, we experiment with open-source MLLMs (Liu
089
      et al., 2024a) by applying C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES in both instruction tuning and inference;
090   again, our method shows performance gains against the baseline (by 6.9% on ScanQA), and the
091   improvement even generalizes to unseen datasets such as SQA3D (+3.1%). These results suggest
092   that C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES works well universally with any model – both closed-source and
093   open-source – that can take in multiple images and understand visual markers.
094
      To further understand why our simple method brings significant improvements on spatial and temporal
095
      understanding, we conducted additional investigations with a small diagnostic benchmark on spatial
096   orientation, as orientation is a key component of spatial understanding and important to navigation
097   tasks. We manually curated a benchmark called SOT to test how well MLLMs understand left-
098   right relationships from different viewpoints on images taken by cameras with different motions.
099   Our experiments reveal that (1) even GPT-4o struggles with understanding images taken by a
100   camera moving right to left (instead of the more common motion left to right) and (2) that C OARSE
101   C ORRESPONDENCES leads to improvements on this benchmark. These results indicate that C OARSE
102   C ORRESPONDENCES mitigates the camera motion bias in MLLMs – their tendency to understand
103   images better when the camera moves in a particular direction – when processing visual signals and
104
      helps them learn a more equivariant internal visual representation.
105   Overall, we want to highlight with this work that, despite its simplicity and being underestimated for
106   semantic tasks in deep learning, visual correspondence can still bring significant utility to spatial and
107   temporal understanding in MLLMs, just as it has long contributed to 3D reconstruction Schonberger
      & Frahm (2016). Although MLLMs still exhibit some non-negligible shortcomings in spatial

117   Problem formulation. Given a question Q and a sequence or set of observations in an environment
118   [I1 , . . . , In ], our aim is to design a visual prompt P(. . .) that modifies the input image set. These
119   image inputs don’t have to be a video. They can also represent a set of images of a specific scene
120
      from multiple viewpoints. We evaluate the prompt by measuring its utility in prompting an MLLM
      M:

130   C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCE
131
132   Our prompting method, C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES, contains four steps: (1) tracking correspon-
133
      dences, (2) sparsify frames, (3) selecting, and (4) visualizing coarse correspondences.
134   (1) Tracking correspondences. Given n input images, [I1 , . . . , In ], we first use an off-the-shelf
135   video object tracking model, such as Tracking Anything (Yang et al., 2023c). This model extracts
136   class-agnostic instance segmentation masks (M1 , . . . , Mn ) for each image. Each Mi is a H × W
137   dimensional matrix where H and W are the height and width of the input image Ii . Each pixel
138   location in Mi contains an instance ID, indicating which instance the pixel at that position belongs to
139
      within the image sequence.
140   (2) Sparsify frames. Since most MLLMs contain a large number of parameters, directly using them
141   to process long image sequences is very computationally intensive. Additionally, proprietary MLLMs
142   like GPT-4O can also incur significant costs if the number of image tokens that need to be processed
143   increases. Reducing the number of input images might lose vital information necessary for MLLMs.
144   C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES strikes a balance in this tradeoff by extracting meaningful video object
145   tracks (a relatively cheaper operation) from high-frame-rate image sequences, and then samples a
146   few image inputs along with the tracks, to retain—and even improve—performance while reducing
147   the MLLM’s computation cost. From this extracted video object tracks, we perform temporal
148   downsampling, retaining only m << n uniformly sampled images and their corresponding masks,
149   denoted as [Is1 , . . . , Ism ] and [Ms1 , . . . , Msm ], where si ∈ {1, . . . , n}. This downsampling reduces
150   the number of images we feed into M.
151   (3) Selecting coarse correspondences. Prompting an MLLM with all the detected correspondences
152   results in information overload. In fact, our ablations (discussed in Sec 5) find that adding all the
153   correspondences reduces the MLLM’s performance. Therefore, we select a subset of prominent
154   instances to retain. We select the prominent instances of the top-K objects that co-occur in the most
155   number of frames. We first calculate the occurrence frequency and area sum of each unique instance
156   ID in the retained m masks using the following equation:
157
158                                          \mathcal {F}req(\text {ID}) &= \sum _{i=s_1}^{s_m} \mathbf {1}_{\{\text {ID} \in M_i\}}, \\ \mathcal {A}rea(\text {ID}) &= \sum _{i=s_1}^{s_m} \sum _{p \in M_i} \mathbf {1}_{\{\text {ID} = p\}}.

218   Benchmarks. The validation set of ScanQA dataset contains 4675 questions about 71 scenes. Ques-
219   tions in ScanQA require basic recognition, 3D localization, and 3D embodied capabilities (Duan et al.,
220   2022). The validation set contains two ground-truth answers per question for evaluation with models
221   that produce free-form answers. OpenEQA Dataset is an open-vocabulary dataset benchmarking
222   spatial environment understanding and embodied reasoning. We evaluate on OpenEQA’s EM-EQA
223   data split, which contains over 1600 high-quality human-generated questions. The subset tests the
224
      episodic memory of an agent moving through a 3D environment over time.
225   Baselines. For ScanQA, we evaluate C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES by augmenting both GPT-
226   4{V,O}, Gemini and Claude models. Besides, we also consider 3D specialized models (Yu et al., 2019;
227   Azuma et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2024) fine-tuned on ScanQA. For OpenEQA, we compare against
228   language-only models to account for language bias (LLaMA2 (Touvron et al., 2023)), commonly
229   used general-purpose multimodal LLMs (GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024), Claude3 (Anthropic, 2024),
230
      Gemini-Pro (Team et al., 2024), GPT-4V with 15 and 50 frames.
231   Metrics. For ScanQA, following prior works, we adopt BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) scores,
232   METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005), ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004), and CIDEr (Vedantam et al., 2015)
233   as our evaluation metrics. For OpenEQA, we follow their evaluation approach by using GPT-4 to
234   compare the generated answers with the ground-truth answers and assign a score. We report the
235   average score across all questions.
236   Results. For ScanQA, as shown in Table 1, compared to raw input, C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES
237   consistently improves the overall performance of different proprietary models. For instance, on the
238   strongest model, GPT-4o, C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES brings improvements of 5.7 BLEU-2, 3.2
239   METEOR, 6.5 ROUGE-L, and 15 CIDEr points. Compared to methods that are specifically designed
240   for 3D understanding tasks, fine-tuned with specialized 3D SFT data, or even those that use 3D point
241   clouds instead of 2D images as input, we observe that a general-purpose MLLM can still outperform
242
      them, especially when enhanced with C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES. Moreover, we found that this
      can be achieved using far fewer images as input.
243
244   We also demonstrated the same conclusion on OpenEQA, as indicated in Table 2. By applying
245   C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES, we significantly improved the performance of both GPT-4v and
246   GPT-4o, achieving better results with fewer input images. These findings suggest that general-purpose
247   MLLMs are indeed capable of understanding 3D space, and C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES can
248
      significantly enhance their spatial understanding while simultaneously reducing the number of views
      needed, which could lower the inference cost and make MLLMs more useful for embodied tasks.

      3.3     NAVIGATION
301
302   Building on the improvements in 3DQA and VideoQA, we hope that our method can also prove
303   effective in embodied tasks such as navigation. Navigation requires an agent to understand 3D space,
304   such as being able to determine the spatial relationship between objects in the instruction and itself,
305   while also performing temporal reasoning to assess the progress toward completing the instruction.
306   We consider conducting experiments on the VLN-CE benchmark (Krantz et al., 2020), which is a
307   continuous simulation environment for low-level action execution in indoor scenes.
308   Setup. We adopt the val-unseen split from R2R (Krantz et al., 2020) for evaluation. Unlike the
309   previous QA tasks, where all images could be processed at once, in navigation tasks, each image is
310   processed in an online fashion. Specifically, we feed in one image at each iteration of the conversation.
311   Given the significant variation in viewpoints during navigation, we use SAMv2 (Ravi et al., 2024),
312   the state-of-the-art model for long-range tracking, to label each new input image based on episodic
313   history. Then, using the prompted images, we induce the MLLM to output one of four actions at
314
      each step: FORWARD (distance), TURN-LEFT (rotation angle), TURN-RIGHT (rotation angle),
      and STOP. We follow NavGPT (Zhou et al., 2024) to craft input prompts. Considering the high
315
      computational cost of navigation tasks, we selected 100 samples from the val-unseen split. Our
316
      primary goal is to demonstrate that our method can enhance GPT models’ capabilities in zero-shot
317   navigation tasks, which remains a significant challenge for various types of models.
318
319   Metrics. We follow the standard VLN evaluation metrics to evaluate the navigation performance,
320
      including success rate (SR), oracle success rate (OS), success weighted by path length (SPL),
      trajectory length (TL), and navigation error from goal (NE). Note that an episode is considered
321
      successful if the agent calls the STOP action within 3m of the goal in the VLN-CE.
322
323   Results. As shown in Table 4, our method achieved improvements across all metrics. It can be
      observed that while GPT-4o performs impressively on many QA tasks, its zero-shot performance


                                                         6
324
      on navigation tasks is relatively low. This may partly be due to the lack of specialized training on
325   action data, making it less accurate in outputs such as determining how many meters to move forward.
326   However, our method reveals another dimension of the problem: MLLMs’ understanding of the
327   3D spacetime in which they operate can be further enhanced. This is evidenced by the significant
328   improvements in navigation when using C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES. We believe that our approach
329   holds great potential for embodied tasks, which can be explored in future research.

363   5.1   T HE SOT BENCHMARK FOR S PATIAL O RIENTATION T EST
364
      Considering that a crucial aspect of embodied tasks like navigation is the judgment of left-right
365
      orientation, we aimed to gain a deeper understanding of how C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES influ-
366   ences MLLMs’ comprehension of left-right spatial orientation. Specifically, we focused on two key
367   questions: 1) Are MLLMs robust to camera motion bias? Ideally, MLLMs’ understanding of left-right
368   orientation in 3D space should be independent of whether the camera is moving from left to right or
369   right to left, meaning MLLMs should be robust to camera motion bias. 2) Do MLLMs possess spatial
370   perspective-taking ability, i.e., the ability to imagine how an object or scene would appear from a
371   perspective different from the current camera viewpoint? Numerous studies in humans (Newcombe,
372   1989; Tversky & Hard, 2009) have shown that this ability is closely related to the development of
373   spatial intelligence in children.
374   However, current benchmarks face three issues: 1) They may have been partially used in MLLM
375   training data, 2) Current benchmarks lack annotations regarding whether the 3D space scan was
376   conducted from left to right or right to left, making it difficult to analyze the impact of camera
377   motion on MLLMs, and 3) Existing benchmarks evaluate a model’s 3D spatial awareness from the
      perspective of the camera-wielding observer.

379   (SOT). Once again, we show that C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES improves GPT-4V,O’s abilities on

      Data curation. We manually curated ten real-world scenes, both indoor and outdoor, using different
401
      mobile devices at various viewpoints. We instructed 10 human participants to take two videos in their
402   environment from two viewpoints. When in each viewpoint, they were asked to remain in place as
403   they laterally pan their mobile devices to scan their 3D environment. From 20 collected scenes, we
404   filtered to and retained 10 scenes that satisfied the following four criteria: First, we could uniquely
405   describe one viewpoint from the perspective of the other and vice-versa. For example, in Figure 2,
406   we define the other viewpoints as ’a person stepping out of an elevator.’ Second, we ensured that no
407   single frame captured the entire 3D space, ensuring that models can not short-cut answers using any
408   single view. Third, all scans move the camera from left to right. Fourth, to avoid privacy concerns,
409   we ensured that no people appeared in the videos. Each scene scan lasts between 3 to 5 seconds.
410   For each scene, we designed five carefully crafted questions, each asking the model to determine
411   if one object is to the left or to the right of another from a specific viewpoint. The first three
412   questions are from the observer’s (camera’s) perspective, while the final two describe the perspective
413   in language, thereby, testing for a model’s spatial perspective-taking ability. Human performance on
414   these questions is 100%. We design SOT questions to have a bias towards asking about relationships
415   between objects that appear in the first last frame of the scan, ensuring that the has to use multiple
416
      frames to answer. In total, across the 10 scenes, SOT has a modest 50 questions.

418   wild data. For instance, when using only the first and last frames, our method results in a 13.4 %
419   improvement. As illustrated in Figure 2, our method enables GPT-4O to understand the 3D spatial
420   structure represented by the images using minimal overlap, whereas GPT-4O alone performs only
421   slightly better than random guessing.

423   accuracy on videos filmed from left to right compared to those filmed from right to left, indicating

      How does C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES differ from other visual prompting methods? Our
474   proposed method calculates and highlights correspondences between images, aiming to elicit 3D
475   and temporal understanding. Other visual prompting methods (namely Set-of-Mark (Yang et al.,
476   2023a), 3DAxiesPrompts (Liu et al., 2023), and Chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2023)) can also be
477   viewed as alternative prompting methods. Given that the ground-truth answers in existing benchmarks
478   are relatively brief, we selected a scene from ScanQA and manually designed a new question. We
479   qualitatively compare C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES against other prompting methods on this new
480   question, as shown in Figure 4.
481   The orange part of Figure 4 shows our Coarse Correspondence labels are recognized by GPT-4V. The
482   output answer provides evidence that our coarse correspondence helps GPT-4V develop a mental
483   3D model of the scene. Set-of-Marks provides no spatial corresponding information and therefore is
484   unhelpful. The Axis labels in 3DAxies can be easily misrecognized by GPT-4V, leading to misleading
485   spatial information. Though Chain-of-Thought helps identify objects, it fails to resolve the “spatial
      perspective-taking” issue.

526   6    C ONCLUSION .
527
528   We propose a framework called C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES prompting. By using off-the-shelf
529   video tracking models to obtain class-agnostic, instance-level correspondences, and conveying this
530   information to MLLMs through visual prompting, we discovered that this simple method, using
531   only 2D images as input—without any specialized architectural design or task-specific SFT—can
532   effectively enhance MLLMs’ understanding of 3D space and long videos. This improvement extends
533
      to embodied tasks like navigation. Our method not only works on proprietary models but also
      generalizes to open-source models, and it performs well on both in-domain and out-of-domain
534
      datasets. Moreover, it enhances not just inference but also training. Further analysis shows that our
535
      method helps MLLMs become more robust to camera motion bias. Additionally, we identified that
536   even GPT models struggle with perspective-taking capability, a fundamental component of human
537   visual intelligence, which presents an important avenue for future exploration to further improve
538   MLLMs.

      A    B ROADER I MPACT
760
761   Our method aims at improving the trustworthiness and reliability of deployment of MLLMs in real
762
      world application, including but not limited to Vision Pro, autonomous driving, and also humanoid
      robots. To have a virtual assistant like JARVIS in Marvel films, it’s necassry to align the understanding
763
      of vision-language model with human’s understanding, so that we can ensure safe application of these
764
      applications. Further, we are committed to reducing the carbon emissions produced by these models.
765   By employing our coarse correspondence prompting method, we use a much smaller tracking module
766   to reduce the number of input used as input to large GPT model. Besides, we also improve the
767   speed and lower the cost of calling OpenAI API to understand a 3d scene. This enables democratize
768   MLLMs so that more people and small companies can create their own real-world applications based
769   on GPT-4V. We hope our work can make large AI models more effectively used for social good.
770
      Still, we would like to point out that with the development of MLLMs, increased reliance on advanced
771   MLLMs could also lead to a reduction in human skills, especially in interpreting and interacting
772   with visual content. Over-dependence on these models might erode critical thinking and analytical
773   abilities in the long term.

      B    R ELATED WORK
777
778
      Multimodal language models Multimodal LLMs(Liu et al., 2024b; Bai et al., 2023) integrate vision
      encoders (Radford et al., 2021) into large LLMs (Chiang et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023), allowing
779
      them to directly reason over visual input. Many proprietary models, such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023),
780
      Gemini (Team et al., 2024), and Claude (Anthropic, 2024), as well as open-source models like the
781   LLaVA series (Liu et al., 2024b) and BLIP series (Li et al., 2023), have made significant progress in
782   2D vision-language tasks like image captioning (Chen et al., 2015) and visual question answering
783   (VQA) (Hudson & Manning, 2019; Goyal et al., 2017). Beyond these language-related tasks, many
784   newer attempts applying MLLMs to applications such as autonomous driving (Tian et al., 2024) and
785   robotics (Yang et al., 2023b). Many of these tasks require understanding the 3D space in which they
786   are deployed and reason about how things are changing temporally. We improve the 3D space-time
787   capabilities of such models.
788   Visual prompting. Effective prompting has been widely proven to improve LLMs across multi-
789   ple domains. Methods, such as chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al., 2023), force the model to
790   reason before answering a question. For multimodal LLMs, methods such as Red-circle prompt-
791   ing (Shtedritski et al., 2023) and Set-of-marks (Yang et al., 2023a) can enhance the grounding
792   abilities of CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and GPT-4V. PIVOT (Nasiriany et al., 2024) employs visual
793   prompting combined with iterative VQA to induce GPT-4V to generate outputs for robotics control.
794   3DAxies (Liu et al., 2023) enhances GPT-4V’s ability to use numerical expressions to describe
795
      3D relationships of objects in a single image by annotating a scaled 3D coordinate system on the
      image. Unlike these works, C OARSE C ORRESPONDENCES prompts MLLMs to understand the
796
      spatial relationships within a complete 3D scene from an image sequence.
797
798   Video understanding. Videos carry rich information about both the 3D structure as well as temporal
799   changes in the physical world. To perform better long-horizon reasoning, work has begun incorporat-
800   ing video inputs into MLLMs. Recent work (Lin et al., 2023) has improved performance on video
801
      dense captioning (Krishna et al., 2017) and videoQA (Xiao et al., 2021; Grunde-McLaughlin et al.,
      2021). To further advance the understanding of temporal relationships in videos, EgoSchema (Man-
802
      galam et al., 2023) introduced a benchmark for long video understanding, which is more challenging
803
      than previous video-language benchmarks. Meanwhile, understanding 3D spatial relationships in
804   videos received relatively less attention. 3D-LLM (Hong et al., 2024) converts multiview images into
805   3D point clouds and then feeds them into LLMs, demonstrating better results on the ScanQA (Azuma
806   et al., 2022) benchmark for 3D understanding. OpenEQA (Majumdar et al., 2024) is also a benchmark
807   dedicated to evaluating MLLM’s understanding of 3D physical space, with outputs that are more
808   open-vocabulary compared to ScanQA. In this paper, we propose a framework that does not require
809   any training in modifying MLLMs; it extracts meaningful information from videos using off-the-shelf
      tracking models and achieves state-of-the-art performance on the benchmarks mentioned.

      Visual correspondences. Visual correspondences have been a vital area of research in computer
811   vision for a few decades. Applications such as Structure-from-Motion(Schonberger & Frahm, 2016)
812   utilize correspondences to better reconstruct 3D scenes. In the past, we relied on handcrafted features
813   like SIFT (Lowe, 2004) or SURF (Bay et al., 2006) to obtain good correspondence. Today, features
814   extracted from deep models (Tang et al., 2023) can also provide increasingly accurate correspondences.
815   Generally, people aim to achieve precise geometric and semantic correspondences at the pixel level.
816   However, in this paper, we use coarse visual correspondence to prompt MLLMs, which can be easily
817   obtained from off-the-shelf video tracking models (Yang et al., 2023c).

      E    M ORE D ISCUSSIONS
919
920
      Limitations. Our method relies on off-the-shelf video tracking models to obtain instance-level
921   correspondences. Although the performance of tracking models has significantly improved with the
922   advent of tools like SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023), achieving good results on long-form in-the-wild
923   videos remains challenging. This is particularly evident on the 180-second EgoSchema benchmark,
924   where Track-Anything often loses track of objects after 100 seconds, leading to inconsistent instance
925   segmentation masks between the beginning and end of the video clip. Despite observing consistent
926   and significant improvements on EgoSchema, we believe that accurate correspondence would further
927   enhance the benefits of our approach.
928   Relation to SlowFast SlowFast (Feichtenhofer et al., 2019) is a framework for video recognition that
929   includes two parallel pathways: a Slow pathway that captures motion information at a high frame
930   rate and a Fast pathway that captures semantic information at a low frame rate. The information
931   from both pathways is fused through lateral connections for downstream video recognition tasks. In
932   a way, our coarse correspondence prompting can be seen as another form of SlowFast. However,
933   unlike SlowFast, where the Slow and Fast pathways operate in parallel, our framework operates
934   sequentially. First, it captures low-level, class-agnostic motion information at a high frame rate
935
      using a lightweight tracking model. Then, at a low frame rate, it performs recognition and reasoning
      requiring semantic understanding using larger MLLMs. The two stages are bridged through visual
936
      prompting. Moreover, while SlowFast learns a representation of the input video for pure vision
937
      recognition tasks such as action classification and detection, our coarse correspondence framework
938   aims to better understand the 3D spatial structure and temporal information contained in the input
939   video to achieve spatiotemporal perception and reasoning simultaneously.
940
941
      Eulerian vs Lagrangian If deep learning-based methods represent camera or object motion in videos
      from an Eulerian viewpoint—i.e., expressing how features at fixed locations evolve over time through
942
      a multi-dimensional tensor—then our framework adds a Lagrangian viewpoint to this representation.
943
      The Lagrangian viewpoint describes the trajectories of entities moving through space and time in
944   the video. Previously, the Lagrangian viewpoint in video descriptions has been shown to better aid
945   human action recognition (Rajasegaran et al., 2023). Here, we demonstrate that it can more generally
946   help MLLMs understand the 4D spatiotemporal context represented in videos.
