011                                          Abstract
012
013           Text-based Person Search (TBPS) in full images aims to locate a target
014           pedestrian within uncropped images based on natural language descrip-
015           tions. Existing TBPS methods typically rely on candidate region generation
016           and cross-modal matching. However, in complex scenes,especially those
017           with multiple pedestrians in the image.It is often challenging to distinguish
018           the target pedestrian from the background or other individuals. This leads
019           to limited generalization capabilities. To address these issues, we propose a
020
              new TBPS framework named ProtoDis-TBPS, which integrates three key
              components: Semantic Context Decoupling (SCD), Prototype Embedding
021
              Learning (PEL), and a Cross-modal Person Re-identification (ReID) mod-
022
              ule. Specifically, SCD enhances cross-modal feature discrimination by sep-
023           arating background and irrelevant contextual information. PEL improves
024           the model’s robustness in complex scenes by learning prototype features for
025           pedestrian categories. Finally, the ReID module, based on a Transformer
026           architecture, further boosts the accuracy of both text-based pedestrian de-
027           tection and re-identification in full images. Experiments demonstrate that
028           our proposed method presents a significant challenge to existing approaches
029           in this field.

032   1   Introduction
033
034
      Text-based person search in full images has significant practical relevance in real-world
035
      scenarios, particularly when no images of the target individual are available, and the search
036   relies solely on witness-provided textual descriptions. (Zhang et al., 2024) The goal of this
037   task is to accurately locate a specific pedestrian from complex, uncropped images based
038   on natural language descriptions. However, this task faces numerous challenges, especially
039   when dealing with cluttered backgrounds or multiple pedestrians, where it can be diﬀicult
040   for models to accurately distinguish the target pedestrian. Existing methods often rely
041   on generating a large number of candidate regions for matching, but their robustness and
042   cross-modal matching capabilities are limited, especially in complex scenes.
043   To address these issues, we propose an end-to-end learning framework based on Transform-
044   ers, called ProtoDis-TBPS. Inspired by Nies (2023); Zhuang et al. (2023); Gao et al. (2024);
045   Wang et al. (2021); Jiang & Ye (2023),this framework optimizes text-based person search
046   in full images through three key tasks. First, the Semantic Context Decoupling (SCD) task
047   identifies critical information from both visual and textual features, reducing interference
048   from background and irrelevant context. Second, the Prototype Embedding Learning (PEL)
049   task enhances the model’s generalization ability in complex scenes by learning prototype rep-
050
      resentations of pedestrians. Finally, the Cross-modal Person Re-identification (ReID) task
      performs pedestrian detection and identity recognition based on multimodal features.
051
052   By applying this framework to real-world text-based person search tasks in full images,
053   we validated its effectiveness. Experimental results indicate that in complex scenes with
      multiple pedestrians, the multimodal feature extraction model, which incorporates context

      decoupling and prototype learning, can more accurately retrieve the target pedestrian based
055   on textual descriptions.

      2   Related works
059
      In the existing literature, the most relevant work to our proposed method primarily fo-
060
      cuses on person search. Approaches in person search are generally based on two commonly
061
      used methods from the object detection field: single-stage methods(Liu et al., 2016), where
062   pedestrian detection and feature extraction are performed simultaneously, and two-stage
063   methods(Du et al., 2020), where candidate regions are first generated and then refined
064   through classification and bounding box regression.
065
      Common models for the first approach include the YOLO(Terven et al., 2023) and SSD
066
      series(Liu et al., 2016), which are highly eﬀicient and offer an end-to-end training and
067
      inference pipeline. However, since detection and feature extraction are done simultaneously,
068   the extracted features may not be detailed enough, potentially affecting retrieval accuracy.
069
070   For the second approach, typical models are based on Faster R-CNN, which generates candi-
071
      date regions through a Region Proposal Network (RPN) and then performs refined detection
      and classification on each candidate region. Although this method achieves high accuracy
072
      and more detailed feature extraction, it often involves computationally intensive modules
073
      like Non-Maximum Suppression (NMS)(Bodla et al., 2017).
074
075   In addition to these two common approaches, several improved methods have emerged re-
076   cently, such as anchor-free methods(Zand et al., 2022). These methods no longer rely on
077
      predefined anchor boxes but instead detect objects by directly regressing key points or the
      center of the object, with representative models like FCOS(Tian et al., 2019) and Cen-
078
      terNet(Zhou et al., 2019). Furthermore, there are also Transformer-based methods(Carion
079
      et al., 2020), which use various attention mechanisms to perform global feature modeling
080   while simultaneously handling pedestrian detection and feature extraction tasks.

106
107   Fig.1 illustrates the overall flow of the ProtoDis-TBPS framework. The primary goal of
      this framework is to perform person search in full images based on textual descriptions.

      Specifically, the framework consists of three key tasks: Semantic Context Decoupling (SCD),
109   Prototype Embedding Learning (PEL), and Cross-modal Person Re-identification (ReID).
110
111   First, the image path uses a pre-trained ResNet and Transformer to extract image features,
112
      generating a feature representation for the entire image. On the text path, a pre-trained
      BERT model is used to encode the query description, producing textual feature representa-
113
      tions corresponding to the target pedestrian.
114
115   In the SCD task, the features from the image and text paths are fed into a Multi-Head
116   Attention (MHA) module, where the image features are used as the query (Q), and the
117   text features serve as the keys (K) and values (V). This process generates a Common Se-
118
      mantic Feature (CSF). The CSF is then processed by a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP)
      and combined with enhanced image features derived from another MLP. These combined
119
      features are used to compute a relevance score, thereby fusing salient features from both
120
      modalities to generate a discriminative visual representation. This process helps decouple
121   background information and highlight the target pedestrian’s features, reducing interference
122   from irrelevant context and background during pedestrian recognition.
123
124
      In the PEL task, the discriminative visual representation is passed to the Prototype Learn-
      ing module, which learns typical feature representations of pedestrians. By capturing key
125
      features of the target pedestrian and feeding them into a prototype queue for feature-based
126
      clustering, this module generates pedestrian features with category-generalized representa-
127   tions. These features are then combined with the discriminative visual representation from
128   the SCD module, forming the Multi-modal Fusion Features.
129
130
      In the ReID task, the fused multi-modal features and the original image features are fed into
      a Bounding Box Prediction (BBP) module, which is based on a Transformer and MLP. This
131
      module outputs the predicted bounding box of the target pedestrian. Finally, the regressed
132
      bounding box and original image features are input into the ReID Head module, where
133   online instance matching is performed to learn and differentiate identity representations of
134   different pedestrians. For more details, please refer to Appendix A.1.

      A.1     Method Details
246
247   A.1.1    Training Framework
248
249   The ProtoDis-TBPS framework consists of three key subtasks: Semantic Context De-
250   coupling (SCD), Prototype Embedding Learning (PEL), and Cross-modal Text-
251   based Person Re-identification (ReID).
252   The SCD process calculates the similarity between salient objects and contextual informa-
253   tion through a cross-modal attention mechanism, enhancing target features while suppress-
254   ing background interference. Simultaneously, a phrase attention mechanism is employed to
255   enhance contextual information in the description (such as attributes and relationships),
256   further improving the distinction between the target and context in the language represen-
257   tation.
258   The PEL process captures key features of the target pedestrian and performs feature-based
259   clustering by feeding them into a prototype queue. This allows the model to learn and inherit
260   prototype features, ensuring strong robustness and accuracy in varying environments and
261   scene conditions.
262
      The ReID process applies cross-modal features and enhanced visual features for cross-modal
263   pedestrian re-identification.
264
265   For each image, we use a pre-trained ResNet as the backbone to capture local fine-grained
266
      image features. A Transformer is then used as the salient feature embedding module to
      extract global image features and model the visual context. The image is processed by both
267
      modules to obtain a global image feature representation Gi .
268
269   For the target pedestrian’s textual description in each image, we use a pre-trained BERT
      model(Devlin et al., 2019) to encode the query description, obtaining a global textual feature


                                                    5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      representation Gt corresponding to the target pedestrian in the full image. Thus, we have
271   the following representations:
272
273                                    Gi = VE(I),         Gt = TE(T )                           (1)
274
275   In the first Semantic Context Decoupling (SCD) task, a multi-head attention mech-
276
      anism (MHA) is used to perform cross-modal information interaction between the global
      image features Gi and the global textual features Gt associated with the target pedestrian.
277
      This results in a fused feature representation FCSF , capturing the common semantics be-
278
      tween the visual and textual modalities. Next, we pass this fused feature FCSF through a
279   fully connected network (MLP) to further refine and enhance the semantic representation.
280
281   To further improve the discriminative power of cross-modal features, we compute the cor-
282
      relation between the visual features Gi and the fused semantic features FCSF . Specifically,
      two independent MLPs are used to project Gi and FCSF , respectively, obtaining their cor-
283
      relation score to measure the similarity between the visual and semantic features. Finally,
284
      the visual features Gi are adjusted using the correlation score to generate a discriminative
285   visual feature representation Fdisc :
286
287                                      FCSF = MHA(Gi , Gt )                                    (2)
288                                score = MLPv (Gi ) ⊗ MLPs (FCSF )                             (3)
289
                                            Fdisc = Gi ⊙ score                                   (4)
290
      where ⊗ denotes the correlation calculation between features, and ⊙ represents applying the
291
      correlation score to the visual features. In this way, the model can focus more on the salient
292
      target objects in the image and suppress irrelevant contextual information, thus improving
293   the target distinction capability in complex scenes.
294
295   In the second task, Prototype Embedding Learning (PEL), the goal is to capture
296
      the key features of pedestrians and generate discriminative multimodal representations.
      Specifically, the visual features Fdisc are processed by the PrototypeExtractor to extract
297
      the prototype feature representation Fproto of the pedestrian. These features represent
298
      high-level semantic information and are dynamically stored and updated in a prototype
299   memory queue.
300
301   During training, pedestrian features are clustered to form a discrete set of prototypes. Each
302
      prototype represents the cluster center of a pedestrian feature class and is stored in the
      prototype memory queue Qproto for subsequent inference. During inference, the model
303
      searches the prototype queue to find prototypes that semantically match the input features.
304
      Specifically, the input visual feature Fv is fused with the pedestrian prototype feature Fproto
305   from the prototype memory to generate the final multimodal feature representation Fmulti :
306
307
308
                                           Fproto = PE(Fdisc )
309                                     Qproto = {P1 , P2 , . . . , Pk }
310                                       Fmulti = Fv ⊕ Fproto
311
312   Here, the visual features Fdisc are processed by the PrototypeExtractor (PE) to generate
313   the prototype feature representation Fproto , which captures high-level semantic information.
314
      The prototype memory queue Qproto contains k prototypes (with a default value of k =
      2048), where each prototype Pi represents a cluster center of a pedestrian feature class,
315
      and these prototypes are dynamically updated during training. The input visual feature
316
      Fv is concatenated with the prototype feature Fproto from the memory queue using the
317   concatenation operation ⊕, resulting in the final multimodal feature representation Fmulti .
318
319   In the third task, Cross-modal Person Re-identification (ReID), the multimodal fea-
320
      ture Fmulti is combined with the original image features Gi and fed into a Bounding Box
      Prediction Module (BBoxPredictor) based on a Transformer and MLP. This module
321
      generates the bounding box prediction of the target pedestrian.
322
323   Next, the regressed bounding box and original image features Gi are passed to the ReID
      Head module, where an Online Instance Matching (OIM) loss is used to learn and

324
      differentiate pedestrian identities,commonly used in object detection tasks(Li et al., 2020).
325   During this process, we use Smooth L1 loss to measure the error between the predicted and
326   ground truth bounding boxes, yielding the bounding box regression loss Lossbbox_reg . Ad-
327   ditionally, Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE) loss is used to measure the difference between
328   the predicted and true labels, yielding the bounding box classification loss Lossbbox_cls .
329
      We then perform contrastive learning (ITC), commonly used in cross-modal tasks(Li et al.,
330
      2022), between the image features Gbox within the bounding box and the textual features,
331   aligning the visual and textual features at the local level and further reducing the ”modality
332   gap” between the two modalities. Finally, the cross-modal person re-identification loss
333   Lossreid is computed using the OIM loss.
334
      The overall loss function consists of four parts, which measure the errors in the bounding
335
      box regression, bounding box classification, and re-identification tasks. The final total loss
336
      is expressed as:
337                                    BBoxpred = BBP(Fmulti )                                   (5)
338
339
                              Lossbbox_reg = SmoothL1(BBoxgt , BBoxpred )                       (6)
340                           Lossbbox_cls = BCE(pred_scores, gt_labels)                        (7)
341                                  Lossreid = OIM(BBoxpred , Gi )                             (8)
342
                                       Losscma = LIT C (Gbox , Gt )                             (9)
343
344
                Losstotal = α · Lossbbox_reg + β · Lossbbox_cls + γ · Lossreid + θ · Losscma   (10)
345   where α, β, γ, θ are hyperparameters set to 0.2, 0.2, 0.3, and 0.3, respectively. Finally, the
346   loss is jointly optimized throughout the training process.

348   A.1.2    Inference
349
      During inference, the query text is passed through the BERT model to extract the query
350   text features. Then, multimodal features are computed separately using the query text and
351   gallery images. The cosine similarity between the multimodal features and the query text
352   features is calculated, and a threshold function is applied to reduce the complexity of this
353   process.
354
      The filtered multimodal features are then combined with the visual features of the corre-
355
      sponding gallery images to generate enhanced visual features of the potential target pedes-
356   trian. These enhanced visual features are once again compared with the query text features
357   by calculating cosine similarity, which is used for the subsequent ranking and accuracy
358   assessment of the candidate gallery images.

360   A.2     Experimental Results and Analysis
361
362   A.2.1    Datasets and Evaluation Metrics
363
      Datasets: We conducted experiments on two recently introduced text-based pedestrian
364
      retrieval datasets from full images: CUHK-SYSU-TBPS and PRW-TBPS. For CUHK-
365
      SYSU-TBPS, the training set contains 11,206 scene images and 15,080 person bounding
366   boxes with 5,532 different IDs, while the query set includes 2,900 target persons with anchor
367   boxes. Each box in the training set is associated with two textual descriptions, whereas each
368   box in the query set has one description. As for PRW-TBPS, the training set contains
369   5,704 images and 14,897 boxes with 483 different IDs, and the query set contains 2,056
370   boxes. Each box in the training set has one textual description, while each box in the query
371   set has two descriptions. In our experiments, all dataset splits follow the oﬀicial training
372   and testing set divisions to ensure comparability of results.
373   Evaluation Metrics: Top-K Accuracy (K=1,5,10): Top-1 accuracy represents the
374   proportion of cases where the first returned candidate matches the textual description cor-
375   rectly. For each text query, the model ranks all candidate pedestrians based on the matching
376   degree between the image features and the text features, returning the bounding boxes of
377   the candidates. The evaluation function computes the cosine similarity between the en-
      hanced visual feature vectors of the candidate pedestrians and the text feature vectors of


                                                     7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      the queried person to rank all candidates. Additionally, the IoU (Intersection over Union)
379   threshold between the predicted and ground-truth bounding boxes is dynamically adjusted
380   based on the size of the target pedestrian. Similarly, Top-5 and Top-10 accuracies are
381   computed, reflecting the proportion of correct matches in the top 5 or top 10 candidates.
382
      mAP (Mean Average Precision): For each text query, the evaluation function calculates
383
      the predicted scores of all candidate pedestrians. The candidates are ranked based on cosine
384
      similarity, and the average precision (AP) is computed for each query. The AP is calculated
385   by averaging the precision at different recall levels, and the final mAP value is the average
386   of all queries’ APs. The mAP calculation in the evaluation function also considers the IoU
387   between bounding boxes with dynamic thresholds. A higher mAP indicates more stable and
388   robust retrieval performance across different queries and thresholds.

390   A.2.2   Implementation Details
391
392
      Data Preprocessing: During the training phase, images are padded and randomly shifted
      to a fixed size of (640, 640) to enhance the model’s robustness to different scenes. Addition-
393
      ally, each image is accompanied by a binary mask, with the same size as the image. The
394
      mask marks the actual image region, while the padded parts are set to zero. This mask is
395   used throughout the training process to indicate the valid regions of the image.
396
397   Training Details: We initialize our visual backbone with a ResNet-50 model, pre-trained
398
      on ImageNet for global feature extraction, and a pretrained DETR model for local feature
      extraction. ResNet-50 enhances visual features by integrating text features, while DETR
399
      is utilized to compute semantic similarity with text inputs. For text feature extraction,
400
      we employ a BERT model. Our model was implemented using PyTorch and tested on an
401   NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU. The batch size was set to 2, with each image being resized to
402   640 × 640 pixels. Optimization was performed using stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with
403   an initial learning rate of 0.003, which was adjusted during training. The momentum and
404   weight decay for SGD were set at 0.9 and 5 × 10−4 , respectively. Additionally, we configured
405   a loop queue size of 5000/500 for the Open-set Identity Management (OIM).
