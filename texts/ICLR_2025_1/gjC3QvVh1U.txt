009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural scaling laws are observed in a range of domains, to date with no clear un-
012            derstanding of why they occur. Recent theories suggest that loss power laws arise
013            from Zipf’s law, a power law observed in domains like natural language. One the-
014            ory suggests that language scaling laws emerge when Zipf-distributed task quanta
015            are learned in descending order of frequency. In this paper we examine power-
016
               law scaling in AlphaZero, a reinforcement learning algorithm, using a theory of
               language-model scaling. We find that game states in training and inference data
017
               scale with Zipf’s law, which is known to arise from the tree structure of the envi-
018
               ronment, and examine the correlation between scaling-law and Zipf’s-law expo-
019            nents. In agreement with quanta scaling theory, we find that agents optimize state
020            loss in descending order of frequency, even though this order scales inversely with
021            modelling complexity. We also find that inverse scaling, the failure of models to
022            improve with size, is correlated with unusual Zipf curves where end-game states
023            are among the most frequent states. We show evidence that larger models shift
024            their focus to these less-important states, sacrificing their understanding of impor-
025            tant early-game states.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
      Neural scaling laws, describing the scaling of model performance with training resources, have been
030
      documented across many architectures and use cases (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022;
031   Henighan et al., 2020; Zhai et al., 2022; Neumann & Gros, 2022). The performance of these models,
032   typically measured by test loss, follows a power law in either compute, model size or training data.
033   The robustness of these power laws and their usefulness in training large models, especially large
034   language models (LLMs) (Wei et al., 2022), prompted a range of attempts to find a model explaining
035   the mechanism behind neural power-law scaling (Sharma & Kaplan, 2020; Bahri et al., 2021; Hutter,
036   2021; Song et al., 2024). Several models connect performance power laws to Zipf’s law, a universal
037   power law appearing in natural language (Michaud et al., 2023; Hutter, 2021; Bordelon et al., 2020;
038   Cui et al., 2021; Maloney et al., 2022; Cabannes et al., 2023).
039   Scaling laws in reinforcement learning (RL) are so far relatively rare compared to supervised learn-
040   ing (Hilton et al., 2023; Tuyls et al., 2023; Springenberg et al., 2024). AlphaZero, a multi-agent
041   RL algorithm that beat human champions in games like chess and Go (Silver et al., 2017a), exhibits
042   power laws of Elo score with training resources, as well as a power law relation between compute
043   and optimal model size (Neumann & Gros, 2022; Jones, 2021; Neumann & Gros, 2021).
044   In this paper, we explore the mechanism behind RL scaling laws, often comparing it to the quanti-
045   zation model of LLM scaling (Michaud et al., 2023). Our main findings are:
046
047        1. AlphaZero agents that exhibit scaling laws (Neumann & Gros, 2022) produce train and test
048           data that follows Zipf’s law, suggesting learned tasks scale in a similar way. We show how
049
              agent strategies smooth and augment the Zipf power law that is known to originate from
              the tree-structure of board games.
050
051        2. The correlation between the Zipf curve and scaling exponents can be calculated by modu-
052
              lating policy temperature.
053        3. In agreement with quanta scaling theory, agents minimize loss on game states in descending
              order of frequency, counter-intuitively achieving better performance on harder tasks.


                                                        1

054
               4. Inverse scaling, the abrupt failure of scaling laws at large sizes, coincides with an unusual
055               state-frequency distribution. Large models scale negatively on games with an unusual tree
056               structure that increases the frequency of strategically-unimportant late-game states. Larger
057               agents achieve better loss on these late-game states, sacrificing performance on more im-
058               portant early-game states. A combination of the quantization model and selective non-
059               stationarity of targets is suggested to explain inverse scaling in AlphaZero.

121   Finding Zipf’s law in board games To our knowledge, no study has been made on Zipf’s law
122   in AI games. Blasius & Tönjes (2009) and Georgeot & Giraud (2012) show that the popularity of
123   openings in human game datasets follows Zipf’s law in chess and Go, respectively. Blasius & Tönjes
124
      (2009) are the first to propose a connection to the branching-tree structure of chess, without proof.
      We provide evidence supporting such a connection in section 5.1.
125
126   Analyzing concept learning in AlphaZero In sections 6 and 7 we present results on the scaling
127   of AlphaZero loss on individual states. A similar analysis is done by McGrath et al. (2022) and
128   Lovering et al. (2022), who both analyze how human concepts are learned by AlphaZero by measur-
129   ing accuracy. McGrath et al. (2022) map how human chess concepts are learned, both as a function
130   of training length and neural network depth. They measure the accuracy of a few hand-crafted
131   concepts, compared to our analysis that measures loss on up to 105 states. Similar to our inverse-
132   scaling results in section 7, Lovering et al. (2022) observe differences between how short-term and
133   long-term concepts are learned in the game of Hex.
134
      Effects of state frequency on training Our work discusses the effects of state frequency in the
135
      training data on AlphaZero performance. Another observation on this relation of data frequency
136   with performance is found in Ruoss et al. (2024), where Transformers are trained with supervised
137   learning on annotated human chess games. The authors perform an ablation study where they change
138   the frequencies of board states in the dataset, either sampling states uniformly or sampling them
139   according to the frequency they were played by humans, which should follow Zipf’s law. Testing
140   on chess puzzle accuracy, changing the state frequency seems to have a strong effect on model
141   performance, in favor of uniform sampling.

181   5.1    Z IPF ’ S LAW IN GAMES WITH SCALING LAWS
182
183   AlphaZero board state distribution We find a Zipf distribution in games played by AlphaZero
184   agents that exhibit scaling laws. Fig. 1 shows the frequency distribution of board states played
185   by Connect Four and Pentago AlphaZero agents of varying sizes during training. The number of
186   times each state was visited follows a clear Zipf power law when sorted in descending order. The
187
      exact value of the exponent defined in Eq. 1 fluctuates in the range α ∼ 0.8 − 1.0 for different
      models and games, close to the value observed in human Go games (Georgeot & Giraud, 2012) and
188
      to the exponent of random games, discussed in the next section. Appendix B.1 contains plots of
189
      individual-agent Zipf curves, and discusses the variation of the power-law exponent α. The plateaus
190   at the tail-end of each distribution are due to finite-size effects, and smooth-out when more state-
191   frequency data is available.
192
193
      This result strengthens the assumption that Zipf’s law is not caused by human decision-making,
      but is rather derived from the game rules. The existence of Zipf’s law in human games has long
194
      since been established, but its cause is not entirely clear. The frequency of popular opening moves
195
      in games like Chess and Go follows a descending power law in rank, both for expert and amateur
196   human players (Blasius & Tönjes, 2009; Georgeot & Giraud, 2012). The fact that an RL model
197   trained with no human knowledge produces Zipf’s law similar to humans implies that Zipf’s law is
198   not caused by human strategy preference.
199
200   Visualizing the origin of Zipf’s law We give some intuition to the source of Zipf’s law in Fig. 2.
201   The relation of Zipf’s law to the underlying branching process of board games was already pointed
202   out by Blasius & Tönjes (2009). It is easy to show that an ideal game, with a constant branching
203   factor and a tree structure free of loops, would produce a Zipf’s law distribution. Specifically, one
204   can show that the frequency distribution in such a game is a series of plateaus around the line
205   S(n) ∝ n. The same reasoning explains the appearance of Zipf’s law in randomly-generated texts
206
      (Li, 1992). In Fig. 2A, we visualize this ideal-game Zipf’s law by plotting the state-frequency
      distribution for a non-looping, constant branching game with fixed length. Increasing the branching
207
      factor widens the plateaus, spreading them out. We use the initial branching factor of Connect Four.
208
209   In Fig. 2B we show that adding game rules molds the plateaus into a smoother curve. Using a uni-
210   formly random policy to play Connect Four and Pentago smooths the power-law-centered plateaus,
211   somewhat merging them into a straight line. The toy-model exponent changes slightly after adding
212
      game rules, but the general power-law trend remains intact.
213   Appendix B.2 contains additional results on the emergence of Zipf’s law, and shows that non-
214   uniform policies can also smooth the distribution into a power law. As we see in Fig. 1, a learned
215   policy smooths the distribution further, keeping the power-law shape while slightly changing the
      exponent.

      5.2   T EMPERATURE AND CORRELATION WITH SCALING LAWS
233
234   Although measuring the correlation between Zipf’s law and scaling laws is not straightforward, we
235   present how this can be done using temperature. Natural language Zipf exponents are impossible
236   to change in real-world data. In contrast, RL Zipf laws can be augmented by changing the policy
237   temperature T . AlphaZero uses Monte-Carlo tree search (MCTS) to generate a policy π:
238
239
                                                           N (s0 , a)1/T
                                              π(s0 , a) = P            1/T
                                                                                                                   (5)
240                                                        b N (s0 , b)
241
      where N (s, a) is the number of times the state-action pair (s, a) was probed, see appendix D for a
242
      detailed explanation. The temperature T interpolates between a deterministic policy choosing the
243   best move at T = 0, and a uniform policy at T = ∞. At inference time, e.g. when calculating Elo
244   ratings, T is either set to zero or reduced to a low value, the latter to avoid deterministic policies.
245
246
      Temperature has a strong influence on the state-frequency curve, as we show for Connect Four games
      in Fig. 3A. As T approaches zero, the Zipf curve starts to bend due to the formation of plateaus of
247
      popular games that are played repeatedly when temperature is low. Deviations from these games
248
      make up the post-bend tail. The Zipf exponent of the tail is not well-defined for T → 0 for a finite
249   number of agents and finite numbers of games played. The bending of the frequency curve with
250   decreasing T is somewhat intuitive, considering how temperature affects games. At low T , fewer
251   unique states are played, meaning the curve must end at a lower rank. This pushes up the rest of the
252   frequencies, since the total number of states played remains constant.
253
254   Exponent correlation In Fig. 3C we tune the inference temperature T to analyze the correlation
255   between the size scaling law (Fig. 3B) and the Zipf exponent (Fig. 3A). The two exponents change
256   together, indicating that the inference-time state distribution does affect the scaling law, as predicted
257   by the quantization model. Unlike the linear correlation predicted by the quantization model for
258   LLMs, we observe a non-linear relation. The non-linearity is expected when one considers the
259   T → 0 limit, in which RL scaling laws converge to a finite exponent while the Zipf tail exponent
260   diverges to infinity.
261   Additional experiments confirm that the relation between the state distribution and the scaling law is
262   causal, meaning the scaling law changes only because of the changing Zipf distribution. A possible
263   non-causal effect can be Elo scores changing due to the degradation of the policy π at high T . We
264   measure the effect of T on policy quality in appendix C and find that the probability policy π(T )
265   assigns to optimal moves only starts decreasing at T > 0.5. The correlation in Fig. 3C is strictly
266   causal, since it falls in the temperature range where policy quality is preserved. Interestingly, the
267
      Zipf exponent only changes in the temperature where policy quality is optimal, and is fixed for
      T > 0.5.

      Comparison to LLMs The quantization hypothesis assumes LLMs learn tasks in a binary way,
      sharply reducing the loss on each task in descending order of frequency. As a function of neural
304
      net size N , this assumed loss function is a step function that changes from low to high loss at rank
305
      n ∝ N (Michaud et al., 2023). In comparison, we find that AlphaZero loss increases smoothly with
306   rank rather than resembling a step function, likely because board positions are not independent task
307   quanta.

311

362   7     C ONNECTING INVERSE SCALING TO THE GAME STRUCTURE
363
364   Inverse scaling, i.e. performance scaling negatively with training resources, is known to occur in
365   LLM training (McKenzie et al., 2023). RL models can similarly fail to scale reliably, sometimes
366   decreasing in performance as neural-network size and compute budget are increased. We see a
367   clear example of inverse scaling for AlphaZero agents trained on Checkers and Oware, as shown
368   in Fig. 5A. These agents follow a steady scaling curve that abruptly breaks down beyond a certain
369   neural network size, where Elo starts to scale negatively with size.
370
371   7.1   I NVERSE SCALING COINCIDES WITH A FREQUENCY- DISTRIBUTION ANOMALY
372
373   As we show in Fig. 5B, the state-frequency distribution of Oware games is visibly different from the
374   Zipf’s law present in other games. The Oware Zipf curve is interrupted by a bump starting after rank
375   100, caused by a group of states with roughly constant frequency. The Zipf power-law exponent
376   remains unchanged before and after this interruption. In this section, we show that the anomalous
377   Zipf curve is caused by a property of the Oware game tree. The same anomaly occurs in Checkers,
      but is harder to detect solely by looking at frequency curves.


                                                       7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Turn distribution anomaly The distribution of state turn numbers exposes a distinct difference
379   between games with clean scaling laws and games with inverse scaling. In Fig. 6 we plot the average
380   number of moves played in an episode before encountering each state. Connect Four and Pentago
381   turn numbers are strongly correlated with state rank, due to the number of possible states diverging
382   exponentially with time. In contrast, Oware and Checkers display forked turn distributions where a
383   substantial fraction of the most frequent states appear late in the game. In Oware, the sharp change
384   in the fraction of late states (Fig. 6B) happens at the same rank where the Zipf curve bump appears.
385   States above and below the dashed line in Fig. 6A are clearly distinguishable when visualized, see
386   examples in appendix E. Late-game states above the dashed line are positions close to the end of a
387   game, when the board is mostly empty, while early-game states below the line are opening moves,
388   i.e. small variations of the initial board state.
389
390   Anomaly is caused by game rules The prevalence of high-frequency late-game states in Oware
391   and Checkers data is a direct result of the game rules, which shape the game-tree structure. In both
392   games, pieces are successively removed from the board, ending the game when one of the players
393   has no pieces left (infinite-loop draws are rarer). This win condition produces game trees that expand
394   and then contract: starting from a predetermined position, expanding exponentially every move, but
395
      then contracting again towards the end of the game as the number of pieces decreases and with it
      the number of possible board-state permutations. The probability that a game ends in a frequently-
396
      visited board state is high.
397
398   In contrast, Connect Four and Pentago have a different tree structure. These are line-completion
399   games where pieces are repeatedly added to an empty board, and the number of possible full-board
400   combinations is still high. Go and Chess also do not have converging game trees; a Go game
401
      typically ends with many pieces on the board, with large combinatorial complexity. Chess is a
      piece-elimination game similar to Checkers, but winning is not conditioned on capturing all the
402
      opponent’s pieces and checkmates can occur while the board is relatively full.
403
404
405   7.2   R ELATION TO INVERSE SCALING
406
407   The occurrence of inverse scaling at large model sizes as well as an anomaly in the train-set distribu-
408   tions suggests that the two phenomena might be linked. A possible connection is that an increased
409
      neural capacity leads to higher-rank states having more influence on model training. Here we present
      evidence for such a connection between state distribution and inverse scaling.
410
411
412   Oware loss spikes at transition point. As we show in Fig. 6, Late-game states (above the dashed
413   line) dominate the Oware distribution at higher ranks, in a sharp transition from 0% to > 80%
414   of all states. This transition is drastic enough to visibly skew the frequency distribution seen in
415
      Fig. 5B, bending the Zipf power law. The distribution shift causes Oware value loss to spike, as we
      show in Fig. 7A. The loss of all but the largest models jumps by up to an order of magnitude at the
416
      transition point, showing that models that fit early-game states well have a hard time fitting late-game
417
      states. This counter-intuitive behavior of getting worse loss on easier states, which we discussed in
418   section 6, is even more surprising here: Oware states after the transition point are predominantly
419   end-game configurations, and should be significantly easier to model than early-game states using
420   search algorithms.
421
      The quantization model provides an explanation for the sharp rise in loss. As models fit the most
422
      frequent states, smaller models do not have enough capacity to additionally model less frequent
423
      states accurately. The large qualitative difference between early- and late-game states makes the
424   difference in loss especially pronounced. Interestingly, larger agents converge on a smooth loss
425   curve similar to that of Connect Four, at the cost of worse loss on early-game states.
426
427   Large models overfit on late-game states. Splitting the loss curve to early- and late-game states
428   paints a clearer picture. Loss on early-game positions scales smoothly for all models (Fig. 7B), with
429   smaller models achieving lower loss than larger models. Surprisingly, large models have better loss
430   on late game states (Fig. 7C) following a reversed order compared to early-game states. We note that
431   the data is collected from games at late stages of training, when game quality is high and relatively
      constant.

      7.3   W HY DO LARGE MODELS OVERFIT ?
471
472   The degradation of large model performance on early-game states is an example where RL scaling
473   diverges from LLM scaling theory. The quantization model applied to LLMs explains how larger
474   models could fit more tasks, without forgetting higher-frequency tasks. In RL, we see that fitting
475   less-frequent states coincides with lower performance on the most frequent states, when states split
476   into two distinct groups. The difference between LLM and RL scaling might be caused by a shift of
477   the target distribution during training, which is known to cause training failure, e.g. through dormant
478   neurons (Sokar et al., 2023).
479
480   Large models selectively forget. During training, large models seem to forget what they learn
481   about early-game states, but not about late-game. In Fig. 8 we plot the loss of the largest Oware
482
      model at different stages of training. We see a clear difference between the learning dynamics of
      early- and late-states: late-game loss improves gradually throughout training, while early-game loss
483
      improves, then degrades at late training.
484
485   A possible explanation of this selective forgetting is a selective shift in the distribution of the target
      labels (the value of each state). Sokar et al. (2023) have shown that changes to the target distribution


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      in RL can harm performance by creating dormant neurons. Unlike early-game states, late-game
487   states have relatively fixed label distributions since the outcome is already decided by that point of
488   the game. A hypothetical mechanism of inverse scaling then follows: 1) Models fit states by order
489   of frequency, gradually expanding the fraction of late-game states in the pool of learned states. 2)
490   As new states are optimized, higher-frequency states learned earlier can be forgotten. 3) In Oware,
491   late-game states are immune to forgetting due to their strongly fixed labels. 4) Large Oware models
492   are trapped in local optima, fitting many end-game states well but forgetting a smaller fraction of
493   early-game states.
494
      Comparison to inverse scaling in supervised learning We note a certain similarity between this
495
      inverse scaling phenomenon and one of the causes for inverse scaling identified by McKenzie et al.
496   (2023), namely distractor tasks. Some LLM benchmarks require mastering a set of tasks T , but
497   as model scale increases, models first learn a set of easier tasks D that harm performance, leading
498   to inverse scaling. Another similar concept is competing tasks, which can cause inverse scaling in
499   a supervised learning setting (Ildiz et al., 2024). While the concept of competing/distractor tasks
500   bears resemblance to the Oware case, the mechanism behind these phenomena is different. These
501   examples of supervised learning inverse scaling are driven by the similarity of tasks, while in our
502   case early- and late-game states are far from similar, which is why we believe inverse scaling is
503   caused by indirect interaction between tasks through forgetting.
504
      Limitations. We note two limitations of our analysis of inverse scaling. First, unlike Oware, the
505   influence of late game configurations is harder to visualize in Checkers. Since the transition to late-
506   game states is smoother in Checkers (Fig. 6B), the Zipf curve only visibly skews at very high rank
507   numbers (Fig. 5C). As a result, one would need exceedingly large amounts of data to accurately plot
508   the rank at which late-states dominate the distribution.
509
      The second limitation is the use of game states as proxies for independent task quanta. Oware inverse
510
      scaling cannot be solved simply by removing all late turns from the training distribution, possibly
511   because the group of tasks that models overfit on only partially correlates with accuracy on late-
512   game states. Fig. 7 misses a large chunk of information about the dataset due to the large number
513   of states that only appear once in training. These states, at the tail-end of the frequency curve, have
514   low individual frequency but are the majority of states encountered in training. In appendix F we
515   visualize data frequency over a different axis and show that the tail-end of the state distribution
516   is crucial for accurately calculating frequency scaling along other axes. A better grouping of task
517   quanta might allow one to know which states to exclude from training in order to prevent inverse
518   scaling.
519
520   8   D ISCUSSION
521
522   We presented evidence connecting the quantization model of LLM neural scaling to AlphaZero
523   power laws. In particular, we found that AlphaZero data follows Zipf’s law and that agents optimize
524   states according to their frequency rank, counter-intuitively achieving worse loss on easier states.
525   We also shed light on the mechanism behind inverse scaling, showing that inverse scaling correlates
526   with an abnormal frequency distribution. This in turn can cause agents with larger capacity to focus
527   on end-game states, getting better loss on these states but worse loss on crucial opening moves.
528   While this work touches several aspects of scaling law theory, we still lack a full model of RL scaling
529   that can fully connect state distribution to Elo scaling laws. We provided clear evidence to the effect
530   of Zipf’s law on loss, but loss is only a proxy for performance and the multi-agent Elo metric is
531   connected to it in a non-trivial way. A significant problem with applying the quantization model to
532   explain scaling laws is the difficulty of identifying task quanta, both for LLMs and RL, which is
533   why we use game states as a proxy of tasks. Identifying the tasks or concepts that AlphaZero learns
534   is a challenging interpretability problem, mostly limited to human game knowledge (McGrath et al.,
535   2022; Schut et al., 2023).
536   We hope the analysis presented here will help advance the understanding of RL scaling. RL methods
537   are often challenging to scale, and understanding why is a prominent topic in the field today (Kumar
538   et al., 2020; Lyle et al., 2022; Sokar et al., 2023; Farebrother et al., 2024). Our results hint at a
539   possibility for improved RL algorithms using a curriculum informed by the frequency distribution,
      similar to recent attempts in supervised learning (Ruoss et al., 2024).

      A     BACKGROUND : A LPHA Z ERO
703
704
      AlphaZero is an RL algorithm for learning two-player zero-sum games, learning solely from self
705   play (Silver et al., 2017a). The algorithm performs a Monte-Carlo tree search on the tree of future
706   moves, guided by a neural network. The neural net receives the current board state as input, pro-
707   viding an estimate of its value, v, together with a policy prior, p. The loss function is composed of
708   separate elements for v and p:
                                                         2
709                                         L = (z − v) − π ⊤ log p .                                   (6)
710
      During training, the agent visits board states s, having ground-truth labels z, for which it gener-
711   ates estimates v and π for the state value and target prior, respectively. The MSE value-loss term
712           2
      (z − v) pushes v to fit the observed game outcome z ∈ {1, 0, −1}, representing win, draw and loss
713   respectively. The cross-entropy policy loss −π ⊤ log p pushes p to fit the actual policy π generated
714   by the tree search. AlphaZero is known to follow scaling laws in certain games (Neumann & Gros,
715   2022; Jones, 2021).

      Actor-critic methods are a family of popular policy-gradient algorithms, such as PPO and SAC
919   (Konda & Tsitsiklis, 1999; Schulman et al., 2017; Haarnoja et al., 2018). These models use an
920   architecture where two neural network outputs are used, namely a value estimation v (or alternatively
921   a Q value) and a policy vector p. Actions are sampled from the policy vector, which in turn is trained
922   on the output of the value net v. While the loss of the value net on test data should be low for better
923   agents, it has no effect on the actions played at test time.

       F    F REQUENCY SCALING ON A DIFFERENT AXIS : CAPTURE DIFFERENCE
1039
1040
       The quantization model of neural scaling laws states that models will learn independent task quanta
1041
       by descending order of frequency. In language modalities, it is assumed that the known Zipf’s law
1042   of word frequencies will lead to a Zipf’s law of tasks. Similarly, we show that board states follow
1043   Zipf’s law, suggesting that AlphaZero task quanta will also scale with a frequency power law. The
1044   states themselves are not independent quanta, since many of them share similarities, and strategic
1045   knowledge of one state can often transfer to another.
1046
       Here we show a different way to visualize training data frequency, to illustrate that more than one
1047
       dimension of the data can have clear frequency scaling. When models fit tasks by frequency, some
1048   most-frequent tasks will not be represented at all on the state-rank curve, because states do not
1049   correspond to tasks directly.
1050
1051
       To illustrate the high-dimensionality of the training data, we plot in Fig. 15 the frequency of capture-
       differences in Oware and Checkers board states. Capture difference is defined as the difference
1052
       between the number of pieces captured by each player at a certain point in the game, in absolute
1053
       value. We see that frequency drops smoothly in log-scale with capture difference, as states with a
1054   higher score-difference between players are rarer. According to the quantization model, one would
1055   expect agents to fit states according to the exponential distribution of capture-differences, giving
1056   exponentially-decreasing importance to states with higher differences.
1057
       The distribution of capture-differences cannot be represented correctly in our state-frequency anal-
1058
       ysis. We demonstrate this in Fig. 15, where we plot in yellow the same distribution, but omitting the
1059
       tail of the Zipf’s law state-frequency distribution. The tail is mostly composed of one-time states,
1060   i.e. states that appeared only once in training. In fact, the majority of unique states in the dataset
1061   have a frequency of 1. By discarding these states, as we do in our main results, we lose information
1062   about the exponential decay of capture-difference frequencies. Our main results cannot take these
1063   states into account, because exponentially-more games must be played in order to correctly measure
1064   the frequencies of one-time states.
1065
       Capture difference is an arbitrary measure, but it showcases the difficulty of visualizing data fre-
1066   quency. By visualizing one dimension of the data, we lose useful information about the frequency
1067   of other properties. If task quanta are indeed learned by the models, then these tasks would likely
1068   have a non-trivial representation, and visualizing their frequency could be difficult.
