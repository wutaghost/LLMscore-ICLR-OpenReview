009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Domain Generalization (DG) aims to train models that perform well not only on
012            the training (source) domains but also on novel, unseen target data distributions.
013            A key challenge in DG is preventing overfitting to source domains, which can
014            be mitigated by finding flatter minima in the loss landscape. In this work, we
015            propose Quantization-aware Training for Domain Generalization (QT-DoG) and
016            demonstrate that weight quantization effectively leads to flatter minima in the loss
017            landscape, thereby enhancing domain generalization. Unlike traditional quantiza-
018
               tion methods focused on model compression, QT-DoG exploits quantization as an
               implicit regularizer by inducing noise in model weights, guiding the optimization
019
               process toward flatter minima that are less sensitive to perturbations and overfitting.
020
               We provide both an analytical perspective and empirical evidence demonstrating
021            that quantization inherently encourages flatter minima, leading to better general-
022            ization across domains. Moreover, with the benefit of reducing the model size
023            through quantization, we demonstrate that an ensemble of multiple quantized mod-
024            els further yields superior accuracy than the state-of-the-art DG approaches with
025            no computational or memory overheads. Our extensive experiments demonstrate
026            that QT-DoG generalizes across various datasets, architectures, and quantization
027            algorithms, and can be combined with other DG methods, establishing its versatility
028            and robustness.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Many works have shown that deep neural networks trained under the assumption that the training and
033
      test samples are drawn from the same distribution fail to generalize in the presence of large training-
      testing discrepancies, such as texture (Geirhos et al., 2019; Bahng et al., 2020), background (Xiao
034
      et al., 2020), or day-to-night (Dai & Van Gool, 2018; Michaelis et al., 2019) shifts. Domain
035
      Generalization (DG) addresses this problem and aims to learn models that perform well not only in
036   the training (source) domains but also in new, unseen (target) data distributions (Blanchard et al.,
037   2011; Muandet et al., 2013; Zhou et al., 2022).
038
039
      In the broader context of generalization, with training and test data drawn from the same distribution,
      the literature has revealed a relationship between the flatness of the loss landscape and the general-
040
      ization ability of deep learning models (Keskar et al., 2017; Dziugaite & Roy, 2017; Garipov et al.,
041
      2018; Izmailov et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2020; Foret et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023). This relationship
042   has then been leveraged by many recent works, demonstrating that a flatter minimum also improves
043   Out-of-Distribution (OOD) performance (Cha et al., 2021; Ramé et al., 2023; Arpit et al., 2022). At
044   the heart of all these DG methods lies the idea of weight averaging (Izmailov et al., 2018), which
045   involves averaging weights from several trained models or at various stages of the training process.
046
      In this work, we demonstrate that flatter minima in the loss landscape can be effectively achieved
047
      through weight quantization using Quantization-aware Training (QAT), making it an effective ap-
048   proach for DG. By restricting the possible weight values to a lower bit precision, quantization imposes
049   constraints on the weight space, introducing quantization noise into the network parameters. This
050   noise, as discussed in prior works (An, 1996; Murray & Edwards, 1992; Goodfellow et al., 2016;
051   Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1994), acts as a form of regularization that naturally encourages the opti-
052   mization process to converge toward flatter minima. Furthermore, our results show that models trained
053   with quantization not only generalize better across domains but also reduce overfitting to source
      domains. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to explicitly explore the intersection of

      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
086
             • Quantization for Domain Generalization. We are the first to demonstrate that quantization-
087            aware training, traditionally used for model compression, can serve as an implicit regularizer,
088            with quantization noise enhancing domain generalization.
089
             • Flat Minima via QAT: We empirically demonstrate that QAT promotes flatter minima in
090
               the loss landscape and provide an analytical perspective behind this effect. Additionally, we
091
               show that QAT stabilizes model behavior on OOD data during training.
092
093          • Resource Efficiency: In contrast to traditional DG methods that often increase model size or
094
               computational cost, QT-DoG not only improves generalization but also significantly reduces
               the model size, enabling efficient deployment in real-world applications. EoQ, for instance,
095
               requires nearly 6 times less memory than Arpit et al. (2022) and 12 times less training
096
               compute compared to Ramé et al. (2023), which trains 60 models for diverse averaging.
097
098          • EoQ We introduce EoQ, a strategy that combines the power of quantization and ensembling
099            for generalization. EoQ achieves state-of-the-art results on the DomainBed benchmark while
100
               matching the computational cost and memory footprint as a single full-precision model.

105   2.1   D OMAIN G ENERALIZATION
106
      Numerous multi-source domain generalization (DG) methods have been proposed in the past. In this
107
      section, we review some of the recent approaches, categorizing them into different groups based on
      their methodologies.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2.1.1   D OMAIN A LIGNMENT
109
110
      The methods in this category focus on reducing the differences among the source domains and learn
      domain-invariant features (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Krueger et al., 2021; Rame et al., 2022a; Sun et al.,
111
      2016; Sagawa et al., 2020; Ganin et al., 2016; Li et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2024). The core idea is
112
      that, if the learnt features are invariant across the different source domains, they will also be robust to
113   the unseen target domain. For matching feature distributions across source domains, DANN (Ganin
114   et al., 2016) uses an adversarial loss while CORAL (Sun & Saenko, 2016) and DICA (Muandet et al.,
115   2013) seek to align latent statistics of different domains. Unfortunately, most of these methods fail
116   to generalize well and were shown not to outperform ERM on various benchmarks (Gulrajani &
117   Lopez-Paz, 2021; Ye et al., 2022; Koh et al., 2021a).
118
119   2.1.2   R EGULARIZATION
120   In the literature, various ways of regularizing models (implicit and explicit) have also been proposed
121   to achieve better generalization. For example, invariant risk minimization (Arjovsky et al., 2019)
122   relies on a regularization technique such that the learned classifier is optimal even under a distribution
123   shift. Moreover, (Huang et al., 2020) tries to suppress the dominant features learned from the
124   source domain and pushes the network to use other features correlating with the labels. Furthermore,
125
      (Krueger et al., 2021) proposes risk extrapolation that uses regularization to minimize the variance
      between domain-wise losses, considering that it is representative of the variance including the target
126
      domain.
127
128   2.1.3   V ISION T RANSFORMERS
129
      Recent studies have increasingly utilized vision transformers for domain generalization (Shu et al.,
130
      2023; Sultana et al., 2022). Some approaches enhance vision transformers by integrating knowledge
131
      distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) and leveraging text modality from CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to
132   learn more domain-invariant features (Moayeri et al., 2023; Addepalli et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024;
133   Huang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024).
134
135   2.1.4   E NSEMBLING
136
      Ensembling of deep networks (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Hansen & Salamon, 1990; Krogh
137
      & Vedelsby, 1995) is a foundational strategy and has consistently proven to be robust in the past.
138
      Many works have been proposed to train multiple diverse models and combine them to obtain
139   better in-domain accuracy and robustness to domain shifts (Arpit et al., 2022; Thopalli et al., 2021;
140   Mesbah et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022; Pagliardini et al., 2023). However, ensembles
141   require multiple models to be stored and a separate forward pass for each model, which increases the
142   computational cost and memory footprint, especially if the models are large.
143
144   2.1.5   W EIGHT AVERAGING
145
      Combining or averaging weights from different training stages or models has emerged as a robust
146   approach to improve OOD generalization (Wortsman et al., 2022b; Matena & Raffel, 2022; Wortsman
147   et al., 2022a; Gupta et al., 2020; Choshen et al., 2022; Wortsman et al., 2021; Maddox et al., 2019;
148   Benton et al., 2021; Cha et al., 2021; Jain et al., 2023; Ramé et al., 2023). Techniques like SWAD (Cha
149   et al., 2021) leverage weight averaging to identify flat minima, reducing overfitting and enhancing
150   generalization under distribution shifts. Similarly, DiWA (Rame et al., 2022b) combines weights
151   from independently trained models to improve robustness through increased diversity.
152   Arpit et al. (2022) integrates ensembling with weight averaging, yielding superior performance
153   compared to either method alone, albeit with significant memory and computational costs. To address
154   these challenges, we demonstrate that quantization can improve generalization while reducing
155   resource demands.
156
      Although flatter minima are not universally indicative of better domain generalization (An-
157
      driushchenko et al., 2023), they remain a valuable tool for improving robustness in many scenarios.
158   Moreover, recent findings (Mueller et al., 2023) highlight that selective application of SAM (Foret
159   et al., 2021), such as restricting it to normalization layers, can further refine its effectiveness. The
160   consistent empirical success of SAM underscores its reliability as a method for enhancing domain
161   generalization, despite the nuanced relationship between flatness and performance across different
      settings.

      2.2   M ODEL Q UANTIZATION
163
164   Model quantization is used in deep learning to reduce the memory footprint and computational
165   requirements of deep network. In a conventional neural network, the model parameters and activations
166
      are usually stored as high-precision floating-point numbers, typically 32-bit or 64-bit. The process of
      model quantization entails transforming these parameters into lower bit-width representations, such
167
      as 8-bit integers or binary values. Existing techniques fall into two main categories. Post-Training
168
      Quantization (PTQ) quantizes a pre-trained network using a small calibration dataset and is thus
169   relatively simple to implement (Nagel et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Frantar & Alistarh, 2022; Zhao
170   et al., 2019; Cai et al., 2020; Nagel et al., 2019; Shao et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024; Chee et al., 2023).
171   Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) retrains the network during the quantization process and thus
172   better preserves the model’s full-precision accuracy. Yang et al. (2023); Esser et al. (2020); Zhou et al.
173   (2017); Bhalgat et al. (2020); Yamamoto (2021); Yao et al. (2020); Shin et al. (2023). In the next
174   section, we provide some background on quantization and on the method we will use in our approach.
175   Our goal in this work is not to introduce a new quantization strategy but rather to demonstrate the
176   impact of quantization on generalization.
177
      3     D OMAIN G ENERALIZATION BY Q UANTIZATION
178
179   We build our method on the simple ERM approach to showcase the effects of quantization on the
180   training process and on the generalization to unseen data from a different domain. Despite the
181   simplicity of this approach, we will show in Section 4.3 that it yields a significant accuracy boost on
182
      the test data from the unseen target domain. Furthermore, it stabilizes the behavior of the model on
      OOD data during training, making it similar to that on the in-domain data. In the remainder of this
183
      section, we focus on providing some insights on how quantization enhances DG.
184

186

      of negative and positive quantization levels, respectively. We define the quantization process that

      computes w̄, a quantized and integer scaled representation of the weights, as

206   3.2   Q UANTIZATION L EADS TO F LAT M INIMA
207
      In the literature (Rame et al., 2022b; Arpit et al., 2022; Krueger et al., 2021; Cha et al., 2021; Rame
208
      et al., 2022b; Foret et al., 2021), it has been established that a model’s generalization ability can
209
      be increased by finding a flatter minimum during training. This is the principle we exploit in our
210   work, but from the perspective of quantization, and provide an analytical view into how it contributes
211   to achieving flatter minima. In practice, ERM can have several solutions with similar training loss
212   values but different generalization ability. Even when the training and test data are drawn from the
213   same distribution, the standard optimizers, such as SGD and Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015), often lead
214   to sub-optimal generalization by finding sharp and narrow minima (Keskar et al., 2017; Dziugaite &
215   Roy, 2017; Garipov et al., 2018; Izmailov et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2020; Foret et al., 2021). This has
      been shown to be prevented by introducing noise in the model weights during training (An, 1996;


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Murray & Edwards, 1992; Goodfellow et al., 2016; Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1994). Here, we
217   argue that quantization inherently induces such noise and thus helps to find flatter minima.
218
219   Let yˆi = f (x, w) represent the predicted output of the network f , which is parameterized by the
220
      weights w. A quantized network can then be represented as
221                                     f (x, wq ) = f (x, w + ∆) = yˆi q ,
222
223   where wq denotes the quantized weights and yˆi q the corresponding prediction. The quantized weights
224   can thus be thought of as introducing perturbations (∆) to the full-precision weights, akin to noise
225   affecting the weights.
226
      Such noise induced by the weight quantization can also be seen as a form of regularization, akin to
227
      more traditional methods. For small perturbations, (An, 1996; Murray & Edwards, 1992; Goodfellow
228   et al., 2016) show that this type of regularization encourages the parameters to navigate towards
229   regions of the parameter space where small perturbations of the weights have minimal impact on the
230   output, i.e., flatter minima.
231
      When noise is introduced via quantization, second-order Taylor series approximation of the loss
232
      function for the perturbed weights w + ∆ can be expressed as
233
234                                                         1
                                L(w + ∆) ≈ L(w) + ∇L(w)⊤ ∆ + ∆⊤ H∆,                                         (4)
235                                                         2
236
237   where L(w) is the loss at the original weights w, ∇L(w) is the gradient of the loss at w, and
238
      H = ∇2 L(w) is the Hessian matrix, which contains second-order partial derivatives of the loss
      function with respect to the weights, representing the curvature of the loss surface.
239
240   Eq. 4 shows how the quantization noise ∆ interacts with the curvature H of the loss function. In
241   regions with large curvature (sharp minima), the Hessian H has large eigenvalues, and even small
242   perturbations ∆ result in large increases in the loss (Dinh et al., 2017). In contrast, in flat regions
243   (small eigenvalues of H), the loss remains nearly unchanged for small perturbations. Quantization
244
      noise acts as an implicit regularizer by introducing perturbations ∆ that disrupt the model’s weight
      updates. In sharper minima, where the Hessian H eigenvalues are large, small noise significantly
245
      increases the loss, causing the model to "escape" these regions and search for flatter, more stable
246
      minima. In flatter regions, where the Hessian H eigenvalues are small, the noise has less impact,
247   helping the model settle into these regions with lower loss. This encourages convergence to solutions
248   that are less sensitive to small changes in the input or model parameters, which is beneficial for
249   out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization.
250
      In the case of quantization-aware training, the induced noise ∆ is influenced by the quantization bin
251
      width or the quantizer step size s, and thus ranges between − 2s and + 2s . This s is directly dependent
252
      on the quantization levels or the bit-width chosen for weight quantization. As the number of bits
253   per weight decreases, the amount of induced noise increases. Hence, the impact of the additional
254   noise can be weighed by choosing an optimal bit-width. As will be shown in Section 4, certain
255   bit-widths thus yield better and flatter minima that enhance generalization. However, if we induce
256   too much noise(very low bit-precision), it introduces over-regularization. This excessive noise can
257   overly restrict the search space, preventing the model from reaching a good solution. Instead, the
258   optimization process may focus on minimizing the loss in a way that avoids sharp regions, but
259   sacrifices the ability to find true minimum of the loss function. This is also evident in Table 10 in the
260   appendix.
261   Moreover, Rissanen (1978); Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997) show that a flatter minimum cor-
262   responds to a low complexity network and requires fewer bits of information per weight. More
263   importantly, Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997) demonstrates the importance of the bit-precision
264   of the network weights and adds a regularization term in the loss function that seeks to lower the
265   weight bit-precision to lead to flatter minima. Here, by using quantization, we are explicitly reducing
266   bit-precision of the network weights, thus achieving the same goal.

302
      in-domain validation set. Specifically, we seek to show that accuracy on the in-domain validation data
303

304   during training, the model selection criterion based on this validation data can select the best model for
305   the OOD data even if the model starts to overfit. In other words, it is expected that the out-of-domain
306   evaluation at each point of the training phase should improve or rather stay stable if the model is close
307   to overfitting to the in-domain data. For these experiments, we use the TerraIncognita dataset (Beery
308   et al., 2018) and consider the same number of iterations as for the DomainBed protocol (Gulrajani &

354   We demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed method on diverse classification datasets used for

356
      PACS (Li et al., 2017) is a 7 object classification challenge encompassing four domains, with a total
357
      of 9,991 samples. It serves to validate our method in smaller-scale settings. VLCS (Fang et al.,

      2013) poses a 5 object classification problem across four domains. With 10,729 samples, VLCS

361   samples. It presents a 65-way classification challenge featuring everyday objects across four domains.
362   TerraIncognita (Beery et al., 2018) addresses a 10 object classification challenge of animals captured
363   in wildlife cameras, with four domains representing different locations. The dataset contains 24,788
364   samples, illustrating a realistic use-case where generalization is crucial. DomainNet (Peng et al.,
365   2019) provides a 345 object classification problem spanning six domains. With 586,575 samples, it is
366   one of the largest datasets.
367   We report out-of-domain accuracies for each domain and their average, i.e., a model is trained
368   and validated on training domains and evaluated on the unseen target domain. Each out-of-domain
369   performance is an average of three different runs with different train-validation splits for the quantized
370   models. We then combine the predictions of the different quantized models for our EoQ results.

      4.3.1    C OMPARISON WITH DG METHODS
409   Table 1 reports out-of-domain performances on five DG benchmarks and compares our proposed
410   approaches to prior works. These results demonstrate the superiority of EoQ across five DomainBed
411   datasets, with an average improvement of 0.4% over the state-of-the-art EoA while reducing the
412   memory footprint by approximately 75%. Compared to DiWA, we significantly reduce the computa-
413   tional burden and memory requirements for training, achieving a 12-fold reduction, as DiWA requires
414
      training 60 models for diverse averaging. EoQ achieves the most significant gain (7% improvement)
      on TerraIncognita (Beery et al., 2018), with nonetheless substantial gains of 3-5% w.r.t. ERM on
415
      PACS (Li et al., 2017) and DomainNet (Peng et al., 2019).
416
417   The results also demonstrate that simply introducing quantization into the ERM-based approach (Gul-
418   rajani & Lopez-Paz, 2021) surpasses or yields comparable accuracy to many existing works, although
419   the size and computational budget of our quantization-based approach is significantly lower than that
420
      of the other methods. For our results in Table 1 and Figure 1, we employed 7-bit quantization on the
      network. Therefore, as shown in Figure 1, the model size is drastically reduced, becoming more than
421
      4 times smaller than the other methods. Being smaller in memory footprint, our quantization-based
422
      approach can utilize ensembling without increasing the memory storage and computational resources.
423   Moreover, quantization not only reduces the memory footprint but also the latency of the model. For
424   example, running a ResNet-50 model on an AMD EPYC 7302 processor yields a latency of 34.28ms
425   for full-precision and 21.02ms for our INT8 quantized model.

469   In this section, we perform an ablation study by replacing LSQ (Esser et al., 2020) with other
470   quantization algorithms. We use INQ (Zhou et al., 2017) as another quantization-aware training
471
      method but also perform quantization using OBC (Frantar et al., 2022), that uses a more popular
      post-training quantization (PTQ) approach to quantize a network. We perform this ablation study on
472
      the PACS dataset, and the results are shown in Table 2. All the experiments are performed with 7-bit
473
      quantization. We observe that, while the QAT approaches tend to enhance generalization, the PTQ
474   approach fails to do so. This is due to the fact that there is no training involved after the quantization
475   step in PTQ. That is, with PTQ, we do not train the network with quantization noise to find a flatter
476   minimum.

513   5       D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
514   Despite showing success and surpassing the state-of-the-art methods in terms of performance, EoQ
515   also has some limitations. First, it requires training multiple models like Rame et al. (2022b); Arpit
516   et al. (2022), to create diversity and form an ensemble. This ensemble creation increases the training
517   computational load. Nevertheless, our quantized ensembling models are much smaller in size.
518
      Another limitation of this work is the challenge of determining the optimal bit precision for achieving
519
      the best performance in OOD generalization. In our experiments on the DomainBed benchmark,
520   we identified 7 bits as the optimal precision. However, this may not hold true for other datasets. A
521   potential future direction is to utilize a small number of target images to identify the optimal bit
522   precision, which would significantly reduce the computational overhead associated with this process.
523
      Lastly, given our utilization of a uniform quantization strategy, it would be interesting to investigate
524
      whether specific layers can be more effectively exploited than others through mixed-precision
525
      techniques to have even better domain generalization performance.
526
527   6       C ONCLUSION
528   We introduced QT-DoG, a novel generalization strategy based on neural network quantization. Our
529   approach leverages the insight that QAT can find flatter minima in the loss landscape, serving as
530   an effective regularization method to reduce overfitting and enhance the generalization capabilities.
531   We empirically demonstrated, supported by analytical insights, that quantization not only enhances
532   generalization but also helps stabilize the training process. Our extensive experiments across diverse
533   datasets show that incorporating quantization with an optimal bit-width significantly enhances
534   domain generalization, yielding performance comparable to existing methods while reducing the
535
      model size. Additionally, we proposed EoQ, a powerful ensembling strategy that addresses the
      challenges of memory footprint and computational load by creating ensembles of quantized models.
536
      EoQ outperforms state-of-the-art methods while being approximately four times smaller than its
537
      full-precision ensembling counterparts.

1010   I     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
1011
1012
       We use the same training procedure as DomainBed (Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2021), incorporating
1013
       additional components from quantization. Specifically, we adopt the default hyperparameters from
1014   DomainBed (Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2021), including a batch size of 32 (per-domain). We employ
1015   a ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016) pre-trained on ImageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2015) as initial model
1016   and use a learning rate of 5e-5 along with the Adam optimizer, and no weight decay. Following
1017   SWAD(Cha et al., 2021), the models are trained for 15,000 steps on DomainNet and 5,000 steps on
1018   the other datasets. In the training process, we keep a specific domain as the target domain, while the
1019   remaining domains are utilized as source domains. During this training phase, 20% of the samples
1020   are used for validation and model selection. We validate the model every 300 steps using held-out
1021   data from the source domains, and assess the final performance on the excluded domain (target).
1022   We use LSQ (Esser et al., 2020) and INQ (Zhou et al., 2017) for model quantization, with the same
1023   configuration as existing quantization methods (Esser et al., 2020; Bhalgat et al., 2020; Dong et al.,
1024   2019; Yao et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2017), where all layers are quantized to lower bit precision except
1025   the last one. We quantize the models at 8,000 steps for DomainNet and 2,000 steps for the other
       datasets. Moreover, each channel in a layer has a different scaling factor.
