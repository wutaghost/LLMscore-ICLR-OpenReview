009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The representations of conditional entropy and conditional mutual information
012            are significant in explaining the unique effects among variables. The previous
013            works based on conditional contrastive sampling have successfully eliminated in-
014            formation about discrete sensitive variables, but have not yet addressed continu-
015            ous cases. This paper introduces a framework of Information Subtraction capable
016            of representing arbitrary information components between continuous variables.
017            We implement a generative-based architecture that outputs such representations
018            by simultaneously maximizing an information term and minimizing another. The
019
               results highlight the representations’ ability to provide semantic features of con-
               ditional entropy. By subtracting sensitive and domain-specific information, our
020
               framework effectively enhances fair learning and domain generalization.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   In complex systems, the variables could be entangled and correlated, thereby providing mutual
027
      information to each other. Such uncertainties and information can be quantified using information-
      theoretic measures depicted in Figure 1. These measurements help identify the relationships between
028
      variables, such as correlation and Granger causality (Pearl, 2009). Beyond merely recognizing such
029
      relationships, many works aim to further explain and represent them (Yao et al., 2021; Xu et al.,
030   2023), which enhance our understanding and control of the system. These representation learning
031   approaches generate representations that maximize their information about the targets, as they must
032   be capable of accurately recovering the targets during the reconstruction part (Kingma & Welling,
033   2013; Clark et al., 2019). However, while most methods effectively represent entropy (the entire
034   circle in Figure 1(a)), few address the representation of other information terms like conditional
035   mutual information and conditional entropy.
036   The representation of conditional mutual information is significant because it can reveal the unique
037   effect of certain factors on the target that other factors cannot provide. For example, representing the
038   unique effect of funding on scholars’ publications can guide policy suggestions, such as terminating
039   funding that shows insignificant boosting. Additionally, removing the effects of sensitive factors
040   helps to create fair and unbiased representations. For instance, we may want to eliminate biases in
041   employee performance evaluations due to discriminatory factors such as gender and race. We can
042   achieve this objective through the representation of conditional entropy.
043   To represent conditional entropy H(Y |X) = H(Y )−I(X; Y ), we should overcome the challenge to
044   selectively include information about Y while excluding the information about X. This requires the
045   architecture to optimize the representation by simultaneously maximizing and minimizing certain
046   information terms during training. (Ma et al., 2021) bypasses this challenge through conditional
047   sampling that selects samples with same conditions to be the input of neural networks. However,
048   this sampling method is limited to cases with continuous conditional variables, where (Tsai et al.)
049   proposes to apply clustering algorithms to select samples with similar conditions.
050   To address the challenge of selectively maximizing and minimizing different information terms, we
051   introduce the concept of Information Subtraction. It aims to subtract specific information from a
052   representation, which we implement through a generative-based architecture. The resulting rep-
053   resentation retains the desired information within the target scope Y and filters out the undesired
      information X beyond it. Furthermore, by iteratively applying Information Subtraction, we can rep-

132   3   R ELATED W ORKS
133
134   Many frameworks aim to maximize the information content of their representations for target vari-
135
      ables, assuming that target observations are driven or caused by a set of unobserved latent signals or
      representations. These frameworks are types of self-supervised learning (Zhang et al., 2023), which
136
      inputs target observations itself to generate representations without auxiliary supervising labels or
137
      information. Within this domain, two common approaches are generative-based and contrastive-
138   based methods.
139
140   The objective of generative-based approaches (Hyvärinen et al., 2008; Hyvarinen & Morioka, 2017;
141
      Hjelm et al., 2018; Klindt et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2020) is to optimize the posterior distributions
      of representations given the observations (encoding) and vice versa (decoding). The optimal en-
142
      coder, which maximizes the mutual information between representation and observations, is attained
143
      through various methods (Wu et al., 2020; Hjelm et al., 2018), including Variational Autoencoder
144   (VAE) (Kingma & Welling, 2013; Louizos et al., 2017; Yao et al., 2021) and Dynamical Component
145   Analysis (DCA) (Clark et al., 2019; Bai et al., 2020; Meng et al., 2022). Although these methods
146   ensure the identifiability of the latent variables, their structures do not specifically aim to represent
147   conditional information, thus motivating our work to achieve this goal.
148
      Following the idea that similar samples should have similar representations, contrastive-based ap-
149
      proaches (Ma et al., 2021) focus on contrasting the representations of positive or similar pairs of
150   observations against negative or dissimilar pairs. They aim to generate a contrastive representation
151   Z of the target Y that maximizes the conditional mutual information I(Z; Y |X). This is achieved
152   through conditional sampling: X is first sampled, followed by the sampling of positive and negative
153   pairs Y that share the same X. This approach performs outstandingly with discrete and categori-
154   cal X, but not with continuous cases. To address this limitation, (Tsai et al.) suggests identifying
155   samples with similar X values using clustering algorithms. Rather than performing conditioning at
156   the sampling stage, our method manipulates the information in the training objective and effectively
157   handles continuous X at the training stage.
158   Our work performs information maximization and minimization simultaneously, sharing a similar
159   objective to that of (Chen et al., 2016; Moyer et al., 2018; Kim et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2021; Ragonesi
160   et al., 2021). These works are capable of eliminating the information of biasing or sensitive terms
161   from the representations, and hence can be applied to fair learning and domain generalization prob-
      lems. Besides, there are other works that perform operations similar to information minimization,


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      such as the use of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) penalty (Louizos et al., 2015), β-VAE
163   (Higgins et al., 2017), and maximum cross-entropy (Alvi et al., 2018). While we share similar
164   architectures objectives with these works, their structures are not designed for conditional represen-
165   tations. We build upon and expand their frameworks to perform representation learning on condi-
166   tional entropy and conditional mutual information.

      5.5   R EAL CASE FOR D OMAIN G ENERALIZATION : C OVER T YPE DATA
479
480   In the fair learning example, the data is collected from several domains, such as ‘male’ and ‘female’.
481   Both the training and testing sets contain data from these two domains, and it is assumed that the
482   data in both sets is independently and identically distributed (i.i.d.). However, there are scenarios
483   where the testing set is consisted of data that is out-of-distribution from the training set. We might
484   train our models on data from multiple domains present in the training set, but subsequently test
485   them on data from other domains. This challenge is known as domain generalization (Wang et al.,
      2022; Zhou et al., 2022).

      For this purpose, we select the cover type dataset (Blackard, 1998), which includes features of
487   30 × 30 meter cells, such as elevation, aspect, slope, hillshade, and soil-type, across four distinct
488   regions within the Roosevelt National Forest of Northern Colorado. Based on these features X,
489   the task is to predict the predominant type of plant cover Y in these cells. The label indicating the
490   different domain areas C is provided. The training set is composed of samples from three regions
491   with higher elevation, while the testing set includes samples form the lowest region.
492
      In this case, we have to estimate landscape testing samples that does not appear in the training set,
493   and their distributions could be significantly different. Directly applying a model trained on the
494   original features X (which is correlated to C) could result in severe over-fitting. Our architecture
495   is designed to generate representation Z that excludes the domain-specific information (Zhu et al.,
496   2021). By representing H(X|C) with Z, the model focuses on learning general or universal features
497   that are consistent across different regions. The standard metrics for domain generalization include
498   the average-case and worst-case performance across different testing domains. In this instance, we
499   have three training domains and a single testing domain. Therefore, we present the accuracy of

517   is more’: including Z, which consists of a portion of X’s information, drives the model away from
518   the gradient descent trajectory of ŶX and reaches a better local optimum. Without introducing new
519   information during training, this approach enhances the model’s adaptability to out-of-distribution

522   6   C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this work, we have highlighted the significance of conditional representation learning. By captur-
525   ing the relationships and effects between variables, our results demonstrate the potential applications
526   of these representations in fair learning and domain generalization. We introduce a generative-based
527   architecture for Information Subtraction, which is designed to generate representations that retain
528   information about the target while eliminating that of the conditional variables.
529   We demonstrate that the resulting representations contain semantic features of the target that the
530   conditional variables do not provide. Moreover, the unbiased representation has shown advanced
531   fairness in both synthetic and real-world fair learning experiments. Finally, we demonstrate that
532   combining factors X, which carry the domain-specific information, and representations Z, which
533   carry universal features, leads to improved prediction for unseen out-of-distribution data.
534
      Many future improvements can be made regarding this topic. Firstly, we note that the current overall
535   objective is a simple weighted average of two information terms, and our architecture is not specifi-
536   cally designed for disentangling information. A more specialized architecture could achieve a better
537   representation Z with higher I(X; Z, C) and lower (Z; C). Additionally, in our domain general-
538   ization experiments, we simply put together X and Z as the input for estimating Y . An advanced
539   integration of X and Z could lead to more satisfying predictions in future works.
