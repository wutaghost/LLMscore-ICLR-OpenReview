008                                              Abstract
009
010             Link prediction—the task of distinguishing actual hidden edges from ran-
011
                dom unconnected node pairs—is a quintessential task in graph machine
                learning. Despite being widely accepted as a universal benchmark and a
012
                downstream task for representation learning, its validity is seldom ques-
013
                tioned. Here, we show that the common edge sampling procedure in
014             link prediction introduces an implicit bias toward high-degree nodes and
015             produces a skewed evaluation that favors methods overly reliant on node
016             degree, to the extent that a “null” method based solely on node degree
017             can nearly match optimal performance. To address this, we propose a
018             degree-corrected link prediction task that offers a more accurate assess-
019             ment that aligns better with performance in recommendation tasks. Fi-
020             nally, we demonstrate that this degree-corrected benchmark can more ef-
021             fectively train graph machine-learning models by reducing overfitting to
022
                node degrees and facilitating the learning of relevant structures in graphs.

024   1     Introduction
025
026   Standardized benchmarks like ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009; Krizhevsky et al., 2012) and
027   SQuAD (Rajpurkar et al., 2016; 2018) play a pivotal role in driving progress in machine
028   learning by fostering competition through setting clear, measurable goals. In graph ma-
029   chine learning, a core benchmark is link prediction, which involves identifying missing edges
030   in a graph, with diverse applications including the recommendations of friends and con-
031   tents (Kunegis & Lommatzsch, 2009; Wang et al., 2014; Huang et al., 2005; Menon &
032   Elkan, 2011), knowledge discoveries (Sun et al., 2019; Bordes et al., 2013), and drug devel-
033
      opment (Abbas et al., 2021; Breit et al., 2020; You et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2015; Crichton
      et al., 2018; Yue et al., 2020; Ali et al., 2019). Link prediction benchmarks have been essen-
034
      tial for quantitative evaluations, advancing graph machine learning techniques (Ghasemian
035
      et al., 2020; Liben-Nowell & Kleinberg, 2003; Mara et al., 2020; Breit et al., 2020; Yue et al.,
036   2020; Ali et al., 2019; Narayanan et al., 2011).
037
038   Despite its significant role in graph machine learning, the link prediction benchmark itself
039
      is rarely scrutinized for effectiveness, reliability, and bias. Typically, it evaluates methods
      based on their ability to classify node pairs as connected or unconnected (Kunegis & Lom-
040
      matzsch, 2009; Ghasemian et al., 2020; Mara et al., 2020). Connected pairs (edges) are
041
      randomly sampled from existing edges as the hidden positive set, while an equal number
042   of unconnected node pairs are sampled randomly. Criticisms often highlight its disconnect
043   from real-world scenarios. For instance, unconnected pairs vastly outnumber connected ones
044   because of the graph sparsity (Newman, 2018; Barabási & Pósfai, 2016), leading to biased
045   performance evaluations (Li et al., 2024a; Menand & Seshadhri, 2024; Yang et al., 2015;
046   Huang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2021). Additionally, the benchmark tests a predefined set
047   of edges, while real-world tasks involve identifying potential edges across the entire graph.
048   Despite this misalignment, high benchmark performance is often seen as a marker of suc-
049   cessful learning in graph machine learning (Ghasemian et al., 2020; Zhang & Chen, 2018;
050
      Breit et al., 2020; Crichton et al., 2018; Yue et al., 2020; Ali et al., 2019; Grover & Leskovec,
      2016; Ou et al., 2016; Goyal & Ferrara, 2018; Cai et al., 2021).
051
052   Here, we argue that the standard link prediction benchmark has a fundamental and severe
053   bias favoring methods that exploit node degree (the number of edges a node has). This bias
      arises from the edge sampling process: a node with k edges is k times more likely to be

      2     Design flaw of the link prediction benchmark
100
101
      2.1   Preliminary
102
103   We focus on unweighted, undirected graph G = (V, E), where V is the set of nodes and E
104   is the set of edges. We assume that G has no self-loops, no multiple edges, and is highly
105   sparse (|E| ≪ |V|2 ), a common characteristic of real-world graphs (Newman, 2018; Barabási
106   & Pósfai, 2016). Degree ki of a node i ∈ V is the number of edges connected to it. We use ∼
107   to denote proportional relationships. Node attributes, if present, are excluded to maintain
      consistency across all link prediction methods.

      2.2   Link prediction benchmark
109
110   The standard link prediction benchmark procedure is as follows (Kunegis & Lommatzsch,
111   2009; Zhang & Chen, 2018; Breit et al., 2020; Crichton et al., 2018; Yue et al., 2020; Ali
112   et al., 2019; Grover & Leskovec, 2016; Ou et al., 2016; Goyal & Ferrara, 2018; Cai et al.,
113   2021). First, a fraction β of edges is randomly sampled from the edge set E as positive edges.
114   Second, an equal number of unconnected node pairs is randomly sampled with replacement
115   from the node set V as negative edges. Negative edges are resampled if they form a loop
116   or are already in the positive or test edges. Third, each node pair (i, j) is scored by a
117
      link prediction method, where a higher score sij indicates a greater likelihood of an edge.
      Fourth, the method’s effectiveness is evaluated using the Area Under the Receiver Operating
118
      Characteristic Curve (AUC-ROC), which represents the probability that the method gives
119
      a higher score to a positive edge than a negative edge. While alternative benchmark designs
120   use different evaluation metrics or sampling strategies for negative edges (Li et al., 2024a;
121   Menand & Seshadhri, 2024; Yang et al., 2015; Huang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2021; Wang &
122   Derr, 2022; Russo et al., 2024; He et al., 2024) (Section 4), this outlined procedure is widely
123   adopted (Kunegis & Lommatzsch, 2009; Zhang & Chen, 2018; Breit et al., 2020; Crichton
124   et al., 2018; Yue et al., 2020; Ali et al., 2019; Grover & Leskovec, 2016; Ou et al., 2016;
125   Goyal & Ferrara, 2018; Ghasemian et al., 2020).
126
127   2.3   Sampling bias due to node degree
128
129   A well-known, counterintuitive fact about graphs is that a uniform random sampling of edges
130   introduces a degree bias in node selection (Feld, 1991; Barthélemy et al., 2004; Christakis &
131   Fowler, 2010; Kojaku et al., 2021a;b). The bias arises because a node with k edges appears
132
      k times in the edge list and thus k times more likely to be chosen than a node with k ′ = 1
      edge (e.g., node 1 and 4 in Fig. 1A). Consequently, for a graph with degree distribution p(k),
133
      the nodes in the positive edges have degree distribution ppos (k) proportional to ∼ k · p(k).
134
      By normalizing k · p(k), the degree distribution of the positive edges is given by
135
136                                              1                 1
                                ppos (k) = ∑           k · p(k) =     k · p(k),                  (1)
137
                                               ℓ ℓp(ℓ)            ⟨k⟩
138
139   where ⟨k⟩ is the average degree. By contrast, nodes in the negative edges are uniformly
140   sampled from the node set V, resulting in a degree distribution pneg (k) identical to p(k)
141
      (i.e., pneg (k) = p(k)).
142   We demonstrate the degree bias using the Price graph with N = 105 nodes and M = 106
143   edges, which follows a power-law degree distribution p(k) ∝ k −3 (Fig. 1B). We uniformly
144   sample β = 0.25 of the edges from E as positive edges, together with an equal number of
145   unconnected node pairs sampled uniformly from V. The degree distributions for nodes in
146   the positive and negative edges align with ppos (k) and pneg (k), respectively, confirming the
147   sampling bias due to node degree. This degree bias is not specific to the Price graph but
148
      occurs in any graph with a non-uniform degree distribution.

150   2.4   Impact of degree bias on the link prediction benchmark
151
152   We demonstrate the impact of degree bias on link prediction benchmarks (Fig.1D) by eval-
153
      uating 29 link prediction methods across 95 graphs from various domains, including social,
      technological, informational, biological, and transportation graphs. These methods include
154
      7 network topology-based methods (e.g., Common Neighbors (CN) (Liben-Nowell & Klein-
155
      berg, 2003)), 13 graph embedding methods (e.g., Laplacian EigenMap (EigenMap) (Belkin
156   & Niyogi, 2003)), 2 network models (e.g., Stochastic Block Model (e.g., SBM) (Fortu-
157   nato, 2010)), and 4 graph neural networks (GNNs) (e.g., Graph Convolutional Network
158   (GCN) (Kipf & Welling, 2017)). Detailed method and graph descriptions are available in
159   SI Section 1. We set the test edge fraction to β = 0.25 and repeat the experiment 5 times.
160   We quantify the heterogeneity σ of node degree by fitting a log-normal distribution to p(k)
161   and calculating its variance parameter σ. We will show that σ is a reliable indicator of the
      impact of degree bias in Section 2.5.

162
      We focus on the Preferential Attachment (PA) link prediction method, which calculates the
163   prediction score sij = ki kj using only node degrees. PA is a crude method that neglects key
164   predictive features like common neighbors and shortest distance (Li et al., 2024a; Menand
165   & Seshadhri, 2024; Lichtnwalter & Chawla, 2012; Zhang & Chen, 2018; Mao et al., 2023).
166   However, it still outperforms most advanced methods with an average AUC-ROC of 0.84
167   (ranked 13th out of 29 methods; see Fig. 2A). PA performs better as the heterogeneity of
168   node degrees increases. This outperformance is due to degree bias, where the positive edges
169   are more likely to be formed by nodes with high degree and thereby are easily distinguishable
170   from the negative edges. Thus, the current benchmark design favors methods that make
171
      predictions based largely on node degrees. This spurious performance of PA is evident
      when using Hits@K (SI Section 4.6), indicating that the issue stems from the benchmark
172
      design rather than evaluation metrics. This spurious performance of PA also persists for
173
      larger-scale graphs (SI Section 4.4).

      2.5   Theoretical analysis
176
177   Many empirical graphs exhibit heterogeneous degree distributions, with a few nodes having
178   exceptionally large degrees and most having small ones. These distributions are often char-
179   acterized by power-law degree distribution p(k) ∝ k −α with α ∈ (2, 3] (i.e., scale-free net-
180   works) (Albert & Barabási, 2002; Barabási & Bonabeau, 2003; Holme, 2019; Voitalov et al.,
181   2019) or log-normal distributions (Artico et al., 2020; Broido & Clauset, 2019). While the
182   power-law and log-normal distributions are both continuous, they are often used to approxi-
183   mate discrete degree distribution (Artico et al., 2020; Broido & Clauset, 2019; Clauset et al.,
184   2009; Radicchi et al., 2008; Johnson et al., 1995; Redner, 2005). We show that the AUC-
185
      ROC for PA reaches near-maximum under log-normal distributions with heterogeneous node
      degrees. See SI Section 4.3 for the case of power-law distributions.
186

188   distributions. The AUC-ROC has a probabilistic interpretation (Hand, 2009): it is the
189   probability that the score s+ for positive edges is larger than the score s− for negative
190   edges. Recalling that PA computes sij = ki kj , the AUC-ROC for PA is given by

251   3       The degree-corrected link prediction benchmark
252
253   The link prediction benchmark yields biased evaluations due to mismatched degree distribu-
254   tions between positive and negative edges, i.e., pneg (k) ̸= ppos (k). To mitigate the mismatch,
255   we introduce the degree-corrected link prediction benchmark that samples negative edges
256   with the same degree bias as positive edges (See Algorithm 1 in SI). Specifically, we create
257   a list of nodes where each node with degree k appears k times. Then, we sample negative
258
      edges by uniformly sampling two nodes from this list with replacement until the sampled
      node pairs are not connected and not in the test edge set. Crucially, nodes with degree
259
      k are k times more likely to be sampled than nodes with degree 1, mirroring the degree
260
      bias of the positive edges. Consequently, the positive and negative edges in the degree-
261   corrected benchmark are indistinguishable based on node degrees. A Python package for
262   the degree-corrected benchmark will be made available on GitHub.
263

265
266   We reevaluated the methods using the degree-corrected link prediction benchmark, main-
267   taining the same parameters as in the original benchmark. The results show a significant
268   drop in the performance of PA, with most methods having AUC-ROC scores close to 0.5
269   for most networks (Fig. 1E). We find qualitatively the same results when using the Hits@K
      score (SI Section 4.6).

      We note that our aim is not to completely eliminate degree as a predictive feature, but
271   rather to remove the bias introduced by negative edge sampling. The degree can remain a
272   meaningful predictor after correction if it genuinely correlates with the likelihood of edges
273   between nodes. The ‘biokg_drug’ graph effectively illustrates the precision of our degree-
274   correction method in removing artificial bias while preserving the link predictive power of
275   degree.
276
      The degree-corrected benchmark has some agreement with the original benchmark in terms
277   of the ranking of the methods (Fig. 2A). For example, they rank GAT and LRW as top
278   performers, while NB, SGTAdjNeu, and SGTAdjExp are consistently ranked lower. On the
279   other hand, the degree-corrected benchmark ranks PA as the lowest performer, with its
280   average AUC-ROC dropping from 0.83 to 0.54, placing it last out of 29 methods. Other
281   methods such as LPI, GIN also experience a substantial drop in their rankings from 4nd
282   to 12th and 10th to 21th, respectively (Fig. 2A). On the other hand, GCN, GraphSAGE,
283   node2vec, DeepWalk, and EigenMap increase their rankings substantially (Fig. 2A). (See SI
284   Section 4.1 for the ranking of methods by HeaRT benchmark Li et al. (2024a).)

287   3.2   The degree-corrected benchmark aligns better with recommendation tasks
288
289   Link prediction serves as a computationally eﬀicient proxy for evaluating and training rec-
290   ommendation systems (Li et al., 2024a; Menand & Seshadhri, 2024; Yang et al., 2015; Huang
291   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2021). In the recommendation task, directly optimizing recom-
292   mendation metrics such as Hits@K requires ranking all possible node pairs for each node,
293   which is computationally infeasible for large networks. In contrast, link prediction evaluates
294   on a fixed set of candidate pairs, making it O(M ) (where M is the number of edges) and
295   thus practical for both evaluation and training. This computational advantage has made
296   link prediction benchmarks a de facto standard for developing and training recommendation
297
      models. However, this practice is only valid if link prediction performance correlates with
      recommendation performance. It is thus crucial that a link prediction benchmark accurately
298
      mirrors the performance in recommendation tasks.
299
300   In the recommendation task, a method must rank all potential connections for each node
301   without a predefined candidate set. This differs fundamentally from link prediction where
302   we evaluate on a fixed set of node pairs. Specifically, for each node i, a method recommends
303
      its top C = 50 nodes j based on the highest scores sij . We note that our results are
      consistent regardless of the chosen C value (refer to SI Section 4.5). The effectiveness of
304
      the recommendations is then measured using the vertex-centric max precision recall at C
305
      (VCMPR@C) defined as the higher of the precision and recall scores at C (Menand &
306   Seshadhri, 2024). The VCMPR@C is designed to evaluate recommendation methods and
307   addresses the excessive penalty on the precision scores at C for small-degree nodes, which
308   often have low precision due to their limited number of edges. We perform this task five
309   times and average the VCMPR@C scores across different runs.
310
      For each graph, we evaluate the alignment between the rankings based on the recommen-
311
      dation task and those based on the link prediction benchmarks using Rank Biased Overlap
312   (RBO) (Webber et al., 2010). RBO is a ranking similarity metric with larger weights on the
313   top performers in the two rankings. A larger RBO score indicates that the top performers
314   in the two rankings are more similar. The weights on the top performer are controlled by
315   the parameter p ∈ (0, 1). While we set p = 0.5 in our experiment, we confirmed that our
316   results are robust to the choice of p (SI Section 4.5).
317
      Our results from 95 graphs show that the degree-corrected benchmark achieves higher RBO
318   scores than the original benchmark. For reference, we also tested the HeaRT benchmark (Li
319   et al., 2024a), which is a recent link prediction benchmark that mitigates the distance-based
320   bias. The results show that HeaRT achieves substantially lower RBO scores than both the
321   original and degree-corrected benchmarks. We find consistent results for different values of
322   C and parameter p of the RBO (SI Section 4.5). These results indicate that the degree-
323   corrected benchmark more accurately mirrors the performance in recommendation tasks,
      providing a more reliable measure of the effectiveness of methods in practical applications.

      3.3   Degree-corrected benchmark facilitates the learning of community structure
325
326
327   The link prediction benchmark is a common unsupervised learning objective for
328   GNNs (Hamilton et al., 2017; Kawamoto et al., 2018; Kojaku et al., 2021b; Tang et al.,
329
      2015). The degree bias implies that GNNs trained using the original link prediction bench-
      mark tend to overfit to node degrees because they can easily differentiate between positive
330
      and negative edges based on node degrees. We show that the degree-corrected benchmark
331
      effectively prevents overfitting to node degrees and improves the learning of salient graph
332   structures.
333
334   We evaluate GNNs on the common unsupervised task of community detection in graphs (For-
335
      tunato & Newman, 2022; Fortunato, 2010; Fortunato & Hric, 2016). The community de-
      tection task identifies densely connected groups (i.e., communities) in a graph. Community
336
      detection and link prediction tasks are intimately related Clauset et al. (2008); Peixoto
337
      (2018); Ghasemian et al. (2020). Two nodes will likely have edges if they belong to the
338   same community. By training a graph machine learning model (e.g., GNNs) to learn a
339   node embedding to predict links, nodes in the same community are mapped to be close to
340   each other in the embedding space Kojaku et al. (2023). Communities often correspond
341   to functional units (e.g., social circles with similar opinions and protein complexes) in the
342   graph, and detecting communities is a crucial task in many graph applications (Fortunato
343   & Newman, 2022; Fortunato, 2010; Fortunato & Hric, 2016; Peixoto, 2013; 2018). Specifi-
344   cally, we test the GNNs by using the Lancichinetti-Fortunato-Radicchi (LFR) community
345   detection benchmark (Lancichinetti et al., 2008), a standard benchmark for community de-
346
      tection (Fortunato & Hric, 2016; Fortunato, 2010; Tandon et al., 2021; Kojaku et al., 2023).
      The LFR benchmark generates synthetic graphs with predefined communities as follows.
347
      Each node i is assigned a degree ki from a power-law distribution p(k) ∼ k −τ1 , with max-
348
      imum degree kmax . A smaller τ1 indicates a higher likelihood of large degree nodes, which
349   results in a more heterogeneous degree distribution. Nodes are randomly grouped into L
350   communities, with community sizes (i.e., the number of nodes in a community) following
351   another power-law distribution p(n) ∼ n−τ2 bounded between nmin and nmax . Edges are
352   then formed such that each node i connects to a fraction 1 − µ of its ki edges within its
353   community and the remaining fraction µ to nodes in other communities. We generate 10
354   graphs for µ ∈ {0.05, 0.1, 0.15, . . . , 0.95, 1} using the following parameter values: the number
355   of nodes N = 3, 000, the degree exponent τ1 ∈ {2.5, 3}, the average degree ⟨k⟩ = 25, the
356   maximum degree kmax = 1000, the community-size exponent τ2 = 3, the minimum and
357
      maximum community size nmin = 100 and nmax = 1000. We obtained qualitatively similar
      results for different values of the parameters of the LFR benchmark (SI Section 4.8).
358
359   We train GNNs using either the original or the degree-corrected link prediction benchmarks
360   to minimize binary entropy loss in classifying the positive and negative edges. Using the
361   trained GNNs, we generate node embeddings and apply the K-means clustering algorithm
362   to detect communities, where K is set to the number of true communities. Although the
363
      number K of communities is often unknown, we use the ground-truth number to eliminate
      noise from estimating K and to concentrate on evaluating the quality of the learned node
364
      embeddings, a standard practice in benchmarking node embeddings for the community
365
      detection task (Tandon et al., 2021; Kojaku et al., 2023; Kovács et al., 2024). We measure the
366   performance of GNNs by comparing the detected communities against the true communities
367   using the adjusted element-centric similarity (Kojaku et al., 2023; Kovács et al., 2024; Gates
368   et al., 2019), where higher scores indicate a higher similarity between the node partitions
369   for the true and detected communities. We observe qualitatively similar results for partition
370   similarities based on the normalized mutual information (SI Section 4.7).
371
      All GNNs, except for GIN, perform well when µ ≤ 0.5, where communities are distinct and
372   easily identifiable, but their performance declines as µ increases (Fig. 3A). Across a broad
373   range of µ, degree-corrected GNNs, particularly GIN, GCN, and GraphSAGE, outperform
374   original GNNs in identifying communities, as shown by the area under the performance
375   curve (Fig. 3B). The advantage of degree-corrected benchmarks becomes more evident with
376   a more heterogeneous degree distribution (Fig. 3C and D). This indicates that degree cor-
377   rection effectively reduces overfitting to node degrees, enhancing the learning of community
      structures in graphs.

      4   Discussion
433
434
435   We showed that common link prediction benchmarks are biased due to node degree in edge
436   sampling, favoring methods that overfit to node degree. This bias distorts model evaluations
437
      and leads to suboptimal node embeddings (Fig. 2B, Fig. 3B). The degree bias we focused on
      is artifactual, i.e., it is not present in the actual network data but arises in the set of sampled
438
      positive and negative edges due to the biased sampling algorithm. We have shown that this
439
      artifactual bias significantly distorts the evaluation of link prediction models (Fig. 2B) and
440   can be leveraged by these models to optimize their objective functions, leading to suboptimal
441   learning of node embeddings (Fig. 3B).
442
443
      To better the contribution of degree bias, we decomposed AUC-ROC scores into contri-
      butions from different node degree groups (SI Section 3). Our analysis revealed that in
444
      networks with high degree heterogeneity (σ > 1.3), a single combination—high-degree pos-
445
      itive edges and low-degree negative edges—dominates the evaluation, accounting for over
446   80% of the overall AUC-ROC score. This finding explains why even simple degree-based
447   methods perform well: the benchmark’s evaluation is largely determined by cases that can
448   be easily classified using degree alone. Indeed, our logistic regression analysis shows that
449   in heterogeneous networks, the degree product becomes overwhelmingly important, with its
450   coeﬀicient more than twice as large as other structural features (SI Section 3). These results
451   highlight how the sampling procedure inadvertently creates a shortcut that allows methods
452   to achieve high performance without learning meaningful graph structures.
453   To address the degree bias, we proposed a degree-corrected benchmark that aligns the degree
454   distributions of sampled edges. Our benchmark not only provided accurate evaluations but
455   also improved GNN training by reducing overfitting to degree and enhancing community
456   detection.
457
      While our focus is on degree bias, we acknowledge other biases identified by previous stud-
458   ies (Li et al., 2024a; Lichtnwalter & Chawla, 2012; Zhang & Chen, 2018; Mao et al., 2023).
459   We highlight some of these biases to underscore the uniqueness of degree bias.
460   (1) Distance bias arises because nodes connected by negative edges are generally farther
461   apart than those linked by positive edges (Li et al., 2024a), making them easily distinguish-
462   able by distance. We observed that correcting for degree bias consistently reduces distance
463   bias; the degree-corrected benchmark showed more negative edges connected by paths of
464   length 2 compared to the standard benchmark across all networks. While our degree cor-
465   rection naturally mitigates distance bias, distance debiasing does not address degree bias.
466
      This is evidenced by the strong performance of PA, a purely degree-based predictor, even
      after distance-bias correction (SI Section 4.1). This asymmetry likely arises because node
467
      distances are inherently influenced by degree heterogeneity. In networks with high degree
468
      heterogeneity, high-degree nodes act as hubs, creating short paths between many node pairs.
469   Correcting for degree bias naturally reduces the effect of these hub-mediated short paths.
470   However, correcting for distance alone does not address the underlying degree heterogeneity,
471   which continues to influence network structure and link prediction performance.
472   (2) Study (Huang et al., 2023) highlighted an issue arising from the substantial down-
473   sampling of negative edges to match the number of positive edges. They proposed an
474   “unbiased testing” approach for link prediction by evaluating methods on all possible nega-
475   tive pairs, rather than just a sampled subset. However, we note that the concept of “bias” in
476   their work differs from the sampling bias we address. The degree bias we focus on primarily
477
      stems from positive edge sampling, not negative edges. Sampling all negative edges does not
      resolve this degree bias at all because the node frequency in all negative edges still matches
478
      that of uniformly sampled negative edges.
479
      (3) Another potential bias could stem from overfitting to large-degree nodes, which are more
480   frequently sampled in our degree-corrected sampling method. However, these high-degree
481   nodes are sampled as negative examples, penalizing the model if it overfits them. This
482   encourages the model to learn salient features of other nodes, resulting in embeddings that
483   better capture nuanced network structures, such as community structure (Fig. 3). This
484   effect—eliminating dominant patterns to reveal more nuanced ones—echoes the analysis of
485   stock price time series, where filtering out the dominant market trends (e.g., recession, and
      inflation) can expose finer correlations between individual stocks (MacMahon & Garlaschelli,


                                                      9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      2015). A comparable effect can also be leveraged for community detection (Newman, 2006)
487   and node embedding (Kojaku et al., 2021b).
488
489   To better understand the mechanism of degree bias, we decomposed AUC-ROC scores into
490
      contributions from different node degree groups (SI Section 3). Our analysis revealed that
      in networks with high degree heterogeneity (σ > 1.3), a single combination—high-degree
491
      positive edges and low-degree negative edges—dominates the evaluation, accounting for over
492
      70% of the overall AUC-ROC score. This finding explains why even simple degree-based
493   methods perform well: the benchmark’s evaluation is largely determined by cases that can
494   be easily classified using degree alone. Indeed, our logistic regression analysis shows that
495   in heterogeneous networks, the degree product becomes overwhelmingly important, with its
496   coeﬀicient more than twice as large as other structural features. These results highlight how
497   the sampling procedure inadvertently creates a shortcut that allows methods to achieve high
498   performance without learning meaningful graph structures.
499   In summary, our findings add a new direction to the ongoing examination of the link pre-
500   diction task by demonstrating that node degree—a local and notably simpler attribute than
501   distance—is often suﬀicient for differentiating the positive and negative edges. Crucially,
502   the degree bias arises in any non-regular graph, regardless of the structure of the graph,
503   because the bias stems not from the graph structure but from the edge sampling algorithm
504   used in the link prediction benchmark. More broadly, edge sampling—the source of the
505   degree bias—is a general technique for evaluating and training graph machine learning.
506   For instance, mini-batch training (Hamilton et al., 2017; Hu et al., 2020), which samples
507
      subsets of edges for eﬀicient GNN training, may also exhibit bias due to node degrees,
      leading to skewed training sets. Given the widespread use of edge sampling across various
508
      graph machine-learning tasks, our findings have broad implications beyond link prediction
509
      benchmarks, extending to a range of benchmarks and training frameworks.
510
511   Our study has several limitations. First, we did not explore the reasons behind the vary-
512   ing performance of different link prediction methods. Degree heterogeneity can negatively
513
      impact GNNs (Wang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2021; Subramonian et al., 2023; Li et al.,
      2024b; Liu et al., 2023; Subramonian et al., 2024; Kang et al., 2022; Arun et al., 2023).
514
      Small-degree nodes tend to have poor representation quality due to limited neighborhood
515
      information (Wang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2021), and large-degree nodes benefit from re-
516   inforced structural inequality (Subramonian et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2023).
517   This—how well a method represents low-degree nodes—could be one reason for the differ-
518   ences in performance. It is important to note that there is no single link prediction method
519   that is universally effective for all graphs because the performance of link prediction meth-
520   ods depends on the assumptions on the graph structure to make the predictions (Ghasemian
521   et al., 2020). Second, we focus on community structure to test the effectiveness of the pro-
522   posed benchmark as a training framework. However, other non-trivial graph structures,
523   such as centrality, could be tested through network dismantling benchmarks (Osat et al.,
524
      2023). Third, our degree correction method addresses one form of bias, it may potentially
      introduce new biases, although we could not identify any clear example of such biases. As a
525
      precaution, we investigated whether our debiasing method can exacerbate certain other bi-
526
      ases identified in literature such as the distance bias Li et al. (2024a) and found no evidence
527   of this. In fact, we find that our method mitigates the distance bias not vice-versa. Fourth,
528   our analysis focuses on the transductive setting, where link prediction occurs between nodes
529   in the training graph. We note that degree bias likely persists inductively since new nodes
530   with more connections are still more likely to be selected in edge sampling, mirroring the
531   bias in the transductive setting. Fifth, we focused on undirected and undirected graphs,
532   which are also common. It is trivial to extend our results to directed graphs, where the
533   degree bias is now dependent on both the out-degree and in-degree of nodes. For weighted
534   graphs, if the weight represent multiplicities of edges, our degree-corrected sampling method
535
      can be applied straightforwardly.
536   Despite these limitations, our results suggest that sampling graph data is a highly non-trivial
537   task than commonly considered. Because sampling edges from a graph is integral to eval-
538   uating and training graph machine learning methods, our results underline the importance
539   of careful sampling to ensure the effectiveness of evaluations and training of graph machine
      learning methods.
