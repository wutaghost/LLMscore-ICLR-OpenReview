009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we address the challenge of certifying the performance of a machine
012            learning model on an unseen target network. We consider a source network “A”
013            of K clients, each with private data from unique and heterogeneous distributions,
014            assumed to be independent samples from a broader meta-distribution µ. Our goal
015            is to provide certified guarantees for the model’s performance on a different, un-
016            seen target network “B,” governed by another meta-distribution µ′ , assuming the
017            deviation between µ and µ′ is bounded by either the Wasserstein distance or an
018
               f -divergence. We derive theoretical guarantees for the model’s empirical average
               loss and provide uniform bounds on the risk CDF, where the latter correspond to
019
               novel and adversarially robust versions of the Glivenko-Cantelli theorem and the
020
               Dvoretzky-Kiefer-Wolfowitz (DKW) inequality. Our bounds are computable in
021            polynomial time with a polynomial number of queries to the K clients, preserv-
022            ing client privacy by querying only the model’s (potentially adversarial) loss on
023            private data. We also establish non-asymptotic generalization bounds that consis-
024            tently converge to zero as both K and the minimum client sample size grow. Ex-
025            tensive empirical evaluations validate the robustness and practicality of our bounds
026            across real-world tasks.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The distributed nature of modern learning environments, where training data and computational re-
032   sources are scattered across clients in a network, has introduced challenges for the machine learning
033
      community. Federated learning (FL) addresses some of these challenges by enabling clients to col-
      laboratively train a decentralized model through communication with a central server (McMahan
034
      et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2024). One major challenge is the heterogeneity of data distributions among
035
      clients. This non-IID nature of clients’ data not only impacts the design of training algorithms but
036   also complicates evaluation, particularly when models are applied to unseen clients from the same
037   or different networks (Ye et al., 2023; Zawad et al., 2021).
038
039
      In a standard FL setting, a fixed set of clients with a common learning objective trains a model
      that performs well on their data distributions. For example, in a mobile network, the goal is to
040
      generalize well to test data from observed clients. Most evaluations focus on average performance
041
      across the training clients. However, models are often applied to clients not involved in training
042   (Reisizadeh et al., 2020). These unseen clients might have privacy concerns that prevent sharing
043   any information, or they may be new clients who joined the network post-training (Ma et al., 2024).
044   Furthermore, FL models trained on one network might later be tested on another network with
045   different distributions. For example, a model trained on one city’s clients may be applied to clients
046   from another city. Therefore, it’s crucial to evaluate models on unseen clients and networks beyond
047   the original training clients.
048   In this work, we focus on evaluating FL models on unseen clients and networks. We assume access
049   to clients whose data distributions are i.i.d. samples from a meta-distribution µ, representing the
050   client population (e.g., a mobile network in a specific region). Here, a meta-distribution models
051   higher-level dynamics in the society that clients belong to, such as culture, lingual preferences, etc.
052   Our goal is to provide performance guarantees for unseen clients whose data either follows µ or a
053   different distribution within a bounded distance from µ. The latter corresponds to a different society
      with slightly different styles, habits or preferences (see Figure 4 in Appendix E for a graphical


                                                        1

054
      illustration). To address this challenge, we extend non-asymptotic average loss and CDF estimation
055   guarantees to performance scores collected from observed clients. Specifically, we leverage the
056   Dvoretzky–Kiefer–Wolfowitz (DKW) theorem to provide probably approximately correct (PAC)
057   guarantees for unseen clients following µ (Dvoretzky et al., 1956). These guarantees offer lower
058   and upper bounds on unseen clients’ performance scores with adjustable confidence.
059
      However, in real-world applications, test clients may come from different populations unseen dur-
060
      ing training. To account for this, we extend the DKW theorem to cover meta-distributions within
061   a bounded divergence from the source. This extension includes an upper bound on the CDF for
062   meta-distributions within a bounded f -divergence. We also give performance guarantees via tightly
063   bounding the meta-distributionally shifted average loss when both f -divergence or Wasserstein
064   shifts are considered. The f -divergence approach captures potential reweightings of client types
065   in the target network while Wasserstein distances model more complex distributional shifts (e.g.,
066   new clients from previously unseen regions) (Wang et al., 2022; Kuhn et al., 2019). To our knowl-
067   edge, this is the first extension of the DKW and subsequently Glivenko-Cantelli theorems to such a
068   robust setting.
069   Our bounds are asymptotically minimax optimal, meaning they cannot be improved, at least asymp-
070   totically. They are also consistent, converging to zero as the number of observed clients and data
071   points per client increases. We further show that handling Wasserstein-type shifts can be achieved
072   by querying clients for adversarial loss instead of ordinary loss, without requiring access to user data
073   or centralizing data at the server. Additionally, we demonstrate that the number of client queries can
074   be bounded by a polynomial function of the problem parameters. Lastly, we validate the practicality
075
      and computational efficiency of our bounds through experiments on real-world datasets.

080
081   Evaluation and Generalization of models learned by FL algorithms on unseen clients. The
082   challenge of generalizing FL models to unseen clients and distributions has been studied in sev-
083   eral related works. Li et al. (2020) introduce a method to address the heterogeneity of client data,
084   focusing on improving the generalization of FL models. Similarly, Ma et al. (2024) propose a
085   topology-aware federated learning approach that leverages client relationships to enhance model ro-
086   bustness against out-of-federation data. Also, Zeng et al. (2023) explore adaptive federated learning
087
      techniques to dynamically adjust model parameters based on client data distributions.
088   Distribution shifts and adversarial robustness in FL. The robustness of FL models against distri-
089   bution shifts and adversarial attacks has been the focus of several related references. Reisizadeh
090   et al. (2020) propose a robust federated learning framework to handle affine distribution shifts
091   across clients’ data. Their proposed framework incorporates a Wasserstein-distance-based distri-
092   bution shift model to account for device-dependent data perturbations. Also, Zhang et al. (2023)
093
      conduct comprehensive evaluations on the adversarial robustness of FL models, proposing the de-
      cision boundary-based FL Training algorithm to enhance the the trained model’s robustness. Zhou
094
      et al. (2022) gain insight from the bias-variance decomposition to improve adversarial robustness in
095
      FL. Also, Ben Mansour et al. (2022) propose a robust aggregation method to reduce the effect of
096   adversarial clients.
097
098   Performance guarantees in Heterogeneous FL. The heterogeneous nature of FL, where clients
099
      have different data distributions has been a topic of great interest in the literature. Fallah et al. (2020)
      introduce a personalized federated learning framework based on model-agnostic meta-learning,
100
      which provides performance guarantees by optimizing for data distribution heterogeneity. Luo et al.
101
      (2021) propose a classifier calibration method that adjusts for bias in heterogeneous data, offering
102   improved performance guarantees in non-IID settings. Tan et al. (2022) develop FedProto, a frame-
103   work that leverages prototype learning to improve convergence and robustness under non-convex
104   objectives.. Moreover, Wu et al. (2024) propose FedLoRA, which adapts low-rank parameter shar-
105   ing techniques to mitigate the effects of heterogeneity in personalized federated learning. Also,
106   Cheng et al. (2024) and Jia et al. (2024) introduce group-based customization and local parame-
107   ter sharing strategies, respectively, to provide fairness and efficiency guarantees for heterogeneous
      client types and multiple tasks in FL.

526   8   C ONCLUSIONS
527
528   In this work, we proposed new performance bounds, computable in polynomial time with polyno-
529   mial query complexity, to establish guarantees for any model h on an unseen network B, based on
530   observations and queries from network A. The key assumption is that the meta-distributions of both
531   networks’ user data are ε-close, for some known or assumed ε > 0. We considered two measures of
532   ”closeness”: f -divergence shifts and Wasserstein shifts, which capture different practical scenarios,
533   making our approach widely applicable.
534   Our bounds are supported by rigorous proofs, with vanishing generalization gaps, ensuring con-
535   sistency as the network size K and sample per client nk / log K increase asymptotically. We also
536   introduced a robust version of the GC theorem and DKW bound for f -divergence shifts, which is
537   novel. Numerical experiments demonstrate the bounds’ practical tightness and computability, as the
538   proposed optimization problems are convex or quasi-convex, solvable in polynomial time. Future
539   work could explore uniform convergence bounds for risk CDF under Wasserstein shifts, or consider
      other shift types beyond f -divergence and Wasserstein.
