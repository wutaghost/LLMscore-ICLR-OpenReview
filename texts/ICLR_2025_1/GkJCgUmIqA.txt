                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) represent a significant advancement in
012
               Scientific Machine Learning (SciML), which integrate physical domain knowledge
013            into an empirical loss function as soft constraints and apply existing machine learn-
014            ing methods to train the model. However, recent research has noted that PINNs may
015            fail to learn relatively complex Partial Differential Equations (PDEs). This paper
016            addresses the failure modes of PINNs by introducing a novel, hard-constrained deep
017            learning method — trust-region Sequential Quadratic Programming (trSQP-PINN).
018            In contrast to directly training the penalized soft-constrained loss as in PINNs, our
019            method performs a linear-quadratic approximation of the hard-constrained loss,
020            while leveraging the soft-constrained loss to adaptively adjust the trust-region radius.
021
               We only trust our model approximations and make updates within the trust region,
               and such an updating manner can overcome the ill-conditioning issue of PINNs.
022
               We also address the computational bottleneck of second-order SQP methods by
023
               employing quasi-Newton updates for second-order information, and importantly,
024            we introduce a simple pretraining step to further enhance training efficiency of our
025            method. We demonstrate the effectiveness of trSQP-PINN through extensive ex-
026            periments. Compared to existing hard-constrained methods for PINNs, such as
027            penalty methods and augmented Lagrangian methods, trSQP-PINN significantly
028            improves the accuracy of the learned PDE solutions, achieving up to 1-3 orders of
029            magnitude lower errors. Additionally, our pretraining step is generally effective for
030            other hard-constrained methods, and experiments have shown the robustness of our
031            method against both problem-specific parameters and algorithm tuning parameters.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Partial Differential Equations (PDEs) are essential mathematical tools used to model a variety of phys-
037   ical phenomena, including heat transfer, fluid dynamics, general relativity, and quantum mechanics.
038   These equations are often derived from fundamental principles, such as the conservation of mass and
039
      energy, and describe how physical quantities change over space and time. However, deriving analytical
      solutions of PDEs in real-world applications is usually infeasible. Numerical methods like finite differ-
040
      ence methods (Thomas, 1995), finite element methods (Zienkiewicz et al., 2013), and pseudo-spectral
041
      methods (Fornberg, 1996) are commonly employed to approximate solutions. These methods, though
042   effective, require finer meshing of the domain and can be computationally intensive. With the advent
043   of machine learning (ML), there has been a growing interest recently in applying ML methods to
044   solve PDEs. Deep neural networks, known for their large capacity and expressivity, are particularly
045   useful in learning nonlinear mappings between inputs (e.g., spatial/temporal coordinates) and outputs
046   (e.g., measurements of physical quantities), thus leading the way in such applications. This emerging
047   field, called Scientific Machine Learning (SciML), combines data-driven approaches (mostly neural
048   network training) with traditional scientific modeling (i.e., PDEs/ODEs) to address the computational
049   challenges of classical numerical methods and enhance solution accuracy (von Rueden et al., 2021;
050
      Willard et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2022).
051   The Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN) is a recent development that integrates domain-driven
052   principles with data-driven ML methods for learning PDE solutions (Cuomo et al., 2022; Hao et al.,
053   2022; Raissi et al., 2019). The idea behind PINN is simple: it incorporates PDEs into an empirical
      loss function as soft constraints, and then optimizes the soft-constrained loss towards zero using


                                                          1

054
      classical (unconstrained) ML methods (e.g., Adam and L-BFGS). PINN is generally applied within a
055   supervised-learning framework, utilizing paired labeled datasets to define the loss. Unlike classical
056   PDE solvers that require meshing, PINN is mesh-free, making it well-suited for large-scale problems
057   with irregular domains. PINN has been successfully employed to various SciML applications, includ-
058   ing nano-optics materials (Chen et al., 2020), biomedicine (Sahli Costabal et al., 2020), fluid mechan-
059   ics (Raissi et al., 2020), optimal control (Mowlavi & Nabi, 2023; Barry-Straume et al., 2022), and
060   inverse design (Lu et al., 2021; Wiecha et al., 2021).
061   However, recent research has observed some negative results of PINN: it can fail to learn relatively
062   complex PDEs despite employing deep neural network architectures with ample expressivity power
063   (Krishnapriyan et al., 2021). This issue is largely attributed to optimization challenges, including ill-
064   conditioning issue and complex loss landscape, stemming from the presence of differential operators
065   in soft penalty terms within the loss function of PINN (Krishnapriyan et al., 2021; Rathore et al., 2024;
066   Basir, 2022). An imbalance between the empirical loss and the soft penalties can skew training, favor-
067   ing certain constraints, and thus reducing the accuracy and generalizability of PINN. The nonconvex
068   loss landscape also makes PINN difficult for gradient-based methods to find the optimal solution.
069
      Additionally, the complexity of neural network models and their sensitivity to variations in PDEs, cou-
      pled with the reliance on fine tuning of hyperparameters, can lead to poor convergence and physically
070
      infeasible solutions when applying PINN in practice.
071
072   To address the failure modes of PINNs, multiple strategies have recently been proposed, the majority
073   of which attempt to alleviate the ill-conditioning issue and/or improve the loss landscape. For example,
074   Krishnapriyan et al. (2021) proposed two resolutions. In one approach, the authors suggested using
075
      curriculum regularization, where the penalty term starts from a simple PDE regularization and be-
      comes progressively more complex as neural network gets trained. Another approach is to pose the
076
      problem as a sequence-to-sequence learning task (via spatio-temporal decomposition), rather than
077
      learning to predict the entire domain at once. Liu et al. (2024) incorporated a preconditioner into the
078   loss function to mitigate the condition number. Subramanian et al. (2022) introduced an adaptive
079   collection scheme that progressively collects more data points in the regions of high errors. Wang et al.
080   (2021; 2022a;b) reformulated the loss function by reweighting or resampling to balance different reg-
081   ularization components in the loss. Rathore et al. (2024) developed a hybrid optimization method by
082   integrating Newton-CG with Adam and L-BFGS to better adapt to PINN’s landscape. While the afore-
083   mentioned literature has demonstrated the effectiveness of their strategies in learning various PDEs
084   under favorable conditions, they still primarily revolve around regularized, soft-constrained loss func-
085   tions, which may not yield feasible solutions regardless of how we assign weight to the penalty term.
086
      There exist simple examples that, for any finite weight, the soft-constrained loss cannot recover the
      solution to the hard-constrained problem1 .
087
088   Motivated by this concern, another series of literature aims to impose PDEs as hard constraints and ap-
089   ply constrained optimization methods to train the model. For example, Nandwani et al. (2019) con-
090   verted soft-constrained PINN problems into alternating min-max problems and applied gradient
091   descent ascent methods to solve them. Dener et al. (2020) applied (stochastic) augmented Lagrangian
092
      methods to approximate the Fokker-Planck-Landau collision operator, using stochastic gradient de-
      scent to solve inner unconstrained minimization subproblems. Subsequently, Lu et al. (2021) proposed
093
      using penalty methods and augmented Lagrangian methods within the PINN framework for inverse
094
      design problems. In contrast to dealing with the PINN loss directly, the above literature reverts the
095   soft-constrained regularizations back to hard constraints (aligning with our original goals of solving
096   PDEs), and primarily applies either penalty methods2 or augmented Lagrangian methods to solve the
097   resulting hard-constrained problems.
098
      Although existing hard-constrained methods based on penalty methods and augmented Lagrangian
099
      methods have shown significant improvements over PINNs, these two methods have largely been sup-
100
      planted by Sequential Quadratic Programming (SQP) methods in the numerical optimization literature,
101   which typically exhibit superior convergence properties (Gill et al., 2005; Nocedal & Wright, 2006).
102   Compared to penalty methods, SQP does not regularize the loss in the search direction computation,
103   so it preserves the problem structure (especially important for PDE and control problems) and does
104   not suffer from ill-conditioning issues. Compared to augmented Lagrangian methods, SQP is more

      robust to dual initialization, exhibits much faster convergence (quadratic/superlinear for SQP while
109   linear for augmented Lagrangian), and produces solutions with fewer objective function and gradient
110   evaluations (Curtis et al., 2014; Dener et al., 2020; Hong et al., 2023). These promising advantages
111   motivate our study in this paper — design an SQP-based method to leverage recent developments of

113
      In particular, we develop a trust-region SQP method called trSQP-PINN. Following the literature
114
      Nandwani et al. (2019); Dener et al. (2020); Lu et al. (2021), we first formulate the PDE problem as a
115   hard-constrained problem, where we minimize the empirical loss subject to nonlinear PDE constraints.
116   Then, we apply a trust-region SQP method to impose hard constraints. At each step, we perform a
117   quadratic approximation to the empirical loss and a linear approximation to the constraints. Inspired
118   by the local natural of the approximation, we additionally introduce a trust-region constraint to the
119   parameters. We only trust the approximation and make updates within the trust region. Another critical
120   element of the method is the merit function, which is a scalar-valued function that indicates whether a
121   new iterate is better or worse than the current iterate, in the sense of reducing optimality and feasibility
122   errors. We employ the soft-constrained loss as our merit function. The trust-region radius selection and
123
      the step rejection mechanism are both based on the ratio between the actual reduction and the predicted
      reduction in the soft-constrained loss. If the ratio is large, indicating that our approximation is reliable,
124
      we accept the step and enlarge the radius; otherwise, we reject the step and reduce the radius.
125
126   A major difference of trSQP-PINN compared to existing penalty and augmented Lagrangian methods
127   is that the trust-region, linear-quadratic subproblem does not rely on any penalty coefficient, effectively
128   avoiding the ill-conditioning issue. The soft-penalized loss is only used to determine whether or not
129
      accept the new update. Two potential computational bottlenecks when applying our method are (i)
      solving the trust-region subproblem, and (ii) obtaining the Hessian information of neural network
130
      models. For (i), we do not solve the trust-region subproblem exactly (which is still much easier than
131
      the ones in penalty and augmented Lagrangian methods), but instead obtain a point that is better than
132   gradient descent (i.e. satisfying the Cauchy reduction). For (ii), we employ quasi-Newton updates
133   such as SR1 and (damped) BFGS to approximate the Hessian information.
134
135
      To further enhance training efficiency and reduce data intensity, we introduce a simple pretraining
      step focused solely on minimizing the feasibility error. Throughout extensive experiments, we demon-
136
      strate that trSQP-PINN significantly outperforms existing hard-constrained methods, improving the
137
      accuracy of learned PDE solutions by 1-3 orders of magnitude. Moreover, the improvement of our
138   method is robust against both problem-specific parameters and algorithm tuning parameters, while
139   our pretraining step is broadly effective for other methods as well.

519   5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
520
      We designed a direct, hard-constrained deep learning method — trust-region Sequential Quadratic Pro-
521
      gramming (trSQP-PINN) method — to address the failure modes of PINNs for solving PDE problems.
522   At each step, the method performs a linear-quadratic approximation to the original nonlinear PDE
523   problem, and incorporates an additional trust-region constraint to respect the local nature of the ap-
524   proximation. The method employs quasi-Newton updates to approximate second-order information
525   and leverages a soft-constrained loss to adaptively update the trust-region radius. Compared to popular
526   hard-constrained methods such as penalty methods and augmented Lagrangian methods, our extensive
527   experiments have shown that trSQP-PINN robustly exhibits superior performance over a wide range
528   of PDE coefficients, achieving 1-2 orders of magnitude lower errors. The enhanced performance
529   stems from leveraging trust-region technique and second-order information, helping trSQP-PINN to
530
      mitigate ill-conditioning issues and navigate through complex loss landscapes efficiently.
531   Future work includes exploring more numerical techniques for solving SciML problems and applying
532   SQP-type methods to inverse problems and operators learning problems. For example, we can utilize
533   Randomized Numerical Linear Algebra techniques (e.g., sketching or subsampling) to approximately
534   solve trust-region subproblems to further reduce the computational cost of trSQP. Lu et al. (2021) has
535   demonstrated the effectiveness of penalty and augmented Lagrangian methods for solving inverse
536
      problems. Designing a suitable SQP-type method for such cases is also an important open direction.
537   Ethics Statement. This paper does not involve human subjects, nor does it present potentially harmful
538   methodologies or applications that could result in discrimination against different communities.
539   Reproducibility. The detailed experimental setup is presented in Appendix B, and the source code is
      provided in the supplementary material.
