009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Rectified flow is a generative model that learns smooth transport mappings be-
012            tween two distributions through an ordinary differential equation (ODE). Unlike
013            diffusion-based generative models, which require costly numerical integration of a
014            generative ODE to sample images with state-of-the-art quality, rectified flow uses
015            an iterative process called reflow to learn smooth and straight ODE paths. This
016            allows for relatively simple and efficient generation of high-quality images. How-
017
               ever, rectified flow still faces several challenges. 1) The reflow process requires
               a large number of generated pairs to preserve the target distribution, leading to
018
               significant computational costs. 2) Since the model is typically trained using only
019
               generated image pairs, its performance heavily depends on the 1-rectified flow
020            model, causing it to drift away from the real data.
021
               In this work, we expose the limitations of the original rectified flow and propose
022            a novel approach that incorporates real images into the reflow procedure. By pre-
023            serving the ODE paths toward real images, our method effectively reduces reliance
024            on large amounts of generated data. Instead, we demonstrate that the reflow pro-
025            cess can be conducted efficiently using a much smaller set of generated and real
026            images. In CIFAR-10, we achieved significantly better FID scores, not only in
027            one-step generation but also in full-step simulations, while using only 7.2% of
028            the generative pairs compared to the original method. Furthermore, our approach
029            induces straighter paths and avoids saturation on generated images during reflow,
030
               leading to more robust ODE learning while preserving the distribution of real im-
               ages.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Rectified flow (Esser et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2023)
036   demonstrates state-of-the-art image generation with fewer sampling steps than diffusion models
037   (De Bortoli et al., 2021; Vargas et al., 2021; Song et al., 2020b; Ho et al., 2020; Tzen & Raginsky,
038   2019). K-rectified flow involves k reflow steps that make ODE paths smooth and straight (Liu et al.,
039   2022). This allows the model to generate high-quality images simply and efficiently in just one or
040   a few steps. Intriguingly, all rectified flow models (Flux, SD3, and AuraFlow) achieve state-of-the-
041   art quality with 1-rectified flow and require about 30 NFEs (number of function evaluations) (Esser
042   et al., 2024).
043   In this paper, we find the pitfalls of k-rectified flow because of its reflow procedure and propose to
044   use real images and their inversions as solutions. 1) The flow drifts away from the real distribution.
045   For example, to train a 2-rectified flow, random starting noises and their corresponding generated
046   images through the 1-rectified flow supervises the ODE paths to be straight, i.e., the generated im-
047   ages themselves are reused as training data. Using the real samples keeps the supervision toward the
048   real distribution. 2) The number of required fake samples is very large. Using the real samples and
049   their conic neighbor outperforms with much fewer samples. 3) Reflow procedure lowers image qual-
050
      ity of full-step generation. Our method improves image quality in all 1-step, few-step, and full-step
      generation.

      2-rectified flow from 12.21 to 5.98 while using only 7.2% of the generative pair, and show that

      3.1   R EFLOW STEPS DRIFT THE FLOW AWAY FROM THE REAL DISTRIBUTION .
109
110   As shown by Liu et al. (2022), the trajectory Ztk between the generated pairs in the reflow process
111   becomes smoother and straighter with each iteration of reflow, due to the fact that the ODE induce
112   a deterministic smooth solution while preserving the same marginal distribution as the original tra-
113   jectory. This straightened path is essential for generating high-quality images with small number of
114   sampling steps rather than SDE based generative models. (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020b;a)
115   We use subscript F to denote the fake pairs from the original rectified flow as follows:

121   Interestingly, the k-rectified flow underperforms the (k − 1)-rectified flow in terms of image quality.
122   This is obvious because the fake samples have lower quality than the real samples, as we do not
123   have the optimal generative model (Figure 3a). Figure 2 illustrates a toy example of the distribution
124   shift due to the difference between real and fake samples. The drift accumulates over the subsequent
125   reflow steps and harms image quality of higher-order rectified flows.

517   5 LIMITATION AND DISCUSSION
518   Balanced conic rectified flow integrates real pairs into training by using Slerp to adjust noise of pairs
519   towards the Gaussian manifold. This approach significantly improves the quality of 1-step generation
520   compared to the baseline, even when using few fake pairs, and enhances pre-trained rectified models
521   that utilize only real pairs.
522
      However, limitations remain. First, while empirically effective, the practice of halving the amount of
523
      noise added by Slerp at each conic step lacks theoretical justification. Determining a better schedule
524   for noise addition remains an open question. Second, since our method also uses fake pairs during the
525   reflow process, it cannot completely resolve the slight degradation in many-step image generation
526   quality in the reflow procedure. Another limitation is the extension of conic reflow—which adds
527   noise—to Image-to-Image translation. A straightforward approach might be to train an image-to-
528   noise-to-image model, but further investigation is needed in this area.
529
530
      6     C ONCLUSION
531   In conclusion, the balanced conic reflow method addresses key limitations of traditional rectified
532   flow, which relies heavily on fake pairs and leads to a lower-quality velocity field during the re-
533   flow process. By incorporating real pairs into training, even with a small number of fake pairs, our
534   approach significantly improves the generation quality of k-rectified flow. Its simplicity and versa-
535   tility make it applicable to various rectified flow-based generative models like SD3 and InstaFlow.
536
      Also, there is potential for improvement through techniques like applying additional loss functions
      or combining with diffusion-based fake pair generation (Lee et al., 2024).
537
538   We demonstrated empirically that our method forms a velocity field with lower curvature and ini-
539   tial velocity delta. This approach can positively impact not only image generation but also text-to-
      image/-video generation and editing tasks, offering improved quality and control in future work.

931   I   M ISC . CONFIGURATIONS
932
933   Our experiments on CIFAR10 is configured as follows. For rectified flow, we utilize the same net-
934   work architecture as the rectified flow model based on DDPM++ in (Song et al., 2020b; Liu et al.,
935   2022) with batch size 256. The training process is smoothed using an exponential moving average
936   (EMA) with a decay rate of 0.999999, following the approach in Song et al. (2020b). The Adam
937   optimizer (Diederik, 2014) is used with a learning rate of 2e-4, and a dropout rate of 0.15 is applied.
938   For the high-resolution (LSUN), both our method and the baseline 2-rectified flow were trained for
939   300,000 steps with a batch size of 16. Training started from the same 1-rectified flow checkpoints
940   from the official rectified flow repository. During inference, all models used fixed seeds and identical
941   noise conditions corresponding to the sampling steps.
