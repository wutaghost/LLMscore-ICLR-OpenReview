009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural work certification has established itself as a crucial tool for ensuring the
012            robustness of neural networks. Certification methods typically rely on convex
013            relaxations of the feasible output set to provide sound bounds. However, com-
014            plete certification requires exact bounds, which strongly limits the expressivity of
015            ReLU networks: even for the simple “max” function in R2 , there does not exist a
016            ReLU network that expresses this function and can be exactly bounded by single-
017            neuron relaxation methods. This raises the question whether there exists a convex
018
               relaxation that can provide exact bounds for general continuous piecewise linear
               functions in Rn . In this work, we answer this question affirmatively by showing
019
               that (layer-wise) multi-neuron relaxation provides complete certification for gen-
020
               eral ReLU networks. Based on this novel result, we show that the expressivity of
021            ReLU networks is no longer limited under multi-neuron relaxation. To the best
022            of our knowledge, this is the first positive result on the completeness of convex
023            relaxations, shedding light on the practice of certified robustness.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Neural networks have been shown vulnerable to adversarial attacks (Szegedy et al., 2014), where a
029   small perturbation to the input can lead to a misclassification. The area of adversarial robustness,
030   which measures the robustness of a model with respect to adversarial perturbations, has received
031   much research attention in recent years, reflecting a major concern in the application of neural
032
      networks, especially in safety-critical domains such as autonomous driving and medical diagnosis.
      However, computing the exact adversarial robustness of a neural network is generally NP-hard (Katz
033
      et al., 2017), while adversarial attacks which try to construct an adversarial perturbation can only
034
      provide an upper bound on the robustness of the model. To tackle this issue, neural network cer-
035   tification (Singh et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2018; Bunel et al., 2020) has been proposed to provide
036   robustness guarantees. Complete certification methods (Katz et al., 2017; Tjeng et al., 2019) that
037   can provide exact bounds for all ReLU networks are computationally expensive due to the inherent
038   hardness of the problem, and thus incomplete methods (Wong & Kolter, 2018; Singh et al., 2018;
039   Weng et al., 2018; Gehr et al., 2018; Xu et al., 2020) have been widely investigated, typically fo-
040   cusing on convex relaxations, which can provide efficient and scalable certification at the cost of
041   losing precision. Beyond certification, all existing algorithms for training certifiable models (Shi
042   et al., 2021; Müller et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2023; 2024a; Palma et al., 2023; Balauca et al., 2024)
043
      are also based on convex relaxations. Due to their central role in certified robustness, it is critical to
      understand the trade-off between the efficiency and precision of convex relaxations.
044
045
      Expressivity of ReLU networks under convex relaxations In this work we focus on studying
046
      the expressivity of ReLU networks when convex relaxations are used. It has been previously shown
047
      that ReLU networks are expressive: they can precisely express every continuous piecewise linear
048   function (Hertrich et al., 2021) and thus can approximate every continuous function within an arbi-
049   trary error rate. A key question here is: do existing convex relaxation methods limit this expressive
050   power? The latest research results suggest a nuanced answer. Interval Bound Propagation (IBP)
051   applies the least precise single-neuron interval relaxation to each neuron, but for every continuous
052   function in Rn and an arbitrarily small error rate δ, there exists a ReLU network that approximates
053   the function with error δ and the relaxation error of IBP for this network is less than δ (Baader
      et al., 2020). However, Mirman et al. (2022) show that IBP cannot provide exact bounds for gen-

054
      eral continuous piecewise linear functions. Baader et al. (2024) further show that the most precise
055   single-neuron convex relaxation (strictly more precise than IBP), namely the Triangle relaxation
056   (Wong & Kolter, 2018), cannot provide exact bounds for any ReLU network that expresses the
057   “max” function on a compact domain in R2 . This is the case even though the function can be easily
058   expressed without error by a ReLU network with only two ReLU neurons. These results raise the
059   question of whether there exists a convex relaxation P that does not limit the expressive power of
060   ReLU networks. Concretely:
061   Given an arbitrary continuous piecewise linear function in Rn with a compact domain, can we find a
062   ReLU network that expresses this function such that applying P to the network returns the function’s
063   range exactly?
064
065
066   This work: multi-neuron relaxations do not restrict the expressive power of ReLU networks
067
      In this work we address the above question and show that in fact a multi-neuron relaxation which
      computes the convex hull of input and output variables layer-wise is complete for general (feedfor-
068
      ward and skip-connected) ReLU networks with cost related to the number of unstable neurons per
069
      layer. When limited to computing the convex hull of only output variables layer-wise, the relaxation
070   is still complete for feedforward networks with cost relying on the number of neurons per layer
071   (network width). Based on these novel results, we show that a multi-neuron relaxation can precisely
072   express every continuous piecewise linear function in Rn , in sharp contrast to any single-neuron re-
073   laxation. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first positive result on the completeness of convex
074   relaxations and their expressiveness for continuous piecewise linear functions in high dimensions,
075   leading to a deeper understanding of convex relaxations and their application to certified robustness.

078   2   R ELATED W ORK
079
080   We now briefly review related work most closely related to ours.
081
082
      Neural Network Certification. Existing methods for neural network certification can be catego-
      rized into complete methods and incomplete methods. Complete methods provide exact bounds for
083
      the output of a network, usually relying on solving a mixed-integer program (Tjeng et al., 2019) or
084
      a satisfiability modulo theory problem (Katz et al., 2017). These methods are naturally computa-
085   tionally expensive and do not scale well. Incomplete methods, on the other hand, provide sound but
086   inexact bounds, based on convex relaxations of the feasible output set of a network. Xu et al. (2020)
087   characterizes widely-recognized convex relaxations (Mirman et al., 2018; Wong et al., 2018; Zhang
088   et al., 2018; 2022; Ferrari et al., 2022) as linear constraints, equivalent to linear programming in the
089   corresponding linear systems. We distinguish three concrete convex relaxation methods typically
090   considered by theoretical work: Interval Bound Propagation (IBP) (Mirman et al., 2018; Gowal
091   et al., 2018), which ignores the interdependency between neurons and use interval {[a, b] | a, b ∈ R}
092   as the convex relaxation; Triangle relaxation (Wong & Kolter, 2018), which approximates the ReLU
093
      function by a triangle in the input-output space; and multi-neuron relaxations (Singh et al., 2018)
      which considers a group of ReLU neurons jointly in the linear system.
094
095   Convex Relaxation Theories. Baader et al. (2020) first show the universal approximation theo-
096   rem for certified models, stating that for every continuous piecewise linear function f : Rn → R
097   and any error rate ϵ > 0, there exists a ReLU network that expresses f and its IBP analysis can
098   provide bounds with error at most ϵ. This result is generalized to other activations by Wang et al.
099
      (2022). However, Mirman et al. (2022) show that there exists a continuous piecewise linear function
      for which IBP analysis of any finite ReLU network expressing this function cannot provide exact
100
      bounds. This means that even for continuous piecewise linear functions, IBP requires a network
101
      with infinitely many parameters to provide exact bounds. Further, Mao et al. (2024b) show that IBP
102   introduces a strong regularization on the parameter signs to provide good bounds, severely limiting
103   the network capability. Beyond IBP, Baader et al. (2024) show that even Triangle, the most precise
104   single-neuron relaxation, cannot precisely express the “max” function in R2 with a finite ReLU net-
105   work, although it can precisely express more functions than IBP in R. In sharp contrast, this work
106   shows that a multi-neuron relaxation can precisely express every continuous piecewise linear func-
107   tion in Rn with a finite ReLU network, providing a positive result on the expressiveness of convex
      relaxations for continuous piecewise linear functions in high dimensions.

145   Single-Neuron and Multi-Neuron Relaxations. Within the framework of layer-wise convex relax-
146   ations, the constraint set of an affine layer y = Ax + b is always C(x, y) = {Ax + b − y, −Ax −
147
      b + y} ≤ 0, which translates to the equality y = Ax + b. No loss of precision, therefore, is intro-
      duced in affine layers. The core difference between different relaxation methods is how they handle
148
      the ReLU function. Single-neuron relaxation methods relax each ReLU neuron separately and dis-
149
      regard the interdependence between neurons, while multi-neuron relaxations consider a group of
150   ReLU neurons jointly. Concretely, for the ReLU layer y = ρ(x) with x ∈ Rd , the constraint
151   sets computed by single-neuron relaxations are of the form C(xi , yi ) with i ∈ [d]. In contrast,
152   multi-neuron relaxations produce constraint sets of the form C(xI1 , yI2 ) with I1 , I2 ⊆ [d].
153
      Singh et al. (2019) propose the first multi-neuron relaxation called k-ReLU. For each ReLU layer,
154
      it considers at most k unstable neurons jointly, i.e., C(x, y) is of the form C(xI , yI ), with I ⊆
155
      [d], |I| ≤ k. However, k-ReLU is not complete for general ReLU networks (see §7), thus we
156   consider a stronger multi-neuron relaxation which only restrict the number of output variables in
157   the constraints, allowing C(x, y) to be of the form C(x, yI ) with I ⊆ [d], |I| ≤ k. We denote this

161   C(x, ρ(x)) is in the form of {C(ρ(xI )) | I ⊆ [d], |I| ≤ k}, and only one index set I is allowed per

299   6    M ULTI -N EURON E XPRESSIVITY WITH B OUNDED U NSTABLE N EURONS
300
301   We have shown in §5 that the output-only multi-neuron relaxation Mok returns the exact output set
302   for ReLU networks of width at most k. This result essentially relies on the fact that in a feedforward
303
      ReLU network, Mok does not lose precision for layers with at most k neurons, although it discards
      the dependency between input variable and output variable in each layer after processing. However,
304
      this result does not directly apply to ReLU networks with skip connections, where neurons between
305
      non-adjacent layers might be connected by a skip-connection. While it is also possible to convert a
306   ReLU network with skip connections into a feedforward network by introducing additional neurons
307   in those layers, the width of the resulting feedforward network becomes unnecessarily large, thus k
308   also needs to be as large which leads to significant computational overhead.
309
      In this section, we tackle this problem by developing a general result with Mk that applies to all
310
      ReLU networks, including those with skip connections. Specifically, we show that Mk is precise
311
      for ReLU networks with at most k unstable neurons in each hidden layer. Since the number of
312   unstable neurons in each layer will not increase when converting a network with skip connections to
313   a feedforward network, this result generalizes to ReLU networks with skip connections as well.
314
315

      sically unstable neurons are those that switch their activation pattern in the input set, while bounded
316
      unstable neurons are those that are not guaranteed to be stable by a convex relaxation, i.e., they have
317
      a positive upper bound and a negative lower bound under the given relaxation.
318

      this neuron and x2 does not activate it. Otherwise, it is called intrinsically stable on X.

413
414   Baader et al. (2024) prove that there does not exist a ReLU network that can express the “max”
415   function in the compact domain [0, 1]2 ⊂ R2 such that the network outputs can be bounded exactly
416
      by single-neuron relaxations. In this section, we take the “max” function in Rd , d ≥ 2, on domain
      [0, 1]d , as an example to show that a multi-neuron relaxation easily resolve such impossibility results,
417
      as a confirmation of our main result.
418
419   First, consider d = 2. In this case, we can represent the “max” function with the ReLU network
420   f = x2 + ρ(x1 − x2 ), illustrated in Figure 6. This network has width equals two (node c and d) and
421   maximum unstable neurons per layer equals one (node c). We will thus show that Mo2 and M1 can
422
      return the exact bounds of the functional range, i.e., [0, 1].
423   The input constraints are (x1 ≥ 0) ∧ (x1 ≤ 1) ∧ (x2 ≥ 0) ∧ (x2 ≤ 1). Besides, the constraints for
424   affine layers are (a = x1 − x2 ) ∧ (b = x2 ) ∧ (f = c + d). With these constraints, we can compute
425   the bounds of the output of the first affine layer with linear programming, yielding a ∈ [−1, 1] and
426   b ∈ [0, 1]. Therefore, a is bounded unstable and b is bounded stable.
427   We now show that M1 computes the exact bounds of f . For the bounded stable node b, the constraint
428   is d = b. For the bounded unstable node c, the constraint is (c ≥ 0) ∧ (c ≥ a) ∧ (c ≤ 1 − b).
429   Therefore, we have f = c + d = c + x2 ≥ 0 + x2 ≥ 0 and f = c + d = c + x2 ≤ 1 − x2 + x2 = 1.
430   Thus, M1 returns the exact bounds of the output of the ReLU network, which is [0, 1]. We remark
431   that 1-ReLU (which is equivalent to the Triangle relaxation) cannot return the exact upper bound, as
      its constraint for node c is (c ≥ 0) ∧ (c ≥ a) ∧ (c ≤ 0.5a + 0.5) since it only allow the constraint of

460   8   D ISCUSSION
461
462   Complete Certification with Multi-neuron Relaxations. This work establishes that two partic-
463   ular multi-neuron relaxations are complete verifiers for ReLU networks. Despite their theoretical
464   power, there is currently no algorithmic implementation of these relaxations. In particular, their al-
465   gorithmic complexity is unknown. Developing efficient algorithms for these relaxations is important
466   for future work, and we suggest a few possible directions below.
467
      The first question is how to compute the convex hull. While this might be exactly computed (e.g., for
468   affine layers), an approximate convex hull might be sufficient for practical purposes (Müller et al.,
469   2022). Therefore, one may rely on “constraint mining”, i.e., finding valid constraints sequentially.
470   Since the convex hull is the intersection of all valid constraints, one can iteratively add constraints
471   to the linear system until the convex hull is fully covered. While effective constraint mining is non-
472   trivial, we remark that due to the completeness of multi-neuron relaxations, the expensive branch-
473   and-bound as deployed by Müller et al. (2022) is no longer required to find the exact bounds. In
474   addition, similar constraint mining approaches are deployed by Zhang et al. (2022), but they consider
475   all constraints possibly involving different layers, which is a much larger constraint space than that
476
      for a single layer.
477   The second question is how to solve the linear system efficiently, especially in the process of con-
478   straint mining where multiple strongly overlapping linear programming problems need to be solved.
479   This question might be relatively easy, because we can expect the optimal solution of the previous
480   linear programming to be a good initial guess for the next linear programming. In particular, the
481   simplex algorithm might be a good choice for this task because the new optimum must lie on the
482
      vertices introduced by new constraints.
483   The last question is how to check whether we have reached the exact bounds. We suggest two
484   possible approaches. The first approach essentially relies on the effectiveness of constraint mining:
485   if the constraint mining algorithm can no longer find a new constraint that improves the bound, then
      the current bound is exact. The second approach is to reconstruct the input of the network and check


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      whether its output matches the current bound. This approach is more straightforward because when
487   solving for Mk , we directly have the values for the input of the network.
488
489   Importance of Certified Training. Our work shows that ReLU networks with width at most d+3
490   and only three unstable neurons per layer are enough to express any continuous piecewise linear
491   function in Rd , and multi-neuron relaxations can provide exact bounds for these networks. This
492   implies that if we can train customized models, the complexity of certification can be drastically
493   reduced. Therefore, along with more powerful certification tools, the field should develop more
494   powerful training algorithms that can train networks that are easily certifiable.
495
496   9   C ONCLUSION
497
498   We proved the first positive result on the completeness of convex relaxations and the expressivity of
499   ReLU networks under convex relaxations. While single-neuron relaxations that relax each neuron
500   separately are incomplete, we proved that (layer-wise) multi-neuron methods, where multiple neu-
501   rons in the same layer are processed jointly, are complete. Specifically, for networks of width no
502   more than k, one computes the convex hull of the range of each layer, proceeding in a layer-wise
503   manner. Then, the resulting set of linear constraints induces exact upper and lower bounds on the
504   output set of the network. In addition, when the network width is unbounded, but the number of
505   unstable neurons is at most k in each layer, we can retain the exact bounds by jointly considering the
506
      input-output set of those k neurons. Our results demonstrate that the expressivity of ReLU networks
      is no longer limited under multi-neuron relaxations, in contrast to single-neuron relaxations which
507
      have previously been shown to severely limit the expressivity of networks they can certify exactly.
