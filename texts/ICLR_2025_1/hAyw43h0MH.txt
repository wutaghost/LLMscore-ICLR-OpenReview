009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Mixture-of-experts (MoE) architectures have recently emerged as an effective ap-
012            proach for scaling model capacity while managing computational costs by lever-
013            aging expert sparsity, where only a subset of experts is activated during inference.
014            Despite their computational efficiency, MoE models face challenges in training
015            stability compared to their dense counterparts, largely due to the introduction of
016
               expert sparsity. While several methods have been proposed to mitigate this in-
               stability, the underlying relationship between expert sparsity and training stability
017
               remains unclear. In this work, we develop a theoretical framework that demon-
018
               strates an inverse correlation between training stability and expert sparsity, with
019            gradient smoothness serving as the bridge. We derive an upper bound on training
020            stability, formalizing for the first time the sparsity-stability trade-off in MoE mod-
021            els. Our findings show that activating more experts enhances gradient smoothness
022            and improves training stability but at the cost of reduced sparsity. We validate our
023            theory through extensive experiments on various architectures and datasets, and
024            propose a novel MoE structure that addresses stability without sacrificing spar-
025            sity. This design introduces independent router heads and a soft top-K selection
026            via sampling without replacement, which smooths the gradient landscape while
027
               maintaining expert sparsity. Further analysis confirms the promise of this struc-
               ture in striking the optimal balance between sparsity and stability, offering a new
028
               direction for optimizing MoE architectures in large-scale models.

032
033   Mixture of Experts (MoE) was introduced to im-
034   plement conditional computation within a model
035   to enhance training and inference speeds (Jacobs
036   et al., 1991; Jordan & Jacobs, 1994). MoE was
037   later extended to deep learning architectures, in-
038   cluding CNNs, RNNs, and transformers (Eigen

050   more efficient to run but less stable to train

070
      In this work we investigate the above question through both theoretical and empirical investigations,
071   where our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
072
073          • We propose a theoretical framework connecting expert sparsity and training stability
074            via gradient smoothness. By introducing the concept of gradient smoothness and ana-
075            lyzing the Lipschitz constants of both the loss function and its gradient, we establish the
076            quantitative relationship between sparsity and stability. We obtain the theoretical upper
077            bound on training stability for MoEs, formulating the sparsity-stability trade-off in MoE
078            models for the first time. Our theory shows that activating more experts enhances gradient
079
               smoothness and improves training stability upper bound, however at the cost of reduced
               sparsity.
080
081          • Our experimental investigation across representative architectures verify the univer-
082            sal efficacy of our theory. Specifically, we perform extensive experiments using MoE
083            architectures based on Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs), Convolutional Neural Networks
084
               (CNNs), and transformers, applied to synthetic, image, and text datasets. These exper-
               iments consistently demonstrate that denser MoEs result in smoother gradients and more
085
               stable training across various architectures and datasets, supporting our theoretical findings.
086
087          • We introduce a novel MoE structure that improves stability without sacrificing spar-
088
               sity, striking the sparsity-stability trade-off. Under the guidance of our theory, we ad-
               dress two key issues in conventional MoE models: zero gradients in top-1 MoEs and the
089
               deterministic nature of top-K selection. Our design introduces independent router heads
090
               and uses a soft top-K selection through sampling without replacement, smoothing the gra-
091            dient landscape while maintaining expert sparsity. This structure achieves stability bounds
092            comparable to dense models, offering a solution to the sparsity-stability trade-off in MoEs.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
099
100
      Training Instability of MoEs: Compared to dense networks, MoEs have been noted for their poorer
101
      stability and generalization, as highlighted by (Shazeer et al., 2017; Fedus et al., 2022; Zoph et al.,
102   2022). These issues make MoEs prone to overfitting and challenging to fine-tune. Foundational
103   works by Shazeer et al. (Shazeer et al., 2017) and subsequent studies by Fedus et al. (Fedus et al.,
104   2022) and Zoph et al. (Zoph et al., 2022) identified these challenges and initiated the exploration of
105   methods to mitigate them. To address the instability of MoEs, researchers have explored stochastic
106   or differentiable top-K selection for soft routing (Xie & Ermon, 2019; Paulus et al., 2020; Hazimeh
107   et al., 2021) and different dense training strategies bypassing sparse routing (Nie et al., 2021; Ko-
      matsuzaki et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2024). These studies provided critical insights


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      into the instability problem but did not fully address the connection between expert sparsity and
109   training stability, which our work aims to explore further.
110
111   Gradient Smoothness and Training Stability: Mini-batch Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)
112
      (Robbins & Monro, 1951) is a widely utilized optimization method in deep learning. The conver-
      gence properties of mini-batch SGD are well-established under certain smoothness and convexity
113
      conditions (Garrigos & Gower, 2023). Recent studies have extensively examined the critical role of
114
      gradient smoothness in achieving stable and generalizable mini-batch SGD methods (Hardt et al.,
115   2016; Charles & Papailiopoulos, 2018; Kuzborskij & Lampert, 2018; Wu et al., 2018; Lei & Ying,
116   2020). Techniques such as weight decay (Krogh & Hertz, 1991), gradient clipping (Mikolov et al.,
117   2012), network pruning (Srivastava et al., 2014), and batch normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015)
118   have been proposed to enhance the stability of mini-batch SGD. These foundational studies on gra-
119   dient smoothness and stability directly inform our work, as we extend these concepts to the sparsity
120   of MoEs. By connecting gradient smoothness with the expert selection process in MoEs, our work
121   builds upon these established principles to propose a theoretical framework that links expert sparsity
122   with training stability.

158   3.2   S MOOTHNESS OF L OSS F UNCTION
159
160   Smoothness of a loss function is an important factor that impacts the performance of gradient descent
161   methods. A smooth loss function typically has a gradient that varies gradually with changes in the
      parameters, leading to a more stable convergence behavior during the optimization process. In


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      contrast, a non-smooth or highly irregular loss function can result in a noisier gradient estimate,
163   which may slow down the convergence of gradient descent. Here, we outline the key mathematical
164   concepts pertaining to the smoothness of loss functions.
165
166
      A loss function L : Ω × X → R is convex if, for all Θ, Θ′ ∈ Ω, the following holds:
                                                                        ⊤
167                            L (Θ′ ; x) ≥ L (Θ; x) + ∇Θ L (Θ; x) (Θ′ − Θ) .
168
169   And a loss function L is L-Lipschitz if, for all Θ ∈ Ω and x ∈ X, the gradient satisfies
170   ∥∇Θ L (Θ; x)∥2 ≤ L, i.e., L is an upper bound on the 2-norm of the loss gradients. This implies:
171                                 ∥L (Θ; x) − L (Θ′ ; x)∥ ≤ L ∥Θ − Θ′ ∥ .
172   Similarly, L is β-smooth if its gradient ∇Θ L is β-Lipschitz, meaning:
173
                                ∥∇Θ L (Θ; x) − ∇Θ′ L (Θ′ ; x)∥ ≤ β ∥Θ − Θ′ ∥ .

533   only enriches the theoretical discourse on MoE stability but also provides actionable strategies for

650   Data: We consider a straightforward scenario where the input data x are uniformly distributed
651   across the interval [−1, 1], denoted as U [−1, 1]. This uniform distribution is selected to provide
652   a controlled and interpretable setting for analyzing the behavior of the Mixture of Experts (MoE)
653   model. The target output for all input data points is set to 0, simplifying the assessment of the
654   model’s optimization performance.
655
      Model: The model employed in this experiment is a one-layer MoE consisting of two linear experts.
656   The expert functions are defined as f1 (θ1 ; x) = θ1 x and f2 (θ2 ; x) = θ2 x, where θ1 and θ2 are the
657   respective   parameters of the experts. The router function is fixed with equal probabilities (p1 , p2 ) =
658     1 1

          ,
        2 2    , ensuring that any input x is equally likely to be routed to either f1 or f2 . The output y of the
659   MoE varies depending on the top-k setting. When top-k = 2, the output is calculated as a weighted
660   sum of contributions from both experts: y = p1 f1 + p2 f2 . For top-k = 1, the output is randomly
661   selected as either f1 or f2 . The loss function is defined as the squared 2-norm between the target
662   value 0 and the output y, expressed as l(θ1 , θ2 ) = y 2 . The optimization is carried out using gradient
663   descent with a fixed learning rate of 0.1, over 20 steps. This simplified MoE setup is designed to
664   illustrate the relationship between expert sparsity, gradient smoothness, and training stability within
