011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Spiking neural networks (SNNs) provide a low-power, energy-efficient solution by
014            utilizing the spike-based and sparse nature of biological systems. Since the advent
015
               of Transformers, SNNs have struggled to compete with artificial networks on long
               sequential tasks, until the recent emergence of state space models (SSMs), which
016
               offer superior computational efficiency and modeling capability. However, apply-
017
               ing the highly capable SSMs to SNNs for long sequences learning poses three
018            major challenges: ❶ The membrane potential is determined by the past spiking
019            history of the neuron, leading to reduced efficiency for sequence modeling in par-
020            allel computing scenarios. ❷ Complex dynamics of biological spiking neurons are
021            crucial for functionality but challenging to simulate and exploit effectively in large
022            networks. ❸ It is arduous to maintain high sparsity while achieving high accuracy
023            for spiking neurons without resorting to dense computing, as utilized in artificial
024            neuron-based SSMs. To address these challenges, we propose a sparse, precise
025            and efficient spiking SSM framework, termed SPikE-SSM. For ❶, we propose a
026
               boundary compression strategy (PMBC) to accelerate the inference of the spiking
               neuron model, enabling parallel processing for long sequence learning. For ❷, we
027
               propose a novel and concise neuron model incorporating reset-refractory mech-
028
               anism to leverage the inherent temporal dimension for dynamic computing with
029            biological interpretability. For ❸, we hierarchically integrate the proposed neuron
030            model to the original SSM block, and enhance the dynamics of SPikE-SSM by
031            incorporating trainable thresholds and refractory magnitudes to balance accuracy
032            and sparsity. Extensive experiments illustrate the effectiveness and robustness
033            of SPikE-SSM on the long range arena benchmarks and large language dataset
034            WikiText-103, showing the potential of dynamic spiking neurons in efficient long
035            sequence learning. The code will be publicly available.

      Spiking neural networks (SNNs) recently emerged as a competitive paradigm to improve AI en-
040
      ergy efficiency. SNNs transmit information as binary spikes between synapses to perform sparse
041
      and event-driven computation. Despite being increasingly more competitive with artificial neural
042   networks (ANNs) in vision tasks, SNNs still struggle with long-sequence modeling – a critical task
043   for a wide range of temporal or sequential data-driven machine learning applications, such as text

050   et al., 2017) in Transformers are computationally intensive, contrasting with the energy-efficient
051   properties of event-based representations and the sparse computation inherent to SNNs. As a com-
052   petitive alternative to Transformer, state space models (SSMs) have garnered significant attention

      2022), S5 (Smith et al., 2022) and Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023). Notably, SSMs can achieve fast in-

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
129
      2.1   L ONG S EQUENCES L EARNING M ODELS
130
131   Long sequence modeling has gained significant attention recently due to its widespread applica-
132   tion across different domains such as text comprehending (Zhou et al., 2023), computer vision (Shi
133   et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2024) and electroencephalograms spanning (Tang et al., 2023). The key
134   challenge in long-sequence modeling lies in efficiently compressing context into a manageable state
135   while capturing information spread across observations separated by thousands of timesteps. To
136   address them, Transformer and Attention (Vaswani et al., 2017; Dao et al., 2022; Dao, 2023) are
137   proposed to retain the entire context during auto-regressive inference, which is effective but requires
138   quadratic-time computational complexity. Although some Transformer variants (Kitaev et al., 2020;
139
      Katharopoulos et al., 2020) are proposed to reduce the compute and memory requirements, their
      performances on long-range reasoning remain considerably suboptimal (Gu et al., 2021). Inspired
140
      by RNNs, RWKV (Peng et al., 2023) combines the parallel training of transformers with the effi-
141
      cient inference of RNNs. Similarly, other recurrent models aim to compress context into a finite
142   state, offering constant-time inference and linear-time training, but their effectiveness is limited by
143   the quality of compression and a fixed representation space (Qin et al., 2023). More recently, SSM-
144   based methods (Smith et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2022; Mehta et al., 2022) have emerged as a promising
145   alternative to sequence models such as RNNs and Transformers. For example, HiPPO (Gu et al.,
146   2020) pioneered compressing long inputs into dynamic representations using orthogonal polynomi-
147   als, while S4 (Gu et al., 2021) advanced this with low-rank corrections for stable diagonalization
148   and simplified Cauchy kernel operations. Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) focuses on selective state
149   representations to optimize efficiency and effectiveness, using a selection mechanism and hardware-
150
      optimized algorithms to maintain robust contextual information capture. All above methods are
      based on artificial neurons with analog-valued output, resulting in dense vector-matrix multipli-
151
      cation (VMM) and huge computational costs. In contrast, the proposed SPikE-SSM utilizes the
152
      compatibility between the remarkable LRDs modeling ability of SSMs and the intrinsic dynamics
153   of SNNs, promoting sparse training and fully parallel inference with trainable temporal dynamics.
154
155
      2.2   SNN S - BASED S EQUENCE M ODELING AND A PPLICATIONS
156
157   SNNs (Ghosh-Dastidar & Adeli, 2009) have gained attention as a compelling bio-plausible and
158   computational efficient substitute for traditional artificial neural networks (ANNs) in many vision
159   tasks. However, SNNs have struggled to make significant progress in long-sequence modeling tasks
160   due to the inherent serial computing nature. Therefore, to train SNNs in parallel, PSN (Fang et al.,
161   2024) simplifies spiking neuron by omitting the reset mechanism, leading to reduced sparsity. To
      handle this issue, a probabilistic reset mechanism is proposed in PSU (Li et al., 2024) to achieve


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      parallel computing with elevated sparsity by decoupling the integration-spiking-resetting process,
163   which comes at the expense of higher computational complexity. With the recent resurgence of
164   SSMs, there has been a renewed focus on applying efficient parallel computing to SNNs. For ex-
165   ample, SpikeS4 (Du et al., 2024) integrates LIF neurons with S4 layers for speech learning. Binary
166   S4D builds a binary SSM by applying a spiking activation directly to the sum of hidden states,
167   enabling parallel training but neglecting neuronal dynamics (Stan & Rhodes, 2023). To further en-
168   hance sparsity, a stochastic spiking neuron is proposed in S6-based SNN (Bal & Sengupta, 2024),
169   which is trained with stochastic noises in gradients, resulting in accuracy degradation. More re-
170   cently, SpikingSSMs (Shen et al., 2024) utilizes a surrogate dynamic network (SDN) to approxi-
171
      mate the dynamics of LIF neurons, which extremely accelerates the training and inference by par-
      allel computing. However, the pre-training requirement of SDN could constrain its application on
172
      more general dynamic spiking neurons which are hard to approximate. Due to the effectiveness of
173
      spike-based sequence learning, some SNNs-based language models are proposed for more efficient
174   language modeling, such as SpikeGPT (Zhu et al., 2023) and SpikeBERT (Lv et al., 2023). In con-
175   trast to existing spiking SSMs, SPikE-SSM proposed in this paper realizes comprehensive parallel
176   acceleration with trainable temporal dynamics, efficiently achieving both high sparsity and excel-
177   lent accuracy for long-range dependencies learning, which possesses the potential and prospects for
178   constructing low-energy language models and enabling widespread applications.

300   3.3       R EFRACTORY LIF N EURON M ODEL
301
302   In biological neurons, spiking is usually followed by a refractory period during which new spiking is
303   more difficult. This mechanism improves the overall sparsity of the network and could substantially
304   reduce its energy consumption. Therefore, to simulate the intrinsic temporal dynamics of realistic
305   neurons and further improve network sparsity, we introduce an innovative refractory LIF neuron
306
      model based on the soft reset mechanism, which effectively addresses Challenge ❷. The LIF neuron
      with a refractory period can be mathematically described as:
307
308                              ut = τ ut−1 +It − Rt Uth , st = Hs (ut − vth ),                          (9)
309                                   where Rt = τr Rt−1 + st−1 ,                                        (10)
310   In our refractory neuron model, τr is the refractory magnitude. Rt denotes the refractory period-
311   based sliding pulse, which is determined by both spiking signal st−1 and Rt−1 in the last time step.
312   From Eq. (10) we can observe that the larger the value of the previous sliding pulse Rt−1 , the greater

343   3.4     T HE BLOCK OF SP IK E-SSM
344
345
      For Challenge ❸, due to the exceptional long sequence modeling capability of SSMs, we integrate
      the proposed refractory neuron with soft reset mechanism and PMBC to the inherent SSM block,
346
      which aims to maintain both the high sparsity and excellent accuracy in the inference progress.
347
      In the proposed SPikE-SSM, we choose the original block of S4D model (Gu et al., 2022) as the
348   backbone since it can achieve pragmatic simplification to enhance model efficiency as the latest
349   diagonal version of SSM. Then the output y of the S4D block is activated by the proposed refractory
350   neuron, hence Eq. (9) is rewritten as follows with Eq. (10) unchanged:
351
                                yt = C̄ht ,       ut = τ ut−1 + yt − Rt Uth ,                   st = Hs (ut − vth ).                              (13)
352
353   Inspired from (Rathi & Roy, 2021), we render vth and Uth as trainable parameters within the SPikE-
354
      SSM block. This approach is motivated by their pivotal role in regulating the neuron’s spiking
      rate, thereby not only bolstering the SPikE-SSM’s capability to attain exceptional performance and
355
      expedite the convergence of PMBC, but also serving as a further stepping stone to tackle Challenge
356
      ❸ with greater efficacy. The results of Eq. (13) are fed into a linear layer that comprises a Conv1D
357   operation followed by a GLU activation function (Dauphin et al., 2017). The Conv1D enables
358   efficient local feature extraction, while the GLU activation selectively gates the information flow,
359   improving the model’s ability to capture critical patterns in sparse binary data. Since the Heaviside
360   function Hs is non-differentiable at x = 0, we adopt the surrogate gradient (SG) method in SPikE-
361   SSM. The details of SG in our method are provided in the Appendix B.3.

363   the original S4 blocks. Notably, from a neurobiological perspective, the SPikE-SSM block resem-
364   bles a multi-time scale dendritic neuron (London & Häusser, 2005; Zheng et al., 2024), where h
365   represents the dendrites and y the soma, both showing self-recurrent temporal dynamics.

530   5       C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we introduced SPikE-SSM, a novel spiking state space model designed to address key
532
      challenges in long-sequence learning with SNNs. Specifically, we innovatively address the conflict
533
      of event-driven neuronal dynamics with parallel computing in long sequence modeling by the PMBC
534   method, enabling explicit and efficient training of neuronal dynamics. Subsequently, a concise reset-
535   refractory neuron model is proposed to exploit the functionality of biological-plausible temporal
536   dynamics. Its effective integration with the SSM block and incorporation of trainable thresholds
537   and refractory magnitudes realize a balance between sparsity and accuracy. Extensive experiments
538   on sequential vision tasks, LRA benchmarks, and WikiText-103 language modeling validate the
539   superior efficiency, accuracy, and sparsity of SPikE-SSM. Our work shows the potential of dynamic
      spiking neurons in efficient long sequence learning.

       C.1.1    S EQUENTIAL V ISION DATASETS
1004   The MNIST dataset (Deng, 2012) is a classic benchmark in machine learning, featuring 70,000
1005   grayscale images of handwritten digits (0-9), with 60,000 samples for training and 10,000 for test-
1006   ing, each image sized at 28×28 pixels. It has long been a cornerstone for evaluating image classi-
1007   fication models due to its simplicity and widespread availability. The sequential MNIST (sMNIST)
1008   dataset (Le et al., 2015) transforms these 2D images into sequences of 784 elements by flattening
1009   the pixel grid into a 1D sequence. This transformation poses a more complex challenge, as models
1010   must process and retain information over longer time steps to accurately classify the digit. To further
1011   increase the difficulty, the permuted sequential MNIST (psMNIST) (Le et al., 2015) applies a fixed
1012
       random permutation to the pixel sequences, effectively scrambling their spatial order and disrupting
       any inherent structure. This variant demands even greater computational ability from models, as
1013
       they must learn to extract meaningful features from a sequence with no obvious temporal or spatial
1014
       coherence, making psMNIST a far more challenging task compared to the original sMNIST. The
1015   sCIFAR10 dataset is a sequential version of the CIFAR-10 dataset (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), where
1016   the original static images are processed in a temporal manner, typically by feeding the image pixels
1017   row-by-row or in a pre-defined sequence.
