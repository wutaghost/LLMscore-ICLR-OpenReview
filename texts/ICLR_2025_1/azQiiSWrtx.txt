009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Cross-lingual sentence encoders (CLSE) create fixed-size sentence represen-
012            tations with aligned translations. Current pre-trained CLSE approaches use
013            sentence-level objectives only. This can lead to loss of information, especially for
014            tokens, which then degrades the sentence representation. We propose MEXMA,
015            a novel approach that integrates both sentence-level and token-level objectives.
016            The sentence representation in one language is used to predict masked tokens in
017            another language, with both the sentence representation and all tokens directly
018            updating the encoder. We show that adding token-level objectives greatly im-
019
               proves the sentence representation quality across several tasks. Our approach out-
               performs current pre-trained cross-lingual sentence encoders on bitext mining as
020
               well as several downstream tasks. We also analyse the information encoded in our
021
               tokens, and how the sentence representation is built from them.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Creating general-purpose multilingual embeddings has attracted significant attention from the re-
      search community in recent years, driven by the growing need for efficient and effective cross-
028
      lingual representations. Cross-Lingual Sentence Encoders (CLSE) create fixed-size sentence rep-
029
      resentations that are able to capture the relevant information in a sentence, and are aligned across
030   languages. By capturing relevant sentence information in a shared multilingual space, these aligned
031   representations enable efficient comparison and retrieval based on distance measures, thereby facil-
032   itating their effective utilization in various downstream applications.
033
      Current CLSE (Duquenne et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2022) typically build upon pre-trained encoders,
034
      often language models (Conneau et al., 2020; Devlin et al., 2019) or translation models (NLLB Team
035
      et al., 2022). These pre-trained encoders have been trained using objectives that focus on individual
036   words or tokens, i.e. token-level objectives. Examples of such objectives include unmasking, where
037   the model is required to predict each token individually, and all predictions are used to update
038   the encoder directly. However, Muennighoff et al. (2023); Hu et al. (2020) show that pre-trained
039   encoders without objectives that consider entire sentences, i.e. sentence-level objectives, do not
040   create good sentence representations. This means that CLSE need to train using sentence-level
041   objectives, in order to effectively capture the relevant information of the sentences.
042   Although CLSE start from encoders pre-trained with token-level objectives, they are commonly
043   trained with sentence-level objectives that only update the encoder through the sentence represen-
044   tation (Duquenne et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2019; Artetxe & Schwenk, 2019a),
045   without any objective for each token individually. We hypothesize that token-level objectives should
046   be kept during the training of CLSE, coupled with the sentence-level objectives, to better update
047   the encoder and improve sentence representation quality and alignment. The intuition is that only
048   using sentence-level objectives leads to a degradation of token level information, especially lexical
049   information, which in turn can impact the sentence representation.
050   Recently, there have been approaches exploring the use of both token-level and sentence-level objec-
051   tives for better sentence representations. In DAP (Li et al., 2023), the token-level objective is only
052   used to update the token representations in the encoder, without influencing directly the sentence
053   representation. In RetroMAE (Xiao et al., 2022), the tokens are not directly updated with the same
      token-level objective as the sentence representation.

054
      To effectively combine token and sentence-level objectives, we propose MEXMA, a new approach
055   that uses the sentence representation in one language to predict masked tokens in another language,
056   and uses both the sentence and tokens’ information to update the encoder. This token-level objective
057   is combined with a sentence-level objective to enforce sentence alignment across languages.
058
      Our approach outperforms state-of-the-art pre-trained cross-lingual sentence encoders, namely
059
      LaBSE and SONAR, on several key tasks including bitext mining, classification, and pair classi-
060
      fication. Specifically, we report notable gains on the xsim++ benchmark computed over the FLO-
061   RES200 test set, where MEXMA achieves an error rate of 9.6%, surpassing SONAR’s 12.1%. Addi-
062   tionally, in classification tasks evaluated on MTEB and SentEval, MEXMA achieves an accuracy of
063   65.4% compared to SONAR’s 63.0%. The larger supervision in MEXMA enables training smaller
064   models with better alignment than LaBSE (≈2×) and close to SONAR’s performance (≈3×).
065
      Our main contributions are:
066
067         • We introduce a novel architecture leveraging both sentence-level and token-level objectives
068           outperforming current approaches.
069         • We perform ablation studies that show the impact of token-level objectives on the sentence-
070           level representations performance.
071         • We provide an extensive analysis of the inner working of our model, by analysing its to-
072           kens’ contents, and the way the sentence embedding is built. We show that as a byproduct
073           of our training, individual tokens are also well aligned across languages.
074         • We show that our approach can also be coupled with existing alignment approaches, specif-
075           ically contrastive learning, and improve its quality.

079   2   R ELATED W ORK
080
081   Sentence embeddings have been well studied in the last decade. Initially, recurrent networks were
082   trained to predict previous and next sentence (Kiros et al., 2015) or sentence entailment (Conneau
083   et al., 2017). Universal Sentence Encoder (Cer et al., 2018) trains a transformer network on both
084
      tasks. Reimers & Gurevych (2019) propose to continue the training of a BERT model to include a
      sentence-level objective. These initial works have been extended to multilingual settings, to capture
085
      the relevant information in the sentences, and to have aligned representations across languages.
086
      These new approaches are called cross-lingual sentence encoder. We describe those works next.
087
088
      U PDATE VIA SENTENCE REPRESENTATION Most current cross-lingual sentence encoder ap-
089   proaches only update their encoder via the sentence representation objective, without having any
090   token-level objective in the output of the encoder that would update each token individually (Guo
091   et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2019; Feng et al., 2022; Artetxe & Schwenk, 2019a; Duquenne et al., 2023;
092   Heffernan et al., 2022). They are most commonly based on contrastive learning (Hadsell et al.,
093   2006) methods, that aim to reduce the distance between positive pairs (translations) and increase
094   the distance between negative pairs (non-translations) (Guo et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2019; Feng
095   et al., 2022). Notably, LaBSE (Feng et al., 2022) uses the contrastive loss, with the additive margin
096   softmax approach of Yang et al. (2019). Non-contrastive approaches reduce the distance between
097
      positive pairs (translations) only, being prone to collapse. A common solution to collapse is to use
      an auto-regressive decoder to prevent it. For CLSE, it is common to use translation (Artetxe &
098
      Schwenk, 2019a; Duquenne et al., 2023) with a fixed-size sentence representation after the encoder
099
      (bottleneck), assuming that a model can translate a sentence into many languages only if a good
100   sentence-level conceptual representation is learned. The bottleneck, however, prevents gradients
101   from the decoder to directly update the individual token representations of the encoder, which we
102   hypothesize leads to a degradation of token level information and consequently of the sentence rep-
103   resentation. Our method also uses a sentence representation as context for the unmasking, but allows
104   direct token-level gradients to propagate to the encoder token representations.
105
106   U PDATE VIA SENTENCE AND TOKEN REPRESENTATIONS Recent approaches (Li et al., 2023;
107   Xiao et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2021; Fan et al., 2022) have shown that combining token and sentence
      level objectives can improve sentence representations. RetroMAE (Xiao et al., 2022), is an Infor-

      3   M ETHODOLOGY
151
152
153
      We propose MEXMA, a novel multilingual alignment technique based on both token-level and
      sentence-level objectives. The goal is to create a sentence representation that is able to encode the
154
      syntactic, semantic and lexical information in a sentence, with representations well aligned across
155
      languages. To achieve this goal, inspired by monolingual masked auto-encoding techniques (Xiao
156   et al., 2022), we use the sentence representation in one language to unmask the tokens in another
157   language, updating both the sentence and individual tokens. This also forces the sentence rep-
158   resentation to encode the relevant parts of the sentence. Using masking also allows us to use a
159   non-contrastive loss to align sentence representations, since it prevents the collapse. Both sentence
160   and token-level objectives are used to improve the quality of the sentence representation. Our archi-
161   tecture is depicted in Figure 1, and is composed of several components that we describe now. For
      the explanation, we refer to inputs, models and outputs that have no masking as clean, and masked

      for their masked counterparts. Additionally, we consider two languages, language A and language
163   B, which are associated with the sentence representations SA and SB (from the clean encoders).

165
      T HE CROSS - UNMASKING To ensure that our sentence vector captures the meaningful informa-
166
      tion of the sentence, we mask a significant portion of the input tokens in language A. This makes it
167
      challenging for the encoder and the MLM head to recover the missing tokens without any additional
168   context. To overcome this challenge, we provide the unmasking head with the sentence vector SB ,
169   derived from the clean sentence in language B. This forces the model to leverage the information
170   in SB to predict the masked tokens in language A. By doing so, we encourage the sentence vector
171   to capture the essential information of the sentence. Furthermore, by alternating languages, we en-
172   force the sentence vector to encode information that is useful across languages. We formulate this
173   component into a symmetrical cross-entropy loss (CE), applied over the outputs of the encoders:
174
175                              Lmlm = CE([SB , Â], A) + CE([SA , B̂], B),
176
      where Â and B̂ are the outputs of the masked encoders without the CLS embedding, A and B the
177
      masked tokens’ targets, and [X, Y ] represents the concatenation of X and Y.
178
179
180   T HE ALIGNMENT LOSS The cross-unmasking generates an implicit alignment due to the switch-
181
      ing of languages to perform the unmasking. However, as is, that implicit alignment does not strongly
      enforce the same sentence representations in two different languages to be close in the embedding
182
      space. Following SONAR (Duquenne et al., 2023), to further reinforce the spatial proximity of se-
183
      mantically equivalent sentences across languages, we use an additional non-contrastive alignment
184   objective. The two losses, unmasking and alignment, complement each other to provide both aligned
185   and meaningful vector representations of sentences in multiple languages. We formulate this com-
186   ponent as a Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss between sentence representations:
187
188                                     Lalignment = M SE(SA , SB ),
189
190   T HE SYMMETRICAL ARCHITECTURE To align all languages and maximize data usage, we
191   adopt a symmetrical approach that unmasks the tokens of language A with SB , and vice versa,
192   simultaneously. We thus create four instances of the encoder (with shared parameters). For each
193   language, we have two versions of each sentence: one heavily masked and one clean. This allows us
194
      to generate two clean sentence vectors, SA and SB , which is essential for aligning representations
      between languages. A non-symmetrical approach with only two encoders (one per language) would
195
      not produce the desired alignment as it would force the model to align a heavily masked sentence
196
      vector with a clean one, which is not ideal.
197

      T HE KO L EO LOSS In preliminary experiments, we noticed that our representations exhibited
      more anisotropy than those learned with contrastive approaches. This has been shown to impact the
200
      quality of the representations (Godey et al., 2024). Inspired by DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2024), we
201
      employ the KoLeo loss (Sablayrolles et al., 2019) to encourage sentence representations to spread
202   out evenly in the latent space. The KoLeo loss is based on the Kozachenko-Leonenko differential
203   entropy estimator (see Beirlant et al. (1997)). We define below the KoLeo loss, LKoLeo , for a set of
204   n representations, as well as the symmetrical version, LK , we use to train our models:
205

      3.1      E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
228
229   E NCODER BACKBONE As our encoder, we utilize a modified version of the XLM-RoBERTa
230   model (Conneau et al., 2020) provided by HuggingFace that uses a more efficient attention (details
231   in Appendix A). Our sentence representation from the encoder is obtained via the CLS embedding
232   of the last layer, without any further processing.
233
234   T RAINING DATA Our training dataset is a subset of the NLLB-200 corpus (NLLB Team et al.,
235   2022), which comprises 200 languages. We cover 81 languages, utilizing only publicly available
236   data, all sourced from Opus (Tiedemann, 2012). The specific languages used are listed in Ap-
237   pendix C. We always train using one sentence in English associated with its translation in one of
238
      the remaining 80 languages, as done in SONAR. The dataset consists of a combination of human-
      translated and synthetic data, where we attempt to impose a minimum of 15 million sentences per
239
      language. For languages with limited human-annotated data, we supplemented the dataset with
240
      mined data from NLLB (Schwenk et al., 2020; Fan et al., 2020; NLLB Team et al., 2022) to reach
241   the 15 million sentence threshold. Conversely, to ensure that our dataset is somewhat balanced
242   across languages, for languages with abundant human-annotated data, we capped the dataset at 25
243   million sentences per language. The datasets used are detailed in Table 17.

248
249   To assess the quality and alignment of our embeddings, we evaluate them on a range of tasks. These
250   tasks fall into two categories: mining tasks and other downstream tasks. Mining tasks measure how
251   aligned our representations are across languages, while downstream tasks evaluate the generalization
252   power and overall quality of our embeddings.

254   4.1      M ULTILINGUAL ALIGNMENT THROUGH MINING
255
256   We evaluate our model on three alignment tasks: xsim1 , xsim++ (Chen et al., 2023), and BUCC
257   (Zweigenbaum et al., 2018; 2017). Both xsim and BUCC involve retrieving the correct translation of
258   a query sentence from multilingual datasets. xsim++ adds complexity by introducing hard negatives
259   in English sentences. Following Heffernan et al. (2022), we exclude Tatoeba due to limited data and
260
      low-quality translations.
261   xsim and xsim++ use a margin-based similarity approach (Artetxe & Schwenk, 2019b), while BUCC
262   employs cosine similarity. xsim and xsim++ scores are the error rate of misaligned sentences,
263   whereas BUCC uses the F1 score, evaluated with the MTEB benchmark (Muennighoff et al., 2023).
264   BUCC covers German, French, Russian and Chinese. We evaluate our model using xsim and
265   xsim++ on the FLORES200 dataset, covering the 81 languages supported by our model (listed
266   in Appendix C). For fairer comparison, we also report results for the 72 languages supported by
267   LaBSE, SONAR, and MEXMA (”o-xsim”), and separately for the 34 languages common to DAP
268   and the other models (”d-xsim”). Results per language are available in Appendix E .

417   5.4   M ASKING RATIO
418
      NLP models typically use masking percentages around 15%, whereas vision papers have explored
419
      much higher masking ratios, ranging from 40% in BEiT (Bao et al., 2022) to as high as 90% in MAE
420
      (He et al., 2022) and V-JEPA (Bardes et al., 2024), usually aligning augmentations. For text, there is
421   less redundancy and the representations are more information-dense. In our case, we are aligning the
422   same sentence in several languages, which can be viewed as augmentations of a pivot sentence, i.e.
423   the sentence in English. We need to know how much we can mask, to make the unmasking task hard,
424   but to not deteriorate the performance of our encoder. More information is provided in Appendix B.
425   The range 30%-60% seems to be the best operating region. We selected 40% for all experiments
426   conducted in this paper, since it had the best balance between alignment and classification.

      5.6   S ENTENCE VECTOR ANALYSIS
503
504
      Sentence representations are created by combining token representations in various ways (average
505   or CLS/attention pooling). The previous section examined properties encoded in tokens, and this
506   section explores how these representations are combined to form the sentence embedding.
507
508
      For SONAR, the attention weight distribution is uniform, given that SONAR averages the tokens to
      create their sentence representation. MEXMA and LaBSE both use a CLS token to perform pooling
509
      over the tokens in the sentence.
510
511   MEXMA’s and LaBSE’s attention distribution are rather different, with LaBSE having a more uni-
512   form attention distribution across its tokens, and MEXMA having a more skewed representation.
513   We verify this by computing the average entropy of the attention probabilities in the last layer given
514
      by the CLS token, for both models on the test set of the FLORES200, in the languages supported
      by both LaBSE and MEXMA. LaBSE gets an entropy of ≈ 3.4, while MEXMA gets an entropy
515
      of ≈ 2.5. The entropy values obtained for LaBSE and MEXMA are difficult to interpret in abso-
516
      lute terms, but the relative difference between them is informative. Specifically, LaBSE exhibits a
517   higher entropy compared to MEXMA, suggesting that it has a more uniform distribution of attention
518   probabilities. We provide examples of the distributions in Appendix G.
519
520
      We perform an additional analysis, where we push the uniformity of the sentence representation to
      the extreme, by using the average of tokens as our sentence representation. By doing this for both
521
      MEXMA and LaBSE, we aim to understand the importance/impact of the attention distribution for
522
      each model. The results are provided in Table 10. The deltas are computed in terms of relative
523   change from the uniform to the CLS representation. We can see that for all tasks, MEXMA has a
524   larger change in performance compared to LaBSE, showing that indeed since our representations are
525   more skewed, we suffer more from an increase in uniformity of the distribution. For those tasks, it is
526   noticeable that MEXMA having a uniform distribution, will lose its ability to focus on the important
527   tokens, decreasing its results. For LaBSE the decrease is not as accentuated, since it was already not
528   focusing as much on the important tokens with its more uniform CLS pooling.

      6     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      We introduced MEXMA, a novel multilingual alignment technique that leverages both token-level
533
      and sentence-level objectives. We show that integrating token-level objectives into the training of
534   cross-lingual sentence encoders (CLSE) greatly improves sentence representation quality, outper-
535   forming current state-of-the-art pre-trained CLSE in bitext mining and other downstream tasks. We
536   additionally validate these improvements via ablations. Notably, MEXMA also achieves strong to-
537   ken alignment across languages and effectively encodes meaningful information within each token.
538   Since the sentence representation is built from these tokens, as we analysed, this leads to better sen-
539   tence representations. Looking ahead, we plan to explore MEXMA’s scalability to more languages,
      and potentially modalities.

      A     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
865
866
      A.1   E NCODER BACKBONE
867
868   The available implementation of XLM-RoBERTa in HuggingFace employs an inefficient attention
869   mechanism, which we have modified to incorporate the memory-efficient attention from xFormers
870   (Lefaudeux et al., 2022). This modification was necessary due to the random batching process used
871   in our training, which results in a significant amount of padding and increased computational cost.
872   To address this issue and eliminate padding, we have employed the BlockDiagonalMask 2 , which
873
      through custom CUDA kernels, avoids computations in padding altogether. With this change we are
      able to increase our batch size in each GPU by a factor of ≈ 8.
874
875
876   A.2   U NMASKING HEAD
877
      For the unmasking head, we use 6 transformer layers, also leveraging the memory-efficient attention.
878
879
      A.3   C OMPUTE AND TRAINING LENGTH
880
881   Our models were trained on a single node of 8 A100 GPUs. Each GPU had a batch size of 150,
882   totalling 1,200 batch size across all GPUs. We accumulated two gradients, making our effective
883   batch size 2,400. We trained our models for 300k steps.
884
885   A.4   L OSSES
886
887   Our models were trained with α = 1, β = 12 and γ = 0.01
                                                           2 .
888
889   A.5   T RAINING PARAMETERS
890
891
      We utilize the AdamW optimizer for our training process. The learning rate is linearly increased
      from 1e-5 for the 300k steps. To optimize memory usage, we employ mixed precision training,
892
      where the model is stored in float32, while most computations are performed in float16. The max-
893
      imum sequence length for our input data is set to 200 tokens. Finally, we apply a masking ratio of
894   40% to the input data.

      In Table 11, we ablate the different components of our architecture described in Section 3. We briefly
935
      explain each entry in the table. Model 1 has only two encoder instances, one for each language,
936   where one of the inputs is masked, and the other is left clean. The token unmasking is performed
937   with the clean sentence representation as context. The languages are randomly swapped at every new
938   sample, to eliminate potential biases. The gradients from the unmasking task are only propagated
939   back to the encoder via the sentence representation, and there is no gradient propagation from the
940   individual tokens back to the encoder. There is also neither alignment nor koleo losses. Model 2
941   adds two additional encoder instances, totalling four instances, two for each language, where now
942   each language has its clean and masked input. This allows to unmask language A with language B,
943   and vice-versa, and will also allow (once added) to align two clean sentence representations. Model
944    3 adds the alignment loss, but it is performed between the masked sentence representation of lan-
945   guage A and the clean sentence representation of language B, to better emphasize the advantages of
946
      having a symmetrical architecture with an alignment loss between two clean representations. Model
947
       4 then changes the alignment loss to be performed between the two clean sentence representations
948
      of each language. In model 5 we allow the gradients from the unmasking to be propagated to the
949
      encoder via each individual token, as well as its sentence representation. Finally, model 6 adds the
      KoLeo loss.
950
951   The results indicate that each component always enhances performance on at least two out of the
952   three tasks. Notably, the alignment loss, 3 - 4 , and token-level gradients, 5 , emerge as the most
953   critical components. More precisely, the alignment loss yields substantial improvements on two
954   tasks while also resulting in a notable decline in performance on another task. In contrast, the
955   token-level gradients consistently provide significant performance gains across all three tasks.

984    B.3   M ASKING RATIO
985
986    Classical NLP masked encoders like BERT use a small masking percentage, usually ≈ 15%, without
987    aligning any augmentations. Recent vision approaches use much higher masking percentages. BEiT
988    (Bao et al., 2022) was one of the first masked image modelling (MIM) approaches, in a BERT-style
989
       training, and masked 40%. MAE (He et al., 2022) is another BERT-like model for images, and masks
       75%, but shows that even masking 80% or 90% still achieves good results. DINO v2 (Oquab et al.,
990
       2024) and I-BOT (Zhou et al., 2022) mask between 10%-50% in a block-wise masking scenario,
991
       aligning augmentations. I-BOT can use 65%-75% masking ratio, if randomly masking (instead of
992    block-wise masking). For videos, V-JEPA (Bardes et al., 2024) masks with a very high percentage
993    of 90%. Recent textual approaches, namely RetroMAE experiment with masking percentages of up
994    to 50∼70%, but this task will not update the actual encoder.
995
       For MEXMA, since these masking gradients are updating our encoder, we need to strive for a bal-
996
       ance where unmasking is hard, and cannot be done by the encoder and head, but also not too much
997
       that will deteriorate the representations of the encoder. Table 13 shows the results we obtained for
998    the different masking ratios.

       In this section, we provide some examples of MEXMA and LaBSE’s attention probabilities given by
1731   the CLS token to the word tokens. The examples are provided in Figures 7, 8, 9 and 10. Across all
1732   figures, it is possible to see that LaBSE tends to be more uniform across all tokens, while MEXMA
1733   tends to focus more attention on a smaller subset of the tokens. All examples are taken from the
1734   FLORES200 test set with the xsim++ extension, where some words in the original sentences are
1735   replaced, and the models have to be able to still match the correct translation, and not a sentence
1736   with a small change. From Figure 7 to Figure 8 ”nineteen” is replaced with ”twenty nine”. From
1737   Figure 9 to Figure 10 the word ”white” is replaced with ”black”.
1738   Figure 7 shows the attention placed by MEXMA and LaBSE on the same sentence in English and
1739   Portuguese. It is possible to see that MEXMA in Portuguese places most of the attention in two
1740   tokens, ”governador” and ”19”, where the token in ”19” is very relevant here since it is the one
1741   needed to distinguish the examples in xsim++. LaBSE seems to have many tokens with a lot of
1742   attention, and does not have ”19” as one of the tokens with the most attention.
1743   In Figure 8, we have the English example with nineteen (as previously shown in Figure 7) compared
1744   to the same sentence with nineteen replaced by twenty-nine. Interestingly, LaBSE places more
1745   attention on the ”##teen” token than the ”nine” token, but similar attention to the ”twenty”, ”-” and
1746   ”nine” tokens. MEXMA places similar attention in all nineteen tokens, and in twenty nine it places
1747   a small amount of attention on the irrelevant ”-”, with a higher degree of attention in ”nine” and a
1748   smaller amount of attention in ”twenty”. MEXMA also seems to do a good job ignoring irrelevant
1749   tokens like ”of”, while LaBSE places a lot of attention in it.
1750   Figure 9 has the same sentence in English and Portuguese, where, in xsim++ the models need to be
1751   able to match the color ”white” instead of other colors. It is possible to see that, for LaBSE, white
1752   is not one of the most relevant tokens in English, but for MEXMA it is, along with ”television”.
1753   In Portuguese the behavior is similar, the token ”bran” in ”esbranquiçada” has a large degree of
1754   attention from MEXMA, while for LaBSE is it not a token with a lot of attention, and ”çada” which
1755   is a token that does not convey the idea of white, is the one with the most attention out of the 4 tokens
1756   of the word, for LaBSE. In Portuguese it is also noticeable that MEXMA gives a small amount of
1757
       attention to most of the less relevant tokens, while LaBSE seems to have a lot more tokens with a
       high degree of attention.
1758
1759   Figure 10 shows the same English sentence as Figure 9, with the word white replaced with the word
1760   black. Interestingly, MEXMA’s attention remains the same with black and white, while for LaBSE
1761   the token ”black” seems to get less attention than the token ”white”. The remaining tokens get
1762   similar attention in both models.
1763   Additionally, Figure 11, provides a comparison for MEXMA and LaBSE with the probabilities of
1764   all heads, and all tokens, using BertViz (Vig, 2019). It is possible to see that MEXMA places a lot
1765   of attention on the EOS token, </s>, which is used as an attention dump, i.e. an irrelevant token
1766   that receives a very large attention probability, a common phenomena in transformers, as explored in
1767   Xiao et al. (2024); Darcet et al. (2024); Sun et al. (2024). This happens frequently with MEXMA. It
1768   is, again, possible to see the difference in uniformity for MEXMA and LaBSE, with LaBSE having
1769
       a more uniform attention in the figure. If we remove the BOS and EOS tokens from the entropy
       computation, we now get an entropy of ≈ 3.5 and ≈ 3 for LaBSE and MEXMA, respectively.
1770
       MEXMA’s entropy increases, while LaBSE stays mostly similar, which shows that MEXMA indeed
1771
       frequently uses the EOS token as a dump. However, MEXMA still has a lower entropy and a more
1772   skewed distribution over its word tokens, with or without BOS and EOS, as shown by the lower
1773   entropy and the Figures 7-10.
