009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The prevailing mindset is that a single decision tree underperforms random forests
012            in testing accuracy, despite its advantages in interpretability and lightweight struc-
013            ture. This study challenges such a mindset by significantly improving the testing
014            accuracy of an oblique regression tree through our gradient-based entire tree opti-
015            mization framework, making its performance comparable to random forests. Our
016            approach reformulates tree training as a differentiable unconstrained optimiza-
017            tion task, employing a scaled sigmoid approximation strategy. To ameliorate nu-
018
               merical instability, we propose an algorithmic scheme that solves a sequence of
               increasingly accurate approximations. Additionally, a subtree polish strategy is
019
               implemented to reduce approximation errors accumulated across the tree. Exten-
020
               sive experiments on 16 datasets demonstrate that our optimized tree outperforms
021            random forests by an average of 2.03% improvements in testing accuracy.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   The single decision tree attracts significant attention in machine learning primarily due to its inherent
027   interpretability. Its transparent “IF-THEN” decision rules make it highly useful for tasks that require
028   clear decision-making logic behind predictions. However, its adoption is often limited by lower test-
029   ing accuracy, particularly when compared to tree ensemble methods like random forests Breiman
030   (2001). Random forests, built from multiple decision trees, are widely recognized for their superior
031   testing performance over single tree models (Tan & Dowe, 2006), and are considered among the best
032   models for accuracy (Fernández-Delgado et al., 2014; Grinsztajn et al., 2022). However, a random
033
      forest, which typically consists of hundreds of decision trees, diminishes—or even eliminates—the
      interpretability that a single decision tree provides. This trade-off between interpretability and ac-
034
      curacy has become widely accepted, fostering a common mindset that random forests outperforms
035
      single decision trees in testing accuracy, though at the expense of interpretability.
036
037   Such a mindset forces resorting to random forests when high test accuracy is essential, even in cases
038   where a lightweight structure and interpretability are also demanded. For instance, in embedded
039
      systems with limited hardware resources and power budgets, a lightweight algorithm like decision
      tree is ideally preferable due to its fewer parameters and less energy consumption (Narayanan et al.,
040
      2007; Alcolea & Resano, 2021); yet, in practice, the subpar performance of a single tree often
041
      compels a shift toward tree ensembles, such as random forest (Elsts & McConville, 2021; Van Essen
042   et al., 2012). This shift introduces two major issues: first, it significantly aggregates computational
043   costs and memory consumption due to more parameters from multiple trees. Second, it sacrifices
044   the interpretability, which is crucial in certain decision-support scenarios, such as piece-wise control
045   law in explicit model predictive control (Bemporad et al., 2002) and threshold-based well control
046   optimization in subsurface energy management (Kuk et al., 2022). These two concerns can be
047   effectively addressed by a single decision tree if it could match the accuracy of random forests.
048   Aiming at a single tree with higher accuracy and fewer parameters, the oblique decision tree, a pivot
049   extension of the classic orthogonal decision tree, holds great potential. Oblique decision trees use
050   linear combination of features to create hyperplane splits. When the underlying data distribution fol-
051   lows hyperplane boundaries, oblique decision trees tend to simply tree structures, generating smaller
052   trees with higher accuracy (Costa & Pedreira, 2023). Nevertheless, inducing oblique decision trees
053   presents substantial computational challenges, owing to the innumerable linear combinations of fea-
      tures at each node (Zhu et al., 2020). Earlier works mainly focus on finding the optimal feature

054
      combinations at an individual node using greedy top-down algorithms, such as CART-LC (Breiman
055   et al., 1984) and OC1 (Murthy et al., 1994). Besides, alternative methods rely on greedy orthogonal
056   decision trees CART to induce oblique trees by rotating the feature space, exemplified by HHCART
057   (Wickramarachchi et al., 2015) and RandCART (Blaser & Fryzlewicz, 2016). Despite their advance-
058   ments, such greedy methods that focus on optimal splits at current nodes, might lead to suboptimal
059   solutions due to the weaker splits at subsequent child nodes. Considering the optimization of splits
060   at all nodes, Bertsimas & Dunn (2017) presented optimal decision tree method to formulate tree
061   training as a mixed-integer programming (MIP) problem. However, the practical application of
062   MIP-based methods often face challenges in scalability and computational efficiency, especially in
063
      optimal oblique decision trees where the search space is expanded. Recent efforts in optimal oblique
      trees (Boutilier et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2020) have been confined to classification tasks with a lim-
064
      ited number of categorical prediction values. In contrast, addressing regression tasks with an infinite
065
      number of possible prediction values remains an extremely challenging task. In response to this
066   limitation, the originators of MIP-based work further proposed an alternative local search method
067   ORT-LS (Dunn, 2018) for tasks that are unsolvable by MIP. However, ORT-LS still suffers from
068   high computational costs and suboptimal accuracy, as observed in our comparative studies.
069
      In this work, we reformulate the training of an entire tree as an unconstrained optimization task,
070
      offering significant solvability advantages over MIP reformulations. This reformulation makes it
071
      easily solvable through exiting powerful frameworks of gradient-based optimization. Given the
072   non-differentiability of indicator functions in hard splits, two intuitive solutions has been used in
073   recent literatures: treating the gradient of those indicators as one via straight-through estimators
074   (Karthikeyan et al., 2022; Marton et al., 2023) and approximating indicators with sigmoid functions
075   (Wan et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2018; Frosst & Hinton, 2017). However, straight-through estimators
076   may neglect crucial gradient information, resulting in suboptimal outcomes, as observed in both
077   their work (Marton et al., 2023) and our experiments. In light of those works with sigmoid approx-
078   imation, two major concerns arise. Firstly, previous efforts predominantly focused on constructing
079   “soft” decision trees (İrsoy et al., 2012), characterized by soft splits and probabilistic predictions.
080
      Nonetheless, there do exist scenarios where a hard-split tree with deterministic predictions is not
      only appropriate but also imperative. Further, the probabilistic soft splits significantly deviates from
081
      the interpretable True-False, IF-THEN decision logic. Secondly, the simple use of sigmoid functions
082
      leaves a considerable gap from indicator functions, necessitating a delicate balance between approx-
083   imation accuracy and numerical solvability by scaling the sigmoid function (Hehn & Hamprecht,
084   2017). However, identifying the optimal scale factor also remains a challenge. More importantly,
085   less attention has been paid to the approximation error that can accumulate across the entire tree, par-
086   ticularly in deep trees with numerous nodes. These issues substantially degrade the testing accuracy
087   of a gradient-based tree, far lagging behind the performance of random forests.
088
      Our contributions: Firstly, we propose a strategy of iterative scaled sigmoid approximation to
089   narrow the gap between the original indicator function and its differentiable approximation. This
090   strategy uses the solution from an optimization task with a smaller scale factor to effectively warm-
091   starts optimization with a larger scale factor. By starting with a smaller, smoother scale factor and
092   gradually increasing it, this strategy enhance the approximation degree, while mitigating numerical
093   instability typically associated with larger scale factors. Secondly, unlike soft trees with probabilis-
094   tic predictions, we remain the hard-split decisions and deterministic predictions, only using soft
095   approximation for gradient computations. Thirdly, to address severe approximation errors accumu-
096   lated across each split in the entire tree, we propose a subtree polish strategy to further improve
097
      the training optimality. Finally, we provide an extensible Gradient-based Entire Tree optimization
      framework for inducing a tree with both constant predictions (termed as GET) and linear predictions
098
      (termed as GET-Linear), easily implemented in existing deep learning frameworks, as available
099
      in https://github.com/anonymweblinks/GET.
100
101   Performance: Experiments show that our method can produce a tree with testing accuracy compa-
102   rable to, or even exceeding, that of random forests, challenging the prevailing mindset.
103   • Empirically and statistically, our oblique tree GET outperforms compared decision tree methods in
104     test accuracy. Notably, it outperforms CART by 7.59% and the state-of-the-art ORT-LS by 3.76%.

      In this section, we explore oblique regression trees from an optimization perspective by formulating
      tree training as an optimization problem. For ease of understanding, we primarily follow the notation
115
      for optimal decision trees as used in the original work of Bertsimas & Dunn (2017).
116
117   Consider a dataset comprising n samples denoted as {xi , yi }ni=1 with input vectors xi ∈ [0, 1]p
118   and true output values yi ∈ [0, 1]. A binary tree of depth D comprises T = 2D+1 − 1 nodes,
119   where each node is indexed by t ∈ T = {1, · · · , T } in a breadth-first order. The nodes can be
120   categorized into two types: branch nodes, which execute branching tests and are denoted by indices
121
      t ∈ TB = {1, · · · , ⌊T /2⌋}, and leaf nodes denoted by t ∈ TL = {⌊T /2⌋ + 1, · · · , T }, responsible
      for providing regression predictions. Each branch node comprises a split weight at ∈ Rp and a split
122
      threshold bt ∈ R to conduct a branching test (aTt xi ≤ bt ) for the samples allocated to that particular
123
      branch node. If a sample xi passes the branching test (aTt xi ≤ bt ), it is directed to the left child
124   node at index 2t; otherwise, to the right child node at index 2t + 1. Each leaf node comprise the
125   parameters of kt ∈ Rp and ht ∈ R to provide a prediction value for current leaf. The training of
126   oblique regression trees involves solving the following optimization problem:

197   additional errors that may arise from the trained leaf values. Ideally, with a high approximation
198   accuracy and optimal optimization, the trained leaf values from soft approximations should closely
199   match those calculated by hard splits. However, achieving this level of optimality is challenging due
200   to potential errors in soft approximation. This concern justifies the use of hard splits in the inference
201   phase, which are more likely to yield more accurate final predictions.

      4.1   I TERATIVE S CALED S IGMOID A PPROXIMATION
210
211   In response to early-mentioned concerns regarding an optimal α, we propose a strategy of iterative
212   scaled sigmoid approximation to enhance the approximation accuracy to indicator functions. The
213   key challenge of lies in the selection of α. A larger α may destabilize optimization process, whereas
214   a smaller α tends to be easier to solve for gradient-based optimization. Our strategy leverage this
215   insight by using a solution from an optimization task with a smaller scale factor to effectively warm-
      starts optimization with a larger scale factor. By starting with a smaller scale factor and gradually


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      increasing it, this strategy enhance the approximation accuracy, while mitigating numerical instabil-
217   ity typically associated with larger scale factors.
218
219   Specifically, the procedure begins by randomly sampling a set of scale factors within a predeter-
220
      mined range, ensuring a broad exploration of possible α values. These sampled scale factors, de-
      noted as {α1 , · · · , αn }, are then applied in ascending order, from small to large. We initiate the
221
      optimization with the smallest sampled scale factor to generate the initial optimized tree candidate.
222
      This candidate then serves as the starting point for the subsequent optimization task with a slightly
223   larger α. This iterative process is repeated until all sampled scale factors have been utilized. Detailed
224   implementation steps are integrated within our systematic optimization framework, Algorithm 1.

227   4.2    G RADIENT- BASED E NTIRE T REE O PTIMIZATION F RAMEWORK
228
229   Unlike greedy methods that optimize each node sequentially, our approach concurrently optimizes
230
      the entire tree, encompassing tree split parameters A and b at all branch nondes, and leaf prediction
      parameters K and h at all leaf nodes. Our entire tree optimization, outlined in Algorithm 1, begins
231
      at multiple random initialization (Line 4 - 6). This multiple-initialization serves two purposes: First,
232
      multi-start increases the chance of finding the optimal solution of the unconstrained reformulation.
233   Second, in Section 4.1, the iterative scaled sigmoid approximation involves randomly sampling
234   scale factors for each iteration. These multiple starts enable sampling diverse scale factors in a
235   wider range, thereby enhancing the robustness and approximation accuracy. For each start, the
236   optimization with iterative scaled sigmoid approximation is implemented to produce an optimized
237   tree candidate (Line 7 - 17). Importantly, our method deterministically calculates the leaf values
238   based on hard-splits (Line 12). The specific deterministic calculations for K and h are given in
239   Appendix E. Finally, the optimal tree is determined by comparing each candidate from multiple
240   starts (Line 14 - 15). This optimization framework is readily implementable using existing powerful
241
      tools that embed gradient-based optimizers, such as PyTorch and TensorFlow.
242   The framework is applicable to decision trees with both constant and linear predictions. The only
243   minor difference is that for constant predictions, the parameters K remain zero without gradients.
244   For clarity, we term our approach Gradient-based Entire Tree optimization as GET when applied to
245   trees with constant predictions; otherwise, termed as GET-Linear for trees with linear predictions.

290   Subtree Polish Strategy to improve training optimality: In Algorithm 1, Our method is designed
291

293

299

300   next subtree, rooted at next branch node. This iterative process continues until the subtree rooted at
301   the last branch node is polished. The final best tree is returned after polishing all branch nodes. The

      5.1   T ESTING ACCURACY C OMPARISON AGAINST R ANDOM F ORESTS
325
326   For testing accuracy comparison, we conduct depth tuning by cross validation to determine the op-
327   timal depth across depths from 1 to 12 for our methods. For a fair comparison, comprehensive
328   hyperparameter tuning is also performed for RF. Specifically, the number of trees in a forest is a
329   critical parameter. It is well-recognized that testing performance improves with an increase in the
330   number of trees; however, the marginal gains become less pronounced as additional trees are added
331   (Probst & Boulesteix, 2018; Oshiro et al., 2012). Accordingly, the number of trees for RF is tuned
332
      across a set of {50, 100, 200, 300, 400, 500}. Moreover, the maximum tree depth for RF is tuned
      over a broader range, from 1 to 50, to potentially capture optimal depth settings, given that RF em-
333
      pirically benefits from overly-deeper trees for enhanced testing accuracy. Other hyperparameters for
334
      RF, including the number of features per split and the number of samples per tree, are maintained at
335   default settings. These parameters have been shown to balance the bias-variance trade-off, typically
336   yielding robust performance with default values (Probst & Boulesteix, 2018).
337
338

      16 datasets. In contrast, GET-Linear significantly outperforms RF by 2.03%, as detailed in Ta-
339
      ble 1. This superiority is further supported by the Friedman Rank, where GET-Linear ranking
340
      the highest, followed by RF and GET. Among 16 datasets, GET-Linear outperforms RF in 8

477
478   Our extensive experiments are conducted on datasets containing less than 20,000 samples, without
479   exploring large-scale datasets. While our approach exhibits great scalability compared to the state-

481   methods CART and RF. This limitation is generally acceptable for a single tree model, particularly
482
      because it significantly improves accuracy, aligning with our primary focus on testing accuracy,
      interpretability, and prediction time. However, the longer training time could potentially limit the
483
      application of our method in scenarios involving large-scale datasets.

495   6   C ONCLUSION
496
497   In conclusion, our development of the gradient-based entire tree optimization method, is not neces-
498   sarily to bring the best regression tree, but rather to explore the potential of a single decision tree
499   in achieving comparable testing accuracy to random forests. This makes a single tree more prefer-
500   able for scenarios where a lightweight structure and interpretability are valued alongside predictive
501   performance. Our approach reformulates decision tree training as a differentiable unconstrained
502   optimization task, incorporating an iterative scaled sigmoid approximation. The tree optimization
503   capability is further enhanced by a subtree polish strategy. Extensive experiments show that our
504
      optimized tree not only achieves but also statistically confirms its testing accuracy comparable to
      random forest, challenging the mindset that a single decision tree typically underperforms random
505
      forests in testing performance.

      The scale factor α significantly influence the approximation degree to indicator function and the
725
      behavior of its gradient in optimization. A larger α leads to a better approximation than sigmoid
726
      function, achieving closer proximity to an indicator function as α approaches infinity. Nonetheless,
727   a larger α also results in a more unstable gradient, which may adversely affect the optimization
728   process. Specifically, a larger α may cause the gradient to be too small or even zero, leading to the
729   stagnation of gradient descent.
730
      The gradient of scaled sigmoid function, denoted as S(x) = (1 + e−αx )−1 is given by ∂S        ∂x =
731
      αS(x)(1 − S(x)). When α is larger, either S(x) or 1 − S(x) tend towards zero for value of x away
732
      from origin, potentially causing the gradient to approach zero. Consequently, the α represents a crit-
733   ical balance between achieving high approximation degree and maintaining stability in optimization
734   processes.
735
736
      To show how the scale factor α impacts the training optimality of the unconstrained optimization
      problem (our method without using the strategy of iterative scaled sigmoid approximation), we
737
      conduct the comparison experiments across different α values at different depths. The findings,

      summarized in Table 5, reveal the relationship between α and training performance, as measured by
739   the average training accuracy R2 . Notably, we observe that the training accuracy at the extremes
740   of α = 1 (sigmoid function) and α = 1000 are inferior compared to intermediate α values. This
741   observation underscores two critical insights: firstly, relying solely on the sigmoid function (α = 1)
742   yields suboptimal optimization results; secondly, the high α value may not necessarily lead to better
743   optimality.
