011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training (CLIP) shows promise in medical im-
014            age analysis but requires substantial data and computational resources. Due to
015            these restrictions, existing CLIP applications in medical imaging focus mainly
016            on modalities like chest X-rays that have abundant image-report data available,
017
               leaving many other important modalities under-explored. Here, we propose one of
               the first adaptations of the full CLIP model to mammography, which presents sig-
018
               nificant challenges due to labeled data scarcity, high-resolution images with small
019
               regions of interest, and data imbalance. We first develop a specialized supervision
020            framework for mammography that leverages its multi-view nature. Furthermore,
021            we design a symmetric local alignment module to better focus on detailed features
022            in high-resolution images. Lastly, we incorporate a parameter-efficient fine-tuning
023            approach for large language models pre-trained with medical knowledge to address
024            data limitations. Our multi-view and multi-scale alignment (MaMA) method out-
025            performs state-of-the-art baselines for three different tasks on two large real-world
026            mammography datasets, EMBED and RSNA-Mammo, with only 52% model size
027            compared with the largest baseline. The code is attached in the supplement file and
028
               will be released on GitHub upon acceptance.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Contrastive learning (Chen et al., 2020; He et al., 2019; Grill et al., 2020) has become one of the
034   most popular self-supervised representation learning paradigms due to its intuitive concept and
035   robust performance. Contrastive learning removes the reliance on a supervised signal by optimizing
036   the semantic distance for similar pairs in the representation space in a contrastive manner. More
037   recently, the introduction of natural language signals to contrastive learning (Radford et al., 2021) has
038   given rise to modern visual-language models (Li et al., 2022; 2023; Liu et al., 2024a). Contrastive
039
      Language-Image Pre-training (CLIP) (Radford et al., 2021) has also been widely applied in the
      medical imaging domain (Wang et al., 2022b; Huang et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022a; Zhang et al.,
040
      2022; Wu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Eslami et al., 2023) and shows promising improvement
041
      in medical image understanding when large-scale medical imaging datasets are available (Johnson
042   et al., 2019; Irvin et al., 2019; Eslami et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023). However, the CLIP model in
043   the natural image domain usually demands more than hundreds of millions of image-text pairs to be
044   properly trained (Radford et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2023a; 2024; 2023b), which is almost impossible
045   in the medical domain due to privacy and security concerns. Existing medical CLIP methods either
046   build general-purpose CLIP models with multiple anatomical sites and modalities from public online
047   databases (Eslami et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023) or focus on imaging modalities with large-scale
048   (less than a million) datasets, e.g., chest X-ray or pathology images (Zhang et al., 2022; Huang et al.,
049   2021; Wang et al., 2022a; Wu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022b; Zhou et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023;
050
      Wan et al., 2024; Lai et al., 2023). This means other imaging modalities, such as mammography,
      have yet to fully benefit from such visual-language pre-trained models.
051
052   Mammography is a critical medical imaging modality for breast cancer screening and diagnosis,
053   as breast cancer is one of the most commonly diagnosed cancers globally and a leading cause of
      cancer-related mortality in women (Sung et al., 2021). While visual-language pre-training (VLP) has

069   the potential to improve mammography interpretation, there are two major obstacles: 1) Limited data
070   and annotation: Recent work has introduced a large-scale mammography image and tabular dataset
071   of more than 110,000 patients, i.e., EMBED (Jeong et al., 2023), but no corresponding clinical reports
072
      are available. 2) Nature of mammography: Different from the single view natural image or chest X-
      ray, each mammography study usually contains four high-resolution (∼2,000-by-2,000 pixels) views
073
      of the same patient: left and right side, each with craniocaudal (CC) and mediolateral oblique (MLO)
074
      views. Such multi-view mammography has the critical properties of bilateral asymmetry (Donnelly
075   et al., 2024) and ipsilateral correspondence (Liu et al., 2021). Bilateral asymmetry means images
076   from different sides of the same patient can contain different information, e.g., density, calcification,
077   and mass findings. Ipsilateral correspondence means different views of the same side share similar
078   information from different viewpoints. Clinicians consider both properties and all four images at once
079   as a cross reference when reading a study. Meanwhile, lesions of interest are often relatively small
080   compared with high-resolution mammograms, which further challenges a model’s ability to focus on
081   local details. This pixel-level imbalance compounds the problem of image-level imbalance, in which
082   the vast majority of mammograms will not contain cancer. While recent works (Chen et al., 2024;
083
      Ghosh et al., 2024) attempt to address these issues by leveraging VLP, they either simply fine-tune
      pre-trained CLIP with a small amount of data (Chen et al., 2024) or apply contrastive language-image
084
      pre-training with hand-crafted prompt (Ghosh et al., 2024), rather than capitalizing on mammography
085
      domain information.
086
087   To address these challenges, we propose a novel Multi-view and Multi-scale Alignment i.e., MaMA,
088   contrastive language-image pre-training framework that exploits the multi-view property of mammog-
089
      raphy and aligns multi-scale features simultaneously. Our work offers the following contributions:
090            • Multi-view Design: We extend the CLIP-style method to leverage the unique multi-view
091              nature of mammography images, introducing 1) an inter-study image-to-image contrastive
092              loss, and 2) symmetric image-text loss to resolve contradictions during pre-training.
093
               • Symmetric Local Alignment (SLA): Designed for the relatively small ROIs in mammog-
094              raphy, the SLA module improves model understanding of local features without needing
095              dense annotation.
096
               • PEFT-LLM Text Encoder: Replacing the traditional BERT encoder with PEFT-LLM
097              improves the understanding of the text while addressing data scarcity. Our evaluation of
098              3 SOTA LLMs (Bolton et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023) creates a
099              benchmark for future work.
100
               • Other Contributions: We propose two important strategies specifically for mammography
101              VLP: 1) a template-based method to generate structured free-text captions from tabular data
102              that mimics realistic clinical report format and 2) meta-information masking augmentation
103              to mitigate zero-shot performance loss when training with complex captions.
104
105   We validate our method on two large-scale mammography datasets, EMBED (Jeong et al., 2023) and
106   RNSA-Mammo ((Carr & et.al., 2022)), with multiple settings compared with state-of-the-art medical
107
      CLIP methods. The proposed method surpasses all the baselines with a considerable gap with only
      52% model size, showing promise on multiple mammography-related tasks.

      Medical Visual-Language Pre-training Existing medical VLP methods can be divided into
      two types depending on the training data. The first type is the general-purpose medical CLIP
112
      model trained with a large-scale medical-image dataset with multiple anatomical sites and imaging
113
      modalities derived from PubMed (Eslami et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023). This approach mainly
114   focuses on scaling dataset size while using a vanilla CLIP design (Radford et al., 2021). These
115   models show promising generalization ability on multiple sites but are often suboptimal compared
116   with modality-specific models due to the lack of a specific design for the individual image modality.
117   The other type of VLP models mainly focuses on chest X-ray (Zhang et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2021;
118   Wang et al., 2022a; Wu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022b; Zhou et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Wan
119   et al., 2024) due to the availability of large datasets, trained on either MIMIC-CXR (Johnson et al.,
120   2019) or CheXpert (Irvin et al., 2019) datasets. While these methods show impressive performance
121   on chest-specific tasks, they are specially designed for single-view images like regular CLIP (Radford
122
      et al., 2021). Some of the methods further require full clinical reports paired with the image (Wang
      et al., 2022a; Wan et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2023), which makes them harder to adopt. Recently, Chen
123
      et al. (2024) proposed a first attempt to introduce CLIP to mammography. It fine-tunes a pre-trained
124
      CLIP model with an added multi-view image aggregation module to a zero-shot classification task.
125   However, the method does not perform contrastive pre-training, ignores pixel-level data imbalance,
126   and cannot correlate the medical report with fine-grained ROIs. Furthermore, they only fine-tuned
127   a pre-trained CLIP model with a few thousand private cases. While Ghosh et al. (2024) proposed
128   a Mammography CLIP-style pre-training method called Mammo-CLIP, it ignored the multi-scale
129   nature of the mammograms and was trained and evaluated on a much smaller dataset with only
130   20, 000 images. This limits the generalizability of the method and may lead to a greater potential
131   domain shift in the application.
132
133
      Multi-view Contrastive Learning To obtain a more robust self-supervised contrastive learning
134   framework, methods like SLIP (Mu et al., 2022) (Fig. 1 (b)) and DeCLIP(Li et al., 2021a) exploit
135   image-image contrastive learning along with image-text contrastive learning simultaneously. Such
136   ideas have been applied to 3D shape recognition (Delitzas et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023) by exploiting
137   the nature of 3D shapes from different viewpoints and also to the action recognition task in the real
138   world (Shah et al., 2023). These methods all exploit the multi-view nature of the specific image
139   modality, where images of the same object from different viewpoints share the same semantic
140   meaning while having different appearances. Multi-view contrastive learning has also been utilized in
141   mammography (Li et al., 2021b; Du et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2022), where the multi-view consistency
142
      is leveraged to actively learn high-level shared information within the multi-view mammography.
      However, to the best of our knowledge, none of the existing works combine multi-view mammography
143
      contrastive learning with CLIP to fully utilize the supervising signal from the multimodal data.
144
145
146   Unsupervised Local Contrastive Learning Correlating a dense visual representation with fine-
147   grained semantic meaning is not only helpful for image understanding but vital to tasks like semantic
148   segmentation. Recent work address this problem in the challenging unsupervised scenario (Huang
149   et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022a; Zheng et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
150   2024; Shah et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b). Zhang et al. (2024) rely on a pre-trained object detector or
151
      segmentation model to extract the region of interest. Other methods either aggregate dense similarity
      scores and conduct image-level contrastive learning (Zheng et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
152
      2024b), which may ignore too much visual information during training, or exhaustively conduct
153
      token-level language-image matching and optimize patch-level contrastive loss (Huang et al., 2021;
154   Wang et al., 2022a; Shah et al., 2023), with the cost of additional computation.

184   3.1      S TRUCTURED R EPORT C ONSTRUCTION
185
186   Different from chest X-ray datasets that provide paired images with corresponding clinical reports,
187   e.g., MIMIC-CXR (Johnson et al., 2019), large-scale mammography datasets with the full report
188
      available are rare. Rather, existing datasets in this domain (Jeong et al., 2023; Carr & et.al., 2022;
      Nguyen et al., 2021) mainly provide a tabular structure annotation including both the anonymized
189
      meta information as well as the clinical findings, e.g., breast density type, calcification findings,
190
      tumor description, and Breast Imaging Reporting and Data System (BI-RADS) assessment category
191   (Sickles et al., 2013). Clinical findings serve as cross-validation evidence for the final diagnosis.
192   Using a CLIP-style (Zhao et al., 2023) caption with only the simple class label for cancer will result
193   in a highly simplified caption and limit the model’s understanding of the image due to missing details.
194
      We propose a template-based caption construction method following the standard clinical report
195
      structure (Onken et al., 2010) (Fig. 2 (a)). We first create a report template with segments describing
196
      study procedure, patient meta-information, image meta-information, breast composition, findings,
197   clinical impression and the final overall assessment in a natural language report style. Each segment
198   contains keywords that can be replaced with the corresponding meta-information in the tabular data.
199   By replacing these keywords and concatenating these segments, we can build a complete clinical
200   report for each specific image, and provide more details for language-image contrastive learning. We
201   provide the full template and a few image-caption examples in the appendix.
202
203   Meta-Info Masking The increased information from patient and image-specific meta-data may
204   be memorized by the model during the contrastive training and result in learning shortcuts for the
205
      model decision. To focus more on the diagnosis and disease-related information, we propose a data
      augmentation method that randomly masks each patient or image meta-information keyword with a
206
      probability of m when constructing the caption.

      3.2      M ULTI - VIEW VLP
209
210   We introduce the multi-view contrastive VLP framework here. Let D = {(xi , yi ), i = 0, 1, . . . , N }
211   be a multimodal dataset, where there are N individual images xi and corresponding text captions yi .
212   Our framework optimizes both image-to-image and symmetric image-to-text contrastive loss.
213

215   images (Fig. 2 (a)). We define a study to include the data from the same imaging session for a patient,
      including one or more image-text pairs. For a random image-text pair (xi , yi ) from the dataset D, we

254   3.3   S YMMETRIC L OCAL A LIGNMENT (SLA)
255
      Mammography usually contains high-frequency details and the region of interest is usually very small.
256
      These properties require a higher image resolution for the deep learning method to work properly. It
257
      also challenges the model’s ability to extract important local information and filter out less meaningful
258   background and tissue unrelated to diagnosis. To address these challenges, we propose a symmetric
259   local alignment (SLA) module. Specifically, the SLA module allows the model to determine the local
260   correspondence relationship between each sentence and image patch (Fig. 2 (b)).
261

262
      vision encoder fV and text encoder fT respectively, followed by corresponding local projection head
263

265   sentence-level features by selecting the embedding corresponding to the [SEP] token, which results

267   the image patch-level features by removing the extra functional tokens like [CLS] tokens, resulting
268   in a sequence of patch embeddings pi ∈ RP ×d , where P is the number of patches. We then compute
269   the sentence-patch correspondence matrix Ci,i ∈ RS×P in the form of cosine similarity, which

288   The intuition behind this design is to mimic the process of radiologic interpretation of a medical
289   image in the real world. On the one hand, in mammography, the clinician will look for the image

      3.5    LLM WITH PEFT AS T EXT E NCODER
303
304   Lastly, we incorporate parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) of a pre-trained large language model
305   (LLM) as our text encoder (e.g., BioMedLM (Bolton et al., 2024)) rather than use a small pre-trained
306   BERT encoder (Alsentzer et al., 2019). Using a pre-trained LLM with strong domain knowledge can
307   help improve the model’s understanding of the text caption and provide a more robust supervised
308   signal for the visual-language pre-training. Moreover, PEFT (e.g., LoRA (Hu et al., 2021)) can
309
      greatly reduce the cost of adapting LLM to scenarios with a shortage of computing resources while
      maintaining a strong performance after fine-tuning. Adapting an LLM with PEFT thus has the
310
      potential to greatly improve performance while reducing trainable parameters and GPU memory
311
      costs compared to learning the commonly adopted BERT-style encoder.

316   4.1    P RE - TRAINING S ETTINGS
317   Dataset We pre-trained our model on the Emory EMBED (Jeong et al., 2023) dataset, which is
318   one of the largest open mammography datasets with public access. The current release contains
319   72,768 multi-view mammography studies for 23,356 patients collected from 4 hospitals. We focus
320   on 2D mammography, which has 364,564 individual images in total. The dataset provides tabular
321   annotation about the patient, imaging meta-information, and corresponding image-level findings

342   Implementation Details We choose to use DiNOv2-ViT-B (Oquab et al., 2023) and
343   BioMedLM (Bolton et al., 2024) as our image and text encoder respectively. We adapt LoRA (Hu
344   et al., 2021) to the text encoder to fine-tune it efficiently. We choose DiNOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023)
345   ViT as it is pre-trained with a larger image size which is suitable for mammography. Note that our
346   method does not depend on a specific text encoder design. We also report the performance of our
347   model with a more common BioClincialBERT (Alsentzer et al., 2019) encoder. The meta masking
348
      ratio m is 0.8 during training. We train our model with the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter,
      2017) using a learning rate of 4E-5, weight-decay of 0.1, and cosine annealing scheduler for 40k
349
      steps. We also adapt warm-up from 1E-8 for 4k steps. The SLA loss is added after k = 8k steps.
350
      We use a batch size of 144 and train the model on 4 RTX A5000 GPUs with BFloat-16 precision.
351   We set d = 512 and τ1 = τ2 = τlocal = 0.07. We provide more details for hyper-parameters in the
352   appendix Appendix A.5.

      Tasks and Datasets We primarily evaluate our method on the EMBED (Jeong et al., 2023) dataset
357   for both BI-RADS assessment category (7 classes) and breast density (4 classes) prediction tasks.
358   Note that the real-world distribution of labels for both tasks is extremely imbalanced. To demonstrate
359   the behavior of each model in a more realistic scenario, we further sub-sample 7,666 images for
360   BI-RADS prediction and 7,301 images for breast density prediction from the test split following
361   the dataset distribution. To avoid insufficient test data and possible bias, we use all the images with
362   BIRADS 5 and 6 in the BIRADS prediction test set. Detailed class distribution is provided in the
363   appendix. We also use the RSNA-Mammo (Carr & et.al., 2022) dataset for out-of-domain evaluation
364   for binary cancer detection, which only released a training set with 54k images. We split it into a
365
      training set of 85% data and used the remaining as the evaluation. Given the extremely imbalanced
      distribution of both datasets, we choose to report balanced accuracy and AUC as our primary metrics.
366
      We also report the sensitivity and specificity of the RSNA-Mammo cancer detection task. We do not
367
      assess zero-shot classification on this dataset since only a binary cancer label is available.

      Baselines As the very first attempt at full contrastive language-image pre-training for mammogra-
      phy, we choose to compare with the following baselines: 1) ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Oquab et al.,
400
      2023): we compare with vision-only baselines with both random initialization and DiNOv2 (Oquab
401
      et al., 2023) pre-training. 2) CLIP (Radford et al., 2019): the vanilla CLIP model without other
402   additional design; 3) SLIP (Mu et al., 2022): a contrastive learning framework that optimizes both
403   image-image and image-text loss; 4) MM-MIL (Wang et al., 2023): a CLIP model that learns local
404   image-language relationship via a multiple instance learning paradigm; 5) ConVIRT (Zhang et al.,
405   2022): one of the first Chest X-ray specific CLIP models; 6) MGCA (Wang et al., 2022a): one of the
406   SoTA CLIP models for Chest X-ray that applies multi-granularity feature alignment. We pre-train
407   and fine-tune all these baselines with the same settings as our model. We also replaced the original
408   DeiT (Touvron et al., 2021) ViT with DiNOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023) for a fair comparison since
409   DeiT-ViT (Touvron et al., 2021) is only trained with a smaller image size. All the baseline methods
410
      use fully fine-tuned BioClinicalBERT (Alsentzer et al., 2019) as text encoder. While we acknowledge
      that there are other recent medical VLP methods (Huang et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2023; Wan et al.,
411
      2024; Wang et al., 2022b), they either adapt domain-specific design and require annotations not
412
      presented in our dataset (Wang et al., 2022b; Wan et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023) or were shown to
413   perform worse in other studies than the chosen baselines (Huang et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2023). We
414   also do not compare to related work that has no official implementation released (Liu et al., 2024b;
415   Chen et al., 2024) or pre-trained with different dataset (Ghosh et al., 2024).

      5      D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this work, we presented a complete and novel multi-view and multi-scale alignment contrastive
524   language-image pre-training method for mammography. We proposed utilizing the multi-view nature
525
      of mammography and providing local image-sentence correspondence to help address the challenges
      of small ROIs and high image resolution and provide fine-grained visual clues for decisions. The
526
      proposed method greatly outperforms multiple existing medical CLIP baselines.
527
528
529   Limitation and Future Work As we mainly focus on image representation learning, we have yet to
530   evaluate other downstream tasks like image-text retrieval, object detection, and segmentation. While
531
      also limited by accessible data in this domain, our method will be evaluated on more downstream tasks
      in future work. Additionally, the EMBED data comes from the Atlanta, GA region. While the dataset
532
      is highly ethnically diverse, the geographic focus could limit generalizability to other populations,
533
      e.g., the breast density distribution may differ from data gathered in other regions of the world.
534   Meanwhile, the caption is created using the template-based method, which may potentially harm the
535   model due to limited caption diversity. Future works may consider augmenting the template-based
536   prompt with LLM to generate a more diverse prompt. We plan to extend this current framework to
537   more mammography imaging modalities including C-view and digital breast tomosynthesis to further
538   enhance its understanding of mammography. Meanwhile, we also plan to integrate this pre-trained
539   component into a multi-modal question-answering and grounding model, to further explore the
      potential of medical VLP.

786    1: # fp, fs: local patch, sentence projectors
787    2: # N, tau_local: batch size and SLA loss temperature
788    3: # patch_feats: patch-wise image feature. (N, num_patch, C)
       4: # sent_feats: sentence-wise text feature.
789
       5: # list of N tensors, (num_sent, C)
790    6: def SLA_loss(patch_feats, sent_feats):
791    7:   t2v_scores = [] # cV : visual localization correspondence
792    8:   v2t_scores = [] # cT : textual localization correspondence
793    9:   patch_feats = normalize(fp(patch_feats))
      10:   # Each report may have different num_sent
794
      11:   for sent in sent_feats:
795   12:     sent = normalize(fs(sent))
796   13:     score = torch.bmm(path_feats, sent.T) # (N, num_patch, num_sent)
797   14:     # Visual localization: Max over patches + Avg over sentences
798   15:     t2v_scores.append(score.max(dim=1, keepdim=True).mean(dim=2))
      16:     # Textual localization: Max over sentences + Avg over patches
799
      17:     v2t_scores.append(score.max(dim=2, keepdim=True).mean(dim=1))
800   18:   t2v_scores = torch.stack(t2v_scores, dim=0).squeeze() # (N, N)
801   19:   v2t_scores = torch.stack(v2t_scores, dim=0).squeeze() # (N, N)
802   20:   t2v_scores /= tau_local
803   21:   v2t_scores /= tau_local
      22:   labels = torch.arange(N)
804
      23:   loss0 = cross_entropy(t2v_scores, labels)
805   24:   loss1 = cross_entropy(v2t_scores, labels)
806   25:   return 0.5 * (loss0 + loss1)

      A.4     C OMPARISON WITH E XISTING L OCAL C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING M ETHODS
831
832   The proposed SLA module mainly differs from the existing local dense contrastive learning method
833   from the following two perspectives: 1) Bi-directional optimization: SLA optimizes localization
834   alignment bi-directionally (patch-to-sentence and sentence-to-patch alignment), unlike existing
835   methods (Huang et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023) focusing on asymmetric text-
836   to-image localization. This symmetric approach improves localization granularity and prevents
837   blurry results as shown in Fig. 5 Sentence embeddings: Using sentence embeddings instead of
838   word embeddings can provide better high-level semantic information, critical to clinical reports.
839
      Word-embedding (Wang et al., 2023; 2022b) localization loss may fail in cases such as “no cancer”,
      which will be tokenized into “no” and “cancer”, leading to contradicting results. This relates to our
840
      caption construction, which correlates each sentence with one specific finding.

       Different Visual Contrastive Learning Scheme We here provide additional analysis of the
       influence of using different visual contrastive learning schemes by comparing a variation of the
1011
       proposed model, i.e., MoCo-style image-to-image contrastive loss (He et al., 2019), where a memory
1012
       queue of size 4096 is used to store the negative samples during pre-training. This can properly address
1013   the sensitivity of the image-to-image contrastive loss to the batch size, as there will always be a large
1014   number of negative examples during pre-training (see Tab. 2 in He et al. (2019), where a batch size
1015   of 256 was sufficient). Here, we provide a comparison between the proposed method (SimCLR style
1016   image-to-image loss) and MoCo-style variation in Tab. 9.
1017
       We note that there is no clear difference between the two models. The chosen SimCLR method
1018
       is slightly better from a general perspective. This result potentially suggests that the batch size
1019   may not be that important in our task, or that the used batch size was large enough. We provide
1020   two possible explanations for this result: 1) Different from natural images, where the difference
1021   between each sample is fairly large, the inter-sample difference for mammograms is much smaller.
1022   Mammography generally has very similar global content. Thus, fewer negative samples are sufficient
1023   to provide a robust contrastive signal during image-to-image contrastive pre-training. 2) Apart from
1024   the image-to-image loss, the symmetric image-to-text loss between the caption and two images also
1025   indirectly minimizes the distance between the two images, which helps alleviate the necessity of a
       large batch size.
