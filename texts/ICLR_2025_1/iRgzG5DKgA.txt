009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This work presents Fair4Free, a novel generative model to generate synthetic fair
012            data using data-free distillation in the latent space. Fair4Free can work on the
013            situation when the data is private or inaccessible. In our approach, we first train
014            a teacher model to create fair representation and then distil the knowledge to a
015            student model (using a smaller architecture). The process of distilling the student
016
               model is data-free, i.e. the student model does not have access to the training
               dataset while distilling. After the distillation, we use the distilled model to gen-
017
               erate fair synthetic samples. Our extensive experiments show that our synthetic
018
               samples outperform state-of-the-art models in all three criteria (fairness, utility
019            and synthetic quality) with a performance increase of 5% for fairness, 8% for
020            utility and 12% in synthetic quality for both tabular and image datasets.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Nowadays, people rely on Artificial Intelligence-based applications to seek answers or make deci-
026   sions. These AI-based models are trained with the data available in the real world. However, the
027   available data in the real world is often full of machine or human biases (Liu et al., 2022). So, there
028
      is a possibility that those models will reflect biases when making a decision. For this reason, there is
      a strong need for bias mitigation models or bias-free datasets to ensure fairness within the models or
029
      datasets themselves. Furthermore, not all data is publicly accessible due to proprietary or sensitive
030
      cases, i.e. medical records. So, there is a need for a way to deal with these situations, too. Over the
031   years, various approaches have been proposed to mitigate the bias issue from the model or datasets
032   themselves, and researchers have categorized these techniques into three categories: Pre-processing,
033   in-processing and post-processing techniques (Caton & Haas, 2024; Ntoutsi et al., 2020; Mehrabi
034   et al., 2021). In the pre-processing technique, the dataset is processed to lower the correlation be-
035   tween the sensitive and non-sensitive attributes. However, in this process, valuable information can
036   be lost. Post-processing techniques involve changing the output of the model in such a manner that
037   the outcome of the model becomes fair towards demographics. These models also suffer accuracy
038   as the model output is changed. In the in-processing techniques, the model is trained in such a way
039
      that during training time, the output of the model becomes fair. Though these approaches suffer
      from optimization problems (Oussidi & Elhassouny, 2018), a trade-off exists between fairness and
040
      accuracy. So, there is room for improvement.
041
042   Fair Generative Models (FGMs) are examples of in-processing bias-mitigation techniques. Over
043   the years, different generative approaches have been proposed to tackle this issue. Variational au-
044   toencoder, Generative Adversarial Networks, and Diffusion-based models have seen outstanding
045
      performance in the tabular, text, and image domains (Jung et al., 2021; Choi et al., 2020; Wang
      et al., 2022). Different fairness constraints have been used to enforce the fairness quality in the gen-
046
      erative samples, i.e., FairDisco (Liu et al., 2022) uses a distance correlation minimization method to
047
      weaken the connection between the sensitive and non-sensitive attributes. FairGAN (Li et al., 2022)
048   uses dual discriminator-based generative adversarial networks architecture for generating fair syn-
049   thetic samples. TabFairGAN (Rajabi & Garibay, 2022) generates synthetic fair data in two stages,
050   first training a GAN to generate synthetic data, then adding a constraint on the synthetic samples
051   to make it fair. FLDGMs (Ramachandranpillai et al., 2023) generate fair synthetic samples by gen-
052   erating the latent space with the help of GAN and diffusion architecture. Though these models
053   generate high-fidelity fair synthetic samples, training these requires heavy computational resources;
      sometimes, these models do not converge while training, resulting in poor-quality synthetic samples.

067   Transferring knowledge from one learned model (teacher model) to another model (student model,
068   generally smaller architecture than the teacher model) is known as knowledge distillation. Usually,
069   the student model tries to learn by taking the output of the intermediate or last layer of the teacher
070
      model. This approach helps reduce computational resources. However, one of the downsides of
      this approach is that the data label needs to be known for distillation. Recent, fair distillation works
071
      (Dong et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024) rely on data labels, and these approaches cannot be used while
072
      distilling data representation.
073
074   So, from these motivations, in this work, we present Fair4Free, a novel generative approach for
075   generating high-fidelity synthetic fair data, where we use knowledge distillation to distil the fair
076
      representation. The main contribution of our work is that while distilling the fair representation, we
      do not use any training data; we only use noise as input for the distilled model, so the approach for
077
      distillation is entirely data-free. The data-free distillation mitigates the issue of the dataset being
078
      sensitive and inaccessible. We first train a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) to learn the fair repre-
079   sentation, then use it as a teacher model and take a smaller architecture (student model) to distil the
080   fair representation. After distilling the fair representation, we use the trained decoder (from VAE)
081   and student model to reconstruct high-fidelity fair synthetic samples. Using a smaller architecture
082   also allows the possibility of deploying the model in edge devices. We do substantial experiments
083   with both tabular and image data and show that our data-free distillation-based generative model
084   performs better on the fairness, utility and synthetic samples than the state-of-the-art models. Table
085   1 shows the high-level comparison of other works with ours.
086   Our contributions to this work are as follows:
087
088         1. We present a Fair4Free, a novel data-free distillation-based fair generative model for gen-
089            erating fair synthetic samples.
090         2. Our distillation process works on latent space and requires no training data.
091
            3. We show our distillation performance for both tabular and image datasets.

094   2    R ELATED W ORK
095
096   Research on data fairness and fair models has recently seen advancement due to their usefulness
097   in real-life decision-making and other matters. To create a fair model, some research focuses on
098   creating fair representation, Learning Fair Representation (LFR) (Zemel et al., 2013) create fair
099
      representation by turning the process into an optimization problem. Their results show better fair-
      ness gain performance in the downstream task. Optimal Fair Classifier (Zhao & Gordon, 2022)
100
      gives a lower bound in the classification settings to characterize the balance between accuracy and
101
      fairness. FFVAE (Creager et al., 2019) learns fair representation by disentangling the fair latent
102   space for multiple sensitive attributes. Different adversarial approaches have been taken over the
103   years to learn fair representations, i.e. NRL (Feng et al., 2019) learns the fair representation using
104   a generator and a critic with the help of a min-max game they have designed. A fair contrastive
105   learning approach has been proposed to create fair representation for datasets where sensitive at-
106   tributes are not present (Chai & Wang, 2022). Besides creating fair representation, focuses have
107   also been drawn into the fair generative models category. Various generative models such as VAEs,
      GANs, and Diffusion-based approaches have been proposed with different fairness constraints to

      generate fair synthetic data in different modalities (tabular, image, texts). FairGAN (Li et al., 2022)
109   learns exposure-based fairness by taking negative feedback while training. TabFairGAN (Rajabi &

135

      3.3   K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION AND G ENERATIVE M ODELS
150
151   Knowledge distillation works by taking a learned model and transfer the knowledge to a smaller ar-
152   chitecture (smaller in layers and/or number of neurons) (Hinton et al., 2015). In the earliest distilla-
153   tion work (Hinton et al., 2015), the student model learned by taking the output label from the teacher
154   model and the outcome of the student model using a supervised manner, also called response-based
155   distillation. Most of the knowledge distillation work follows this approach (Li et al., 2023; Huang
156   et al., 2022). However, it becomes challenging when we want to distil some distribution as they
157   do not have any labels. Recently, Sikder et al. (2024a) used a combination of distillation loss and
158
      data-utility loss to distil the representation space without a label; however, that model requires train-
      ing data to distil the latent space. Thus, distilling the model trained on private data will be difficult,
159
      especially since the data is inaccessible.
160
161   Generative models are used to learn data distribution and generate synthetic data that is not identical
      to the original data but follows the same distributions. Over the years, different kinds of generative

      4     FAIR G ENERATIVE M ODELS USING DATA -F REE D ISTILLATION
187
188
      This section presents our approach for generating a data-free, fair, generative model. The gener-
189
      ation of fair synthetic samples involves training a fair teacher model that takes biased dataset D
190
      and produces a fair representation, then distilling the fair representation using a student architecture
191   (smaller architecture and not using the training data in the distillation process, thus data-free dis-
192   tillation) and finally reconstruct synthetic fair samples from the distilled fair-representation. While
193   training the fair teacher model, we use the distance correlation minimization technique (Liu et al.,
194   2022) to weaken the connection between the sensitive and non-sensitive attributes. We break down
195   the entire distillation and synthetic sample generation process into three stages:
196
197         1. We first train a VAE that produces fair representation and acts as the teacher model for the
198            distillation process. We take the biased data and minimize the relationship between the
199            sensitive and non-sensitive features, so for any downstreaming task, the output will be free
200
               from the influence of sensitive features.
201         2. We use a student model (smaller architecture than the teacher model) to distil the fair rep-
202            resentation. In this process, the student model does not have access to the training data; it
203            takes noise as input, so this process is data-free.
204         3. Finally, we reconstruct high-fidelity fair synthetic samples using the distilled fair represen-
205            tation from the student model and the trained decoder of VAE from stage 1.
206
207

459   6.4   D ISCUSSION
460
461   This work presents Fair4Free, a data-free distillation-based fair generative model. With this ap-
462   proach, we generate high-fidelity fair synthetic samples with the help of knowledge distillation. We
463
      distil the fair representation from the trained model (teacher model) to another architecture (student
      model). During the distillation process, we do not use any training data; thus, the training of the
464
      student model is data-free. This helps when the dataset is unavailable for security and privacy rea-
465
      sons. Also, as we use a small architecture, we reduce the computational cost, and we can deploy the
466   model into an edge device with better performance than the teacher model. Tables 2 and 3 show that
467   our model is over-performing the state-of-the-art models in the perspective of data utility, fairness
468   and synthetic quality. Besides empirical evaluation, Figure 1, 2 shows the synthetic data samples,
469   and Figure 4 shows the usefulness of fair synthetic samples in decision-making.
470
471   Social Impact The Fair Generative model can play a vital role in the decision-making process
472   because the generated samples do not consider the sensitive attributes while making decisions. This
473   process helps to reduce bias and discrimination in the real-world scenario, i.e. fair recommendations
474   in the healthcare system, fair economic recommendation (decision making on loans). Also, the fair
475   model can help build trust with the user so it can be widely used in society.
476
477   Limitations and Future Works In our experiments, we use a single sensitive attribute to train the
478
      generative model, i.e., we use either Gender or Race (for both Adult-Income and Compas datasets).
      So, in order to tackle intersectional bias, we need to work on a generative model that handles multiple
479
      sensitive attributes. However, we believe the data-free distillation process we present in this work
480
      can also be used if we have fair representation with multiple sensitive attributes.

483   7     C ONCLUSION
484
485   Real-world data is filled with human and/or machine biases, and in the era of AI-powered decision-
      making systems, these biased data can cause harm to specific people as these models are trained with

486
      them. Furthermore, some datasets are not available publicly due to proprietary cases or restricted by
487   data protection laws like GDPR or HIPPA to protect privacy. So, one way to tackle the data limitation
488   and bias issue is to use a generative model. This work presents Fair4Free, a novel fair generative
489   model that uses data-free distillation to generate fair synthetic samples. We pre-train a VAE with
490   the biased data and produce fair representation in the latent space, then use another architecture to
491   distill the fair representation. The distillation process is completely data-free, and then we use the
492   distilled fair representation to create fair synthetic samples. Our extensive experiment shows that the
493   quality of the synthetic samples outperforms state-of-the-art models regarding fairness, utility, and
494   synthetic quality. As we use a distillation process and smaller architecture for the distilled model,
495
      these models can be deployed in the edge devices. Also, as the synthetic samples are fair towards
      demographics, these can help to mitigate the biased data issue.
