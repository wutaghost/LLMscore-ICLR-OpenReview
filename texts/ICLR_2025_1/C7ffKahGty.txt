009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The growing popularity of Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining (CLIP) has
012            led to its widespread application in various visual downstream tasks. To enhance
013            CLIP’s effectiveness, efficient few-shot adaptation techniques have been widely
014            adopted. Among these approaches, training-free methods, particularly caching
015            methods exemplified by Tip-Adapter, have gained attention for their lightweight
016            adaptation without the need for additional fine-tuning. In this paper, we revisit
017            Tip-Adapter from a kernel perspective, showing that caching methods function as
018
               local adapters and are connected to a well-established kernel literature. Leveraging
               this insight, we offer a theoretical understanding of how these methods operate and
019
               suggest multiple avenues for enhancing over the Tip-Adapter baseline. Notably,
020
               our analysis shows the importance of incorporating global information in local
021            adapters. Therefore, we subsequently propose a global method that learns a
022            proximal regularizer in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) using CLIP as a
023            base learner. Our method, that we call ProKeR (Proximal Kernel ridge Regression),
024            has a closed form solution and achieves state-of-the-art performance across 11
025            datasets in the standard few-shot adaptation benchmark.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large scale vision-language models (VLMs) trained with contrastive learning have gained an increas-
031   ing attraction in the recent years [41; 21]. These models have shown remarkable performance across
032   a wide range of tasks such as classification [41], segmentation [30] and video understanding [29].
033
      CLIP is one of the most established VLMs [41] offering remarkable zero-shot capabilities in various
      downstream tasks. It operates by using only the class label within a textual prompt such as “A photo of
034
      a {CLASS}” where {CLASS} is the groundtruth text label for each class. However, it is well-known
035
      that the zero-shot performance of CLIP is limited in scenarios with large domain shift from the
036   pre-training distribution [10; 34]. To further improve CLIP’s generalization, multiple follow-up
037   works proposed to include few-shot data [66; 12; 62] which has shown remarkable performance gains
038   compared to zero-shot CLIP.
039
      Few-shot adaptation of CLIP can be categorized into two types of methods based on whether they
040
      require fine-tuning on the few-shot samples or not. Among fine-tuning based methods, prompt
041
      learning involves learning continuous tokens instead of hand-crafted templates in CLIP as proposed
042   by CoOp [66] and CoCoOp [65]. Additionally, adapter-based fine-tuning methods operate in the
043   features space to train their classifiers [12; 28]. Despite their promising performance on downstream
044   tasks, fine-tuning methods require additional training costs to learn the new learnable parameters. Tip-
045   Adapter proposed a training-free few-shot adaptation alternative [62]. Using a caching mechanism,
046   Tip-Adapter captures knowledge from the few-shot samples without additional fine-tuning and
047   ensembles it with zero-shot CLIP. This cache model has shown significant improvement over zero-
048   shot performance which has led to follow-up works exemplified by APE [67] and Tip-X [53].
049   In order to understand caching effectiveness and limitations, we undertake a theoretical analysis to
050   explore the nature of Tip-Adapter. We begin by demonstrating that the adaptation term of Tip-Adapter
051   is a modified version of the well-known Nadaraya-Waston (NW) estimator [33], a local nonparametric
052   regression method that allows Tip-Adapter to effectively capture various distributions. However,
053   the NW estimator is also known to be strongly biased [25; 36]. To mitigate this bias, we leverage
      existing tools from the literature of kernel methods. An effective extension to NW estimator is locally


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      linear regression [46]. By fitting a local linear regression around each test point using a closed-form
055   solution, we significantly improve the performance of Tip-Adapter. However, the parameters are
056   estimated locally, which is prone to overfitting in high-dimensional problems [36; 16].
057
058
      Our analysis shows that caching methods can be understood as local nonparametric regression
      methods regularised through CLIP pointwise zero-shot predictions. However, this regularization only
059
      acts locally and doesn’t provide any global information about the few-shot task. Conversely, recent
060
      training-based methods rely on global regularizers to incorporate global information [50]. Hence,
061   we ask ourselves the question, how can we leverage global regularizers for few-shot adapters while
062   conserving the benefits of training-free methods? In order to design such a global regularizer, we
063   devise two important design choices. Firstly, we restrain the hypothesis space of the learned function
064   to be a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). Secondly, using the RKHS norm, we introduce
065   a proximal regularization term to ensure that the obtained solution is close to the base predictor
066   i.e. fclip . Thanks to the properties of the RKHS, minimizing the difference between two functions
067   using the RKHS norm ensures that they are close pointwise. Our method ProKeR provides a more
068   effective way to preserve prior knowledge from the zero-shot predictor and maintains the expressive
069
      capacity of the learned functions. Through extensive experiments, we show the effectiveness of
      our method ProKeR which achieves consistent gains over state-of-the-art training-free methods on
070
      standard few-shot classification benchmarks [62] with an absolute average improvement of 3.94%
071
      accuracy. In addition, we highlight the robustness and generalizability of the proposed method across
072   different architectures and on out-of-distribution datasets.
073
074   Summary of contributions:
075
076
            1. We frame the cache model of Tip-Adapter as a Nadaraya-Waston estimator, a classical kernel
               regression method and provide a theoretical understanding of how Tip-Adapter operates.
077
078         2. Under this new perspective, we propose multiple improvements for Tip-Adapter either using
079            a closed-form local linear regression fit or by incorporating global information.
080
            3. We propose ProKeR, a training-free method that leverages global regularization in a RKHS.
081            Through extensive experiments, ProKeR outperforms existing methods and sets a new
082            state-of-the-art on standard few-shot classification benchmarks.

087   2.1   V ISION -L ANGUAGE P RE - TRAINED M ODELS
088
089   In recent years, visual-language models (VLMs) have gained an increasing popularity. These methods,
090   exemplified by CLIP [41], DeCLIP [27] and ALIGN [21], employ a contrastive learning framework
091   to learn a vision encoder and a language encoder with a shared representation space between text and
092
      images. Trained on large-scale datasts of image-text pairs, VLMs have shown remarkable zero-shot
      capabilities on downstream tasks without additional fine-tuning, paving the way for open vocabulary
093
      recognition [14]. VLMs have been extended to few-shot classification [66] as well as other tasks
094
      beyond image classification such as video understanding [29; 54], image segmentation [30; 52],
095   image generation [43] and 3D reconstruction [63; 7].
096
097
098   2.2   F EW- SHOT A DAPTATION
099
      Few-shot adaptation methods in the context of classification can be categorized into prompt learning
100
      and adapter based approaches. Inspired from the recent advances in natural language processing [26],
101
      prompt learning aims to learn effective global text or visual prompts for the downstream tasks [66;
102   65; 23; 2; 61; 58; 47; 6; 45; 49]. Although prompt learning methods have brought significant
103   improvements over the zero-shot baseline, they require back-propagating through the entire text
104   encoder and require having access to the text encoder [38]. On the other hand, adapter-based
105   approaches operate in the feature space and do not require having access to the pre-trained model
106   weights. We distinguish two families of adapter-based approaches. The first one is training-based
107   methods [28; 12; 60] which either train a linear layer [28; 60] or a two-layer MLP such as CLIP-
      Adapter [12] to perform residual feature blending of the zero-shot classifier.

129   While fine-tuning adapters have achieved a good level of performance, they still require additional
130   training time which is impractical for limited resources and might suffer from the caveats of gradient
131
      optimization. To alleviate these issues, Tip-Adapter [62] emerges as a training-free solution based

132

133
      foundation models for the cache model and augments text prompts using a Large Language model.
134   SuS-X and its module Tip-X [53] first generates the few-shot set using Stable Diffusion then uses the
135   inter-modal similarities between the test images and the few-shot set. More recently, GDA proposes
136   to use a Linear Discriminant Analysis to set a strong baseline for training-free methods [55]. Despite
137   the good level of performance, there is currently no theoretical framework for better understanding
138   the motivation behind caching models.

      3.5   P ROXIMAL K ERNEL R IDGE R EGRESSION
263
264   As can be seen in Figure 2, incorporating global information about the task through a global metric
265   effectively outperforms both Tip-Adapter and LLR. However, the choice of the global metric for
266   NW estimator beyond the Mahalanobis distance remains challenging [56], especially in the few-shot
267   setting. Furthermore, despite being equipped with a global metric, the NW estimator remains in
268   essence a local method and still lacks a global regularization. Whilst the use of a global regularization
269   has been recently addressed in training-based methods [50], using a truly global regularization in a
      training-free manner remains challenging and unexplored.

      These limitations highlight the necessity for regularization in this adaptation process. The main idea
271   is to balance the need to maintain the expressive capacity of the learned functions while ensuring
272   stability and robustness. To this end we devise two important design choices. Firstly, we restrain the
273   hypothesis space of the learned function to be a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). Secondly,
274   using the RKHS norm, we introduce a proximal regularization term to ensure that the obtained
275   solution is close to the base predictor i.e. fclip . Thanks to the properties of the RKHS, minimizing
276   the difference between two functions using the RKHS norm ensures that they are close pointwise.
277   Consequently, this proximal term serves as global regularization that preserves prior knowledge from
278   the zero-shot predictor, resulting in more robust solutions that are less prone to overfitting on the
279
      few-shot data.
280   Given the multi-output nature of the problem, the employed reproducing kernel Kβ is a reproducing
281   kernel for vector valued functions. The main difference is that the kernel is matrix valued. Several
282   instances of multi-output kernels have been proposed in the literature [5; 1], with separable kernels
283   being among the most widely used for learning vector-valued functions due to their simplicity and
284   computational efficiency. These kernels are formulated as a product of a kernel function for the

      In this section, we evaluate our method on multiple image classification benchmarks. We compare our

      other state-of-the-art conventional methods, such as APE [67], Tip-X [53], GDA [55], CLIP [41] and

504   5   L IMITATIONS & F UTURE W ORK
505
506   Based on our analysis, caching methods can be understood as local nonparametric regressors. These
507   methods lack a global regularization from zero-shot CLIP which limits their generalization ability
508   when performing adaptation. On the other hand, global methods may lack the flexibility when dealing
509   with complex data. In the future work, we will explore how both local and global methods can be
510   combined to benefit from the best of both worlds. Furthermore, our formulation of caching methods
511   as a Nadaraya-Watson estimator offers multiple options for the choice the regularization term, the
512   metric used in the kernel as well as the bandwidth selection [39]. These choices constitute different
513
      ways to reduce the bias-variance trade-off inherent to these methods [16].
514
515   6   C ONCLUSION
516
517   In this paper, we propose a theoretical understanding of Tip-Adapter, a training-free caching-based
518   method. Our analysis suggests that Tip-Adapter is a local nonparametric regression that has well-
519   known bias limitation. We propose multiple angles of improvement that has shown significant
520   amelioration over Tip-Adapter’s baseline. Subsequently, we demonstrate that incorporating global
521   information in a training-free method can be achieved using a global regularization in a reproducing
522
      kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), which conclusively further improves the state-of-the-art for training-free
      methods.
