009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Before taking actions in an environment with more than one intelligent agent, an
012            autonomous agent may benefit from reasoning about the other agents and utilizing
013            a notion of a guarantee or confidence about the behavior of the system. In this
014            article, we propose a novel multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) algorithm
015            CAMMARL , which involves modeling the actions of other agents in different
016            situations in the form of confident sets, i.e., sets containing their true actions with a
017            high probability. We then use these estimates to inform an agent’s decision-making.
018
               For estimating such sets, we use the concept of conformal predictions, by means
               of which, we not only obtain an estimate of the most probable outcome but get to
019
               quantify the operable uncertainty as well. For instance, we can predict a set that
020
               provably covers the true predictions with high probabilities (e.g., 95%). Through
021            several experiments in two fully cooperative multi-agent tasks, we show that
022            CAMMARL elevates the capabilities of an autonomous agent in MARL by modeling
023            conformal prediction sets over the behavior of other agents in the environment and
024            utilizing such estimates to enhance its policy learning.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Developing systems of autonomous agents capable of effective multi-agent interactions can be very
030   useful in modern cooperative artificial intelligence (AI). For instance, service robots, surveillance
031   agents, and many more similar applications require profound collaboration among agents (and with
032   humans), without prior coordination. Now, to enable complex, constructive behaviors to emerge
033
      from unsupervised interactions among agents, an essential skill for an agent to have is the ability to
      reason about other agents in the environment. There has been considerable research addressing this
034
      problem of agent or opponent modeling (Albrecht & Stone, 2018). Generally, it involves constructing
035
      models of other agents that learn useful attributes to inform its own decision-making (such as the
036   future actions of the other agents, or their current goals and plans) from current or past interaction
037   history (such as the previous actions taken by other agents in different situations).
038
039
      We are interested in the particular aspect of an interactive, autonomous agent that involves learning
      an additional, independent model to make predictions about the actions of the other agents in the
040
      environment, supplemental to its reinforcement learning-based policy to make decisions related
041
      to its downstream task. An autonomous agent can then incorporate those estimates to inform its
042   decision-making and optimize its interaction with the other agents. While there exist several methods
043   for developing such models for other agents (Albrecht & Stone, 2018), there is currently no method
044   or theory to the best of our knowledge that would allow an agent to consider the correctness or
045   confidence of the predictions of the learned model.
046
      Conformal Predictions. Conformal predictions or inference is a fitting method for generating
047
      statistically accurate uncertainty sets for the predictions from machine learning classifiers. It is
048   steadily gaining popularity owing to its explicit and non-asymptotic guarantees over the produced
049   sets (Angelopoulos & Bates, 2021). In other words, we can obtain conformal sets that provably
050   contain the true predictions with high probabilities, such as 95%, chosen in advance. This can be
051   very useful and successful in high-risk learning settings, especially in decision-making in medical
052   applications from diagnostic information, for instance, which demand quantifying uncertainties to
053   avoid insufferable model failures. What if we only prefer to use the predictions when the model is
      confident? For example, doctors may only consider a predicted medical diagnosis when the model

092   2       R ELATED W ORKS
093
094   Decision-making without reasoning about other agents in the environment can be very challenging, for
095   instance, due to weak or no theoretical guarantees, non-stationarity (single agent’s perspective), and
096
      inefficient coordination for a considerable coherent joint behavior (Matignon et al., 2012). Modeling
      other agents in an environment is not new and has been studied in the past (Albrecht & Stone, 2018;
097
      Albrecht et al., 2020). However, our proposal of predicting conformal sets of actions of the other
098
      agents in the environment (with high probability) is novel and has not been attempted to the best of
099   our knowledge.
100
101   Learning world models. Model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL) has certainly shown its
102
      advantages in data efficiency, generalization, exploration, counterfactual reasoning, and performance
      in many tasks and domains (Hafner et al., 2020; 2021; Jain et al., 2022; Moerland et al., 2023; Pal
103
      & Leon, 2020; Polydoros & Nalpantidis, 2017) in single-agent RL, and now, it has also started to
104
      attract attention in MARL (Wang et al., 2022). However, most of the current works in model-based
105   MARL do not yet focus on teammate or opponent modeling. Some recent works (Park et al., 2019b;
106   Zhang et al., 2021) incorporated dynamics modeling and a prediction module to estimate the actions
107

      of other agents within the construction of the environment model. However, these prediction models
109   were trained without accessing the true trajectories from the other agents which can be problematic
110   in several use cases.
111
112
      Learning agent models. A widely popular technique to reason about other agents in the environment
      is to learn representations of different properties of other agents. For instance, learning to reconstruct
113
      the actions of other agents from their partial observations (He et al., 2016; Mealing & Shapiro, 2015;
114
      Panella & Gmytrasiewicz, 2017; Albrecht & Ramamoorthy, 2015), modeling an agent or its policy
115   using encoder-decoder-based architectures Grover et al. (2018); Zintgraf et al. (2021), learning latent
116   representations from local information with or without utilizing the modeled agent’s trajectories
117   (Papoudakis et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021) or modeling the forward dynamics of the system through
118   relational reasoning using graph neural networks (Tacchetti et al., 2018). Theory-of-Mind Network
119   or TomNet learned embeddings corresponding to other agents in the environment for meta-learning
120   (Rabinowitz et al., 2018). Some works also constructed I-POMDPs to utilize recursive reasoning
121   (Albrecht & Stone, 2018) assuming unrestricted knowledge of the observation models of other agents.
122   Nevertheless, CAMMARL involves no form of reconstruction of other agent’s policy or rewards, or
123
      state models. Any of these techniques can be used with CAMMARL which, however, is not the
      objective of this work. Also, unlike CAMMARL, many of these aforementioned techniques evaluate in
124
      fully-observable environments or rely upon direct access to other agents’ experience trajectories even
125
      during execution. This can be infeasible in various settings.
126
127   Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL). Numerous deep MARL research works that focus
128   on partial observability in fully cooperative settings indirectly involve reasoning about the intentions
129
      of teammates or opponents in an environment (Gronauer & Diepold, 2022). For instance, many
      works allow agents to communicate, enabling them to indirectly reason about the others’ intentions
130
      (Lazaridou et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016; Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Das et al., 2017; Mordatch &
131
      Abbeel, 2018; Gupta et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2022). On the other hand, some studied the emergence of
132   cooperative and competitive behaviors among agents in varying environmental factors, for instance,
133   task types or reward structures (Leibo et al., 2017). Recent work in hierarchical reinforcement
134   learning also attempts to develop a hierarchical model to enable agents to strategically decide
135   whether to cooperate or compete with others in the environment and then execute respective planning
136   programs (Kleiman-Weiner et al., 2016). However, none of these works study the improvement in an
137   autonomous agent’s decision-making via directly modeling the other agents in the environment or
138   predicting their actions or current or future intentions.
139   Inverse reinforcement learning (IRL). Research in the field of IRL also relates to our work because
140   we share the key motive of inferring other agents’ intentions and then use it to learn a policy that
141   maximizes the utility of our learning agent (Arora & Doshi, 2021). However, IRL addresses this
142   by deducing the reward functions of other agents based on their behavior, assuming it to be nearly
143   optimal. On the other hand, in CAMMARL we directly model the other agent’s actions based on their
144   observations and use these estimates to indirectly infer their goal in an online manner.
145   Conformal prediction. Estimating well-grounded uncertainty in predictions is a difficult and
146   unsolved problem and there have been numerous approaches for approximating it in research in
147   supervised learning (Gawlikowski et al., 2021). Recent works in conformal predictions (Angelopoulos
148   et al., 2020; Lei et al., 2018; Hechtlinger et al., 2018; Park et al., 2019a; Cauchois et al., 2020;
149   Messoudi et al., 2020) have now significantly improved upon some of the early research (Vovk
150   et al., 2005; Platt et al., 1999; Papadopoulos et al., 2002), for instance in terms of predicted set sizes,
151   improved efficiency, and providing formal guarantees. For this article, we adapt the core ideas from
152
      Regularized Adaptive Prediction Sets (RAPS) (Angelopoulos et al., 2020) to our setting owing to its
      demonstrated improved performance evaluation on classification benchmarks in supervised learning
153
      (Angelopoulos et al., 2020). Key description of conformal prediction is in Appendix B.

158   3.1   M OTIVATION
159
160   The primary motivation behind CAMMARL is to enhance an agent’s decision-making in multi-
161   agent environments by incorporating predictions about other agents’ actions. CAMMARL utilizes
      conformal prediction, a technique that provides statistically valid uncertainty quantification. In the


                                                          3

162
      context of CAMMARL, conformal prediction allows us to generate sets of possible actions for other
163   agents, with a guaranteed probability of containing the true action. This approach offers a principled
164   way to represent uncertainty in action predictions since the prediction sets are automatically calibrated,
165   ensuring that the specified coverage probability is achieved in practice.

      We focus on four cooperative multi-agent environments: Cooperative Navigation The agents learn

      to visit the two landmarks avoiding collisions. In Level-based Foraging, agents collect foods based

303   4.2   BASELINES
304
305   To show the benefits of conformal action set prediction, we compare CAMMARL with the performances
306   of agents in different settings with varying pieces of information made available to Nself during
307   training.
308   No-Other-Agent-Modeling (NOAM). At first, we train Nself without allowing it to model Nother .
309   This baseline, as expected, underperforms when compared to any other settings (where any kind
310   of agent modeling is allowed). It is indicative of a lower bound to the learning performance of our
311   model where no kind of benefit from agent modeling is utilized by Nself . We call this baseline —
312   No-Other-Agent-Modeling or NOAM.
313   True-Action-Agent-Modeling (TAAM). Advancing from the inputs available in NOAM, we imple-
314   ment TAAM by allowing Nself to additionally utilize Nother ’s true actions to train. This baseline
315   helps us evaluate CAMMARL against works that estimate other agents’ actions in the environment
316   and use those predictions to enhance the decision-making of their controlled autonomous agents. By
317   giving the true actions as inputs, this baseline can act as an upper bound to such works(He et al.,
318   2016; Grover et al., 2018; Zintgraf et al., 2021; Mealing & Shapiro, 2015; Panella & Gmytrasiewicz,
319   2017; Albrecht & Ramamoorthy, 2015).
320   True-Observation-Agent-Modeling (TOAM). As discussed in Section 2, learning world models of-
321   ten involves reconstructing observation as an additional task while learning task-related policies (Jain
322   et al., 2022; Hafner et al., 2020; 2021). Inspired by this research, we implement the TOAM baseline
323   where we allow access to Nother ’s true observations to Nself during training and execution. In other
      words, we augment Nself ’s partial observations with the other agent’s local observations too. This


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      baseline can act as an upper bound to the performances of research works that learn to reconstruct
325   states for agents (Hafner et al., 2020; 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Park et al., 2019b; Zhang et al., 2021).
326
327   Global-Information-Agent-Modeling (GIAM). On the other extreme, we also implement GIAM,
328
      where Nself trains with complete access to both (1) Nother ’s true action trajectories (aother ), and
      (2) Nother ’s true observations (oother ) as additional information. This can be infeasible in real-
329
      world scenarios, however, theoretically represents an upper bound on the performance of agents in
330
      CAMMARL and other settings.
331
332   Exact-Action-Prediction (EAP). Building over the inputs of TOAM, we construct a stronger
333   baseline, EAP, in which Nself uses an additional neural network classifier to model a probability
334
      distribution over Nother ’s actions. In other words, instead of predicting conformal sets of actions (like
      in CAMMARL), in this baseline, Nself tries to model Nother ’s actions from the latter’s observations
335
      without accounting for any uncertainty quantification. This baseline is inspired by works that explicitly
336
      model the other agent’s actions in the environments and utilize them to inform their controlled agent’s
337   decision-making (for instance, He et al. (2016); Grover et al. (2018); Zintgraf et al. (2021)). Hence,
338   here, a cross-entropy loss is used to train the added sub-module that predicts the Nother ’s actions
339   along with a PPO loss to train Nself ’s policy network.
340
      CAMMARL . Now, we implement CAMMARL , where the conformal action prediction model periodi-
341
      cally trains on collected observations of Nother and predicts a corresponding conformal set of actions.
342
      Nself uses these estimates of Nother ’s actions along with its own observations and then decides upon
343   its actions in the environment.

346   4.3   R ESULTS WITH 2 AGENTS
347
348   Figure 3 shows performance curve of all algorithm in environments in Cooperative Navigation and
349   Level Based Foraging. Using additional information, both TAAM and TOAM do reasonably better
350   than NOAM in both tasks. Here, the difference in returns in TOAM and TAAM can be attributed
351
      to the fact that the local observations of other agents include more information that can be useful to
      infer their behavior. For instance, in CN, knowing the relative positions of other agents with respect
352
      to the landmarks can be more useful to infer which landmark that agent might be approaching when
353
      compared to knowing its current (or history) actions.
354
355   GIAM achieves higher returns compared to all other settings in both environments. This is intuitive
356   because it benefits from more information. GIAM is conditioned on Nother ’s true experiences and
357
      consequently demands access to them even during execution. CAMMARL agents are able to distinctly
      perform better than EAP in LBF, however, interestingly, the performance curve for this baseline nearly
358
      overlaps with CAMMARL in CN. Also, in LBF, the curves for TOAM and EAP seem to significantly
359
      overlap. We speculate that in a complicated task like LBF, estimating the exact action of Nother
360   can be difficult, and with unaccounted uncertainty in the predictions, Nself suffers from a lower
361   return. In CN, which is comparatively simpler, the closeness of returns in EAP and CAMMARL seem
362   reasonable as even the conformal model predictions eventually start predicting the most probable
363   action with higher probabilities and hence a set of size one (more on this in Section 6).
364
      Figure 3 shows that CAMMARL agents obtain returns that are much closer to the upper bound, GIAM,
365
      than the lower bound, NOAM. Furthermore, CAMMARL’s better performance compared to TOAM in
366   both environments can be attributed to the fact that it can be difficult to predict the Nother ’s intentions
367   by only using oother without any information pertaining to its actions in those situations. And, in
368   TAAM, Nself is expected to implicitly encode information regarding Nother ’s observations from
369   its own local observations or in the latent space and map it to Nother ’s true actions. We speculate
370   that this could be a strong assumption and consequently very difficult, hence, CAMMARL agents
371   outperform TAAM too. Note here that the sets output by the conformal action prediction model are
372   of varying sizes in each iteration. Now, to be able to use these dynamically changing inputs for Nself
373   in CAMMARL, we convert the output sets to a corresponding binary encoding (by firing up the bits
374
      in a zero vector at indices corresponding to the actions predicted by the model). We discuss some
      more ways to be able to use conformal prediction sets with dynamic sizes and compare CAMMARL’s
375
      performances in all variations later in the Appendix.

      7   C ONCLUSION
524
525
      In this article, we propose a novel MARL algorithm, CAMMARL, which calls for confident reasoning
      about other artificial agents in the environment and benefiting from inferences about their behavior.
526
      Through experiments in two cooperative multi-agent tasks, CN and LBF, we showed that guiding an
527
      agent’s decision-making by inferring other agents’ actions in the form of conformal sets, indeed helps
528   in achieving better performances of the learning agents. By using conformal prediction, we were also
529   able to ensure the estimation of predictive sets that covered the real predictions of the intentions of
530   other agents with a very high pre-specified probability of 95%.
531
      Limitations and Future Works: In our paper, we analyzed CAMMARL with two agents, however,
532
      CAMMARL is certainly generalizable to bigger networks or more simple classifiers, and analyzing
533
      its changing performance on varying buffer sizes can help in better comprehending its efficiency.
534   Second, it would be interesting to investigate the CAMMARL’s scalability to a system of many agents
535   (say 100 or 1000) or on more complicated multi-agent environments such as tasks requiring a higher
536   need for coordination. Thirdly, our mathematical model in Section 3.2 makes an assumption that the
537   state space is accessible globally which may not be the case in some problems. Finally, in this work,
538   we restricted the agents to infer the behavior of other agents only via conformal sets; it would be
539   interesting to study the cases where more ways of sharing information or modeling agents’ behavior
      are additionally allowed.

      B    C ONFORMAL P REDICTION
780
781
      Conformal prediction is a statistical framework for constructing prediction intervals or sets with
      guaranteed coverage probabilities, regardless of the underlying data distribution. This method
782
      provides a way to quantify uncertainty in machine learning predictions without making strong
783
      distributional assumptions. The key idea behind conformal prediction is the use of a nonconformity
784   measure, which quantifies how different a new example is from previously observed data. This
785   measure is used to determine a prediction set that includes all outcomes sufficiently similar to the
786   observed data. The framework is flexible and can be applied to various machine learning models,
787   including neural networks, support vector machines, and random forests, without modifying their
788   internal workings. One of the main advantages of conformal prediction is its ability to provide valid
789   uncertainty quantification under minimal assumptions. It offers finite-sample guarantees, meaning
790   that the prediction sets are valid not just asymptotically, but also for finite sample sizes. This
791   makes conformal prediction particularly useful in high-stakes applications where reliable uncertainty
792
      estimates are crucial for decision-making and autonomous systems.
793
794   C    VARIATIONS OF THE CAMMARL ALGORITHM
795
796   As noted earlier, the output of the conformal prediction model, i.e., the conformal sets, are of varying
797   sizes in different situations. So, we came up with numerous ways to be able to use them to implement
798   CAMMARL. In this section, we discuss and compare them and speculate some pros and cons for each
799   of them.
800   CAM - PADDING. The conformal model outputs a set containing the action predictions in the decreas-
801   ing order of their probability of being the true actions. In other words, the first element in the set is
802   the highest probable action, the next is second highest probable, and so on. The maximum set size
803   being |Aother |, we try padding the set with zeros to fix its size when used as input in CAMMARL. We
804   hypothesise that in this way, Nself must be able to learn to infer the information about the actions
805   along with the notion of their importance. Figure 8 also supports this hypothesis by showing the
806
      reasonable performance of this version of CAMMARL(green curve). Nevertheless, such padding can
      be undesirable, particularly because zeros need not necessarily mean “no information”, and we try to
807
      remove it in the versions discussed next.
808
809   CAM - BINARY . In this method (also discussed earlier in Section 4), we encode the conformal sets
      into binary strings. We start with a vector of zeros of size |Aother | and fire up the bits corresponding


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      to the output actions in the sets. The orange curve in Figure 8 shows that this version of CAMMARL
811   outperforms all the other versions.
812
813   CAM - PENULTIMATE . Here, we modify CAMMARL to now share the embedding (representations)
814
      learned in the conformal prediction model as input to Nself . The size of embedding is fixed and this
      way, padding of conformal action sets in CAMMARL can be avoided. As shown in Figure 8 (blue
815
      curve), CAMMARL with representations also manages to obtain returns close to binary-CAMMARL
816
      and padded-CAMMARL. We also note here that, binary-CAMMARL and padded-CAMMARL still
817   performs slightly better than CAMMARL with representations.
818
819   Interestingly, all versions perform almost equivalently for the use-cases tested in this article. Any
820
      method can be used depending on the application at hand. For instance in medical use-cases, the
      knowledge of ranks of conformal actions is critical, and so cp-padding can be used. In this article,
821
      we choose cp-binary. It is a simple way to ensure fixed-sized inputs for Nself in CAMMARL and
822
      empirically works very well, and hence we propose this as a generic solution. We note here that, the
823   embeddings here are not being passed in addition to the set of predictions.

      The ablation study results on Cooperative Navigation with different alpha values provide interesting
857
      insights into the performance of CAMMARLṪhe results suggest an optimal range for alpha exists where
858   CAMMARL performs best. This range balances the trade-off between uncertainty quantification and
859   decision precision: Too much uncertainty (low alpha) may lead to indecisiveness or overly cautious
860   behavior. Too little uncertainty (high alpha) may not provide enough flexibility for agents to adapt
861   to changing situations. The sweet spot (around 0.7-0.9) allows agents to make informed decisions
862   while accounting for a reasonable level of uncertainty in other agents’ actions. This ablation study
863   highlights the importance of carefully tuning the conformal prediction parameters in CAMMARL to
      achieve optimal performance in cooperative multi-agent task.

885   We use proximal policy optimization (PPO) Schulman et al. (2017) to update the decision-making
