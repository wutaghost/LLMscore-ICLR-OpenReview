011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            We investigate whether in-context examples, widely used in decoder-only lan-
014            guage models (LLMs), can improve embedding model performance in retrieval
015            tasks. Unlike in LLMs, naively prepending in-context examples (query-document
016
               pairs) to the target query at inference time does not work out of the box. We in-
               troduce a simple approach to enable retrievers to use in-context examples. Our
017
               approach, RARe, finetunes a pre-trained model with in-context examples whose
018
               query is semantically similar to the target query. This can be applied to adapt
019            various base architectures (i.e., decoder-only language models, retriever models)
020            and consistently achieves performance gains of up to +2.72% nDCG across var-
021            ious open-domain retrieval datasets (BeIR, RAR-b). In particular, we find RARe
022            exhibits stronger out-of-domain generalization compared to models using queries
023            without in-context examples, similar to what is seen for in-context learning in
024            LLMs. While our approach incurs additional computational cost to encode length-
025            ier queries, the impact is less pronounced in large-corpus scenarios. We further
026            provide analysis on the design choices of in-context example augmentation and
027
               lay the foundation for future work in this space.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In-context learning (ICL) (Brown et al., 2020) has emerged as a powerful paradigm enabling diverse
033
      applications without parameter updates in large language models (LLMs). By conditioning on input-
      output examples that demonstrate a specific task, LLMs can generate predictions while maintaining
034
      fixed parameters. While in-context learning has been extensively studied for LLMs (Xu et al., 2023;
035
      Min et al., 2022a; Dong et al., 2024), its potential for retriever models remains unexplored.
036
037   We study how in-context examples can be effectively leveraged to enhance performance in retriever
038   models. Unlike in decoder-only LLMs where in-context examples expand model capacity at genera-
039
      tion time, in-context examples may primarily provide task-relevant information rather than increas-
      ing model capacity. Specifically, we study injecting in-context examples to build a dense retriever
040
      model (Karpukhin et al., 2020) which embeds queries and documents into a shared representational
041
      space for efficient search over a large corpus. Text retrieval is a core component of many natural
042   language processing (NLP) tasks, serving as a key component for retrieval-augmented language lan-
043   guage models (Lewis et al., 2021). State-of-the-art retriever models started to leverage decoder-only
044   models as a backbone (Wang et al., 2024b; BehnamGhader et al., 2024; Muennighoff et al., 2024;
045   Meng et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024a), further motivating our study of applying in-context examples.
046
      We begin by naively prepending in-context examples to the target query and provide it to existing
047
      retriever models (BehnamGhader et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Meng et al., 2024), observing
048   that this leads to significant performance drop. We propose a new approach to construct retrieval
049   models that can leverage in-context examples, which we name as RARe: Retrieval Augmented
050   Retrieval with In-Context Examples. Our approach modifies the query format of retrieval systems
051   by providing in-context examples whose query is semantically similar to the target query. Then,
052   we apply standard continued fine-tuning with contrastive loss. We conduct a comprehensive
053   evaluation of new query format across various experimental settings, initializing from both
      decoder-only checkpoints and pre-trained retriever model checkpoints. We demonstrate that RARe

160   Base Models We explore two training setups: fine-tuning decoder-only models for retrieval, and
161   fine-tuning existing retriever models. For the first setup, we train the Llama-3 family of models,
      following the training methodology outlined by Ma et al. (2023); Weller et al. (2024b). For the


                                                                      3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      second setup, we use two high-performing publicly available embedding models that were trained
163   with task-specific instructions: LLM2Vec-Llama-3-8b-Supervised (BehnamGhader et al., 2024) and
164   E5-Mistral-Instruct (Wang et al., 2024b). We chose these two models because, unlike some other
165   strong performers (Meng et al., 2024; de Souza P. Moreira et al., 2024), they were not trained on
166   most of the datasets used in our downstream benchmarks. The LLM2Vec-Llama-3-8b-Supervised
167   model is initially trained using an unsupervised text reconstruction objective and then fine-tuned
168   with supervised contrastive learning on a public subset of the E5 dataset, which incorporates various
169   supervised training datasets (Gao et al., 2021; Nguyen et al., 2016; Kwiatkowski et al., 2019). In
170   contrast, E5-Mistral-Instruct undergoes further training on synthetic data that is not publicly avail-
171
      able. These models are chosen to assess the impact of additional supervised training on an existing
      retriever model versus training a generative model for retrieval from scratch.

186   4.2   E VALUATION
187
      Datasets We evaluate on the widely used BeIR retrieval benchmark (Thakur et al., 2021). For
188
      ablative experiments, we follow prior work and focus on low-resource datasets (Wang et al., 2023)
189   that potentially benefit more from few-shot examples. Since the BeIR benchmark contains a few
190   datasets whose training sets are in the E5 dataset mixutre, we categorize them as in-domain and
191   out-of-domain i.e. not seen during training. See Table 7 in the Appendix for a list of in-domain
192   and out-of-domain datasets from BeIR. We also evaluate on a subset of the RAR-b (Xiao et al.,
193   2024) benchmark, which requires complex reasoning for retrievers. Specifically, we evaluate on
194   HellaSwag (Zellers et al., 2019), PIQA (Bisk et al., 2020), ARC-C (Clark et al., 2018), TempReason-
195   L1 (Tan et al., 2023), WinoGrande (Sakaguchi et al., 2021), α-NLI (Bhagavatula et al., 2020),
196   SiQA (Sap et al., 2019), and Quail (Rogers et al., 2020). Unlike BeIR, some RAR-b queries are
197
      composed of sentences with (multiple) indicators (e.g., Start:, End:). Each dataset is associated
      with a task-specific instruction, following prior work (Muennighoff et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b;
198
      BehnamGhader et al., 2024). We provide additional preprocessing details in Appendix A.

      7     R ELATED W ORK
477
478
      In-context learning ICL (Brown et al., 2020) allows models to adapt to new tasks in a few-shot
479
      manner by conditioning on the input data and the context provided at inference time. ICL has been
480   effectively applied to a wide range of tasks such as classification (Milios et al., 2023), translation
481   (Zhu et al., 2024), mathematical reasoning (Wei et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022), and code generation
482   (Li et al., 2023a). Recent advancements have enhanced the ICL capabilities of language models
483   through additional training procedures (Huang et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024). Min
484   et al. (2022a) and Chen et al. (2022) perform meta-learning with in-context examples on a wide col-
485   lection of tasks, with the goal of adapting to a new task at inference time through few-shot in-context
      examples. Other works have explored improving performance through more principled approaches


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      to select in-context examples during inference (Zhang et al., 2022; Sorensen et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
487   2024c; Qin et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024c). A simple and popular approach is to retrieve examples
488   that are most similar to the input (Liu et al., 2022; Rubin et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023c). Providing
489   in-context examples to re-ranking models has been studied in prior work (Drozdov et al., 2023), but
490   the potential of augmenting retrievers themselves by leveraging in-context examples remains un-
491   explored. Muennighoff et al. (2024) explored providing an in-context example out-of-the-box, but
492   showed an overall decrease in performance compared to zero-shot inference.
493
494   Retrieval Large language models pre-trained with autoregressive setups (Jiang et al., 2023; Dubey
495   et al., 2024) have shown remarkable performance when adapted to retrieval tasks (Wang et al.,
496   2024b; BehnamGhader et al., 2024), outperforming encoder-style retrievers (Izacard et al., 2022;
497
      Wang et al., 2024a). Despite these advancements, a challenge that remains is the ability to tailor
      retrieval systems to specific tasks or queries. To address this, a recent line of work explores incorpo-
498
      rating instructions into retrieval by training models to use task-specific instructions along with the
499
      query (Su et al., 2023; Asai et al., 2023). Oh et al. (2024) and Weller et al. (2024a) further propose
500   using instructions that are specific to each query. Another well-established technique in retrieval
501   is query expansion (Jagerman et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024), where the query is
502   augmented with additional terms to enrich the embedding as a form of relevance feedback (Lv &
503   Zhai, 2009). Recent efforts have focused on applying LLMs to expand the original query before
504   retrieval (Wang et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2024). These techniques are not mutually exclusive, and
505   can be integrated with our approach.
506
507
      8   C ONCLUSION
508
509
      In this paper, we explored augmenting in-context examples to retrieval models. Building on the lim-
510   itations of existing retriever models in following in-context examples, we introduced RARe, a sim-
511   ple strategy that equips retrievers with the ability to leverage in-context examples by training with
512   semantically similar in-context examples. Through detailed experiments and analyses, we demon-
513   strated that RARe consistently improves performance across various architectures and downstream
514   retrieval tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of in-context learning for retriever models.
515
516
      9   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
517
518
      Similar to in-context settings in autoregressive models, a limitation of our approach is the require-
519
      ment for a set of in-context examples in the form of (q, d ) pairs at inference time. RARe also
                                                                    +
520
      introduces additional latency at inference time due to the encoding of in-context examples in the
521   augmented query. This latency becomes more pronounced with longer documents, resulting in cor-
522   respondingly extended queries. While the overhead is particularly significant for small indexes, it
523   diminishes as the size of the index grows. To address these challenges, future research could explore
524   several avenues, such as using efficient long-context retrievers (Saad-Falcon et al., 2024; Zhang
525   et al., 2024) as a backbone, or developing extractive and/or abstractive compression techniques on in-
526   context documents to reduce query length. In this work, we used BM25 due to its lightweight nature
527   to retrieve nearest neighbour examples. Future work could explore stronger models and approaches
528   to reduce latency. Our current experiments are limited to English-language tasks, with potential to
529
      expand the scope to multilingual settings. Future work could explore curating synthetic data, an in-
      creasingly popular area of study for embedding models (Lee et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024b; Weller
530
      et al., 2024b), but for training with in-context examples. Future work could also explore developing
531
      new contrastive objectives to provide better signals during training with in-context examples.

868   Hyperparameters For fine-tuning Llama-3-8B, we follow the setting outlined in Ma et al. (2023).
869   We train on 4 H100 GPUs with per-device batch size 8 and gradient accumulation steps 4. We
870   apply LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) with r=32, temperature of 0.01, learning rate 1e-4 with 100 warmup
871   steps. We use a sequence length of 512 for documents and 1024 for queries as in-context augmented
872   queries are longer. For RARe we use a mixture of 70% examples with in-context examples and 30%
873   without Table 16.
874   When fine-tuning existing retriever models (E5-Mistral-Instruct, LLM2Vec-Llama-3-8B-
875   Supervised), we follow a setting similar to BehnamGhader et al. (2024). We train on 8
876   H100 GPUs with a largest possible per-device batch size of 32 along with 2 gradient accumulation
877   steps. We consider a random subset of 100K examples from the public E5 dataset mixture (Springer
878   et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b). We use a learning rate of 2e-4, maximum sequence length 1024,
879   warmup ratio 0.1 for 1 epoch. For E5-Mistral-Instruct, we apply LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) r=16, and
880
      r=4 for LLM2Vec-Llama-3-8B-Supervised since a higher rank was leading to severe overfitting on
      the instruction baseline.
