011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Electrocardiograms (ECGs) capture the electrical activity of the heart, offering
014            rich diagnostic and prognostic insights. Traditionally, electrocardiograms are in-
015            terpreted by human experts, but deep learning is now encroaching on this do-
016
               main and combining human-like intelligence with machine precision for a deeper
               insight. Self-supervised pretraining is essential for maximising the potential of
017
               scarce medical data. Applied to ECGs, patient-contrastive learning has shown
018
               promising results, by utilising the natural variations in the cardiac signals. In
019            this study, we introduce Temporally Augmented Patient Contrastive Learning of
020            Representations (T A-P CLR), a novel approach that incorporates temporal aug-
021            mentations into a patient contrastive self-supervised foundation model. Trained
022            on one of the largest diverse cohorts of more than six million unlabelled electro-
023            cardiograms from three continents, we demonstrate the efficacy of our approach
024            and show its value as a feature extraction tool for small and medium-sized la-
025            beled datasets. We also validate the performance on an open-source external co-
026            hort, surpassing other pretraining approaches while outperforming an ensemble
027
               of fully supervised deep networks on some labels. Additionally, we conduct a
               detailed exploration of how the pretraining and labeled electrocardiogram dataset
028
               distributions impact supervised task performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Electrocardiograms (ECGs) record cardiac electrical activity as a graph of voltage versus time.
034
      Electrodes are placed on the body surface to detect the electrical changes resulting from cardiac
035
      muscle depolarization and repolarization. The standard electrode positions define twelve different
036   leads (signals between two specific electrodes) representing cardiac activity along twelve axes, es-
037   sential for localising the underlying processes. The heart’s electrical activity has been recorded
038   since 1887 (Waller, 1887) and has been an important source of information for cardiologists and
039   physicians. Human understanding has since evolved to interpret ECG patterns as manifestations of
040   different health conditions. Being a simple non-invasive investigation, it is part of routine medical
041   care although the expertise for accurate interpretation is not so readily available.
042   Deep learning has excellent pattern recognition capabilities, surpassing any other methodology, and
043   holds great potential for medical sciences (Esteva et al., 2019). The traditional ECG interpretation
044   depends on the distinct patterns of the waveforms combined with an understanding of the heart func-
045   tion and clinical observations. Human perception is limited by low visual accuracy, gaps in theoret-
046   ical knowledge, and the complexity of the diverse, non-linear, interrelations (Strodthoff et al., 2021)
047   . Deep learning has demonstrated high precision in predicting cardiac diseases (Sau et al., 2023;
048   2024; Pastika et al., 2024) while opening the possibility of predicting novel labels like age and sex
049   that are beyond human capabilities (Attia et al., 2019). Artificial intelligence has the potential to
050
      surpass human capabilities while allowing for accurate diagnosis and risk stratification.
051   Contrastive learning, a self-supervised pretraining approach, can greatly improve the accuracy for
052   subsequent supervised tasks, especially where the labeled datasets are quite small (Chen et al., 2020).
053   Contrastive learning extracts meaningful representation using a notion of positive and negative in-
      stances (Chen et al., 2020). Representations of positive instances are trained to be more similar


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      and distinct from representations of negative instances. There are diverse contrastive learning ap-
055   proaches, mostly differing in their definitions of the positive and negative instances and the loss
056   computations. In the visual image domain, the positives are usually augmentations (transforma-
057   tions) of the same image while the negatives are augmentations from others. The training retains the
058   meaningful features shared between the positives while discarding the extraneous features.
059
      Contrastive learning has been leveraged for feature extraction from biomedical data with various
060
      definitions of data augmentations (Mohsenvand et al., 2020). The augmentations applied to medical
061   data should preserve clinically significant information. Data from the same patient across time is a
062   robust way to encode trivial transformations (Jamaludin et al., 2017; Diamant et al., 2022), thereby
063   allowing the model to reject any artifacts arising from instrument noise, different lead locations, pa-
064   tient movement, etc. Combining different types of random augmentations has remarkably enhanced
065   the performance for contrastive learning (Chen et al., 2020). Incorporating temporal augmentations
066   with patient-based contrastive learning is a key novel feature of our work. We hypothesise that
067   multiple transformations enhance the quality of representations and the selection of clinically in-
068   significant augmentations is essential for the generalisation of the approach. We thus improve on
069
      existing patient contrastive methods, by adding temporal augmentation like zero-masking (Soltanieh
      et al., 2022) and random cropping for our Temporally Augmented Patient Contrastive Learning for
070
      ECG Representations (T A-P CLR). In the medical domain, labeled data is often highly scarce,
071
      and thus pretraining approaches are essential for maximum utilisation of available data. Foundation
072   models can be pretrained on large unlabeled datasets, to learn general features that can be leveraged
073   for a range of subsequent supervised tasks (Zhang & Metaxas, 2024). We train a foundation model
074   on 6, 174, 025 ECGs to improve generalisation for ECG feature extraction and explore the effect
075   of pretraining data on the quality of the learned representations. The main contribution of our work
076   is to present a new foundation model for ECG interpretation. Our model achieves state-of-the-art
077   results due to:
078
079
             • a new method: Temporally Augmented Patient Contrastive learning that incorporates aug-
               mentations along the temporal axis.
080
081          • a new multi-center dataset we constructed for training with over six million ECGs with
082            four cohorts from three different continents.
083
      Our augmentation techniques improve on previous contrastive learning techniques based on con-
084
      trasting between exams from the same patient (Diamant et al., 2022). Our newly constructed dataset
085   allows us to showcase, the effect of training data demographics, on the quality of the learned rep-
086   resentations. We present a foundation model that outperforms other, much larger, self-supervised
087   foundation models (Song et al., 2024) and is close to highly optimised fully supervised bench-
088   marks (Strodthoff et al., 2021).
089
      Section 2 presents a brief overview of the past efforts conducted for the contrastive learning of
090
      electrocardiograms. Section 3 describes the implementation details of our approach, followed by
091   the performance analysis in Section 4. Section 5 concludes with the highlights of our research
092   findings and presents some suggestions for future research.

      2   L ITERATURE BACKGROUND
096
097
      Contrastive learning Contrastive learning has shown remarkable improvement for image classi-
      fication tasks, by incorporating a self-supervised pretraining (Hadsell et al., 2006). In the computer
098
      vision domain, pretraining enhances the similarity of the images sharing context (positives) while
099
      reducing that from distinct images (negatives). Efficient techniques such as Inf oN CE (van den
100   Oord et al., 2018) present a robust encoding of the contrastive loss, and the SimCLR (Chen et al.,
101   2020) introduces the idea of a non-linear projection layer for performance improvement.
102
103   Data augmentations Data augmentations are essential for contrastive learning to enhance mean-
104   ingful context and reject spurious information. The augmentations of visual images can be per-
105   formed by using different segments, views, and coloring of the original image, retaining the mean-
106   ingful context (Chen et al., 2020). The dimensions for augmentation are more limited for the
107   time series data and mainly involve noise addition, time-masking, cropping, shuffling, inverting,
      etc (Wen et al., 2021). Previous work has shown that good augmentations are crucial to pre-


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      serving only meaningful features and greatly affect the generalisation for subsequent supervised
109   tasks. The diversity in negative and positive examples is also essential for learning meaning-
110   ful features. Combining multiple augmentations has been proved to reinforce the learning pro-
111   cess (Chen et al., 2020; Gopal et al., 2021). For medical data, augmentations are also restricted by
112   possible clinical implications, therefore synthetic augmentations have to be taken with care. Past
113   work has shown that while some augmentations might improve the performance on one task, they
114   may have adverse effects on others (Lee et al., 2022; Raghu et al., 2022).
115
116   Contrastive learning for ECGs Contrastive learning has been employed for ECG representation
117   learning. CLOCS (Kiyasseh et al., 2021) presents the idea of using ECG from the same patient
118   as a meaningful context, with the different leads and non-overlapping slices from the same ECG as
119
      positives, thereby incorporating multiple positive ECGs in the batch, thus improving performance
      over the SimCLR baseline (Chen et al., 2020). P CLR (Diamant et al., 2022) takes the concept
120
      further defining ECG from the same patient over time as positive instances and demonstrates su-
121
      perior performance compared to previous approaches. The contrastive heartbeats (CT -HB) (Wei
122   et al., 2022) splits the individual heartbeats from an ECG recording and defines heartbeats from
123   the same ECG as positives and implements a variant of triplet loss (Wang et al., 2019). Soltanieh
124   et al. (2022) systematically explores a spectrum of time series augmentations for ECGs including
125   time-warping, permutation (slice and shuffle), inverting, and scaling, which nonetheless could have
126   clinical implications. Physiologically-inspired spatial and temporal augmentations including axis ro-
127   tation, scaling, and zero-masking, are combined by the 3KG (Gopal et al., 2021) for self-supervised
128   pretraining, improving ECG classification performance. ECG − F M (McKeen et al., 2024) em-
129   ploys a multi-layer convolutional feature extractor and a transformer encoder for feature extrac-
130
      tion with random-lead-masking augmentation. The joint cross-dimensional contrastive learning
      approach (Liu et al., 2023) is based on learning ECG representation by contrasting ECG signals
131
      against images incorporating several modes of augmentations. M ERL (Liu et al., 2024) contrasts
132
      ECGs with clinical reports to provide the possibility of zero-shot inference.
133
134   Generative pretraining: Self-supervised pretraining has been implemented following genera-
135   tive approaches, such as masked autoencoders (M AE) reconstructing random masked ECG seg-
136   ments (Gedon et al., 2021; Na et al., 2024). Hybrid techniques combining contrastive learning and
137   generative pretraining based on transformer architecture have been implemented for ECG feature
138   extraction (Song et al., 2024).
139
140   Foundation models Foundation models are defined by the flexibility to facilitate generic down-
141   stream tasks by exploiting huge pre-training cohorts (Zhang & Metaxas, 2024). All of the self-
142   pretraining methodologies have the potential to adapt to any generic task and large cohorts can fur-
143   ther enhance the model capabilities. HeartBeiT (Vaid et al., 2023) exploits vision transformer archi-
144
      tecture to present an ECG-based foundational model. (Song et al., 2024) trained a foundation ECG
      model on more than a million ECGs exploiting a hybrid approach combining contrastive learn-
145
      ing with generative pretraining involving vision transformers. ECG − F M (McKeen et al., 2024)
146
      employs wav2vec architecture with a convolutional feature extractor and a BERT-like transformer
147   encoder trained on 1.6 million ECGs. Foundation models have also been developed following a
148   supervised approach (Li et al., 2024) where the generalization capabilities may be limited.
149
150   Our approach Our work is a natural extension and improvement of these efforts. We combine
151   patient-based augmentation with simple temporal augmentations based on random zero-masking and
152   cropping without impacting the underlying clinical information, thus making the approach robust.
153   We further train on a large diverse cohort for improved generalization and explore the impact of the
154   pretraining data demographics on the learned representations. We also demonstrate that label-based
155   performance comparisons for diverse datasets may not reflect the true merits of an approach.

      3.3   AUGMENTATION
210
211   Generalization is an important aspect of a foundation model, we refrain from scaling, rotating, or
212   frequency-warping employed in some past pre-training approaches (Soltanieh et al., 2022) that may
213   potentially impact the performance for any unforeseen future supervised tasks where the scale, axis,
214   or rhythm may be important information (Raghu et al., 2022). We reason that contrasting for patient
215   identity (Diamant et al., 2022) is a natural way of encoding trivial transformation and we combine
      it with temporal augmentations, such as zero-masking (Gopal et al., 2021; Soltanieh et al., 2022)


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      and random cropping thus ensuring that the pretraining will be relevant for any potential future
217   supervised task.
218
219   We define the positive views as augmentations of random slices from different ECGs of the same
220
      patient. The number of ECGs from each patient greatly differs thus the training epoch is defined
      as one complete iteration for all unique patients with the positive views randomly sampled at train-
221
      ing time. In this way, the training is not biased by patients having more ECGs, while exploiting
222
      the available data diversity. In contrast to Diamant et al. (2022), ECGs in a positive pair are al-
223   ways unique instances. The input window size of seven seconds allows random cropping (RC)
224   by using a different patch from the same ten-second ECG for each epoch. The additional tem-
225   poral augmentation includes zero-masking (Soltanieh et al., 2022; Raghu et al., 2022) that is unim-
226   pactful of any intrinsic clinical information. The transformations are applied at the training time
227   so for the same ECG, the slices and masks are unique for each epoch, increasing the diversity of
228   the positive samples. We experimented with applying zero-masking to the same random segments
229   for each lead (RZM ), different random segments for each lead (RLZM ), masking random leads
230   (RLM ) (Oh et al., 2022), and a novel notion of using the raw and filtered ECGs as augmentations
231
      (RF )1 . We retained the simpler configuration of RZM , which showed the best performance.
232
233
      3.4   P REPROCESSING
234
235
      The standard procedure for ECG recording involves measurements from 12 leads recorded for 10
236
      seconds with sampling rates typically at 400 to 500 samples per second (Hz). For model develop-
237   ment, we used eight ECG leads as four leads are linear combinations of other leads and thus do
238   not impart additional information (Eem et al., 2020). We apply a bandpass filter (0.5 to 100 Hz)
239   and a notch filter relevant to the mains frequency and interpolate ECGs from different sources to
240   a standard sampling frequency of 400 Hz. We retain the original scale of the ECGs in millivolts.
241   The final input shape to the contrastive learning model is 2800 × 8 (7 second signal).

244   3.5   A RCHITECTURE
245
246   Figure 1 presents an overview of the T A-P CLR architecture. The ECGs for the same patient are
247   treated as positive views while all other ECGs in the batch are negative views. For a fair compari-
248
      son, we use the backbone architecture from Ribeiro et al. (2020) with non-linear projections (similar
      to P CLR (Diamant et al., 2022)). The contrastive loss is applied to the non-linear projections of the
249
      ECGs. The output of the model is 256 features or embeddings learned from the ECGs that can be
250
      exploited for any downstream supervised training. The model is implemented in Tensorflow (Abadi
251   et al., 2015) (2.10.1). The total number of parameters is less than 6 million and thus the model is
252   much more compact, as compared to the transformer-based architectures like ECG − F M , having
253   more than 300 million parameters (McKeen et al., 2024).

256   3.6   T RAINING
257
258   The contrastive loss performs best with larger batches due to the larger variation of the negative
259   instances (Chen et al., 2020), but the computation resources limit the batch size. We use a batch size
260   of 1024 (512 patients) and train the model for 200 epochs using the Adam optimizer (Kingma &
261   Ba, 2014). The initial learning rate is 0.1 and then decayed according to a half-period cosine sched-
262
      ule (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2016), similar to previous approaches (Chen et al., 2020; Diamant et al.,
      2022). The training time in minutes per epoch on NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3090 is approximately 5
263
      for the BIDM C dataset and 50 for the BCSV dataset. This is notably shorter than comparative
264
      approaches in the literature taking weeks on multi-gpu configurations (Cheng et al., 2021; Song
265   et al., 2024; McKeen et al., 2024).

291   4     P ERFORMANCE A NALYSIS
292
293   The T A-P CLR is a pretraining paradigm for unlabelled data, thus the performance of the learned
294   embeddings is evaluated by subsequent supervised training similar to previous works (Chen et al.,
295   2020). Following are the different performance evaluation configurations employed in the current
296   work, depending on the experiment at hand:
297
298          • Linear evaluation: A linear probe is a standard methodology to compare the expressiveness
299
               of the features learned during unsupervised pretraining approaches (Chen et al., 2020). The
               feature-generating model is frozen while a single neuron is trained to predict each label.
300
301          • Multi-layer perceptron (M LP ): We also train a two-layer M LP for the supervised task to
302            allow the model to learn a non-linear mapping of the T A-P CLR generated features.
303          • Fine-tuning: After training a linear model the feature-generating model can also be allowed
304            to update and further improve the performance.
305
306   Validation is performed along two main dimensions: proof of concept by internal validation of the
307   approach for different experimental configurations, and external validation by comparing to previous
308   benchmarks. The test evaluation involves a single prediction from a random crop of each test ECG.
309   We mainly report macro-averaged area under the receiver-operating characteristic curve AU ROC
310
      or AU C as a threshold-free assessment of classification performance and mean absolute error M AE
      in years for the age regression performance, as suggested previously (Wagner et al., 2020; Strodthoff
311
      et al., 2021). Unless otherwise specified, we report the mean of ten independent runs for each task.

      distributions, patient count, and ECG numbers. The BIDM C, CODE, SHZS, P T B-XL, and
      U KB datasets contain the age and sex of the patients for each ECG. The pretrained models are
394
      used to extract features from these cohorts. The features are then leveraged for age and sex predic-
395
      tion with the train/val/test splits for all datasets consisting of 10k/2k/2k labeled instances, to remove
396   any dataset size bias from the supervised training. Figure 3 compares the performance of the age
397   and sex prediction across multiple pretrained feature extraction models and labeled datasets, using
398   a similar configuration for the supervised setup. The right-hand panel presents the AU C for the sex
399   prediction while the left-hand panel shows the M AE for age prediction, as the mean of six runs.
400
401
      How does the pretraining cohort affect performance? The following observations and insights
402   can be obtained from the test that we believe to be essential for future work. The model pretrained on
403   BCSV (i.e. the combination of BIDM C, CODE, SHZS, and V U M C datasets) outperforms for
404   all labels and has the best generalisation capabilities for a foundation model. Apart from the BCSV ,
405   the model pretrained on the same labeled dataset generally performs the best, thus highlighting the
406   importance of external open-source cohorts for performance comparison. It is interesting to note,
407   that while the CODE and SHZS have more patients and ECGs, the performance is generally de-
408   creased compared to the secondary care cohorts of BIDM C and V U M C. A plausible explanation
409   can be that the learned features are more expressive when pretrained from a more diseased popula-
410
      tion as there is more diversity in ECG patterns, which is the case for the BIDM C and V U M C
      datasets. Moreover, the performance does not only depend on the number of unique patients but
411
      also the diversity of the positive examples i.e., the ECGs per patient. Looking back at Table 1,
412
      the BIDM C has far fewer unique patients and ECGs than V U M C, but has a higher number of
413   ECGs per patient that may help it achieve comparable performance.
414
415
      Does the labeled dataset impact performance? The performance metric for a labeled dataset also
416
      depends on the distribution of the underlying population health. The U KB supervised tasks show
417
      the highest AU C and lowest M AE for the same pretrained model, being a healthy volunteer cohort.
418   Therefore, a metric for a particular label cannot be used for performance comparison across diverse
419   datasets. Several previous works compare their results based on a specific training task (McKeen
420   et al., 2024), which may be meaningful only when model evaluation is compared on the same dataset
421   and ideally the same train/test splits. The model, pretrained on the CODE and SHZS datasets,
422   shows lower performance compared to much smaller datasets. One reason can be that these datasets
423   come from a healthier population and consequently reduced diversity. Additionally, the population
424   of SHZS being more ethnically distinct can also impact the performance on diverse datasets. The
425   performance of the patient-based approach is highly dependent on the diversity, number, and health
426
      of the underlying population.

523   5       C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
524
525
      In this work we present T A-P CLR, applying a novel combination of temporal augmentations
526
      and patient-based ECG contrastive learning, enhancing performance in downstream supervised
527
      tasks. We demonstrate that T A-P CLR is superior to fully supervised training methods on small to
528   medium-sized datasets, proving to be especially valuable in scenarios where labeled data is limited.
529
530   Pretraining with combined datasets from three continents, forming one of the largest and most di-
531
      verse, multi-site ECG cohorts, further improves the performance for downstream supervised tasks
      and sets new benchmarks when evaluated in external validation using the P T B − XL dataset.
532
      Looking ahead, we plan to explore the impact of additional data augmentations on the learned rep-
533
      resentations and focus on enhancing the interpretability of the features learned by T A-P CLR (some
534   work on interpretability is presented in Appendix C). The current work demonstrates the capabilities
535   of our foundation model for a range of generic tasks without increasing the model’s complexity. An
536   interesting research direction for future work is exploring model scalability while noting that the rule
537   of ten times more data than model parameters from prior research (Alwosheel et al., 2018) and our
538   remarkable results attest to the fact that the model size maybe adequate. The strong performance,
539   generalizability, and efficiency of T A-P CLR in terms of training time and network size, positions
      it as a powerful foundation model.

      Figure 6 further explores the interpretability of the model using GradCam (Selvaraju et al., 2017)
912   . The top two ECGs are examples with positive class ST T C, while the bottom two ECGs are
913   negatives. The gradients are superimposed on the input signal and are represented in red color
914   where the darker color indicates higher importance. The ST T C class is related to abnormalities in
915   the ST segment (de Luna et al., 2005) of the ECG. The plots show that the model can recognize the
