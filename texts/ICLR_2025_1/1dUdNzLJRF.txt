009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Given the widespread adoption and usage of Large Language Models (LLMs), it is
012            crucial to have flexible and interpretable evaluations of their instruction-following
013            ability. Preference judgments between model outputs have become the de facto
014            evaluation standard, despite distilling complex, multi-faceted preferences into a
015            single ranking. Furthermore, as human annotation is slow and costly, LLMs are
016            increasingly used to make these judgments, at the expense of reliability and in-
017            terpretability. In this work, we propose TICK (Targeted Instruct-evaluation with
018            ChecKlists), a fully automated, interpretable evaluation protocol that structures
019
               evaluations with LLM-generated, instruction-specific checklists. We first show
               that, given an instruction, LLMs can reliably produce high-quality, tailored evalu-
020
               ation checklists that decompose the instruction into a series of YES/NO questions.
021
               Each question asks whether a candidate response meets a specific requirement of
022            the instruction. We demonstrate that using TICK leads to a significant increase
023            (46.4% → 52.2%) in the frequency of exact agreements between LLM judge-
024            ments and human preferences, as compared to having an LLM directly score an
025            output. We then show that STICK (Self-TICK) can be used to improve generation
026            quality across multiple benchmarks via self-refinement and Best-of-N selection.
027            STICK self-refinement on LiveBench reasoning tasks leads to an absolute gain of
028            +7.8%, whilst Best-of-N selection with STICK attains +6.3% absolute improve-
029            ment on the real-world instruction dataset, WildBench. In light of this, structured,
030
               multi-faceted self-improvement is shown to be a promising way to further advance
               LLM capabilities. Finally, by providing LLM-generated checklists to human eval-
031
               uators tasked with directly scoring LLM responses to WildBench instructions, we
032
               notably increase inter-annotator agreement (0.194 → 0.256).

      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Instruction-tuned Large Language Models (LLMs) are widely used as conversational assistants,
039
      where users expect responses to closely follow their intents (Wei et al., 2022a; Mishra et al., 2022;
      Bai et al., 2022a; Ouyang et al., 2022). The broad usage of LLMs creates a critical demand for
040
      reliable, flexible, and transparent ways of evaluating their instruction-following abilities. How-
041
      ever, standard evaluation methods, such as preference labeling (Ouyang et al., 2022), direct scoring
042   (Novikova et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2023b), and Elo rating (Bai et al., 2022a; Glaese et al., 2022),
043   tend to obscure the reasoning behind evaluations. These methods also often result in significant dis-
044   agreements, both among human annotators (Hosking et al., 2024) and between models and humans
045   (Qin et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2023).
046
      To address these limitations, we introduce TICK (Targeted Instruct-evaluation with ChecKlists),
047
      a novel approach to LLM-as-judge evaluation that uses the judge LLM to decompose instructions
048   into checklists consisting of a series of YES/NO evaluation questions. These checklists provide
049   interpretable, fine-grained assessments of whether a model response satisfies specific requirements
050   of the instruction. Crucially, TICK eliminates manual effort in checklist creation, a substantial
051   cost for existing checklist-based benchmarks (Qin et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2024). We rigorously
052   demonstrate that current LLMs can already generate checklists matching the quality of human-
053   written ones. In experiments, we show that using TICK leads to an absolute increase in the frequency
      of exact agreements between an LLM judge and human preferences of 5.8%.

089   In summary, we make the following contributions:
090
      1. We rigorously show that LLMs can generate evaluation checklists similar in quality to those
091
         written by trained human annotators across multiple diverse instruction-following datasets.
092
093

094
         fully automated, it is cheaper and faster to run than existing checklist-based evaluations, and can
095

096

097

098

099
         ing an LLM output, and find that this improves inter-annotator agreement.

132   Language Critiques: LLM critiques are an intuitive way of addressing the “black box” nature
133
      of evaluations. These critiques are intended to point out the strengths and weaknesses of outputs
      generated by the same LLM, or a different LLM. Critiques can be used to improve the quality of
134
      overall evaluations performed by an LLM judge or reward model (Ankner et al., 2024; Bai et al.,
135
      2022b; Wang et al., 2023a; Ye et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024), inform human evaluations (Saunders
136   et al., 2022; McAleese et al., 2024), or provide feedback that can be used to refine a response in-
137   context (Scheurer et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2024; Madaan et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024). Meanwhile,
138   a number of papers provide evidence that naively prompting LLMs to self-correct or find reasoning
139   errors can lead to performance degradation (Huang et al., 2024; Tyen et al., 2024; Kamoi et al.,
140   2024). By using the targeted and structured nature of checklist-based evaluations, we achieve self-
141   refinement that outperforms unstructured feedback and works on a broad range tasks.

143   3     TICK: TARGETED I NSTRUCT- EVALUATION WITH C HEC K LISTS
144
145   We present an approach to automatically and robustly evaluating instruction-tuned LLMs that is
146   not restricted to any particular dataset. To do so, we use an LLM to generate checklists of targeted
147   YES/NO evaluation questions for a given instruction. We then also use an LLM to evaluate responses
148   with respect to each checklist question, exploiting the fact that the decomposed task of answering a
149   single, targeted question is much simpler than coming up with a holistic score or preference ranking.
150   Individual checklist answers can then be aggregated to produce an overall score or preference. In
151   this section, we provide details for each of these steps and experimentally validate their effectiveness
152   by analysing agreement between LLMs and a pool of trained human annotators at each stage. All
153
      prompts used are included in Appendix G.
154

161   the question is targeting. To obtain these instruction-specific checklists, we prompt an LLM with
      a few-shot template that specifies the instruction and the YES/NO constraint. This prompt also

342   4     I N -C ONTEXT S ELF -I MPROVEMENT WITH STICK (S ELF -TICK)
343
344   Having shown that TICK provides a signal of model response quality on par with trained human
345   annotators, we investigate how it can be used to improve responses. We first explore using STICK
346   (Self-TICK) evaluations as feedback for self-refinement. Our hypothesis is that because STICK is
347   targeted and interpretable, it is more informative for response refinement than unstructured feedback
348
      such as vanilla Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2023). Secondly, we investigate STICK’s effectiveness
      at Best-of-N selection. In both cases, we are using the generating LLM as its own test-time judge.
349
350
351   4.1   S ELF -R EFINEMENT WITH C HECKLISTS AS F EEDBACK
352
353
      Approach: Given an instruction, we first generate an initial response from an LLM. We then
      use the same LLM to generate a checklist and evaluate its original response against this checklist.
354
      As checklist evaluations contain precise information about how a response succeeded or failed,
355
      they can be used as targeted, actionable feedback. Whenever the previous response did not pass
356   all checklist evaluations, we prompt the LLM with a refinement prompt template (see Appendix
357   G), which contains the instruction, the previous response, and the STICK evaluations, in order to
358   generate a refined response. This process can be optionally repeated for several iterations.
359
360   Validation: We compare self-refinement with STICK against the use of unstructured feedback
361   gathered by prompting the LLM to provide a detailed critique of its previous response. We refer
362   to this baseline as vanilla Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2023). We first run four iterations of self-

399   4.2      B EST- OF -N S ELECTION WITH C HECKLIST-BASED S CORES
400
401   Approach: A common approach to maximising response quality for a given LLM is to sample N
402
      responses to an instruction, and then use a scoring function, such as a reward model or LLM-as-
      judge, to select the best response. Best-of-N selection thus produces higher quality responses at the
403
      cost of additional inference compute (Snell et al., 2024). Focusing specifically on self -selection, as
404
      this assumes no access to an external reward model or superior LLM, we use STICK for Best-of-N
405   selection by generating N candidate responses to an instruction from an LLM, using the same LLM
406   to self-evaluate each candidate with STICK, and using the STICK score for selection.
407
408
409   Validation: We compare STICK for Best-of-N selection against direct self-scoring (i.e., prompt-
410   ing for a single holistic score). We include using an external reward model, ArmoRM-Llama3-
411   8B-v0.1 (Wang et al., 2024a), as an additional baseline, despite the fact that doing so breaks the
412   assumption of only having access to the generating LLM for scoring responses. We evaluate on
413   InFoBench and WildBench, again evaluating by using each benchmarks’ standard evaluation metric
414
      (defined above). For each score function, if multiple generations have the same maximum score
      according to that score function, we keep all of the tied responses. We then compute the true score
415
      according to the task’s actual evaluation protocol, and average across all selected responses in the
416
      case of ties. We also compute the precision of each score function. When there are no ties under the
417   Best-of-N selecting score function or the ground truth score function, computing precision reduces
418   to computing accuracy. When there are ties, precision penalises selecting any additional responses
419   that are not the best, or tying for best, under the ground truth score function. However, unlike recall,
420   precision does not penalise failing to select more than one response out of any that are tying for
421   best under the ground truth score function. We use Command-R+ to generate responses, as well as
422   self-evaluate responses with STICK and direct self-scoring.3

426   function, meaning that it most closely aligns with selections made under each benchmarks’ ground
427   truth evaluation. STICK is more precise on InFoBench than WildBench, whilst the inverse is true for
428   the other scores. This is likely because InFoBench scores responses against evaluation checklists,

      5.1   M OTIVATION
444
445
      In spite of recent progress in automatic evaluation (Ankner et al., 2024; Chiang & Lee, 2023; Verga
446   et al., 2024; Vu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a;b; Ye et al., 2024), human evaluation remains a
447   critical component of LLM quality assessment. We thus investigate whether LLM-generated eval-
448   uation checklists can help with arriving at a consistent score for a given response. Using checklists
449   partially decomposes the annotation task to be more cognitively feasible, and helps to ensure that
450   specific considerations relevant to each instruction are not missed.
451
452
453   5.2   C ASE S TUDY: S CORING R ESPONSES TO W ILD B ENCH I NSTRUCTIONS
454
455   We conduct two rounds of human evaluation on a set of Command-R+ responses to WildBench in-
456   structions. In both rounds, annotators provide an integer score from 1-5 for each response (further
457   details in Appendix H.4). In one round, annotators are asked to first answer checklist questions
458   generated by GPT-4o before providing each overall score (i.e., check-then-score). Annotators are
459   instructed to use the checklists to inform their score where appropriate, but not to limit their as-
460   sessment to the checklists. This holistic scoring is important for human evaluation, as we found
461
      that a number of edge cases arise that can lead evaluators to answer most checklist questions with
      YES and still justifiably provide a low overall score (see Appendix H.5 for an example). Each re-
462
      sponse is triply annotated and we compute inter-annotator agreement using Krippendorff’s alpha
463
      (Krippendorff, 1980).
464
465   Results in Table 6 indicate that annotating the checklist before scoring yields stronger agreement
466   among evaluators than direct scoring. We also find that the average evaluation score for Command-
467
      R+ across both settings stays consistent, implying that the increase in agreement corresponds to
      variance reduction without having a biasing effect on the aggregate score. However, we note that
468
      agreement is still low, despite being improved, which highlights the challenges of gathering annota-
469
      tions with strong agreement on real-world instruction data.

      6     C ONCLUSION
473
474
475   We introduce TICK, a fully automatic evaluation protocol that structures evaluations with an LLM-
476   generated, instruction-specific checklist. We show that LLMs can produce high-quality checklists
477
      that improve agreement between judge LLMs and humans. Because TICK is fully automatic, it
      can easily and cheaply be applied in new settings, avoiding the need for humans to write or re-
478
      view checklists. We next demonstrate that STICK (Self-TICK) can be used for in-context self-
479
      improvement by either self-refinement or by Best-of-N selection. Our experiments show that both
480   strategies of employing STICK lead to substantial improvements over baselines on multiple diverse
481   instruction-following datasets, including LiveBench, which covers challenging math, code, and rea-
482   soning prompts that baselines fail to improve on. Finally, when we provide human annotators LLM-
483   generated checklists for evaluating LLM outputs, we find inter-annotator agreement improves con-
484   siderably. Overall, we show that LLMs are capable of accurately evaluating instruction-following
485   ability when using structured checklists, and demonstrate the potential of this rich fine-grained feed-
      back to further improve LLM capabilities.

      L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
487
488   TICK and STICK are useful tools for evaluation and self-improvement, respectively. However, we
489   acknowledge that checklists are only one heuristic for structuring evaluation; learned or discovered
490   evaluation structures are an exciting direction for future work. Checklist evaluations do not present
491   an advantage in all settings, especially given the additional inference cost of generating the checklist.
492   For example, basic knowledge retrieval is best evaluated as simply correct or incorrect. Relying on
493   LLMs at all steps in the evaluation protocol may also propagate, and even exacerbate, LLM biases.
494
      In this work, we do not investigate self-improvement by fine-tuning on synthetic, STICK-selected
      data, but doing so is a natural next step. Training reward models to condition on, or jointly produce,
495
      checklist evaluations is also a promising direction for future study.

1960   ## Key Concept: Checklist Question
1961
1962   ### Definition
1963
1964
       Checklist questions are Yes/No questions corresponding to whether
       specific criteria relevant to the user request were successfully
1965
       followed in a model response.

1967   ## Tips and Tricks
1968   - **Answer ‘Yes‘ to a checklist question if the response entirely
1969   fulfils the condition.** Note that even minor inaccuracies should
1970   exclude the response from receiving a ‘Yes‘ rating.
1971   - **Answer ‘No‘ **to a checklist question if the response fails
1972   to meet the condition or provides no information that could be
1973   used to answer the question.** For instance, if the question
1974   asks, ‘‘Is the second sentence in the generated text a compound
1975
       sentence?’’ and the generated text only has one sentence, it
       offers no relevant information to answer the question.
1976
       Consequently, the answer should be ‘No‘.
1977
       - **Use answers to checklist questions to partially inform the
1978   overall response score.** A response that mostly fails the
1979   checklist should receive a low score. However, a response can
1980   also pass all of the checklist questions and still have a
1981   middle-range score if the overall quality is low. For example, a
1982   response could hypothetically pass all of the checklist questions
1983   but still be uninsightful, repetitive or poorly worded.

1990   this provides further evidence that LLM-generated checklists simplify the task of rating individual
