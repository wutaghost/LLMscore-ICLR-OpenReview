                                                  A BSTRACT
013
               Despite growing interest in incorporating feedback to improve language models,
014
               most efforts focus only on sequence-level annotations. In this work, we explore
015
               the potential of utilizing fine-grained span-level annotations from offline datasets
016            to improve model quality. We develop a simple finetuning algorithm, called Train-
017            ing with Annotations (TWA), to directly train machine translation models on such
018            annotated data. TWA utilizes targeted span-level error information while also
019            flexibly learning what to penalize within a span. Moreover, TWA considers the
020            overall trajectory of a sequence when deciding which non-error spans to utilize as
021            positive signals. Experiments on English-German and Chinese-English machine
022            translation show that TWA outperforms baselines such as Supervised FineTun-
023            ing on sequences filtered for quality and Direct Preference Optimization on pairs
024
               constructed from the same data.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Language models have advanced to the point where it is often difficult to improve them substantially
029
      via supervised finetuning on high-quality human-written examples alone; instead, recent efforts to
030   improve language model or sequence-to-sequence model performance have largely relied on anno-
031   tations of model generations, from preferences to per-sequence scores (Bai et al., 2022; Ethayarajh
032   et al., 2022; Lambert et al., 2023; Kopf et al., 2023). Such data, coupled with techniques to learn
033   from it (Christiano et al., 2017; Rafailov et al., 2023; Gulcehre et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2023), have
034   yielded impressive results for many top language models.
035
      Most efforts, however, consider only sequence-level labels, usually in the form of a scalar score
036   assigned to the entire output. In contrast, this work investigates the potential of using fine-grained
037   span-level annotations from offline datasets to enhance language model training. Unlike sequence-
038   level annotations, span-level annotations provide information about specific segments within a se-
039   quence, offering more detailed information for model learning. Moreover, in many situations, col-
040   lecting fine-grained information is similar effort to collecting sequence-level labels (?), making the
041   former a practical form of data for improving model performance given a method that can take
042   advantage of the information.
043   To explore the potential of fine-grained annotations, we focus on the Multidimensional Quality Met-
044   rics (MQM) data from previous Workshop on Machine Translation (WMT) Shared Tasks (Freitag
045   et al., 2021a). This data, used to evaluate the quality of machine translation systems, contains span-
046   level annotations of the errors present in a given translation as well as their category (e.g., fluency,
047   accuracy) and severity (e.g., major and minor). While MQM data has previously been used to de-
048   velop auxiliary reward or metrics models (Juraska et al., 2023; Rei et al., 2022), it has not been
049   directly employed for training machine translation (MT) models.
050   To directly utilize these translations and their span-level annotations to finetune an MT model, we
051   introduce a new algorithm called Training with Annotations (TWA). TWA utilizes span-level in-
052   formation from the annotations to treat error and non-error spans differently. For error spans, the
053   TWA loss seeks to decrease the probability of the span given the context while allowing the model
      to learn which tokens in the span to penalize to do so. For non-error tokens, TWA takes into account

085   2   MQM DATA
086
087   Each year, the Workshop on Machine Translation (WMT) hosts a shared task competition to assess
088   general machine translation capabilities across different domains and genres. Submitted MT systems
089   are scored and evaluated by humans, with top systems annotated via the Multidimensional Quality
090   Metrics (MQM) scheme (Freitag et al., 2021b; Rei et al., 2022). Namely, given the source text and
091   MT output, professional translators annotate any error spans in the output translation. Each error
092   span is annotated with the category of the error as well as the severity of the error. Each error span
093   is assigned a score of 25 for a non-translation, 5 for a major error, 0.1 for a minor punctuation error,
094   and 1 for any other minor error. The overall MQM score of an example sequence is the sum of the
095
      MQM scores of the annotated error spans in the sequence.
096   MQM annotations have been used to evaluate MT systems, as described above, but not as addi-
097   tional training signal to finetune MT models. Utilizing these annotations during training requires
098   developing a method that can take this information into account. We describe our proposed method,
099   Training with Annotations, in Section 4.
100
101   3   R ELATED W ORK
102
103   Utilizing MQM data. TWA is the first method to use span-level MQM data to directly finetune
104   machine translation models, but there exist other methods which also utilize sequence-level MQM
105   data indirectly. Namely, existing automated metrics in machine translation such as MetricX (Juraska
106   et al., 2023) utilize MQM scores as labels for training data, so methods which utilize these neural-
107   based automated metrics indirectly benefit from MQM data. Such approaches include QE reranking
      (Fernandes et al., 2022) or MBR decoding (Freitag et al., 2022) with neural quality metrics. Both


                                                                2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      methods can be used in tandem with TWA, as one could always decode a TWA-trained model with
109   either of these approaches. One could also use the results of such decoding methods to directly fine-
110   tune a model, commonly known as MBR or QE finetuning (Finkelstein & Freitag, 2024). However,
111   given the models powering automated metrics such as Metric-X are trained on multiple sources of
112   data beyond that of MQM data alone, MBR and QE finetuning are not directly comparable with
113   TWA.
114
115   Utilizing fine-grained annotations. There exist other methods which consider fine-grained anno-
116   tations, but they consider a different setting than TWA. Fine-grained RLHF (FG-RLHF) (Wu et al.,
117   2023) adapts RLHF to reward models which provide finer-grained feedback than a single sequence-
118   level score. Similar to our work, Wu et al. (2023) achieve better performance using fine-grained
119
      RLHF with span-level rewards than using RLHF with sequence-level rewards. The difference be-
      tween FG-RLHF and TWA is that the former is a reinforcement learning method that requires an
120
      auxiliary fine-grained reward model to annotate model generations online, while the latter is a fine-
121
      tuning method that can work directly with offline annotated data without the need for additional
122   models during training. The performance of FG-RLHF depends on the quality of the fine-grained
123   annotator model, which can be difficult to develop (see Pang et al. (2023) and Appendix C). More-
124   over, accuracy of the annotations aside, a reinforcement learning approach which only takes into
125   account online data misses out on the opportunity to learn from offline examples themselves, not
126   just their annotations.
127
      Next, Targeted Negative Training (TNT) (Zhang et al., 2024) is a method for training on token-level
128   annotations of negative examples, but its motivation is to achieve a targeted update, i.e., reducing
129   unwanted behavior while minimally changing the model otherwise. TWA, on the other hand, is not
130   concerned with making precise updates but rather improving overall quality as much as possible.
131   Finally, FUDGE (Yang & Klein, 2021) is an alternative decoding technique which utilizes a token-
132   level auxiliary reward model to sample from the model conditioned on a given attribute a; namely,
133   given reward model which approximates p(a|y≤t , x), FUDGE samples from p(yt |y<t , x, a) using
134   the original model p(yt |y<t , x) and reward model p(a|y<=t , x). TWA, on the other hand, is a
135   finetuning-based approach that does not alter the test-time behavior of the model and does not require
136
      an auxiliary reward model.

138   4     T RAINING WITH A NNOTATIONS
139
140   Training with annotations (TWA) is a finetuning algorithm that takes into account example outputs
141   and their span-level error annotations. TWA proceeds as follows: first, the example is tokenized and
142   given weights corresponding to its annotations: tokens which contain any characters within an error
143   span are given a negative weight, and tokens outside an error span are given a non-negative weight.
144   Then, during training, the TWA loss for a given sequence is a sum of the losses from the error spans
145
      and the non-error tokens. Below, we describe and motivate the choices for the constituent losses.
146
147   4.1   H ANDLING ERROR SPANS
148
      An annotated error span provides information to the model that such a continuation is undesirable
149
      given the preceding context (and thus should be unlikely under the model). To decrease the proba-
150   bility of error spans given their context, TWA utilizes the unlikelihood loss, − log(1 − p). The loss
151   is high when the probability p is high and 0 when p is zero. In Section 6, we consider alternative
152   choices of loss for error tokens and find that the unlikelihood loss outperforms other choices. More-
153   over, the unlikelihood loss is efficient to compute as it only requires access to the current model
154   being trained.
155
      Applying unlikelihood to each token in an error span may not be desirable, however. Take the output
156   in Figure 1, for example. Imagine the correct translation was “Give me an example of a blessing
157   in adversity”, but the submitted translation was “Give me a story about a blessing in disguise”, as
158   shown in the figure. Moreover, say the sequence was tokenized in the way shown in the figure, with
159   “disguise” being tokenized into “dis” and “guise”. First, even though “disguise” is an inaccurate
160   translation of “adversity”, “guise” is perhaps the most reasonable continuation of the sequence given
161   the prefix ends with “blessing in dis”. Penalizing “guise” given its prefix does not necessarily reflect
      the intention of the error span; rather, it is probably more appropriate to assign a low probability to

      “dis” given its prefix while maintaining a high probability for “guise” given a prefix ending in “a
163   blessing in dis”. Second, the error annotation around “a story about” does not necessarily mean that
164   the article ‘a’ and the preposition “about” should be assigned a low probability given their prefixes.
165   The above examples are just a few instances of the broader idea that not all tokens in an error span
166   should be penalized.
167
      Given these examples and others, one might be able to come up with a series of heuristics to trans-
168
      form the resulting span-level errors into corresponding token-level losses. However, as is common
169   in natural language, manually creating rules can be difficult and error-prone (whether due to low
170   precision or recall). Instead, we choose to let the model learn what to penalize within a span by uti-
171   lizing a span-level unlikelihood term instead of a token-level one. We additionally take into account
172   the severity of the error by scaling the loss by the absolute value of the severity weight w assigned to
173   the span, equal to the error span’s negative MQM score: -0.1 for minor punctuation, -1 for all other
174   minor errors, and -5 for major errors.1 The loss for an error span is the following:

      4.2   H ANDLING NON - ERROR SPANS
183
      When the overall quality of the data is high relative to the base model, using supervised finetuning
184   (SFT) to maximize the likelihood of the translations in the data can improve the model. On the other
185   hand, when the overall quality of the data is low relative to the model, SFT can hurt performance, by
186   teaching the model to reproduce errors. Thus, to optimize model quality, most efforts seek to filter
187   out low-quality examples and train just on high-quality ones. However, in reality, there is likely often
188   a spectrum of translation quality even within an example itself. Fine-grained annotations provide
189   extra information about this variation in quality by pinpointing exactly where errors exist. Then, for
190   all other tokens, we can proceed with typical maximum likelihood training via cross entropy loss,
191   without worrying about maximizing the likelihood of errors.
192   However, all the subsequent tokens after an error are out-of-support since their prefixes contain an
193   error that should be low or zero probability under the intended new model. We call these subsequent
194   tokens off-trajectory. Generalization aside, off-trajectory tokens at best are irrelevant to the model
195   distribution and at worst could provide noisy signal. While there is an argument that high-quality off-
196   trajectory tokens could provide signal that generalizes to trajectories the model will actually sample,
197   we find empirically that ignoring these tokens in the overall loss can greatly improve performance
198
      in some settings (see Table 4). TWA on non-error spans is thus as follows:

205   4.3   OVERALL METHOD
206
207   Combining the insights from the above two sections, we have a simple finetuning algorithm for TWA
208   as depicted in Figure 1. First, we tokenize the output sequence and its corresponding annotations.
209   The latter become weights which are negative values for tokens with characters contained in an
210   annotated error span, zero for all tokens following the first error span, and one for all other non-error
211
      tokens. Then, we group tokens into spans based on weight (i.e., all contiguous tokens with the same
      weight are in the same span) and employ either the TWA error span loss (Equation (1)) or the TWA
212
      non-error span loss (Equation (2)). The overall TWA loss for a given sequence is the sum of all the
213
      span losses.

      5.3      BASELINES
265
      We compare TWA with Supervised FineTuning (SFT) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)
266   (Rafailov et al., 2023) as baselines. SFT on the MQM annotated data is analogous to distilling the
267   outputs of other MT systems, without taking into account the annotations. DPO is a preference
268   learning algorithm which operates on pairs of responses to the same input given the knowledge that
269

              https://github.com/google/paxml

      one response in the pair is preferred to another. We construct response pairs for DPO using the
271   sequence-level MQM scores (i.e., the sum of the MQM scores of all the error spans), creating pairs
272   from all combinations of system translations to the same source input where the MQM score is
273   distinct. We arrived at this setting after testing multiple variations; see Appendix B for details. In
274   other words, DPO utilizes the annotations as additional information, but only at a sequence level.
275
      When using both submissions and references for finetuning, we treat references as error-free for
276
      TWA and TWA-seq, and treat them as better than all submissions for constructing DPO pairs; the
277   resulting dataset for DPO thus contains all the pairs constructed from submissions only, plus ad-
278   ditional (reference, submission) pairs for every submission. We also consider two additional base-
279   lines. First, given the quality of the data makes a big difference in the efficacy of SFT, we construct
280   a dataset of only the references and error-free submissions and run SFT on this filtered dataset. We
281   call this baseline Filter + SFT. Second, we also run a sequence-level analogue to TWA, where we
282   apply a sequence-level unlikelihood loss to an output if it contains any error and cross entropy loss
283   otherwise. We call this baseline TWA-seq.
284   For all the methods, we use a batch size of 8192 (4096 pairs for DPO), a learning rate of 2e-6 with
285   a constant schedule, and no label smoothing. Greedy decoding is used throughout the experiments.

287   5.4   E VALUATION
288
289   For evaluation, we use MetricX-23 (Juraska et al., 2023) and COMET-20 (Rei et al., 2020) as quality
290   metrics. MetricX-23 is a reference-based metric which scores a translation based on a reference and
291   a hypothesis, without taking into account the source text. COMET-20 takes into account the source
292   text, hypothesis, and reference translation. Moreover, MetricX-23 has been finetuned on MQM
293   WMT’20-’21 data, while COMET-20 has not. Given their differences, considering both automated
294
      quality metrics helps guard against overfitting to the idiosyncrasies of either. Lower is better for
      MetricX while higher is better for COMET-20; hence, for checkpoint selection, we average the
295
      values of MetricX-23 and the negative COMET-20 on the validation set every 500 steps, selecting
296
      the checkpoint with the lowest score. Throughout the rest of the paper, we use MetricX and COMET
297   to denote MetricX-23 and COMET-20, respectively.
298
299   We use the generalMT2022 test set (Kocmi et al., 2022) as our validation set for checkpoint selec-
300
      tion, and report all results on the WMT’23 (Kocmi et al., 2023) test set. The validation set contains
      roughly 2, 000 and 1, 900 source texts (along with their corresponding reference translations) for
301
      En→De and Zh→En, while the test set contains 600 and 2, 000 examples for En→De and Zh→En,
302
      respectively. Note that the WMT’23 En→De test set is paragraph-level.

      Next, we isolate the effect of the individual components of TWA in Table 4. Starting from the base
      model, we note first that training on all the submissions (+ SFT on submissions) improves results.
370
      Then, given knowledge of span-level errors, the most obvious next step is to treat the tokens with and
371
      without errors differently. Absent a method to deal with errors, the most straightforward next step is
372   to include only the non-error tokens in the loss, ignoring the error tokens to prevent the model from

461   7      D ISCUSSION
462
463   In this work, we introduce Training with Annotations (TWA), a method for finetuning a language
464   model on data with span-level error annotations. While most existing efforts have focused on uti-
465   lizing sequence-level annotations, TWA can take advantage of finer-grained information for more
466   effective learning. Our experiments on English-German and Chinese-English machine translation
467   highlight the performance gains TWA offers compared to methods that focus solely on sequence-
468   level information.
469   As model capabilities continue to improve, it will be increasingly difficult to rely on the collection
470   or construction of high-quality examples as training signals. In fact, many of the MT system sub-
471   missions in WMT’24 were found to surpass the quality of human-constructed reference translations,
472   highlighting the need to move beyond demonstration data for improving existing models. MQM an-
473   notations of model generations offer a valuable alternative source of information for model training,
474   and TWA unlocks the potential to utilize such rich information directly and simply.
475   Yet while the experiments focus on MQM data for the task of machine translation, TWA can be
476   used for span-level annotations broadly, paving the way for other applications of fine-grained an-
477   notations. While fine-grained information may be more expensive to collect than sequence-level
478   information for some tasks, Wu et al. (2023) find that for long-form question-answering, the time
479   required for humans to annotate span-level errors is comparable to the time required to label the
480   sequence overall. Many other tasks likely fall into this same category: for instance, one needs to
481   locate the hallucination in order to label a sequence as “has hallucination”; similarly, identifying
482
      specific spans of bias or misinformation is necessary before assigning a label such as “biased” or
      “inaccurate”.
483
484   There exist multiple ways to build upon TWA. One avenue for future work would be to apply TWA
485   in settings beyond machine translation or to language models in general. Another would be to addi-
      tionally take into account the fine-grained annotation information in other ways—for instance, given


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      fine-grained information provides a natural ranking of inputs, one could consider directly providing
487   the model with this relative quality information as well. Other interesting questions to investigate
488   include assessing TWA on online data, analyzing the impact of the quality of the generations and
489   annotations on resulting model performance, and exploring the repeated use of TWA for iterative
490   refinement of a model. Finally, the fact that ignoring off-trajectory tokens was highly beneficial in
491   one language pair but not in the other, provides an opportunity to further refine TWA to better handle
492   off-trajectory tokens since the latter might contain additional useful information for training.
493   In summary, TWA offers a straightforward method to capitalize on existing span-level annotation
494   data as well as a reason to begin collecting span-level information in applications which currently
495   do not. By taking advantage of previously overlooked sources of supervision, methods such as TWA
496   can help unlock new avenues for pushing the frontier of model development.
