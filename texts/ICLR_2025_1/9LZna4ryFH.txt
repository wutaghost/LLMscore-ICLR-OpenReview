009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011             Direct preference optimization (DPO) has shown success in aligning diffusion
012             models with human preference. Previous approaches typically assume a con-
013             sistent preference label between final generations and their corresponding noisy
014             samples at intermediate steps, and directly apply DPO to these noisy samples for
015             fine-tuning. However, we identify a significant issue with this assumption, as ap-
016             plying DPO to noisy samples from different trajectories based on final preferences
017             may disrupt the optimization process. We first demonstrate inherent issues in pre-
018
                vious methods from two perspectives: gradient direction and preference order,
                and then propose a Tailored Preference Optimization (TailorPO) framework for
019
                aligning diffusion models with human preference, underpinned by some theoret-
020
                ical insights. Our approach directly ranks the preference of noisy samples based
021             on their step-wise reward, and effectively resolves the gradient direction issues
022             through a simple yet efficient design. Additionally, to the best of our knowledge,
023             we are the first to consider the distinct structure of diffusion models and leverage
024             the gradient guidance in preference aligning to enhance the optimization effective-
025             ness. Experimental results demonstrate that our method significantly improves the
026             model’s ability to generate aesthetically pleasing and human-preferred images.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Direct preference optimization (DPO), which fine-tunes models on paired data to align model out-
032   puts with human preferences, has demonstrated success in large language models (LLMs) (Rafailov
033
      et al., 2023). Recently, researchers generalized this method to diffusion models for text-to-image
      generation (Black et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024a; Wallace et al., 2024). Given paired images
034
      generated from the same prompt and human-labeled preference, DPO increases the probability of
035
      generating the preferred sample while decreasing the probability of another sample, which enables
036   the model to generate more visually appealing images that better align with human preferences.
037
038   Specifically, previous researchers (Yang et al., 2024a) leverage the trajectory-level preference to
039
      rank the generated samples. As shown in Figure 1(a), given a text prompt c, they first sample a pair
      of denoising trajectories [x0T , . . . , x00 ] and [x1T , . . . , x10 ] from the diffusion model, and then rank them
040
      according to the human preference on the final generated images x00 and x10 . It is assumed that the
041
      preference order of (x00 , x10 ), at the end of the generation trajectory, can consistently represent the
042   preference order of (x0t , x1t ) at all intermediate steps t. Then, the DPO loss function is implemented
043   using the generation probabilities p(x0t−1 |x0t , c) and p(x1t−1 |x1t , c) at each step t to fine-tune the
044   diffusion model, which is called the step-level optimization.
045
      However, we notice that the above trajectory-level preference ranking and the step-level optimiza-
046
      tion are not compatible in diffusion models. First, the trajectory-level preference ranking (i.e., the
047
      preference order of final outputs (x00 , x10 )) does not accurately reflect the preference order of (x0t , x1t )
048   at intermediate steps. Considering the inherent randomness in the denoising process, simply assign-
049   ing the preference of final outputs to all intermediate steps will detrimentally affect the preference
050   optimization performance. Second, the generation probabilities p(x0t−1 |x0t , c) and p(x1t−1 |x1t , c) in
051   two trajectories are conditioned on different inputs, and this causes the optimization direction to be
052   significantly affected by input differences. In particular, if x0t and x1t are located in the same linear
053   subspace of the diffusion model, then the optimization of DPO probably increases the probability of
      generating dis-preferred samples. Section 3.2 provides a detailed theoretical analysis of these issues.

065
      Therefore, in this paper, we propose a Tailored Preference Optimization (TailorPO) framework
066   to fine-tune diffusion models with DPO, which addresses the aforementioned challenges. As Fig-
067   ure 1(b) shows, we generate two noisy samples (x0t−1 , x1t−1 ) from the same input xt at each step.
068   Then, we directly rank the preference order of two samples (x0t−1 , x1t−1 ) based on their step-wise
069   reward. To this end, one of the most straightforward methods is to directly evaluate the reward of
070   these noisy samples using a reward model. However, existing reward models are trained on natural
071   images and do not apply to noisy samples. To address this issue, we formulate the denoising process
072   as a Markov decision process (MDP) and derive a simple yet effective measurement for the reward
073   of noisy samples. Then, we utilize p(x0t−1 |xt , c) and p(x1t−1 |xt , c) to compute the loss function for
074   fine-tuning. In this way, the gradient direction is proven to increase the probability of generating
075
      preferred samples while decreasing the probability of generating dis-preferred samples.
076   Moreover, we notice that TailorPO generates paired samples from the same xt , potentially causing
077   two samples to be similar in late denoising steps with large t. Such similarity may reduce the diver-
078   sity of paired samples, thereby impacting the effectiveness of the DPO-based method. To mitigate
079   this issue, we propose to enhance the diversity of noisy samples by increasing their reward gap.
080   Specifically, we employ gradient guidance (Guo et al., 2024) to generate paired samples, leveraging
081
      the gradient of differentiable reward models to increase the reward of preferred noisy samples. This
      strategy, termed TailorPO-G, further improves the effectiveness of our TailorPO framework.
082
083   In experiments, we fine-tune Stable Diffusion v1.5 using TailorPO and TailorPO-G to enhance its
084   ability to generate images that achieve elevated aesthetic scores and align with human preference.
085   Additionally, we evaluate TailorPO on user-specific preferences, such as image compressibility. The
086   experimental results indicate that diffusion models fine-tuned with TailorPO and TailorPO-G yield
087
      higher reward scores compared to those fine-tuned with other RLHF and DPO-style methods.
088   Most close to our work, Liang et al. (2024) also noticed the inconsistency of the preference order
089   between intermediate-step outputs and final images, and they proposed to train an additional step-
090   wise reward model to address this issue. In comparison, we are the first to explicitly derive the
091   theoretical flaws of previous DPO implementations in diffusion models, and we propose distinct
092   solutions to address these issues. Experiments also demonstrate that our framework outperforms
093
      SPO on various reward models.
094   Contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows. (1) Through theoretical analysis and
095   experimental validation, we demonstrate the mismatch between the trajectory-level ranking and the
096   step-level optimization in existing DPO methods for diffusion models. (2) Based on these insights,
097   we propose TailorPO, a simple DPO framework tailored to the unique denoising structure of dif-
098   fusion models. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first framework that explicitly considers
099
      the properties of diffusion models for DPO. Experimental results have demonstrated that TailorPO
      significantly improves the model’s ability to generate human-preferred images. (3) Furthermore,
100
      inspired by the success of gradient guidance in adapting model outputs towards user-specified ob-
101
      jectives, we incorporate gradient guidance of differentiable reward models in TailorPO-G to increase
102   the diversity of training samples for fine-tuning to further enhance performance.

                          529   5    C ONCLUSIONS
                          530
                          531   In this study, we rethink the existing DPO framework for aligning diffusion models and identify
                          532   the potential flaws in these methods. We analyze these issues from both perspectives of preference
                          533   order and gradient direction. To address these challenges, we consider the unique characteristics
                          534   of diffusion models and introduce a novel tailored preference optimization framework for aligning
                          535   diffusion models with human preference. Specifically, at each denoising step, our approach gener-
                          536   ates noisy samples from the same input and directly ranks their preference order for optimization.
                          537
                                Furthermore, we propose integrating gradient guidance into the training framework to enhance the
                                training effectiveness. Experimental results demonstrate that our approach significantly improved
                          538
                                the reward scores of generated images, and exhibited good generalization over different prompts and
                          539
                                different reward models.

      Diffusion models contain a forward process and a reverse denoising process. Given an input x0
811   sampled from the real distribution pdata , the forward process can be formulated as follows, which is
812   a uniform formulation for DDPM, DDIM, and other methods.
813
814                                                xt = st x0 + st σt ϵ,
815   where xt is the noisy sample at timestep t. st represents a scale schedule coefficient and σt represents
816   the noisy schedule coefficient. At timestep t, we have p(xt |x0 ) ∼ N (st x0 , s2t σt2 I). st and σt are
817   usually selected to ensure the final output xT follows a certain Gaussian distribution.
818
      The reverse process aims to recover the distribution of original inputs x0 from a Gaussian noise xT .
819
      According to [1], the reverse ODE process is given as follows.
820
821                                             ṡt                         x

824

825   tion, we can solve the reverse process. For a set of discrete timesteps, we can obtain a sequence
826   [xT , xT −1 , . . . , xt , . . . , x0 ], and our study focuses on the optimization of sθ (·) at each timestep to
827   generate x0 with better image quality.

1040   G     L IMITATIONS AND DISCUSSIONS
1041
1042   In this section, we discuss the potential limitations of our method. Like other methods based on
1043   an explicit pre-trained reward model, including DDPO, D3PO, and SPO, TailorPO has the potential
1044   of being prone to reward hacking (Skalse et al., 2022), if we fine-tune the model on very simple
1045   prompts for too many iterations. It means that the generative model is overoptimized to improve the
1046   score of the reward model but fails to maintain the original output distribution of natural images. We
1047   provide some examples in Figure 13 to demonstrate this phenomenon.

1059   The problem of reward hacking is related to the quality of reward models. Given the fact that
1060   these pre-trained reward models are usually trained on a finite training set, they cannot perfectly
1061   fit the human preference for natural and visually pleasing images. Therefore, the optimization of
1062   generative models towards these reward models may lead to an unnatural distribution of images.
1063   In order to alleviate the reward hacking problem, TailorPO can be further improved from the follow-
1064   ing perspectives.
1065
1066           • Using a better reward model that well captures the distribution of natural and visually pleas-
1067             ing images. A better reward model can avoid guiding model optimization towards unnatural
1068             images.
1069           • Utilizing the ensemble of multiple reward models to alleviate the bias of a single reward
1070             model. While each single reward model has its own preference bias, considering multiple
1071             reward models altogether may be able to alleviate the risk of falling into a single model. To
1072             this end, Coste et al. (2024) have shown that the reward model ensembles can effectively
1073
                 address reward hacking in RLHF-based fine-tuning of language models. Therefore, we are
                 hopeful that the reward model ensembles are also effective for diffusion models.
1074
1075           • Searching for a better setting of the hyperparameter β in the loss function to strike a balance
1076             between natural images and high reward scores. In DPO-style methods, the coefficient β
1077
                 controls the deviation from the original generative distribution (the KL regularization). In
                 this way, we can search for a better value of β to avoid the model being fine-tuned far away
1078
                 from the original base model. For example, Wu et al. (2024) have provided a method to
1079
                 dynamically adjust the value of β.
