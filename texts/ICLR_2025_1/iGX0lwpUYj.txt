                                                                       A BSTRACT
008
009
010
                       A significant challenge in maintaining real-world machine learning models is re-
                       sponding to the continuous and unpredictable evolution of data. Most practitioners
011
                       are faced with the difficult question: when should I retrain or update my machine
012
                       learning model? This seemingly straightforward problem is particularly challeng-
013                    ing for three reasons: 1) decisions must be made based on very limited informa-
014                    tion - we usually have access to only a few examples, 2) the nature, extent, and
015                    impact of the distribution shift are unknown, and 3) it involves specifying a cost
016                    ratio between retraining and poor performance, which can be hard to characterize.
017                    Existing works address certain aspects of this problem, but none offer a com-
018                    prehensive solution. Distribution shift detection falls short as it cannot account
019                    for the cost trade-off; the scarcity of the data, paired with its unusual structure,
020                    makes it a poor fit for existing offline reinforcement learning methods, and the
021
                       online learning formulation overlooks key practical considerations. To address
                       this, we present a principled formulation of the retraining problem and propose an
022
                       uncertainty-based method that makes decisions by continually forecasting the evo-
023
                       lution of model performance evaluated with a bounded metric. Our experiments
024                    addressing classification tasks show that the method consistently outperforms ex-
025                    isting baselines on 7 datasets. We thoroughly assess its robustness to varying cost
026                    trade-off values and mis-specified cost trade-offs.

032   et al., 2014). This means that a model, fθ , that was trained on a fixed dataset, D, will become
033   outdated. This usually translates to a cost in the form of a missed opportunity. However, retraining

036
      with assigning experts to deploy and maintain the model, as well as collecting and cleaning data.
      Deploying a new model also generally comes with a higher risk. Therefore, the optimal retraining

      schedule depends on this comprehensive cost of retraining, on the cost of making mistakes, and on

      future model performance. Figure 1 provides a visualization of the task.

      Although this retraining problem is ubiquitous in industry (Gama et al., 2014), there are few works in
055   the machine learning literature that tackle it directly. It has been framed as an application of the dis-
056   tribution shift detection problem (Bifet & Gavaldà, 2007), where the conventional strategy involves
057   triggering retraining whenever a substantial shift is detected (Bifet & Gavaldà, 2007; Cerqueira et al.,
058   2021; Pesaranghader & Viktor, 2016). However, this approach overlooks retraining costs. This can
059   be particularly problematic when training is expensive, as demonstrated in our experiments. Oth-
060   ers have reduced the need for retraining by incorporating robustness to distribution shifts (Schwinn
061   et al., 2022) or adapting to them (Filos et al., 2020), but these methods have limits on the extent of
062   the shift they can handle. Other related areas are online or adaptive learning (Hoi et al., 2021) and
063
      life-long learning which updates models with a continuous stream of data through gradual gradient
      updates, and transfer learning which adapts model from one distribution to another. However, this
064
      differs from our problem, as it focuses on maximizing performance while abstracting the practical
065
      retraining costs involved in production deployment. In practice, the cost of retraining can go beyond
066   the number of gradient updates or sample complexity, as discussed above. Finally, because this is
067   a sequential decision problem, it can be framed within the offline reinforcement learning frame-
068   work (Levine et al., 2020). In theory, offline RL methods should be applicable, but few, if any,
069   are designed for very low-data settings. They require substantial amounts of data for training and
070   hyperparameter tuning, and are therefore largely unsuitable to use in this context.
071
      A direct treatment of the cost consideration in the retraining problem is presented by Žliobaitė et al.
072
      (2015) and by Mahadevan & Mathioudakis (2024). The formulation by Mahadevan & Mathioudakis
073   (2024) accounts for the trade-off between the cost of retraining and the cost of performance. Their
074   method, CARA, relies on approximating the performance of a model on new data, and the retraining
075   decision is based on this value. However, this approach makes several limiting assumptions: 1)
076   the relative cost objective assumes that the “difficulty” of the task remains constant; and 2) the
077   performance approximation assumes the data distribution is almost stationary.
078
      Instead, we consider a more general objective that combines both the retraining cost and the average
079   performance over a specified horizon. We detail the relationship between our objective and CARA’s
080   objective in Appendix 8.10. Our formulation is more general and does not depend on strong as-
081   sumptions regarding the data distribution and its impact on performance. There is no constraint
082   on how the ”retrained” model is obtained. It can be obtained through fine-tuning from a previous
083   model, adapted, trained from scratch, or any other procedure.Additionally, our method can lever-
084   age new observations of the model’s performance. Our proposed method involves forecasting the
085   performance of both future and current models and making decisions based on the uncertainty of
086   our predictions. We show the effectiveness of our approach on five real datasets and two synthetic
087
      datasets. We make the following contributions:
088
      • We introduce a principled formulation of a practical version of the retraining problem. We also
089
        provide its connection to existing formulations and offline reinforcement learning.
090
091   • We establish upper limits on the optimal number of retrains based on performance bounds and
092
        show how existing results can be used to determine whether you should retrain or not.
093   • We propose a novel retraining decision procedure based on performance forecasting. Our pro-
094     posed algorithm is robust and outperforms existing baselines. It requires minimal performance
095     data by fully leveraging the problem structure, employing compact regression models, and bal-
096     ancing the uncertainty caused by data scarcity through an uncertainty-informed decision process.
097   • We show that accounting for uncertainty in our method improves the performance.

101
102   We discuss related work and fields relevant to the retraining problem. A more detailed literature
103
      review, including connections to other related fields is provided in Appendix 8.1.
104   Retraining problem Few works explicitly target the retraining problem. Žliobaitė et al. (2015) pro-
105   pose a return on investment (ROI) framework to monitor and assess the retraining decision process,
106   but do not introduce a method for actually deciding when to retrain. Mahadevan & Mathioudakis
107   (2024) develop a retraining decision algorithm, CARA, which integrates the cost of retraining and
      introduces a “staleness cost” for persisting with an old model. CARA approximates the staleness


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      cost using offline data consisting of several trained models and their historical performance. Three
109   versions of CARA are proposed: (i) retraining if the estimated staleness exceeds a threshold; (ii) re-
110   training based on estimated cumulative staleness; or (iii) identifying an optimal retraining frequency.
111   While providing promising results, CARA requires access to some of the data that will be used for
112   retraining, and is very computationally intensive, so there is no adaptation to data obtained during
113   the online decision period.
114
      Distribution shift detection The retraining problem is closely connected to distribution shift detec-
115   tion and mitigation (Wang et al., 2024a; Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017; Rabanser et al., 2019). Some
116   approaches decide to adapt a model after detection of a changed distribution (Sugiyama & Kawan-
117   abe, 2012; Zhang et al., 2023). Since the signal is designed to adapt a model rather than trigger a
118   full retraining, these methods are not appropriate as retraining signals. Other approaches, however,
119   directly treat the detection of a distribution shift as a cue for retraining. ADWIN (Bifet & Gavaldà,
120   2007) uses statistical testing of the label or feature distribution. Another approach is to directly
121   monitor the model’s performance. FHDDM (Pesaranghader & Viktor, 2016) employs Hoeffding’s
122   inequality, while (Raab et al., 2020) relies on a Kolmogorov-Smirnov Windowing test. These ap-
123
      proaches work well with low retraining costs, but perform poorly when retraining costs are high,
      as they tend to recommend retraining far too often. Additionally, they lack adaptability to varying
124
      costs, and it is difficult to determine the correct significance level to use for a given retraining-to-
125
      performance cost ratio.
126
127   Offline reinforcement learning Lastly, we discuss the connection to the offline reinforcement learn-
128   ing (ORL) setting, where the agent must learn a policy from a fixed dataset of rewards, actions, and
129
      states. This subset of RL is particularly challenging, as the agent cannot explore and can only rely on
      the dataset to infer the underlying dynamics and handle distribution shifts. See (Levine et al., 2020)
130
      for an extensive review. Q-learning and value function methods, which focus on predicting future
131
      action costs, have become the preferred approaches for ORL (Levine et al., 2020; Kalashnikov et al.;
132   Hejna et al., 2023; Kostrikov et al., 2022). Some methods incorporate epistemic uncertainty into the
133   Q-function to address distribution shifts of unseen actions (Kumar et al., 2020; Luis et al., 2023).
134
135
      If we view the states as encoding both time and the model in use, and actions as either retraining
      or maintaining the current model, we can frame our problem as ORL. However, most existing RL
136
      approaches focus on scaling to large state or action spaces, employ large models, and assume ac-
137
      cess to abundant data, making them unsuitable for our context. A more detailed discussion on the
138   connections and limitations of ORL methods is included in Appendix 8.9.

230   4   M ETHODOLOGY
231
232   A retraining decision algorithm must specify the decision functions gϕ (t, I<t ) ∈ {0, 1} (where ϕ
233   contains the parameters of the algorithm) used to build the decision vector θ. To make perfect
234   decisions, we would need future performance values, i.e., pei,j ∀(i > t or j > t). This is infeasible;
235   however, we assume that there is an underlying temporal autocorrelation between the performance of
236
      different models trained at different times, which we aim to exploit to build a predictive model. We
      therefore propose to 1) model these future values as random variables and learn their distributions;
237
      and 2) base our decisions on the predicted distributions to construct our method, the Uncertainty-
238
      Performance Forecaster (UPF). As our methodology involves forecasting future performance as a
239   key subtask, we evaluate and quantify the impact of success in this task on the overall performance
240   of our algorithm, as detailed in Appendix 8.5.

335   offline RL, where the state space is finite and enumerable, the training data are extremely limited, the
336   transition function is deterministic and fully known, and the reward structure is highly structured.
337
      In fact, our methodology can be reinterpreted as an offline variant of a Q-learning approach with
      a specific parameterization of the Q function, further justifying the motivation behind our method.
338
      We explore and formalize this connection in Appendix 8.9. However, as we have explained in the
339
      related work section, existing ORL methods are not suitable for this setting. We provide the results
340   for one ORL baseline in Appendix 8.9 to examplify that point.

345   Evaluation Metrics The performance of a retraining decision method is evaluated based on both
346   the average performance and the total retraining cost. The tradeoff between these factors is con-
347
      trolled by α. When using the zero-one loss in classification, α can be seen as the ratio of retraining
      cost to the cost of misclassifications. In practice, α is application-dependent and should be set by
348
      the practitioner. The retraining cost would be low (small α) for situations such as fine-tuning small
349
      models. By contrast, when retraining large language models, or in high-stakes settings requiring
350   extensive validation, the retraining cost is high (large α). The retraining decision method should be
351   robust across all scenarios. The appropriate value of α can be very difficult to estimate and will likely
352   be an approximation in practice. Consequently, we present experiments that test the robustness of
353   the method to inaccuracies in α in Section 6.
354
      In our experiments, we address classification tasks with a zero-one loss, and set eN = 1. We report

409   Baselines and algorithm settings We set the confidence threshold of our UPF algorithm to
410   δ = 95%, as it is a standard value used for confidence intervals. For µϕ (ri,j ), we use a linear
411   regression model, ElasticNetCV (Zou & Hastie, 2005), from the scikit-learn library. All other op-
412   timization parameters are set to default choices from the scikit learn libraries. We report results on
413
      shift detection baselines and the three variants of the CARA baseline, as well as the oracle.
414   For the distribution shift detection baselines, we set the window size to the size of an individual
415   dataset ∣D∣, and retrain when the algorithm detects a distribution shift. (Then we reset the algorithm
416   with the dataset of the last retrained model.) As these methods cannot take into account the cost of
417   retraining, we vary the significance level threshold δ to obtain different frequencies of retraining.
418   We include ADWIN-δ (Bifet & Gavaldà, 2007), which is based on statistical testing of the label
419
      distribution, FHDDM-δ (Pesaranghader & Viktor, 2016), which is based on Hoeffding’s inequality,
      and KSWIN-δ (Raab et al., 2020), which is based on the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test.
420
421   CARA (Mahadevan & Mathioudakis, 2024) searches for the best strategy with fixed parameters
422   using the offline data. The standard version, CARA, searches for the best threshold of approximate
423   performance and retrains when it drops below it. The cumulative version, CARA cumul., searches
424   for the best threshold of the cumulated approximate performance; and the periodic strategy, CARA
425
      per., searches for the best retraining frequency. Appendix 8.10 provides additional details on the
      CARA baseline in the context of our experiments.

526   7           C ONCLUSION AND LIMITATIONS
527
528   We have proposed a practical formulation of the important problem of model retraining, which has
529   been neglected in the literature, and highlighted its complexity. Our method outlines a promis-
530   ing avenue, as our experiments have shown that even with distribution shift, it is not unreasonable to
531
      expect some patterns in future performance that could be predicted with the help of uncertainty mod-
      eling. This data-driven approach is lightweight, practical, and outperforms existing approaches. It is
532
      robust to varying cost settings and has demonstrated resilience to misspecified cost-to-performance
533
      ratios. We have also highlighted the quantities of interest to estimate in order to better understand the
534   characteristics of a specific problem. While our study demonstrates promising results in predicting
535   optimal retraining schedules, several aspects warrant further exploration. Our main experiments in-
536   vestigate a setting where the offline dataset (w = 7) is non-negligible in size. However, we achieved
537   good performance even with a reduced dataset, which shows that initial training costs can be reduced
538   (see Appendix 8.11).We evaluated the method individually for each dataset, but future work could
539   further reduce costs by transferring schedulers across datasets and tasks. Additionally, adapting
      techniques from Hyperparameter Optimization could enhance performance forecasting.

747   8.1   E XTENDED D ISCUSSION OF R ELATED W ORK
748
      Retraining problem Few works explicitly target the retraining problem. Žliobaitė et al. (2015)
749
      propose a return on investment (ROI) framework to monitor and assess the retraining decision pro-
750   cess. Mahadevan & Mathioudakis (2024) develop a retraining decision algorithm, CARA, which
751   integrates the cost of retraining into its formulation. It introduces the concept of a “staleness cost”
752   which represents the cost of not retraining. The approach involves approximating the staleness cost
753   and optimizing various strategies to reduce the overall cost, based on some offline data. The offline
754   data consist of a few trained models, each with an associated dataset that was collected prior to the
755   retraining decision process. Mahadevan & Mathioudakis (2024) propose three methods: the first
      retrains when the estimated staleness cost exceeds a threshold; the second tracks the accumulated


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      staleness cost and applies a threshold on that value; and the third searches for the optimal retraining
757   frequency. The staleness cost approximation for using a model on a dataset relies on the loss of in-
758   dividual known samples. This loss is scaled by the average similarity between the features of these
759   known samples and the features of the dataset of interest. Consequently, it assumes access to the
760   features of some of the samples at a given time before deciding to retrain. Moreover, the search for
761   the threshold or the period is computationally intensive and therefore can only be done once using
762   some offline data; it cannot modify the parameters as new information arrives.
763   Distribution shift detection The retraining problem is closely connected to distribution shift detec-
764   tion and mitigation (Wang et al., 2024a; Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017; Scheirer et al., 2013; Cerqueira
765   et al., 2021; Bar-Shalom et al., 2023; Rabanser et al., 2019). Some methods adapt the model to ad-
766   just to evolving distributions (Sugiyama & Kawanabe, 2012; Zhang et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2020;
767   Pesaranghader et al., 2018). Since the signal is designed to adapt a model rather than trigger a
768   full retraining, these methods are not appropriate as retraining signals. Some approaches, however,
769   directly treat the detection of a distribution shift as a cue for retraining (Bifet & Gavaldà, 2007;
770   Pesaranghader & Viktor, 2016; Raab et al., 2020), and can be used as baselines. ADWIN (Bifet
771
      & Gavaldà, 2007) uses statistical testing of the label or feature distribution. Another approach is
      to directly monitor the model’s performance. FHDDM (Pesaranghader & Viktor, 2016) employs
772
      Hoeffding’s inequality, while (Raab et al., 2020) relies on a Kolmogorov-Smirnov Windowing test.
773
      These approaches may work well when retraining costs are low, but they become unsuitable when
774   retraining is expensive – it is not always optimal to retrain after every minor shift. This is tied to a
775   more general weakness of lacking adaptability to varying costs. While the significance level param-
776   eteter can be adjusted, the appropriate significance level for a given retraining-to-performance cost
777   ratio is unknown and difficult to estimate.
778
      Changepoint detection Another closely related field is changepoint detection, which is similar to
779
      the distribution shift problem. Changepoint detection is the task of identifying points in a sequence
780   where the statistical properties of the data change abruptly. This problem was introduced and pre-
781   sented by Adams & MacKay (2007), where they aim to infer the most probable distribution of the
782   most recent changepoint in an online setting. Recent work, such as (Li et al., 2021), has expanded
783   on this problem in ways closer to our retraining setting, as they incorporate adaptation into the
784   changepoint detection process,The sensitivity of the detection is controlled by certain sensitivity
785   parameters.
786   However, to transform the changepoint detection problem formulated by Li et al. (2021) into the
787   retraining problem we consider, we would need to introduce a cost for adaptation, a cost for accu-
788   racy loss, and then formulate an optimization problem to find the appropriate sensitivity parameter
789   for achieving the optimal number of adaptations. However, since this parameter lacks a specific
790   physical or practical meaning, it is unclear beforehand how the choice of its value will impact the
791   adaptation rate. Furthermore, in our setting, the optimal rate of adaptation (or retraining frequency)
792   is unknown. Determining this optimal retraining frequency is one of the major challenges of the
793   retraining problem.
794   Bayesian Optimization Our method is based on forecasting future model performance using his-
795   torical data. This approach closely aligns with Bayesian Optimization (see (Shahriari et al., 2016)
796   for a review on this topic), commonly used in the Hyperparameter Optimization (HPO) field. The
797   Freeze-Thaw method, introduced by Swersky et al. (2014), leverages Gaussian Processes to predict
798   the trajectory of validation loss, enabling early stopping and optimization of the hyperparameter
799   search space. It remains a relevant technique (Rakotoarison et al., 2024). Similarly, Dai et al. (2019)
800
      derive a Bayes-optimal stopping rule using a related approach. This method can be extended to pre-
      dict the performance of other models and address hyperparameter optimization challenges (Wang
801
      et al., 2024b). In our context, we predict the performance of different models under potential distri-
802
      bution shifts, but the underlying idea is similar.
803
804   Label-free performance estimation Similarly, our approach is also related to the general fields of
805   performance estimation without labels Garg et al. (2020); Guillory et al. (2021); Chen* et al. (2021)
806
      and active testing Kossen et al. (2021). Part of the problem is similar in that the goal is to estimate
      performance; however, the similarity ends there, as these methods generally assume access to the
807
      model f for which performance is estimated, as well as access to the features of the dataset Garg
808
      et al. (2020). Our approach involves forecasting performance not only for known models but also
809   for unknown models. While our approach does not explicitly differentiate strategies, it is true that


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      we have access to additional information. Therefore, extensions that leverage existing techniques in
811   this area could strengthen our method.
812
813   This forecasting problem can seem similar to the problem of uncertainty quantification Hendrycks
814
      & Gimpel (2017); Liu et al. (2020), but we are targeting average performance of unknown models,
      not the probability of error of a given model at a given input P (f (x) = y∣x).
815
816   Offline reinforcement learning Lastly, we discuss the connection to the offline reinforcement learn-
817   ing (ORL) setting, where the agent must learn a policy from a fixed dataset of rewards, actions, and
818   states. This subset of RL is particularly challenging, as the agent cannot explore the entire MDP and
819   can only rely on the dataset to infer the underlying dynamics and handle distribution shifts (Ross
820
      et al., 2011; Levine et al., 2020; Hejna et al., 2023). Policy gradient methods can be adapted to the
      offline setting using variants of importance sampling, but they are generally prone to high variance
821
      and require large amounts of data to be effective (Levine et al., 2020). For this reason, Q-learning and
822
      value function methods, where the task is to predict the future costs of actions, have emerged as the
823   preferred approaches for ORL (Levine et al., 2020; Kalashnikov et al.; Hejna et al., 2023; Kostrikov
824   et al., 2022). Lagoudakis & Parr (2003) presents a classical method that uses a linear approximation
825   of the Q-function, while (Kalashnikov et al.) employs convolution-based Q-function architectures
826   for vision tasks.Others have leveraged advancements in sequential learning, applying transformer-
827   based architectures to predict rewards(Janner et al., 2021) or Q-functions(Chebotar et al., 2023).
828   Some methods integrates epistemic uncertainty on Q-function to account for the distribution shift of
829   unseen actions (Kumar et al., 2020; O’Donoghue et al., 2017; Luis et al., 2023).
830   If we view the states as time and the model in use, and actions as either retraining or maintaining
831   the current model, we can frame this problem as an offline reinforcement learning (RL) problem.
832   The problem would also feature a deterministic transition matrix and a highly structured reward
833   which unusual in RL. However, most existing approaches focus on scaling to very large state spaces,
834   employing large models, and assuming access to abundant data, making them unsuitable for our
835   context. A key requirement for our approach is that it must be highly efficient to train. If the
836   resources required for making a retraining decision are comparable to those for retraining the model
837
      itself, the approach becomes impractical.
