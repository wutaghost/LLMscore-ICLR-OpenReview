009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Tabular data represents the most prevalent and extensively utilized form of struc-
012            tured data in various domains. Traditionally dominated by tree-based algorithms,
013            researchers are actively exploring the application of deep neural networks on tab-
014            ular data. Notably, the TabTransformer (Huang et al., 2020) and FT-transformer
015            (Gorishniy et al., 2021) showed that feeding column embeddings of the tabular
016            features into a transformer could learn a representation of the columns and how
017            the embeddings interact with one another. This paper introduces PlicoTabTrans-
018
               former, an enhancement of the previous methods, which is designed to learn mul-
               tiple representations of the column embeddings. By incorporating a transformer
019
               with multiple learnable position embeddings and a contrastive learning loss, our
020
               method learns multiple distinct and orthogonal representations (denoted as plico
021            vectors) of the column embeddings. We evaluated the PlicoTabTransformer with
022            the pytorch-frame benchmark. Our experimental demonstrated that the PlicoTab-
023            Transformer is overall top ranked algorithm and achieves state of the art perfor-
024            mance in several datasets compared to other deep learning method closing the gap
025            with tree based algorithms. Our method provides an added advantage to visualise
026            redundancies and a potential dimensionality reduction technique.

045   Structured tabular data is among the most prevalent and extensively utilized form across various
046   domains and industries. Sectors including healthcare (e.g., patient outcomes and treatment effi-
047   cacy), financial services (market trends, assess risks), and retail (sales patterns, manage inventory)
048   rely heavily on tabular data to enhance data-driven decision-making processes. Neural networks
049   have great success in unstructured data including computer vision and natural language processing
050
      (NLP) beating traditional machine learning and decision tree algorithms in tasks ranging from image
      classification, language translation, time series forecasting, etc. However, decision tree algorithms
051
      generally still outperform perform neural networks in tabular data.
052
053   The recent works in neural networks bring down the performance gap by utilizing attention mech-
      anisms for tabular data (Arik & Pfister, 2021; Du et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2020; Somepalli et al.,

054
      2021; Gorishniy et al., 2022; 2021). In general, researchers have found that embedding both cate-
055   gorical and numerical features and feeding the embeddings into a transformer could learn a repre-
056   sentation of the columns and how the columns interact with one another. The representation is then
057   fed into a downstream model to predict the dataset’s task.
058
      We present the PlicoTabTransformer (plico is Latin for folding) that builds upon previous methods
059
      to learn multiple distinct representations of the embeded categorical and numerical tabular features.
060
      In our method, we feed the embedded input features into the transformer architecture multiple times
061   each with a separate learnable positional embedding to encourage the transformer to attend to dif-
062   ferent columns within the dataset. Each pass of the input features through the transformer produces
063   a representation of the embedded input features (denoted as plico vectors). We adapted a contrastive
064   loss paradigm to force the plico vectors to be distinct and orthogonal to each other. The plico vectors
065   are then fed into a downstream predictor for the dataset’s task.
066
      We evaluated our model with pytorch-frame (Hu et al., 2024), which includes state of the art decision
067   tree algorithms and neural network models. We evaluated our method using the framework’s stan-
068   dard benchmarking scripts, datasets, and the dataset splits. When compared to other deep learning
069   methods, we found that PlicoTabTransformer achieved state of the art performance on a subset of the
070   standard datasets and achieved comparable performance on the remaining datasets. Our experiments
071   revealed that PlicoTabTransformer was the highest ranked among deep learning methods.
072
      Our key contributions are:

087   2.1   TABULAR D EEP NETWORKS
088
      Tabular deep networks are specialized for structured tabular data, using embeddings, attention mech-
089
      anisms, knowledge graphs, etc. to build representations of the input data facing challenges like
090
      missing values, categorical features, and sparsity. TabNet (Arik & Pfister, 2021) introduced a novel
091   method which uses sequential attention mechanisms to selectively process input features at each de-
092   cision step. On the other hand, TabularNet (Du et al., 2021) decodes the intricate semantic structures
093   inherent in tabular formats, going beyond traditional spatial relationships to also consider relational
094   information between data elements. TabTransformer (Huang et al., 2020) aimed to build a strong
095   representation for categorical features by embedding the features and feeding it into a transformer.
096   SAINT (Somepalli et al., 2021) extends TabTransformer by integrating self-attention mechanisms
097   not only across the features but also along the sequence of rows with a contrastive self-supervised
098   pre-training method. By doing this, SAINT was able to capture more complex inter-feature and
099
      intra-feature relationships. FT-Transformer (Gorishniy et al., 2022) and (Gorishniy et al., 2021) fur-
      ther refines the transformer approach by incorporating feature tokenization, transforming categori-
100
      cal and numerical features into a unified representation before processing them through transformer
101
      blocks. Suggesting that input feature embeddings (for both categorical and numerical respectively)
102   was a major contributor to improving neural network’s performance. Trompt introduces prompt
103   learning in tabular data to derive feature importances instead of focusing on the interactions among
104   column like the regular transformer based models (Chen et al., 2023b). Authors of ExcelFormer
105   (Chen et al., 2023a) introduce an inductive bias into the self-attention mechanism (semi-permeable
106   attention) that selectively limits the influence of less informative features. By doing this only more
107   informative features are permitted to propagate. In Ruiz et al. (2024), the authors use auxilary knowl-
      edge graphs describing input features to regularize multi layer perceptron. It updates each feature

      embedding using a trainable message-passing function, which is optimized based on the supervised
109   loss objective for the tabular data.

      2.2   L EARNABLE POSITION EMBEDDINGS
112
113   Unlike recurrent neural networks, transformers do not have information on the relative or absolute
114   position of the tokens in the sequence (Vaswani et al., 2017). Learnable positional embeddings
115   (Gehring et al., 2017) was introduced by feeding the sequence indexes into embeddings layers to
116   provide positional information to the neural network. Vaswani et al. (2017) showed that feeding the
117   sequence indexes through sinusoidal functions at different frequencies could effectively inject posi-
118   tional information into a transformer, reducing the computational requirements. Recent works have
119   shown the possibility of enhancing positional embeddings. Learnable sinusoidal positional encod-
120
      ing (LSPE) showed that feeding sinusoidal positional encoding through a feed forward network had
      better performance that just the sinusoidal positional encoding for document understanding tasks
121
      (Wang et al., 2022). Flow based Transformer (FLOATER) introduces a flexible positional encoding
122
      scheme that learns position information dynamically and is not restricted to the maximum length of
123   the input (Liu et al., 2020).
124
125
      2.3   C ONSTRASTIVE LOSS
126
127   Contrastive learning aims to minimize the distance between embeddings of certain samples while
128   maximizing the distance between embeddings of other samples. Chopra et al. (2005) trained a
129   network to minimize the distance between image embeddings from image pairs in the same class and
130   maximize the distance if they come from different classes. Chen et al. (2020) presented SimCLR
131   where augmentations of images are generated and the embedding of the augmentations (positive
132   samples) are trained to be close to the original image (anchor) and far from other images (and
133   their augmentations). Supervised Contrastive Learning (Khosla et al., 2020) extended SimCLR
134
      incorporating label supervision, encouraging clusters of similar instances in the embedding space.
      StableRep (Tian et al., 2024) addresses the instability in contrastive learning by introducing methods
135
      to stabilize representation learning, ensuring robustness across diverse training scenarios.

139
140   PlicoTabTransformer consists of three main components: columns embedding, plico vectors en-
141   coder, and downstream predictor. Let (X, y) be the feature-target pair. X ≡ {Xcat , Xcont } where
142   Xcat denotes categorical features and Xcont continuous features. X has a total of D columns, we
143   denote D1 as the number of columns of categorical features and D2 as the number of columns of
144   continuous features so D1 + D2 ≡ D. Xcat ≡ {x1 , . . . xD1 } where xi is a column of categorical
145
      features and Xcont ∈ RD2 . Depending on the dataset, y could also be categorical or continuous
      leading to classification or regression task, respectively.
146
147   We first feed the input X into the columns embedding component to obtain embeddings for each
148   D columns. The embedded input feature is then fed through the plico vector encoder to extract
149   M distinct representations from the D columns. The downstream predictor is an MLP trained for
150   classification or regression.

194   3.2     P LICO V ECTORS ENCODER
195
196   The plico Vectors encoder takes the embedded input features E and returns M plico vectors
197   {p1 , p2 . . . pM } where pm ∈ RB×C . M is a hyperparameter that could be tuned depending on
198   the dataset.
199   Similar to the FT-transformer (Gorishniy et al., 2021), we model each column within E ≡
200   {e1 , e2 . . . eD } as a sequence and feed them through transformer layers so that each column em-
201   bedding can learn to attended to the other column embeddings. Unlike the FT-transformer, we fed
202   E through the transformer M times (denoted as multi-pass) each with different learnable positional
203   embeddings (LPE, details are described in Subsection 3.2.1). Column-wise sum was performed
204   during each of the M passes resulting in M plico vectors. We included a contrastive loss to ensure
205
      that the plico vectors are distinct and orthogonal to one another (details are described in Subsection
      3.2.2). Figure 4 presents the architecture.
206
207
208   3.2.1    L EARNING POSITIONAL EMBEDDING AND MULTI - PASS TRANSFORMER
209
210   We trained M separate positional embeddings to encourage the transformer to attend to different
211   columns for each pass. We found that tokenizing the column indexes {1 . . . D} to an embedding
212   layer as a mapping of the position provided the best results (Gehring et al., 2017). For each m ∈
213   {1 . . . M } pass, we trained positional embeddings is in the form of P Em = {pem,1 , . . . , pem,D }.
214   The positional embedding is combined with Em resulting in {e1 + pem,1 , e2 + pem,2 . . . eD +
215   pem,D } then fed into the transformer. Column-wise sum was performed on the output of the trans-
      former to get a plico vector pm ∈ RB×C .

235   3.2.2    M AXIMISE DIFFERENCES BETWEEN PLICO VECTORS
236
      To encourage the plico vectors to be distinct and orthogonal, we included a loss function on the plico
237
      vectors where vectors from the same mth pass are pulled together and vectors from other passes are
238
      pushed away, similar to a contrastive learning paradigm.
239
240   We adapted the self-supervised contrastive loss, as described in (Khosla et al., 2020), to our appli-
241   cation that takes the form of:

248
      {1 . . . M } \ {i}. · is the dot product, τ is a temperature parameter, and A(i) ≡ I \ {i}.
249   Please note that in equation 1 of (Khosla et al., 2020), the authors designed their loss function to pull
250   the embeddings of different augmentations (a.k.a "views") together. Our contrastive loss needs to
251   perform the opposite (push the embeddings within the same view away from one another). Hence,
252   we removed the negative sign in Equation 1.
253   We also experimented with other loss functions including the cross entropy and multi margin loss.
254   To feed the plico vectors into the other loss functions, the plico vectors are concatenated and flattened
255   to R(B·M )×C . The label for the loss function was generated by tiling {1 . . . M } B times resulting
256   in labels of size B · M .

510   5   C ONCLUSION
511
512   In this paper, we introduced the PlicoTabTransformer, a novel approach that leverages multiple
513   passes of data through a transformer model with separate learnable position embeddings to learn
514   multiple distinct and orthogonal representations of tabular datasets. Our method demonstrated
515   state-of-the-art performance when compared to existing deep learning techniques in a subset of
516
      the datasets and was among the top ranked deep learning algorithms.
517   Given the inherent diversity in structured tabular datasets, including variations in the number of
518   columns and the nature of column data, it is evident that different algorithms have different advan-
519   tages. We believe that PlicoTabTransformer is a compelling option among the available algorithms
520   which could be used for tabular data.
521   To our knowledge, PlicoTabTransformer is among the first works to perform multiple passes of em-
522   beddings into transformer with multiple positional embeddings and creating distinct representations
523   with contrastive learning. We hope that researchers could build upon this framework and apply this
524   method to other neural network architecture and applications.
