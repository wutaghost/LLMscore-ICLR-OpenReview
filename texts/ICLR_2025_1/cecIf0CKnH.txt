009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Machine learning techniques for Optimal Power Flow (OPF) problems have re-
012            cently garnered significant attention, reflecting a broader trend of leveraging ma-
013            chine learning to approximate and/or accelerate the resolution of complex opti-
014            mization problems. These developments are necessitated by the increased volatility
015            and scale in energy production for modern and future grids. However, progress
016            in ML for OPF is hindered by the lack of standardized datasets and evaluation
017            metrics, from generating and solving OPF instances, to training and benchmarking
018
               machine learning models. To address this challenge, this paper introduces PGLearn,
               a comprehensive suite of standardized datasets and evaluation tools for ML and
019
               OPF. PGLearn implements realistic data generation procedures that capture both
020
               global and local variability, ensuring that datasets are representative of real-world
021            conditions. In addition, it supports multiple OPF formulations, including AC, DC,
022            and second-order cone formulations. Standardized datasets are made publicly
023            available to democratize access to this field, reduce the burden of data generation,
024            and enable the fair comparison of various methodologies. PGLearn also includes a
025            robust toolkit for training, evaluating, and benchmarking machine learning models
026            for OPF, with the goal of standardizing performance evaluation across the field.
027            By promoting open, standardized datasets and evaluation metrics, PGLearn aims
028            at democratizing and accelerating research and innovation in machine learning
029
               applications for optimal power flow problems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      The rapid evolution of energy systems, driven by mass integration of renewable and distributed energy
034
      resources, is creating new challenges in the maintenance, expansion, and operation of power grids.
035
      The increased volatility and scale of power generation in modern and future grids calls for innovative
036   solutions to manage uncertainty and ensure reliability (Zhang et al., 2021). Machine learning (ML)
037   has emerged as a powerful tool in this context, offering the potential to address key problems such as
038   optimizing grid operations and predicting power demand, enabling the use of previously intractable
039   applications such as real-time risk analysis (Chen et al., 2024). However, the success of ML models
040   depends on the availability of high-quality data, which is essential for training accurate and reliable
041   models (Khaloie et al., 2024; Lovett et al., 2024).
042   Optimal Power Flow (OPF) is a fundamental problem in power systems operations, focusing on
043   how to efficiently operate a power transmission system while satisfying physics, engineering, and
044   operations constraints. Most market-clearing algorithms for real-time electricity markets are based on
045   OPF, which makes it paramount to real-time operations. In addition, OPF forms the building block of
046   security-constrained unit commitment (SCUC) formulations used in day-ahead markets (Chen et al.,
047   2023), as well as transmission expansion planning problems. In practice, many instances of OPF
048   need to be solved at once in order to account for uncertainties in renewable power generation and/or
049   demand, making it a computationally intensive task – especially given the non-convex physics of
050
      AC power flow. Besides its real-world applications, OPF has also garnered significant attention as a
      test-bed for research methods integrating mathematical programming and machine learning.
051
052   The key motivation for using ML to address parametric OPF stems from the increased volatility and
053   scale in power generation in modern and future grids. Indeed, the growing penetration of intermittent
      renewable energy sources, especially wind and solar generation, is driving a significant growth in

086   1.1   M OTIVATION : DATA S CARSITY IN ML FOR OPF R ESEARCH
087
      Despite strong interest from industry, real, industry-scale data is scarce, mainly due to regulatory
088
      barriers that restrict the sharing of sensitive information on power grids. Thus, most previous ML for
089
      OPF works generate their own artificial datasets, often based on the Power Grid Lib Optimal Power
090   Flow (PGLib-OPF) (Babaeinejadsarookolaee et al., 2019) benchmark library – a collection of grid
091   snapshots originally designed for benchmarking AC-OPF optimization algorithms.
092
093
      While machine learning methods usually require many thousands of data points to train accurate
      models, PGLib-OPF only provides a single snapshot per grid. Hence, a data augmentation strategy
094
      is needed to “sample around” the provided snapshots in a realistic fashion. There is however no
095
      consensus in prior literature for how to perform this sampling, which has led to a highly fragmented
096   ecosystem where it is impossible to directly compare results from different works due to the use of
097   very different data distributions (Khaloie et al., 2024). Indeed, the characteristics of the learning
098   problem may vary substantially when considering different augmentation schemes, for example
099   correlated versus uncorrelated noise.
100
      To ensure that ML for OPF research is useful in practice, it is important to carefully consider the
101
      choices involved in designing a data augmentation scheme. For example, most works surveyed in
102   Khaloie et al. (2024) sample instances by perturbing individual loads independently of each other.
103   Figure 2 illustrates the limitations the resulting data distribution, for a system with 1354 buses. The
104   figure shows that, i) the resulting distribution displays a very narrow range of total demand, and ii)
105   the resulting OPF solutions exhibit simplistic patterns that do not capture global dynamics over a
106   wider range of total demand. As a consequence, ML models trained on data that does not capture
107   correlations can only be expected to perform well for a small total demand range, severely limiting
      their usability in practice.

      1.2   R ELATED W ORK
133
134   Due to strong interest from academia and industry, several OPF datasets and data generation packages
135   have been released in recent years. However, none simultaneously meet the requirements of being
136   actively maintained, considering large power networks, and using realistic sampling schemes.
137
      Some prior works (Donon et al., 2020; Chatzos et al., 2022; Klamkin et al., 2024) report results on
138   industry-based datasets, i.e., using data obtained from transmission system operators. Although these
139   works report more closely match real-world systems, the corresponding datasets are not publicly
140   available due to regulations around privacy and security.
141
      Despite a growing literature on ML for OPF, few datasets have been made publicly available. The
142
      datasets initially released alongside the OPFSampler (Robson et al., 2019) codebase are no longer
143
      available, and the code is unmaintained. The OPFLearn library (Joswig-Jones et al., 2022) provides
144   data augmentation tools built on top of PowerModels Coffrin et al. (2018), as well as a collection
145   of 10,000 samples of AC-OPF instances and their solution for five systems with up to 118 buses.
146   This code is no longer maintained, only considers uncorrelated demand perturbations, and reports
147   incomplete primal/dual solutions. More recently, and closest to this paper, OPFData (Lovett et al.,
148   2024) is a collection of AC-OPF datasets which considers systems with up to 13,659 buses. The
149   collection also includes instances with perturbed topology, obtained by randomly removing individual
150   lines or generators in the system. The main limitation of OPFData, however, is that it only considers
151   uncorrelated demand perturbation, leading to simplistic data distributions as illustrated in Figure 2.
152
      Additionally, OPFData only reports AC-OPF solutions, does not include dual solutions nor metadata
      such as solve times, and does not include the source code used to generate and solve samples.

156

157   ML and OPF. The contributions of PGLearn are as follows:
158
159          • PGLearn provides a collection of standardized datasets for large-scale OPF instances,
160            generated using a realistic and reproducible data augmentation methodology. The entire
161            collection totals over 1,000,000 OPF samples, split between training and testing data to
               allow for direct comparison of results.

      2     OPF F ORMULATIONS IN PGL EARN
186
187
      A unique feature of PGLearn is that it provides, for each OPF instance, solutions to several OPF
      formulations. This allows to compare, for the same input data, the performance of ML models
188
      trained using different formulations. Namely, PGLearn currently supports the nonlinear, non-convex
189
      AC-OPF, the second-order cone relaxation SOC-OPF, and the linear approximation DC-OPF. A brief
190   summary of each formulation is provided below. Due to space considerations, full OPF formulations
191   are stated in Appendix A.
192
193
      2.1   OPF F ORMULATIONS
194
195   AC-OPF The AC-OPF is considered the “full” steady-state optimal power flow formulation.
196   PGLearn uses the rectangular-power polar-voltage form, matching the ACPPowerModel formu-
197   lation implemented in PowerModels (Coffrin et al., 2018). This formulation includes quadratic
198   generator cost functions and non-convex AC power flow physics to accurately model the power
199   system. The full non-linear programming formulation is included in Model 1.
200
201   SOC-OPF The SOC-OPF is a second-order-cone relaxation of the AC-OPF proposed by Jabr
202
      (2006b). The SOC-OPF better approximates the full-physics AC-OPF compared to the linear DC-
      OPF, but is more complicated to solve. A description of how to derive the SOC-OPF, and its full
203
      conic programming formulation, is included with Model 2.
204
205
      DC-OPF The DC-OPF is a sparse linear approximation to the AC-OPF (Christie et al., 2000). It is
206
      commonly used in industry to approximate AC-OPF in cases where solving AC-OPF within time
207   constraints is intractable. Among other simplifications, it considers only active power and fixes all
208   voltage magnitudes to 1. A list of all assumptions required to derive the DC-OPF, and its full linear
209   programming formulation, is included with Model 4.
210

277   factor of each load is varied by sampling the local noise independently for the active and reactive
278   components. This sampling procedure mimics real power system behavior since in practice, machine
279   learning training takes time, so models are trained day-ahead on demand ranges given by forecasting
280   systems for the next day (Chen et al., 2022). The local noise is applied to generate diverse samples at
281
      all values of total load, ensuring the usability of the machine learning system under various demand
      settings. The global correlation is important to ensure that the model captures a wide total demand
282

      Status Sampling To generate the N − 1 datasets, disabled branches/generators are sampled
304   following the procedure used in OPFData (Lovett et al., 2024). Either one generator or one (non-
305   bridge, to preserve connectedness of the network) branch is disabled per instance.

307   Train-Test Split The training and testing sets contain only feasible input samples, i.e. those inputs
308   for which a solution was found for all formulations. The feasible samples are then shuffled using a
309   seeded random number generator (MersenneTwister(42) from Julia 1.10.5). Then, the first 80%
310   of the shuffled feasible samples are selected as training data and the remaining 20% as testing data.
311   Users should further split the training data to create validation and/or calibration sets as needed; the
312   testing data should be kept unchanged to allow for direct comparison of reported results. This creates
313   three datasets – train, test, and infeasible, where samples in the infeasible category are infeasible for
314
      at least one of the formulations considered.

345   5     B ENCHMARKING M ACHINE L EARNING M ODELS FOR OPF
346
347
      This section describes evaluation metrics for optimization proxies, catering to the specific context
348
      of learning the solution maps of optimization problems. It also provides guidelines for comparing
349   and benchmarking models. It is important to recognize that there is no universal metric, and that
350   researchers should report a combination of metrics to accurately capture the behavior and performance
351   of their models, keeping in mind the downstream use-cases of their contribution.
352
353
354
      5.1   ACCURACY METRICS
355
      The following lists several important metrics in the evaluation of optimization proxy models, i.e.
356
      models that predict the output of a parametric optimization problem given the parameters. They are
357   stated below on a per-instance basis; aggregations should be performed by taking the mean/standard
358   deviation and maximum (e.g. over the test set samples).
359
360
361
      Optimality gap This metric reports how close the predicted objective value (i.e. the objective value
      of the predicted solution) is to the true optimal value. It is important to note that predicted solutions
362
      are often infeasible, hence it is not fair to assess solutions based on optimality gap alone.
363
364
365   Constraint violations This metric reports the magnitude of constraint violation, aggregated per
366   group of constraints. Here, “group” refers to constraint of the same type, e.g. the |N | Kirchhoff’s
367
      current law constraints in DC-OPF. In addition to average/maximum violation magnitude, researchers
      should also report (for each group of constraints) the proportion of constraints violated and the total
368
      violation (the sum of violations within each group).
369
370
371   Distance to feasible set This metric reports how far the predicted solution is from the closest feasible
372   point. This requires solving the corresponding projection problem for each instance (replacing the
373
      objective with ∥x − x⋆ ∥ where x⋆ is the predicted solution and x is subject to the original constraints).
374
375   Distance to optimal solution This metric reports how far the predicted solution is from the optimal
376   solution. Note that a solution can exhibit small residuals but large distance to feasibility. In real-life,
377   this can mean that a solution with small residual may need large changes to become feasible, with
      potentially a large increase in cost.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      5.2   C OMPUTATIONAL PERFORMANCE METRICS
379
380   These metrics evaluate how fast the proxy models are, compared to optimization solvers. It’s
381   important to recognize that ML proxies are only heuristics, whereas optimization solvers have
382   stronger guarantees. Two types of metrics should be reported: computing time for applications where
383   a single instance is solved at a time and throughput for applications that need to solve large batches
384   of instances (e.g. large-scale simulations). Timing results should be reported using CPU/GPU.hour
385   (i.e. 2 CPU cores for 1 hour corresponds to 2 CPU.hr). Similarly to the accuracy metrics, aggregated
386   timing results should report both the mean/standard deviation and the maximum across samples.
387   Finally, in line with general guidelines for reporting ML results, researchers should always report the
388
      device (CPU and/or GPU) used for running experiments.
389
390   Data-generation time This metric reports the total time spent obtaining the ground-truth pri-
391   mal/dual solutions required for the training (and validation) set of the proxy model – the time spent
392   generating the test set can be excluded.
393
394
395
      Training time This metric reports the time spent training the optimization proxy model. In addition,
      researchers should comment on how often the model would need to be re-trained in a practical
396
      application. For instance, it is not realistic to train a model every hour if the training time is 6 hours.
397
398
399   Inference time This metric reports the time spent producing a solution to a single instance. This
400   is useful if the downstream application involves solving instances sequentially, for instance, when
401   solving a market-clearing problem every 5 minutes. Researchers should report the maximum
402   time across samples, especially for architectures that involve an iterative scheme such as gradient
403
      correction (Donti et al., 2021), implicit layers (Agrawal et al., 2019), or optimization solvers, because
      of performance variability.
404
405
406   Instance throughput This metric reports how many instances can be processed per unit of time
407   with a fixed computational resource budget. This metric is relevant for settings where large number
408   of instances need to be evaluated, e.g., when running large-scale simulations that require solving
409   multiple OPF instances. In addition, it better captures the batched processing advantages of GPU
410   devices.
411   The solve time metadata included with the PGLearn datasets can be used to obtain data-generation
412   time and instance throughput for optimization solvers. However, it is important to note that PGLearn
413   datasets are generated using a single thread per instance, and utilize multiple processes per machine.
414   Such settings are known to have an adverse impact on the performance of optimization solvers.
415   Hence, the solving times reported in the metadata are likely over-estimates compared to solving one
416   instance at a time in a “clean” environment or with multi-threaded solvers.

419   6     L IMITATIONS
420
421   6.1   DATA L IMITATIONS
422
423   In the absence of publicly-available, granular datasets released directly by system operators, PGLearn
424   is limited, like prior works, to using a data augmentation scheme to generate samples around a static
425   snapshot. Nevertheless, it is important to note that the reference snapshots selected for PGLearn are
426   based on real power grids in France, Europe, Texas, and the American midwest (Josz et al., 2016;
427   Fliscounakis et al., 2013; Kunkolienkar et al., 2024; University of Wisconsin-Madison, 2024).
428   Another limitation of PGLearn is the limited variety of topologies, and the nature of topology changes.
429   Namely, PGLearn considers topology variations by removing individual lines. In contrast, real-
430   life operations include multiple categories of topology changes, such as switching multiple lines
431   and reconfiguring buses within a substation. Additional research is needed to better capture this
      lesser-studied facet of power grid operations.

      6.2   F UTURE C OLLECTIONS
433
434   PGLearn aims to provide curated datasets that are updated over time, to integrate new data-generation
435   procedures and OPF formulations. To that end, future versions of PGLearn will comprise OPF
436   formulations that include elements present in market-clearing formulations used by system operators.
437   This includes, for instance, the integration of reserve products and support for piece-wise linear
438   production curves Ma et al. (2009). Future releases will also include datasets for larger systems.
439   Furthermore, two avenues for future work include the implementation of security-constrained OPF
440   formulations, as well as the integration of time-series data that capture the temporal dynamics of
441   power grids. This latter aspect is essential for real-life operations.

      7     C ONCLUSION
444
445
      This paper has introduced PGLearn, an open-source learning toolkit for optimal power flow. PGLearn
446   addresses the lack of standardized datasets for ML and OPF by releasing several datasets of large-
447   scale OPF instances. It is the first collection that comprises complete primal and dual solutions for
448   multiple OPF formulations. In addition to releasing public datasets, PGLearn provides open-source
449   tools for data generation, and for the training and evaluation of ML models. These open-source tools
450   enable reproducible and fair evaluation of methods for ML and OPF, thereby democratizing access to
451   the field.
452   The PGLearn collection contains, in its initial release, over 1,000,000 OPF samples, split between
453   training and testing data. It is released alongside extensive documentation and code, allowing users
454   to generate additional datasets as needed, and to benchmark the performance of ML models. The
455   paper has also provided several performance metrics and guidelines on how to report them. These
456   guidelines aim at capturing specific aspects of ML for OPF that fall outside the scope of traditional
457   ML applications. This includes, for instance, the fundamental importance of measuring and reporting
458   constraint violations, as well as accurate reporting of data-generation, training and inference times
459   when evaluating computational performance.
460   Finally, PGLearn aims to democratize access to research on ML and OPF by removing the barrier to
461   entry caused by the computational requirements of large-scale data generation. It also aims to align
462   academic research more closely to the scale and complexity of real-world power systems. This will,
463   in turn, unlock the potential for modern AI techniques to assist in making future energy systems more
464   efficient, reliable, and sustainable.

765   PGLearn supports any nonlinear optimization solver supported by JuMP. The default configuration
766   solves AC-OPF instances using Ipopt (Biegler & Zavala, 2009) with the MA27 linear solver (Duff &
767   Reid, 1982) via LibHSL (Fowkes et al., 2024). Note that, unless a global optimization solver is used,
768   global optimality of AC-OPF solutions is not guaranteed. Nevertheless, previous experience suggests
769   that solutions obtained by Ipopt are typically close to optimal Gopinath et al. (2020).
770
771   A.2.2      SOC O PTIMAL P OWER F LOW
772
      PGLearn also implements the second-order cone relaxation of AC-OPF proposed by Jabr in (Jabr,
773   2006a; 2007), herein referred to as SOC-OPF. This convex relaxation can be solved in polynomial
774   time using, e.g., an interior-point algorithm, and is exact on radial networks Molzahn & Hiskens
775   (2019).
776
      The SOC-OPF relaxation is obtained by introducing variables
777
778                                                   wi = vi2                                                                    (8a)
779                                                   were = vi vj cos(θi − θj )                                                  (8b)
780
                                                     weim = vi vj sin(θi − θj )                                                   (8c)
781
782   together with the valid (non-convex) constraint
783
784
                                        (were )2 + (weim )2 = wi wj ,          ∀e = (i, j) ∈ E.                                    (9)
785   Then, constraints (7b), (7c), (7d), (7e), (7f), (7g), (7j), and (7n) are reformulated using these new
786   variables, and constraint (9) is convexified into the so-called Jabr inequality
787
788
                                        (were )2 + (weim )2 ≤ wi wj ,          ∀e = (i, j) ∈ E.                                   (10)
789   Finally, valid lower and upper bounds for wre , wim variables are derived from (7n), (7j) and (9), using
790   the same strategy as Coffrin et al. (2018). Model 2 states the resulting SOC-OPF formulation, in
791   conic form.
792
      The implementation in PGLearn slighly differs from Coffrin et al. (2018) in the definition of wre , wim
793
      variables. Namely, in Coffrin et al. (2018), wre , wim variables are defined per bus-pair, defined as
794
      a pair of buses (i, j) linked by at least one branch e = (i, j) ∈ E. In contrast, PGLearn defines
795   wre , wim variables for each branch; the two formulations are equivalent unless parallel branches are
796   present. This design choice was motivated by the simplicity of the branch-level formulation and the
797   corresponding data formats, and was found to have a marginal impact on the quality of the relaxation.
798
      The dual SOC-OPF model is stated in Model 3. Dual variables λp , λq , λpf , λqf , λpt , λqt are associated
799
      to equality constraints (11b), (11c), (11d), (11e), (11f), (11e), and are therefore unrestricted. Dual
800
      conic variables ν f , ν t and ω are associated to conic constraints (11h), (11i) and (11j), respectively.
801   Dual variables µθ and µ̄θ are associated to the lower and upper side of voltage angle difference
802
      constraint (11k). Finally, dual variables µw , µ̄w , µpg , µ̄pg , µqg , µ̄qg , µpf , µ̄pf , µqf , µ̄qf , µpt , µ̄pt , µqt , µ̄qt ,
803
      µwr , µ̄wr , µwi , µ̄wi are associated to lower and upper bounds on variables w, pg , qg , pf , qf , pt , qt ,
804
805
      wre , wim , respectively.
806   Note that users need not interact with the dual SOC-OPF problem directly, as interior-point solvers
807   typically report both primal and dual information. The formulation in Model 3 is stated for com-
808   pleteness and to support research on predicting dual solutions. PGLearn supports the use of any
809   JuMP-supported conic solver. By default, PGLearn solves SOC-OPF instances using Clarabel
      (Goulart & Chen, 2024).

      A.2.3    DC O PTIMAL P OWER F LOW
898
899   The DC-OPF is a popular linear approximation of AC-OPF, which underlies most electricity markets.
900   The DC approximation is motivated by several assumptions, whose validity mainly holds for trans-
901   mission systems. Namely, the DC approximation assumes that all voltage magnitudes are fixed to
902   one per-unit, voltage angles are assumed to be small (i.e. sin(θ) ≈ θ), and that reactive power and
903
      power losses can be neglected. The reader is referred to Molzahn & Hiskens (2019) for additional
      background on the DC approximation.
904
905   Model 4 states the DC-OPF formulation used in PGLearn. Constraint (13b) enforces nodal power
906   balance through Kirchhoff’s current law. Constraint (13c) expresses active power flows on each
907   branch according to Ohm’s law, and constraint (13d) restricts the voltage angle difference between
908   each branch’s endpoints. Constraint (13e) fixes the reference bus’ voltage angle to zero. Finally,
909
      constraints (13f) and (13g) enforce lower and upper limits on active power generation and active
      power flows.
