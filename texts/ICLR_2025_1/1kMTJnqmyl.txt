009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            A plethora of jailbreaking attacks have been proposed to obtain harmful responses
012            from safety-tuned LLMs. These methods largely succeed in coercing the target
013            output in their original settings, but their attacks vary substantially in fluency and
014            computational effort. In this work, we propose a unified threat model for the prin-
015            cipled comparison of these methods. Our threat model combines constraints in
016
               perplexity, measuring how far a jailbreak deviates from natural text and computa-
               tional budget in total FLOPs. For the former, we build an N-gram language model
017
               on 1T tokens, which, unlike model-based perplexity, allows for an LLM-agnostic
018
               and inherently interpretable evaluation. We adapt popular attacks to this new, re-
019            alistic threat model, with which we, for the first time, benchmark these attacks on
020            equal footing. After a rigorous comparison, we find attack success rates against
021            safety-tuned modern models to be lower than previously presented and that at-
022            tacks based on discrete optimization significantly outperform recent LLM-based
023            attacks. Being inherently interpretable, our threat model allows for a comprehen-
024            sive analysis and comparison of jailbreak attacks. We find that effective attacks
025            exploit and abuse infrequent N-grams, either selecting N-grams absent from real-
026            world text or rare ones, e.g., specific to code datasets.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   As LLMs can be used to facilitate fraud, spread fake news, conduct hacking attacks, etc. (Willison,
031   2023; Greshake et al., 2023; Carlini et al., 2021; Geiping et al., 2024), model providers often safety-
032   tune LLMs to minimize the risks of potential misuse, mitigate harm, and avoid complying with
033   malicious queries (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022). However, while this alignment
034   ensures average-case safety, it does not currently extend to adversarial scenarios (Carlini et al.,
035   2024; Qi et al., 2024), where an attacker actively tries to bypass the safety measures.
036   Such attacks on safety alignment are often done using jailbreaks, which we refer to as adversarially
037   designed text inputs that circumvent safety tuning and elicit harmful behavior. In this work, we
038   focus on threat models for these attacks. A threat model formalizes a security question by clearly
039   defining the goals and constraints of both attackers and defenders, specifying the environment, the
040   actions each can take, and the sequence in which they operate.
041
      While adversarial attacks in computer vision commonly adopt lp -ball threat models to be impercep-
042   tible to humans but still fool the model (Madry et al., 2018), jailbreaks in language come in all shapes
043   and sizes, and optimize for different metrics. These attacks range from completely gibberish suf-
044   fixes (Zou et al., 2023) to human-like social engineering techniques applied to an LLM (Zeng et al.,
045   2024). While all these methods are designed to succeed in terms of attack success rate (ASR), they
046   also commonly report their efficacy based on different combinations of metrics such as fluency (aka
047   readability, stealthiness, human-likeness) (Liu et al., 2024b; Yang et al., 2024; Mehrotra et al., 2023;
048   Sadasivan et al., 2024), query efficiency (Chao et al., 2023; 2024), runtime (Geisler et al., 2024;
049   Sadasivan et al., 2024), length of a jailbreak (Zou et al., 2023; Sadasivan et al., 2024), etc. This
050
      heterogeneity prevents a clear understanding of the jailbreaking attack landscape and complicates
      the fair comparison of different methods.
051
052   Among these metrics, fluency is a key distinguishing factor between attacks. It is implicitly enforced
053   in many jailbreaking methods (Liu et al., 2024b; Yang et al., 2024; Mehrotra et al., 2023; Sadasivan
      et al., 2024) and is often measured by perplexity using LLMs. Moreover, perplexity-based filters

083

084
085   To address these issues, we propose to use an N-gram language model (N-gram LM) perplexity.
086   This approach is: i) LLM-agnostic: The N-gram LM perplexity is a principled way to measure
087   text fluency, allowing for comparison of attacks across different LLMs; ii) interpretable: Each
088
      N-gram’s contribution to the perplexity can be examined and has a direct interpretation - the higher
      the perplexity of a particular N-gram, the rarer it is in the train data; iii) simple: The N-gram LM is
089
      a simple model of the co-occurrence of tokens, which is harder to exploit for adversarial attacks; iv)
090
      fast-to-evaluate: Any N-gram LM is implemented as a hash table, and thus N-gram LM perplexity
091   is computed through a simple lookup. In contrast, computing LLM-based perplexity requires an
092   auxiliary model and thus depends on the number of parameters of a given model.

094
      Our contributions are as follows:
095   • In Section 3, we construct a lightweight bigram LM from the Dolma dataset based on 1T tokens,
096     which does not require any GPU RAM for inference.
097   • In Section 3, we introduce a threat model for jailbreaking attacks using fluency measured by our
098     bigram LM perplexity and compute cost in total floating point operations (see Appendix E).
099   • In Section 4, we adapt popular attacks for the proposed threat model. Then, in Section 5 we
100     benchmark them on several open-source models, evaluate the Llama model family across different
101     sizes and generations, and investigate the utility-robustness trade-off of the threat model.
102   • In Section 5.4, we use the inherent interpretability of our N-gram LM to attribute the employed
103     bigrams in the attacks to the corresponding datasets of Dolma. We show that the baseline PRS
104     attack (Andriushchenko et al., 2024) relies heavily on unseen bigrams, whereas our adaptive PRS
105     attack uses often seen ones. For Llama3-8b, we show that our adaptive GCG attack uses mainly
106     bigrams from code data, while adaptive PRS uses datasets of natural text, such as Reddit and
107     Gutenberg. Moreover, our threat model enables cross-model comparisons, offering insights into
        the failure modes of different LMs.

123   2    R ELATED W ORK
124
      Red Teaming LLMs. LLM providers strive to minimize harmful interactions with their models. To
125
      do so, manual red teaming is incorporated, where human testers probe the bounds of the model’s
126
      safety tuning (Ganguli et al., 2022), and the model is updated to give outputs only on safe queries.
127   This, however, has been shown to miss many, often unnatural, but very successful automated attacks
128   (Zou et al., 2023; Andriushchenko et al., 2024). Over the last year, there has been an explosion
129   of automated jailbreaking techniques. We summarize a few representative attacks in Table 1 from
130   three important categories: i) white-box (access to both the probabilities and gradients); ii) score-
131   based black-box (access to the output probabilities); iii) decision-based black-box (access only to
132   the generated text). They lack a standardized threat model, which renders them incomparable.
133   Threat Model in Computer Vision. In computer vision, on the other hand, the community quickly
134   converged on a single threat model, namely attacks in an lp ball of a chosen radius r (Croce et al.,
135   2021). By decreasing r and increasing p, this constraint allows for creating stealthy attacks that are
136   imperceptible to humans. Having an accepted threat model led to significant progress in adversarial
137   robustness in the computer vision domain, but stymied work on other more realistic threat models.
138
      LLM Safety Guardrails. Using safe system prompts is the simplest approach to defend against
139   jailbreaks and other harmful inputs. It has been shown to significantly reduce the performance
140   of simple attacks (Xu et al., 2024; Samvelyan et al., 2024). A more complex approach is to use
141   additional models that evaluate a model’s response before it is sent. The earliest proposals here
142   already use model-based perplexity (Alon & Kamfonas, 2023; Jain et al., 2023). However, only
143   Jain et al. (2023) also constructed adaptive attacks. They perform adaptive attacks only against
144   non-safety-tuned models, and their GCG attacks report low ASR, even for Vicuna-7B. Using our
145   threat model, in which we construct strong adaptive attacks, we come to different conclusions, find-
146   ing that these attacks can indeed work under perplexity constraints and against strong, safety-tuned
147
      models. In this paper, we focus on the N-gram perplexity constraint; however, in Appendix I, we
      additionally show that one can easily replace N-gram perplexity with the model-based perplexity.
148
      We further clearly characterize the full utility-robustness trade-off of the perplexity constraint.
149
      More recent work on guardrails has turned to dedicated safeguard LLM models, e.g. Llama Guard
150   (Inan et al., 2023; Llama Team, 2024). However, since these models are themselves based on neural
151   networks, they can be broken by adversarial attacks, as shown in Yuan et al. (2024) and Zou (2023).
152
153
      LLM Jailbreaking Benchmarks. Existing jailbreaking benchmarks for LLMs (Xie et al., 2024;
      Mazeika et al., 2024; Chao et al., 2024) aim to standardize the evaluation of attacks and defenses
154
      but fail to account for the significant differences in existing jailbreaking methods or to agree on a
155
      consistent evaluation method. As a result, comparisons between attacks are often inconsistent, with
156   each attack using its own set of metrics and without consideration of adaptive attacks. In Table 1, we
157   have compiled the key metrics identified by authors in previous studies to provide a more objective
158   analysis of existing attacks. Among existing benchmarks, HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024) stands
159   out as the most comprehensive, both in terms of the number of models and attacks investigated and
160   as it addresses many previous evaluation flaws. In this work, we construct a realistic threat model
161   comparable across LLMs, see Section 3, for which we adapt popular jailbreaking attacks which we
      combine with HarmBench to provide a strong evaluation of LLMs in our threat model.

245   3.3       D EFINING THE T HREAT M ODEL
246
247   To construct the threat model using the perplexity filter, we assume a realistic setting to generate
248
      and evaluate jailbreaks: A single-turn chat scenario, where an attacker sends a text input to the
      model and receives a text output in response. The attacker cannot prefill the model’s response,
249
      exclude the system prompt, or modify the chat template. To establish a lower bound for the model’s
250
      safety and simplify scenarios where an attacker may already have access to a related LLM, we
251   allow the attacker white-box access (however, only GCG requires it). This, however, still allows
252   for the successful transfer of the created attacks to strong black-box models, such as GPTs, as we
253   additionally show in Appendix H and as reported in Zou et al. (2023); Andriushchenko et al. (2024).
254
      We assume that the provider uses both the chat template and a safe system prompt, which we choose
255
      to be that of Llama2. For a target LLM M, a jailbreak xjailbreak is in the threat model T, if:
256
257   1. it has the bigram perplexity at a rolling window S8 less than γ0.999 .
258   2. a judge classifies it as successful, i.e., J (M(xjailbreak ), xmalicious ) = 1.
259   3. it is generated with a total number of FLOPs lower than attacker budget α. We set α = 1019 .

       5.3      A DAPTIVE ATTACKS IN THE P ROPOSED T HREAT M ODEL
433
434
       In Figure 4, we demonstrate that almost all attacks adapted to our threat model have lower ASR and
435
       a higher computational budget. Adaptive PRS performs best by attaining the highest ASR within
436
       the given computational budget. As we additionally show in Table 4, PRS and GCG show a similar
437    drop in performance as PAIR, BEAST, and AutoDan while satisfying the perplexity constraint. This
438    means that PRS is superior when compared under the same threat model. We observe that the
439    adaptive PRS attack achieves an even higher ASR than the baseline attack for Llama3 models. We
440    speculate that it might be due to specific adversarial training employed as a part of the safety-tuning
441    procedure, such that infrequent tokens manifest a spurious feature for an unsafe prompt (Dubey et al.,
442    2024). We sort rows in Table 4 by the model generation and size and observe that for the Llama
443    model family, there is no consistent improvement in robustness neither across model generations
444    nor across model size. This might be surprising given that stronger efforts in safety-tuning are likely
445
       done in the more recent models: Adaptive PRS achieves the highest ASR on Llama3-8b, higher
       than on LLama2-7b, and PRS ASR on Llama3-8b is even higher than that of the smallest model -
446
       Llama3.2-1b. For the Gemma model family, attacks against the newer model, Gemma2-2b, have a
447
       higher ASR than against the older Gemma-7b. We see, moreover, that, despite the extensive safety
448    tuning in Zhu et al. (2023), Starling-7b-α is less robust than the non-safety tuned model Vicuna-13b.
449    Similarly to Andriushchenko et al. (2024), we also observe that Zephyr-7b-R2D2 is not robust.
450
451
       These observations stress the importance of having an LLM-agnostic threat model and adaptive
       attacks: Only this way can we compare jailbreaking attacks well. Next, we analyze the effect of
452
       varying TPR of our threat model on ASR and investigate the behavior of the attacks in more detail.

455    5.4      A NALYSIS OF JAILBREAKING ATTACKS AND M ODELS
456
457    Tightening the Threat Model. To explore the trade-off between the utility of the threat model and
458    the robustness of an LLM in it, we vary the TPR threshold of our N-gram perplexity filter on the
459    first 50 malicious queries in Figure 5: Using a less conservative threshold than a TPR of 99.9%,
460    further reduces the ASR for Llama2-7b significantly. However, the reduced utility because of higher
461    rejection of normal inputs might not be acceptable in practice. In Appendix H, we transfer attacks
462    generated for varying values of TPR to GPTs.
463    Investigating the Models and Attacks. Previously, we could only understand the behavior of a par-
464    ticular attack on a particular model from their ASR or qualitative examples. Here, for the first time,
465    we show how to compare them in more detail. Concretely, we investigate two safety-tuned mod-
466    els - Llama2-7b and Llama3.1-8b. First, in Figure 6, we show how the distribution of the bigrams
467    used for the successful jailbreaks compares to that of the distribution of the bigrams in our selected
468    subset of Dolma and external Alpaca dataset (Ruebsamen, 2023). We can see that they confirm the
469
       intuition that lower-perplexity adaptive attacks have a distribution of bigrams shifted to the left. It is
       more pronounced for the Llama2-7b model. Next, we show in Figure 7 how we can use our dataset
470
       selection, introduced in Section 3, to do a more fine-grained train dataset attribution (TDA) across
471
       attacks on Llama3-8b. On the pie charts, we see that, unlike adaptive PRS, successful jailbreaks of
472    adaptive GCG rely significantly on code data. More details and examples are in Appendix D.

      6                                    C ONCLUSION
527
528
      Despite recent efforts to develop jailbreaking benchmarks, the absence of a unified threat model
529   complicates attack comparisons and leaves attacks and models not transparent. To address this, we
530   propose a realistic and interpretable threat model based on the N-gram language model perplexity
531   and total FLOPs, adapting popular attacks within this framework. Our evaluation shows that most
532   attacks fail to achieve an ASR above 50% on safety-tuned models, with only PRS and GCG effec-
533   tively maintaining high ASR while satisfying perplexity constraints. Moreover, we show that by
534   using our adaptation methods, best-performing methods can easily be constrained to the perplexity
535   constraint, such that they outperform all methods that aim to decrease perplexity by design.

        We collect a selection of datasets from Soldaini et al. (2024): MegaWika, Project Gutenberg, Stack-
        Exchange, arXiv, Reddit, StarCoder, and Refined Web into one dataset D, which we join and then
922
        split into Dtrain and Dval . On Dtrain , we compute the N-gram language model. We choose diverse
923
        datasets to better estimate the probability distribution of text which one expects during the use of
924     large language models, which increases the utility of the proposed threat model.
925
926     Metric and Window Size. A naturalness metric should be able to differentiate well between natural
927
        text and non-natural representative jailbreaks. To find one, we compute different scores for the case
        of N = 2 (bigrams) and check their separation quality.
928
929     We aim to find statistics robust to different adversarial examples and outliers in sliding windows of
930     a fixed length W , based on starting points from previous work (Jain et al., 2023). The advantage
931     of this approach over computing scores on the whole string is that we can select and evaluate a
932     threshold for a metric measured on a window of a fixed size.
933     We choose the metric and the respective threshold for which the lowest FPR on AW for W ∈
934     {2, 4, 8, 16} is achieved. We further validate it by computing the TPR on an external set of 27630
935     prompts from the cleaned Alpaca dataset (Ruebsamen, 2023) which have lengths of 16 or more
936     tokens after the tokenization.
937     N-gram Depth. In the main body, we only show perplexity constraints based on bigram models.
938     We find this to be an optimal choice, trading off the precision and robustness of the resulting binary
939     classifier. Our ablation of this choice can be found in Figure 9. We plot results for N-gram LMs up
940     to N = 6, based on the Infini-gram implementation (Liu et al., 2024a), for window sizes (2, 4, 8, 16)
941     for each N-gram.
942
        Threshold Selection. To prevent a significant drop in the utility of an LLM the threshold for the
943     score should be chosen such that one has a very high rate of correctly detecting natural text as
944     natural. Therefore, we select a set NW of 1e7 windows of size W of natural text from Dval as

948     of P (wn |wn−N +1 , . . . , wn−1 ).

1104   As noted by Jain et al. (2023), the computational budget is critical for a realistic attacker, especially
1105   since defenses can significantly increase the already substantial computational burden. To accurately
1106   reflect the attacker’s perspective, we use the total number of floating point operations (FLOPs) as
1107   our primary metric, encompassing all components of an algorithm involved in achieving a jailbreak.
1108
       This includes any auxiliary models, such as the target or judge models being a part of a jailbreaking
       algorithm.
1109
1110   We calculate total FLOPs using the commonly accepted estimate k × d × 2, where k represents the
1111   number of input and output tokens, and d is the model size (Kaplan et al., 2020). The backward pass
1112   is estimated to be twice the cost of the forward pass (Hobbhahn, 2021).
1113   A limitation of this metric is that it applies only to algorithmically generated jailbreaks, not those
1114   pre-calculated and shared online. For instance, a simple look-up table of ready-made jailbreaks
1115   would register as a zero-FLOPs attack. While this is a trivial example, more sophisticated attacks
1116   like AutoDan (Liu et al., 2024b) and PRS (Andriushchenko et al., 2024) rely on pre-made prompts
1117   and suffixes, masking their true computational cost. From a practical standpoint, we argue that
1118   using pre-made templates and suffixes significantly limits the attack’s value, as these are likely to be
1119   patched in newer models, rendering the attack inherently short-lived.
