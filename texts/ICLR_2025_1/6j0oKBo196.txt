009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011              Based on topological proximity message passing, graph neural networks (GNNs)
012              can quickly model data patterns on graphs. However, at test time, when the
013              node feature and topological structure of the graph data are out-of-distribution
014              (OOD), the performance of pre-trained GNNs will be hindered. Existing test-time
015              methods either fine-tune the pre-trained model or overlook the discrepancy be-
016
                 tween the prior knowledge in pre-trained models and the test graph. We propose
                 a novel self-supervised test-time adaptation paradigm (GOAT1 ), through graph
017
                 augmentation-to-augmentation strategy, that enables a simple adapter to mitigate
018
                 the distribution gap of training data and test-time data. GOAT reduces general-
019              ization error for node classification in various pre-trained settings through experi-
020              ments on six benchmark datasets spanning three distinct real-world OOD scenar-
021              ios. Remarkably, GOAT outperforms state-of-the-art test-time methods, and our
022              empirical study further demonstrates the interpretability of the OOD representa-
023              tion generated from our method.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
027
      Graph pre-training has emerged as a powerful technique for preserving information from large-scale
028
      upstream data (Kipf and Welling, 2016; Hamilton et al., 2017; Veličković et al., 2018; Hu et al.,
029   2019; Lu et al., 2021), enabling graph neural networks (GNNs) to learn rich representations that
030   can be transferred to various downstream graph tasks. Whereas, the effectiveness of pre-trained
031   GNNs is often hampered by distribution shifts (Zhu et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021a; Koh et al., 2021;
032   Yehudai et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022a), especially in real-world scenarios where the test data is out-
033   of-distribution (OOD) and labels are unavailable. This poses a significant challenge for the practical
034   application of GNNs, as their performance tends to deteriorate severely under such distribution
035   shifts. Intuitively, in Table 1, the pre-trained GNN’s performance degrades when the OOD test data
036   evolves through time. Therefore, adapting pre-trained GNNs to test graphs with no labels and OOD
037
      data is crucial.
038   To address the issue of distribution shift, various approaches have been proposed, such as invariant
039   risk minimization (Arjovsky et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2022), domain-invariant learning (Muandet et al.,
040   2013), and invariant representation learning (Wu et al., 2021b; Li et al., 2022b). These methods aim
041   to learn representations that are robust to distribution shifts by explicitly optimizing for invariant
042   variables across different domains or environments. However, a common limitation of these ap-
043
      proaches is their reliance on labeled data from the target domain during training, which may not
      always be available in real-world scenarios. Moreover, these methods are designed to be applied
044
      during the training phase and do not deal with the challenges of adapting pre-trained GNNs to OOD
045
      data at test time, when access to labeled data is often limited or nonexistent.
046
047   On the other hand, existing graph test-time adaptation approaches, such as reconstructing the clas-
048   sifier head of GNNs (Wang et al., 2022) or fine-tuning the entire model’s parameters (Zhang et al.,
049
      2024; Wang et al.), trying to focus on leveraging the generalization ability of the pre-trained model.
      These model-centric approaches face difficulties in handling OOD scenarios due to their reliance on
050
      learned parameters that may not generalize well to unseen distributions (Hendrycks and Dietterich,
051
      2019; Arjovsky et al., 2020). Furthermore, fine-tuning the model’s parameters often requires sig-
052   nificant computational resources, making it challenging or even infeasible in resource-constrained

074
      2023; Yu et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024) have shown promise in bridging the gap
075   between pre-trained models on upstream tasks and downstream tasks in the graph domain. Existing
076   prompt methods aim to adapt pre-trained GNNs to new tasks by designing task-specific prompts
077   that guide the model to generate relevant representations. Nevertheless, prompt-based methods still
078   rely on labeled data to adapt to new tasks and the designed prompts do not explicitly address the
079   data-centric differences between the upstream training set distribution and the test data distribution.
080
      Present work. We propose Graph Out-of-distribution Augmentation-to-augmentaion adaptation
081   in Test time (GOAT), a novel self-supervised test-time tuning strategy that allows the model to
082   dynamically adapt to unknown test distributions without requiring access to source training data or
083   training details. (1) A key contribution of GOAT is considering the symmetry and consistency of the
084   OOD test graph by collaborating the point estimation with the commutativity of the representation of
085   GNN and the adapter. (2) In addition to achieving parameter efficiency and interpretability, GOAT
086   introduces a topology-aware feature bias adapter, which acts as a data-centric distribution mapping,
087   indicating the OOD degree of the test graph. (3) We conduct extensive experiments on multiple real-
088   world datasets, demonstrating the superiority of GOAT in handling distribution shifts on different
089
      backbones and comparing them to state-of-the-art baselines.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
092
093
      Distribution Shift on Graphs. Graph-structured data often exhibits out-of-distribution (OOD) phe-
094
      nomena (Li et al., 2022a; Song and Wang, 2022). To tackle this challenge, researchers have proposed
095
      methods for learning invariant representations (Muandet et al., 2013; Arjovsky et al., 2020; Wu et al.,
096   2021b; 2022; Li et al., 2022b), generalizing pre-trained GNNs (Hu et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2021;
097   Zhu et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022a; Song and Wang, 2022; Guo et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023), de-
098   tecting OOD instances (Li et al., 2022c; Bazhenov et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024). Most of these
099   approaches often require access to multiple source domains, rely on specific model architectures and
100   train-time paradigms, or may lead to performance degradation. For a thorough review, we refer the
101   readers to a recent survey (Wu et al., 2024).
102   Graph Test-time Adaptation. Graph test-time adaptation (TTA), as first introduced by Chen et al.
103   (2022b), aims to adapt pre-trained models to the test distribution without requiring labeled data
104   or modifying the model’s parameters (Sun et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2022b). Methods like Tent
105   (Wang et al.), MEMO(Zhang et al., 2022), GTrans (Jin et al., 2022), GT3 (Wang et al., 2022),
106   and GraphCTA (Zhang et al., 2024) have been proposed, but they have limitations such as reliance
107   on specific architectures, over-smoothing, or lack of interpretability. These works inspire us to
      introduce self-supervised tasks at test time to improve the robustness and generalization performance

187   3.2   G RAPH T EST-T IME O UT- OF -D ISTRIBUTION A DAPTER
188
      In this section, we introduce a novel approach to adapt pre-trained GNNs to out-of-distribution
189
      (OOD) data by using a Graph Test-time Distribution Adapter, instead of devising a specially de-
190   signed prompt method on GNNs or simply adding a randomly initialized bias on the input test
191   graph. Our adapter aims to fit a function playing the role of isomorphic mapping, capturing OOD
192   representation, and reintegrating the modified test graph’s node feature back into the GNN, thereby
193   effectively representing distribution-shifted environments without the combinatorial explosion in
194   edge search space (Jin et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024).
195
      We introduce the following proposition to ensure that the additional learnable parameters on pre-
196   trained GNN are reasonable and do not introduce an excessive learning burden.
197
198   Proposition 1. Parameter-Efficient Graph Test-time Adaptation. We define a function gψ : G → G,
199
      parameterized by ψ, that modifies the graph structure within the learned parameter space of a pre-
      trained GNN model fθ∗ . This function optimizes a given objective in Eq.(2). It should be ensured
200
      that the parameter count and computational complexity of gψ should be significantly lower than
201
      those of fθ∗ .
202   In addition, since the pre-trained θ∗ are fixed at test time and the test graph is the focus of this paper,
203   we drop the subscript in Gte and fθ∗ to simplify notations in the rest of the paper.
204
205
      Proposition 2. Given a pre-trained GNN f , for each G ∼ p(G|e = ei ), where ei ∈ E, there exists
206   a specific matrix Ê representing the OOD under p(e = ei |e1 , f ).

212   into a lower-dimensional space that captures essential information across different OOD environ-
213   ments, which is crucial for maintaining model performance under distribution shifts. The adapter
214   operation is formally expressed as:

507   5     C ONCLUSION
508
509   We propose a novel augmentation-to-augmentation approach to effectively adapt pre-trained GNNs
510   to unlabeled OOD test graphs, regardless of the pre-training architecture or method. We introduce
511   a new LA2A self-supervised loss function, which enhances the extraction of inductive information
512   from pre-trained GNNs. Additionally, by employing a low-rank adapter to the node feature, the
513   inductive generation of OOD representation becomes more efficient. Our method, GOAT, generally
514   outperforms state-of-the-art techniques across all datasets. An intriguing future direction is to ex-
515   tend such adapter to other tasks involving OOD distributions (such as graph classification and edge
516
      prediction) and delve into its availability to model OOD distribution that generates discrete edges.

       G.2   M ORE EFFECT OF OOD ON G RAPH -S TRUCTURE DATA
1153
1154   It is worth noting that the OOD problem in graph models can lead to significant risks and negative
1155   consequences in real-world applications. For instance, when GNNs are applied in financial risk
1156   control systems, distribution shifts in the input data may cause a large number of misjudgments,
1157   leading to severe economic losses or compliance issues. Similar risks exist in other high-stakes
1158   domains such as healthcare and criminal justice, where the reliability and robustness of graph-based
1159   decision-making systems under distributional changes are critical. Therefore, it is crucial to develop
1160   effective methods to detect and adapt to OOD scenarios in graph learning, and to carefully analyze
1161   and mitigate the potential negative societal impacts. Our work aims to contribute to this important
1162
       research direction.
1163   Another illustrative example of the potential negative impact of OOD issues in graph models is in the
1164   context of social network analysis for misinformation detection. GNNs have been widely adopted
1165   to identify fake news and rumors based on the propagation patterns and content features in social
1166   networks. However, the characteristics of misinformation can evolve rapidly over time, leading to
1167   distribution shifts between the training and test data. If the GNN-based misinformation detectors
1168
       fail to adapt to such changes, they may miss emerging misinformation or cause false alarms, which
       can have severe societal consequences such as public panic, political manipulation, and erosion of
1169
       trust in media. This urges the development of graph OOD detection and adaptation methods that can
1170
       robustly handle the dynamic and adversarial nature of online misinformation. Our GOAT framework
1171   takes a step towards this goal by enabling test-time adaptation of GNNs to evolved data distributions.
