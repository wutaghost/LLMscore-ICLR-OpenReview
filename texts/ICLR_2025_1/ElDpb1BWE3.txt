009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Multiphysics simulation, which models the interactions between multiple phys-
012            ical processes, and multi-component simulation of complex structures are criti-
013            cal in fields like nuclear and aerospace engineering. Previous studies often rely
014            on numerical solvers or machine learning-based surrogate models to solve or
015            accelerate these simulations. However, multiphysics simulations typically re-
016
               quire integrating multiple specialized solvers—each responsible for evolving a
               specific physical process—into a coupled program, which introduces significant
017
               development challenges. Furthermore, no universal algorithm exists for multi-
018
               component simulations, which adds to the complexity. Here we propose com-
019            positional Multiphysics and Multi-component Simulation with Diffusion mod-
020            els (MultiSimDiff) to overcome these challenges. During diffusion-based train-
021            ing, MultiSimDiff learns energy functions modeling the conditional probability
022            of one physical process/component conditioned on other processes/components.
023            In inference, MultiSimDiff generates coupled multiphysics solutions and multi-
024            component structures by sampling from the joint probability distribution, achieved
025            by composing the learned energy functions in a structured way. We test our
026            method in three tasks. In the reaction-diffusion and nuclear thermal coupling prob-
027
               lems, MultiSimDiff successfully predicts the coupling solution using decoupled
               data, while the surrogate model fails in the more complex second problem. For
028
               the thermal and mechanical analysis of the prismatic fuel element, MultiSimDiff
029
               trained for single component prediction accurately predicts a larger structure with
030            64 components, reducing the relative error by 40.3% compared to the surrogate
031            model.

034   1   INTRODUCTION
035
036   Multiphysics simulation involves the concurrent modeling of multiple physical processes—such
037   as heat conduction, fluid flow, and structural mechanics—within a single simulation framework
038   to accurately capture the coupling effects between different physical processes. Similarly, multi-
039
      component simulation focuses on simulating complex structures composed of multiple similar com-
      ponents. Component is defined as: a repeatable basic unit that makes up a complete structure. For
040
      example, the reactor core typically consists of hundreds or thousands of fuel elements arranged in
041
      a square or hexagonal pattern. These simulations are essential across various scientific and engi-
042   neering disciplines, including nuclear engineering (Ma et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021), aerospace
043   engineering (Candeo et al., 2011; Wang et al., 2023a), civil engineering (Sun et al., 2017; Meyer
044   et al., 2022), and automotive industry (Ragone et al., 2021). Despite their significance, both multi-
045   physics and multi-component simulations share a common challenge: while simulating individual
046   components or physical processes is relatively straightforward, modeling the entire system with all
047   its interactions is vastly more complex.
048   Numerous numerical algorithms have been developed for multiphysics simulation, which are
049   broadly categorized into loose coupling and tight coupling (Hales et al., 2015). Loose coupling in-
050   volves solving each physical process independently while treating the others as constant. Solutions
051   for one physical process are iteratively transferred to related physical processes until convergence
052   is achieved, often using techniques like operator splitting (MacNamara & Strang, 2016) and Picard
053   iteration (Terlizzi & Kotlyar, 2022). Tight coupling, on the other hand, assembles equations of all
      physical processes into a large system, solving them simultaneously (Knoll & Keyes, 2004). While

      putational costs, varying spatial and temporal resolutions, and differing numerical methods across
077
      physical processes, leading to a more common use of loose coupling in engineering applications. In
078
      multi-component simulation, directly simulating the overall structure requires high computational
079   cost and may encounter difficulties in convergence due to the increase in degrees of freedom. Sub-
080   structure methods have been used in fields like nuclear engineering (Chen et al., 2021) and civil
081   engineering (Sun et al., 2017) to reduce modeling and computational costs for repetitive compo-
082   nents.
083
      Despite advances in numerical algorithms, several significant challenges remain. In multiphysics
084
      simulations, considerable time and effort are required to develop programs that couple different
085   specialized solvers. Furthermore, the complexity of the system increases due to coupling, requiring
086   more computing resources. While some studies employ machine learning-based surrogate models to
087   accelerate multiphysics simulations (Sobes et al., 2021; Park et al., 2021), these models still depend
088   on coupled data for training, which necessitates the prior development of coupled numerical solution
089   programs. In the case of multi-component simulations, the substructure method has primarily been
090   applied to mechanical problems, with no widely applicable general method for multi-component
091   systems. Consequently, current approaches often rely on selecting representative units or imple-
092   menting simplifications for the analysis of complex structures, which can compromise the accuracy
093
      and scope of the simulation.
094   To address these challenges, we propose compositional Multiphysics and Multi-component
095   Simulation with Diffusion models (MultiSimDiff). The core innovation of MultiSimDiff is its treat-
096   ment of multiphysics and multi-component simulations as generative probabilistic modeling, where
097   interactions between multiple physical processes or components are captured through composing
098   learned energy functions conditioned on others in a structured way. In multiphysics simulation,
099
      MultiSimDiff generates coupled solutions (accounting for interactions between different physical
      processes) from decoupled data (assuming other fields are known and focused on solving a single
100
      field) by modeling the solutions of physical processes as a joint probability distribution. The solu-
101
      tion for each individual process is treated as a conditional probability distribution, based on Bayes’
102   theorem. By training diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020) on decoupled data, we capture these condi-
103   tional distributions. During inference, the model combines these distributions and performs reverse
104   diffusion to produce the coupled solution. For multi-component simulations, MultiSimDiff models
105   each component’s solution as a conditional probability distribution using the local Markov property,
106   conditioned on neighboring components. By training diffusion models on small structures, we create
107   conditional models for individual components. During inference, reverse diffusion is applied itera-
      tively across all components, yielding the solution for the entire structure. We have mathematically

      2   RELATED WORK
127
128   Multiphysics simulation. Most existing studies develop unified surrogate models for all physical
129
      processes by coupling solutions(Tang et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2020; Park et al., 2021; Wang et al.,
      2023b). For complex problems, programs for each physical process are typically independent. It
130
      is often feasible to establish a surrogate model for one specific physical process and then integrate
131
      it with other numerical programs (El Haber et al., 2022; Han et al., 2019). Alternatively, surrogate
132   models can be constructed separately for each physical processes and iteratively converged through
133   an iterative process (Sobes et al., 2021). Because the purpose of our algorithm is to infer coupled
134   solutions through models trained with decoupled data, and establishing the surrogate model for all
135   physical processes requires coupling solution training models, we adopt the method of establish-
136   ing surrogate models for each physical process separately as the baseline to validate the proposed
137   algorithm.
138   Multi-component simulation. To our knowledge, there do not exist utilized machine learning meth-
139   ods specifically designed for multi-component simulation. A relevant study is the CoAE-MLSim
140   algorithm (Ranade et al., 2021). This algorithm combines neural networks with numerical itera-
141   tion. It first partitions the computational domain into multiple subdomains, and then trains a neural
142   network to learn the flux conversation between subdomains. During inference, the neural network
143   with flux conservation is applied sequentially in each subdomain, looping until convergence. We
144   further extend this algorithm to multi-component simulation and use it as a baseline. Besides, graph
145   neural network (GNN) (Wu et al., 2020) can learn on small graphs and inference on larger graphs
146
      (Xu et al., 2019); Graph Transformer (Kreuzer et al., 2021) employs the Laplacian matrix of a graph
      to characterize its structure, and by leveraging the Transformer architecture, it achieves learning on
147
      graphs. We also compare MultiSimDiff with GNN and Graph Transformer.
148
149   Compositional models. Recent research has extensively explored the compositional combination of
150   generative models for various applications, including 2D image synthesis (Du et al., 2020; Liu et al.,
151   2021; Nie et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2022; Du et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023c), 3D
152
      synthesis (Po & Wetzstein, 2024), video synthesis (Yang et al., 2023a), multimodal perception (Li
      et al., 2022), trajectory planning (Du et al., 2019; Urain et al., 2023; Gkanatsios et al., 2024; Yang
153
      et al., 2023b), inverse design (Wu et al., 2024b), and hierarchical decision making (Ajay et al.,
154
      2024). A particularly effective approach for combining predictive distributions from local experts is
155   the product of experts framework (Hinton, 2002; Cohen et al., 2020; Kant et al., 2024; Tautvaišas &
156   Žilinskas, 2023). Their focus is on how a single object is influenced by multiple factors, such as
157   generating images that meet various requirements in image generation (Du et al., 2023) or enhancing
158   the lift-to-drag ratio under the influence of two wings in inverse design (Wu et al., 2024b). How-
159   ever, our problem involves multiple objects, such as multiple physical processes and components,
160   requiring the capture of interactions between these fields or components. Existing research is not
161   applicable to multiphysics and multi-component simulation. To the best of our knowledge, we are
      the first to introduce a compositional generative approach to multiphysics and multi-component sim-

      ulations, demonstrating how this framework enables generalization to far more complex simulation
163   tasks than those encountered during training.

309
      In the experiments, we aim to answer the following questions: (1) Can MultiSimDiff predict coupled
310
      solutions (accounting for interactions between different physical processes) from models trained in
311   decoupled data (assuming other processes are known and focus on solving a single process)? (2)
312   Can MultiSimDiff predict large structure solutions from a model trained in small structure data?
313   (3) Can MultiSimDiff outperform surrogate model2 in both tasks? To answer these questions, we
314   conduct experiments to assess our algorithm’s performance on two problems across three scenar-

318   4.2 examines a more complex scenario involving various types of coupling: region, interface, strong,
319
      weak, unidirectional, and bidirectional to further test the algorithm’s capacity to handle multiphysics
      simulation. Section 4.3 simplifies actual engineering problems to evaluate the algorithm’s effective-
320
      ness with multi-component simulation. Each experiment uses two network architectures, training

518   5   L IMITATION AND FUTURE WORK
519
520   There are also several limitations of our proposed MultiSimDiff that provide exciting opportunities
521   for future work. Firstly, in multiphysics simulation, although the MultiSimDiff trained on decoupled
522   data can predict coupled solutions more accurately than baseline surrogate models, the prediction er-
523   rors are still higher compared to single physical processes predictions. In addition, there is a certain
524   gap in accuracy compared to models trained through coupled data, as shown in Appendix E. Fu-
525   ture efforts can focus on improving dataset generation, training methods, and incorporating physical
526
      information to boost accuracy. Secondly, we plan to explore additional accelerated sampling algo-
      rithms, aiming to significantly improve efficiency while maintaining prediction accuracy. Lastly, the
527
      experiments in this paper simplify many aspects compared to real engineering problems, and future
528
      work will aim to validate the algorithm in more complex real-world scenarios.

531   6   CONCLUSION
532
533
      This work presents MultiSimDiff as a novel method for multiphysics and multi-component simula-
534   tions. In multiphysics scenarios, models trained on decoupled data can predict coupled solutions,
535   while in multi-component simulations, models trained on small structures can extrapolate to larger
536   ones. We develop three datasets to validate MultiSimDiff and compare it to the surrogate model
537   method. Results show that MultiSimDiff effectively predicts coupled solutions in multiphysics sim-
538   ulations where surrogate models fail, and exhibits greater accuracy in predicting larger structures in
539   multi-component simulations. We believe this approach provides a new approach to address multi-
      physics and multi-component simulations, important across science and engineering.

      As noted, assuming the distribution of physical field data in high-dimensional problems is chal-
      lenging. We recommend a pre-iteration method for data generation. Initially, we assume constant
910
      values for all other physical fields and calculate the current field. This process repeats until all
911
      fields are computed. If there are n physical fields, pre-iteration requires n - 1 calculations plus one
912   iteration for data generation, totaling 2n - 1 calculations. To accelerate data generation, the most
913   time-consuming field can be excluded from pre-iteration. In this problem, the fluid fields’ compu-
914   tation time is approximately three times that of the other fields, so it is excluded from pre-iteration.
915   The process begins by assuming constant fluid fields and solid temperatures to calculate the neutron
916   physics field, followed by using the resulting neutron physics field and assumed fluid fields’ temper-
917   ature to calculate the solid temperature. The data generation proceeds sequentially with calculations

1528   I   E FFICIENCY ANALYSIS .
1529
1530   This section compares the computational efficiency of MultiSimDiff, surrogate model, and numeri-
1531   cal programs. The time unit for each experiment is defined as the time required for a single neural
1532   network inference. These three experiments all use the most accurate model. Since the surrogate
1533   model and MultiSimDiff both use the same network architecture and have consistent network pa-
1534   rameters, it is assumed that the time for a single inference using these two methods is equal. The
1535   numerical programs are run on the CPU and have all been optimized to the best parallel count.
1536   Let the number of physical processes be denoted by N , the number of iterations for the surrogate
1537   model by M , the number of diffusion steps by S, and the number of outer loop iterations for the
1538   diffusion model by K. The computation time for the surrogate model is M × N , while the diffusion
1539   model is K × S × N . The specific choices of N, M, S, K for each experiment are presented in Table
1540   19.
1541   The results are presented in Table 20. In experiment 1, the problem is relatively simple, and the
1542   numerical algorithm achieves efficient solutions through explicit time stepping, while the introduc-
1543   tion of MultiSimDiff actually reduces efficiency. However, in experiment 2, which addresses more
1544   complex problems, MultiSimDiff achieves a 29-fold acceleration compared to numerical programs.
1545   In experiment 3, comparing the results of 16 components with 64 components, it is observed that as
1546   the computational scale increases, the acceleration effect of MultiSimDiff becomes increasingly sig-
1547   nificant. Furthermore, when dealing with multi-component problems, the surrogate model requires
1548
       iteration to ensure the convergence of solutions across all components. Due to the large number
       of components, the number of iterations needed significantly increases compared to multiphysics
1549
       problems, resulting in higher efficiency for MultiSimDiff. In addition, we have only compared the
1550
       efficiency of single computations for all experiments. When dealing with multiple problems si-
1551   multaneously, the acceleration provided by MultiSimDiff will be even more pronounced due to the
1552   parallel nature of GPU computing.
1553
1554
       In general, the more complex the problem, the more pronounced the acceleration effect of Multi-
       SimDiff becomes. In fact, the problems in experiment 2 and experiment 3 have been simplified to
1555
       a certain extent, and the actual situations are even more complex. Therefore, MultiSimDiff holds
1556
       significant value in solving real-world complex engineering problems.

       J     A PPLICATION S CENARIOS FOR M ULTI -C OMPONENT SIMULATION .
1576
1577
       In this section, we discuss the application scenarios of MultiSimDiff for multi-component simulation
1578
       from both theoretical and practical perspectives.
1579
1580   From a theoretical perspective, in the derivation of Section 3.2, we make an assumption: the solu-
1581   tion on a multi-component structure is an undirected graph that satisfies the local Markov property,
1582
       meaning that any two non-adjacent variables are conditionally independent given all other variables.
       Using this property, we derived Eq. 14. We believe this assumption is applicable to most problems
1583
       because physical fields are continuous in space, and the information exchange between any two
1584
       points must be transmitted through the points in between. However, there is a class of problems
1585   to which current methods cannot be directly applied, which is the partial differential equation that
1586   requires determining eigenvalues:
1587                                                Mϕ = λϕ                                           (20)
1588
       Here M is the operator, λ is the eigenvalue, ϕ is the physical field to be solved. The λ varies
1589   with different systems, and the relationships we learn on small structures may not be applicable to
1590   large structures. Solutions to these problems may be similar to numerical algorithms, requiring the
1591   addition of an eigenvalue search process, which will be undertaken in future work.
1592
       From a practical implementation perspective, for a complex structure, it is necessary to clearly de-
1593
       termine its basic components and the relationships between these components and their surrounding
1594
       components, so that we can understand how the components are affected by their surrounding com-
1595   ponents. In addition, training data must encompass all possible scenarios that each component in a
1596   large structure might encounter, such as all possible boundary conditions and the relationships with
1597   surrounding components.
