011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Efficient traffic management at urban intersections is vital for reducing congestion
014            and improving safety. This paper presents MD3DQN, the first End-to-End novel
015            reinforcement learning model using surveillance video for real-time traffic signal
016
               control. The model features two main components: an image reception module,
               capturing traffic data from cameras positioned on signal poles, and a multi-agent
017
               decision module, where each agent manages a traffic phase. These components
018
               are connected via a bridge module for seamless integration.
019
               Our novel Entropy Attention Mechanism enhances the multi-decision turn-based
020
               traffic signal control by leveraging uncertainty and signal phase delays, leading
021            to more optimized decisions. Results show MD3DQN improved cumulative re-
022            ward by an average of 85.2% over Fixed-time 40 and 54.4% over DQN-VTP.
023            The entropy mechanism contributed to a 41.8% improvement upon ablation study,
024            demonstrating its impact on faster convergence and better performance.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Traffic congestion at intersections remains a critical issue in urban areas, causing significant delays
030   and economic losses [Fonseca & Garcia (2021); Cheng et al. (2023)]. Efficient Traffic Signal Control
031
      (TSC) is essential, especially as urbanization continues to increase [Wei et al. (2019)].
032   Recent advances in Reinforcement Learning (RL) have shown great promise in improving TSC
033   systems by dynamically adjusting signal timings in response to real-time traffic conditions, often
034   outperforming traditional approaches in simulations [Koh et al. (2020); Li et al. (2021); Noaeen
035   et al. (2022)]. As a result, RL has become a popular research direction for intersection control, with
036   a growing body of literature exploring its potential.
037   However, despite the impressive progress in RL-based TSC models, real-world deployment remains
038   limited. One key reason is the reliance of most RL models on processed inputs such as queue lengths,
039   which are easily obtained in simulation environments but challenging to measure accurately in real-
040   world settings [Comert & Cetin (2021)]. Data from loop detectors or cameras often lacks precision,
041   and additional algorithms are required to extract accurate queue length estimates from these sources
042   [Kessler et al. (2021); Gouran et al. (2023)]. Consequently, a seamless end-to-end solution is still
043
      lacking and two crucial components are needed: robust methods for extracting queue length from
      diverse data sources and the integration of these inputs into existing RL models. Until this challenge
044
      is addressed, the full potential of RL-based TSC systems cannot be realized in practical deployments.
045
046   To address this challenge, our work presents the first end-to-end solution that directly utilizes real-
047   time video from traffic cameras to train RL models for urban TSC control. Unlike previous ap-
048   proaches that rely on pre-processed inputs, our model operates on raw traffic data, advancing recent
049
      studies on deep RL in complex urban networks [Xu et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023b); He et al. (2023);
      Wu et al. (2023)]. By training on real-world traffic data, our approach bridges the gap between sim-
050
      ulation and practical application, making the model ready for immediate deployment in real-world
051
      traffic systems.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
070
071   The integration of video surveillance into traffic management systems has been shown to enhance
072   vehicle detection and traffic flow predictions through deep learning techniques applied to surveil-
073   lance images [Dilshad et al. (2020); Hu et al. (2021)]. Research has focused on detecting vehicle
074   density from video data for real-time signal optimization [Jamebozorg & Hami (2024)]. Addition-
075   ally, sensor fusion combining video cameras with LiDAR improves vehicle localization, though the
076   high cost of LiDAR limits its scalability [Liu et al. (2023)]. Our research leverages the widespread
077   deployment of traffic cameras to bridge the gap between theoretical solutions and practical imple-
078   mentation in signal control [Luo et al. (2018)].
079   Real-time vehicle detection in challenging conditions such as fog or low light has benefited from
080   models like YOLO, which is widely adopted for traffic applications [Wang et al. (2022b); Meng et al.
081   (2023)]. Incorporating such deep learning models into traffic systems improves detection accuracy
082   and signal timing adjustments [Patel & Ganatra (2023); Meng et al. (2023)]. Multi-stream temporal
083   structures have further enhanced congestion detection from video, directly supporting traffic control
084   strategies [He et al. (2023)].
085   While RL methods have been effective for optimizing traffic signals [Wei et al. (2021); Xu et al.
086   (2023)], current approaches focus primarily on theoretical simulation without practical considera-
087   tion of real-world sensor inputs like surveillance cameras. Our MD3DQN model extends multi-agent
088   RL concepts by incorporating real-time video data, offering more adaptive and practical traffic con-
089   trol solutions [Liang et al. (2019); Huang et al. (2021)]. Multi-agent RL has demonstrated great
090   promise in improving urban traffic flow, and video input enhances decision-making capabilities fur-
091
      ther [Wang et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2023)].
092   Our research addresses the limitations of existing RL-based traffic control systems, which often lack
093   real-world applicability, by integrating video data to provide a scalable and intelligent solution for
094   practical intersection management [He et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2023)].

      4.1   S OLUTION OVERVIEW
179
180   Our proposed solution utilizes image-based inputs to manage traffic signal control through rein-
181   forcement learning. The images are captured at three time stamps: t − 30, t − 15, and tnow , enabling
182   the model to capture both current and past traffic dynamics. This temporal information is crucial for
183   allowing the model to understand evolving traffic states [Wang et al. (2020)].
184
      The model consists of three main components:
185
186   - Image Processing Module: This component extracts high-level semantic features from the input
187   images, transforming them into a lower-dimensional feature space Φ(It ). The module is designed to
188   be modular, allowing flexibility to replace it with various image processing techniques as required.
189   - Feature Space Mapping (Bridge): This part of the model serves as a bridge, aligning and map-
190   ping the extracted image features with additional traffic metrics, such as vehicle density and queue
                                                                                               ′   ′
191   lengths, into a unified decision-making space. The mapping is represented by f : RH ×W ×D →
192   Sbridge , where Sbridge is the intermediate space for reinforcement learning.
193
      - Multi-Decision Agent: Each agent focuses on one lane set, processing its features through an
194   entropy module. The entropy module incorporates both phase timing (phase timing distance, i.e.,
195   the time until the next or subsequent signal phases) and state uncertainty. The agent optimizes a
196   policy π(st ), balancing rewards such as minimizing queue lengths and reducing vehicle stopping
197   times.
198
      This modular and flexible architecture enables the system to make informed, real-time traffic control
199
      decisions based on image data and traffic metrics. As shown in Figure 2, the model integrates these
200   components seamlessly to improve traffic flow and decision-making efficiency.

337   5.1   M ULTI M INI D ECISION AGENT P RE - TRAINING ON SUMO
338
339   The Multi Mini Decision Agent Module was pre-trained in the SUMO traffic simulation environment
340
      using non-image features such as vehicle counts, queue lengths, and cumulative stopping times. The
      objective was to establish decision-making capability in traffic signal control before introducing
341
      complex video inputs.
342
343   The intersection settings mirrored those in later stages, with 3 timestamps (t − 30, t − 15, and tnow )
344   per lane set. Each timestamp captured cumulative stopping time tstop (li ) and queue length q(li ) for
345   lane set li . This simplified pre-training setup ensures faster convergence and explainability before
346
      integrating video-based inputs.
347
348   5.2   C ARLA F INE -T UNING
349
      Fine-tuning in the Carla simulation environment involved using real-time image inputs to capture
350   dynamic traffic flow. Carla offers more realistic vehicle dynamics, making it crucial for testing in
351   real-world intersection scenarios. The setup and data collection were similar to SUMO, with images
352   taken at t − 30, t − 15, and tnow .
353
      A key component is the weighted reward function [Li et al. (2023a); Peters et al. (2010)]:
354
355                            Rweighted = 0.4 · r1 + 0.3 · r2 + 0.2 · r3 + 0.1 · r4
356   This reflects the cumulative impact of decisions, with rewards normalized across light (plight = 0.01)
357   and heavy (pheavy = 0.04) traffic flows. Stopping time weight α = 0.1 and queue length weight
358   β = 1 were chosen to balance both metrics based on prior training.
359
      Fine-tuning showed that the MD3DQN model can handle complex, real-world scenarios by lever-
360
      aging both image inputs and weighted rewards, optimizing signal control in adaptive and intelligent
361   ways.

369   them in a buffer B: B = D0 .
370
371   2. Offline Training In the offline phase, πθ is updated using mini-batches of size 150 from B. The
372   policy parameters are adjusted as θ ← θ − η∇θ L(θ), where η = 0.0005. We run 5 iterations per
373
      cycle, improving sample efficiency.
374
375
      3. Periodic Online Updates After each offline cycle, new experiences Dn are collected from E
      and added to B, replacing old data: B ← B ∪ Dn . Roughly 10-20% of the buffer is updated.

      5.7   A BLATION S TUDY: I MPACT OF E NTROPY ATTENTION M ECHANISM

483   to make more informed decisions, leading to more efficient and adaptive traffic management, even
484   under challenging weather conditions.

504
      This paper introduced MD3DQN, the first End-to-End video-to-signal control framework for urban
505
      intersections, integrating real-time video inputs with reinforcement learning. MD3DQN surpasses
