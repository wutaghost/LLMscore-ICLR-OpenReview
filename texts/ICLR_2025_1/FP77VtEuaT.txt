009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                Large Language Models (LLMs) have been touted as AI models possessing ad-
012                vanced reasoning abilities. However, recent works have shown that LLMs often
013                bypass true reasoning using shortcuts, sparking skepticism. To study the reasoning
014                capabilities in a principled fashion, we adopt a computational theory perspective
015                and propose an experimental protocol centered on 3-SAT – the prototypical NP-
016
                   complete problem lying at the core of logical reasoning and constraint satisfaction
                   tasks. Specifically, we examine the phase transitions in random 3-SAT and char-
017
                   acterize the reasoning abilities of LLMs by varying the inherent hardness of the
018
                   problem instances. Our experimental evidence shows that LLMs are incapable
019                of performing true reasoning, as required for solving 3-SAT problems. More-
020                over, we observe significant performance variation based on the inherent hardness
021                of the problems – performing poorly on harder instances and vice versa. Impor-
022                tantly, we show that integrating external reasoners can considerably enhance LLM
023                performance. By following a principled experimental protocol, our study draws
024                concrete conclusions and moves beyond the anecdotal evidence often found in
025                LLM reasoning research.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The success and versatility of Large Language Models (LLMs) have sparked widespread interest
030   and debate on whether LLMs are capable of reasoning. The answer to this question may depend
031   on the perspective on reasoning one takes, whether it is more oriented toward common sense rea-
032   soning (Davis & Marcus, 2015) or towards logical or deductive reasoning (Genesereth & Nilsson,
033   1987). We will adhere to Leon Bottou’s definition, which defines reasoning as “algebraically ma-
034   nipulating previously acquired knowledge in order to answer a new question” (Bottou, 2014). This
035   is aligned with Russell and Norvig’s description of artificial intelligence as rational thinking (Russell
036   & Norvig, 2010).
037   Recent studies suggest that LLMs are inherently capable of zero-shot reasoning (Kojima et al.,
038   2022) (i.e. performing multistep inference processes in previously unseen situations). This ability
039   has been shown to emerge and improve with scale (Wei et al., 2022a; Srivastava et al., 2023), and
040   can be further enhanced by using smart prompting techniques that encourage LLMs to think step-
041   by-step (Kojima et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022b; Zhou et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023b; Prasad et al.,
042   2023). Demonstrations include, inter alia, planning (Huang et al., 2022; Ahn et al., 2022; Singh
043
      et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Hazra et al., 2024b), theorem proving (Jiang
      et al., 2023; Welleck et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022), search and optimization (Yang et al., 2024;
044
      Romera-Paredes et al., 2024; Hazra et al., 2024a), self-reflection (Yao et al., 2023a; Madaan et al.,
045
      2023; Shinn et al., 2023), and tool usage (Shen et al., 2023; Schick et al., 2023).
046
047   Conversely, a growing body of research presents a more critical view of these emergent abilities. For
048   instance, LLMs may exhibit limitations in consistent logical reasoning (Arkoudas, 2023; Saparov
049
      & He, 2023), effective planning (Valmeekam et al., 2022), and accurate self-evaluation of their out-
      puts (Stechly et al., 2023). During training, language models can fit on statistical features (Zhang
050
      et al., 2023) or identify reasoning shortcuts, much like the Clever Hans Cheat (Bachmann & Nagara-
051
      jan, 2024), thus bypassing true reasoning. There is also growing concern about dataset contamina-
052   tion1 from open-source benchmarks (Zhang et al., 2024) that can inflate the reasoning performance

146   2.1       P HASE T RANSITIONS IN R ANDOM 3-SAT
147
148   We study the reasoning capabilities of LLMs on random 3-SAT problems. 3-SAT constitutes one of
149   the most fundamental problems in computer science as it is the prototypical NP-complete problem,
150   lying at the foundation of computational complexity theory. Moreover, various prevalent reasoning
151   problems in artificial intelligence, such as planning and constraint satisfaction, can be reduced to
152   solving 3-SAT problems (Garey & Johnson, 1990).
153   By randomly sampling 3-SAT formulas3 , we avoid domain-specific biases, and it lets us control the
154   complexity of the generated formulas. In fact, an interesting empirical observation is the presence
155   of a phase transition in random 3-SAT problems (Cheeseman et al., 1991). When randomly sam-
156   pling 3-SAT formulas, one can observe a sharp change in the probability of a 3-SAT formula being
157   satisfiable when plotted against α = m/n, where m is the number of clauses and n is the number
158   of variables. For random 3-SAT, this phase transition occurs at αc ≈ 4.267 (Mertens et al., 2006;
159
      Ding et al., 2015), i.e. the point at which a randomly sampled 3-SAT formula has equal probabil-
      ity to be satisfiable or unsatisfiable. This naturally divides 3-SAT problems into three regions: the

      2.2       SAT S OLVERS
166
167   SAT solvers are tools to automatically verify the satisfiability of propositional logic formulas. The
168   Davis–Putnam–Logemann–Loveland (DPLL) algorithm (Davis et al., 1962) is a key component of
169   modern SAT solvers. It consists of a backtracking-based search algorithm, enriched with deductive
170   rules and heuristics, to efficiently explore the search space and determine whether a formula is
171   satisfiable. The core backtracking algorithm simply selects a literal and assigns a truth value to it.
172   This step produces a simplified formula where all the parts made true by the assignment are removed.
173
      This step can be applied recursively until all clauses are removed, meaning that the original formula
      is satisfiable. Modern SAT solvers are based on Conflict-Driven Clause Learning (CDCL) (Silva &
174
      Sakallah, 1996) which enhances DPLL with conflict analysis and clause learning. In Figure 2 we
175
      show how the time to determine the satisfiability of a random 3-SAT formula varies in function of
176   α. Most prominently, we see a pronounced peak in time-to-solution around the αc .
177
178   Analogously to characterizing SAT solvers by their behavior on solving problems with varying α,
179
      we study the reasoning capabilities of LLMs with respect to the phase transition in random 3-SAT
      problems.4 . This contrasts with other works that characterize reasoning by evaluating performance
180
      on benchmark datasets (Cobbe et al., 2021; Hendrycks et al., 2021a).
181
182
183   3       R ELATED W ORK
184
185   Existing works have focused on improving the reasoning abilities of LLMs by combining them
186   with verifiers and heuristics (Olausson et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Lightman et al., 2024). In
187
      terms of solving SAT problems with LLMs, SATLM (Ye et al., 2024) proposes a satisfiability-aided
      language modeling using an LLM to parse an NL SAT input to a SAT problem specification which
188
      consists of a set of logical formulas, then obtain the solution by invoking a SAT solver. Similarly,
189
      AutoSAT (Sun et al., 2024) uses LLMs to optimize heuristics in SAT solvers. Our goal is not
190   to build 3-SAT solvers powered by LLMs. On the contrary, we use the 3-SAT phase transition to
191   assess the reasoning abilities of LLMs using a well-established experimental protocol. Closest to our
192   work is the NPHardEval (Fan et al., 2024), which examines reasoning across various computational
193   complexity classes, but we additionally explore phase transition characteristics and investigate how
194   the performance varies with inherent hardness of problems.
195
      Our work also aligns with the findings of Dziri et al. (2023) that investigates the performance of
196   LLMs on compositional tasks with varying levels of complexity. Their experiments reveal a signif-
197   icant performance decline as task complexity increases, measured by problem size and reasoning
198   depth. The findings indicate that while models can memorize single-step operations, potentially
199   due to their prevalence during training, they fail to compose these steps into correct reasoning paths
200   effectively. Similar observations were made on BERT-based models for a computationally tractable
201   class of problems (not NP-complete) (Zhang et al., 2023). Our work extends these findings by
202   demonstrating that the observed performance decline is better explained by the inherent hardness of
203   problems, as defined by phase transitions, rather than problem size or depth. Large 3-SAT formulas
204
      with many variables or clauses may belong to either easy or hard regions. However, in easy regions,
      LLMs can still exploit statistical patterns, while in hard regions, they struggle due to the absence of
205
      effective heuristics. Similarly, statistical features can help reduce search depth in easy regions, but
206
      in hard regions, LLMs are forced to perform multi-step reasoning, revealing their limitations.
207
208   Merrill & Sabharwal (2023) established that multi-layer transformers belong to the complexity class
209   log-uniform TC0 . Subsequently, Peng et al. (2024) demonstrated that multi-layer transformers can-
210
      not solve problems such as Derivability, 2-SAT, Horn SAT, and Circuit Evaluation unless L=NL.
      While these results provide worst-case performance bounds for transformer architectures, their rel-
211
      evance to average-case complexity is limited (Coarfa et al., 2000), and therefore, they offer only
212
      partial insights into the practical reasoning capabilities of LLMs. Notably, recent works have shown
213   that T chain of thought (CoT) steps can extends transformers’ abilities beyond TC0 , up to those solv-
214   able by Boolean circuits of size T (Li et al., 2024). Our experiments on 3-SAT complement these

224   To use LLMs as 3-SAT solvers, we reframe the 3-SAT problem as a natural language menu-selection
225   problem, termed as SAT-Menu. As shown in Box 1, the prompt input to the LLM consists of a task
226   outline, along with a specific scenario detailing the dietary preferences of a set of people. The LLM’s
227   objective is to identify a combination of orderable (akin to positive literals) and non-orderable (akin
228   to negative literals) food items that meet these preferences; or declare the situation unsatisfiable
229
      (unSAT) if no valid combination exists. Note, that the prompt example in Box 1 constitutes a min-
      imal example stripped of all details. The complete system prompt incorporates techniques known
230
      to enhance the apparent reasoning capabilities of LLMs, such as chain-of-thought (CoT) (Wei et al.,
231
      2022b) and in-context learning (Brown et al., 2020) (see Box 2 in Appendix for the full prompt).
232
233   Additionally, we introduce a second problem formulation where the LLM is directly given the un-
234   derlying 3-SAT formula in Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF). We refer to this scenario as SAT-CNF.
235
      Specifically, in this setting, the problem is presented as a list of integers to the LLM, similar to the
      approach outlined in SAT Game (Figure 1). For more details about the prompt, we refer the reader
236
      to Box 3 in the Appendix.

      To assess the reasoning capabilities of LLMs, we analyze their performance on two variants of the
254   3-SAT problem. The first variant is the 3-SAT Decision problem, where the LLM acts as a solver
255   and must determine whether a given 3-SAT problem is satisfiable. If the problem has a satisfiable
256   assignment, the LLM should respond with “yes” and with “no” if it is unsatisfiable. The second
257   variant is the 3-SAT Search problem, where the LLM’s task extends beyond providing a simple
258   “yes” or “no” response. If the formula is satisfiable, the LLM should also return an assignment to
259   the variables that satisfies the formula. However, if the formula is found to be unsatisfiable, the LLM
260   should once again respond with “no”.
261   Due to the widespread adoption and best-in-class performance5 we use GPT-4 Turbo (specifically,
262   GPT-4 1106-preview model) as the main LLM reference to perform our experimental evaluation.

264   cluding both open-source (Llama 2 70B chat-hf (Touvron et al., 2023), Mixtral 8 × 7B (Jiang et al.,
265   2024)) as well as proprietary models (GPT-3.5 Turbo (OpenAI, 2022), Gemini 1.0 Pro (Google,

      4.2   DATASET G ENERATION
299
300   To generate 3-SAT data, we varied α = m/n as a parameter to guide the generation process. For
301   each n ∈ [1, 10], we selected α ∈ [1, 11] based on feasible values of m. The full range of values is
302   provided in Table 1 in the Appendix. Each instance is labeled either as satisfiable or unsatisfiable
303   using MiniSAT v2.2 (Eén & Sörensson, 2003). Additionally, each instance is annotated with model
304   count (i.e. number of feasible solutions for a formula) using the D4 model counter (Lagniez &
305   Marquis, 2017).
306   For SAT-Menu setup, we map each instance (i.e. CNF formula) to a menu selection puzzle. The
307   goal is to select a combination of orderable and non-orderable food items in a way that satisfies
308   everyone’s preferences. To this end, a food item is sampled without replacement corresponding to
309   the list of variables in the formula. Then, every clause in the formula is treated as the preferences
310   for an individual, leading to the creation of two distinct lists for each person: “Likes,” for food items
311   linked to positive literals, and “Dislikes,” for those associated with negated literals. This approach
312   is exemplified in Box 2.
313   Our dataset consists of 60,000 formulas, among which 39,909 are satisfiable (SAT) and the re-
314   maining 20,091 are unsatisfiable (unSAT) instances. For our experiments, we randomly selected
315   approximately 6,000 samples from this pool. Detailed statistics of our data distribution are provided
316   in Appendix A.

      5.2   C AN AN LLM + S OLVER BOOST PERFORMANCE ?
379
380
      To aid LLMs in reasoning tasks, recent studies have explored pipelined approaches using LLMs
381
      to parse inputs into solver-compliant outputs, leveraging off-the-shelf solvers to derive the final
382   answer (Ye et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023). This approach is aligned with neurosymbolic tech-
383   niques (De Raedt et al., 2020), which combine the universal function approximation capabilities
384   of neural networks with the precision of symbolic systems.
385
      To explore a similar setting in the context of 3-SAT, we ask whether we can augment LLMs with an
386
      external solver wherein the LLM translates (pseudo)-natural language into a format that a symbolic
387
      SAT solver, such as MiniSAT, can process. To this end, we prompt the LLM to translate 3-SAT
388   formulas, which we provide in the SAT-Menu input format, into solver-compliant 3-SAT formulas.
389   We then use a 3-SAT solver to solve the translated instance (see Box 4 in Appendix). We dub this
390   approach SAT-Translate and plot its performance in Figure 5.
391
      We observe that when LLMs have access to an external solver, there is a significant increase in their
392
      accuracy, reaching at best, in GPT-4’s case, ≈ 100% across the entire range of α. We attribute
393
      this to the relatively lower computational complexity of translating 3-SAT formulas compared to
394   solving them (i.e. finding satisfying assignments). Interestingly, we find that varying the input
395   format between SAT-CNF and SAT-Menu does not significantly enhance LLMs inherent reasoning
396   capabilities. The marked improvement in performance is primarily observed when they are equipped
397   with an external solver. While ours is a straightforward approach, one could also explore tighter
398   integrations as proposed in LLM-Modulo frameworks (Kambhampati et al., 2024a) which augments
399   LLMs with critics and verifiers (Lightman et al., 2024; Hazra et al., 2024b), recognizing the ability
400   of LLMs as approximate idea-generators for problems as against directly solving them..
401

403   6     D ISCUSSION
404
405
      At first sight, our experimental evaluation can be interpreted as indicating that LLMs, specifically
406
      GPT-4, exhibit a certain degree of reasoning capabilities: in the easy regions, GPT-4 solves some
407
      3-SAT problems. One might then argue that GPT-4 does not reach perfect accuracy in these regions
408   because of the scale of the model, and that simply increasing this will resolve the issue. For the hard
409   region, however, it is unlikely that scaling up the model will result in radical improvements.
410
411
      To explain this, reconsider Figures 2 and the time vs. α plot specifically. The reason why MiniSAT
      is capable of solving problems in the easy regions faster than problems around αc is due to the
412
      heuristics built into the solver that guide the search for satisfying solutions (e.g. unit propagation
413
      and clause learning (Marques-Silva & Sakallah, 1999)). That is, heuristics work well when they
414   can exploit statistical features in the problem instance to be solved. To date, there are no known
415   heuristics that work well around αc (and they are unlikely to exist due to the NP-hardness of 3-
416   SAT). Solvers therefore have to resort to brute force search around αc .
417
      In this light, we can reinterpret the experimental performance of GPT-4 on 3-SAT problems: GPT-4’s
418
      apparent reasoning capabilities (in the easy regions) is due to the presence of statistical features that
419
      it can leach onto. For instance, GPT-4 may oversimplify and deem a problem unsatisfiable due to
420   the high number of input tokens, which often works for overconstrained formulas (see Appendix B).
421   Conversely, in the hard region, the drop in performance can be attributed to GPT-4’s – and by
422   extension current transformer-based LLMs’ – inability to reason according to Bottou’s definition.
423   A similar observation has been made for a computationally tractable class of problems (not NP-
424   complete) using BERT-style language models (Zhang et al., 2023).
425
      Even though the basic random 3-SAT phase transition provides a rigorous framework for studying
426   the reasoning capabilities of LLMs, we need to point out that there exists many more results about
427   3-SAT that might be considered as well. For instance, it has been found that certain aspects of the
428   hardness of 3-SAT instances cannot be explained using the easy-hard-easy regimes induced by the
429   phase transition (Mu & Hoos, 2015). Nevertheless, the phase transition seems to be adequate in
430   our settings as demonstrated by the drop in performance of LLMs in the vicinity of αc . It should,
431   however, be noted that 3-SAT instances encountered in practice often fall into the easy regions.
      Therefore, decision problems of interest do not usually exhibit worst-case complexity.

502   7   C ONCLUSION
503
504   A superficial analysis of the reasoning capabilities of LLMs suggests they possess strong and com-
505   plex reasoning capabilities – a common fallacy (Bubeck et al., 2023). However, our detailed experi-
506   mental analysis indicates that what appears as reasoning capabilities could be a mirage, with LLMs
507   predominantly exploiting statistical features present in the data and would explain why LLMs have
508   been termed “statistical parrots” (Bender et al., 2021). These findings are supported by our exper-
509
      iments on lower complexity classes (2-SAT and fragments of Horn-SAT), where GPT-4 performed
      comparatively well on NL-complete problems but rather poorly on P-hard problem. We believe
510
      that the comprehensive experimental protocol we established for 3-SAT can be deployed in future
511
      studies to further investigate the reasoning capabilities of LLMs – at a more fine-grained level than
512   worst-case theoretical studies.
513
514   This is not to say that LLMs lack value – quite the opposite. LLMs are highly effective at translating
515
      problems into a formal language and then passing these problems on to solvers. This utility is
      demonstrated by the relatively superior performance of a straightforward LLM+Solver framework.
516
      This suggests that effective reasoning with LLMs should involve decomposing tasks into easy LLM
517
      problems and hard symbolic problems (when possible), rather than solely relying on scaling models
518   with more training data and compute for natural language reasoning. In a more general context, it
519   would require that the class of the problem is recognized correctly – here 3-SAT – and that the right
520   solvers are available.
521
      Furthermore, LLMs can serve as valuable knowledge bases, leveraging their extensive common
522
      sense and world knowledge through natural language queries to guide search processes. This can
523
      be utilized by either solvers guiding LLMs (Hazra et al., 2024b) or vice versa, where LLMs assist
524   solvers (Wong et al., 2023).
