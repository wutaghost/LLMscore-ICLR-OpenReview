011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            We delve into a challenging variant of the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP),
014            namely tunnel TSP, which incorporates a new important constraint requiring the
015            traversal of a prescribed set of tunnels. While traditional deep reinforcement learn-
016            ing (DRL) based neural TSP algorithms excel in optimizing routes without tunnel
017            restrictions, they often struggle to achieve optimal performance in tunnel TSP due
018            to the neglect of the crucial role of tunnel attributes during solution generation. To
019
               address this challenge, we propose a simple but effective and flexible technique,
               called Double-Encoder Transformer (DET), which can be seamlessly integrated
020
               into various existing autoregressive neural TSP solvers. DET processes node and
021
               tunnel location information separately and encodes them in two distinct feature
022            spaces. Following an efficient fusion strategy, DET then integrates the encoded
023            information from nodes and tunnels, harnessing their intricate interactions. Exper-
024            imental validation demonstrates that integrating DET into existing autoregressive
025            neural solvers significantly improves performance, enabling us to reduce the aver-
026            age optimality gap for tunnel TSP from 12.58% (of the previous Single-Encoder
027            model) to 7.35%.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Combinatorial Optimization (CO) aims to find some optimal solutions within a combinatorial space,
033
      guided by an objective function and subject to constraints. The Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP)
      serves as a classic example, where the goal is to determine the shortest route that visits a set of fixed
034
      locations exactly once. However, real-world applications frequently involve additional complexities
035
      such as time constraints, capacity limitations, and infrastructure restrictions, necessitating the devel-
036   opment of various TSP variants tailored to specific industries (Wahyuningsih & Sari, 2021; Toaza &
037   Esztergár-Kiss, 2023).
038
039
      One such variant is the Clustered TSP (CTSP) (Chisman, 1975). In CTSP, travelers are required to
      visit all nodes within a cluster before moving to other nodes. CTSP is an NP-hard problem (Mestria,
040
      2018) and finds broad applicability in various domains, including route optimization in automated
041
      warehouses (Bock et al., 2024; Baniasadi et al., 2020), business transactions between supermarkets
042   and suppliers, manufacturing (Laporte et al., 1998), integrated circuit testing, emergency vehicle
043   dispatching and examination timetabling (Laporte & Palekar, 2002). Although CTSP has numerous
044   applications and several existing algorithms specifically tailored to it (Mestria, 2018; Lu et al., 2020;
045   Ahmed, 2014; Jiang et al., 2020; Bao et al., 2023; Dasari & Singh, 2023), most existing CTSP
046   solvers employ heuristic methods, with no neural solvers specifically designed for this problem.
047   Although (Chisman, 1975) proposes a method to reformulate a CTSP instance into a TSP instance,
048   this approach is incompatible with current neural solvers since existing TSP neural solvers are built
049   upon 2D Euclidean space, which cannot accommodate such a transformation. See Appendix A for
050
      a counterexample.
051   We focus on addressing the simplest special case of CTSP, where the cluster sizes are limited to a
052   maximum of 2, and explore the potential of neural solvers for this problem. We refer to it as tun-
053   nel TSP, due to the clustering constraint resembling the traversal of a tunnel connecting two nodes.
      Although tunnel TSP may appear straightforward, it holds considerable significance. When priori-


                                                         1
054
      tizing inter-cluster path optimization in CTSP, especially for large-scale TSP problems (Ding et al.,
055   2007; Pan et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2021; Zong et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2023; Falkner & Schmidt-Thieme,
056   2023; He et al., 2023) or during CTSP algorithm testing, considering tunnel TSP can intuitively nar-
057   row down the solution space, enabling a quicker resolution. Initially, each cluster’s interior is treated
058   as a small-scale TSP problem, determining a path within it. Subsequently, we simplify the cluster’s
059   elements to two, representing the start and end points of the sub-TSP problem. This transformation
060   converts the inter-cluster path optimization in CTSP into tunnel TSP, significantly enhancing the
061   training efficiency for large-scale TSP problems.
062   In this paper, we systematically define tunnel TSP and introduce two Markov Decision Process
063   (MDP) frameworks to model it. To enhance the utilization of tunnel TSP problem information, we
064   propose a generalized strategy named DET (Double Encoder Transformer), which optimizes the so-
065   lution process by separately inputting node information into a node encoder and tunnel information
066   into a tunnel encoder to obtain their corresponding embeddings. During the decoding period, both
067   encoder embeddings are concurrently considered for computation. While DET increases memory
068   usage compared to a single encoder, it fully leverages tunnel information and explores its properties,
069
      yielding superior results.
070   In our experiments, we first compared the performance of several autoregressive TSP neural solvers
071   for the tunnel TSP problem. Subsequently, we tested the performance of these algorithms after in-
072   corporating DET. The results show that DET, which can serve as a plugin seamlessly integrated into
073   these DRL-based autoregressive TSP neural solvers, is compatible with both MDP procedures for
074   solving tunnel TSP. Compared to those Single-Encoder algorithms, those incorporating DET signif-
075
      icantly improve performance, enabling us to reduce the average optimality gap for tunnel TSP from
      12.58% (using the Single-Encoder model) to 7.35%(using the Double-Encoder model). We firmly
076
      believe that this innovative approach has the potential to inspire further research into combinatorial
077
      optimization problems that involve specific spatial constraints. Our contribution can be concluded
078   as follows:
079
080          • We address the tunnel TSP problem, a useful variant of TSP. To the best of our knowl-
081            edge, no prior research has utilized deep reinforcement learning (DRL) to solve tunnel TSP
082            problems.
083
084          • We proposed a model named DET, which encodes node and tunnel information distinctly
085
               to address tunnel TSP. DET can be seamlessly integrated as a plugin into various autore-
               gressive neural TSP solvers. Experimental results show that incorporating the DET model
086
               enhances these solvers’ ability to handle tunnel TSP problems effectively.
087
088          • By combining the proposed DET model with the Regret model, we develop the DET-
089            POMO-Regret model. Experimental results showcase that this model achieves state-of-
090            the-art (SOTA) performance on tunnel TSP problems of varying scales while preserving
091            accuracy on the standard TSP problems.

096   DRL neural methods for solving TSPs With the incorporation of DRL into the routing problems,
097   some heuristics that are already commonly used achieve better results than the original heuristics (Ye
098   et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2023; d O Costa et al., 2020). Apart from these, starting with Pointer Net-
099
      work (Vinyals et al., 2015; Bello et al., 2016), more algorithms that leverage the characteristics of
      the problem are employed to solve the routing problem. Neural TSP solvers can be roughly divided
100
      into autoregressive solvers and Non-autoregressive solvers (Joshi et al., 2020). non-autoregressive
101
      solvers often apply GCN (Joshi et al., 2019; Fu et al., 2021) and GNN (Senuma et al., 2022) archi-
102   tectures, while autoregressive solvers often apply attention mechanism or recurrent neural networks.
103   Attention Model (Kool et al., 2018) was the first autoregressive solver to use transformer (Vaswani,
104   2017). POMO (Kwon et al., 2020) exploits the symmetry of TSP routes and trains multiple in-
105   stances simultaneously to enhance AM’s efficiency. Furthermore, Pointerformer (Jin et al., 2023)
106   employs a multi-pointer network to achieve better results. (Sun et al., 2024) introduces a regret
107   mechanism that allows the autoregressive model to withdraw previous choices. Our DET is also
      based on autoregressive methods, which can be integrated into various autoregressive models.


                                                         2
108
      Recent research on TSP problems has focused more on considering the problem structure to improve
109   their generalization or reduce training costs. Sym-NCO (Kim et al., 2022b) proposes broader sym-
110   metries of different routing problems than POMO(Kwon et al., 2020). BQ-NCO (Drakulic et al.,
111   2024) proposes a general framework to transform the CO problems into MDP. ELG (Gao et al.,
112   2023) learns auxiliary strategies from locally transferable topological features, thereby improving
113   cross-scale generalization performance. InViT (Fang et al., 2024) reduces the search space based on
114   a statistical conclusion about the optimal solution. Starting from the unique structure of tunnel TSP,
115   our DET greatly improves the generalization of different tunnel TSP problems by fully exploiting
116   the structural characteristics of tunnel TSP.
117
118   Different variants of TSPs To address complex real-world scenarios, researchers have developed
119
      many variations of the TSP problem. These variants include but are not limited to clustered TSP
      (Chisman, 1975), asymmetric TSP (Zhang et al., 2023), black and white TSP (Bourgeois et al.,
120
      2003), multi-commodity pickup and delivery TSP (Hernández-Pérez & Salazar-González, 2009; Ma
121
      et al., 2022) and generalized TSP (GTSP) (Pop et al., 2024). As heuristic algorithms have advanced,
122   solvers like LKH3 (Helsgaun, 2017) have proven their proficiency in handling these TSP variants.
123   However, the traditional heuristic algorithm faces the problem that the computation time increases
124   greatly with the problem size.
125
      The introduction of DRL has further broadened the horizon of TSP research. By harnessing the
126
      power of machine learning, researchers have explored new variants of TSP. (Zhang et al., 2021) in-
127
      troduces Dynamic TSP (DTSP), where the size of TSP instances is dynamically adjusted throughout
128   the solution process. The most recently studied variant of the TSP problem is the min-max mTSP
129   problem (Mahmoudinazlou & Kwon, 2024; Park et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2022a; Son et al., 2024;
130   2023), which seeks to minimize the maximum cost among all salesmen. These variants underscore
131   the potential of TSP to address multiple optimization criteria. In addition, the DRL algorithm also
132   greatly reduces the computation time (Kool et al., 2018; Jin et al., 2023), making it possible to
133   solve large-scale problems. However, some pre-existing variants remain challenging to solve using
134   DRL neural solvers, such as the CTSP and the GTSP. Our DET offers a solution to tunnel TSP, the
135   simplest form of CTSP, marking the first attempt to use DRL to solve CTSP.

      To solve tunnel TSP, we propose the Double Encoder Transformer (DET), which includes two en-
245
      coders to process node and tunnel information in parallel. See Figure 1 for an overview of its
246   pipeline. Recall that any node can be viewed not only as the node itself but also as an end of a
247   generalized tunnel in the generalized tunnel graph.
248
249   Input features of encoders We input the relevant features of nodes into the node encoder, for-
250   matted as (x, y) × 8. Here, (x, y) signifies the coordinate of a single node, the ×8 indicates that
251   we employed an 8-fold data augmentation strategy, a commonly used technique in previous work
252   (Kwon et al., 2020), which generates 8 equivalent instances of each instance by flipping and rotating
253   G. We input the relevant features of generalized tunnels into the tunnel encoder, which has a form
254   denoted as (x1 , y1 , x2 , y2 ; Dtunnels ) × 8. Here (x1 , y1 ) and (x2 , y2 ) represent the coordinates of
255   the two points that make up a generalized tunnel, Dtunnels represents the enhancement aimed at
256
      emphasizing tunnel features, which may include multiple elements. Since directivity is naturally
      introduced in this tunnel representation, we use the symmetry method to avoid the bias in undirected
257
      tunnels. Specifically, for each tunnel, we create a ‘mirror’ version that has inverted coordinates and
258
      negative additional features (i.e. (x2 , y2 , x1 , y1 , −Dtunnels )). The two versions are entered into the
259   tunnel encoder together, and their encoding values are averaged to obtain the final tunnel encoding.
260   This ensures that the encoding is direction-agnostic. In subsequent experiments, we set Dtunnels to
261   be the intrinsic length of the tunnel. For dummy generalized tunnels, Dtunnels is set to 0.
262
263   Numbers of different element inputs into the tunnel encoder We input the features of m − n
264   generalized tunnels into the tunnel encoder. This strategy aims to ensure that every node in G can be
265   effectively integrated into the encoding process of the tunnel encoder. This strategy also guarantees
266   that nodes located at both ends of the same generalized tunnel maintain consistency at the tunnel
267   embedding level, meaning their corresponding tunnel embedding is identical.

      5.1   E XPERIMENT SETTING
358
359   To evaluate the efficiency of our DET, we compared the performance of the original Single-Encoder
360   model and the model integrated with DET on tunnel TSP, using some state-of-the-art (SOTA) deep
361   reinforcement learning (DRL)-based autoregressive TSP solvers as a baseline. All models were
362   trained on a single Nvidia RTX 4090 24GB GPU using the same hyperparameters as in the original
363   work. Training consisted of 500 epochs, with each epoch comprising 100 batches. The batch size
364   varied depending on the problem size and available memory, ranging from 10 to 100 instances per
365   epoch. For the TTSP-50-12 problem, each epoch took approximately 2.5 to 3 minutes to train.

      information and tunnel connection information. To our knowledge, there is no dataset specifically

      designed for tunnel TSP.

      Baselines We consider the following DRL Algorithms as our baselines:
379
380           • LKH3 (Helsgaun, 2017): LKH3 algorithm is an efficient heuristic algorithm for solving
381             TSP, which uses local search and edge exchange strategies to find the optimal or near-
382             optimal path solutions. To apply the LKH3 algorithm to the tunnel TSP problem, we follow
383             the idea of (Lu et al., 2020) and set the elements corresponding to tunnels in the distance
384             matrix to the negative values of the maximum distances between nodes, guiding the LKH3
385             algorithm to select tunnels actively.
386           • POMO (Kwon et al., 2020): Leveraging the symmetry inherent in TSP problems, POMO
387             utilizes N parallel instances to generate diverse trajectories during the training phase for
388             dominance estimation. Additionally, it introduces a data augmentation technique to effec-
389             tively reduce experimental variance.
390           • InViT (Fang et al., 2024): InViT employs a nested design and incorporates invariant views
391             within its encoders. This design enhances the model’s generalization capabilities, enabling
392             it to perform stably across problem instances with varying distributions or scales.
393           • LCH-Regret (Sun et al., 2024): Building upon the existing learning construction methods,
394             LCH-Regret introduces a learnable regret coding vector. This vector allows for rollback to
395             the previous node during construction, avoiding the local optimal scheme caused by the
396             greedy decoding strategy.

430   Performance of different existing models Considering the differences between tunnel TSP and
431   the standard TSP, some methods that perform well in solving the standard TSP may not excel when
      addressing the tunnel TSP. Let’s first consider the performance of different baseline methods.


                                                          8
432
      We notice a significant widening of the performance gap when TSP models are applied to the tun-
433   nel TSP. Taking POMO as an example, the gap for solving TTSP-100-12 (20.73%) is nearly four
434   times that of solving TTSP-100-0 (equivalent to TSP-100) at 5.34%. Similarly, POMO-Regret’s gap
435   for TSP-100-12 (10.88%) is also over four times that for TTSP-100-0 (2.47%). Notably, in some
436   scenarios with a small number of tunnels n, we even observe that the results of some models numer-
437   ically underperform their TSP-m counterparts. As the number of tunnels increases, the performance
438   gaps between models widen further. However, interestingly, when reaching TTSP-100-50, POMO’s
439   gap slightly narrows compared to TTSP-100-37. We believe that the reasons for this phenomenon
440   are as follows:
441   Those connected nodes significantly elevate the problem’s complexity and diminish the potential
442   optimization space. Moreover, such tunnel constraints are more likely to lead algorithms to prema-
443   turely converge to suboptimal solutions within the search space. Additionally, TTSP-m-0 comprises
444   solely of standalone nodes, TTSP-m- m   2 exclusively of connected nodes. In all other tunnel TSP
445   instances, both standalone and connected nodes coexist. The Single Encoder model struggles to dif-
446   ferentiate between these two types of nodes, thereby compromising its effectiveness. However, our
447
      Double-Encoder model distinguishes between these standalone nodes and connected nodes, con-
      tributing to our algorithm’s superior performance.

450   Compare DET-Model with Single-Encoder models We have integrated the DET model into
451
      both the POMO and POMO-Regret models. It is noteworthy that the modifications in the Regret
      model are primarily made during the decoding period, where a regret flag is introduced during
452
      action selection for optimal use. In contrast, the alterations in DET centered around the encoding
453
      period and affected the method of calculating scores. Since these two models do not conflict, we
454   can combine them and utilize the score calculation formula mentioned in Section 4 for decoding the
455   DET-POMO-Regret framework. The results demonstrate that the DET-POMO model outperforms
456   the POMO model across all problem scales, with particularly significant improvements observed
457   when n ̸= 0, m 2 . In some cases, its performance is close to that of POMO-Regret. Thanks to the
458   synergy between DET and Regret, DET-POMO-Regret further reduces the gap across all problem
459   scales compared to both DET-POMO and POMO-Regret. DET-POMO-Regret represents the current
460   best DRL solution for solving tunnel TSP. It is worth noting that the specific implementation details
461   of the two encoders are not our primary concern, and we will later validate this in ablation studies.
462   We also adapted InViT to create DET-InViT, whose implementation details are shown in Appendix
463   C due to space constraints. The InViT algorithm’s effectiveness is partly attributed to the statistical
464   prior that ‘98% of the path nodes in the optimal path of the TSP problem are within the 8-nearest
465   neighbors of the corresponding node’. However, we found that this prior conclusion does not apply

467   TSP problem is not much better than POMO and is significantly behind POMO-Regret. Regarding
468
      DET-InViT, although its performance is not significantly improved when n = 0, m     2 , and may not be
      as good as InViT, it is still guaranteed to solve the tunnel TSP problem better than the original InViT
469
      when n ̸= 0, m2 . But among the various DET models, DET-InViT is the one with the worst relative

      performance.

529   6   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this work, we focus on an important variant of the TSP problem: tunnel TSP. It can be regarded
532
      as a special case of Clustered TSP. Notably, there is currently a lack of research on Clustered TSP
533
      using DRL. To fully exploit the information of tunnel TSP, we innovatively propose the DET ar-
534   chitecture, which efficiently encodes node features and tunnel features separately and effectively
535   integrates them. Through a large number of experimental validations, we demonstrate that DET can
536   seamlessly integrate into various autoregressive neural TSP solvers, significantly enhancing their
537   ability to solve tunnel TSP. We believe that the DET architecture offers new perspectives and ap-
538   proaches for solving combinatorial optimization problems with specific constraints and holds broad
539   application prospects. In the future, we will continue to deepen this research by applying DRL
      methods to address more complex CTSP problems.

869   Global tunnel encoder Our primary modification involves the introduction of a global tunnel en-

873   encoder. However, considering the storage cost, we opted to use a single global tunnel encoder,
874   When integrating tunnel information into each state or action encoder, we prepend an independent
875
      linear layer to facilitate the conversion process, and the generalized tunnel transformation matrix is
      utilized after this step. Since DET does not alter the training methodology, we train DET-InViT in

      the same manner as InViT.

      Combination strategy Put simply, in InViT, the embeddings of the last visited node and the first
879
      visited node (or depot) serve as the query input for the decoder, while the embeddings of other
880   potential candidates act as the keys and values within the decoder. After encoding each generalized
881   tunnel, its encoded value is added to the state encoder and action encoder at each step through
882   numerical summation. We offer two distinct fusion methods: add tunnel embedding only to the
883   query in the decoder; and add tunnel embedding to all the queries, keys, and values in the decoder.
884   As stated in Section 5.2, the statistically derived prior conclusion that ‘98% of the path nodes in
885   the optimal path of the TSP problem are within the 8-nearest neighbors of the corresponding node’
886   is the basis of the outperformance of InViT, but it may not fit for tunnel TSP. When the number of
887   both standalone nodes and connected nodes is not very small, adding tunnel information to the key
888
      and value can greatly improve the results, as it allows for better distinction between the two types of
      nodes. Therefore, when n is large (n > 0.45m, i.e. 90% of the nodes are connected nodes), and the

      Tunnel checking When adopting a multi-instance synchronous training strategy similar to POMO

      ing the restrictions imposed by tunnels on the action space. However, to adhere to the constraints
