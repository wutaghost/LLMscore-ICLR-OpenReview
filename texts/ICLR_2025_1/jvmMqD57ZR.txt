009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            With the rise of large foundation models, split inference (SI) has emerged as a
012            popular computational paradigm for deploying models across lightweight edge
013            devices and cloud servers, addressing both data privacy and computational cost
014            concerns. However, most existing data reconstruction attacks have focused on
015            smaller classification models like ResNet, leaving the privacy risks of founda-
016            tion models in SI settings largely unexplored. To address this gap, we propose a
017            novel data reconstruction attack based on guided diffusion, which leverages the
018            rich prior knowledge embedded in a latent diffusion model (LDM) pretrained on
019
               a large-scale dataset. Our method performs iterative reconstruction on the LDM’s
               learned image manifold, effectively generating high-fidelity images closely resem-
020
               bling the original data from their intermediate representations (IR). Extensive ex-
021
               periments demonstrate that our approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art
022            methods, both qualitatively and quantitatively, in reconstructing data from deep-
023            layer IRs of the vision foundation model. The results highlight the urgent need for
024            more robust privacy protection mechanisms for large models in SI scenarios.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      The rapid development of deep learning has revolutionized various aspects of daily life—from AI
030   assistants to autonomous vehicles. However, the substantial computational resources required by
031   these emerging models often hinder their deployment on edge devices. Therefore, offloading in-
032   tensive computation to cloud servers become a popular alternative. Following this paradigm, Split
033   inference (SI) (Kang et al., 2017) has emerged as one of the most promising solutions, as it bal-
034   ances both computational and privacy concerns. This approach enables efficient utilization of cloud
035   resources, reduces the computational burden on local devices, and facilitates the integration of com-
036   plex models into everyday technologies by partitioning neural network computations between edge
037   devices and cloud servers, with data processed locally before being sent to the server.
038   Despite its advantages, recent studies (He et al., 2019; Dong et al., 2021; Li et al., 2024; Xu et al.,
039   2024; Sa et al., 2024) have uncovered significant privacy risks associated with SI, particularly in
040   the form of data reconstruction attacks (DRA). In DRA, adversaries attempt to reconstruct clients’
041   input data by exploiting the exchanged intermediate representations (IR) between clients and servers,
042   posing serious threats that break user’s privacy.
043   However, the growing adoption of more powerful models, such as Vision Transformers (Dosovitskiy
044   et al., 2020), raises concerns about the effectiveness of existing defenses. Vision Transformers
045   have demonstrated superior performance across various vision tasks and are widely used in modern
046   applications. Despite this, the privacy implications of deploying these models in SI settings remain
047   underexplored.
048   In this paper, we address this gap by investigating privacy leaks in vision transformers in the con-
049   text of SI. We propose a novel attack based on guided diffusion that effectively utilizes the prior
050   knowledge captured by large latent diffusion models (LDM) Rombach et al. (2022) pretrained on
051   large-scale datasets (e.g., Stable Diffusion) to reconstruct input data from deep-layer IR. Leveraging
052   this prior knowledge, we successfully invert IR back to the original input data across various natural
053   image datasets, revealing a critical privacy vulnerability in the SI framework. Additionally, we eval-
      uate our attack on models equipped with existing defenses (Singh et al., 2021; Vepakomma et al.,

055   defenses. Our key contributions are summarized as follows:

068   2.1   S PLIT I NFERENCE / C OLLABORATIVE I NFERENCE
069
070
      Split inference (SI) (Kang et al., 2017) is a method aimed at speeding up inference and/or reduc-
      ing power consumption on end-point devices while ensuring data privacy. Unlike the approach of
071
      performing inference completely on the cloud, which requires sending raw data to the server, SI
072
      enhances privacy by sending only transformed, non-trivially interpretable IR to the cloud. In SI, the
073   model f is split into two part: the client model fc : X → H, deployed on the edge device, and
074   the server model fs : H → Y, deployed on the cloud. During inference, the private data xprivate
075   is first processed by fc on the edge, producing the “smashed” data tensor Hprivate = fc (xprivate ).
076   This IR is then sent to the cloud, where fs completes the remaining computation to obtain the result
077   yprivate = fs (Hprivate ). SI therefore addresses the constraints of limited computational resources on
078   edge-side while preventing direct exposure of private data to the cloud.
079
080   2.2   DATA R ECONSTRUCTION ATTACK
081
082   One line of prior research focuses on the privacy of users’ input data. In the context of SI, an
083   adversary may carry out data reconstruction attacks to extract private information by reconstructing
084
      the input data. According to He et al. (2019), data reconstruction attacks can be classified into three
      types: 1) white-box attacks, 2) black-box attacks, and 3) query-free attacks.
085
086   For the white-box attacks, He et al. (2019) first introduced regularized Maximum Likelihood Esti-
087   mation (rMLE), which optimizes the zero-initialized input x to minimize the distance between the
088   IR of the reconstructed input H = fc (x) and Hprivate obtained during the message exchanges. Total
089   Variation (Rudin et al., 1992) loss is applied as an image prior to ensure the optimization process
090
      not only minimizes the distance but also produces results that appear natural to human perspective.
      Singh et al. (2021) further improved reconstruction quality by incorporating a deep image prior
091
      (Ulyanov et al., 2018), resulting in the Likelihood Estimation (LM) approach. Dong et al. (2021),
092
      on the other hand, considers to improve the training of inverse network (He et al., 2019) by applying
093   a cycle loss. Li et al. (2024) proposed GAN-based Latent Space Search (GLASS), which constrains
094   the search space of x by optimizing the latent code in StyleGAN2 (Karras et al., 2020), achieving
095   high quality reconstructions and circumventing several defenses (He et al., 2019; Singh et al., 2021;
096   Titcombe et al., 2021; Mireshghallah et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Osia et al., 2020).
097
      However, these works primarily focus on evaluating small CNN models, such as ResNet18 (He et al.,
098
      2016), leaving their effectiveness against more advanced architecture, such as Vision Transformer
099   (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), unclear.
100
101

106   further expanded the ability to generate high-resolution, diverse images, with subsequent works
107   (Ramesh et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023) extending control within the latent diffusion framework.
      Beyond conditional generation through training, another line of research (Chung et al., 2023; Bansal

139
      Following prior works (He et al., 2019; Singh et al., 2021; Dong et al., 2021; Li et al., 2024; Sa
140
      et al., 2024), we examine the privacy risks in the context of split inference (Kang et al., 2017). In
141   this scenario, a client with limited computation resources seeks to offload the inference computation
142   to a cloud server without exposing the private data xprivate . To achieve this, the client decomposes
143   the model f = fs ◦ fc into two parts, only deploying fs to the server. During inference, the client
144   first feeds the data xprivate to obtain the hidden state Hprivate = fc (xprivate ), then sends Hprivate to the
145   server. The server finishes the remaining computation tasks and sends output y = fs (Hprivate ) to the
146   client.
147   The server, acting as an honest-but-curious participant, aims to reconstruct the private data xprivate
148   from the hidden state Hprivate . We then assess the privacy threat posed by adversaries with white-box
149   access to the model. In this scenario, the adversary is assumed to have full knowledge of the archi-
150   tecture and parameters of the client’s model fc . Specifically, the client uses a foundation model as
151   the backbone for their applications, keeping its parameters frozen for downstream tasks. Following
152   the taxonomy of (He et al., 2019), there are two ways to formulate the problem: optimization based
153   and learning based.
154   Optimization Based. The adversary aims to find input data x whose hidden state H closely matches
155   Hprivate through optimization:

      3.3     L EARNING - BASED DATA R ECONSTRUCTION ATTACK
217
218   When the split point is too deep, optimizing latent zt from randomly initialized noise cannot guar-
219   antee high-quality reconstructions. To address this, we propose using an auxiliary dataset collected
220   from publicly available resources to train a lightweight model fr : H → X by minimizing Eq. (2).
221   This model consists of a transformer encoder block, a linear layer, a learnable position embedding
222   layer, and a CNN-based post-processing module. This setup resembles the decoder architecture in
223   ViT-MAE (He et al., 2022). Details can be found in Fig. 1 (b).
224   The coarse reconstruction result from fr serves as a better initialization for the latent zt during
225   optimization-based data reconstruction. Specifically, we apply DDPM diffusion, adding random
226   noise with a small timestep t ≤ T , to project the coarse reconstruction onto an editable manifold.

233   We evaluate our method using three datasets: (1) FFHQ (Karras et al., 2019), (2) MSCOCO (Lin

238   solely on Dpublic . The model’s utility and privacy index are evaluated using the validation set Dval ,
239   which is not used in fine-tuning or attacker model training. For the optimization-based attack, we
240
      randomly sample 10 images from the validation split of each dataset as the target images. For the
      learning-based attack, we use the entire validation split as the evaluation target.

359   5.3   S HUFFLE ( AND D ROP T OKEN ) D EFENSE
360
361   ViTs naturally exhibit an adaptive computation capability compared to CNNs, allowing them to
362   reduce inference time by identifying and halting the forward propagating of redundant tokens. Pre-
363   vious work (Yin et al., 2022) explores strategies to reduce redundant tokens in intermediate layers.
364   From a privacy protection perspective, shuffling patch tokens complicates data reconstruction for
365   attackers, as the loss function for guiding reconstruction (Eq. (7)) is sensitive to token order. Clients
366   can even retain all patch tokens without compromising model performance. For tasks where token
367   order is irrelevant (e.g., classification), shuffling patch tokens provides a straightforward defense
368
      against data reconstruction attacks. Moreover, this method is simple for clients to implement, requir-
      ing only token shuffling before transmission to the server, with minimal memory copying overhead.
369
370   We further evaluate the attack performance against the shuffling defense in a white-box setting.
371   To simulate the scenario of token dropping, we design the following protocol: the client shuffles
372   patch tokens and randomly drops rN patch tokens before sending them to the server, where r is the
373   ratio of the tokens being dropped (see Fig. 4). Note that a realistic implementation would be more
374
      complicated than this evaluation protocol, as clients may combine multiple strategies to both reduce
      inference time and enhance data privacy.
375
376   As noted in Darcet et al. (2024), tokens retain information about their original positions, which can
377   be inferred using a linear layer. Based on this observation, we train a 2-layer MLP classifier to
      predict the probability that a token Hi,: is originally at position arg max pθ (Hi,: ). Once trained, the

      accuracy in predicting token positions, with an average ℓ1 distance of 3.077 from the correct posi-

425   6   C ONCLUSION
426
427   This work reveals significant privacy risks in Split Inference (SI) with large vision foundation mod-
428   els like Vision Transformers, extending beyond previous attacks on smaller models like ResNet18.
429   We propose a novel data reconstruction attack leveraging LDMs pretrained on large-scale datasets.
430   Our method generates high-fidelity images from IR and outperforms state-of-the-art approaches in
431   reconstructing data from deep-layer IR. These findings underscore the need for stronger defenses to
      protect privacy when deploying transformer-based models in SI settings.
