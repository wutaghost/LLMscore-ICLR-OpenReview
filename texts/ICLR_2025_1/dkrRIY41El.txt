                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Conventionally trained image classifiers recently excel in accuracy across diverse
012
               tasks. One practical limitation is however that they assume all potential classes
013
               to be seen during training, i.e. they can not tell ”I don’t know” when encounter-
014            ing an unknown class. Open set recognition (OSR), which solves this problem of
015            detecting novel classes during inference, therefore remains an open problem and
016            is receiving increasing attention. Thereby, a crucial challenge is to learn features
017            that are relevant for unseen categories from given data, for which these features
018            might not be discriminative. Previous work has shown that the introduction of
019            self-supervised contrastive learning to supervised paradigms can support diverse
020            feature learning and thereby benefit OSR. However, the diversity in contrastive
021            learning is commonly introduced through crafted augmentation schemes. To im-
022
               prove upon this aspect and ”optimize to learn” more diverse features, we propose
               GradMix, a data augmentation method that dynamically leverages gradient-based
023
               attribution maps of the model during training. The idea is to mask out the activated
024
               areas in previous epochs so that the models can pay attention to broader areas and
025            learn to extract features beyond of what is most discriminative for every class.
026            The resulting models are expected to learn more diverse features from the same
027            data source and thus to improve in OSR and model generalization. Extensive ex-
028            periments on open set recognition, close set classification, and out-of-distribution
029            detection reveal that our method performs well on these tasks that can often out-
030            perform the state-of-the-art. GradMix is also beneficial for increasing robustness
031            to common corruptions. In self-supervised learning, GradMix can increase the
032            accuracy of downstream linear classifiers compared with baselines, indicating
033
               its benefit for model generalization. Our code is publicly released on https:
               //anonymous.4open.science/r/comprehensive_osr-8EEF/

      Deep neural networks have achieved remarkable performance across various fields, particularly in
      object classification tasks, where they can even surpass human accuracy in certain aspects Rus-
040
      sakovsky et al. (2015). However, traditional learning paradigms require the entire dataset to be
041
      available prior to training, and models are typically limited to recognizing only the classes present in
042   the training set. In real-world scenarios, however, new and previously unseen classes often emerge,
043   which may be difficult to collect in the short term or even impossible to anticipate beforehand. As a
044   result, the challenge of recognizing novel classes during inference becomes unavoidable. This task is
045   referred to as open set recognition (OSR), and it has garnered significant research attention in recent
046   years Yu et al. (2017); Ge et al. (2017); Hassen & Chan (2020); Dhamija et al. (2018); Yoshihashi
047   et al. (2019); Zhou et al. (2021); Cao et al. (2021); Miller et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2022a); Vaze
048   et al. (2022); Xu et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024). Throughout this paper, we will refer to known
049   classes as ”in set” and unknown classes as ”open set”.
050   Existing OSR approaches leverage a range of techniques, including generative models Yu et al.
051   (2017); Ge et al. (2017), novel learning objectives Vaze et al. (2022); Miller et al. (2021), and en-
052   sembling Wang et al. (2024). Most of these methods are fundamentally based on the principle of
053   comprehensively modeling the in set data. Theoretical work in Wang et al. (2024) has demon-
      strated that OSR performance is positively correlated with feature diversity. In this paper, we pro-

      2.1   O PEN S ET R ECOGNITION
095
      Open set recognition (OSR) involves identifying novel class samples during inference. OSR ap-
096   proaches generally fall into two categories: discriminative and generative models. Discriminative
097   models Yu et al. (2017); Miller et al. (2021); Hassen & Chan (2020); Chen et al. (2022a); Vaze et al.
098   (2022); Xu et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024) often involve designing novel learning objectives to im-
099   prove the recognition of known classes. For example, Miller et al. (2021) introduces a loss function
100   that encourages each in set sample to be close to its class center while being as far as possible from
101   other classes. Similarly, in Chen et al. (2022a), each in set class is separated from other classes and
102   the learned open set space. Additionally, some methods in this category, such as Yu et al. (2017),
103   utilize synthesized open set samples generated by techniques like GANs.
104   Another category of OSR approaches uses generative models to model in set data Ge et al. (2017);
105   Cao et al. (2021). For instance, Ge et al. (2017) employs OpenMax to model in set data and detects
106   open sets by comparing features learned from generators between testing data and in set training
107   data. Similarly, Cao et al. (2021) utilizes a Gaussian mixture variational autoencoder to model in set
      data. For a comprehensive review of open set recognition methods, see Mahdavi & Carvalho (2021).

      2.2   M IXING DATA AUGMENTATION
109
110   Mixing data augmentation methods involve combining raw samples with other images from within
111   or outside the dataset. These methods can be broadly categorized into two types. The first category
112   is pixel-wise mixing, initially introduced as Mixup Zhang et al. (2017). In this approach, augmented
113   data is created by taking a weighted average of the original samples and their corresponding labels.
114   An extension of this method, known as manifold Mixup Verma et al. (2019), extends the concept
115   from mixing raw inputs to mixing latent representations. This extension has been shown to improve
116
      classification performance.
117   The second category is patch-wise mixing, where a portion of the original samples is replaced. An
118   early method in this category is CutOut DeVries & Taylor (2017), which involves masking out a
119   patch of the original samples. CutMix Yun et al. (2019) extends this idea by replacing patches
120   with resized samples from the same minibatch. SaliencyMix Uddin et al. (2020) further refines
121   CutMix by identifying the most representative areas in the original data using saliency detection
122
      techniques Montabone & Soto (2010). In addition to these methods, there are many other mixing
      data augmentation approaches. For a comprehensive review, see Lewy & Mańdziuk (2023).

128   nents: the incorporation of self-supervised learning within supervised paradigms and the application
129   of gradient-based mixing augmentation. In this section, we introduce our framework for open set

      recognition and outline the key methodological components that drive its effectiveness. For clar-
      ity in the following discussion, we adopt the following notation: uppercase letters represent sets of

      scalers, while lowercase letters refer to individual samples within those sets. Vectors are indicated

      by bold letters, and uppercase bold letters are used to denote sets of vectors or matrices.

      3.1   S UPERVISED AND S ELF - SUPERVISED C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING
135
136   Supervised learning can sometimes lead to class collapse Jing et al. (2021), a phenomenon where the
137   features of sub-classes become suppressed, resulting in diminished feature diversity. On the other
138   hand, self-supervised learning has recently gained substantial attention for its ability to extract high-
139   quality features without the need for labeled data Chen et al. (2020); He et al. (2020); Grill et al.
140   (2020). Several studies have demonstrated that combining self-supervised learning with supervised
141   learning helps prevent class collapse and promotes greater feature diversity, as shown in works like
142
      Xue et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2022b). Based on these findings, we integrate this approach into our
      open set recognition framework.
143
144   We pair SimCLR Chen et al. (2020) and supervised contrastive learning (SupCon) Khosla et al.
145   (2020) in our method due to their shared foundational principles. SimCLR is a self-supervised con-
146   trastive learning method that learns representations by minimizing the distances between the original
147   data and its augmented counterpart in the feature space. For a sample xi and its augmented version
148
      xj within a minibatch of size 2N (with N original samples and their corresponding augmentations),
      their representations are denoted as zi and zj . xi and xj form a positive pair, while xi and all other
149
      samples in the minibatch constitute negative pairs. This learning objective is derived from InfoNCE
150
      Oord et al. (2018) and is described in equation 1. SimCLR is designed to maximize the mutual infor-
151   mation between zi and zj Oord et al. (2018), encouraging the model to capture more of the inherent
152   features in the sample itself. In equation 1, 1[k̸=i] ∈ 0, 1 is an indicator function that flags negative
153   pairs, i.e., xi paired with any sample other than xj in the minibatch. The function sim(·) measures
154   similarity, typically using cosine function. The temperature parameter τ is a hyperparameter that
155   controls the learning dynamics between positive and negative pairs. It is clear from equation 1 that
156   minimizing Lsimclr (i) is equivalent to maximizing sim(zi , zj ) while minimizing sim(zi , zk ). The

      3.2   G RADIENT- BASED M IXING AUGMENTATION
176
177   To enable the models to learn more diverse features, we propose an additional data augmentation
178   method that encourages the models to focus on broader areas within the data. Inspired by the mixing
179   augmentation techniques discussed in section 2.2, our approach masks out a portion of the learned
180   areas after each epoch during training. Rather than random masking, we hope the model can pay
181   more attention to unlearned areas in data. To address this, we propose detecting the activated ar-
182   eas in the input data directly using the models during training, combined with visual explanation
183   techniques, such as GradGAM Selvaraju et al. (2017) and LayerGAM Jiang et al. (2021).
184   One foundational method for visual explanation is GradGAM, which visualizes the learning process
185   by performing a weighted combination of forward activation maps. We denote the localization map
186   of the activated areas computed by GradGAM as Mgradcam . As shown in equation 4, Mgradcam is

      is calculated as the average of the partial gradients with respect to each spatial position in Ak , where

277   Following the OSR testbench that widely used in the literature Chen et al. (2022a); Vaze et al. (2022);

279   FAR+10, CIFAR+50, and TinyImageNet, which are created using the source datasets MNIST Deng
280   (2012), SVHN Netzer et al. (2011), CIFAR10 Krizhevsky et al. (2009a), CIFAR100 Krizhevsky
281   et al. (2009b), and TinyImageNet Deng et al. (2009). For each protocol, we repeat the experiments
282   five times with different splits of in set and open set classes, and then average the results. In ad-
283   dition to GradMix, we apply the standard augmentations used in SimCLR Chen et al. (2020) for
284
      all protocols except MNIST, to avoid producing confusing samples (e.g., vertically flipping a ”7”
      could resemble a ”1”). Due to the same reason and the simpleness of the dataset, GradMix is not
285
      utilized to MNIST. But it can still surpass state-of-art performances. The number of training classes
286
      (denoted by I), the total number of testing classes (denoted by S), and their data sources for each
287   protocol are provided in table 6 pin appendix A.1. The complexity of each protocol is measured by
288
      the openness metric, O = 1 − I/S, which describes the proportion of open set classes relative
289   to the total number of classes. All models are trained on known classes only and evaluated on test
290   samples from both known and unknown classes. ResNet18 He et al. (2016) is used as the feature
291   encoder backbone in all experiments in this section, with the output feature dimension set to 128.

526   6                C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this work, we proposed a novel approach for open set recognition, combining self-supervised
529   learning with a gradient-based data augmentation method, guided by the idea of learn diverse fea-
530   tures. Experimental results demonstrate that our approach surpasses most state-of-the-art methods
531   in OSR, closed set classification, and OOD tasks. Additionally, GradMix enhances model robust-
532   ness against common corruptions and boosts downstream linear classification performance in self-
533   supervised learning, further highlighting its effectiveness for improving model generalization.
534   Limitations Recent works have reported that model sparsity can increase model robustness to adver-
535   sarial attacks Timpl et al. (2022). Besides the experiments in section 4.4 on common data corruption,
536   GradMix could be further evaluated for its effectiveness on adversarial attacks, which is beyond our
537   current study. Furthermore, as analyzed in Chen et al. (2022c), model sparsification can allow new
538   neural connections to grow and help the models to escape bad local minima, and hence reduce over-
539   fitting. It remains to explore if GradMix, probably other data augmentation methods in general, can
      achieve the same effects, which can also be the key of learning diverse attribute-related features.
