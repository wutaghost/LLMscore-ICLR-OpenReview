011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Exploring how brain activity translates into visual perception offers valuable in-
014            sights into the biological visual system’s representation of the world. Recent ad-
015            vancements have enabled effective image classification and high-quality recon-
016            struction using brain signals obtained through Functional Magnetic Resonance
017            Imaging (fMRI) or magnetoencephalography (MEG). However, the cost and bulk-
018            iness of these technologies hinder their practical application. In contrast, Elec-
019
               troencephalography (EEG) presents advantages such as ease of use, affordability,
               high temporal resolution, and non-invasive operation, yet it remains underutilized
020
               in related research due to a shortage of comprehensive datasets. To fill this gap,
021
               we introduce EEG-ImageNet, a novel EEG dataset featuring recordings from 16
022            participants exposed to 4000 images sourced from the ImageNet dataset. This
023            dataset offers five times the number of EEG-image pairs compared to existing
024            benchmarks. EEG-ImageNet includes image stimuli labeled with varying levels
025            of granularity, comprising 40 images with coarse labels and 40 with fine labels.
026            We establish benchmarks for both object classification and image reconstruction
027            based on this dataset. Experiments with several commonly used models show
028            that the best-performing models can achieve object classification with an accu-
029            racy around 60% and image reconstruction with two-way identification around
030
               64%. These findings highlight the dataset’s potential to enhance EEG-based vi-
               sual brain-computer interfaces, deepen our understanding of visual perception in
031
               biological systems, and suggest promising applications for improving machine
032
               vision models.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Recent advancements in reconstructing visual experiences from the human brain have seen
039   significant progress, largely driven by the extensive use of functional magnetic resonance
040   imaging (fMRI) (Heeger and Ress (2002); Logothetis (2008); Logothetis et al. (2001)) and
041   magnetoencephalogram (MEG) (Benchetrit et al. (2023)) datasets. fMRI and MEG are widely
042   used to investigate various cognitive functions, neurological disorders, and brain connectivity pat-
043   terns (Antonello et al. (2024); Ye et al. (2024); Toneva et al. (2022); Tang et al. (2024)). Driven
044   by the use of deep neural networks, particularly diffusion-based and transformer-based models, it is
045
      even possible to reconstruct human’s visual perceptions from fMRI or MEG recordings (Takagi and
      Nishimoto (2023); Scotti et al. (2024); Ozcelik and VanRullen (2023); Cheng et al. (2023)). These
046
      models, with their powerful generalization and learning capabilities, have accelerated advancements
047
      in the field. This success in reconstructing visual objects from fMRI signals is largely attributed
048   to the availability of large-scale datasets, which offer comprehensive data essential for conducting
049   extensive studies and in-depth analyses (Richards et al. (2019); Lin et al. (2014)). For example, the
050   Natural Scenes Dataset (Allen et al. (2022)), contains up to hundreds of thousands of high-quality
051   natural image-fMRI pairs of 8 subjects, providing a solid data foundation for recent work in visual
052   neuroscience. These models and large-scale datasets, in turn, have opened new avenues for under-
053   standing the brain’s intricate functions and for developing advanced applications in brain-computer
      interfaces, neuroimaging, and beyond (St-Yves et al. (2023)).

084
085   In addition to fMRI and MEG, electroencephalography (EEG) is another vital tool in neuroscience
086   research. EEG is easy to use, cost-efficient, and has high temporal resolution, making it a valuable
087   tool for capturing rapid and real-time brain dynamics on the order of milliseconds (Teplan et al.
088   (2002)). EEG signals can be obtained non-invasively by placing electrodes on the scalp, making
089   it a less intrusive method for monitoring brain activity. These attributes position EEG as another
090   promising modality for visual neuroscience research.
091   Although visual reconstruction has been achieved using fMRI and MEG, the high cost and incon-
092   venience of these devices limit their widespread application in practical settings. In contrast, EEG
093   presents advantages over both fMRI and MEG with its cost-efficient and portable features. However,
094   studies on visual perception with EEG signals are limited because of two challenges: (1) the lack
095   of large-scale, high-quality EEG datasets and (2) existing EEG datasets typically featured coarse-
096   grained image categories, lacking fine-grained categories.
097   To the best of our knowledge, the most frequently used dataset is the data set provided by Spamp-
098   inato et al. (2017), which involves 6 participants each watching 2000 image stimuli. However, this
099   dataset’s scale is smaller than existing fMRI datasets, limiting the possibilities for investigating neu-
100   ral aspects related to visual perception and developing deep learning models for relevant visual clas-
101   sification and reconstruction tasks. The limited data volume of current EEG datasets limits research
102   findings’ statistical power and generalizability.
103   On the other hand, the labels in existing EEG datasets are frequently coarse and lack the granularity
104   needed for detailed analysis. Multi-granularity labels are essential because they allow for a more nu-
105   anced analysis at different levels of detail. For instance, labels can range from broad categories like
106   “panda” or “golf ball” to more specific attributes like “Rottweiler” or “Samoyed”. These challenges
107   underscore the necessity for new, large-scale EEG datasets with high-quality, multi-granularity la-
      bels. Such datasets would enable researchers to explore the intricacies of visual processing with

      greater accuracy and depth, facilitating advancements in both basic neuroscience and applied fields
109   like brain-computer interfaces and clinical diagnostics.
110
111   To address these challenges, we present EEG-ImageNet, a novel EEG dataset specifically designed
112
      to promote research related to visual neuroscience, biomedical engineering, etc. As shown in Fig-
      ure 1, EEG-ImageNet is a comprehensive dataset that includes EEG recordings from 16 subjects,
113
      each exposed to 4,000 images sourced from the ImageNet-21k (Ridnik et al. (2021)). These images
114
      span 80 different categories, with 50 images per category. The dataset is structured to support multi-
115   granularity analysis, with 40 categories dedicated to coarse-grained tasks and 40 to fine-grained
116   tasks. We further establish benchmarks for two primary tasks: object classification and image re-
117   construction. For the object classification task, we evaluated the dataset using several commonly
118   used models, achieving a best accuracy of 60.88% on the 80-class classification. In the image re-
119   construction task, our experiments with advanced generative models, with the best model achieving
120   a two-way identification of 64.67%. These benchmarks demonstrate the dataset’s potential for ad-
121   vancing EEG-based research. By addressing the lack of large-scale, high-quality EEG datasets,
122   EEG-ImageNet aims to drive EEG-based visual research forward, improve machine learning mod-
123
      els, and provide deeper insights into visual perception and processing.

      2.1   V ISUAL R ECOGNITION DATASET
132
      Visual recognition is a cornerstone of computer vision, driven by datasets like ImageNet (Ridnik
133   et al. (2021)), CIFAR (Krizhevsky et al. (2009)), and MS COCO (Lin et al. (2014)). ImageNet
134   contains over 14 million annotated images, CIFAR-10/100 consist of thousands of 32x32 images in
135   10 and 100 classes, respectively, and MS COCO is known for its rich annotations supporting tasks
136   such as object detection and segmentation.
137
      Efforts to combine visual recognition with neuroscience have led to datasets like the SALICON
138
      dataset (Jiang et al. (2015)), which extends MS COCO with eye-tracking data, enabling the study of
139   visual attention and saliency through large-scale annotations. Neuroscience datasets utilizing fMRI
140   have further enriched this field. The BOLD5000 dataset (Chang et al. (2019)) includes fMRI data
141   from subjects viewing 5000 images, aiding the exploration of visual perception. The Generic Object
142   Decoding dataset (Horikawa and Kamitani (2017)) captures brain activity while subjects view and
143   imagine objects, facilitating the decoding of mental images. The VIM-1 dataset (Kay et al. (2008))
144   from the study “Identifying Natural Images from Human Brain Activity” demonstrates the feasibility
145   of decoding viewed images from brain activity. Additionally, the NSD (Allen et al. (2022)) is a large-
146   scale fMRI dataset in visual neuroscience, recording high-resolution (1.8-mm) whole-brain 7T fMRI
147
      data from eight subjects exposed to 9,000–10,000 color natural scenes from the MS COCO dataset
      over the course of a year. Integrating neuroimaging and eye-tracking datasets with visual recognition
148
      tasks has opened new avenues for understanding how the brain interprets visual information, leading
149
      to insights and applications in brain-computer interfaces and neural decoding.

152   2.2   EEG DATASET
153
      fMRI is renowned for its high spatial resolution, allowing researchers to obtain detailed images of
154
      brain activity by measuring changes in blood flow (Logothetis et al. (2001)). This capability makes
155
      fMRI particularly effective for identifying the specific brain regions involved in various cognitive
156   and sensory tasks. In contrast, EEG offers several distinct advantages over fMRI. EEG is relatively
157   easy to use and cost-efficient, with a straightforward setup that involves placing electrodes on the
158   scalp to measure electrical activity. One of the most significant benefits of EEG is its exceptional
159   temporal resolution, which captures neural dynamics on the order of milliseconds (Teplan et al.
160   (2002)). This high temporal resolution makes EEG ideal for studying fast-occurring brain processes
161   and real-time neural responses, providing insights into the timing and sequence of neural events (Liu
      et al. (2021)). EEG’s non-invasive nature also makes it suitable for a wider range of participants.

214   from participants watching short video clips designed to evoke specific emotional states. The Things

      tremely short image presentation durations (50/100 ms) and collects EEG-image pairs from large-

      3.1       E THICS AND P RIVACY
235
236   To protect participants’ privacy and physical health, our user study adheres to strict ethical guidelines
237   for human research, with approval from the ethics committee1 . The study has undergone a compre-
238   hensive ethical review to safeguard participants’ rights. In accordance with ethical standards, we
239   have taken several steps to protect participants’ privacy, including data anonymization and obtaining
240   informed consent from all participants. Additionally, participants are thoroughly informed about the
241   study’s objectives, procedures, and potential outcomes. The EEG data collection method employed
242
      in this research is non-invasive and poses no risk to participants. This approach ensures compliance
      with ethical standards while maintaining the integrity of the research findings.

      3.2       PARTICIPANTS
246
      We enlist a total of 16 participants via social media, including 10 males and 6 females. These partic-
247   ipants are all college students aged between 21 and 27, with an average age of 24.06 and a standard
248   deviation of 1.69. Their majors encompass computer science, mechanical engineering, chemistry,
249   and environmental engineering, and they range from undergraduate to postgraduate levels. All par-
250   ticipants are right-handed and assert their proficiency in utilizing image search engines in their daily
251   routines. Each participant dedicates approximately 2 hours to complete the experiment, which in-
252   cludes 30 minutes for equipment setup and task instructions. Before the experiment, participants are
253   informed of a compensation of US$11.8 per hour upon completion, to ensure the quality of the data
254   collected for the study.

256   3.3       S TIMULI DATASET
257
      The dataset used for visual stimuli was a subset of ImageNet21k, containing 80 categories of objects.
258
      Each category comprises 50 manually curated images, ensuring that each image has a width and
259
      height greater than 300 pixels and prominently features an object corresponding to its class label in
260   ImageNet. Additionally, every image is free of watermarks. In this manner, we have selected a total
261   of 4000 high-quality natural images as our visual stimulus dataset.
262
263
      Among all categories, the first half is consistent with the EEG-Classification dataset (Spampinato
      et al. (2017)), comprising 40 significantly distinct categories from ImageNet1k. We treat these as
264
      coarse-grained tasks. The latter 40 categories are designed as a fine-grained task, divided into 5
265
      groups with 8 categories each. The categories within the same group share the same parent node in
266   WordNet, and each category label is either a leaf node or a sub-leaf node in WordNet. This selection
267   ensures that the chosen categories represent similar granularity while avoiding overly obscure cate-
268   gories, thereby minimizing potential biases in the experimental results. For instance, coarse-grained

275   3.4   P ROCEDURE
276
277   A pilot study can ensure the correctness of the overall experimental process and the reliability of the
278   acquisition equipment. We conduct a pilot study on two participants whose data are not included
279   in the final analysis to determine hyperparameters of the experimental design, such as font size,
280   fixation time, number of categories, etc.
281   Before engaging in the user study, participants are required to fill out an entry questionnaire and
282   sign a consent about the protection of privacy security. They will receive an orientation regarding
283   the primary tasks and operational procedures. Additionally, they will be notified of their right to
284   withdraw from the study at any point. Before the main trials, participants will undergo a series of
285   training trials designed to acquaint participants with the procedures of the formal experiments.
286   Every participant is required to select a random seed before the experiment to randomize the order
287   of the categories. This randomization guarantees a fair distribution of categories and images among
288   participants. The experimental platform follows a sequential and repetitive process as illustrated
289   in Figure 1. (S1) The experimental platform presents the current category label. Participants can
290   proceed to the next stage by pressing the space key. (S2) A fixation cross is shown at the center
291   of the screen, ensuring attention is drawn when images are displayed. This fixation period lasts
292   for 500 ms. (S3) The 50 images of this category are sequentially presented using the Rapid Serial
293   Visual Presentation (RSVP) paradigm, which is commonly employed in psychological experiments.
294
      Each image is presented for a duration of 500 ms (Kaneshiro et al. (2015)). (S4) Random tests are
      conducted to verify the participant’s engagement in the experiment after the presentation. Data from
295
      categories for which participants fail the test will not be included in final analyses. The EEG signals
296
      of the participant will be captured and recorded continuously during the entire process. The program
297   will cycle back to step S1 and display the next category, repeating this process until all the images
298   have been presented.
299
300
      Ultimately, the dataset we construct includes the EEG signals of participants exposed to each image
      visual stimulus in each valid session, along with the corresponding category’s wnid and the image’s
301
      index in ImageNet21k.

      3.5   DATASET D ESCRIPTION
304
305   The EEG-ImageNet dataset contains a total of 63,850 EEG-image pairs from 16 participants. Each
306   EEG data sample has a size of (n channels, fs · T ), where n channels is the number of EEG elec-
307   trodes, which is 62 in our dataset; fs is the sampling frequency of the device, which is 1000 Hz in
308   our dataset; and T is the time window size, which in our dataset is the duration of the image stimulus
309   presentation, i.e., 0.5 seconds. Due to ImageNet’s copyright restrictions, our dataset only provides
310   the file index of each image in ImageNet and the wnid of its category corresponding to each EEG
311   segment. Additional information about the dataset is shown in Appendix A.1.

314
315   In this section, we detail the benchmarks of our study by outlining the preprocessing steps, feature
316   extraction methods, task definitions, and models used.
317
318
      4.1   P REPROCESSING
319
320   We perform a series of preprocessing steps for the raw EEG data we collect to eliminate noise and ar-
321   tifacts and improve signal quality (Ye et al. (2024)). The preprocessing pipeline includes the follow-
322   ing stages: First, re-referencing: Re-referencing is done using the offline linked mastoids method,
323   which uses the average of the M1 and M2 mastoid electrodes as the new reference point (Yao et al.
      (2019)). This minimizes potential biases and improves the signal-to-noise ratio. Then, filtering:


                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Filtering is performed using a 0.5 Hz to 80 Hz band-pass filter to remove low-frequency drifts and
325   high-frequency noise. Additionally, 50 Hz environmental noise is eliminated. Finally, artifact re-
326   moval: Artifact removal eliminates abnormal amplitude signals and artifacts caused by blinks or
327   head movements.
328
329   4.2   F EATURE E XTRACTION
330
331   In our benchmarks, for models requiring time-domain signals as direct input, we extract the 40ms-
332   440ms segment of each EEG signal as the feature input. This approach helps to minimize the
333   influence of preceding and subsequent image stimuli on the current stimulus. For models requiring
334   frequency-domain features as input, we extract the differential entropy (DE) of the extracted time-
335
      domain signals as features, as this characteristic effectively captures the complexity and variability
      of brain activity in the frequency domain (Duan et al. (2013)). According to the general division
336
      in neuroscience, the frequency bands are categorized as delta (0.5-4 Hz), theta (4-8 Hz), alpha
337
      (8-13 Hz), beta (13-30 Hz), and gamma (30-80 Hz). We use the Welch method with a sliding
338   window to estimate the power spectral density P (f ) in each frequency band. Then,
                                                                                      R we normalize the
339   data and calculate the differential entropy (DE) using the formula, DE = − P (f ) log(P (f ))df .
340   Consequently, for each segment of EEG signals, we obtain the differential entropy (DE) for each
341   electrode and each frequency band.
342
343   4.3   TASK D EFINITION
344
345   In our benchmarks, we test our dataset on two tasks: object classification and image reconstruction.
346   The object classification task aims to classify the category of the corresponding image stimulus
347   the participant is exposed to with their EEG signals. We evaluate the models using classification
348
      accuracy. In the image reconstruction task, given a specific EEG segment, the goal is to reconstruct
      the image stimulus the participant is exposed to. We evaluate the generated results using two-way
349
      identification (Scotti et al. (2024)) under different visual neural networks (refer to Appendix A.3 for
350
      details.).
351
352   On our multi-granularity labeled image dataset, we test various tasks with different levels of gran-
353   ularity. Additionally, due to the inherent significant inter-individual differences in EEG signals,
354
      all models in our benchmarks are trained exclusively in an intra-subject experimental setup. Fur-
      thermore, to mitigate the significant temporal effects (Li et al. (2020)) observed in the EEG-
355
      Classification dataset, all our experimental setups strictly adhere to a dataset split where the first
356
      30 images of each category are used as the training set, and the last 20 images are used as the test
357   set. This approach ensures that the model learns features that are less correlated with irrelevant
358   information. We strongly recommend that all researchers using the EEG-ImageNet dataset adopt a
359   similar dataset split methodology. We also explain the impact of this split in Appendix A.5.
360
361   4.4   M ODELS
362
363   In the object classification task, we employ simple machine-learning classification models such
364   as ridge regression, KNN, random forest, and SVM. Additionally, we implement deep learning
365   models including MLP, EEGNet (Lawhern et al. (2018)), and RGNN (Zhong et al. (2020)). These
366   models are the most commonly used and have demonstrated excellent performance in EEG-related
367   research, which is why we have selected them for our study. MLP consists of two hidden layers,
368
      while EEGNet and RGNN are implemented using their original architectures. We use cross-entropy
      as the loss function. We train the models for each participant for 2000-3000 epochs on an NVIDIA
369
      4090 GPU.
370
371   In the image reconstruction task, we use a Stable Diffusion 1.4 (Rombach et al. (2022)) as the
372   backbone and train a two-layer MLP as an encoder to generate the prompt embeddings for input.
373   For our reconstructions, we use 50 denoising timesteps with PNDM noise scheduling to generate
374
      512x512 images. We use MSE as the loss function to align the encoder’s output with the CLIP (ViT-
      L/14) (Radford et al. (2021)) embeddings of the captions of real images obtained through BLIP (Li
375
      et al. (2022)). The model for each participant is trained for 2000 epochs with on an NVIDIA 4090
376
      GPU.
377
      For model and training details, please refer to the open-source code and Appendix A.3.

      6   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we introduced EEG-ImageNet, a novel EEG dataset designed to advance research
489
      in visual neuroscience. EEG-ImageNet is comprehensive and richly annotated, comprising EEG
490
      recordings from 16 subjects exposed to 4000 images from 80 different categories. We established
491   benchmarks for two primary tasks using EEG-ImageNet: object classification and image reconstruc-
492   tion. The introduction of EEG-ImageNet and the established benchmarks provide a robust founda-
493   tion for subsequent research. We outline the limitations of our dataset and explore how it could
494   guide future research efforts to advance machine learning and brain-computer interface design.
495
      Limitation. Firstly, while our dataset is more comprehensive than similar works, each participant’s
496
      data is still relatively limited. This necessitates the development of inter-subject models to overcome
497   this limitation and enhance generalizability. Additionally, it is limited in representation, as partici-
498   pants were drawn from a convenience sample at our university. This results in an age distribution
499   skewed towards college-aged individuals and a racial composition predominantly White and Asian,
500   which limits the dataset’s generalizability. Future work should aim to include a more diverse and
501   extensive participant pool. Secondly, while we employed methods such as reducing the segment
502   length of each EEG recording and sequentially splitting the training and test sets to mitigate the
503   temporal effect, we were unable to eliminate it completely. Future work should explore more so-
504   phisticated techniques to address this issue. Lastly, our benchmarks did not incorporate many of the
505
      latest deep-learning methods. We believe that recent advancements in deep learning could greatly
      benefit from our comprehensive dataset, potentially leading to significant breakthroughs in visual
506
      neuroscience.
507
508   Insight for ML. The EEG-ImageNet dataset provides a comprehensive resource for developing
509   models in visual recognition tasks, enabling the development of sophisticated deep-learning models
510   capable of capturing intricate patterns within EEG data. Future research could leverage the dataset
511
      to enhance domain adaptation and transfer learning techniques, facilitating effective inter-subject
      task completion. Researchers might develop state-of-the-art models for the benchmarks we defined,
512
      or even create new tasks. By offering a diverse set of visual stimuli and supporting multi-level clas-
513
      sification tasks, EEG-ImageNet could foster the creation of hierarchical models that mirror human
514   cognitive processes and improve the generalization capabilities of machine learning algorithms.
515
516   Insight for BCI. As hardware technology progresses, portable EEG devices are becoming increas-
517
      ingly feasible, offering new opportunities for real-time BCI applications. Researchers could use the
      dataset to develop robust BCI systems that accurately interpret user intent from EEG signals. The
518
      comprehensive size and diverse visual stimuli in EEG-ImageNet allow for the creation of adaptive
519
      BCI systems that learn and respond to individual user patterns. This paves the way for personalized
520   neurotechnology solutions, particularly enhancing human-computer interaction for individuals with
521   disabilities. Furthermore, addressing privacy protection and ethical concerns will be crucial as BCI
522   technology advances, ensuring user data is securely handled and individual rights are respected.
523
      Ethics Statement. Our dataset was collected from human participants, making privacy protec-
524
      tion and ethical use of the data a priority. The user study received approval from the institutional
525
      ethics committee, and all participants provided informed consent, agreeing to the public release of
526   the anonymized data for research purposes. It is important to note that our dataset exhibits certain
527   biases, including racial bias, which was influenced by the demographics of available participants.
528   Researchers using this dataset should be aware of this limitation and consider it in their analysis.
529   Additionally, all participants were right-handed. This decision was made to control for variability,
530   as previous research has shown significant differences in EEG signals between right- and left-handed
531   individuals Provins and Cunliffe (1972). By selecting only right-handed participants, we aimed to
532   ensure the consistency and reliability of the EEG signals across subjects. Researchers should con-
533   sider this factor when conducting studies where handedness is not a variable of interest to minimize
534
      variability in the results.

736   A.3   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP D ETAILS
737
738   In the object classification task, we conduct experiments under three different granularity settings:
739   the ”all” task includes all 80 categories; the ”coarse” task includes 40 coarse-grained categories;
740   and the ”fine” task includes 8 fine-grained categories that belong to the same parent node, with
741
      the average accuracy calculated across 5 groups. Each classification model maintain parameter
      consistency across the three tasks. We train one model per participant, ensuring that the parameters
742
      are consistent between models for different participants as well.
743
744   The model structures and hyperparameters are as follows. For SVM, we try linear, polyno-
745   mial, and radial basis function (RBF) kernels. The regularization parameter is tested from values
746   {10−3 , 10−2 , 10−1 , 1, 101 , 102 , 103 }. For RandomForest, we try to set the number of trees in the
747
      forest from values {20, 50, 100, 200, 500}, with all other parameters set to their default values. For
      KNN, we set the number of neighbors to {5, 10, 15, 20}. For ridge regression, all parameters are set
748
      to their default values. For RGNN, when calculating the edge weights between electrodes, we use
749
      the hardware parameters of our data collection device to determine the topological coordinates of
750   each electrode. In addition to the standard implementation, we add two batch normalization layers.
751   The main hyperparameters adjusted are the number of output channels of the graph convolutional
752   network (i.e., the hidden layer dimension) and the number of hops (i.e., the number of layers). These
753   are set to {100, 200, 400} and {1, 2, 4} respectively. For EEGNet, we use the standard implemen-
754   tation and set the length of the first step convolution kernel to half the number of sampling time
755   points, which is 200. The main hyperparameters adjusted are the number of output channels for the
      first convolutional layer (F1) and the depth multiplier (D), which are set to {8, 16, 32} and {2, 4, 8}


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      respectively. For MLP, we set two hidden layers with dimensions of 256 and 128, respectively. Each
757   linear layer is followed by a batch normalization layer and a dropout layer with a probability of 0.5.
758
759   For all deep models, we use the cross-entropy loss function. In MLP and EEGNet, we use the SGD
760
      optimizer with learning rate 10−3 , weight decay 10−3 , and momentum 0.9, training for 2000 epochs.
      After that, we adjust the learning rate to 10−4 and weight decay to 10−4 and continue training for
761
      another 1000 epochs. In RGNN, we use the Adam optimizer with learning rate 10−3 and weight
762
      decay 10−3 , training for 2000 epochs. Subsequently, we adjust the learning rate to 10−4 and weight
763   decay to 10−4 and train for an additional 1000 epochs. The batch size is uniformly set to 80.
764
765   In the image reconstruction task, we first use blip-image-captioning-base to obtain captions for each
766
      stimulus image, with parameters set to ”max length”: 200 and ”num beams”: 20 to achieve relatively
      detailed descriptions. Then, we use the CLIP ViT-L/14 version’s tokenizer and text encoder to
767
      compute the CLIP embedding for each caption, resulting in dimensions of (77, 768). Next, we
768
      map the frequency domain features of the EEG signals to (77, 768) using an MLP with two hidden
769   layers, having dimensions of 1024 and 2048, respectively. This output is then fed into a frozen Stable
770   Diffusion 1.4 model built with the diffusers library. After 50 steps of inference, we obtain image
771   outputs with dimensions of 512x512. The Stable Diffusion model we built specifically consists of the
772   UNet2DConditionModel and AutoencoderKL, and we use PNDMScheduler as the noise scheduler.
773
      Two-way identification refers to a method where the generated image is compared with the original
774
      image and one distractor image. The task is to correctly identify the original image from the pair.
775   This method evaluates the ability of the reconstruction pipeline to produce images that are distin-
776   guishable and recognizable as the original stimuli. In this study, we selected the second and fifth
777   convolutional layers of AlexNet, the output before the linear layer of Inception, and the embeddings
778   from CLIP ViT-L/14 as comparison features to calculate two-way identification.
779
      All the implementations mentioned above are open-sourced and available in the GitHub repository.

866   In our experimental paradigm, to reduce the cognitive load on participants, we group images of the
867   same category together and use the RSVP (Rapid Serial Visual Presentation) paradigm for contin-
868   uous rapid stimulation. This approach may cause the model to learn temporal continuity features
869   rather than the intrinsic characteristics of the category stimuli. In this study, we use narrower cropped
870   time segments as input features and divide the training and test sets based on temporal order to re-
871   duce the impact of temporal effects. In Figure 6, we plotted the average classification accuracy for
872
      images at different index positions in the test set under various training and test set splits.

890   also used several statistical methods for analysis. For the 30-20 split, we first calculated the sliding
891
      window standard deviation to measure local volatility. With a sliding window size of 5, the standard
      deviation at the 14th data point was less than 0.01, indicating a convergence trend at this point. We
892

893
      and a p-value of 0.6790, suggesting that the data might not have fully converged. For the 20-30 split,
894   the standard deviation at the 16th data point was less than 0.01 with a sliding window size of 5. The
895   ADF test yielded a statistic of -3.8505 and a p-value of 0.0024, indicating that the data is stationary
896   and convergent.

      Further, our dataset publicizes the time series information for each EEG segment along with the
900   corresponding image presentation order. This information enables classification experiments to bet-
901   ter address temporal effects by performing the data partitioning. As such, our dataset serves as a
902   valuable benchmark, and we hope that our dataset can contribute significantly to future research
903   aimed at mitigating temporal effects in the RSVP paradigm.
904
      In RSVP paradigms, temporal effects are inherently present due to the sequential presentation of
905
      stimuli. Employing a fully randomized sampling method can exacerbate this issue, as the preced-
906   ing and succeeding images introduce temporal effects that impact the semantic alignment of brain
907   signals for a given image. To address this, we adopted a refined experimental design, as illus-
908   trated in Figure 7, to ensure consistent EEG responses corresponding to the same image semantics.
909   Our two-phase experiments separated training and testing stages, avoiding block design overlaps.
910   This separation ensures no temporal dependencies between experimental conditions, enabling ro-
911   bust signal decoding. Thus far, data from three participants have been collected, with four more
912   participants scheduled for data collection. Importantly, the data acquisition methods and formats re-
913   main consistent with those of the original dataset, ensuring compatibility and facilitating integration
914
      for analysis. The experiment will result in a total of 14 additional hours of recordings and 28,000
      EEG-image pairs, with preliminary results from the new dataset summarized in Table 6.
