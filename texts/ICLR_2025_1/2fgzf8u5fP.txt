011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Diffusion models excel at capturing the natural design spaces of images, molecules,
014            and biological sequences of DNA, RNA, and proteins. However, for many appli-
015            cations from biological research to biotherapeutic discovery, rather than merely
016            generating designs that are natural, we aim to optimize downstream reward func-
017
               tions while preserving the naturalness of these design spaces. Existing methods
               for achieving this goal often require “differentiable” proxy models (e.g., classi-
018
               fier guidance) or computationally-expensive fine-tuning of diffusion models (e.g.,
019
               classifier-free guidance, RL-based fine-tuning). Here, we propose a new method,
020            Soft Value-based Decoding in Diffusion models (SVDD), to address these chal-
021            lenges. SVDD is an iterative sampling method that integrates soft value functions,
022            which looks ahead to how intermediate noisy states lead to high rewards in the fu-
023            ture, into the standard inference procedure of pre-trained diffusion models. Notably,
024            SVDD avoids fine-tuning generative models and eliminates the need to construct
025            differentiable models. This enables us to (1) directly utilize non-differentiable fea-
026            tures/reward feedback, commonly used in many scientific domains, and (2) apply
027            our method to recent discrete diffusion models in a principled way. Finally, we
028
               demonstrate the effectiveness of SVDD across several domains, including image
               generation, molecule generation (optimization of docking scores, QED, SA), and
029
               DNA/RNA generation (optimization of activity levels).

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Diffusion models have gained popularity as powerful generative models. Their applications extend
035   beyond image generation to include natural language generation (Sahoo et al., 2024; Shi et al.,
036   2024; Lou et al., 2023), molecule generation (Jo et al., 2022; Vignac et al., 2022), and biological
037   (DNA, RNA, protein) sequence generation (Avdeyev et al., 2023; Stark et al., 2024). In each of
038   these domains, diffusion models have been shown to be very effective at capturing complex natural
039
      distributions. However, in practice, we might not only want to generate realistic samples, but to
      produce samples that optimize specific downstream reward functions while preserving naturalness by
040
      leveraging pre-trained models. For example, in computer vision, we might aim to generate natural
041
      images with high aesthetic and alignment scores (Black et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2023). In drug
042   discovery, we may seek to generate valid molecules with high QED/SA/docking scores (Lee et al.,
043   2023; Jin et al., 2018) or RNAs (such as mRNA vaccines (Cheng et al., 2023)) with high translational
044   efficiency and stability (Castillo-Hair and Seelig, 2021; Asrani et al., 2018), and regulatory DNAs
045   that drives high cell-specificity of expression (Gosai et al., 2023; Taskiran et al., 2024; Lal et al.,
046   2024).
047
      The optimization of downstream reward functions using pre-trained diffusion models has been
048   approached in various ways. In our work, we focus on non-fine-tuning-based methods because
049   fine-tuning generative models (e.g., when using classifier-free guidance (Ho et al., 2020) or RL-based
050   fine-tuning (Black et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2023; Uehara et al., 2024; Clark et al., 2023; Prabhudesai
051   et al., 2023)) often becomes computationally intensive, especially as pre-trained generative models
052   grow larger in the era of “foundation models”. Although classifier guidance and its variants (e.g.,
053   Dhariwal and Nichol (2021); Song et al. (2020); Chung et al. (2022); Bansal et al. (2023); Ho et al.
      (2022)) have shown some success as non-fine-tuning methods in these settings, they face significant

      Non-fine-tuning methods. We discuss two main methods for optimizing rewards in diffusion
109   models without fine-tuning. We further cover closely relevant methods based on sequential Monte
110   Carlo (SMC) in Section 6 and Appendix B, after presenting our method.

112
      • Classifier guidance (Dhariwal and Nichol, 2021; Song et al., 2020): It has been widely used
        to condition pre-trained diffusion models without fine-tuning. Although these methods do not
113
        originally focus on optimizing reward functions, they can be applied for this purpose (Uehara
114
        et al., 2024, Section 6.2). In this approach, an additional derivative of a certain value function is
115     incorporated into the drift term (mean) of pre-trained diffusion models during inference. Subsequent
116     variants (e.g., Chung et al. (2022); Ho et al. (2022); Bansal et al. (2023); Guo et al. (2024); Wang
117     et al. (2022); Yu et al. (2023)) have been proposed to simplify the learning of value functions.
118     However, these methods require constructing differentiable models, which limits their applicability
119     to non-differentiable features/reward feedbacks commonly encountered in scientific domains as
120     mentioned in Section 1. Additionally, this approach cannot be directly extended to discrete diffusion
121     models (e.g., (Lou et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024; Sahoo et al., 2024)) in a principle way. Our approach
122     aims to address these challenges.
123     Note a notable exception of classifier guidance tailored to discrete diffusion models has been
124     recently proposed by Nisonoff et al. (2024). However, SVDD can be applied to both continuous
125     and discrete diffusion models in a unified manner. Furthermore, their practical method requires the
126
        differentiability of proxy models, unlike SVDD. We compare its performance with our method in
        Section 7. We provide further details in Section C.
127
128   • Best-of-N: The naive way is to generate multiple samples and select the top samples based on
129     the reward functions, known as best-of-N in the literature on (autoregressive) LLMs (Stiennon
130
        et al., 2020; Nakano et al., 2021; Touvron et al., 2023; Beirami et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2023). This
        approach is significantly less efficient than ours, as SVDD uses soft-value functions that predict
131
        how intermediate noisy samples lead to high rewards in the future. We validate this experimentally
132
        in Section 7.
133
134
135
      Fine-tuning of diffusion models. Several methods exist for fine-tuning generative models to
      optimize downstream reward functions, such as classifier-free guidance (Ho and Salimans, 2022) and
136
      RL-based fine-tuning (Fan et al., 2023; Black et al., 2023) or its variants (Dong et al., 2023; Wallace
137
      et al., 2024). However, these approaches often come with caveats, including high computational
138   costs and the risk of easily forgetting pre-trained models. In our work, we propose an inference-time
139   technique that eliminates the need for fine-tuning generative models.
140
141   Discrete diffusion models. Based on seminal works Austin et al. (2021); Campbell et al. (2022),
142   recent work on masked diffusion models (Lou et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024; Sahoo et al., 2024)
143   has demonstrated their strong performance in natural language generation. Additionally, they have
144   been applied to biological sequence generation (e.g., DNA, protein sequences in Campbell et al.
145
      (2024); Sarkar et al. (2024)). In these cases, the use of diffusion models over autoregressive models
      is particularly apt, given that many biological sequences ultimately adopt complex three-dimensional
146
      structures. We also note that ESM3 (Hayes et al., 2024), a widely recognized foundation model in
147
      protein sequence generation, bears similarities to masked diffusion models. Despite its significance,
148   it cannot be integrated with standard classifier guidance, because adding a continuous gradient
149   to a discrete objective is not inherently valid. Unlike standard classifier guidance, SVDD can be
150   seamlessly applied to discrete diffusion models.

342   5   A DVANTAGES , E XTENSIONS , L IMITATIONS OF SVDD
343
344   We discuss the advantages, extensions, and limitations of SVDD.
345   5.1 A DVANTAGES
346
      No fine-tuning (or no training in SVDD-PM). Unlike classifier-free guidance or RL-based fine-
347
      tuning, SVDD does not require any fine-tuning of the generative models. In particular, when using
348
      SVDD-PM, no additional training is needed as long as we have r.
349
350   No need for constructing differentiable models. Unlike classifier guidance, SVDD does not
351   require differentiable proxy models, as there is no need for derivative computations. For example, if
352   r is non-differentiable feedback (e.g., physically-based simulations for docking scores in molecule
353
      generation), our method SVDD-PM can directly utilize such feedback without constructing differen-
      tiable proxy models. In cases where non-differentiable computational feedback is costly to obtain, the
354
      usage of proxy reward models may still be preferred, but they do not need to be differentiable; thus,
355
      non-differentiable features or non-differentiable models based on scientific knowledge (e.g., molecule
356   fingerprints, GNNs) can be leveraged. Similarly, when using SVDD-MC, while a value function
357   model is required, it does not need to be differentiable, unlike classifier guidance. Additionally,
358   compared to approaches that involve derivatives (like classifier guidance or DPS), SVDD can be
359   directly applied to discrete diffusion models mentioned in Example 2.
360
361
      5.2 E XTENSIONS
362   Using a likelihood/classifier as a reward. While we primarily consider scenarios where reward
363   models are regression models, by adopting a similar strategy to that in Zhao et al. (2024), they can be
364   readily replaced with classifiers or likelihood functions in the context of solving inverse problems or
365
      conditioning (Chung et al., 2022; Bansal et al., 2023).
366   Fine-tuning by distilling SVDD. The inference-time cost may become slow as M increases in
367   SVDD. This issue can be mitigated by policy distillation, that is further fine-tuning diffusion models
368   to align them closely with policies from SVDD (Salimans and Ho, 2022; Kim et al., 2023). We leave
369   this aspect for future work.
370
      5.3 P OTENTIAL L IMITATIONS
371
372
      Memory and computational complexity in inference time. Our approach requires more compu-
      tational resources (if not parallelized) or memory (if parallelized), approximately M times more than
373
      standard inference methods, as noted in Section 4.2. Taking this aspect into account, we compare
374
      SVDD, with baselines such as best-of-N in our experimental section (Section 7). For gradient-based
375   approaches like classifier guidance and DPS, while a direct comparison with SVDD is challenging, it
376   is important to note that these methods also incur additional computational and memory complexity
377   due to the backward pass, which SVDD avoids. Lastly, it is important to note that this additional
      inference-time burden can be alleviated through distillation, as discussed in Section 5.2.

                                                          7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Proximity to pre-trained models. If significant changes to the pre-trained models are desired,
379   we acknowledge that RL-based fine-tuning (Black et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2023) could be more
380   effective than SVDD for this purpose in certain scenarios, such as image examples. However, this
381   proximity to pre-trained models could also be advantageous in the sense that it is robust against
382   reward optimization, which conventional fine-tuning methods often suffer from by exploiting these
383   out-of-distribution regions (Uehara et al., 2024). Lastly, in cases where reward backpropagation
384   (Prabhudesai et al., 2023; Clark et al., 2023; Uehara et al., 2024) is not applicable, particularly in
385   scientific domains for RL-based fine-tuning, we may need to rely on PPO. However, PPO is often
386   mode-seeking and unstable, highlighting the challenges of RL-based fine-tuning in certain scenarios.
387
388
      6   C OMPARISON BETWEEN SVDD AND SMC-BASED M ETHODS
389   SMC-based methods for diffusion models are closely related to SVDD. These approaches (Wu et al.,
390   2024; Trippe et al., 2022; Dou and Song, 2024; Phillips et al., 2024; Cardoso et al., 2023) use SMC
391   (Del Moral and Doucet, 2014) for sampling from diffusion models. While they are originally designed
392   for conditioning (by setting rewards as classifiers), they can also be applied to reward maximization.
393   Notably, similar to our work, these methods do not require differentiable models.
394   However, these SMC methods are not tailored to reward maximization. Most importantly, they
395   involve resampling across the “entire” batch, which complicates parallelization. Additionally, when
396   batch sizes are small, as is often the case with recent large diffusion models, performance may be
397   suboptimal, since the SMC theoretical guarantees hold primarily with large batch sizes. Even with
398   larger batch sizes, using SMC for reward maximization can result in a loss of diversity across the
399   entire batch, since the effective sample size based on weights, which is a standard diversity measure
400   in SMC, does not ensure “real” diversity in the generated samples. In contrast, our method is highly
401
      parallelizable, performs well even with small batch sizes (as low as 1), and maintains diversity with
      larger batch sizes, as sampling is conducted on a “per-sample basis” (Line 4). We empirically validate
402
      this in Section 7. We provide further details and experiments in Appendix B.

534   We propose a novel inference-time algorithm, SVDD, for optimizing downstream reward functions in
535   pre-trained diffusion models that eliminate the need to construct differentiable proxy models. Future
536   works include applications in other domains, such as protein sequence optimization (Gruver et al.,
537   2023; Alamdari et al., 2023; Watson et al., 2023) and 3D molecule generation (Xu et al., 2023).

917   size does not directly translate into greater diversity in the generated samples. In contrast, SVDD,

1120   function model. The Enformer trunk has 7 convolutional layers, each having 1536 channels. as well
1121   as 11 transformer layers, with 8 attention heads and a key length of 64. Dropout regularization is
1122   applied across the attention mechanism, with an attention dropout rate of 0.05, positional dropout of
1123   0.01, and feedforward dropout of 0.4. The convolutional head for final prediction has 2*1536 input

1130   to both the convolutional and GRU layers. A single GRU layer with dropout of 0.1 is added after the
1131   convolutional layers. The convolutional head for final prediction uses 64 input channels and average
1132   pooling, without batch normalization. For training, the batch size is selected from {16, 32, 64, 128},
