                                                       A BSTRACT
008
009
                Longitudinal data are crucial in several fields, but collecting them is a challeng-
010
                ing process, often hindered by concerns such as individual privacy. Extrapolating
011             in time initial trajectories or generating fully synthetic sequences could address
012             these issues and prove valuable in clinical trials, drug design, and even public
013             policy evaluation. We propose a generative statistical model for longitudinal data
014             that links the temporal dependence of a sequence to a latent diffusion model and
015             leverages the geometry of the autoencoder latent space. This versatile method can
016             be used for several tasks - prediction, generation, oversampling - effectively han-
017             dling high-dimensional data such as images and irregularly-measured sequences,
018             needing only relatively few training samples. Thanks to its ability to generate se-
019
                quences with controlled variability, it outperforms previously proposed methods
                on datasets of varying complexity, while remaining interpretable.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024
      Longitudinal data, also known as panel data, consist of repeated measurements over time that track
025   the evolution of the same entity or individual—more concretely, its trajectory. The total number of
026   observations is relatively small, and their frequency can be sparse, unlike time series data, which
027   typically involve more frequent measurements. Longitudinal data are common in many application
028   fields, such as medicine (e.g., for modeling disease progression (Zhao et al., 2021) or monitoring
029   treatment response (Blackledge et al., 2014)) and econometrics (Baltagi, 1995).
030
      Their dimensionality can range from relatively low (e.g. tabular data) to quite high (e.g. images).
031   Furthermore, the number of different entities followed is most of the time pretty small (in the case
032   of rare diseases for instance). These aspects make them challenging to model; still, the generation
033   of synthetic longitudinal data can have powerful applications (Mosquera et al., 2023; Kühnel et al.,
034   2023), for data augmentation, future prediction and missing data imputation.
035
      In our applications, the ideal generative model for longitudinal data needs to produce varied tra-
036
      jectories while starting from the same situation, but this variability needs to be controlled to limit
037   variations around one or several core tendencies 1 , so that the generated samples remain plausible.
038
039
      R ELATED WORK
040
041   Modeling longitudinal data Prior methods to statistically model longitudinal data and understand
042   underlying evolution dynamics were mainly relying on mixed-effect models (Laird & Ware, 1982)
043   that parameterise a patient’s evolution as a deviation from a reference trajectory (Diggle et al., 2002;
044   Singer & Willett, 2003; Debavelaere et al., 2020). These methods are quickly limited and can
045   not be applied to complex trajectories (especially when there is no clear average evolution). Other
046   modelling tentatives include RNN-based (Cao et al., 2018) or GAN-based methods (Luo et al., 2018;
047
      Sun et al., 2021). The former is not generative - only handling missing data, and the latter relies on
      a difficult adversarial training and does not yield a tractable and interpretable mathematical model.
048
049
      Improving VAE latent space Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling, 2014;
050
      Rezende et al., 2014) are powerful models for capturing distributions. Their latent spaces can reveal
051
      representative features through disentanglement (Higgins et al., 2017) and can be equipped with
052

      Riemannian geometry (Shao et al., 2018) to extract population structure in latent space. However,
055   standard VAEs, assuming i.i.d. representations, fail to capture temporal correlations in data. Sev-
056   eral works have aimed to enhance latent representations using Gaussian processes (Fortuin et al.,
057   2020; Ramchandran et al., 2021) or normalizing flows (Rezende & Mohamed, 2016; Chadebec &
058   Allassonnière, 2023). Yet, these models are mainly designed for missing data imputation or condi-
059   tional tasks, making them less suitable for unconditional sequence generation. Moreover, normaliz-
060   ing flows, being deterministic, fail to introduce variation. Closely related to our work, Li & Mandt
061   (2018) propose disentangling time-dependent features by jointly training a VAE with LSTMs, which
062   results in a complex and computationally heavy training process.
063
064   ODE / SDE Also closely related to our method are approaches involving neural ordinary differ-
065
      ential equations (NODE) that see the forward pass of a residual network as solving an ODE. In
      particular, the latent neural ODE model proposed by Chen et al. (2018) defines a generative model
066
      by assuming that the initial state latent variable follows a given prior distribution and a latent tra-
067
      jectory is then obtained by solving an ODE. Yildiz et al. (2019), Kanaa et al. (2021) extends this
068   method for high-dimensional data but unlike ours, these models are completely deterministic in the
069   latent space, hindering the diversity of generated samples. Going stochastic, Li et al. (2020) use a
070   latent SDE model but do not apply it to high-dimensional datasets.
071
072   High-dimensional data generation Finally, our work relates to image generation method. Par-
073   ticularly, diffusion models - relying on SDE knowledge - have been crucial into generating high-
074   resolution samples (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020). Rombach et al. (2022) used the
075   latent space of a pre-trained VAE to even improve the quality and speed of training. Now, these tech-
076   niques are also used for video generation (Ho et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2024) making them closer to
077   longitudinal data. However, video and longitudinal image generation stay very different by essence,
078
      with regards to the frequency of images (very large in video, low and irregular in longitudinal),
      the necessary interpretability in the longitudinal case and more importantly, the needed number of
079
      training samples (huge for video generation, necessarily low for longitudinal data).
080
081
      O UR CONTRIBUTION
082
083   We propose here a new generative model for longitudinal data that uses a latent diffusion to model
084   the time dependency between the observations of a given sequence: each embedding of the obser-
085   vation of a given sequence is forced to lie on a diffusion trajectory in a VAE latent space.
086
      We demonstrate that our proposed method, named the Longitudinal Latent Diffusion Model
087
      (LLDM), excels at unconditionally generating fully synthetic trajectories with high performance,
088   as well as generating trajectories conditioned on one or more input observations. A key strength of
089   LLDM lies in the diversity of the sampled sequences, even when it is done conditionally, which is
090   made possible by leveraging the inherent stochastic nature of diffusion processes.

      2.1   VARIATIONAL INFERENCE AND A GEOMETRIC PERSPECTIVE ON VAE S
095
096   Let x = (xi )1≤i≤n ∈ (RD )n be a training dataset, i.i.d. from an unknown distribution p(x).
097   A variational autoencoder (VAE) is a generative model that aims at approximating p(x) by a
098   distribution on RD parametrized with a neural network: pθ with θ ∈ Θ. Ideally, the train-
099   ing of a VAE aims at minimizing the Kullback-Leibler divergence between p and pθ , that is
100   solving minθ∈Θ DKL (p∥pθ ). This is exactlyP equivalent as maximizing the joint log-likelihood:
101   maxθ∈Θ Ey∼p [log pθ (y)] ≈ log pθ (x) = n1   pθ (xi ).
102   A VAE relies on variational inference, assuming that the generation process involves latent variables
103   z ∈ Rd with d << D. These variables have an assumed prior (let it be here a standard normal) dis-
104   tribution z ∼ p(z) and then x ∼ pθ (x|z) is assumed to be a simple distribution, parametrized as a
105   neural network known as the decoder (let it be a diagonal Gaussian, N (µθ (z), diag(σθ2 (z))). Unfor-
106   tunately, despite the simplicity of these distributions, the joint log-likelihood pθ (x) (see Equation 1)
107   and the posterior pθ (z|x) are intractable. The latter is approximated using a variational distribution
      qϕ (z|x), parametrized as a neural network known as the encoder (here, N (µϕ (x), diag(σϕ2 (x)))).

184   3.2   P RE - TRAINING OF A LDM
185
186
      As done by Rombach et al. (2022), we first pre-train a vanilla VAE as a first-stage model. Here, we
      leave the longitudinal universe, and consider all the observations without any sequential dependency.
187                                        PN
      Our training set becomes a set of i=1 Ti observations. To be noted that, as discussed in 2.1, this
188
189

191   To then train the LDM per se, we only keep the last observations’ embeddings of each entity (N
192   vectors in total) as a training set. We train a diffusion model (see 2.2) in a similar way as in Rombach

197   3.3   T RAINING OF THE LVAE

199   Let us get back to the longitudinal framework, with a training set of N entities. We consider a VAE
200   that has the same architecture as the pre-trained VAE in 3.2 - to avoid confusion, we will call it the
201   Longitudinal Variational Autoencoder (LVAE).
202   To navigate between the latent embeddings and model the dependency structure between them, we
203   use the forward and the pre-learned backward diffusion processes. In a sense, we have pre-trained
204   trajectories (given by the LDM), and we want now the LVAE to structure the latent embeddings

209   the latent space: pj (·) := URiem (·; M).

      5     C ONCLUSION
525
526
      We present the Longitudinal Latent Diffusion Model (LLDM), a generative approach for high-
527   dimensional longitudinal data that combines VAE embeddings with a latent diffusion process, of-
528   fering remarkable flexibility. By decoding diffusion latent variables at specific time steps, LLDM
529   leverages latent space trajectories rather than merely blurring images like traditional diffusion mod-
530   els. LLDM generates diverse, realistic longitudinal trajectories both unconditionally and condition-
531   ally. The alignment of the real timeline with the granular diffusion timeline enables tasks such as
532   future prediction, oversampling, and imputation, with controlled stochasticity as a key feature. Its
533   efficiency allows for successful training on limited or incomplete datasets.
534   Future research could explore theoretical guarantees that demonstrate the convergence between the
535   modeled and true distributions. Additionally, the use of Riemannian diffusion models (Bortoli et al.,
536   2022; Huang et al., 2022) within the VAE latent space appears promising, as these models may
537   better align with the Riemannian geometry of the space, potentially improving generation quality.
538   However, the applicability of these methods in (relatively) high-dimensional settings remain to be
539   explored, as they have primarily been applied in 2D or 3D manifolds (e.g., torus, hypersphere).
