011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Understanding the agent’s learning process, particularly the factors that contribute
014            to its success or failure post-training, is crucial for comprehending the rationale
015            behind the agent’s decision-making process. Prior methods clarify the learning
016
               process by creating a structural causal model (SCM) or visually representing the
               distribution of value functions. Nevertheless, these approaches have constraints
017
               as they exclusively function in 2D-environments or with uncomplex transition
018
               dynamics. Understanding the agent’s learning process in complex environments
019            or tasks is more challenging. In this paper, we propose REVEAL-IT, a novel
020            framework for explaining the learning process of an agent in complex environments.
021            Initially, we visualize the policy structure and the agent’s learning process for
022            various training tasks. By visualizing these findings, we can understand how much
023            a particular training task or stage affects the agent’s performance in the test. Then,
024            a GNN-based explainer learns to highlight the most important section of the policy,
025            providing a more clear and robust explanation of the agent’s learning process.
026            The experiments demonstrate that explanations derived from this framework can
027
               effectively help optimize the training tasks, resulting in improved learning efficiency
               and final performance.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Reinforcement learning (RL) involves an agent acquiring the ability to make decisions within an
      environment to maximize the total reward obtained over a series of attempts. The recent achieve-
034
      ments in resolving decision-making challenges prove the effectiveness of this paradigm, e.g., video
035
      games (Lillicrap et al., 2016; Mnih et al., 2015), robotic controlling (Kaelbling, 1993). Despite many
036   remarkable achievements in recent decades, applying RL methods in the real world remains chal-
037   lenging. One of the main obstacles is that RL agents lack a fundamental understanding of the world
038   and must, therefore, learn from scratch through numerous trial-and-error interactions. Nevertheless,
039   the challenge of verifying and forecasting the actions of RL agents frequently impedes their use in
040   real-world scenarios.
041
      This difficulty is exacerbated when RL is paired with deep neural networks’ generalization and
042   symbolic power. Insufficient comprehension of the agent’s functioning impedes the ability to
043   intervene when needed or have confidence in the agent’s rational and secure behavior. Explainability
044   in RL refers to the ability to understand and interpret the decisions made by an RL agent. Explanations
045   reflect the knowledge learned by the agent, facilitating in-depth understanding, and they also allow
046   researchers to participate efficiently in the design and continual optimization of an algorithm (Miller,
047   2017). Recent work (Chen & Liu, 2023; Nielsen et al., 2008) in explaining RL models to gain insights
048   into the agent’s decision-making process. Some (Deshpande et al., 2020; Bellemare et al., 2017)
049   propose to understand the agent’s action by visualizing the distribution of the value function or state
050
      value, which is straightforward but limited by the environments, i.e., the distribution of the learned
      value function is easy to understand in 2D environments but unfeasible in 3D environments.
051
052   A line of recent work in explanation and causal RL (Pearl, 1989; Rubin, 1974; Schölkopf et al., 2021)
053   proposes to understand agent’s behavior based on a single moment or a single action. Intuitively
      speaking, a one-step explanation is acceptable, but is it the best way to understand the agent’s

      behavior? For example, Pearl (2009) proposes learning a causal model to understand what causes an
055   agent to succeed or fail in a given task. However, establishing a causal model with precise causal
056   assumptions in complex environments is challenging and inefficient (Martens et al., 2006; Sainani,
057   2018; Cui et al., 2020). On the other hand, this becomes more challenging in a long-horizon task as
058   the agent must do hundreds of steps before accomplishing the task. Hence, we debate the definition
059   of a good interpretability framework for providing explanations in RL. Firstly, to answer the
060   question, "Why can an agent succeed or fail in a task?". Indeed, we may deduce the pertinent
061   factors from the agent’s training process. Similar to how we may make a general assessment of a
062   person’s test performance based on their learning process, an agent also requires certain abilities
063
      to accomplish a specific task. The agent’s mastery of these abilities during the training process
      will directly impact its ability to effectively complete the task. Conversely, a proficient explanation
064
      can enhance the comprehension of the agent’s actions and facilitate the agent’s improvement of its
065
      performance (Schölkopf et al., 2021; Sontakke et al., 2020). Finally, an effective interpretability
066   framework necessitates providing intuitive and comprehensible explanations.

078
      an environment to accomplish complex tasks, while directly training the agent on these tasks is
079   challenging and inefficient. In practical application, we will devise sequences of pre-defined sub-tasks
080   (sub-task 1, sub-task 2,..., sub-task N) for training. In REVEAL-IT, we implement the RL agent in the
081   given environment, allowing it to explore and collect data. Subsequently, we train the controller (the
082   control policy πθ ), using the collected data. Then, we visualize the policy updates with a node-link
083   diagram. The visualization will depict the structure of the policy and any changes in the weights in
084   the training process. Then, we design a GNN-based explainer to highlight the important changes in
085   the policy updates and a GNN-based predictor to predict the learning progress the agent will achieve
086   in each sub-task. Note that, in this framework, we do not have any limitations on the RL algorithm.
087
088   Based on the discussion about a good interpretability mechanism, we propose understanding the
089   agent’s performance after training from a more fundamental perspective, i.e., the learning process
090   of the policy and the sequences of training tasks. By focusing on the policy and tasks, we may
091   effectively mitigate this problem. They possess a minimum of three evident benefits: (1) Both
092   the training tasks and the policy provide well-organized and generalizable information, allowing it
093
      to work in diverse environments. (2) The information presented in the policy is aligned with the
      agent’s capacities. The limitation of the policy rests in our ability to extract intuitive explanations
094
      from the high-dimensional information it provides to understand the actions and behaviors of the
095
      agent. (3) When compared to SCM or counterfactual methods, which can not deal with the big and
096   complex problems that can not be seen in the real world, using structured training tasks as the goal
097   can solve this problem. This can be further enhanced by extracting relevant information from the
098   policy. At this location, the agent’s learning progress can serve as a temporal framework to enhance
099   our comprehension of the agent’s behavior.
100
      Considering the preceding discussion, we propose a novel explanation framework, REVEAL-IT. This
101
      framework can visually represent updating the policy and the agent’s learning process. Additionally, it
102   can offer explanations for the training tasks. This paradigm facilitates a more intuitive comprehension
103   of how agents acquire knowledge in various tasks, identifies which tasks are more efficient in training
104   agents, and explains the reasons for their effectiveness. Simultaneously, this architecture can offer a
105   dependable foundation for developing and optimizing the task sequences. In REVEAL-IT, we first
106   adopt a node-link diagram to visualize the structure of the policy. This design enables humans to
107   directly see the updates made by the RL agent during training. By doing so, we can gain insights
      into the reasons behind the agent’s success or failure during testing. Simultaneously, we can also

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
121
122   Explainability for RL. Puiutta & Veith (2020); Heuillet et al. (2020) categorize the explainable
123   RL methods into two groups. The first group is “Post hoc”, which aims to provide explanations
124
      after the model execution, while the other group is “intrinsic approaches”, which inherently possess
      transparency. Many post hoc explanations, like the saliency map approach (Greydanus et al., 2017;
125
      Mott et al., 2019), are based on correlations. This means that conclusions drawn from correlations
126
      may not be accurate and do not answer questions about what caused what. However, these algorithms
127   may not be able to model complex behaviors (Puiutta & Veith, 2020) fully. Our method proposes a
128   new mechanism in the problem of explainability for RL from the perspective of the agent’s learning
129   process, i.e., it can help to understand how the agent is learning in a given task or environment.
130
      Explanation in real world. Deng et al. (2023) provides a general discussion on the challenges
131
      posed by reality in real-world scenarios. The non-stationary and constant evolution in the real world
132
      requires the RL algorithm to be more robust in handling these changes. Dulac-Arnold et al. (2020);
133   Paduraru et al. (2021) found nine significant problems with RL that make it hard to use in real life.
134   These problems are small sample sizes, unknown or long delays, complex inputs, safety concerns,
135   limited visibility, unclear or multiple reward functions, short delays, learning while offline, and the
136   need to be able to explain. In this paper, we will address the limitations of generating explanations
137   for RL in a high-dimensional environment from the perspective of the training tasks and the learning
138   progress.
139   GNN Explainability. Explainability is a major and necessary characteristic in the field of Graph
140   Neural Network (GNN) study. We primarily focus on GNN explainability strategies that prioritize
141   highlighting the important features of the input rather than offering explanations at the model level.
142   Gradient and feature-based approaches utilize gradients or feature values to evaluate the informa-
143   tiveness of various input features. For example, GRAD (Ying et al., 2019) and ATT (Velickovic
144   et al., 2017) assess the significance of edges by utilizing gradients and attention weights, respectively.
145   Perturbation-based techniques assess the significance of features by analyzing the performance of
146   GNNs when subjected to different input perturbations (Luo et al., 2020; Ying et al., 2019; Yuan et al.,
147
      2021). For example, GNNExplainer (Ying et al., 2019) uses a method to learn a mask that explains
      each prediction individually, whereas PGExplainer (Luo et al., 2020) uses a deep neural network to
148
      identify the most critical connections across many predictions. SubgraphX (Yuan et al., 2021) is a
149
      perturbation approach that utilizes the Shapley values of features obtained from the MCTS algorithm
150   to identify explanations. One recent method, RGExplainer (Shan et al., 2021), utilizes reinforcement
151   learning to generate an explanation subgraph centered on a chosen beginning point. MATE (Spinelli
152   et al., 2021), a novel meta-explanation technique that aims to enhance the interpretability of GNNs
153   during the training process. The objective is to enhance the comprehensibility of the GNN by itera-
154   tively training both the GNN itself and a baseline explanation to acquire an internal representation of
155   the GNN. MixupExplainer (Zhang et al., 2023), similar to our GNN-based explainer, seeks to address
156   the problem of distributional shifts in explanations by combining an explanatory subgraph with a
157   random subgraph. This leads to a clarification that is more closely in line with the graphs observed
158
      during the explanation phase.
159   Curriculum RL. Curriculum RL focuses on improving the efficiency and performance of RL agents
160   by organizing training tasks in a meaningful sequence, progressing from simpler to more complex
161   tasks. Previous works (Narvekar et al., 2020; Held et al., 2017) have explored the role of task
      sequencing in enhancing learning by adapting the difficulty or order of tasks based on the agent’s


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      capabilities. These approaches often rely on heuristics or predefined strategies to design task curricula.
163   In contrast, REVEAL-IT employs a data-driven approach, leveraging a GNN predictor to optimize
164   task sequences based on the agent’s real-time learning progress. By aligning task sequences with the
165   agent’s evolving policy, REVEAL-IT enhances learning efficiency and provides interpretable insights
166   into how tasks contribute to overall agent performance.

      Our framework comprises of two parts. One is the RL policy visualization module that shows the
      policy update in training and policy activation in the test. This module is based on fully connected
173
      neural networks’ well-established node-link diagram representation. The other is a GNN-based
174
      explainer, trained to analyze the visualized results in the first module and underly the critical update
175   in the training phase. We can further explore the relationship between different training tasks and the
176   contribution of a specific training task/phase to the final performance. Now, we provide more details
177   on these two parts:
178
      Reinforcement Learning. The problem of controlling an agent can be modeled as a Markov decision
179
      process (MDP), denoted by the tuple{S, A, P, R, µ0 , γ}, where S and A represent the set of state
180
      space and action space respectively, P : S × A × S → [0, 1] is the transition probability function
181   that yields the probability of transitioning into the next state st+1 after taking an action at at state
182   st , and R : S × A → R is the reward function that assigns the immediate reward for taking an
183   action at at state st . µ0 : S → [0, 1] is the probability distribution that specifies the generation of
184   the initial state, and γ ∈ [0, 1] denotes the discount factor. A is a mapping from states to actions:
185   π : S → A. Every episode starts with sampling an initial state s0 . At every timestep t the agent
186   produces an action basedP∞ on the current state: at = π(st ), A discounted sum of future rewards is
187   called a return: Rt = i=t γ i−t ri . The agent’s goal is to maximize its expected return Es0 [R0 | s0 ].
188   In REVEAL-IT, We can use a deep RL algorithm to train the agent (e.g., DQN (Mnih et al., 2015),
189
      A2C (Mnih et al., 2016), SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018b), PPO (Schulman et al., 2017), etc.). In this
      paper’s experiments, we use DQN and PPO as the foundational RL methods. Note that any online
190
      RL algorithm can be accepted.
191
192   Strucutral visualization of the policy. The visualization should meet the following goals: summarize
193   the unique properties of MLP networks in RL and reflect lessons learned from prior tasks. First, the
194   visualization should handle networks of large enough to solve complex tasks. Second, the visualization
195
      should depict the network architecture with a node-link diagram. Third, the visualization should
      allow users to easily experiment with the input-output process of the network, allowing them to judge
196
      the network’s robustness to translational variance, rotational variance, and ambiguous input. Fourth,
197
      the visualization should allow users to view details on individual nodes, such as the activation level,
198   the calculation being performed, the learned parameters (i.e., the node’s weights), and the numerical
199   inputs and outputs. We choose to visualize the policy with a node-link diagram, and the only issue
200   with a straightforward implementation of a node-link diagram is that large networks yield a dense
201   mass of edges between layers. We base the visualization model on Harley (2015), which can choose
202   to display some or all of the weights connected to a specific node, and record and correspond to the
203   changes in the value of a specific part of the weights during the training process.

      The learning objective of GNN predictor. The overall goal of the GNN explainer is to learn to
      optimize the sequences of training tasks to improve the learning efficiency of the RL agent. We take
262
      inspiration from the learning progress in earlier work on curriculum learning (Narvekar et al., 2020;
263
      Ao et al., 2023), i.e., a higher learning progress signifies that the agent can acquire more knowledge
264   regarding a specific training task, reflecting a higher learning efficiency. Therefore, in REVEAL-IT,
265   we train the GNN predictor to learn to predict how much improvement the RL agent can have in a
266   given task. Based on the discussion in Sec. 4.1, we denote the prediction task of our GNN explainer
267   as Φ: GO → P̂, where P̂ is the predicted improvement of the RL policy. Thus, in a given task n, we
268   train the GNN predictor to minimize the error between P̂ and the true learning progress P defined as:
269
                                 P(taskn , πt ) = R(taskn , πt ) − R(taskn , πt−1 ),                              (1)

272   Train GNN explainer with RL agent simultaneously. In REVEAL-IT, we separately train a GNN
273   explainer that helps to understand how an RL agent learns to complete tasks, i.e., the structural
274
      visualization of the policy allows us to observe which part of the policy weights is updated in one
      iteration, and the GNN explainer learns to highlight the most crucial updates related to the agent’s
275
      final success. One thing to note is that the training data used for the GNN explainer is based on
276
      the RL, which allows the GNN explainer to adapt to the learning process of the RL agent and can
277   provide guidance during the training. More specifically, as the RL agent acquires MDP data from
278   the interactions with the environment and learns from it, we can use any online RL algorithm to
279   update the RL policy. After each policy update, we can obtain a structured update visualization of
280   the policy by comparing the changes in the policy weight parameters and constructing the dataset
281   for the GNN explainer based on the updated information. We can regard the visualized policy as a
282   node-link graph, i.e., the updated weights can be viewed as the edges that connect with nodes in the
283   graph. Thus, the learning objective of the GNN explainer can be described as “learn to find which
284   part of the edges (updated weights) is more important for the success of the RL agent”. Now, we
285
      introduce more details of the GNN explainer to help the understanding.
286   Step 1. Understanding the visualized policy. We first train the GNN explainer on the learning data
287   collected from the RL. During the RL evaluation, the activated nodes in the policy will be tagged and
288   utilized as the ground truth for the GNN explanation.
289   Note that the active nodes during evaluation will vary as the RL policy training progresses. This
290   variability may raise issues regarding the stability of GNN’s training. However, this problem is
291   non-existent. This is because we require the GNN explainer to evaluate the significance of the
292   training job by considering the RL agent’s present level of learning progress. The active nodes in the
293   evaluation and the collected environment reward reflect the capabilities of the current policy. When
294   the policy is updated, especially when the agent’s ability is increased, the different activated nodes
295   and the increase in the environment reward can give the GNN predictor the information it needs.
296   This enables the predictor to acquire knowledge on predicting the agent’s improvement based on the
297
      current learning process. Consequently, the GNN predictor can more precisely assess the value of the
      current training task.
298

337   5.1   E XPERIMENTS S ETUP
338
339   Environments. We base our experiments on two types of benchmarks. The first one is ALFWorld
340   benchmark (Shridhar et al., 2021), a cross-modality simulation platform encompassing a wide range
341   of embodied household tasks. ALFWorld combines a textual environment with a visual environment
342   that the Ai2Thor simulator (Kolve et al., 2017) renders for each task. This textual part uses the
343   Planning Domain Definition Language (PDDL) (Ghallab et al., 1998) to turn each pixel observation
344
      from the simulator into a text-based observation that is equal to it. It then uses the TextWorld
      engine (Côté et al., 2018) to create an interactive environment. The tasks within the ALFWorld
345
      benchmark are categorized into six types: Pick &Place, Clean & Place, Heat & Place, Cool & Place,
346
      Look in Light, and Pick Two Objects & Place. Each task requires an agent to execute a series of
347   text-based actions, such as “go to safe 1”, “open safe 1”, or “heat egg 1 with microwave 1”, following
348   a predefined instruction. These actions involve navigating and interacting with the environment. The
349   other benchmark is evaluated in 6 OpenAI RL benchmark domains (Brockman et al., 2016), which
350   are commonly adopted in RL tasks.
351
      The complex tasks in ALFWorld. In ALFWorld, accomplishing a given task requires the agent
352
      to complete a sequential arrangement of sub-tasks step by step (as shown in Fig. 2). A task may
353   entail interactions with more than 10 items and necessitate over 30 steps for a human expert to
354   solve, thereby testing an agent’s ability in long-term planning, following instructions, and using
355   common knowledge. To facilitate a thorough comprehension, we have included an instance of each
356   task category in the appendix in Fig. 4. Using the original tasks directly to train an RL agent is
357   inefficient. Therefore, it is more suitable to use subtask training intuitively. However, this raises the
358   question of how to structure the subtask sequence, specifically if the subtask sequence should remain
359   consistent throughout the training process. What is the criteria for adjusting the subtask sequence?
360   The crucial factor is determining the optimal subtask sequence that aligns with the agent’s current
361
      training progress.
362   Baselines. The fundamental inquiry of REVEAL-IT is whether it can operate effectively in complex
363   tasks and environments, i.e., does it have the ability to enhance the training efficiency of RL signifi-
364   cantly? Can it be modified to suit various reinforcement learning techniques? Hence, we will focus
365   on answering the first question in the ALFWorld. The baseline methods include MiniGPT-4 (Zhu

      2023). They share state-of-the-art and competitive performance in ALFWorld and solely interact with

      2018a), DQN (Mnih et al., 2015) as the fundamental RL algorithms to assess if REVEAL-IT can

      B    T HE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN REVEAL-IT AND OTHER EXPLANATION
811
           METHODS
812
813
814
      In this section, We want to discuss the novelty of REVEAL-IT with other explanation methods in
      more detail, and we hope this discussion can help clarify the contribution of REVEAL-IT.
815
816   An interactive node-link visualization of convolutional neural networks. Note that utilizing
817   the visualization to show the structure of a CNN/GNN network or an MLP network is previously
818   discussed in Harley (2015). Nevertheless, the use of visualization is uncommon in RL, this is
819   because:
820
821
             • Gaining a visual representation of the policy structure does not enhance our comprehension
               of the possibilities of RL, and the motivation for visualizing CNNs is essentially distinct from
822
               this, i.e., we lack knowledge regarding the specific capabilities of the agent that correspond
823
               to a particular section of the policy. REVEAL-IT confirmed the accuracy of this mapping
824            relationship for the first time (refer to Fig. 2).
825
826          • The process of updating policies necessitates millions of revisions. Without a proficient
827
               interpreter, meaningful conclusions cannot be derived from the visualization outcomes. The
               GNN explanation plays a crucial part in REVEAL-IT.
828
829
      The difference between Gnnexplainer/PGExplainer: Generating explanations for graph neural
830   networks”. The primary objective behind the GNN explainer in REVEAL-IT is to identify the
831   important policy updates that occur during the agent’s learning process. This allows us to correlate
832   these updates with the agent’s abilities, thereby explaining how the agent learns in complex tasks.
833   Simultaneously, the GNN explainer can assist us in determining if the predictor comprehends the
834   inherent connection between the agent’s abilities and the learning process, hence assessing the
835   credibility of the task’s significance. This differs radically from the conventional GNN explainer
836   employed for identifying important edges.
837   How does REVEAL-IT differ from other interpretable RL algorithms? It is important to
838   understand that REVEAL-IT stands apart from conventional interpretable RL algorithms in: (1)
839   REVEAL-IT does not necessitate the use of artificially constructed causal structures. (2) REVEAL-IT
840   does not require additional knowledge of SCM. These two points are the most significant limitations of
841   classical explanation RL in complex and real environments. To overcome this flaw and restriction, we
842   employ a ”task-agent policy-GNN explainer” architecture, enabling RL use in complex environments
843   and task scenarios. This architecture also comprehensively explains how RL agents learn in these
844
      tasks, explaining why an agent can succeed or fail after training.

      D.1   T HE LOSS FUNCTION FOR GNN EXPLAINER
867

      In REVEAL-IT, We use the loss in the PGEexplainer (Luo et al., 2020) to train the GNN explainer,
869   and we use an MSE loss for the GNN predicter. On the other hand, in the traditional GNN explainer
870   method, the learning objectives of the evaluator GNN and the explainer are often consistent, i.e.,
871   maximizing the mutual information between the label and the selected subgraph, and we can express
872   this process as:

                                 Gs

877
      where GS denotes the masked subgraph, the GNN explainer optimizes this function locally by training
878
      an NN to provide a customized explanation for each instance. However, REVEAL-IT optimizes this
879   by adopting an NN to learn the crucial edges on multiple instances (each evaluation results of RL).
880
881
882   D.2   GNN EXPLAINER HIGHLIGHT THE IMPORTANT UPDATE
883
      To extract the explanation subgraph GS , we first compute the importance weights on edges. A
884
      threshold is used to remove low-weight edges and identify the explanation subgraph GS . The ground
885
      truth explanations of all datasets are connected subgraphs. Therefore, we identify the explanation
886   as the connected component containing the explained node in GS. We identify the explanation for
887   the generated graph classification by the maximum connected component of GS . For all basic RL
888   algorithms, we perform a search to find the maximum threshold such that the explanation is at least
889   of size KM . When multiple edges have tied importance weights, all of them are included in the
890   explanation.
891
892
      D.3   H OW TO ENSURE THE TRAINING PROCESS OF GNN+RL IS STABLE ?
893
894   The fundamental principle of the GNN+RL training scheme is mutual learning, where the GNN
895   predicts the value of the training task for the RL agent based on the RL training process, and the GNN
896   predictor influences the training task of the RL agent. During the initial stages of training, the GNN
897   predictor exhibits instability due to inadequate data. We employ a random sampling technique to
898   select training tasks from the complete set. Once RL has accumulated a specific quantity of data, we
899   systematically enhance the significance of the GNN predictor in choosing training tasks. Providing a
900   precise theoretical guarantee for this procedure is challenging, yet it is a commonly used approach in
901
      curriculum RL to choose training tasks, e.g., Ao et al. (2023); Held et al. (2017).
902
903   D.4   H OW DOES GNN EXPLAINER MAP THE SKILLS OF THE RL AGENT ?
904
905   In REVEAL-IT, the central concern revolves around the GNN explainer’s capacity to comprehend
906   the agent’s learning prcess and ascertain whether the agent has achieved the certain abilities in
907   accomplishing the task. The GNN explainer is designed to identify and emphasize important updates
908   in the policy. Suppose the nodes associated with these highlighted updates correspond to the nodes
909
      engaged by the agent during the evaluation. In that case, it can be inferred that the explanation is
      cognizant of the specific sections of the policy that align with the agent’s essential abilities. Hence,
910
      we ascertain the ratio of nodes identified by the GNN explainer and nodes stimulated by the agent in
911
      successful tasks and report it in Fig. 5. In this series of comparisons, we incorporated a pre-trained
912   Graph Neural Network (GNN) explanation that utilizes data from RL training data from different
913   sequences of training tasks in the same environment. An intriguing observation is that we discovered
914   that the efficacy of the GNN-explainer, when trained on various tasks, was inferior than that of the
915   GNN-explainer taught using a reinforcement learning agent. This discrepancy may arise from the
916   varying demands that different tasks place on the training process of reinforcement learning (RL)
917   agents. Additionally, pre-trained graph neural networks (GNNs) may struggle to comprehend the
      ongoing RL training progress accurately due to the incorporation of a priori bias.
