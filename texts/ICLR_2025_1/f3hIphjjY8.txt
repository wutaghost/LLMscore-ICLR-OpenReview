009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The recent rise of distributed diffusion models has been driven by the explosive
012            growth of data and the increasing demand for data generation. However, dis-
013            tributed diffusion models face unique challenges in resource-constrained environ-
014            ments. Existing approaches lack theoretical support, particularly with respect to
015            generation error in such settings. In this paper, we are the first to derive the gen-
016            eration error bound for distributed diffusion models with arbitrary pruning, not
017            assuming perfect score approximation. By analyzing the convergence of the score
018            estimation model trained with arbitrary pruning in a distributed manner, we high-
019
               light the impact of complex factors such as model evolution dynamics and arbi-
               trary pruning on the generation performance. This theoretical generation error
020
               bound is linear in the data dimension d, aligning with state-of-the-art results in the
021
               single-worker paradigm.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Recently, distributed diffusion models have gained significant attention due to the explosive growth
      of data and the growing interest in data generation (Vora et al., 2024; de Goede et al., 2024). Specif-
028
      ically, in federated settings, diffusion models are trained collaboratively across multiple workers
029
      without the need to share personal sensitive data, such as images and audio, directly. This dis-
030   tributed approach enables large-scale data generation while avoiding the privacy risks and practical
031   costs of centralizing data (Tun et al., 2023).
032
033
      In real-world scenarios, workers typically possess limited computational and communication re-
      sources, which significantly hinder the performance (Zhang et al., 2021). When training a
034
      parametrized neural network in the reverse process of diffusion models, it would be unaffordable
035
      for resource-constrained workers to operate model updates. Some efforts have been made to ad-
036   dress this challenge. For example, Li et al. (2024) propose DistriFusion, a method that divides the
037   model input into multiple patches, each assigned to a GPU. This approach tackles the high computa-
038   tional costs involved in generating high-resolution images with diffusion models. Additionally, Lai
039   et al. (2024) introduce an on-demand quantized energy-efficient distributed approach for training
040   diffusion-based models in mobile edge networks. Despite the efforts made by these studies, they
041   primarily focus on improving empirical performance, leaving the theoretical behavior as an open
042   problem.
043   The theoretical lack in the generation performance of distributed diffusion models is driven by the
044   increased complexity in training dynamics resulting from limited resources. The generation error
045   bound of the diffusion model heavily depends on the loss value of the neural network trained in the
046   reverse process (Benton et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023). And the coupling between the loss and the
047   gradient often reflects the convergence rate of the neural network (Zhou et al., 2024). As a result,
048   accurately bounding the generation error requires describing the convergence rate of the parame-
049   terized neural network trained in the reverse process. However, resource limitations in distributed
050
      systems may lead to insufficient training or incomplete transmission of local models (Zhou et al.,
      2024; Qiao et al., 2023), which exacerbates global error accumulation and complicates the analysis
051
      of convergence rates during the reverse process.
052
053   In this paper, we provide formal theoretical support for distributed diffusion models in resource-
      constrained scenarios. To address the performance degradation caused by resource constraints, we


                                                         1

054
      consider introducing pruning operations when training score estimation model in a distributed man-
055   ner during the reverse process, and using coordinate-aware model aggregation (Zhou et al., 2024)
056   to reduce global error accumulation. To obtain the convergence rate during the reverse process, we
057   utilize the smoothness assumption to measure the inconsistency between the local and global gradi-
058   ents. We also implement a refined treatment of pruning errors and utilize the relationship between
059   iterative model updates to explore their cumulative entanglement. By analyzing the convergence of
060   the score estimation model and exploring the error between local and global training losses, we re-
061   veal the impact of complex factors such as the number of communication rounds and the number of
062   workers on the local score estimation error. Using this actual error, rather than the assumed constant
063
      error in the single-worker paradigm (Benton et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; 2022), we derive the
      generation error bound for distributed learning diffusion models in resource-constrained scenarios.
064
      Specifically, our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
065
066   вҖў To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to incorporate the distributed learning dynamic
067     of the score estimation model during the reverse process into the analysis of the final genera-
068     tion error. We theoretically assess the discrepancy between the generated sample distribution and
069
        the actual distribution for each worker using KL divergence. This generation error bound aligns
        with the best-known results in the single-worker paradigm (Benton et al., 2024), exhibiting a linear
070
        dependence on the data dimension d. Notably, our framework can be seamlessly integrated with
071
        the theoretical error bounds of any diffusion model based on the single-worker paradigm under
072     the perfect fractional approximation assumption. This integration ensures that the theoretical error
073     bounds of similar distributed training architectures progress in tandem with advancements in the
074     theoretical error bounds of the single-worker paradigm.
075   вҖў We also derive convergence bounds for distributed learning of the score estimation model under
076     arbitrary pruning, without relying on the bounded gradient assumption. It shows that the average
077     gradient norm can converge at a rate of O( вҲҡО“вҲ—1SQ ), showing the critical roles of the number of
078     local training steps S and the minimum parallel training degree О“вҲ— in enhancing convergence
079     efficiency.

082   2   R ELATED W ORK
083
084   In recent research, diffusion models (Song et al., 2020) have garnered widespread attention due
085   to their remarkable achievements across multiple fields, including computer vision (Harvey et al.,
086   2022), natural language processing (Li et al., 2022), temporal data modeling (Tashiro et al., 2021),
087
      and multi-modal learning (Ramesh et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022). Particularly, some studies highlight
      that diffusion models not only generate high-quality data but also surpass traditional Generative
088
      Adversarial Networks in terms of stability and generation efficiency (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021).
089
090   Recent works have extensively explored the theoretical performance of diffusion models. This pro-
091   vides a robust theoretical foundation for refining the model architecture and optimizing the training
092   process. Initial studies on the convergence of diffusion models often requires restrictive assumptions
093
      about the data distribution, such as adherence to a log-Sobolev inequality (Yang & Wibisono, 2022),
      or results in bounds that are either non-quantitative (Pidstrigach, 2022) or exponential (Block et al.,
094
      2020) with respect to the problem parameters. Subsequent research has made significant improve-
095
      ments. For instance, some studies have achieved polynomial convergence rates for diffusion models
096   without restrictive assumptions on the data distribution. Specifically, Chen et al. (2022) obtain poly-
097   nomial error bounds in total variation (TV) distance, assuming that the score function is Lipschitz.
098   They employ the Girsanov change of measure framework to analyze the discrepancy between the
099   true and approximate reverse processes. Further advances are made by the work (Chen et al., 2023),
100   which develop the Girsanov methodology further and introduce two important theorems: Theorem
101   2.1 shows that the KL divergence is linear in the data dimension but requires that вҲҮ log qt be Lips-
102   chitz; Theorem 2.2 demonstrates that, under an early-stopping setting and with any data distribution
103   having a finite second moment, the error is quadratic in the data dimension. Moreover, Benton et al.
104
      (2024) further improve the results under the early-stopping setting described in the work (Chen et al.,
      2023), achieving the current state-of-the-art error bound that is linear in the data dimension without
105
      smoothness assumptions on the data distribution.
106
107   However, most current research on diffusion models focuses on a single worker, primarily enhancing
      empirical performance or exploring theoretical attributes. With the advent of the big data era, dis-


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      tributed training (Qiao et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024) is emerging as a new trend, offering potential
109   solutions to the scalability challenges posed by increasing data volumes. In response, DistriFusion
110   is introduced in the work (Li et al., 2024), a method designed to run diffusion models across multiple
111   devices in parallel, significantly reducing the latency associated with generating individual samples
112   without compromising the quality of the generated images. Despite its practical effectiveness, the
113   theoretical performance of DistriFusion (Li et al., 2024) is not explored. Additionally, Zhao et al.
114   (2023) proposes FedDDA, a data augmentation-based federated learning architecture that utilizes
115   diffusion models to generate data conforming to the global class distribution, thereby alleviating the
116   non-IID data problem. However, theoretical exploration of this approach is also lacking.
117   In summary, there is a theoretical gap in collaboratively training diffusion models with resource
118   constrains. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to derive both convergence rate of the
119   collaboratively trained score estimation model and error evaluation of locally generated samples.

475   6       C ONCLUSION
476
477   In this paper, we provide the first generation error bound for distributed diffusion models, without
478   assuming perfect score approximation. This theoretical bound is linear in the data dimension d,
479   aligning with state-of-the-art results from the single-worker paradigm. Furthermore, it theoretically
480   demonstrates how distributed training dynamics affect generation performance.
481   Our work enhances theoretical understanding of distributed diffusion models, it also reveals some
482   interesting phenomena. For example, as discussed in Remark 1, suitable Q helps tighten the bound
483   on O(Пө2 ). This depends on the specific scenario, i.e., the target loss function of all workers is the
484   same and the error caused by random sampling is negligible. This also shows that the diffusion
485   model training has low tolerance for errors.

1060   G.1      E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
1061
       We conduct experiments using the Cifar-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011),
1062   and Fashion-MNIST (Xiao et al., 2017) datasets. To simulate a distributed learning scenario, we
1063   partition the training data among 10 workers. As described in Section 3.1, DDPM (Ho et al.,
1064   2020) can be viewed as a special case of our work, so we consider its distributed version (known as
1065   FedDM (Vora et al., 2024)) under resource-constrained conditions. In the experiments, we mainly
1066   consider two pruning techniques: Random Pruning (R) and Top-k Pruning (T) based on model
1067   weight. In particular, in order to explore the heterogeneity of pruning policy caused by resource dif-
1068   ferences among workers, we set for different pruning levels named F (Full), L (Large), M (Medium)
1069   and S (Small):
1070
                вҖў F: All workers with full model;
1071
1072            вҖў L: 80% workers with full model, and 20% workers with 75% model parameters;
1073            вҖў M: 60% workers with full model, 20% workers with 80% model parameters, and 20%
1074              workers with 75% model parameters;
1075            вҖў S: 60% workers with full model, and 40% workers with 75% model parameters.
1076
1077   We utilize multiple metrics to evaluate the performance of distributed training diffusion models with
1078   different pruning levels: Training loss is used to assess the convergence for distributed learning of
1079   score estimation. Additionally, the Inception Score (IS) and FreМҒchet Inception Distance (FID) are
       employed to evaluate the quality of data generation.
