011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Semi-supervised community detection methods are widely used for identifying
014            specific communities due to the label scarcity. Existing semi-supervised com-
015            munity detection methods typically involve two learning stages i.e., learning in
016
               both initial identification and subsequent adjustment, which often starts from an
               unreasonable community core candidate. Moreover, these methods encounter
017
               scalability issues because they depend on reinforcement learning and generative
018
               adversarial networks, leading to higher computational costs and restricting the
019            selection of candidates. To address these limitations, we draw a parallel between
020            crystallization kinetics and community detection to integrate the spontaneity of the
021            annealing process into community detection. Specifically, we liken community
022            detection to identifying a crystal subgrain (core) that expands into a complete
023            grain (community) through a process similar to annealing. Based on this finding,
024            we propose CLique ANNealing (CLANN), which applies kinetics concepts to
025            community detection by integrating these principles into the optimization process
026            to strengthen the consistency of the community core. Subsequently, a learning-free
027
               Transitive Annealer was employed to refine the first-stage candidates by merging
               neighboring cliques and repositioning the community core, enabling a spontaneous
028
               growth process that enhances scalability. Extensive experiments on diverse com-
029
               munity detection datasets demonstrate that CLANN outperforms state-of-the-art
030            methods across multiple real-world datasets, showcasing its exceptional efficacy
031            and efficiency in community detection.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Community detection aims to distinguish node groups with closer inner connections. (defined by
037   concrete contexts) (Jeong et al., 2021; Li et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2018; Abbe, 2023) Unsupervised
038   methods eliminate the need for costly data labeling and are widely utilized due to the label scarcity in
039   community detection (Holland et al., 1983; Amini et al., 2013; de Lange et al., 2014). While these
040   methods have demonstrated strong performance and practicality, they often struggle to accurately
041   identify specific communities with distinct semantic meanings. For instance, in a social network with
042   100 user communities, only 10 of which are fraudulent, unsupervised methods might identify all 100
043   communities but typically struggle to differentiate which ones are fraudulent. This is mainly because
044
      they are typically designed based on general structural information rather than the specific inherent
      features of the targeted communities.
045
046   To improve the detection of communities with semantic meaning, some semi-supervised methods
047   have been introduced that primarily utilize a two-stage approach (Wu et al., 2022; Bakshi et al., 2018).
048   They identify potential community centers first, then expand these centers into final communities.
049   Although semi-supervised methods are intuitive, they still have the following limitations:
050   Community Core Inconsistency: Prevalent growth-based methods scan all vertices (or their k-ego
051   networks) and calculate embedding space distances to the labeled communities to select optimal
052   community cores. However, a single node feature is insufficient to represent structural information.
053   Moreover, the k-hop ego network frequently includes vertices positioned outside of any community.
      Instead, as shown in App. A, clique (where all nodes are connected and cannot be expanded by adding

      tively simulate the seed-growth mechanism, allowing subgrains to merge with others and grow into
      fully crystallized grains, as illustrated in Fig. 1 (a). The annealing seeds are consistently subgrains,

078   laws, we further proposed CLique ANNealing (CLANN), which consists of two main components:
079   the Nucleus Proposer and the Transitive Annealer. As illustrated in Fig. 1 (b), we first identify a
080   clique as community core and gradually merges neighbor cliques to form the final community. The
081   analogy between crystallization and community formation is illustrated in Fig. 1 (c).
082
      Specifically, we first only optimize a single graph encoder to inherently analogize community
083
      formation by integrating four crystallization principles (Stability, Cohesion, Growth, and Status) into
084   the optimization to mitigate Community Core Inconsistency. Instead of evaluating all individual
085   nodes, our Nucleus Proposer selects the most prospective cliques, thus speeding up the selection
086   process. To address the Inferior Growth Scalability, we further propose a learning-free Transitive
087   Annealer that directly leverages the module trained in the Nucleus Proposer to guide the annealing
088   of candidates into communities. This method circumvents the convergence challenges and high
089   computational costs typically associated with reinforcement learning or GANs. Given the clique’s
090   high homophily scores (as shown in App. A), we choose the clique as the core motif in this work;
091   other motifs may also be effective depending on the dataset. In summary, our contributions can be
092
      summarized as follows:
093
             • We introduce a novel model, CLANN, that leverages the annealing process to detect commu-
094            nities providing a new physics-grounded perspective for community detection by studying
095            the subgrain growth process.
096
             • We introduce two key components in CLANN: Nucleus Proposer, which uses crystallization
097
               principles and cliques to learn graph representations and identify community cores, and
098
               Transitive Annealer, which ensures spontaneous growth guided by the Nucleus Proposer.
099
100
             • Empirical evaluations on real-world datasets consistently show that CLANN outperforms
               state-of-the-art methods by a significant margin, demonstrating its effectiveness and effi-
101
               ciency across diverse network analysis scenarios.

      2.1   OVERLAPPING C OMMUNITY D ETECTION WITH U N /S EMI - SUPERVISED M ETHODS
109
110   Unsupervised Methods. Unsupervised methods are particularly valuable for exploratory data
111   analysis, especially in scenarios where no supervision information is accessible. NOCD (Shchur
112   & Günnemann, 2019) uses a generative model for inferring community affiliations. Community-
113   GAN (Jia et al., 2019) applies GANs to generate motifs and optimize vertex embeddings, representing
114   membership strength. ACNE (Chen et al., 2021) employs a perception-based walking strategy and
115   a discriminator to jointly map node and community embeddings. DFuzzy (Bhatia & Rani, 2018)
116
      uses a stacked sparse autoencoder to evolve overlapping and disjoint communities via modularity.
      BigClam (Yang & Leskovec, 2013) identifies densely connected overlaps to improve accuracy and
117
      scalability. ComE (Cavallari et al., 2017) enhances detection through a synergistic loop between
118
      community and node embeddings. vGraph (Sun et al., 2019) utilizes a mixture model to represent
119   nodes as combinations of communities.
120
121   Semi-supervised Methods. In contrast to unsupervised learning methods, which impose strict
122
      limitations on pinpointing specific types of communities, semi-supervised methods can effectively
      utilize labels from previous community members making them more practical in identifying specific
123
      community types. BigClam-A (Bakshi et al., 2018) stands for BigClam-Assisted with graphs modified
124
      by adding extra edges between nodes in the same community. SEAL (Zhang et al., 2020) generates
125   seed-aware communities using a Graph Pointer Network with incremental updates (iGPN). DGL-
126   FRM (Mehta et al., 2019) captures community membership strength and sparse node. LGVG (Sarkar
127   et al., 2020) is designed to learn multi-layered and gamma-distributed embeddings, allowing it to
128   detect communities at both fine-grained and coarse-grained levels. Bespoke (Bakshi et al., 2018)
129   leverages community membership information and node metadata to identify unique patterns in
130   communities beyond traditional structures. CLARE (Wu et al., 2022) builds a locator to find the
131   community seeds and uses a rewriter to modify the candidates. Although the aforementioned
132   semi-supervised methods perform well, many of them focus heavily on architectural design while
133
      underutilizing the inherent graph structures. On the contrary, CLANN can fully exploit the insights
      of community positioning and formation mechanisms from inherent motifs.
134
135
136   2.2   C LIQUE - BASED M ETHODS
137
      A clique is a complete subgraph, naturally capturing densely connected subgraphs. Clique-based
138
      methods are classified into K-clique-based and maximum-clique-based. K-clique methods (e.g.,
139   CPM (Palla et al., 2005), SCP (Kumpula et al., 2008), ECPM (Maity & Rath, 2014), WCPM (Zhang
140   et al., 2017), LOC (Ma & Fan, 2019)) find and merge adjacent K-cliques into communities, while
141   maximum-clique methods (e.g., EA/G (Zhang et al., 2005), MaxCliqueDyn (Konc & Janezic, 2007),
142   GVG-Mine (Lee et al., 2012), ACENV (Cheng et al., 2018), PECO (Svendsen et al., 2015)) select
143   cliques with the largest number of nodes as initial communities. Though these approaches utilize
144   substructures, they often struggle to represent lower-dimensional embeddings while preserving
145   structural complexity (Fan et al., 2020; Bo et al., 2020; Luo et al., 2020; Cheng et al., 2021). In
146   contrast, CLANN generates insightful embeddings while maintaining formation mechanisms.

150   Problem Definition: Give a graph G = (V, E, X ), where the V represent the node set, E represents
151   the edge set and X represents the node feature. The expert-labeled training communities are denoted
152   as C = {C1 , ..., CN } where N is the number of expert-labeled communities. The objective in semi-
153   supervised community detection, is formulated as given a small set of expert-labeled communities
154   C train = {C1 , ..., Cm } as training data, where m (m ≪ N ), to predict N -m communities C pred
155   from G. The predicted communities should be consistent with all rest labeled communities (test
156   communities), C test = C \ C train .
157   Pipeline: As shown in Fig. 2, Nucleus Proposer first identifies cliques with embeddings similar to
158   the training community embeddings as potential core candidates (represented by dashed circles).
159   To get the final integral structure, the Transitive Annealer will expand candidates into communities.
160   Considering community core inherently contains less information than an integral community,
161   Nucleus Propose might inevitably locate the sub-optimal start point. The Nucleus Transition module
      is thus proposed to dynamically relocate the initial candidates to other better cliques.

182   4.1         L INKING C RYSTALLIZATION TO C OMMUNITY D ETECTION
183
184   Many physics methods (Pang & Li, 2013; Greydanus et al., 2019; Cranmer et al., 2020) minimize a
185   global energy function to penalize node assignments that do not correspond to natural communities.
186   Since crystallization kinetics inherently reflect growth mechanisms where a community core can
187   spontaneously expand into a full community without the need for learning, we apply these principles
188
      to simulate community growth and develop a more effective graph encoder, rather than relying
      on previous energy function. These principles can be clearly illustrated through four specific
189
      characteristics of communities, see more details in App. B.
190
191   Community Stability. Community stability correlates with size and outliers. Because members
192   heighten interaction complexity and increase the potential for conflict. Outliers who deviate signifi-
193   cantly from the norm can further disrupt stable dynamics. This is analogous to larger crystals with
194
      defects having higher stored energy, depicted in Fig. 3(a).
195   Higher Cohesion. Higher member similarity promises better community cohesion. Similar traits
196   among members lead to harmonious interactions and stronger unity, akin to subgrains with the same
197   crystallographic orientations merging in crystallization, as illustrated in Fig. 3(b).
198   Spontaneous Growth. Community growth consumes resources, as expanding a community involves
199   resource expenditure to integrate infrastructure and complexity management. In crystallization,
200   subgrains must overcome an Interface Energy Barrier to merge, with the total energy surpassing that
201   of the larger, merged grain, as shown in Fig. 3(c).
202
      Equilibrium Status. Larger and outlier-rich communities risk overgrowth and instability. Conversely,
203
      smaller and more cohesive communities have great potential for further growth (undergrown). This
204   mirrors how a crystal’s size and defect levels decide its status in Fig. 3(d).

531
532
      In this paper, we propose a novel community detection method CLique ANNealing (CLANN) inspired
533
      by the linking of the annealing process and community detection. Inconsistency in community cores
534   and poor scalability are key challenges in semi-supervised community detection. To address these
535   issues, CLANN introduces two main components: the Nucleus Proposer and the Transitive Annealer.
536   The Nucleus Proposer enhances consistency between core candidates and actual community cores
537   by incorporating crystallization kinetics into clique-based optimization. Meanwhile, the Transitive
538   Annealer employs a learning-free growth process to boost scalability. Comprehensive evaluations
539   highlight CLANN’s superior performance, while also shedding light on the underlying mechanisms.
      Additionally, adaptability analysis underscores the model’s applicability to real-world scenarios.

736   that of a sub-clique with size k − 1, consider the modularity formula in detail. When you remove
737
      a node from a k-clique to form a (k − 1)-clique, you also remove all the edges connected to that
      node. This reduces the number of positive terms in the  modularity  summation. Specifically, for
738                                                                     ki ·kj
739   each node pair (i, j) within the original clique, the term ai,j − 2m     contributes positively to the
740   modularity when i and j are connected (which is always true in a clique). By removing a node from
741   the community, you reduce the number of such positive contributions, thereby lowering the overall
742   modularity. Therefore, the modularity of the original k-clique is always greater than that of any
743   (k − 1)-clique derived from it.
744   Interface-Based Loss: When expanding a community,         the
                                                                    new additions must bring more benefit
745                                                        ki ·kj
      than the loss incurred. Referring to the term ai,j − 2m , the first term ai,j is positive if there is

      existing community (resulting in more positive ai,j values), it can potentially overcome the negative
                              ki ·kj

      B             E XPLANATION OF C RYSTALLIZATION K INETICS P RINCIPLES
771
772
      If we compare a network to a crystal lattice structure, nodes signify atoms, and edges depict atomic
773
      bonds. Within an amorphous lattice structure, if we select and scrutinize a random area, we can
774   find defects and deformations in it. This scenario mirrors constructing a subgraph from randomly
775   chosen connected nodes, deemed a community. The so-called ‘defects’ or ‘deformations’ essentially
776   represent mis-included or mis-excluded nodes within the community.
777
      In the process of crystallization, annealing is a heat treatment that mitigates dislocations, repositions
778
      them into a configuration with lower energy, and promotes the formation of better grain boundaries.
779
      According to Rios et al. (2005), this process practically eradicates all dislocations prompted by
780   deformation through the migration of grain boundaries. In the context of community detection, our
781   proposed CLANN model emulates this annealing process. ‘Defects’ and ‘deformations’ within the
782   network undergo ‘annealing’ to form well-structured communities by correctly including and exclud-
783   ing nodes. This process mirrors the organic adjustment of a crystal’s structure, where misalignments
784   are rectified, and a more coherent and unified formation emerges.
785
786                   • Stored Energy is Determined by the Grain Size and Defect Concentration: In a crys-
787                     talline structure, the material itself and those defects (such as dislocations, vacancies, and
788
                        grain boundaries) store a significant portion of the energy. This energy is termed ‘stored
                        energy’. The size of the crystal grain and the concentration of these defects, therefore,
789
                        largely influence the stored energy in the system. Also, the larger the grain, the higher the
790
                        probability of containing defects, which leads to an increase in stored energy.
791
792
                      • Crystallographic Directions Should be Consistent: A well-crystallized crystal signifies
                        that a material has a regular, repeating arrangement of atoms with minimal defects. As
793
                        shown in Fig. 3, one characteristic of such a material is the consistency in crystallographic
794
                        directions across grains, allowing for more uniform physical properties. On the other hand,
795                     if two crystal subgrains share the same crystallographic direction, they are very likely to
796                     form a more integrated grain. For instance, these consistent crystallographic directions can
797                     influence how the crystal behaves under stress or how it conducts heat or electricity.
798
                      • Stored Energy and Boundary Interface Energy Barrier Decide the Crystal Growth:
799                     Crystal growth depends on minimizing the material’s total energy, which comprises the
800                     energy within the crystal itself and the energy at the interface or grain boundary. The driving
801                     force for the growth of a crystal subgrain is the reduction of energy density among the whole
802                     grain. If the energy barrier at the grain boundary is high, it might impede the grain’s growth,
803                     even if there’s a significant reduction in the energy within the crystal itself. Therefore, both
804                     the crystal’s energy and the energy at the interface play a crucial role in determining whether
805                     the crystal will continue to grow.
806                   • Stored Energy and Defect Concentration Decide the Integrity: For a selected area, if
807                     the area is full of well-crystallized crystals, we will get a larger average grain size and
808                     smaller defect concentration. Otherwise, there will be a smaller grain size and larger defect
809                     concentration, as there are more grain boundaries in this area. The comparison is shown in
                        Fig. 3.

      C    C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
811
812
      Let N represent the number of nodes and M the specified number of cores. The entire model consists
813   of three main components:
814
815   Preliminary Core Filter: This component identifies appropriate community center nodes using a
816
      simple Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) model. The training data is constructed based on betweenness
      centrality, but it is computed only on a few small sub-graphs, making the computation trivial and
817
      negligible compared to other parts of the model. Calculating the scores for all nodes to determine
818
      their suitability as community centers has a time complexity of N .
819
820   Nucleus Proposer: The primary time complexity of the Nucleus Proposer arises from clique
821   preparation. Since the Nucleus Proposer only prepares cliques for specific nodes selected by the Core
822
      Filter, we thus only need to find the max cliques for these M selected nodes. For each node, we
      need to find its neighbor nodes (O(N )) and check all edges among them (O(N 2 )). The worst-case
823
      complexity is then O(M · (N + N 2 )), which simplifies to O(N 2 ).
824
825   Transitive Annealer: As detailed in Algo. 2, each iteration of the Transitive Annealer involves 4
826   main steps (blue comments):
827
            1. Check Expandability: Check the integrity score of the current state, thus O(1).
828
829
            2. Collect Extendable Nodes: Check all extendable neighboring nodes, in a worst-case O(N ).
830         3. Check Nucleus Transition: Check the transition condition, thus O(1).
831         4. Check Interface Requirement: Similar to Step 2, in a worst-case O(N ).
832
833   If the maximum number of iterations is C, the total time complexity for Transitive Annealer is:
834   C(O(N ) + O(N )) → O(N ). The total time complexity of CLANN is thus O(N ) + O(N ) +
835
      O(N 2 ) → O(N 2 ).
836   For runtime and convergence analysis, as illustrated in Fig. 4 and 10, CLANN’s total runtime is
837   better than most compared methods. The convergence behavior of the Transitive Annealer is shown
838   in Table 6. In most cases, the Transitive Annealer converges within 3 steps. The case analysis in
839   the appended PDF shows CLANN’s behaviors under 5 settings with different community sizes, and
840   CLANN will converge in at most 3 steps for all of them, which further justifies CLANN’s scalability.
