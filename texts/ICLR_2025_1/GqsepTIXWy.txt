009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, medical image synthesis gains more and more popularity, along with the
012            rapid development of generative models. Medical image synthesis aims to gen-
013            erate an unacquired image modality, often from other observed data modalities.
014            Synthesized images can be used for clinical diagnostic assistance, data augmenta-
015            tion for model training and validation or image quality improving. In the mean-
016            while, the flow-based models are among the successful generative models for the
017            ability of generating realistic and high-quality synthetic images. However, most
018
               flow-based models require to calculate flow ordinary different equation (ODE)
               evolution steps in synthesis process, for which the performances are significantly
019
               limited by heavy computation time due to a large number of time iterations. In
020
               this paper, we propose a novel flow-based model, namely bi-directional Discrete
021            Process Matching (Bi-DPM) to accomplish the bi-modality image synthesis tasks.
022            Different to other flow matching based models, we propose to utilize both forward
023            and backward ODE flows and enhance the consistency on the intermediate images
024            over a few discrete time steps, resulting in a synthesis process maintaining high-
025            quality generations for both modalities under the guidance of paired data. Our ex-
026            periments on three datasets of MRI T1/T2 and CT/MRI demonstrate that Bi-DPM
027            outperforms other state-of-the-art flow-based methods for bi-modality image syn-
028            thesis, delivering higher image quality with accurate anatomical regions.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Medical imaging plays a pivotal role in clinical diagnosis, treatment planning, and monitoring of var-
      ious health conditions. Various imaging modalities such as, Computed Tomography (CT), Magnetic
034
      Resonance Imaging (MRI), and Positron Emission Tomography (PET), are widely used in clinical
035
      workflows, each of which can provide unique and distinct structural, functional, and metabolic in-
036   formation that enhances the overall scope for making accurate and reasonable clinical decisions.
037   Even with huge benefits, some imaging modalities such as PET and CT, come with risks of radi-
038   ation exposure. Moreover, the acquisition of multi-modal images are costly and time-consuming,
039   which may also result in potential artifacts due to long time scanning. Hence, obtaining high quality
040   multi-modality images remains a practical challenge in various clinical applications.
041
      Inspired by the success of generative models for natural images, medical image synthesis provides
042   an efficient solution through the transformation from one source image modality to a desired target
043   one. Medical image synthesis can be used for data augmentation for model training Zhang et al.
044   (2020) and validation Hu et al. (2023). In clinical applications it can also be used for MRI-only ra-
045   diation therapy treatment planning and super-resolution Armanious et al. (2020); Dayarathna et al.
046   (2023). Many novel generative neural network structures and algorithms have emerged to enhance
047   performance in medical image synthesis, for capturing complex non-linear relationship between dif-
048   ferent image modalities and generating synthetic images of high quality. In early time, Generative
049   Adversarial Networks (GANs) Goodfellow et al. (2014) are commonly used as the basic model and
050
      numerous GAN-related methods are proposed for medical synthesis and have remarkable perfor-
      mances Suganthi et al. (2021); Cao et al. (2020); Nie et al. (2018); Zhu et al. (2017).
051
052   Recently, the emergence of diffusion-based methods offer a different while effective tool for image
053   generation and also promote the development on medical image synthesis Dorjsembe et al. (2022);
      Pan et al. (2023); Özbey et al. (2023); Müller-Franzes et al. (2023). From of point of view of image

055   from a Gaussian variable Ho et al. (2020); Song & Ermon (2019). Thus it can not be directly used

057   with similar network structure are put forward, which can generate impressive images with specified
058   style or modality, such as Conditional Flow Matching (CFM) Tong et al. (2023) and Rectified Flow
059   (RF) Liu et al. (2022). Generally speaking, the image synthesis process can be described by a flow

077   In both RF and CFM, the velocity field is pre-definded as the linear interpolation between the source
078
      and target distributions. However, for the medical image from different modality, the intermedi-
      ate states resulting from the interpolation may tend to lack meaningful interpretations. And more
079
      importantly, some paired images are available in most cases and the objective is not only gener-
080
      ate high-quality synthesis images but also preserve the paired information throughout the synthesis
081   process. For instance, the same anatomical region of a patient is suppose to retain consistent tissue
082   structure between the CT and MRI images. Thus the pair information may be crucial to be well
083   utilized for the synthesis. On the other hand, in synthesis process, it is cumbersome to calculate the
084   ODE flow along time from zero to one step by step, for which a small step-size takes a considerable
085   amount of time while a large step-size might not be efficient for generating high quality images.
086   Consequently, the choice of the step-size in flow-based methods like RF and CFM is crucial and
087   requires careful consideration for different tasks as well.
088   In this paper, we propose a novel flow-based method, namely bi-directional Discrete Process Match-
089   ing (Bi-DPM). Our approach ensures consistency between the intermediate steps of the forward
090   and backward equations to learn the transformation between a source image modality and a target
091   modality. Unlike recent mainstream flow-based models, Bi-DPM does not impose constraints on
092   the transport paths. Instead, it focuses on matching intermediate states at pre-selected time steps
093   from both the forward and backward directions of the flow ODE. We design a loss function that
094   handles both fully paired and partially paired data, making our method applicable to a wide range
095
      of real world scenarios. We conduct numerical experiments on various medical image modality
      transfer tasks, and the results demonstrate that Bi-DPM generates high-quality synthesized images,
096
      outperforming other flow-matching methods in terms of FID, SSIM, and PSNR metrics. Addition-
097
      ally, Bi-DPM allows for a faster transfer process, as larger ODE step sizes can be used. Finally,
098   clinical evaluations of the synthesized medical images by doctors highlight the potential for clinical
099   application.

218   Flow-based methods, such as Rectified Flow (RF) or Conditional Flow Matching (CFM), emphasize
219   aligning the entire of transport path. Specifically, both RF and CFM aim to minimize the following
220   loss function with respect to a given true velocity field v(·, ·):

                                                                      0
224                                                                     (z)
      tX1 . And in CFM, the velocity field is defined as v(Xt , t) = tt (z) (Xt µt (z)) + µ0t (z), with the
225

226   each configuration leading to a distinct version of CFM.

312   images, we first extract 2D slices from each image to build our training and testing datsets. The MRI
313   T1/T2 dataset comprises 1000 images pairs for training and 251 for testing. And for the CT/MRI
314   task, we construct two datasets for different anatomical regions: the brain and the pelvis. Each
315   dataset is split into 170 pairs for training and 10 for testing, among which we select 100 central
316   slices for the brain and 50 central slices for the pelvis.
317
      In training, all images are first resized to the resolution of (192, 192) and then normalized to the
318   range of [ 1, 1] Ho et al. (2020); Song & Ermon (2019). The step size for the n step Bi-DPM is
319   1/n, with the weights assigned as w = 1 for t = 0, 1 and w = 0.5 for all the intermediate states re-
320   spectively. For all the experiments, we use UNet Ronneberger et al. (2015) structure parameterize the
321   velocity field, as adopted in other flow-based methods Lipman et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2022); Tong
322   et al. (2023). The optimizer for Bi-DPM is Adam Kingma & Ba (2014), with a constant learning
323   rate of 10 4 in the training process. Besides, Exponential Moving Average Klinker (2011)(EMA)
      is used to update the flow-based models, and the two modality images are trained in pairs. Then we

499   3.3   I TERATION S TEPS OF ODE
500
501   In this part, we use totally paired CT/MRI Brain dataset to evaluate the influence of the number of
502   ODE iteration steps on the synthesis process. The corresponding results for the other two datasets
503   are displayed in Supplementary Materials. For all the other flow-based methods, we compare the
504   synthesis results with 4 different ODE steps, including 1, 2, 5 and 10 steps. For simplicity, we treat
505
      CycleGAN as a one-step transformation method, and for our Bi-DPM, we calculate both one-step
      and two-step formulations. As shown in Figure 7, for VP-CFM, the evaluation indices improve
506
      as the number of ODE steps increases, which indicates that a higher number of steps is required
507
      to generate high-quality images in most cases. However, this comes at the cost of significantly
508   increased computational demands. In contrast, for I-CFM, OT-CFM and RF, the results of 1-step
509   perform best and the indices of multi-step remain relatively consistent or even degrade with more
510   iteration steps, suggesting that the number of ODE steps needs careful tuning for each task to achieve
511   optimal results, which complicates the synthetic process. However, despite of a slightly lower PSNR
512   value compared to the best result of CFM on CT images with 1-step and 2-step generations, our Bi-
513   DPM exhibits superior performances across both SSIM and PSNR indices, which makes Bi-DPM
514   an effective model for generating high-quality images for both modalities.

532   4     C ONCLUSIONS
533
534   We propose a novel flow-based method, termed Bi-DPM for bi-modality images synthesis. Unlike
535   other commonly used flow-based models, Bi-DPM accounts for both directions of the flow ODE and
536   ensures the consistency in the intermediate states of the synthesis process. This approach effectively
537   leverages the guidance from the paired data to generate high-quality synthetic images while pre-
538   serving anatomical structure. Experiments on three independent datasets demonstrate that Bi-DPM
539   outperforms other SOTA flow-based image transfer models in MRI T1/T2 and CT/MRI synthesis
      tasks.
