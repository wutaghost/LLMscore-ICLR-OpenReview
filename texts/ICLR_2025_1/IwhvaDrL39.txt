009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable potential in scien-
012            tific domains, yet a fundamental question remains unanswered: Can we simulate
013            human research communities using LLMs? Addressing this question could deepen
014            our understanding of the processes behind research idea generation and inspire
015            the automatic discovery of novel scientific insights. In this work, we propose
016
               R ESEARCH T OWN, a multi-agent framework for simulating research communi-
               ties. Within this framework, the real-world research community is simplified and
017
               modeled as an agent-data graph (i.e.community graphs), where researchers and
018
               papers are represented as agent-type and data-type nodes, respectively. We also
019            introduce TextGNN, a text-based inference framework that models diverse research
020            activities (e.g., paper reading, paper writing, and review writing) as specific forms
021            of a generalized message-passing process on the agent-data graph. To evaluate the
022            quality of research simulation, we present R ESEARCH B ENCH, a benchmark that
023            uses a node-masking prediction task for scalable and objective assessment. Our
024            experiments reveal three key findings: (1) R ESEARCH T OWN effectively simulates
025            collaborative research activities by accurately predicting the attribute of masked
026            nodes in the graph; (2) the simulation process in R ESEARCH T OWN uncovers in-
027
               sights, like not every author contributes equally to the final paper, which is aligned
               with real-world research communities; (3) R ESEARCH T OWN has the potential to
028
               foster interdisciplinary research by generating reasonable paper ideas that span
029
               across domains.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      LLMs are applied to scientific domains including protein design (Lin et al., 2023), drug discov-
035
      ery (Blanco-Gonzalez et al., 2023), and material design (Jablonka et al., 2023), demonstrating great
036   potential for impact for automatic scientific discovery. Despite the promising finding, It remains an
037   open question, can we simulate human research community with LLMs? Answering such research
038   questions has multiple benefits: (1) simulating research activities helps us understand the underlying
039   process behind the creation of existing research ideas; (2) it can further help humans create novel
040   new research ideas.
041
      However, simulating the human research community is challenging, since it requires a multi-agent
042   LLM framework interacting with lots of heterogeneous data. While existing multi-agent LLM
043   frameworks have been applied to social interaction (Zhou et al., 2023), game simulation (Guyot
044   & Honiden, 2006), and coding (Qian et al., 2023), they could not be directly applied to research
045   community simulation. While there are recent works on using LLM for research automation, such
046   frameworks focus on specific type of research activities, such as machine learning coding (Huang
047   et al., 2024b), idea generation (Girotra et al., 2023) or paper writing (Wang et al., 2024; Lu et al.,
048   2024), rather than simulating the community level of research activities. Notably, community-
049   level research simulation can reveal collaboration, the cornerstone of human research activities, by
050
      modeling researchers from diverse backgrounds and expertise to work together to brainstorm ideas,
      have discussions, and review papers.

      Novel R ESEARCH T OWN framework. In this work, we propose R ESEARCH T OWN, a simulator
078
      of the human research community with multi-agent LLMs. To bridge the gap between existing
079   multi-LLM frameworks with the complexity of research activities, we propose a new graph-based
080   framework, inspired by the message passing algorithm in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), for
081   multi-agent simulation. Concretely, we propose a new concept of agent-data graph with 2 generic
082   types of nodes: agent nodes, suitable for entities like humans and LLM agents, and data nodes,
083   suitable for entities such as research papers, reviews, and blogs. Agent-data graphs are unique from
084   standard heterogeneous graphs; here, the key conceptual difference between agent and data nodes
085   is that an agent node can be considered a function over data nodes. To learn from the proposed
086   agent-data graph, we propose a TextGNN framework where message-passing processes are defined
087
      based on text form information processing with LLMs, thanks to their strong in-context learning
      and reasoning ability (Wei et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024). We apply the proposed agent-data graph
088
      and TextGNN to research community simulation. Here, a research community can be regarded
089
      as a special form of agent-data graph, called community graph, with research agents and research
090   papers as two types of nodes, and we consider three types of edges (review, author, and cite) in the
091   graph. Different community activities, such as paper writing and peer reviewing, can be modeled as
092   TextGNN message-passing process on the community graph.
093
      Novel evaluation of research simulation. Having developed the R ESEARCH T OWN framework,
094
      an additional open research question is to evaluate the quality of the research simulation. Prior
095
      works primarily use LLM-as-a-judge (Huang et al., 2024a) or human evaluation with handcrafted
096   metrics, e.g., novelty and soundness. These approaches inevitably suffer from subjectiveness and
097   high costs. In our work, graph-based R ESEARCH T OWN naturally provides a scalable method for
098   objective evaluation, by masking a given paper node in the community graph and evaluating if an
099   LLM simulator can reconstruct the masked nodes. Such a definition does not rely on high-quality
100   human annotations, making it scalable and objective. With the help of such node masking prediction
101   task, we build a benchmark called R ESEARCH B ENCH to systematically discuss the quality of the
102   simulation process.
103   Main discoveries. Based on the evaluation results from R ESEARCH B ENCH, we highlight three key
104   findings: (1) R ESEARCH T OWN effectively simulates collaborative research activities, achieving a
105   similarity score exceeding 0.66 for paper writing tasks; (2) the simulation process reveals valuable
106   insights, such as the observation that not all authors contribute equally to the final paper, aligning with
107   empirical observations of real-world research communities; (3) beyond the field of machine learning,
      R ESEARCH T OWN demonstrates the potential to foster interdisciplinary research by generating

117   Graph with text attributes. In real-world graph modeling, nodes often carry textual attributes,
118   forming text-attributed graphs (TAGs) (Yang et al., 2021; He et al., 2023). While community graphs
119   also utilize textual paper content as node attributes, our work introduces key distinctions from existing
120
      TAG research. Most TAG research for academic tasks predominantly focuses on predicting node
      classes or predicting links (e.g., ogbl-citation2 and ogbn-arxiv (Hu et al., 2020)) and focus on utilizing
121
      LLM to provide better text embeddings for GNN training (Yan et al., 2023). In contrast, our work
122
      directly conducts text-based inference on graph structures and emphasizes generating new nodes
123   along with their associated text attributes, offering a novel direction for academic and practical
124   applications.

126
      Modeling multi-agent as graphs. LLM-based multi-agent simulations are widely used to model
      collaborative interaction. Recently, there has been some work modeling multi-agent communication
127
      as a graph structure (Zhuge et al., 2024; Martinkus et al., 2022) and design optimization methods
128
      based on this. However, in real cases, data exists together with agents to build applications. There are
129   still no well-defined frameworks to describe a graph where both data and agents exist.

      3    AGENT- DATA G RAPH FOR M ULTI - AGENT LLM S
133
      Definition of agent-data graphs. To initiate our discussion, we provide a formal definition of the
134
      proposed agent-data graph. An agent-data graph is a special type of heterogeneous graph G = (V, E),
135
      where V = Va ∪ Vd is the node set consisting of two types of nodes, agent nodes and data nodes,
136   and E = Eaa ∪ Ead ∪ Edd is the edge set consisting of three types of relations, agent-agent, data-data,
137   and agent-data interactions. Here, each data node v ∈ Vd comes with attributes, e.g., a piece of text,
138   xv ; each agent node u is accompanied with a function, e.g., an LLM fu (·) with its profile prompt xv .
139   Without loss of generality, we assume the data nodes have text attributes, and leave the extension of
140   our work to multi-modal information, e.g., images, audio, and videos, to future works.
141
      Uniqueness of agent-data graphs. Unlike standard heterogeneous graphs, the uniqueness of an
142   agent-data graph is that the agent nodes take functions as their attributes, rather than vectors or text.
143   Concretely, each agent node could take any piece of text, e.g., xv from a given data node, as the
144   input and output new data based on its profile prompt, e.g., xuv = fu (CONCAT(xu , xv )). Such
145   definition greatly facilitates the multi-agent scenarios where intelligent agents could communicate
146   among themselves, with edge type Eaa , interacting with the environment, with edge type Ead , and
147   representing the inherent data relationships within an environment Edd .
148   Example of agent-data graphs. As a concrete example, a human research community can be
149   conveniently expressed as an agent-data graph, named a community graph. As is shown in Figure 1,
150   the community graph definition could be extended to more node types (e.g., codebase, blogs) and
151   edge types (e.g., attend, post, commit). Typically, the appearance of one Twitter post can be directly
152   related to multiple researchers, papers, and other Twitter posts. Therefore, such entities are directly
153   connected with the node representing the Twitter post.

161   text-based message-passing model on an agent-data graph, called TextGNN, where all hidden states

354   To evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed framework for automatic research simulation, we created

356
      it consists of 2,737 paper-writing tasks and 1,452 review-writing tasks. Each paper writing task
      is about reproducing a paper collected from a subset of conference papers accepted by NeurIPS
357
      2024 and ICLR 2024, and each review writing task is about reproducing a review collected from
358
      ICLR 2024. Such a dataset is used for in-distribution evaluation. (2) Cross-bench: it consists
359   of 20 manually selected papers where authors from different types of affiliations (e.g., universities,
360   hospitals, companies, etc.) and the paper topic is related to interdisciplinary research. Such a small
361   dataset is used for out-of-domain applications.

526   11    C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   We propose R ESEARCH T OWN as a graph-inspired multi-agent simulation framework. We start by
530   defining an agent-data graph as an abstract model to describe a real-world research community.
531
      Furthermore, we define a TextGNN framework that describes the message-passing process on
      the agent-data graph. Furthermore, we consider the community graph as a special form of the
532
      agent-data graph and further unify research activities including paper reading, paper writing, and
533
      review writing as an inference process with TextGNN. With the help of R ESEARCH T OWN, we can
534   generate similar results that closely mirror human collaborative efforts. R ESEARCH T OWN also
535   fosters interdisciplinary collaboration from agents in different fields writing cross-domain papers. We
536   demonstrate that by harnessing the strengths of multiple agents, we can write papers that are more
537   robust and aligned with actual research trends, further validating the effectiveness of our simulation
538   framework. Since ICLR 2025 has officially adopted a review agent during the discussion process,
539   we think that R ESEARCH T OWN unblocks more potential systematic evaluation and algorithmic
      development towards automatic research.

      A     E THICAL C ONCERN
649
650   The development and deployment of R ESEARCH T OWN raises several important ethical considerations
651   that we have carefully addressed in our work.
652
653   A.1    P LAGIARISM P REVENTION
654
655
      Generative AI’s capabilities for image and text generation might be used to create content that could
      lead to plagiarism in research (Elali & Rachid, 2023). To mitigate the risk of plagiarism, we have
656
      implemented a series of safeguards. R ESEARCH T OWN is designed as an assistive tool that provides
657
      research proposals based on existing academic works, rather than generating ready-made papers. It’s
658   important to note that these proposals are generic and require further development, so users cannot
659   directly apply them to their research without modification. The generated proposals only contain
660   answers to five important research questions (Widom, 2006) and have a long way to go before they
661   become a complete paper, which includes sections such as an introduction, background, methodology,
662   discussion, and conclusion. The responsibility for refining, and experimenting with these proposals
663   remains with the users. Moreover, they are interdisciplinary by nature and specifically designed not
664   to overlap with existing work or replicate the research styles of individual researchers.
665   Finally, we emphasize that R ESEARCH T OWN is a non-commercial, open-source project. All papers
666   used in R ESEARCH T OWN and R ESEARCH B ENCH are publicly available. In R ESEARCH B ENCH, all
667   inputs and outputs are logged and open for access. Additionally, we keep an accessible record of all
668   supplementary papers referenced during R ESEARCH T OWN’s inference process. All outputs from
669   R ESEARCH T OWN are released by the licenses of the papers used to generate the insights, which are
670   predominantly CC0 or CC-BY 4.0, allowing for redistribution and sharing.
671
672   A.2    R ESEARCH Q UALITY AND FABRICATION
673
674   As mentioned above, R ESEARCH T OWN generates proposals based on current research that require
675
      thorough examination and development before they can be applied in academic work. Furthermore,
      R ESEARCH T OWN adheres to the real-world research pipeline, encompassing submission, review,
676
      rebuttal, and meta-review processes. This structured approach enhances the overall novelty, validity,
677
      significance, feasibility, clarity, and ethical considerations of the insights generated.
678
679   To further mitigate the risk of hallucinations in LLM-generated content (Huang et al., 2023; Lewis
680   et al., 2020), we carefully curate related papers to ground the entire generation process. This combined
681
      with the review mechanism ensures that the proposals provided are not only relevant but also rooted
      in established research, enhancing their reliability and applicability.
682
683
684
      A.3    S IMULATED R ESEARCH P ROFILES
685
      Our agents are designed to act as domain experts rather than impersonating specific human researchers.
686   They are constructed using publicly available academic papers related to particular areas of expertise.
687   We emphasize the use of publicly accessible research to promote the collective advancement of
688   knowledge and avoid attempting to role-play individual researchers.
689
      By implementing these measures, we aim to harness the potential of AI in accelerating research while
690
      maintaining ethical standards, respecting intellectual property rights, and preserving the integrity of
691
      the scientific process. We recognize that ethical considerations in AI-assisted research are evolving,
692   and we remain committed to ongoing evaluation and improvement of our approach.
