011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Federated learning (FL) is a widely used privacy-preserving approach for dis-
014            tributed training that avoids the need to collect data from individual users. In this
015
               paper, we investigate fine-tuning pre-trained language models (PLMs) in an FL
               setting and leverage parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods to reduce com-
016
               putational and communication costs. However, non-IID data in federated learning
017
               significantly degrades the performance of PEFT, with the degradation worsening as
018            data heterogeneity increases. To address this, we propose FeDeRA, an FL approach
019            for fine-tuning PLMs that incorporates an effective extension of the low-rank adap-
020            tation (LoRA) method. Specifically, FeDeRA initializes the low-rank matrices
021            using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) on the pre-trained weight matrices,
022            rather than the zero or random initialization used in the original LoRA method.
023            Analyzing weight updates during training reveals that FeDeRA reduces weight
024            oscillations, enabling faster and more efficient fine-tuning of PLMs in FL with
025            non-IID data. Experimental results across multiple NLP tasks and models show
026
               that FeDeRA outperforms all PEFT-based baselines in task performance and, in
               some cases, even matches or exceeds the performance of full-parameter fine-tuning.
027
               FeDeRA also greatly enhances training efficiency, reducing training time by up to
028
               97.3% compared to full-parameter fine-tuning and up to 74.6% compared to the
029            fastest PEFT baseline in practical FL settings. Furthermore, FeDeRA demonstrates
030            greater robustness to data heterogeneity than all other PEFT methods, highlighting
031            the effectiveness of its proposed initialization in FL systems.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Pre-trained language models (PLMs) have achieved state-of-the-art (SOTA) performances across
037   various NLP tasks, including natural language understanding(Kenton & Toutanova, 2019; Sanh et al.,
038   2019; Liu et al., 2019), text generation(Brown et al., 2020; Zeng et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2023), and
039
      question answering. However, training these models from scratch demands a significant amount of
      resources(Narayanan et al., 2021), making it unattainable for most. Consequently, fine-tuning models
040
      for specific tasks has emerged as the primary method of leveraging large language models. This
041
      process typically involves training a pre-trained model on a significantly smaller dataset rather than
042   the original training set. While fine-tuning in a centralized manner is typically preferred, aggregating
043   all data onto a single device raises concerns about data privacy, thus making centralized training
044   increasingly challenging.
045
      Federated learning(FL) (Konečnỳ et al., 2016; McMahan et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2022; Tang et al.,
046
      2022) has emerged as a promising approach in machine learning to address data privacy concerns
047
      by training a model collaboratively across decentralized clients without sharing raw local data. In
048   FL, clients periodically compute and send model information, such as parameters or gradients, to
049   a central server for aggregation, resulting in a global model. However, fine-tuning PLMs in an FL
050   setting encounters significant challenges. Firstly, FL requires frequent exchange of model parameters
051   or gradients between the server and clients. The massive number of parameters in PLMs, often
052   in the tens or hundreds of billions, leads to substantial communication overheads. Additionally,
053   devices involved in FL often communicate over bandwidth-limited networks, causing significant
      delays during data transmission and reducing training efficiency. Moreover, fine-tuning language

      Recently, parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) techniques such as BitFit(Zaken et al., 2022), adapter
073   tuning(Narayanan et al., 2021; Pfeiffer et al., 2021), prefix tuning(Li & Liang, 2021) and LoRA(Hu
074   et al., 2021) have garnered significant attention for their ability to update only a small portion of a
075   pre-trained model’s parameters, thus offering advantages in memory and computational efficiency.
076   Existing works have demonstrated that these methods match or even surpass the performance of
077   traditional full-parameter fine-tuning (FFT) methods in centralized training. Adopting PEFT in
078   FL setting holds promise in addressing the aforementioned challenges by effectively reducing the
079   number of parameters transmitted between clients and the server, as well as lowering the memory
080   and computation costs associated with model training on each client.
081   In this work, we investigate PEFT methods within an FL setting and find that the heterogeneity of
082   clients’ data results in reduced performance and slower convergence rates for PEFT. As shown in
083   Figure 1, the performance gap between PEFT methods and FFT widens as data heterogeneity increases.
084   To address this issue, we propose FeDeRA, building upon LoRA’s approach of decomposing pre-
085   trained model weight matrices into low-rank matrices. FeDeRA innovatively leverages singular value
086   decomposition (SVD) on pre-trained weight matrices to extract principal components for initializing
087
      low-rank matrices, i.e., the adaptors, while preserving and freezing remaining components in the
      original matrices. This simple yet effective solution addresses the remarkable performance decline
088
      observed in LoRA within federated settings, particularly when confronted with significantly non-IID
089
      data across clients. Experimental outcomes reveal that FeDeRA achieves comparable or superior task
090   performance to FFT across various tasks, significantly surpassing other PEFT methods. Moreover,
091   FeDeRA reduces training time by over 95% compared to FFT, while maintaining consistent task
092   performance. Furthermore, experimental results underscore the robustness of FeDeRA against data
093   heterogeneity, exhibiting much lower performance degradation compared to other PEFT methods.

097   2.1                  F EDERATED L EARNING WITH N ON -IID DATA
098
099
      FL is widely adopted for distributed learning, especially in tasks requiring significant privacy con-
      siderations. However, it suffers unneglectable performance degradation when data across different
100
      clients are non-independently and Identically Distributed (non-IID). Research efforts have surged to
101
      mitigate the effects of non-IID data on FL performance(Li et al., 2020). Some studies address this
102   issue directly on the training data of each client through methods such as sharing a small portion of
103   local data(Zhao et al., 2018), sharing model outputs(Itahara et al., 2021), enhancing data with external
104   datasets(Jeong et al., 2018), generating new data from encoded and protected data of other clients(Shin
105   et al., 2020), or selecting data carefully(Wang et al., 2020). Other approaches involves carefully
106   designing the model training process, such as using adaptive learning rates on clients(Ma et al., 2021)
107   or employing a control variable to prevent excessive drift in local updates(Karimireddy et al., 2020).
      Additionally, some studies have combined FL with other algorithms like Meta-Learning(Jiang et al.,


                                                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2019; Zhang et al., 2021), Lifelong Learning(Shoham et al., 2019; Kopparapu & Lin, 2020), and
109   Knowledge Distillation(Itahara et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022). Existing research suggests that using
110   pre-trained models in FL, rather than training models from scratch, can effectively mitigate the impact
111   of non-IID data on performance(Weller et al., 2022; Nguyen et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023). However,
112   as discussed in section 1, the substantial computational demands and communication overheads
113   associated with PLMs are significant challenges. Thus, optimizing the fine-tuning of PLMs within an
114   FL setting for both computation and communication is urgently needed.
115
116   2.2   PEFT IN F EDERATED L EARNING
117
118   PEFT methods aim to freeze most or all of the weights in PLMs, fine-tuning only a small subset
119
      or newly introduced weights. This approach reduces computational resource requirements and
      significantly decreases communication overhead. Some studies have incorporated various PEFT
120
      methods in FL, demonstrating their efficiency in training and evaluating the comparative advantages
121
      and disadvantages of different PEFT approaches in terms of performance, resource demands, and
122   privacy aspects(Sun et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023). Additionally, some other
123   studies focus on exploring the potential of PEFT techniques within FL, such as using LoRA for
124   enhanced heterogeneous personalized learning in FL(Yi et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024), and incremen-
125   tally enhancing adapter configurations during FL training to accelerate convergence(Cai et al., 2023).
126   However, it has been increasingly recognized that PEFT methods experience significant performance
127   degradation when dealing with highly non-IID data distributions in FL(Zhang et al., 2023; Babakniya
128   et al., 2023). Despite this growing awareness, most prior research has either overlooked this critical
129   issue or failed to evaluate PEFT methods under severe non-IID data conditions.

182   3.2   L OW-R ANK A DAPTATION
183
184   LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) is one of the most widely used PEFT methods of PLMs. This method
185   is based on the observation that when PLMs are adapted to specific tasks, their weight update
186   matrices usually have a lower intrinsic dimension than their dimensions. This implies that projecting
187   these matrices into a lower dimension does not lead to significant information loss. The key idea
188
      behind LoRA is to avoid fine-tuning an entire pre-trained weight matrix Wpre ∈ Rd×k . Instead, as
      illustrated in Figure 2a, LoRA introduces a weight update mechanism based on matrix decomposition:
189
      Wpre + ∆W = Wpre + AB. Here, B ∈ Rd×r and A ∈ Rr×k are trainable matrices, with rank
190
      r ≪ min{d, k}. During training, matrix A is initialized by sampling from a random Gaussian
191   distribution, and matrix B is initialized to zeros, ensuring that initially AB = 0, as illustrated in
192   Fig. 2a. The matrix Wpre remains frozen throughout the training, while AB are updated during the
193   fine-tuning process. The output of a layer after implementing LoRA is given by:
194
                                                            β
195                                          h = Wpre x +      BAx,                                   (5)
                                                             r
196
197   where x is the input, and β is designed to eliminate the need of re-adjusting hyperparameters when
198
      changing r.

      5      E VALUATION
325
326
      In this section, we present numerical results to evaluate the performance of the proposed FeDeRA
327   using the FedML(He et al., 2020) and FedNLP(Lin et al., 2022) frameworks. Specifically, we first
328   compare the performance of FeDeRA with various baselines across six datasets and three NLP tasks,
329   using highly non-IID training data generated according to a Dirichlet distribution. We then implement
330   FeDeRA and the baseline methods on practical devices and access time cost of different approaches
331   to achieve a target accuracy to evalute the training efficiency.
332
333   5.1     BASELINES
334
335   We adopt existing FFT and PEFT methods within the FL setting as baselines. To ensure a fair
336   comparison, the experimental settings for these baselines are consistent with those in their original
337
      works, unless explicitly stated otherwise. The details of the baselines are as follows:
338   FedFT: The conventional approach which updates all the model’s parameters throughout the fine-
339   tuning process.
340
      FedBF: Each client updates only the bias terms while keeping the other parameters fixed.
341
342   FedAP: This approach incorporates adapter-tuning by inserting some trainable adapter layers into the
343   model while keeping the parameters of the original model frozen. Specifically, adapters are inserted
344
      after the attention and feed-forward modules, following the methodology outlined in (Houlsby et al.,
      2019). The adapter module is implemented using the adapters library (Poth et al., 2023; Pfeiffer et al.,
345
      2020).
346
347   FedLR: This approach directly applies the original LoRA to FL. Note that the LoRA operation is
348   applied only to the query and value matrices, while the key matrices are kept fixed. The same applies

521    6                              C ONCLUSIONS
522
523   In this paper, we introduced FeDeRA, a method for fine-tuning PLMs in a federated setting. Specifi-
524   cally, FeDeRA extends LoRA by initializing the low-rank adapters with the results of Singular Value
525   Decomposition on the pre-trained weight matrices. We analyzed the weight update equations and
526   visualized the magnitude and direction curves of weight updates during training, explaining how
527   FeDeRA reduces weight oscillations, thereby accelerating and improving the fine-tuning of PLMs
528   in an FL setting with non-IID data. Our empirical findings demonstrated that FeDeRA outperforms
529
      all PEFT-based baselines in terms of task performance and closely approximates, or even surpasses
      in some cases, the performance of the full-parameter fine-tuning approach. It was also shown that
530
      FeDeRA significantly improves training efficiency, reducing training time by up to 97.3% compared
531
      to the full-parameter fine-tuning baseline, and by up to 74.6% compared to the fastest PEFT-based
532   baseline in a practical setting. Furthermore, FeDeRA is more robust to data heterogeneity than all
533   other PEFT methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed initialization in FL systems.

      categorizing the semantic relations between specific pairs of nominals within given sentences. It
792   delineates nine distinct types of semantic relations, with each relation type being subject to inversion,
793   thereby totaling 19 potential labels when including an additional ’Other’ category for relations that

799   of the primary tasks was Named Entity Recognition (NER), which involved identifying and classifying
800   proper names within text into predefined categories such as persons, organizations, locations, etc. The
801   token labels comprise seven parts of speech, further divided into begin and intermediate positions,
802   along with an additional category that does not belong to any of the aforementioned parts of speech,
803   totaling thirteen labels in all. We observe that each training sample may comprise a unique set of
