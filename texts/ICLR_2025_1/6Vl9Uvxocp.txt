                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Generative Flow Networks (GFlowNets) are a family of probabilistic generative
               models recently invented that learn to sample compositional objects proportional
011
               to their rewards. One big challenge of GFlowNets is training them effectively
012
               when dealing with long time horizons and sparse rewards. To address this, we
013            propose Evolution guided generative flow networks (EGFN), a simple and powerful
014            augmentation to the GFlowNets training using Evolutionary algorithms (EA). Our
015            method can work on top of any GFlowNets training objective, by training a set
016            of agent parameters using EA, storing the resulting trajectories in the prioritized
017            replay buffer, and training the GFlowNets agent using the stored trajectories. We
018            present a thorough investigation over a wide range of toy and real-world benchmark
019            tasks showing the effectiveness of our method in handling long trajectories and
020            sparse rewards.
021

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Generative Flow Networks (GFlowNets) (Bengio et al., 2021; 2023) are a family of probabilistic
026   amortized samplers that learn to sample from a space proportionally to some reward function R(x),
027
      effectively sampling compositional objects over some probability distribution. As a generative
      process, it composes objects by some sequence of actions, terminating by reaching a termination
028
      state.
029
030   GFlowNets have shown great potential for diverse challenging applications, such as molecule discov-
031   ery (Jain et al., 2023a), biological sequence design (Jain et al., 2022), combinatorial optimization
032   (Zhang et al., 2023), latent variable sampling (Liu et al., 2023) and road generation (Ikram et al.,
033
      2023). The key advantage of GFlowNets over other methods such as reinforcement learning (RL) is
      that GFlowNets’s key objective is not reward maximization, allowing them to sample diverse samples
034
      proportionally to the reward function. Although entropy-regularized RL also encourages randomness
035
      when taking actions, it is not general in when the underlying graph is not a tree (i.e., a state can have
036   multiple parent states) (Zhao et al., 2019).
037
038   Despite the recent advancements, the real-world adaptation of GFlowNets is still limited by a major
039
      problem: temporal credit assignment for long trajectories and sparse rewards. For example, real-world
      problems such as protein design often are often long-horizon problems, necessitating long trajectories
040
      for sampling. Since reward is given only when the agent reaches the terminal states, associating
041
      actions with rewards over a lengthy trajectory becomes challenging. Additionally, reward space is
042   sparse in real-world tasks, making temporal credit assignment more difficult. Trajectory balance (TB)
043   objective (Malkin et al., 2022) attempts to tackle the problem by matching the flow across the entire
044   trajectory, but in practice, it induces larger variance and is highly sensitive to sparse rewards (Madan
045   et al., 2023), making the training unstable.
046
      Evolutionary algorithms (EA) (Bäck & Schwefel, 1993), a class of optimization algorithms inspired
047
      by natural selection and evolution, can be a promising candidate for tackling the said challenges.
048   Indeed, the shortcomings of GFlowNets are the advantages of EA, which makes it promising to
049   consider incorporating EA into the learning paradigm of GFlowNets to leverage the best from both
050   worlds. First, the selection operation in EA is achieved by fitness evaluation throughout the entire
051   trajectory, which makes them robust to long trajectories and sparse rewards as they naturally bias
052   towards regions with high expected returns. Secondly, mutation makes EA naturally exploratory,
053   which is crucial for GFlowNets training and mode-finding as they rely on diverse samples for better
      training (Pan et al., 2022). Third, EA’s natural selection biases towards parameters that generate high

073   In this work, we introduce Evolution guided generative flow networks (EGFN), a novel training
074   method for GFlowNets combining gradient-based and gradient-free approaches and benefit from
075   the best of both worlds. Our proposed approach is a three-step training process, as summarized

079
      evaluated trajectories in the prioritized replay buffer (PRB). For the second step, we sample the stored
      trajectories from a PRB and combine them with online samples from a different GFlowNets agent.
080

081
      objectives such as Flow matching (FM), Detailed balance (DB), and Trajectory balance (TB), where
082   they optimize at the transition-level (FM, DB), and trajectory level (TB). The reward-maximizing
083   capability of EA enhances gradient signal through high reward training samples, ensuring stable
084   GFlowNets training even in conditions with sparse rewards and long trajectories. Through extensive
085   evaluation in experimental and a wide range of real-world settings, our method proves effective in
086   addressing weaknesses related to temporal credit assignment in sparse rewards and long trajectories,
087   surpassing GFlowNets baselines in terms of both the number of re-discovered modes and top-K
088   rewards.

094   2.1   G ENERATIVE F LOW N ETWORKS (GF LOW N ETS )
095
096   Generative Flow Networks (GFlowNets) are a family of generative models that samples compositional
097   objects through a sequence of actions. We define the flow network as a single source initial state
098   s0 with in-flow Z, and one sink for each terminal state x ∈ X with out-flow R(x) > 0. We denote
099
      the Markovian composition trajectory (s0 → s1 →       − · · · → x) as τ ∈ T , where T is the set of all
      trajectories. Thus, the problem is formulated as a directed acyclic graph (DAG), (S, E), where each
100
      node in S denotes a state with an initial state s0 , and each edge in E denotes a transition st → st+1
101
      with a special terminal action indicating s = x ∈ X . We specifically consider DAGs that are not
102   tree-structured, thus there exist different paths leading to the same state in the DAG, except for the
103   root, which has no parent. Given a terminal state space X , GFlowNets aim to learn a stochastic
104   policy π that can sample terminal states x ∈ X proportionally to a non-negative reward function
105   R(x), i.e., π(x) ∝ R(x). GFlowNets construct objects x ∈ X by sampling constructive, irreversible
106   actions a ∈ A that transition st to st+1 . An important advantage of GFlowNets is that GFlowNets’s
107   probability of generating an object is proportional to a given positive reward for that object and we
      can train it in both online and offline settings, allowing us to train from replay buffers.

149   2.2   E VOLUTIONARY ALGORITHMS (EA)
150
151   Evolutionary algorithms (EA) (Bäck, 2006; Spears et al., 1993) are a class of optimization algorithms
152
      that generally rely on three key techniques: mutation, crossover, and selection as in biological
      evolution. The crossover operation is responsible for generating new samples based on exchange of
153
      information among a population of samples. The mutation operation alters the generated samples,
154
      usually with some probability pmutation . Finally, the selection operation evaluates the fitness score
155   of the population and is responsible for generating the next population. In this work, we apply EA
156   in the context of the weights of the neural networks, often referred to as neuroevolution (Stanley &
157   Miikkulainen, 2002b; Risi & Togelius, 2014; Floreano et al., 2008; Lüders et al., 2017).
158

410   4.3   S OLUABLE E POXY H YDROLASE ( S EH) BINDER GENERATION TASK |X | ≈ 1016
411
412   Setup. In this experiment, we are interested in generating molecules with desired chemical proper-
413
      ties that are not too similar to one another. Here, we represent molecules states as graph structures
      and actions as a vocabulary of blocks specified by junction tree modeling (Bickerton et al., 2012; Shi
414
      et al., 2020). In the pharmaceutical industry, drug-likeliness (Bickerton et al., 2012), synthesizability
415
      (Shi et al., 2020), and toxicity are crucial properties. Hence, we are interested in finding diverse
416   candidate molecules for a given criteria to increase chances for post-selection. Here, the criteria is the
417   molecule’s binding energy to the 4JNC inhibitor of the soluble epoxide hydrolase (sEH) protein. To
418   this end, we train a proxy reward function for predicting the negative binding energy that serves as the
419   reward function. We perform the experiment following the experimental details and reward function
420   specifications from Bengio et al. (2021). Since we are interested in both the diversity and efficacy
421   of drugs, we define a mode as a molecule with a reward greater than 7.5 and a tanimoto similarity

      6     R ELATED WORK
494
495
      6.1   E VOLUTION IN LEARNING
496
497   There has been many attempts to augment learning, especially RL, with EA. Early works such
498   Whiteson (2006) combine NEAT (Stanley & Miikkulainen, 2002a) and Q Learning (Watkins &
499   Dayan, 1992) by using evolutionary strategies to better tune the function approximators. In a similar
500   manner, Colas et al. (2018) uses EA for exploration in policy gradient, generating diverse samples
501   using mutation. Fernando et al. (2017) use EA for allowing parameter reuse without catastrophic
502   forgetting. Recently, many methods use EA to enhance deep RL architectures such as Proximal Policy
503
      Optimization (PPO) (Hämäläinen et al., 2020), Soft-Actor Critic (SAC) (Hou et al., 2020), and Policy
      Gradient (Khadka & Tumer, 2018). The key idea from these approaches is to use EA to overcome
504
      the temporal credit assignment and improve exploration by getting diverse samples (Lee et al., 2020),
505
      with some exceptions such as Gangwani & Peng (2018); Fujimoto et al. (2018); Pourchot & Sigaud
506   (2019) where they utilize EA to tune the parameter of the actor itself.
507
508
      6.2   GF LOW N ETS
509
510   GFlowNets have recently been applied to various problems (Liu et al., 2023; Bengio et al., 2021).
511   There have also been recent efforts in extending GFlowNets to continuous (Lahlou et al., 2023) and
512   stochastic worlds (Pan et al., 2023b), and also leveraging the power of pre-trained models (Pan et al.,
513   2024). In GFlowNets training, exploration is an important concept for training convergence, which
514   many works attempt in different ways. For example, Bengio et al. (2021) use ϵ-greedy exploration
515   strategy, Kim et al. (2023) learn the logits conditioned on different annealed temperatures, Pan et al.
516
      (2022) introduces augmented flows into the flow network represented by intrinsic rewards, etc. The
      temporal credit assignment for long trajectories and sparse reward is a more recently studied topic for
517
      GFlowNets. Recent works such as Malkin et al. (2022) attempt to tackle this problem by minimizing
518
      the loss over an entire trajectory as opposed to state-wise FM proposed by Bengio et al. (2021),
519   however, it may incur large variance as demonstrated in Madan et al. (2023).
520
521
522   7     C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this work, we presented EGFN, a simple and effective EA based strategy for training GFlowNets,
525
      especially for credit assignment in long trajectories and sparse rewards. This strategy mixes the best
      of both worlds: EA’s population-based approach biases towards regions with long-term returns, and
526
      GFlowNet’s gradient-based objectives handle the matching of the reward distribution with the sample
527
      distribution by leveraging better gradient signals. Besides, EA promotes natural diversity of the
528   explored region, removing the need to use any other exploration strategies for GFlowNets training.
529   Furthermore, by incorporating PRB for offline samples, EA promotes redundancy of high region
530   samples, stabilizing the GFlowNet training with better gradient signals. We validate our method on a
531   wide range of challenging toy and real-world benchmarks with exponentially large combinatorial
532   search spaces, showing that our method outperforms the best GFlowNet baselines on long time
533   horizon and sparse rewards.
534   In this work, we implement a standard evolutionary algorithm for EGFN. Incorporating more complex
535   sub-modules of EA for GFlowNets training such as Covariance Matrix Adaptation and Evolution
536   Strategy (CMA-ES) such as the work in Pourchot & Sigaud (2019) can be an exciting future work.
537   Another future direction could be integrating the gradient signal from the GFlowNets objectives into
538   the EA strategy, creating a feedback loop. Besides, while we use a reward-maximization formulation
539   for the EA in this work, there are works such as Parker-Holder et al. (2020) that directly improves
      diversity by formulation. We leave that for the future work.

898

901   trajectory length |τ | and the number of states |X |, only increasing D increases the number of modes.

903   the probability density.

905
      by a leaky ReLU. The forward layer takes the one-hot encoding of the states as inputs and outputs
906
      action logits. For FM, we simply use the forward layer to model the edge flow. For TB and DB,
907   we double the action space and train the MLP as both the forward and backward flow. We use a
908   learning rate of 10−4 for FM and 10−3 for both TB and DB, including a learning rate of 0.1 for Zθ .
909   The replay buffer uses a maximum size of 1000, and we use a worst-reward first policy for replay
910   replacement. For RL and MCMC baselines, we use the implementation provided by Bengio et al.
911   (2021). For GAFN baseline, we use the official code provided by Pan et al. (2022), performing a
912   hyper-parameter tuning of intrinsic reward and using intrinsic reward = {0.05, 0.03, 0.01} for our

1201   constraining policy updates, ensuring stable and reliable improvement during optimization.
1202
       IQL. Implicit Q-Learning (IQL) is an offline reinforcement learning approach that effectively
1203
       extracts value-based policies by decoupling value estimation from policy improvement, achieving
1204   stable learning from static datasets.
