009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent studies demonstrate that diffusion models can serve as a strong prior for
012            solving inverse problems. A prominent example is Diffusion Posterior Sampling
013            (DPS), which approximates the posterior distribution of data given the measure
014            using Tweedie’s formula. Despite the merits of being versatile in solving various in-
015            verse problems without re-training, the performance of DPS is hindered by the fact
016
               that this posterior approximation can be inaccurate especially for high noise levels.
               Therefore, we propose Diffusion Posterior MCMC (DPMC), a novel inference
017
               algorithm based on Annealed MCMC to solve inverse problems with pretrained
018
               diffusion models. We define a series of intermediate distributions inspired by the
019            approximated conditional distributions used by DPS. Through annealed MCMC
020            sampling, we encourage the samples to follow each intermediate distribution more
021            closely before moving to the next distribution at a lower noise level, and therefore
022            reduce the accumulated error along the path. We test our algorithm in various
023            inverse problems, including super resolution, Gaussian deblurring, motion deblur-
024            ring, inpainting, and phase retrieval. Our algorithm outperforms DPS with less
025            number of evaluations across nearly all tasks, and is competitive among existing
026            approaches.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Diffusion Models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song & Ermon, 2019; Song et al.,
031   2021b) have recently achieved significant success in high-dimensional data generation, including
032   images (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Ramesh et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022),
033   videos (Ho et al., 2022b; OpenAI, 2024; Ho et al., 2022a; Blattmann et al., 2023; Girdhar et al.,
034   2023), audio Kong et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2020), text Li et al. (2022), and and 3D generation (Liu
035   et al., 2023; Poole et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024). Beyond generation
036   (Zhang et al., 2024a; Chang et al., 2024), recent works have applied diffusion models to solve inverse
037   problems in a plug-and-play fashion without the need for fine-tuning (Jalal et al., 2021; Kadkhodaie
038   & Simoncelli, 2021; Kawar et al., 2022; Song et al., 2021b; Choi et al., 2021; Chung et al., 2022a;
039
      2023a; Dou & Song, 2023; Mardani et al., 2023a; Song et al., 2023b; Rout et al., 2023; Song et al.,
      2023a; Chung & Ye, 2022; Feng et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023b; Chung et al., 2023b; Zhang et al.,
040
      2024b), where the goal is to restore data x from degraded measurements y. Among these, one line of
041
      works Jalal et al. (2021); Chung et al. (2023a); Song et al. (2023b); Rout et al. (2023); Song et al.
042   (2023a) proposes to modify the inference process of diffusion models with guidance that encourages
043   samples to be consistent with the measure. Other perspectives include variational inference (Mardani
044   et al., 2023a; Feng et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023b), Bayesian filtering Dou & Song (2023), and solving
045   an inner loop optimization problem during sampling (Song et al., 2022; 2023a).
046
      A typical challenge in this context is that the posterior distribution p(x|y) is only defined for clean
047
      samples x, yet during sampling, an estimation of p(xt |y) is needed at each diffusion step t. DPS
048   (Chung et al., 2023a) approximates the intractable posterior through Tweedie’s formula (Efron, 2011),
049   enabling its application in general inverse problems. However, this approximation might be inaccurate
050   especially on high noise levels, leading to samples of low quality. To address this, ΠGDM (Song
051   et al., 2023b) attempts to improve the approximated guidance by pseudo-inverting the measurement.
052   Additionally, Rout et al. (2023); Song et al. (2023a) aim to enhance DPS in the Latent Diffusion
053   model (Rombach et al., 2022) by introducing extra guidance terms or resampling processes to the
      data space.

054
      In this work, we propose to leverage annealed Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) to improve
055   solving inverse problems with diffusion models. As a family of effective algorithms that draw
056   samples from complex distributions, MCMC is widely used in training Energy-Based Models
057   (EBMs) (Xie et al., 2016; Nijkamp et al., 2019; Du et al., 2021) and sampling from diffusion
058   models (Song & Ermon, 2019; Song et al., 2021b). Annealed MCMC further proposes to facilitate
059   MCMC sampling by gradually sampling from a sequence of distributions from decreasingly lower
060   temperature, to accelerate the mixing. Previous work (Du et al., 2023) has leveraged Annealed
061   MCMC in compositional generation with EBMs or diffusion models. In this work, we propose using
062   MCMC to reduce the error of posterior approximation in solving inverse problems.
063   There are some earlier works (Jalal et al., 2021; Song et al., 2021b) that employ annealed MCMC
064   with Langevin dynamics to solve inverse problems. They tackle the intractability of the posterior
065   distribution by projecting the current sample onto the measurement subspace. However, these
066   approaches might fail when measurements are noisy or the measurement process is non-linear,
067   as discussed in Chung et al. (2023a). Moreover, a single MCMC step is executed at every noise
068   level before moving to the next noise level. Alternatively, we propose to build the the intermediate
069
      distributions of our annealed MCMC with the approximated posterior distributions derived in Chung
      et al. (2023a). Despite the fact that such approximation might be inaccurate for the data distributions
070
      defined by the forward diffusion, they still remain as a valid sequence of distributions that can be
071
      leveraged in annealed MCMC. We further propose to run multiple sampling steps at each noise level
072   similar to Du et al. (2023), that empirically improves the performance.
073
074   In summary, we make the following contributions in this work:
075
             • We propose the Diffusion Posterior MCMC (DPMC) algorithm, which leverages annealed
076
               MCMC with a sequence of posterior distribution of data given measurements, whose formula
077            is inspired by DPS (Chung et al., 2023a).
078
             • We demonstrate that empirically DPMC outperforms DPS in terms of sample quality across
079
               various types of inverse problems in image domain, including both linear and nonlinear
080
               inverse problems. DPMC also establishes competitive performance compared with other
081            existing approaches.
082
             • Through extensive ablation study and comparison with other approaches, we highlight the
083
               effectiveness of MCMC-based approaches for solving inverse problems, and a constant
084
               improvement of performance with increasingly larger number of sampling step.

      single-modal and the estimation x̂0 and the posterior approximation might be accurate. However,

288   number of evaluation steps needed. In practice, DPMC can achieve much better results compared to
289   DPS with an even smaller number of evaluations (NFEs).

      4.1       E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS
298
299   Datasets and Pretrained Model: Following Chung et al. (2023a), we test our algorithm on FFHQ
300   256 × 256 dataset (Karras et al., 2019) and ImageNet 256 × 256 dataset (Deng et al., 2009). Same as
301   Chung et al. (2023a); Dou & Song (2023), we use 1k validation images for each dataset. For FFHQ
302   dataset, we use the pretrained model provided by Chung et al. (2023a). For ImageNet dataset, we use
303   the pretrained model provided by Dhariwal & Nichol (2021).
304   Inverse Problems: We evaluate the effectiveness of our DPMC algorithm on the following inverse
305   problems: (i) Super-resolution with 4x bicubic downsampling as the forward measurement; (ii)
306   Random inpainting using both box masks and random masks; (iii) Deblurring with Gaussian blur
307   kernels and motion blur kernels 1 ; (iv) Phase retrieval, where we perform a Fourier transformation on
308   each image and use only the Fourier magnitude as the measurement. Among these inverse problems,
309   (i), (ii), and (iii) are linear inverse problems, while (iv) is a nonlinear inverse problem. We use
310   Gaussian noise with σ = 0.05 for all tasks and set task-related parameters according to Chung et al.
311   (2023a); Dou & Song (2023) to ensure a fair comparison.
312   Hyper-Parameter Setting: We use the DDIM sampler with default variance σt = 0 as our diffusion
313   proposal sampler. We use T = 200 intermediate distributions and K = 4 MCMC steps at each
314   intermediate distribution. Empirically, we set ηt = ηβt and ξt = ξ ᾱt3 , where η and ξ are task-related
315   constants. We find these schedules work well across all settings. The detailed parameter settings
316   can be found in Section A in the Appendix. As discussed in 3, we apply the proposal-exploration
317   step in the middle 60% of the total sampling steps and resort to original DPS sampler step at the first
318
      30% and the last 10% noise levels. An exception is the inpainting task with a box-type mask on the
      ImageNet dataset, where we find that applying the proposal-exploration step until clean images yields
319
      better results. Considering both the initial proposal step and the MCMC sampling steps, the NFE of
320
      this setting is around 700, which is smaller than the 1000 NFE of DPS. All of the experiments are
321   carried out on a single Nvidia A100 GPU. We report the running clock time of DPMC in Table 6 in
322   Appendix C.

503   5     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
504
      In this study, we propose DPMC, an algorithm based on a specific formula of the posterior distribution
505
      and Annealed MCMC sampling to solve inverse problems. We demonstrate that DPMC results in
506   superior sample quality compared to DPS across various inverse problems with fewer functional
507   evaluations. Our study underscores the benefit of incorporating more sampling steps into each
508   intermediate distribution to encourage exploration. Additionally, it is beneficial to trade in the number
509   of intermediate distributions with the number of MCMC exploration steps. One potential limitation of
510   our current work is the necessity to manually tune the weighting schedule and other hyper-parameters,
511   whose optimal values can vary for different tasks. An interesting future direction is to explore
512   scenarios where an explicit estimation p(x) is provided by an EBM or another likelihood-based
513   estimation technique, enabling the use of more advanced samplers such as Hamiltonian Monte Carlo
514
      (HMC) with adaptive step sizes. As a powerful algorithm that can process images, DPMC might
      have the potential to cause negative social consequences, including deepfakes, misinformation, and
515
      privacy breaches. We believe that more research and resources are needed to mitigate these risks.

       C.3      PSNR AND SSIM
1010   In Table 7 and Table 8, we report the PSNR and SSIM scores for a reference. As discussed in section
1011   4.2, we chose to primarily compare models using FID and LPIPS in our paper for two main reasons.
1012   First, as discussed by Zhang et al. (2018), PSNR and SSIM capture shallow, low-level features and
1013   might fail to account for many nuances of human perception. Second, the inverse problem is an
1014   ill-posed problem. Due to information loss in the degradation process, there can be multiple possible
1015   solutions, and the model does not have to 100% faithfully reproduce all the details. As shown in
1016   the quantitative comparison of Figure 2 and Figure 11, our DPMC can fill in vivid details to the
1017   optimized samples. It is possible that these filled-in details are reasonable but not entirely the same as
1018
       the input, resulting in lower scores on PSNR or SSIM. High level features like LPIPS and FID might
       work better in judging the quality of the generated samples.
