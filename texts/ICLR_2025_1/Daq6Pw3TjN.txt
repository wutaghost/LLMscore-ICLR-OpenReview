009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In the rapidly evolving field of deep learning, specialized models have driven
012            significant advancements in tasks such as computer vision and natural language
013            processing. However, this specialization leads to a fragmented ecosystem where
014            models lack the adaptability for broader applications. To overcome this, we intro-
015            duce AutoFusion, an innovative framework that fusing distinct model’s parame-
016            ters(with the same architecture) for multi-task learning without pre-trained check-
017            points. Using an unsupervised, end-to-end approach, AutoFusion dynamically
018
               permutes model parameters at each layer, optimizing the combination through a
               loss-minimization process that does not require labeled data. We validate AutoFu-
019
               sion’s effectiveness through experiments on commonly used benchmark datasets,
020
               demonstrating superior performance over established methods like Weight Inter-
021            polation, Git Re-Basin, and ZipIt. Our framework offers a scalable and flexible
022            solution for model integration, positioning it as a powerful tool for future research
023            and practical applications.

      1 I NTRODUCTION
030   In the rapidly evolving landscape of technological innovation, deep learning models have become
031   increasingly specialized Dong et al. (2021) Lai et al. (2024a) Li et al. (2024) Lai et al. (2022) Lai
032   et al. (2024b), leading to substantial advancements in diverse fields such as computer vision and
033
      natural language processing Sharifani & Amini (2023). These specialized models, meticulously
      honed to excel in their designated niches, have undeniably propelled numerous breakthroughs Taye
034
      (2023). However, this specialization has inadvertently led to a fragmented ecosystem where models,
035
      although highly effective within their specific domains, lack the adaptability and versatility required
036   to address a wider array of challenges. This raises a pivotal question: Is it possible to amalgamate
037   the strengths of these specialized models into a unified architecture capable of performing multiple
038   tasks proficiently?
039
      The challenge of integrating specialized models into a coherent system is multifaceted. Traditional
040
      approaches to model fusion heavily depend on prior knowledge and require meticulous tuning of
041
      hyperparameters, such as specifying which layers to merge and permuting parameters according to
042   what principle. Ainsworth et al. (2022) Stoica et al. (2023) Qu & Horvath (2024). Do we have to
043   propose a new approach to any new problem? This is obviously costly and has a serious impact
044   on the usefulness of parametric fusion. Moreover, In the case where model parameters do not
045   share pre-trained parameters, merging parameters from different tasks obviously cannot be directly
046   accomplished through the previously common method of parameter permutation based on similarity.
047
      To address these challenges, we propose AutoFusion, an innovative framework designed to fuse the
048   parameters of two models, which do not share the same pre-trained parameters and perform
049   different tasks, into a single parameter capable of simultaneously accomplishing multiple tasks
050   which can be expressed figuratively as Figure 1. Drawing inspiration from the principle that ’more
051   is merrier’, unlike conventional methods that depend on predefined rules or heuristics, AutoFusion
052   aims to learn an effective permutation of model parameters to accomplish the fusing of multi-
053   task model parameters. This unsupervised training process requires no labeled data, making it a
      flexible and scalable solution for model integration.

064   The AutoFusion method is primarily based on two operations: aligning parameters that perform
065   similar functions through permutation, and retaining parameters that perform different functions
066   through their permutation as much as possible. The key to achieving this is the design of the loss
067   function, which guides the learning process. Specifically, the loss function is designed to minimize
068   the discrepancy between the fused model’s output and the outputs of the individual specialized
069   models on their respective tasks. This ensures that the fused model retains the strengths of the
070
      original models while being able to generalize across multiple tasks.
071   The unsupervised nature of AutoFusion is a critical aspect of its design. By not requiring labeled
072   data, AutoFusion can be applied to a wide range of model architectures and datasets, making it a
073   versatile tool for future research and practical applications in deep learning. The end-to-end design
074   of AutoFusion allows for dynamic permuting of model parameters at each layer, resulting in a unified
075   and robust model capable of handling multiple tasks.
076   To evaluate the efficacy of AutoFusion, we conducted experiments on the commonly used bench-
077   mark datasets Xiao et al. (2017) Clanuwat et al. (2018) LeCun et al. (1998) Krizhevsky et al. (2009),
078   simulating the scenario of merging models trained on distinct tasks. Our findings indicate that the
079   merged model achieves high accuracy across all sub-tasks, frequently outperforming established
080   techniques like Weight Interpolation Li et al. (2023), Git Re-Basin Ainsworth et al. (2022), and
081   ZipIt Stoica et al. (2023).
082   Our contributions to the field of model parameter integration can be summarized as follows:
083
      (i) End-to-End Unsupervised Framework: We present an end-to-end, unsupervised approach to
084
      model parameter fusion, eliminating the need for prior knowledge and predefined hyperparameters.
085
      This approach facilitates the dynamic permuting of model parameters at each layer, resulting in a
086   unified and robust model capable of handling multiple tasks.
087
088   (ii) Empirical Validation and Performance: Through extensive experimentation on commonly
089
      used benchmark datasets, we demonstrate the superior performance of AutoFusion compared to
      established methods such as Weight Interpolation, Git Re-Basin, and ZipIt. Our framework achieves
090
      high accuracy across all sub-tasks, highlighting its effectiveness in multi-task scenarios.
091
092   (iii) Scalability and Flexibility: The unsupervised nature of AutoFusion ensures its scalability and
093   flexibility, allowing it to be applied to a wide range of model architectures and datasets without
094   the need for labeled data. This characteristic positions our framework as a versatile tool for future
095
      research and practical applications in deep learning.
096   AutoFusion represents a significant stride forward in the field of model parameter fusion. By offering
097   an end-to-end, unsupervised approach to model fusion, we aim to unify the disparate threads of
098   specialized deep-learning models into a cohesive, adaptable ecosystem. This work not only advances
099   the state-of-the-art in model fusion but also paves the way for future research into more versatile and
100   efficient deep-learning architectures. Our code will be available on GitHub after the explanation of
101
      the double-blind review.

207   3     AUTO F USION
208   AutoFusion proposes a novel parameter fusion method to address the issue of multi-task model
209   parameter fusion in the absence of pre-trained parameters(These models do not share the same pre-
210   training weights and are all trained from a random initialization). An overview of the AutoFusion
211   method is shown in Figure 2. The specific design methodology is detailed in the following subsec-
212   tions.

215   Existing methods primarily rely on manually designed rules for parameter alignment, which are
      limited by the assumption that parameters of the same layer should exhibit high similarity. How-

217   different tasks. During merging, we must not only align parameters with similar functions but also
218   strive to retain parameters with distinct functions, enabling the fused model to perform various tasks
219   simultaneously.
220

221
      ily addressed through prior knowledge Stoica et al. (2023). It cannot be achieved through simple
222
      similarity metrics and straightforward rules, as is the case with parameter similarity alignment. This
223   compels us to consider advancing towards an end-to-end approach, where model parameter fusion
224   is accomplished directly through learning.

272   We have now constructed a learnable permutation matrix using the Sinkhorn operator, which can be
273   directly applied to parameter fusion. Therefore, the next step is to design a reasonable optimization

275   common functionalities while preserving the necessary parameter diversity for handling multiple
276   tasks.
277   According to the conclusions of Yosinski et al. (2014) Taye (2023) Zhou et al. (2022), some repre-

522
523   Currently, most research on parameter fusion testing remains confined to simple models and datasets,
524   and this paper is no exception. Existing methods have yet to yield significant results on complex
525
      datasets. Furthermore, because the automated fusion proposed by AutoFusion cannot be completely
      detached from data, we had to sample some training data to learn the permutation matrix. In the
526
      future, it may be possible to guide model fusion through fixed sets of data, but such endeavors will
527
      have to be left to future researchers.

      6     C ONCLUSION
530
531   This paper proposes a method named AutoFusion, which can learn a permutation matrix using only
532   a few input samples. This permutation matrix effectively merges the parameters of two models
533   designed for distinct tasks, resulting in a fused model capable of handling multiple tasks while
534
      maximizing accuracy across those tasks. AutoFusion can be regarded as the first work to apply an
      end-to-end approach in the field of multi-task parameter fusion. It significantly overcomes the bot-
535
      tleneck of previous works that heavily relied on prior knowledge and provides a valuable paradigm
536
      for the subsequent development of multi-task parameter fusion.

773
774
      MNIST The MNIST dataset comes from the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)
      in the United States. The training set consists of handwritten digits from 250 different individuals,
775
      with 50% from high school students and 50% from employees of the Census Bureau. The test set
776
      also contains handwritten digits in the same proportions, but authors of the test set do not overlap
777   with those of the training set. The MNIST dataset comprises a total of 70,000 images, with 60,000
778   images in the training set and 10,000 images in the test set. Each image is a 28x28 pixel grayscale
779   image representing a handwritten digit from 0 to 9. The images have a black background represented
780   by 0 and the white digits are represented by floating-point values between 0 and 1, where values
781   closer to 1 indicate a whiter color.
782
      CIFAR-10 The CIFAR-10 dataset consists of 60,000 samples, each of which is a 32x32 pixel RGB
783   image (color image). Each RGB image is divided into three channels (R channel, G channel, B
784   channel). These 60,000 samples are divided into 50,000 training samples and 10,000 test samples.
785   CIFAR-10 contains 10 classes of objects, labeled from 0 to 9, representing airplane, automobile,
786   bird, cat, deer, dog, frog, horse, ship, and truck.
787
      Fashion-MNIST Fashion-MNIST is an image dataset that serves as a replacement for the MNIST
788
      handwritten digit dataset. It was provided by the research department of Zalando, a fashion tech-
789   nology company based in Germany. The dataset consists of 70,000 frontal images of 10 different
790   categories of items. The size, format, and division of training and test sets in Fashion-MNIST are
791   identical to the original MNIST dataset. The dataset is divided into 60,000 training samples and
792   10,000 test samples, with 28x28 grayscale images that can be directly used for training models
793   designed for MNIST.
794
      KMNIST KMNIST is derived from Japanese Hiragana and Katakana characters and is maintained
795   and open-sourced by the ROIS-DS Center for Open Data in the Humanities. The dataset consists
796   of 70,000 high-resolution handwritten samples, with 10,000 samples per class, totaling 46 different
797   character types. The purpose of KMNIST is to serve as a Japanese version of the MNIST dataset,
798   used to evaluate the capabilities of machine learning and deep learning models in multi-language
799   text recognition tasks.
800
      For all datasets, we extracted 1000 images as a validation set for parameter tuning. These images
801   were divided from the original training set and do not affect the test set.
