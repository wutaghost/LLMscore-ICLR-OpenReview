009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Is there a way to design powerful AI systems based on machine learning meth-
012            ods that would satisfy probabilistic safety guarantees? With the long-term goal
013            of obtaining a probabilistic guarantee that would apply in every context, we con-
014            sider estimating a context-dependent bound on the probability of violating a given
015            safety specification. Such a risk evaluation would need to be performed at run-
016            time to provide a guardrail against dangerous actions of an AI. Noting that differ-
017            ent plausible hypotheses about the world could produce very different outcomes,
018
               and because we do not know which one is right, we derive bounds on the safety vi-
               olation probability predicted under the true but unknown hypothesis. Such bounds
019
               could be used to reject potentially dangerous actions. Our main results involve
020
               searching for cautious but plausible hypotheses, obtained by a maximization that
021            involves Bayesian posteriors over hypotheses. We consider two forms of this re-
022            sult, in the i.i.d. case and in the non-i.i.d. case, and conclude with open problems
023            towards turning such theoretical results into practical AI guardrails.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027   Ensuring that an AI system will not misbehave is a challenging open problem (Bengio et al., 2024),
028   particularly in the current context of rapid growth in AI capabilities. Governance measures and
029   evaluation-based strategies have been proposed to mitigate the risk of harm from highly capable AI
030   systems, but do not provide any form of safety guarantee when no undesired behavior is detected. In
031   contrast, the safe-by-design paradigm involves AI systems with quantitative (possibly probabilistic)
032   safety guarantees from the ground up, and therefore could represent a stronger form of protection
033
      (Dalrymple et al., 2024). However, how to design such systems remains an open problem too.
034   Since testing an AI system for violations of a safety specification in every possible context, e.g.,
035   every (query, output) pair, is impossible, we consider a rejection sampling approach that declines a
036   candidate output or action if it has a probability of violating a given safety specification that is too
037   high. The question of defining the safety specification (the violation of which is simply referred to
038   as “harm” below) is important and left to future work, possibly following up approaches such as
039
      constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022). We also note that being Bayesian about the interpretation of
      a human-specified safety specification would protect against the AI wrongly believing an incorrect
040
      interpretation. Here we instead focus on a question inspired by risk-management practice (McNeil
041
      et al., 2015): even though the true probability of harm following from some proposed action is un-
042   known, because the true data-generating process is unknown, can we bound that risk using quantities
043   that can be estimated by machine learning methods given the observed data?
044
045
      To illustrate this question, consider a committee of “wise” humans whose theories about the world
      are all equally compatible with the available data, knowing that an unknown member of the commit-
046
      tee has the correct theory. Each committee member can make a prediction about the probability of
047
      future harm that would result from following some action in some context. Marginalizing this harm
048   probability over the committee members amounts to making them vote with equal weights. If the
049   majority is aligned with the correct member’s prediction, then all is good, i.e., if the correct theory
050   predicts harm, then the committee will predict harm and can choose to avoid the harmful action. But
051   what if the correct member is in the minority regarding their harm prediction? To get a guarantee
052   that the true harm probability is below a given threshold, we could simply consider the committee
053   member whose theory predicts the highest harm probability, and we would be sure that their harm
      probability prediction upper bounds the true harm probability. In practice, committee members are


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      not equally “wise”, so we can correct this calculation based on how plausible the theory harbored
055   by each committee member is. In a Bayesian framework, the plausibility of a theory corresponds to
056   its posterior over all theories given the observed data, which is proportional to the data likelihood
057   given the theory multiplied by the prior probability of that theory.
058
      In this paper, we show how results about posterior consistency can provide probabilistic risk bounds.
059
      All the results have the form of inequalities, where the true probability of harm is upper bounded by
060
      a quantity that can in principle be estimated, given enough computational resources to approximate
061   Bayesian posteriors over theories given the data. In addition, these are not hard bounds but only hold
062   with some probability, and there is generally a trade-off between that probability and the tightness
063   of the bound. We study two scenarios in the corresponding sections: the i.i.d. data setting in §3 and
064   the non-i.i.d. data setting in §4, followed by an experiment in §5. In all cases, a key intermediate
065   result is a bound relating the Bayesian posterior on the unknown true theory and the probability of
066   other theories (with propositions labeled True theory dominance). The idea is that because the true
067   theory generated the data, its posterior tends to increase as more data is acquired, and in the i.i.d.
068   case it asymptotically dominates other theories. From such a relationship, the harm risk bound can
069
      be derived with very little algebra (yielding propositions labeled Harm probability bound).
070   We conclude this paper with a discussion of open problems that should be considered in order to
071   turn such bounds into a safe-by-design AI system, taking into account the challenge of representing
072   the notion of harm and reliable conditional probabilities, as well as the fact that, in general, the
073   estimation of the required conditional probabilities will be imperfect.
074   Related Work. The concept of blocking actions based on probabilistic criteria resembles prob-
075   abilistic shielding in Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) (Jansen et al., 2020), but our bounds do
076   not require knowledge of the true model, extending beyond Carr et al. (2023)’s work on partially
077   observable MDPs. While Beckers et al. (2023) and Richens et al. (2022) propose specific frame-
078   works for quantifying harm, our approach remains agnostic, by only requiring harmful outcomes to
079   be representable as binary events 𝐻 = 1, allowing various harm definitions, while providing conser-
080   vative probability bounds for safety-critical contexts. Osband & Van Roy (2017) study translating
081
      concentration bounds from a pure predictive setting to an MDP setting with exploration, whereas we
      address an orthogonal question: providing safety guarantees without relying on potentially harmful
082
      exploration to gain information.

084   2   S AFE - BY- DESIGN AI?
085
086   Before an AI is built and deployed, it is important that the developers have high assurances that the
087   AI will behave well. Dalrymple et al. (2024) propose an approach to “guaranteed safe AI” designs
088
      with built-in high-assurance quantitative safety guarantees, although these guarantees can sometimes
      be probabilistic and only asymptotic. It remains an open question whether and how that research
089
      program can be realized. The authors take existing examples of quantitative guarantees in safety-
090
      critical systems and motivate why such a framework should be adopted if we ever build AI systems
091   that match or exceed human cognitive abilities and could potentially act in dangerous ways. Their
092   program is motivated by current known limitations of state-of-the-art AI systems based on deep
093   learning, including the challenge of engineering AI systems that robustly act as intended (Cohen
094   et al., 2022b; Krakovna et al., 2020; Pan et al., 2021; Pang et al., 2023; Zhuang & Hadfield-Menell,
095   2020; Skalse et al., 2022; 2023; Karwowski et al., 2023; Skalse et al., 2024).
096
      The approach proposed by Dalrymple et al. (2024) has the following components: a world model
097   (which can be a distribution about hypotheses explaining the data), a safety specification (what are
098   considered unacceptable states of the world), and a verifier (a computable procedure that checks
099   whether a policy or action violates the safety specification).
100
      Here, we study a system that infers a probabilistic world model, or theory, 𝜏 and updates its estimate
101
      of 𝜏 via machine learning, using the stream of observed data 𝐷. The observations 𝐷 are assumed
102
      to come from a data-generating process given by a ground-truth world model 𝜏 ∗ , which lies in the
103   system’s space of possible theories. The inference of the theory 𝜏 is Bayesian, meaning that the
104   system maintains an estimate 𝑞 of the true posterior P(− | 𝐷) over theories: 𝑞(𝜏 | 𝐷) ≈ P(𝜏 | 𝐷),
105   where P(𝜏 | 𝐷) is proportional to the product of the prior probability P(𝜏) with the likelihood of the
106   observations under the theory, P(𝐷 | 𝜏). In the simplest case, 𝑞 is a point estimate, optimally placing
107   its mass on the mode of the posterior. Assuming an observation 𝑥 and a theory 𝜏 are independent

401   meeting the description “𝐻𝑡 = 1 when harm is recorded as having occurred” and agreeing with 𝜏 ∗ in
402   its observational predictions otherwise. If, and only if, the recording process is functioning correctly,
403   𝜏 ∗ = 𝜏 † . For as long as the recording process is functioning correctly, P(𝑖 ∗ | 𝑍1:𝑡 )/P(𝑖 † | 𝑍1:𝑡 ) =
404   P(𝑖 ∗ )/P(𝑖 † ). If the recording process ever fails at time 𝑡, then 𝑍𝑡 ∼ P 𝜏 † , not P 𝜏 ∗ , since 𝑍𝑡 is the
405
      result of this recording process; therefore, P(𝑖 ∗ | 𝑍1:𝑡 )/P(𝑖 † | 𝑍1:𝑡 ) would decrease in expectation,
      perhaps dramatically. We should not expect P(𝑖 ∗ ) to naturally win out over P(𝑖 † ), even if there are
406
      no mistakes when recording how harmful certain situations are. However, the following holds with
407
      probability approaching 1 as 𝛼 → 0: for all 𝑡, if the recording process has not failed by time 𝑡,
408   I𝑍𝛼1:𝑡 contains both 𝑖 ∗ and 𝑖 † . If 𝜏 ∗ considers tampering with the recording process to be a ‘harmful’
409   outcome, then an AI system could attempt to avoid a first instance of tampering at time 𝑡, for all 𝑡.

414

415
      arms), which is sampled uniformly at random at the start of each episode and known to the agent.
416   The reward distribution of each arm is fixed for the duration of each episode and assumed to be
417   of the following form: the reward received after taking action 𝑎 follows a unit-variance normal
418   distribution, 𝑟 (𝑎) ∼ N ( 𝑓 𝑎 · 𝑣 ∗ , 1), where 𝑣 ∗ ∈ {0, 1} 𝑑 is some vector sampled uniformly at random
419   at the start of each episode and unknown to the agent. Taking any action and observing the reward
420   gives evidence about the identity of 𝑣 ∗ and thus about the reward distributions of the other actions.
421   The agent maintains a belief over the vector used to compute the reward, beginning with a uniform
422   prior over {0, 1} 𝑑 and updating its posterior with each observation of an action-reward pair.
423   We assume that the agent samples its actions from a Boltzmann policy (with temperature 2) using the
424   expected reward of each action under its posterior given the data seen so far, meaning that a reward
425   vector 𝑣 ∈ {0, 1} 𝑑 determines a distribution over sequences of action-reward pairs. Thus each
426   𝑣 ∈ {0, 1} 𝑑 can be naturally associated with a theory 𝜏𝑣 6 , and thus 𝐼 := {0, 1} 𝑑 is an indexing set for
427   a collection of theories M = (𝜏𝑣 ) 𝑣 ∈ 𝐼 . Inference of 𝑣 with evidence collected on-policy is equivalent
428   to inference of 𝜏𝑣 given data generated by a true theory 𝜏 ∗ := 𝜏𝑣∗ . Since the policy changes across
429   timesteps, so does the distribution of action-reward pairs, so we are in the non-i.i.d. setting.

493   6   C ONCLUSION AND OPEN PROBLEMS
494
495   The approach to safety verification proposed here is based on context-dependent run-time verifi-
496
      cation because the set of possible inputs for a machine learning system is generally astronomical,
      whereas the safety of the answer to a specific question is more likely to be tractable. It focuses
497
      on the risk of wrongly interpreting the data, including the safety specification itself (called “harm”
498
      above) and exploits the fact that, as more evidence is gathered (necessary with i.i.d. data) and when
499   different theories predict different observations, the true interpretation rises towards the maximal
500   value of the Bayesian posterior. The bound is tighter with the i.i.d. data, but the i.i.d. assumption
501   is also unrealistic, and for safety-critical decisions, we would prefer to err on the side of prudence
502   and fewer assumptions. However, it provides a template to think about variants of this idea in future
503   work. Several challenges remain for turning such bounds into an operational run-time safeguard:
504
      1. Upper-bounding overcautiousness. Can we ensure that we do not underestimate the probability
505      of harm but do not massively overestimate it? Some simple theories consistent with the dataset
506      (even an arbitrarily large one) might deem non-harmful actions harmful. Can we bound how
507      much this harm-avoidance hampers the agent? A plausible approach would be to make use of a
508      mentor for the agent that demonstrates non-harmful behavior (Cohen & Hutter, 2020).
509   2. Tractability of posterior estimation. How can we efficiently estimate the required Bayesian
510      posteriors? For computational tractability, a plausible answer would rely on amortized inference,
511      which turns the difficult estimation of these posteriors into the task of training a neural net prob-
512      abilistic estimator which will be fast at run-time. Recent work on amortized Bayesian inference
513
         for symbolic models, such as causal structures (Deleu et al., 2022; 2023), and for intractable
         posteriors in language models (Guo et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2024; Venkatraman et al., 2024; Song
514
         et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024) – which are useful when prior knowledge is encoded in a pretrained
515
         foundation model – suggests that this is feasible. Advances in efficient and adaptive Monte Carlo
516      methods, e.g., for language models (Phan et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024; Lew et al., 2023), can
517      also be useful for this purpose, and MCMC approaches can complement and aid amortization
518      (Hu et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024b; Sendera et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024a).
519   3. Efficient search for a cautious theory. How can we efficiently identify a cautious but plausible
520      theory that upper-bounds the risk, since this requires an optimization at run-time? Again, a
521      plausible answer is to rely on amortized probabilistic inference, e.g., by sampling theories with
522      a low or annealed temperature, a technique that has been used for decades in the nonconvex
523      optimization literature and more recently combined with amortized sequential sampling methods
524
         (Zhang et al., 2023a;b; Zhou et al., 2024, inter alia).
      4. Partial specification of theories. Another issue is that a full explanatory theory 𝜏 for the ev-
525
         idence 𝐷 will generally be too large an object to make the above bound calculations and opti-
526
         mization sufficiently efficient. An alternative would be to derive bounds that only require partial
527      explanations, similarly to how humans tell cautious ‘stories’ about what could go wrong in uncer-
528      tain circumstances when thinking about taking a potentially dangerous decision (Bengio, 2024).
529   5. Harm specification in natural language. How can natural language safety specifications, as
530      in Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022), be turned into a random variable over which conditional
531      probabilities can be estimated? Manually labeling numerous examples matching the desired
532      constraint is impractical. Instead, Bengio (2024) propose learning to interpret natural language
533      statements as formal random variables over which probabilistic causal reasoning can occur. The
534      ambiguity of natural language can then be captured by a distribution over formal interpretations.
535
      6. Effect of approximation error. Finally, because we plan to use machine learning to estimate
         the bound, we have to consider the fact that a learned predictor will be imperfect. How do we
536
         conservatively correct for the potentially dangerous errors made by this predictor? This may
537
         be crucial if we use a neural network to estimate probabilities, since they are subject to lack of
538      robustness and adversarial attacks (Goodfellow et al., 2015; Wei et al., 2023), e.g., how do we
539      ensure that the agent proposing the actions has not learned to find loopholes in the risk estimator?
