009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep Unfolding-based Networks (DUNs) have attracted attention due to their high
012            performance and a certain degree of interpretability. However, existing DUNs
013            often lack flexibility in handling details and features in different images during
014            reconstruction, as they typically involve multiple iterative modules cascading
015            through the same structure for each iteration. To address this limitation, we propose
016            DUMoE, a novel sparsely-activated Deep Unfolding Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)
017
               architecture for Compressive Imaging (CI). By integrating the deep unfolding
               paradigm into the MoE, we enable DUMoE to adaptively reconstruct various
018
               images by utilizing different experts at each iteration stage. Specifically, we unfold
019
               traditional SpaRSA iterations into experts within DUMoE and employ top-1 switch
020            routing to save computational consumption and enhance flexibility. Additionally,
021            we introduce the Degradation-Aware Mask within the self-attention mechanism
022            to prioritize image degradation caused by dimensionality reduction in CI, thereby
023            enhancing reconstruction fidelity. Moreover, we incorporate the Multi-Scale Gate
024            to improve the DUMoE’s adaptability to image features at different scales and
025            facilitate information transmission across iteration stages. Extensive experiments
026            across various CI recovery tasks, including natural image compressive sensing,
027            magnetic resonance imaging, and snapshot compressive imaging, demonstrate the
028
               superior performance and effectiveness of DUMoE. To the best of our knowledge,
               we are the first to leverage the deep unfolding paradigm within the MoE framework.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Compressive Imaging (CI) is an imaging methodology that leverages signal sparsity or compress-
034   ibility principles to enable high-fidelity image reconstruction using markedly fewer samples than
035   conventional methods (Candès & Wakin (2008)). This capability allows CI to dramatically reduce
036   sampling complexity and data storage requirements, while concurrently enhancing imaging speed
037   and efficacy. Therefore, CI finds extensive applications across diverse domains, particularly in natural
038   Image Compressive Sensing (ICS) (Kulkarni & Turaga (2015); Zhang & Ghanem (2018); Zha et al.
039   (2023)), CS Magnetic Resonance Imaging (CS-MRI) (Lustig et al. (2007; 2008); Yang et al. (2016)),
040
      and Snapshot Compressive Imaging (SCI) (Ma et al. (2019); Yuan et al. (2021); Cheng et al. (2023)).
      Specifically, assuming that x ∈ RN denotes the vector of representation coefficients of original
041
      signal, A ∈ RM ×N (M ≪ N ) denotes the linear sampling matrix, and y ∈ RM is the measurement
042
      obtained from underdetermined system y = Ax, traditional CI recovery problem can be formulated
043   as follows:
044                                             1
                                          min ∥Ax − y∥2ℓ2 + τ R(x),                                        (1)
045                                         x 2
046
      Existing methods for CI recovery problem can be classified into three categories: traditional opti-
047
      mization methods, purely Deep Learning (DL)-based networks, and Deep Unfolding-based Networks
048   (DUNs). First, traditional optimization methods, such as Iterative Shrinkage/Thresholding Algorithm
049   (ISTA) (Beck & Teboulle (2009)), Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) (Boyd
050   (2010)), Fixed-Point Continuation (FPC) (Hale et al. (2008)), Sparse Reconstruction by Separable
051   Approximation (SpaRSA) (Wright et al. (2009)), and others (Bioucas-Dias & Figueiredo (2007);
052   Goldstein & Osher (2009); Chambolle & Pock (2011); Donoho et al. (2009)), typically rely on
053   iterative steps to gradually optimize results and achieve (sub)optimal outcomes with theoretical
      guarantees. However, these methods often require hand-crafted parameters fine-tuning, exhibit


                                                         1

054
      limited representation ability across various data, and demand significant computational time to attain
055   satisfactory results. Second, purely DL-based networks (Kulkarni et al. (2016); Gan et al. (2023a;b);
056   Shen et al. (2024)) leverage DL modules such as convolutional neural networks (CNNs), Vision Trans-
057   former (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al. (2021)), and their combinations to learn the mapping relationship
058   between measurements and ground truth images, thus achieving superior reconstruction performance.
059   However, these methods do not possess theoretically proven properties and interpretability, as they
060   often lack insights and knowledge from the CI domain. Third, DUNs (Zhang & Ghanem (2018); Gan
061   et al. (2024b); Yang et al. (2020)) are inspired by traditional iterative optimization algorithms like
062   ISTA, ADMM and FPC. They integrate DL modules into the iterative steps of these optimization
063
      algorithms, creating cascaded networks with multiple stages, where each stage represents an iteration
      within the optimization algorithm. This integration not only leads to fast and accurate CI recovery
064
      but also introduces domain-specific prior knowledge inherent to the CI domain.
065
066   However, existing DUNs often employ multiple iterative modules cascading through the same module
067   for each iteration, limiting flexibility when handling fine-grained details in diverse images. To over-
068   come this limitation, we propose DUMoE, a sparsely-activated Deep Unfolding Mixture-of-Experts
069
      (MoE) framework for CI tasks. Initially, we transform the iterative steps of the SpaRSA algorithm
      into deep unfolding modules, integrating them as experts within DUMoE. During reconstruction,
070
      rather than utilizing all experts, we adopt the top-1 switch routing, thereby reducing computational
071
      overhead and enhancing the model’s flexibility to handle details and features in distinct images.
072   Additionally, we introduce the Degradation-Aware Mask into the self-attention mechanism to enhance
073   DUMoE’s focus on image areas susceptible to degradation in various CI tasks. Furthermore, we
074   integrate U-Block into the Gate modules to leverage multi-scale features for experts selection and
075   enhanced image reconstruction, and improve feature transmission during the reconstruction.
076
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
077
078   (i) We propose DUMoE, a novel sparse MoE framework integrated with deep unfolding SpaRSA.
079   Within DUMoE, we unfold the iterations of traditional SpaRSA into experts, i.e., Deep Unfolding
080   SpaRSA Experts, which are sparsely-activated based on the top-1 switch routing. To the best of our
081
      knowledge, we are the first to leverage the deep unfolding paradigm within the MoE framework,
      yielding state-of-the-art (SOTA) results across various CI tasks.
082
083   (ii) We introduce the Degradation-Aware Mask within the self-attention mechanism of DUMoE,
084   enhancing its adaptability to image degradation in diverse CI tasks. This refinement allows DUMoE
085   to focus more attentively on degraded image details, resulting in higher-quality reconstructed images.
086   (iii) We incorporate a Multi-Scale Gate into DUMoE, which enhances the capacity of model to capture
087   fine-grained feature across different image scales, and facilitates the transmission of multi-scale
088   features at different stages, leading to significant improvements in reconstruction performance.
089
      Comprehensive comparative analyses between DUMoE and other SOTA methods across ICS, CS-
090
      MRI, and SCI, highlight the excellent performance of our proposed DUMoE, demonstrating its
091   effectiveness in various CI tasks.

105   CS-MRI, methods like ADMM-CSNet (Yang et al. (2020)), HiTDUN (Zhang et al. (2022)), MAPUN
106   (Song et al. (2023a)), along with others (Zhang & Ghanem (2018); Neyra-Nesterenko & Adcock
107   (2022); Gan et al. (2024b;a); Wang & Gan (2024)) have been developed to reconstruct high-quality
      images from partial Fourier data, enabling faster imaging and reduced data acquisition. ADMM-

132

133
      fast inference times. For instance, Williams et al. introduced the Switch Transformer (Fedus et al.
134

135   (FFN) layer in the Transformer with a sparse Switch FFN layer and enables stability in the training
136   process of large sparse models.

183   3.2.1              D EGRADATION -AWARE S ELF -ATTENTION
184
      In CI tasks, the reduction in data dimensionality during the sampling process can lead to inevitable
185
      loss of information, resulting in image quality degradation characterized by blurring, noise, and
186
      distortion. To address this challenge, we introduce a Degradation-Aware Mask (DAM) into self-
187   attention mechanism, proposing Degradation-Aware Self-Attention (DA-SA), as shown in Fig. 2e
188   and Fig. 2d. The DAM incorporates two domains of degradation perception: the image-level domain
189   and the measurement-level domain. Specifically, given x(k) as the input of the DAM in k-th iteration
190   stage, x(k) undergoes a DCAB and a 1 × 1 convolution to reduce the channel count to C0 , yielding the
191                              (k)                                                         (k)
      current estimated image x0 . On one hand, we quantify the image-level degradation d1 , resulting
192   from reduced-dimensional sampling in CI as follows:
193

194                                                                                             d1 = x0 − FeA (FA (x0 )).                                                                                                                                                                                                               (4)
195                                                 (k)

      On the other hand, we obtain the degradation d2 in the measurement-level domain by subtracting the
                                                        (k)
197   initial measurement y from the measurement of x0 , which serves as a data fidelity term to maintain
198   consistency between the measurement of current estimated image and the original measurement:
199                                                                                                       (k)                                                                (k)
                                                                                                   d2 = FeA (y − FA (x0 )). 201   Subsequently, we concatenate the d1 and d2 along the channel dimension, and use a 1 × 1
202   convolution and DCAB to increase the channel count, which is succeeded by a Sigmoid function to
203   obtain degradation weights for the degraded regions of the images. We then perform a Hadamard
204   product between x(k) and the obtained degradation weights, followed by a residual connection to
205   obtain the output of the DAM, denoted as d(k) . d(k) then undergoes a 1 × 1 convolution to further
206   increase the channel count to Ch × Nh , where Ch denotes the number of channels per head and
207   Nh represents the number of heads. Subsequently, the obtained features are combined with Value
208
      V in DA-SA using a Hadamard product to prioritize attention to the degraded parts and details in
      the images. The integration of DAM enhances the DUMoE’s ability to perceive degraded details,
209
      consequently improving feature extraction capabilities and resulting in more representative features.

      Different components. We perform ablation experiments to evaluate the contributions of switch
      routing strategy, DUSE, MSGate, and DAM within the DUMoE architecture. Specifically, we obtain
465
      the ablation cases by adopting all the experts instead of switch routing (w/o SR), replacing DUSE
466
      with two 3 × 3 convolutions and GELU activation in between (w/o DUSE), replacing MSGate with a

469   First, as shown in Tab. 4a, DUMoE achieves better or comparable performance with the same param-
470
      eter counts and fewer Floating Point Operations (FLOPs) compared to w/o SR, while also achieving
      superior performance with fewer parameters and FLOPs compared to w/o DUSE, demonstrating the
471
      effectiveness of the switch routing strategy and our proposed DUSE.
472
473   Furthermore, as illustrated in Fig. 4, we visualize and analyze the hidden states (i.e., the input features
474   of the gate module) in the MSGate of DUMoE from the aspect of representation collapse (Chi et al.
475   (2022)). We employ images from the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 test sets (Krizhevsky (2009)),
476
      comprising a total of 20,000 images, for visualizations and analyses.
477   Initially, we use Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to extract the first two principal components
478   from the hidden states. As illustrated in Fig. 4a and Fig. 4b, each data point represents an image to be
479   routed, with its color corresponding to the assigned DUSE. In Fig. 4a, the points are predominantly
480   mixed together, indicative of unbalanced routing. Conversely, in Fig. 4b, DUMoE exhibits a well-
481   structured feature space with clear cluster distinctions, suggesting successful projection of images by
482
      our MSGate while preserving routing features.
483   Subsequently, we apply Gaussian Kernel-Density Estimation (KDE) to the hidden states processed
484   by PCA, using the Scott method as the bandwidth estimator. Compared to Fig. 4c, Fig. 4d showcases
485   uniformly distributed hidden states, indicating balanced expert assignment and reduced representation
      collapse (Chi et al. (2022)).

534   6                      C ONCLUSION
535
536   In this paper, we propose DUMoE, a novel sparse Deep Unfolding MoE framework for CI tasks.
537   DUMoE addresses key challenges in CI recovery problems by integrating innovative components:
538   the DAM, MSGate, and DUSE. Notably, our work represents the first attempt to study deep unfolding
539   paradigm within the MoE framework. Extensive experiments across various CI tasks, including ICS,
      CS-MRI and SCI, demonstrate the superior performance and effectiveness of our proposed DUMoE.

       A.6    L IMITATIONS
1246
1247   In our main paper, we primarily focus on utilizing the ℓ1 -norm as the image prior to enforce sparsity in
1248   the transform domain and employ soft thresholding to address Eq. (8). However, it’s worth noting that
1249   various other image priors exist, including the ℓ0 -norm, total variation, low-rank, etc., for different
1250   applications. Besides, many deep unfolding-based methods leverage deep denoising networks to
1251   replace image prior terms (Song et al. (2023c); Mou et al. (2022); Cai et al. (2022c)). Moving forward,
1252   we aim to explore a broader spectrum of image prior terms, thereby enhancing the versatility of
1253
       DUMoE to address a wider array of image ill-posed problems, such as image super-resolution, image
       deraining, image denoising, etc.
1254
1255   Furthermore, the proposed DUMoE framework is designed as a general approach capable of address-
1256   ing a broad range of CI tasks, including ICS, CS-MRI, and SCI, without being restricted to a single
1257   domain. This generality allows DUMoE to be applied to various CI tasks with diverse data charac-
1258   teristics (e.g., 2D and 3D) and requirements (e.g., different sampling and initialization processes).
1259
       However, this broad applicability may result in performance trade-offs for highly specialized tasks.
       We view this as an area for future improvement, where incorporating more specialized knowledge
1260
       into the DUMoE framework could potentially mitigate these trade-offs and yield more competitive
1261
       results in specialized applications.
