                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
               Large language models (LLMs) demonstrate exceptional performance across a
015
               variety of tasks, yet they are often affected by hallucinations and the timeli-
016
               ness of knowledge. Leveraging knowledge graphs (KGs) as external knowledge
017            sources has emerged as a viable solution, but existing methods for LLM-based
018            knowledge graph question answering (KGQA) are often limited by step-by-step
019            decision-making on KGs, restricting the global planning and reasoning capabili-
020            ties of LLMs, or they require fine-tuning or pre-training on specific KGs. To ad-
021            dress these challenges, we propose Knowledge graph Assisted Reasoning Path
022            Aggregation (KARPA), a novel framework that harnesses the global planning
023            abilities of LLMs for efficient and accurate KG reasoning on KGs. KARPA oper-
024            ates through a three-step process: pre-planning, retrieving, and reasoning. First,
025
               KARPA uses the LLM’s global planning ability to pre-plan logically coherent re-
               lation paths based on the provided question and relevant relations within the KG.
026
               Next, in the retrieving phase, relation paths with high semantic similarity to the
027
               pre-planned paths are extracted as candidate paths using a semantic embedding
028            model. Finally, these candidate paths are provided to the LLM for comprehensive
029            reasoning. Unlike existing LLM-based KGQA methods, KARPA fully leverages
030            the global planning and reasoning capabilities of LLMs without requiring stepwise
031            traversal or additional training, and it is compatible with various LLM architec-
032            tures. Extensive experimental results show that KARPA achieves state-of-the-art
033            performance in KGQA tasks, delivering both high efficiency and accuracy. Our
034            code is available on https://anonymous.4open.science/r/KARPA/.

      In recent years, large language models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Achiam et al., 2023; Bai
      et al., 2023) have revolutionized natural language processing, demonstrating remarkable capabilities
040
      in understanding and generating human-like text across a range of tasks. Their ability to leverage
041
      vast amounts of data leads to impressive performance in areas such as information extraction (Xu
042   et al., 2023), summarization (Jin et al., 2024), and question answering (Louis et al., 2024). However,
043   these models face notable challenges, particularly in maintaining up-to-date knowledge, domain-
044   specific knowledge (Zhang et al., 2024), or dealing with hallucinations (Zhang et al., 2023b; Huang
045   et al., 2023) where the models produce incorrect or nonsensical outputs.
046
      Knowledge graphs (KGs) present a promising solution to enhance the reasoning capabilities of
047
      LLMs by providing structured, reliable external knowledge (Zhu et al., 2024; Pan et al., 2024).
048   Existing approaches that integrate LLMs with KGs generally fall into two categories. The first cate-
049   gory involves direct interaction between the LLM and the KGs (Sun et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023),
050   where the LLM explores the KG step-by-step. The second category, including methods such as
051   reasoning on graphs (RoG) (Luo et al., 2023), involves generating retrieval information to extract
052   knowledge from KGs. This often requires fine-tuning or pre-training the LLM on specific KG data
053   (Li et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2024). However, both approaches have notable limitations: (1) The
      direct interaction method often relies on local search strategies such as beam search, which can result

090   To address these limitations, we propose Knowledge graph Assisted Reasoning Path Aggregation
091   (KARPA), an innovative framework that leverages the global planning capabilities of LLMs along-

093
      of three key steps: pre-planning, retrieving, and reasoning, as shown in Figure 1(c). In the pre-
      planning phase, KARPA enables the LLM to generate initial relation paths for the provided question
094
      using LLM’s inherent reasoning and planning capabilities. With these inital relation paths, KARPA
095
      employs a semantic embedding model (Ruder et al., 2019) to identify candidate relations that are
096   semantically similar to the relations within the initial paths. The LLM can then create coherent re-
097   lation paths that logically connect the topic entity to potential answer entities using these candidate
098   relations. During the retrieving phase, KARPA employs an embedding model to identify candidate
099   paths within the KG that exhibit the highest similarity to the relation paths generated by the LLM in
100   the pre-planning phase. This avoids locally optimal issues encountered in previous methods. Finally,
101   during the reasoning step, the candidate paths and their corresponding tail entities are provided to
102   the LLM to formulate final answers. The detail of our framework is shown in Figure 2.
103   KARPA offers several key advantages over existing LLM-based KGQA methods: (1) KARPA fully
104   exploits the global planning and reasoning abilities of LLMs, generating comprehensive relation
105   paths without the need for iterative traversal within KGs, which significantly reduces interactions
106   between the LLM and the KG. (2) Our embedding-based extraction strategy avoids the locally opti-
107   mal solution that arises from the stepwise interactions between LLMs and KGs, ensuring more ef-
      fective exploration of the KGs. (3) KARPA operates in a training-free manner, making it adaptable

127   2   R ELATED W ORK
128
129   Prompt-Based Reasoning with LLMs. Large Language Models (LLMs), such as LLaMA (Tou-
130   vron et al., 2023a;b), Qwen (Bai et al., 2023), and GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), have made substan-
131   tial progress in enhancing reasoning capabilities by leveraging their vast internal knowledge. Vari-
132
      ous prompt-based methods have been proposed to further optimize these capabilities. For instance,
      Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2022) facilitates a structured reasoning process by
133
      breaking down intricate tasks into manageable steps, significantly boosting performance in areas
134
      such as mathematical reasoning (Jie et al., 2023) and logical inference (Zhao et al., 2023). Building
135   on CoT, several variants have been introduced to further optimize reasoning effectiveness, including
136   Auto-CoT (Zhang et al., 2022), Zero-Shot-CoT (Kojima et al., 2022), and Complex-CoT (Fu et al.,
137   2022). Additionally, newer frameworks like the Tree of Thoughts (ToT) (Yao et al., 2024) and Graph
138   of Thoughts (GoT) (Besta et al., 2024) have expanded the scope of LLM reasoning, enabling the
139   models to generate intermediate steps and sub-goals, thereby enhancing their versatility across di-
140   verse reasoning tasks. Lately, OpenAI o1 series models represent a significant advancement in LLM
141   reasoning, allowing the LLM to develop an extensive internal chain of thought. These developments
142   underscore the importance of tailored prompts in maximizing LLMs’ reasoning potential.
143
144
      LLM-Based Knowledge Graph Question Answering. The integration of KGs with LLMs for
      question answering has emerged as a promising approach to enhance reasoning capabilities and mit-
145
      igate hallucination phenomena. Unlike traditional CoT method that leverage the internal knowledge
146
      of LLMs, the incorporation of KGs facilitates access to structured external knowledge (He et al.,
147   2022; Wang et al., 2023). Approaches such as Think-on-Graph (ToG) (Sun et al., 2023), Interactive-
148   KBQA (Xiong et al., 2024) and StructGPT (Jiang et al., 2023) enable real-time interactions between
149   LLMs and KGs. However, these methods often entail extensive interactions that can lead to inef-
150   ficiencies. Reasoning on graphs (RoG) (Luo et al., 2023) uses instruction-tuned LLaMa2-Chat-7B
151   to generate reasoning paths and achieves state-of-the-art performance on KGQA tasks. Similarly,
152   methods such as chain of knowledge (Li et al., 2023c) and other approaches (Huang et al., 2024; Pan
153   et al., 2024) employ LLMs to generate retrieval information for KGQA tasks. However, these meth-
154   ods require pre-training or fine-tuning process, which can be both costly and time-consuming. Addi-
155
      tionally, methods such as UniKGQA (Jiang et al., 2022) and KG-CoT (Zhao et al., 2024) require the
      training of specific models for KG information retrieval, further complicating their implementation.

187   Knowledge Graphs (KGs). A Knowledge Graph (KG) is a structured representation of information,
188   which can be represented as G = (E, R), where E denotes the set of entities and R denotes the set
189   of relations. Each relation r ∈ R connects a pair of entities (ei , ej ) such that ei , ej ∈ E.
190   Relation Paths and Reasoning Paths. Relation paths are sequences of relations that connect two
191   entities within a KG. A relation path P from topic entity et to answer entity ea can be expressed
192   as: P = (r1 , r2 , . . . , rn ), where each ri ∈ R denotes the relations along the path. Reasoning
193   paths extend this concept of relation paths by incorporating nintermediate entities aloneo the path. A
194                                                                      r1    r2          rn
      reasoning path Pr from et to ea can be represented as Pr = et →       e1 →  e2 . . . →  ea .

196   Knowledge Graph Question Answering (KGQA). Knowledge Graph Question Answering
197   (KGQA) involves the task of responding to questions by leveraging the information stored within
198   KGs. Given a query Q, the goal of KGQA is to retrieve an answer A defined as: A = f (Q, G),
199   where f is a function that extracts the answer based on query Q over the KG G.
200   Embedding Models and Semantic Similarity. Embedding Models facilitate the representation
201   of words and sentences in a continuous vector space, enabling semantic embedding and similarity
202   measurement. An embedding function Φ : R → Rd maps a sentence R to d-dimensional vectors.

214   In this section, we present our proposed Knowledge graph Assisted Reasoning Path Aggregation
215   (KARPA) framework, which leverages the strengths of LLMs and an embedding model to enhance
      KGQA. The approach consists of three key steps: pre-planning, retrieving, and reasoning.

      4.1     P RE -P LANNING WITH LLM
217
218   The pre-planning phase is a crucial component of our KARPA framework, where we leverage the
219   global planning capabilities of LLMs to generate initial relation paths Pinitial . This phase initiates
220   the reasoning process by allowing the LLM to analyze the input question Q and the associated topic
221   entity et . By leveraging the reasoning capability of LLM, KARPA is able to propose paths that are
222   not only logically coherent but also have the potential to lead to the answer entities Ea .
223
224   Initial Planning Using LLM KARPA start by leveraging the LLM’s global planning capabilities
225   to generate initial relation paths based on the provided question Q, as shown in Figure 2. The LLM
226   outputs a set of potential relation paths P as follows:
227                 P = {p1 , p2 , . . . , pm }   where pi = (r1i , r2i , . . . , rni i ) for i = 1, 2, . . . , m.   (2)
228
229   In Equation 2, each pi represents a relation path consisting of ni relations, rji ∈ R, that are logically
230   coherent and could connect a topic entity et to potential answer entities ea . The goal is to create
231   several paths of varying lengths that could serve as candidates for relations extraction.
232
233   Relation Extraction Strategy Once the initial relation paths P are generated, we decompose each
234   path pi into its constituent relations. For each path pi ∈ P , the relations are organized into a relation
235
      list denoted as Ri = {r1i , r2i , . . . , rni i }. For each relation rji in list Ri , we utilize an embedding
      model to extract top-K semantically similar relations from the entire KG, as shown in Figure 2.

242
      relations that best align semantically with the initial relations, ensuring that the LLM has access to
      relevant relations beyond just the immediate neighbors of current entity in the KG.

      4.3     R EASONING WITH LLM
347
348   In the reasoning step, we combine the candidate relation paths with their respective entities into a
349   prompt for the LLM to reference during the final answer determination, as shown in Figure 2. The
350   reasoning process of LLM can be formally expressed as:
351
                                  Answer = LLM(Q, Pc , et , ea ), Pc = {r1 , r2 , . . . , rn }.                        (10)
352
353   Given the top-K candidate relation paths Pc and the question Q, the LLM can effectively assess
354   whether the provided connections lead to a valid answer to Q. If the top-K candidate paths do
355   not yield a precise answer, we leverage the LLM’s inherent knowledge to provide an appropriate
356   response. The KARPA framework facilitates the LLM’s ability to evaluate multiple reasoning paths
357
      in parallel, thereby enhancing the overall efficiency of LLM-based KGQA tasks.

      tween paths of different lengths, we select 16 paths with the highest similarity for each initial path
417
      from relation paths of various lengths, which are then used as candidate paths for the reasoning step.

483   5.3.1    I NTERACTION S TEPS C OMPARISON
484
485   We evaluate the average number of interactions required to obtain an answer for both ToG and
      KARPA across multiple LLMs and datasets. The results, presented in Table 3, show that KARPA


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      consistently reduces the number of interactions by more than half compared to ToG, while main-
487   taining superior performance in terms of answer accuracy and reasoning quality.
488
489   The primary reason for this efficiency lies in the differences between the interaction mechanisms
490
      of the two approaches. In ToG, the stepwise relation selection on KGs is not only time-consuming
      but also leads to a higher demand for computational resources during interaction with the KG. In
491
      contrast, KARPA requires only two interactions with the LLM during the pre-planning step to gen-
492
      erate the initial relation paths. These initial paths form a coherent reasoning chain that serves as
493   the backbone for the subsequent retrieval process. Instead of repeatedly invoking the LLM for re-
494   lation extracting, KARPA leverages an embedding model to extract similar relation paths from the
495   KG based on semantic similarity. This significantly reduces the overall interaction steps and the
496   computational cost of KG-based reasoning.

531
532
      In this paper, we propose KARPA, a novel framework designed to enhance LLM-based KGQA by
533
      utilizing the global planning and reasoning capabilities of LLMs. KARPA addresses key limitations
534   of existing approaches by improving both accuracy and efficiency, while providing a plug-and-play
535   solution through its structured pre-planning, retrieving, and reasoning processes. Our experiments
536   demonstrate that KARPA consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods across multiple datasets
537   and evaluation metrics. Furthermore, its training-free nature enables seamless integration with a
538   variety of LLMs, offering broad applicability to different KGQA tasks. By optimizing LLM-KG
539   interactions, KARPA improves reasoning efficiency and effectiveness, highlighting its potential as a
      robust approach for future retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems.

761   A    A LGORITHM FOR KARPA
762
763   In this section, we present the pseudo-code for the Knowledge graph Assisted Reasoning Path Ag-
764   gregation (KARPA) framework, as shown in Algorithm 1. The pseudo-code outlines the key compo-
765   nents of our approach, including the pre-planning, retrieval, and reasoning phases. It demonstrates
766   the interaction between the large language model (LLM) and the embedding model in generating,
767   retrieving, and refining relation paths, which are crucial for improving LLM-based KGQA tasks.
768
769    Algorithm 1: KARPA Framework
770
        Input: Question Q, Topic entity et , Knowledge Graph KG, Large Language Model LLM ,
771             Embedding Model
772     Output: Answers Ea
773     Pre-Planning Phase:
774     Generate initial paths Pi = {p1 , p2 , . . . , pm } using LLM (Q, et );
775     for each path pi = (r1i , r2i , . . . , rni i ) do
776         Decompose pi into relation list Ri = {r1i , r2i , . . . , rni i };
777         for each relation rji in Ri do
778
                Retrieve top-K similar relations Rji = Top-K(sim(rij , r));
779
            end
780
        end
781
        Re-plan relation paths Preplan = LLM(Q, Rji ) based on retrieved relations Rji ;
782
        Retrieving Phase:
783
784
        Extract relation paths Pr with length L ∈ len(Preplan );
        for each path p in Preplan do
785
            Compute similarity between paths using heuristic value
786         Pretrieved = Heuristic(sim(p, pr ), pr ∈ Pr );
787         Retrieve top-K similar paths P = Top-K(Pretrieved ) as Pcandidate ;
788     end
789     Reasoning Phase:
790
        Combine candidate relation paths Pcandidate = {r1 , r2 , . . . , rn } with et , ea into prompt;
791
        Predict final answer Ea = LLM(Q, Pcandidate , et , ea );
792
        return Ea

796   B    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
797
798   Model Invocation. Our method, KARPA, along with the baseline comparison methods such as
799   CoT (Wei et al., 2022) and ToG (Sun et al., 2023), is all implemented via API calls to various
800   large language models (LLMs). These LLMs are queried dynamically throughout the experimental
801   pipeline to perform pre-planning, retrieving, and reasoning steps.
802
803   Experimental Setup. During the pre-planning stage, the initial paths generated by the LLM are
804   decomposed and stored, along with the query, into a list. For each element in this list, we retrieve
805   the top-k relations, where the total number of retrieved relations does not exceed 30. These relations
806
      are semantically closest to the elements based on the LLM’s initial output.
807   In the retrieving step, KARPA selects the top 16 relation paths with the highest similarity for each
808   initial relation path. These paths serve as candidate paths for reasoning step. In the reasoning step,
809   we limit the number of candidate paths input to the LLM at one time to a maximum of 8, ensuring
      that the reasoning process remains manageable and focused on the most relevant paths.

1151   E.1    LLM C ALL F REQUENCY
1152
1153   KARPA utilizes LLMs in three steps: initial planning, re-planning, and reasoning. However, the
1154   re-planning step often generates multiple candidate paths, especially for complex questions or when
1155   there are multiple topic entities. Each of these candidate paths is matched to paths within the KG
1156   using semantic similarity to retrieve the most relevant reasoning paths. In the reasoning step, the
1157
       top-K retrieved paths of each candidate paths are provided to the LLM in batches to generate the
       final answers. As the complexity of the query increases (e.g., in the CWQ dataset), the number of
1158
       topic entities and candidate paths also increases. Consequently, the number of LLM calls during the
1159
       reasoning step rises.
1160
1161   In Table 3, we observe that the CWQ dataset requires more LLM calls compared to WebQSP due
1162   to its more complex query logic. However, compared to methods that relies on direct interation be-
1163
       tween LLMs and KGs such as ToG, where LLM call frequency increases significantly with question
       complexity, KARPA demonstrates much more stable scaling. For instance, in Table 3, ToG requires
1164
       an average of 3.1 additional calls for the CWQ dataset, while KARPA requires only 0.5 additional
1165
       calls when using GPT-4o and GPT-4.

1168   E.2    E FFECTIVENESS B EYOND KGQA TASKS
1169
1170   While KARPA is currently designed to address challenges in KGQA tasks, following the settings of
1171   prior works such as RoG and ToG, its methodology is generalizable to other knowledge-intensive
1172   tasks.
1173
       KARPA’s core idea lies in letting LLMs generate complete reasoning chains instead of disrupting
1174   reasoning continuity with step-by-step searching. This approach mimics human reasoning processes
1175   and enhances reasoning efficiency. For example, in knowledge-intensive task such as the retrieval of
1176   academic papers, KARPA could generate reasoning chains like “research field → target journal/con-
1177   ference → specific keywords”, and then retrieve the corresponding paper using semantic similarity.
1178   When extracting information from books, the reasoning chain like “book title → relevant chapter
1179   → relevant paragraphs” could streamline the information retrieval. This reasoning-chain generation
1180   aligns with human thought processes, making it both intuitive and adaptable to diverse knowledge-
1181   intensive tasks.

1209   In the field of large language models (LLMs), researchers explore how to combine internal knowl-
1210   edge with external information to enhance reasoning abilities. Existing models utilize a vast internal
1211   knowledge base and achieve significant progress in reasoning tasks. To further optimize these ca-
1212   pabilities, researchers propose various prompt-based methods, such as Chain of Thought (CoT) (Li
1213
       et al., 2023c) prompting. This method breaks down complex tasks into manageable steps, promot-
       ing structured reasoning and excelling in mathematical and logical reasoning. Building on CoT,
1214
       researchers also develop variants like Auto-CoT (Zhang et al., 2022), Zero-Shot-CoT (Kojima et al.,
1215
       2022), Complex-CoT (Fu et al., 2022), and new frameworks such as Tree of Thoughts (ToT) (Yao
1216   et al., 2024), which further expand the application range of LLMs.
1217
1218   Additionally, with regard to the “decoding” problem of the reasoning process, Self-consistency CoT
1219
       (Wang et al., 2022) serves as a representative method. It generates multiple reasoning paths through
       manually designed prompts and employs a “majority voting” mechanism to identify the “most con-
1220
       sistent” path, thereby enhancing CoT performance. CoT verification (Weng et al., 2022) is an-
1221
       other important research direction that allows models to self-verify the correctness of their answers
1222   through multiple rounds of reasoning. Self-Verification samples multiple candidate reasoning paths
1223   and ranks them based on whether the conditions satisfy the conclusions. Recently, OpenAI launches
1224   the o1 series models, marking a significant advancement in LLM reasoning abilities, allowing mod-
1225   els to develop extensive internal chains of thought and further tap into their reasoning potential.

1228   F.2   E MBEDDING MODELS AND E MBEDDING - BASED METHODS .
1229
1230   Embedding models. Embedding models have revolutionized how we represent and understand text
1231   by converting words and sentences into dense vector representations (Mikolov et al., 2013). These
1232   embedding models capture the semantic meaning of the text, enabling models to effectively mea-
1233
       sure the similarity and relationships between different texts. In recent years, significant progress has
       been made in the field of text embeddings, largely due to the emergence of pre-trained language
1234
       models (Vaswani et al., 2017). Models like BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) and its variants have become
1235
       fundamental tools for efficiently encoding the underlying semantics of data. Key advancements in
1236   contrastive learning (Xiong et al., 2020), particularly improvements in negative sampling and knowl-
1237   edge distillation applications (Hofstätter et al., 2021), also contribute significantly to the progress
1238   in this field. As a result, there is a growing trend to develop universal embedding models that can
1239   uniformly support a variety of applications, ranging from information retrieval to natural language
1240   processing tasks. Prominent emerging embedding models include Contriever (Izacard et al., 2021),
1241   LLM-Embedder (Zhang et al., 2023a) and Open Text Embedding (Neelakantan et al., 2022). These
       models significantly advance the application of text embeddings across various general tasks.

       F.3   K NOWLEDGE G RAPHS AND R ETRIEVAL -AUGMENTED M ETHODS .
1243
1244   Knowledge graphs and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020) play a crucial
1245   role in enhancing various downstream tasks, such as question answering, text generation, and infor-
1246   mation retrieval. Early research Sun et al. (2018) uses random walk algorithms to retrieve informa-
1247   tion from knowledge graphs. Subsequent studies Li et al. (2023a); Yu et al. (2021) employ BM25
1248   and DPR algorithms for knowledge graph-based information retrieval, further improving the perfor-
1249   mance of LLMs. UniKGQA Jiang et al. (2022) integrates the retrieval process with LLMs to achieve
1250
       state-of-the-art performance in knowledge graph question-answering tasks. GraphRAG Edge et al.
       (2024) designs a powerful process that extracts structured data from unstructured text using LLMs.
1251
       These studies collectively demonstrate that information retrieved from knowledge graphs signifi-
1252
       cantly enhances the reasoning capabilities of LLMs. KELP (Liu et al., 2024) utilizes an embedding
1253   model to filter reasoning paths from the KG. However, it does not leverage the reasoning capabilities
1254   of LLMs and is limited to reasoning paths within a 2-hop range, restricting its applicability to more
1255   complex queries. KnowledgeNavigator (Guo et al., 2024a) employs an iterative process where the
1256   LLM retrieves and filters relevant knowledge directly from the KG, while Paths-over-Graph (PoG)
1257   (Tan et al., 2024) enhances the reliability of LLM-based reasoning by leveraging KG pruning and
1258   subgraph reasoning. However, similar to ToG, both methods remain fully dependent on repeated in-
1259   teractions between the LLM and KG, which can result in high computational overhead. LightRAG
1260   (Guo et al., 2024b) capitalizes on graph structures by combining LLM-based text indexing with
1261
       a two-layer retrieval mechanism, improving its capability to integrate information across diverse
       sources.
