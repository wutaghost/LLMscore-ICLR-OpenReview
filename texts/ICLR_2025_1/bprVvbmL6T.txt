009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Off-policy evaluation (OPE) is crucial for assessing a target policy’s impact offline
012            before its deployment. However, achieving accurate OPE in large state spaces
013            remains challenging. This paper studies state abstractions – originally designed
014            for policy learning – in the context of OPE. Our contributions are three-fold: (i)
015
               We define a set of irrelevance conditions central to learning state abstractions for
               OPE, and derive a backward-model-irrelevance condition for achieving irrelevance
016
               in (marginalized) importance sampling ratios by constructing a time-reversed
017
               Markov decision process (MDP). (ii) We propose a novel iterative procedure that
018            sequentially projects the original state space into a smaller space, resulting in
019            a deeply-abstracted state, which substantially simplifies the sample complexity
020            of OPE arising from high cardinality. (iii) We prove the Fisher consistencies of
021            various OPE estimators when applied to our proposed abstract state spaces.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026
      Motivation. Off-policy evaluation (OPE) serves as a crucial tool for assessing the impact of a
      newly developed policy using a pre-collected historical data before its deployment in high-stake
027
      applications, such as healthcare (Murphy et al., 2001), recommendation systems (Chapelle & Li,
028
      2011), education (Mandel et al., 2014), dialog systems (Jiang et al., 2021) and robotics (Levine et al.,
029   2020). A fundamental challenge in OPE is its “off-policy” nature, wherein the target policy to be
030   evaluated differs from the behavior policy that generates the offline data. This distributional shift is
031   particularly pronounced in environments with large state spaces of high cardinality. Theoretically, the
032   error bounds for estimating the target policy’s Q-function and value decrease rapidly as the state space
033   dimension increases (Hao et al., 2021a; Chen & Qi, 2022). Empirically, large state space significantly
034   challenges the performance of state-of-the-art OPE algorithms (Fu et al., 2020; Voloshin et al., 2021).
035   Although different policies induce different trajectories in the large ground state space, they can pro-
036   duce similar paths when restricted to relevant, lower-dimensional state spaces (Pavse & Hanna, 2023).
037   Consequently, applying OPE to these abstract spaces can significantly mitigate the distributional shift
038   between target and behavior policies, enhancing the accuracy in predicting the target policy’s value.
039   This makes state abstraction, designed to reduce state space cardinality, particularly appealing for
040   OPE. However, despite the extensive literature on studying state abstractions for policy learning (see
041   Section 2 for details), it has been hardly explored in the context of OPE.
042   Contributions. This paper aims to systematically investigate state abstractions for OPE to address
043   the aforementioned gap. Our main contributions include:
044   1. Introduction of a set of irrelevance conditions for OPE, and derivation of a backward-model-
045      irrelevance condition for state abstractions to achieve irrelevance in marginalized importance
046      sampling ratios by constructing a time-reversed Markov decision process (MDP, Puterman, 2014)
047      that swaps the future and past.
048   2. Development of a novel iterative procedure to sequentially compress the state space. Specifically,
049      within each iteration, our algorithm consistently produces a state space that is either smaller
050
         in size or remains the same. Through its iterative nature, the proposed approach produces a
         deeply-abstracted state space, which substantially reduces the sample complexity of OPE.
051
      3. Validations of various OPE methods when applied to the proposed abstract state spaces.
052
053   Organization. The rest of the paper is structured as follows. Section 2 is dedicated to the literature
      review of related works. MDP-related notions and OPE methodologies relevant to our proposal are

058   2    R ELATED WORK
059
060   Our proposal is closely related to OPE and state abstraction. Additional related work on confounder
061   selection in causal inference is relegated to Appendix A.
062   Off-policy evaluation. OPE aims to estimate the expected return of a given target policy, utilizing
063   historical data generated by a possibly different behavior policy (Dudík et al., 2014; Uehara et al.,
064   2022). The majority of methods in the literature can be classified into the following three categories:
065
      1. Value-based methods that estimate the target policy’s return by learning either a value function
066
         (Sutton et al., 2008; Luckett et al., 2019; Li et al., 2024) or a Q-function (Le et al., 2019; Feng
067      et al., 2020; Hao et al., 2021b; Liao et al., 2021; Chen & Qi, 2022; Shi et al., 2022) from the data.
068   2. Importance sampling (IS) methods that adjust the observed rewards using the IS ratio, i.e., the
069      ratio of the target policy over the behavior policy, to address their distributional shift. There are
070      two major types: sequential IS (SIS, Precup, 2000; Thomas et al., 2015; Hanna et al., 2019; Hu &
071      Wager, 2023) which employs a cumulative IS ratio, and marginalized IS (MIS, Liu et al., 2018;
072      Nachum et al., 2019; Xie et al., 2019; Dai et al., 2020; Yin & Wang, 2020; Wang et al., 2023)
073      which uses the MIS ratio to mitigate the high variance of the SIS estimator.
074   3. Doubly robust methods or their variants that employ both the IS ratio and the value/reward
075
         function to enhance the robustness of OPE (Zhang et al., 2013; Jiang & Li, 2016; Thomas &
         Brunskill, 2016; Farajtabar et al., 2018; Kallus & Uehara, 2020; Tang et al., 2020; Uehara et al.,
076
         2020; Shi et al., 2021; Kallus & Uehara, 2022; Liao et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2023).
077
078   However, none of the aforementioned works studied state abstraction, which is our primary focus.
079   State abstraction. State abstraction aims to obtain a parsimonious state representation to simplify the
080   sample complexity of reinforcement learning (RL), while ensuring that the optimal policy restricted
081   to the abstract state space attains comparable values as in the original, ground state space. There
082   is an extensive literature on the theoretical and methodological development of state abstraction,
083   particularly bisimulation — a type of abstractions that preserve the Markov property in the abstracted
084   state (Singh et al., 1994; Dean & Givan, 1997; Givan et al., 2003; Ferns et al., 2004; Ravindran,
085   2004; Jong & Stone, 2005; Li et al., 2006; Ferns et al., 2011; Abel et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2017;
086   Castro, 2020; Allen et al., 2021; Abel, 2022). In particular, Li et al. (2006) analyzed five irrelevance
087
      conditions for optimal policy learning. Unlike the aforementioned works that focus on policy learning,
      we introduce irrelevance conditions for OPE, and propose abstractions that satisfy these irrelevant
088
      properties. Meanwhile, the proposed abstraction for achieving irrelevance for the MIS ratio resembles
089
      the Markov state abstraction developed by Allen et al. (2021) in the context of policy learning, while
090   relaxing their requirement for the behavior policy to be Markovian.
091
092   More recently, Pavse & Hanna (2023) made a pioneering attempt to study state abstraction for OPE,
093
      proving its benefits in enhancing OPE accuracy. However, they primarily focused on MIS estimators.
      In contrast, our theoretical analysis applies to a broader range of OPE estimators, covering all three
094
      aforementioned categories. Moreover, their abstraction did not achieve MIS-ratio irrelevance. Nor
095
      did they implement the iterative procedure.
096
097   Lastly, state abstraction is also related to variable selection (Kolter & Ng, 2009; Geist & Scherrer,
098   2011; Geist et al., 2012; Nguyen et al., 2013; Fan et al., 2016; Guo & Brunskill, 2017; Shi et al., 2018;
099
      Zhang & Zhang, 2018; Qi et al., 2020; Hao et al., 2021a; Ma et al., 2023) as well as representation
      learning for both policy learning (see e.g., Gelada et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020; Uehara et al.,
100
      2021b) and OPE (see e.g., Wang et al., 2021; Chang et al., 2022; Ni et al., 2023; Pavse & Hanna,
101
      2024).

      3.2   S TATE ABSTRACTIONS FOR POLICY LEARNING
147
148   Let M = ⟨S, A, T , R, ρ0 , γ⟩ be the ground MDP. A state abstraction ϕ is a mapping from the state
149   space S to certain abstract state space X = {ϕ(s) : s ∈ S}. Below, we review some commonly
150   studied definitions of state abstraction designed for learning the optimal policy π ∗ ; see Jiang (2018).
151
152   Definition 1 (π ∗ -irrelevance) ϕ is π ∗ -irrelevant if there exists an optimal policy π ∗ , such that for
153
      any s(1) , s(2) ∈ S whenever ϕ(s(1) ) = ϕ(s(2) ), we have π ∗ (a|s(1) ) = π ∗ (a|s(2) ) for any a ∈ A.
154
      Definition 2 (Q∗ -irrelevance) ϕ is Q∗ -irrelevant if for any s(1) , s(2) ∈ S whenever ϕ(s(1) ) =
155
      ϕ(s(2) ), the optimal Q-function satisfies Q∗ (a, s(1) ) = Q∗ (a, s(2) ) for any a ∈ A.
156
157   Definitions 1 and 2 are easy to understand, requiring the optimal policy/Q-function to depend on
158   a state s only through its abstraction ϕ(s). In practical terms, these definitions encourage the
159   transformation of raw MDP data into a new sequence of state-action-reward triplets (ϕ(S), A, R) for
160   policy learning. However, the transformed data may not necessarily satisfy the Markov assumption.
161   This leads us to define the following model-irrelevance, which aims to preserve the MDP structure
      while ensuring π ∗ - and Q∗ -irrelevance.

195   3.3   OPE METHODOLOGIES
196
197   We focus on four OPE methods, covering the three families of estimators introduced in Section 2.
198   Each method employs a specific formula to identify J(π), which we detail below. The first method
199
      is a popular value-based approach – the Q-function-based method. The second and third methods
      are the two major IS estimators: SIS and MIS. The fourth method is a semi-parametrically efficient
200
      doubly robust method, double RL (DRL), known for achieving the smallest possible MSE among a
201
      broad class of OPE estimators (Kallus & Uehara, 2020; 2022; Liao et al., 2022).
202
203   Method P  1 (Q-function-based method). For a given Q-function Q, define f1 (Q) as the estimating
204   function a∈A π(a|S1 )Q(a, S1 ) with S1 being the initial state. By equation 1 and the definition of
205   J(π), it is immediate to see that J(π) = E[f1 (Qπ )]. This motivates the Q-function-based method
206
      which uses a plug-in estimator to approximate E[f1 (Qπ )] and estimate J(π). In particular, Qπ can
      be estimated by Q-learning type algorithms (e.g., fitted Q-evaluation, FQE, Le et al., 2019), and the
207
      expectation can be approximated based on the empirical initial state distribution.
208
209   Method 2 (Sequential importance sampling). For a given IS ratio ρπ , let ρπ1:t denote the sequential
                Qt
210   IS ratio j=1 ρπ (Aj , Sj ). It follows from the change of measure theorem that the counterfactual
211   reward Eπ (Rt ) is equivalent to E(ρπ1:t Rt ) whose expectation is taken with respect to the offline
212   data distribution. Assuming all trajectories in D terminate after a finite time T , this allows us to
                                                        PT
213   represent J(π) by E[f2 (ρπ )] where f2 (ρπ ) = t=1 γ t−1 ρπ1:t Rt . SIS utilizes a plug-in estimator
214   to first estimate ρπ (when the behavior policy is unknown), and then employs this estimator, along
215   with the empirical data distribution, to approximate E[f2 (ρπ )]. However, a notable limitation of
      this estimator is its rapidly increasing variance due to the use of the SIS ratio ρπ1:t . Specifically, this

216
       variance tends to grow exponentially with respect to t, a phenomenon often referred to as the curse of
217    horizon (Liu et al., 2018).
218
219    Method 3 (Marginalized importance sampling). The MIS estimator is designed to overcome the
220
       limitations of the SIS estimator. It breaks the curse of horizon by taking the structure of the MDP
       model into account. As noted previously, under the Markov assumption, the reward depends only
221
       on the current state-action pair, rather than the entire history. This insight allows us to replace the
222
       SIS ratio with the MIS ratio, which depends solely on the current state-action pair. This modification
223    considerably reduces variance because wπ is no longer history-dependent. Assuming the data
224    trajectory is stationary over time – that is, all state-action-reward (S, A, R) triplets have the same
225    distribution – it can be shown that J(π) = E[f3 (wπ )] where f3 (wπ ) = (1 − γ)−1 wπ (A, S)R for
226    any triplet (S, A, R). Both wπ and the expectation can be effectively approximated using offline data.
227
       Method 4 (Double reinforcement learning). DRL combines        P the Q-function-based method with
228
       MIS. Let f4 (Q, w) = f1 (Q) + (1 − γ)−1 w(A, S)[R + γ a π(a|S ′ )Q(a, S ′ ) − Q(A, S)], where
229    f1 is defined in Method 1 and (S, A, R, S ′ ) denotes a state-action-reward-next-state tuple. Under the
230    stationarity assumption, it can be shown that J(π) = E[f4 (Q, w)] when either Q = Qπ or w = wπ
231    (Kallus & Uehara, 2022). DRL proposes to learn both Qπ and wπ from the data, employing these
232    estimators to calculate E[f4 (Q, w)] and approximate the expectation with empirical data distribution.
233    The resulting estimator benefits from double robustness: it is consistent when either Qπ or wπ is
234    correctly specified.

237
238    This section presents model-free (Section 4.1), model-based irrelevance conditions (Section 4.2) for
239    OPE and analyzes the OPE estimators under these conditions (Lemma 1 & Theorem 1). Motivated
240    by this analysis, we propose our iterative procedure (Section ??) and study its property (Theorem 2).
241
       Our theoretical analysis is concerned with the Fisher consistency of various OPE estimators, named
242
       after, Ronald Fisher, the founder of modern statistics. In particular, the Fisher consistency requires an
243
       estimator to be exactly equal to the ground truth given infinite samples. Specialized to our settings, it
244    imposes two requirements:
245
246    (i) The identification formulas presented in Section 3.3 remain valid when replacing the oracle
247
           Q-function or (M)IS ratio with those projected into the proposed abstract state space;
      (ii) The Q-function or (M)IS ratio defined on the abstract state space is identifiable.
248
249    Once the Fisher consistency is established, the estimator’s finite sample properties can be readily
250    obtained using existing techniques (see e.g., Uehara et al., 2021a). Therefore, for conciseness and to
251    avoid redundancy, we chose not to present finite sample results in our theoretical analysis.

286   4.2   M ODEL - BASED IRRELEVANCE CONDITIONS
287
288   To begin with, we discuss two perspectives of the data generated within the MDP framework; see
289   Figure 2 for a graphical illustration.
290   1. The first perspective is the traditional forward MDP model with all state-action-reward triplets
291      sequenced by time index. This yields the model-based irrelevance condition defined in Definition
292      3. We will discuss the relationship between this condition and Definitions 5-7 below.
293   2. The second perspective offers a backward view by reversing the time order. Specifically, due to
294      the symmetric nature of the Markov assumption — implying that if the future is independent of
295      the past given the present, the past must also be independent of the future given the present —
296
         the reversed state-action pairs also maintain the Markov property. Leveraging this property, we
         define another backward MDP, which forms the basis for deriving model-based conditions for
297
         achieving wπ -irrelevance and motivates the subsequent iterative procedure. This development
298
         represents one of our main contributions.
299
300   Forward MDP-based model-irrelevance. We first discuss the connections between the model-
301   irrelevance given in Definition 3 and the notions of Qπ -, ρπ - and wπ -irrelevance, and introduce
302
      the following conditions to establish Fisher consistency. These conditions are mild and frequently
      imposed in the RL and OPE literature (see e.g., Thomas & Brunskill, 2016; Kallus & Zhou, 2018;
303
      Chen & Jiang, 2019; Fan et al., 2020; Cai et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2021; Kallus & Uehara, 2022).
304
305   Assumption 1 (Boundedness) All immediate rewards are uniformly bounded.
306
      Assumption 2 (Coverage) The denominator in equation 2 is strictly positive.
307
308   Assumption 3 (Stationary) The MDP (St , At , Rt )t≥1 is stationary over time.
309
310   The following lemma summarizes the findings. Results in the first two bullet points are based on
311
      those in the existing literature (see e.g., Li et al., 2006; Pavse & Hanna, 2023).
312
      Lemma 1 (OPE under model-irrelevance) Assume Assumptions 1–3 hold. Let ϕ denote a model-
313   irrelevant abstraction. Suppose ϕ is additionally π-irrelevant. Then:
314   • Qπ -irrelevance & Fisher consistency of Q-function-based method: ϕ is also Qπ -irrelevant,
315      and the corresponding Q-function-based method (Method 1) is thus Fisher consistent, i.e., the
316     Q-function Qπϕ defined on the abstract space is identifiable and satisfies E[f1 (Qπ )] = E[f1 (Qπϕ )];
317   • Fisher consistency of MIS: While ϕ is not necessarily wπ -irrelevant, MIS (Method 3) is Fisher
318      consistent when applied to the abstract state space, i.e., the MIS ratio wϕπ defined on the abstract
319      state space is identifiable and satisfies E[f3 (wπ )] = E[f3 (wϕπ )];
320   • Fisher consistency of SIS: While ϕ is not necessarily ρπ -irrelevant, SIS (Method 2) with a history-
321      dependent IS ratio (detailed in the proof of Lemma 1 in Appendix D.1) is Fisher consistent when
322      applied to the abstract space;
323   • Fisher consistency of DRL: DRL (Method 4) is Fisher consistent when applied to the abstract
         state space.

      The first bullet point establishes the link between model-irrelevance and Qπ -irrelevance and proves
325   the Fisher consistency of the Q-function-based method when applied to the abstract state space. To
326   satisfy Qπ -irrelevance, we need both model-irrelevance and π-irrelevance. In our implementation,

410   4.3   I TERATIVE PROCEDURE FOR DEEP STATE ABSTRACTION
411
412   To summarize, we have reviewed the model-irrelevance condition and proposed a new backward-
413   model-irrelevance condition. Both lead to Fisher consistent OPE estimators when confined to
414   the corresponding abstract state spaces. This motivates us to combine the two procedures for a
415   more condensed state abstraction, resulting in the following iterative algorithm (see Figure 3 for a
416
      visualization):
417   1. Forward abstraction: learn an abstraction ϕ1 from the ground state space S = X0 to X1 using
418      the data triplets (S, A, R) that is both (forward)-model-irrelevant and π-irrelevant.
419   2. Backward abstraction: Learn an abstraction ϕ2 from the abstract state space X1 to X2 using the
420      data triplets (ϕ1 (S), A, R) that is both backward-model-irrelevant and π-irrelevant.
421   3. Iterate the two steps to compute the final abstraction ϕK ◦ · · · ◦ ϕ2 ◦ ϕ1 from the ground space S
422
         to XK where K denotes the number of iterations and ◦ denotes the function composition operator.
423   In particular, our approach alternates between forward and backward abstraction on the state space
424   obtained from the previous iteration. Each iteration guarantees that the cardinality of the state space
425   does not increase, effectively maintaining or reducing complexity. Consequently, such an iterative
426   procedure progressively reducing state cardinality, which ultimately yields a deeply-abstracted state.
427   We thus refer to our approach as deep state abstraction (DSA).
428   To elaborate the usefulness of DSA in reducing state cardinality, we analyze two examples: a bandit
429   example and an MDP example. In both examples, we focus on a specific type of state abstraction
430   known as variable selection, which selects a sub-vector from the original state. Additionally, we
431   focus on the class of state-agnostic target policies where π is independent of the states. This type
      of policy is prevalent in causal inference and A/B testing, where the objective is to learn the global

      A    C ONFOUNDER SELECTION IN CAUSAL INFERENCE
850
851
852   Broadly speaking, confounding refers to the problem that even if two variables are not causes of each
853   other, they may exhibit statistical association due to common causes. Controlling for confounding is
854   a central problem in the design of observational studies, and many criteria for confounder selection
855
      have been proposed in the literature. A commonly adopted criterion is the “common cause heuristic”,
      where the user only controls for covariates that are related to both the treatment and the outcome
856
      (Glymour et al., 2008; Austin, 2011; Shortreed & Ertefaie, 2017; Koch et al., 2020). Another widely
857
      used criterion is to simply use all covariates that are observed before the treatment in time (Rubin,
858   2009; Hernán & Robins, 2010; 2016). However, both of these approaches are not guaranteed to
859   find a set of covariates that are sufficient to control for confounding. From a graphical perspective,
860   confounder selection is essentially about finding a set of covariates that block all “back-door” paths
861   (Pearl, 2009), but this requires full structural knowledge about the causal relationship between the
862   variables which is often not possible. This motivated some methods that only require partial structural
863   knowledge (Vander Weele & Shpitser, 2011; VanderWeele, 2019; Guo & Zhao, 2023). All the
      aforementioned methods need substantive knowledge about the treatment, outcome, and covariates.


                                                       16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Other methods use statistical tests (usually of conditional independence) to trim a set of covariates
865   that are assumed to control for confounding (Robins, 1997; Greenland et al., 1999; Hernán & Robins,
866   2010; De Luna et al., 2011; Belloni et al., 2014; Persson et al., 2017). The reader is referred to Guo
867   et al. (2022) for a recent survey of objectives and approaches for confounder selection.
868
869    Confounder selection can be considered as a special example of our problem under certain conditions:
870   (i) The state transition is independent, effectively transforming the MDP into a contextual bandit;
871   (ii) The action space is binary, with the target policy consistently assigning either action 0 or action
872   1, aimed at assessing the average treatment effect; (iii) State abstractions are confined to variable
873
      selections. While our proposed iterative procedure shares similar spirits with the aforementioned
      algorithms, it addresses a more complex problem involving state transitions. Additionally, our focus
874
      is on abstraction that facilitates the engineering of new feature vectors, rather than merely selecting a
875
      subset of existing ones.
