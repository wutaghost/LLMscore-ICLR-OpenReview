036                                             A BSTRACT
037
038           We introduce GeoGS3D, a novel two-stage framework for reconstructing detailed
039           3D objects from single-view images. Inspired by the success of pre-trained 2D
040           diffusion models, our method incorporates an orthogonal plane decomposition
041           mechanism to extract 3D geometric features from the 2D input, facilitating the
042           generation of multi-view consistent images. During the following Gaussian Splat-
043
              ting, these images are fused with epipolar attention, fully utilizing the geometric
              correlations across views. Moreover, we propose a novel metric, Gaussian Diver-
044
              gence Significance (GDS), to prune unnecessary operations during optimization,
045
              significantly accelerating the reconstruction process. Extensive experiments demon-
046           strate that GeoGS3D generates images with high consistency across views and
047           reconstructs high-quality 3D objects, both qualitatively and quantitatively. Further
048           examples can be found at the anonymous website.

051   1   I NTRODUCTION
052
053   Single-view 3D reconstruction aims to recover 3D geometry and appearance of an object from a
      single RGB image. This task holds immense importance as it allows machines to understand and


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      interact with the real 3D world, enabling various applications in virtual reality (VR), augmented
055   reality (AR) (Kopf et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023) and robotics (Wang et al., 2022). Despite broad
056   applications of the task, it remains highly challenging due to the inherent complexity of inferring 3D
057   structures from a single 2D image.
058
      Poineering works in multi-view diffusion models have made strides in improving 3D reconstruction
059
      by finetuning pre-trained image or video diffusion models on 3D datasets to enable multi-view
060
      synthesis (Liu et al., 2023c; Shi et al., 2023; Kwak et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024; Voleti et al., 2024;
061   Long et al., 2024). However, maintaining multi-view consistency and handling objects with complex
062   geometries remain difficult. Another line of research (Hong et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024a; Xu et al.,
063   2024c;b) proposes generalizable reconstruction models, generating 3D representation from one or
064   few views in a feed-forward process, but these models usually demand substantial computational
065   resources. For instance, LGM (Tang et al., 2024a) requires training on 32 NVIDIA A100 (80G) for 4
066   days.
067   Meanwhile, 3D Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023) has emerged as a promising technique, offering
068   high-quality rendering with competitive training and inference time as it combines the benefits of
069   neural network-based optimization and explicit, structured data storage. Hence, it is desirable to
070   explore Gaussian Splatting for efficient 3D reconstruction. However, current methods (Szymanowicz
071   et al., 2023a; Tang et al., 2023a) using 3D Gaussian Splatting often feed a single image into the
072   model, ignoring the spatial correspondence of multiple views. Additionally, we observe that the
073   original implementation of Gaussian Splatting neglects the distance between 3D Gaussians, causing
074   many unnecessary split and clone operations.
075   To address these limitations, we propose GeoGS3D, a novel two-stage framework for single-image
076   3D reconstruction. Our approach consists of a geometric-aware multi-view generation stage, followed
077   by an accelerated 3D reconstruction stage. In the generation stage, we aim to synthesize 3D-aware
078   images that maintain multi-view consistency. To achieve the goal, 3D features are extracted as
079   geometric conditions by decoupling the orthogonal planes, while the semantic condition is obtained
080   with the CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) encoder. These conditions, together with the input image are
081
      then fed into the diffusion model (Rombach et al., 2022). In the reconstruction stage, we introduce
      epipolar attention to fuse the generated views, fully leveraging the geometric correlations between
082
      them. Furthermore, we accelerate the optimization process by introducing a novel metric, Gaussian
083
      Divergent Significance (GDS), to avoid unnecessary operations during 3D Gaussian Splatting.
084
085   Extensive experiments and ablations on Objaverse (Deitke et al., 2023) and Google Scanned Ob-
086   ject (Downs et al., 2022) datasets demonstrate that our method is able to produce high-quality 3D
087
      objects with strong multi-view consistency and detailed geometry. Our key contributions can be
      summarized as following:
088
089          • We incorporate an orthogonal plane decomposition mechanism with a diffusion model to
090            synthesize multi-view consistent and geometric-aware novel view images.
091
             • In order to take full advantage of the consistent multi-view images, we introduce epipolar
092            attention into the optimization process, allowing for efficient and effective communication
093            between images.
094
             • To accelerate the optimization of Gaussian splatting, we derive a novel metric named
095            Gaussian Divergent Significance (GDS) to prune unnecessary split and clone operations
096            during optimization.

      2.1   M ULTI - VIEW D IFFUSION M ODELS
101
102   Recent 2D diffusion models (Song et al., 2020a; Ho et al., 2020; Song & Ermon, 2019; Song
103   et al., 2020b; Xing et al., 2023c;a;b) make impressive advances in generating images from various
104   conditions. As an intermediate 3D representation, the generation of multi-view images using diffusion
105   models has been explored. The advantage of multi-view images is that they are batched 2D projections
106   and can be directly processed by existing image diffusion models with minor changes. Zero-123 (Liu
107   et al., 2023c) injects camera view as an extra condition to the diffusion model for generating images
      from different perspectives. Additionally, Shi et al. (2023) proposes replacing self-attention with


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      multi-view attention in the UNet to produce multi-view consistent images. Other works (Chan
109   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a; Szymanowicz et al., 2023b; Yang et al., 2024; Tseng et al., 2023;
110   Sargent et al., 2023) follow a similar approach to make diffusion model 3D-aware and enhance
111   generation consistency. However, most of these works are not designed for reconstruction tasks and
112   still rely on SDS loss to derive 3D content. Recent efforts such as SyncDreamer (Liu et al., 2023d)
113   and Wonder3D (Long et al., 2024) generate multi-view consistent 2D representations, subsequently
114   applying reconstruction methods to obtain 3D content. Despite these advancements, the resulting
115   3D outputs often lack comprehensive geometric information. In contrast, our method generates
116   more consistent views with detailed geometric information, facilitating high-quality 3D content
117
      reconstruction in terms of texture and geometry.
118
119   2.2   3D R ECONSTRUCTION P IPELINES
120
      Researchers have explored directly training diffusion models on 3D representations (Jun & Nichol,
121
      2023; Müller et al., 2023; Shue et al., 2023). However, they require exhaustive 3D data and computa-
122   tion resources and are also limited to category-level shape generation with simple textures. Other
123   works (Poole et al., 2022; Melas-Kyriazi et al., 2023; Kwak et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023; Tang
124   et al., 2023a;b; Wang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023) proposed to lift 2D diffusion models for 3D
125   generation via Score Distillation Sampling (SDS), optimizing various 3D representations including
126   NeRF, mesh, SDF and Gaussian Splatting. However, these approaches often face challenges such as
127   prolonged optimization times, the Janus problem, and over-saturated colors. More recent works (Xu
128   et al., 2023; Szymanowicz et al., 2023b; Siddiqui et al., 2024; Charatan et al., 2024; Xu et al.,
129   2024a; Tochilkin et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024) train large reconstruction models to directly map sparse
130
      views to 3D representations. Notably, LRM (Hong et al., 2023), Instant3D (Li et al., 2023), and
      GRM (Xu et al., 2024c) utilize large transformers to predict triplanes from single or sparse views.
131
      TriplaneGaussian (Zou et al., 2024) and LGM (Tang et al., 2024a) instead map sparse views into
132
      more memory-efficient 3D Gaussian Splatting, allowing for much higher resolution in supervision.
133   Our work aligns with Liu et al. (2023b;a); Tang et al. (2024b); Wen et al. (2024), where we first
134   multi-view diffusion model to generate consistent images, and then utilize reconstruction methods to
135   obtain the corresponding 3D model.

139
      The overview of GeoGS3D is shown in Figure 2. Starting from an input image, our geometric-
140
      aware diffusion model first generates multi-view images sequentially in the generation stage (refer
141   to Section 3.2). Subsequently, in the reconstruction stage, epipolar attention is incorporated into
142   the Gaussian Splatting process to reconstruct the high-quality 3D objects (refer to Section 3.3).
143   Additionally, we introduce a metric to accelerate the adaptive density control during Gaussian
144   Splatting (refer to Section 3.4).

      3.2   G EOMETRY- AWARE M ULTI - VIEW G ENERATION
204
205   Considering the success of pre-trained diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022), it is intuitive to
206   finetune them for novel view synthesis under a given camera transformation. However, maintaining
207   multi-view consistency across views remains a substantial challenge. One stream of methods (Woo
208   et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023) addresses the problem by conditioning on previously generated images,
209   which tends to be susceptible to cumulative errors and reduced processing speeds. Another stream of
210   methods (Liu et al., 2023c;a) solely use the reference image and semantic guidance to generate novel
211   views, but suffer from collapsed geometry and limited fidelity.
212   We argue that the key lies in fully utilizing the geometric information provided by the input image.
213   However, directly extracting 3D information from a single 2D image is not feasible. Thus, it is
214   imperative to effectively disentangle 3D features from the image plane (i.e. xy-plane) by decoupling
215   orthogonal planes. We first employ a vision transformer to encode the input image and capture overall
      correlations in the image, generating high-dimensional latent h. Then we leverage two decoders, an


                                                                          4

217   the latent. The image-plane decoder reverses the encoding operation, leveraging a self-attention

219   features while maintaining structural alignment with the image plane, a cross-attention mechanism

      3.3   R ECONSTRUCTION WITH E PIPOLAR ATTENTION
258
259   During the reconstruction stage, we target to exploit the synthesized consistent multi-view images
260   for restoring high-quality 3D objects. However, relying solely on cross-attention to communicate
261   between images of multiple viewpoints is insufficient. Therefore, we propose epipolar attention to
262   allow association between the features of different views. The epipolar line for a given feature point
263   in one view is the line on which the corresponding feature point in the other view must lie, based on
264   the known geometric relationship between two views. It acts as a constraint to reduce the number of
265   potential pixels in one view that can attend to another view. We present the illustration of epipolar

297   3.4   ACCELERATING THE R ECONSTRUCTION
298
299   The optimization of Gaussian Splatting is based on successive iterations of rendering and comparing
300   the resulting image to the training views. 3D Gaussians are first initialized from either Structure-
301   from-Motion (SfM) or random sampling. Inevitably, geometry may be incorrectly placed due to the
302   ambiguities of 3D to 2D projection. The optimization process thus needs to be able to adaptively
303   create geometry and also remove geometry (termed as split and clone) if it is incorrectly positioned.
304   However, the split and clone operations proposed by the original work (Kerbl et al., 2023) overlook
305   the distance between 3D Gaussians, during the optimization process which significantly slows down
306   the process. We observe that if two Gaussians are close to each other, even if the positional gradients
307   are larger than a threshold, they should not be split or cloned since these Gaussians are updating
308   their positions. Empirically, splitting or cloning these Gaussians has negligible influence on the
309   rendering quality as they are too close to each other. For this reason, we propose Gaussian Divergent
310
      Significance (GDS) as a measure of the distance of 3D Gaussians to avoid unnecessary splitting or
      cloning:
311
312
                 ΥGDS (G(x1 ), G(x2 )) = ∥µ1 − µ2 ∥2 + tr(Σ1 + Σ2 − 2(Σ−1    −1 1/2
                                                                       1 Σ2 Σ1 )    ),                       (8)
313
314   where µ1 , Σ1 , µ2 , Σ2 are the position and covariance matrix of two 3D Gaussians G(x1 ) and
315   G(x2 ).
316
      To be specific, we only perform the split and clone operations on the 3D Gaussians with large
317
      positional gradients and GDS. To avoid the time-consuming process of calculating GDS for every
318   pair of 3D Gaussians, we further propose two strategies. Firstly, for each 3D Gaussian, we locate
319   its closest 3D Gaussian by leveraging the k-nearest neighbor (k-NN) algorithm and calculate their
320   GDS for each pair. As a result, the time complexity is reduced from O(N 2 ) to O(N ). Additionally,
321   as mentioned in Section 3.1, the covariance matrix can be factorized into a scaling matrix S and a
322   rotation matrix R: Σ = RSS T RT . We take advantage of the diagonal and orthogonal properties
323   of rotation and scaling matrices to simplify the calculation of Equation 8. Details of GDS will be
      discussed in the Appendix A.

      4.1     E XPERIMENTAL P ROTOCOLS
360
      Datasets We train our multi-view generation model on the recently released Objaverse (Deitke
361   et al., 2023) dataset, which is a large-scale CAD dataset containing 800K high-quality objects. We
362   directly employ the processed rendering data from Zero-1-to-3, which provides 12 random views of
363   each object. During the training, images are resized to 256 × 256 resolution.
364
      We evaluate the synthesized multi-view images and reconstructed 3D Gaussian Splatting (3D-GS) on
365
      the test split of Objaverse where we randomly select 100 objects beyond the training set. In addition,
366
      to test the performance of our model on the out-of-distribution data, we also evaluate the Google
367   Scanned Object (GSO) dataset (Downs et al., 2022), which contains high-quality scanned household
368   items.
369
370
      Implementation Details For the generation stage, we initialize the network with Zero-1-to-3 (Liu
371
      et al., 2023c) pre-trained weights for training efficiency. We utilize a Vision Transformer (ViT) model
372   of depth 6 as the reference image encoder and generate an output of size 1024 × 256. The decoding
373   process involves two decoders, i.e. image plane decoder and orthogonal plane decoder, each with
374   a depth of 3 and outputs a feature map F ∈ R128×128×64 . After the multi-view generation, we
375   directly adopt the implementation of Yang et al. (2022) to select 32 views with the highest perceptual
376   quality score. For the reconstruction stage, the network that maps the input images to the mixtures
377   of Gaussians is architecturally identical to the UNet (Song et al., 2020a). The last layer is replaced
      with a 1 × 1 convolutional layer with 15 output channels. As mentioned in Section 3.3, in order to


                                                        7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      allow the network to coordinate and exchange information between views, we add epipolar attention
379   blocks after residual blocks followed by the cross-attention layers. We use the AdamW optimizer
380   with β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.999 with a learning rate of 10−4 .
381
382
      Metrics We compare generated multi-view images and rendered views from reconstructed 3D-GS
383
      with the ground truth frames, in terms of Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR), Structural Similarity
384   Index (SSIM) (Wang et al., 2004), and Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS) (Zhang
385   et al., 2018).
386
387
      Baselines In terms of multi-view generation, we compare our GeoGS3D with Zero-1-to-3 (Liu
388   et al., 2023c), Syncdreamer (Liu et al., 2023d), and Wonder3D (Long et al., 2024). Zero-1-to-3
389   finetunes Stable Diffusion to achieve novel view synthesis under a given camera pose. Syncdreamer
390   follows a similar intuition but learns the joint distribution of all target views. Wonder3D proposes
391   cross-domain attention to propagate information between normal and image, generating normal maps
392   and color images simultaneously.
393
      For image-to-3D reconstruction, we adopt DreamGaussian (Tang et al., 2023a), LGM (Tang et al.,
394   2024a) and InstantMesh (Xu et al., 2024b). Notably, LGM and InstantMesh also adopt two-stage
395   frameworks to achieve image-to-3D creation, which generates multi-view images and then utilizes
396   large reconstruction models to obtain 3D objects. DreamGaussian initializes geometry and appearance
397   using single-step SDS loss and then extracts a textured mesh with refinement guided by MSE loss.

520   4.4    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
521
522   While GeoGS3D shows promising results in reconstructing 3D objects from single-view images,
523   there are still some limitations that the current framework does not entirely address. First, the number
524   of generated views is fixed in our method. Adaptively generating different numbers of views for
525
      objects with different topological symmetries might further reduce the total reconstruction time.
      Additionally, our current method is restricted to single-object 3D reconstruction. It remains to be
526
      extended to complex scenes or multi-object reconstruction in the future.

528
529   5     C ONCLUSIONS
530
531   In this work, we propose a two-stage model, GeoGS3D, to reconstruct 3D objects from single-view
532   images. This method first synthesizes consistent and 3D-aware multi-view images via a diffusion
533   model under the guidance of an orthogonal-plane decomposition mechanism. During the following
534
      3D-GS reconstruction, epipolar attention is leveraged to communicate between relating multi-view
      images. A novel metric, i.e. Gaussian Divergent Significance (GDS), is proposed to accelerate
535
      optimization. Qualitative and quantitative results show that the proposed method reconstructs 3D
536
      Gaussian representations that 1) are consistent in different viewpoints, 2) are high fidelity to the
537   reference image, and 3) display plausible creativity in the unseen areas.
