011                                                          A BSTRACT
012
013           Recent deep learning models for Long-term Time Series Forecasting (LTSF) of-
014           ten emphasize complex, handcrafted designs and traditional methodologies, while
015           simpler architectures like linear models or MLPs have occasionally outperformed
016           these intricate solutions. In this paper, we revisit and organize the core ideas be-
017
              hind several key techniques, such as redundancy reduction and multi-scale mod-
              eling, which are frequently employed in advanced LTSF models. Our goal is to
018
              streamline these ideas for more efficient deep learning utilization. To this end, we
019
              introduce TimeCapsule, a model built around the principle of high-dimensional
020           information compression that unifies these key ideas in a generalized yet simpli-
021           fied framework. Specifically, we model time series as a 3D tensor, incorporating
022           temporal, variate, and level dimensions, and leverage mode production to capture
023           multi-mode dependencies while achieving dimensionality compression. We pro-
024           pose an internal forecast within the compressed representation domain, supported
025           by the Joint-Embedding Predictive Architecture (JEPA) to monitor the learning
026           of predictive representations. Extensive experiments on challenging benchmarks
027           demonstrate the versatility of our method, showing that TimeCapsule can achieve
028
              performance comparable to state-of-the-art models. More importantly, the struc-
              ture of our model yields intriguing empirical findings, prompting a rethinking of
029
              approaches in this area.

049   Multivariate Time Series (MvTS) data is one of the most ubiquitous forms of naturally generated
050   data in the temporal physical world. Forecasting future events, whether short-term or long-term,
051   based on these collected historical data, can support critical human activities, including finance
052   (Sezer et al., 2020), traffic (Guo et al., 2020), and weather prediction (Karevan & Suykens, 2020).
053   Moreover, it enables us to fundamentally explore the mechanisms underlying the world’s operations
      (Bradley, 1999).

      With the rapid advancement of deep learning models, Long-term Time Series Forecasting (LTSF)
055   has recently gained prominence. Unlike short-term forecasting, LTSF has traditionally posed sig-
056   nificant challenges for classical statistical and machine learning methods, such as VAR, ARIMA,
057   and random forests (Toda & Phillips, 1994; Box & Pierce, 1970; Kane et al., 2014). A series of
058   groundbreaking works (Zhou et al., 2021; Zeng et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) have
059   been proposed to push the boundaries of this field, addressing limitations and advancing the com-
060   munity. Nevertheless, key questions remain about how best to improve existing models, leaving
061   room for highlighting potential synergies among existing methods and combining their strengths.
062   For instance, Informer suggests that the learned attention map of transformers should be sparse and
063
      can be distilled into smaller representations. Models like FiLM (Zhou et al., 2022a) and FEDformer
      (Zhou et al., 2022b) manipulate time series in compact frequency bases to capture key temporal cor-
064
      relations. These advancements raise a series of compelling questions: Can such enhancements be
065
      conveniently extended to other dimensions and more generalized transform domains? Is time series
066   data, or the information it contains, inherently compressible? What’s more, PatchTST applies the
067   idea of patching to LTSF, enabling larger local receptive fields and greater efficiency. However, its
068   reliance on predefined patch lengths introduces a rigidity that can be inflexible when dealing with
069   varying input sequence lengths, thereby limiting its range of application. Additionally, this opera-
070   tion is not differentiable. Unlike image or text data, which contain explicit semantic information,
071   splitting a time sequence into small chunks outside the training process may lead to unrecoverable
072   information loss. While these methods individually have provided promising directions, they may
073   overlook the combination of other critical principles in time series modeling and underestimate the
074
      capabilities of pure deep learning modules to represent these features.
075   Recent discussions in the community have also drawn attention to the surprisingly strong perfor-
076   mance of linear models when compared to transformer-based architectures in LTSF tasks (Zeng
077   et al., 2023). While transformers (Vaswani, 2017) have set milestones across domains like Natural
078   Language Processing (NLP) (Wang et al., 2022) and Computer Vision (CV) (Yuan et al., 2021),
079   their effectiveness in LTSF remains inconsistent, often falling short of simpler linear models (Zeng
080
      et al., 2023). In addressing this issue, iTransformer (Liu et al., 2023) offers valuable insights and
      experiments that transformers can excel when applied in the variate dimensions, but the ongoing
081
      debate over the performance of transformer-based versus linear-based models in LTSF is unresolved
082
      and whether transformers or linear models are more appropriate for LTSF remains open.
083
084   In this work, we argue that a deep learning framework for improving time series forecasting need not
085   exclusively depend on either transformers or linear models. Instead, we propose that an effective
086
      architecture for LTSF should simply consist of two stages: predictive representation learning and
      generalized linear projection, in which the latter serves as an accurate predictor to learn generalized
087
      linear dependencies for the forecasting, while the former extracts abstract and informative repre-
088
      sentation from distinct data information to improve forecasters’ generality. Therefore, we propose
089   TimeCapsule, a novel model employing a Chaining Bits Back with Asymmetric Numeral Systems
090   (BB-ANS) (Townsend et al., 2019) like-architecture, which adopts weak transformer-based blocks
091   as the encoder, while MLP-based blocks as the decoder, striking a balance between powerful rep-
092   resentation learning and computational simplicity. Technically, our model is driven by three key
093   principles:
094
      Multi-level Modeling: Multi-scale modeling stands out as an effective paradigm for improving
095   LSTF performance (Ferreira et al., 2006). Existing methods incorporate multiresolution analysis
096   by up/downsampling through moving average and convolutional pooling layers (Challu et al., 2023;
097   Wang et al., 2024), or designing hierarchical structures (Liu et al., 2021; Zhang & Yan, 2023) to ag-
098   gregate multi-scale features. Besides, time series decomposition is also a traditional and commonly
099   used strategy to improve LTSF performance (Oreshkin et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2022b; Wu et al.,
100   2021). In avoidance of complexity, we propose adding an extra dimension called level to the original
101   time series, allowing the model to learn multi-level features within the representation space. This
102   approach generalizes multi-scale learning and time series decomposition, in the meanwhile, making
103
      the learning process model-independent.
104   Multi-mode Dependency: iTransformer has shown the benefits of leveraging correlations across
105   dimensions other than time. However, focusing too heavily on non-temporal dimensions can risk
106   neglecting important temporal dependencies, potentially degrading forecasting accuracy. To address
107   this, we introduce a Mode-specific Multi-head Self-Attention (MoMSA) mechanism, leveraging



                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      tensor-based mode product techniques to capture dependencies along and across multiple dimen-
109   sions including temporal, variate, and level.
110
111   Compressed Representation Forecasting: To enable efficient information utilization, fast multi-
112
      mode attention computations, and better long-range history processing, we conclude that compres-
      sion is all we need. To be specific, rather than focusing exclusively on sparse attention and re-
113
      dundancy reduction, we employ low-rank transforms to replace patching, reducing dimensionality
114
      ahead of the attention computation, thus leading to an economic way to employ transformers. This
115   strategy compresses the representation space, enabling efficient computation and robust long-range
116   forecasting. The compressed space can serve as the intermediate stage for learning forecasts, allow-
117   ing us to map the representation into the future landscape and recover it back into the real temporal
118   domain.
119
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
120
121          • We propose simple yet effective generalizations to existing LTSF techniques, including a
122            model-independent multi-level modeling strategy and a Mode-Specific Multi-head Self-
123            Attention (MoMSA) mechanism, resulting in a versatile forecasting model capable of han-
124            dling diverse data characteristics.
125          • We introduce JEPA into time series forecasting, making it a useful tool for monitoring and
126            analyzing the process of predictive representation learning.
127          • Extensive experiments on real-world datasets demonstrate the superiority of our approach,
128            identifying areas for further exploration.

      2   RELATED WORK

      Recent Advancements in Long-term Time Series modeling Given the advantage of capturing
133   long-term dependencies in long sequence data, researchers have increasingly applied transformers
134   to LTSF tasks (Wen et al., 2022). Most recently, several significant works have been proposed, either
135   to improve the forecasting performance of transformers or to reduce their computational complexity.
136   Additionally, some models that diverge from transformer architectures have also exhibited consider-
137   able promise in this area. Based on the strategies by which they achieve success, we can categorize
138   these models into four groups, with potential overlaps, as presented in Fig. 1. The first group
139   (e.g., Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021), N-BEATs (Oreshkin et al., 2019), Pyraformer (Liu et al., 2021),
140   N-Hits (Challu et al., 2023), Crossformer (Zhang & Yan, 2023), TimeMixer (Wang et al., 2024))
141
      focuses on multi-level modeling, which incorporates multiresolution/multi-scale analysis and series
      decomposition within the model. These techniques enable the model to learn both coarse and fine-
142
      grained features within time series, facilitating the capture of hierarchical temporal patterns. The
143
      second group (e.g., Informer (Zhou et al., 2021), FEDformer (Zhou et al., 2022b), PatchTST (Nie
144   et al., 2023)), leverages information redundancy to filter and extract high-energy temporal correla-
145   tions, or models time series in the form of temporal patches, avoiding the inclusion of finer, noisier
146   information in the forecasting process, improving the model’s robustness and prediction accuracy.,
147   These advancements lead to progressive improvements in both effectiveness and efficiency. The
148   third group (e.g., Crossformer, iTransformer (Liu et al., 2023)) explores the impact of attention
149   applied across various dimensions of time series, offering novel perspectives for time series cor-
150   relation extraction. This group demonstrates that capturing dependencies across different modes
151   can significantly enhance forecasting performance, by providing a richer understanding of temporal
152
      and variable relationships. The fourth group (e.g., DLinear (Zeng et al., 2023), N-BEATs, N-Hits,
      TsMixer (Chen et al., 2023), TimeMixer) emphasizes the importance of generalized linear depen-
153
      dency modeling and capitalizes on linear or MLP-based architectures to establish highly effective
154
      forecasters. This validates the crucial role of learning the appropriate coefficients to combine the
155   captured base components of time series and the dependencies they contain.
156
157   These four groups highlight four key factors in answering how to model and forecast time series
158
      effectively from different aspects. However, each model typically focuses on constructing com-
      plex modules that address only parts of these factors. In contrast, our model integrates all of these
159
      interesting factors into a comprehensive yet streamlined design.
160
161   Joint-Embedding Predictive Architecture JEPA (LeCun, 2022) realizes representation learning
      by optimizing an energy function between predicted representations of inputs and targets. It doesn’t

      rely on explicit contrastive losses (Khosla et al., 2020) but instead creates compatible embeddings
163   through prediction, enhancing the flexibility and efficiency of representation learning. While JEPA
164   has demonstrated success in learning predictable representations in vision (Assran et al., 2023) and
165   video tasks (Bardes et al., 2024), these domains often benefit from spatial locality and semantic
166   coherence, which are different from the temporal dependencies and complex long-range patterns
167   present in time series data. Such distinctions pose unique challenges when applying JEPA to LTSF.
168   Although some recent works like LaT-PFN (Verdenius et al., 2024) and TS-JEPA (Girgis et al.,

171
      model, demonstrating that JEPA can reinforce the latent embedding space of time series learning
      and result in a superior zero-shot performance. In contrast, the latter, TS-JEPA, harnesses JEPA to
172
      facilitate the realization and enhancement of the effectiveness of semantic communication systems.

198   Following the convention of Multivariate Time Series (MvTS) forecasting, we aim to predict the next
199
      Ty steps of the time sequence, denoted as Y = {ytx +1 , ytx +2 , · · · , ytx +ty } ∈ Rv×ty , based on the
200
      observed sequence X = {x1 , x2 , · · · , xtx } ∈ Rv×tx , where v represents the number of variates and
      tx denotes the length of the input sequence. The key distinction in this work is that we treat MvTS
201
      as 3D tensor data, allowing the data itself to handle multi-scale modeling and series decomposition
202
      by introducing an additional dimension. Specifically, we extend the input from X ∈ Rv×tx to
203   X ∈ Rv×tx ×1 . Throughout this paper, we use T, V, and L to represent the temporal dimension,
204   variate dimension, and level dimension, respectively, with t, v, and l indicating their corresponding
205   lengths. We define 1 ≤ tc ≪ t, 1 ≤ vc ≪ v, and lc ≥ 1 as the compressed lengths of the respective
206   dimensions. Additionally, d denotes the size of the embedding space.

      3.2   MAIN COMPONENTS
245
246   Mode Specific Multi-head Self-Attention (MoMSA). The MoMSA is designed to achieve two
247   primary objectives: (a) to extend the ideas of crossformer (Zhang & Yan, 2023) and iTransformer
248   (Liu et al., 2023) by forming MvTS tokens from a multi-mode view and abstracting dependencies
249   along each mode; and (b) to maintain the same volume of information while shortening the length
250   of each dimension, thereby reducing the overall computational cost of multi-mode self-attention.
251   To accomplish this, we introduce the mode-k product (see Appendix B for the definition). For
252   instance, given an MvTS tensor A ∈ Rva ×ta ×la and a transform factor M ∈ Rm×ta , MoMSA
253   regarding to the temporal dimension operates as follows:
254
255                             Â = A ×2 M, A1 = T-MSA(Â(2) ) ∈ Rm×va la                              (11)
256   where ×2 denotes the mode-2 product, which represents a matrix multiplication along the second
257   dimension of a 3D tensor. T-MSA denotes the vanilla multi-head self-attention (Vaswani, 2017)
258   applied to the T (temporal) dimention, and Â(2) is the mode-2 folding of Â (See Appendix B). It
259   is notable that with the setting of m ≤ va , the dimension can be compressed to an arbitrary length,
260   resulting in a distilled attention map.

333   ETTh2, ETTm1, ETTm2, Electricity), environment (Weather), energy (Solar-Energy), transporta-
334   tion (PEMS04, Traffic) and health (ILI). The forecasting methods we compare against include

340   loss (Meyer, 2021) as the default loss function. Results are obtained using the random seed 2021.
341   The batch size for each case is selected within the range of 32 to 128, and the learning rate is
342   determined through a grid search between 1e-4 and 2e-3. TimeCapsule consists of 0 to 2 blocks of
343   tunnels, with the compression dimension length chosen from the set {4, 8, 32}. To test the model’s
344   ability to utilize long-range historical data, we use a look back window of 512 for most datasets.
345   Main Results. Given the numerous benchmarks proposed in this area and a recent hidden bug
346   discovered in the testing phase codes, making fair and trustworthy comparisons under a unified

399

400

401   the compensation and replacing it with the original information. The results, listed in Table 2, reveal
402   a dramatic drop in performance without paying back the lost information. This phenomenon indi-
403   cates that the compressed information cannot be independently recovered under our default settings.
404   However, when comparing our results with those of FEDformer and Informer, which also focus on
405   redundancy reduction, our model demonstrates competitive results, validating the efficacy of our
406   compression strategy. Additionally, compared to using the original information, the improvements
407   observed on datasets like ETTh2 and ETTm2 are marginal. However, as the prediction horizon
408   increases, the benefits of residual information become more pronounced. This suggests that redun-
409
      dancy reduction plays a more critical role in very long-term forecasting. For shorter forecasting
      lengths, the available information is typically sufficient. In contrast, when the forecasting horizon
410
      approaches or exceeds the input length, more compact and precise information is necessary, mak-
411
      ing the forecaster more sensitive to the quality and purity of the information provided. Notably,
412   some degradations persist even with complete original information since it is sometimes challeng-
413   ing for our model to retrieve useful parts amid redundancy, which underscores the importance of our

507
508   We propose and empirically evaluate a generic model called TimeCapsule for long-term multivariate
509
      time series forecasting. We avoid incorporating explicit designs focused on the core characteristics
      of time series modeling, but only leverage the learning capacity of generic deep learning modules,
510
      complemented by simple strategies such as 3D tensor modeling and multi-mode transforms. By con-

      ceptualizing the forecasting process as an information compression task, we integrate JEPA to both

      B     M ODE P RODUCTION
681
      Mode production is a common arithmetic operation in tensor methods (Kernfeld et al., 2015), which
682
      relies on two fundamental concepts: tensor folding and tensor unfolding. For simplicity and easy
683
      understanding, we provide informal definitions through a 3D tensor example.
684
685   Given a real 3D tensor X ∈ Rn1 ×n2 ×n3 , the result of mode-3 unfolding of X is the matrix X(3) ∈
686   Rn3 ×n1 n2 , denoted by
687                                         Fold(3) (X ) = X(3)
688   and the mode-3 folding operation recovers the matrix back into the tensor, denoted by
689
                                               Unfold(X(3) ) = X
690
691   We then define the mode-3 production as
692                                  X ×3 M = Unfold(MX(3) ) ∈ Rn1 ×n2 ×m
693
694   where M ∈ Rm×n3 is the transform matrix. The production can be generalized to any mode of any
695
      tensor, leading to the definition of mode-k product.

1170   F       L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
1171
1172   As discussed by current LTSF benchmark works (Qiu et al., 2024), no existing model can emerge as
1173   the best across all cases. While we are are pleased to find that TimeCapsule can consistently achieve
1174   SOTA performance on diverse datasets with competitive computation speeds, its flexibility also
1175   introduces many challenges such as hyperparameter tuning. In the following, we elaborate on several
1176   limitations of our proposed model, which may stimulates further explorations and refinements.
1177   Compression Setting: The model currently relies on fixed, hard-coded compression dimensions as
1178   hyperparameters, meaning that it is enforced to compress information into a predefined representa-
1179   tion size. This constraint might restrict the model’s full potential, especially for datasets that benefit
1180   from adaptive compression. Hence, exploring more mechanisms about time series decomposition
1181   and the compressibility of time series could lead to more flexible and effective architectures.
1182   Component Utilization: TimeCapusle’s modular structure sometimes reveals uneven utilization of
1183   its components. For instance, transformer blocks for compressed representation learning and de-
1184   pendency capture are highly effective in some cases but may go underused in others. Likewise, the
1185   dimension of linear projection within the MLP usually can significantly impact results. This vari-
1186   ability points to a potential waste of computational resources when dealing with different datasets.
1187   Regarding to this issue, we will aim to analyze each component’s role in the model and then distill
       the structure into a more compact form for practical applications.

       Generality: The current version of TimeCapsule is specifically tailored to address one of the most
1189   important challenges in time series research—long-term time series forecasting (LTSF). Despite the
1190   shown initial classification results (see Appendix G.2), its design and functionality are primarily
1191   centered around optimizing LTSF, which may inherently limit its expressiveness and effectiveness
1192   for other downstream tasks, such as classification and imputation. Exploring TimeCapsule’s adapt-
1193   ability to these broader applications, including investigating structural adjustments to better accom-
1194   modate diverse practical applications, represents an urgent modification direction for the proposed

       G.1           T IME C APSULE P RE - TRAINING USING JEPA
1201
1202   Due to the introduction of JEPA and the explicit division of functionalities within TimeCapsule,
1203   where the encoder learns the predictive representation and the decoder makes predictions based on
1204   the compressed representation, it is natural to explore its forecasting ability under a pre-training
1205   scheme.
1206
       Instead of using traditional masked pre-training, we design and test a specialized efficient JEPA-
1207
       based pre-training and fine-tuning strategy for TimeCapsule. Specifically, in the pre-training phase,
1208   we decouple the training process of the encoder and decoder: we first train the encoder using the
1209   JEPA loss, and then, with the encoder and representation predictor frozen, train the decoder using
1210   only the final prediction loss. Afterward, we fine-tune the entire model by optimizing the sum of the
1211   two losses.
1212
       We evaluate the performance of this training strategy on several large datasets. We set the maximum
1213
       pre-training epochs as 10 and 50 for the encoder and decoder, respectively, and fine-tune the entire
1214   model for a maximum of 10 epochs. As shown in the following table, the results indicate that
1215   this pre-training strategy leads to comparable performance, or even worse results, than supervised
1216   learning.
