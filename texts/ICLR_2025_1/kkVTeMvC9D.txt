009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
012            We examine the geometry of neural network training using the Jacobian of trained
013            network parameters with respect to their initial values. Our analysis reveals low-
014            dimensional structure in the training process which is dependent on the input data
015            but largely independent of the labels. We find that the singular value spectrum
016            of the Jacobian matrix consists of three distinctive regions: a “chaotic” region
017            of values orders of magnitude greater than one, a large “bulk” region of values
018
               extremely close to one, and a “stable” region of values less than one. Along
               each bulk direction, the left and right singular vectors are nearly identical, in-
019
               dicating that perturbations to the initialization are carried through training almost
020
               unchanged. These perturbations have virtually no effect on the network’s output
021            in-distribution, yet do have an effect far out-of-distribution. While the Jacobian
022            applies only locally around a single initialization, we find substantial overlap in
023            bulk subspaces for different random seeds.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
031   Prior work suggests that neural network training is in some sense “intrinsically low-dimensional.”
032   For example, Li et al. (2018) show that projecting gradient descent updates onto a randomly oriented
033   low-dimensional subspace has minimal impact on the final network’s performance. For problems
034   like image classification on MNIST or CIFAR-10, the intrinsic dimensionality appears to be in the
035   hundreds or low thousands.
036   Relatedly, Gur-Ari et al. (2018) find that during SGD training, the stochastic gradient is approx-
037   imately contained in the dominant eigenspace of the Hessian matrix, a space of dimensionality
038   roughly equal to the number of classes (which may be as low as ten). However, Song et al. (2024)
039   find that restricting training to the dominant Hessian eigenspace causes the loss to plateau at a high
040   level, while training in the complement of this subspace performs as well as standard training. Addi-
041   tionally, the effect noted by Gur-Ari et al. seems to disappear as the batch size increases, indicating
042   that it is an artifact of minibatch noise. This cautionary tale shows that different ways of operational-
043   izing the “dimensionality” of training can yield different results.
044   In this work, we propose a new way of approaching this question, based on linearizing training
045   around a given initialization. For small networks, we are able to differentiate through the entire
046   training process, obtaining the Jacobian of final parameters with respect to initial ones. The singular
047   value decomposition of this matrix specifies a basis for initial parameters, and another basis for final
048   parameters, such that training can be locally approximated as an affine map that stretches or shrinks
049   parameter space along these two bases.
050   We find that the singular value spectrum of the Jacobian consists of three distinctive regions: a
051   “chaotic” region of values orders of magnitude greater than one, a large “bulk” region of values
052   extremely close to one, and a “stable” region of values less than one. We find that this bulk depends
053   only weakly on the initialization point, loss function, and labels, but depends strongly on the data
      distribution; in particular, random data does not have a bulk.

      2                        L INEARIZING T RAINING
071
072
      Let θ0 ∈ RN be an initial parameter vector and let B ⊂ RN be a small ball of radius ϵ centered at θ0 .
073   Now consider how B as a whole is warped by the training function f : RN → RN . For sufficiently
074   small ϵ, the training process is well-approximated by its first order Taylor series around θ0 ,
075
076                                                           f (θ) ≈ f (θ0 ) + J(θ0 )(θ − θ0 )                                                       (1)
077   where J(θ0 ) is the Jacobian of the trained parameters with respect to their initial values, a matrix
078   we will call the training Jacobian. The image of B is then an ellipsoid whose principal axes are the
079   left singular vectors of J(θ0 ), and whose radii are proportional to the corresponding singular values.
080
      For a simple example, let’s consider the case of a quadratic objective L(θ) = 12 θT Hθ in the gra-
081
      dient flow limit. Then the parameters at any time t are a linear function of the initial parameters:
082
      f (θ0 , t) = Jt (θ0 ) = exp(−Ht)θ0 . Trivially, if the Hessian H is positive definite, then the training
083   Jacobian tends to the zero matrix as t → ∞, since there is a unique minimum at θ = 0.1 In this case,
084   the “dimensionality of training” is clearly equal to N , the full dimensionality of parameter space.
085
086
      But in the loss landscapes of deep neural networks, the Hessian is known to be highly singular
      (Sagun et al., 2017; Ghorbani et al., 2019; Yao et al., 2020). In the toy quadratic model above, each
087
      zero eigenvalue of H corresponds to a singular value of exp(0) = 1 in the training Jacobian. Along
088
      these zero curvature directions, the parameters do not change at all, and B retains its original radius
089   ϵ. Clearly, training “does not happen” along this subspace, and the dimensionality of training is
090   equal to rank(H) = rank(Jt (θ0 ) − I).
091
092
      For more sophisticated objectives and optimizers, the quantity rank(Jt (θ0 ) − I) continues to be
      well-defined, while rank(H) does not since the Hessian varies across the training trajectory. It
093
      measures the dimension of the subspace along which a small ball centered around the initialization
094
      retains its original radius— that is, the subspace along which volumes are locally preserved.

      3.1                              S PECTRAL A NALYSIS OF THE JACOBIAN
132
      The singular value spectrum of the training Jacobian is shown in Figure 1a. Strikingly, about 3000
133
      out of 4810 singular values are extremely close to one. Furthermore, the left and right singular
134
      vectors corresponding to these singular values are nearly identical (Figure 1b). This indicates that
135   local perturbations along these directions, making up nearly two-thirds of the dimensionality of
136   parameter space, are carried through training virtually unchanged. In what follows, we will refer to
137   the span of these singular vectors as the bulk subspace, or simply the “bulk.”
138
      The first 500 or so singular values are also notable. Along these directions, perturbations to the
139
      initial parameters are magnified by training. These are due to the nonconvexity of the objective:
140
      gradient descent on a convex objective with an appropriate step size will always have a spectral
141   norm no greater than one. Interestingly, while left and right singular vectors are not identical in this
142   part of the spectrum, they still have cosine similarity in excess of 0.6, which is very high for vectors
143   of this dimensionality. We will refer to the span of these singular vectors the chaotic subspace.
144
      Finally, the last 750 or so singular vectors are less than one. Along this stable subspace, perturba-
145
      tions to the initial parameters are partially canceled out by the optimizer.
146

      3.4                      R ELATIONSHIP WITH THE PARAMETER - FUNCTION JACOBIAN
239
      To complement the above experiments, we investigate the effect of bulk directions on network
240
      behavior by comparing the training Jacobian to the the Jacobian of network predictions (log-
241
      probabilities) on an entire dataset with respect to network parameters. We will refer to this as the
242   parameter-function Jacobian (PFJ).
243
244   We compute the PFJ on a randomly initialized model, for two different sets of inputs: the digits
245
      test set and a set of white noise images. As shown in (Figure 4a), the PFJ for the test set has a
      distinct cluster of approximately 600 extremely-small singular values, indicating a large number of
246
      parameter-space directions that do not significantly affect the model’s predictions. For the white
247
      noise dataset, this cluster is absent.
248
249   For the smallest singular values, we compare the corresponding singular vectors (the approximate
250   nullspace) to the bulk subspace from the training Jacobian (Figure 4b). The nullspace for the digits
251
      dataset overlaps strongly with the training Jacobian bulk, while the white-noise dataset nullspace
      has no more overlap than a random subspace. This provides further confirmation that the bulk has a
252
      real effect on network behavior, but primarily on inputs that are far out of distribution.

255   3.5                      T HE B ULK IS ROUGHLY I NDEPENDENT OF R ANDOM S EED AND L ABELS
256
257   The results of Sec. 3.2 show that along the bulk directions, training behaves linearly across several
258   orders of magnitude. This suggests that the bulk subspaces for independently trained networks
259   may be “similar.” To measure the similarity of subspaces, we use the method of principal angles.
260
      Specifically, for two subspaces U ⊆ RN and V ⊆ RN of the same dimensionality, we record the
      mean of the cosines of the principal angles, yielding an interpretable score in the range [0, 1].3
261
262   Since there is no hard boundary between the bulk and other subspaces, we want to ensure that our
263   results are not sensitive to any particular threshold. To achieve this, we sort the singular vectors in
264   ascending order by |σi − 1|, and consider the “bulk at k” to be the span of the first k vectors in
265   this ordering. We then sweep over a grid of values for k, reporting results for each one of them on
266
      the x-axis of Figure 5a. As a baseline, we also record the similarity of the bulk at k to a random
      subspace of the same dimensionality.

      fold, and these did not substantially change the results.

298   Following Li et al. (2018) and Song et al. (2024), we examine how the training dynamics of our net-
299   work change when training is constrained to each of the subspaces we identified above. Specifically,
300   we re-parameterize the network as follows:

302

304                               (n)
      random initialization, and θt is vector of learned parameters, initialized with zeros. For ease of
305
      comparison, we use identical hyperparameters to those used in Section 1, even though additional
306

307
308   Results are plotted in Figure 6. As before, we choose the span of the first k vectors associated with
309   each region and sweep k. We choose the k smallest for the stable region, the k largest for the chaotic
310
      region and the k closest to 1 for the bulk. We find that when neural network training is constrained

312

315   4                          C ONCLUSION
316
317   In this work, we introduced a novel lens for understanding neural network training dynamics based
318   on the training Jacobian. We identified a high-dimensional subspace of parameter space, called the
319   bulk, along which the initial parameters are left virtually unaltered by training. The bulk appears to
320
      arise from the structure of the input data, largely independent of the labels, and all but disappears
      when the data lacks structure (e.g. white noise images).
321
322   We hypothesize that the structure of the training Jacobian, and especially its bulk subspace, could
323   help explain the inductive biases of neural networks. Our results suggest that the network archi-
      tecture and input data jointly specify a relatively low-dimensional subspace of parameters which
