009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Transformers (GTs) have demonstrated superior performance compared
012            to traditional message-passing graph neural networks in many studies, especially
013            in processing graph data with long-range dependencies. However, GTs tend to
014            suffer from weak inductive bias, overfitting and over-globalizing problems due to
015            the dense attention. In this paper, we introduce SFi-attention, a novel attention
016
               mechanism designed to learn sparse pattern by minimizing an energy function
               based on network flows with ℓ1 -norm regularization, to relieve those issues caused
017
               by dense attention. Furthermore, SFi-Former is accordingly devised which can
018
               leverage the sparse attention pattern of SFi-attention to generate sparse network
019            flows beyond adjacency matrix of graph data. Specifically, SFi-Former aggregates
020            features selectively from other nodes through flexible adaptation of the sparse
021            attention, leading to a more robust model. We validate our SFi-Former on various
022            graph datasets, especially those graph data exhibiting long-range dependencies.
023            Experimental results show that our SFi-Former obtains competitive performance on
024            GNN Benchmark datasets and SOTA performance on Long-Range Graph Bench-
025            mark (LRGB) datasets. Additionally, our model gives rise to smaller generalization
026            gaps, which indicates that it is less prone to over-fitting.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Traditional graph representation learning methods, such as GCN (Defferrard et al., 2016; Kipf
031   & Welling, 2016; Zhang et al., 2019), GAT (Veličković et al., 2017), GIN (Xu et al., 2018) and
032   GatedGCN (Bresson & Laurent, 2017), typically rely on a local message-passing mechanism that
033   integrates the features of a node’s neighbors with those of directly or closely connected nodes. This
034   design effectively captures the topological structure of the graph, but it faces issues such as over-
035   smoothing (Oono & Suzuki, 2019), over-squashing (Alon & Yahav, 2020), and an inability to handle
036   graph data with long-range dependencies. As the transformer architectures have achieved widespread
037   successes in other domains, it also receives a growing interests to graph learning. To this end, Graph
038   Transformers (GTs) have been proposed, enabling each node to interact with all other nodes in
039
      the graph through self-attention mechanism (Dwivedi & Bresson, 2021; Ying et al., 2021; Müller
      et al., 2024). Such short-cut connections between nodes are in sharp contrast with message-passing
040
      based GNNs, making GTs beneficial for many realistic applications such as generating molecular
041
      graphs (Mitton et al., 2021), generating texts from knowledge graphs (Koncel-Kedziorski et al., 2019),
042   improving recommendation systems (Li et al., 2023) and so on. However, GTs effectively operates
043   on an auxiliary fully-connected graph, disregarding the original graph structure of the problem,
044   which results in a weak inductive bias with respect to the graph’s topology (Wang et al., 2024).
045   To address this weakness, positional and structural encodings (PE/SE), such as graph Laplacian
046   eigenvectors (Makarov et al., 2021; Kreuzer et al., 2021a), are commonly used in GTs to incorporate
047   structural information from the original graph. To combine the strengths of message-passing GNNs
048   and GTs, GraphGPS framework is proposed, providing a flexible platform for experimenting with
049   new model designs and learning methods (Rampášek et al., 2022).
050   Recent works (Shirzad et al., 2023; Fournier et al., 2023) have shown the effectiveness of using
051   sparsity for simplifying the computational complexity of GTs. In this study, we aim to explore
052   another potential advantage of sparsity in improving the performance and stability of GTs, through
053   the design of novel sparse attention mechanisms. In vanilla transformers, each node aggregates
      features from all other nodes, making it susceptible to attending to irrelevant or spurious information,

072   which is particularly harmful for problems of small and medium sizes. This has been shown in
073   prior studies, such as the large gaps between the training and testing evaluation metrics in dense
074   GTs (Dwivedi et al., 2022b), as well as in our own experiments presented below. According to the
075   conventional wisdom in statistics, sparse variable selection and other shrinkage methods are helpful in
076   reducing variance of estimates and improving generalization (Hastie et al., 2009). Therefore, we aim
077   to develop adaptive sparse transformers that are better suited for graph-related tasks where each node
078
      selectively aggregates information from other nodes, in order to enhance performance and improve
      generalization. We do not aim to address the computational bottleneck of GTs at this point.
079
080   To achieve this, we draw inspiration from recent advancements in a study of flow-based semi-
081   supervised learning (Rustamov & Klosowski, 2018). In this approach, an unlabeled node of interest is
082   treated as a sink node that receives sparse network flows from a set of labeled nodes. The flow patterns
083   are determined by minimizing an energy function, and the unlabeled node gathers information from
084
      each labeled node, with the weight of the information based on the total outflow from each labeled
      node. For our purposes, we treat each query node as a sink node that receives sparse flows from
085
      key nodes, with the magnitudes of these flows determining the attention scores. Further motivated
086
      by recent findings that GTs tend to overly focus on distant nodes (Xing et al., 2024), we enhance
087   the sparse-flow-based attention with hard-wired local connections with adjacent nodes. This allows
088   the sparse-flow attention to focus on the residual information in addition to those from adjacency
089   components, which further exploits the graph structure information. We refer to our GT architecture
090   based on the Sparse-Flow-induced attention mechanism as SFi-Former, where the architecture is
091   shown in Figure 1.
092
      Our main contributions are as follows. (1) We propose SFi-Former, an adaptive sparse attention
093   mechanism induced by sparse network flows from key nodes to query nodes. It demonstrates robust
094   performance on capturing dependencies among nodes. (2) We propose an energy-based framework for
095   attention, which incorporates the standard self-attention mechanism as a special case, and provides
096   a flexible framework to accommodate additional modeling elements. (3) Built upon the recent
097   GraphGPS framework (Rampasek et al., 2022), our SFi-Former outperform alternatives in processing
098   graph data with long-range dependencies, which achieves SOTA performance on the LRGB datasets.
099   It can alleviate overfitting compared to GTs with dense attention mechanism and demonstrates
100   competitive performance across various graph datasets.

102   2    R ELATED WORK
103
104   Graph Transformers (GTs). Recently, transformer architectures and attention mechanisms
105   (Vaswani et al., 2017) have achieved tremendous successes in natural language processing
106   (NLP) (Kalyan et al., 2021) and computer vision (CV) (d’Ascoli et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2021;
107   Han et al., 2022), with growing efforts to apply them to graph structures as well. However, because
      graph transformers (GTs) rely on global attention mechanisms, they suffer from a weak inductive

108
      bias, limiting their ability to fully exploit graph structure. Later research introduced various positional
109   encoding (PE) methods, such as SAN (Kreuzer et al., 2021b), Graphormer (Ying et al., 2021), and
110   SAT (Chen et al., 2022a), within the transformer framework. Concurrently, structural encoding (SE)
111   methods (Dwivedi et al., 2022a; Bodnar et al., 2021; Bouritsas et al., 2022) were also developed. Both
112   PEs and SEs aim to mitigate the weak inductive bias problem. GraphGPS (Rampasek et al., 2022)
113   offers a unified framework that integrates positional and structural encodings in GTs. Nevertheless,
114   both PE and SE may still be insufficient to fully capture the inductive bias of the graph structure.
115   Sparse transformers. The original transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) has the quadratic
116   complexity in the number of tokens, which becomes a bottleneck for processing long sequences
117   in NLP tasks. Various sparse transformers, including Performer (Choromanski et al., 2021), Big-
118   Bird (Zaheer et al., 2020), and Reformer (Kitaev et al., 2020), have been developed to address this
119   bottleneck (Catania et al., 2023). However, these methods have not demonstrated competitive perfor-
120   mance on graph data with long-range dependencies (Rampasek et al., 2022). Sparse attention has also
121   been considered in message-passing based Graph Attention Networks (Ye & Ji, 2021). Exphormer
122   leverages the idea of virtual global nodes and expander graphs to create sparse GTs (Shirzad et al.,
123
      2023). While such sparse transformers effectively reduce computation, they are often sub-optimal for
      enhancing performance.
124
125   Energy-based graph models. In addition to traditional GNNs and GTs, graph neural diffusion models
126   and energy-based graph models are significant research areas for learning from graph data (Chamber-
127   lain et al., 2021; Bronstein et al., 2021). The work by (Rustamov & Klosowski, 2018) introduces a
128   flow-based model for semi-supervised learning, improving label propagation. Elastic Graph Neural
129
      Networks (EGNN) (Liu et al., 2021) employ ℓ1 and ℓ2 -minimizaiton induced graph smoothing for
      semi-supervised learning. Graph Implicit Nonlinear Diffusion (GIND) (Chen et al., 2022c) proposes
130
      a method for feature aggregation using non-linear diffusion induced by the optimization of an energy
131
      function. Additionally, DIGNN (Fu et al., 2023) introduced implicit GNN layers as fixed-point solu-
132   tions to Dirichlet energy minimization. The survey by (Han et al., 2023) provides a good overview
133   of this growing area. However, these studies do not address GTs, which are the main focus of the
134   current work.

318   4.1   M OTIVATION FOR THE A RCHITECTURE
319
320   A graph representation learning task usually comes with a graph G̃ = {Ṽ, Ẽ}, with the node set
321   Ṽ = {v1 , v2 , · · · , vn } and the edges set Ẽ = {e1 , e2 , · · · , em }. Note that G̃ differs from the
322   complete graph G for attention patterns introduced in Sec. 3.1. We denote the adjacency matrix of the
323   graph G̃ as A ∈ Rn×n , and let D be the diagonal degree matrix where Di,i represents the degree of
      node vi . Denote the d-dimensional feature vectors for all nodes as X ∈ Rn×d .

      Defining Ã = D̂ − 2 (A + I)D̂ − 2 , the message-passing step in Graph Convolutional Networks
325
       GCN, Kipf & Welling (2016) can be expressed as X (k+1) = σ(ÃX (k) W ). In GCNs and
326
      many other message-passing-based GNNs, nodes are restricted to focus on 1-hop neighbors when
327   constructing representations at each layer. As a result, multiple layers are needed to capture long-
328   range interactions, but this approach is hindered by over-smoothing and over-squashing effects. In
329   contrast, GTs can easily capture long-range dependencies of one node by its direct attentions to
330   all others. However, not all information is relevant for downstream tasks; irrelevant or spurious
331   patterns can propagate, even when having low attention scores. We believe that a more selective
332   sparse attention can enable more effective and robust feature aggregation. Accordingly, we propose
333   an architecture that combines SFi-attention with message-passing to achieve the best of both worlds.

      6   C ONCLUSION
528
      In this paper, we introduce SFi-Former, a novel graph transformer architecture featuring a sparse
529
      attention mechanism that selectively aggregates features from other nodes through adaptable sparse
530
      attention. The sparse attention patterns in SFi-Former correspond to optimal network flows derived
531   from an energy-minimization problem, offering an interesting electric-circuit interpretation of the
532   standard self-attention mechanism (as a special case of our framework). This framework also provides
533   flexibility for extending to other innovative attention mechanisms by adjusting the energy function
534   and related components. Further augmented by an adjacency-enhanced method, SFi-Former is
535   able to balance local message-passing and global attetion within the graph transformer module,
536   effectively capturing long-range interactions across various graph datasets and achieving state-of-
537   the-art performance. Additionally, SFi-Former shows smaller train-test gaps, demonstrating reduced
538   susceptibility to overfitting. We envisage that SFi-Former and the proposed flow-based energy
539
      minimization framework hold promise for future research in other areas of machine learning.
