                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Autoformalization, the conversion of natural language mathematics into formal
               languages, offers significant potential for advancing mathematical reasoning. How-
011
               ever, existing efforts are limited to formal languages with substantial online corpora
012
               and struggle to keep pace with rapidly evolving languages like Lean 4. To bridge
013            this gap, we propose a large-scale dataset Formalization for Lean 4 (F ORM L4) de-
014            signed to comprehensively evaluate the autoformalization capabilities of large lan-
015            guage models (LLMs), encompassing both statements and proofs in natural and for-
016            mal languages. Additionally, we introduce the Process-Driven Autoformalization
017            (PDA) framework that leverages the precise feedback from Lean 4 compilers to
018            enhance autoformalization. Extensive experiments demonstrate that PDA improves
019            autoformalization, enabling higher compiler accuracy and human-evaluation scores
020            using less filtered training data. Moreover, when fine-tuned with data containing
021
               detailed process information, PDA exhibits enhanced data utilization, resulting in
               more substantial improvements in autoformalization for Lean 4.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Autoformalization is the automatic conversion of natural language mathematics into formal lan-
      guages (Wang et al., 2018; Szegedy, 2020). It reduces the high cost of formalization and bridges
028
      the gap between automated mathematical reasoning research and the vast body of natural language
029
      mathematical knowledge (Wu et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2023c).
030
031   Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) showed promising capabilities for various
032   tasks (Achiam et al., 2023; Anthropic, 2024; Meta, 2024), opening up possibilities for LLM-based
033
      autoformalization. While researchers have explored using few-shot prompting (Wu et al., 2022;
      Gadgil et al., 2022) or training LLMs on large-scale datasets containing both informal and formal
034
      data (Azerbayev et al., 2023a;b; Jiang et al., 2023a; Ying et al., 2024c;a), existing efforts are limited
035
      to formal languages with a substantial online corpus, e.g., Lean 3 (de Moura et al., 2015).
036
037   Recently, due to the improved performance and advanced compilation features, the community
038   has pivoted towards Lean 4 (de Moura & Ullrich, 2021), a next-generation theorem prover and
039
      programming language. This transition has created a pressing need for comprehensive datasets
      and models tailored specifically to Lean 4 (Ullrich & de Moura, 2022b;a; Nawrocki et al., 2023).
040
      Meanwhile, the rapid evolution of Lean 4 poses significant challenges for autoformalization efforts
041
      due to its complex syntax and extensive lemma corpora. This underscores the need for methods that
042   focus on the semantic aspects of mathematical theorems, an area previously underexplored due to
043   difficulties in automated assessment (Lu et al., 2024b). Addressing these semantic elements could
044   enhance autoformalization techniques to better adapt to Lean 4’s ongoing development.
045
      To address key gaps in autoformalization for Lean 4, we introduce Formalization for Lean 4
046
      (F ORM L4), an extensive dataset for training and evaluating LLMs’ autoformalization capabilities.
047
      F ORM L4 is derived from Mathlib 4 theorems, automatically informalized, and then rigorously quality-
048   checked manually. In addition, we propose a Process-Driven Autoformalization (PDA) framework
049   for iterative performance improvement and automated assessment. As illustrated in Figure 1, PDA
050   begins with training an autoformalization model on F ORM L4. The model’s output is then processed
051   by the Lean 4 Compiler, generating automated feedback. This feedback generates process-level
052   annotations for the autoformalization output, utilized to train a process-supervised verifier (PSV).
053   The autoformalization model is then fine-tuned based on the verifier’s feedback. This iterative cycle
      enables mutual improvement between autoformalization and verifier models.

      The unique strength of F ORM L4 lies in its inclusion of both statements and their corresponding
081
      proofs in natural and formal languages. This approach enables a comprehensive evaluation of model
082
      autoformalization outputs, contrasting with existing datasets (Jiang et al., 2023a; Ying et al., 2024a),
083   which focus solely on statements. There are three key reasons for appending proofs to theorem
084   statements in F ORM L4, each contributing to improved data quality, evaluation granularity, and
085   process-driven enhancement. First, including proofs provides valuable context that aids in the
086   generation of higher-quality statements during the dataset construction phase of F ORM L4. This
087   context also serves as a prompt, potentially enhancing the performance of autoformalization models.
088
      More importantly, including proof in F ORM L4 empowers the PDA framework to use the compiler
089   feedback of combined statement and proof steps as the proxy for evaluating the quality of statement
090   autoformalization. Formal languages offer syntactic rigidity that allows for automatic assessment by
091   compilers, eliminating ambiguity in formal language generation (Yang et al., 2023a). By utilizing
092   both statements and proofs, F ORM L4 facilitates comprehensive feedback from the Lean 4 compiler1 ,
093   enabling strict assessments of syntax and semantic integrity in reasoning logic. Lastly, we can
094   leverage the precise feedback provided by Lean 4 compilers to improve autoformalization. Building
095   on F ORM L4, our PDA is distinct from existing informal mathematical reasoning methods that rely
096   heavily on human or machine annotation (Lightman et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a).
097   Extensive experiments demonstrate that PDA significantly enhances autoformalization in Lean 4,
098   achieving better results with less training data. When fine-tuned with higher-quality data, PDA
099   utilizes this information effectively, leading to further improvements. Our key contributions are:
100
101              • We construct an extensive pioneer dataset F ORM L4 for evaluating autoformalization in Lean
102                4, encompassing the complete process from natural language questions to formal proofs.
103              • We propose a process-driven framework PDA that leverages formal languages to provide
104                process feedback on reasoning, enhancing the autoformalization capabilities of LLMs.
105              • We conduct a comprehensive study featuring robust quantitative and qualitative analysis,
106                along with human evaluation. We fully open-source F ORM L4 and PDA to facilitate research.

      Autoformalization with LLMs Autoformalization is the task of automatically converting informal
111   theorems and proofs into machine-verifiable formats (Wang et al., 2018; Szegedy, 2020). Early
112   approaches employed neural machine translation methods to translate texts into the Mizar lan-
113   guage (Wang et al., 2020). Recent advancements in LLMs have opened up new possibilities for
114   autoformalization. Researchers have explored using few-shot prompting to enable LLMs to translate
115   mathematical problems into formal formats, including Isabelle and Lean (Wu et al., 2022; Gadgil
116   et al., 2022). Other studies have adopted a more structured approach to this task. Notably, the DSP
117   system (Jiang et al., 2023c) utilizes LLMs to draft informal proofs and map them into formal sketches,
118   with automated theorem-proving systems employed to fill in the missing details in the proof sketch.
119
      Additionally, a line of research has focused on training LLMs on large-scale datasets containing both
      informal and formal mathematical data to evaluate their performance in autoformalization (Azerbayev
120
      et al., 2023a;b; Jiang et al., 2023a; Ying et al., 2024c). Unlike existing efforts that often neglect the
121
      detailed compilation information available in ITPs, our proposed method utilizes process feedback
122   from the Lean 4 compiler to further improve the autoformalization abilities of LLMs.
123
124   Process and Outcome Supervision Recent efforts explore enhancing the reasoning capabilities of
125
      LLMs by using verifiers to select the best answer from multiple candidates. There are two main types
      of verifiers: the Outcome-Supervised Verifier (OSV) and the Process-Supervised Verifier (PSV). OSV
126
      is supervised with a signal based on the final answer (Cobbe et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2023a), while PSV
127
      is with detailed feedback which requires evaluating individual reasoning steps (Uesato et al., 2022;
128   Li et al., 2023; Lightman et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2023). Despite the time-consuming annotation cost,
129   PSV offers several advantages that make it preferable to OSV. PSV can provide fine-grained feedback
130   by pinpointing the location of errors, which is valuable for reinforcement learning and automatic
131   correction (Lightman et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023). To alleviate the extensive human annotation,
132   recent efforts (Wang et al., 2023a; 2024) propose a machine annotation framework using Monte Carlo
133   Tree Search (Coulom, 2006; Silver et al., 2016). This annotation process demands a lot of computing
134   resources, potentially imposing a limitation on the usage. PDA leverages formal languages that can
135   naturally provide precise feedback on the reasoning process, enabling automatic process annotation
136
      without substantial human or machine annotation costs.

138   3       F ORM L4: DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
139
140   The rapid development of Lean 4 (de Moura & Ullrich, 2021) necessitates a benchmark to assess
141   LLMs’ autoformalization capabilities. Existing datasets (Jiang et al., 2023a; Ying et al., 2024a) aims
142   to create benchmarks by informalizing formal theorems from existing libraries. However, they rely
143   on zero-shot instructions to collect natural language statements from GPT-4 without quality checks
144   or rigorous post-processing. Additionally, it focuses solely on translating theorems, overlooking the
145   benefits of using proofs as context, which could enhance both the dataset quality and the evaluation
146
      of autoformalization performance.
147   Instead, we implement a deliberate informalization framework to curate a high-quality autoformal-
148   ization dataset F ORM L4 for training and evaluation. F ORM L4 incorporates proof steps alongside
149   statement translation, leveraging formal proof generation as an auxiliary task. Proof steps could
150   enhance formalized statement quality by providing additional context for model reasoning (Huang
151   et al., 2024b). F ORM L4 encompasses formal-informal pairs of proof steps along with statements,
152
      enabling a comprehensive assessment of an LLM’s autoformalization capabilities. Additionally,
      F ORM L4 is constructed using a fine-grained pipeline and rigorous quality checks to ensure high
153
      translation quality. In this section, we will introduce the data source of F ORM L4 (Section 3.1), the
154
      informalization approach (Section 3.2), the curation process (Section 3.3), and comparisons with
155   existing datasets (Section 3.4).

194   3.2    I NFORMALIZATION
195
      To obtain natural language data for the extracted formal theorems, we employ a two-step process: 1)
196
      We utilize a LLM to translate formal mathematical statements into natural language (i.e., formaliza-
197   tion) 2) Next, we generate new informalized versions by first explaining the formalized proof and
198   then providing a step-by-step proof in natural language. This process avoids verbatim mentions of
199   Lean 4 functions. Our construction pipeline was further augmented with the following techniques, to
200   elicit high-quality informalization output from LLMs.
201
      Statement and Proof Conversion: We instruct the model to convert all components of the formal
202
      content – both statements and proofs – into natural language. While this is computationally heavier
203   and more challenging as it requires the model to understand the syntax of Lean 4 and the logical
204   reasoning steps within each proof, the inclusion of proof steps has several benefits in both dataset
205   construction and evaluation: (1) during informalization, the provided proof steps could potentially
206   add informative context to the preceded formal theorem statement in the prompt, hence improving
207   informalization quality (Liu et al., 2023a); (2) in autoformalization, the existence of proof steps also
208   enables us to examine autoformalization performance by assessing the validity of the formalized com-
209   bination of theorem statements and proof using a compiler, increasing the difficulty and granularity
210   of autoformalization evaluation. This is supported both in our human evaluation results (Table 6) and
211
      in previous research (Huang et al., 2024b).

      Decomposition Strategy: To address the complexity of informalizing both statements and proofs,
222
      we implement a decompositional prompting strategy inspired by task decomposition approaches in
223   scalable oversight research (Christiano et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2021). Our strategy breaks down the
224   informalization process into sequential subtasks: translating the formal statement, explaining each
225   proof step, and then constructing a natural language proof. This approach effectively differentiates
226   between explaining Lean 4 terms and creating an independent natural language proof, crucial for
227   meaningful autoformalization evaluation. The strategy is augmented with few-shot examples to
228   align the model output with our expectations. Please check Appendix C for the detailed rationale
229   and Appendix D for the complete prompt template.

      Preprocessing: Before informalization, we conducted several preprocessing steps on the extracted
      theorems to enhance the quality of our formalization output. These steps include retaining spe-
234

235
      preprocessing approach can be found in Appendix O.1.
236
237   Model Selection: To ensure high-quality LLM-based informalization, we evaluated two state-of-the-

239
      study involving human annotators, Gemini-Pro-1.5 consistently outperformed GPT-4, achieving

291   This section presents our approach to enhancing the autoformalization capabilities of LLMs using
292   process feedback. We establish a baseline by fine-tuning an LLM on the F ORM L4 training set. Then
293
      we further introduce a Process-Supervised Verifier (PSV) that incorporates Lean 4 compiler feedback

      4.1     V ERIFICATION M ODEL
298
299   We propose to train the verifier by leveraging the granular, process-level feedback provided by the
300   Lean 4 compiler. This method diverges from previous approaches (Wu et al., 2022) that rely solely
301   on binary compilation outcomes. Instead, we employ a more nuanced strategy that assigns labels
302   to each step in the training data based on the “first error location” principle introduced by Uesato
303   et al. (2022). Our labeling strategy is as follows: steps preceding the first compiler-detected error are
304   labeled as “correct”, while subsequent steps are labeled as “incorrect”. This approach allows us to
305   incorporate rich, step-wise information throughout the compilation process, in contrast to traditional
306   result-centered methods that use rejected sampling or apply binary outcomes to train reward or verifier
307
      models. The parameters and variables used in our verifier models are summarized in Table 3. To
      evaluate the efficacy of our process-supervised training, we compare two models:

338   hypothesize that our method is more suitable and efficient for the task of autoformalization, as it
339   captures the nuanced progression of the proof construction process rather than relying solely on the
340   outcome. The comparative performance analysis of these models is presented in Table 5.
341
342   4.2   F URTHER E NHANCEMENT WITH BACK -P ROPAGATED P ROCESS F EEDBACK
343
344   An iterative refinement strategy is designed to leverage feedback from the Lean 4 compiler to
345
      continuously improve both the autoformalizer and verifier. This process comprises three key steps:
346   Step 1: Autoformalizer Improvement The verifier evaluates the autoformalizer’s outputs, assigning
347   labels based on their estimated likelihood of successful compilation. This filtering process ensures
348   that subsequent training phases focus on the most promising solutions. The autoformalizer is then
349   fine-tuned using the verifier’s labels, effectively leveraging the outputs that PSV evaluates correctly.
350   This approach enhances the autoformalizer’s learning efficiency and output quality.
351   Step 2: Lean 4 Process Feedback Integration The enhanced autoformalizer, when applied to the
352   training dataset, demonstrates an improved rate of successful compilations. These outputs are then
353   processed by the Lean 4 compiler, which provides detailed process feedback through syntax checking
354   and reasoning verification.
355   Step 3: Verifier Enhancement We further fine-tune the verifier using the high-quality data (with
356   an increased proportion of positive examples) generated by the enhanced autoformalizer. This fine-
357   tuning incorporates process-level supervision derived from the Lean 4 compiler’s feedback, allowing
358   the verifier to learn from a more nuanced and accurate representation of the compilation process.
359
      The cyclical nature of this process, with feedback from the Lean 4 compiler at its core, offers
360
      significant advantages. It provides an objective measure of progress, mitigating the potential for bias
361   arising from isolated interactions between the autoformalizer and verifier.

403
      We compare four autoformalizer models: 1) Baseline Autoformalizer (Baseline) 2) Rejective Sam-
404
      pling Fine-tuned (RFT) Autoformalizer (Yuan et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2022) 3) Verifier-Enhanced
405
      Autoformalizer (VEA) 4) Combined RFT and Verifier-Enhanced Autoformalizer (RFT+VEA).
406   Results are presented in Table 4, and our analysis reveals three key findings:
407
408   Effectiveness of Finetuning on F ORM L4: Even our baseline model, which is finetuned on the
409
      F ORM L4 training data, significantly outperforms both open-source and closed-source LLMs across
      all test sets. This dramatic improvement indicates the effectiveness of our dataset and training
410
      approach in enhancing autoformalization performance.
411
412   Complementary Strengths of RFT and VEA: RFT significantly improves autoformalization across
413   all test sets but is time-consuming due to its reliance on the Lean 4 compiler. In contrast, VEA offers
414   a more time-efficient approach by using predictive labels from our trained verifier, though it may not
415
      match RFT’s data quality. This trade-off between performance and efficiency suggests that these
      methods could be valuable in different scenarios, depending on the specific requirements of the task.
416
417   Synergistic Benefits of Combined Approach: The RFT+VEA model, which combines the strengths
418   of both methods, shows the best performance across all test sets. This finding is particularly
419   noteworthy, as it demonstrates that the verifier, despite being trained using feedback from the Lean 4
420   compiler, can contribute additional value when combined with direct compiler feedback for filtering
421
      training data. We propose this is due to the limitations of compilation alone in ensuring semantic
      alignment between formal and informal statements Lu et al. (2024b). The Lean 4 compiler can
422
      only validate the formal proof’s correctness, not its semantic correspondence to the original natural
423
      language. In contrast, our verifier can take both the formal statement and the informal statement
424   with proof as input, and the superior performance of RFT+VEA suggests a potential solution to the
425   long-standing challenge of ensuring semantic alignment between formal and informal statements
426   in autoformalization. The success of the combined RFT+VEA approach further underscores the
427   potential for iterative improvements in autoformalization techniques.

531
532
      In the current study, we introduce a new benchmark F ORM L4 specifically designed to assess the
533
      autoformalization capabilities of LLMs in Lean 4, and propose a processs-driven autoformalization
534   (PDA) training pipeline with iterative process-level feedback. Unlike existing datasets, which focus
535   on translating questions into statements, F ORM L4 focuses on extracting each statement’s proof
536   steps, enabling a more comprehensive, fine-grained, and effective evaluation of autoformalized
537   statements. Importantly, PDA leverages the precise feedback naturally provided by the Lean 4
538   compiler to improve autoformalization, significantly enhancing performance and enabling more
539   effective utilization of high-quality training data. For future work, we plan to extend our benchmark
      and apply our method to more formal languages such as Isabelle, HOL Light, Agda, and Coq.

       Formal Mathematics Formal languages, such as Isabelle (Wenzel et al., 2008), Lean (de Moura
1005
       et al., 2015), HOL Light (Harrison, 1996), and Coq (Barras et al., 1997), have become integral tools
1006   in modern mathematics verification systems. These interactive theorem provers (ITPs) function as
1007   programming languages, allowing users to input statements and proofs in a formal language for
1008   automatic correctness verification. Among these ITPs, Lean 4 (de Moura & Ullrich, 2021) stands out
1009   for its recent advancements, offering full extensibility and addressing previous limitations (Ullrich
1010   & de Moura, 2019; 2022b;a; Nawrocki et al., 2023). However, keeping up with Lean 4’s rapid
1011   development, including its evolving syntax, semantics, library, and other aspects, remains a challenge,
1012   even for human experts and powerful LLMs like GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023). To bridge this gap, we
1013   introduce F ORM L4 for training and testing autoformalization of LLM for Lean 4. Unlike the existing
1014
       Lean 4 dataset MMA (Jiang et al., 2023a), which focuses on translating questions to statements,
       F ORM L4 provides a “complete” autoformalization from natural language questions and answers to
1015
       statements and proofs in Lean 4. This more challenging task requires understanding Lean 4’s syntax
1016
       and the reasoning steps in each proof, enabling valuable feedback from the Lean 4 compiler on both
1017   syntax and reasoning verification.
1018
1019
       Formal Datasets The field of formal datasets has seen significant progress in extracting and
1020   cleaning theorems and proofs from established formal libraries and verification projects. Several
1021   datasets have been developed for popular proof assistants, focusing on extracting information from
1022   existing formalizations. For Coq, notable datasets include Gamepad (Huang et al., 2019), CoqGym
1023   (Yang & Deng, 2019), and PRISM (Reichel et al., 2023). For Isabelle, datasets like IsarStep (Li
1024   et al., 2021) and Magnushammer (Mikula et al., 2023) leverage the Archive of Formal Proofs and
1025   Isabelle Standard Library. Similarly, LeanStep (Han et al., 2022), LeanDojo (Yang et al., 2023b), and
       MLFMF (Bauer et al., 2023) utilize the mathlib library in Lean. LeanDojo, in particular, extracts


                                                         19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       over 98,000 theorems and proofs with 130,000 premises from Mathlib. Beyond extracting data
1027   from existing projects, several works have focused on manually annotating or formalizing problems
1028   expressed in natural language. miniF2F (Zheng et al., 2022b) stands out by manually formalizing 488
1029   Olympiad-level problems across four proof systems, equally splitting them into validation and test
1030   sets. FIMO (Liu et al., 2023a) and ProofNet (Azerbayev et al., 2023a) formalize theorem statements
1031   from IMO and undergraduate-level problems in Lean. For domain-specific problems, TRIGO (Xiong
1032   et al., 2023b) focuses on formalizing trigonometric reduction problems. UniGeo (Chen et al., 2022)
1033   and FormalGeo (Zhang et al., 2023) annotate proof steps for geometry proving problems. These
1034   datasets provide valuable resources for researchers working on automated theorem proving, proof
1035
       verification, and natural language processing in the context of formal mathematics.
1036
1037
       Improving Reasoning Abilities of LLMs To enhance the reasoning capabilities of LLMs, prior
       research primarily focuses on specific prompting techniques. Existing efforts include few-shot
1038
       prompting with intermediate steps augmented demonstrations (Wei et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023b;
1039
       Xiong et al., 2023a) or zero-shot prompting with specific instructions (Kojima et al., 2022; Yasunaga
1040   et al., 2023). Although these methods have shown promising results, their effectiveness is often
1041   constrained by their task-specific nature and the labour-intensive process of designing prompts,
1042   leading to inconsistent outcomes across different tasks (Ye & Durrett, 2022; Zhou et al., 2023).
1043   Another strategy to facilitate reasoning involves instruction tuning or knowledge distillation, which
1044   elicits reasoning paths from LLMs without explicit prompting (Mukherjee et al., 2023; Gunasekar
1045   et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023; 2024c). These approaches typically involve resource-intensive fine-
1046   tuning over LLMs and require a large set of examples annotated with chain-of-thoughts (CoT). To
1047   address these challenges, verification techniques have emerged as a promising solution (Uesato et al.,
1048
       2022; Lightman et al., 2024). Verification models are trained to evaluate and potentially correct the
       reasoning process generated by LLMs. This approach aims to mitigate the risk of relying solely on
1049
       the top-1 result, which may not always be reliable (Wang et al., 2023a; Lu et al., 2024a).
1050
1051
       Learning From Feedback Improving LLMs through learning from feedback has become a preva-
1052   lent strategy, notably through reinforcement learning from human feedback, which seeks to align
1053   LLMs with human values by refining their outputs based on feedback (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al.,
1054   2022). However, this method faces challenges such as high costs due to manual labor and a lack of
1055   real-time feedback capabilities. An alternative strategy involves using self-correcting LLMs, which
1056   rely on automated feedback to iteratively adapt and understand the consequences of their actions
1057   without heavy reliance on human intervention. This feedback can be derived from inside sources such
1058   as the model itself (Madaan et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023) or generation logits (Yao et al., 2024),
1059   and outside sources such as tools (Gou et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024a), knowledge bases (Gao et al.,
1060
       2023; Yu et al., 2023b), or evaluation metrics (Jung et al., 2022; Welleck et al., 2023). Our method
       leverages formal languages that can naturally provide precise feedback on the reasoning process,
1061
       enabling automatic process annotation without substantial human or machine annotation costs.

1064   C    D ETAILED D ECOMPOSITION S TRATEGY
1065
1066   Our decomposition strategy for informalization involves instructing the model to perform the follow-
1067   ing subtasks sequentially:
1068
             1. Translate the formal statement into a natural-language problem.
1069
1070         2. Explain the meaning of each step of the formal proof in natural language, based on the
1071            definition of the employed lemma or tactics.
1072         3. Write a step-by-step proof of the problem in natural language without verbatim mention of
1073            any Lean 4 function.
1074
1075   For F ORM L4 construction, we extract only the translated natural-language problem and the step-by-
1076
       step proof from the model output to form the natural-language data.
1077   The strategy of explaining each tactic step before writing the natural-language proof serves two
1078   crucial purposes: 1. It creates a reasoning buffer for the model. 2. It effectively differentiates between
1079   “listing and explaining each Lean 4 term from the formal proof in natural language” and “proving the
       problem statement step by step in natural language”, with the latter being our intended goal.


                                                          20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       Our empirical observations indicate that a naive instruction prompt without decomposition often
1081   leads to ambiguity. Models tend to write natural-language proof steps by explaining each term in the
1082   formal proof steps (and even in the formal statement), regardless of verbal emphasis on the distinction.
1083   This approach would render autoformalization evaluation meaningless, as the formal content would
1084   already exist in the input.
1085
       In contrast, decomposing our complex goal into separate subtasks effectively addresses this issue,
1086
       as verified by human expert evaluators. The decomposition strategy ensures that the resulting
1087   natural language proof is genuinely independent of the formal proof structure, making it suitable for
1088   autoformalization tasks.

       expected format and goal. The complete prompt template, including these examples, can be found in
