009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Variational Autoencoder (VAE) underscores its indispensable role along the grow-
012            ing prominence of Latent Video Diffusion Models (LVDMs). Nevertheless, cur-
013            rent latent generative models are generally built upon image VAEs, which com-
014            press the spatial dimension only. While, it is vital for video VAE to model tem-
015            poral dynamic patterns to produce smooth high quality video reconstruction. To
016
               address these issues, we propose UniVAE, which compresses videos both spa-
               tially and temporally while ensuring coherent video construction. Specifically,
017
               we employ 3D convolutions at varying scales in the encoder to temporally com-
018
               press videos, enabling the UniVAE to capture dependencies across multiple time
019            scales. Furthermore, existing VAEs only reconstruct videos at a low resolution
020            and fps, bounded by limited GPU memory, which makes the entire video gener-
021            ation pipeline fragmented and complicated. Thus, in conjunction with the new
022            encoder, we explore the potential of the VAE decoder to perform frame interpola-
023            tion, aiming to synthesize additional intermediate frames without relying on stan-
024            dalone add-on interpolation models. Compared with existing VAEs, the proposed
025            UniVAE explores a unified way to compress videos both spatially and temporally
026            with jointly designed encoder and decoder, thus achieving accurate and smooth
027
               video reconstruction at a high frame rate. Extensive experiments on commonly
               used public datasets for video reconstruction and generation demonstrate the su-
028
               periority of the proposed UniVAE. The code and the pre-trained models will be
029
               released to facilitate further research.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The compression and reconstruction of visual data are fundamental to the generative research Rom-
035   bach et al. (2022); Yu et al. (2023). As latent diffusion models Blattmann et al. (2023); Guo et al.
036   (2023); Yang et al. (2024); Gong et al. (2024) become central to generative tasks, the quality of the
037   latent space largely determines the upper bound of generation performance, which is contingent on
038   the modeling capability of VAE models. While comparing to relatively mature image VAEs Rom-
039
      bach et al. (2022); Podell et al. (2023), video data presents greater challenges due to the larger data
      volume, temporal redundancy, and GPU memory constraints Chen et al. (2024a). These issues raise
040
      the question how to design unified frame-enriched video VAEs dedicated to videos.
041
042   Current VAEs can be categorized into two main types: (1) Image VAE approachs Guo et al. (2023);
043   Blattmann et al. (2023), treating a video as a series of individual frames. This kind of VAE effec-
044   tively preserves spatial pixel contents and typically utilizes lightweight 2D convolution Rombach
045
      et al. (2022). However, it may result in temporal instability and inadequate temporal compression in
      the latent space, as well as large GPU memory consumption of diffusion models. (2) Video VAEs
046
      with temporal compression Chen et al. (2024a); Zheng et al. (2024), which can compress video
047
      along the temporal dimension, achieving higher compression ratios. Recently, many DiT based
048   video diffusion models Brooks et al. (2024); Lab & etc. (2024) have improved VAEs to achieve bet-
049   ter temporal modeling. It greatly increases the number of input tokens, allowing for the generation
050   of videos with more frames. However, the simplistic temporal compression designs in current video
051   VAEs, such as using the primitive temporal pooling operation, limit their ability to effectively cap-
052   ture temporal consistency, considerly restricting the performance of LVDMs. Thus, we believe that
053   a VAE’s ability to capture temporal relationships in video data is essential, which shall be designed
      jointly and integrated in the diffusion generation process for optimal results.

072   Such a unification is challenging for VAEs, since it involves effectively combining temporal model-
073   ing with deep integration into diffusion models. Specifically, the video generation pipeline is much
074   more complicated than that of image generation Brooks et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2024), and is
075   severely bounded by GPU memory. As a result, existing VAEs primarily focus on compressing and
076   reconstructing inputs at a low resolution and frame rate, e.g., 256×256 at 8fps. The interpolation of
077   frames and pixels is delegated to standalone add-on super-resolution Saharia et al. (2022); Shi et al.
078   (2024) or frame interpolation models Reda et al. (2022) connected at the end of the video generation
079
      pipeline. This fragmentation complicates the use and optimization of the video generation pipeline,
      motivating our pursuit of a unified approach. Our observations indicate that VAEs can assume mul-
080
      tiple roles within the current video generation stream. If the encoder can effectively model temporal
081
      variations, the decoder could theoretically synthesize additional frames, leading to high fps videos
082   without the need for separate interpolation models. This unified VAE structure could reduce the
083   number of frames required by diffusion models to improve training efficiency while streamlining
084   the video generation pipeline.
085
      These issues motivate us to investigate a new approach, UniVAE, to compress videos both spatially
086
      and temporally and jointly design the encoder and decoder, thus achieving accurate and smooth
087
      video reconstruction at a high frame rate. Through the analysis of existing VAEs, we observe that
088   most methods typically utilize single-scale convolution kernels for temporal downsampling Chen
089   et al. (2024a). It limits the ability of VAEs to effectively model motions and changes occurring over
090   varying time scales, making it hard to maintain inter-frame coherence in the reconstructed videos,
091   such as flickering or blurriness. In fact, previous work Yang et al. (2024) has shown that encoding
092   in the temporal dimension in VAEs is more difficult than encoding in the spatial dimension, which
093   suggests that improving the temporal encoding capability is the key challenge.
094   To this end, we propose a multi-scale temporal convolution architecture for the encoder in UniVAE.
095   Specifically, during the temporal compression, we apply 3D convolution Ji et al. (2012) with vary-
096   ing kernel sizes to downsample the videos in the temporal dimension, followed by concatenating
097   and fusing the extracted features. Our multi-scale convolutions are designed across the channel di-
098   mension, without introducing additional computational cost. It allows UniVAE to capture dynamic
099   patterns across different time scales, enabling a comprehensive understanding of the video’s tempo-
100   ral structure. Furthermore, we integrate the frame interpolation function into the decoder to generate
101   high frame rate video, enabling the production of smooth video content. For this purpose, we pro-
102
      pose a latent-guided refinement training scheme, which introduces a refinement decoder to UniVAE
      with the assistance of a pre-trained VAE decoder.
103
104   We summarize our contributions as follows:
105
106            • We propose the UniVAE which is designed specifically for video data. Both the encoder
107              and decoder in UniVAE are enhanced to compress and reconstruct videos temporally and
                 spatially, devoted to video generation via latent diffusion models.

117   2     R ELATED W ORKS
118
119   Video Generation Models. Video generation may find killer applications in short video apps, video
120   ads, and filming. Ho et al. (2020). With the emergence of OpenAI SORA Brooks et al. (2024), the
121
      immense potential of video generation models has become evident, prompting researchers to invest
      passion and resources into this field. Some works Ho et al. (2022); Singer et al. (2022); Zhang et al.
122
      (2023) directly generate videos in pixel space. Due to the computational constraints, recent works
123
      have shifted toward learning video distributions in a latent space. The popular approach involves
124   leveraging a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) to compress videos into a latent space, followed by
125   employing diffusion models to learn the distribution within that space. The works such as Animate-
126   Diff Guo et al. (2023), Stable Video Diffusion Blattmann et al. (2023) adopt the U-Net structure
127   from T2I diffusion models, while others, such as SORA Brooks et al. (2024), Open-Sora Zheng
128   et al. (2024), Latte Ma et al. (2024), CogVideoX Yang et al. (2024), and Open-Sora-Plan Lab &
129   etc. (2024), utilize transformer as the denoisers. Regardless of the denoiser architecture, VAE plays
130   a decisive role in determining the quality of the latent space for training and the final video recon-
131   struction, which is crucial for latent video diffusion models (LVDMs).
132   Variational Autoencoder. Variational Autoencoder (VAE) Kingma (2013) is designed to gener-
133   ate new data by sampling from learned latent distribution. Recently, it has been widely used in
134   generative models, which can be divided into two categories, discrete and continuous. The first
135   one is discrete VQ-VAEs Van Den Oord et al. (2017), which compress an input sample into a latent
136   space and quantize it into discrete tokens with a codebook. These tokens are then fed into subsequent
137   autoregressive-based generative models Yu et al. (2023). The second one is continuous VAEs, which
138
      compress an input sample into a continuous latent space and have been widely used in latent diffu-
      sion models. Stable Diffusion Rombach et al. (2022) is the first work to introduce VAE to diffusion
139
      models. For LVDMs, a straightforward approach is to apply Stable Diffusion VAE Rombach et al.
140
      (2022) to compress input videos frames by frames. Stable Video Diffusion VAE Blattmann et al.
141   (2023) further explore the temporal relations based on Stable Diffusion VAE. However, these VAEs
142   only compress videos spatially, while ignoring the temporal dimension. Recently, several works
143   have explored designing VAEs that compress videos both temporally and spatially. OS-VAE Zheng
144   et al. (2024) adopts a cascaded architecture, where a temporal VAE is applied to the latent space of a
145   spatial VAE. CV-VAE Zhao et al. (2024) proposes to compress videos temporally while focusing on
146   aligning its latent space with that of the existing Stable Diffusion VAE. OD-VAE Chen et al. (2024a)
147   also proposes to compress video temporally. Building on previous research, the proposed UniVAE
148   explores a unified way to compress videos both spatially and temporally with jointly designed en-
149
      coder and decoder, thus achieving accurate and smooth video reconstruction at a high frame rate.

194   3.2   M ULTI -S CALE T EMPORAL D OWNSAMPLING
195
196   In addition to spatial compression, video VAEs also compress vidoes temporally to further reduce the
197   computational cost of video generation in LVDMs. However, the temporal compression inevitably
198   leads to details loss, resulting in issues such as jitter and flickering in the reconstructed videos.
199   Upon reviewing the existing video VAE architectures, we observe that most of them rely on fixed-
200   size convolutional kernels for temporal downsampling, with a kernel size of 3 being the common
201
      choice Chen et al. (2024a). This limits their ability to capture dynamic temporal features across
      different time scales in videos. In fact, previous work Yang et al. (2024) has shown that temporal
202
      compression in video VAEs is more challenging than spatial compression for videos. Therefore, it
203
      is crucial to explore better schemes for temporal compression.
204
205   To solve this issue, we propose a multi-scale temporal downsampling architecture to compress
206   videos temporally in the causal 3D encoder. Unlike previous works Chen et al. (2024a); Zhao
207
      et al. (2024) that only utilize fixed-size convolutional kernels to compress videos temporally, we
      design a series of convolutional kernels with varying kernel sizes for temporal compression, as
208
      shown in Fig. 2. Specifically, we apply multiple causal 3D convolution F = {f1 , f2 , ..., fp }, where
209
      fi ∈ Rcin ×cout ×ti ×hi ×wi (i ∈ [1, ..., p]). ti , hi , and wi represent the temporal size, height, and
210
      width of the convolution kernel. Each fi has a unique kernel size ti along the time axis, which en-
211
      dows F with the ability to capture dynamic patterns of different time scales in videos. On the other
212   hand, for all fi ∈ F, they share the same hi and wi .
213
214   Given an input video tensor x ∈ RN ×H×W ×C , we first partition it into p parts along the channel
                                                                             C
215   dimension. We can get x = [x1 , x2 , ..., xp ], where xi ∈ RN ×H×W × p . Then, we leverage the
      multi-scale convolution F to compress x temporally and spatially. Specifically, we first perform


                                                                                                                                                                                            4

      convolution between each kernel fi and its corresponding segment xi , and then concatenate the
217   resulting yi along the channel dimension to produce the final output y, which can be formulated as:
218
219
                                      y = F ⊗ x = [f1 ⊗ x1 , f2 ⊗ x2 , ..., fp ⊗ xp ],
220   where ⊗ is the convolution operation. Compared with previous video VAEs, UniVAE leverages
221   convolutional kernels with varying scales for video temporal compression. This allows the UniVAE
222   to capture temporal dependencies across different time scales. fi with smaller ti will focus on lo-
223
      cal temporal variations, capturing fast movements and transient changes, while those with larger ti
      capture long-term dynamics such as slow motions or extended trends. This enhances the diversity
224
      and richness of the features extracted by E, providing more varied and informative latent represen-
225
      tations for subsequent video diffusion models. Moreover, the multi-scaled features supports the D2
226   to interpolate additional frames in between with consistent object appearance and coherent motion,
227   maintaining the coherence of the enhanced videos, which will be discussed in Sec. 3.3. Our design
228   also improves the VAE’s generalization ability, enabling it to adapt to videos with different motion
229   speeds and temporal patterns.

231   3.3   L ATENT-G UIDED R EFINEMENT T RAINING S TRATEGY
232
233   Existing video VAEs typically employ an encoder to compress a video X into lower-dimensional la-
234   tent representation Z temporally and spatially to reduce the computational cost of LVDMs, and then
235   utilize a decoder to reconstruct the corresponding a video X̂ to ensure high-quality video genera-
236   tion, where X and X̂ have the same number of frames. Beyond the common paradigm, we wonder
237   whether 3D VAEs have the potential to generate richer video content beyond reconstruction.
238
      Specifically, by interpolating frames during the decoding process, the decoder can produce content-
239   rich videos X̃. This could allow LVDMs to generate smooth, high-fps videos without increasing
240
      computational costs of diffusion models.
241   To this end, we introduce a causal 3D refinement decoder to the UniVAE, which can interpolate
242   video frames, as illustrated in Fig. 2. In order to train the proposed decoder and refinement decoder
243   effectively, we propose a two-stage latent-guided refinement training scheme. As shown in Fig. 2,
244   we train the VAE encoder E and decoder D1 in the first stage. Given the original video X, we
245   send it to the encoder to get the latent representation Z, which is Z = E(X). Then, we utilize the
246   decoder to reconstruct video X̂, which is X̂ = D1 (Z). Following the training of video VAEs in
247   LVDMs Chen et al. (2024a); Zhao et al. (2024), we use a combination of reconstruction loss Zhang
248
      et al. (2018), adversarial loss Goodfellow et al. (2020), and KL regularization Kingma (2013) as the
      training objective, formulated as:
249
250                        LV AE (E, D1 ) = Lrecon (X, X̂) + Ladv (X, X̂) + LKL (X, Z),                                      (1)
251   After training E and D1 , we introduce a refinement decoder D2 to UniVAE and train it independently
252   in the second stage. E and D1 are frozen in this stage. The refinement decoder D2 shares almost the
253   same architecture as the standard decoder D1 , with the primary difference being that D2 employs a
254   higher temporal upsampling rate in the final temporal decoding module, as illustrated in Fig. 2. This
255   enables D2 interpolate an extra intermediate frame between every two consecutive reconstructed
256   frames frames, improving the smoothness of the reconstructed videos. Since D1 has been trained
257   to decode video from Z, we leverage it to facilitate the training of D2 . Specifically, given a video
258   X ∈ R(N +1)×H×W ×3 , we send it to E to get Z. Then, we feed Z into D1 and D2 , respectively.
259

      4.1    E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
317
318   Training Details. In the first stage, we train the encoder E and the decoder D1 of the UniVAE with
319   Adam optimizer for 380k steps, where β1 and β2 are set to 0.9 and 0.999, respectively. The learning
320   rate is set to a constant value of 1×10−5 , and the batch size is set to 8. Following Chen et al. (2024a),
321   the input videos are pre-processed to a length of 25 frames with a resolution of 256 × 256. In the
322   second stage, we independently train the refinement decoder D2 for 1,000k steps. In this stage, the
323   input videos are processed to a length 25 frames with a resolution of 128 × 128. The entire UniVAE

      the potential of VAE to interpolate reconstructed videos. It enables users to flexibly customize the

419   ever, the generated additional frames still perform well. We attribute this to the multi-timescale

      4.5   A BLATION S TUDY
465
      In this section, we perform comprehensive ablation studies to demonstrate the effectiveness of our
466
      designs in the proposed UniVAE. Specifically, we evaluate the benefits of multi-scale temporal
467
      downsampling module and the latent-guided refinement training strategy.
468
469   Effectiveness of the Multi-Scale Temporal Downsampling Module. To better capture the dy-
470   namic patterns across varying time scales in videos, we propose to utilize 3D convolution kernels
471
      with multiple kernel sizes to compress the video temporally. As described in Tab. 3, our UniVAE
      utilize 3D convolutions with four different kernel sizes (3, 5, 7, and 9) to perform temporal com-
472
      pression. We also design two variants for comparison. Among them, “UniVAE-V” uses only two
473
      different convolutions with kernel size 3 and 5. “Baseline” employs a single convolution with fixed
474   size 3 for temporal compression, which is popular in current video VAEs Chen et al. (2024a). As we
475   can see, UniVAE and UniVAE-V outperform “Baseline” by 2.97dB and 2.92dB in terms of PSNR,
476   respectively, which suggests that using multi-scale convolutions is beneficial for temporal compres-
477   sion. On the other hand, we observe that UniVAE, with diverse set of convolutions, obtains slightly
478   better performance than UniVAE-V. It indicates that increasing the diversity of convolutions in F
479   can effectively capture temporal features in videos, thereby improving the reconstruction quality.
480   Effectiveness of the Latent-Guided Refinement Training Strategy. We propose a latent-guided
481   refinement training strategy to train the standard decoder D1 and refinement decoder D2 in different
482   stage. To verify the effectiveness of this, we propose a variant named UniVAE-V2, which directly
483   trains D2 without the assistance of D1 . As shown in Fig 7, we can see that our latent-guided re-
484   finement training strategy can better facilitate the additional frames generation in D2 . The proposed
485   latent-guided refinement training strategy not only ensures UniVAE standard video compression and
      reconstruction capability, (i.e. , D1 ), but also better encourages D2 to deliver smooth outputs.

      5   C ONCLUSIONS
487
488
      In this study, we analyze the critical role of VAEs in current latent diffusion generative models, and
489   present a unified video VAE for LVDMs, named UniVAE. We propose to utilize convolutions with
490   varying kernel sizes in the encoder for temporal compression. Moreover, we explore the potential of
491   VAE to generate additional frames to deliver smooth video content, and delicately design a latent-
492   guided refinement training strategy for this purpose. Extensive experiments on video reconstruction
493   and generation demonstrate the effectiveness of the UniVAE. We hope our work can motivate further
494   research in VAE to advane the development of generative models.
