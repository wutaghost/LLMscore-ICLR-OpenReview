009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            For several decades, the standard approach to evaluating a learned model has been
012            to compute a numerical loss that summarizes the quality of the model based on a
013            previously unseen test set. Two models for the same task can then be compared by
014            looking at their scores on this set. However, recent experience with large language
015            models (LLMs) has shown that comparing summary statistics of two broadly-
016
               capable models may not provide a reliable predictor of performance on real-world
               tasks. This has led to a growing use of crowd-sourced human feedback directly
017
               comparing outputs from pairs of models. While helpful, this approach requires
018
               a process that involves significant time and human effort, limiting the number
019            of models that can be thoroughly evaluated. To address the need for a scalable
020            method of comparing modern LLMs, we present a novel approach to evaluation
021            via tournament-style model competitions that are constructed automatically from
022            pre-existing benchmarks. We use these automatically-constructed tournaments to
023            compute ratings for a range of models on a diverse set of tasks that use automated
024            scoring via both multiple-choice and free-form text generation. We compare four
025            prominent rating systems: Elo, Glicko, TrueSkill™ , and the Bradley-Terry model,
026            and find that automatically-constructed tournaments provide reliable information
027
               about the relative performance of LLMs while using only a fraction of the amount
               of data required by current benchmark-based evaluation methods. We discuss
028
               implications for model evaluations and propose future directions for large-scale
029
               LLM comparisons.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Machine learning models have historically been developed for narrowly defined tasks, which has
035   enabled a simple and now well-understood procedure for evaluation: collect a large number of
036   instances of the task, partition those instances into training and evaluation sets, find weights that
037   minimize a loss function on the training set, and compute the loss on the previously unseen eval-
038
      uation set. We declare models to have “learned” or “generalized” if performance on the unseen
      examples meets our requirements. Early definitions of machine learning go so far as to include the
039
      specification of a task in the definition of learning (Mitchell, 1997).
040
041   Recent developments have called into question the continuing utility of this approach: internet-scale
042   datasets and the transformer architecture have led to the creation multi-billion parameter models that
043   display broad capabilities in relation to language processing and vision. These large models have
044
      not necessarily been trained on a single task, making it hard to assess the extent to which the models
      generalize. In some cases it is not even possible to assess whether or not a potential instance of test
045
      data is truly “unseen” or “in distribution.”
046
047   There have been two broad approaches to addressing this challenge: on the one hand, researchers
048   have pursued the traditional approach, and have created a large number of benchmarks, with both
049   training and test sets. These benchmarks have grown in size and difficulty as language models have
050
      improved. A new model is typically evaluated on several benchmarks, and its scores are either
      reported directly or aggregated into a single summary statistic. There are now several leaderboards
051
      that use benchmark performance to rank models.
052
053   At the same time, researchers have recognized the importance of direct comparison of language
      models, and have constructed systems that allow human comparison of the output of two language


                                                        1

054
      models on a single task instance. This direct comparison data is used to compute scores using ap-
055   proaches such as Elo ratings that are updated over many comparisons. While helpful, this approach
056   requires a process that involves significant time and human effort, limiting the number of models
057   that can be thoroughly evaluated.
058
      To address the need for a scalable method of comparing modern LLMs, we present a novel ap-
059
      proach to evaluation via tournament-style model competitions that are constructed automatically
060
      from pre-existing benchmarks. We build off of the framework of the Evaluation Harness developed
061   by EleutherAI (Gao et al., 2024). We use these automatically-constructed tournaments to compute
062   ratings for a range of models on a diverse set of tasks that use automated scoring via both multiple-
063   choice and free-form text generation. In this work we outline the details of this approach, and then
064   describe our empirical evaluation of the method on a number of open models across a range of tasks.
065   Our system is available as an open source project.1

      2       R ELATED W ORK
068
069
      Pairwise ranking systems have been used in a variety of LLM comparisons, using human feedback
070
      (Nichol et al., 2021; Askell et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2022), an LLM-as-judge (Dettmers et al., 2023;
071   Zheng et al., 2023), or with synthetic data (Boubdir et al., 2023). The most popular is LMSYS’
072   Chatbot Arena (Chiang et al., 2024), where users get to rank the output between two different mod-
073   els. Chatbot Arena’s leaderboard initially used Elo but has been updated to use the Bradley-Terry
074   model (with a similar initial ranking and scaling factor to Elo) (Chiang et al., 2023). They report the
075   Bradley-Terry model to be favorable due to model performance being static and it producing better
076   confidence intervals.
077   Boubdir et al. (2023) illustrates the properties of Elo as a ranking system between different LLMs,
078   highlighting the importance of the hyperparameters in getting stable rankings. They use both syn-
079   thetic data generated from a binomial distribution and human feedback to validate their experiments.
080   Peyrard et al. (2021) utilizes the Bradley-Terry model, Elo, and TrueSkill to compare NLP systems,
081   finding that Bradley-Terry disagrees with the mean and the median aggregation about 30% of the
082   time about state-of-the-art models.
083   TrueSkill has been adopted in a couple of instances for NLP evaluation. Sakaguchi et al. (2014)
084   adapt TrueSkill to rank machine translation based on human annotations. They note that the number
085   of pairwise annotations needed to accurately rank models decreases when sampling the space of
086   completions non-uniformly. Dušek et al. (2018) and Deriu et al. (2020) continue in this direction
087   using TrueSkill, alongside a bootstrapping resampling technique, to cluster results for natural lan-
088   guage generation and a Turing Test-like completion respectively. Chen et al. (2024) use TrueSkill in
089   their LLMArena to assess performance in multiagent dynamic environments.

093   3.1       A B RIEF H ISTORY OF M ETRICS FOR L ANGUAGE M ODELS
094
095   NLP has utilized various metrics to evaluate performance of language models since its inception.
096   Early metrics for information retrieval focused on precision and recall or combined them into
097   an F-measure (Manning et al., 2008; Jurafsky & Martin, 2024), while machine translation tasks
098   often used bilingual human translators to evaluate performance. Papineni et al. (2002) introduced
099   BLEU (Bilingual Evaluation Understudy), an automatic metric that evaluates machine translation
100
      quality by comparing the language model output to one or more human translations using n-gram
      overlap count. BLEU provided a quick, inexpensive, and language-independent metric that initially
101
      correlated well with human judgements, allowing for quicker development cycles for machine
102
      translation systems. Banerjee & Lavie (2005) introduced METEOR (Metric for Evaluation of
103   Translation with Explicit ORdering) which improved upon BLEU by using heuristics to reward
104   n-grams beyond exact matches, allowing for synonyms and paraphrases in the translation. Moving
105   beyond n-gram metrics, Zhang et al. (2020) proposed BERTscore, which leverages contextual
106   embeddings from a language model (i.e. BERT) to better capture semantic similarity between
107

112

      ment and hyperparameter tuning, Q&A datasets have become standard benchmarks. Human evalua-
113

118   many of the metrics, as well as potential data contamination in pretraining, the leaderboard has been
119   updated (Fourrier et al., 2024). It now uses: IFEval (Zhou et al., 2023), BBH (Suzgun et al., 2022),
120   MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021b), GPQA (Rein et al., 2023), MuSR (Sprague et al., 2024), and
121   MMLU-PRO (Wang et al., 2024).

123   4    T OURNAMENT E VALUATION
124
125   We propose to use benchmark Q&A tasks designed for evaluation of a single model to construct
126   matches that enable direct head-to-head comparisons between two models. To enable the compari-
127   son of multiple models, we organize sets of matches into tournaments that allow us to derive scores
128   for each model of interest.
129   We define a task to be a triple T = (D, F, µ), where D is a set of instances, i.e. data points from a
130   benchmark dataset, F is a filter function (typically for string normalization and regex-based string
131   processing), and µ is a metric function. In the standard approach to evaluation, a language model
132   L is applied to each instance xi ∈ D. The output yi = L(xi ) of the model is then passed through
133   the filter zi = F (yi ), and the filtered output is used to compute a score via the metric function
134   mi = µ(zi ). The score mi for each instance i is then aggregated into a single summary statistic that
135   captures the performance of the model L on the task T .

182   5     R ATING S YSTEMS
183
184   Let f : M → R be a function that maps a match to a rating, where: M is the set of all possible
185   matches and R is the set of possible ratings. A rating system is defined as a tuple (M, R, f, P, U)
186   where: M is a non-empty set of matches, R ⊆ R is a non-empty set of possible ratings, f :
187   M → R is the rating function, P is a non-empty set of participants, and U : P × M → R is
188
      an update function that adjusts a participant’s rating based on a match outcome. We require the
      conditions that ∀m ∈ M, ∃p1 , p2 ∈ P, where p1 ̸= p2 , representing the participants in the match,
189
      and ∀p ∈ P, ∀m ∈ M, U(p, m) = r′ , where r′ is the updated rating for participant p after match
190
      m.

      5.1     E LO R ATING S YSTEM
193
194   The Elo rating system is a method for calculating the relative skill levels in two-player competitions.
195   Initially proposed by Arpad Elo in 1967 (Elo, 1967) for chess rankings, it has since been adapted
196   for various sports and board games (Silver, 2014; Lezard, 2024). Elo calculates an expected score
197   for a player, based on both their and their opponents current ratings. If player A has a rating of RA
198   and player B has a rating of RB then the expected score for player A is:
199                                                              1
200                                          EA =                            .                                  (4)
                                                     1 + 10(RB −RA )/400
201
202   Due to the scaling factor above, a score difference of 400 points translates into a 10:1 odds in favor
203   of the higher scored player. After each match, the player’s rating is updated as follows:
204                                                                                ′

207   SA is the outcome of the match, which for our tournaments can be 1, 0.5, or 0, for wins, draws, and

      K determines the maximal change in a player’s rating after a single match. We investigate the effect
209
      of the K factor in Section 6.1.4.

228   5.2.2    G LICKO
229
230   The Glicko rating system, developed by Mark Glickman in 1995 (Glickman, 1995), offers an ex-
231   tension to the Elo rating system that addresses some of its limitations. The key innovation is to the
232
      Glicko system is the introduction of rating deviation, which measures the uncertainty in a players
      rating. Glicko-2 (Glickman, 2022) adds another parameter, rating volatility, which measures how
233
      consistent a player’s performance is over time.
234
235
      5.2.3    T RUE S KILL
236
237   TrueSkill (Herbrich et al., 2007; Minka et al., 2018), developed and patented by Microsoft Research
238   for Xbox Live, is a Bayesian skill rating system that generalizes both Elo and Glicko to support
239   multi-player competitions. It models each player’s skill as a Gaussian distribution with a mean µ,
240   the perceived skill, and a standard deviation σ, the uncertainty. TrueSkill employs factor graphs and
241
      message passing algorithms, specifically expectation propagation, to efficiently compute marginal
      skill distributions.

244   6     E MPIRICAL E VALUATION
245
246   We have conducted a series of tests to determine if the tournament-based evaluation method pro-
247   posed above is suitable for practical evaluation of language models. Suitability can be defined based
248   on several factors, but we focus broadly on two sets of criteria, related to what can be called inter-
249
      nal consistency and benchmark consistency. Internal consistency refers to whether a set of ratings,
      considered on its own, gives a coherent snapshot of the performance of a set of models, while bench-
250
      mark consistency refers to the extent to which tournaments (which may be derived from benchmark
251
      datasets using only a few samples) tend to be consistent with the results obtained by simply run-
252   ning benchmarks in the conventional manner. We also examine how the choice of rating system
253   impacts tournament rankings and share some findings related to model quantization and benchmark
254   saturation.
255
256   6.1     I NTERNAL C ONSISTENCY
257
258   Internal consistency is a baseline requirement for any model evaluation method. If possible, an eval-
259   uation should produce a total ordering on the evaluated models, and this ordering should be invariant
260   under perturbations of the experimental design that produced the order. We first examine the extent
261   to which tournament evaluations converge on stable ratings and rankings, and then examine how
262
      much the resulting rankings satisfy transitivity and invariance to experimental setup.
263
      6.1.1    S CORE C ONVERGENCE
264
265   In their most basic form, Elo updates can become unbounded in either the positive or negative
266   direction depending on the number of tournaments run and the relative strength or weakness of the
267   competitors. Like many others, we have found it useful to manually impose bounds on the Elo
268   scores of the models under test. We use a hard floor of 100 for all models and a “soft ceiling”
269   whereby updates for models with a rating above a threshold (chosen as 3000 in our experiments) are
      exponentially decayed in proportion to the difference between the model rating and the threshold.

294   6.1.2   TRANSITIVITY
295
      Previous work has shown that using Elo ratings with human evaluation can result in inaccurate
296
      rankings between models (Boubdir et al., 2023). That work observes that Elo ratings may fail to
297   satisfy transitivity and reliability. To define transitivity, we introduce a relation ⪯: Given models A
298   and B, we write A ⪯ B to indicate that model B is expected to beat model A. Given ratings RA
299   and RB , we expect at a minimum to have
300
301                                         A ⪯ B ⇐⇒ RA < RB .                                           (8)
302
303
      Because ratings are real numbers, for any three models A, B, C, we always have a linear order on
      the ratings. If the ordering is RA < RB < RC , we would expect then that A ⪯ B and B ⪯ C.
304
      Transitivity would then hold if A ⪯ C. However, Boubdir et al. (2023) show that in some instances
305
      we can have RA < RB < RC , but C ⪯ A. A rating system that satisfies transitivity allows us to
306   compare models without having to run model-to-model comparisons for all pairs of models.
307
308   To explore the extent to which transitivity may hold for our tournament evaluation design, we chose
309
      a set of models, ran head-to-head tournaments for all pairs of models in our set, and then examined
      whether the resulting orderings, taken together, satisfy transitivity as described above. For evalu-
310
      ating transitivity, we selected seven widely-used open source models and ran them in head-to-head
311
      tournaments on a subset of the tasks used in version one of the popular Hugging Face LLM leader-
312   board (Fourrier et al., 2024). The tasks used were ARC (Clark et al., 2018), Hellaswag (Zellers et al.,
313   2019), TruthfulQA Lin et al. (2022), Winogrande (Sakaguchi et al., 2019), and GSM8k (Cobbe et al.,
314   2021).
315
      Each head-to-head comparison was a single tournament between two models that consisted of 4
316
      matches of 128 randomly-sampled instances from one of the tasks; this was done for each of the
317
      five tasks, so that each pair of models was evaluated on 2560 instances representing less than 20%
318   of the total number of available instances. Updated Elo ratings were computed after each match.
319   The models and a directed graph showing the order structure that resulted from the tournaments are
320   displayed in Figure 2. That this results in a linear order is clear from the fact that all nodes are
321   connected by at least one arc and the nodes can be topologically sorted in the linear order presented
322   in Figure 2. We find that as long as enough instances are used to reach stable rankings among a
323   set of models that transitivity failures do not appear to be a significant problem with tournament
      evaluation.

      In addition to the requirement of transitivity, the work of Boubdir et al. (2023) discusses what they
339
      call “reliability,” which encompasses two aspects: sensitivity to model ordering and hyperparameter
340   sensitivity. We examine both of these aspects in our tournament setting, finding empirically that
341   automatically-constructed tournaments are reliable in both senses. To test order invariance, we
342   examined GPT-2 (137 million parameters) and Phi-3.5 (3.82 billion parameters). We ran two sets of
343   tournaments in which each model competed against its 8- and 4-bit quantized variants on matches
344   built from Hellaswag questions. We ran round-robin tournaments for all six permutations of model
345   ordering and found in all cases that the resulting rankings were identical, and that the standard
346   deviation in the resulting ratings was less than 2.95 averaged across the quantization levels.

466   6.3         C OMPARING R ATING S YSTEMS
467
468   We compare Elo to three other alternative ranking systems: Bradley-Terry, Glicko-2, and TrueSkill.
469   With all three alternative rating systems we initialize the scores to be 1200 and attempt to scale the
470   results to be in the same range as our Elo implementation. Our implementation of Glicko-2 follows
471   (Glickman, 2022), initializing τ to 0.5, the rating deviation to 350, and volatility to 0.06. Keeping
472
      TrueSkill within similar ranges required a custom implementation, for the hyperparameters we set
      µ = 1200, σ = 400/3, β = 200, τ = 5 and used a draw probability of 0.1. For consistency, we
473
      implemented a hard floor of 100 and a soft ceiling of 3000 in all three alternative rating systems.
474
475   We ran a round-robin tournament for each rating system consisting of 4 rounds of match size 128,
476   sampling instances from both ARC and Hellaswag. As seen in Figure 5, Glicko-2 has the most
477   variance, while Bradley-Terry has the least. Our TrueSkill implementation tracks Elo very closely.
478
      These results warrant further research, which we discuss in Section 7.

480   7         D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
481
482   There are a number of avenues to consider for possible future work. Our work has been largely em-
483   pirical thus far. We suspect that a theoretical analysis would provide insight into when tournament-
484   based evaluation is most useful, and may provide guarantees of asymptotic behavior and bounds on
485   rating differences based on benchmark performance. One of the practical challenges we have en-
      countered in our evaluations is knowing how to set the “tournament hyperparameters” of the number


                                                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      of matches per tournament and the match size. Additional empirical or theoretical guidance in this
487   area would be helpful to develop.
488
489   Relatedly, our initial work here has shown that the choice of rating system can make a difference
490
      in how models are ultimately ranked against each other. This, along with the fact that more sophis-
      ticated rating systems offer additional information suggests that better characterizing rating system
491
      performance merits additional investigation. This will particularly be the case for tournament struc-
492
      tures that differ significantly from the round-robin approach we have used in the present work.
493   Factors such as Glicko’s rating deviation will be particularly useful in tournament structures that in-
494   volve elimination, where not all models will run the same number of times. Working out the details
495   of how this impacts evaluations on a larger scale will be interesting future
496
      Our evaluation software currently tracks the performance of every model on every instance that is
497
      used in an evaluation. For each such instance, the rating of the model at the time of evaluation is
498
      recorded. This means we can compute an average rating of models that do well on an instance,
499   opening the door to more sophisticated sampling strategies. In particular, we are exploring how to
500   create “synthetic tasks” by sampling from a set of otherwise unrelated data sets based on a target
501   range of Elo ratings. This will allow us to construct tasks that are tailored to the capabilities of the
502   models under test, which should be more informative than tasks chosen at random. The feedback
503   from models back to data sets may also open other lines of research that we have not yet fully
504   considered.

509
      Evaluating language models and other transformer-based neural network systems will likely remain
      challenging for the foreseeable future, while we work to understand the full extent of such broadly
510
      capable models. However, the use of head-to-head comparison of models in the form of our proposed
511
      tournament approach offers a method to easily and automatically compare models with as much or
512   as little data and compute as may be available. We hope that our approach and related software
513   can simplify the seemingly insurmountable challenge of deciding which of a set of capable models
514   should actually be used in practical applications.
