009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep neural networks perform well on train data, but are often unable to adapt to
012            data distribution shifts. These are data which are rarely encountered, and thus are
013            under-represented in our training data. Examples of this includes data under ad-
014            verse weather conditions, and data which have been augmented with adversarial
015            perturbations. Estimating the robustness of models to data distribution shifts is im-
016            portant in enabling us to deploy them into safety critical applications with greater
017            assurance. Thus, we desire a measure which can be used to estimate robustness.
018
               We define robustness in 4 ways: Generalization Gap, Test Accuracy (Clean &
               Corrupted), and Attack Success Rate. A measure is said to be representative of
019
               robustness when consistent (non-contradicting) relationships are found across all
020
               4 robustness definitions. Through our empirical studies, we show that it is difficult
021            to measure robustness comprehensively across all definitions of robustness, as the
022            measure often behave inconsistently. While they can capture one aspect of robust-
023            ness, they often fail to do so in another aspect. Thus, we recommend that different
024            measures be used for different robustness definitions. Besides this, we also fur-
025            ther investigate the link between sharpness and robustness. We found that while
026            sharpness has some impact on robustness, this relationship is largely affected by
027            the choice of hyperparameters such as batch size.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) provide state-of-the-art performances across various visual tasks.
033   However, a key problem exists when deploying DNNs in real-world conditions. These DNNs often
034
      encounter out-of-distribution (OOD) data during deployment. This can come in the form of differ-
      ing environmental conditions (e.g., rain, haze, fog). However, perhaps most concerning of all are
035
      adversarial attacks (Szegedy et al., 2013; Goodfellow et al., 2014). Adversarial attacks aim to fool
036
      DNNs into making incorrect decisions. Given the critical use cases of DNNs in our applications,
037   there is a need to both measure and improve the robustness of DNNs to OOD data in the wild. Doing
038   so would provide us with assurances that our DNNs are safe and robust when deploying them in the
039   real-world. In this work, we focus on identifying a measure of robustness for DNNs. This measure
040   will serve as a metric to determine how robust an arbitrary DNN is. In our experiments, we study
041   the existence of such a measure for the Image Classification task. To quantify robustness, we use the
042   Generalization Gap, Clean Test Accuracy, Corruption Test Accuracy, and the Attack Success Rate
043   (ASR). A measure is said to be representative of robustness if it consistently achieves a high corre-
044   lation across all these definitions of robustness. In our experiments, we adopt the approach taken by
045
      Jiang et al. (2019); Dziugaite et al. (2020), which conducted large scale empirical studies to discover
      correlations between their introduced measures and the Generalization Gap. However, in addition
046
      to the measures introduced by Jiang et al. (2019); Dziugaite et al. (2020) we use other measures
047
      such as boundary thickness (Yang et al., 2020) and gradient norm measures (Ross & Doshi-Velez,
048   2018). Furthermore, we extend this study to consider the relationship between the measures and
049   Test Accuracy (Clean & Corrupted images). We also study their relationship with the ASR of vari-
050   ous adversarial attacks. We then follow up this study by analyzing the significance of each measure
051   across the different definitions of robustness. Our experiments show that none of the measures we
052   studied are consistent across all definitions of robustness. Conflicting relationships with the differ-
053   ent definitions of robustness are formed. Additionally, we found that the choice of hyperparameters
      such as batch size significantly influences the robustness of DNNs. This calls the reliability of these

054
      measures into question. These findings lead us to conclude that there is no one measure that can
055   comprehensively reflect the robustness of DNNs. Thus, we recommend that separate measures for
056   the different robustness definitions be used. As we are concerned with OOD data, we focus our
057   recommendations on measures for Corruption Test Accuracy and ASR. When concerned with Cor-
058   ruption Test Accuracy, we found the weight gradient norm and hessian eigenvalue (sharpness) to
059   best reflect it. On the other hand, when concerned with ASR, we found boundary thickness to be
060   most representative of it. Besides identifying a measure for robustness, we also investigated the rela-
061   tionship between sharpness and robustness. While substantial number of works have advocated that
062   flatness of loss landscape leads to improved robustness (Foret et al., 2020; Kwon et al., 2021), other
063
      works (Dinh et al., 2017; Andriushchenko et al., 2023) have proven otherwise. The discrepancies
      in these studies lead us to conduct this investigation. We found that while low sharpness can lead
064
      to improved robustness, this relationship is significantly influenced by the choice of batch size used
065
      when training DNNs.
066
067   We summarize our contributions and findings below:
068
069          • Conducted large scale empirical studies to find a measure for robustness. Different from
070            previous studies, we capture robustness more comprehensively by considering it from 4
071            different angles. In terms of the Generalization Gap, Test Accuracy (Clean & Corrupted),
072            and ASR.
073
074          • Identified hessian eigenvalue and weight gradient norm to be most promising when con-

077
078          • Demonstrated that the link between sharpness and robustness is significantly impacted by
079            the choice of hyperparameters such as batch size.

082   2   R ELATED W ORK
083
084
085   While DNNs yield excellent performances on In-distribution (ID) data, they tend to suffer a per-
086   formance drop when they encounter OOD data. This problem is further exacerbated when they are
087   faced with adversarial examples. Ideally, we want robust DNNs which can both maintain perfor-
088
      mance on OOD data and are robust to adversarial examples. To improve the robustness of DNNs,
      we first require a way to measure robustness. This is obviously not as simple as directly measuring
089
      the Test Accuracy (Clean & Corrupted) or ASR, as this set of OOD data is generally unknown. What
090
      we instead seek is a metric that captures a property of a DNN which is in turn reflective of the DNNs
091   robustness. Most works (Jiang et al., 2019; Dziugaite et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2024) in this field in-
092   vestigate this matter through large scale empirical studies. They train numerous DNNs, perform the
093   relevant measures, before performing correlation analysis with robustness. The measure that yields
094   the highest correlation will be deemed as the most reflective measure of robustness. However, these
095   works are limited in scope. Jiang et al. (2019) only studied the relationship between their selected
096   measures and the Generalization Gap. Andriushchenko et al. (2023) looked into the relationship
097   between both Clean Test Accuracy and the Generalization Gap. However, they neglected the rela-
098   tionship between Corruption Test Accuracy and the ASR. In this work, we argue that it is equally
099
      important to consider all aspects of robustness when finding a measure that reflects robustness.
      Hence, we define the robustness of DNNs in 4 ways. We use the Generalization Gap, Clean Test
100
      Accuracy, Corruption Test Accuracy, and ASR to represent robustness. We then utilize measures to
101
      perform correlation analysis via the Kendall rank correlation coefficient against all these definitions
102   of robustness. A measure that yields high correlation scores against all robustness definitions will be
103   taken as a reflective measure of robustness. Another question we want to tackle is the relationship
104   between sharpness and robustness. While Jiang et al. (2019) found that their sharpness measures
105   were strongly correlated with robustness (Generalization Gap), Andriushchenko et al. (2023) ob-
106   served weak correlation between sharpness and robustness. In fact, it is training parameters like
107   the learning rate that influences whether the relationship with robustness is positively or negatively
      correlated. The contention between these findings leads us to further investigate this matter.

      3.2   M EASURING ROBUSTNESS OF DNN S
137
      We seek a measure that reflects how robust an arbitrary DNN is. This means that given a DNN, by
138   performing this measurement on the DNN, we can use the measurement obtained to estimate the
139   robustness of the DNN. To do so, we first need to identify what we want to measure. Intuitively,
140   these measures should capture the properties of the DNNs. In this subsection, we take a closer look
141   into the measures we used when measuring properties of DNNs. Given the numerous measures we
142   use, we categorised them into 4 categories.
143
      Complexity Measures. Complexity-based measures are typically calculated using the weight ma-
144   trix of trained DNNs. They give us an indication of how complex the learnt function is. Typically,
145   the less complex a solution is, the more generalizable and thus robust the DNN is. In our experi-
146   ments, we utilize several complexity-based measures based on the norm of the weight matrix. This
147   includes the number of parameters, L2 norm, Path-norm (Neyshabur et al., 2015), Spectral norm,
148   and Frobenius norm. For norm-based complexity measures, smaller measures indicate less complex
149   DNNs. Besides norm-based complexity measures, we also use the sparsity of the weight matrix (Liu
150   et al., 2022) as a measure.
151   Decision Boundary Measures. Decision boundary-based measures estimates the distance between
152   class boundaries. A small distance between class boundaries implies that just a small amount of
153   perturbation is required to cross over the class boundaries. This indicates poor robustness. In this
154   work, we consider two measures to estimate decision boundaries. Inverse margin and boundary
155   thickness (Yang et al., 2020).
156   Sharpness Measures. Sharpness has been linked to robustness (generalizability). The intuition
157   behind this is that with smoother loss landscapes (low sharpness), DNNs would be less sensitive to
158   perturbations. This implies improved robustness. Despite several works supporting this claim, other
159   works have instead found that there is little to no correlation between sharpness-based measures
160   and robustness. Given this conflict, we found it fit to conduct our own study. In our experiments,
161   we consider the Hessian eigenvalue, Hessian trace, and Average sharpness (Andriushchenko et al.,
      2023) as estimates for the sharpness of DNNs.

162
      Gradient Measures. In this work, we study the use of input gradient norm (Ross & Doshi-Velez,
163   2018) and weight gradient norm (Zhao et al., 2022) as measures for robustness. These measures
164   have been incorporated as terms to be regularized in the DNN training process. As such, it is not
165   uncommon to associate low gradient norm values with better robustness.

      3.3       C ORRUPTIONS AND A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS
168
169   We are interested to understand the DNNs performance on OOD data in the form of corruptions
170   and adversarial examples. In our experiments, we consider 14 common corruptions (Hendrycks &
171   Dietterich, 2019) to represent both noise and adverse weather conditions. For adversarial attacks, we
172   consider only whitebox attacks. Adversarial examples were crafted using the Fast-Gradient-Sign-
173   Method (FGSM) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) (Madry et al.,
174   2017) algorithms. We chose these algorithms as they provide the best balance between high ASR
175
      and compute efficiency.
176
177   3.4       S HARPNESS O PTIMIZERS
178
179
      Sharpness optimizers introduces an additional term into the learning objective termed as loss sharp-
      ness, which aims to encourage smoother loss landscapes. For this to occur, the optimizers first finds
180
      the loss value in the worst case by perturbing the learnt parameters at the current timestep. There-
181
      after, they minimise this value. This transforms the learning objective into a min-max optimization
182   problem. Through introducing this learning paradigm, they hope to find parameters that lie in flat
183   neighbourhoods (smooth loss landscape) having uniformly low loss. This leads to DNNs which
184   are more robust. By introducing sharpness optimizers into the training pipeline, we hope to obtain
185   DNNs with vastly different loss landscapes and sharpness values. Doing so will help us to perform
186   a more thorough study into the connection between sharpness and robustness.

      4.1       I MAGE C LASSIFIERS
192
      In this work, we look to discover a measure that is reflective of robustness across all 4 definitions
193   for the Image classification task. To perform a comprehensive study to seek convincing measures
194   of robustness, we took an empirical approach. This involves training a large pool of well-trained
195   classifiers with vastly different robustness behaviors. In our experiments, we utilize the Residual
196   Neural Network (ResNet) architecture (He et al., 2016) for our image classifiers. We trained mul-
197   tiple ResNet classifiers under different hyperparameter configurations on the Imagenette1 dataset,
198   training till convergence (cross-entropy 0.01), and repeating each experiment 3 times with different
199   initialization values. Performing this resulted in 486 different hyperparameter configurations and a
200   total of 1458 classifiers. We detail the different hyperparameter configurations in appendix E.1.
201

211
      majority of the corruptions have little impact on the Test Accuracy, with only Fog and Contrast
      causing significant drops in Test Accuracy.

214   of adversarial examples. To generate adversarial examples, we use the FGSM and PGD algo-

      4.3   S HARPNESS O PTIMIZERS
220
221   In our experiments, we also want to further understand the relationship between sharpness and ro-
222   bustness. To do so, we trained ResNet classifiers both with and without sharpness optimizers. We
223   utilize 2 variants of sharpness optimizers: Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) (Foret et al., 2020)
224   and Adaptive Sharpness-Aware Minimization (ASAM) (Kwon et al., 2021). Both these methods op-
225   timize towards obtaining a local minimum in a smooth region. However, while SAM calculates the
226   worst case via a fixed radius, ASAM is scale invariant and calculates this adaptively. This removes
227   the drawback that SAM has to sensitivity of parameter re-scaling. We hope that by introducing
228
      different sharpness optimizers, we can capture more varying properties and behaviors.
229
230   4.4   M EASURES
231
      To discover a representative measure of robustness, we select various measures, implement them,
232
      and measured our 1458 trained classifiers. When performing the measures, there exists hyperpa-
233
      rameters to be set. We detail these in appendix E.2. As we have 3 classifiers corresponding to
234   each hyperparameter configuration (just with different seed values), we took the average of the mea-
235   sured values across the 3 classifiers. This results in our subsequent analysis being conducted on 486
236   classifiers. We hope that by doing so, we can reduce the impact which randomness may have and
237   further increase the validity of our experiments. Following this measurement phase, we perform
238   correlation analysis via the Kendall Rank Correlation Coefficient for each of the measures against
239   the Generalization Gap, Test Accuracy (Clean & Corrupted), and ASR.

      6     C ONCLUSION
476
477
      In this work, we studied various measures and their ability to measure robustness. Through our
      experiments, we found that while certain measure appears as convincing candidates, inconsisten-
478
      cies were a common occurrence. While a measure might be reflective of a particular definition of
479
      robustness, it will imply a conflicting relationship with respect to another definition of robustness.
480   This leads us to conclude that there is no one measure that is representative of robustness across
481   all definitions. Thus, we suggest that rather than seeking a “one-shoe-fits-all” solution, we should
482   instead use different measures to measure the different robustness definitions. As we are particularly
483   interested in robustness from the perspective of corruptions and adversarial examples, we identified
484   the hessian eigenvalue and weight gradient norm to be most representative of the Corruption Test
485   Accuracy. For ASR, we identified boundary thickness to be most representative. In this work, we
      also studied the significance of sharpness in relation to robustness. Through our empirical studies,


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      we found that while there exists a relationship between sharpness and robustness, this relationship is
487   tenuous. While low sharpness implies high Test Accuracy, it also implies high Generalization Gap.
488   The relationship between sharpness and ASR is also weak. Furthermore, we found evidence of this
489   relationship to be largely influenced by batch size. Analysis of other measures such as boundary
490   thickness likewise yielded similar findings. We also found the effectiveness of boundary thickness
491   to be influenced by the choice of hyperparameters such as batch size and learning rate. Neverthe-
492   less, through our analysis, we determined boundary thickness to be the most promising measure.
493   It produced significant correlation scores across all the definitions of robustness. Despite the issues
494   surfaced, we hope that boundary thickness can serve as a starting point in our bid to better understand
495
      robustness.

883
884   C.1     S IGNIFICANCE OF THE D IFFERENT M EASURES
885
      Putting aside the issue of inconsistencies that we surfaced earlier, several viable measures for each
886
      robustness definitions do exist. We are interested to understand which measure contributes most to
887   robustness. However, given that the link between these measures and robustness is still not well
888   understood, and the existence of inconsistencies, this is a difficult task. Thus, we decided to take an
889   unconventional approach to instead offload this task to an auxiliary model.
890
      In particular, we trained a set of 4 different regression models to predict each robustness definition
891
      (Generalization Gap, Clean Test Accuracy, Corruption Test Accuracy, and ASR). These models take
892
      in the viable measures as input and the different robustness definitions as output. In most cases, we
893   deem a measure to be viable if its correlation score is > |0.2|. After training these models, we utilize
894   SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) values to explain how important each feature (measures) is
895   to the model when it predicts robustness. SHAP values indicates to us each features contribution to
896   the predicted output. To aid our analysis of SHAP values, we utilize Beeswarm plots. Beeswarm
897   plots tell us the relative importance of the features and their actual relationships with the predicted
898   outcomes.
899   Through this analytical process, we offload the problem of understanding how significant a measure
900   is with respect to each other to the regression model. While this method has its flaws, we believe
901   that it gives us an indication of which measure holds greater significance.

       D.2   D ECISION B OUNDARY M EASURES
1135
1136   These measures provide some insight into what is the distance or the perturbation that is required to
1137   cross over the class boundaries. Intuitively, the harder it is to cross over the decision boundaries, the
1138   more robust the DNN is.
1139
       Inverse margin. The margin γ between class boundaries for each data point is defined as the
1140   difference between the top-2 logit values. To get a margin value that is representative of the entire
1141   train dataset, we first calculate the margins for all examples in the train dataset. Thereafter, we take
1142   the 10th percentile of the calculated margins as the final margin value. Following this, the inverse
1143   margin is calculated by taking the reciprocal of the square of the final margin value.
1144

                                                                     γ2
1147
1148   Boundary thickness. Introduced by Yang et al. (2020), boundary thickness measures the average
1149   distance (L2 norm) between the set of adversarial examples and the set of natural examples. This
1150   can be seen as a generalized form of margin which only takes the worst case (difference between
1151
       the top-2 logit values). However, when calculating boundary thickness, we randomly sample n
       times from a mixup of the adversarial and natural examples and calculate the distance between the
1152
       selected points. This aims to capture how thick the boundary between the set of adversarial and
1153
       natural examples is.

       D.3   S HARPNESS M EASURES
1156
1157   These aim to estimate the sharpness of the loss landscape. This follows from works that attempt to
1158   link the robustness of DNNs to the sharpness of loss landscapes.
1159
       Hessian measures. These measures are based on the Hessian matrix of a DNNs loss function with
1160
       respect to its parameters. This contains second-order partial derivatives which provides information
1161   on the curvature of the loss landscape. In our experiments, we measure both the maximum eigen-
1162   value of the Hessian and the trace of the Hessian. These 2 measures capture different aspects of the
1163   loss landscape.
1164
1165          • Hessian eigenvalue. The maximum eigenvalue of the Hessian matrix indicates the direc-
1166            tion of the largest curvature (worst-case). The larger in magnitude these values are, the
1167            sharper the loss landscape is. Additionally, while a positive value indicates the loss land-
1168
                scape at the point is concave upwards, a negative value indicates that the loss landscape is
                concave downwards.
1169
1170          • Hessian trace. The hessian trace is the sum of all Hessian eigenvalues. This measures
1171            the overall curvature of the loss landscape. The larger these values are, the sharper the loss
1172
                landscape is.
1173
       Average sharpness. Estimates sharpness by taking the difference in loss values between a DNN
1174   with injected noise and without noise. Noisy DNNs are constructed by injecting noise into the orig-
1175   inal DNNs parameters at random. Large differences in loss values indicates a sharp loss landscape.
1176   We conduct a few variants of this measure. In particular, we explored adding noise with the L2 and
1177   L-infinity constraints. We also varied the variance in which noise was added to the DNNs when
1178   constructing noisy DNNs.
1179

1183   gradients. Works have found that through regularizing input-gradient norms (Ross & Doshi-Velez,
1184
       2018), it leads to a smoothing effect which increases the robustness of DNNs to adversarial exam-
       ples. Thus, by measuring the input-gradient norm of DNNs, we hope to link it to the robustness of
1185
       DNNs.

1212         6. Weight Decay: Varies between weight decays of {0, 0.0001, 0.0005}.
