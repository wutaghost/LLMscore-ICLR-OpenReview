009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
012
               Neural networks have shown superior performance in many different domains.
               However, a precise understanding of what even simple architectures actually are
013
               doing is not yet achieved, hindering the application of such architectures in safety-
014
               critical embedded systems. To improve this understanding, we think of a network
015            as a continuous piecewise linear function. The network decomposes the input space
016            into cells in which the network is an affine function; the resulting cells form a
017            polytopal complex. In this paper we provide an algorithm to derive this complex.
018            Furthermore, we capture the local and global behavior of the network by computing
019            the maxima, minima, number of cells, local span, and curvature of the complex.
020            With the machinery presented in this paper we can extend the validity of a neural
021            network beyond the finite discrete test set to an open neighborhood, covering large
022            parts of the input domain. To show the effectiveness of the proposed method we
023
               run various experiments on the effects of width, depth, and regularisation. We
               further find that under regularization, less cells capture more of the volume, while
024
               the total number of cells stays in the same range. Together, these findings provide
025
               novel insights into the network and its training parameters.

      Furthermore, for each vertex v of the polytopal complex, we can take the star st(v) to capture the
067   behavior of the network in the neighborhood of the vertex. Here the neighborhood of the vertex is the
068   union of the cells of its star. For an arbitrary data point x we take the star of the face containing x.

070   of F around x. Moreover, we can assess the descent and curvature by analysing the (finite) set of
071   faces of the star.
072
      With the machinery of this paper we extend the validity of a neural network beyond a discrete test
073   data point to its neighborhoods, specifically by (1) finding the cell of the complex in which x lies,
074   (2) building the star of the cells vertices, and (3) computing properties of the network on the star.
075   Moreover, we can assess how much volume of the input domain is covered by the cells of the complex
076   which contain test data points.

      Summary of contribution. (1) The core of the paper is an algorithm which outputs a decomposition
079   of the input space of a relu mlp in convex polytopes, which itself constitutes a polytopal complex. (2)
080   We analyze the algorithm and introduce checks for the validity of the decomposition. (3) We leverage
081   the decomposition for various analyses such as analyzing the star of the vertices of the complex, we
082   capture the local behavior of the network, such as the number of extrema, the span, and the curvature.
083   (4) We study the generalization error with our analytical framework. (5) Finally, we study the effects
084   of depth, width, and regularization on the complex.

091   Literature. There exists a well established literature on the theoretical notions of the paper. In this
092
      section we highlight the subset used in this paper.
093   For pl-manifolds we refer to Rourke & Sanderson (1982). The cells of a 1-layered mlp can be
094   understood as a hyperplane arrangement; for more on hyperplane arrangements see Stanley (2006).
095   The theory of polytopes is covered in Ziegler (1995) and Grünbaum et al. (2003). We refer to Polthier
096   (2002) for the definition of discrete surface curvature, for the Griewank function we refer to Griewank
097   (1981), and for the Himmelblau function see Himmelblau (1972).
098
099   Complex and star. A finite family C of polytopes in RD is called a complex if (i) every face of a
100   member of C is itself a member of C , and (ii) the intersection of any two members of C is a face
101   of each of them. Let C be a complex and C ∈ C an element of that complex. The star of C in
102   C , denoted by st(C, C ), is the smallest sub-complex of C containing all the members of C which
103   contain C.

      3     C OMPUTING THE DECOMPOSITION

      3.1   A LGORITHM
171
172   Task. Given a multilayer perceptron F : RD → RS defined by its weight matrices, bias vectors,
173   and activation functions, the task is to decompose a bounded box of the input space into a finite set of
174   convex polytopes such that the network F is linear on each polytope. Furthermore, we want to know
175   the neighboring relations of the polytopes. In other words, we want to compute a (polytopal) complex
176   C induced by the network on B ⊂ RD .
177
178   Steps. The proposed algorithm operates iteratively in three steps. Given a set of polytopes it
179   partitions each polytope into sub-polytopes with the hyperplanes given by the network. For each
180
      polytope: (α) It derives a partition into sub-polytopes by a set of hyperplanes. (β) It identifies
      vertices of attached polytopes, (γ) It computes the (sub)-faces of the set of polytopes.
181
182
      (α0 ) - Initial step.   We define the decomposition of the axis-parallel bounding box {b0 , ..., b2D −1 }:
183
184                              vertices = {b0 , ..., b2D −1 }                                                      (6)
185                                 links = {{b0 , b1 }, {b0 , b3 }, ..., {b2D −2 , b2D −1 }}                        (7)
186                              D -faces = {{b0 , b1 , ..., b2D −1 }}                                               (8)
187
      The intermediate steps of the algorithm only use the vertices, the links, and the D-faces.
188
189
      (α1 ) - Set of cutting hyperplanes of cell. At each cell Ck the i-th layer of the network before the
190   activation is an affine function. This affine function is determined by the activation pattern of the cell
191   C ∈ Ci−1 . To compute it, we take the midpoint x of the cell, derive the activation pattern of the cell,
192   set the corresponding rows and columns of the weights and biases to zero, and output the product
193   Hi,k = Âi Âi−1 · · · Â0 for the linear part and bi,k = Âi (Âi−1 (...(Â1 b̂0 + b̂1 ) + b̂2 )...) + b̂i for the
194   bias term of the network at layer i and cell C.
195
196   (α2 ) - Partition cell by hyperplanes. In step (α2 ) we iteratively go through the hyperplanes given
197   by (Hi,k , bi,k ). We begin with cell Ck,i , cut it if necessary, and continue with the next hyperplane
198   and the current decomposition of Ck,i . In this way we cut the initial cell step by step into more and
199   more pieces.
200
201   (α3 ) - Partition single cell. A cell C is given by the convex hull of its vertices {v0 , ..., vk }. The
202   hyperplane assigns a sign to the set of vertices which we collect in I+ , I− and I0 . We further compute
203
      the cuts J of the hyperplane and the cell by cutting the 1-faces of the cell. The convex hull of J ∪ I0
      is the common face of the cuts C+ and C− of the cell C = C+ ∪ C− . Thus, C+ = conv I+ ∪ I0 ∪ J
204
      and C− = conv I− ∪ I0 ∪ J.
205
206
      (β)-Identifying vertices. This steps identifies vertices of polytopes which share a common face.
207
208   (γ) -Intersection semilattice. Finally, we obtain the inner k-faces by taking the pairwise intersec-
209   tion of all cells and determining the affine dimension of the space spanned by the common vertices.
210   To obtain the faces on the boundary, we intersect the cell with the 0-faces, 1-faces, 2-faces, ...,
211   (D − 1)-faces of the bounding box.

215   Uniqueness and independence of cutting order. We argue that it does not matter in which order
      we cut the cells by the neurons of a layer. In other words we argue that the derived polytopal complex

229   is unique. This can be shown by induction. For a zero-layered network the algorithm outputs the
230   polytopal complex of the bounding box which is unique. At each layer the algorithm partitions
231   the cells by its corresponding set of hyperplanes. Reduced to each cell this partition is unique, this
232   follows as an hyperplane arrangement uniquely partitions the input space - each partition is uniquely
233   identified by its sign vector. It remains to show that to the partitions of two pasted adjacent cells
234
      match. This follows as the cutting (bended) hyperplanes, defined by a sign change in one neuron is
      continuous: Let us suppose cells A,B are adjacent in layer k with shared (D − 1)-face C. If cell
235
      A and its (D − 1)-face C is cut by hyperplane P (as defined by the network in cell A), then there
236
      is a hyperplane P ′ defined by the network in cell B which equals P when restricted to the shared
237   (D − 1)-face C. To go from P to P ′ one changes the sign vector of P at the neuron of the shared
238   (D − 1)-face.
239
240
      Validity of decomposition. We can assess the validity of the decomposition by (1) checking that
241   the volume of the cells equals the volume of the bounding box, (2) we can further check if any derived
242   vertex lies outside of the input cube, (3) checking if the sum of even faces equals the sum of odd
243   faces plus one; in other words we compute the Euler characteristic of the complex, and (4) we can
244   compare the number of 0-faces derived by computing the intersection lattice in the final step of the
245   algorithm with the number of vertices derived during the decomposition.
246
247   Degenerate polytopes. Degenerate polytopes can arise during the cutting and in the final step while
248   computing the intersection lattice. For the first case: As we cut the cells sequentially, we detect if
249   a hyperplane leads to a degenerate polytope, as in this case all vertices of such a polytope lie in a
250   closed half-space. For the second case: While intersecting two polytopes of the complex we derive
251   the affine dimension of the intersection. These argument work in theory, in practice (as laid out in the
252
      appendix) almost equal hyperplanes may lead to degenerate polytopes. This is similar to meshing
      errors and can not be avoided in floating point or fixed-point arithmetic.
253
254
255   Remark on used data structure. Internally, we used the coordinates of the vertices v ∈ RD . For
256
      each vertex we stored its supporting hyperplanes H1 , ..., Hk . We further stored 0-faces, 1-faces, and
      D-faces, as list of indices, pointing to the vertices. In this way we can compute the intersection of
257
      two faces, by the set theoretic intersection of indices to determine the vertices of the intersection,
258
      followed by a computation of the rank of the intersection. Furthermore, this allows us to derive
259   the intersection of a hyperplane and 1-face between vertex v and w by (1) computing the spanning
260   vectors of its affine space - again we can achieve this by taking the intersection of indices of the
261   supporting hyperplanes of both vertices. (2) adding the intersecting hyperplane to the supporting
262   hyperplanes and computing its point of intersection.

295   Remark. We added the bounding box to the algorithm mainly to avoid numerical errors caused by
296   floating points. If we consider networks with almost similar weights, this happens if we regularize
297   the network heavily, then the positions of the intersecting vertices will be quite large. Combining this
298   with vertices close to the origin is asking for numerical troubles. We can exclude this by using the
299   bounding box. In practise this is no issue as (physical) signals of embedded systems are bounded
300
      anyway.

302   3.4   T IMING
303
      A note on complexity. The present algorithm and that of Berzins (2023) have some similarities.
304
      The main difference is that Berzins (2023) uses the 1-skeleton of the decomposition whereby only
305
      computing the vertices of the complex. The algorithms share enough similarity that their run time
306   analysis carries over to our algorithm, giving a complexity of O(|vertices|).
307
308   Width, depth, and dimension. In order to assess the run time of the algorithm empirically we
309   trained several architectures of different width and depth on the Himmelblau data. In particular, for
310   the width experiments we considered networks of type 2-10-1,...,2-100-1. For the depth experiments
311   we considered networks of type 2-10-1,...,2-10-...-10-1. Furthermore, to assess dependence on the
312   input dimension we sampled data from four concentric spheres of dimension D with radii 1,2,3, and
313   4. The task of the network of type D-5-5-4 was to classify the correct sphere. We repeated each
314   experiment ten times and report the median. Figure 5 shows the resulting curves. To analyze this
315   further we varied depth and width at the same time. For the plot of Figure 6. we trained a network
316
      of type 2-width-...-width-1 on the himmelblau function, and decomposed it. We can see that deeper
      models tend to take longer than wider models.

406
      Motivation. It is desirable to control the pl-structure of the trained network. Yet at the same time
      this will be quite challenging. At present we can only do this indirectly. The most direct way is
407
      by choosing depth and width of the network. A more indirect way is through the training data.
408
      Finally, we can also regularize the network. In this section we study the effects of depth, width, and
409   regularization.
410
411
      Controlling depth and width. In this set of experiments we investigated the effect of depth and
412   width on the number of cells of the complex, as well as the final MSE. We trained several networks
413   of type 2-width-...-width-1 on the Himmelblau data. Figure 7 reports the results. We see that the
414   networks should at least have two preferable three layers in order to achieve a good fit as measured
415   by the MSE. Further we infer that increasing width and depth at the same time yields more complex
416   networks, but with no effect on the MSE.
417
418   Controlling regularization factor. Regularization offers another way to control the structure of
419   the network, yet it remains unclear what it controls. For the experiment we added l2 -regularization to
420   the loss function. We considered 24 different regularization factors. For each regularization factor we
421   trained one hundred times a MLP of type 2-50-50-1 on Himmelblau data. In Figure 8 we observe
422   that the number of faces remains roughly constant, until training collapses. In the second plot of the
423
      figure, we notice that the span of the stars decreases, in other words as we regularize the stars become
      more flat. Finally, the last two plots show the spectral bound of the network defined on the bounding
424
      box, and the product of the spectral norm of the matrices. We see the regularization decreases the
425
      spectral norm, but we also note the large gap between the true spectral norm and its upper bound.

430   Counting of linear regions and activation patterns. This line of research connects the expres-
431   siveness of the network and its architecture, motivated by the questions such as Is deeper better
      than wider? Deriving the number of cells for neural network dates back to bounding the number of

434   et al. (2024), Wang (2022), Serra et al. (2018). Besides linear regions also activations patterns have
435   been counted Hanin & Rolnick (2019a).
436
437   Expressiveness of neural networks. It is impossible to compute the polytopal complex for net-
438   works of large input dimensions. But we can sneak into these networks by studying planes or paths in
439   the input space. This is done for example in Raghu et al. (2017),Novak et al. (2018), Humayun et al.
440   (2024b). Another approach is to consider a subset of the linear regions, see Gamba et al. (2022),
441
442   Related algorithms. The closest algorithms to the present paper Balestriero & LeCun (2023)
443   provides an algorithm which returns the exact number of cells and their activation pattern, but they
444   do not return the vertices and their structure. In a sense this algorithm operates entirely on the
445   h-representation of the polytopes.
446   Berzins (2023), provides an algorithm for the enumeration problem of neural networks. Their core
447   idea is a sequential cutting of the 1-skeleton of the complex per layer. There is some similarity to
448   step (α3 ) of our algorithm but our algorithm derives the entire structure of the polytopal complex not
449   just the 1-skeleton. This cell structure is necessary to derive curvature and volume.
450   Finally, Humayun et al. (2024a), Humayun et al. (2023) provides an algorithm for two-dimensional
451   input based on planar geometry. A deeper discussion of the related algorithms can be found in
452   Appendix C.1.
453
454   Polytopal complex. Polytopes (and convex analysis) have been used to analyse and improve
455   properties of networks such as robustness Croce et al. (2019), bounds on architecture Arora et al.
456   (2016). In another direction Liu et al. (2023) used the polytopal complex to study topological signals
457   of manifolds in the input space from samples. More recently, tropical geometry has provides a related
458   path to study neural networks Brandenburg et al. (2024).
459
460   Vizualisation and explainability. Finally, we briefly mention other approaches to visualize and
461   explain neural network, Zeiler & Fergus (2014) focusses on feature visualization. Olah et al. (2018)
462
      address the need to understand how neural networks predict on given data points, Ergen & Pilanci
      (2020) studied two-layer ReLU Networks based on formulating a convex optimization problem with
463
      infinitely many constraints. Lipton (2018) points out, important desiderata for interpretability are
464
      trust and informativeness. There is also a line of work Li et al. (2018) that analyses the loss landscape
465   of neural nets to better understand training dynamics.

468   6    C ONCLUSION
469
470   Summary. We have provided an algorithm to compute the polytopal complex of a neural network.
471   This allowed to compute several statistics of a trained network. We analysed an example network
472
      qualitatively and quantitatively, by looking at its level sets, by computing the percentage of the
      volume covered by data, among others. We have seen that width and depth control the number of
473
      cells of the decomposition on average. Regularization, in contrast, did not change the number of cells
474
      much, but it controlled the span of the stars and the spectral radius of the network.
475
476
      Limitations. This work is limited to neural networks with a finite computational budget, a small
477   number of inputs, bounded input domain, and a continuous piece-wise linear activation function.
478   However, this is a typical setting in embedded systems and virtual sensors.
479
480   Future directions. We did not look at discrete curvature beyond two dimension. Another extension
481   is the approximation of (bounded) continuous activation functions such as the tanh function by ReLU
482   activations. It is practically impossible to analyze the polyhedral complex of a network with input
483   dimension greater than ten. But similar to Humayun et al. (2024a) it would be interesting to intersect
484   the input space with a low dimensional subspace and study the (reduced) polyhedral complex. Finding
485   a more direct way of controlling the pl-structure different or finding a novel loss term is also an
      exciting and important research direction.

809   decomposition of the input space C˜, and select all k-faces of C˜ for which F equals to c at all vertices
      of the k-faces.

       C.1    A LTERNATIVE ALGORITHMS
1389
1390
       Related algorithms. For small networks Balestriero & LeCun (2023) provides an algorithm
1391
       which returns the exact number of cells and their activation pattern. It has some similarities to
1392   our algorithm but differs in scope. More crucially, it keeps track of the current decomposition by
1393   storing the activation patterns of non-void cells. In a sense this algorithm operates entirely on the
1394   h-representation of the polytopes. In contrast, we keep track of the cells by storing its vertices. In fact
1395   we use both v- and h-representation of the polytopes. The vertices are necessary to derive the stars,
1396   which in turn capture the local behavior of the network.
1397
       Berzins (2023) provides an algorithm for the enumeration problem of neural networks. Their core
1398   idea is a sequential cutting of the 1-skeleton of the complex per layer. So at a layer, all potential
1399   hyperplanes are generated by going through all possible activations patterns of the neurons. If the
1400   hyperplane cuts an edge, the sign pattern of the vertices of the edge differ and we get two edges and a
1401   novel vertex. In addition one has to consider 2-faces as the intersection with the hyperplane gives a
1402   novel edges as well. There is some similarity to step (α3 ) of our algorithm but our algorithm derives
1403   the entire structure of the polytopal complex not just the 1-skeleton. This cell structure is necessary
       to derive curvature and volume.


                                                           26
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1404
       Another work presented in Humayun et al. (2024a), Humayun et al. (2023) provides an impressive
1405   algorithm to compute the polyhedral complex of a two dimensional network and its decision bound-
1406   aries. But the algorithm presented in Humayun et al. (2023) works only for two dimensional inputs -
1407   furthermore it seems challenging to extend the core idea of the algorithm to higher dimensions. Our
1408   algorithm does not have a dimensional restriction. We further complement the subsequent work on
1409   grokking of Humayun et al. (2024b) by analysing the effect of regularisation on the distribution of
1410   cells.

1412   C.2   P ITFALLS
1413
1414   Numerical issues. Partitioning input spaces by neural networks may lead to numerical issues. The
1415
       main culprit are imprecisions caused by floating numbers. We identified three different causes. Our
       algorithm partitions the polytopes of layer k separately. This implies that we compute vertex as the
1416
       intersection of a set hyperplanes several times. Each time these hyperplanes are not the same. While
1417
       attaching polytopes in step (β) of the algorithm we have to decide if two of such vertices are in fact
1418   the same. Another error can be introduced while computing the affine dimension of a set of vertices -
1419   we do this based on the svd. Finally while cutting a polytope by a hyperplane, we determine if the
1420   vertices of the polytope lie in that hyperplane. Further we intersect the hyperplane with the links of
1421   the polytope. Again we have to decide if the point of intersection lies in the polytope. To do this we
1422   compute the sign of the vertex with respect to the hyperplane - this can be numerically challenging if
1423   we are close to zero.
1424   Most numerical issues arise for almost similar weights. This causes almost identical features which
1425   give rise to arbitrary close vertices while intersecting another feature.
1426
1427
       We observe this during heavy regularization. Here the network tend to cluster the weights leading to
       sets of almost equal weights. But also while constructing examples one tends to overdetermined the
1428
       vertices leading to challenging problems.
1429
1430   For our implementation we increased the number of precision to 30 while doing internal computations
1431   using the mpmath library. Furthermore, computing the Euler characteristics and volume consider-
1432   ations give further evidence of the validity of a decomposition. Let us remark that also mention
1433
       numerical difficulties and resolve this by going to double precision on their GPU implementation.
1434
1435
       Computational resources total number of experiments. We trained and decomposed many
       networks during the preparation of this paper. Although the networks are small their shear number
1436
       makes it hard to repeat this in reasonable time. A small network (2-5-5-1) is decomposed in a few
1437
       seconds at most, the larger ones (2-100-100-1) can take up some time maybe 30minutes. Particularly
1438   time consuming is the computation of the levels sets. We did all of this on an internal cluster on an
1439   CPU, implemented in pure python. We estimate the number of experiments of the main paper to be
1440   5000. The additional experiments in the appendix about another 10000. A single experiment can be
1441   done easily on a personal pc.
