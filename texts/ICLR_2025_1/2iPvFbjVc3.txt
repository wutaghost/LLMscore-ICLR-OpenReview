011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Given the accelerating progress of vision and language modeling, accurate eval-
014            uation of machine-generated image captions remains critical. In order to eval-
015            uate captions more closely to human preferences, metrics need to discriminate
016
               between captions of varying quality and content. However, conventional met-
               rics fall short of comparing beyond superficial matches of words or embedding
017
               similarities; thus, they still need improvement. This paper presents VisCE2 , a vi-
018
               sion language model-based caption evaluation method. Our method focuses on
019            visual context, which refers to the detailed content of images, including objects,
020            attributes, and relationships. By extracting and organizing them into a structured
021            format, we replace the human-written references with visual contexts and help
022            VLMs better understand the image, enhancing evaluation performance. Through
023            meta-evaluation on multiple datasets, we validated that VisCE2 outperforms the
024            conventional pre-trained metrics in capturing caption quality and demonstrates
025            superior consistency with human judgment.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The evaluation of the machine-generated image caption is a core research topic to illustrate mod-
031   els’ ability to describe their visual observation in textual forms and shape the branch of vision and
032   language modeling studies into meaningful and grounded directions. In the early stage of neural
033
      image captioning research, such as neural image caption generator (Vinyals et al., 2015), attention-
      based (Xu et al., 2015), and sentinel and spatial attention (Lu et al., 2017), models had enabled
034
      more and more detailed and accurate descriptions. Hence, they had achieved better and better per-
035
      formance on reference-based automatic evaluation metrics such as BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002)
036   and CIDEr (Vedantam et al., 2015). SPICE (Anderson et al., 2016) was also proposed to assess
037   better correspondences of reference and generated captions in grammatical aspects. More recently,
038   BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2020) and CLIPScore (Hessel et al., 2021) have been introduced to mea-
039   sure the similarity between the embeddings of generated captions and references.
040
      In very recent advances in vision and language models (VLMs), however, model generations become
041
      so detailed that they often exceed the capability of the automatic evaluation metrics and even the
042   entire coverage of annotated references. Both InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023) and LLaVA (Liu et al.,
043   2023b) follow textual instructions and generate tailored descriptions that are not even similar to
044   references but are of high quality. Considering the great advancements of these recent models, we
045   go back to the basics of image captioning to tailor new evaluation metrics: first capturing the contents
046   of the images and then rearranging them to describe and composing a phrase. In assessing image
047   captioning abilities, they correspond to different aspects: coverage of image contents, accuracy in
048   describing them, and compositional sophistication. In this line of evaluation metrics, InfoMetIC (Hu
049   et al., 2023) relies on matching image regions and words in the captions. While it is quite sensitive
050
      to the alignments of objects in images and captions, it becomes less sensitive to supportive facts
      such as attributes or interactions of objects. Unfortunately, they are also learning-based metrics and
051
      depend on fine-tuning with multiple captioning datasets.
052
053   This paper concentrates on reference-free image caption evaluation and proposes a new Vision Lan-
      guage Model-based Caption Evaluation Method leveraging Visual Context Extraction (VisCE2 ). In

      Crossmodal methods. Covering the rich visual information in an image with only pre-defined
130
      reference captions is difficult. To alleviate this loss of information, evaluation methods based on
131   vision and language models (VLMs), which leverage features of the images, have been proposed.
132   TIGEr (Jiang et al., 2019) uses a pre-trained SCAN model (Lee et al., 2018), fine-tuned on the
133   COCO dataset (Chen et al., 2015), and calculates how much the candidate caption is grounded in
134   the image. ViLBERTScore (Lee et al., 2020) extracts features of the images and calculates the score
135   as with BERTScore with a pre-trained ViLBERT model (Lu et al., 2019). FAIEr (Wang et al., 2021)
136   connects images and texts via scene graphs and computes scores according to their overlap.
137   Image-only (reference-free) methods. To simultaneously improve evaluation performance and re-
138   duce the cost of annotation for references, several methods have been proposed for image caption
139   evaluation. CLIP-S (Hessel et al., 2021) attaches the score by simply calculating the modified cosine
140   similarity between embeddings of an image and that of a candidate caption using CLIP (Radford
141   et al., 2021). PAC-S (Sarto et al., 2023) refined the pre-training method of CLIP with data aug-
142   mentation using an image captioner (Li et al., 2022) and image generator (Rombach et al., 2022),
143   resulting in improved evaluation performance. Other methods employ quality estimators for eval-
144   uation. UMIC (Lee et al., 2021) fine-tunes UNITER (Chen et al., 2020) via contrastive loss for
145
      gold and automatically perturbated caption pair. InfoMetIC (Hu et al., 2023) fuses image and lan-
      guage modalities by stacking CLIP and Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), fine-tuned with large
146
      image-caption datasets (Young et al., 2014; Aditya et al., 2015).
147
148   Unlike these methods, our method utilizes visual context to detail the structure of the image content
149   for caption evaluation. In our evaluation protocol, the VLM extracts the visual context to encapsulate
150   the comprehensive image content and feeds it to the VLM itself. By doing so, it is possible to refer
151
      to information from both the vision and language sides, and hence, it is expected to improve the
      accuracy of quality estimation.

160   refined pre-trained methods by enhancing the quality of image and text data by the image captioner.
161   OFA (Wang et al., 2022) integrated various V&L tasks by modifying the input-output architecture.
      Several other studies also presented remarkable performance, which are still being upgraded (Bai

170   3     P ROPOSED M ETHOD : V IS CE2
171
172   We introduce a reference-free automatic caption evaluation method, VisCE2 . Figure 2 shows the
173   overview of VisCE2 . Our method takes an image and a caption as inputs and predicts a rating
174   score for how accurately the caption describes the image. This assessment is conducted via two key
175   components: visual context extraction and VLM-based caption evaluation.
176   Visual Context Extraction. VisCE2 initially extracts a detailed visual context from an input image
177   using VLM, named VLMExt . We define visual context as the content of an image classified into
178   objects, object attributes (including color, shape, and size), and relationships between objects, fol-
179   lowing the similar notion of scene graphs (Xu et al., 2017). To extract relevant image details, we
180   instruct the VLMExt to articulate the visual context in a bullet list format by category rather than
181   employing the structured graphical representation due to its complexity and the difficulties associ-
182
      ated with textual representation. We believe that providing the successive VLMEval module with
      detailed visual context in a structured format helps bridge the gap between the reference image and
183
      the candidate text. This facilitates the model’s evaluation both consistently and comprehensively.
184
185   Vision-LM Caption Evaluation. In the next step of VisCE2 , VLMEval evaluates the candidate
186   caption based on the extracted visual context and an input image. As with many other LLM-based
187   tasks, VisCE2 treats caption evaluation as a multimodal text completion task. The VLMEval is given
188
      a prompt combining a reference image, visual context, and a candidate caption as input and then
      generates an output sentence that encapsulates the overall quality scores ranging from 0 to 100.
189
      In the preliminary experiments, we found that some VLMs occasionally disregard instructions to
190
      generate only evaluation scores, instead producing sentences that include these scores (e.g., “The
191   score is X out of 100”.) In the postprocessing phase, we eliminated these canonical phrases and
192   designated the first integer value in the output sentences as the evaluation score. In contrast to
193   previous embedding-based methods, such as CLIP-S (Hessel et al., 2021), which cannot determine
194   the quality of scores without multiple examples, our approach can provide absolute scores close to
195   human intuition, allowing for determining the caption’s quality by just looking at a single caption.

476   6   C ONCLUSION
477
478
      We have proposed VisCE2 , a prompting method for automatic VLM-based evaluation of image cap-
479
      tions. VisCE2 deviates from the traditional method of evaluating similarity using only reference text
480   and images, weaving visual context into the evaluation framework with a compositional form. This
481   technique allows the VLMs to understand the detailed visual dependencies of images better and val-
482   idate them based on more exhaustive content than the reference captions provide. Meta-evaluation
483   experiments revealed that scores output by VisCE2 have excellent consistency with human judg-
484   ments, especially in caption accuracy, which outperforms existing evaluation metrics. Future work
485   includes providing VLM-based automatic evaluation along various perspectives, which would pro-
      vide even more fine-grained information for humans.

705
706   THumB 1.0 (Kasai et al., 2022). consists of 500 images sourced from MSCOCO, each assigned one
707   human-written caption and four automatic-generated captions. The evaluation of candidate captions
708   involves manual assessment based on three criteria: precision (how precisely the caption describes
