009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               The broad capabilities of Language Models (LMs) can be limited by their sensitiv-
012
               ity to distractor tasks: LMs can infer secondary tasks from the prompt in addition
013            to the intended one, leading to unwanted outputs. For example, prompt injection
014            attacks can cause models to deviate from explicit directives. In some ‘inverse
015            scaling’ cases, this unwanted behaviour actually worsens as models scale up to at
016            least 540B parameters. We present a theoretical framework that interprets LMs as
017            a product of experts that combine multiple data generation processes. Based on
018            this framework, we introduce prior-aware decoding (PAD) – a simple contrastive
019            inference method to reduce the influence of distractor tasks. We apply PAD to
020            eleven models, across four datasets, and find improvements in 41 out of 44 task-
021
               model combinations, with a median increase in task completion proportion of
               40%. The results suggest a promising direction for further development towards
022
               more reliable language models.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Language models (LMs) have come to occupy a central role in a wide range of tasks, from data
029
      processing to the creation of instruction-following assistants. These models seem to both increase
030   in performance and also develop new capabilities as they scale up in parameters, especially when
031   given examples of a task (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2022). They see
032   both widespread public use, and increasing integration into sensitive tasks where their versatile
033   capabilities are key (Javaid et al., 2022). However, this increasing reliance on LMs raises concerns
034   about their reliability and potential vulnerabilities. Their extremely general ability to recognise
035   patterns may come with a cost: language models are susceptible to distractor tasks, unintended
036   secondary tasks which a model can infer from the prompt in addition to the intended one, leading to
037   unwanted outputs (Wei et al., 2023).
038   Distractor tasks include both semantically complex cases like prompt injection, in which the distrac-
039   tor task of following the most recently injected instruction overrides the intended task of following
040   the initial instructions, and much more semantically simple cases where a model defaults to out-
041   putting a common pattern regardless of prior context (Greshake et al., 2023; McKenzie et al., 2023).
042   Even cutting-edge models struggle with trivial tasks in the presence of very common patterns. For
043   example, as of September 2024 GPT-4o, when prompted with ”Write all the numbers up
044   to 30 except multiples of 13, and nothing else”, will correctly exclude 13 but
045   fail to exclude 26. Other examples of such distractor tasks include repeating common misconcep-
046   tions instead of true answers (Lin et al., 2022) and failing to write mathematical proofs when the
047   correct proof is very similar to an incorrect but much more common proof (Collins et al., 2024).
048   Furthermore, for some tasks of this type, larger models actually perform worse: similar models
049   with more parameters can be more likely to repeat common misconceptions and succumb to prompt
050   injection attacks (McKenzie et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2022). This phenomenon, known as ‘inverse
051   scaling’ (Lin et al., 2022), reveals a pressing need to better understand how these problems arise and
052   how they can be fixed. Unlike other problems with LMs which are usually resolved by scaling up
053   the model and training data size, the existing problems might become even more pronounced, and
      other similar issues might emerge.

      1.1    P RIOR -AWARE D ECODING
055
056   To better understand and address the problem of distractor tasks, we characterise language models
057   as a product of experts (Hinton, 1999) which combine the predictions of several component models,
058   each component model predicting the next token assuming a particular task or context. This frame-
059   work, presented in detail in Section 4, allows us to decompose the model’s behavior into distinct
060   ”experts” that may be responsible for different aspects of the output, which in turn lets us interpret
061   the problem of distractor tasks as models overweighing a particular expert.
062   Using this framework, we present a technique to reduce the influence of distractor tasks in cases
063   where the prompt can be divided into one part containing the intended task and one part containing
064   the distractor task and other important information. In this case, we can contrast the outputs the
065   model gives for the full prompt with the outputs it gives for just the part which yields the distractor
066   task, extrapolating to infer how it would behave with less influence from the distractor task. This
067   is a form of contrastive decoding (Li et al., 2023). Our approach differs from previous methods by
068   specifically targeting the influence of distractor tasks and leveraging the product of experts model
069   More specifically, our method generates the original logits LO from the original prompt, as well
070   as the weakened logits LW from the portion of the prompt which does not contain the original
071   task. Even though LO and LW would both, by default, favour undesired outputs consistent with the
072   distractor task, LO gives a higher probability to outputs consistent with the intended task. So, by
073   sampling instead from a linear combination of the logits, LO + α(LO − LW ) we can selectively
074   favour outputs which are more consistent with the original task. (α here is a tunable hyperparameter.)

      We test the technique on eleven different models including GPT-2, Llama-2, and Mistral, using four
109   tasks to evaluate susceptibility to distractor tasks. Three of these tasks are from the Inverse Scaling
110   Dataset (McKenzie et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023) which exhibit strong priors, including a prompt
111   injection task, and one task is a custom task which challenges models to produce modifications of
112   common sequences.
113
      Our primary baseline is the unmodified completion according to the same model. Across the 44
114
      model-task combinations we find that our technique outperforms the baseline on 41 combinations,
115   with a median absolute increase in proportion of tasks completed of 40%. See Figure 2 and Sec-
116   tion 6 for the results. These improvements demonstrate the potential of our method to significantly
117   enhance the reliability and performance of language models across a variety of model architectures
118   in scenarios featuring distractor tasks.
119
      The technique requires two choices: selection of the weakened prompt, and the coefficient α. We
120
      offer consistent approaches to generating the weakened prompts for each of our datasets and also
121   propose a general method for weakened prompt construction. We explore a range of values for
122   α. Crucially, this method does not require retraining: it operates at inference time by making two
123   queries instead of one and computing a linear combination of the resulting logits.
124
      The improvements in accuracy due to our technique suggest that viewing LMs as product of experts
125
      models can be a useful framework for understanding and improving the behaviour of these mod-
126
      els. Our research provides new approaches for utilizing capabilities which are dormant in models
127   and which have been hard to elicit with prompting alone. This includes capabilities of immediate
128   practical importance, such as resistance to prompt injection attacks, as well as understanding how to
129   correct for biases learned in the training data.

      Inverse Scaling. For most tasks, performance scales together with the model scale (model size,
      dataset size, and training compute). However, certain tasks exhibit inverse scaling, where increasing
167
      the scale of the model leads to worse performance. The term was introduced by Lin et al. (2022)
168
      to characterise the tendency of LMs to repeat common misconceptions. McKenzie et al. (2023)
169   further developed the notion and provided the Inverse Scaling dataset as well as several informal
170   categories. Wei et al. (2023) adds to the discussion, showing that of the original eleven tasks in the
171   Inverse Scaling dataset, only four persist in demonstrating inverse scaling on models of up to 540B
172   parameters. We apply our method to three of those four tasks. Our work is the first to offer a formal
173   account of the mechanisms that have so far only been informally hypothesised in Wei et al. (2023),
174   and we use this to develop a novel technique that improves performance.
175   Problems Related to Strong Priors. While in this paper we investigate the problem of strong priors
176   in the domain of language modelling (discussed primarily in Section 3), a somewhat similar problem
177   also occurs in diffusion models for image generation. Diffusion models have been observed to some-
178   times struggle to generate images that have a precise correspondence to the prompt. This problem is
179   especially pronounced for complex prompts that include rare words, or describe implausible scenes,
180   spatial relations, or compositionality. One technique for mitigating this problem in diffusion models
181   is classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2021). It samples the diffusion model using a linear
182   combination of conditioned and unconditioned score estimates (1 + w) · e(zt , c) − w · e(zt ), where
183
      zt is the image at the denoising step t, c is the conditioning information such as the prompt, e is the
      model and w is the guidance weight. High guidance weight improves text-image alignment (Saharia
184
      et al., 2022), although high values often lead to over-saturated images or have a generally unnatural
185
      appearance. The technique that we present in this paper uses a somewhat similar combination of
186   differently conditioned model outputs to mitigate the problem of strong priors in LMs.
187
188   Surface Form Competition and DC-PMI. Holtzman et al. (2021) coin ‘surface form competition’
189
      as a phenomenon where different tokens representing the same concept compete for probability. For
      instance, the terms “computer” and “PC” represent the same concept but are different surface forms.
190
      Since the probability mass is finite, this competition lowers the probability of the correct answer.
191
      Domain Conditional Pointwise Mutual Information (DC-PMI) addresses this issue by reweighing
192   each option according to its likelihood within the context of a specific task, calculated by sampling
193   from a subset of the prompt. This approach yields gains in zero-shot performance for a variety of
194   GPT-2 and GPT-3 models on a selection of multiple-choice datasets. Our work applies a similar
195   kind of technique - reweighing based on likelihood - but we do so to address a different set of tasks,
196   including those which exhibit inverse scaling.
197
      Contrastive Inference Methods. Contrastive Inference methods steer language models towards
198   desired outputs by taking multiple samples across different settings and favouring outputs which are
199   comparatively more likely in one sample than in another. The original implementation in Li et al.
200   (2023) takes output distributions using the same prompt in two models, one smaller and one larger. It
201   then contrasts the logits from the two models, selecting the token with the largest difference between
202   the original (LO ) and smaller model (LS ), i.e. maxt∈T ′ (LO (t) − LS (t)), limited to a subset T ′ of
203   top tokens to mitigate noise in low-likelihood tokens.
204   Our approach is most similar to the subsequent techniques introduced in Shi et al. (2024) and Malkin
205   et al. (2022), which apply contrastive decoding to pairs of shorter and longer prompts, using the same
206   formula we apply. Malkin et al. (2022) contrasts the full prompt against only the text generated so far
207   to increase long-range semantic coherence, finding improvement in generic language understanding
208   and knowledge retrieval tasks. Shi et al. (2024) uses an equivalent formula to handle conflicts
209   between prior knowledge and information in the context. They also interpret the combination as a
210   product-of-experts.
211   Compared to these works, ours frames the language model itself as a product-of-experts that must
212   select between specific competing tasks, some of which are unwanted distractors, and the resulting
213   manipulation of logits as a reweighing of the experts. Furthermore, while Malkin et al. (2022) and
214   Shi et al. (2024) focus on models failing to account for knowledge and facts, we emphasise how they
215   can include even extremely semantically simple patterns like sequences of numbers. We also draw
      the connection to inverse scaling.

      3   S TRONG P RIORS IN G ENERATIVE M ODELS
217
218
      In this section, we introduce the concept of strong priors within generative models, focusing on their
219
      manifestation in language models and discussing their broader implications.
220
221   The term “strong priors” in this context was introduced by McKenzie et al. (2023) as one of the cat-
222   egories of LM tasks that exhibit inverse scaling trends. The difficulty of this category of tasks seems
223
      to largely stem from local context overly influencing the predicted continuation. We aim to extend
      the informal characterisation in McKenzie et al. (2023) with a more comprehensive definition.
224
225   General Strong Priors. Generative models can exhibit a phenomenon where a part of the input
226   disproportionately affects the output, substantially diminishing the impact of other parts of the input
227   that are essential for the model to complete its assigned task. This general notion of strong priors
228   is applicable to generative models for various domains, including language modelling and image
229
      generation.
230   One example of the phenomenon is an autoregressive language model that has been prompted to
231   write a common sequence (the alphabet) with a variation in the middle (a skipped letter). We em-
232   pirically observe that once the LM has written enough of the common sequence, it fails to add the
233   variation. We hypothesise that the model is overgeneralising from a frequent pattern in its training
234   data, ignoring other context.
235   Definition (Strong Prior). Consider a model input {T, D}, where T is the task description part
236   that may include examples, and D is the data part, consisting of the task input and possibly part of
237   the output. Let M denote a generative model, with M determining any parameters of the generation
238   process, such as temperature, and let pM (O|T, D) represent the probability of M producing O as
239   its output given T and D. Finally, let ≈ be a similarity relation on probability distributions. Then D
240   induces a strong prior in M relative to T if:
241                                         pM ( · |T, D) ≈ pM ( · |D)
242
243
      This implies that the contribution of T to the model’s output is insignificant, and the output is mostly
244
      determined by D. The result is that the model tends towards specific outputs when provided with
245   data D, regardless of the task description T provided.
246
247   This definition is intentionally broad, to allow adaptations based on the domain, modality, and the
248
      specific case in focus. The partitioning of the model input, or the prompt, into task T and data D
      components, is context-dependent, and may not be feasible or pertinent in every setting. Moreover,
249
      the interpretation of pM ( · |T, D) ≈ pM ( · |D) depends on the modeling assumptions, with our
250
      proposed default metric being the total variational distance δ. This would imply the presence of
251   strong priors if δ (pM ( · |T, D), pM ( · |D)) ≤ ε, where ε > 0 is a predetermined threshold.
252
253   While the Kullback–Leibler divergence DKL (pM ( · |T, D)||pM ( · |D)) may be more suitable than
254
      δ in certain contexts, it is susceptible to significant influences from relatively improbable events that
      exhibit substantial shifts in log-probability.
255
256   Strong priors in language models. In this subsection, we adapt the general concept of strong priors
257   for language models. We also explore the common case of local strong priors, where we assume
258   that the model input is a prompt of the form P = T D. Additionally, we suggest using the next
259   token as the main unit of output in the distribution pM (·|P ). Despite potential ambiguity in the
260
      separation into task description T and data D, this representation aligns with the structure of many
      LM benchmarks and common prompting strategies. We can characterise strong local priors as cases
261
      where prepending the task description to the data is not significantly influencing the distribution:
262
263                                       δ (pM ( · |T D), pM ( · |D)) ≤ ε
264
265   In instances where pM (·|T D) ≈ pM (·|D), the difference between the two distributions may hold
266   significant information: the correct continuation is often more probable in pM (·|T D) than pM (·|D).
267   We present a novel method to compensate for strong local priors based on our model in Section 5.
268   This intuition would predict that our method works better in the presence of strong priors, that is
269   with smaller values of δ as introduced above. This relationship on our dataset is indeed demonstrated
      in Appendix C and Figure 4.

      5     M ETHODOLOGY AND E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
342
343
      We employ our proposed technique of Prior-Aware Decoding to mitigate the influence of distractor
      tasks. The influence of the undesired underlying prior is captured by querying the model for logits
344
      using a weakened prompt, which is more susceptible to the prior’s influence.
345
346   The PAD technique is implemented through the following steps. For a more detailed description of
347   the algorithm, see Appendix D.
348
            1. For each prompt in the dataset, we generate two versions:
349
350               • An original prompt containing both the task description and the data.
351               • A weakened prompt more likely to give outputs consistent with the distractor task.
352         2. We query the model on both the original and weakened prompts to obtain two sets of logits.
353         3. We compute a linear combination of these logits: L = LO + α(LO − LW ), where LO are
354            the original logits, LW are the weakened logits, and α is our extrapolation parameter.
355
            4. We sample from this modified distribution to generate the output.
356
            5. We calculate the average performance across the entire dataset.
357
358   For our main results we produce weakened prompts by removing the task component from the
359   prompt. We illustrate examples of splitting prompts from each dataset in Table 2: the split was
360   unambiguous for the tasks we selected, and generated by a regex script. We also discuss weakening
361   prompts by prepending a system prompt in Appendix E.

399   PAD using a smaller model from the same family as the weakened model, essentially following the
400   approach in the original contrastive method used by Li et al. (2023), and find little gain, with only
401   10 out of 16 tasks-model pairs showing any improvement at α = 1.0 (in contrast to 41 of 44 of
402
      task-model pairs of our main result), and with a median increase in task completion proportion of
      0.3% (in contrast to 27% of our main result). See Appendix H for details.
403
404   Extrapolation from a system prompt generally gives worse results, although notably it outperforms
405   the baseline in all four task/model/parameter combinations where extrapolation from the truncated
406   prompt does not. We give more details in Appendix E.
407   The three cases where extrapolation to α = 2 yield lower results (pattern matching suppression on
408   gpt3.5-turbo instruct, pattern matching suppression on gpt2-medium, and redefine on text-ada-001)
409   do not seem to point to any general weaknesses of the technique. Interestingly, gpt3.5-turbo-instruct
410   and ada-001 exhibit the highest TVD on pattern matching suppression and redefine, respectively,
411   both substantially higher than the next highest, suggesting the potential absence of a strong prior in
412   these cases (see AppendixF for a full table of TVD values).
413   We explore a wider range of values of α in Appendix G. In particular, Figure 5 and Figure 6 show
414   the probability of correct completion on a variety of models across a range of values of α from -2
415   to 3. We show that range to more clearly demonstrate general trends in completion as α changes.
416   In general, higher values of α sometimes continue to produce better results, but often the benefits
417   tail off, and in some cases the graphs show a peak beyond which higher values of α cause slow
418   degradation.

423   This paper introduces Prior-Aware Decoding (PAD), a novel technique for mitigating the influence
424   of distractor tasks in language models. Our approach is grounded in a theoretical framework that
425   conceptualizes language models as products of experts, offering a new perspective on how distractor
426   tasks emerge and persist.
427   The primary theoretical contribution of this work is the framing of language models as products
428   of experts. This model provides a specific explanation for the emergence of distractor tasks and
429   offers insights into why we observe counterintuitive scaling behaviors, including inverse scaling, on
430   certain tasks. Importantly, our framework suggests that the balance between these “experts” is not
431   necessarily dictated by model size, which could explain the lack of straightforward scaling effects
      and even the inverse scaling phenomena observed in some cases.
