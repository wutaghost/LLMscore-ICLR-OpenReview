011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Dataset distillation (DD) entails creating a refined, compact distilled dataset from
014            a large-scale dataset to facilitate efficient training. A significant challenge in DD
015            is the dependency between the distilled dataset and the neural network (NN) archi-
016
               tecture used. Training a different NN architecture with a distilled dataset distilled
               using a specific architecture often results in diminished trainning performance for
017
               other architectures. This paper introduces MetaDD, designed to enhance the gen-
018
               eralizability of DD across various NN architectures. Specifically, MetaDD parti-
019            tions distilled data into meta features (i.e., the data’s common characteristics that
020            remain consistent across different NN architectures) and heterogeneous features
021            (i.e., the data’s unique feature to each NN architecture). Then, MetaDD employs
022            an architecture-invariant loss function for multi-architecture feature alignment,
023            which increases meta features and reduces heterogeneous features in distilled data.
024            As a low-memory consumption component, MetaDD can be seamlessly integrated
025            into any DD methodology. Experimental results demonstrate that MetaDD signif-
026            icantly improves performance across various DD methods. On the Distilled Tiny-
027
               Imagenet with Sre2L (50 IPC), MetaDD achieves cross-architecture NN accuracy
               of up to 30.1%, surpassing the second-best method (GLaD) by 1.7%.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Neural networks (NNs) rely heavily on data, and their performance is directly influenced by the
      scale and quality of the data Devlin et al. (2018); Ramesh et al. (2022). However, as datasets grow,
034
      the cost of training NNs increases significantly. Dataset distillation (DD) Cui et al. (2022) addresses
035
      this issue by compressing datasets, producing a smaller, distilled version that can serve as an effi-
036   cient substitute. This technique is particularly valuable in environments with limited memory and
037   computational resources, such as edge devices or real-time applications. With DD, researchers can
038   achieve competitive model performance while significantly reducing both data size and resource
039   consumption.
040
      A significant challenge in DD is its limited transferability across different NN architectures. Dis-
041
      tilled datasets tailored for specific NNs often experience notable performance drop when applied
042   to other architectures. Existing solutions to this cross-architecture gap have various shortcomings.
043   Some do not effectively integrate with vision transformers Zhong & Liu (2023), others only enhance
044   performance for the architectures directly involved in the distillation process Zhou et al. (2024a), and
045   some incur high memory costs due to reliance on image generators Cazenavette et al. (2023). These
046   challenges underscore the need for more robust and versatile DD methods that can maintain strong
047   performance across diverse architectures.
048   To investigate the causes of DD’s cross-architecture gap, we visualize NN architecture biases in fea-
049   ture preferences using Class Activation Maps (CAMs) Zhou et al. (2016); Selvaraju et al. (2017).
050   CAMs highlight the image regions most relevant to a network’s predictions, revealing the key fea-
051   tures that drive its decision-making. We define the overlap between CAM regions from different
052   architectures as the shared decision-making consensus (meta features), while the non-overlapping
053   regions represent each architecture’s unique feature preference (heterogeneous features). Our in-
      vestigation focuses on two key aspects: (1) why the original dataset shows better cross-architecture

080   By exploring both differences, we find that distilled datasets have massive heterogeneous features
081   of the distilled architecture and rare meta features. As shown in Figure 1: (1) Original images
082
      show substantial meta features across different architectures trained on the original dataset, whereas
      distilled images show almost no meta features. This meta feature comparison explains why original
083
      dataset performs more consistently across different NNs than distilled data. (2) Distilled images
084
      have significantly more heterogeneous features with the same architecture used for DD compared to
085   crossed architectures. These heterogeneous features represent the same architecture can extract more
086   semantic information from distilled data, which crossed NN architecture cannot capture, leading to
087   cross-architecture gap.
088
      Based on the above observations, we propose MetaDD to improve the cross-architecture general-
089
      ization of DD. MetaDD begins with using an architecture-invariant loss to obtain and maximize
090
      the exposure of diverse features across different NN architectures. Then, MetaDD decouples het-
091   erogeneous and meta features by transferring distilled data’s CAMs to a common space. By driv-
092   ing the evolution of distilled data towards maximizing meta features, MetaDD encourages to form
093   a generalized consensus cross different NN architectures. MetaDD maintains low memory con-
094   sumption by persistently freezing various NN architectures during DD. We conducted comprehen-
095   sive experiments using DC, DM, MTT, and Sre2L as baselines. Incorporating MetaDD improved
096   DD performance in cross-architecture training. MetaDD covers typical NN architectures, ensuring
097   that even unconsidered models benefit from the meta features generated. On TinyImagenet and
098   ILSVRC-2012, MetaDD has an average accuracy increase of 1.6% and 1.0% compared with GlaD

      Cazenavette et al. (2023).

      2.2    C LASS ACTIVATION M APPING
144
145
146   CAM Kundu (2020); Wang et al. (2020a); Selvaraju et al. (2017); Fu et al. (2020); Jiang et al.
147
      (2021); Wang et al. (2020b); Chattopadhay et al. (2018); Omeiza et al. (2019) algorithms constitute
      an important class of methods in the field of deep learning, aimed at enhancing the interpretability of
148
      NNs. The original CAM Zhou et al. (2016) involves modifying the network architecture to connect
149
      directly from the global average pooling layer to the output layer, allowing the model to highlight
150   important areas of the image for predictions of specific classes. Subsequent developments, such as
151   Grad-CAM Selvaraju et al. (2017), offer a universal solution that does not require modifications to
152   the network architecture. Additionally, variants such as Grad-CAM++ Chattopadhay et al. (2018)
153   and Score-CAM Wang et al. (2020b) have further improved the accuracy and robustness of the
154   heatmaps.
155
      Although CAM was initially designed for CNNs, researchers have begun exploring how similar
156   concepts can be applied to transformer-based visual models. To achieve this application, CAMs are
157   generated by analyzing the attention weights from the last layer Sun et al. (2023). Consequently,
158   researchers have begun developing new techniques and approaches to improve the vision trans-
159   former CAMZhu et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2022), for example, by adjusting or combining attention
160   weights from different layers, or developing specialized interpretative modules to produce clearer
161   and more meaningful visual explanations. facilitating further optimization of NN architectures and
      interpretability improvements.

175   tilling and training on the same model architecture ϑv . Our objective is to minimize the total cross-

529   5     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      We introduce MetaDD, a new component specifically designed to enhance the cross-architecture
532
      generalizability of DD. MetaDD delivers the dual advantages of minimal additional computational
533
      overhead and improved performance. By delving into the factors that limit cross-architecture gener-
534   alizability, MetaDD uncovers the unique feature recognition mechanisms inherent to different neural
535   network architectures, which often prioritize diverse and heterogeneous features. However, these ar-
536   chitectures also adhere to certain shared aesthetic or structural standards. MetaDD enhances cross-
537   architecture generalizability by amplifying the representation of meta features that align with these
538   shared standards. It achieves this by synthesizing meta features through the integration of unified
539   CAM outputs from various neural networks, ensuring these meta features are broadly recognized
      and effectively utilized across different architectures.

752   The experimental results, as shown in Table 5, indicate that as the number of auxiliary models
