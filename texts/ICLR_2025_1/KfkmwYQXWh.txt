009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Humans perform a variety of interactive motions, among which duet dance is one
012            of the most challenging interactions. However, in terms of human motion gener-
013            ative models, existing works are still unable to generate high-quality interactive
014            motions, especially in the field of duet dance. On the one hand, it is due to the
015            lack of large-scale high-quality datasets. On the other hand, it arises from the
016            incomplete representation of interactive motion and the lack of fine-grained opti-
017
               mization of interactions. To address these challenges, we propose, InterDance,
               a large-scale duet dance dataset that significantly enhances motion quality, data
018
               scale, and the variety of dance genres. Built upon this dataset, we propose a new
019
               motion representation that can accurately and comprehensively describe interac-
020            tive motion. We further introduce a diffusion-based framework with an interaction
021            refinement guidance strategy to optimize the realism of interactions progressively.
022            Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our dataset and algorithm.
023            Our project page is https://inter-dance.github.io/.

040   1   I NTRODUCTION
041
042   Dance is one of the most important elements in film, animation, and game production. The rapid
043   generation of high-quality 3D dances using computer algorithms has significant practical value. In
044   duet dance, two dancers perform interactive dances with music and can be typically divided into
045   a leader and a follower. The leader is responsible for initiating and guiding the dance, while the
046   follower responds to the leader’s cues and follows the leader (Siyao et al., 2024). Following this
047   paradigm, we focus on the task of reactive dance generation. Given the music and the leader’s
048   dance as inputs, our goal is to generate the follower’s dance with accurate interactive movements
049   and physical realism.
050   However, this task presents significant challenges. Firstly, large-scale, high-quality datasets for duet
051   dances are scarce. Secondly, current algorithms fail to adequately model strong interactions, re-
052   sulting in generated follower dance movements that lack both interactivity and physical realism.
053   Although some datasets are currently available for solo dances (Li et al., 2023b; 2021a), high-
      quality duet dance data remains extremely scarce. Le et al. (Wang et al., 2022) introduce the


                                                         1

054
      AIOZ-GDANCE dataset, which includes YouTube videos of 2-7 dancers. They used monocular
055   SMPL pose estimation algorithms to obtain SMPL-format motion. However, the predicted motion
056   is low quality and lacks finger movements because estimating 3D multi-human motion from monoc-
057   ular video is a challenging problem, plagued by issues such as video motion blur and multi-person
058   occlusion. Duolando (Siyao et al., 2024) propose a duet dance dataset DD100 that includes finger
059   movements, but it only contains 1.92 hours of data with 10 ballroom dance genres, making it dif-
060   ficult to train robust and generalizable networks. To further improve the scale and quality of duet
061   dance data, we introduce the InterDance dataset. We employ experienced dancers and professional
062   MoCap equipment to record 3.93 hours of music-paired duet dance. The InterDance dataset includes
063
      not only ballroom dances but also folk, classical, and street dances, making a total of 4 major cate-
      gories and 15 diverse fine-grained genres. InterDance features precise body and finger movements,
064
      realistic physical interactions between dancers, and accurate foot-ground contact.
065
066   Generating the follower’s dance based on the music and the leader’s dance has significant chal-
067   lenges in ensuring accurate interactivity and physical realism. Duet dances often involve strong
068   interactions, such as hand-holding and shoulder touches. However, existing methods fail to model
069
      these strong interactions accurately. This failure arises partly because current algorithms use inter-
      nal joint positions to represent motion, inherently lacking surface body information, which results in
070
      inaccurate contacts. Additionally, current methods generate interactive movements based solely on
071
      learned data priors without incorporating specific feedback on contacts and penetration during the
072   generation process. To address these issues, we propose a new motion representation suitable for re-
073   active dance generation. This representation expresses body and hand movements in canonical space
074   and includes downsampled body surface vertices, along with contact labels to enhance the dancers’
075   interaction accuracy. Moreover, we introduce a diffusion-based reactive dance generation algorithm.
076   This algorithm employs a contact and penetration guided sampling strategy based on contact labels
077   and signed distance fields to improve the accuracy and physical realism of interactions.
078   Our main contributions are as follows: (1) We introduce a duet dance dataset featuring body and
079   finger movements with high physical realism, comprising 3.93 hours of music-paired duet dance
080   across 15 different dance genres. (2) We propose a new motion representation. It includes body sur-
081   face information and contact labels, helping algorithms generate more realistic interactions. (3) We
082   propose a diffusion-based reactive dance motion generation model with Interactive Refine Guidance
083   to enhance the accuracy and realism of interactive dance movements.
084   Code, model, and data will be publicly available.

087   2   R ELATED W ORKS
088
089   Music-Dance Datasets. Existing works mainly focus on single-person dance datasets, where move-
090   ments are either estimated from dance videos using algorithms or captured by professional MoCap
091   equipment and dancers. The former method does not require specialized motion capture equipment,
092   making it easier to implement. Li et al. (Li et al., 2021b) used multiple cameras to capture dancers
093
      from different angles, obtaining multi-view dance videos. They then used algorithms to estimate the
      dance movements, creating the AIST++ dataset of 5.2 hours for solo dance. However, accurately es-
094
      timating complex dance movements from videos is challenging due to video occlusion and blur. This
095
      often results in failures to capture fine-grained finger movements and complex rapid actions such as
096   ”Backflip”, ”Thomas rotation”, etc. Professional MoCap Equipment can precisely capture complex
097   movements and is widely used in animation and film production. Therefore, FineDance (Li et al.,
098   2023b) collects a dataset of solo dance performances totaling 14.6 hours, including accurate body
099   and finger movements. Recently, Duolando (Siyao et al., 2024) introduced the DD100 dataset, which
100   comprises only 1.92 hours of duet dance data with paired music. Additionally, ReMoCap (Ghosh
101   et al., 2024), InterHuman Liang et al. (2024), and Inter-X (Xu et al., 2024a) are also high-quality,
102   large-scale Human-Human Interaction datasets. While they include a small portion of the dance,
103   they are more focused on text-driven generation and lack music.
104   Dance generation. Recent advancements in the music-to-dance generation have seen significant

106   Qi et al., 2023). FACT (Li et al., 2021b) utilizes cross-modal transformer blocks with strong se-
107   quence modeling capabilities to generate single-person dance from given music. Bailando (Siyao
      et al., 2022; 2023) introduces a two-stage framework, with the first stage encoding and quantizing

137   There is still a lack of large-scale duet datasets with accurate strong interactions. To address the
138   shortcomings of existing datasets, we collect as much duet dance data as possible and use profes-

143   3.1        T HE FEATURES OF THE I NTER DANCE DATASET
144
145
      The proposed InterDance dataset has the following key features: Large-scale Data: InterDance
      contains 3.93 hours of duet dance data, providing a substantial amount of training data to improve
146
      model generalization and reduce overfitting. To the best of our knowledge, this is currently the
147
      largest duet dance dataset. Strong Interactions: This dataset emphasizes strong interactions, such
148   as handshakes, waist holds, which are essential elements for duet dance. High Motion Quality:
149   This dataset is collected using state-of-the-art professional MoCap equipment for precise body and
150   finger movements, with manual review to ensure the motion quality. High Artistic quality: We
151   invite professional dancers with over a year of experience to perform their familiar dances. Diverse
152   Dance Genres: Among duet dance datasets, InterDance includes the widest variety of 15 dance
153   genres. Long Duration per Sample: InterDance reach the longest average sample duration (T̄ /s) of
154   142.7 seconds among duet dance datasets. Dances with longer durations contain rich and complete
155   choreographic patterns.
156   3.2        T HE DETAILS OF CONSTRUCTING THE DATASET

159   cameras, with a resolution of 2048x1536 and a frame rate of 120 fps. The subjects wore tight-fitting
160   suits with 53 markers attached. The effective capture space is 7m x 7m x 3m. Due to the close
161   articulation and occlusion issues of hand movements, the hand motions were captured using inertial
      data gloves.

179   Dataset Collection: We invited professional dancers to perform duet dances based on music and
180   used motion capture equipment to record their body and hand movements. To prevent foot floating
181   issues, we required the dancers to wear flat shoes. The raw motion captured by MoCap was con-
182   verted into SMPL-X (Pavlakos et al., 2019) format and manually inspected. For any problematic
183   data, the dancers were invited to re-record the performance.
184   Post process: The MoCap system outputs the 3D coordinates of 53 markers on the body surface.
185   After processing with the MoCap system software, it also provides the 3D coordinates of 55 internal
186   human joints, including those of both the body and hands. Since the 3D coordinates of the 53 mark-
187   ers on the body surface contain detailed body surface information, we first used the SOMA (Ghor-
188   bani & Black, 2021) method to get the SMPL-X body shape parameters and body pose parameters
189   from the markers. Next, we fixed the body shape parameters and used the 55 joint coordinates to
190
      optimize the poses for the body and hands, similar to Mosh++ Mahmood et al., 2019. Since the body
      and fingers operate in different motion spaces and have different data scales, we optimized the body
191
      and finger poses independently. We refined the body poses using 22 body joints and adjusted the
192
      finger poses based on the relative distances of 30 finger joints to the wrist. Finally, we combined the
193   body and hand poses to obtain the human motion in SMPL-X format. The test and validation sets
194   were randomly sampled proportionally from various dance genres, comprising 16.22% and 6.25%
195   of the total data, respectively.

198   4      M ETHODOLOGY
199
200   Given music and the leader’s dance xl as input, our goal is to generate the follower’s dance xf . For
201   the given music, we follow (Li et al., 2021a) and employ Librosa (McFee et al., 2015) to extract the
202
      2D music feature map m ∈ RT ×35 , where T is the time length, 35 is the music feature channels with
      1-dim envelope, 20-dim MFCC, 12-dim chroma, 1-dim one-hot peaks, and 1-dim one-hot beats. For
203
      the dance, we represent it as x ∈ RT ×C , where C is the number of channels, details in the fol-
204
      lowing chapter. Our method aims to model pθ (xf |m, xl ). The main challenge lies in the accuracy
205   and physical interaction in the generated dance. We argue that previous methods encounter these
206   issues due to the use of inappropriate motion representations and the lack of explicit fine-grained
207   optimization for interactive movements during generation. To address these problems, we propose
208   a new motion representation suitable for reactive motion generation and introduce a baseline gener-
209   ation algorithm based on diffusion. To enhance the physical realism of interactive movements, we
210   also propose contact diffusion guidance and penetration diffusion guidance to optimize the quality
211   of these interactions.

362   Implementation Details. The initial learning rate is 1e-4, with a weight decay of 2e-5. The model
363
      trains for 1000 epochs. The number of heads in multi-head attention is uniformly set to 8. In the
      ablation study, all variants are trained for 500 epochs under the same experimental settings, except

366
367   Evaluation Metrics. 1) Frechet Inception Distance (FID). We use FID to measure the degree of
368   closeness between the generated motion of the follower and the ground truth. 2) Diversity (Div).
369
      We use Div to assess the average feature distance of generated dances. FID and Div are calculated
      on kinematic (Onuma et al., 2008) and graphical (Müller et al., 2005) features, and described as
370
      FIDk , FIDg , Divk , Divg respectively. 3) Cross Distance (cd). Following Duolando(Siyao et al.,
371
      2024), we compute pairwise distances between ten joints of the leader and follower (including the
372   pelvis, knees, feet, shoulders, head, and wrists) as interaction features, and use these features to
373   calculate FIDcd and Divcd . 4) Contact Frequency (CF). CF represents the ratio of contact frames
374   to all frames. We define contact as vertices distances between dancers being less than 1cm. 5)
375   Penetration Rate (PR). We use the ratio of penetration vertices to all vertices as the Penetration
376   Rate. 6) Beat Echo Degree (BED). Following Duolando(Siyao et al., 2024), we use BED to measure
377   the rhythm consistency between two dancers. 7) Beat-Align Score (BAS). We follow AIST++(Li
      et al., 2021a) and use BAS to assess the rhythm matching between music and dance.

410   Quantitative Comparisons. We compare our method with several state-of-the-art approaches from
411   related fields on our InterDance dataset, all of which are adapted for benchmarking in the context
412   of react dance generation. In the field of solo dance generation, we choose EDGE (Tseng et al.,
413   2023) for comparison. For group dance generation, we select the latest GCD (Le et al., 2023a)
414
      for comparison. For text-driven human interaction generation, we select InterGen (Liang et al.,
      2024). We also evaluate against Duolando (Siyao et al., 2024), currently the only available method
415
      for generating react dance. As shown in Table 3, our method demonstrates superior performance
416
      compared with previous approaches, particularly in quality-related FID and Div metrics. In terms of
417   contact metrics CF, our method achieves a significant improvement of 4.84% compared to EDGE,
418   demonstrating our advantages in interaction generation. Although the motions generated by EDGE
419   and GCD have low PR metric, their CF remains poor. The text-driven interactive generation method,
420   InterGen, performs well on interaction quality but relatively poorly on motion quality due to the
421   variation in signal modalities. Compared with Duolando, which is carefully designed for reactive
422   dance generation, our method performs better in most metrics. This is because Duolando employs a
423   two-stage training framework that stores motions in a codebook, making it difficult to optimize fine-
424   grained interactions. In contrast, our method explicitly optimizes interactions within the original
425
      motion space during sampling.
426   Qualitative Comparisons. We provide visualizations of the results generated by different methods
427   in Figure 5. This figure demonstrates that while GCD (Le et al., 2023a) initially generates relatively
428   good follower motions, it later exhibits shifts in the relative positions of the dancers. InterGen (Liang
429   et al., 2024) produces monotonous motions that fail to appropriately respond to the leader. Although
430   the results of Duolando (Siyao et al., 2024) are relatively good, it also suffers from significant contact

      floating or penetration issues. In contrast, our method produces more responsive and interactively
      coherent motions, achieving superior contact quality and mitigating penetration problems.

524   6       C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
525
526   In this work, we introduce InterDance, which includes a large-scale, high-quality duet dance dataset
527
      and a reactive/duet dance generation method. To enhance the quality of the generated interactive
      dance, we train the diffusion model using our proposed new motion representation and optimize in-
528
      teraction quality with diffusion guidance techniques. Our method shows excellent results, as demon-
529
      strated by both quantitative and qualitative experiments. However, there are still limitations, such
530   as the trade-off between quality and scale in our dataset, inefficient for generating long-sequence
531   duet dances in our method. The potential societal impact is that, as dance generation and human
532   interaction technology become more advanced, highly realistic virtual humans might lead users to
533   become so immersed in the virtual world that they detach from real-world participation.

180   F      M ORE E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS OF I NTERACTION Q UALITY.
181
182   In the main paper, we employ the contact metric CF to represent the percentage of frames in which
183
      the distance between dancers is below a certain threshold. However, this metric is highly suscepti-
      ble to outliers and weak interactions, preventing it from accurately reflecting the quality of contact.
184
      To address this issue, we propose three new metrics, contact leader rate (CLR), contact follower
185
      rate (CFR), and contact vertices rate (CVR), which calculate the proportion of vertices where the
186   distance between dancers is below the threshold. This approach mitigates the influence of isolated
187   points on the evaluation results. CLR represents the proportion of leader vertices in contact, while
188   CVR is the average of CLR and CFR (the proportion of follower vertices in contact). The exper-
189   imental results for the aforementioned metrics are displayed in Table 4. Our method exceeds all
190   others regarding contact metrics, achieving more than twice the performance of Duolando, which is
191   specifically designed for generating react dances.

237   H        I NSTRUCTIONS FOR DANCERS
238
239   During data collection, we provided the dancers with some guidance, as listed below:
240   (1) Motion Capture Equipment: Dancers wear motion capture equipment according to specified
241   procedures and body positions. Dancers don motion capture equipment following specified proce-
242   dures and body positions.
243
      (2) Initialization Pose: Prior to the official performance, dancers must adopt an A pose to initialize
244
      the motion capture system.
245
246   (3) Starting Position: Performances commence from the center of the stage, with movement being
247   both encouraged and permitted.
248   (4) Pair Interactions: Dancers perform in pairs, maximizing interaction during the dance.
249
      (5) Clarity of Movements: Movements should be clear and distinct, utilizing highly recognizable
250
      actions.
251
252   (6) Harmony with Music: Careful attention must be paid to synchronizing dance movements with
253   the music beat and ensuring stylistic harmony between the dance and the music.
