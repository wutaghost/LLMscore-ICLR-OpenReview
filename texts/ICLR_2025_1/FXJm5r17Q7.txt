009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large-scale transformer models have achieved remarkable empirical successes,
012            largely due to their in-context learning capabilities. Inspired by this, we ex-
013            plore training an autoregressive transformer for in-context Reinforcement Learn-
014            ing (RL). In this setting, we initially train a transformer on an offline dataset con-
015            sisting of trajectories collected from various RL instances, and then fix and use this
016            transformer to create an action policy for new RL instances. Notably, we consider
017            the setting where the offline dataset contains trajectories sampled from suboptimal
018            behavioral policies. In this case, standard autoregressive training corresponds to
019            imitation learning and results in suboptimal performance. To address this, we pro-
020
               pose the Decision Importance Transformer (DIT), which emulates the actor-critic
               algorithm in an in-context manner. In particular, we first train a transformer-based
021
               value function that estimates the advantage functions of the behavior policies that
022
               collected the suboptimal trajectories. Then we train a transformer-based policy
023            via a weighted maximum likelihood estimation loss, where the weights are con-
024            structed based on the trained value function to steer the suboptimal policies to the
025            optimal ones. We conduct extensive experiments to test the performance of DIT
026            on both bandit and Markov Decision Process problems. Our results show that
027            DIT achieves superior performance, particularly when the offline dataset contains
028            suboptimal historical data.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large-scale transformer models (LTMs) such as Large Language Models have achieved remarkable
034
      empirical successes (Radford et al., 2019; OpenAI et al., 2024). In particular, LTMs trained on vast
035
      amount of data have shown remarkable in-context learning (ICL) capabilities in supervised learning,
036   effectively solving new tasks with just a few demonstrations and without requiring any parameter
037   updates (Brown et al., 2020a; AkyuМҲrek et al., 2022). Meanwhile, substantial evidence demonstrates
038   that autoregressive LTMs excel at solving individual Reinforcement Learning (RL) tasks where an
039   LTM-based agent is trained and tested on the same task (Li et al., 2023b).
040
      In-context RL. Inspired by these, recent research has explored the use of LTMs for in-context
041
      RL (Laskin et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2024). In this setting, we pretrain LTMs on an offline dataset
042   consisting of trajectories collected from various RL instances. After pretraining, we deploy LTMs
043   to new and unseen RL instances. When presented with the context containing history of environ-
044   ment interactions collected by unknown and often suboptimal policies, pretrained LTMs predict the
045   optimal actions for current states from the environmental information provided in the context. See
046   Figure 1 for a visual illustration. Two recent works, Algorithm Distillation (AD) (Laskin et al.,
047   2022) and Decision Pretrained Transformer (DPT) (Lee et al., 2024), have demonstrated impressive
048   in-context RL abilities, inferring near-optimal policies for new RL instances.
049   Challenges. However, existing approaches focus on training LTMs to imitate the actions in the
050   pretraining datasets and thus have stringent requirements on the pretraining datasets. For example,
051   DPT requires access to optimal policies to generate a set of optimal action labels for its supervised
052   pretraining of LTMs. To overcome these limitations, this work considers training LTMs for in-
053   context RL using only suboptimal historical data. While this presents significant challenges, it
      also offers substantial potential benefits by significantly improving the feasibility of in-context RL,

                    Contributions. In pursuit of this goal, we introduce the Decision Importance Transformer (DIT),
          076
                    a supervised pretraining framework for in-context RL using only historical trajectories collected by
          077
                    suboptimal behavioral policies across distinct RL instances. When the pretraining datasets contain
          078       only suboptimal trajectories, existing approaches correspond to imitation learning and thus result in
         079        suboptimal performance. DIT overcomes this challenge through several techniques:
         080
         081                  вҖў DIT learns to infer near-optimal actions from suboptimal trajectories through an exponen-
         082                    tial reweighting technique that assigns good actions in the offline dataset with more weights
         083                    during supervised pretraining. These assigned weights guide the suboptimal policies to-
         084                    ward the optimal ones.
         085
         086                  вҖў In particular, the assigned weights are constructed from the advantage functions of the
         087
                                behavior policies such that actions with high advantage values receive more weights during
                                pretraining, leading to guaranteed policy improvements over the behavior policies.
         088
         089                  вҖў Notably, although advantage weighted regression has been studied in standard RL, it re-
         090                    mains unclear how to generalize this approach to ICRL. The main reason is that the weight-
         091                    ing function needs to be task dependent for ICRL. Thus, it is necessary to estimation the
         092                    advantages functions for all RL tasks in the pretraining dataset. The most severe technical
         093                    challenge for this is that because the source tasks of training trajectories are unknown and
         094                    thus we cannot combine the trajectories from the same RL tasks to improve estimation , we
         095                    need to estimate the advantage functions individually for each trajectory in the pretraining
         096                    dataset. To address this formidable challenge, DIT trains an LTM-based advantage estima-
         097
                                tor that interpolates across tasks for an in-context estimation of the advantage functions to
                                facilitate the weighted supervised pretraining. See Figure 1 for a visualization.
         098
         099
                   Empirical Results. Through extensive experiments on various bandit and Markov Decision Process
         100
                   (MDP) problems, we demonstrate that pretrained DIT models generalize to unseen decision-making
         101
                   problems. On bandit problems, the performance of DIT models matches that of the theoretically
         102       optimal bandit algorithms (e.g., Thompson Sampling (Russo et al., 2018)). In four challenging
         103       MDP problems including two navigating tasks with sparse rewards (Dark Room (Laskin et al., 2022)
         104       and Miniworld (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2023)) and two complex continuous control tasks (Meta-
         105       World (Yu et al., 2020) and Half-Cheetah (Todorov et al., 2012)), DIT models achieves superior
         106       performance, particularly when the pretraining dataset contains suboptimal trajectories. Notably,
         107       in most scenarios, DIT is comparable to DPT in both online and offline testings, despite being
                   pretrained without optimal action labels.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Offline Reinforcement Learning. Since we consider pretraining with historical data, our work
111   falls within the broader field of offline RL. While online RL algorithms (Kaelbling et al., 1996;
112   FrancМ§ois-Lavet et al., 2018) learn optimal policies by interacting with the environments through trial
113   and error, offline RL (Levine et al., 2020; Matsushima et al., 2020; Prudencio et al., 2023) aims
114   to infer optimal policies from historical data collected by (suboptimal) behavioral policies. One of
115   the substantial challenges for offline RL is the distribution shift caused by the mismatch between
116   behavioral policies and optimal policies (Levine et al., 2020; Kostrikov et al., 2021). To this end,
117   offline RL algorithms learn pessimistically by either policy regularization or underestimating the
118
      policy returns (Wu et al., 2019; Kidambi et al., 2020; Kumar et al., 2020; Rashidinejad et al., 2021;
      Yin & Wang, 2021; Jin et al., 2021; Dong et al., 2023; Fujimoto & Gu, 2021). While the goal of
119
      offline RL is solve the same RL tasks from where the offline datasets are collected, the goal of in-
120
      context RL is to efficiently generalize to unseen tasks after pretraining with offline datasets from
121   diverse RL tasks.
122
123   Transformer Models and Autoregressive Decision Making. Large Language Models and autore-
124
      gressive models (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020b; Wu et al., 2023b; Touvron et al., 2023;
      OpenAI et al., 2024) have achieved astonishing empirical successes in a wide range of application
125
      areas, including medicine (Singhal et al., 2023; Thirunavukarasu et al., 2023), education (Kasneci
126
      et al., 2023), finance (Wu et al., 2023a; Yang et al., 2023), etc. As it is natural to use autoregressive
127   models for sequential decision making, transformer models have demonstrated superior performance
128   in both bandit and MDP problems (Li et al., 2023a; Yuan et al., 2023). In particular, Decision Trans-
129   former (DT) (Chen et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Yamagata et al., 2023) uses
130   return-conditioned supervised learning to tackle offline RL. Although salable to multi-task settings
131   (i.e., one model for multiple RL problems), DT is commonly criticised for its inability to improve
132   upon the offline datasets and provably sub-optimal in certain scenarios, e.g., environment with high
133   stochasticity (Brandfonbrener et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022; Yamagata et al., 2023). More impor-
134   tantly, DT cannot generalize to unseen RL problems in context. To this end, Algorithm Distillation
135
      (AD) (Laskin et al., 2022) uses sequential modeling to emulate the learning process of RL algo-
      rithms, i.e., meta-learning (Vilalta & Drissi, 2002). The work most closely related to ours is the
136
      Decision Pretrained Transformer (DPT) (Lee et al., 2024), a supervised pretraining approach for in-
137
      context decision making. DPT trains transformers to predict the optimal action given a query state
138   and a set of transitions. As delineated in Section 1, AD and DPT have stringent assumptions on the
139   pretraining datasets. Our work overcomes those drawbacks and does not require query to optimal
140   policies nor the complete learning histories of RL algorithms (Laskin et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2024).

532   6            D ISCUSSION
533
534   We have proposed DIT for pretraining LTMs from suboptimal historical data for in-context RL. DIT
535   has guaranteed policy improvements over the suboptimal behavior policies and thus demonstrated
536   superior empirical performance. Despite these strengths, DIT still requires the behavior policies that
537   collected the historical data from various RL instances to have reasonable rewards. Most historical
538   data typically adheres to this constraint. That said, it is highly unlikely to infer near-optimal actions
539   solely from random trajectories without any information about optimal policies. To this end, we will
      further explore the limits of the weighted pretraining framework in future work.

789
      signed to perform in-context learning for reinforcement learning (RL) tasks by leveraging a super-
790   vised pretraining approach. The core idea is to train a transformer model to predict optimal actions
791   given a query state and a corresponding in-context dataset, which contains interactions from a vari-
792   ety of tasks. These interactions are represented as transition tuples consisting of states, actions, and
793   rewards, offering context for decision-making. During pretraining, DPT samples a distribution of
794   tasks. For each task Ti , an in-context dataset Di is constructed to include sequences of state-action-
795   reward interactions that represent past experience with that task. Additionally, a query state sвҲ— is
796   sampled from the MDPвҖҷs state distribution, and the model is trained to predict the optimal action
797   based on this query state and the context Di . Formally, the training objective is to minimize the
798
      expected loss over the sampled task distribution by predicting a distribution over actions given the
      state and context.

      Algorithm Distillation (AD). Algorithm Distillation (AD) transforms the process of reinforce-
811   ment learning (RL) into an in-context learning task by training a transformer model to predict op-
812   timal actions based on a cross-episodic trajectory. AD gathers trajectories from training episodes,

      C     MDP E NVIRONMENTS
956
957   Dark Room. The agent is randomly placed in a room of 10 Г— 10 grids, and there is an unknown
958
      goal location on one of the grid. Thus, there are 10x10 = 100 goals. The agentвҖҷs observation is its
      current position/grid in the room, i.e., S = [10] Г— [10]. The agent needs to move to the goal location
959
      by choosing from 5 actions: to move in one of the 4 directions (up, down, left, right) or stay still.
960
      The agent receives a reward of 1 only when it is at the goal; otherwise, it receives 0. The horizon
961   for Dark Room is 100. We follow Lee et al. (2024) to use the tasks on 80 out of the 100 goals for
962   pretraining, and reserve the rest 20 goals for testing our modelsвҖҷ in-context RL capability for unseen
963   tasks. The optimal actions are defined as: move up or down until the agent is on the same vertical
964   position as the goal; otherwise move left or right until the agent reaches the goal.
965
966   Miniworld. The agent is placed in a room with four boxes of different colors, one of which being
967   the target box. The goal is to reach a box of a specific color in the room. The agent receives a
968   (25 Г— 25 Г— 3) color image and its 2-D direction as input, and can choose from four possible actions:
969   to turn left/right, move straight forward, or stay still. Similar to Dark Room, it receives a reward
970   of 1 only when it is near the target box while the horizon is 50. The optimal actions are defined as
971   follows: turn left/right towards the correct box if the agentвҖҷs front is not within 15 degrees of the
      correct box; otherwise move forward and stay if the agent is near the box.

972
       Meta-World. The agent needs to control a robotic arm to pick up an object and place it at a
973    designated target location. In each task, the state space is in 39 dims including the gripperвҖҷs position
974    and state (open or closed), the 3D position of the object to be manipulated, and the coordinates of the
975    target location. The agent operates in a continuous action space, where it can adjust the gripperвҖҷs 3D
976    position and control the open/close state to enable successful grasping and releasing of the object. It
977    provides partial rewards for moving the gripper towards the object, grasping it correctly, transporting
978    it to the target location, and successfully releasing it there. The task goal is to learn an optimal policy
979    that efficiently achieves the sequence of actions required to pick up and accurately place the object
980    at the specified location. Each task has a different goal position. We train in 15 tasks and test in 5
981
       tasks.
982
983    Half-Cheetah. The agent needs to control a 2D half-cheetah robot to achieve and maintain varying
984    target velocities, which change across episodes. The state space contains the cheetahвҖҷs motion,
985    including joint angles, velocities, body velocity, and position. These observations enable the agent
986
       to learn intricate movement patterns and maintain balance while running. The action controls the
       torques applied to each joint of the cheetah, thus dictating its locomotion and stability. The reward
987
       is designed to align with the core task objective: matching the agentвҖҷs velocity to the target velocity.
988
       Each task has different target velocity, and we use 35 tasks to train and 5 to test.

991    D    P RETRAINING DATASET
992
993    Pretraining Datasets for Dark Room and Miniworld. To ensure coverage of optimal actions
994    (so that optimal policies can be inferred), at every step, with probability p (respectively 1 вҲ’ p) we
995    use optimal policy (respectively random policy) to choose action. We choose p so that the average
996    reward of the trajectories in the pretraining dataset is less than 30% of that of the optimal trajectories,
997    reflecting the challenging yet common scenarios. For Dark Room, to test whether DIT models can
998    generalize to unseen RL problems in context, we collect context datasets from only 80 out of the
999    total 100 goals and reserves the rest 20 for testing. For each training goal, we follow the setting
1000
       of DPT to collect 1k context datasets, leading to a total of 80k context datasets in the pretraining
       dataset (64k for training and 16k for validation). For Miniworld, we collect 40k context datasets
1001
       (32k for training and 8k for validation), 10k datasets for each of the four tasks corresponding to four
1002
       possible box colors.
1003
1004   Pretraining Datasets for Meta-World and Half-Cheetah. We construct the pretraining datasets
1005   using historical trajectories generated by agents trained with Soft Actor Critic (SAC). Specifically,
1006
       SAC is trained until convergence for each task, then we sample from its learning trajectories to build
       the dataset. Our SAC model training follows the settings outlined in Haarnoja et al. (2018). For the
1007
       Meta-World environment, we use its built-in deterministic policy as the optimal policy; for Half-
1008
       Cheetah, we use the optimal SAC policy. In Meta-World, we used 15 tasks to train and 5 to test.
1009   Similarly, for Half-Cheetah, we used 35 tasks to train and 5 to test.
