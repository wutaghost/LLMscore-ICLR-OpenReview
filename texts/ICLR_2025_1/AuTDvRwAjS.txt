009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Games have played a pivotal role in advancing artificial intelligence, with AI
012            agents using sophisticated techniques to compete. Despite the success of neu-
013            ral network based game AIs, their performance often requires significant com-
014            putational resources. In this paper, we present Rapfi, an efficient Gomoku agent
015            that outperforms CNN-based agents in limited computation environments. Rapfi
016            leverages a compact neural network with a pattern-based codebook distilled from
017            CNNs, and an incremental update scheme that minimizes computation when input
018            changes are minor. This new network uses computation that is orders of magnitude
019
               less to reach a similar accuracy of much larger neural networks such as Resnet.
               Thanks to our incremental update scheme, depth-first search methods such as the
020
               α-β search can be significantly accelerated. With a carefully tuned evaluation and
021
               search, Rapfi reached strength surpassing Katagomo, the strongest open-source
022            Gomoku AI based on AlphaZero’s algorithm, under limited computational re-
023            sources where accelerators like GPUs are absent. Rapfi ranked first among 520
024            Gomoku agents on Botzone and won the championship in GomoCup 2024.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Artificial intelligence in board games like Go, Chess, and Shogi has progressed rapidly with the
030   advent of deep neural networks. Notable examples include AlphaGo (Silver et al., 2016), AlphaGo
031   Zero (Silver et al., 2017b), Katago (Wu, 2019), and other efforts in Chess (Schrittwieser et al., 2020)
032   and Shogi (Silver et al., 2017a; Schrittwieser et al., 2020; Nasu, 2018). These methods rely heavily
033   on deep neural networks, requiring powerful accelerators like high-end GPUs. Our objective is to
034   create a specialized network that maintains similar prediction accuracy but runs significantly faster,
035   aiming to outperform current state-of-the-art solutions under constrained computation.
036   Gomoku, a straightforward yet complex perfect information board game, that serves as an excel-
037   lent benchmark for evaluating agent performance. Although Gomoku with free openings has been
038   solved (Allis et al., 1994), achieving optimal play with arbitrary balanced openings remains chal-
039   lenging. Current methods often fail to exceed top human players or are computationally intensive.
040   Therefore, we choose Gomoku as our testbed, as it demands precise position evaluation and deep
041   tactical search, making the balance between evaluation and search critical.
042   Deep models like convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are highly effective at predicting values and
043   policies from 2D inputs, playing a crucial role in reinforcement learning. evaluating them can be
044   computationally intensive. Our key observation is that, in board games with black and white stones,
045   much of this computation is spent extracting features from local patterns. While CNNs excel at this,
046   the process is often redundant, as features of the same patterns remain fixed, and most of the board
047   doesn’t change during gameplay. By decomposing the board into local patterns and pre-computing
048   their features, we can greatly reduce computational demands.
049   To address this, we introduce a lightweight neural network named Mixnet, which requires far less
050   computation than CNNs while maintaining comparable accuracy. Our method uses a pattern-based
051   codebook derived from a larger network and includes an incremental update scheme that reduces
052   computation further, especially with depth-first game tree traversal like Alpha-Beta search. We also
053   introduce several improvements in the feed-forward process to enhance the accuracy of value and
      policy prediction. In summary, our contributions include:

      2.1   E FFICIENT N EURAL N ETWORKS
069
070   With the rapid advancement of computer vision, the landscape of neural network design has seen
071   a surge in innovative architectures aimed at minimizing computational demands while maximiz-
072   ing performance. Notable designs include Mixtures of Experts (MoE) (Jacobs et al., 1991), Mo-
073   bileNets (Howard et al., 2017; Sandler et al., 2018; Howard et al., 2019), SENet (Hu et al., 2018),
074   SKNets (Li et al., 2019) and CondConv (Yang et al., 2019). These models are designed to improve
075   both inference speed and prediction accuracy. Unlike the approaches mentioned above, our method
076   takes a novel direction by precomputing a pattern-based feature lookup table and takes advantage of
077
      the incremental nature of the gameplay structure. We also incorporate structural improvements in the
      feedforward components of the network, including depth-wise and point-wise convolutions (Zhang
078
      et al., 2020), dynamic convolution (Chen et al., 2020), grouped average pooling, and the star opera-
079
      tion (Ma et al., 2024).
080
081
082   2.2   S EARCH A LGORITHMS FOR G AME T REES
083
      The most notable game tree search algorithms are Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) (Coulom,
084
      2006) and α-β search (Knuth & Moore, 1975). MCTS is commonly used by Go agents and has
085   many variants that enhance its performance, such as UCT (Gelly & Wang, 2006), RAVE (Rimmel
086   et al., 2010), and EMCTS (Cazenave, 2007). Additionally, parallel versions of MCTS (Chaslot
087   et al., 2008; Cazenave, 2022) have improved search speed and efficiency on multi-core hardware.
088   On the other hand, α-β search has a long history with many enhancements, including futility prun-
089   ing (Heinz, 1998; Hoki & Muramatsu, 2012), razoring (Birmingham & Kent, 1988), null move prun-
090   ing (Donninger, 1993; Hoki & Muramatsu, 2012), late move reduction (Hoki & Muramatsu, 2012),
091   and history reduction (Schaeffer, 1983). For Gomoku, algorithms like proof-number search (Allis
092   et al., 1994) and threat-space search (Allis et al., 1993) significantly reduce computation for proving
093
      positions and improving search efficiency in specific scenarios. Since the traversal order of the game
      tree may affect the inference speed of our proposed network, we evaluate it using both MCTS and
094
      α-β search in our experiments.

097   2.3   AGENTS FOR THE G AME OF G OMOKU
098
099
      Gomoku is a two-player game featuring simple rules yet significant strategic depth, making it an
      excellent testbed for evaluating evaluation models and search techniques. Played on a 15x15 grid,
100
      players alternate placing black and white stones, with the black player starting first. The goal is to
101
      align five stones in a row—horizontally, vertically, or diagonally. If the board fills without a win-
102   ner, the game ends in a draw. Research has shown that the first player can always achieve a win,
103   but reaching optimal play from a balanced position remains a complex challenge, with many meth-
104   ods still falling short of optimal performance. Notable advancements in Gomoku agents include
105   threat-space search (Allis et al., 1993), proof-number search (Allis et al., 1993), first-player winning
106   strategies (Allis et al., 1994), adaptive dynamic programming (Zhao et al., 2012), and genetic algo-
107   rithms (Wang & Huang, 2014). Additionally, several neural network advancements (Wu, 2019; Xie
      et al., 2018; Wang, 2018) have also been inspired by AlphaZero (Schrittwieser et al., 2020).

      In this section, we present the design of our proposed Mixnet. We begin with an overview of the
111   network architecture, followed by the pattern-based feature codebook and an incremental update
112   scheme. Lastly, we describe the feed-forward components, including various improvements to the
113   value and policy prediction heads.

115   3.1   OVERVIEW OF M IXNET A RCHITECTURE
116
117   The Mixnet takes a one-hot encoded binary board plane x ∈ 0, 12×H×W as input and predicts the
118   policy distribution π̂ ∈ RH×W and the categorical value v̂ ∈ R3 (win, loss, and draw rates). Unlike
119   a convolutional neural network, it first decomposes the board into directional line patterns, using
120   these local patterns to construct a feature map. The Mapping network extracts local pattern fea-
121
      tures, which are then combined by a depth-wise 3 × 3 convolution layer. The Mapping network
      can be exported to a pattern-indexed codebook losslessly, allowing Mixnet to efficiently retrieve
122
      features during inference with minimal computation. An incremental update scheme further opti-
123
      mizes computation by only recalculating features for updated stones. After building the feature map,
124   lightweight policy and value heads predict the position’s evaluation in a feed-forward manner. The
125   pipeline of Mixnet is illustrated in Fig. 1.

193

194

195   Instead, we utilize a specialized Mapping network with a 3 × 3 kernel that performs convolution
196   operations along specific directional lines. The kernels, shown in Fig. 2, have non-zero weights only
197   in the designated direction. Different weights are used for horizontal/vertical and diagonal directions
198   to capture subtle differences caused by directionality. We refer to this convolution layer as Dir Conv.
199

200
      field per layer, we use five Dir Conv layers to ensure the network captures line segment patterns of

202   feature extraction and apply skip connections to facilitate smoother training. The mapping network
203   operates on an internal feature map with M channels and uses the final point-wise convolution to
204   produce the output directional feature map with C channels.
205
      Since the mapping network functions as a convolution network with shared parameters, it can be
206

207   mapping network by enumerating all N patterns as a plane of shape 2 × 1 × (i + 1 + j) and feed
208   them into the network. We rearrange all kernel weights of Dir Conv as shown in Fig. 2 (a). By
209   recording the feature outputs at (1, i + 1), we export the mapping network losslessly as a pattern-

254   3.5      E NHANCING F EED -F ORWARD H EADS
255
256   After the incremental update phase, we compute the policy and value heads from the processed
257   feature map F ′ in a feed-forward manner. To improve the accuracy of Mixnet without a signifi-
258
      cant increase in computation, we propose three enhancements: dynamic policy convolution, value
      grouping, and star block.
259
260   Dynamic convolution enhances policy prediction accuracy, as illustrated in Fig. 4 (a). The policy
261   head first applies average pooling on F ′ to compute the global feature mean, followed by two linear
262   layers with a ReLU activation to generate the weights and bias for dynamic point-wise convolution,
263   which is applied to the first P channels of F ′ . A subsequent point-wise convolution transforms
264
      the 16-channel policy features into the raw policy output π̂ ∈ RH×W . As shown in Sec. 3, this
      dynamic convolution enables the policy head to capture global board information and adaptively
265
      adjust predictions by modulating channel contributions, significantly improving accuracy with min-
266
      imal computational cost.
267

      4.3   S EARCH
345
346   A key difference between algorithms for general reinforcement learning and perfect-information
347   board games is that the latter can use ”look-ahead” search to reduce approximation errors from
348   imperfect evaluations. Improving evaluation and search often conflict due to their computational
349   demands, creating a tradeoff. Our goal is to find the optimal balance that maximizes performance
350   within a fixed computation budget. To assess model effectiveness, we need to test them with a
351   practical search algorithm, considering the variability in evaluation accuracy and inference speed.
352   We validate the efficiency of our proposed model using two search algorithms. First, we employ the
353   best-first Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with the Predictor Upper Confidence Bounds applied to
354   Tree (PUCT) variant, which combines policy and value for selective search. Given that MixNet ben-
355   efits from incremental updates suited for depth-first traversal, we also test it with Alpha-Beta search,
356   implementing the Principal Variation Search (PVS) variant and incorporate various enhancements
357   that improve performance. Further details on our implementations of these search algorithms are
358   available in the appendix.

510
511
      We present Rapfi, an efficient Gomoku AI agent optimized for resource-limited environments. Our
512
      proposed model, Mixnet, decomposes the board plane into local line-shaped patterns and distills a
513   pattern-indexed codebook from a specially designed mapping network. This approach, combined
514   with an incremental update scheme and enhancements in the feed-forward heads, allows Mixnet
515   to match the performance of large CNNs while drastically reducing computational requirements.
516   Experimental results show that Mixnet significantly outperforms Resnet baselines and the state-of-
517   the-art Gomoku agent Katagomo under the same computational constraints. Combining Mixnet
518   with a carefully tuned Alpha-Beta search, Rapfi ranks top among 520 Gomoku agents on Botzone,
519   and successfully won the championship in the 2024 Gomocup tournament against 54 competitors.
520   Details of the match results are available on the website of Botzone and Gomocup.
521   In conclusion, we have showcased the potential of carefully designed compact neural networks,
522   especially in scenarios where evaluation speed is crucial for agent performance. The success of
523   our model stems from the novel pattern decomposition and the precomputation of a pattern-indexed
524   feature codebook. Our incremental update mechanism further minimizes computational costs when
525   inputs change partially, making it well-suited for game tree traversal with sequential evaluations of
526   similar positions. Additionally, enhancements in feed-forward heads significantly boost prediction
527
      accuracy without adding excessive computation costs. However, this paper does not address certain
      limitations, such as the model’s shallowness and its scalability to larger networks. We hope our
528
      findings will inspire further research into efficient neural networks and advancements in game AI.

723
      value head and the linear layers that produces the dynamic weights and bias in the policy head, we
724   use 8-bit matrix multiply with 32-bit accumulation quantization for all linear layers.
