009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               In this paper, we introduce M ASK 2M ESH (M2M), a novel privacy-preserving data
012            augmentation framework that effectively bridges the realism gap seen in synthetic-
013            based action recognition methods. Traditional privacy-enhancing techniques, such
014            as feature masking and synthetic data supplementation, tend to degrade data quality
015            and reduce model performance. In contrast, our method leverages the SMPL-X
016            model to replace real humans with detailed 3D meshes in video data, preserving the
017            subtle nuances of human movement and expressions that are crucial for accurate
018            action recognition. By augmenting real data with superimposed meshes, M2M
019            simplifies both pre-training and fine-tuning processes, without introducing the
020
               overheads and biases typically associated with synthetic data. Empirical results
               show that our approach achieves performance within 0.5% of models trained
021
               on unmodified video data, proving that overlaying meshes leads to no significant
022
               performance loss in action recognition tasks. This work presents a practical solution
023            for data anonymization without compromising accuracy, offering valuable insights
024            for more efficient and scalable video data processing techniques in computer vision
025            and action recognition.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Action recognition, the process of classifying human activities based on video sequences, is crucial
032   for applications such as surveillance, human-computer interaction, and video analytics (Herath et al.,
033
      2017). Traditional action recognition systems rely heavily on extensively annotated datasets to
      achieve optimal performance. With advancements in deep learning and the emergence of vision
034
      transformers (ViTs) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), pre-training models on large datasets has become
035
      standard practice to enhance accuracy and generalization (Pham et al., 2022). However, these datasets
036   often include identifiable individuals, raising significant privacy and ethical concerns (Yoo & Choi,
037   2013).
038
039
      Data sharing, particularly without obtaining explicit consent from individuals, necessitates robust
      de-identification methods. Conventional anonymization techniques, such as blurring and pixelation,
040
      often degrade data quality, thereby reducing its efficacy for action recognition tasks. Moreover, these
041
      methods rely on heuristics and may not effectively balance privacy protection with data utility (Ren
042   et al., 2018). Ensuring individual privacy while sharing video data can significantly advance research
043   and development in action recognition and computer vision, where large datasets are imperative.
044   Privacy-preserving techniques that maintain data quality can enhance the accuracy and reliability
045   of machine learning models, facilitating more robust and fair applications. Nonetheless, these
046   methods do not fully address the realism gap introduced by synthetic methods, nor do they completely
047   safeguard privacy, as variable visuals like skin tone and gender can still be discerned (Zhong et al.,
048   2023; Dave et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022a).
049   Our research aims to address these challenges by demonstrating that using mesh bodies can effectively
050   remove biases and close the realism gap in action video recognition while preserving privacy. In
051   particular, we: (1) investigate whether meshes can preserve privacy while approximating real-world
052   data and reducing the realism gap; (2) explore the potential of using meshes to mitigate biases related
053   to background, scene-object interactions, race, and gender, examining whether they can serve as a
      standardization technique for the human form.

086   To do so, we introduced a unified framework, SMPLy Private, that combines mesh-based pretraining
087   and supervised label-alignment stages to address privacy concerns and maximize action recognition
088   performance. Unlike prior methods, our approach integrates mesh representations into both pretrain-
089   ing and alignment, ensuring that privacy-preserving augmented features are fully leveraged across
090   training phases. Specifically:
091   * Pre-training: We use mesh-based augmented data for masked autoencoder (MAE) (He et al., 2021)

093   dynamics.

095   ensure consistency and maximize transferability to downstream tasks.

098
      training phases, addressing the limitations of prior approaches that treat pre-training and alignment as
      a slightly more disjoint processes.
099
100   Our approach is motivated by the premise that mesh bodies can replace humans in video data,
101   maintaining privacy. However, to illustrate our interest in exploring the impact of mesh bodies on
102   mitigating gender and race biases, consider an example. Video footage from the National Basketball
103   Association (NBA) in the 1980s-1990s, predominantly features African-American players. Training
104
      models on these videos to predict contemporary players, who are more diverse, could introduce bias
      by predominantly associating basketball with African-American individuals. This concern extends to
105
      gender bias as well (Burns et al., 2019). Employing meshes could standardize player representation
106
      across different demographics, thereby reducing biases and promoting ethical model development.

124   2    R ELATED W ORKS
125
126
      Preservation of Privacy in Action Recognition. Anonymization techniques, such as blurring, down-
127   sampling, adversarial augmentations, masking faces and other identifiable features, are commonly
128   adapted strategies to maintain human confidentiality (Dai et al., 2015; Butler et al., 2015; Piergiovanni
129   & Ryoo, 2020; Zhang et al., 2021; Dave et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2020; Pittaluga et al., 2019) but can
130   be left susceptible to revealing an individual’s identity based on characteristics such as color and size
131   (Yang et al., 2022; Oh et al., 2016). We eliminate the human form and replace it with 3D meshes,
132   thus curating training data that does not have explicitly identifiable forms or features of the original
133   human.
134   Body Mesh Recovery. Estimating 3D human body poses and shapes is a complex challenge addressed
135   by various methods. SMPL-X uses a unified 3D model trained on extensive 3D scans, providing
136   detailed and realistic representations. HybrIK-X (Li et al., 2023) effectively combines 3D keypoint
137   estimation with body mesh reconstruction by converting precise 3D joint locations into relative
138   body-part rotations. However, its application is limited to single individuals, which is not suitable
139   for videos featuring multiple people. In contrast, our approach utilizes OSX (Lin et al., 2023),
140   which excels in multi-human mesh recovery. OSX employs a unified encoder-decoder architecture
141
      integrated with a component-aware transformer. This setup not only predicts body parameters but
      also enhances segmentation, crucial for accurate face and hand estimation. By eliminating the need
142
      for separate networks and manual post-processing, OSX provides more natural and plausible 3D
143
      meshes. Given its simplicity and effectiveness in handling complex scenes with multiple individuals,
144   OSX is our chosen method for accurate human body mesh recovery for M2M.
145
146   Biases and Synthetic Data. Object-scene bias in video action recognition refers to the tendency
147
      of models to rely on static objects or backgrounds rather than the dynamic actions themselves for
      classification (Yun et al., 2020). To address this issue, various augmentation strategies such as loss
148
      augmentation (Choi et al., 2019), action-scene swapping (Zou et al., 2022), and video compositing
149
      (Gowda et al., 2022) are prevalent but not privacy-preserving. One method mitigates this bias by first
150   learning background information from real data and then temporal information from entirely synthetic
151   data rather than augmenting components of the actual video (Zhong et al., 2023). Although this
152   approach is privacy-preserving and focuses on learning background and actions, thereby addressing
153   object-scene bias, the use of synthetic videos still leaves a desire for the realism gap to be bridged
154   (Friedman et al., 2023). Instead, our approach returns to augmenting real videos by masking,
155   in-painting, and overlaying appropriate mesh bodies in place of the original human. The plain
156   “mannequin-like” mesh bodies remove any discriminatory bias, unlike the synthetic “video-game-like”
157   humans, which tend to have features such as hair color, gender, skin tone, etc.
158   Self-Supervised Pretraining in Action Recognition. The training scheme for action recognition
159   models is crucial to the performance on downstream tasks, given that most data in nature is unlabeled.
160   Self-supervised learning (SSL) has proven to be a powerful pretraining mechanism in such schemes
161   (Balestriero et al., 2023). Furthermore, the default choice of encoder has shifted from convolutional
      neural networks (CNNs) like temporal segment networks (TSNs) (Wang et al., 2016) and inflated


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      3D convolutional networks (I3Ds) (Carreira & Zisserman, 2018) to vision transformers (ViTs)
163   (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), as they effectively process frames as patch sequences, enabling the capture
164   of long-range dependencies and patterns in videos. SSL is typically categorized into four paradigms:
165   deep metric learning (Chen et al., 2020; Dwibedi et al., 2021; Koohpayegani et al., 2020; Khosla et al.,
166   2020), self-distillation (Grill et al., 2020; Chen & He, 2021; Caron et al., 2021), canonical correlation
167   analysis (Bardes et al., 2021; Zbontar et al., 2021; Caron et al., 2020; Ermolov et al., 2021), and
168   masked image modeling (He et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2022; Chang et al., 2023). We chose the latter
169   paradigm, employing a masked autoencoder (MAE) training scheme, specifically using VideoMAE
170   (Wang et al., 2022), which typically incorporates the base vision transformer architecture (ViT-B)
171
      and has previously achieved state-of-the-art performance on benchmarks like UCF101 (Soomro et al.,
      2012) and Kinetics (Kay et al., 2017).

176   3.1   T RANSITIONING FROM S YNTHETIC TO AUGMENTED
177
178   SMPLy Private marks a significant transition over current state-of-the-art approaches like SynAPT and
179   PPMA, which rely predominantly on fully synthetic data for privacy preservation (Kim et al., 2022;
180   Zhong et al., 2023). PPMA also follows a two-stage process: MAE pre-training on human-removed
181   data, then supervised alignment using synthetic and human-removed datasets. However, its reliance on
182   video game-like synthetic datasets (i.e, SURREACT, PHAV, ElderSim (Varol et al., 2021; De Souza
183
      et al., 2017; Hwang et al., 2023)), which modify entire scenes and objects alongside subjects, limits
      their ability to capture nuanced, real-world contextual features present in action recognition datasets
184
      like Kinetics (Jordon et al., 2024; Hao et al., 2024), thereby hindering downstream transferability.
185
      M2M overcomes this limitation by employing mesh-based augmentation to synthesize privacy-
186   preserving action data while retaining real-world scenes and objects. Instead of creating entirely
187   new environments, M2M overlays parametric human motion meshes over removed human subjects,
188   preserving contextual and environmental nuances while obfuscating identifiable human features.
189   This approach bridges the realism gap, yielding richer temporal and contextual representations
190   without compromising privacy. The core distinction lies in data treatment. While PPMA integrates
191   synthetic and human-obfuscated real data to enhance temporal and contextual understanding, its
192   dependency on synthetic datasets introduces domain shifts away from real-world distributions. In
193   contrast, M2M maintains real-world contexts by embedding privacy-preserving meshes, achieving
194
      balanced representation. Furthermore, M2M extends alignment by integrating mesh representations
      directly into pretraining, seamlessly incorporating temporal and contextual features for improved
195
      downstream transferability.

      3.3   M ASK 2M ESH AUGMENTATION
237
238   Our proposed M2M augmentation framework is designed to achieve privacy-preserving video data
239
      by replacing real humans in videos with 3D mesh representations while preserving essential motion
      details. The framework consists of two main modules: (1) the masking and inpainting module; (2)
240
      the body mesh recovery module. We leverage the Kinetics-150 dataset curated in the aforementioned
241
      Section 3.2, resizing all video clips to 432 × 240 to standardize the input data. The first step in our
242   framework involves detecting and removing human figures from the video frames. This process is
243   divided into two sub-tasks: human detection and in-painting. We utilize the Segment Anything Model
244   (SAM) (Kirillov et al., 2023), to generate masks for human figures in each video frame. The generated
245   masks are passed on to the subsequent inpainting module, which involves filling the regions occupied
246   by human figures with plausible background content. We employ E2 FGVI (Li et al., 2022b), an
247   optical flow-based inpainting method. E2 FGVI leverages temporal coherence and spatial context to
248   generate high-quality inpainted frames, effectively removing humans while maintaining the integrity
249   of the background.
250   After removing humans from the video frames, we focus on reconstructing human motion using 3D
251   mesh models. The body mesh recovery module processes the resized videos to extract detailed 3D
252   meshes of the human figures. We use the SMPL-X model for its comprehensive representation of the
253   body, hands, and facial expressions, and the OSX algorithm for mesh recovery due to its multi-human
254   support. The 3D meshes are then superimposed onto the inpainted frames, replacing real humans
255   while retaining essential motion cues for accurate action recognition and anonymization. We also
256
      address occlusion challenges (Appendix B.2: Figure 7) involving peripheral objects in the scene. The
      augmentation framework (Figure 2) preserves objects typically occluded (on-body and peripheral) by
257
      the recovered mesh while maintaining privacy, handling erroneous occlusions and object integrity in
258
      pose estimation as follows:
259
260   (1) Segment the input image to obtain masks for all objects, including the human subject.
261   (2) Use the human mask to inpaint and remove the subject, creating a clean background.
262
263
      (3) Do mesh recovery on the original image and overlay the mesh onto the inpainted background.
264   (4) Extract objects from the original image using their masks and overlay the composite image.

      3.4   T RAINING P ROCEDURE
291
292
      Pre-training is vital in video action recognition, enabling models to learn generalized spatial and
293   temporal features from large, diverse datasets. This foundation enhances the model’s ability to
294   recognize complex actions with less labeled data and improves performance and efficiency in
295   downstream tasks (Schiappa et al., 2023). Our training procedure consists of two stages: (1) self-
296   supervised pre-training utilizing VideoMAE; (2) supervised pre-training to ensure label alignment.
297
      Step 1: Video Masked Autoencoder for Self-Supervised Pre-training. We employ the traditional
298
      training methodology for MAEs (He et al., 2021) tailored explicitly for video data. This entails a
299   configuration consisting of an encoder and a decoder, in which the model acquires the ability to
300   approximate masked pixel values within video frames. During this stage, the encoder and decoder
301   undergo joint training. Once the model is sufficiently trained, the decoder is removed, leaving only
302   the encoder.
303
      Step 2: Supervised Pre-training for Alignment of Labels. The pre-trained VideoMAE encoder is
304
      then augmented with a linear classification head. The encoder and the linear classifier are trained in
305   tandem, using the action labels for supervision.
306
307   Putting it All Together. M2M serves as the basis of our data preparation process across both the
308
      pretraining and alignment phases. During pretraining, M2M-augmented videos replace the original
      dataset, ensuring privacy-preserving inputs to the VideoMAE framework. This augmentation involves
309
      detecting human subjects, masking them, inpainting the occluded regions, and superimposing meshes.
310
      The resulting anonymized dataset allows VideoMAE to learn spatiotemporal representations in a
311   self-supervised manner. During alignment, the same M2M pipeline is employed to prepare training
312   data, ensuring the VideoMAE encoder aligns with action recognition labels. This two-step process
313   ensures that the M2M augmentation is central to both stages of training, as summarized in Table 1.

514   5     C ONCLUSION
515
516   This study presents M2M, a framework that replaces humans in videos with detailed 3D SMPL-X
517   meshes, ensuring privacy while maintaining action recognition accuracy. By augmenting real data,
518   M2M avoids synthetic biases Kim et al. (2022). Models trained on M2M-augmented data outperform
519
      privacy-preserving benchmarks and rival non-privacy-preserving ones. A gender representation
      analysis shows gender-neutral meshes improve performance in biased classes. Additionally, M2M-
520
      augmented data accelerates representation learning, bridging the realism gap and standardizing action
521
      recognition datasets.
522
523   Limitations & Future Work. We perform image-instance segmentation without considering tempo-
524   ral relationships between frames. However, given that our data is video-based, an optimal approach
525
      would involve video-instance segmentation. This would likely yield more accurate masks and im-
      proved performance, mitigating issues such as transient mesh disappearances. Lastly, our present
526
      method focuses on body mesh recovery for individual video frames, whereas this process could be
527
      extended to entire videos to incorporate temporal relations. Most contemporary approaches to body
528   mesh recovery leverage 3D pose estimation from videos, a complex problem (Needham et al., 2021).
529   Accurately recovering 3D meshes in videos is challenging but could significantly reduce glitches
530   and enhance performance. We can better capture and use temporal information by replacing frame-
531   by-frame processing with video-level analysis. Lastly, we acknowledge the potential of the MANO
532   model (Romero et al., 2017), which is tailored for hand-specific tasks in egocentric datasets such
533   as Something-Something (Goyal et al., 2017) and EPIC-KITCHENS (Damen et al., 2018). These
534   datasets primarily involve hand and wrist actions, presenting opportunities for further exploration.
535   Ethical Implications. By replacing real individuals in video footage with 3D meshes, M2M addresses
536   privacy concerns and aligns with regulations like GDPR (Union, 2016) and ADPPA (Congress, 2022).
537   It mitigates risks of identity theft and privacy invasion, while reducing biases related to race and
538   gender. However, its capability to generate highly realistic video data could also be repurposed for
539   more invasive surveillance systems, enhancing monitoring capabilities in workplaces or public areas
      and infringing on individual privacy and autonomy.

818   all experiments, we employ the ViT-B backbone and to uphold rigorous privacy preservation, we
819   develop conduct training without pre-trained weights from ImageNet. We pre-train VideoMAE for
820   200 epochs using a tube masking technique that masks 90% of image patches to enhance learned
821   video representations. After pre-training, we remove the VideoMAE decoder, retaining only the
822   encoder. For label alignment, we conduct supervised pre-training for 50 epochs using the same subset
823
      of 150 Kinetics classes as SynAPT (Kim et al., 2022). For downstream evaluation, we fine-tune (FT)

      W as the video input is resized to 224 × 224 and normalized.

       The male, female, and neutral meshes differ primarily in their body shape and proportions, which are

1092   shapes, enabling its use when the gender is unknown or ambiguous.
