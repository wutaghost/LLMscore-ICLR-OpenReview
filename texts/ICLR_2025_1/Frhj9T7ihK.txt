                                                   A BSTRACT
013
014
               Recent advances in machine learning have enabled AI applications in mental dis-
015
               order diagnosis, but many methods remain black-box or rely on post-hoc explana-
016            tions which are not straightforward or actionable for mental health practitioners.
017            Meanwhile, interpretable methods, such as k-nearest neighbors (k-NN) classifi-
018            cation, struggle with complex or high-dimensional data. A network-based k-NN
019            model (NN-kNN) combines the interpretability with the predictive power of neu-
020            ral networks. The model prediction can be fully explained in terms of activated
021            features and neighboring cases. We experimented with the model to predict the
022            risks of depression and interviewed practitioners. The feedback of the practi-
023            tioners emphasized the model’s adaptability, integration of clinical expertise, and
024
               transparency in the diagnostic process, highlighting its potential to ethically im-
               prove the diagnostic precision and confidence of the practitioner.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      The booming era of Artificial Intelligence sees a rise of its application to mental health-related issues
030   (Graham et al., 2019) and specifically to mental disorder diagnosis (“diagnosis” for short) (Bzdok
031   & Meyer-Lindenberg, 2018; Chattopadhyay, 2017; Graham et al., 2019; Iyortsuun et al., 2023).
032   Although various AI technologies have achieved high accuracies in diagnosis, they generally lack
033   explanation for their decision making, and the process remains a black box for both mental health
034   practitioners (“practitioners” for short) and clients seeking counseling services (“clients” for short)
035   (Jarvie & Lindén, 2024; Lau et al., 2023). Therefore, there is still much caution and concern about
036   the use of AI for diagnosis (Kerz et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024).
037   This gives rise to recent trends in using Explainable AI (XAI) technologies for mental health: Prac-
038   titioners and clients may rely on AI tools “to the extent they can economise on human oversight,
039   monitoring and verification of the system’s outputs” (Joyce et al., 2023). Most XAI methods are
040   based on post-hoc explanation methods such as SHAP (Shapley, 1953) and LIME (Ribeiro et al.,
041   2016). However, Rudin (2019) argues that post-hoc explanations are often inadequate for black-box
042   models. The explanations may justify the model’s decision after the decision is made, but not how
043   the model reached the decision internally. In the worst cases, post-hoc explanations are excuses for
044
      a model’s mistakes and offer no opportunity to debug or fine-tune a trained black-box model.
045   To address the interpretability and adjustability of AI models that facilitate practitioners in diagnosis,
046   we propose to use a recently invented model, a neural network based k-nearest-neighbor algorithm
047   (NN-kNN). As a k-nearest neighbor algorithm, NN-kNN can explain each model decision with
048   activated cases, and each activated case can be attributed to its feature distances with the query. As a
049   neural network, NN-kNN allows end-to-end training for both feature weights and case weights, and
050
      is compatible with other neural network methods (Ye et al., 2024).
051   Our study introduces a novel approach to human-machine interaction drawing on insights and
052   methodology from both AI and psychology. Specifically, we study how NN-kNN facilitates prac-
053   titioners in aspects beyond traditional diagnosis, including feature exploration, explanation of deci-
      sions, past case retrieval, and manual weight adjustment. We conducted a qualitative study, using

      2     N EURAL N ETWORK BASED K-N EAREST N EIGHBORS

      2.1   K-N EAREST N EIGHBORS C LASSIFIERS
068   K-nearest neighbors classifiers (k-NN) examine the task domains involving cases C in the form
069   of (x, Lx ). For each case x ∈ C, f (x) =< x1 , x2 , ..., xm > is a feature vector describing a
070   client’s information (survey answers, medical record information, etc.) and Lx is the diagnosis label
071   associated with the client (risk of depression). The function f might describe a feature extraction
072   method or simply use the surface features of x. A naive k-NN calculates the distance between two
073   cases as the sum of distances between corresponding features, using a simple Minkowski distance
074   measure such as the Euclidean distance (Dasarathy, 1991). Better k-NN methods use feature weights
075   in the calculation of distance. Traditional methods use a global feature weighting, while others allow
076
      certain sets of cases (or even each individual case) to have their own feature weighting (Manzali
      et al., 2024; Aha & Goldstone, 1992; Friedman, 1994; Ricci & Avesani, 1995; Marchiori, 2013;
077
      Bonzano et al., 1997). Some methods assign case weights, so that certain cases contribute more in
078
      the voting of the final prediction (Bicego & Loog, 2016; Aguilera et al., 2019). The case weights
079   may be based on the distance between the case and the query, or it may be a learned parameter
080   of the case. Other methods such as neighborhood components analysis (NCA) and large margin
081   nearest neighbor (LMNN) transform the feature space to extract high-level features before distance
082   calculation (Goldberger et al., 2004; Weinberger & Saul, 2009).
083

      3.1   E XPLAINABLE AND I NTERPRETABLE AI
143
144   According to the survey by Joyce et al. (2023), the majority of XAI methods in mental health are
145   based on feature importance methods such as SHAP (Shapley, 1953). Feature importance methods
146   estimate the weights of features by measuring the influence on the model’s output after perturbing a
147   feature. Only two methods in the survey are regression-based and interpretable by design. Similarly,
148   most studies in the wider field of XAI are post-hoc methods because they are flexible and easily
149   applicable to different models (Saleem et al., 2022). Post-hoc methods build an interpretable model
150   that mimics the behavior of a black-box model and use this new model as an explanation. However,
151   post-hoc explanations are problematic for high-stakes decision making in mental health applications:
152
      (1) post-hoc explanations may not faithfully represent the original model’s computations, (2) they
      may lack the detail necessary to fully understand the black-box model, and (3) they do not permit
153
      manual calibration by domain experts (Rudin, 2019).
154
155   Our XAI method is interpretable by design and explains decisions using features and cases. This
156   is similar to the explanation done in case-based reasoning (CBR) (Schoenborn et al., 2021; Gates
157   & Leake, 2021), where decisions on queries are explained by past cases similar to queries. Most
158
      CBR systems weight features (Wettschereck et al., 1997), while fewer weight cases (Bicego & Loog,
      2016). NN-kNN takes the extra step to train both feature weights and case weights at the same time
159
      in an end-to-end manner.
160
161   While most modern machine learning methods learn parameters through training, some interpretable
      AI systems allow parameters to be set using expert knowledge or external methods. For example, in


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      k-NN, feature or case weights can be determined using mutual information (Garcı́a-Laencina et al.,
163   2009), the analytic hierarchy process (Bhattacharya et al., 2017), or fuzzy membership functions
164   (Biswas et al., 2018). NN-kNN offers a hybrid approach: feature and case weights are initially
165   learned by the network, then experts can fine-tune them for retraining.

      3.2   M EMORY AUGMENTED N ETWORKS
168
169
      Weston et al. (2014) proposed the class of memory networks. A memory network consists of a
170   memory m and four components: an input feature map I that extracts features of the query, a gener-
171   alization process G that updates memory given the query, an output feature map O that produces the
172   output using the query and the memory, and lastly a response R converts output to desired format.
173   They implemented a memory network for question answering purpose. In their example, the query
174   is a textual question, m stores texts, O finds k supporting memories and produces an output feature,
175   and R produces a textual response using RNN on the output feature.
176   Matching networks (Vinyals et al., 2016) extend the memory networks for one-shot learning. The
177   authors do so by incorporating characteristics from non-parametric models, allowing a trained net-
178   work to be directly used on a new support set. Similarly, prototypical networks (Snell et al., 2017)
179   learn a metric space and perform classification by computing the distances between the query and
180   prototypes of each class. Li et al. (2018) propose a neural network model that stores auto-encoded
181   embeddings of learned prototypes and makes prediction by comparing the query embedding with
182   the prototype embeddings.
183   NN-kNN fits the class of memory networks: m is the case layer, I is the feature extraction layer,
184   G is the training of network parameters, and O is the prediction layer based on activated cases.
185   NN-kNN is similar to the matching networks and prototypical networks because the layers until the
186   target activation layer serve as a similarity metric for the cases. In fact, NN-kNN can be considered
187   as a generalization of the previous methods. It works with any case (not just prototypes) or any
188   feature (not just embeddings extracted by an autoencoder). Its k-NN nature allows easy insertion
189   and deletion of cases and easy explanation through activated cases and features.

191   3.3   M ENTAL H EALTH D IAGNOSIS BY P RACTITIONERS
192
193   The development of efficient diagnosis has stagnated for decades, facing several challenges:
194
195         1. Time-Consuming Diagnostic Processes: Clinicians continue to rely on diagnostic man-
196            uals such as the DSM-V and ICD-10. The time-consuming diagnostic process takes away
197            valuable time from direct therapeutic interventions (Perkins et al., 2018)
198         2. Insufficient Training Opportunities: Only 23% of American Psychological Association
199            (APA)-accredited doctoral programs provide trainee clinicians with the training sites nec-
200            essary for structured diagnostic interviews (Mihura et al., 2017).
201
            3. Inadequacy of Diagnostic Manuals: A systematic review involving 2,228 participants
202
               found that clinicians often view diagnostic manuals as unhelpful due to incomplete or in-
203
               accurate symptom descriptors (Perkins et al., 2018). They tend to focus on categorizing
204            symptoms rather than identifying the underlying formations of psychological symptoms.
205
206         4. High Rates of Misdiagnosis: In a study of 309 psychiatric patients, 39.16% patients with
207            severe psychiatric disorders were misdiagnosed, with schizoaffective disorder having the
208
               highest misdiagnosis rate (75%), followed by major depressive disorder (54.72%) (Ayano
               et al., 2021).
209
210         5. Comorbidity and Diagnostic Complexity: Mental health diagnoses are further compli-
211            cated by comorbid conditions. For instance, individuals with autism spectrum conditions
212            are more likely to be diagnosed with mental health disorders than non-autistic individuals,
213            increasing the potential for misdiagnosis (Au-Yeung et al., 2019).
214
215   Given these ongoing challenges, the field of health service psychology urgently requires a more
      effective and accurate diagnostic system to support clinicians in making better informed decisions.

      3.4   M ENTAL H EALTH D IAGNOSIS BY AI
217
218   Many studies have demonstrated the efficacy of AI-enhanced tools in supporting clinical diagnosis
219   Graham et al. (2019). For example, Zhang et al. (2022) conducted a comprehensive narrative review
220   of 399 studies on NLP applications in mental illness detection over the past decades. Their finding
221   suggested a significant upward trend in research focused on NLP for mental illness detection, with
222   deep learning approaches showing better performance than traditional machine learning methods.
223   In a recent study, Lau et al. (2023) examined deep learning models to automate depression severity
224
      assessment by using parameter efficient tuning techniques and pre-trained large language models.
      Their results suggested that prefix tuning allowed for more efficient model training to reduce over-
225
      fitting and offered a scalable solution of automatic depression assessment.
226
227   Despite the great promise of AI-assisted clinical diagnosis, both patients and clinicians remain hes-
228   itant to accept its application. In one study, patients have expressed a preference for AI tools that
229   are tailored, person-centered, and adaptable to their individual treatment plans and expressed con-
230
      cern that they must adapt to technology (Li et al., 2024). In addition, clinicians often hesitate to
      fully integrate AI technology into their practice due to concern about their understanding of how
231
      AI systems work (Kerz et al., 2023). As such, there is a need for transparency and explainability in
232
      AI systems to foster trust with mental health practitioners. However, among the mental health XAI
233   projects surveyed by Joyce et al. (2023), none interviewed specialists about their experience with the
234   XAI models. Surveys in the broader field of XAI (Saeed & Omlin, 2023; Das & Rad, 2020; Molnar
235   et al., 2020) also identified evaluating the explainability of XAI models as a major challenge. In
236   general, there is no agreed upon measure of explainability/interpretability.
237
      In this study, we embrace the idea that explainability is not a rigorous formal concept and that
238
      an explanation is only as good as the intended audience perceives it. We take a human-centered
239   approach to evaluate explainability in a qualitative approach that is more common in social science.

249   4.1   T HE DATASET
250
251   The model is trained on the dataset from Orozco-del Castillo et al. (2021). The original dataset is
252   answers to a survey of 117 items of true/false answers (see Appendix C). The survey was designed
253   for depression screening according to the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders
254   (American Psychiatric Association, 2013) and answered by 157 undergraduate students. After data
255   preprocessing (see Appendix D), our final dataset contains 117 cases; Each case has 102 features of
256
      binary values and a class label of 0, 1 or 2, representing low, medium and high risk.
257   In these experiments, NN-kNN achieves an average accuracy of 0.646, outperforming both standard
258   k-NN (0.417) and LMNN (0.492). We urge caution due to the small dataset size and the unstable
259   results. We did not test extensively on multiple datasets against various models, as previous research
260   (Ye et al., 2024) has already conducted such comparisons. The primary focus of this study is on the
261   interpretability and adjustability of the model for practical use by clinicians.
262
263   4.2   T HE I NTERPRETABILITY AND A DJUSTABILITY FOR P RACTITIONERS
264
      NN-kNN is both interpretable and transparent according to the Transparency and Interpretability for
265
      Understandability framework (Joyce et al., 2023). Specifically, when predicting depression risk, the
266   model’s design emphasizes the following key aspects:
267
268          • All cases share the same feature weights (wxδi = wyδi = wδi for any two cases x and y),
269            reducing the overall number of parameters. This global feature weighting approach assigns
               a single weight to each feature across all cases.


                                                        5

270
            • Initial settings for each case x include feature weights set to wxδi = 1, case bias at bx = 50,
271           case weight at w(x,L) = 1, and class biases at bL = 1. In this configuration, all features
272           and cases start with equal importance. Through training, the model adjusts these weights
273           to improve prediction accuracy. Practitioners can then examine the resulting weights to
274           determine which features or cases the model has prioritized.
275         • The calculations involved in feature distances, case activations, and class activations rely
276           on basic operations like subtractions and summations. This simplicity ensures that practi-
277           tioners can easily understand the model’s operations. In addition, they can choose to focus
278           on the most or least weighted features/cases for a quick overview of the model’s results.
279         • Each parameter in the model has an interpretable role. The feature weight wxδi and the case
280           weight w(x,L) respectively reflect the relevance of a feature/case to depression. The case
281           bias bx and the class bias bL respectively indicate the inherent importance of a case and a
282           class. Practitioners can easily identify outliers or particularly influential features/cases.
283         • Because each parameter has a specific semantic meaning and a preset initial value, practi-
284           tioners can manually adjust the feature or case weights based on their expert knowledge.
285           After making adjustments, the model can retrain to further refine the predictions. Practi-
286           tioners can then assess whether the updated model aligns better with their clinical expertise.
287           We demonstrated this functionality to practitioners and collected their feedback on the ad-
288           justable model.

290   4.3   Q UALITATIVE I NTERVIEW D ESIGN
291
292   The effectiveness of an explanation ultimately depends on how well it is understood and perceived
293
      by its intended audience. To better understand the experiences and perceptions of mental health
      practitioners, we conducted interviews with 10 licensed clinicians from the US using the interpre-
294
      tative phenomenological analysis (IPA) approach, a widely recognized qualitative research method
295
      (Eatough & Smith, 2017). While quantitative methods are prevalent in XAI research—often involv-
296   ing comparisons of models across datasets and using metrics such as prediction accuracy—we chose
297   a qualitative approach for several key reasons:
298
299         • Focusing on a specialized population:Our target audience consists of licensed clinicians,
300           a small and specialized group. A qualitative approach allows us to zoom in on this specific
301           population, gaining in-depth insights into their experiences with the model, which could be
302           missed in a broader, quantitative study.
303         • Tailored model demonstrations: Demonstrating our model to potential users individually
304           allows us to observe first-hand how each clinician interacts with the system. This person-
305           alized approach helps capture the intricacies of their responses, including any challenges
306           they face or specific features they find most valuable.
307         • Understanding nuanced reactions: AI models can evoke a range of responses, particu-
308           larly when introduced in sensitive fields such as mental health. A qualitative approach is
309           well suited to capturing these nuanced reactions, such as concerns about integrating AI into
310           clinical practice, the potential for AI to aid in diagnostic processes, or hesitations about in-
311
              terpretability. This method allows for a deeper understanding of users’ trust and confidence
              in the model (Maxwell, 2021).
312
313         • Building trust and bridging theory to practice: By engaging directly with clinicians
314           through interviews, we can address their concerns, answer questions, and foster trust in the
315           model’s practical use. This is a crucial step in translating theoretical AI advancements into
316
              real-world applications that mental health practitioners are willing to adopt.
317
      Four licensed psychologists at the doctorate level and five licensed clinicians at the master level
318   participated in the interviews, among which four identified as men and six as women. Individually,
319   we demonstrated our model in a Jupyter notebook through Zoom, then invited practitioners to adjust
320   the parameters based on their clinician judgements for depression, followed by 30 minutes devoted
321   for them to answer our qualitative questions. Sample interview questions include: “did you feel like
322   the model has become more useful clinically after you tuned the feature weight?” and “since our
323   model can detect bias, if the model’s explanations differ from your clinical judgements, what would
      you do?” (See Appendix E for a full list of questions).

518   6   C ONCLUSION
519
520   We propose NN-kNN for mental disorder diagnosis not as a solution to the inherent challenges of
521   diagnosis, nor solely for its predictive accuracy. All models, including NN-kNN, are biased because
522   they are at most as effective as the data they are trained on. What sets NN-kNN apart is its full
523
      interpretability and adjustability, enabling practitioners to detect and correct biases within both the
      model and the data. By offering the ability to adjust model parameters based on expert knowledge,
524
      NN-kNN empowers clinicians to make more informed and ethical decisions. Practitioners can man-
525
      ually adjust the model parameters, discover unforeseen patterns or biases, and decide when it is
526   appropriate to rely on AI in clinical settings.
527
528   Through our qualitative interviews, practitioners expressed appreciation for the model’s trans-
529
      parency, flexibility, and the way it integrates their clinical expertise into the diagnostic process.
      This balance of AI-driven insights and human judgment has the potential to build greater trust and
530
      utility in AI-based diagnostic tools. Looking ahead, we plan to extend NN-kNN by incorporating
531
      complex data from additional modalities, further supporting clinicians in delivering more holistic
532   and data-rich diagnoses.

      The dataset was used to train neural networks for depression screening and then later used for an
      explainable AI challenge in the Explainable AI Challenge at the 2022 International Conference on
881
      Case-Based Reasoning (Wilkerson et al., 2022). We obtained a version of the dataset with 104
882
      cases with 102 attributes and a risk score (from 1 to 5) calculated from the number of physical
883   symptoms related to depression. We oversampled less frequent classes to counteract class imbalance
884   and transformed risk scores into three classes (low, medium, and high risk of depression) following
885   the example of Wilkerson et al. (2022). Our final dataset contains 117 cases; Each case has 102
886   features of binary values and a class label of 0, 1 or 2, representing low, medium and high risk.
