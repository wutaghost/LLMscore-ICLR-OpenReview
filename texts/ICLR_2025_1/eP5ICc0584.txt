009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Extending multivariate time series forecasting to resource-limited devices is a crit-
012
               ical demand for real applications, especially with the advancements in IoT tech-
013
               nologies. A common scenario is where the variates are distributed vertically on
014            different devices and each device needs to do local forecasting. This paper studies
015            a resource-efficient solution for this scenario based on vertical federated learning
016            (VFL). Prior VFL frameworks are designed for situations where only one party
017            holds the labels and would struggle to meet the demand of the targeted scenario, as
018            storage resources usage would increase dramatically with the number of devices.
019            Going beyond VFL, we design multivariate vertical federated learning (MVFL) as
020            a novel federated learning framework, where we separate communication features
021            and local features in an embedded feature space. This design enables MVFL to
022
               utilize storage and communication resources more efficiently by eliminating the
               redundant models. MVFL outperforms VFL approaches in both efficiency and
023
               accuracy. On four real-world benchmarks, compared to VFL, when the storage
024
               resources are equally utilized, MVFL yields a 12.1% relative improvement on
025            loss with a 43% relative improvement on communication resources usage. Even
026            when both MVFL and VFL employ the same main model size, MVFL achieves a
027            75% reduction in storage resources compared to VFL while maintaining the loss
028            at the same level of VFL.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Time series forecasting on end devices is widely used in many aspects such as traffic or energy
035   planning, weather and disease propagation forecasting, and is gaining increasing importance with
036   the development of new methods such as digital twins (Fahim et al. (2022)), multi-modal models
037   (Hayat et al. (2022)), IoT technologies (Bauer et al. (2021)) and so on. In these real-world scenarios,
038   there is always a critical need to gather useful information from different devices in order to make
039
      predictions. The devices may hold distinct features and heterogeneous data (Lin et al. (2024), Yang
      et al. (2019a)). Furthermore, ensuring the privacy of each device is essential for security purposes
040
      (Tawalbeh et al. (2020)). In this paper, we focus on time series forecasting of multivariate data with
041
      the variates distributed across different devices. Recent studies has primarily employed the federated
042   learning (FL) method to to address privacy concerns (Gosselin et al. (2022)), with vertical federated
043   learning (VFL) emerging as a particularly effective method for handling vertically partitioned data
044   (Yang et al. (2019b)).
045
      However, the forecasting task becomes extremely challenging under the scenario where each de-
046
      vice is expected to make predictions on its own data: The VFL approach would demand significant
047
      computational and storage resources, as each device needs to build separate models for all other de-
048   vices (see Section 3). Compounding the issue, many devices in this targeted scenario are inherently
049   resource-limited due to factors such as low processing power, limited memory, and energy con-
050   straints (Imteaj et al. (2021)). These limitations arise from the need for devices to be cost-effective
051   and energy-efficient, which is particularly crucial in IoT applications where large numbers of de-
052   vices are deployed in the field. Consequently, this resource scarcity leads to challenges in handling
053   complex tasks like forecasting, as devices struggle to balance storage demands with their available
      resources.

054
      To address the aforementioned challenge, we focus on the useful information extracted from each
055   device. We notice that some information extracted from one device of the targeted scenario might be
056   beneficial for many other devices as well. For instance, in weather forecasting, a rising temperature
057   trend not only aids in predicting future temperatures but can also contribute to forecasting wind
058   direction and rainfall. Constructing separate models to extract similar information would thus be
059   inefficient. Therefore, in this paper, we seek a method to merge the separate models on a given
060   device in the VFL approach into one integrated model in order to save storage resources without
061   sacrificing accuracy. In typical VFL settings, the passive parties (devices without labels) send their
062   extracted information to the active party (the device with labels). For any device in our scenario,
063
      we try to extract communication features from an embedded feature space of the local model to
      facilitate interaction with other devices..
064
065   Based on the above motivations, we propose an original Multivariate Vertical Federated Learn-
066   ing (MVFL) in place of VFL for forecasts of multivariate time series with the variables verically
067   distributed across different devices. MVFL retains the core principles of VFL but refines the overall
068   architecture into a more compact and efficient structure. This design allows each device to main-
069
      tain only one model for the prediction tasks. MVFL also introduces the concepts of communication
      features and local features which allows us to distinguish between information that is valuable for
070
      communication with other devices and information that is useful solely for the local device. MVFL
071
      has been demonstrated to achieve lower loss and improved efficiency in storage and communication
072   resources compared to the traditional VFL approach. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
073
074          • We identify the challenges that classical federated learning approaches face in a new re-
075            search scenario of multivariate time series forecasting, where the variables of the dataset
076            are vertically distributed across different devices, and each device must predict its own
077            future data.
078          • We propose MVFL with a compact structure and the separation of communication features
079            and local features. We adopt an approach a posteriori rather than a priori, meaning the
080            features evolve throughout the training process rather than being predefined.
081          • Across four real-world benchmarks, MVFL achieves a loss that is 87.9% of that of VFL
082            with a 83% storage resources usage and a 57% communication resources usage. In more
083            extreme situations, while maintaining the loss at the same level, MVFL could reduce the
084            storage resources usage to only 25% of that of VFL.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS

      2.1   F EDERATED LEARNING & VERTICAL FEDERATED LEARNING
089
090   Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a powerful paradigm to enable decentralized machine learn-
091   ing, where devices collaboratively train a model without sharing raw data, thereby preserving privacy
092   (Mammen (2021)). Early foundational works (McMahan et al. (2017)) introduced a framework that
093   reduces communication costs by averaging model updates across devices. Since then, FL has been
094   extensively applied to domains where data privacy is crucial (Truex et al. (2019), Mothukuri et al.
095   (2021)), including healthcare, financial services, and IoT (Kairouz et al. (2021), Bonawitz (2019)).
096   However, traditional FL techniques primarily assume horizontally partitioned data, where different
097   devices hold different samples of the same features.
098   There are FL approaches concentrating on multi-modality (Che et al. (2023), Peng et al. (2024),
099   Sundar et al. (2024)) that could provide useful insights about vertically distributed data. However,
100   For all horizontal FL-based approaches, once all the models have finished training, they could do
101   their tasks solely relying on local data and model while in the targeted scenario, a device would need
102   the simultaneous data (or at least the extracted information of the data) from other devices in order
103   to perform local forecasting.
104   In contrast, Vertical Federated Learning (VFL) is designed for scenarios where different parties hold
105   different features for the same set of data instances (Romanini et al. (2021), Wu et al. (2020), Gu et al.
106   (2021)). VFL has proved to be effective in domains like finance and healthcare, where features are
107   naturally distributed across different organizations (Liu et al. (2024), Chen et al. (2022)). Yang et al.
      (2019b) presented a comprehensive overview of VFL methodologies and challenges, highlighting


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      issues such as communication efficiency, model design, and privacy concerns when multiple parties
109   collaborate under a vertically partitioned data framework.
110
111   Nevertheless, current VFL frameworks are primarily designed for scenarios where only one party
112
      holds the labels (Chen et al. (2020), Zhang et al. (2021), Gu et al. (2020)), incompetent when dealing
      with the targeted scenario. Xia et al. (2021) is the only work we could find that deals with a similar
113
      scenario with the targeted scenario, where labels are horizontally partitioned and the parties only
114
      hold partial labels. However, in the targeted scenario, both labels and data are vertically distributed.
115   Besides, in Xia et al. (2021), there is still the distinction of active parties, passive parties and a
116   collaborator, since the framework isn’t specially designed for time series forecasting tasks. As a
117   result, none of the parties are equipped with sufficient labels to perform a complete local training,
118   which is not the case in the targeted scenario, as local future data are natural labels for time series
119   forecasting tasks.

121   2.2   M ULTIVARIATE TIME SERIES FORECASTING
122
123   Multivariate time series forecasting is becoming increasingly important in IoT contexts (Papaste-
124   fanopoulos et al. (2023)), where devices handle tasks like predicting energy consumption, traf-
125
      fic flow, and weather patterns (Papastefanopoulos et al. (2023), Han et al. (2021), Bitencourt &
      Guimarães (2021)). Recent approaches include transformer-based models such as Zhou et al. (2021)
126
      and other approaches (Wu et al. (2021), Yu et al. (2024), Oreshkin et al. (2019)) that further inte-
127
      grates decomposition methods. However, many of these devices are resource-limited, with con-
128   straints on processing power, memory, and energy (Imteaj et al. (2021)). Federated Learning (FL)
129   has been applied to IoT systems to mitigate the challenges of decentralized data processing (Imteaj
130   et al. (2022)). Specially, Chen et al. (2023) introduces a prompt learning mechanism to accom-
131   modate the communication and computational constraints of low-resource sensors. However, most
132   researches has not adequately addressed how to handle multivariate forecasting with vertically dis-
133   tributed variates.
134   Efforts have been made to reduce FL’s resource footprint (Imteaj & Amini (2021), Zakariyya et al.
135   (2022), Shen et al. (2020)), but those methods do not address the redundancy issues inherent in
136   VFL (see Section 3) for large-scale multivariate time series forecasting with vertically partitioned
137   data, where each device needs to build separate models for any other variate. The lack of research
138   addressing these specific challenges represents a critical gap in the literature.
139
      To the best of our knowledge, our study addresses this gap by proposing a novel framework,
140   Multivariate Vertical Federated Learning (MVFL), which is tailored for the targeted scenario.
141   Unlike previous methods, MVFL improves both storage efficiency and forecasting accuracy by elim-
142   inating redundant models and efficiently utilizing device resources.

280   4.3   S EPARATION OF COMMUNICATION AND LOCAL FEATURES
281
282   The key insight behind this solution is that among all the features extracted by the (n − 1) exchange
283
      models in the classical VFL solution, some features may be redundant. Besides, a feature extracted
      from a specific variate that is useful for predicting its own future data can also be beneficial for
284
      forecasting other variates. Therefore, maintaining separate exchange models can be inefficient. In-
285
      stead, we should directly examine the embedded feature space of the local main model to identify
286   the features that are useful for forecasting the variates on other devices while retaining the remaining
287   features for local use.
288
289

290

303
304   to compare the communication resources usage of VFL and MVFL, we could fix the size of commu-
305   nication features of any client of MVFL as e. Meanwhile, for VFL, we fix the size of the exchanged
306   features of any client to any other client also as e.
307   In MVFL framework, for every round of training, for a client i, what are communicated with the
308   server include:
309
310          • What are sent to the server: the communication features Ci obtained from local model of
311            size e and the gradients gio for the communication features of other clients of size e·(n−1).
312          • What are received from the server: The communication features form all other clients
313            Ci,other of size e · (n − 1) and the gradients for own communication features gic of size e.

315   The overall communication resource usage of MVFL is thus of size 2n · e.

               e · (n − 1).

335   5.1       DATASETS
336
337   The datasets that we use are ETTm, ETTh, weather and exchange. All are multivariate datasets.
338   ETTm and ETTh each has 7 variates. The weather dataset has 21 variates, the exchange dataset has
339   8 variates. All data are normalized using a standard scaler which ensures the mean value of a variate
340   is 0 and the standard deviation is 1.
341   Here is a brief description (Wu et al. (2021)) of the datasets used: (1) ETT (Zhou et al. (2021))
342   dataset contains the data collected from electricity transformers, including load and oil temperature
343   between July 2016 and July 2018. For ETTh, the data are recorded every 1 hour. For ETTm, the
344   data are recorded every 15 minutes. (2) weather1 is recorded every 10 minutes for 2023 whole year,
345   which contains 21 meteorological indicators, such as air temperature, humidity, etc. (3) exchange
346   (Lai et al. (2018)) records the daily exchange rates of eight different countries ranging from 1990 to
347
      2016.
348
349   5.2       I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
350
      Our models are trained with the L2 loss (l(a, b) = (a − b)2 ), using the ADAM optimizer with an
351
      initial learning rate of 0.001. Batch size is set to 32. The training process has its learning rate
352   decayed if there is a stagnation of loss. All experiments are implemented in PyTorch and conducted
353   on a single NVIDIA GPUs. We use Mean Squared Error (MSE) as the core metric to compare
354   performances.
355
      Both the MVFL model and the VFL main model contain 3 hidden layers. For MVFL, the 2nd hidden
356
      layer serves as the embedded feature space to obtain the communication features. To maximize
357
      control over variables and ensure best performances, for VFL, the exchange models all have 1 hidden
358   layer, and the exchanged features obtained from other devices are concatenated with own past data.
359   The storage resources usage are in KB and are obtained by saving all the models locally and adding
360   the sizes of the models saved. The communication resources usage are in bytes and are calculated
361   as shown in Section 4.4.

410   reduce the loss to 81% of that of VFL with only 21% of the storage resources usage under the
411   input-96 setting for the exchange dataset. Overall, MVFL yields a loss that is 87.9% of that of VFL
412   with a 83% storage resources usage and a 57% communication resources usage. Or in more extreme
413
      situations, MVFL could maintain the loss to 96% of that of VFL with only 25% storage resources
      usage and 57% communication resources usage.
414
415
416
      5.4   R ESULTS ANALYSIS
417
      The benefits of MVFL over VFL is firstly related to the number of devices involved in the process.
418   In a general case, VFL would require n − 1 times more storage resources usage compared to MVFL.
419   The more devices there are, the more storage resources are saved by MVFL.
420
421
      In addition, the exchange models of VFL take the same input size of the main local model. This
      means that the exchange model size m increase with the input size. As a result, MVFL is more
422
      advantageous when the input size is large. This could be verified by the experiment results: The
423
      storage results could be saved more when input size is 96 than when input size is 24 (case MVFL2),
424   and if we force the storage resources usage of MVFL to approach the storage resources usage of
425   VFL (case MVFL1), the loss of MVFL under input size 96 is much more advantageous.

475   6    C ONCLUSIONS
476
477
      In this work, we proposed a novel framework, Multivariate Vertical Federated Learning (MVFL)
478
      to address the challenges of resource-efficient multivariate time series forecasting on resource-
479
      constrained IoT devices. By revisiting traditional Vertical Federated Learning (VFL) in the tar-
480   geted scenario where each device must perform local forecasting with vertically partitioned data,
481   We identified its limitations with high storage space on local devices. Our proposed MVFL frame-
482   work separates communication features and local features in an embedded space, offering a more
483   efficient approach with better performances. This also prevents a rapid linear increase of model size
484   with the increase of devices. Future work may explore the adaptation of MVFL to more sophisti-
485   cated model structures or existing resource-efficient approaches and further optimize its performance
      under diverse conditions.
