009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable success, yet aligning
012            their generations with human preferences remains a critical challenge. Existing
013            approaches to preference modeling often rely on an explicit or implicit reward
014            function, overlooking the intricate and multifaceted nature of human preferences
015            that may encompass conflicting factors across diverse tasks and populations. To
016
               address this limitation, we introduce Latent Preference Coding (LPC), a novel
               framework that models the implicit factors as well as their combinations behind
017
               holistic preferences using discrete latent codes. LPC seamlessly integrates with
018
               various offline alignment algorithms, automatically inferring the underlying fac-
019            tors and their importance from data without relying on pre-defined reward func-
020            tions and hand-crafted combination weights. Extensive experiments on multi-
021            ple benchmarks demonstrate that LPC consistently improves upon three align-
022            ment algorithms (DPO, SimPO, and IPO) using three base models (Mistral-7B,
023            Llama3-8B, and Llama3-8B-Instruct). Furthermore, deeper analysis reveals that
024            the learned latent codes effectively capture the differences in the distribution of
025            human preferences and significantly enhance the robustness of alignment against
026            noise in data. By providing a unified representation for the multifarious prefer-
027
               ence factors, LPC paves the way towards developing more robust and versatile
               alignment techniques for the responsible deployment of powerful LLMs.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Alignment has emerged as a key step in the development of large language models (LLMs) (Ouyang
033   et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024). The goal of alignment is to
034   leverage human feedback to gauge the generative distributions of LLMs, steering their outputs to be
035   helpful, honest, and harmless (Askell et al., 2021). To this end, human annotators are tasked with ex-
036   pressing preferences among human-curated or machine-generated texts, and these preference anno-
037   tations serve as supervision signals to further optimize LLMs. Amid the surge in alignment research,
038   significant attention has been focused on optimization objectives, considering both online (Schulman
039
      et al., 2017; Munos et al., 2023; Calandriello et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024b) and offline (Rafailov
      et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023; Azar et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024) environments
040
      as well as different types of preference annotations, such as scalar ratings (Richemond et al., 2024)
041
      and pairwise rankings (Rafailov et al., 2024). Optimization with the well-designed objectives has
042   been widely validated for effectively reducing toxicity (Dai et al., 2024) while significantly improv-
043   ing the truthfulness and coherence of LLM outputs (Touvron et al., 2023). In this work, we study
044   the alignment of LLMs from a different perspective: Can we exploit the supervision signals more
045   effectively through fine-grained modeling of complex human preference?
046
      The common practice for preference modeling typically involves estimating a single reward func-
047
      tion from human annotations as a proxy for human preference (Schulman et al., 2017; Gulcehre
048   et al., 2023). Recently, human annotations have also been used directly as supervision signals in
049   optimization (Rafailov et al., 2024). These approaches, however, often overlook the challenges in
050   preference modeling that arise from the inherent complexity of human preference (Casper et al.,
051   2023): (1) Human preference may hinge on multiple factors. The multifaceted factors entailed
052   by a prompt may not be easily represented by a single reward function, especially when some factors
053   conflict with one another. A typical example is the divergence between “helpfuness” and “safty,”
      which differ dramatically in their preferred response patterns, making it difficult for a single reward

054
      model to achieve the best of both worlds (Mu et al., 2024). (2) The factors may vary across tasks
055   and populations. There lacks a unified way to represent all factors. For instance, in text generation,
056   pivotal factors that influence human preference may include informativeness, adherence to length
057   constraints, diversity of expressions, etc. In contrast, when solving math problems, correctness of
058   answers, rigor of reasoning, and clarity and conciseness of solutions could be more dominant. (3)
059   Accurately determining the relative weights of factors for a prompt is challenging, even if the
060   factors are well-defined. This is particularly significant when the weights are sensitive to nuances
061   in prompt expression. For example, the prompt “how can I kill a Python process” demands less
062   consideration on safety than “how can I kill someone” despite similar superficial phrasing.
063   In light of the challenges in preference modeling, we aim to develop a unified framework for cap-
064   turing the intricate nature of human preferences, with the goal of achieving (1) the framework can
065   broadly represent human preferences across diverse tasks; (2) the framework allows for automatic
066   learning of preference representations without the need for pre-defined sub-rewards and hand-crafted
067   weights that are required by many existing approaches (Zhou et al., 2024; Rame et al., 2024; Yang
068   et al., 2024c); and (3) the framework is generally applicable to various alignment algorithms and
069
      can effectively and consistently enhance their performance.
070   To this end, we propose Latent Preference Coding (LPC), a novel framework that captures the multi-
071   faceted nature of human preferences through discrete latent codes. LPC introduces a discrete latent
072   space where each code represents an underlying factor influencing holistic preferences. Through
073   variational inference, LPC estimates the latent codes from data, and learns both a prior network and
074   a posterior network. The posterior network infers weights of the latent codes from observed pref-
075
      erence annotations, while the prior network is trained to predict the inferred weights based on the
      input prompt. Together, the latent codes and the predicted combination weights form a mixture of
076
      factors that represent prompt-specified human preferences, guiding the generation of completions in
077
      LLMs1 . More importantly, the formulation of LPC is general, allowing for integration with a wide
078   range of offline preference algorithms, including DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024), SimPO (Meng et al.,
079   2024), IPO (Azar et al., 2024), and others.
080
081
      We conduct extensive experiments to assess LPC across diverse downstream tasks, employing
      Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023), Llama3-8B, and Llama3-8B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024) as base
082
      LLMs, paired with DPO, SimPO, and IPO as alignment algorithms. Evaluation results indicate
083
      that LPC consistently improves LLM performance across various combinations of base models and
084   alignment algorithms. More interestingly, further analysis over the learned latent codes reveals that
085   LPC effectively captures the underlying distribution of human preferences collected from different
086   data sources, and exhibits robustness against noisy annotations. These results confirm that LPC pro-
087   vides a unified approach for representing the complex structures underlying human preferences and
088   is readily applicable to a wide range of existing alignment algorithms.
089
      Our contributions are threefold: (1) We identify the critical challenge of modeling complex human
090   preferences in LLM alignment and propose Latent Preference Coding (LPC) to address the chal-
091   lenge through discrete latent variables; (2) We derive a tailored optimization objective under the
092   LPC framework, which can seamlessly integrate with and enhance the performance of various of-
093   fline preference learning algorithms; and (3) Extensive experiments on multiple benchmarks, using
094   various base LLMs and alignment algorithms, validate the consistent effectiveness of LPC over the
095   vanilla counterparts.

101   The inherent complexity of natural language has motivated the employment of latent variable mod-
102   els in natural language generation (NLG) tasks. These models capture the language characteristics
103   by learning latent variables that govern the generation process. A crucial aspect is the specifica-
104   tion of the posterior distribution over the latent variables. Continuous distributions, such as the

109   been widely adopted for modeling response diversity (Zhao et al., 2017; Ke et al., 2018). Recently,
110   discrete distributions have emerged as a promising alternative, offering several compelling advan-
111   tages, including mitigating the notorious posterior collapse issue (Bowman et al., 2016), enabling

      ploration of latent variable models, their applications to the alignment of LLMs remains largely
118
      unexplored. Our work represents a pioneering effort to leverage the expressive power of discrete
119
      latent variables for capturing multifaceted human preferences. While a concurrent study by Poddar
120   et al. (2024) employs continuous latent variables to represent various personalized needs, we aim to
121   model the intricate preferences obscured in the prompts through the more interpretable approach of
122   discrete latent variables.

125   2.2   L EARNING FROM H UMAN F EEDBACK
126
127   Learning from human feedback has been a crucial paradigm in aligning LLMs. Various forms of
128   feedback have been explored, including labels (Hastie et al., 2009), scalar ratings (Silver et al., 2021;
129
      Richemond et al., 2024), expert trajectories (Hussein et al., 2017), and pairwise rankings (Wirth
      et al., 2017; Rafailov et al., 2024), all of which can be viewed as carriers of underlying human
130
      preferences. Recently, reward modeling techniques, particularly those based on pairwise rankings,
131
      have emerged as a promising approach for providing scalable feedback, such as the Bradley-Terry
132   model (Bradley & Terry, 1952). Such reward models can then be leveraged to align LLMs with hu-
133   man preference through reinforcement learning algorithms like PPO (Schulman et al., 2017). This
134   has been applied to ensure safety (Dai et al., 2024), enhance helpfulness (Nakano et al., 2021),
135   and promote honesty (Tian et al., 2024) in LLMs. However, the complex implementation, hyper-
136   parameter tuning, sample inefficiency, and computational overhead of PPO (Choshen et al., 2020)
137   have motivated the exploration of simpler approaches, including rejection sampling (Touvron et al.,
138   2023) that fine-tunes LLMs on responses with the highest reward among a number of samples, and
139   direct preference optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) that directly optimizes LLMs from
140
      human preference data without an explicit reward model. Following DPO, various preference opti-
      mization objectives have been proposed, such as KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024), DRO (Richemond
141
      et al., 2024), SimPO (Meng et al., 2024), and GPO (Tang et al., 2024). Despite these advancements,
142
      a common limitation of existing methods is their assumption of a single, unified reward function,
143   which may fail to capture the multifaceted nature of human preferences.
144
145
146   2.3   M ULTI -O BJECITVE O PTIMIZATION
147
148   Multi-objective optimization for aligning LLMs has garnered significant attention, as it mitigates
149   potential dichotomies between competing objectives (Bai et al., 2022) and caters to diverse user
150   needs (Dong et al., 2023). Existing approaches to multi-objective alignment can be broadly cat-
151   egorized into three groups: (1) Reward Model Combination, which transforms multi-objective
152   alignment into a single-objective optimization problem by linearly combining rewards from individ-
153
      ual reward models (Wu et al., 2023) or via parameter interpolation (Rame et al., 2023). Then, they
      use standard RL approaches to maximize the scalar reward. (2) Policy Model Combination, which
154
      applies the spirit of linear combination to policy models, i.e., combining policy models learned
155
      from different reward models through token-wise probability interpolation (Jang et al., 2023). (3)
156   Combination-aware Learning, which trains a single policy model conditioned on both the user
157   instruction and the expected combination weights of different objectives (Dong et al., 2023; Wang
158   et al., 2024). All these methods require explicit human feedback for each objective and demand pre-
159   specified weights for combining multi-objective rewards, imposing a substantial burden on human
160   annotators. In contrast, our approach aims to automatically infer both the implicit factors and their
161   relative importance from holistic feedback data, without relying on pre-defined objective weights or
      explicit reward models.

      3.2   L EARNING O BJECTIVE OF L ATENT P REFERENCE C ODING
228
229   Recognizing the diverse and multifaceted nature of human preferences, our approach deviates from
230   traditional RLHF methods that rely on a single reward model rϕ (x, y) to evaluate all data in-
231   stances (either explicitly (Schulman et al., 2017) or implicitly (Rafailov et al., 2024)). Instead,
232   we aim to capture the factors that underpin intricate holistic human preferences. To this end, two
233   problems must be addressed: (1) How to model the mixture of factors implied by a prompt? And (2)
234   How to automatically and effectively learn the mixtures of factors from data in an unsupervised fash-
235   ion? To answer these questions, we propose latent preference coding (LPC) that implicitly models
236
      the underlying factors behind human preferences using latent variables.
237   We assume that holistic human preference is a mixture of multiple unobserved factors, and can be
238   modeled by a latent variable z. Hence, the preference model p(yw ≻ yl |x) in Eq. 5 can be factorized
239   as p(yw ≻ yl |z, x) · p(z|x), where p(z|x) is a prior modeling the induction of a mixture of factors
240   as a specific preference pattern with respect to prompt x, and p(yw ≻ yl |z, x) measures how yw is
241   preferred over yl under the prompt and the preference pattern. Following the assumption, the loss
242

344   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
345
346   Configuration. To comprehensively evaluate the efficacy of LPC, we conduct experiments us-
347   ing three open-source LLMs: Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023), Llama3-8B, and Llama3-8B-
348   Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024). Furthermore, to demonstrate the compatibility and flexibility of LPC,
349   we integrate it with three widely used offline preference learning algorithms: DPO, IPO, and SimPO.
350   These algorithms encompass different optimization strategies and inductive biases, enabling a com-
351
      prehensive evaluation of LPC’s performance across diverse preference learning methods. In total,
      we investigate nine distinct configurations, resulting from the combination of three base models and
352
      three preference learning algorithms. For each configuration, we compare against two baselines: the
353
      base model and its optimized version using the corresponding preference learning algorithm.
354
355   Dataset. We utilize the widely-adopted UltraFeedback dataset (Cui et al., 2023) in experiments.
356   The dataset is a comprehensive collection of user preferences spanning diverse domains. It contains
357   63,967 instances from 6 publicly available datasets, including TruthfulQA, FalseQA, Evol-Instruct,
358   UltraChat, ShareGPT, and FLAN. We randomly sample 1,000 instances for validation and an addi-
359   tional 1,000 instances for testing. The rest of the instances are used for training LPC and the baseline
360   alignment methods. We adopt the same data preprocessing pipeline as outlined in (Tunstall et al.,
361   2023) to construct the preference pairs. For each instance, four completions are generated by dif-
362   ferent LMs. The completion with the highest overall score is denoted as yw , while yl is randomly
363
      sampled from the remaining completions.

458   4.3   L ATENT C ODE A NALYSIS
459
460
      To gain deeper insights into the proposed LPC framework, we conduct experiments to unravel two
      pivotal research questions: (1) What is the optimal size of the latent codebook to effectively cap-
461
      ture the intricate landscape of human preferences? (2) Does LPC truly capture the implicit factors
462
      underpinning holistic preferences as hypothesized?
463
464
      Investigating the Optimal Codebook Size. To investigate the optimal codebook size, we train
465
      a series of models with distinct codebook sizes ranging from the set {8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256}. As
466   illustrated in Figure 2 (Top Left), the preference accuracy exhibits a distinct pattern: initially in-
467   creasing with larger codebook sizes, peaking around 32 to 64 codes, and then gradually declining,
468   suggesting that it is crucial for LPC’s performance to striking the right balance in the size of the
469   latent codebook. When the codebook is small (e.g., 8 codes), it may be insufficiently expressive
470   to capture the diversity of implicit preference factors, thereby limiting performance. Conversely,
471   when the codebook is excessively large (e.g., 256 codes), LPC does not appear to derive significant
472   benefits from an expanded latent space. This could be attributed to several factors: (1) the model
473   may struggle to effectively utilize such a high-dimensional latent space given the limited training
474
      data, or (2) the risk of overfitting increases as the codebook size grows.
475
476   Investigating the Capability to Distinguish Implicit Factors. The core rationale behind predict-
477
      ing implicit preference factors using the prior network p(z|x) lies in the assumption that the prompt
      x accurately reflects the underlying preference structure. To validate this critical assumption, we
478

479

481   ated with these flipped instances. Subsequently, we train Llama3-8B using DPO with or without
482   LPC on this distorted dataset to assess whether LPC can effectively differentiate between flipped
483   preferences and normal ones. Then, we calculate the preference accuracy on the original test set of
484   UltraFeedback. As illustrated in Figure 2 (Bottom Left), LPC outperforms the baseline DPO by a
485   larger margin than in the ordinary setting, indicating that in more complex preference environments
      with intermixed preferences—some of which are even completely opposite—LPC’s capability to

531   5     C ONCLUSIONS
532
533
      In this work, we propose LPC, a framework that enables LLMs to capture the multifaceted nature of
534   human preferences. LPC introduces discrete latent codes where each code represents an underlying
535   factor influencing holistic preferences. Through variational inference, LPC can model the implicit
536   factors without the need for fine-grained preference annotations. Besides, LPC can be integrated
537   with a variety of offline preference algorithms, including DPO, IPO, SimPO, and so on. We conduct
538   extensive experiments evaluating LPC on three open-source LLMs, showing that LLMs can achieve
539   better performance across multiple benchmarks by modeling the underlying factors of human pref-
      erence.
