009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Equalized odds, as a popular notion of algorithmic fairness, aims to ensure that sen-
012            sitive variables, such as race and gender, do not unfairly influence the algorithm’s
013            prediction when conditioning on the true outcome. Despite rapid advancements,
014            current research primarily focuses on equalized odds violations caused by a single
015            sensitive attribute, leaving the challenge of simultaneously accounting for multiple
016            attributes largely unaddressed. We bridge this gap by introducing an in-processing
017            fairness-aware learning approach, FairICP, which integrates adversarial learning
018
               with a novel inverse conditional permutation scheme. FairICP offers a theoretically
               justified, flexible, and efficient scheme to promote equalized odds under fairness
019
               conditions described by complex and multi-dimensional sensitive attributes. The
020
               efficacy and adaptability of our method are demonstrated through both simulation
021            studies and empirical analyses of real-world datasets.

      Machine learning models are increasingly important in aiding decision-making across various appli-
026
      cations. Ensuring fairness in these models—preventing discrimination against minorities or other
027   protected groups—remains a significant challenge (Mehrabi et al., 2021). To address different needs,
028   several fairness metrics have been developed in the literature (Mehrabi et al., 2021; Castelnovo et al.,
029   2022). Our work focuses on the equalized odds criterion (Hardt et al., 2016), defined as
030
031                                                  Ŷ ⊥
                                                        ⊥ A | Y.                                              (1)
032   Here, Y is the true outcome, A is the sensitive attribute(s) that we care to protect (e.g. gender/race/age),
033   and Ŷ is the prediction given by any model.
034
035   While fairness-aware machine learning has progressed rapidly, most existing algorithms targeting
036
      equalized odds can only handle a single protected attribute. However, real-world scenarios can
      involve biases arising from multiple sensitive attributes simultaneously. For example, in healthcare
037
      settings, patients’ outcomes might be influenced by a combination of race, gender, and age (Ghassemi
038
      et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022). Furthermore, ignoring the correlation between multiple sensitive
039   attributes can lead to fairness gerrymandering (Kearns et al., 2018), where a model appears fair when
040   considering each attribute separately but exhibits unfairness when attributes are considered jointly.
041
042
      To address these limitations, we introduce FairICP, a flexible fairness-aware learning scheme that
      encourages equalized odds for complex sensitive attributes. Our method leverages a novel Inverse
043
      Conditional Permutation (ICP) strategy to generate conditionally permuted copies Ã of sensitive
044
      attributes A given Y without the need to estimate the multi-dimensional conditional density and
045
      encourages equalized odds via enforcing similarity between (Ŷ , A, Y ) and (Ŷ , Ã, Y ). An illustration
046
      of the FairICP framework is provided in Figure 1.
047
048   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
049

        as conditional permutations of A given Y , without estimating the multi-dimensional conditional

        density of A|Y . This makes our method scalable and applicable to complex sensitive attributes.
052
053   • Theoretical Guarantees: We theoretically demonstrate that the equalized odds condition holds
        asymptotically for (Ŷ , Ã, Y ) when the Ã is generated by ICP. By combining ICP with adversarial

      Background and related work Fairness in machine learning has emerged as a critical area of
079   research, with various notions and approaches developed to address potential biases in algorithmic
080   decision-making. These fairness concepts can be broadly categorized into three main types: (1) group
081   fairness (Hardt et al., 2016), which aims to ensure equal treatment across different demographic
082   groups; (2) individual fairness (Dwork et al., 2012), focusing on similar predictions for similar
083   individuals; and (3) causality-based fairness (Kusner et al., 2017), which considers fairness in
084   counterfactual scenarios. Given a fairness condition, existing fair ML methods for encouraging it
085   can be classified into three approaches: pre-processing (Zemel et al., 2013; Feldman et al., 2015),
086   in-processing (Agarwal et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018), and post-processing (Hardt et al., 2016).
087   Our work focuses on in-processing learning for equalized odds fairness (Hardt et al., 2016), a group
088   fairness concept. Equalized odds requires that predictions are independent of sensitive attributes
089   conditional on the true outcome, unlike demographic parity (Zemel et al., 2013), which demands un-
090   conditional independence. The conditional nature of equalized odds makes it particularly challenging
091   when dealing with complex sensitive attributes that may be multidimensional and span categorical,
092   continuous, or mixed types. While several studies have successfully addressed demographic parity un-
093
      der multiple sensitive attributes (Kearns et al., 2018; Creager et al., 2019), existing work on equalized
      odds primarily considers one-dimensional sensitive attributes, with no prior work designed to handle
094
      multi-dimensional continuous or mixed-type sensitive attributes. For example, Mary et al. (2019)
095
      introduces a penalty term using the Hirschfeld-Gebelein-Rényi Maximum Correlation Coefficient
096   to accommodate for a continuous sensitive attribute in both regression and classification setting.
097   Another line of in-processing algorithms for equalized odds uses adversarial training for single
098   sensitive attribute (Zhang et al., 2018; Louppe et al., 2017; Romano et al., 2020). Our proposed
099   FairICP is the first in-processing framework specifically designed for equalized odds learning with
100   complex sensitive attributes.
101

104   for parametric or non-parametric conditional independence, measuring the degree of conditional
105   dependence for multi-dimensional variables remains challenging. We note recent progress in two
106   directions. One direction is the resampling-based approaches (Sen et al., 2017; Berrett et al., 2020;
107   Tansey et al., 2022). These methods allow flexible and adaptive construction of test statistics for
      comparisons. However, their accuracy heavily depends on generating accurate samples from the

176   2.2 FAIR ICP: FAIRNESS - AWARE LEARNING VIA INVERSE CONDITIONAL PERMUTATION
177   To circumvent the challenge in learning the conditional density of A given Y , we propose the Inverse
178   Conditional Permutation (ICP) sampling scheme, which leverages Conditional Permutation (CP)
179   (Berrett et al., 2020) but pivots to estimate Y given A, to generate a permuted version of Ã which is
180   guaranteed to satisfy the equalized odds defined in eq. (1) asymptotically.
181

188   permutation Π uniformly at random, CP assigns unequal sampling probability to permutations based

      The estimation of conditional densities is a crucial part of both our method and previous work (Berrett
310
      et al., 2020; Romano et al., 2020; Mary et al., 2019; Louppe et al., 2017). However, unlike the
311
      previous work which requires the estimation of A | Y , our proposal looks into the easier inverse
312   relationship of Y | A. To provide more theoretical insights into how the quality of density estimation
313   affects ICP and CP differently, we have additional analysis in Appendix C.
314
315
      In practice, ICP can easily leverage the state-of-the-art density estimator and is less disturbed by the
      increased complexity in A, due to either dimension or data types. Unless otherwise specified, in this
316
      manuscript, we applied Masked Autoregressive Flow (MAF) (Papamakarios et al., 2017) to estimate
317
      the conditional density of Y |A when Y is continuous and A1 , . . . , Ak can take arbitrary data types
318   (discrete or continuous) 1 . In a classification scenario when Y ∈ {0, 1, . . . , L}, one can always fit a
319   classifier to model Y |A. To this end, FairICP is more feasible to handle complex sensitive attributes
320   and is suitable for both regression and classification tasks.

465
466   In this section, we consider several real-world scenarios where we may need to protect multiple
467   sensitive attributes. For all experiments, the data is repeated divided into a training set (60%) and a
468
      test set (40%) 100 times, with the average results on the test sets reported .
469
       • Communities and Crime Data Set: This dataset contains 1994 samples and 122 features. The
         goal is to build a regression model predicting the percentage of violent crimes for US cities based
470
         on neighborhood characteristics while protecting ethnic information. Specifically, we take all three
471
         minority races (African American, Hispanic, Asian, referred to as "3 dim") as sensitive attributes
472      instead of only one race as done in the previous literature. We keep all the sensitive attributes
473      used as continuous variables (instead of binarizing them as most previous work did), representing
474      the percentage of each race. We also consider the case where A only includes one race (African
475      American, referred to as “1 dim") for better comparisons.
476    • ACSIncome Dataset: We use the ACSIncome dataset derived from the American Community
477      Survey (ACS) (Ding et al., 2021). We subsample 100,000 instances with 10 features such as
478      education, occupation, and marital status. The task is a binary classification to predict whether an
479      individual’s annual income exceeds $50,000. For sensitive attributes, we consider a mixed-type set:
480      sex (male, female), race (Black, non-Black), and age (continuous). To the best of our knowledge,
481      this is the first work exploring fairness with mixed-type sensitive attributes in this context.
482    • Adult Dataset: The dataset contains census data extracted from the 1994 U.S. Census Bureau
483      database and consists of 48,842 instances. Each instance represents an individual with attributes
484      such as age, education level, occupation, and more. The task is to predict whether an individual’s
485      annual income exceeds $50,000, making it a binary classification problem. We use both sex and
         race as sensitive attributes to construct a fair classifier that satisfies the equalized odds criterion.

531   We introduced a flexible fairness-aware learning approach FairICP to achieve equalized odds with

      numerical experiments on both synthetic and real data to demonstrate its efficacy and flexibility. We
