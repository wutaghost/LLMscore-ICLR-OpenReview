009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               We study what provable privacy attacks can be shown on trained, 2-layer ReLU
012            neural networks. We explore two types of attacks; data reconstruction attacks, and
013            membership inference attacks. We prove that theoretical results on the implicit
014            bias of 2-layer neural networks can be used to provably reconstruct a set of which
015            at least a constant fraction are training points in a univariate setting, and can also
016            be used to identify with high probability whether a given point was used in the
017            training set in a high dimensional setting. To the best of our knowledge, our work
018            is the first to show provable vulnerabilities in this setting.
019

021   1   I NTRODUCTION
022
023
      Recently, it was shown that theoretical tools used to study the implicit bias properties of successfully
024
      trained neural networks can be leveraged to reconstruct certain portions of the dataset on which the
025   neural network was trained (Haim et al., 2022). The idea behind these attacks is that under some
026   conditions, trained neural networks must satisfy certain properties that are a consequence of the
027   implicit bias of the training algorithm, which can be used to extract information on the training
028   set. This was followed by many other works that applied the same techniques in a broader setting
029   (Buzaglo et al., 2023a;b; Andrew et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Boenisch et al., 2024), raising this
030   vulnerability as a potential practical concern for the widespread use of neural networks. However,
031   despite the fact that these works were motivated by theory, none of them give an explanation for
032   why such a reconstruction is possible, since a given neural network, which satisfies these properties,
033
      may have been trained on potentially many different datasets, including some that are significantly
      different than the actual data the neural network was trained on.
034
035   In this paper, we take what is to the best of our knowledge the first step in developing a theoretical
036   understanding of the privacy vulnerabilities induced by the above implicit bias, by showing that
037   such attacks can be provably executed on trained neural networks under various assumptions. This
038   indicates that such attacks are successful since all neural networks satisfying these properties must
039
      store at least some information on the training data, which can be used by a malicious attacker. More
      specifically, we use known results on the implicit bias of ReLU neural networks, which establish
040
      that such networks tend to converge to a certain margin maximization solution (Lyu and Li, 2020;
041
      Ji and Telgarsky, 2020). This characterization of the implicit bias of neural networks allows us
042   to rigorously analyze certain cases in which the neural network memorizes the training data. In
043   particular, this includes examples where an attacker is capable of reconstructing certain portions of
044   the data in a univariate setting, or perform membership inference attacks with high success rates in a
045   high dimensional setting, effectively distinguishing between instances that are in the training set and
046   fresh instances that were generated by the same distribution that was used to generate the training
047   set.
048   While our attacks are applicable under certain input’s dimension, we also conduct experiments that
049   show that these vulnerabilities can be a concern in more generality, even when our assumptions on
050   the dimension of the input are not met. Nevertheless, it is currently not clear what is the extent
051   of the vulnerabilities that we reveal, and to what extent they can be circumvented. We leave the
052   intriguing question of how to provably defend against such exploits to future work, and we hope
053   that our work will pave the way for and motivate additional rigorous study of privacy attacks and
      defenses in trained neural networks.

      The remainder of the paper is structured as follows: After specifying our contributions in more
055   detail below, we turn to discuss related work. In Section 2 we present our notations, some required

061   O UR CONTRIBUTIONS
062
063   Our main contribution is to provide rigorous guarantees in this setting, since to the best of our
064   knowledge, all previous works are empirical. In more detail, our contributions can be summarized
065   as follows:
066
067
             • We prove that in the univariate case, under Assumption 2.1, which states that the weights
               of a trained neural network reach a stationary point of a maximum-margin problem that
068
               can be expressed as a function of the training data, an attacker can reconstruct a portion
069
               of the training data with a constant probability, which is independent of the training set
070            and the size of the network. We show how to extract that portion of the training data in
071            Algorithm 1.
072
             • We prove that in the high dimensional case, under Assumption 4.1, i.e. that the vectors
073
               in the training data are nearly orthogonal w.h.p., an attacker can execute a membership
074
               inference attack with high success rates. We show that some commonly used continuous
075            distributions satisfy Assumption 4.1, and we also provide in Subsection 4.1 examples of
076            different attacks that can be performed depending on the information available to the at-
077            tacker.
078
             • We empirically show that the membership inference attack we analyze in Section 4 may
079
               still be executed successfully when we slightly relax Assumption 4.1. This suggests that
080            the vulnerabilities we study in this paper are potentially even more widespread than what
081            our theory establishes.

      R ELATED W ORK
084
085   Privacy attacks in neural networks were studied extensively in recent years. Since this paper focuses
086   on two specific types of attacks, we only review here papers that also study these kinds of attacks,
087   or those that closely relate to it.
088
089   Data reconstruction attacks. Data reconstruction attacks aim to fully recover the training set
090   or parts of it. These include attacks on generative models such as large language models (Carlini
091   et al., 2019; 2021; Nasr et al., 2023), diffusion models (Somepalli et al., 2022; Carlini et al., 2023),
092   and in federated learning settings (Zhu et al., 2019; He et al., 2019; Hitaj et al., 2017; Geiping et al.,
093   2020; Huang et al., 2021; Wen et al., 2022). Perhaps the most relevant works that are concerned with
094   reconstruction attacks are Haim et al. (2022) and Buzaglo et al. (2023a). Using a known result on the
095
      implicit bias of trained neural networks, they define and optimize over a loss function, which upon
      empirical minimization, allows for the recovery of some of the training set. Inspired by these works,
096
      we use the same constraints implied by the implicit bias to study this problem, but to rigorously
097
      prove the existence of privacy vulnerabilities rather than empirically demonstrate them.
098
099
      Membership inference attacks. The second type of attacks we consider in this paper are member-
100
      ship inference attacks (Shokri et al., 2017; Hu et al., 2022a; Olatunji et al., 2021; Shejwalkar et al.,
101
      2021), which discern the inclusion or exclusion of a particular data point within the training set.
102   This attack exploits the observation that machine learning models often behave differently on the
103   data that they were trained on versus fresh test examples. One such difference is that trained models
104   tend to output more confident predictions on training examples compared to test examples. This
105   difference can be used to determine if a certain point was in the training set or not. Olatunji et al.
106   (2021) used this confidence-based technique on graph neural networks. Jha et al. (2020); Farokhi
107   and Kaafar (2020) use tools from information theory to upper bound the probability of success of a
      membership inference attack on neural networks, which is in contrast to our result which exempli-


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      fies settings with provable lower bounds on the success rates. Attias et al. (2024) also show provable
109   membership inference attacks, but for models whose objective function is a convex function.
110
111
      Differential privacy. A fundamental theoretical framework in the study of privacy is differential
112   privacy (Dwork, 2006; Abadi et al., 2016; Gong et al., 2020; Pannekoek and Spigler, 2021), which is
113   intuitively used to guarantee that sharing some information on a given dataset is done without leaking
114   too much information on specific instances. This framework constitutes a rather strong standard for
115   privacy guarantees, whereas we consider a setting where our assumptions on the implicit bias of
116   neural networks are typically not differentially private. Namely, we study a setting where our base
117   assumption is that there is already some data leakage in terms of differential privacy, and our work
118   explores what is the extent of the information that can be extracted. Thus, our results are not directly
119   comparable to those which study differential privacy.
120
121   Benign overfitting. Another well-studied phenomenon in the theory of deep learning, which may
122   explain the prevalence of privacy vulnerabilities, is benign overfitting (Bartlett et al., 2020; Cao et al.,
123
      2022; Li et al., 2021). This is when a neural network overfits on the training set, essentially achieving
      perfect training error, but still enjoys very good generalization on previously unseen instances. This
124
      suggests that even well-performing neural networks can memorize their training sets, and therefore
125
      become more prone to privacy attacks. While this provides a potential theoretical explanation for
126   this phenomenon, as does our work, it does not immediately imply a method for extracting any
127   information on the training set, nor does it prove the existence of such a method.

      In very high dimensional settings, under many commonly used data distributions, we have that the
325   dataset is almost orthogonal with high probability. We exploit this property to show that also with
326   high probability over drawing the training set, all the points in the training set will lie on the margin.
327   On the other hand, if we draw a new data point from the same distribution, the neural network
328   will output a target value which is typically much smaller than the margin. These key observations
329   will allow us to make the distinction between training points and test points, effectively answering
330   membership inference queries.
