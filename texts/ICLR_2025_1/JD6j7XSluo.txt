009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Latent diffusion models have enabled continuous-state diffusion models to handle
012            a variety of datasets, including categorical data. However, most methods rely
013            on fixed pretrained embeddings, limiting the benefits of joint training with the
014            diffusion model. While jointly learning the embedding (via reconstruction loss) and
015            the latent diffusion model (via score matching loss) could enhance performance,
016            our analysis shows that end-to-end training risks embedding collapse, degrading
017            generation quality. To address this issue, we introduce CATDM, a continuous
018
               diffusion framework within the embedding space that stabilizes training. We
               propose a novel objective combining the joint embedding-diffusion variational
019
               lower bound with a Consistency-Matching (CM) regularizer, alongside a shifted
020
               cosine noise schedule and random dropping strategy. The CM regularizer ensures
021            the recovery of the true data distribution. Experiments on benchmarks show that
022            CATDM mitigates embedding collapse, yielding superior results on FFHQ, LSUN
023            Churches, and LSUN Bedrooms. In particular, CATDM achieves an FID of 6.81
024            on ImageNet 256 √ó 256 with 50 steps. It outperforms non-autoregressive models
025            in machine translation and is on a par with previous methods in text generation.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Continuous-state diffusion models (CSDMs) (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song
031   et al., 2020b) have recently achieved notable success in various application domains, including
032   computer vision (He et al., 2024; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Chen et al., 2022), natural language
033
      processing (Li et al., 2022), and audio (Chen et al., 2021; Kong et al., 2021; Hernandez-Olivan et al.,
      2023). These probabilistic models learn the inverse of a Markov chain that gradually converts data
034
      into pure Gaussian noise, using noise-conditioned score functions (i.e., gradients of log density),
035
      which are defined only for continuous data. The core concept is to progressively recover the original
036   data distribution using a learned transition kernel. Diffusion models are notable for their high-
037   fidelity generation (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Lai et al., 2023a;b). They offer stable and relatively
038   efficient training procedures that contribute to their success. Recent advances, such as consistency
039   models (Song et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023), have further enhanced diffusion models
040   by reducing the number of sampling steps, making them more practical for real-world applications.
041
      Despite the widespread popularity of CSDMs, their extension to categorical data remains limited.
042   Previous attempts to address this limitation (Austin et al., 2021; Hoogeboom et al., 2021b; Campbell
043   et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2023; Lou et al., 2023) have focused on discrete-state diffusion models
044   (DSDMs), which define discrete corruption processes for categorical data and mimic Gaussian
045   kernels used in continuous space. For instance, D3PMs (Austin et al., 2021) implemented the
046   corruption process as random masking or token swapping and learned to reverse this process from
047   the noisy data. However, unlike continuous diffusion processes, these corruption techniques do not
048   gradually erase the semantic meaning of the data, which ideally would place similar tokens close
049   together and dissimilar ones further apart. This discrepancy leads to an unsmooth reverse procedure
050
      and limits their ability to fully exploit the advancements made in CSDMs.
051   Alternatively, categorical data can be mapped into a continuous embedding space (Vahdat et al., 2021;
052   Rombach et al., 2022; Sinha et al., 2021), followed by the application of CSDMs with Gaussian
053   kernels, which enables progressive learning signals (Ho et al., 2020) and fine-grained sampling. This
      approach has been successful in various domains. However, it may not inherently yield comparable

054
      results (Li et al., 2022; Strudel et al., 2022; Dieleman et al., 2022). First, it requires a well-trained
055   embedding for each new dataset (Li et al., 2022) before training CSDMs. Since the embedding
056   space and the denoising model are not trained end-to-end, this can result in suboptimal performance.
057   Second, jointly training both components is challenging and prone to the embedding collapse
058   problem (Dieleman et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2024), where all embeddings converge to a single vector.
059   While this convergence helps the diffusion model predict clean embeddings, it does not result in a
060   meaningful model and instead leads to poor generation. To alleviate embedding collapse, previous
061   work have explored normalizing embedding vectors to a fixed bounded norm (Dieleman et al., 2022)
062   or mapping the predicted embedding to its nearest neighbor within the finite set of vectors (Li et al.,
063
      2022). However, our experiments have shown that these manipulations do not yield satisfactory
      results in practice.
064
065   In response, this paper presents a simple but effective method called Enhanced Embedding for
066   CATegorical Data in Diffusion Models (CATDM), specifically designed to address the embedding
067   collapse problem. Our key contributions are summarized as follows:
068
      (1) We suggest that embedding collapse is driven by two factors: (1) the reconstruction loss does not
069
          provide enough learning feedback to maintain diverse embeddings, leading to collapse, and (2)
070
          the denoising score matching disproportionately influences the variational lower-bound objective,
071       overshadowing the contributions of the embeddings in the reconstruction loss.
072
      (2) We introduce several techniques to prevent trivial or collapsed embeddings. A new loss function
073
          is proposed to stabilize training. In particular, we enforce a Consistency Matching (CM) regular-
074       ization that requires the model predictions to remain consistent over time. This ensures that the
075       model produces stable outputs throughout the generation process. To enhance generation quality,
076       we implement (i) shifted cosine noise schedule and (ii) random dropping of embeddings.
077
      (3) We theoretically show that our newly proposed CM regularization helps learn the true data
078       density (Theorem 1) at its optimal. Furthermore, we connect this CM regularizer to heuristic
079       regularizations found in the literature (Dieleman et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2024) (Proposition 2).
080
081   Comprehensive experiments across a range of benchmark datasets are conducted to evaluate CATDM.
082   This comprehensive evaluation provides an in-depth analysis of the adaptability and performance of
083   our proposed approach in image generation, text generation, and machine translation tasks. The results
084
      show that CATDM effectively mitigates the embedding collapse issue and consistently outperforms
      several baseline methods. Although our main focus is vision and text generation, CATDM can
085
      be applied to any task involving categorical variables. CATDM performs on a par with baselines
086
      in text generation and machine translation, and achieves FID scores of 7.25 on FFHQ, 4.99 on
087   LSUN Churches, 4.16 on LSUN Bedrooms, and 6.81 on ImageNet 256 √ó 256 in image generation,
088   outperforming discrete-based models.

      5.2       N OISE SCHEDULE
309
310   Determining the right amount of noise added to the embeddings in each timestep can play an
311   important role in both the forward and reverse processes of CATDM. If the embedding norms are
312   large, denoising would be a trivial task for low noise levels. This is not desired because the denoising
313   model has only a small time window to generate the global structure of the meaningful embedding.
314   Instead, we note that adjusting the noise schedule (NS) by shifting its log SNR curve (Hoogeboom
315   et al., 2023) is crucial. In particular, we use the shifted cosine noise schedule, defined as
316                                     log SNR(t) = ‚ąí2 log tan(ŌÄt/2) + s ,
317   where s ‚ąą R is the shifting hyperparameter. When s = 0, it corresponds to the cosine noise
318   schedule (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021). Essentially, NS implies different weights in the diffusion loss
319   per noise level (Kingma & Gao, 2023). As illustrated in Figure 3, by moving the curve to the left, it
320
      gives more importance for higher degrees of noise. Adjusting NS also increases the speed of noise
      injection in the forward process when t ‚Čą 0, making the generation task more challenging for the
321
      model to prevent trivial generation and avoid embedding collapse.

461   6.3    M ACHINE TRANSLATION
462
463   Significant efforts have been made to apply non-autoregressive iterative refinement models to the task
464   of machine translation. This section explores the application of CATDM for machine translation.
465
      Datasets. We consider two standard datasets, including IWSLT14 German-English (IWSLT14
466
      De-En) (Cettolo et al., 2014) and WMT14 English-German (WMT14 En-De) (Bojar et al., 2014).
467
      These datasets are the most popular and widely used benchmarks for evaluating a machine translation
468   system. IWSLT14 De-En consists of transcripts from the TED talks, which are relatively informal,
469   spoken language. WMT14 En-De consists of sentences collected from a variety of sources, including
470   formal as well as less structured text.
471
      Baselines. We compare CATDM with the autoregressive Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) and
472
      other recent diffusion models, including Difformer (Gao et al., 2024), Multinomial (Hoogeboom et al.,
473
      2021b), Diffusion-LM (Li et al., 2022), CDCD (Dieleman et al., 2022), DiffuSeq (Gong et al., 2022),
474   and SeqDiffuSeq Yuan et al. (2022). For a fair comparison, we do not adopt any sequence-level
475   knowledge distillation to distill the original training set. For Difformer, we rerun the code provided by
476   the corresponding author. For CATDM, we use the same architecture and setup as that of Difformer,
477   which consists of an encoder-decoder architecture with two distinct Transformer stacks. Unlike
478   autoregresive models where the sequence length is modeled by the EOS token, we explicitly predict
479   the target length using the encoder output. The BLEU score is reported to evaluate our machine
480   translation models.
481   Results. All results, except Difformer and CATDM, were taken from previous studies. As shown
482   in Table 5, CATDM achieves the best results among non-autogressive models, with a BLEU score
483   of 29.61 on on IWSLT14 De-En and 21.67 on WMT14 En-De. Overall, CATDM underperforms
484   compared to the autoregressive model of the same size. This outcome aligns with previous find-
485   ings (Dieleman et al., 2022), which can be attributed to the fact that non-autoregressive models are
      not trained to handle repeated or missing tokens.

518   7     C ONCLUSION
519
520   This paper introduces CATDM, a continuous diffusion model tailored for modeling categorical distri-
521   butions, which jointly learns the embeddings and the denoising model. CATDM effectively addresses
522   the issue of embedding collapse. We provide an empirical analysis of this phenomenon, identifying
523   two key mechanisms responsible for collapse: insufficient feedback from the reconstruction loss and
524
      the diffusion loss that promotes constant embeddings. Experimental results show that CATDM not
      only alleviates the embedding collapse problem, but also exceeds the baseline diffusion models.
525
526   Limitations and future work. In this work, CATDM is implemented using the Transformer
527   architecture, but we emphasize that the architecture choice is orthogonal to the proposed framework
528   and can be extended to other architectures. While we have thoroughly evaluated CATDM on image
529   and text generation tasks, future work will focus on applying it to additional data types, such as
530
      graphs and audio. Furthermore, we plan to explore more advanced sampling techniques to improve
      the overall generation quality of CATDM.

      A    R ELATED WORK
789
      While autoregressive models (ARs) (Bengio et al., 2000; Brown et al., 2020) appear to dominate
790
      categorical data modeling, generating samples from these models incurs significant computational
791
      costs. Moreover, controlling these models is challenging because the generation order has to be
792   predetermined (Lou et al., 2023), making them unsuitable for control tasks such as infilling tasks. In
793   contrast, diffusion models predict all tokens simultaneously, allowing efficient and rapid sampling
794   without the sequential attention mechanism of ARs. Below, we present an overview of diffusion
795   models specifically tailored for handling categorical data.
796
      DSDMs. The idea is to establish a similar iterative refinement process for categorical data. The
797
      corruption process involves transitioning discrete values from one to another. This concept was
798   initially introduced by Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015) for binary sequence problems. Later, it was
799   extended in multinomial diffusion (Hoogeboom et al., 2021b). Austin et al. (2021) improved
800   discrete diffusion by introducing diverse corruption processes, going beyond uniform transition.
801   Based on the former framework, several extensions have been introduced for image modeling, e.g.,
802   MaskGIT (Chang et al., 2022), VQ-Diffusion (Gu et al., 2022), Token-Critic (Lezama et al., 2022),
803   Muse (Chang et al., 2023), and Paella (Rampas et al., 2022). Additionally, Campbell et al. (2022)
804   utilized Continuous Time Markov Chains for discrete diffusion. Despite their initial success, the
805   corruptions introduced by these methods are characterized by their coarse-grained nature, making
806
      them inadequate for effectively modeling the semantic correlations between tokens.
807   CSDMs. Li et al. (2022) addressed the challenge of controlling language models (LMs) with
808   Diffusion-LM, a non-autoregressive language model based on continuous diffusion. A similar idea
809   has been introduced in SED (Strudel et al., 2022), DiNoiSer (Ye et al., 2023), CDCD (Dieleman et al.,
      2022), Bit Diffusion (Chen et al., 2022), Plaid (Gulrajani & Hashimoto, 2024), and Difformer (Gao


                                                          15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      et al., 2024). Recently, Meng et al. (2022); Lou et al. (2023) proposed an alternative concrete score
811   function for discrete settings, which captures the surrogate ‚Äúgradient‚ÄĚ information within discrete
812   spaces. However, the challenge of end-to-end training for both embeddings and CSDMs has not been
813   fully addressed in these methods. To avoid embedding collapse, existing techniques either normalize
814   the embeddings (Dieleman et al., 2022) or use heuristic methods (Li et al., 2022), which are not
815   generally effective and may lead to training instability (Dieleman et al., 2022; Strudel et al., 2022).

889   It is important to generate images corresponding to a given condition. In CATDM, the condition is
890   incorporated directly into the prediction network through Adaptive Layer Normalization (Ba et al.,

892   to reconstruct the input. However, we often observe that CATDM generates outputs that are not
893
      correlated well with the condition. The reason is that the corrupted input contains rich information;
      therefore, the network can ignore the condition during training.

896   ance (Ho & Salimans, 2021). Essentially, it guides the sampling trajectories toward higher-density
897   data regions. During training, we randomly drop 10% of the conditions and set the dropped conditions
898   to the null token. During sampling, CATDM predicts the categorical variable x as follows
