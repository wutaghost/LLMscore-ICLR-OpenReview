009                                                            A BSTRACT
010
011              Foundation models have emerged as a promising approach in time series forecasting
012              (TSF). Existing approaches either repurpose large language models (LLMs) or build
013              large-scale time series datasets to develop TSF foundation models for universal
014              forecasting. However, these methods face challenges due to the severe cross-
015              domain gap or in-domain heterogeneity. This paper explores a new road to building
016              a TSF foundation model from rich, high-quality natural images. Our key insight is
017
                 that a visual masked autoencoder, pre-trained on the ImageNet dataset, can naturally
                 be a numeric series forecaster. By reformulating TSF as an image reconstruction
018
                 task, we bridge the gap between image pre-training and TSF downstream tasks.
019
                 Surprisingly, without further adaptation in the time-series domain, the proposed
020              V ISION TS could achieve superior zero-shot forecasting performance compared
021              to existing TSF foundation models. With fine-tuning for one epoch, V ISION TS
022              could further improve the forecasting and achieve state-of-the-art performance in
023              most cases. Extensive experiments reveal intrinsic similarities between images and
024              real-world time series, suggesting visual models may offer a “free lunch” for TSF
025              and highlight the potential for future cross-modality research. Our code is available
026              in the Supplementary Material.

076   We have recently witnessed two roads to building a TSF foundation model for universal forecasting.
077   The first tries to repurpose large language models (LLMs) that have been pre-trained on text data for
078
      TSF tasks (i.e., text-based) (Zhou et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2024), based on the observation that LLMs
      and TSF models share a similar left-to-right forecasting paradigm. However, due to the significant
079
      gap between these two modalities, the effectiveness of such transferability between language and
080
      time series has recently been questioned by Tan et al. (2024).
081
082   The second road focuses on constructing large-scale time-series datasets collected from diverse
083   domains to train a TSF foundation model from scratch (i.e., time series-based or TS-based) (Woo
084
      et al., 2024; Das et al., 2024). Nevertheless, unlike images or language with unified formats, time
      series data is highly heterogeneous in length, frequency, number of variates, domains, and semantics,
085
      limiting the transferability between pre-training and downstream domains. Until recently, constructing
086
      a high-quality dataset remains challenging and is still in the early exploration stage.
087
088   In this paper, we investigate a third road that is less explored yet promising: building TSF foundation
089   models with pre-trained visual models. Our key idea is that pixel variations in a natural image can be
090
      interpreted as temporal sequences, which share many intrinsic similarities with time series: 1 Similar
      modalities: Unlike discrete texts, both images and time series are continuous; 2 Similar origin:
091
      Both time series and images are observations of real-world physical systems, whereas languages are
092
      products of human cognitive processes; 3 Similar information density: Languages are human-
093   generated signals with high semantic density, while images and time series are natural signals with
094   heavy redundancy (He et al., 2022); and 4 Similar features: As shown in Fig. 2, images often
095   display many features of real-world time series, which are rarely found in language data. Based on
096   these findings, images could be a more promising modality for transferring to TSF. We are motivated
097   to answer the question: Can a visual model pre-trained on images be a free-lunch foundation model
098   for zero-shot time series forecasting?
099   We focus on visual masked autoencoder (MAE)1 , a popular CV foundation model (He et al., 2022)
100   by self-supervised pre-training on ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009). As an image reconstruction and
101   completion model, MAE can naturally be a numeric series forecaster. Inspired by the well-known
102   prompt technique in NLP (Schick & Schütze, 2021), we propose a simple method to reformulate TSF
103   as a patch-level image reconstruction task to bridge the gap between pre-training and downstream
104   tasks. Specifically, we transform 1D time series data into 2D matrices via segmentation. Then, we
105   render the matrices into images and align the forecasting window with masked image patches. This
106   method allows us to make zero-shot forecasting without further adaptation.

      We evaluate our proposed V ISION TS on 43 TSF benchmarks across various domains, including
109   long-term TSF datasets (Zhou et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021), Monash (Godahewa et al., 2021), and
110   PF (Woo et al., 2024). As demonstrated in Fig. 1, without any further adaptation in the time-series

112   the state-of-the-art (SOTA) zero-shot TSF foundation models, including text-based and TS-based
113   methods. By fine-tuning MAE on each downstream dataset for only one epoch, V ISION TS can lead to
114   a SOTA performance on most long-term TSF benchmarks.
115   To further understand and explain the transferability, We use an MAE encoder to visualize both
116   modalities, showing a level of similarity between time series and natural image representations.
117   Additionally, we observe considerable heterogeneity within time series data, and images can serve as
118   a bridge to connect these isolated time series representations. Our findings suggest that time series
119   and natural images may be two sides of a coin, and visual models can be a free lunch for time series
120   forecasting. We hope our findings can inspire future cross-modality research on CV and TSF.
121   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
122
123   • We explore a road to building a TSF foundation model from natural images, which is conceptually
124     different from the existing text-based and TS-based pre-training methods.
125
      • We introduce V ISION TS, a novel TSF foundation model based on a visual MAE. To bridge the gap
126     between the two modalities, we reformulate the TSF task into an image reconstruction task.

      • Comprehensive evaluations of V ISION TS on 43 benchmarks across multiple domains demonstrate
        its significant zero-shot forecasting performance, surpassing few-shot text-based TSF foundation
129
        models and achieving comparable or superior results to zero-shot TS-based models.

132   2    P RELIMINARIES
133
134   Time Series Forecasting (TSF) For a multivariate time series with M variables, let xt ∈ RM
135   represent the value at t-th time step. Given a historical sequence (i.e., look-back window) Xt−L:t =
136   [xt−L , · · · , xt−1 ] ∈ RL×M with context length L, the TSF task is to predict future values (i.e.,
137   forecast horizon) with prediction length H: X̂t:t+H = [xt , · · · , xt+H−1 ] ∈ RH×M .
138
139   Patch-Level Image Reconstruction To obtain high-quality visual representation for downstream
140   CV tasks, He et al. (2022) proposed masked autoencoder (MAE) to pre-train a Vision Transformer
141   (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) using a patch-level image reconstruction task on ImageNet. Specif-
142
      ically, for an image of size W × W (where W represents both the width and height, as ImageNet
      images are square), the image is evenly divided into N × N patches, each with a width and height
143
      of S = W/N . During pre-training, some random patches are masked, while the remaining visible
144
      patches are fed into the ViT with their position encodings. MAE are trained to reconstruct the masked
145   pixel values from these visible patches.
146
147
148   3    M ETHODOLOGY
149
150   As noted in the Introduction, time series and images share intrinsic similarities, suggesting the transfer
151   potential of pre-trained visual models (particularly MAE in this paper) for TSF tasks. We explain how
152
      to reformulate TSF tasks into MAE’s pre-training task, i.e., patch-level image reconstruction.
153   Our high-level idea is straightforward: map the look-back/forecasting windows to visible/masked
154   patches, respectively. This idea is supported by the recent success of prompt tuning (Schick &
155   Schütze, 2021) in NLP, where the predictions for [mask] token in pre-trained language models, e.g.,
156   BERT (Devlin et al., 2019), are directly used for downstream tasks. By unifying the forms of the
157   two tasks, we bridge the gap between the two domains, enabling a MAE for zero-shot TSF directly
158
      without adapting the pre-trained parameters.
159   Notably, this idea is limited to univariate forecasting since multivariates are intractable to be encoded
160   in a single image. Fortunately, recent work shows that channel independence — predicting each
161   variable separately for multivariate forecasting — can be highly effective (Nie et al., 2022; Han et al.,
      2024). Therefore, we leave the exploration of multivariate interactions for future work.

184   However, implementing this idea poses a challenge: the dimension of time-series data (1D) is different
185   from images (2D). Moreover, the size of images in the pre-training dataset is fixed at 224 × 224,
186   while the lengths of time series data can vary dynamically. In the following, we describe the details
187   of V ISION TS to address this challenge. Our architecture is depicted in Fig. 3.
188
189   Segmentation Given a univariate input X ∈ RL , the first goal is to transform it into a 2D matrix.
190   We propose to segment it into ⌊L/P ⌋ subsequences of length P , where P is the periodicity. Notably,
191   when the time series lacks clear periodicity, we can set P = 1 directly, which is also effective in our
192   experiments (Appendix B.4). In practice, P can be determined using statistical methods like Fast
193
      Fourier Transform (Wu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024) or domain knowledge like sampling frequency
      (Godahewa et al., 2021; Alexandrov et al., 2020). In this paper, we select P based on the sampling
194
      frequency, elaborated in Appendix A.2.
195
196   After that, these subsequences are then stacked into a 2D matrix, denoted by Iraw ∈ RP ×⌊L/P ⌋ .
197   This encoding strategy is proven to be efficient by recent work like TimesNet (Wu et al., 2023) and
198   SparseTSF (Lin et al., 2024), as it allows for the simultaneous capture of both variations within the
199   same period (i.e., intra-period) and across periods with the same phase (i.e., inter-period). Moreover,
200
      it ensures that each element in Iraw and its neighbors align with the spatial locality property of images
      (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), where nearby pixels tend to be similar due to the inherent cohesiveness of
201
      objects in the real world. Therefore, this further narrows the gap between time series and images.
202
203
      Normalization MAE standardizes each image based on the mean and standard deviation computed
204   on ImageNet. Therefore, we apply instance normalization to Iraw , which is also a standard practice in
205   current TSF (Kim et al., 2022). Notably, we observed that normalizing Iraw to a standard deviation
206   of r, where r is a hyperparameter less than 1, yields superior performance. One explanation is that
207   the magnitude of inputs/outputs during MAE pretraining is constrained by the limited range of color
208   values. Therefore, reducing the magnitude of Iraw prevents exceeding these limits. However, an
209   excessively low r can result in values that are difficult to distinguish. We found that a moderate value
210   (0.4) of r performs well across most scenarios (See Appendix B.8 for more details). Let Inorm denote
211   the normalized matrix, which is computed as follows:

215   Rendering It is well-known that each image has three channels. We simply render Inorm as a
      grayscale image Igrey ∈ RP ×⌊L/P ⌋×3 , where all three channels are identical to Inorm . This choice

216
      is purely result-driven: In our early experiments, we added a convolutional layer with three output
217   channels to convert the grayscale image into a color image and then fine-tuned it to find the optimal
218   color transformation, which, however, did not significantly influence the performance.
219
220   Alignment Our goal is to predict the columns on the right of Igrey to forecast the future sequence.
221   A straightforward approach is to treat Igrey as the visible left portion and the predicted columns as the
222   masked right portion. However, since the image size during pre-training may not match the size of
223   Igrey , we propose to resize Igrey to align with the pre-training data. Formally, let the total number of
224   2D patches used in pre-training be N × N and the size of each patch be S × S. We set the number
225   of visible patches to N × n and the masked patches to N × (N − n), where n = ⌊N · L/(L+H)⌋ is
226
      determined by the ratio of context length L to prediction length H. We resample the image Igrey to
      adjust the size from the original dimensions (P, ⌊L/P ⌋) to (N · S, n · S), making it more compatible
227
      with MAE. We select bilinear interpolation for the resampling process.
228
229   Moreover, we found that reducing the width of the visible portion can further improve performance.
230   One possible explanation is that MAE uses a large masked ratio during pre-training, with only 25% of
231   patches visible. Reducing the image width may align the masked ratio more closely with pre-training.
232
      Therefore, we propose multiplying n by a hyperparameter c ∈ [0, 1]. Similar to r, we found that
      setting c = 0.4 performs well in our experiments (See Appendix B.8). Final n can be formulated as:
233

237   Reconstruction and Forecasting After obtaining the MAE-reconstructed image, we simply reverse
238   the previous steps for forecasting. Specifically, we resize the entire image back to the original time
239
      series segmentations through the same bilinear interpolation, and average the three channels to obtain
      a single-channel image. After de-normalizing and flattening, the forecasting window can be extracted.

      4.1   Z ERO -S HOT T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING
251   Setups We first evaluate V ISION TS’s zero-shot TSF performance without any fine-tuning on time-
252   series modalities. To prevent data leakage and assess the out-of-distribution capabilities, we selected
253   six widely-used datasets from the long-term TSF benchmark that are not included in M OIRAI ’s
254   pre-training set for evaluation. Since most baselines cannot perform zero-shot forecasting, we
255   report their few-shot results by fine-tuning on the 10% of the individual target datasets. We also
256   evaluate the Monash benchmark (including 29 test datasets) and PF benchmark (including 6 test
257   datasets). Notably, the Monash benchmark is more challenging for V ISION TS since they were used in
258   M OIRAI ’s pre-training but not for V ISION TS. We set the hyperparameters to r = c = 0.4. Following
259
      common practice (Nie et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023; Woo et al., 2024), we conduct hyperparameter
      tuning on validation sets to determine the optimal context length L, detailed in Appendix B.1.
260
261
      Results on Long-Term TSF Benchmark Table 1 shows that V ISION TS surprisingly achieves the
262
      best forecasting performance in most cases (7 out of 14). Specifically, V ISION TS demonstrates a
263
      relative average MSE reduction of approximately 6% compared to M OIRAISmall and M OIRAILarge , and
264   performs comparably to M OIRAIBase . When compared to the various few-shot baselines, V ISION TS
265   shows a relative average MSE reduction ranging from 8% to 84%. Given that all baselines except for
266   V ISION TS are trained on the time-series domain, this result is particularly encouraging. It suggests
267   that the transferability from images to time-series is stronger than from text to time-series,
268   and even comparable to the in-domain transferability between time-series. We also include a
269   comparison with traditional algorithms (ETS, ARIMA, and Seasonal Naïve) in Appendix B.3, where
      V ISION TS still outperforms all of these traditional methods.

472
473   Depending on the pre-training data, TSF foundation models can be categorized into Text-based and
474   TS-based. We first review these related works, and then introduce recent research for image-based
475
      time series analysis.
476
477
      Text-based TSF Foundation Models Large Language Models (LLMs) pre-trained on large
      amounts of text data are being applied to TSF tasks. For example, Zhou et al. (2023) fine-tuned a
478
      pre-trained GPT (Radford et al., 2019) on each time-series downstream task, such as forecasting,
479
      classification, imputation, and anomaly detection. Based on Llama (Touvron et al., 2023), Jin et al.
480   (2024) froze the pre-trained LLM and reprogrammed the time series to align with the language
481   modality. Bian et al. (2024) adopted a two-stage approach by continually pre-training GPT (Radford
482   et al., 2019) on the time-series domain. Nevertheless, the TSF performance of LLMs has recently
483   been questioned by Tan et al. (2024), which designed several ablation studies to show that textual
484   knowledge is unnecessary for forecasting. In this paper, we attribute it to the large modality gap.
485   Some recent approaches focus on directly transforming the time series into natural texts for LLMs,
      allowing for zero-shot forecasting. For example, PromptCast (Xue & Salim, 2023) used pre-defined


                                                                                                   9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      templates to describe numerical time series data, while LLMTime (Gruver et al., 2023) directly sepa-
487   rated time steps using commas and separates digits using spaces to construct the text input. However,
488   due to the efficiency issue of the autoregressive decoding strategy and the expensive inference cost of
489   large language models, their practical use is limited.
490
491
492
      Time Series-Based TSF Foundation Models Self-supervised pre-training a TSF model on the
      same dataset used for downstream TSF tasks is a well-explored topic (Ma et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
493
      2024), such as denoising autoencoders (Zerveas et al., 2021) or contrastive learning (Woo et al.,
494
      2022a; Yue et al., 2022). They follow a similar paradigm to the masked autoencoder (MAE) in
495   computer vision. However, these methods rarely examine the cross-dataset generalization capabilities.
496   Recently, research has shifted towards training universal foundation models, by collecting large-scale
497   time series datasets from diverse domains (Goswami et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Das et al., 2024;
498   Dong et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024) or generating numerous synthetic time series data (Fu et al., 2024;
499   Yang et al., 2024). As a representative method, Woo et al. (2024) collected 27 billion observations
500   across nine domains and trained TSF foundation models of various scales, achieving strong zero-shot
501   performance. However, given the severe heterogeneity, constructing high-quality large datasets poses
502   significant challenges for building these foundation models.
503
504   Image-Based Time-Series Analysis Previous research has investigated encoding time series data
505   into images and used convolutional neural networks (CNNs) trained from scratch for classification
506   (Wang & Oates, 2015a;b; Hatami et al., 2018) or forecasting (Li et al., 2020; Sood et al., 2021;
507   Semenoglou et al., 2023). Recent researchers explored using pre-trained models for these imaging
508   time series. Li et al. (2024) used a pre-trained vision transformer (ViT) for classification. Wimmer &
509   Rekabsaz (2023) and Zhang et al. (2023) employed vision-language multimodal pre-trained models
510
      to extract predictive features and generate text descriptions. Yang et al. (2024) generated synthetic
      time series data to pre-train a vision model for the TSF task. However, these studies did not deeply
511
      examine the transferability from natural images to TSF. Despite early efforts by Zhou et al. (2023) to
512
      fine-tune a BEiT (Bao et al., 2022) trained on images for time series forecasting, it still falls short of
513   the leading text-based and TS-based TSF foundation models. To the best of our knowledge, we are
514   the first to show that an image-based foundation model, without further time-series adaptation, can
515   match or even surpass other types of TSF foundation models.
516
517
518   6    C ONCLUSION
519
520   In this paper, we explore a novel approach to building a time series forecasting (TSF) foundation
521   model using natural images, offering a new perspective distinct from the traditional text-based and
522   TS-based methods. By leveraging the intrinsic similarities between images and time series, we
523   introduced V ISION TS, an MAE-based TSF foundation model that reformulates the TSF task as an
524   image reconstruction problem. Our extensive evaluations demonstrate that V ISION TS achieves
525   outstanding forecasting performance in zero-shot and full-shot settings, being a free lunch for a TSF
526   foundation model. We hope our findings could open new avenues for further cross-modality research.

531   • Exploring Other Architectures: As a preliminary study, we employed a basic MAE model.
532     Utilizing more advanced models like diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Peebles & Xie, 2023)
533     presents a promising research direction.
534
      • Expanding Time Series Capacities: Due to limitations in the visual model, V ISION TS cannot
535     utilize exogenous covariates and perform distribution forecasting. Future modifications to the
536     model structure may empower it with more time series capabilities.
537
538   • Continual Pretraining: As discussed in Table 4, larger visual models may overfit image-specific
539     features, limiting their transferability to time series. Investigating whether continual pretraining on
        large-scale time series can reduce the gap between the two modalities is an interesting avenue.

       A.1   B ENCHMARK AND BASELINES
759
760    Long-Term TSF Benchmark We evaluate our model on 8 widely used long-term TSF datasets
761    (Zhou et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021), including ETTh1, ETTh2, ETTm1, ETTm2, Electricity, Traffic,
762    Illness, and Weather. Performance is assessed using Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Mean Absolute
763    Error (MAE), with lower values indicating better forecasting accuracy.
764
765    Monash Benchmark Following Woo et al. (2024), we tested 29 Monash datasets (Godahewa
766    et al., 2021) using GluonTS (Alexandrov et al., 2020), including M1 Monthly, M3 Monthly, M3
767
       Other, M4 Monthly, M4 Weekly, M4 Daily, M4 Hourly, Tourism Quarterly, Tourism Monthly, CIF
       2016, Australian Electricity Demand, Bitcoin, Pedestrian Counts, Vehicle Trips, KDD Cup, Weather,
768
       NN5 Daily, NN5 Weekly, Carparts, FRED-MD, Traffic Hourly, Traffic Weekly, Rideshare, Hospital,
769
       COVID Deaths, Temperature Rain, Sunspot, Saugeen River Flow, and US Births. Performance is
770    assessed using MAE.
771
772    PF Benchmark Woo et al. (2024) tested their methods on six datasets for evaluating the probability
773    forecasting ability (PF), including Electricity, Solar, Walmart, Weather, Istanbul Traffic, and Turkey
774    Power. Since MAE cannot output distributions, we report the point forecasting metrics on these six PF
775    datasets, including the symmetric mean absolute percentage error (sMAPE), mean absolute scaled
776    error (MASE) (Hyndman & Koehler, 2006), normalized deviation (ND), and normalized root mean
777    squared error (NRMSE) (Yu et al., 2016).
778
779    Baselines The baseline models selected for comparison are briefly described below:
780
       1. M OIRAI (Woo et al., 2024) is a TSF foundation model trained on the Large-scale Open Time
781
          Series Archive (LOTSA), with over 27B observations across nine domains. It has three variants:
782
          small, base, and large.
783
       2. TimesFM (Das et al., 2024) is a decoder-style TSF foundation model, using a large time-series
784
          corpus comprising both real-world and synthetic datasets.
785
786    3. Time-LLM (Jin et al., 2024) is a text-based TSF foundation model built on Llama, which
787
          reprograms time series data to align with the language modality, keeping the LLM frozen.
788    4. GPT4TS (Zhou et al., 2023) (OneFitsAll) is another text-based model based on GPT, fine-tuned
789       for forecasting tasks.
790    5. LLMTime (Gruver et al., 2023) encodes time series data to a text sequence, supporting zero-shot
791       forecasting.
792    6. DLinear (Zeng et al., 2023) proposes a linear forecasting model, enhanced by seasonal-trend
793       decomposition or normalization.
794    7. PatchTST (Nie et al., 2022) uses Transformer encoders with patching and channel independence
795       techniques for improved predictions.
796
       8. TimesNet (Wu et al., 2023) applies convolution kernels along the time dimension, using temporal
797       decomposition and periodical segmentation to capture temporal patterns.
798
       9. FEDformer (Zhou et al., 2022) employs a sparse frequency domain representation, using
799
          frequency-enhanced blocks for cross-time dependency.
800
801
      10. Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021) uses series decomposition blocks and Auto-Correlation to capture
          cross-time dependency.
802
803   11. Stationary (Liu et al., 2022) introduces stationarization and de-stationary attention mechanisms.
804   12. ETSFormer (Woo et al., 2022b) leverages exponential smoothing principles, including exponen-
805       tial smoothing and frequency attention mechanisms.
806   13. Informer (Zhou et al., 2021) proposes ProbSparse self-attention and distillation operations.
807
808    For the long-term TSF benchmark, we include TS-based foundation model results from their original
809    papers, Text-based model results from Tan et al. (2024), and other baseline results from Zhou et al.
       (2023). For the Monash and PF benchmark, we include results from Woo et al. (2024).
