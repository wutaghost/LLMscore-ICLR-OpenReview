009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            System 2 reasoning is one of the defining characteristics of intelligence, which
012            requires slow and logical thinking. Human conducts System 2 reasoning via the
013            language of thoughts that organizes the reasoning process as a causal sequence
014            of mental language, or thoughts. Recently, it has been observed that System 2
015            reasoning can be elicited from Large Language Models (LLMs) pre-trained on
016            large-scale natural languages. However, in this work, we show that there is a
017
               significant gap between the modeling of languages and thoughts. As language is
               primarily a tool for humans to share knowledge and thinking, modeling human
018
               language can easily integrate into language biases that are not related to thoughts.
019
               Furthermore, we show that the biases may mislead the eliciting of “thoughts” in
020            LLMs to focus only on a given part of the premise. To this end, we propose a
021            new prompt technique termed Language-of-Thoughts (LoT) to alleviate the issue.
022            Instead of directly eliciting the chain of thoughts from partial information, LoT
023            instructs LLMs to focus and expand based on all the relevant information. We
024            show that the simple strategy significantly reduces the language modeling biases in
025            LLMs and improves the performance of LLMs across a variety of reasoning tasks.

      Dual-Process theory (Sloman, 1996; Kahneman, 2011) is an accounts to mental activities with two
      systems. System 1 describes unconscious and automatic reflection in the mind, such as feeling
031
      emotions from others; system 2 refers to intended and conscious efforts to solve complex tasks
032
      like math. Despite its controversy (Evans & Stanovich, 2013), the description of system 2 is
033   consistent with desired characteristics of machine intelligence (Turing, 1950). Such thinking processes
034   are hypothesized as causal transitions over mental events expressed by mental language (Fodor,
035   1975; Pinker, 1995; Rescorla, 2024). Since the success of deep learning in achieving System 1
036   tasks (Goodfellow et al., 2016), there have been significant efforts devoted to designing machine
037   learning methods to imitate the System 2 human intelligence (Bengio, 2017; Schölkopf et al., 2021;
038   Bengio et al., 2021; LeCun, 2022).
039   Recently, Large Language Models (LLMs) which are pre-trained onto massive natural language
040   written by humans, have demonstrated impressive System 2 capabilities (Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI,
041   2022; Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023). Specifically, when given proper instructions such as
042   Chain-of-Thoughts (CoT), LLMs reason by explicitly generating and following the intermediate steps
043   to derive the answer, and achieve significant success in a variety of reasoning tasks (Wei et al., 2022).
044   Howeve, CoT may simulate System 2 imperfectly via the continuous application of System 1, and
045   can still not resolve complex tasks such as planning (Kambhampati et al., 2024; Stechly et al., 2024),
046   or even lead to decreased performance (Wang et al., 2024; Sprague et al., 2024a) and exacerbate
047
      biases (Shaikh et al., 2023). Unlike humans, who may elicit reasoning through mental language,
      LLMs utilize written language directly. Therefore, it raises a curious research question:
048
049              Can LLMs properly elicit mental-like reasoning by training on written language?
050
051   In this work, we show LLMs struggle to properly utilize given premises due to a modeling gap in
052   next-token prediction on written language. As language is primarily a tool used by humans for the
053   communication of thoughts, the same thoughts can be expressed in multiple forms (Fedorenko et al.,
      2024). Consequently, modeling thoughts merely from the language can easily integrate the language

      The Interplay between language and thoughts has intrigued scholars for a long time (Fodor,
101
      1975; Rescorla, 2024; Fedorenko et al., 2024). The Language of Thought Hypothesis considers that
102   human thinking and reasoning are built upon mentalese – the language spoken in our mind during
103   thinking (Fodor, 1975; Pinker, 1995). This hypothetical language organizes the reasoning process as
104   a causal sequence upon mental representations of concepts, or thoughts, which is different from the
105   language used for communication (Fedorenko et al., 2024). In fact, human infants without acquiring
106   the language capability can already learn to perform System 2 reasoning of the world (Gopnik et al.,
107   2004; Spelke, 2022). Therefore, language is not necessary for organizing thoughts (Fedorenko et al.,
      2024). In this work, we extend the discussion to the context of LLMs, which are pre-trained upon a


                                                                   2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      massive scale of human languages (Brown et al., 2020), and have gained huge success that is even
109   considered as sparks of artificial general intelligence (Bubeck et al., 2023). However, due to the
110   language-thought gap, we find that modeling merely based on human languages is not sufficient to
111   model human thoughts, and hence can fail to perform reliable reasoning like humans.
112
113   Natural Language Understanding In the NLP literature, it is formally studied how to formally
114   distinguish the semantic content with its forms (Bender & Koller, 2020), and also how to further
115   utilize world knowledge and commonsense information in reasoning procedures (Yu et al., 2024a).
116   Asher & Bhar (2024) focuses on whether the representations of language models can capture the
117   semantics of logical operators, which are built upon different training paradigms as LLMs studied
118   in this work. Chaturvedi et al. (2024) discuses whether language models can truly understand the
119   semantics through multiple thought experiments. However, this work focuses more on the reasoning,
120   operating in a more abstract level upon understanding the meanings of the texts.
121
122   Chain-of-Thought reasoning is an emerging paradigm along with the scaling up of LLMs (Wei
123   et al., 2022). By prompting LLMs to reason upon a series of intermediate steps like humans, CoT
124   has gained huge success in improving the reasoning performances of multiple LLMs in a variety of
125   reasoning tasks (Wei et al., 2022), and has inspired a series of sophisticated prompting techniques to
126
      better imitate human reasoning (Yao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023c; Zhou et al., 2023; Besta et al.,
      2024; Wang et al., 2023b; Saha et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024b). Empirically, it can be beneficial to
127
      encourage LLMs to explore various reasoning paths through contrastive demonstration (Chia et al.,
128
      2023) and argument generation for possible answers (Miandoab & Sarathy, 2024). Furthermore,
129   researchers attempt to endorse LLMs with intrinsic CoT capabilities by constructing CoT instruction
130   tuning examples (Weston & Sukhbaatar, 2023; Yu et al., 2024c; Zelikman et al., 2024), or test-time
131   intervention (Wang & Zhou, 2024; Snell et al., 2024). Notably, the recent release of o1-preview model
132   again demonstrated the remarkable success of the CoT paradigm (OpenAI, 2024b). Nevertheless, it
133   remains elusive whether LLMs with the CoT paradigm can model human thoughts from the languages
134   to resolve the complicated System 2 reasoning tasks.
135
136   Understanding Chain-of-Thought reasoning has also attracted a surge of attention from the
137   community to understand the theoretical mechanism and empirical behaviors of CoT (Wang et al.,
138   2023a; Feng et al., 2023; Prabhakar et al., 2024; Merrill & Sabharwal, 2024). Despite the success
139   of CoT, especially, pitfalls have also been found. Kambhampati et al. (2024); Stechly et al. (2024)
140   reveal that CoT can still not resolve complex tasks such as planning, or even lead to decreased
141
      performance (Wang et al., 2024). Moreover, CoT can also exacerbate biases (Shaikh et al., 2023).
      Sprague et al. (2024a) find that CoT primarily helps with the execution of mathematical or logical
142
      calculation instead of planning when solving complex reasoning tasks. Therefore, it calls for a sober
143
      look and understanding of the limitations of the existing CoT paradigm in imitating human reasoning.

148   In this section, we formalize our conjecture on the language-thought modeling gap in LLMs trained
149   via the next-token prediction scheme Brown et al. (2020). To be concrete, we clarify how such a gap
150   forces LLMs to draw conclusions with pretraining-led biases or to not fully use a premise when it is
151   expressed in an implicit way.

      not follow the topological order. When the order is not topological to the causal graph, there at least

206
      language model to learn a biased logic, which we term as language modeling bias.

288   3.2      L ANGUAGE - OF -T HOUGHT P ROMPTING
289
290   To resolve these issues, we propose a novel prompt technique called Language-of-Thoughts (LoT).
291   LoT consists of two important parts: Echo and Expand.
292   Echoing information The first part of the prompt is to observe and echo the relevant information
293   given in the context. The purpose of this part is to encourage the language model to go through
294   the given problem and collect all the relevant premises instead of directly skipping to the thought
295   generation process as the language modeling bias triggers to do so. The intuition is to select proper
296   premises and emphasize them by echoing. This component of LoT is designed to tackle the issue 1.
297   Expanding thoughts After the echo process, we instruct the model the expand those collected
298   information. The purpose is to encourage the language model to make some exploration so that it
299   may have a chance to dig out the implicit information into explicit language. This would enable itself
300   to notice and utilize more necessary premises to find the answer. As an analogy, some analysts would
301   draw more insight from data by visualization, so that some implicit numerical patterns get more more
302   clear. This component of LoT is designed to tackle issue 2.
303   Echo and Expand gives a more comprehensive reasoning Instead of allowing the language model
304   to directly begin the thinking process, we insert two designed instructions to alleviate the shortcut
305   reasoning by transferring the implicit premises into explicit ones. After these processes, the language
306   model could notice and utilize more explicit premises to give comprehensive results.

524   6      C ONCLUSIONS
525
526   In this work, we studied the modeling of thoughts in LLMs to imitate human reasoning. Despite
527   the success of the CoT paradigm, we identified the language-thought modeling gap and formalized
528   the existence of language modeling bias. The intrinsic bias introduced by the next-token prediction
529   training will lead to the failure of LLMs to imitate human thinking and reasoning. To alleviate
530   the gap, we introduced a new prompting technique called LoT, and demonstrated its effectiveness
531   in reducing the language modeling biases during LLM reasoning. Furthermore, we conducted a
532
      comprehensive empirical evaluation of LoT against CoT, and verified the effectiveness of LoT in
      more general reasoning tasks. The advance of LoT over CoT, nevertheless, calls for more attention to
533
      the language-thought modeling gap, and lays the foundation for future investigation in fully bridging
534
      this gap by resolving the fundamental limitations of next-token prediction.
