009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Molecular Relational Learning (MRL) expands the scope of molecular represen-
012            tation learning by incorporating additional molecules, aiming to understand the
013            interactions between pairs of molecules. While MRL has shown promising results,
014            the existing methods have not been able to generalise to real world scenarios. In-
015            variant learning is pivotal in addressing Out-of-Distribution (OOD) generalization
016
               challenges. However, two major obstacles impede the progress of invariant learn-
               ing in MRL: (1) Unlike single-molecular cases, interactions between molecules
017
               introduce added complexity, with a heavy reliance on molecular substructure recog-
018
               nition, often leading to the misspecification of invariant patterns. (2) Accurate
019            modeling of interactions can effectively improve generalizations. However, previ-
020            ous methods focus on node interactions, which is limited by the expressiveness of
021            GNN, and long-range interactions cannot be captured. To address these, we propose
022            a novel Relational Invariant Learning (RIL) framework that uses a multi-granularity
023            interaction approach to improve OOD generalization for MRL, and the framework
024            is denoted as RILOOD. Specifically, we model the environment diversity distri-
025            bution of molecules by mixup-based Conditional Modeling. Then, we employ a
026            multi-granularity refinement strategy to learn the Context-Aware Representation,
027
               which is essential for capturing multi-level interaction. We further design an in-
               variant learning module to capture the invariant patterns that robustly generalize
028
               across unseen environments. Extensive experiments on molecular datasets show
029
               that our method achieves stronger generalization against state-of-the-art methods
030            in the presence of various distribution shifts. Our code will be released after our
031            paper is accepted.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Predicting molecular properties in solvent is crucial, given that most chemical and biological processes
037   occur in solution. Solvent-based molecular property prediction, also referred to as Solute-Solvent
038   Interaction in Molecule Relational Learning (MRL)(Lim & Jung, 2019; Subramanian et al., 2020;
039
      Panwar et al., 2021; Low et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023a;b), has played a pivotal
      role in chemical and biological research, including battery manufacture, pharmaceutical industry
040
      (Chung et al., 2022; Varghese & Mushrif, 2019). It is an evolving field that aims to understand
041
      interactions between solutes and solvents at the molecular-level, allowing for predicting molecular
042   property through a prior. More importantly, it significantly extends the conventional molecular
043   property prediction practices by taking solvent molecular as additional inputs, thereby achieving
044   promising performance and chemical interpretability.
045
      Despite their notable success, existing methods are based on the assumption that training and test
046
      data are sampled from an independent and identical distribution (I.I.D.). However, the real world
047
      is open, diverse, and uncertain. Out-of-Distribution (OOD) refers to scenarios where the test data
048   or new data encountered by a model significantly differ from the training data. For single molecule,
049   OOD can occur not only in the molecule structure itself—such as differences in size or scaffold—but
050   also in the target properties. OOD generalization(Krueger et al., 2021), which seeks to address this
051   challenge by learning invariant representations across multiple environments (e.g., scaffolds, sizes),
052   has garnered significant attention. Typically, the privileged substructure remains invariant concerning
053   a molecular’s properties. However, one important nature of solvated molecules is the non-stationary
      property, indicating that its statistical features are changing over solvent. As shown in Fig.1, previous

106   Molecular Relational Learning(Lim & Jung, 2019; Subramanian et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2023a;b;
107   Pathak et al., 2020), which aims to study the relationship between moleculars, can be divided
      into molecular interaction prediction and Drug-Drug Interaction prediction. Molecular interaction

      prediction, i.e. solvent-based molecular property prediction, includes solvent free energy prediction,
109   solubility prediction, chromophore absorption prediction, and so on. Unlike traditional molecular
110   property prediction, the model need predict the properties exhibited by the same molecular exposed
111   to multisolvent. Recent works leverage message-passing network to encode atomic representations
112   and further improving the interpretability of model using an interaction map(Lee et al., 2023a;b;
113   Pathak et al., 2020).
114
115   2.2   O UT OF D ISTRIBUTION G ENERALIZATION
116
117   Generalizing well-trained method to unseen environment with different data distributions is challeng-
118   ing and promising problem on machine learning due to wide applicability. Current state-of-the-art
119
      approaches can be roughly categorized into three types. (1) Invariant learning method. There are
      plentiful studies in invariant learning without environment labels. However, ZIN(Lin et al., 2022)
120
      argue that it is impossible to identify the invariant features without given environment labels in
121
      Euclidean data, and propose to leverage additional auxiliary information for invariant learning. (2)
122   Causal inference theories utilize Structural Causal Model (SCM)(Chen et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2021)
123   or Independent Causal Mechanism (ICM)(Peters et al., 2017; Gui et al., 2024) assumption to filter out
124   spurious correlation and strengthen the invariant causal patterns. (3) Disentangled learning requires
125   strong prior assumptions that can effectively separating semantic factors into two categories: (i)
126   invariant features that consistently predict the label across distributions, and (ii) spurious features
127   that have unstable correlations with the label. Current methods are mainly to discover and define
128   the invariant factors in the data collection process, and design effective algorithms based on the
129   invariance to guide the model to achieve out-of-distribution generalization.
130
131   2.3   I NVARIANT L EARNING IN M OLECULAR R ELATIONAL L EARNING
132
      Current research on invariant learning in MRL prediction is still sparse. Among these works, one
133
      scheme is the identification of the core substructure(Lee et al., 2023a), which involves utilizing the
134
      minimum sufficient information related to the task according to the principle of graph information
135   bottleneck. Another scheme(Lee et al., 2023b) proposes to learn causal substructure using causal
136   intervention to solve distribution shift. In OOD scenario, assessing model generalization typically
137   involved dividing datasets into scenarios like "unseen solvent" or "unseen domain", where the test
138   set exclusively contained certain bias. However, previous evaluations often remain within the intra-
139   domain framework, which does not fully align with real-world conditions. Despite invariant learning
140   success on graph(Wu et al., 2022a; Yang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022), it still be confined on graph
141   pairs by two critical limitations: (1) Different from Euclidean data such as image, the environmental
142   label of the graph is not easy to obtain. The existing environment is handcrafted or rule-based, not
143
      structured, which could provide insufficient information for capturing the fundamental relations across
      domains from the casual data-generating perspective. (2) Invariant patterns, spurious correlations are
144
      entangled with shortcuts, and latent invariant representations are not easy to decouple.

153   3.1   N OTATIONS AND P ROBLEM F ORMULATION .
154
155   The goal of MRL task is to predict the target label Y given the associated input molecule pairs
156
      (G1 , G2 ). It can be formulated as modeling the conditional distribution p(G1 |G2 ).
157   Problem formulation. Given a dataset D = {((G1i , G2i ), Y i )}N i=1 , where G1 is solute molecule,
158   and G2 is solvent molecule, each molecule pairs is associated with a target label Y . N is the total
159   number of dataset. The objective is to train a model to predict Y based on the input (G1 , G2 ). The
160   model should effectively learn the relationships between the input features and the target variable,
161   leveraging the information from both G1 and G2 to accurately predict Y. The model’s performance
      will be evaluated based on the RMSE of the predicted output Ŷ in comparison to the true labels Y.

      experiments and theoretical analysis prove the superiority of our method.
