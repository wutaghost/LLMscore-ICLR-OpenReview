011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advances in large language model (LLM) pruning have shown state-of-the-
014
               art (SotA) compression results in post-training and retraining-free settings while
               maintaining high predictive performance. However, previous research mainly
015
               considered calibrating based on English text, despite the multilingual nature of
016
               modern LLMs and their frequent use in non-English languages. In this paper,
017            we set out to investigate calibrating the pruning of multilingual language mod-
018            els for monolingual applications. We present the first comprehensive empirical
019            study, comparing different calibration languages for pruning multilingual models
020            across diverse languages, tasks, models, and SotA pruning techniques. Our results
021            offer practical suggestions, for example, calibrating in the target language can ef-
022            ficiently retain the language modeling capability but does not necessarily benefit
023            downstream tasks. Through further analysis of latent subspaces, pruning masks,
024            and individual neurons within pruned models, we find that while pruning generally
025
               preserves strong language-specific features, it may fail to retain language-specific
               neuron activation patterns and subtle, language-agnostic features associated with
026
               knowledge and reasoning that are needed for complex tasks.
027
028
029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   State-of-the-art language models often rely on over-parameterization with millions or billions of
032   parameters, resulting in significant memory and computational demands (Zhang et al., 2017; Allen-
033   Zhu et al., 2019; Xu & Du, 2023). Pruning is a model compression technique that removes unim-
034   portant weights to reduce memory footprint and inference computation (Gholami et al., 2022; Hoe-
035
      fler et al., 2021; Kuzmin et al., 2023). Recent pruning methods for language models, such as
      SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023) and Wanda (Sun et al., 2024), demonstrated SotA perfor-
036
      mance in a post-training and retraining-free setting (Zhu et al., 2023). The pruning process involves
037
      passing a small number of examples, i.e. calibration data, to the model to determine the importance
038   of weights for subsequent pruning (Kuzmin et al., 2022; Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Kuzmin et al.,
039   2023).
040
041
      Notably, existing studies typically use calibration data in English and evaluate the post-pruning per-
      formance in English. On the other hand, most SotA LLMs are multilingual and frequently employed
042
      for tasks in non-English languages (Touvron et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023). It
043
      remains unclear how to calibrate pruning to optimize the post-pruning performance of multi-
044   lingual LLMs on tasks in non-English languages. In other words, the current literature provides
045   little insight into efficient calibration strategies for multilingual LLMs targeting specific non-English
046   languages. For example, if we aim to prune a multilingual LLM and use the pruned LLM for tasks
047   in German, should we calibrate the compression process with text in English, German, or both?
048
      This research, as the first, presents an extensive study investigating the impact of calibration data
049
      language on pruning LLMs for language-specific tasks. Further, we performed a set of analyses
050   at the latent subspace level, the matrix level, and the neuron level, within the pruned models, to
051   investigate the underlying causes for our observations. We summarize our key findings as follows:
052
053          • Calibrating on the target language consistently yields the lowest perplexity (Section 4.1,
               but will not guarantee optimal performance on downstream tasks (Section 4.2).

054
             • Pruning generally impairs the language-agnostic features of multilingual models, such as
055            reasoning capability (Section 4.2) and the storage and retrieval of knowledge (Section 4.3),
056            which are essential for downstream tasks (Section 5).
057
             • Pruning generally affects language-agnostic features, which is potentially associated with
058            reasoning and knowledge storage, more than it impacts language-specific features (Sec-
059            tion 5.1); Pruning struggles to consistently identify essential neurons in the attention output
060            and FFN down projection, potentially responsible for reasoning (Section 5.2); pruning also
061            struggles to retain language-specific neurons with low activation probability (Section 5.3).

      2     R ELATED W ORK
064

      2.1   M ULTILINGUAL L ANGUAGE M ODELS
066
067   Most SotA LMs, such as Llama-3 (Meta AI, 2024) and Phi 3(Abdin et al., 2024), are trained on mul-
068   tilingual data, enabling them to understand and generate text in multiple languages (Huang et al.,
069   2023; Holmström et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Meta AI, 2024). Although these multilingual LMs
070   follow the general training paradigm of training monolingual LMs, they are often found to behave
071   differently or show unique features from monolingual models (Xu et al., 2024). For example, Deng
072
      et al. (2024) reveals that multilingual LMs are prone to generate unsafe outputs on malicious in-
      structions in non-English languages, i.e. multilingual jailbreak. Wang et al. (2023) also identify that
073
      all LLMs tested in their study produce significantly more unsafe responses for non-English queries
074
      than English ones. Furthermore, previous work on model explanation finds that multilingual models
075   are different from counterpart monolingual models in terms of their internal processes, i.e., different
076   importance distributions on the same input (Jørgensen et al., 2022; Zhao & Aletras, 2024). A general
077   conclusion drawn from these studies is that findings from monolingual settings, particularly those in
078   English, are unlikely to hold in multilingual settings involving non-English languages.
079
080   2.2   C ALIBRATION OF P OST- TRAINING P RUNING
081
082   Unlike sparse training (Yuan et al., 2021; Hoang & Liu, 2023; Zhang et al., 2023) or pruning-aware
083   training (Liu et al., 2021), which require iterative training to achieve sparsity, post-training prun-
084
      ing (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2024) does not require training but eliminates redundant
      weights based on the importance of weights that are calculated with calibration data. The retraining-
085
      free feature of post-training pruning makes it a more efficient approach for LLMs.
086
087   Prior research has primarily focused on calculating the importance of weight to optimize the perfor-
088   mance of pruned models, examining importance metrics and importance ranking ranges (Frantar &
089   Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). Two studies have investigated the impact of
090
      calibration data, looking at the quantity of calibration data (Zhang et al., 2024) and the sources of the
      data (Williams & Aletras, 2023). These studies have been limited to English. The effects of pruning
091
      on the multilingual capabilities of language models, and the impact of the language of calibration
092
      data on performance in other target languages remain unknown.
093

095   3     M ETHODOLOGY
096
097   To investigate the impact of the language of calibration data on the pruned model, we compare the
098
      performance (pruning error, Signal-to-noise ratio, perplexity, and downstream tasks) in a range of
      languages between pruned models and their counterpart full-sized models, where different calibra-
099
      tion data are applied.
100
101   Models Our experiments use two SotA LLM families: Llama-3 (Meta, 2024), the open-source
102   SotA at the time of writing, and Aya-23 (Aryabumi et al., 2024), renowned for its multilingual pre-
103   training.As our evaluation focuses on instructed generation tasks, we employ the instruction-tuned
104
      versions of both families (Chrysostomou et al., 2023). Consequently, our experimental setup in-
      cludes Llama-3-instruction models in 8B and 70B parameter sizes, alongside Aya-instruction mod-
105
      els in 8B and 35B parameter sizes.
106
107   Languages We include seven languages in our study: Arabic (AR), German (DE), English (EN),
      Spanish (ES), Russian (RU), Swahili (SW), and Chinese (ZH). This selection spans six language

108
      families, four writing systems, and encompasses both high and mid-resource languages. We include
109   Swahili as an outlier calibration language, as neither Llama-3 or Aya-23 includes it in their pre-
110   training corpora. A summary of languages used in our paper is given in Table 5 in Appendix B.
111
112
      Pruning and Calibration We construct calibration sets in seven different languages from mC4 (Raf-
      fel et al., 2019). Specifically, following Frantar & Alistarh (2023); Sun et al. (2024), we randomly
113
      sample 128 sequences of 8192 tokens for each language, from the mC4 split for that language.1
114
115   We focus on two SotA post-training pruning methods, Wanda (Sun et al., 2024) and SparseGPT
116   (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023). Unless stated otherwise, we prune for 50% unstructured sparsity to
117   impose fewer restrictions while keeping all other hyperparameters as in the original paper.
118   Evaluation Downstream Tasks We compare the performance of the pruned models calibrated on
119   different languages using the perplexity on a subset of the mC4 validation set, and a selection of
120   downstream tasks in different target languages: MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021), MKQA (Longpre
121   et al., 2020), and Belebele (Bandarkar et al., 2023). To better isolate the impact of the calibration
122   language, we also chose mARC and mHellaSwag (Dac Lai et al., 2023) to compare with their
123   original versions, ARC (Clark et al., 2018) and HellaSwag (Zellers et al., 2019) in English for
124
      their low sensitivity to the choice of calibration samples (Williams & Aletras, 2023). These tasks
      primarily assess commonsense reasoning, reading comprehension, and question answering using
125
      multiple choice questions. We present further details of each task in Appendix C. Unless stated
126
      otherwise, we evaluate in a zero-shot setting.
127
128   Implementation Details We adopt the code from Sun et al. (2024) for implementing model pruning.
129   We use EleutherAI Evaluation Harness (Gao et al., 2024) for a robust and reproducible evaluation.
130
      We use Huggingface (Wolf et al., 2020) for loading datasets and models. All experiments are con-
      ducted with at most two NVIDIA A100 GPUs.

344   5     A NALYSIS
345
346   In reviewing Table 1 and Table 7 together, we poist that calibrating on the target language effectively
347   preserves language-related features but not language-agnostic features, such as knowledge memories
348   and reasoning abilities. This implies that the smallest weights or weights of smallest activations
349
      contribute to the model’s nuanced reasoning or knowledge memorization and retrieval processes.
      The rationale here is that both SNR and pruning error measure the extent of model change after
350
      pruning, which is dominated by large values, that pruning aims to preserve. On the other hand,
351
      perplexity reflects the overall language modeling capability. The similar diagonal patterns across the
352   three metrics, SNR, pruning error and perplexity, as shown in Table 1, suggest that calibrating on the
353   target language, i.e. identifying and pruning the smallest values, preserves the next-token prediction
354   capability (perplexity) in the target language, along with the optimal pruning performance in terms
355   of pruning the smallest values (SNR and pruning errors).
356
      However, optimal performance in downstream tasks requires more than just surface-level language
357
      modeling; it also depends on robust reasoning capabilities. The suboptimal performance observed in
358   downstream tasks may be attributed to the degradation of language-agnostic reasoning capabilities.
359   This phenomenon suggests that removing the weights or weights of the smallest activations might
360   inadvertently compromise the model’s ability to process reasoning, underscoring the reasoning role
361   of these pruned weights, i.e. the smallest weights or weights of the smallest activations.
362
      To validate our hypothesis, we investigate the internal changes of pruned models on three different
363
      levels: subspace level, matrix level, neuron level (columns in a matrix, followed by non-linearity).
364   Previous studies on multilingualism of LLMs have sought to separate language-specific features
365   from language-agnostic features, either at the neuron level (Tang et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024;
366   Wang et al., 2024) or from an extracted subspace perspective (Xie et al., 2022). To comprehend
367   the underlying mechanisms driving the disparities in pruning metrics and downstream performance
368   when using different calibration languages, we examine the shifts in language-specific and agnostic
369   features and neurons induced by the pruning process.
370
371   5.1   LANGUAGE - SPECIFIC S UBSPACE R EPRESENTATIONS
372
373   The core idea of Low-rank Subspace for language-Agnostic Representations (LSAR) by Xie et al.
374   (2022) is to construct a mean embedding matrix M ∈ Rd×L by concatenating L language em-
375   beddings. Subsequently, LSAR decomposes M into a vector µ representing shared signals across
376   languages and a matrix Ms specifying a low-rank subspace on which different languages express
377
      different linguistic signals. This decomposition process is achieved via singular value decomposi-

      tion on solving: minµ,Ms ,Γ M − µ1⊤ − Ms Γ⊤ F s.t. µ ⊥ Span(Ms ) with the orthogonality

      constraint. Γ ∈ RL×r represents the coordinates of language-specific signals along the subspace’s
379   r components, and 1 ∈ Rd is a vector of all ones. We can extract language-specific features a
380   from a sentence embedding e using Ms by projecting e into and back from the low-rank, language
381   signal retaining subspace, a = e − s = e − Ms MsT e. See Appendix D.2 for more details and the
382   experiment setup.
383
      We employ LSAR to decomposite the token-wise averaged embedding output of each layer of
384
      SparseGPT-pruned Llama-3 8B into language-specific and language-agnostic features, and estimate
385   their changes after pruning. Figure 1 shows the layer-wise magnitude of differences (∆ magnitude)
386   of (a) language-agnostic features and (b) language-specific features after pruning. From a high-level
387   perspective, the greater ∆ magnitude suggests the greater changes in terms of hidden state after
388   pruning, thus the greater pruning error and the worse pruning performance. Unlike the pruning er-
389   ror presented in Table 1, here we separately analyze the pruning error for language-agnostic and
390   language-specific features. See Figure 5 in Appendix E for layer-wise full plots.
391   First, calibrating on the target language leads to relatively smaller pruning errors in terms of
392   language-specific features as shown in the sub Figure (b) in Figure 1. This phenomenon is ob-
393   served across layers, particularly noticeable in layer 32, as pinpointed by stars in Figure 1. This
394   potentially explains the findings in Section 4.1 that calibration on the target language leads to the
395   lowest perplexity, which is associated with a robust language-specific linguistic modeling capability.
396   On the other hand, as indicated by the relatively flat horizontal lines across languages and layers in
397   sub Figure (a), the pruning error on the language-agnostic features remains similar regardless of
398   the calibration languages. This pattern helps explain the sub-optimal downstream task performance
399
      observed in Table 7, where no single calibration language consistently yields optimal performance
      across downstream tasks, including cases where the calibration is performed on the target language.
400
      That is, the selection of calibration language is unlikely to impact the language-agnostic features
401
      associated with understanding, reasoning, and knowledge retrieval. In contrast, the preservation
402   of language-specific features, such as modeling capabilities related to perplexity, depends on the
403   selection of calibration languages.
404
405
      Second, the middle layers (as shown in the second and third columns in Figure 1) exhibit greater
      ∆ magnitude on language-agnostic feature representations and smaller ∆ magnitude on language-
406
      specific feature representations. This indicates that pruning errors can be predominantly at-
407
      tributed to the pruning errors on language-agnostic features, with less pruning error arising from
408   language-specific features. Therefore, we conclude pruning generally affects language-agnostic
409   features—potentially associated with reasoning and knowledge storage—more significantly than
410   it impacts language-specific features.

      5.3     L ANGUAGE - SPECIFIC N EURONS
479
480   This section investigates neuron-level changes after pruning using Language Activation Probability
481   Entropy (LAPE) as introduced by Tang et al. (2024). We focus on neurons of the up projections of
482   the feed-forward layers (FFNs) followed by the non-linearity. A neuron is considered activated when
483   the non-linearity output is greater than zero. LAPE measures the likelihood of individual neurons
484   activating across different language inputs, taking neurons with high activation probability for one
485   or two languages and lower probabilities for all others (i.e. low LAPE score) as language-specific.
      Details on the LAPE calculation and experiment settings are provided in Appendix D.4.

       6        C ONCLUSION
529
530
       In this paper, we empirically demonstrate that the choice of calibration language influences the
       downstream performance of pruned models across various evaluation languages. Particularly, cal-
531
       ibration with the target language mainly benefits preserving perplexity performance but does not
532
       necessarily help maintain the downstream task performance. Our analysis of changes within the
533    model after pruning indicates that calibrating in the test language does not reliably benefit layers as-
534    sociated with semantic understanding. Additionally, we recommend practitioners do not depend on
535    perplexity for accessing the pruned model, or performance in English to estimate the performance
536    on target languages.

      A    L IMITATIONS
757
758
      Generality of Findings. Due to resource constraints, we predominantly experimented with the
759
      small versions of Llama-3 and Aya-23, and validated our findings with fewer pruning runs on their
760   counterpart large version. Since our results translate between model families, and to bigger model
761   sizes, we assume a certain degree of generalization. Nonetheless, other models trained with different
762   techniques or for other tasks might show different behavior. Given the pace of this research field, it
763   is also unclear whether these results translate to future models.
764
765   Underrepresented Languages. Our experiments focused on languages with sufficient model and
766   downstream task support. However, this selection does not encompass all languages of interest, par-
767   ticularly mid and low-resource languages that are underrepresented in the pre-training, and challeng-
768   ing to evaluate due to the lack of benchmark support. Future research could benefit from including
769   more languages to explore the interplay between different language families or writing systems and
770   performance after pruning.
