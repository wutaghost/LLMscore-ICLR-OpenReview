009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Pre-trained large language models (LLMs) typically undergo instruction fine-tuning
012            to improve alignment. Recent research highlights that the quality and diversity of
013            instruction data are more critical than data quantity, prompting the selection of
014            diverse, high-quality instruction subsets to reduce training costs. However, how to
015            evolve these selected subsets alongside the development of new instruction data
016            remains insufficiently explored. To achieve LLMs’ ongoing alignment, we intro-
017            duce Instruction Bank (InsBank), a continuously updated repository that integrates
018
               the latest valuable instructional data. We further propose Progressive Instruction
               Bank Evolution (PIBE), a novel framework designed to evolve InsBank effectively
019
               and efficiently over time. It firstly employs a gradual data selection strategy to
020
               maintain long-term efficiency, utilizing a representation-based diversity score that
021            captures relationships between data points and retains historical information for
022            comprehensive diversity evaluation. This also allows for flexible combination of di-
023            versity and quality scores during data selection and ranking. Extensive experiments
024            demonstrate that PIBE significantly outperforms baseline methods in evolving
025            InsBank. Additionally, PIBE enables users to flexibly extract smaller subsets based
026            on their specific budget.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Instruction fine-tuning are widely adopted to refine pre-trained LLMs to accurately understand human
032   instructions and provide precise, pertinent and harmless responses (Longpre et al., 2023; Qin et al.,
033
      2024a). LIMA (Zhou et al., 2023) has proved that the quality and diversity of instruction data are
      significantly more critical than its sheer quantity for training, motivating recent efforts in instruction
034
      data selection to reduce unnecessary training costs by eliminating low-quality and redundant data
035
      (Qin et al., 2024a). However, how to evolve the selected instruction subset in parallel with the
036   development of the instruction data remains underexplored.
037
038   Specifically, with the continuous emergence of new instruction datasets, it becomes necessary to
039
      regularly update the instruction subset to incorporate the latest high-quality instruction data, ensuring
      ongoing improvements in the alignment capabilities of LLMs. Simultaneously, the subset size must
040
      be controlled to avoid excessive growth that could lead to increased training costs. To address these
041
      practical challenges, we propose a novel concept termed InsBank (Instruction Bank). InsBank is
042   initially established through a selective process applied to current available instruction data. As
043   new instruction datasets is proposed, the bank evolves by selecting new data while phasing out
044   an equivalent amount of older data, thereby maintaining an optimized instruction subset. Figure
045   1 illustrates this pipeline. Additionally, the data in InsBank should be ordered, allowing users to
046   efficiently extract a smaller subset tailored to their specific training budget based on the ranking.
047
      Quality can be easily scored through manual annotation or model annotation. However, regarding
048   diversity, global measurement between data is required, which demands significant storage and
049   computational costs. Naively, the evolution of subset can be achieved by data re-selection across all
050   available data at each evolution iteration. However, the vast volume of instruction data (Qin et al.,
051   2024a) and its rapid development (Longpre et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023) make
052   the costs in this manner unacceptable. Additionally, since the data in InsBank must be ordered, each
053   instruction requires an individual score for ranking purposes. Existing methods, however, struggle to
      properly represent and combine diversity and quality scores.

076   To address these challenges, we propose Progressive Instruction Bank Evolution (PIBE) to con-
077   tinuously and efficiently select the current optimal instruction subset. Firstly, it employs a gradual
078
      manner of selection to evolve InsBank, ensuring long-term efficiency. Unlike the naive method,
      we significantly reduce costs by excluding the large volume of data already filtered out in previous
079
      iterations, and only require the newly proposed and current instruction data in InsBank to update
080
      it. Based on this foundation, since discarded data can influence the diversity score of new data,
081   preserving historical information throughout the evolution process is essential. Previous diversity-
082   driven data selection methods (Liu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023) can be classified into two categories:
083   k-nearest neighbor (k-NN) (Dong et al., 2011) and geometry-based coreset sampling (Guo et al.,
084   2022). However, both of them rely solely on information from a few surrounding points, making
085   it difficult to record and utilize the rich information of historically eliminated points. For the same
086   reason that they cannot capture the distribution relationships between global points, they also fail
087   to provide robust individual diversity scores for ranking. Inspired by Affinity Propagation (Frey &
088   Dueck, 2007), we pass information between two types of messages, where multiple iterations and
089
      the preservation of historical distribution through similarity propagation enable the diversity score to
      capture relationships between samples, quantifying how well an instruction represents others while
090
      remaining irreplaceable. Furthermore, existing data selection methods either focus on quality or
091
      diversity alone (Chen et al., 2024), or consider them sequentially (Liu et al., 2024), failing to address
092   both simultaneously with equal consideration. Our diversity score can be seamlessly combined with
093   the quality score, enabling comprehensive and flexible instruction selection and ranking.
094
095
      We simulate the process of instruction set development with five datasets: Self-Instruct (Wang et al.,
      2023), Alpaca (Taori et al., 2023), Dolly (Conover et al., 2023), ShareGPT (Chiang et al., 2023) and
096
      WizardLM (Xu et al., 2023). We apply PIBE on them and fine-tune the Llama3-8B (AI@Meta, 2024)
097
      model on the InsBank obtained from each evolution iteration. We evaluate the fine-tuned models
098   on AlpacaEval (Li et al., 2023b) and MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023). Experimental results show
099   that PIBE outperforms the baseline data selection methods and successfully evolves the instruction
100   bank in parallel with the development of instruction sets. Besides, analysis on ordering of InsBank
101   indicates that users can flexibly select a smaller subset based on their budget.

               capabilities and allowing flexible combination with quality scores.

124   2.2   S ELECTION M ETRICS
125
126   Many studies (Liu et al., 2024; Qin et al., 2024a) have highlighted that the effectiveness of instruction
127   set selection depends on both quality and diversity. In line with this understanding, we also focus
128   these two aspects in this paper:
129
130   Quality The quality of instruction data primarily refers to the accuracy and rationality which
131   estimate the consistency and coherence of the instruction context, as well as whether the response
132   accurately corresponds to the instructions (Qin et al., 2024a). Leveraging the strong power of
133   ChatGPT models (i.e. GPT-3.5-turbo and GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023)), recent works typically employ
134
      a GPT-model to annotate the quality of instruction data with a specifically designed prompt (Chen
      et al., 2024). We adopt the quality evaluation model and method from DEITA (Liu et al., 2024) in
135
      this work.
136
137
      Diversity The diversity of dataset is critical to the generalization ability of the trained model (Qin
138
      et al., 2024a). There are currently two major approaches to measuring diversity.: k-nearest neighbor
139
      (k-NN) (Dong et al., 2011) and geometry-based coreset sampling (Guo et al., 2022). The former
140   measures sample’s diversity by its distance (or similarity) to its j-th k-nearest neighbor (k-NN) with
141   the help of text embeddings as shown in Eq. 2:
142
143                                        kN Nij = d(e(xi ), e(Nj (xi )))                                   (2)
144
      where Nj (xi ) denotes the j-th closest neighbor of xi in the embedding space projected by e(·),
145
      and d(·, ·) calculates the euclidean distance between xi and Nj (xi ). The latter is to find the most
146
      informative-and-diverse subset that represents the entire dataset the most through controlling the
147   minimum distance between any two samples for subset selection (Guo et al., 2022; Sener & Savarese,
148   2018). However, both methods rely solely on local information from nearby points, making it difficult
149   to capture the global distribution relationships or utilize historically eliminated points, resulting in
150   inadequate individual diversity scores for subset evalution.
151
152   2.3   A FFINITY P ROPAGATION
153
154   Affinity Propagation (AP) (Frey & Dueck, 2007) is a clustering algorithm that leverages message-
155   passing to uncover the global distribution of the data. It identifies exemplars by iteratively transmitting
156   two kinds of messages between data points:
157
158
             • Responsibility (R[i, k]) This message sent from point i to point k represents how suitable
               point k is to serve as the exemplar for point i.
159
160          • Availability (A[i, k]) This message sent from point k to point i represents how appropriate
161            it would be for point i to choose point k as its exemplar, taking into account the current
               responsibilities sent from other points to k.

294   The individual representation score encapsulates the exemplar election information, reflecting both
295   the willingness of other samples to be represented by a specific sample and the unwillingness of the

      5    R ELATED W ORK
487
488
      Instruction Fine-Tuning are widely adopted to stimulate the instruction following capability of pre-
489   trained LLMs. Early approaches focused on fine-tuning LLMs with large amounts of instruction data
490   (Wei et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022) manually aggregated from large NLP task collections (Longpre
491   et al., 2023). With the development of generative language models, Wang et al. (2023) made their
492   attempt to expand instruction data through synthetic data generation, inspiring the following works
493   to evolve instruction data in this automated manner (Taori et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2023; Xu et al.,
494   2023). Zhou et al. (2023) proved that the quality and diversity of instruction data are significantly
495   more critical than its sheer quantity, motivating recent efforts in instruction data selection to remove
496   unnecessary training costs by eliminating low-quality and redundant data. Existing data selection
497
      methods can be systematically categorized into three types (Qin et al., 2024a):
498
499
      Quality-based Selection The quality of instruction data primarily refers to the accuracy and
      rationality which estimate the consistency and coherence of the instruction context, as well as whether
500
      the response accurately corresponds to the instructions. Humpback (Li et al., 2023a) selects high-
501
      quality samples through an iterative self-curation process where quality predictions are produced by
502   the fine-tuned model of each turn. Recent works typically employ a GPT-model to annotate the data
503   quality. For example, ALPAGASUS Chen et al. (2024) employs ChatGPT to score the accuracy of
504   instruction data and select data according to a threshold.
505
506   Diversity-based Selection The diversity-based selection aims to deduplicate the instruction data
507   and maximize the coverage of selected data. Recent methods typically achieve this purpose by control
508   the nearest neighbor distance (Liu et al., 2024) or maximize the average distance between the selected
509   data through text embedding (Wu et al., 2023). INSTAG (Lu et al., 2024) identifies semantics and
510   intentions of instructions by tags and it assumes that a dataset is considered more diverse if it covers
511
      more individual tags.
512
513
      Model-specific Importance-based Selection The importance refers to the necessity of adding one
      sample into the training set (Liu et al., 2024) whose indicator are typically model-specific (Xia et al.,
514
      2024; Li et al., 2024a). However, in this work we focus on the general data selection scenarios and
515
      emphasize the quality and diversity of selected data.
516
517   InfoGrowth (Qin et al., 2024b) also aims to address the continuous expansion of datasets, but it
518   primarily deals with image data and focuses on relabeling noisy samples, making it less relevant to this
519
      paper. InfoGrowth and DEITA take both data quality and diversity into consideration. However, both
      of them handle the two aspects sequentially and fail to combine them together as an overall individual
520
      score. Besides, previous efforts primarily aggregate all candidate data first before performing data
521
      selection and are not experimented under the progressive instruction bank evolution task. While
522   in this paper, we propose PIBE to efficiently obtain the optimal current instruction subset with
523   comprehensive characterization and integration of diversity and quality scores.
524
525
526
      6    C ONCLUSION
527
528
      In this paper, we introduced the concept of the Instruction Bank (InsBank) to address the ongoing
      challenge of evolving instruction datasets for large language models (LLMs). Our proposed Progres-
529
      sive Instruction Bank Evolution (PIBE) framework facilitates the effective and efficient evolution of
530
      InsBank by integrating new, high-quality instruction data while ensuring long-term scalability and
531   efficiency. By leveraging a representation-based diversity score that retains historical information,
532   PIBE provides a robust mechanism for comprehensive diversity evaluation, enabling the flexible
533   combination of diversity and quality scores in the process of data selection and ranking. Extensive
534   experimental results demonstrate that PIBE significantly outperforms baseline methods, providing
535   more optimal and adaptable instruction subsets. Furthermore, the framework’s flexibility allows users
536   to extract smaller, tailored subsets based on their specific budget constraints, contributing not only to
537   cost-effective training but also to the ongoing refinement and alignment of LLMs. This approach
538   paves the way for more dynamic and adaptable instruction tuning strategies, enhancing both the
539
      efficiency and effectiveness of LLM development over time.

      Fine-grained Quality and Complexity Scoring We adopt the quality predictor 4 provided by Liu
759   et al. (2024) to score the instructions.

761   Representation-based Progressive Data Selection: During the PIBE data selection process, we
762
      set the momentum coefficient α = 0.5, the momentum decaying rate λ = 0.99, the damping rate
      β = 0.5 and the weighting coefficient γ = 1. Besides, we adopt instruction embedding (Li et al.,
763
      2024b) to encode the instructions. As for affinity propagation, we use negative euclidean distance to
764
      initialize the similarity matrix and fill the diagonal of similarity matrix with 0.
765
766   Instruction Fine-Tuning: We utilize 8 NVIDIA A100 SXM4 40GB GPUs to fine-tune Llama3 8B
767   model. We employ LlamaFactory (Zheng et al., 2024), DeepSpeed Zero-Stage 3 (Ren et al., 2021)
768
      and fp16 precision to facilitate the training process. We adopt the Llama3-style template, and set
      the effective batch size to 128 (per device train batch size=1 and gradient accumulation steps=16),
769
      training epochs to 6, learning rate to 1e-5, warmup ratio to 0.1 and maximum input length to 2048.
770
771   Evaluation: For AlpacaEval inference, we set temperature=0.7, top p=0.9, top k=40, num beams=1
772   and max length=512. For MT-Bench inference, we follow the default setting of FastChat5 except
773   for that max length is set to 512. Llama3 template is applied to both AlpacaEval inference and
774
      MT-Bench inference. For Open LLM LeaderBoard evaluation, we adopt the code of LM Evaluation
      Harness(Gao et al., 2024)6 and follow the setting from https://huggingface.co/spaces/
775
      open-llm-leaderboard-old/open_llm_leaderboard.
