010                                                A BSTRACT
011
012            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated exceptional capabilities in
013            solving various tasks, progressively evolving into general-purpose assistants. The
014            increasing integration of LLMs into society has sparked interest in whether they
015            exhibit psychological patterns, and whether these patterns remain consistent across
016            different contexts—questions that could deepen the understanding of their behav-
017
               iors. Inspired by psychometrics, this paper presents a comprehensive benchmark
               for quantifying psychological constructs of LLMs, encompassing psychological
018
               dimension identification, assessment dataset design, and assessment with results
019
               validation. Our work identifies five key psychological constructs—personality,
020            values, emotional intelligence, theory of mind, and self-efficacy—assessed through
021            a suite of 13 datasets featuring diverse scenarios and item types. We uncover signif-
022            icant discrepancies between LLMs’ self-reported traits and their response patterns
023            in real-world scenarios, revealing complexities in their behaviors. Our findings also
024            show that some preference-based tests, originally designed for humans, could not
025            reliably analyze LLMs’ response patterns. This paper offers a thorough psychome-
026            tric assessment of LLMs, providing insights into reliable evaluation and potential
027            applications in AI and social sciences. Our dataset and code can be accessed via
028
               this link.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The development of large language models (LLMs) has marked a milestone in artificial intelligence
034   (AI) (Bommasani et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2023a). LLMs demonstrate remarkable performance
035   beyond traditional natural language processing (NLP) tasks (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Qin et al., 2023),
036   with remarkable problem-solving (Yao et al., 2024; Shen et al., 2024) and decision-making abilities
037   (Li et al., 2022a; Shinn et al., 2024). The evolving capabilities of LLMs facilitate their expansion into
038   broader real-world applications (Ma et al., 2023a; Mehandru et al., 2024), directing a significant shift
039
      from software tools to general-purpose assistants for humans (Qian et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024).
      It is thus crucial to move beyond merely evaluating performance on specific tasks. Inspired by how
040
      psychology facilitates the understanding of human behaviors, we investigate psychology in LLMs,
041
      aiming to better describe and predict the behaviors of LLMs.
042
043   Psychometrics, a systematic evaluation framework, emerges as a promising tool for assessing the
044   psychological patterns of LLMs (Jones and Thissen, 2006; Rust and Golombok, 2014; Huang et al.,
045
      2024; Wang et al., 2023a). It is distinguished by its predictive power and rigorous measurement
      (Wang et al., 2023a). Psychometrics evaluates psychological dimensions, termed constructs, which
046
      are the hypothesized factors to explain and predict the behaviors of humans (Embretson and Reise,
047
      2013; Slaney, 2017; Cronbach and Meehl, 1955; Wang et al., 2023a). For instance, personality
048   has been shown to predict extensive social outcomes such as career choices and criminal behaviors
049   (Ozer and Benet-Martinez, 2006; Strickhouser et al., 2017). Leveraging the predictive power of
050   psychometrics, we intend to identify psychological dimensions and provide insights into the behaviors
051   of LLMs. Additionally, psychometrics emphasizes the importance of evaluation quality by measuring
052   the reliability of the tests (Rust and Golombok, 2014). We extend the psychometric test quality
053   assurance framework to determine whether reliable conclusions can be drawn from our tests and to
      shed light on the sensitivity and variability of LLMs’ behaviors (Xiao et al., 2023).

      As LLMs increasingly fulfill roles as general-purpose assistants, there is a growing research interest
055   in quantifying their psychological patterns (Jiang et al., 2024a; Safdari et al., 2023; Huang et al.,
056   2023; Jiang et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023b; Sabour et al., 2024; Kosinski, 2023; van Duijn et al.,
057   2023; Wu et al., 2023). Existing evaluations mainly focus on specific dimensions, such as personality
058   (Bodroza et al., 2023; Safdari et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023a) or theory of mind
059   (Kosinski, 2023; van Duijn et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023). In addition, Miotto et al. (2022) provided the
060   initial efforts of psychological assessments for dimensions of personality, values, and demographics
061   in GPT-3. Huang et al. (2024) explored psychological portrayals of LLMs, examining dimensions of
062   personality traits, interpersonal relationships, motivational tests, and emotional abilities.
063   However, there are still two challenges that hinder a holistic understanding of LLM psychology:
064
      • Existing benchmarks lack diversity and comprehensiveness in both assessment scenarios and
065
        item types, limiting the analysis of LLM behaviors across various contexts (Miotto et al., 2022;
066
        Huang et al., 2024). Most tests only involve self-reported questions (i.e., requiring LLMs to
067     rate themselves), which constrains the exploration of their psychological tendencies in real-world
068     situations. Additionally, since users primarily interact with LLMs through open-ended questions, it
069     is crucial to understand how these models exhibit their psychological patterns through open-ended
070     responses rather than through closed-form answers.
071   • Concerns persist regarding the reliability of the tests. These concerns have two aspects: (1) It is
072     unclear whether psychometric tests designed for humans apply to LLMs. Psychometrics assumes
073     the existence of psychological attributes in humans, indicating a certain degree of behavioral
074     consistency. However, there is a lack of evidence supporting the consistency of these psychological
075
        patterns in LLMs. For instance, questions arise such as whether LLMs consistently respond to
        similar situations, whether their preferences for closed-form questions correlate with their responses
076
        to open-ended ones, and whether their tendencies remain robust against adversarial attacks; (2) It
077
        remains uncertain whether the tests are subject to measurement errors. Besides potential problems
078     caused by position bias (Zheng et al., 2023) and prompt sensitivity (Huang et al., 2024), our use
079     of LLM-as-a-judge (Zheng et al., 2023) approach for the open-ended responses raises concerns
080     about the reliability of LLM raters. To address these challenges, we present a comprehensive
081     psychometric benchmark to investigate psychology in LLMs, which encompasses dimension
082     identification, dataset design, and assessment with results validation. We administer evaluations
083     across five psychological dimensions: personality, values, emotional intelligence, theory of mind,
084     and self-efficacy, and discuss how psychometrics can assist in evaluating the intelligence of LLMs.
085   Findings. Our investigation of nine popular LLMs across thirteen datasets yields the following
086   insights and findings regarding the aforementioned challenges:
087
088
      • Reliability of Psychometric Tests for LLMs. Psychometric datasets, originally designed for humans,
        do not necessarily yield meaningful conclusions for LLMs. Some models respond inconsistently to
089
        similar situations, making it unreliable to determine the psychological patterns of LLMs based on
090
        these responses. Therefore, we cannot truly attribute certain patterns to LLMs. This finding also
091     emphasizes the importance of robust evaluation frameworks to discern genuine model capabilities
092     from statistical randomness.
093   • Discrepancies between closed-form and open-ended responses. LLMs exhibit discrepancies in
094     psychological tendencies when responding to closed-form versus open-ended questions. For exam-
095     ple, a model might score low on extraversion in closed-form assessments but display extraversion
096     in open-ended responses. This pattern is also observed in humans, where individuals may provide
097     socially desirable answers on rating scales, while open-ended questions allow for more nuanced
098     expressions that better reflect complex thoughts (Hift, 2014; Baburajan et al., 2022). LLMs may
099
        simulate responses based on their training data, and open-ended queries might more accurately
        reveal the model’s underlying generation patterns. These differences highlight inconsistencies in
100
        the model’s learned behavior, suggesting that LLMs lack an internal representation that aligns their
101
        self-reported answers with their responses to real-world questions.
102   • Position bias and prompt sensitivity. We provided a more comprehensive perspective on the prompt
103     sensitivity problem. The influence of option position bias is almost negligible for models such as
104     GPT-4 and Llama3-70b, whereas it is more pronounced in models like ChatGPT and Llama3-8b.
105     Moreover, LLMs exhibit varying degrees of prompt sensitivity in psychometric tests. While most
106     models handle simple substitutions (e.g., noun changes) with minimal impact, logical alterations
107     often lead to inconsistent outcomes. Additionally, models are particularly vulnerable to prompt
        perturbations when facing challenging questions.

      2      O UR F RAMEWORK OF P SYCHOMETRIC B ENCHMARK
135
      Our work links to psychometrics by treating LLMs as respondents in structured evaluations, similar
136
      to psychological tests, to analyze their reasoning, consistency, and biases in decision-making tasks.
137   Although LLMs are trained on extensive datasets that encompass human opinions and thoughts, it
138   is essential to recognize the fundamental differences between humans and LLMs when conducting
139   psychometric assessments. First, humans can reflect their genuine feelings and thoughts derived from
140   personal experiences, whereas LLMs lack such mechanisms; LLMs’ responses reflect “a multitude of
141   characters” from their training data (Shanahan et al., 2023). Second, LLMs are highly sensitive to
142   prompt perturbations that humans might find trivial (Lin, 2024; Sclar et al., 2024). Acknowledging
143   these differences, we present our framework for a psychometric benchmark for LLMs, consisting of
144   three crucial components: psychological dimension identification, assessment dataset design, and
145
      assessment with results validation, as shown in Fig. 1.
146
147   2.1      P SYCHOLOGICAL D IMENSION I DENTIFICATION
148
      We identify psychological dimensions that could explain and predict the behaviors of LLMs. We
149
      adopt a top-down approach to identify dimensions, which involves drawing on psychological theories
150   and analogies between humans and LLMs (Hankin and Abela, 2005; Raykov and Marcoulides,
151   2011). Specifically, we initially draw upon social science and psychology literature as sources of
152   supporting theories for dimension identification. However, this analogy may not always hold due
153   to the differences between humans and AI models. To bridge this gap, we establish the following
154   guidelines for identifying psychological dimensions for LLMs:
155
      • Appropriateness: This guideline suggests that psychological dimensions should be appropriate and
156     valid constructs to predict behaviors. One example of an inappropriate dimension is astrological
157     signs. Though popular in some cultural contexts for predicting traits, astrological signs lack
158     scientific credibility in psychology and show no consistent impact on human behavior or cognition.
159     In contrast, psychological dimensions that are grounded in scientific theories or empirical evidence
160     possess predictive power that can effectively explain behaviors.
161   • Meaningfulness: This guideline asserts that psychological dimensions should be relevant to the
        capabilities or functions of LLMs that yield meaningful assessment results. For instance, emotional

        variability can be a psychological dimension for humans, influencing behaviors in high-stakes
163     environments. However, applying the same concept to LLMs is not meaningful, as emotions in
164     humans arise from biological mechanisms that LLMs do not possess. Conversely, the ability to
165     understand emotion is meaningful for both humans and AI; it enables AI chatbots to comprehend
166     user requests more effectively.
167
      Following these guidelines, we develop datasets to evaluate five psychological dimensions: person-
168
      ality, values, emotional intelligence, theory of mind, and self-efficacy. Additionally, we provide a
169   separate discussion on intelligence, an important and well-studied dimension, in Appx. H.

      2.2    A SSESSMENT DATASET D ESIGN
172
173   For evaluating these psychological dimensions, we curate datasets using three sources: standard
174   psychometrics tests, established datasets, and self-designed scenarios. In total, 13 datasets (shown
175   in Table 1) are curated with the guidelines detailed in Appx. A. These datasets are curated to
176   comprehensively assess each psychological dimension, facilitating an in-depth understanding of
177   LLMs’ behaviors. The construction of each dataset follows the procedure involving content curation,
178   item design, and prompt design.
179   Content Curation. The contents of the datasets are either sourced from standard psychometric
180   tests or based on established theories. These theories not only validate the datasets but also guide
181   the enhancement of dataset diversity. For instance, research on the Theory of Mind (ToM) involves
182   multifaceted tasks encompassing various scenarios and different levels of ToM reasoning. This
183   informs our inclusion of a diverse range of scenarios and reasoning levels in ToM problems.
184
      Item Design. One innovation of this benchmark is its capacity to uncover the psychological patterns
185   of LLMs under various evaluation settings, such as self-reported and real-world scenarios. This is
186   achieved by using varied item types to assess a psychological dimension. For instance, to evaluate
187   personality, we incorporate both rating-scale Big Five Inventory and open-ended vignette tests. This
188   approach enables a direct comparison between LLMs’ self-evaluation scores and their narrative
189   responses to real-world scenarios.
190
      Prompt Design. The prompt design includes system prompts, instruction prompts, and answer rules,
191   each tailored to different item types. We manually craft each prompt and subsequently test it with
192   various LLMs to verify that it accurately conveys the intended task. Detailed information about the
193   prompt design process is provided in the respective evaluation sections and the appendix.

215   Model Selection. We assess nine popular LLMs regarding the identified psychological dimensions on

217   2023b), GLM4 (AI, 2024), Qwen-Turbo (Bai et al., 2023), Mistral-7b (Jiang et al., 2023b), Mixtral

220
      Results Validation. We conduct rigorous validation to ensure that the assessment results are
221
      reliable and interpretable (Rust and Golombok, 2014). Extending the reliability considerations in
222
      psychometrics, we focus on five forms of reliability: internal consistency, parallel forms reliability,
223   inter-rater reliability, option position robustness, and adversarial attack robustness (more discussions
224   in Appx. B). Here, we outline the approaches for the reliability check:
225
226   • Internal Consistency: Measures whether LLMs respond consistently to questions examining
227
        the same aspect. Low consistency indicates inconsistent responses, limiting result validity and
        generalizability (Hays and Revicki, 2005).
228
      • Parallel Forms Reliability: Examines whether different versions of a test yield similar results. Low
229
        reliability suggests sensitivity to variations such as paraphrasing, reducing test generalizability.
230   • Inter-Rater Reliability: Evaluates agreement between raters (e.g., GPT-4, Llama3-70b). High
231     reliability ensures consistent assessment and valid interpretation of open-ended responses.
232   • Option Position Robustness: Assesses if answer arrangement in multiple-choice tests biases
233     outcomes. Low robustness implies susceptibility to position bias, reducing assessment reliability.
234   • Adversarial Attack Robustness: Tests LLMs’ resistance to adversarial prompts. Low robustness
235     indicates vulnerability to deceptive inputs, risking reliability in real-world scenarios.

241   Personality is a set of characteristics that influences an individual’s cognition, emotion, motivation,
242   and behaviors (Friedman and Schustack, 1999). In psychometrics, personality assessments effectively
243   depict and predict human behaviors (Ozer and Benet-Martinez, 2006; Strickhouser et al., 2017).
244

245

246
      assessments and evaluate their consistency. We also administer vignette tests to investigate their
247   responses to real-world scenarios. Furthermore, we use role-playing prompts to investigate how such
248   prompts influence their personality.

309   4    E VALUATION ON VALUES
310
311
      Human values are “internalized cognitive structures that guide choices by evoking a sense of basic
312   principles of right and wrong, a sense of priorities, and a willingness to make meaning and see
313   patterns” (Oyserman, 2015). Unlike humans, LLMs do not innately develop values; instead, their
314   values are derived from patterns in the training data they have been exposed to (Shanahan et al.,
315   2023), i.e., LLMs do not “hold” values but reflect patterned responses based on the data. Given that
316   LLMs are trained on extensive text corpora, it is important to investigate what culturally-specific
317   values they exhibit. Analyzing these values ensures that LLMs align with ethical standards and
318   societal norms. We also examine LLM decision-making in scenarios involving moral dilemmas and
319   trade-offs between human benefits and other considerations. Additionally, we assess the robustness
320
      of human-centered values against adversarial perturbations. We probe values in LLMs across three
      sub-dimensions: cultural orientation, moral values, and human-centered values.
321

323   sub-dimension of values: (1) Evaluation of cultural orientation. We use the “Dimensions of Culture
      Questionnaire” from the GLOBE project (House, 2004), which assesses cultural orientation through

      6      E VALUATION ON T HEORY OF M IND
413
414
415   Theory of Mind (ToM) refers to the ability to attribute mental states to oneself and others, essential for
416   effective communication and interaction (Premack and Woodruff, 1978; Baron-Cohen et al., 1985).
417
      ToM involves reasoning about others’ thoughts and beliefs to predict their behaviors (Baron-Cohen
      et al., 1985). We apply the concept of ToM to LLMs to investigate whether they can infer perspectives
418
      and thoughts from textual scenarios. Different from humans, where ToM is a fundamental cognitive
419
      ability, evaluation of ToM in LLMs is to understand their reasoning abilities in textual scenarios
420   based on linguistic cues and patterns. Additionally, we examine the performance consistency of ToM
421   abilities across different tasks and real-world scenarios.
422
423
      Setup. To evaluate ToM in LLMs, we conduct three tests, spanning various scenarios that require
      different orders of ToM reasoning: (1) Evaluation on false belief task. This task assesses the ability
424
      to understand that others hold incorrect beliefs (Kosinski, 2023). Our false belief task comprised two
425
      sub-tasks: unexpected content task and unexpected transfer task, with all items being alternative-
426   choice. (2) Evaluation on strange story task. The strange stories scenarios cover seven non-literal
427   language uses (e.g., metaphors) that can be misinterpreted without ToM (van Duijn et al., 2023).
428   Each item contains an open-ended question, asking about the understanding of the protagonists’
429   thoughts. We also use LLM raters, GPT-4 and Llama3-70b, to evaluate the responses with reference
430   answers. (3) Evaluation on imposing memory task. This task includes alternative-choice items
431   with statements about the intentionality of characters in the scenario, and LLMs should judge if the
      statements correctly reflect the characters’ intentions.


                                                                       8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      Results. We include detailed discussions in Appx. F and summarize our key findings here. As
433   illustrated in Table 25, GPT-4 and Llama3-70b achieve remarkable performance over all ToM tests.
434   In contrast, ChatGPT, GLM4, and Mixtral-8*7b exhibit great performance variability across tests.
435   For example, GLM4 excels at unexpected content tasks but struggles with unexpected transfer tasks.
436   Similarly, Mixtral-8*7b has an 83.3% accuracy rate on imposing memory test but performs poorly on
437   the unexpected transfer test. These results indicate that while some LLMs have abilities in ToM tasks,
438   they lack a comprehensive set of capabilities to handle a wide range of ToM challenges.
439   Validation. We conduct rigorous test validation for the reliability of results for LLMs in ToM tasks.
440   For test (1), we validate two forms of reliability: (i) Position bias robustness. Table 26 shows most
441   LLMs demonstrate robustness against position bias, evidenced by high match rate (M R) (defined
442   in Equation 3). However, Llama3-8b and Mistral-7b show low M R scores, indicating significant
443   performance inconsistency. (ii) Parallel form consistency. To mitigate biases from word order
444   and language tendencies, we modify the false belief task by swapping labels on the container and
445   its contents in the scenario. Achieving consistent results in these modified tasks is essential for
446   determining ToM capabilities. Table 27 reveals that models such as Mixtral-8*7b display low M R
447
      values, demonstrating poor consistency and randomness in their responses. In test (2), we assess
      inter-rater reliability, and we propose a metric termed agreement rate (AR) as “similarity” between
448
      two evaluations (defined in Equation 4). Table 28 shows LLM raters have high consensus with AR
449
      values above 0.8 for all models. Therefore, we conclude that LLM raters can reliably evaluate the
450   responses with reference answer in our cases. In test (3), we evaluate parallel form reliability by
451   altering the names and genders of characters in the stories. This modification prevents LLMs from
452   associating specific mental states with a character in alternative-choice tasks. We employ the M R
453   score (defined in Equation 3) to assess the parallel form’s reliability. As shown in Table 29, all
454   models record M R values of above 0.9, which validates the parallels form reliability of the test. High
455   parallel forms reliability demonstrates that LLMs can consistently provide reliable answers despite
456   variations in items, such as changes in nouns, highlighting their genuine capability to address such
457   challenges. In general, similar to emotion intelligence tests, we find that the reliability of ToM tests is
458
      high. Therefore, it indicates that LLMs are more consistent in responding to ability-based evaluations,
      where ground truth labels are predetermined.

      Self-efficacy is defined as the belief to overcome challenges (Bandura, 1977), and we interpret this
      notion as the perceived capability or “confidence” of LLMs to handle user queries. In this section,
466
      we explore the self-efficacy of LLMs across various user query types and examine whether the
467
      self-efficacy they report aligns with their responses to actual queries.
468
469   Setup. To explore the self-efficacy of LLMs, we conduct two tests: (1) Evaluation of self-reported
470   LLM self-efficacy. We create LLM Self-Efficacy questionnaire that gauges LLMs’ self-
471
      reported confidence in handling queries that are challenging or beyond their capabilities. Query types
      are identified by Gao et al. (2024), including real-time data retrieval and specialized professional
472
      queries. (2) Evaluation of operational LLM self-efficacy. We utilize the H ONE S ET dataset (Gao et al.,
473
      2024), which consists of 930 user queries across the same six query types. This evaluation determines
474   whether LLMs display confidence or recognize their limitations in response to specific queries. We
475   introduce a metric termed confidence rate, defined as the likelihood of LLMs successfully responding
476   to a query without admitting limitations (detailed in Appx. G).
477
      Results. Tests (1) and (2) assess self-efficacy, or “confidence” of LLMs through different evaluation
478
      scenarios. Test (1) employs the self-reported questionnaire for LLMs to rate their confidence, whereas
479

506   8    R ELATED W ORK
507
508   Burnell et al. (2023) found that the performance of LLMs can be explained by a small number
509   of latent constructs. Existing evaluations have explored specific psychological constructs such as
510
      personality (Bodroza et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023a), emotion (Zhan et al., 2023; Sabour et al.,
      2024), and theory of mind (Kosinski, 2023; van Duijn et al., 2023), with detailed discussions in
511
      Appx. I. Other studies investigate a broader scope of constructs, such as Miotto et al. (2022) on
512
      GPT-3, assessing personality, values, and demographics, and Huang et al. (2024) covering personality,
513   relationships, motivations, and emotional abilities. However, not enough attention has been paid to
514   reliability and the interpretation of results. On the other hand, some prior works are conceptually
515   related to ours in suggesting reliability examinations for evaluation. For example, Jacobs and Wallach
516   (2021) and Wang et al. (2023a) emphasized the importance of stable, reliable measurements in AI
517   through psychometric frameworks. Van der Wal et al. (2024) discussed key reliability measures
518   such as test-retest reliability to ensure that the biases identified are not caused by random noise or
519   inconsistencies. Building on these insights, we integrate reliability examination as a key element of
520   our benchmark.

522   9    C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this paper, we present a comprehensive psychometric benchmark for LLMs, covering the evalu-
525   ation of five psychological dimensions and thirteen datasets to assess their psychological patterns.
526   Different from existing studies, our psychometric benchmark challenges the assumption of consistent
527   responses—central to human psychometrics—by testing LLMs across diverse evaluation scenarios,
528   including self-reported questionnaires, open-ended questions, and multiple-choice questions. Our
529   work not only focuses on examining the response tendencies of LLMs but also proposes a rigorous re-
530
      liability framework for validating results. Our findings highlight the diversity and variability of LLMs
      across evaluation scenarios. This variability undermines the validity of certain psychometric tests
531
      in eliciting consistent response patterns and poses a challenge for evaluations to remain unaffected
532
      by statistical randomness. Based on these findings, we offer insights to the AI and social science
533   communities and explore potential applications. Limitations and future directions are discussed in
534   Appx. J.

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      This paper provides a comprehensive analysis of LLMs to better understand and predict their
543
      behaviors through the lens of psychometrics. It carries significant social and ethical implications.
544   Our psychometrics benchmark enhances LLM evaluation by identifying biases and inconsistencies,
545   promoting more ethically responsible AI (Yao et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024; Gallegos et al., 2024).
546   It also supports the development of personalized AI assistants in sectors such as healthcare and
547   education (Kasneci et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023) and enhances public trust by improving user
548   experience. However, we are aware of the potential risks of misuse and misinterpretation of the results
549   from our benchmark. One potential misinterpretation is the humanization of LLMs, leading to beliefs
550   that LLMs are already capable or have reached human-level intelligence. Misinterpreting LLM
551   capabilities might lead to unrealistic expectations, such as assuming these models can make moral
552
      judgments or replace human decision-making in critical areas like healthcare or law. This can result in
      over-dependence and neglect of human oversight. Additionally, these misinterpretations could be used
553
      to spread misinformation, automate and scale biased decision-making, or even develop manipulative
554
      technologies under the guise of advanced AI. One potential way to mitigate such problems is through
555   psychology-related safety evaluations (Zhang et al., 2024). This approach examines stereotypes,
556   discriminatory practices, and deceptive behaviors of LLMs.

       A    G UIDELINES FOR DATASET
1135
1136
       Our benchmark includes 13 datasets from three sources: standard psychometrics tests, established
1137   datasets, and self-designed scenarios. In developing datasets, we adhere to the following guidelines:
1138
1139   • Authoritative and Established Datasets: The psychometrics datasets used in our benchmark
1140
         are both authoritative and well-established. We select datasets that are widely recognized in
         psychology research to enhance the authority of our assessments. For instance, we utilize the Big
1141
         Five personality test (John et al., 1999), which is a standard personality assessment. In contrast,
1142
         we exclude the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) from our personality evaluations due to its
1143     limited use in scientific research and ongoing debates regarding its validity. In our benchmark, we
1144     ensure that the questions in self-curated datasets are grounded on established principles.
1145   • Comprehensive Evaluation of Each Dimension: Our datasets are designed to assess wide aspects
1146     of each dimension, incorporating various tasks to thoroughly evaluate the performance of LLMs.
1147     In the theory of mind dimension, for example, we incorporate false beliefs, strange stories, and
1148     imposing memory tasks. These tasks assess both first-order and higher-order theory of mind
1149     capabilities, offering a comprehensive view of this dimension in LLMs.
1150   • Diverse Dataset Items: Our dataset diversity is further enhanced by including a variety of scenarios
1151
         and item types. These scenarios mimic real-world situations, providing insights into how LLMs
         respond to diverse circumstances. The item types—including alternative-choice, multiple-choice,
1152
         rating-scale, and open-ended items—are chosen to tailor specific needs of measuring psychological
1153
         attributes. For instance, we use rating scales to assess cultural orientations. This item type captures
1154     the intensity of values and preferences on a continuum, allowing for precise interpretations of
1155     LLMs’ cultural orientations.
1156
1157
1158   B    R ESULTS VALIDATION
1159
1160   Results validation in psychometrics ensures that tests produce reliable and interpretable results. A
1161   fundamental principle of psychometrics in test validation is reliability, defined as the degree to
1162
       which a test is free from error (Rust and Golombok, 2014). Reliability pertains to the consistency
       of a test under various conditions, including over time (test-retest reliability), across different
1163
       versions (parallel forms reliability), and among different evaluators (inter-rater reliability).Due to
1164
       the differences between humans and LLMs, applying psychometric tests to LLMs poses unique
1165   challenges. Therefore, we extend reliability considerations from psychometrics and focus on five
1166   forms of reliability. Internal consistency, parallel forms reliability, and inter-rater reliability are
1167   derived from psychometrics and assist in ensuring trustworthy interpretation of results. While
1168   option position robustness and adversarial attack robustness are specifically designed for LLMs,
1169   their concepts are interconnected with reliability in the psychometric framework. Option position
1170   robustness assesses the extent to which the arrangement of options in multiple-choice items influences
1171   assessment outcomes. It can be considered a type of parallel forms reliability, involving items that
1172   probe the same construct but with shuffled option positions. Adversarial attack robustness represents
1173
       the extent to which LLMs remain unaffected by adversarial prompts. While these adversarial forms
       can be validated through parallel forms reliability to check if they measure the same construct, the core
1174
       idea is to compare LLM performance with and without adversarial attacks. This assessment provides
1175
       an additional dimension to understand LLM behavior, particularly their resilience to deceptive inputs,
1176   which is critical for real-world applications. Below is the detailed description of each reliability
1177   measure:
1178
1179
       • Internal Consistency refers to the degree of homogeneity among test items (Hays and Revicki,
         2005). It assesses whether LLMs exhibit consistent preferences in response to questions examining
1180
         the same aspect. Low internal consistency suggests that LLMs respond inconsistently to similar
1181
         contexts, invalidating evaluation results and limiting their generalizability.
1182   • Parallel Forms Reliability assesses whether two different yet equivalent versions of a test yield
1183     consistent results, reflecting the generalizability of the test to similar contexts. Parallel forms of tests
1184     can be constructed through paraphrasing or altering the objects from the original tests. Low parallel
1185     forms reliability implies that LLMs’ responses vary significantly between test forms measuring the
1186     same construct, suggesting the LLM is overly sensitive to variations such as paraphrasing.
1187   • Inter-Rater Reliability measures the level of agreement between different raters’ judgments. In this
         work, we use two competent LLMs, GPT-4 and Llama3-70b, as raters when evaluating open-ended


                                                            22
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
         responses. It is crucial to validate the raters’ reliability, aiming for a high inter-rater reliability,
1189     which indicates the consistency of the assessment process and ensures the validity of interpreting
1190     open-ended responses.
1191   • Option Position Robustness assesses the extent to which the arrangement of options in multiple-
1192     choice items influences test outcomes. It is vital to ensure that evaluations remain unbiased against
1193     answer choice configurations. Low option position robustness implies that assessments are prone to
1194     errors caused by position bias. This susceptibility undermines the reliability of assessments when
1195     LLMs are expected to demonstrate comprehension based on content rather than option placement.
1196   • Adversarial Attack Robustness represents the extent to which LLMs remain unaffected by adver-
1197
         sarial prompts. We test this by comparing standard datasets with those infused with adversarial
         elements to determine the robustness of the models’ response. Low adversarial attack robustness
1198
         indicates that the LLM is easily misled by deceptive inputs, posing a significant risk in real-world
1199
         deployments where malicious inputs are possible. This robustness is critical for ensuring LLMs
1200     interpret and react appropriately across a wide range of queries.

       Personality is an enduring set of traits one exhibits (Mischel, 2013). Understanding the distinct
       personality attributes of LLMs can optimize their functionality in downstream tasks. Testing these
1209
       traits not only deepens our understanding but also fosters innovation in AI’s social adaptability
1210
       and human-computer interaction (HCI) technologies. For instance, an LLM characterized by an
1211   extraverted personality may be particularly effective in educational applications that demand extensive
1212   user interaction, potentially enhancing user satisfaction and engagement. Furthermore, investigating
1213   the personalities of LLMs, especially darker traits, presents an opportunity to enhance the trustwor-
1214   thiness of these models (Li et al., 2022b; Sun et al., 2024). For example, personality testing can
1215   proactively identify and mitigate toxic behaviors before deployment. Additionally, by adjusting
1216   specific traits—such as reducing neuroticism and increasing agreeableness—we aim to make interac-
1217   tions with LLMs safer and more inclusive, thereby improving the overall user experience with these
1218   technologies (Safdari et al., 2023).
1219   In this section, we examine two distinct categories of personality: the general personality traits (Big
1220   Five), and the adversarial traits (Dark Triad). We aim to address the following research questions:
1221   What personality traits do LLMs exhibit? (2) Are the personality traits in LLMs consistent when
1222   assessed through self-report questionnaires? (3) Do the personality traits self-reported by LLMs
1223   align with those demonstrated in responses to open-ended questions about real-world scenarios? (4)
1224   How do role-playing prompts influence personality traits of LLMs?

1770   Values significantly impact decision-making processes by providing a framework that guides choices
1771   and behaviors. For example, a value in fairness may lead an individual to make decisions that they
1772   perceive as equitable. Therefore, it is an important cognitive dimension that plays a crucial role in
1773   explaining human behaviors (Horley, 1991). In social science, values are used to characterize cultural
1774   groups, societies, and individuals (Schwartz, 2012).
1775
       Analyzing values in LLMs is essential to ensure that LLMs align with ethics and societal norms,
1776   particularly given their growing influence in shaping public opinion. LLMs are trained on diverse
1777   and vast text corpora, it is important to investigate the consistency and reliability of their responses to
1778   questions eliciting values. Investigating the values of LLMs helps enhance their trustworthiness and
1779   applicability in diverse cultural and social contexts. In addition, such investigation would illustrate
1780   how these models process conflicting information from the training data and the level of certainty they
1781   ascribe to their outputs. This evaluation is particularly vital in applications where decision-making
       relies on the model’s outputs, as fluctuations in confidence levels and inconsistencies in beliefs could

       lead to unpredictable behaviors. Given their training datasets, LLMs may produce a wide range of

1785   reflected in the response of LLMs? (2) Are the values encoded in LLMs consistent and robust against

1790   Cultural orientations refer to generalizations or archetypes that allow us to study the general tendencies
1791   of a cultural group, which represent the collective behavioral standards and conventions unique to
1792   specific groups, bridging cultural symbols with underlying values (Hofstede et al., 2010). Cultural
1793
       orientation involves being observant and aware of the similarities and differences in cultural norms
       across various cultural groups (Goode, 2006). Such value is essential in understanding the needs of
1794
       people from diverse cultural backgrounds (Carter and Wheeler, 2019). A better understanding of
1795
       diverse cultures in the workplace also leads to improved teamwork efficiency (Shepherd et al., 2019).
1796
1797   Evaluating the cultural orientation of LLMs is of great significance for the following reasons. First,
1798   such a test enhances our understanding of models’ cultural sensitivity and fairness, which is often
1799
       reflected in how the model processes inputs from diverse cultural contexts. This deeper insight can
       contribute to the development of more ethical LLMs by reducing cultural biases and misunderstand-
1800
       ings (Sun et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023a). Furthermore, as different cultures frequently correlate with
1801
       distinct languages, evaluating cultural orientation can also provide valuable insights into improving
1802   the model’s ability to handle cross-cultural contexts effectively (Qin et al., 2024).
1803
1804   Dataset. To assess the cultural orientation of LLMs, we utilize the “Dimensions of Culture Ques-
1805
       tionnaire” from the GLOBE project (House, 2004). This questionnaire is structured as a multi-
       dimensional, rating-based test. Here are the definitions of each dimension in the dataset (House,
1806
       2004):
1807
1808   • Assertiveness: Assertiveness is the degree to which individuals are forceful, confrontational, and
1809     aggressive, as opposed to cooperative and compassionate.
1810   • Power Distance: Power distance is the degree to which people accept an unequal distribution of
1811
         power and status privileges.
       • Uncertainty Avoidance: The degree to which people are uncomfortable with risk, change, and
1812

1813

1814     standards, and excellent performance are encouraged and rewarded.
1815   • Future Orientation: The degree to which delayed gratification and planning for the future are
1816     valued over short-term gains is called future orientation.
1817   • Humane Orientation: The degree to which fairness, altruism, generosity, and kindness are
1818     encouraged and valued is a measure of a country’s humane orientation.
1819   • Institutional Collectivism: Institutional collectivism is the degree to which organizational and
1820     societal institutions encourage individuals to be integrated into groups and organizations.
1821   • In-Group Collectivism: In-group collectivism is the degree to which individuals express pride,
1822
         loyalty, and cohesiveness in their organizations or families.
       • Gender Egalitarianism: The degree to which male and female equality is actualized is called

2031   D.3     H UMAN -C ENTERED VALUES
2032
2033   The development of AI should be aligned with human-centered values, such as fundamental freedoms,
2034   equality, and rule of law (Zeng et al., 2018; Jobin et al., 2019; AI, 2019; Yeung, 2020). Many
2035   human-centered values, such as truthfulness and transparency, are well-explored as trustworthiness in
2036   LLMs (Wang et al., 2023c; Sun et al., 2024). These prior endeavors evaluate whether LLMs would
2037
       have benign answers that violate principles including safety, fairness, and accountability. Ethics
       Guidelines for Trustworthy AI underlines AI is not an end in itself, but rather a promising means
2038
       to increase human flourishing (AI, 2019). That is, LLMs, as virtual assistants that have increasing
2039
       interactions with humans, are expected to be aware of human-centered values. Therefore, it is crucial
2040   to assess whether AI systems also prioritize human-centered needs and make decisions that consider
2041   human well-being (Council et al., 1996; Shneiderman, 2020). We not only examine the extent to
2042   which LLMs’ responses align with human-centered values but also assess the robustness of these
2043   values against adversarial attacks.
2044
       Dataset.     To evaluate the human-centered values embedded in LLMs, we introduce
2045
       Human-Centered Survey. This dataset includes hypothetical scenarios that mirror real-world
2046   dilemmas faced by users. These scenarios often involve value conflicts, such as the tension between
2047   economic profit and the well-being of public or broader human communities. LLMs are expected to
2048   prioritize and protect human well-being. This value tension scenario construction was suggested by
2049   Sorensen et al. (2024), which examine the value-driven decision-making of LLMs through scenarios
2050   that present competing values, thereby shedding light on the trade-offs in LLM decision-making
2051   processes. Our dataset comprises alternative-choice items with predetermined correct answers and
       includes two versions:


                                                                       38
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2052
       • Regular (57 scenarios): Each scenario presents a choice between a favorable action aligned with
2053     human-centered values and an unfavorable one.
2054   • Adversarial (57 × 3 scenarios): Built upon the regular version, the adversarial scenarios is con-
2055     structed to make the ethically less options more compelling using three types of persuasive
2056     adversarial attacks (Zeng et al., 2024), while maintaining the same favorable and unfavorable action
2057     choices as the regular scenarios.
2058
       We ground our scenarios within the framework provided by the Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy
2059
       AI (AI, 2019). These guidelines include seven key requirements for trustworthy AI, i.e., human

2334   Theory of mind (ToM) is crucial for effective communication and interaction (Baron-Cohen et al.,
2335   1985) as it equips individuals to better interpret the intentions and perspectives of others. Research in
2336   cognitive science has identified three major components that facilitate ToM in interactions: shared
2337
       world knowledge, perception of social cues, and interpretation of actions (Byom and Mutlu, 2013).
       Shared world knowledge involves an understanding of the contextual dynamics, such as the settings
2338
       of interactions and interpersonal relationships (Wilson, 2002; Sebanz et al., 2006). The perception
2339
       of social cues involves interpreting signals such as facial expressions, gaze, and vocal tones, which
2340   are indicative of others’ mental states (Baron-Cohen et al., 1995; De Sonneville et al., 2002). The
2341   interpretation of actions allows for the inference of intentions based on observed behaviors (Clark,
2342   1996). This intricate psychological procedure underscores the multifaceted capabilities required
2343   for ToM. Understanding ToM in LLMs helps develop LLMs with more advanced communication
2344   abilities. With ToM, LLMs could significantly enhance the efficiency of human-AI communication,
2345   enabling AI to better serve human needs. Furthermore, LLMs would effectively analyze and respond
2346   to the contextual information of users, inferring their intentions and delivering tailored responses that
2347   improve performance in tasks requiring empathy and contextual awareness. In our benchmark, we
2348
       include three distinct ToM tasks: the false belief task, the strange story task, and the imposing memory
       task, with scenarios encompassing a wide range of real-world situations and entailing different orders
2349
       of ToM reasoning.

2357   Dataset. False belief is a classic task for evaluating ToM. We adopt the false belief task developed by
2358   Kosinski (2023), and it contains two subtasks: unexpected content subtask and unexpected transfer
2359   subtask.
2360
       • Unexpected content subtask: First designed by Perner et al. (1987), this subtask has a typical setup
2361
         of a protagonist being presented with an opaque container with inaccurate labels. The protagonist
2362     has not previously seen the container or its contents. The participant’s task is to recognize that
2363     the protagonist, unaware of the discrepancy, will incorrectly assume the label accurately describes
2364     what is inside the container.
2365   • Unexpected transfer subtask: In this subtask, the protagonist observes a situation and then leaves
2366     the scene (Wimmer and Perner, 1983). While the protagonist is absent, the participant witnesses
2367     an unexpected alteration in this situation. A participant equipped with ToM should recognize that
2368     although they are aware of the change, the protagonist, having not witnessed it, will still hold on to
2369     their original belief about the situation.
2370   Each subtask contains 20 items with hypothetical scenarios and questions. Each item is accompanied
2371   by two questions, the first question examines LLMs’ ToM, and the second question assesses LLMs’
2372   task comprehension. Another rationale for the second question is that ToM scholars have highlighted
2373   that false-belief tasks might be solved without ToM by simply presuming the protagonist will make
2374   mistakes (Fabricius et al., 2010). All questions are alternative-choice. The scenarios mimic real-world
2375   situations that entail LLMs to infer the thoughts or beliefs of the people in the scenario. Examples of
       unexpected content subtasks and unexpected transfer subtasks are shown below.

2660   Self-efficacy (Bandura, 1977)—the belief in one’s ability to manage challenges—are useful for
2661   understanding humans’ behaviors. Similarly, we apply the notion of self-efficacy to LLMs. High
2662
       self-efficacy indicates a strong belief in managing challenges effectively. For LLMs, which serve

2663

2667
       Dataset. To provide a comprehensive view of LLM self-efficacy under various contexts, we utilize
2668   two datasets:
2669
2670   • LLM Self-Efficacy questionnaire: A self-curated questionnaire comprising six rating-scale
2671     items. These items are based on six categories of questions (Gao et al., 2024) that challenge LLMs
2672
         or that LLMs struggle to answer, such as assessing real-time stock information.
       • H ONE S ET dataset (Gao et al., 2024): An established dataset featuring 930 open-ended items with
2673

2674

2686

2833   H     D ISCUSSION ON I NTELLIGENCE
2834
2835   Intelligence, a multifaceted construct, has captivated psychology and AI researchers. Recent studies
2836   have explored various aspects of intelligence in LLMs, including arithmetic (Cobbe et al., 2021)
2837
       and symbolic reasoning (Wei et al., 2022). Given the extensive evaluation of LLMs’ intelligence,
       we did not include experiments in our benchmark. Instead, we discuss a critical question: How
2838
       can psychometrics improve the evaluation of LLMs’ intelligence? Traditional benchmarks often
2839
       rely on classical test theory (Crocker and Algina, 1986), which simply sums or averages scores
2840   from correct responses. This method does not consider the varying difficulties of test items nor
2841   provides predictive power for performance on unseen tasks. Item Response Theory (IRT) (Baker,
2842   2001; Yen and Fitzpatrick, 2006) in psychometrics offers a more nuanced assessment by modeling the
2843   probability of a subject correctly answering an item based on the ability level and the item’s difficulty.
2844   IRT allows for the selection of items tailored to the subject’s proficiency, enabling direct comparisons
2845   across different benchmarks and enhancing the efficacy of LLMs’ intelligence assessments.

2847   I   R ELATED W ORK
2848
2849
       The evaluation of LLMs from psychological perspectives is receiving increasing attention due to
2850   its crucial role in offering insights into LLM behavior and advancing the development of lifelike
2851   AI assistants. This section presents a comprehensive review of existing research that focuses on
2852   evaluating LLMs from diverse psychological dimensions.
2853
       Assessments on LLMs Personality. The integration of personality traits into language models
2854
       has attracted significant interest. For instance, Caron and Srivastava (2023) presented an early
2855
       endeavor of conducting personality tests on BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) and GPT2 (Radford et al.,
2856   2019), suggesting the potential for controlled persona manipulation in applications such as dialogue
2857   systems. Bodroza et al. (2023) assessed the GPT-3’s personality, highlighting the varying consistency
2858   of different aspects of personality, while exhibiting socially desirable traits. Karra et al. (2022)
2859   quantified the personality traits of many LLM models, aiming to enhance model applications through
2860   a better understanding of anthropomorphic characteristics. Moreover, Safdari et al. (2023) adopted a
2861   rigorous evaluation framework for investigating personality in LLMs and measuring the validation
       of the test. Similarly, Frisch and Giulianelli (2024) explored personality consistency in interacting


                                                            53
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2862
       LLM agents, emphasizing the importance of maintaining personality integrity in dynamic dialogue
2863   scenarios. Huang et al. (2023) revisited the reliability of psychological scales applied to LLMs,
2864   finding consistent personality traits in responses, which supports the use of LLMs in substituting
2865   human participants in social science research. Jiang et al. (2023a) and La Cava et al. (2024) further
2866   used prompt engineering to elicit specific personalities in LLMs. Cui et al. (2023) proposed a
2867   fine-tuning method to encode MBTI traits into LLMs, ensuring consistent preferences.
2868
       Assessments on LLMs Values. LLMs have been widely used in open-ended contexts, and the values
2869   they reflect in their response have a profound impact on shaping societal views (Santurkar et al., 2023).
2870   Miotto et al. (2022) presented an early study of values of GPT-3 employing psychometric tools. Ziems
2871   et al. (2024) investigated the use of LLMs in political science and benchmarked ideology detection,
2872   stance detection, and entity framing. Hendrycks et al. (2021) introduced the ETHICS dataset to
2873   evaluate LLMs against human moral judgments, providing a foundation for aligning AI outputs
2874   with societal values. Santurkar et al. (2023) presented O PINIONS QA, which aligns LLM-generated
2875   opinions with diverse U.S. demographics, revealing significant biases that could influence societal
2876   perceptions. Durmus et al. (2023) introduced G LOBAL O PINION QA, which includes cross-national
2877
       question-answer pairs designed to capture diverse opinions on global issues across different countries.
       The evaluation on G LOBAL O PINION QA reveals that by using prompts to indicate the specific culture,
2878
       the response of LLMs can adjust to the specific cultural perspectives while reflecting harmful cultural
2879
       stereotypes. Sorensen et al. (2024) introduced a dataset named ValuePrism, which includes scenarios
2880   that multiple correct human values are in tension, and they build an LLM that could generate, explain,
2881   and assess decision-making related to human values. In terms of evaluation, Röttger et al. (2024b)
2882   advocated more naturalistic assessments that reflect real-world user interactions with these models
2883   when evaluating LLMs on opinions and values.
2884
       Assessments on LLMs Emotions. Investigating emotion-related abilities in LLMs is essential for
2885
       these models to interact with and serve humans. Wang et al. (2023b) developed a psychometric
2886   assessment to quantitatively evaluate LLMs’ emotional understanding. Sabour et al. (2024) introduced
2887   E MO B ENCH, which includes emotion understanding and emotion application tasks for a more
2888   comprehensive evaluation of emotion intelligence in LLMs. Further, Zhan et al. (2023) highlighted
2889   the important subjective cognitive appraisals of emotions for LLMs in understanding situations
2890   and introduced a dataset to evaluate such abilities in LLMs. Some literature also examined how
2891   emotion would affect the performance of LLMs. For instance, Li et al. (2023b) found that LLMs
2892   can understand emotional stimuli, and they also explored the application of emotional prompts to
2893   improve LLMs’ performance across numerous tasks, demonstrating that such stimuli can significantly
2894
       boost effectiveness. In addition, Li et al. (2024a) proposed a novel prompting method named
       Emotional Chain-of-Thought, which aligns LLM outputs with human emotional intelligence, thereby
2895
       refining emotional generation capabilities. Coda-Forno et al. (2023) applied computational psychiatry
2896
       principles to study how induced emotional states like anxiety can affect LLMs’ decision-making
2897   and biases. This exploration contributes to understanding LLMs’ behaviors under various emotional
2898   conditions but also indicates the potential impact of emotions on AI’s effectiveness and ethical
2899   implications.
2900
       Assessments on LLMs Theory of Mind (ToM). ToM is an essential cognitive ability for social
2901
       interactions. Therefore, researchers have been interested in whether LLMs have ToM as an emergent
2902
       ability. Kosinski (2023) modified from classic Anne-Sally Test and curated false belief tasks, each
2903   include a set of prompts containing false-belief scenario and true belief control scenarios to ensure the
2904   validity of the test, and the results show that GPT-4’s performance is on par with six-year-old children,
2905   and earlier LLMs barely solve the tasks. van Duijn et al. (2023) evaluated instruction-tuned models
2906   on non-literal language usage and recursive intentionality tasks, suggesting that instruction-tuning
2907   brings LLMs with ToM. Wu et al. (2023) evaluates high order ToM on LLMs, resulting in a decline
2908   in performance. Sclar et al. (2023) presented a plug-and-play approach named SymbolicToM to
2909   track belief states and high-order reasoning of multiple characters through symbolic representations
2910   in reading comprehension settings, which enhances accuracy and robustness of ToM in out-of-
2911
       distribution evaluation. Zhou et al. (2023a) presented a novel evaluation paradigm for ToM, which
       requires models to connect inferences about others’ mental states to actions in social scenarios,
2912
       consequentially, they suggested a zero-shot prompting framework to encourage LLMs to anticipate
2913
       future challenges and reason about potential actions for improving ToM inference. Some prior studies
2914   also examined ToM of LLMs in more complex settings. For instance, Ma et al. (2023b) treated
2915   LLMs as an agent and created scenarios to make them physically and socially situated in interactions

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2916
       with humans, and provided a comprehensive evaluation of the mental states. Verma et al. (2024)
2917   investigated ToM in a human-robot interaction setting, where robots utilize LLMs to interpret robots’
2918   behaviors. The initial tests indicated strong ToM abilities in models of GPT-4 and GPT-3.5-turbo,
2919   further perturbation tests exposed significant limitations, demonstrating the models’ difficulties in
2920   handling variations in context.

       Assessments on LLMs Self-Efficacy. The self-efficacy for LLMs is an under-explored dimension

       J   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
2929
2930   In this study, we introduce a psychometric benchmark for LLMs that covers six psychological
2931
       dimensions, provides an evaluation framework to ensure test reliability, and offers a comprehensive
       analysis of the results. In this section, we will discuss the limitations of our current work and explore
2932
       potential future directions for integrating psychology and AI. Future research could focus on the
2933
       following directions:
2934
2935   Dynamic and Interactive Evaluation. Our current assessment limits evaluation to single-turn
2936   conversations, which may not fully capture the dynamic psychological attributes of LLMs. Future
2937
       research should focus on dynamic and interactive assessments through multi-turn conversations or
       interactions, potentially exploring the evolution of psychological attributes within sandbox environ-
2938
       ments (Zhou et al., 2023b; Park et al., 2023). This simulation could yield insights into the social
2939
       dynamics.
2940
2941   Test Enrichment. Despite the vast capabilities of LLMs, our observations highlight inconsistencies
2942   across different scenarios and item types. Our tests, limited to several parallel forms and prompt
2943
       templates, necessitate a broader scope to understand LLM behavioral patterns comprehensively.
       Future expansions should include a variety of tests within our current framework, providing deeper
2944
       understanding into behavioral patterns of LLMs.
2945
2946   Broader Psychological Dimensions Evaluation. Future research could explore broader psycho-
2947   logical dimensions to deepen our understanding of LLM behaviors. Currently, our approach to
2948   identifying these dimensions is top-down, grounded in established psychological theories. However,
2949
       future studies could benefit from an inductive method, deriving insights directly from empirical
       observations to refine or develop new theories (Rosenberg, 2015; Kernis, 2003; Hankin and Abela,
2950
       2005; Raykov and Marcoulides, 2011). This shift will not only enhance our comprehension of LLMs
2951
       but also improve the reliability of their evaluations as our conceptual frameworks evolve.
2952
2953   Mechanism Design for Assessment. Our psychometric benchmark currently follows to classical
2954   test theory, which may not adequately account for item difficulty variability or predict performance
2955
       on unseen test items. To improve the predictive power of our assessments, we suggest future work to
       adopt Item Response Theory (IRT) (Crocker and Algina, 1986; Baker, 2001; Yen and Fitzpatrick,
2956
       2006). IRT allows for modeling the probability of a correct response based on the ability levels,
2957
       facilitating more accurate evaluations by selecting items that best match the LLMs’ proficiency.

2960   K    A PPLICATIONS
2961
2962   In this section, we explore the opportunities presented by our study and discuss potential applications
2963   of the benchmark.
2964   Enhancing Understanding of LLMs’ Behaviors. Different from most existing benchmarks that
2965   assess the specific capabilities of LLMs, our work focuses on a higher-level, abstract analysis. We
2966   aim to comprehend LLM behaviors from a psychological perspective. Utilizing the psychometric
2967   paradigm, we establish comprehensive profiles that can track changes in LLMs over time. For
2968   example, proprietary LLMs such as GPT-4 are periodically updated based on user feedback, though
2969   the details of such updates are often not disclosed publicly. While Chen et al. (2023) suggested
       to quantify these changes in LLM abilities, we argue that evaluating and understanding these


                                                          55
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2970
       modifications through psychological dimensions—such as cultural orientations—is critical. These
2971   evaluations not only facilitate the integration of LLMs into complex systems but also enhance the
2972   predictability of their outputs. Furthermore, examining the psychological dimensions of LLMs opens
2973   new avenues for research in human-AI collaboration, exploring how LLMs’ psychological traits can
2974   improve user trust and influence interactions between humans and AI.
2975
       Empowering LLM-based Agents. Our psychometrics benchmark presents a starting point for
2976
       developing more sophisticated LLM-based agents. Previous research has implemented personas
2977   within LLM-based agents (Shanahan et al., 2023; Park et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023d), directing
2978   these agents to engage in role-playing. This benchmark serves as a tool not only for evaluating human-
2979   like psychological attributes but also for assessing the consistency of these attributes across various
2980   contexts. Furthermore, it facilitates the creation of more intricate, diverse, and realistic simulations
2981   for multi-agent systems (Zhang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b). By examining the variability in
2982   behaviors of LLM-based agents, developers can design interactions that more accurately replicate
2983   human communication patterns, leading to the development of more effective multi-agent systems.
2984   Improving User Experience. Assessing the psychology of LLMs enables the customization of
2985   their characteristics to better align with diverse applications (Jiang et al., 2023a). For example,
2986   LLMs designed with distinct personalities can adopt tailored communication styles, where certain
2987   traits may enhance user engagement and trust in specific contexts. For instance, LLMs exhibiting
2988   traits of openness are well-suited for the education sector, where engaging user interaction is crucial.
2989   Additionally, equipping LLMs with the ability to understand and mirror specific cultural orientations
2990   can significantly enhance their capacity to provide contextually appropriate recommendations. Such
2991
       cultural adaptability not only improves the user experience for individuals from targeted cultural
       backgrounds but also increases the technology’s acceptability across varied audiences (Li et al.,
2992
       2024c).
2993
2994   Facilitating Interdisciplinary Collaboration. Due to exceptional generative capabilities, LLMs
2995   have significantly propelled interdisciplinary research across various fields, including education
2996   (Kasneci et al., 2023), the medical domain (Liu et al., 2023b), and social sciences (Ziems et al., 2024).
2997
       Our benchmark creates opportunities for interdisciplinary collaborations. Specifically, social science
       researchers can employ LLMs to simulate social behaviors and interactions. This benchmark provides
2998
       a framework that helps researchers identify which LLMs best meet their specific requirements in
2999
       simulating social science research participants in their studies. Similarly, in the healthcare sector,
3000   LLMs are increasingly utilized to simulate patient-doctor interactions (Liao et al., 2024; Li et al.,
3001   2024d; Fareez et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024b). Our study serves as a useful tool that enables healthcare
3002   researchers and practitioners to evaluate and select LLMs that simulate medical dialogues more
3003   accurately. This functionality is crucial in preparing medical staff to manage sensitive or complex
3004   situations effectively. As these models become more refined, their ability to function as reliable
3005   proxies in training and therapeutic contexts increase, and our benchmark serves to contribute to this
3006   integration by providing a rigorous and reliable evaluation of the attributes of LLMs.
