                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Decision Transformer (DT), free from optimal value functions fitting and policy
012            gradient computation, attempts to solve offline reinforcement learning (RL) via
013            supervised sequence modeling. During inference, sequence modeling requires an
014            initial target returns assigned with expert knowledge, which blocks comprehensive
015            evaluation on more diverse datasets. As a result, existing sequence modeling only
016            focuses on limited evaluation on Gym datasets and some understanding is severely
017            biased. In this paper, we aim to revisit the design choices, including architecture
018            and context length, in sequence modeling on more diverse datasets. We utilize the
019            max-return sequence modeling that replaces the manual target returns with max-
020
               imized returns predicted by itself. We systematically investigate the impact of 1)
               architectural choices and 2) context lengths in max-return sequence modeling on
021
               nine datasets with varying data distributions. Abundant experiments and thorough
022
               analyses reveal that design choices are highly influenced by the dataset character-
023            istics, which further underscores the significance of more diverse evaluation.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Classical online reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms such as Q-learning (Watkins & Dayan,
029   1992) or policy gradient (Sutton et al., 1999) are derived from the Markov Decision Process (MDP)
030   (Sutton et al., 1998) formulation. Sequence modeling (Chen et al., 2021), developed in data-driven
031   offline scenario (Levine et al., 2020; Fu et al., 2020), maximizes the likelihood of actions based on
032   the whole historical trajectories that including state, action and returns. In this way, offline RL is
033
      addressed from one paradigm similar to the supervised learning. A particularly enticing prospect is
      that the successes of supervised sequence modeling in other domains may be replicable within the
034
      offline realm, potentially catapulting the rapid advancement and progress of reinforcement learning.
035
036   However, the existing evaluation of sequence modeling is insufficient and consequently the cor-
037   responding understanding is biased and limited, which has hindered the further development of
038   sequence modeling. The insufficient evaluation stems from the choice of the initial returns target
039
      during the sequence modeling inference (Zheng et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2022).
      The initial returns target serves as a inference hyperparameter that should be meticulous determined
040
      using expert knowledge or extensive experiments. Decision transformer (DT) (Chen et al., 2021)
041
      proposes the initial returns targets on D4RL-Gym datasets using domain knowledge and online de-
042   cision transformer (ODT) (Zheng et al., 2022) further optimizes hyperparameters via exhaustive
043   experimental comparison. in contrast, the initial returns targets on other representative datasets
044   (Antmaze, Maze2d, Kitchen and Adroit) are under explored. As a result, subsequent re-
045   search on sequence modeling has been almost exclusively confined to the Gym, neglecting other
046   datasets (Zhuang et al., 2024). Even the paper that examines the advantages and disadvantages of
047   DT in comparison to CQL (Kumar et al., 2020) and BC (Pomerleau, 1988) exhibit a similar bias
048   (Bhargava et al., 2023). This status results in an insufficient investigation of the impact of dataset
049   characteristics on sequence modeling, and explorations about historical sequence length and se-
050
      quence model architecture are consistently biased.
051   In this paper, we aim to systematically investigate the impact of 1) dataset characteristics 2) archi-
052   tectural choices and 3) the length of historical sequences on the performance of sequence modeling.
053   To overcome the limitation of the human designed initial returns targets, we adopt the max-return
      sequence modeling introduced by Reinforced Transformer (Reinformer) (Zhuang et al., 2024). The

      fundamental premise of max-return sequence modeling is to bring the concept of return maximiza-
055   tion back to the supervised paradigm of sequence modeling. In terms of implementation, max-return
056   sequence modeling predicts a maximized return at each timestep to guide the generation of actions,
057   free from specifying an initial returns target. We have conducted exhaustive experiments and ana-
058   lytical studies, leading to the following conclusions and findings:
059
060   • Overall, the dataset characteristics of the have greater an impact on sequence modeling than the
061
        model architecture and context length. Discussing the impact of other factors without considering
        dataset characteristics is quite one-sided. In trajectory stitching problems, sequence modeling is
062
        inherently at a disadvantage compared to RL algorithms (Brandfonbrener et al., 2022). Sequence
063
        modeling is more adept at long-term tasks and tasks that include part of expert data.
064
      • In terms of architecture, the Reinformer tends to consider global information, while Reinconver
065
        (Reinforced Convformer) and Reimba (Reinforced Mamba) focus more on local information.
066
067
      • The impact of context length on performance is relatively minor. A shorter context length is more
        advantageous for trajectory stitching. Moreover, it is surprising to find that models trained on
068
        long sequences perform exceptionally well during inference with short sequences, significantly
069
        enhancing their trajectory stitching capabilities.

137
138   Since supervised sequence modeling does not explicitly consider return maximization, the core ob-
139
      jective of RL, the concept of max-return sequence modeling is introduced. The key lies in utilizing
      the maximized return to guide the generation of next actions during inference.
140
141   Concretely, Reinforced Transformer (Reinformer) adopt the following historical trajectories τt−K :

145

146

190
191   Now, we are ready to systematically investigate the impact of 1) dataset characteristics 2) archi-
192   tectural choices and 3) context length on the performance of max-return sequence modeling. The
193   Overview of the experimental setting is summarized in Figure 1.
194
195   Dataset Characteristics We selected nine representative datasets from the widely-used offline
196   benchmark D4RL to evaluate the sequence modeling, which are detailed as follows:
197   • Halfcheetah-medium-expert, Hopper-medium-replay and Walker2d-medium:
198     The abbreviations are respectively HC-me, HP-mr and WK-m. For Gym tasks, we select only
199     one dataset from each environment, which encompasses three distinct data distributions. The
200     “medium-replay” dataset consists of samples in the replay buffer observed during online training
201     until the policy reaches the “medium” level, approximately 1/3 the performance of the “expert”.
202   • Antmaze-medium-play and Antmaze-medium-diverse: The abbreviations are respec-
203     tively AT-mp and AT-md. Antmaze datasets have a sparse reward to show if the ant reach the
204     goal in the maze. The medium dataset requires the algorithm to navigate to the target point by
205     stitching the suboptimal trajectories into the successful trajectories. These datasets require the
206     trajectory stitching ability, which is particularly challenging for sequence modeling.
207
      • Kitchen-partial: The abbreviation is KC-p. The desired goals are to complete 4 subtasks:
208     open the microwave, move the kettle, flip the light switch, and slide open the cabinet door. The
209     ”partial” dataset includes subtrajectories where the 4 target subtasks are completed in sequence.
210
211
      • maze2d-large: The abbreviation is MZ-l. The dataset is collected by a PD controller that
        memorizes the reached waypoints during data collection, so the Markov property does not hold.
212
213   • Pen-human and Pen-cloned: The abbreviations are respectively P-h and P-c. This envi-
214     ronment controls a 24-DoF simulated Shadow Hand robot to twirl a pen. Human dataset contains
215     25 trajectories of expert demonstration. Cloned dataset uses a 50-50 split between demonstra-
        tion data and trajectories sampled from a behavior cloned policy trained on the demonstrations.

261   Context Length Traditional offline RL algorithms, derived from Markov Decision Processes
262   (MDPs), predict actions solely based on the current state st . In contrast, sequence modeling also
263   takes into account historical trajectories of length K, thereby breaking the Markov property. Thus,
264   the context length is a factor worthy of exploration in sequence modeling.
265   We consider 4 context length K = 2, 5, 10, 20, with the maximum value of 20 being the default
266   context length for DT, and the minimum of 2 corresponding to the shortest sequence length. The
267   intermediate values are determined by exponential interpolation. Typically, the context length during
268   inference should be consistent with the training training. However, there are exceptions, such as
269   ODT (Zheng et al., 2022), which manually adjusts the sequence length during inference, and EDT
      (Wu et al., 2023), which dynamically adjusts K based on whether the current trajectory is optimal.

      4     R ELATED W ORK
271
272
      Offline Reinforcement Learning (Levine et al., 2020) breaks free from the traditional paradigm of
273
      online interaction (Sutton et al., 1998) and learns policy from fixed offline dataset collected by
274
      arbitrary or even unknown process (Lange et al., 2012; Fu et al., 2020). Most offline RL algorithms
275   are developed based on classical online algorithms, such as CQL (Kumar et al., 2020) based on
276   SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018), TD3+BC (Fujimoto & Gu, 2021) based on TD3 (Fujimoto et al.,
277   2018) and BPPO (Zhuang et al., 2023) based on PPO (Schulman et al., 2017). In contrast, Decision
278   Transformer (DT) (Chen et al., 2021) directly maximizes the action likelihood, solving offline RL
279   from supervised sequence modeling paradigm. Following upside-down RL (Srivastava et al., 2019;
280   Schmidhuber, 2019), DT considers returns when predicting the action. Some works equip DT with
281   classical RL components including dynamics programming (Yamagata et al., 2023), critic guidance
282   (Wang et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024), return maximization (Zhuang et al., 2024), online finetuning
283
      (Zheng et al., 2022) and trajectory stitching (Wu et al., 2023). On the other hand, DT is investigated
      from supervised learning perspective such as unsupervised pretraining (Xie et al., 2023; Carroll
284
      et al., 2022) and scaling ability (Lee et al., 2022; Shridhar et al., 2023). As for model architecture,
285
      LSTM (Siebenborn et al., 2022), one-dimension convolution network (Kim et al., 2023; Yan et al.,
286   2024) and linear RNN (David et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2024; Ota, 2024; Lv et al., 2024; Huang et al.,
287   2024) are adopted to replace the transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) in DT. However, the evaluation
288   of these sequence models is based on limited datasets, including only the Gym and antmaze-umaze
289   datasets (Fu et al., 2020), which biases the drawn conclusions. Reinformer (Zhuang et al., 2024)
290   proposes using maximized returns during inference to replace the manually designed initial target
291   returns in DT, significantly expanding the range of evaluated datasets. This paper aims to leverage
292   the max-return sequence modeling proposed by the Reinformer (Zhuang et al., 2024) to conduct a
293   systematic evaluation of sequence models and reveal the future direction.

522   6   C ONCLUSION , D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
523
524   In this paper, we systematically revisit the impact of 3 architectural choices and 4 context lengths on
525   9 diverse datasets in max-return sequence modeling. Through extensive experiments, we find:
526
      • Architectures: On more diverse datasets, the Transformer architecture still remains a competitive
527
        model. Reinformer exhibits better stability in the presence of input perturbations, focusing more
528     on global sequence information. In contrast, Reinconver and Reimba more focus on local infor-
529     mation. The inclusion of positional embedding may not be advantageous for trajectory stitching
530     but aids in information extraction in long sequences.
531
      • Context lengths: In scenarios with high data quality, sequence modeling often outperforms clas-
532
        sical offline RL derived from MDPs. However, on datasets that heavily emphasize stitching,
533
        classical offline RL surpasses sequence modeling. With exceptionally high data quality, longer
534     sequence models converge faster. Our astonishing discovery is that masking out a portion of
535     historical trajectory information during inference may enhance trajectory stitching.
536
537   In summary, we recommend using sequence modeling when data quality is high, resorting to classi-
538   cal Offline RL or a combination of sequence modeling and classical Offline RL when stitching data
539   is crucial. In the future, we will explore how to better integrate classical RL with sequence modeling
      to harness both of their strengths.
