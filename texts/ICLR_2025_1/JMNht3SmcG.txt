009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            As Large Language Models (LLMs) continue to advance in performance, their
012            size has increased significantly, with current LLMs containing billions or even tril-
013            lions of parameters. In this study, we identify notable redundancy across the layers
014            of LLMs, where some layers contribute minimally to overall network functional-
015            ity. To quantify this, we introduce a metric called Block Influence (BI) which use
016            the similarity between layer’s input and output to measure the importance of each
017            layer. Based on the observation of layer redundancy, we propose a straightforward
018            pruning method: layer removal, which eliminates redundant layers based on their
019
               BI scores. Our approach, termed ShortGPT, demonstrates superior performance
               over previous state-of-the-art pruning methods. Moreover, ShortGPT is orthog-
020
               onal to quantization-like methods, enabling further reduction in parameters and
021
               computation. The ability to achieve better results through simple layer removal,
022            as opposed to more complex pruning techniques, suggests a high degree of redun-
023            dancy across layers, not only in transformer models but also in non-transformer
024            models. We hope this work will contribute to future research in LLM compres-
025            sion.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The field of large language models (LLMs) has witnessed rapid development recently, with LLMs
031   achieving impressive performance across various domains. Guided by the scaling laws identified
032   in prior work (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022), current LLM research tend to increase
033   model parameters to boost performance. As a result, modern LLMs, which can comprise billions to
034   trillions of parameters, require significant hardware resources for deployment, creating substantial
035   barriers to their practical use.
036   To mitigate the hardware demands of large models, model compression techniques have become a
037   critical area of focus (Zhu et al., 2023). These techniques are generally divided into quantization
038   (Liu et al., 2021; Gholami et al., 2022; Dettmers et al., 2022; 2024) and pruning(LeCun et al., 1989;
039   Han et al., 2015; Frantar & Alistarh, 2023). Quantization reduces the precision of model parameters,
040   but its effectiveness often requires specific hardware support. In contrast, pruning method removes
041   redundant parameters to decrease the model’s size and computation, offering a more flexible and
042   hardware-agnostic approach. Despite its advantages, many existing pruning methods are complex;
043   for example, some require gradient information (Ma et al., 2024), which limits their practicality.
044   In this paper, we focus on the issue of layer redundancy in LLMs and propose a novel approach for
045   simplifying these models. We introduce Block Influence (BI), a metric that quantifies how much the
046   hidden state changes after passing through each layer, providing a more direct measure of a layer’s
047   importance. Leveraging this insight, we propose a simple yet effective pruning method ShortGPT,
048   which identifies and removes layers with lower BI scores, significantly reducing model size without
049   sacrificing much performance.
050   To evaluate our approach, we conducted evaluation across comprehensive benchmarks. Our experi-
051   ments revealed that our method exhibits a smaller performance decrement compared to the previous
052   methods. For instance, removing 10 layers (25% of the total 40 layers) from the LLaMA 2-13B
053   model resulted in only a slight drop in performance on the MMLU benchmark (Hendrycks et al.,
      2020), from 55.0 to 52.2. Our findings highlight substantial redundancy in current LLMs and suggest

141
142   In this section, we present the methodological framework of our layer removal approach for LLMs,
143   elucidating the underlying principles and techniques employed. We begin by introducing Block
144   Influence (BI), a novel metric designed to assess the hidden states transformation of each layer.
145   Leveraging BI, we then detail our layer removal method.

      3.1   L AYER IMPORTANCE
148
149   As outlined in the preceding section, the layers of LLMs exhibit redundancy, with varying degrees
150   of redundancy across different layers. To capture this, we introduce a new metric, Block Influence
151   (BI), to measure the degree of transformation performed by each layer. The BI score of ith layer can
152   be calculated as follows:

174   3.2                   L AYER R EMOVAL
175
176   Our goal is to obtain a pruned model that remains as close as possible to the original model. Since
177   an LLM functions as a series of transformations applied to hidden states across its layers and we
178
      can determine the importance of each layer, we propose a straightforward pruning method: layer
      removal, which we refer to as ShortGPT. We delete certain layers in LLMs based on BI score. First
179
      of all, we construct a calibration set, which is a set of unlabelled text samples such as PG19 (Rae
180
      et al., 2019). Then we collect the hidden states of each layer during inference on these samples.
181   Next, we calculate the BI score based on the collected hidden states. Finally, we sort layers in
182   ascending order according to the BI, and delete the layers with the lower BI score. The number of
183   layers to be deleted can vary to trade off the speed and performance. The details of our layer removal
184   setting can be found in Appendix D.

246   4.2     M AIN R ESULTS
247
248   To validate the efficacy of our proposed method, we conducted comparative experiments against
249   baseline techniques commonly employed in large language model evaluation. Considering the cur-
250   rent structured pruning methods generally reduce parameters by no more than 30%, we performed
251   experiments with approximately 1/4 of the parameters pruned. The experimental results are pre-
252   sented in Table 2. Additional experiments exploring different parameter reduction proportions will
253   be discussed in the subsequent section.
254   The results demonstrate that the performance of the model pruned by our method significantly sur-
255   passes that of the baseline methods, maintaining most of the large language model’s capabilities.
256   Furthermore, we note that the approach of reducing the number of layers (ShortGPT/LaCo) out-
257   performs the method of reducing the embedding dimensions (LLMPru./SliceGPT), implying that
258   the model exhibits more redundancy in depth than in width. Further experimental analysis will be
259   presented in the ensuing section.
260   In Table 2, we fully adopted the benchmark, model, and pruning ratio in the LaCo paper. In order
261   to make a more fair comparison with LLMprun. and SliceGPT, we compared them with the same
262   benchmark, model, and pruning ratio in their original paper. The experimental results are shown
263   in Appendix C. Consistent with our findings in Table 2, these experiments further demonstrate the
264   significant layer redundancy present in existing large language models, and ShortGPT achieves su-
265   perior performance compared to other pruning methods.
266   The results show that coarse-grained pruning methods, such as removing entire layers, often outper-
267   form fine-grained approaches like Slice GPT or LLM Pruner. We speculate that the reason is that
268   the large language model is actually very robust, as shown in Figure 1, removing any deep layer
269   individually actually has very little impact on the final output, which means it is difficult to define
      the importance of a finer grained module and perform pruning.

      6   R ELATED WORKS
439
440
      To reduce the inference cost of large language models and increase their practical applications, there
441
      have been many recent works on compressing models, which can be classified into two categories:
442   model pruning and quantization. Besides, there are some works aim to study the redundancy of
443   model which is essential for compressing models.
444
445
      Model pruning: model pruning (LeCun et al., 1989; Han et al., 2015) is a classic and effective
      method of reducing model redundancy modules to compress models. The model pruning methods
446
      mainly include unstructured pruning and structured pruning. The unstructured pruning simplifies an
447
      LLM by removing specific parameters without considering its internal structure, such as SparseGPT
448   (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023) and LoRAPrune (Zhang et al., 2023). However, this method disregards
449   the overall LLM structure, resulting in an irregular sparse model composition. Another more practi-
450   cal approach is structured pruning, GUM(Syed et al., 2023) makes an analysis of several structured
451   pruning methods for decoder-only LLMs. LLM-Pruner (Ma et al., 2024) selectively removes non-
452   critical structures according to gradient information. ShearedLLaMA (Xia et al., 2023) employs
453   targeted structured pruning and dynamic batch loading. LaCo (Yang et al., 2024) used layer merg-
454   ing to compress the model. Compared to the previous method, our method is a simple and efficient
455   structured pruning method.
456   Quantization: quantization (Liu et al., 2021; Gholami et al., 2022; Dettmers et al., 2022; 2024)
457   is a widely accepted technique in the field of model compression, which can significantly save the
458   storage and computational costs of deep learning models. Traditional models are generally stored as
459   floating-point numbers, but quantization converts them into integers or other discrete forms. LUT-
460   GEMM (Park et al., 2022) quantifies only weights and optimizes matrix multiplication in LLM using
461   BCQ format. SPQR (Dettmers et al., 2023) identifies and isolates abnormal weights, stores them
462
      with higher accuracy, and compresses all other weights into 3-4 bits. Our model pruning method
      and quantization method are orthogonal, which means quantification based on our pruned model can
463
      further compress the model.
464
465   Model redundancy: researchers have long noticed the significant redundancy in nonlinear models
466   (Catchpole & Morgan, 1997). In recent years, the transformer model architecture has been widely
467   applied, and researchers have also studied its redundancy. In (Bian et al., 2021), researchers ana-
468
      lyzed redundancy in attention mechanisms, in which clear and similar redundancy patterns (cluster
      structure) are observed among attention heads. In (Dalvi et al., 2020), researchers dissect two pre-
469
      trained models, BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) and XLNet (Yang et al., 2019), studying how much
470
      redundancy they exhibit at a representation level and a more fine-grained neuron-level. However,
471   the redundancy in current large language models based on decoder-only structures still needs to be
472   explored.
473
474
475
      7   C ONCLUSION
476
477
      In this work, we uncovered the significant layer-wise redundancy of LLMs, Our research demon-
      strates that certain layers contribute minimally to overall network functionality and can be removed
478
      without substantially compromising model performance. Based on our observation, We introduce
479
      Block influence to quantify the importance of each layer and propose a simple and straightforward
480   pruning method: layer removal. Our experiments demonstrates that it is possible to maintain up
481   to approximately 90% of a LLM’s performance while reducing the model’s parameter amount and
482   computational requirements by approximately 25%. Besides, our method is orthogonal to quan-
483   tization methods and can be further improved by continual training. We hope that our work can
484   provide some insight for future model compression techniques. Moreover, our work suggests poten-
485   tial avenues for improving the efficiency of model training by reducing inherent redundancy in the
      future.

      G    E VALUATION B ENCHMARKS
811
812
      In order to comprehensively evaluate the changes in the ability of large language models before and
813   after pruning, we conducted evaluations on the most commonly used Benchmark MMLU Hendrycks
814   et al. (2020), CMMLU Li et al. (2024) for evaluating large models. In addition, we also followed
815   LaCo Yang et al. (2024) to evaluate a wider dataset.
816
      MMLU Hendrycks et al. (2020) is a benchmark aimed at measuring the knowledge acquired dur-
817
      ing pre-training by specifically evaluating models in zero-shot and few-shot settings. This makes
818
      benchmarks more challenging and similar to the way we evaluate humans. This benchmark covers
819   57 subjects including STEM, humanities, social sciences, etc. Its difficulty ranges from beginner to
820   advanced professional level, and it tests world knowledge and problem-solving ability.
821
822
      CMMLU Li et al. (2024) is a comprehensive Chinese language assessment dataset designed specif-
      ically to evaluate LLM’s advanced knowledge and reasoning abilities in the context of Chinese
823
      language and culture. CMMLU covers 67 topics, from elementary school to university or profes-
824
      sional level. Including natural sciences, as well as humanities and social sciences, it also includes
825   many contents with Chinese characteristics.
826
827   CMNLI Xu et al. (2020) is part of the Chinese language understanding assessment benchmark. It
828
      consists of two parts: XNLI and MNLI. HellaSwag (HeSw) Zellers et al. (2019) is a challenging
      dataset for evaluating commonsense NLI that is especially hard for state-of-the-art models, though
829
      its questions are trivial for humans. PIQA Bisk et al. (2020) is a multi-choice question and answer
830
      dataset that focuses on daily scenarios. This dataset explores the model’s grasp of the laws of the real
831   physical world through daily scenarios. CHID Zheng et al. (2019) is an idiom cloze test dataset that
832   mainly focuses on the selection of candidate words and the representation of idioms. CoQA Reddy
833   et al. (2019) is a large-scale dataset used for conversational question-answering tasks, containing
834   over 127000 questions and their corresponding answers. BoolQ Clark et al. (2019) is a question-
835   answer dataset containing 15942 examples of yes/no questions. These problems occur naturally -
836   they are generated in an environment that is silent and unconstrained. Race Lai et al. (2017) is a
837   large-scale reading comprehension dataset collected from English examinations in China, which are
838   designed for middle school and high school students. XSumHasan et al. (2021) is used to evaluate
839
      abstract single document summarization systems. The goal is to create a short, one-sentence new
      summary of what the article is about. C3 Sun et al. (2020) is a machine reading comprehension
840
      dataset with multiple choices, consisting of multiple-choice questions, reading materials from Chi-
841
      nese proficiency exams, and ethnic Chinese exams. PG19 Rae et al. (2019) is a long document
842   dataset from books used to test the effectiveness of language modeling.
