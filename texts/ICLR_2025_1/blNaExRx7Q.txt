009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The task of “unlearning” certain concepts in large language models (LLMs) has
012            attracted immense attention recently, due to its importance in mitigating undesir-
013            able model behaviours, such as the generation of harmful, private, or incorrect
014            information. Current protocols to evaluate unlearning methods largely rely on
015            behavioral tests, without monitoring the presence of unlearned knowledge within
016            the model’s parameters. This residual knowledge can be adversarially exploited to
017            recover the erased information post-unlearning. We argue that unlearning should
018            also be evaluated internally, by considering changes in the parametric knowledge
019
               traces of the unlearned concepts. To this end, we propose a general evaluation
               methodology that leverages vocabulary projections to inspect concepts encoded in
020
               model parameters. We use this approach to localize “concept vectors” — parameter
021
               vectors that encode concrete concepts — and construct C ONCEPT V ECTORS, a
022            benchmark dataset containing hundreds of common concepts and their parametric
023            knowledge traces within two open-source LLMs. Evaluation on C ONCEPT V EC -
024            TORS shows that existing unlearning methods minimally impact concept vectors
025            and mostly suppress them during inference, while directly ablating these vectors
026            demonstrably removes the associated knowledge and significantly reduces the
027            model’s susceptibility to adversarial manipulation. Our results highlight limitations
028            in behavioral-based unlearning evaluations and call for future work to include
029            parameter-based evaluations. We release our code and benchmark at https://
030
               anonymous.4open.science/r/ConceptVectors_review-98EF.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Recently, there has been surging interest in developing methods for unlearning information captured
036   in large language models (LLMs) (Jang et al., 2023; Chen & Yang, 2023; Yao et al., 2023; Eldan &
037   Russinovich, 2023; Si et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a;c). Such methods are important for removing
038   sensitive or harmful information, biases, and outdated facts. A key challenge in developing unlearning
039
      methods is evaluating their performance, namely, how to validate the erasure of the unlearned
      information. Existing evaluation protocols largely rely on behavioural tests, such as the ability to
040
      answer questions or complete queries about the removed information (Stoehr et al., 2024; Hase et al.,
041
      2023; Chen & Yang, 2023). However, growing evidence suggests that it is often possible to steer the
042   model to generate the unlearned information post-unlearning (Lynch et al., 2024; Patil et al., 2024),
043   indicating that the target knowledge has not in fact been exhaustively removed from the model. This
044   work presents the first benchmark for parameter-based internal evaluation of unlearning methods.
045
      We highlight the existence of “parametric knowledge traces”, which are specific sets of parameters in
046
      the model that strongly correlate with the knowledge to be erased (see Figure 1a for illustration). We
047
      show that this residual knowledge causally influences the model’s ability to generate information about
048   the target concept, and argue that its internal erasure should be a goal of unlearning. Specifically,
049   we leverage recent methods that inspect the information encoded in model parameters through
050   vocabulary projections (Dar et al., 2023; Geva et al., 2022b). Using this approach, we identify
051   parametric “concept vectors” in LLMs that are suitable for testing unlearning (§3); these vectors
052   are located in the model’s MLP layers and strongly affect the generation of their corresponding
053   concepts, without influencing unrelated ones. By applying this methodology to two open-source
      LLMs — LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023) and OLMo (Groeneveld et al., 2024) — we construct the

073   We use C ONCEPT V ECTORS to evaluate various unlearning methods, including gradient-based unlearn-
074   ing, preference-based optimization, parameter-specific interventions and representation engineering
075
      (§4). Our results show that while existing unlearning methods prevent models from generating con-
      cept information, they only affect negligible changes to its parametric knowledge traces (Figure 1b).
076
      At the same time, directly intervening in a certain concept vector effectively erases the information it
077
      encodes about the concept, thereby having a pronounced effect on the model’s generation (Figure 1d).
078   Lastly, we showcase the importance of erasing parametric knowledge to improve robustness against
079   adversarial attacks (§5). We apply multiple adversarial attacks (Lynch et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2023b;
080   Deng et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Zou et al., 2023b) to jailbreak the model after unlearning,
081   measuring their impact on the concept vectors’ activations and the generation of knowledge that was
082   presumably unlearned. Our experiments show that (Figure 1b-d) (a) jailbreak bypass unlearning
083   by increasing the activations of concept vectors, (b) existing unlearning methods suppress the para-
084   metric knowledge rather than erase it, and (c) better removal of parametric knowledge can enhance
085   unlearning robustness and reduce jailbreak success.
086   To conclude, we argue that unlearning methods should be evaluated not only on external performance
087   but also on their ability to erase parametric knowledge. We propose a methodology for creating such
088   evaluations and introduce C ONCEPT V ECTORS, the first benchmark for parameter-based intrinsic
089   evaluation of unlearning. Our experiments show that existing unlearning methods fail to remove
090   parametric knowledge, allowing these knowledge traces to be reactivated during text generation post-
091   unlearning. These results highlight the challenge and importance of fully erasing learned concepts in
092
      LLMs and call for new methods that effectively remove parametric knowledge traces.

094   2    PARAMETRIC K NOWLEDGE T RACES FOR U NLEARNING E VALUATION
095
096   We focus on the case of concept erasure, where the information to unlearn is any knowledge about a
097   given concrete concept. For example, if the concept to erase is the fictional character Harry Potter,
098   then after unlearning the model should not be able to generate information about Harry Potter, such
099   as his best friends being Hermione Granger and Ron Weasley and his creator being J.K. Rowling.
100   We posit that to evaluate unlearning performance, it is essential to verify that information has been
101   removed from the model parameters, rather than solely relying on behavioural tests. Namely, if some
102   parameters are strongly associated with a certain concept, then this association should be scratched
103   post-unlearning. We formulate this idea next.
104   Recent works have shown that parametric associations with concrete concepts can be observed by
105   “reading” the information encoded in parameters through projection to the model’s vocabulary space
106   (Dar et al., 2023; Geva et al., 2022b). Specifically, Geva et al. (2022b) showed that outputs from the
107   MLP layers in transformer-based LLMs (Vaswani et al., 2017) can be viewed as a linear combination
      of parameter vectors in the second MLP layer, each promoting a concept in the vocabulary space

133
      We leverage the idea of parametric concept vectors to construct a benchmark for unlearning methods,
134   consisting of both intrinsic and behavioural evaluation. We describe our data collection methodology
135   in §3.1, and the resulting benchmark from applying this methodology to two recent LLMs2 — LLaMA
136   2 7B (chat version) (Touvron et al., 2023) and OLMo 7B (Groeneveld et al., 2024) — in §3.2.
137
138   3.1     B ENCHMARK C ONSTRUCTION M ETHODOLOGY
139
140   We wish to create a benchmark that tests the ability of unlearning methods to erase concept informa-
141   tion, at both the parametric level and the behavioral level. To this end, we design a four-step data
142   collection process, illustrated in Figure 2 and explained in detail next. Eventually, each generated
143   example consists of a concept, a parameters vector corresponding to that concept, and a set of
144
      behavioural tests with question-answer pairs and text completion queries.
145
      Design Considerations To demonstrate the importance of intrinsic erasure, we focus on cases
146
      where localized concept vectors exist, are relatively easy to find, and are causally important. By
147
      this criterion, our benchmark is necessarily not exhaustive: it may omit additional existing concept
148   vectors that were not identified, and it may omit concepts for which no selective concept vectors exist.
149   While future work should further study the existence of selective concept vectors as a phenomenon
150   and shed light on the settings in which they do exist, we argue that this benchmark is still highly
151   valuable as it focuses on cases that any effective unlearning methods must address: cases where the
152   information about the erased concept is localized in the model and is causally significant to the ability
153   of the model to generate texts about the concept. Indeed, in §4 we show that even the potentially
154   partial set of concept vectors we identified poses a major challenge for unlearning methods.
155

193   Step 2: Generating Behavioural Tests In addition to our intrinsic evaluation, we create data
194   for behavioural evaluation. Having both types of tests is valuable as it allows for studying the
195   gap between parametric and behavioural changes. These two approaches complement each other;
196   intrinsic evaluations directly look at the information encoded internally in the model parameters,
197   while behavioral evaluations measure the downstream effects of unlearning on model outputs. We
198

199

200
      in §A). For text completion, we obtain Wikipedia articles about every concept and then sample a
201   maximum number of m paragraphs per concept from these articles. From each paragraph we take
202   the first half as a query for the model. Note that in both settings there is no need for gold answers or
203   references, as our goal is to evaluate the effect of unlearning on the model’s outputs. Thus, for both
204   settings we collect as references the generated answers and completions by LLaMA and OLMo.
205
206
      Step 3: Causal Validation of Concept Vectors To validate that the selected concept vectors
207   promote the concepts observed in their vocabulary projections (and not other concepts), we conduct
208   a simple causal validation step. For every concept vector vjℓ corresponding to some concept c, we
209
      damage this vector by adding Gaussian noise vjℓ ←   − vjℓ + ε where ε ∼ N (0, 0.1), while keeping all
210   other parameters in the model intact. We set the standard deviation of the noise to 0.1 as it is sufficient
211   for erasing the concept knowledge. Then, we use the QA pairs collected in Step 2 to evaluate the
212   effect of this intervention on the ability of the model to answer questions about the concept c and r
213   other concepts c1 , ...cr . We compare the model’s generated answers with and without the added noise
214   using BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) and Rouge-L (Lin, 2004). Finally, we take only concept vectors
215   for which adding noise leads to responses that are substantially different for the concept-related
      questions but similar for concept-unrelated questions. For additional details, see §A.3.

260
261
      We use C ONCEPT V ECTORS to evaluate how well existing unlearning methods erase parametric
262   information compared to suppressing behavioural extraction of that information. To this end, we split
263   the concepts of each model into 10% validation set and 90% test set. We use the validation set for
264   hyperparameter tuning (see details in §E) and report results on the test set.
265
266   4.1     U NLEARNING M ETHODS
267
268   We evaluate a series of existing methods for concept unlearning, including methods that rely on
269   gradient ascent, preference optimization, and localized model editing. We also evaluate an oracle
      baseline, called Needle, that given a concept erases the information in its concept vector.


                                                            5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Likelihood Maximization Gradient ascent, a simple and widely adopted unlearning method,
271   maximizes the next-token prediction loss over a set of text sequences that we wish the LLM to forget,
272   thereby “revert” the optimization on the forget set via gradient descent during pretraining. For a
273   given concept, we fine-tune the model on Wikipedia articles about the concept (collected as described
274   in §3.1, Step 2). We use two optimization variations: vanilla gradient ascent Jang et al. (2023)
275   and gradient difference Yao et al. (2024), which adds a regularization term to minimize the KL
276   divergence between the unlearned and the original LLM on a reference text dataset, thus preventing
277   the model from catastrophic deterioration of its general capability.
278
279   Preference Optimization We fine-tune the models on a dataset with preference feedback
280   ⟨xi , yi+ , yi− ⟩ where yi+ , yi− are the two responses for the input xi , generated by a pretrained LLM,
281   and yi+ is a preferred output by over yi− . For unlearning, the unfavored response yi− would be the
282   original response to xi (before unlearning), and the favored yi+ is our expected model response after a
283   concept has been erased. To unlearn concepts in C ONCEPT V ECTORS, we use text completion queries
284   collected as described in §3.1. For a concept c, we take a query qc as the input xi and the model’s
285
      response rc to qc before unlearning as the negative output yi− . For a positive output yi+ , we take the
      model’s response rc′ to a query qc′ about a different concept c′ ̸= c. For queries about c, this training
286
      should steer the model to output paragraphs about irrelevant concepts. We test three preference
287
      optimization methods on concept unlearning: (a) direct preference optimization (DPO) (Rafailov
288   et al., 2023), which maximizes the log-likelihood ratio between generating the preferred and the
289   unfavored responses, while retaining a small shift from the original LLM predictive distribution, (b)
290   negative preference optimization (NPO) (Zhao et al., 2024a), which discards the favored responses
291   and only minimizes the prediction probability of the unfavored answers, and (c) NPO+KL which
292   adds to NPO a KL divergence loss between the model’s outputs before and after unlearning.
293
      The above methods optimize all the LLM parameters indistinguishably. To account for the fact that
294   concept vectors are located in the MLP modules, we additionally employ NPO+KL while restricting
295   it to optimize only the second MLP matrices in the network, i.e. WVℓ for ℓ ∈ [1, ..., L].
296
297   Model Editing Editing methods perform local parameter updates to LLM modules that encode
298   knowledge about target concepts. In this setting, facts are typically viewed as subject-relation-object
299   triplets ⟨s, r, o⟩, where the goal is to update a given triplet in the model with a new object, i.e.,
300             − ⟨s, r, o′ ⟩ where o′ ̸= o. For example, changing the team for which Lionel Messi plays
      ⟨s, r, o⟩ →
301   from PSG to Inter Miami could be represented as the update ⟨Lionel Messi, team, PSG⟩ →              −
302   ⟨Lionel Messi, team, Inter Miami⟩. We use a prominent model editing algorithm,
303   MEMIT (Meng et al., 2023), which applies updates to the model’s MLP modules. Specifically,
304   we follow Patil et al. (2024), who have proposed multiple methods to adapt MEMIT from knowl-
305
      edge editing to knowledge removal. We use the two best-performing methods reported in Patil
      et al. (2024). The first method is empty response, which sets the new target in the editing task
306
      to a “dummy” meaningless object. For example, the fact that J.K. Rowling is the author of Harry
307
      Potter will be removed through the update ⟨Harry Potter, author, J.K. Rowling⟩ →                    −
308   ⟨Harry Potter, author, dummy⟩. The second method is max entropy, which replaces the
309   original objective of MEMIT with a new objective that suppresses tokens related to the object from
310   appearing with high probability in the vocabulary projection of hidden representations at during
311   inference. This is achieved by maximizing the entropy of the next-token probability distribution over
312   the vocabulary for every layer. In this method, the object in the new triplet is the same as in the
313   original fact, i.e. o′ = o.
314   To apply MEMIT on C ONCEPT V ECTORS, we obtained factual triplets about every concept from
315   Wikidata (Vrandečić & Krötzsch, 2014). Then, we converted the triplets into facts in natural language,
316   using per-relation templates generated by GPT-4 which we verified manually. In addition, we use
317   handcrafted templates written for knowledge editing benchmarks — RippleEdits (Cohen et al.,
318   2024) and CounterFact (Meng et al., 2022). Overall, we obtained 247 templates for the concepts in
319   C ONCEPT V ECTORS, which cover an average of 47.3 facts per concept.

      methods is to perturb the model’s activations on the target data, making it more difficult for the model

      to process and recall this knowledge, rather than directly erasing the knowledge stored in the the
325   model’s parameters. We evaluate RMU (Li et al., 2024), a representative method in this group, on
326   C ONCEPT V ECTORS. Specifically, we consider two variants: the original method of RMU, which
327   modifies fixed layers for all samples, and a more dynamic version dubbed RMU (enhanced) that
328   modifies the layer containing the concept vector and the two preceding layers.
329
330   Needle (Oracle) We evaluate a baseline that, given a concept, damages its corresponding concept
331   vector. To this end, Needle directly ablates the concept vector by adding a Gaussian noise vector to it,
332   namely, vjℓ ←− vjℓ + ε where ε ∼ N (0, 0.1) (we choose a value of 0.1 as it is sufficient for erasing
333   the encoded knowledge, see details in §E).
334
335   4.2   E VALUATION M ETRICS
336
      We evaluate concept unlearning performance in terms of both changes in the parametric concept
337
      vectors (intrinsic evaluation) and the inability of the model to generate information about the con-
338
      cept (behavioural evaluation). For parametric intrinsic, we compare the concept vector vjℓ and its
339
      corresponding set of top-tokens Tjℓ before and after unlearning. Let v̂jℓ be the concept vector after
340
341
      unlearning, we first report the cosine similarity and the L2 distance between vjℓ and v̂jℓ . Similarly,
342   we compare Tjℓ and T̂jℓ , the top-tokens set corresponding to v̂jℓ , using Jaccard similarity. For
343   behavioural evaluation, we use our collected QA pairs and text completion queries. For a given
344
      concept c, we evaluate model performance on the set of questions and queries about c and about five
      other concepts, reporting the average BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) and Rouge-L (Lin, 2004) scores.

      4.3   R ESULTS
348
      Results are shown in Table 3, and example outputs before and after unlearning are provided in §B.
349   While gradient-based and preference-based optimization methods substantially restrict models from
350   generating information about the concept (with Target QA and text completion scores < 0.25), they
351   introduce only minimal changes to the concept vectors, with almost all of the concept-related tokens
352   retained in the top of the projection (Jaccard similarity scores > 0.98). Similar trends also hold
353   for the NPO+KL baseline, which directly optimizes the MLP layers where the concept vectors are
354   located. Overall, this shows that while fine-tuning methods influence the behaviour of the model, they
355   fail to erase the information about the concept from its parameters.
356   In contrast, Needle (which directly impairs the concept vector), successfully removes the encoded
357   information about the concept (Jaccard similarity of < 0.05) while demonstrating prominent effect
358   on the model’s outputs (40% − 60% decrease in QA performance). Moreover, Needle exhibits the
359   biggest gap between the target and unrelated QA scores of 41 and 45 BLEU points difference in
360   LLaMA and OLMo, respectively, compared to <30 points difference by other methods. This suggests
361   that Needle achieves the best trade-off between preserving unrelated knowledge and erasing target
362   knowledge. The higher target QA scores of Needle compared to other methods could be attributed to
363
      the fact that it modifies only a single vector — a small fraction (< 0.001%) of the model’s parameters
      — while there are likely other parameters encoding information about the same concept. Notably,
364
      ablating a random concept vector in the model results in a target QA score close to 1. Therefore,
365
      the fact that erasing a specific parameter vector introduces such a dramatic decrease in the target
366   QA score indicates that this vector is indeed crucial for encoding the concept’s knowledge. Overall,
367   these results further show the effectiveness and potential of unlearning methods that target relevant
368   parametric knowledge traces. As discussed in §3.1, the benchmark was constructed to include
369   concepts for which localized, behaviorally relevant vectors can be identified. As such, the intrinsic
370   performance of Needle is an upper bound for any unlearning method on our benchmark.
371
      Notably, compared to finetuning-based methods, knowledge editing achieves lower target QA scores
372   on both models, while maintaining higher unrelated QA scores. This superior behavioral performance
373   aligns with its greater impact on the target concept vector observed in intrinsic metrics compared to
374   finetuning-based methods. Although RMU also impacts the concept vector to a similar degree, its
375   objective function primarily aims to disrupt the model’s activation on target knowledge, rather than
376   directly editing the target knowledge as MEMIT does. Consequently, RMU under-performs MEMIT.
377   Furthermore, when considering unlearning specificity, finetuning-based unlearning methods cause
      significant interference with unrelated knowledge. We further elaborate on this in §5.2.

      information contributes to the success of jailbreak attacks, then its removal should make these attacks

491   6    R ELATED W ORK
492
493   Evaluating Unlearning Several benchmarks and evaluation metrics have been developed to assess
494   the effectiveness of unlearning in LLMs. Eldan & Russinovich (2023) designed a specific task to forget
495   the concept of ’Harry Potter’, while Maini et al. (2024) introduced TOFU, a task involving learning
496
      and then forgetting knowledge about fictitious author profiles. Li et al. (2024) created the WMDP
      benchmark to measure the impact of alignment algorithms in unlearning harmful knowledge from
497
      LLMs, and Lynch et al. (2024) presented unlearning evaluation methods which include assessment
498
      of robustness against jailbreak attacks. However, the aspect of knowledge unlearning by tracking
499   parametric knowledge traces remains unexplored.
500
501
      Knowledge Localization in LLMs Recent studies showed that LLMs store factual associations in
502
      MLP weights (Geva et al., 2022b; Dar et al., 2023), which are recalled and transmitted to the output
503   layer during inference (Geva et al., 2023b; Meng et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2024). Additionally, Dai et al.
504   (2022) and Meng et al. (2022) demonstrated that manipulating knowledge traces associated with a
505   specific concept could alter model responses. Our work leverages these findings to establish an initial
506   link between knowledge localization and unlearning concepts. Chang et al. (2023) trained a small
507   set of LLM parameters to inject artificial concept knowledge, and then assessed the effectiveness of
508   knowledge localization methods in deleting memorized sequences. Unlike this work, our approach
509   relies on knowledge traces created through the natural training process of LLMs.
510
511   LLM Safety and Adversarial Attacks There is growing evidence that current LLM safety align-
512   ments can be easily “jailbroken” (Zou et al., 2023a; Andriushchenko et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2023).
513   Lee et al. (2024) found that MLP vectors in GPT-2 associated with toxic language remained largely
514   unchanged after applying alignment via preference optimization, and Lynch et al. (2024) demon-
515
      strated that jailbreak can elicit knowledge about “erased” concepts. Recently, Patil et al. (2024) also
      showed that adversarial attacks could recover information unlearned by model editing algorithms.
516
      We show via comprehensive evaluation that such superficiality issue is shared across all unlearning
517
      methods, and that erasing parametric knowledge could reduce susceptibility to malicious attacks.
518
519
520   7    C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
521
522   We present the C ONCEPT V ECTORS benchmark for evaluating the ability of unlearning methods to
523   erase parametric knowledge encoded as “concept vectors”. Experiments on C ONCEPT V ECTORS
524   demonstrate that existing unlearning methods fail to produce significant parametric modifications,
525   while ablating the located concept vectors effectively erases the corresponding concept knowledge,
526   making it much harder to elicit concept knowledge from LLMs through adversarial attacks. Our
527
      findings highlight a deficiency in behavior-based unlearning evaluation, which may overlook residual
      knowledge in the model. This motivates future work toward developing more thorough and robust
528
      methods of unlearning.
529
530
531
      Limitations Our data collection process does not guarantee a coverage of all the parameters
      encoding the concept. Particularly, we only examine the MLP layers, whereas factual information may
532
      also be stored in other modules (Geva et al., 2023b). Therefore, although existing unlearning methods
533
      impose minimal changes to vectors in C ONCEPT V ECTORS, they may have ablated other concept-
534   related parameters in the model. However, our jailbreak analysis shows that C ONCEPT V ECTORS
535   covers a crucial subset of knowledge parameters, whose presence impairs robustness against jailbreak
536   attacks. Second, while our benchmark is constructed around concepts with vectors that strongly
537   express them, in practice, concepts in LLMs are often encoded in superposition (Elhage et al., 2022).
538   This phenomenon makes both unlearning and its evaluation harder, as editing a certain concept could
539   still inadvertently affect unrelated ones (Huang et al., 2024). Future work should develop unlearning
      methods and evaluation protocols that consider disentangled editing of concept knowledge.

      Our work has considerable social implications, especially in safeguarding private information, reduc-

545   However, we recognize that our findings could also have negative implications. Specifically, some

838

840   0.05, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, and 1.0. We found that setting a value of 0.1 is sufficient to significantly erase the
841   target knowledge, resulting in noticeably different performance trends on concept-related questions
842   compared to unrelated ones. Therefore, we chose 0.1 as the standard deviation for the added Gaussian
843   noise in our validation experiments.
844
      When injecting a Gaussian noise into the target concept vector while keeping all other model
845   parameters unchanged, the quality of model-generated answers related to the target concept decreases
846   substantially. In contrast, for QA tests unrelated to the target concept, the average model answer
847   quality remains almost unchanged. The two right plots in Figure 4 further show the breakdown
848   distributions of model-generated answer BLEU scores on C ONCEPT V ECTORS for both target and
849   unrelated QA tests. These results suggest that the concept vectors we identified are crucial for storing
850   the target knowledge and are essential for any effective unlearning method to erase such knowledge.
851   Finally, we selected vector candidates where the BLEU score difference between the target QA
852   and unrelated QA exceeded 0.2 before and after noise addition. These vectors were added to our
853
      benchmark, indicating that at least a substantial portion of the selected vectors are objectively related
      to the target knowledge. This resulted in a benchmark of an appropriate size.

      A.7      AUTOMATED A PPROACH FOR S ELECTING C ONCEPT V ECTORS
905
906   We describe a more automated approach here to fulfill the requirement of larger-scale parametric
907   benchmarks in the future, thereby minimizing the labor required. For identifying concept vectors
908   responsible for any given topic or concept, we can utilize a set of concept-related keywords, to
909   automatically identify the candidate vector, whose projection onto the vocabulary space has the
910   highest probabilities for the tokens contained in this keyword set, among all candidate vectors in the
911   model. A similar approach has been demonstrated in prior work (Geva et al., 2022a). Then, we can
912
      proceed with the usual automated causal verification for this potential concept vector following the
      step 3 detailed in §3.1.

995    C.1   D ETAILS OF U NLEARNING JAILBREAK
996
997
       Table 9 listed the four manual jailbreak prompts we use to test the robustness of unlearning methods,
       along with additional experimental results showing the Rouge scores of two models in Figure 5.
998
       In particular, the first two prompts are handcrafted adversarial attack templates taken from (Lynch
999
       et al., 2024), and the third one is a low resources language attack template, where we translate the
1000   target questions into German and then pose them to the target model to verify the unlearning effect.
1001   The fourth type is an in-context learning attack, where we include a 2000-token Wikipedia passage
1002   about the target concept in the prompt, attempting to make the unlearned model recall the relevant
1003   knowledge about the concept, and then use the corresponding QA example for testing.
1004
       In order to extend the applicability of our experimental results to more advanced jailbreak methods,
1005
       we also evaluated two of the most prominent approaches in the area of automatic jailbreak prompt
1006   generation: Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) (Zou et al., 2023b) and AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2024b).
1007   A brief overview of each method is provided below:
1008
1009          • Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG): Zou et al. (2023b) propose Greedy Coordinate Gradient
1010            (GCG), a gradient-based jailbreak attack. In this method, they append adversarial suffixes to
1011            the prompts and compute top-k substitutions for the suffix token at each position. The suffixes
1012            are then optimized to find the best adversarial prompt. Experimental results demonstrate that
1013            the suffixes trained on a white-box model can even transfer effectively to different public
1014            black-box models.
1015
              • AutoDAN: AutoDAN, proposed by Liu et al. (2024b), is an interpretable, gradient-based
1016
                attack method designed for bypassing LLM safety alignments using hierarchical genetic
1017            algorithms. It generates adversarial suffixes in a stepwise process, optimizing each new
1018            token using the Single Token Optimization algorithm. This approach balances the need
1019            for both effective jailbreaks and high readability, ensuring the suffix remains semantically
1020            meaningful. As a result, AutoDAN successfully bypasses perplexity filters and achieves
1021            improved attack success when transferred to models like GPT-4.
1022
1023   In both methods, we use the unlearned models as the base for optimizing the adversarial prompt. The
1024   original outputs of the vanilla model for the same queries serves as the target for prompt optimization
1025   training. Additionally, we keep the other hyperparameters of the training consistent with the settings
       from the original papers.

1161
1162   For LLaMA2-7B-chat (Touvron et al., 2023), for each finetuning-based unlearning method we

1170   chosen from {20, 25, 30}. Additionally, for editing each concept, we set the editing layers to the four
1171   layers including and preceding the layer containing the concept vector.
