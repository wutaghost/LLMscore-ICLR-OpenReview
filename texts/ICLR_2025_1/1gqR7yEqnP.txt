009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Training a deep model is fundamentally about reducing loss, and we often believe
012            that a “good model” is one that trained with a “good loss.” This paper investi-
013            gates that belief. We show that even when learning with unstructured, randomized
014            labels, models can still discover generalized features. We propose that generaliza-
015            tion in deep learning is not about learning the structure of data through a well-
016            structured loss, but rather a process akin to “pan for gold,” where gradient descent
017            shakes through the function space, naturally stabilizing useful features. To support
018
               this, we present quantitative and qualitative experimental evidence, and introduce
               the Panning through Unstructured Label (PUL) algorithm. We demonstrate its ef-
019
               fectiveness across various fields, showing improvements in unsupervised domain
020
               adaptation, state-of-the-art performance in object discovery, and its ability to mit-
021            igate massive attention issues. Finally, we offer a new interpretation of existing
022            deep learning assumptions, challenging the conventional beliefs in the field.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Most modern deep learning involves reducing a certain loss in an overparameterized model using
028   SGD. Since SGD tries to minimize a given loss, this inherently reflects the assumption that the
029   lower the loss, the better the model. The loss serves as a metric for how well the model captures
030   the structure in the data during training. A lower loss implies that the model has learned more about
031   the data structure from the training data. For instance, in image classification problems, we define
032   a classification loss that reflects the structure of image-category pairs (Deng et al., 2009) or use
033
      a consistency loss (Chen et al., 2020; He et al., 2020; Grill et al., 2020; Caron et al., 2021b) that
      encodes our belief that two transformed versions (e.g., through cropping or rotation) of the same
034
      image should represent the same object. In any case, this process rests on two key ideas: 1) SGD
035
      reduces the given loss, and 2) meaningful reduction requires a well-defined structure.
036
037   This paper deals with the following question: Is structure itself the essence of a ‘good’ result? We
038   approached this question with a proof by contradiction: if structure were essential for producing
039
      good results, removing the structure should lead to poor results. We completely removed the struc-
      ture from the learning process by randomizing the class labels, and found that the model actually
040
      was able to learn from data despite the complete randomization and even performed better from a
041
      generalization perspective. Based on these observations and mathematical analysis, we propose a
042   hypothesis about the driving force of generalization in deep learning. We argue that the success of
043   deep learning is not due to the encoding of human beliefs, but to the stochasticity of SGD and diverse
044   exploration of the loss surface of overparameterized models throughout the learning process. The
045   process can be compared to panning for gold: just as randomly shaking a pan sifts out all the impuri-
046   ties except for the gold, there is a very strong shaking in the functional space throughout the learning
047   process which automatically shakes out the noisy features and leaves the ‘good’ features. In doing
048   so, we show that the unstructured random labels leaves more ‘gold’. In this paper, we present strong
049   experimental evidence supporting our bold hypothesis. Furthermore, we present an algorithm that
050
      exploits our hypothesis to dramatically improve performances on existing deep learning problems
      reaching state-of-the-art performance in some areas in a very simple way.
051
052   To analyze this observation, we view the development of deep learning in the functional space.
053   First, we investigate the condition of functional descent necessary for loss reduction. Alongside this
      analysis, we observed a swing phenomenon, where the output of the neural network fluctuates dra-

      matically during the early stages of training. We explain this phenomenon using the Neural Tangent
055   Kernel (NTK) (Jacot et al., 2018). Based on our observations and analysis, we propose the “pan for
056   gold” hypothesis, which suggests that as learning progresses, the swings of the function diminish,
057   highlighting only the important features of the given dataset. To verify our hypothesis of Pan for
058   Gold, we conducted several experiments. First, we confirmed the presence of effective functional
059   descent by demonstrating the positive definiteness of the NTK and observing a flattened loss sur-
060   face along the gradient direction. Next, using Grad-CAM visualizations (Selvaraju et al., 2017), we
061   illustrated that even models trained with unstructured labels naturally focus on meaningful features.
062   Based on our hypothesis, we proposed an algorithm, Panning through Unstructured Label (PUL)
063   that improves the generalization ability of existing deep learning models by using unstructured la-
064   bels. First, we demonstrated that PUL enhances performance in unsupervised domain adaptation,
065   where accessing target labels is restricted. By simply assigning random labels to the unlabeled tar-
066   get data, we increased performance in classification and object detection. Moreover, we achieved
067   state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance in the object discovery task within just a few epochs using

069
      improvements demonstrated that unstructured labels can enhance attention maps. We also observed
      a significant improvement in the activation distribution and successfully alleviated the widespread
070
      attention issues (Xiao et al., 2024), which are a major challenge in the quantization field. The pro-
071
      vided results show that our ‘gold-preserving’ algorithm significantly enhances performance across
072   various domains that require generalization. Lastly, we argued that our methodology not only im-
073   proves existing algorithms quantitatively but also offers a new perspective on certain phenomena.
074   For example, while the edge-biasing issue observed in XAI methods like Grad-CAM (Selvaraju
075   et al., 2017) is often regarded as a flaw of these methods, we confirmed that it is not a defect, but
076   rather a natural outcome of the model’s learning process.

080   2.1   C ONVENTIONAL B ELIEF ON DATA
081
082   The goal of deep learning is to learn from data according to structures defined by humans. These
083   structures are shaped by human prior knowledge or established beliefs. They form the essence of the
084   model’s learning process. People mathematically conceptualized this belief as ‘Energy’, and we use
085   the Energy-Based-Models (EDM) (Xie et al., 2022) for developing our idea.
086   Humans have a belief that data can be categorized based on certain criteria that are used to define
087   input-query pairs. For a given data x ∈ X , they classify it and assign a structured label y ∈ Y
088   according to these assumptions. The supervision energy function Esup , which represents the state
089   of the model fθ , and hence the data, can be defined as follows for an arbitrary distance metric d:

126   3     PAN FOR GOLD
127
128
      We propose a new hypothesis regarding the learning process of deep models, which we call the
129
      “pan for gold.” We suggest that, akin to sifting for gold in a river, stochastic gradient descent (SGD)
130   inherently filters out unnecessary features as it navigates through the function space of an overpa-
131   rameterized model. Just as water and sand (irrelevant features) pass through a sieve, leaving behind
132   valuable gold (essential features), this filtering occurs naturally, without any active search for use-
133   ful elements. We argue that this process takes place independently of human-imposed structures or
134   belief systems.
135
136
      Overview Our claim is that model generalization naturally arises when certain conditions (Eq. (3))
137
      are satisfied. In Sec. 3.2, we analyze what these conditions mean in terms of functions, under the
138
      idea that because overparameterized models memorize any training dataset, the corresponding loss
139   should decrease in an overparameterized setting. We explore experimentally in Sec. 3.3 that this
140   process can be highly unstable, and analyze it through NTK. From these observations, we deduce
141   the “pan for gold” hypothesis in Sec. 3.4. Finally, in Sec. 3.5, we qualitatively assess whether our
142   hypothesis holds true and if the learning process follows the pattern we propose.

      3.1   P RELIMINARIES
146
147
      A deep learning model can be viewed as a function conditioned by parameters, which generates
      outputs for various inputs. From this perspective, the change in weights during gradient descent can
148
      be interpreted as the development of the function over time. By observing how the function evolves
149
      over time, we can gain a deeper understanding of the model’s learning process.
150
151   In this context, the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) (Jacot et al., 2018) serves as a powerful tool
152   for such functional analysis. NTK models the deep learning model f (·, θ) as a kernel k(·, ·), and by
153
      analyzing this kernel, we can perform functional analysis of the learning dynamics. Through this
      approach, we can mathematically interpret the complex learning process of deep learning models.
154
      By analyzing the eigenvalues of the NTK, for instance, we can determine how rapidly the model is
155
      changing during training and how efficiently it is minimizing the loss.

299   3.4   C ONJECTURE : PAN FOR G OLD
300
301
      Based on the observations above, we propose the following hypothesis: the generalization of deep
      learning arises from the process of learning the function itself. This learning process is highly un-
302
      stable and causes drastic change of the function. SGD naturally resolves these instabilities and as
303
      the learning process stabilizes, what we call ‘gold features’ naturally emerge. In this context, gold
304   features refer to elements that satisfy Eq. (3). During the learning process, the model must be able to
305   distinguish between gradients and noise, and furthermore, it should not be significantly affected by
306   the curvature of the loss function’s surface as it traverses it. By retaining gold features while elimi-
307   nating the remaining characteristics within the function space, the model achieves generalization.
308
309   Unstructured Labels Leave Out More Gold According to our assumption, the generalization of
310   deep learning is ultimately a process of leaving only the ‘gold features,’ and if we simply perturb
311   the model, those ‘gold’ will be automatically obtained. The remaining question, then, is how can we
312   leave behind more of this gold? We can find the answer directly in Eq. (3). Generalization is the dis-
313
      tinction between the stochastic gradient g̃ and the unintended noise ε from a functional perspective,
      and the difficulty of this distinction increases drastically when unstructured labels are used. To dis-
314
      tinguish g̃, we must understand it, which implies that g̃ must be consistent over time t. In the case of
315
      unstructured labels, since there is no structure in the data, this consistency is much lower compared
316   to structured labels. Therefore, to enhance this consistency, as much ‘gold feature’ as possible must
317   be retained. With this approach, we can simply improve the generalization capability of the existing
318   models, as demonstrated in Sec. 4.

535   In this paper, we present a provocative claim that the process of “learning from data” occurs inde-
536   pendently of human-imposed structures. To support this, we introduce the bold alternative hypoth-
537   esis called the “Pan for Gold”. Through extensive experiments, we quantitatively and qualitatively
