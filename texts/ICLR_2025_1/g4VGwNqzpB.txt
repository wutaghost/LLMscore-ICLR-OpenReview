                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            We introduce a novel framework for analyzing neural networks based on the con-
011            cepts of dynamic and static neurons, which describe the stability of neuron acti-
012            vation under specific inputs. From these concepts, we propose neuron entropy as
013            a metric to quantify network expressiveness. Our analysis reveals that better gen-
014            eralization correlates with diverse activation patterns and higher neuron entropy.
015            Building on this, we propose our HENP method, a dynamic pruning technique
016            that regulates dying neurons and sparsifies the network during training. Exper-
017
               imental results demonstrate that our HENP improves both network sparsity and
               performance, offering a new approach to efficient neural network optimization.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024
025   Deep neural networks equipped with piecewise linear activation functions, such as the Rectified
026   Linear Unit (ReLU), have gained widespread popularity due to their remarkable performance across
027
      various domains and computational efficiency Zhou (2021); Jiang (2018). However, understanding
      the underlying mechanics of these networks remains a significant challenge. In this paper, we pro-
028
      pose a comprehensive framework for analyzing the structure and behavior of deep neural networks,
029
      specifically focusing on the partitioning of the input space of a network N , defined on Rn with a
030   piecewise linear activation function π. This partitioning results in multiple linear zones, where the
031   mapping within each zone is linear, collectively enabling the approximation of complex, non-linear
032   functions.
033
      Recent studies have established that the number of linear zones serves as a quantitative measure of
034
      a model’s complexity Cohan (2022). However, as the depth and width of a network increase, the
035
      significance of analyzing a single linear zone diminishes. To provide a more nuanced evaluation
036   of large-scale models, our framework investigates neuron activation across multiple linear zones,
037   capturing not only the linear transformations within individual zones but also the intricate transitions
038   between them, which are crucial for enhancing the performance of deep neural networks.
039
      By analyzing neuron behavior across different activation zones, we gain insights into the represen-
040
      tational capacity and stability of these models, addressing the challenges posed by the increasing
041
      complexity of modern architectures. Our contributions include a detailed examination of the fun-
042   damental structures and formalisms of feedforward neural networks, as outlined in Section 3. In
043   Section 4, we explore the global stability of neuron activation zones, revealing that dynamic neu-
044   rons significantly enhance the model’s ability to capture complex functions. Conversely, a high
045   proportion of static neurons can lead to linear approximations, undermining representational capa-
046   bilities. To quantify neuron uncertainty, we introduce a novel neuron entropy metric, demonstrating
047   its correlation with representation capacity in deeper layers.
048   Furthermore, we address the issue of dying neurons Ziping (2023); Lu (2020), where activations
049   remain unchanged regardless of input. To mitigate this, we propose a Hybrid Entropy-Norm Pruning
050   (HENP) technique, enabling effective network pruning without additional training or accuracy loss
051   by eliminating these dying neurons. In Section 5, we detail the integration of norms and entropy in
052   HENP and present experimental results illustrating its effectiveness, including the computation of
053   neuron entropy on the test set and the application of a masking strategy on neurons with the lowest
      entropy.

      2.1   O N N EURAL N ETWORKS A NALYSIS
057
058   To understand the remarkable performance of neural networks, early research addressed fundamen-
059   tal challenges like vanishing and exploding gradients Bengio (1994); Hochreiter & Schmidhuber
060   (1997), prediction instability Szegedy (2013), and constraints on model capacity Montúfar (2014).
061   These challenges spurred extensive efforts to analyze the internal workings of neural networks
062   Goodfellow (2017); Xavier & Bengio (2010), utilizing methods such as feature attribution Samek
063   (2017); Montavon (2018); Ancona (2018) and complexity analysis Raghu (2017). Feature attribu-
064
      tion techniques, such as Integrated Gradients Sundararajan (2017), enhance interpretability, which
      is crucial for transparency and trust in neural networks Doshi-Velez & Kim (2017). In parallel, the-
065
      oretical research has advanced, particularly regarding over-parameterization and model complexity
066
      trade-offs Bubeck (2020), as well as the linear zones of ReLU networks, which relate to expressivity
067   and approximation capacity Zhang (2021); Pascanu (2013). There is also a growing emphasis on
068   interpretable models, with methods integrating decision trees Murdoch (2019); Letham (2015) or
069   prototype-based classifiers like ProtoPNet Chen (2019), offering human-like recognition and trans-
070   parency Caruana (2015). Further studies have examined the piecewise linear properties of networks
071   Arora (2018), quantifying their complexity Hanin & David (2019), which informs generalization
072   capabilities Goodfellow (2015).
073
074   2.2   T OWARDS N ETWORK C OMPRESSION
075
076   Model compression is a crucial research area in deep learning, particularly for deploying large mod-
077
      els on resource-constrained devices. The goal is to reduce computational and memory costs while
      maintaining predictive accuracy. Techniques include pruning, which removes redundant weights
078
      or neurons, with structured pruning targeting filters or channels to enhance hardware compatibil-
079
      ity Blalock (2020); Lemaire (2019). Quantization lowers the precision of weights and activations,
080   typically from 32-bit to 8-bit, using post-training or quantization-aware methods to preserve accu-
081   racy Krishnamoorthi (2018); Jacob (2018); Gholami (2021). Knowledge distillation trains smaller
082   models to replicate the output of larger ones, efficiently transferring knowledge without significant
083   performance loss Hinton (2015); Tian (2022); Gou (2021). Neural Architecture Search (NAS) au-
084   tomates the design of efficient models, employing multi-objective optimization for accuracy and
085   speed, while recent advancements like differentiable NAS reduce search costs, making it more fea-
086   sible for hardware-constrained deployment Elsken (2019); Cai (2020); Radosavovic (2020); Xu
087   (2020); Zela (2020). Together, these techniques drive significant improvements in compression,
088
      enabling more practical applications of deep learning Liu (2019); Han (2016); Cheng (2018).

090   2.3   U NDERSTANDING DYING N EURONS
091
      The ReLU activation function can lead to neuronal death, where neurons stop contributing to the
092
      network due to weight updates. While ReLU avoids gradient vanishing issues common in sigmoid
093   functions and helps prevent gradient saturation, its drawbacks include neuron inactivation when the
094   learning rate is too high. Specifically, the update rule w′ = w − η∆w, where η is the learning
095   rate and ∆w is the gradient, can result in w′ becoming negative if η∆w exceeds w. This negativity
096   causes inputs to be zeroed after passing through ReLU, and once ReLU outputs 0, its derivative is
097   also 0, leading to permanent inactivation of the neuron as the gradient no longer updates the weights.
098   Solutions such as resetting dead neurons or concatenating ReLU activations can mitigate this issue
099   Whitaker & Whitley (2023); Utku (2020); Mike (2023); Joo Hyung (2023); Sokar (2023); Abbas
100   (2023). Neurons that consistently receive negative inputs face a similar problem, as their weights and
101
      biases cannot be updated due to zero gradients. This results in neuron ”death,” where certain neurons
      remain inactive throughout training, never contributing to the learning process Ziping (2023); Lu
102
      (2020).

      3.1   C ONSISTENCY OF N EURON S TATES AND T RAJECTORIES
112
113   The behavior of trajectories can be categorized based on the consistency of the neurons along them.
114   We define dynamic and static neurons and trajectories as follows:
115
      Definition 1 (Dynamic and Static Neuron States). A neuron ni is called a dynamic neuron if its
116   activation state varies within a subspace S ⊆ Rn ; otherwise, it is a static neuron.
117
118
      Definition 2 (Dynamic and Static Activation Flows). A flow τ (x) is static if all neurons along it are
      consistent within a zone S ⊆ Rn ; otherwise, it is a dynamic flow.
119
120   Static trajectories contribute consistent outputs within a zone, while dynamic trajectories introduce
121   more nonlinearity and variability.
122
123
      3.2   DYNAMIC N EURON C OVERAGE
124
125   We explore the concept of dynamic neurons within a convex activation zone. The following lemma
126   describes how the activation status of dynamic neurons can define a cover for the zone.
127   Lemma 1 (Dynamic Neuron Coverage Lemma). For any convex activation zone C, let the set of
128   dynamic neurons be denoted as F. Then the convex activation zone C can be covered by a union of
129   smaller zones defined by the activation status of the dynamic neurons. Specifically, the activation
130   status of all static neurons remains the same throughout this zone, while the status of the dynamic
131   neurons may vary across the different sub-zones.
132
133   This lemma provides a method to approximate the coverage of a convex activation zone by focusing
134
      on the dynamic neurons, which introduces flexibility in the activation zones within the zone.

137

143   ber of neurons maintain identical activation regardless of the input, resulting in a reduced expressive
144
      capacity. (see Appendix A.5, A.6 and A.7). Due to space constraints, the detailed discussion of
      these concepts is provided in subsequent sections.

354   4.4   HENP A LGORITHM
355
356   As discussed above, we propose the neuron entropy metric to evaluate the representational capacity
357   of neural networks and show that a certain amount of dying neurons fails to contribute to network
358   predictions. Based on this observation, we introduce our Hybrid Entropy-Norm Pruning method,
359   which eliminates unnecessary parameters from the network by combining norms and neuron en-
360   tropy. Algorithm 1 in Appendix A.9 outlines the detailed HENP procedure for training and pruning.
361   At the beginning of training, a binary mask M is initialized, matching the shape of the network
362   parameters. This mask M denotes the neurons that remain active during training. During the forward
363   pass, as shown in step 5 of Algorithm 1, the model computes predictions ŷ by applying the masked
364   parameters θ ⊙ M . Subsequently, step 6 updates the parameters θ by minimizing the loss function
365   L(y, ŷ) using backpropagation. The pruning operation is triggered if the current epoch t matches one
366   of the pruning milestones P , as indicated in step 10. In this case, the Pruning function is invoked,
367
      relying on entropy to select neurons for pruning. An activation zone counter pc is initialized, where
368
      pc(i) j[k] represents the occurrence count of zone k for neuron (i, j). For each block hi , the pre-
369   activation zi is computed at step 17, followed by the activation zone â(i) j for each neuron at step
370   18. The current zone’s counter â(i) j for neuron (i, j) is incremented. After iterating over the entire
371
      dataset, the frequency distribution of each zone estimates the zone distribution for neuron (i, j). The
372
      entropy E (i) j for neuron (i, j) is calculated based on the zone distribution pc(i) j. Finally, neurons
373   meeting the pruning criteria are removed by setting the corresponding entries in the mask M (i) j to
374
      zero.
375   For clarity, we separate the training and pruning phases in Algorithm 1. In practice, however, the
376   computation of neuron entropy can be incorporated into the training phase, with the zone counter
377   updates being performed on a batch-wise basis. In Section 5, we explore the impact of different
      pruning criteria on the performance of pruning methods, taking ResNet18 as a case study.

      A.5   E XPRESSIVE P OWER OF N EURAL N ETWORKS AND N EURON E NTROPY R ATIONALE
911
912   The expressive power of neural networks largely depends on the activation behavior of the neurons.
913   Theorem 1 suggests that the difference between the outputs of a neural network for different inputs
914   can be decomposed into a static part, which is a (semi-)linear transformation, and a dynamic part,
915   which introduces non-linearity. If the neurons in a neural network remain static in their activation
916   over the support set of the input data distribution D, meaning their output does not change with
917   varying inputs, the network’s function would degrade to a linear transformation, significantly reduc-
      ing its capacity to handle complex tasks. This indicates that a network with an insufficient number

919   number of dynamic neurons increases, the representational capacity of the network also increases.

921   Motivated by the above intuition, we introduce the concept of neuron entropy to better capture the
922
      diversity of neuron activation. Neuron entropy quantifies the uncertainty in a neuron’s activation
      zones. Specifically, higher entropy indicates greater uncertainty in the neuron’s activations, leading
923
      to stronger non-linear representation capacity, and thus enhancing the overall expressive power of
924
      the network.

928   We establish a rigorous connection between connection diversity and model representational capac-
929   ity. Let (q, c) and (q ′ , c′ ) be two samples drawn from a dataset S, where c ̸= c′ . For the model
930   P to correctly classify these samples, it must distinguish between the inputs q and q ′ with high ac-
931   curacy. We introduce connection diversity as a crucial factor that impacts the model’s capability in
932   differentiating these inputs.
933   The difference in model outputs for the two samples, h(q) − h(q ′ ), can be dissected into linear
934   and nonlinear components. The linear component is dictated by the local connection structure at
935   q, which can be analyzed through a connection matrix that captures the model’s orientation. The
936   nonlinear component, on the other hand, is influenced by the number of activated connection flows
937   (ACFs) between the sample pairs {q, q ′ }. As the number of ACFs increases, so does the complexity
938   of the function difference, h(q) − h(q ′ ), implying enhanced model representation capacity.
939   As the number of activated connection flows (ACFs) increases, the complexity of the function differ-
940   ence h(q)−h(q ′ ) also increases. This enhancement in complexity suggests that the model is capable
941   of capturing more intricate nonlinear relationships between inputs, which is critical for improving
942   classification accuracy.
943
      We extend the analysis from individual sample pairs to the entire dataset S. Connection diversity
944   is formalized as a metric that assesses the expected nonlinearity introduced by the model across the
945   dataset. It reflects the model’s capacity to navigate complex decision boundaries by evaluating the
946   overall distribution of activated connection flows. The higher the connection diversity, the greater
947   the model’s ability to differentiate between diverse sample pairs.
948
      To establish a formal connection between model representational capacity and connection diversity,
949
      we introduce the following theorem:
950
      Theorem 2. Consider two models, P and P ′ , which share the same architecture but have different
951
      parameter sets. The following assumptions hold:
952
953         1. The dataset S is continuous, implying that the probability of any specific input vector oc-
954            curring is zero.
955
            2. The parameter sets of both models are sampled from the same distribution.
956
957         3. Model P has lower connection diversity than model P ′ .
958
959
      Then, the expected difference in representational capacity between the models can be formalized as:
960                             E(h(q; θ) − h(q ′ ; θ)) ≤ E(h(q; θ′ ) − h(q ′ ; θ′ ))
961   where θ and θ′ are the respective parameter sets for models P and P ′ .
962
      This theorem suggests that, under the given assumptions, model P , which exhibits lower connection
963
      diversity, is less effective at distinguishing between two sample points than model P ′ . Thus, a higher
964
      connection diversity implies a greater representational capacity.
965
966   We extend our discussion to multiclass classification tasks. In scenarios involving multiple classes,
967   models that can better differentiate between various classes generally exhibit higher complexity.
968
      This observation aligns with the importance of connection diversity, as models with greater connec-
      tion diversity are better equipped to handle the increased complexity inherent in multiclass problems.
969
970   Subsequent sections will present empirical results to validate the proposed metric of connection
971   diversity. These experiments will demonstrate its effectiveness in predicting and analyzing model
      performance across various tasks, underscoring its relevance in practical applications.
