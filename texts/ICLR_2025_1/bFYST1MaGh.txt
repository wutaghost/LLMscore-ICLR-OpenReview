009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Communication between multiple language model (LM) agents has been shown
012            to scale up the reasoning ability of LMs. While natural language has been the
013            dominant medium for inter-LM communication, it is not obvious this should be
014            the standard: not only does natural language communication incur high inference
015            costs that scale quickly with the number of both agents and messages, but also
016
               the decoding process abstracts away too much rich information that could be oth-
               erwise accessed from the internal activations. In this work, we propose a simple
017
               technique whereby LMs communicate via activations; concretely, we pause an
018
               LM B’s computation at an intermediate layer, combine its current activation with
019            another LM A’s intermediate activation via some function f , then pass f ’s output
020            into the next layer of B and continue the forward pass till decoding is complete.
021            This approach scales up LMs on new tasks with zero additional parameters and
022            data, and saves a substantial amount of compute over natural language communi-
023            cation. We test our method with various functional forms f on two experimental
024            setups—multi-player coordination games and reasoning benchmarks—and find
025            that it achieves up to 27.0% improvement over natural language communication
026            across datasets with <1/4 the compute, illustrating the superiority and robustness
027
               of activations as an alternative “language” for communication between LMs.
028
029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Language is for the purpose of communication. As large language models (LLMs) have been in-
032   creasingly used to power autonomous, goal-driven agents capable of reasoning, tool usage, and
033   adaptive decision-making (Yao et al., 2023; Xi et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Ahn et al., 2022;
034   Schick et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023; Park et al., 2023; Nakano et al., 2022), communication be-
035   tween multiple cooperating agents has emerged as an intuitive approach to amplify the reasoning
036   capabilities of LLMs (Wu et al., 2023). Explicit communication in natural language between multi-
037   ple LLMs has been shown to encourage divergent thinking (Liang et al., 2023), improve factuality
038   and reasoning (Du et al., 2023), enable integration of cross-domain knowledge (Sukhbaatar et al.,
039
      2024), and allow for modular composition of abilities in a complementary manner (Wu et al., 2023;
      Prasad et al., 2023).
040
041   A critical problem with natural language communication, however, is that it incurs extremely high
042   inference costs that scale quickly with the number of agents as well as length and number of mes-
043   sages (Du et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023). Restricting LLM communication to
044   natural language also raises the question: as LLMs are increasingly capable of handling larger, more
045
      complex tasks (sometimes with “super-human” ability) (Wei et al., 2022; Burns et al., 2023), might
      they communicate more effectively in representations of higher dimension than natural language?
046
      While using natural language as a communicative medium is appealing due to its interpretability,
047
      we claim that it may not be optimal for inter-LLM communication. Natural language generation
048   uses only one token to represent the model’s belief over the entire vocabulary, which risks losing
049   information embedded within the model output logits (Pham et al., 2024); furthermore, a model’s
050   belief over the entire vocabulary is itself not always better (for communicative purposes) than the
051   model’s (often richer) representation of the input in earlier layers. Indeed, Hernandez et al. (2024)
052   find that by around the halfway point of an LM’s computation, it has developed “enriched entity rep-
053   resentations” of the input, where entities in the prompt are populated with additional facts about that
      entity encoded in the model’s weights; but by the later layers these embeddings are transformed into


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      a representation of the next word which leverages only parts of the previous, richer representations,
055   when that full embedding would be quite useful for communication.
056
057   Motivated by these concerns, this work outlines a simple technique whereby LLM agents communi-
058
      cate via activations, thus enabling more efficient (i.e., higher-entropy) communication at a fraction
      of the number of forward passes required at inference time. Concretely, we (1) pause a Transformer
059
      LM B’s computation at intermediate layer j in the residual stream; (2) combine its post-layer j acti-
060
      vation with another LM A’s post-layer k activation via some function f ; and then (3) pass f ’s output
061   into the next layer j + 1 of B and continue its forward pass till decoding is complete. This approach
062   scales up LLMs on new tasks by leveraging existing, frozen LLMs along with zero task-specific pa-
063   rameters and data, applying to diverse domains and settings. Furthermore, in requiring only a partial
064   forward pass through A and one forward pass through B, this method saves a substantial amount of
065   compute over traditional natural language communication, which we quantify in Section 3.2.
066
      We validate our method by testing this approach with various functional forms f on two experi-
067   mental setups: two multi-player coordination games, where B is asked to complete a task requiring
068   information provided in a prompt to A; and seven reasoning benchmarks spanning multiple do-
069   mains: Biographies (Du et al., 2023), GSM8k (Cobbe et al., 2021), MMLU High School Psychol-
070   ogy, MMLU Formal Logic, MMLU College Biology, MMLU Professional Law, and MMLU Public
071   Relations (Hendrycks et al., 2021). Our activation communication protocol exhibits up to 27.0%
072   improvement over natural language communication across these datasets, using <1/4 the compute.
073   Critically, unlike prior work which test inter-LLM communication only on large-scale (>70B) mod-
074   els (Du et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2023), we find that our approach generalizes across a wide array of
075
      LLM suites and sizes, enabling even smaller LLMs to unlock the benefits of communication.
076   In summary, our contributions are two-fold:
077
078       • We propose a novel inter-model communication protocol for LLM agents that is purely
079         activation-based.
080
          • We perform comprehensive experiments to validate the improved performance of activation
081         communication over traditional natural language communication. We also formally quantify
082         our approach’s compute savings over natural language communication, illustrating the supe-
083         riority and robustness of activations as an alternative “language” for communication between
084         LMs.

087   2    R ELATED W ORK
088
089   Multi-agent communication The field of multi-agent communication has a long-standing history.
090   Notably, prior works on emergent communication have showed that agents can autonomously evolve
091   communication protocols when deployed in multi-agent environments that enable cooperative and
092   competitive game-play (Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016; Lazaridou et al., 2017). How-
093
      ever, recent experiments have demonstrated that learning meaningful languages from scratch, even
      with centralized training, remains difficult (Lowe et al., 2020; Chaabouni et al., 2019; Jaques et al.,
094
      2019).
095
096   With the emergence of large pre-trained language models, allowing communication between LLMs
097   in natural language has hence become a promising approach to enable coordination among multiple
098   LLM agents (Li et al., 2023). Recent works have demonstrated that such conversations enable inte-
099
      gration of cross-domain knowledge (Sukhbaatar et al., 2024), modular composition of abilities in a
      complementary manner (Wu et al., 2023), and improved task performance via splitting into subtasks
100
      (Prasad et al., 2023). Most notable is multiagent debate introduced by Du et al. (2023), where LLMs
101
      provide initial responses and then make refinements by iteratively considering inputs from peers.
102   While such methods have been shown to improve performance on various tasks over vanilla and
103   majority-vote (Wang et al., 2023) style prompting, these experiments have only focused on large
104   models (GPT-3.5/4, LLaMA2-70B and up), leaving the efficacy of debate on smaller, open-source
105   models underexplored; our study addresses this gap by reimplementing Du et al. (2023) in exper-
106   iments with smaller-scale (1 − 70B) models. More crucially, debate and similar natural language
107   communication methods are extremely computationally expensive, which this work addresses (Yang
      et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023).


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Notably, Pham et al. (2024) propose CIPHER, which uses input (tokenizer) embeddings (as opposed
109   to activations) to enable multi-agent communication; specifically, CIPHER passes the average tok-
110   enizer embedding (weighted by the LLM’s next-token probabilities) between models. While (Pham
111   et al., 2024) show this approach outperforms natural language debate, it (i) still faces substantial
112   information loss relative to the model activations and (ii) does not save compute, as the number of
113   these “average embeddings” passed between models is the same as the number of tokens passed
114   between models in natural language communication.
115
116   Activation engineering Activation engineering involves editing an LLM’s intermediate layer rep-
117   resentations during a forward pass to create desired changes to output text (Li et al., 2024; Turner
118   et al., 2023). Past work has explored extracting latent steering vectors from a frozen LLM to control
119   quality and content of completions (Subramani et al., 2022), as well as using “direction” vectors
120   (computed as the difference in activations between two prompts) that enable inference-time control
121
      over high-level properties of generations (Li et al., 2024; Turner et al., 2023). This work involves
      activation editing that is similar to such prior works at a high level, though for the purpose of com-
122
      munication between LLM agents.
123
124
      Model composition and grafting Composing expert models has been a recurring strategy to im-
125
      prove large models, with different methods imposing different restrictions on the types of base LLMs
126
      that can be combined. Mixture of Experts (Shazeer et al., 2017) requires that all experts are trained
127   simultaneously using the same data; Branch-Train-Mix (Sukhbaatar et al., 2024) trains a single base
128   LM multiple times on different datasets, then learns a router on outputs. Crucially, these methods do
129   not work when neither model can do the task at hand well (i.e., they solve the problem of choosing
130   which of several outputs is best, not that of generating a high-quality output by recombining the
131   disparate abilities of the various base LMs).
132
      Model grafting, in contrast, seeks to merge different models immediately prior to or at inference-
133   time. Past works have explored this at the parameter level (e.g., task vector averaging as in Ilharco
134   et al. (2023), which requires that the base models be well aligned), probability distribution / token
135   level as in Shen et al. (2024) (which imposes few restrictions on the relationship between the base
136   models, but by virtue of being token-based can result in cascading errors during decoding), and
137   activation level (e.g., CALM (Bansal et al., 2024) which learns an attention layer on top of two
138   models’ intermediate layer activations and thus enables broader integration of model abilities than
139   token-level methods, but requires re-tuning of the attention mechanism for every model pair). In
140   this work, we seek to unify CALM and other activation-level grafting techniques under a single
141
      framework, parameterized by the function f used to combine activations; crucially, we explore
      simple forms of f (e.g., sum, mean) that—unlike Bansal et al. (2024)—require zero additional task-
142
      specific parameters and data, and are far more compute-efficient.

247   3.3   W HY SHOULD THIS WORK ?
248
249   Recall that Pham et al. (2024) propose CIPHER—communicating the average tokenizer embedding
250
      (weighted by the LLM’s next-token probabilities) between models. We build upon the intuition be-
      hind CIPHER, which goes as follows: the token sampling process during decoding risks substantial
251
      information loss from the model’s output logits, and communicating a model’s weighted-average
252
      tokenizer embedding essentially entails communicating both that model’s final answer and its belief
253   in that answer (over the entire vocabulary).
254
255   Communicating activations, then, can be thought of as communicating a strict superset of {next-
256
      token prediction, belief over entire vocabulary}, as activations of late-enough layers essentially en-
      code the model’s entire knowledge about the provided context as well as its predicted completion
257
      and confidence in that completion (see Figures 1 and 7 in Hewitt & Manning (2019) and Hernandez
258
      et al. (2024), respectively, which show that linear probes tasked with predicting certain output char-
259   acteristics from a Transformer’s intermediate layer embeddings of its input work poorly for early
260   layers, extremely well after around the halfway point of computation, but then probe accuracy drops
261   closer to the final layers).2 Indeed, these curves of probe accuracy by layer indicate that the final lay-
262   ers and LM head “throw away” information not useful for next-token prediction that very well could
263   be useful for communicative purposes; this is precisely why our proposed activation communication
264   technique is not an iterative approach (there is no notion of “rounds” like in debate and CIPHER,
265   which require an additional token budget to extract more and more information out of the LM), as
266   one activation grafting step from A to B inherently communicates to B all of A’s knowledge/beliefs
267       2
            Note one important critique of multiagent debate: that in cases where multiple agents are uncertain about
268   the answer, there is no reason why referencing other agents’ answers would generate more factual reasoning.
269   Both CIPHER and activation communication solve this problem, as some notion of model confidence is being
      communicated along with its next-token prediction.

413   we measure the accuracy of the majority-held final-round answer across agents when the answer is

415   final-round answer across agents otherwise (Biographies).
416   For GSM8k and the MMLU datasets, we report the proportion of samples in the dataset for which

419   fact in a ground-truth biography is supported (1), partially supported (0.5), or unsupported (0) in the

      5   C ONCLUSION
493
494
      We present a simple approach to enable effective and computationally efficient communication be-
495
      tween language models by injecting information from the activations of one model into the acti-
496   vations of another during the forward pass. Salient features of this approach include: (i) Scales up
497   LLMs on new tasks by leveraging existing, frozen LLMs along with zero additional task-specific pa-
498   rameters and data, (ii) Applies to diverse domains and settings, and (iii) Saves a substantial amount
499   of compute.
500
      There are some limitations to this method. First, when not using the learned model-specific mapping
501
      discussed in Section 3.1, our method requires both models to have aligned embedding spaces, such
502   that the activation of one model roughly retains its meaning in the other’s activation space (note
503   that unlike past works such as Pham et al. (2024) we do not require shared tokenizers or aligned
504   vocabularies, only aligned embeddings). While less restrictive than past works (Pham et al., 2024),
505   this assumption is somewhat limiting, but can be relaxed when we let f be the learned model-specific
506   mapping; and in practice we find that even amongst different models in the LLaMA family, no such
507   mapping is required for state-of-the-art results.
508   Second, this method requires access to embeddings and will not work with black-box API access;
509   however exploring API-only approaches is highly limiting, and recent releases of powerful open-
510   source models (Dubey et al., 2024) merit the development of embedding-based techniques.
511
      Third, while a concern might be the limited interpretability of communicating activations as opposed
512
      to natural language, we note the following. First, there is a fundamental tradeoff between inter-
513
      pretability and information preservation (as activations, by virtue of being much higher-dimensional
514   than the space of natural language, allow proportionally higher-entropy communication) (Pham
515   et al., 2024), which merits discussion beyond the scope of this work. But second, we actually
516   posit that our method suggests a new avenue towards interpreting LM activations: “translating” ac-
517   tivations based on the beliefs they induce as messages in listening agents, similar to the method put
518   forward in Andreas et al. (2018). We recognize this as a promising avenue for future research.
519
      Additional directions of future work include using AC to allow large LMs to leverage small, tunable
520   LMs as “knowledge bases” during decoding (Lee et al., 2024), as in collaborative decoding (Shen
521   et al., 2024) setups; and testing our approach on more complex coordination games (e.g., Lewis-style
522   negotiation games (Lewis et al., 2017), Diplomacy).

1209   B.1   M ODIFYING ACTIVATIONS OF A LL T OKENS
1210
1211   Recall that AC grafts the last-token layer-k activation of A into B’s last-token layer-j activation.
1212   But is modifying just the last token activation enough to communicate information from A to B?
1213   Note that after applying masked attention in each of the previous Transformer layers, the last to-
1214   ken activation of A attends to all tokens before it, hence incorporating information from the entire
1215   sequence. Indeed, this must be the case for activation communication to recover the gap between
1216   the zero-communication and skyline setups on both coordination games, which (for Tip Sheets in
1217   particular) require information starting at the first few tokens of A’s prompt to be communicated.
1218   To verify this empirically, we experiment with summing the activations of all tokens in the sequence
1219   rather than just the last (we cannot replace all tokens as this would just replace B’s layer-j activation
1220   with A’s layer k-activation). Results are shown in Table 4.
1221
       Indeed, applying f to all tokens decreases performance relative to applying f to just the last token.
1222
       Note that the fact performance generally decreases from f = replace to f = sum, and further
1223
       with all tokens, is expected. The high performance of AC with f = replace means that the edited
1224   last-token activation b retains some meaning in B’s activation space; it is less likely for this to be
1225   the case when f = sum (at the very least b has norm roughly 2× that of B’s original last-token
1226   activation), and when doing this for all tokens we’d expect performance to decrease even further
1227   as now all activation vectors, not just the last, are out-of-distribution with respect to B’s activation
1228   space.

1236
       (where B gets all the information encoding A’s “beliefs” about the prompt via AC, hence CoT on
1237
       A’s side is not needed). To verify this, we experiment with prompting A with CoT, generating a full
1238   response, and then passing the layer-k last-token activation of the CoT response to B as part of AC.
1239   Results are shown in Table 5.

       Indeed, we empirically find our above intuition (in orange) to hold, as there is no significant im-
       provement over vanilla AC when generating from A using CoT.

1259   Recall our reasoning about the AC (W ) results from Section 4.2: “We hypothesize that the benefits
1260   from the learned linear layer are less consistent across datasets because the subset of C4 data used
1261   to train W likely contains text more semantically similar to some datasets than others, hence some

1263
       AC.”
1264   Indeed, we verify this hypothesis by training W on the GSM8k train set (to produce Win dist ) and
1265   then evaluating with this task-specific linear layer on the GSM8k test set. Results are shown in
1266   Table 6.
1267   Indeed, learning W in-distribution significantly boosts performance, confirming our hypothesis.
1268   Unfortunately we cannot run this experiment for the other datasets, as there is no in-distribution
1269   training data available for MMLU (we use all public data for testing).
1270
       Hence, this suggests that AC (W ) should unilaterally improve over vanilla AC if we choose a
1271
       training set with good coverage across many tasks and distributions, such that there are sentences
1272
       semantically similar to prompts across the span of downstream task datasets.

       B.4   C OMMUNICATING ACTIVATIONS B ETWEEN I DENTICAL M ODELS
1275
1276   Note that AC as described in Section 3.1 only supports communication between distinct models.
1277   We can extend AC to work for communication between identical models as follows: let A and B
1278   be instances of the same model. We can sample a completion from A with temperature and graft
1279   the last-token layer-k activation of the completion into B at layer j as part of the AC procedure.
1280   This still saves a substantial amount of compute over NLD between 2 model instances, showing our
1281   technique can apply to this setting. Table 7 shows the results of this experiment.
1282   Indeed, while communication between multiple model instances doesn’t always show improvement
1283   over the single model itself (a well-known result from (Du et al., 2023)), AC matches/outperforms
1284   NLD on five of the seven datasets.
1285
       The intuition behind debate between multiple identical model instances is that sampling multiple
1286
       completions (with temperature) from the same model yields diverse reasoning paths that can be
1287   recombined into a stronger final answer. The above experiment shows that the same intuition holds
1288   for AC—we are sampling multiple times from the same model, but passing responses between agents
1289   via AC rather than as NL messages.
