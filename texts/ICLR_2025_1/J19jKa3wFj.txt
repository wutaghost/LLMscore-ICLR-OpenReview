009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose a hierarchical protein backbone generative model that separates coarse
012            and fine-grained details. Our approach called LSD consists of two stages: sampling
013            latents which are decoded into a contact map then sampling atomic coordinates
014            conditioned on the contact map. LSD allows new ways to control protein generation
015            towards desirable properties while scaling to large datasets. In particular, the
016
               AlphaFold DataBase (AFDB) is appealing due as its diverse structure topologies but
               suffers from poor designability. We train LSD on AFDB and show latent diffusion
017
               guidance towards AlphaFold2 Predicted Alignment Error and long range contacts
018
               can explicitly balance designability, diversity, and noveltys in the generated samples.
019            Our results are competitive with structure diffusion models and outperforms prior
020            latent diffusion models.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   A challenge across diffusion models for protein backbone generation has been scaling to large
026   datasets: ideally benefiting from improved diversity and generalization, but this empirically results
027
      in unwanted biases from low quality protein structures (Huguet et al., 2024). In this work, we aim
      to develop a diffusion model that scales to the AlphaFold DataBase (AFDB) (Varadi et al., 2022)
028
      with the ability to control for desired properties. Previous approaches use structure-based diffusion
029
      models (SDMs) over atomic coordinates, but this presents challenges in respecting equivariance and
030   physical constraints such as bond lengths and angles. Unfortunately, this can hinder optimization and
031   generalization of deep learning models – recent works in structure prediction (Abramson et al., 2024),
032   conformer generation (Wang et al.), and material design (Yang et al., 2023) have found improved
033   results by removing equivariance and physical constraints.
034
      We hypothesize SDMs can be improved by conditioning the generation process on sampled contact
035
      maps, defined as a 2D binary matrix representing whether each pair of residues are within a short
036   distance of each other. A contact map is sufficient to describe a protein’s fold topology while the
037   coordinates can capture biomolecular conformations and elucidate a protein’s function. Prior works
038   have shown generative models fail to learn meaningful representations and can benefit from alignment
039   (Yu et al., 2024) or high-level conditioning (Li et al., 2024). We follow the latter intuition to propose
040   using latent diffusion models (LDMs) to generate contact maps followed by SDMs to generate atomic
041   coordinates conditioned on the contact map (Fig. 1). Together, our method called LSD (Latent and
042   Structure Diffusion) allows for training over large datasets then guiding for desired properties.
043   Our main technical contribution is developing LDMs for contact map generation. We encode protein
044   structures into a L × K dimensional latent space where L is the protein length and K is the latent
045   dimensionality. The decoder then reconstructs the contact map given the latents. Next, we train a
046   modified Diffusion Transformer (DiT) (Peebles & Xie, 2023a) to sample from learned latent space
047   while FrameFlow (Yim et al., 2024a) is trained to generate atomic coordinates conditioned on contact
048   maps and latents. Lastly, we demonstrate high-level control over the generated structures by guiding
049   latent generation towards lower mean Predicted Alignment Error (PAE) and increased Long Range
050
      Contacts (LRC) for improved designability and diversity.
051   We evaluate LSD on protein backbone generation by training on the AlphaFold Database (AFDB)
052   (Varadi et al., 2022) which we show is challenging to learn with only FrameFlow. Our results show
053   LSD improves generalization to more diverse fold topologies which suggests combining LDMs and
      SDMs can be beneficial for scaling to large datasets like AFDB. We achieve improved results when

      3     M ETHOD : L ATENT AND S TRUCTURE D IFFUSION (LSD)

141
      In this section, we present our method for hierarchical protein backbone generation. The method
142
      consists of three components: a structure-to-contact autoencoder (Sec. 3.1), LDM to sample latents
143
      from the autoencoder latent space and a SDM to samples structures from the sampled latents (Sec. 3.2).
144   We discuss training and sampling in Sec. 3.3. Lastly, we discuss PAE and LRC guidance in Sec. 3.4.

      3.1    S TRUCTURE - TO - CONTACT AUTOENCODER
147
148   We denote a protein backbone’s atomic coordinates as x ∈ RL×3×3 where L is the length of the
149   protein (number of residues), 3 corresponds to the Nitrogen, Carbonα , and Carbon atom in each
150   residue. For our encoder, we use the ProteinMPNN (Dauparas et al., 2022) architecture to embed
151   x into a latent pϕ (x) = z ∈ RL×K where K is the latent dimensionality with K ≪ L. We modify
152   ProteinMPNN to output the mean and variance Gaussian parameters of each latent.
153   We aim to learn a coarse representation of x in the latent space. Our approach is to train the decoder
154   to predict the contact map c ∈ {0, 1}L×L where cij = 1 if the distance between the Carbonα atoms
155   of xi , xj is less than 8Å and cij = 0 otherwise.2 . The decoder pψ takes the Kronecker product of the
156   latents and predicts contact map probabilities: pψ (zi ⊗ zj ) for all i, j. We parameterize pψ with a 3
157   layer multi-layer perceptron with ReLU activations. The Kronecker product zi ⊗ zj ∈ RK×K is the
158   matrix of all possible products between the entries of zi and zj . The encoder and decoder are trained

172   3.2   L ATENT AND S TRUCTURE D IFFUSION M ODELS
173
174   The LDM requires learning the latent score function sθ (z(t) , t) where z(t) is a noisy version of the
175   encoded latents z(0) = pϕ (x) as defined in eq. (2). For neural network architecture, we use the
176   Diffusion Transformer (DiT) (Peebles & Xie, 2023b) since it is successfully used across computer
                                                                             (t)
177   vision. We adapt DiT for our purposes by treating each residue latent zi as a token. We use Rotary
178   Positional Encodings (RoPE) (Su et al., 2024) instead of absolute positional encodings as done in
179   Hayes et al. (2024).
180
      The SDM is a modified version of FrameFlow (Yim et al., 2024a) that is trained to predict the
181
      denoised atomic coordinates x̂φ (x(t) , ĉψ (z(0) ), z(0) , t) where φ are the FrameFlow neural network
182
      weights. We condition the SDM by concatenating z(0) and ĉψ (z(0) ) to the initial set 1D and 2D
183
      features provided to FrameFlow. FrameFlow uses flow matching over SE(3) (Chen & Lipman, 2024)
184   which is equivalent to the probabilistic Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) perspective of diffusion
185   models. We follow the training and sampling procedure of FrameFlow with the addition of our latent
186   conditioning, no self-conditioning, and no rotation annealing. We choose FrameFlow over other
187   SDMs due to the ease of its open sourced code but any other SDM can be used in our framework.
188   Due to the architecture choice, our LDM is SE(3) invariant while the SDM is SE(3) equivariant.

190   3.3   M ULTI - STAGE T RAINING AND S AMPLING
191
192   The VAE training loss is described in Sec. 3.1 while the LDM and SDM losses are described in
193
      Sec. 3.2. While end-to-end training of all models is possible, we found this to be unstable and
      difficult to optimize. We instead use a training procedure inspired by Rombach et al. (2022) where
194
      the autoencoder is frozen during latent diffusion training. It involves three stages: (1) pre-training the
195
      VAE by itself, (2) jointly training the VAE and SDM, and (3) freezing the VAE weights and training
196   only the LDM. In our experiments, we use the same optimizer and learning rate across all stages.
197   App. B.1 provides more details on the training setup.
198
199   To sample, we first generate latents z(0) from the LDM using eq. (4) and obtain the contact map
200   ĉψ (z(0) ). Both z(0) and ĉψ (z(0) ) are then provided to the SDM to sample atomic coordinates condi-
201   tioned on ĉψ (z(0) ) using the SE(3) flow, see Yim et al. (2024a). Fig. 1 illustrates the sampling process.
202   In the next section, we describe sampling with guidance towards desired high-level properties.

      4    R ELATED W ORK
253
      Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs). Latent Score-based Generative Model (LSGM) (Vahdat et al.,
254
      2021) first proposed using a combination of a VAE and diffusion model over the latent space. Stable
255
      diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) extended LSGM with architectural and training improvements that
256   achieved state-of-the-art (SOTA) results in image synthesis. DiT (Peebles & Xie, 2023a) and SiT
257   (Ma et al., 2024) further improved the scalability with a Transformer (Vaswani, 2017) architecture
258   tailored for diffusion models.
259
      The success of LDMs has motivated their use in protein applications. OmniProt (McPartlon et al.,
260
      2024) is a LDM for protein-protein docking but with no open source code. LatentDiff (Fu et al., 2023)
261
      is the only other LDM for protein structure generation to the best of our knowledge. We also consider
262   methods FoldToken (Gao et al., 2024) and ESM3 (Hayes et al., 2024) that learn discrete latent tokens
263   and use autoregressive masked language models to be discrete LDMs for protein structure generation.
264   Likewise, DiffTopo (Correia, 2024) and TopoDiff (Zhang et al., 2023) do not exactly fit the LDM
265   framework but are related by using diffusion to sample a coarse protein fold topology followed by a
266   diffusion model to produce a structure conditioned on the topology. DiffTopo and TopoDiff do not
267   have open source code to compare with. In Sec. 5, we use LatentDiff and ESM3 as baselines.

      5.1   S ET- UP
305
306   Training Details. We train LSD on the Foldseek (Van Kempen et al., 2024) clustered AlphaFold
307   DataBase (AFDB) (Varadi et al., 2022) as done in GENIE2 (Lin et al., 2024). We filter out examples
308   that are longer than 128 residues and minimum pLDDT (AlphaFold2 predicted confidence metric)
309   lower than 80. The latter is a commonly used filter to remove low quality protein structures from
310   AFDB but is not suffucient (Varadi et al., 2022). This results in 282936 training examples. Dimensions
311   and training details of all our neural networks are provided in App. B.1.
312
313   Evaluation Details. For each method, we sample 10 proteins of each length between 60-128.
314   Standard metrics for protein backbone generation are designability (Des), diversity (Div), and
315
      novelty (Nov) as described in Yim et al. (2023). Novelty was computed against the AFDB database.
      Designable Pairwise TM-score (DPT) is defined as the average pairwise TM-score (Zhang & Skolnick,
316
      2004) between designable proteins. We include H/S/C as the average helix, strand and coil secondary
317
      structure composition (Kabsch & Sander, 1983) of the designable samples, i.e. 60/10/20 means each
318   structure is made up of 60% helix, 10% strands, and 20% coil on average. Designability is not a
319   accurate indicator of how well a generative model matches the training distribution since the training
320   dataset is far from 100% designable (Huguet et al., 2024). Instead, we measure how well a protein
321   structure generative model captures the training distribution by computing Secondary Structure
322   Distance (SSD), defined as the Wasserstein distance between the discretized secondary structure
323   distribution of the training dataset and the generated proteins with no designability filtering. Alg. 1
      describes how we compute SSD. See App. B.3 for more explanation of our metrics.

      6   D ISCUSSION
520
521
      The goal of LSD is to develop a new framework for protein structure generation capable of separating
522   high- and low-level details during the generation process. We combined latent and structure diffusion
523   to break up the generative procedure into first sampling latents, contact maps, and finally the atomic
524   coordinates. We showed how including intermediate contact maps helps learn large datasets such
525   as AFDB and how guidance techniques can improve the quality of the generated structures. We
526   compared LSD to existing protein structure generation methods and showed that it is competitive
527   with state-of-the-art SDMs and outperforms prior LDMs for protein backbone generation.
528   Our limitations include not achieving state-of-the-art performance on all metrics with a single setting
529   compared to SDMs and limiting LSD to unconditional backbone generation. We propose several
530   directions to address these. First, performance can likely be improved with further investigation into
531   the neural network architectures, i.e. triangle update layers that are widely utilized in Huguet et al.
532   (2024); Lin et al. (2024) or swapping out FrameFlow with GENIE2 as the SDM in our framework.
533   Second, we can optimize the LDM with the latest techniques report in the LDM for computer vision
534   literature such as Autoguidance (Karras et al., 2024) and Stable Diffusion 3 (Esser et al., 2024).
535   Lastly, we plan to extend our method to all-atomic biomolecular generation (Abramson et al., 2024)
536
      and design tasks – binder design and motif-scaffolding (Krishna et al., 2024). Extending to protein
      complexes involving multiple chains will require scaling up the model size to handle larger proteins.
537
      Our latent space only encode protein backbone coordinates but a natural extension is to include
538
      side-chain coordinates and protein sequence information. Having a malleable latent space opens up
539   new possibilities for protein generative modeling and design.

      Here we provide a formal derivation of eq. (4) based on linear SDEs. Using SDEs for generative
759   modeling can be traced back to Song et al. (2021); Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015); Ho et al. (2020).
760   Our derivation is not novel and follows the same steps as Song et al. (2021); Zheng et al. (2023). It
761   comprises of two main objects: a forward SDE to corrupt data and a reverse SDE to generate data
762   from noise. The most common SDE for generative modeling is of the Itô form and with linear drift
763   and diffusion coefficients. The forward SDE is defined as
764

765
766   where a(t) : [0, 1] → R and b(t) : [0, 1] → R are the drift and diffusion coefficients, respectively,
767   and w(t) is a Wiener process. The seminal result of Anderson (1982) showed that eq. (7) can be
768   reversed in time analytically with the following reverse SDE

770                       dz(t) = a(t)z(t) − b(t)2 ∇z(t) log p0 (z(0) ) dt + b(t)dw(t)                 (8)

774   dependent noising process in eq. (2). With this, we can plug a(t) and b(t) into the reverse SDE in
775   eq. (8) to generate samples from the latent space. The time derivative of the mean and covariance of
776   eq. (7) at each time t is a result found in Section 5.5 of Särkkä & Solin (2019),

      B.3      M ETRIC DETAILS
928
      We describe each metric used in Sec. 5 for completeness. Designability, diversity, and novelty are
929   standard metrics used in multiple prior related works (Yim et al., 2023; Watson et al., 2022; Lin et al.,
930   2024; Bose et al., 2024; Huguet et al., 2024). Designable Pairwise TM-score and secondary structure
931   composition are an additional metric reported in recent works as well (Lin et al., 2024; Bose et al.,
932   2024; Huguet et al., 2024). We introduce Secondary Structure Distance to supplement the above
933   metrics. Below we describe each metric.
934
935           1. Designability (Des): Let x be a protein backbone structure sampled from a protein structure
936              generative model. We use the open-sourced ProteinMPNN code4 to generate 8 sequences
937              for each backbone. ESMFold (Lin et al., 2023) then predicts the structure of each sequence.
938
                 We compute the atomic Root Mean Squared Deviation (RMSD) of each predicted structure
                 against the sampled structure x. If RMSD < 2.0 then we consider x to be designable in
939
                 the sense that a sequence can be found which would fold into the x structure. Clearly this
940
                 evaluation is purely in-silico and only serves as a approximation of whether a structure is
941              designable. Despite that, designability has been found to correlate with wet-lab success
942              especially when more specialized sequence and structure prediction models are used (Watson
943              et al., 2022; Zambaldi et al., 2024). We report designability as the percentage of samples
944              that are designable. It is currently debated whether a protein generative model should aim
945              to have as high designability as possible since designability is influenced by the inductive
946              biases of the structure prediction model. Many natural occurring proteins are known to
947              not pass the designability crition (Huguet et al., 2024; Campbell et al., 2024) yet are real
948              proteins. We present our experiments with designability as a metric we wish to increase in
949
                 order to follow prior works.
950           2. Diversity (Div): A generative model can achieve 100% designability by repeatedly sampling
951              the same designable structure. To detect this exploitation, we report diversity as the number
952              of clusters after running a clustering algorithm over the designable samples. Following prior
953              works starting with (Trippe et al., 2022), we MaxCluster (Herbert & Sternberg, 2008) to run
954
                 hierarchical clustering over all designable structures with average linkage, 0.5 TM-score
                 cutoff, and no sequence filtering. The goal is to maximize the number of clusters in the
955
                 samples.
956
957           3. Designable Pairwise TM-score (DPT): Reporting the number of clusters can be biased
958              since there are many hyperparameters and algorithms for clustering. To present a unbiased
959
                 view of diverisy, we report the average TM-score of all the pairwise TM-scores between
                 designable samples. This is part of an auxiliary output after running MaxCluster.
960
961           4. Novelty: We measure how a method extrapolates beyond the training set by computing
962              the average of the maximum TM-scores of each designable structure x when compared
963              to AFDB. We use FoldSeek with the following command: foldseek easy-search
964
                 <path-to-designable-pdb-files> <path-to-afdb-database>
                 alignments.m8 tmp --alignment-type 1 --format-output
965
                 query,target,alntmscore,lddt --tmscore-threshold 0.0
966
                --exhaustive-search --max-seqs 10000000000 --comp-bias-corr
967              0 --mask 0. Foldseek commands are chosen to ignore sequence filtering and turn off
968              pre-filtering steps before running a efficent structural search algorithm against all structures
969              in the AFDB. The goal is to minimize the novelty metric as this corresponds to more
970              extrapolation beyond the training set while adhering to the designability criterion. We note
971
         4
             https://github.com/dauparas/ProteinMPNN

                this is just one definition of novelty and other definitions as possible but we aim to follow
973             precedent set by prior works.
974
             5. (Designable) Helix/Strand/Coil percentage (H/S/C): Out of the designable samples, we
975
                use DSSP (Kabsch & Sander, 1983) to compute the average alpha-helix, beta-strand, and

       Following Appendix A.3.6 of ESM3, which outlines the procedure for unconditional genera-
1261
       tion, we employed the open-source 1.4B parameter model available at https://github.com/
1262
       evolutionaryscale/esm.
1263
1264   To generate a protein sequence of length l, we input a sequence of mask tokens of the same length
1265   into the model. We set the temperature to 0.5 and configured the number of decoding steps to equal
1266
       the protein length l. Subsequently, we conditioned the 1.4B model to generate structure tokens using
       the same number of decoding steps (l) with argmax decoding, where the temperature was set to 0.
1267
1268   We conducted ablation studies to determine the optimal number of decoding steps by testing values
1269   of l/2, 2l/3, and l with a temperature of 0.7. Our experiments revealed that setting the number of
1270   decoding steps to l yielded higher diversity than 2l/3 with slightly lower designability . Additionally,
1271   we performed ablation on the temperature parameter by evaluating temperatures of 0.3, 0.5, and 0.7
1272
       for the optimal number of decoding steps l. We found that a temperature of 0.5 provided the best
       results.
