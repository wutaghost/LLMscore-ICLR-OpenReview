009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            To extend the context length of Transformer-based large language models (LLMs)
012            and improve comprehension capabilities, researchers often encounter constraints
013            stemming from finite computational resources and bounded memory capacities.
014            This work proposes a novel approach, termed Recurrent Context Compression
015            (RCC), designed to efficiently expand the context window length of LLMs. Fur-
016
               thermore, we delve into the prevalent issue of degraded model performance when
               both instructional prompts and contextual information undergo compression for
017
               downstream tasks. To address this challenge, we propose a novel instruction
018
               reconstruction methodology aimed at mitigating the detrimental effects of this
019            compression process. The effectiveness of our proposed approach was validated
020            across multiple tasks while achieving an impressive context compression rate of
021            at least 32×. On text reconstruction task, we maintain a BLEU-4 score close to
022            0.95. On passkey retrieval task, we achieve nearly 100% accuracy involving an
023            extensive sequence length of 1 million tokens. On long-text question-answering
024            task, we obtain comparable performance with the non-compressed LLM in F1
025            and Rouge scores. Our method also demonstrated competitive performance in
026            long-text question-answering tasks compared to non-compressed methods, while
027
               significantly saving storage resources.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   With the rapid advancement of natural language processing technologies, Transformer-based large
032   language models (LLMs) have become a key driving force in this field. However, when grappling
033   with lengthy text inputs that extend beyond a certain scope, LLMs frequently encounter limitations
034   imposed by their finite context window length capabilities. These limitations stem from several in-
035   herent factors in the model architecture and training methods. Firstly, during the inference phase,
036   models are constrained by the pretraining text length, leading to a significant decline in quality when
037   the generated sequence exceeds the pretrained context window. Secondly, the design of the Trans-
038   former architecture requires storing information from the entire input sequence, which leads to a
039
      significant memory usage from the KV-Cache during inference. To address these issues, related
      works have extended the context window of LLMs (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Child et al.,
040
      2019; Wu et al., 2022; Rae et al., 2019; Bulatov et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Mohtashami & Jaggi,
041
      2023; Beltagy et al., 2020) by optimizing training methods, model structures, and context compres-
042   sion. Among these, context compression techniques (Rae et al., 2019; Snell et al., 2022; Chevalier
043   et al., 2023; Wingate et al., 2022; Mu et al., 2023; Ge et al., 2023; Munkhdalai et al., 2024; Ren
044   et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023) are popular promising because they reduce the length of contexts or
045   prompts while maintaining performance. This allows for the inference of longer context windows
046   within limited resources. Figure 1 compares the memory resource consumption of our method with
047   non-compression methods. Additionally, most compression-based methods can be integrated with
048   other context window extension techniques to further enhance performance.
049   However, existing context compression methods face with three major challenges in long-text lan-
050   guage modeling. Firstly, The current SOTA model, ICAE (Ge et al., 2023), can only achieve up
051   to 8× compression; beyond that, performance significantly declines. Secondly, most compression
052   methods still conduct experiments within a 4k context range, which is unable to keep pace with the
053   development of current large language models (LLMs). For example, GPT-4 has reached a context
      length of 128k. Therefore, it is crucial to continue extending the context length further to adapt to

      To address the aforementioned issues, this paper makes the following contributions:
099
100   Firstly, we propose a context compression model, called Recurrent Context Compression (RCC)
101   architecture. RCC significantly reduces information loss during the compression process, greatly
102   enhancing compression efficiency. In experiments, we achieved nearly 0.95 BLEU-4 scores with a
103   32× compression rate on text reconstruction tasks, as shown in Figure 4.
104   Secondly, we propose a recurrent compression mechanism to extend the model’s ability to compress
105   texts beyond the encoder’s window length. When training long-text context compression language
106   models, the length of the context sequence input to the encoder is proportional to the required com-
107   putational resources, limiting the extension of context length during training. Therefore, we use
      a simple yet effective solution: initially, conducting full-parameter training on shorter sequences.

110   inference stage, with the capability to further extend lengths up to 1M. For more details, please refer

      Lastly, in downstream text generation tasks, we found that when both context and instruction are

128   Context Compression: Early approaches to context compression aimed to derive sentence repre-
129   sentation vectors for tasks such as document retrieval. Transformer-based autoencoder architectures
130   like TSDAE (Wang et al., 2021) and Nugget (Qin & Van Durme, 2023) are relevant to our work. In
131   TSDAE, noise such as word deletion or swapping is added to input sentences to train sentence em-
132   bedding vectors. The encoder compresses the corrupted sentence into a fixed-size vector, which the
133   decoder then reconstructs into the original input text. However, such approaches cannot be directly
134   applied to text generation tasks. Git(Mu et al., 2023) compresses prompts by fine-tuning LLM.
135
      During training, a specific masking matrix compresses prompts into a few Gist tokens, which can
      still prompt the language model for responses. Similar prompt compression work was proposed by
136
      (Wingate et al., 2022). However, these tasks only compress prompts.
137
138   Several works (Snell et al., 2022; Chevalier et al., 2023; Ge et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2023; Li et al.,
139   2023; Jiang et al., 2023b; Munkhdalai et al., 2024) have focused on context compression. ICAE(Ge
140   et al., 2023) is similar to our work but suffers from lower compression efficiency and lacks exten-
141
      sive research on longer sequences. Additionally, AutoCompressors(Chevalier et al., 2023), which
      recursively compress long texts into summary vectors to extend context length, are also relevant to
142
      our approach. Selective context, proposed by (Li et al., 2023), identifies and prunes redundancy
143
      in the input context to enhance LLM inference efficiency, making inputs more compact. Recently,
144   (Munkhdalai et al., 2024) proposed a similar work combining two attention mechanisms with con-
145   text compression functionality, showing promising results. However, this new attention mechanism
146   cannot be directly applied to pre-trained open-source LLMs and faces attention optimization chal-
147   lenges in practical applications. None of the studies have thoroughly investigated the problem of
148   instructional confusion that arises when both instructions and contextual information are subjected
149   to compression. Our work introduces a new solution to mitigate this issue.
150   Long Context LLM: LLMs typically fix the context window length during training, such as the
151   Pythia (Biderman et al., 2023), LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a;b), and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023a)
152   series. Consequently, researchers have explored various methods to extend the context window
153   length of pre-trained language models. These methods(Chen et al., 2024; Tworkowski et al., 2023;
154   Chen et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; 2024; Yen et al., 2024), which have achieved notable results based
155   on existing pre-trained models. Our approach can be combined with these methods, applying them
156   to either the encoder or the decoder to achieve more extended compression effects.
157   Additionally, our work is inspired by language models with recurrent structures (Hochreiter &
158   Schmidhuber, 1997; Gu & Dao, 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023). These models com-
159   press historical context within a certain range into the hidden state of a single time step, enabling the
160   current token to access information from the previous step for inference. They demonstrate strong
161   competitiveness with Transformer models, indicating that compressing token information over a
      certain length can achieve lossless inference.

      3.1      RCC M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
165
166   As shown in Figure 3, the RCC model architecture is similar to ICAE(Ge et al., 2023), consisting
167   of an encoder and a decoder. In connecting the encoder and decoder, RCC differs from ICAE and
168   AutoCompressors(Chevalier et al., 2023). While ICAE and AutoCompressors uses the final layer’s
169   vectors from the encoder as input for the decoder, we utilize the output information from each layer
170   of the encoder. This information is then using MLP layers and inputed into the decoder. This method
171
      obtains richer feature information. The encoder can be a Transformer-based LLM or an RNN-based
      LLM, while the decoder is a Transformer-based LLM. The encoder is responsible for compressing
172
      the information which is utilized by the decoder for inference. The decoder can fully learn the
173
      compressed information vector at any position using the attention mechanism. After training, the
174   maximum context length of RCC is the decoder context window length multiplied by the encoder
175   compression rate, e.g., 1M = 2k × 512.

200   3.1.1        RCC E NCODER
201
      The design of RCC’s encoder is inspired by the Mamba-based LLM (Gu & Dao, 2023). Mamba
202
      is essentially a state space model, similar to an RNN. In Mamba, the current token only needs to
203
      access the state vector from the previous timestep to complete the current inference step. However,
204   as the context length increases, the performance of Mamba deteriorates. This indicates that the state
205   vector at each time step in Mamba can store only a limited length of historical context information.
206   Therefore, we propose a compromise: for long sequences, we can divide them into fixed-length short
207   sequences and iteratively compress each short sequence into a state vector. Finally, we concatenate
208   the state vectors of each short sequence as the historical state information to be input into the decoder.
209   This approach retains complete historical information while leveraging the model’s compression
210   capabilities to save memory. This divide and iteratively compress strategy can also be combined
211   with transformers. Our experiments show that Transformers also have this capability because a
212
      Transformer can be viewed as a special state space model or RNN (Feng et al., 2024).
213   The primary task of the RCC encoder is to compress long sequences. The initialized encoder is a
214   pretrained language model, which can be based on either Mamba or Transformer architectures. By
215   setting a compression rate, we divide long sequences into fixed-length short sequences and itera-
      tively feed these short sequences into the encoder. As illustrated in Figure 3, we find the last token


                                                                                     4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      of each short sequence, and use these tokens’ output vectors from each layer as compression vectors.
217   We concatenate these compression vectors, pass them through a linear layer, and finally input them
218   into the corresponding layer of the decoder. Through this method, the RCC’s encoder accomplishes
219   the compression modeling of the entire long context.

      3.1.2    RCC D ECODER
222
223   The decoder of the RCC is a Transformer-based language model responsible for the final text in-
224   ference. Its inputs include the compressed vectors from the encoder and token embedding vectors
225   related to the prompts. Each layer’s compressed vector from the encoder passes through a linear
226   layer before being input into the decoder. For the first layer’s mapped vector, we concatenate it with
227   the decoder’s token embedding vector and then feed it into the first layer of the decoder. Subsequent,
228
      each encoder layer’s output is connected to the corresponding decoder layer’s compressed vector.
      This connection is established through residual connections, as shown in Figure 3. It is crucial to
229
      note that only the output vectors corresponding to the compressed information will have residual
230
      connections, while the other output parts remain unchanged. If the number of compressed vector
231   layers does not match the number of decoder layers, we apply simple rule-based mappings, either
232   by duplicating to increase the number of layers or averaging to reduce the number of layers.

      3.2     M ODEL T RAINING
235

      3.2.1    T WO -S TAGE A PPROACH
237
238   We use an iterative segmented computation method to process long sequences. During the model
239   inference phase, we only need to store the compressed vectors, so memory limitations do not become
240   a bottleneck for inferring long texts. However, during the training phase, we also need to store the
241   gradient information of the entire long sequence, which significantly exceeds the memory limit. We
242   mitigate this issue using a simple yet effective two-stage training method. In the first stage, we
243
      perform full-parameter fine-tuning with a large number of shorter sequences, allowing the encoder
      to sufficiently learn how to compress the context into a single vector. Correspondingly, the decoder
244
      learns to infer or reconstruct from the compressed vectors. After the first stage of training, the
245
      encoder can produce stable compressed vectors. At this point, we input longer sequences for training
246   while freezing the encoder to save memory resources. This approach not only reduces training costs
247   but also enables the decoder to learn how to handle a larger number of compressed vectors. This
248   method does not require complex gradient optimization algorithms or substantial GPU memory
249   resources(Liu et al., 2023; Chevalier et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) and can efficiently scale to longer
250   sequences. We validated the effectiveness of this method on a 1M-length key retrieval task.
251
252   3.2.2    R ECONSTRUCTION AND C ONTINUATION TASKS
253
254   For the model training tasks, we require the model to possess both contextual memory and contextual
255   reasoning abilities. Therefore, we select text reconstruction and text continuation tasks. Traditional
256   autoencoding text reconstruction tasks (Ge et al., 2023) can only perform holistic reconstruction in
257
      a fixed manner, making it impossible to handle long texts. To address this issue, we propose another
      new training task called random prompt text reconstruction task. Specifically, we randomly extract
258
      a short text segment from the encoder’s input text as a prompt to the decoder, requiring the decoder
259
      to reconstruct the content following the prompt by leveraging the compressed information and the
260   prompt. This task enhances the model’s memory and retrieval ability. Additionally, to maintain the
261   model’s reasoning ability, we employ text continuation tasks. Relevant formulas can be found in
262   Appendix B.

      4.1               T EXT R ECONSTRUCTION
302
303   In the Pre-training phase, we use a combination of random prompt text reconstruction tasks and text
304   continuation tasks, with a ratio of 9:1. Afterwards, we fine-tune the model using a small amount
305   of random prompt text reconstruction tasks data. The input sequence length for the encoder is set
306   to 2048 tokens, while the uncompressed part of the decoder had an input length of 512 tokens. For
307   the random prompt text reconstruction task, the decoder needs to reconstruct the content following
308   a given prompt. The prompt and the content to be reconstructed are a subsequence of the com-
309
      pressed sequence. The prompts begin this subsequence and are excluded from the loss calculation
      during training. The loss was calculated only for the reconstructed text following the prompts. In
310
      the text continuation task, the decoder’s uncompressed input sequence was the continuation of the
311
      compressed sequence. We evaluated the model’s compression performance using the BLEU-4 score,
312   comparing the reconstructed text to the actual text. We created 100 encoder input samples, each with
313   a token length of 2048. To ensure fairness in the scores, we selected 5 text segments as prompts for
314   the decoder at every 300-token interval from the sample. Each decoder then calculated the recon-
315   struction score for prompts at 5 different positions, and we averaged these scores. The prompt text
316   length was about 10 tokens, and the reconstruction text length was about 500 tokens. Reconstruction
317   examples can be found in Appendix D.
318   As shown in Figure 4, we compared different models under a 64× compression rate. Both RCC-64-
319   mamba and RCC-64-transformer achieved a BLEU-4 score close to 0.8, but mamba’s training time
320   was nearly 1.5 times that of transformer. RCC-64-transformer-last-hidden-layer, which uses only
321   the encoder’s last layer compression vectors, achieved a BLEU-4 score of approximately 0.6. This
322   approach, common in traditional autoencoder models (Wang et al., 2021; Qin & Van Durme, 2023;
323   Ge et al., 2023), retains less textual information compared to using compression vectors from all
      layers. Additionally, ICAE performed poorly under a 64× compression rate, with a BLEU-4 score

      4.2     PASSKEY R ETRIEVAL TASK
344
345   We utilize the passkey retrieval task (Mohtashami & Jaggi, 2023) to validate the effectiveness of the
346   two-stage approach mentioned in section 3.2.1. Additionally, we observe that our method exhibits
347   certain length extrapolation capabilities. This enables it to handle compressed vector lengths dur-
348   ing inference that far exceed those seen during training. This indicates that the compressed vectors
349   generated by our method can be reliably recognized by the encoder, with minimal influence from
350   positional encoding. Passkey retrieval task involves embedding a random number into a long se-
351   quence composed of repeated fixed short phrases. The task requires the model to accurately retrieve
352
      the hidden number from these long sequences. Detailed construction methods for passkey retrieval
      task samples are provided in Appendix C. In this task, we employed a compression rate of 512×.
353
      Although this compression rate might not be effective for reconstruction tasks, experiments show
354
      that the fine-tuned RCC model performs well in the passkey retrieval task. The model was first
355   pre-trained on a dataset containing only the random prompt text reconstruction task, with an en-
356   coder input length set to 8k and a non-compressed decoder input length of 512. After pre-training,
357   we constructed nearly 30,000 passkey retrieval task samples with context lengths of 8k and 32k,
358   respectively. These samples formed the fine-tuning dataset. We conducted a two-stage fine-tuning
359   process. In the first stage, we fine-tuned the entire parameter set using the 8k context length samples.
360   After completing the first stage, we proceeded to the second stage with the 32k context length sam-
361   ples. During this stage, we froze the encoder parameters to accommodate the constraints of limited
362   available memory.

367   the effectiveness of our two-stage training method, even with the encoder parameters frozen at this

      First, we conducted pretraining on the random prompt text reconstruction task and the text continua-
      tion task with a ratio of 1:9. We use two-stage approach for training, the first stage involves training
388
      with full parameters on texts with a length of 2k. In the second stage, the encoder is frozen, and
389
      training is conducted on texts with a length of 16k. For question answering instruction fine-tuning,
390   we used the Prompt-with-Context (PwC) (Ge et al., 2023) and hotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018) datasets.
391   These datasets include context with instructions and outputs, teaching the model to use context for
392   answering questions rather than relying solely on internal knowledge. We concatenated the instruc-
393   tions and context as the encoder’s input, while the instructions and output text were concatenated
394   as the decoder’s uncompressed input. We repeated the instructions twice to train the decoder to
395   reconstruct instructions, enhancing mixed instruction and context effectiveness during inference.
396   The PwC dataset has 240k samples, and hotpotQA has 90k samples. Additionally, to improve in-
397   struction reconstruction and maintain the decoder’s instruction-following capability, we randomly
398
      selected 50k instruction samples from the orca dataset (Mukherjee et al., 2023). These samples lack
      explicit context fields and typically mix instructions with context. We set the context of the orca
399
      dataset to be empty and then processed it in the same way as PwC. During fine-tuning, the encoder’s
400
      input context length was set to 2048 tokens. The uncompressed part of the decoder’s input was
401   specified as 512 tokens, while maintaining a compression rate of 32.

      5   C ONCLUSION
492
493
      Utilizing compression techniques to mitigate challenges in long-text training and inference has
      proven to be a highly promising strategy. Our work quantitatively analyzes the impact of differ-
494
      ent context lengths on compression performance. It also achieves higher compression rates than
495
      previous methods, significantly enhancing the ability of LLM to handle long texts. To address the
496   substantial resource consumption of long-text training, we proposed a two staged training strat-
497   egy that further improved the efficiency of the model in processing long-text training. RCC model
498   demonstrated outstanding performance across multiple tasks, including context compression recon-
499   struction, long-document question answering, and passkey retrieval tasks with sequences up to 1
500   million tokens. Additionally, we analyzed the issues arising from simultaneous compression of con-
501   text and instructions and propose an instruction reconstruction method that effectively alleviated
502   these problems.
503   The RCC method has significantly advanced text compression efficiency. It has also improved long-
504   document question answering, but it has limitations. Although the parameter count of RCC’s en-
505   coder and decoder is twice that of Pythia, the impact of the model’s parameter count on storage
506   space significantly diminishes with increased text length. Additionally, the lack of long-text in-
507   struction fine-tuning data has caused performance bottlenecks for RCC. Our experiments show the
508   critical impact of training data on the model’s performance. The effectiveness of language models
509   fine-tuned with instructions depends largely on the quality and coverage of those instructions. These
510
      issues provide clear directions for our future research.
