                                                           A BSTRACT
008
009
                   Data-driven Riemannian geometry has emerged as a powerful tool for inter-
010
                   pretable representation learning, offering improved efficiency in downstream
011
                   tasks. Moving forward, it is crucial to balance cheap manifold mappings with ef-
012                ficient training algorithms. In this work, we integrate concepts from pullback Rie-
013                mannian geometry and generative models to propose a framework for data-driven
014                Riemannian geometry that is scalable in both geometry and learning: score-based
015                pullback Riemannian geometry. Focusing on unimodal distributions as a first step,
016                we propose a score-based Riemannian structure with closed-form geodesics that
017                pass through the data probability density. With this structure, we construct a Rie-
018                mannian autoencoder (RAE) with error bounds for discovering the correct data
019                manifold dimension. This framework can naturally be used with anisotropic nor-
020
                   malizing flows by adopting isometry regularization during training. Through nu-
                   merical experiments on various datasets, we demonstrate that our framework not
021
                   only produces high-quality geodesics through the data support, but also reliably
022
                   estimates the intrinsic dimension of the data manifold and provides a global chart
023                of the manifold, even in high-dimensional ambient spaces.

026   1       I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Data often reside near low-dimensional non-linear manifolds as illustrated in Figure 1. This mani-
029   fold assumption (Fefferman et al., 2016) has been popular since the early work on non-linear dimen-
030   sion reduction (Belkin & Niyogi, 2001; Coifman & Lafon, 2006; Roweis & Saul, 2000; Sammon,
031   1969; Tenenbaum et al., 2000). Learning this non-linear structure, or representation learning, from
032   data has proven to be highly successful (DeMers & Cottrell, 1992; Kingma & Welling, 2013) and
033   continues to be a recurring theme in modern machine learning approaches and downstream appli-
034   cations (Chow et al., 2022; Gomari et al., 2022; Ternes et al., 2022; Vahdat & Kautz, 2020; Zhong
035
      et al., 2021).
036   Recent advances in data-driven Riemannian geometry have demonstrated its suitability for learning
037   representations. In this context, these representations are elements residing in a learned geodesic
038   subspace of the data space, governed by a non-trivial Riemannian structure1 across the entire am-
039   bient space (Arvanitidis et al., 2016; Diepeveen, 2024; Hauberg et al., 2012; Peltonen et al., 2004;
040   Scarvelis & Solomon, 2023; Sorrenson et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024). Among these contributions,
041
      it is worth highlighting that Sorrenson et al. (2024) are the first and only ones so far to use infor-
      mation from the full data distribution obtained though generative models (Dinh et al., 2017; Song
042
      et al., 2020), even though this seems a natural approach given recent studies such as Sakamoto et al.
043
      (2024); Stanczuk et al. (2022). A possible explanation for the limited use of generative models
044   in constructing Riemannian geometry could lie in challenges regarding scalability of the manifold
045   mappings. Indeed, even though the generative models can be trained efficiently, Sorrenson et al.
046   (2024) also mention themselves that it can be numerically challenging to work with their induced
047   Riemannian geometry.
048
      If the manifold mapping scalability challenges were to be overcome, the combined power of Rie-
049
      mannian geometry and state of the art generative modelling could have profound implications on
050   how to handle data in general. Indeed, beyond typical data analysis tasks such as computing dis-
051   tances, means, and interpolations/extrapolations of data points as illustrated in Figures 2a to 2d, a
052   data-driven Riemannian structure also offers greater potential for representation learning and down-

      1.1   C ONTRIBUTIONS
095
096   In this paper, we take first steps towards striking such a balance and propose a score-based pull-
097   back Riemannian metric assuming a relatively simple but generally applicable family of probability
098   densities, which we show to result in both scalable manifold mappings and scalable learning algo-
099   rithms. We emphasize that we do not directly aim to find the perfect balance between the two types
100   of scalability. Instead we start from a setting which has many nice properties, but will allow for
101   generalization to multimodal densities, which we reserve for future work.
102   Specifically, we consider a family of unimodal probability densities whose negative log-likelihoods
103   are compositions of strongly convex functions and diffeomorphisms. As this work is an attempt to
104   bridge between the geometric data analysis community and the generative modeling community, we
105   break down the contributions in two ways. Theoretically,
106
107         • We propose a score-based pullback Riemannian metric such that manifold mappings re-
              spect the data distribution as illustrated in Figures 2a to 2d.

                 • We demonstrate that this density-based Riemannian structure naturally leads to a Rieman-
109                nian autoencoder2 and provide error bounds on the expected reconstruction error, which
110                allows for approximation of the data manifold as illustrated in Figure 1.
111
                 • We introduce a learning scheme based on adaptations of normalizing flows to find the

115   Practically, this work showcases how two simple adaptations to the normalizing flows framework
116   enable data-driven Riemannian geometry. This significantly expands the potential for downstream
117   applications compared to the unadapted framework.

133   Let M be a smooth manifold. We write C ∞ (M) for the space of smooth functions over M. The
134   tangent space at p ∈ M, which is defined as the space of all derivations at p, is denoted by
135   Tp M and for tangent vectors we write Ξp ∈ Tp M. For the tangent bundle we write T M and
136   smooth vector fields, which are defined as smooth sections of the tangent bundle, are written as
137
      X (M) ⊂ T M.
138   A smooth manifold M becomes a Riemannian manifold if it is equipped with a smoothly varying
139   metric tensor field ( · , · ) : X (M) × X (M) → C ∞ (M). This tensor field induces a (Rieman-
140   nian) metric dM : M × M → R. The metric tensor can also be used to construct a unique affine
141   connection, the Levi-Civita connection, that is denoted by ∇( · ) ( · ) : X (M) × X (M) → X (M).

143

      6.1      M ANIFOLD MAPPINGS
354
355   As discussed in Diepeveen (2024), the quality of learned manifold mappings is determined by two
356   key metrics: the geodesic error and the variation error. The geodesic error measures the average
357   deviation form the ground truth geodesics implied by the ground truth pullback metric, while the
358   variation error evaluates the stability of geodesics under small perturbations. We define these error
359   metrics for the evaluation of pullback geometries in Appendix D.
360   Our approach introduces two key modifications to the normalizing flow (NF) training framework:
361
362           1. Anisotropic Base Distribution: We parameterize the diagonal elements of the covariance
363              matrix Aθ1 , introducing anisotropy into the base distribution.
364
              2. ℓ2 -Isometry Regularization: We regularize the flow φθ2 to be approximately ℓ2 -isometric.
365
366   To assess the effectiveness of these modifications in learning more accurate and robust manifold
367   mappings, we compare our method against three baselines:
368
369           (1) Normalizing Flow (NF): Uses an NF with a standard isotropic Gaussian base distribution
370               N (0, Id ) and no isometry regularization of the flow.
371

372         We note that without these constraints (accommodating multimodality) the learned mappings can in prin-
373   ciple be used to construct Riemannian geometry and a RAE. However, from the theory discussed in this paper
      we cannot guarantee stability of manifold mappings nor that the RAE has the right dimension.
374       8

375   as rational quadratic flows Durkan et al. (2019) is due to our need for high regularity for stable manifold map-

      To evaluate the scalability of our method to higher-dimensional manifolds, we conducted additional
461
      experiments on the Hemisphere(5,20) and Sinusoid(5,20) datasets.
462
463   Our theory suggests that the learned variances indicate the importance of each latent dimension:
464   higher variances signal more important dimensions for reconstructing the manifold, while dimen-
465
      sions with vanishing variances are considered insignificant and are disregarded when constructing
      the Riemannian autoencoder. To test the model’s ability to correctly identify important and unimpor-
466
      tant latent dimensions, we report the average ℓ2 reconstruction error for each dataset as a function
467
      of the number of latent dimensions used. In the reconstruction error plots (see figs. 5b and 5d),
468   we report three variance-based orders for adding latent dimensions: decreasing variance order (blue
469   line), increasing variance order (green line), and random order (red line).
470
471
      For the Hemisphere(5,20) dataset, the model identified five non-vanishing variances (see fig. 5a),
      perfectly capturing the intrinsic dimension of the manifold. This is reflected in the blue curve in
472
      fig. 5b, where the first five latent dimensions, corresponding to the largest variances, are sufficient
473
      to reduce the reconstruction error almost to zero. In contrast, the green curve illustrates that the
474   remaining ambient dimensions do not encode useful information about the manifold. The red curve
475   demonstrates improvement only when an important latent dimension is included.
476
477
      For the more challenging Sinusoid(5,20) dataset, our method still performs very well, though not
      as perfectly as for the Hemisphere dataset. The first six most important latent dimensions explain
478
      approximately 97% of the variance, increasing to over 99% with the seventh dimension (see fig. 5c).
479
      This is reflected in the blue curve in fig. 5d, where the first six latent dimensions reduce the recon-
480   struction error to near zero, and the addition of the seventh dimension brings the error effectively to
481   zero. The slight discrepancy between our results and the ground truth likely arises from increased
482   optimization difficulty, as the normalizing flow must learn a more intricate distribution while main-
483   taining approximate isometry. We believe that with deeper architectures and more careful tuning
484   of the optimization loss, the model will converge to the correct intrinsic dimensionality of five.
485   Currently, it predicts six dimensions at a threshold of ϵ = 0.05 and seven at ϵ = 0.01, slightly
      overestimating due to the manifold’s complexity.

521   7    C ONCLUSIONS
522
523   In this work we have taken a first step towards a practical data-driven Riemannian geometry frame-
524   work, striking a balance between scalability of training a data-driven Riemannian structure and of
525   evaluating its corresponding manifold mappings. We have considered a family of unimodal prob-
526   ability densities whose negative log-likelihoods are compositions of strongly convex functions and
527   diffeomorphisms, and sought to learn them. We have shown that once these unimodal densities have
528   been learned, the proposed score-based pullback geometry gives us closed-form geodesics that pass
529   through the data probability density and a Riemannian autoencoder with error bounds that can be
530   used to estimate the dimension of the data manifold. Finally, to learn the distribution we have pro-
531
      posed an adaptation to normalizing flow training. Through numerical experiments, we have shown
      that these modifications are crucial for extracting geometric information, and that our framework
532
      not only generates high-quality geodesics across the data support, but also accurately estimates the
533
      intrinsic dimension of the approximate data manifold while constructing a global chart, even in
534   high-dimensional ambient spaces. Current challenges of the method lie in balancing the expressiv-
535   ity of the network architecture, e.g., through additional layers or more expressive architectures, and
536   satisfying approximate ℓ2 -isometry on the data support. For future work we aim to overcome these
537   challenges, extending the method to multimodal distributions, while making it scalable for higher-
538   dimensional data sets. After that, we believe that this line of work has wide variety of downstream
539   applications as many of the applications mentioned to motivate this line of work will benefit from
      more interpretable representation learning.

1160
1161
       We applied our training framework9 to model complex real-world and synthetic distributions, specif-
1162   ically focusing on the subset of digit “1” from the MNIST dataset and a synthetic dataset of 10-
1163   dimensional Gaussian blobs introduced in Stanczuk et al. (2022). The subset of digit “1” is chosen
1164   as it is likely to be represented well by the unimodal parametric family 3. The Gaussian blobs dataset
1165   is included because its intrinsic dimension is known (10), providing a reliable baseline for evaluating
1166   the accuracy of the RAE’s intrinsic dimension estimation.
1167
       Modeling such distributions effectively requires more expressive normalizing flow architectures,
1168   such as affine coupling flows combined with 1 × 1 invertible convolutions for pixel reshuffling,
1169   or rational quadratic (RQ) spline flows. These architectures, however, are not guaranteed to have
1170   zero second derivatives, which can cause the higher-order terms in Theorem 1 to become signifi-
1171   cant, potentially inflating the expected reconstruction error of the Riemannian Auto-encoder (RAE).
1172   Furthermore, enforcing ℓ2 isometry regularization becomes more challenging in these cases.
1173
       Despite these issues, our experiments indicate that the deviations from isometry and the presence of
1174   non-zero second derivatives do not visibly impact the quality of the manifold mappings. However,
1175   they can affect the overall performance of the RAE.
1176
