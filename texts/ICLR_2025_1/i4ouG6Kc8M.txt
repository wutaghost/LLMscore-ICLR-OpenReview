011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Selecting appropriate models during self-supervised training of vision transform-
014            ers in histopathology is challenging. Recent efforts to quantify the quality of
015            self-supervised learning representations through rank estimation approaches have
016
               shown promise in natural image classification tasks. However, their effective-
               ness in histopathology, particularly for non-linear tasks such as instance segmen-
017
               tation and classification from whole slide images, remains unexplored. This study
018
               proposes an approach for model selection in histopathology by combining task-
019            specific metrics (such as accuracy) and task agnostic metrics (such as rank estima-
020            tion). This work shows that by training several small-scale histopathology models
021            and applying the proposed model selection approach, one can achieve instance
022            segmentation performance comparable to state-of-the-art models trained on much
023            larger datasets. The proposed approach also allows for obtaining a model based
024            on the type of downstream task. Towards this end, three types of model selection
025            based on the downstream task performance were evaluated: classification-best,
026            segmentation-best, and a best all-round one. When evaluated on held-out classifi-
027
               cation and weakly supervised learning tasks, the most performant checkpoints of-
               ten occur earlier in training, indicating potential performance saturation mid way
028
               in the training for histopathology models. These results highlight the importance
029
               of appropriate model selection for self-supervised learning in histopathology.

032   1     I NTRODUCTION

034   1.1   M OTIVATIONS
035
036   Mirroring advancements in self-supervised pre-training of vision transformers (ViT) (Dosovitskiy
037   et al., 2020) for natural images, a significant number of models have been proposed in computational
038   pathology literature. As transformers significantly benefit from scaling in dataset size and compute
039   (Kolesnikov et al., 2020), histopathology models are increasingly getting larger, with proportional
040   increases in the amount of data, compute and therefore the associative power consumption required
041
      for training these models.
042   Despite considerable interest and progress of self-supervised learning (SSL) in histopathology, there
043   is an important and often overlooked challenge: estimating the the generalizability of a model in
044   order to terminate training is challenging. This is because minimizing the typical training objective
045   in self-supervised learning may not translate to better downstream task performance (Geiping et al.,
046   2023). Furthermore, as label-free learning algorithms strive for generality in a diverse landscape of
047
      downstream tasks, finalizing on a predefined set of training iterations may trade balance in favor of
      one type of downstream task over another, such as trading image classification performance over
048
      image segmentation performance. Under current practices in histopathology literature, gauging
049
      model efficacy is done based on tile-level (patches1 extracted from scanned tissue images, also
050   called whole slide images, or WSI) benchmark tasks, and slide-level benchmark tasks. But does
051   benchmark performance correlate with downstream task performance? Is one kind of benchmark
052

054
      task suitable over the other? As of writing this article, answers to these questions remain unclear.
055   Some effort to quantify the quality of SSL representations, particularly by estimating the rank of
056   representations, have been made in natural image literature (briefly elaborated in §2.2). The main
057   idea here is that there are some salient, desirable properties that representations must have in order
058   to be beneficial to downstream tasks, thus making the assumption that these quality metrics directly
059   correlate with downstream performance. Yet, they have only thus far been tested on linear probing
060   tasks, which indeed benefit from an appropriate structure within the representation’s eigenvectors,
061   such as its eigenspectrum decay for instance, or its entropy. Therefore, their usefulness on other
062   tasks, particularly instance segmentation tasks that are crucial to histopathology, is not known.
063   In the absence of theoretical frameworks that explore stopping criteria in self-supervised learning
064   approaches, the core thesis of this work is that the appropriate approach is a combination of out-
065   of-distribution benchmark performance, which is termed task-dependent metrics, and representation
066   quality estimation approaches, which is termed task-agnostic metrics 2 . Therefore, this work at-
067   tempts to build a bridge between these two topics by proposing a simple but effective model selection
068   procedure that give improved performance on downstream tasks.

071   1.2   C ONTRIBUTIONS
072
073   This study proposes a model selection procedure for field of histopathology by combining pub-
074   licly available histopathology benchmark task-specific metrics and representation quality based task-
075   agnostic metrics to propose a simple approach for model selection for self-supervised histopathology
076   models. The approach is described in §3.
077   Several encoders are trained by varying the dataset, model size, and model architecture, in or-
078   der to identify appropriate checkpoints for downstream clinical use using the proposed approach.
079   The model selection is segregated into three approaches based on the type of tile-level tasks: a
080   classification-best checkpoint obtained using only classification benchmarks, a segmentation-best
081   checkpoint on benchmark tasks based only on instance segmentation benchmarks, and a task-
082   agnostic model that provides favorable performance on both types of tasks. The performance of
083   each checkpoint type is investigated on out-of-distribution benchmark tasks in §5.2 and slide-level
084   tasks in §5.3.
085   The code and accompanying task-specific and task-agnostic metric data for all of our experiments
086   will be released with this paper.

111   2.1   S ELF - SUPERVISED LEARNING IN DIGITAL HISTOPATHOLOGY
112
113
      Self-supervised learning approaches: The surge in foundation model training for digital
      histopathology follows the successes of joint-embedding self-supervised learning (SSL) techniques
114
      in natural images (Geiping et al., 2023). The goal of these techniques is to embed and align two sep-
115
      arately augmented variations of an image using various alignment objectives, such as the contrastive
116   learning target used in Azizi et al. (2023); Ciga et al. (2022). This target utilizes the infoNCE loss
117   (Oord et al., 2018) introduced in methods like SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020), where representations
118   of image augmentations are aligned, or CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and SigLIP (Zhai et al., 2023),
119   where text and image pairs are aligned.
120
      A common SSL framework used by many foundation models is the dinov1 (Caron et al., 2021) and
121
      dinov2 (Oquab et al., 2023) approach, both of which employ a teacher-student network. In this
122   network, the teacher’s representations are skewed from the student’s using a centered softmax, and
123   the teacher’s weights are updated using an exponential moving average over the student. Dinov2
124   improves upon dinov1 in terms of training stability over larger batch sizes using the Ko-Leo regular-
125   izer (Sablayrolles et al., 2018) and by replacing the centering step in dinov1 with a Sinkhorn-Knopp
126   centering step (Caron et al., 2020). Dinov2 also includes the iBOT loss (Zhou et al., 2021), which
127   introduces patch-level losses in addition to the image-level losses present within Dinov1. The vanilla
128   dino framework has been immensely successful in digital histopathology and forms the foundation
129   for a majority of models trained on histopathology data (Zimmermann et al., 2024; Vorontsov et al.,
130
      2024; Chen et al., 2024; Saillard et al., 2024; Nechaev et al., 2024; Campanella et al., 2023; Dippel
      et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Juyal et al., 2024).
131
132   Recently, modifications to the Dino framework have been proposed to cater to the specialized field
133   of digital histopathology. For example, Zimmermann et al. (2024) replaced the Ko-Leo regularizer
134   with the Kernel Density Estimator (KDE) and observed an increase in performance and stability,
135   since a typical minibatch of digital histopathology images is relatively more similar compared to
136
      natural images spanning thousands of classes. Juyal et al. (2024) proposed merging masked image
      modelling with the vanilla Dino approach by including the Masked Auto-Encoder (MAE) loss ob-
137
      jective (He et al., 2022). This is a similar style of including a patch-level modeling approach as in
138
      iBOT, but is an image modelling approach instead of a joint-embedding approach. Therefore, the
139   loss is estimated over a reconstruction of the masked image with the input. In this work, they also
140   introduce a Fourier reconstruction loss (Wang et al., 2024), which decomposes the Fourier transform
141   of the reconstructed image from the MAE branch into low and high frequency components using a
142   band-pass filter. This is perhaps included in order to counter a known issue with MAE which causes
143   the representations to favor high-frequency components in an image over low-frequency ones (Bao
144   et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2021). As these developments continue to progress, this work chose to
145   use the original Dino framework by Caron et al. (2021), as modifications can successively be intro-
146   duced to the learning approach later on. Training stability in the dinov1 approach can be achieved
147
      using small batch-sizes, which is ideal for the single-tissue scale of the experiments in this work.
148   Multi-scale adaptation: In a typical slide-evaluation procedure, histopathologists examine tissue
149   samples at multiple scales. This is because at larger fields of view, the tissue architecture is distinct,
150   while higher magnifications enables cellular features to be distinguished. Therefore, histopathology
151   benchmarks tend to be distributed across magnifications, from 2 microns/px to 0.25 microns/px, the
152   latter being more favorable for cell segmentation tasks. Histopathology models often exposed to
153
      increasingly larger resolutions of patches extracted from whole-slide images in the final stage of the
      pre-training (Chen et al., 2024). Recently that some works have considered the multi-scale aspect
154
      of histopathology during pre-training. For example, the HIPT model (Chen et al., 2022) considers
155
      a hierarchical set of feature extractors on a series of varying patch sizes of the vision transformer
156   patch embedding module (16×16, 256×256, and 4096×4096) in order to capture cellular, tile level,
157   and region level information. Juyal et al. (2024) use the FlexiViT architecture (Beyer et al., 2023)
158   to introduce a range of scales into the encoder training. Finally, GigaPath (Xu et al., 2024) utilizes
159   the LongNet architecture (Ding et al., 2023) as a decoder to produce slide-level embeddings from a
160   set of tile-level embeddings extracted using a standard vision transformer backbone. These multi-
161   scale adaptations are largely architectural, but a simpler approach is to pre-train vanilla architectures
      on multi-scale data, thus introducing data variability and the ability of the encoder to adapt to the


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      myriad of scales of downstream tasks. Published works include Kang et al. (2023) and Zimmermann
163   et al. (2024), which randomly mix the FOV of the images during pre-training without changes to
164   the image resolution. Kang et al. (2023) uses tiles extracted from magnifications of 0.25 and 0.5
165   µm/px, while Zimmermann et al. (2024) use 0.25, 0.5, 1, and 2 µm/px in their dataset. One of the
166   benefits observed by Kang et al. (2023) seems to be improved convergence during training. But the
167   most important benefit is that this enables the encoder to use a benchmark’s native resolution and
168   magnification for assessment, and therefore is the approach followed by this work.

170   2.2    TASK - AGNOSTIC QUALITY METRICS
171
172   Joint-embedding self-supervised learning (SSL) approaches train encoders solely at the representa-
173   tion level, making it challenging to predict when the training process has reached a level suitable for
174   downstream tasks. To address this, recent research has focused on rank-based representation quality
175
      metrics, which operate under the assumption that optimal metric values will lead to improved bench-
      mark performance, and have been reported to correlate with downstream performance. Garrido et al.
176
      (2023) introduced RankMe, which computes the Shannon entropy of the eigenvalues of a set of rep-
177
      resentations as the effective rank of the embedding matrix, serving as a proxy for representational
178   power. RankMe demonstrated a strong correlation with downstream linear probing performance
179   across various SSL methods and architectures. Thilak et al. (2023) extended RankMe by applying
180   linear discriminant analysis (Martinez & Kak, 2001), estimating a generalized covariance matrix
181   using representations of different images and transformed variants of the same image, particularly
182   those used in the SSL method. They then estimate the entropy of the eigenvalues of the generalized
183   covariance matrix, capturing the representation behavior explicitly as determined by the SSL objec-
184   tive. Building on theoretical insights, Agrawal et al. (2022) proposed α-ReQ, which measures the
185   decay rate of the eigenspectrum of the representation covariance matrix, arguing that an optimal rate
186
      balances expressiveness and generalization. However, as noted by Thilak et al. (2023), α-ReQ is
      sensitive to linear transformations that arbitrarily influence the eigenspectrum matrix, allowing for
187
      high rank registration despite potential degradation in downstream performance.
188
189   One of the key advantages of rank-based representation quality metrics is their ability to measure
190   dimensional collapse, where one eigenvalue dominates while others contribute minimally towards
191   the representation. This provides valuable insights into the expressiveness and generalization capa-
192
      bilities of the learned representations. However, a significant limitation of rank approximation as a
      quality metric is their reliance on the linear behavior of eigenvalues in representing the quality of
193
      learned features. While this linear approach may be suitable for linear-probing tasks, it may not
194
      be adequate for inherently non-linear tasks in the histopathology domain, such as multiple instance
195   learning. Using these task-agnostic metrics in conjunction with task-specific metrics from a set of
196   benchmark tasks can help mitigate the drawbacks of rank-estimation approaches, and is the track
197   followed in this work.

201

      The approach described in Algorithm 1 is a simple process that identifies the best-performing check-
      point across a set of out-of-distribution benchmark tasks, yielding task-specific metrics such as the
203
      aggregated jaccard index (Kumar et al., 2019) or the weighted F1 metric, and task-agnostic repre-
204
      sentation quality metrics, such as RankMe, LiDAR, and α-ReQ. Given N task-specific metrics, M
205   task-agnostic metrics, and E checkpoints saved during a particular run (e.g., every 5 epochs), an
206   N · M number of samples are obtained, indicating the result between a performance metric for each
207   task and its representation quality across the E saved checkpoints.
208

209

210
      mance dataset. Each task’s result is determined by a normalized benchmark metric between 0 and
211   1, such as the aggregated Jaccard index for instance segmentation (Kumar et al., 2019) and the
212   weighted F1 score for classification tasks3 . To estimate the best checkpoint for each benchmark

504   6   C ONCLUSIONS
505
506
      This study addressed the challenge of model selection for histopathology encoders trained in a self-
507
      supervised manner. It was shown that training histopathology models for arbitrarily large number
508   of training epochs is actually detrimental to its downstream performance, despite the training loss
509   behavior continuing to monotonically reduce as epochs progress. A model selection procedure for
510   self-supervised encoder training was proposed that combines out-of-distribution task-specific met-
511   rics and task-agnostic metrics. In the analyses conducted on several models trained on histopathol-
512   ogy data, it was observed that the instance segmentation performance (quantified using the aggre-
513   gated Jaccard index) was comparable and often exceeded state-of-the-art models from the literature,
514   despite significantly smaller model size, dataset size, and dataset scope in terms of tissue types.
515   As part of the analyses, model selection criteria were constructed, which differentiated based on
516   the type of benchmark tasks involved in the selection procedure: checkpoints that yield the best
517   results on classification tasks, instance segmentation tasks, and an all-round model considering both
518   task types. These checkpoints were then used to estimate the performance of two held-out tasks
519   measured in terms of AUC: a patch-level LUAD subtype classification task and a slide-level EGFR
520   classification task. While some models showed a preference for one checkpoint type over others, the
521   most performant checkpoints generally occurred mid-way in the training process relative to the final
522
      epoch. This is in contrast with the experiences in self-supervised model training on natural images
      which suggest that longer training leads to better generalization.
523
524   A key limitation of this work is that exploring the generalization of each checkpoint is expensive.
525   This is due to the broad range of benchmark tasks, both in terms of scope and field of view, typically
526   encountered in histopathology and the limited ability of current representation quality estimation ap-
527   proaches to estimate instance segmentation performance. Rank estimation approaches may only pre-
528
      dict linear probing performance, while typical histopathology classification tasks involve slide-level
      aggregation of patch-level features, introducing a non-linearity that cannot be directly modeled by
529
      an embedding rank. While this limitation was ameliorated in this work by using out-of-distribution
530
      benchmark tasks in conjunction with the representation rank, further work is necessary in devel-
531   oping more comprehensive representation quality metrics. This involves a deeper understanding
532   of properties of representations that correlate with the performance of complex downstream tasks
533   beyond classification.

710   A.1     M ODEL TRAINING DATASET
711
712   A.1.1    E NCODER TRAINING DATASET
713
714
      The dataset consisted of 224×224 non-overlapping patches extracted from digitized slides of LUAD
      patients. The scanning was performed at 5×, 20×, and 40× magnifications, utilizing Leica Aperio
715
      AT2 for scanning at 20× magnification (0.5 microns/px), and the Leica Aperio GT450 for scan-
716
      ning at 40× (0.25 microns/px). For each scan at native resolution, a 5× magnification scan is also
717   facilitated by both scanners, i.e., at 2 microns/px. For each magnification, upwards of 5 million
718   samples were selected, extracted from tissue slides stained using hematoxylin and eosin dye. This
719   is collected by randomly sampling 1000 patches per slide at the chosen pixel resolution, performing
720   Otsu thresholding to remove background, i.e., white space.
721
722   A.1.2    T ILE LEVEL LUAD SUBTYPE CLASSIFICATION
723
724   The LUAD tissue slides used for generating the training set also include slide-level segmentation of
725
      commonly found features, specifically regions exhibiting the following characteristics: Acinar, Lep-
      idic, Papillary, Micropapillary, and Solid Tumor. These segmentation labels are utilized to associate
726
      each patch with the corresponding majority label, i.e., the class that is predominantly present within
727
      the patch. This labeled dataset is then employed for patch-level classification, and the features are
728   most distinctive at lower magnifications or larger fields of view. Consequently, patches extracted at
729   5× magnification with a resolution of 224 × 224 pixels are used for this task. A total of 600,000
730   images are extracted for this purpose, and their features are obtained for performing classification on
731   top of frozen features. The methodology adopted for this analysis is in accordance with the approach
732   described in appendix A of Radford et al. (2021).
733
734   A.1.3    S LIDE LEVEL EGFR TRAINING DATASET
735
736   For the MIL aggregator, a real-world clinical dataset of digitized slides of LUAD patients scanned
737
      using an in-house scanning apparatus was chosen, paired with ground truth EGFR mutational status
      obtained from the IMPACT sequencing panel Cheng et al. (2017). In this case, every tile extracted
738
      from the slides is taken after Otsu’s thresholding is applied to remove white space. Ten 225/75
739
      train/test splits are utilized from the same 300 set of slides. Only WSIs scanned at 0.25 microns per
740   pixel are considered, as a pseudo-20× magnification feature set can be obtained from the slides by
741   resizing 448x448 pixel non-overlapping patches back to the encoder’s native resolution of 224×224
742   pixels.
743
744   A.2     T ILE LEVEL OUT- OF - DISTRIBUTION BENCHMARKS
745
746   The out-of-distribution generalization of the encoders is evaluated on the following four public
747   histopathology image datasets. For all datasets, minimal preprocessing is applied - resizing/cropping
748   to a uniform 224 × 224 input size and converting to RGB format where needed. No stain normal-
749   ization is applied to preserve the natural variation present in histopathology images.
750   PanNuke Gamper et al. (2020): A large multi-organ nuclei segmentation dataset containing 189,744
751   nuclei instances across 19 tissue types is used. Images are 224 × 224 pixels at 40× magnification.
752   The official 3-fold cross-validation splits are used, with folds 1-2 for training/validation and fold 3
753   for testing. For benchmarking at 40× magnification, the original image size is retained. At 20×, the
754   image is resized to 112 × 112 and a 96 × 96 center crop is applied. For the Virchow and Virchow2

925
926
927   For the slide-level EGFR prediction task, feature vectors are obtained for all (non-overlapping)
928   patches from the WSI dataset. MIL pooling is then applied on the patch-level feature vector to
929   obtain a slide-level representation of the WSI. This slide-level representation is subsequently used
930   for the binary EGFR classification task. The gated multi-head attention (GMA) Ilse et al. (2018) is
931
      used for slide-level aggregation, keeping the encoder frozen during the downstream task.
932   Single field-of-view: Representations come from a single field-of-view of 0.5 microns/pix or 0.25
933   microns/pix, and 224 × 224 pixel patches. They are concatenated as is standard in GMA-MIL to
934   obtain slide-level representations and used for the binary classification task.
935   Multiple fields-of-view: In this case, only WSI scans at 0.25 microns/pix are chosen. Represen-
936   tations come from both 0.5 microns/pix and 0.25 microns/pix at 224 × 224 pixel patches. For the
937   former, 448 × 448 non-overlapping patches are extracted from the WSI, and the patches are down-
938   sampled to 224 × 224 to mimic a 0.5 microns/pix patch before being fed to the encoder. For the
939   0.25 microns/pix, 224 × 224 non-overlapping patches are extracted as in the single field-of-view
940   approach. The patches are naively concatenated, and the GMA-MIL aggregator is utilized to obtain
941   slide-level representations which are used for classification.

      C.1   E STIMATION PROCEDURE
949
950
951   To estimate the quality of the learned representations, three metrics are considered: RankMe Garrido
952   et al. (2023), LiDAR Thilak et al. (2023), and α-ReQ Agrawal et al. (2022). These metrics are
953   calculated using the test set of the pre-training dataset, which is 20% of the training set size. For
954   LiDAR, 50 augmentations per image are generated to capture the representation behavior under
955   different transformations.
956   RankMe: The RankMe metric is calculated using a random subset of 30,000 embeddings from
957   the encoder to reduce computational complexity. The singular values are obtained through singular
958   value decomposition (SVD) and normalized to sum to one. The final RankMe metric is calculated
959   as the exponential of the entropy of the normalized singular values.
960   LiDAR: A random subset from test features of 1000 samples, each containing 300 augmentations,
961   is chosen, thus giving a total of 300,000 embeddings in a 300×1000 matrix. In this case, the embed-
962   dings are derived from the last but one layer of the Dino projector head which has 256 dimensions.
963   Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) is then performed on the reshaped features by estimating the
964   inter-class (between clean images) and intra-class (between augmentations of an image) covariance
965   matrix, and the eigenvalues of the resulting LDA matrix are computed. The eigenvalues are nor-
966   malized to sum to one, and the LiDAR metric is calculated as the exponential of the entropy of the
967   normalized eigenvalues.
968   α-ReQ: The test features are first centered by subtracting the mean, and then the covariance matrix
969   is computed. The eigenvalues of the covariance matrix are obtained and sorted in descending order.
970   The decay coefficient α is then calculated by performing linear regression on the logarithm of the
971   eigenvalues against the logarithm of their indices. The α-ReQ metric is calculated as the negative of
      the slope obtained from the linear regression.
