009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Transformer models excel in time series tasks due to their attention mechanisms.
012            However, they often suffer from “block-like” attention patterns caused by high
013            feature correlation, leading to feature confusion and reduced performance. In this
014            study, we mathematically prove and quantify this limitation, demonstrating how
015            it affects the sparsity of the attention matrix and hinders effective feature repre-
016            sentation. To overcome this issue, we propose a novel, model-agnostic, and plug-
017            and-play method called SEAT (Sparsification-Enhanced Attention Transformer)
018
               that leverages frequency domain sparsification. By transforming time series data
               into the frequency domain, our method induces inherent sparsity, reduces feature
019
               similarity, and mitigates block-like attention, allowing the attention mechanism
020
               to focus more precisely on relevant features. Experiments on benchmark datasets
021            demonstrate that our approach significantly enhances the accuracy and robustness
022            of Transformer models while maintaining computational efficiency. This provides
023            a mathematically grounded solution to inherent flaws in attention mechanisms,
024            offering a versatile and effective approach for advancing time series analysis.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   In long-term sequence forecasting (LTSF) tasks, attention mechanisms play a crucial role in cap-
030   turing dependencies within time series data. However, existing approaches, particularly those em-
031   ploying “Point-wise Attention” exhibit significant limitations. This strategy maps various features
032   at each time step to embeddings, treating each as a token. Consequently, attention mechanisms allo-
033
      cate weights to each corresponding token. Experimental studies Zhang & Yan (2023) have revealed
      that, in conventional Transformers for LTSF tasks, cross-dimensional dependencies are not explicitly
034
      captured during the embedding process. This limitation adversely affects forecasting capabilities,
035
      as attention values tend to exhibit segmentation; adjacent data points often receive similar attention
036   weights. The “Point-wise Attention” approach typically results in a block-like distribution of feature
037   map values within time series representations. This block-shaped pattern causes approximate values
038   of different features in the attention mechanism to become conflated, leading to model overfitting to
039   noise. Consequently, the decision boundaries of the model become ambiguous, negatively impacting
040   overall performance.
041
      Subsequent research has attempted to mitigate these issues by employing alternative attention strate-
042   gies, such as using pairs of patches. For instance, PatchTST Nie et al. (2023) utilizes patching
043   and channel independence within Transformer architectures to significantly enhance performance,
044   demonstrating that Transformers retain considerable potential for improvement in time series fore-
045   casting when appropriately adapted. Pathformer Chen et al. (2024) adopts a patch-based method
046   to divide the time series into various temporal resolutions. Through this multi-scale division, dual
047   attention is applied to these patches, enabling the capture of global correlations and local temporal
048   dependencies.
049   Despite these advancements, existing methods primarily focus on enhancing feature extraction with-
050   out fundamentally improving the quality of time series feature representation. Block-like attention
051   may result in the attention weight matrix assigning significantly higher weights to certain time slices
052   compared to others, leading to the aggregation of similar features within the attention output and
053   increasing feature confusion. Such effects can adversely impact the model’s learning capability and
      performance limits, as will be substantiated in the Methods section.

054
      To address these challenges, we propose SEAT, a model-agnostic enhancement framework appli-
055   cable to any Transformer architecture’s input signals. SEAT employs frequency modelling across
056   the entire time series and introduces a finite energy representation in the frequency domain, cap-
057   italizing on the sparse features present in time series data. This design enables the Transformer’s
058   attention mechanism to focus on independent feature representations as “Channel-wise Attention.”
059   By explicitly modelling the Fourier transform to reconstruct features as incremental signals, SEAT
060   enhances the model’s ability to distinguish approximate features and reduce feature redundancy and
061   similarity. Consequently, this approach improves predictive performance in long-term forecasting
062   tasks across various Transformer-based models. In summary, our contributions are threefold:
063
            1. We provide a theoretical analysis of the limitations in attention mechanisms within time
064            series forecasting Transformers, investigating the causes of feature confusion and suscepti-
065            bility to overfitting.
066
            2. Based on rigorous mathematical proofs, we design SEAT, a sparse sensing enhancement
067
               framework tailored for time series attention, ensuring the independence and sparsity of
068            input features within Transformer architectures.
069
            3. Our framework is decoupled from the underlying model architecture, offering plug-and-
070
               play functionality and compatibility with any existing Transformer-based architecture.

076
      Time series forecasting has been significantly advanced by the integration of Transformer archi-
077
      tectures Ashish (2017). Leveraging the self-attention mechanism, Transformer-based models have
078   demonstrated exceptional performance in capturing long-range dependencies, a critical aspect for
079   effective LTSF. Notable models in this domain include:
080
081
      Autoformer Wu et al. (2021) introduced an auto-correlation mechanism specifically designed to
      leverage the inherent periodicity of time series data. This mechanism discovers dependencies and ag-
082
      gregates representations at the sub-series level, significantly improving the model’s ability to utilize
083
      long-range information. By focusing on periodic patterns, Autoformer overcomes the information
084   bottleneck that constrains the original Transformer architecture, thereby enhancing its forecasting
085   performance for non-stationary time series.
086
087
      Pyraformer Liu et al. (2022a) further addresses these limitations through a hierarchical time se-
      ries decomposition approach. Its pyramid structure exhibits multiresolution properties, allowing the
088
      model to decompose time series into distinct temporal scales. At coarser resolutions, long-term
089
      dependencies are captured, while finer resolutions discern intricate short-term variations. This hi-
090   erarchical decomposition facilitates a nuanced understanding of temporal dynamics, enhancing the
091   model’s ability to accurately forecast future trends and patterns. However, distinguishing long-term
092   and short-term features is often limited by window parameter selection and data resolution, necessi-
093   tating model structure modifications for different datasets, which reduces robustness and increases
094   susceptibility to overfitting.
095
      Nonstationary Transformer Liu et al. (2022b) introduces a dual approach to improve the mod-
096   elling of non-stationary time series. It enhances data stationarity through techniques that mitigate
097   non-stationarity within the time series and reformulates the Transformer’s internal mechanisms to
098   reintegrate non-stationary information. This twofold innovation significantly advances the Trans-
099   former’s capability in handling non-stationary data, improving both predictability and forecasting
100   performance.
101
      iTransformer Liu et al. (2024) marks a significant advancement by surpassing traditional Trans-
102   former models in time series forecasting. It treats individual series as variate tokens, utilizing atten-
103   tion mechanisms to capture multivariate correlations and employing layer normalization and feed-
104   forward networks to learn robust series representations. However, relying solely on time-domain
105   tokens poses challenges in comprehensively portraying integral properties of time series data, such
106   as overarching trends and periodic fluctuations. Additionally, the quadratic complexity and large
107   parameter count inherent in Transformer models make them prone to overfitting, especially when
      applied to non-stationary time series.

      2.2   F REQUENCY- DOMAIN - BASED T RANSFORMERS
109
110   Time-frequency transforms offer a promising avenue for transforming long-time series into sparse
111   representations. The development of frequency domain methods can be traced back to the introduc-
112   tion of Fourier transforms and wavelet transforms, which provided novel perspectives for analyzing
113   periodicity and global dependencies within time series data. Frequency-domain methods, tradi-
114   tionally used for signal processing tasks, excel at decomposing time series into their constituent
115   frequencies, revealing periodic and seasonal components that are often crucial for accurate fore-
116   casting. Early frequency domain approaches, such as FNet Lee-Thorp et al. (2021) and AFNO
117
      Guibas et al. (2021), were primarily designed to enhance computational efficiency by leveraging
      Fourier transforms to replace self-attention or token-mixing mechanisms, thereby achieving faster
118
      computation.
119
120   There has been a growing trend towards integrating frequency-domain methods with sophisticated
121   techniques such as attention mechanisms to achieve superior predictive performance in time series
122   analysis. Traditional Transformer architectures applied to time series data rely on point-wise atten-
123
      tion mechanisms, where individual temporal points undergo attention computations and predictions
      in isolation. As a result, these models often struggle to maintain and accurately model holistic,
124
      global features that inherently encode crucial information for accurate forecasting. In contrast, FED-
125
      former Zhou et al. (2022) represents a paradigm shift by leveraging Transformer structures within
126   the spectral domain for feature extraction. This innovative approach enables FEDformer to more
127   effectively capture global characteristics vital for comprehending the intricate dynamics within time
128   series data. By harnessing the complementary strengths of both temporal and spectral representa-
129   tions, FEDformer fosters a deeper, more nuanced understanding of underlying patterns and trends,
130   thereby enhancing its predictive capabilities.
131
      Similar to the objectives of numerous decomposition methods Wu et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2021),
132   the adoption of spectral domain approaches aims to facilitate the decomposition of time series data
133   into distributions that are more conducive to learning. By transforming the time series into the
134   frequency domain, these methods enable a more effective decomposition of temporal dynamics,
135   transforming the data into representations that are easier for models to comprehend and utilize for
136   prediction tasks. However, they share a common limitation: when dealing with complex time series,
137   most frequency models tend to prioritize learning low-frequency features while overlooking high-
138   frequency features, exhibiting a frequency bias. For example, FITS Xu et al. (2024) incorporates
139   a low-pass filter in the frequency domain to capture essential time series information, but this in-
140
      evitably results in the loss of high-frequency components. This bias can hinder the model’s ability
      to fully capture the intricate dynamics present across all temporal scales, potentially impacting the
141
      accuracy and robustness of forecasts.
142
143   Fredformer Piao et al. (2024) aims to alleviate frequency bias by equally learning features across
144   different frequency bands. This approach helps prevent the model from overlooking lower amplitude
145   features crucial for accurate predictions. In these previous studies, attention layers are designed to
146
      function directly in the frequency domain to enhance spatial or frequency representations.
147   Transformer-based models for long-term time series forecasting (LTSF) have primarily advanced
148   through two approaches. Time-domain models focus on enhancing attention mechanisms and em-
149   ploying patch-based training strategies. In contrast, frequency-domain models develop specialized
150   filters and address biases between high and low-frequency components to improve performance.
151   However, these methods mainly concentrate on updating and iterating Transformer architectures
152
      without deeply considering the inherent input properties of time series data and their impact on at-
      tention mechanisms, making it difficult to effectively mitigate high feature confusion. In the follow-
153
      ing sections, we provide a mathematical definition of this issue and introduce our SEAT framework
154
      to address it.

185   In the following, we define two primary attention mechanisms.
186
187
      Point-wise Attention (Temporal Attention) Definition: Point-wise attention, also known as tem-
      poral attention, is a mechanism within deep learning architectures that assigns significance scores to
188
      individual data points or temporal instances within a sequence. This approach enables the model to
189
      dynamically adjust its focus on specific points in time, capturing nuances and salient features that
190   may be crucial for downstream tasks. By selectively attending to these key points, point-wise atten-
191   tion enhances the model’s ability to comprehend complex temporal patterns and dynamics within the
192   data. Notably, the effectiveness of this approach has been demonstrated in seminal works (Ashish
193   (2017); Hu et al. (2018); Nie et al. (2023)).
194
      Channel-wise Attention Definition: Channel-wise attention, on the other hand, emphasizes the
195
      significance of individual feature channels within a multidimensional tensor. In this framework, each
196   channel represents a unique feature map capturing distinct aspects of the input data. Channel-wise
197   attention aims to dynamically reweight these feature channels, allowing the network to concentrate
198   its representational power on the most informative and discriminative channels. Specifically, for
199   time series data, each variable token is embedded into a high-dimensional space, where channel-
200   wise attention operates to capture intricate multivariate correlations. By highlighting the channels
201   that are most relevant to the task at hand, this mechanism enhances the overall feature representation,
202   leading to improved performance in tasks such as classification, regression, or forecasting. Notable
203   papers have highlighted the effectiveness of channel-wise attention (Wang et al. (2017); Woo et al.
204
      (2018); Liu et al. (2024)).
205   Compared to “Point-wise Attention” features, using multivariate data as tokens at the same time
206   step results in naturally similar tokens for adjacent time steps. Although the sampling is discrete,
207   the time series varies continuously, leading to many similar features that yield high similarity scores.
208   Sparse feature representation facilitates the computation of a sparse attention matrix. This means
209   that the attentionPmatrix of size N ∗ N needs to meet a specified minimal error threshold ϵ > 0,

212
      and independent feature sets yield a smaller Sim value, indicating that sparse features exhibit lower
      similarity compared to dense features.

236   3.3   F OURIER D OMAIN OF THE T IME S ERIES
237
238   The key to effectively addressing the challenge of block-wise attention lies in the ability of time-
239   frequency transformation to impart sparse representations to time-series data. In this context, we
240
      will embark on a rigorous mathematical derivation to prove that long time-series data exhibit sparse
      representations under frequency domain transformations. Furthermore, we posit that by integrating
241
      feature partitioning into patches with frequency domain transformation, we can achieve an even
242
      more sparse representation of time-series data than either method alone.
243
244   Theorem 1: Sparse Representation in the Frequency Domain Given a signal x(t) of time series
245   data in L2 space, if its Fourier transform X(f ) is supported on a finite set of frequencies, then x(t)
246
      has a sparse representation in the frequency domain.
247   The Nyquist Sampling Theorem (Vaidyanathan (2001)), also known as the Shannon Sampling The-
248   orem, fundamentally establishes the conditions under which a continuous-time signal can be accu-
249   rately reconstructed from its discrete-time samples, ensuring that the Discrete Fourier Transform
250   (DFT) yields equivalent results to the Continuous Fourier Transform (CFT) within the context of
251   the sampled signal’s representation. We assume that the sampled data of the time series satisfies the
252
      sampling theorem. This implies that the sampling frequency fs used to acquire the discrete samples
      x[n] of the original continuous signal x(t) is sufficiently high, specifically fs ≥ 2B, where B is the
253
      maximum frequency content of the signal’s spectrum X(f ).
254
255   Furthermore, we assume that the signals we acquire adhere to the finite bandwidth theorem, which
256   states that the spectral content of the signal is confined within a limited frequency range. Given
257   that the signals we collect are inherently discrete, due to the nature of the sampling process, it is
258
      crucial to consider the appropriate frequency resolution for the specific prediction task at hand. For
      instance, in tasks involving daily analysis, frequency features at the minute level may be deemed
259
      irrelevant or treated as noise. To ensure compliance with the finite bandwidth principle in practical
260
      sampling scenarios, we can adopt suitable sampling strategies that capture the signal within a rea-
261   sonable frequency bandwidth range, thereby facilitating effective and efficient data processing for
262   the intended prediction tasks.
263
264
      Proof of Theorem 1: Given the assumptions mentioned above, we can deduce that in our long time-
      series analysis tasks, where the sampling theorem is satisfied, the use of the Discrete Fourier Trans-
265
      form (DFT) becomes equivalent to the Continuous Fourier Transform (CFT) for solving time-series
266
      problems. Thus, for these tasks, we can confidently employ the DFT as a valid and computationally
267   efficient tool for analyzing and processing time-series data.

305
306   Normalization Layer: Reversible instance normalization (RevIN)Kim et al. (2021), a generally

308
      known and widely used as the normalization layer. For better comparison of different models abili-

309
310   SEAT Block: We drew a precise schematic diagram of the SEAT block structure in Fig 1. This
311   novel framework is designed to induce sparsity in time series data through frequency domain trans-

313   compatible with any underlying attention architecture. By focusing on sparsity induction via fre-
314
      quency domain transformations, SEAT is specifically designed to enhance the overall performance
      of attention mechanisms from a new perspective in the input stage.
315
316   Feature Extraction Layer: This is a temporal feature extractor specifically based on transformer ar-
317   chitecture since we designed SEAT to enhance the ac- curacy and robustness of attention mechanism.
