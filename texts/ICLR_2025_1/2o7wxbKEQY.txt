009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph outlier detection aims to identify anomalous substructures in graphs that
012            deviate significantly from normal patterns. Traditional methods primarily focus
013            on static graphs, overlooking the dynamic nature of real-world networks and ig-
014            noring valuable temporal signals crucial for outlier detection. While Transformers
015            have revolutionized machine learning on time-series data, existing Transformers
016            for temporal graphs face limitations in (1) restricted receptive fields, (2) over-
017            head of subgraph extraction, and (3) suboptimal generalization capability be-
018
               yond link prediction. In this paper, we propose TGTOD, a novel end-to-end
               Temporal Graph Transformer for Outlier Detection. TGTOD employs global
019
               attention to model both structural and temporal dependencies within temporal
020
               graphs. To tackle scalability, our approach divides large temporal graphs into
021            spatiotemporal patches, which are then processed by a hierarchical Transformer
022            architecture comprising Patch Transformer, Cluster Transformer, and Temporal
023            Transformer. We evaluate TGTOD on three public datasets under two settings,
024            comparing with a wide range of baselines. Our experimental results demon-
025            strate the effectiveness of TGTOD, achieving AP improvement of 61% on El-
026            liptic dataset. Furthermore, our efficiency evaluation shows that TGTOD re-
027            duces training time by 44× compared to existing Transformers for temporal
028            graphs. To foster reproducibility, we make our implementation publicly available
029
               at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/tgtod.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Outlier detection, a critical task in machine learning, aims to identify data points that significantly
034
      deviate from normal patterns. This task has become increasingly important in various domains,
035
      including fraud detection (Huang et al., 2022), anti-money laundering (Weber et al., 2019), and
036   misinformation identification (Dou et al., 2021). Graph-structured data, which represents complex
037   relationships as nodes connected by edges, has been widely adopted across numerous fields, such as
038   citation networks (Zhao et al., 2020), social networks (Dou et al., 2021), and molecular structures
039   (Wu et al., 2024a). Graph outlier detection focuses on identifying anomalous substructures within
040   graphs. The inherent complexity of graph data, due to its non-Euclidean nature, makes this task
041   particularly challenging. Traditionally, graph outlier detection methods have primarily focused on
042   static graphs, where the structure and attributes remain constant over time. However, real-world
043   graphs are often dynamic, evolving over time and providing rich temporal information that can
044
      be crucial for detecting outliers. Current static graph methods fall short when applied to temporal
      graphs, as they fail to capture the important temporal aspects necessary for effective outlier detection.
045
046   Recently, Transformers have revolutionized machine learning on language (Vaswani et al., 2017;
047   Huang et al., 2024b) and vision (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) with their powerful ability to model
048   complex dependencies in data through self-attention mechanisms. Unlike traditional recurrent neu-
049   ral network architectures that rely heavily on sequential processing, Transformers utilize attention
050
      mechanisms to weigh the importance of different parts of the input data, allowing them to capture
      long-range dependencies effectively and efficiently. This self-attention mechanism in Transformers
051
      offers a promising direction by integrating temporal dynamics into the graph representation learning.
052
053   Although some recent efforts have adapted Transformers to temporal graphs, challenges remain.
      DyGFormer extracts one-hop interactions and feeds their neighbor, link, time, and co-occurrence

      encoding into a Transformer to capture temporal edges between nodes (Yu et al., 2023). Similarly,
055   SimpleDyG also models neighbors in temporal graphs as a sequence and introduces a temporal
056   alignment technique to capture temporal evolution patterns (Wu et al., 2024b). Despite their poten-
057   tial, existing methods using Transformers on temporal graph face significant limitations.
058
059   • Limited receptive field: Current methods typically extract only one-hop neighboring subgraphs,
060     restricting Transformers’ receptive field and overlooking long-range spatiotemporal dependencies.
061   • Training inefficiency: These approaches often rely on subgraphs extraction for each edge (Hamil-
062     ton et al., 2017), leading to significant computational overhead and limiting the training efficiency.
063   • Task misalignment: Existing Transformers are pretrained on link prediction, which may cause
064     suboptimal generalization capability to node-level outlier detection due to mismatch of objectives.
065
066   To address these limitations, we propose TGTOD, a novel paradigm to apply Transformers on
067
      temporal graphs. (Novelty 1) We explore adopting global spatiotemporal attention on the entire
      temporal graph for node-level outlier detection. It allows TGTOD to capture not only the local
068
      spatiotemporal dependencies between temporal neighbors but also the global spatiotemporal depen-
069
      dencies across the entire graph and multiple timestamps. However, due to the quadratic complexity
070   of query-key multiplication in the attention mechanism, direct global spatiotemporal attention is
071   computationally infeasible for large-scale temporal graphs. (Novelty 2) Inspired by visual patching
072   method used in video model (Brooks et al., 2024), we divide the large temporal graph into spa-
073   tiotemporal patches to alleviate the challenge of scalability. For the spatial aspect, we use a graph
074   clustering algorithm to partition the large graph into relatively small clusters. For the temporal as-
075   pect, we aggregate the interactions within each cluster into a snapshot with a specific time interval.
076   These patches are then fed into a hierarchical Transformer, which includes Patch Transformer, Clus-
077   ter Transformer, and Temporal Transformer. Through this approach, TGTOD significantly reduces
078
      the complexity of attention mechanisms while preserving global spatiotemporal receptive fields.
      (Novelty 3) Furthermore, unlike existing Transformers on temporal graphs pretrained on link pre-
079
      diction task, TGTOD is trained end-to-end on node-level outlier detection, ensuring better alignment
080
      with the downstream task and thus providing stronger generalization capability. Our contributions
081   in this work are summarized as follows:
082
083   • Global attention: We propose TGTOD, making the first attempt to leverage global spatiotemporal
084     attention for Transformers on temporal graphs for end-to-end node-level outlier detection.
085   • Spatiotemporal patching: We introduce a spatiotemporal patching method, which significantly
086     improves the scalability of TGTOD and enables outlier detection on large-scale temporal graphs.
087   • Evaluation: Analysis and experiments demonstrate the effectiveness and efficiency of TGTOD.

097   2.1   G RAPH O UTLIER D ETECTION
098
099
      Graph outlier detection is an essential task in machine learning, aimed at identifying anomalous
      structures within graphs. Due to its structural simplicity and broader applicability, most research fo-
100
      cuses on node-level outlier detection, which can be extended to edge-level and graph-level tasks (Liu
101
      et al., 2024). A significant body of work focuses on unsupervised approaches, where outliers are de-
102   tected based solely on the data without ground truth labels. For instance, DOMINANT (Ding et al.,
103   2019) adopts a graph autoencoder (Kipf & Welling, 2016b) to reconstruct the input graph and iden-
104   tify anomalies based on the reconstruction error. CoLA (Liu et al., 2021b) detects anomalous nodes
105   using contrastive learning (Ma et al., 2024). However, these unsupervised methods may fall short
106   in scenarios requiring the identification of specific outliers with domain knowledge. In such cases,
107   (semi-)supervised graph outlier detectors, which can learn from ground truth labels, are more suit-
      able. For example, GAS (Li et al., 2019) employs an attention mechanism to detect spam reviews.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Studies like PCGNN (Liu et al., 2021a) conduct message passing in selected neighborhoods. Ad-
109   ditionally, GATSep (Zimek et al., 2014) separates representation learning from anomaly detection.
110   BWGNN (Tang et al., 2022) discerns both low-frequency and high-frequency signals, adaptively
111   integrating them across various frequencies. GHRN (Gao et al., 2023) addresses heterophily from a
112   graph spectrum perspective. Extensive works explore data augmentation (Liu et al., 2022a; 2023),
113   active learning (Chang et al., 2024a), and fairness (Chang et al., 2024b) on graph outlier detection.
114   Despite the proliferation of graph outlier detection methods, few consider the crucial aspect of time.
115   In this work, we bridge this gap by capturing temporal information via Transformers.
116
117   2.2   T EMPORAL G RAPH L EARNING
118
119
      Temporal graph learning has gained significant attention due to its ability to model dynamic rela-
      tionships in real-world networks. For instance, EvolveGCN (Pareja et al., 2020) adapts the weights
120
      of graph neural networks over time using recurrent neural networks. TGN (Rossi et al., 2020) uses
121
      memory modules to capture long-term dependencies in temporal graphs. TGAT (Xu et al., 2020)
122   adopts a self-attention mechanism and develops a functional time encoding technique. GraphMixer
123   (Cong et al., 2023b) incorporates the fixed time encoding function into a link encoder to learn from
124   temporal links. TGB (Huang et al., 2024a) provides a temporal graph benchmark for machine learn-
125   ing on temporal graphs. Despite these advancements, most existing temporal graph networks are
126   trained on link level, e.g., for link prediction tasks. When the task is at the node level, e.g., node-level
127   outlier detection, a node-level decoder is trained on the top of the frozen encoder. This two-stage
128   training scheme may suffer from suboptimal generalization capability to node-level outlier detec-
129   tion. In addition, these methods require temporal subgraph extraction for each edge during training,
130
      leading to limited receptive fields and high computational overhead. Our work aims to address these
      limitations by leveraging the power of Transformers for temporal graph learning.
131
132
133   2.3   G RAPH T RANSFORMERS
134
      Graph Transformers have emerged as a powerful approach for learning on graph data, combining
135   the strengths of graph neural networks and Transformer architectures. These models aim to over-
136   come limitations of traditional graph neural networks, such as over-smoothing and limited receptive
137   fields, by leveraging the attention mechanisms of Transformers. Early work in this area includes
138   Graphormer (Ying et al., 2021), which introduces centrality encoding, spatial encoding, and edge
139   encoding to improve graph level representation. At the node level, NodeFormer (Wu et al., 2022)
140   enables efficient computation via kernerlized Gumbel-Softmax, reduced the algorithmic complex-
141   ity to linear. DIFFormer (Wu et al., 2023) proposes a graph Transformer with energy constrained
142   diffusion. SGFormer (Wu et al., 2019) further improves the scalability of graph Transformers to
143
      handle large graphs more efficiently. CoBFormer (Xing et al., 2024) adopts bi-level architecture in
      global graph Transformers to alleviate over-globalization problem and improve the efficiency. These
144
      methods lay solid foundation for our work. Our work extends the global attention beyond node
145
      level to both spatial and temporal aspects on temporal graphs. Although some efforts have adapted
146   Transformers to temporal graphs, existing methods inherit the problems of temporal graph network
147   methods. SimpleDyG (Wu et al., 2024b) and DyGFormer (Yu et al., 2023) model one-hop temporal
148   neighbors as a sequence and feed their encodings into Transformer to capture temporal edges. While
149   these methods have shown promising results in temporal link prediction tasks, their application to
150   node-level outlier detection remains limited. In this paper, we rethink the use of Transformers on
151   temporal graphs and conduct global spatiotemporal attention on the entire temporal graph.

239   perform global spatiotemporal attention across the entire temporal graph. To achieve this with

      3.5   E ND - TO -E ND O UTLIER D ETECTION T RAINING
287
288   Existing methods (Yu et al., 2023) on temporal graphs handle node-level tasks (e.g., node classifica-
289   tion) in a two-stage manner. The temporal graph encoder is first pretrained on link prediction task in
290   an unsupervised manner (i.e., without node labels) to learn a general embedding for each node, and
291   a decoder specifically designed for node-level task is trained on the top of the obtained embedding
292   in a supervised manner (i.e., with node labels). This two-stage training pipeline separates the repre-
293   sentation learning from the specific task, which may result in suboptimal generalization capability
294   to node-level outlier detection due to the mismatch in objectives.
295   Different from the above methods, TGTOD can be trained end-to-end for node-level tasks, e.g.,
296   node-level outlier detection. It enables direct optimization of the outlier detector objective during

298   level outlier detection. Moreover, this end-to-end training paradigm in TGTOD avoids the need for
299   separate pretraining and downstream training stages, leading to a more efficient training process. In

313   3.6   C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
314
315   TGTOD aims to perform global spatiotemporal attention across the entire temporal graph while
316   maintaining manageable computational complexity. Here, we analyze the reduction in computa-
317   tional complexity achieved by each component of TGTOD. In the original attention mechanism,
318   the multiplication of query and key matrices leads to quadratic complexity. Considering each node
319   at each timestamp as a token, we have N T tokens in total, where N is the number of nodes and
320
      T is the number of timestamps. The complexity of direct global spatiotemporal attention is thus
      Ω(Attn) = N 2 T 2 .
321
322   By separating Spatial Transformer and Temporal Transformer, we prioritize the more important and
323   denser attention across time within patches in the same position, while omitting the less critical and
      sparser attention between patches in different positions and time slots. This reduces the complexity


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      to N 2 +T 2 . For Spatial Transformer, we partition the graph with N nodes into C clusters, where the
325   average cluster size is M = N/C. Separating PFormer and CFormer further reduces the complexity
326   to M 2 + C 2 + T 2 . Additionally, the approximation in Patch Transformer reduces its complexity
327   from quadratic to linear, resulting in a final complexity of M + C 2 + T 2 .
328
      To illustrate the significance of this reduction, consider a temporal graph with N = 106 nodes, T =
329
      1000 timestamps, and C = 1000 clusters. The complexity of direct global spatiotemporal attention
330
      would be 1,000,000,000,000,000,000, while the complexity of TGTOD is reduced to 2,001,000,
331   representing a substantial improvement in computational efficiency.

       5         C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
473
474    By rethinking the application of temporal graph Transformers for outlier detection, in this study,
475    we present TGTOD, making the first attempt to leverage global spatiotemporal attention for outlier
476    detection in temporal graphs. Our method significantly improves scalability through spatiotemporal
477    patching while preserving a global receptive field, enabling effective end-to-end outlier detection
478    on large-scale temporal graphs. Through comprehensive analysis and experiments on real-world
479    datasets, we demonstrate that TGTOD not only outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods in
480    detection performance but also exhibits superior computational efficiency.
481    This work establishes a new benchmark in temporal graph outlier detection and opens up promis-
482    ing avenues for future research. Potential further explorations include extending TGTOD and spa-
483    tiotemporal patching to broader temporal graph learning tasks beyond outlier detection, as well as
484    pretraining it as a foundational model for various downstream tasks. We believe these research di-
485    rections will foster the development of more effective and scalable algorithms capable of managing
       the complex spatiotemporal dynamics inherent in real-world graph data.

675   B     ATTENTION M ECHANISM IN T RANSFORMERS
676
677   In this section, we introduce two types of attention mechanisms used in TGTOD, including vanilla
678   attention in Section B.1 and an attention approximation with linear complexity in Section B.2.
679
680   B.1     VANILLA ATTENTION
681
682   The attention mechanism is a core component of Transformers, introduced by (Vaswani et al., 2017).
683   Its primary purpose is to enable the model to focus on different parts of the input sequence. The
684
      attention mechanism models the dependencies across the entire sequence regardless of their distance,
      which is essential for handling long-range dependencies in sequence data.
685
686   The Transformer utilizes a self-attention mechanism, also known as scaled dot-product attention,
687   which computes attention scores between every pair of tokens in the input sequence. The inputs to
688   the attention mechanism are three matrices: Queries (Q), Keys (K), and Values (V ), all of which
689   are linear projections of the input embeddings. These projections are defined as:
690                                   Q = XWQ ,      K = XWK ,       V = XWV                              (4)
691
692   where X ∈ Rn×d is the input sequence of n tokens, each with dimension d, and WQ , WK , WV ∈
693   Rd×dk are learned projection matrices for the queries, keys, and values, respectively, with dk denot-
694   ing the dimensionality of the queries and keys.
695
      The attention weights are computed as the scaled dot product of the queries and keys:
696
                                         QK ⊺                    XWQ (XWK )⊺

697
                    Attn(X) = softmax √           V = softmax           √            XWV                  (5)
698                                         dk                            dk
699
700   where √1d is a scaling factor. The softmax function is applied to the dot products to normalize the
                k
701   attention weights, ensuring they sum to 1. The weights indicate how much focus each query should
      place on each value, enabling the model to weigh different parts of the input sequence differently.

      This attention mechanism is extended to Multi-Head Attention (MHA). Rather than computing a
703   single set of attention scores, the model projects the queries, keys, and values into h different sub-
704   spaces, each of dimensionality dk , and computes independent attention heads:
705
706       MHA(Q, K, V ) = concat(head1 , . . . , headh )WO ,       headi = Attn(QWQi , KWKi , V WVi )          (6)
707
708   and WO ∈ Rhdk ×d is the output projection matrix. The multi-head mechanism allows the model
709   to attend to different parts of the input sequence from different representational subspaces, thereby
710
      enhancing its capacity to capture diverse dependencies.
711   Despite the powerness of attention mechanism, its quadratic complexity of query-key multiplication
712   in Equation 5 limits the scalability of the model. In Appendix B.2, we will introduce an attention
713   approximation to reduce the complexity to linear.
714
715   B.2    L INEAR ATTENTION A PPROXIMATION
716
717   Wu et al. (2022) introduces a novel approach to reduce the computational complexity of attention on
718   large graphs, reducing the complexity of attention mechanism from quadratic to linear. Specifically,
719   a kernelized Gumbel-Softmax operator is employed to approximate the all-pair attention. The key
720   idea is to transform the dot-product attention into a kernel function, which can then be efficiently
721   approximated using random feature maps. In this attention mechanism, the message-passing at node
722
      level can be expressed as:
723                                           N

                                              X

727   where WQ , WK , and WV are the learnable parameters for the queries, keys, and values, respec-
728   tively, in the l-th layer, and κ(·, ·) is a pairwise similarity function (i.e., the kernel function). Instead
729   of computing the full dot-product attention, we approximates the kernel function κ(a, b) using ran-
730   dom features map as proposed by Rahimi & Recht (2007). The approximation is given by:
731

733
      where ϕ(·) is a random feature map that approximates the kernel. The dot product between ϕ(a) and

      ϕ(b) is significantly faster to compute than the original dot-then-exponentiate operation, reducing
735   the overall complexity from O(N 2 ) to O(N ).

738   C     D ETAILED D ESCRIPTIONS OF DATASETS
739
740   DGraph (Huang et al., 2022): DGraph is a large-scale graph dataset provided by Finvolution Group,
741   including around 3 million nodes, 4 million dynamic edges, and 1 million node labels. The nodes
742   represent user accounts within a financial organization that offers personal loan services, with edges
743   indicating that one account has designated the other as an emergency contact. Nodes labeled as
744   fraud correspond to users exhibiting delinquent financial behavior. For accounts with borrowing
745   records, outliers are accounts with a history of overdue payments, while inliers are those without
746
      such a history. The dataset includes 17 node features derived from user profile information.
747   Elliptic (Weber et al., 2019): This graph dataset contains over 200,000 Bitcoin transaction nodes,
748   234,000 directed payment flow edges, and 165 dimensional node features. The dataset maps Bitcoin
749   transactions to real-world entities, categorizing them into both licit categories, including exchanges,
750   wallet providers, miners, and legal services, and illicit categories, such as scams, malware, terrorist
751   organizations, ransomware, and Ponzi schemes.
752   FiGraph (Wang, 2024): This dataset presents a temporagph graph for financial anomaly detection.
753   Spanning from 2014 to 2022, FiGraph captures the dynamics of financial interactions through 9
754   distinct temporal snapshots. The graph consists of 730,408 nodes and 1,040,997 edges. It has five
755   types of nodes and four types of edges. The dataset focus on target nodes with features, while the
      background nodes without features provide structure information for anomaly detection.

      In this section, we provide a detailed description of the metrics we used in this paper for the evalua-
759   tion of both effectiveness and efficiency.
760
761
      D.1    E FFECTIVENESS M ETRICS
762
763   AUC: area under receiver operating characteristic curve, which is constructed by plotting the true
764   positive rate against the false positive rate across varied determined threshold levels. An AUC of
765   1 indicates perfect prediction, while an AUC of 0.5 suggests the model cannot distinguish between
766   classes. AUC is preferable to accuracy for evaluating outlier detection tasks because it is not affected
767   by imbalanced class distributions.
768   AP: average precision, also known as area under precision-recall curve, summarizes the precision-
769   recall curve by calculating the weighted mean of precision values at each threshold, where the weight
770   corresponds to the increase in recall from the previous threshold. As a metric that balances both
771   recall and precision. In most outlier detection applications, FPR and FNR are both important.
772
      Rec@k: Outliers are typically rare compared to the large number of normal samples, but they are the
773   primary focus in outlier detection. We propose to use Recall@k to assess how effectively detectors
774   rank outliers relative to normal samples. Here, k is set to the number of outliers in labels. Recall@k
775   is the number of true outliers among the top-k samples in the ranking list, divided by k.
776
777   D.2    E FFICIENCY M ETRICS
778
779   Number of parameters: the total count of learnable parameters in the model. This metric pro-
780   vides insight into the model’s complexity. A lower parameter count indicates a more efficient
781   model. In implementation, we count the number of parameters with numel() method for each
782
      torch.nn.Module.parameters() in the model.
783   Training time: the time required to train the model for one epoch using the maximum batch size that
784   fits within the 40GB GPU memory constraints of NVIDIA A100. For methods employing two-stage
785   training, the total training time is calculated as the sum of the training times for both stages.
786   Memory consumption: the peak main memory usage of the method during training on the given
787   dataset, measured when using CPU only. This metric provides insight into the method’s memory
788   efficiency, which is particularly important for large-scale graph processing tasks.
