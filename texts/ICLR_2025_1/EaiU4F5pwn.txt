009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Machine learning (ML) models are increasingly explored in fluid dynamics as a
012            promising way to generate high-fidelity computational fluid dynamics data more
013            efficiently. A common strategy is to use low-fidelity data as computational-
014            efficient inputs, and employ ML techniques to reconstruct high-fidelity flow
015            fields. However, existing work typically assumes that low-fidelity data is artifi-
016            cially downsampled from high-fidelity sources, which limits model performance.
017            In real-world applications, low-fidelity data is generated directly by numerical
018
               solvers with a lower initial state resolution, resulting in large deviations from
               high-fidelity data. To address this gap, we propose PG-Diff, a novel diffusion
019
               model for reconstructing high-fidelity flow fields, where both low- and high-
020
               fidelity data are generated from numerical solvers. Our experiments reveal that
021            state-of-the-art models struggle to recover fine-grained high-fidelity details when
022            using solver-generated low-fidelity inputs, due to distribution shift. To overcome
023            this challenge, we introduce an Importance Weight strategy during training as
024            self-guidance and a training-free Residual Correction method during inference
025            as physical inductive bias, guiding the diffusion model toward higher-quality re-
026            constructions. Experiments on four 2D turbulent flow datasets demonstrate the
027            effectiveness of our proposed method. Code and further details are available here.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   High-fidelity simulations of computational fluid dynamics (CFD) are crucial for understanding fluid
033
      interactions in engineering systems, greatly impacting design and application outcomes (Wang et al.,
      2024; McGreivy & Hakim, 2024). Traditional approaches such as Direct Numerical Simulation
034
      (DNS) (Orszag, 1970) offer high-resolution solutions. However, they are computationally expen-
035
      sive, especially for complex dynamics such as turbulence with high Reynolds numbers (Zhang et al.,
036   2023). Therefore, learning neural-based simulators from data become attractive alternatives, balanc-
037   ing between efficiency and simulation fidelity (Huang et al., 2023).
038
039
      One popular strategy is to reconstruct high-fidelity data from low-fidelity inputs, which usually
      reduces the discretization grid size in the spatial domain to improve computational efficiency (Shu
040
      et al., 2023; Pradhan & Duraisamy, 2021). Various machine learning models, including those based
041
      on Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) (Fukami et al., 2019), Generative Adversarial Networks
042   (GANs) (Li & McComb, 2022), and Diffusion Models (Shu et al., 2023), have been developed to
043   reconstruct high-fidelity CFD data from low-fidelity inputs. Majority of them are categorized as
044   direct mapping models, which require both low- and high-fidelity data for training and can only
045   capture the relationship between particular low-fidelity and high-fidelity pairs. In contrast, diffusion
046   models only require high-fidelity data during training and can reconstruct from out-of-distribution
047   low-fidelity data during inference, by treating them as intermediate samples in the denoising stage.
048   Motivation: one fundamental drawback in existing studies is that they assume low-fidelity data is ar-
049   tificially downsampled from high-fidelity data at the same timestamp. Such data inherently has more
050   information compared to solver-generated low-fidelity data in reality, where coarser discretization
051   grids are used in numerical solvers for saving computational resources. As illustrated in Figure 1,
052   the former follows “integrate then downsample”, which starts from the high-fidelity initial states to
053   rollout trajectories, and then downsample at each timestamp. The latter follows “downsample then
      integrate”, which downsamples the high-fidelity initial state to obtain coarser discretization grids

      2.1   AI FOR C OMPUTATIONAL F LUID DYNAMICS (CFD)
113
114   Recent advances in machine learning have led to various learning-based surrogate models for accel-
115   erating scientific discoveries (Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; 2021). In the field of
116   high-fidelity CFD reconstruction, researchers have developed powerful models rooted from image
117   super-resolution domain in computer vision. Specifically, GANs (Ledig et al., 2016; Wang et al.,
118   2019) and normalizing flows (Lugmayr et al., 2020) have achieved impressive results for image re-
119   construction. Later on, diffusion models (Saharia et al., 2021) have challenged the long-standing
120   dominance of GANs. Various physical inductive biases (Raissi et al., 2019; Bai et al., 2020; Chen
121   et al., 2021) have been injected into these methods for CFD reconstructions, which enhances the
122
      robustness and accuracy of the reconstruction task. For example, (Erichson et al., 2020; Pradhan
      & Duraisamy, 2021) developed super-resolution models based on Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs).
123
      Fukami et al. (2019) introduced a CNN-based hybrid Downsampled Skip-Connection Multi-Scale
124
      (DSC/MS) model, while Fukami et al. (2021) adapted existing CNN models for use with moving
125   sensors. Li & McComb (2022) further advanced the field by proposing physics-informed GANs
126   for super-resolving multiphase fluid simulations. Additionally, Fu et al. (2023) proposed a porposal
127   and refinement network to address the issue with limited high-fidelity data. Ren et al. (2023); Jiang
128   et al. (2020) also leveraged CNN for spatial-temporal super resolution. These models rely on low-
129   and high-fidelity data pairs during training and can only capture specific relationships between these
130   pairs. As a result, when the test data deviates significantly from the training data, their performance
131   degrades. To overcome this limitation, Gao et al. (2021) utilizes physical properties of the fluid
132   such as conservation laws and boundary conditions for super resolution. Shu et al. (2023) lever-
133
      aged diffusion model, which is trained exclusively on high-fidelity data and enables reconstruction
      from any type of low-fidelity input. However, these methods assume the low-fidelity data is arti-
134
      ficially downsampled from high-fidelity sources, which limits their performance during inference
135
      when reconstructing from solver-generated low-fidelity data in reality.

140

      We present a novel diffusion framework PG-Diff for reconstructing high-fidelity CFD from solver-
197
      generated low-fidelity input, which has larger distribution shifts and more information loss compared
198
      to artificially downsampled low-fidelity inputs. PG-Diff features an Importance Weight strategy that
199   scores different components in the flow fields through the loss function as self-guidance, forcing
200   the model to recover more fine-grained high-fidelity details during training. In addition, a training-
201   free Residual Correction module applies physics-informed correction during 2inference, ensuring
                                                                 2
202   physical coherence in reconstructed samples. The two moduli jointly guide the model towards high-
203   quality reconstruction from wide-range of low-fidelity inputs. The overall framework is depicted in
204   Figure 2. We now introduce each component in detail.

208   control over the data generation process (reverse diffusion) during inference. Instead of starting from
209   random noises, starting from low-fidelity inputs as intermediate diffusion steps; and 2.) reconstruct
210   from any form of low-fidelity data, as the training does not depend on low- and high-fidelity pairs.

268   coherence of the reconstructed data during inference. In the reverse diffusion process, we apply such

274   steps at which the correction is applied), the number of gradient descent steps N , and the step size
275   η, which we study their impacts in Exp 4.5. Our findings suggest that applying residual corrections
276   at both the beginning and the end of diffusion steps strikes the ideal balance between reconstruction
277   L2 error and the physical coherence. We utilize the Adam algorithm Kingma & Ba (2015) for the
278   gradient descent and summarize the physics-guided inference procedure using DDIM (Song et al.,
279
      2020) in Algorithm 1.
280   One existing work proposes a conditional diffusion model (Shu et al., 2023), where residual at every
281   training diffusion step is concatenated with the current state to generate the diffusion state at next
282   timestamp, serving as the condition. One key advantage of PG-Diff is that it is training-free, allow-
283   ing the correction process to be applied without additional learning to the original diffusion model.
284   Also, our approach offers the flexibility to adjust the residual correction schedule dynamically. This
285
      adaptability enables an optimal balance between predictive accuracy and physical consistency of
      the reconstructed sample. We empirically show our method achieves better performance in Sec 4.3.
286
      Additionally, diffusion model guided generation has been widely studied in Chung et al. (2023);
287
      Huang et al. (2024); Zhu et al. (2023); Shysheya et al. (2024), our Residual Correction differs by ap-
288   plying guidance to denoised samples instead of noisy samples. Our physical guidance also involves
289   multiple steps of Adam gradient descent at selected backward diffusion steps, in contrast to a single
290   step of gradient descent applied at every backward diffusion step in other works.

      4.1   DATASET
316
317
      We generated four 2D turbulent flow datasets with different characteristics: 1.) Taylor Green Vortex,
318   featuring how vortices diminish in turbulent flows, where large-scale vortices gradually break down
319   into smaller turbulent structures; 2.) Decaying Turbulence, describing turbulence that evolves natu-
320   rally without external forces. As time progresses, the turbulence weakens due to viscous effects; 3.)
321   Kolmogorov Flow, which portrays turbulence influenced by a sinusoidal external force combined
322   with a drag component; 4.) McWilliams Flow (Mcwilliams, 1984), which describes the behavior
323   of isolated vortices in turbulent conditions. It is the most challenging one, demanding accurate
      modeling of inverse energy transfer and multi-scale vortex interactions.

      The four datasets are generated by the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations following (Li et al.,
325   2021; Kochkov et al., 2021)
326
327                         ∂ω(x, t)                         1 2
                                     + u(x, t) · ∇ω(x, t) =    ∇ ω(x, t) + f (x),
328                           ∂t                            Re
329                                           ∇ · u(x, t) = 0, ω(x, 0) = ω0 (x),
330
      where ω represents the vorticity, u denotes the velocity field, Re is the Reynolds number, and
331
      f (x) is an external forcing term. ω0 represents the initial vorticity distribution. The PDE is nu-
332   merically solved by pseudo-spectral solver (Orszag, 1972) on equispaced discretization grids. The
333   high-fidelity data are generated with 2048 × 2048 discretization grid and then uniformly downsam-
334   pled to 256 × 256, while those on the lower-resolution grids are considered low-fidelity. For each
335   dataset, we use 80% of the trajectories for training. 10% for validation, and 10% for testing. More
336   details can be found in Appendix D. Note that PG-Diff also utilizes the PDE for residual correction
337   discussed in Sec 3.2.

339   4.2   E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS
340
341   Task Setup and Baselines. We evaluate two reconstruction settings with different low-fidelity res-
342   olutions: 64 × 64 → 256 × 256 (4× upsampling) and 32 × 32 → 256 × 256 (8× upsampling).
343
      To benchmark our approach, we compared against both direct mapping models and diffusion mod-
      els: bicubic interpolation (Gonzalez & Woods, 2007), a CNN-based model (Fukami et al., 2019),
344
      a GAN-based model (Li & McComb, 2022), the vanilla diffusion model (Diff) and its conditional
345
      variant (Cond Diff) from Shu et al. (2023). In addition, we perform two ablation studies, namely PG-
346   Diff w/o IW and PG-Diff w/o Cor , where we remove Importance Weight and Residual Correction
347   respectively. More implementation details are provided in Appendix C.2.
348
349
      Evaluation Metrics. We assess the reconstructed flow fields using three key metrics. We first
      use two standard metrics suggested by Shu et al. (2023): L2 norm for measuring the pointwise
350
      error between prediction and ground truth; unnormalized residuals of the governing equation (Res.)
351
      for assessing adherence to the underlying physics. In addition, we conduct a novel multi-scale
352   evaluation using DWT: we transform the predicted and ground truth flow fields into wavelet space
353   and decompose them into four subdomains: LL (low-low), LH (low-high), HL (high-low), and HH
354   (high-high). The LL subdomain captures large-scale, low-frequency information, while LH, HL,
355   and HH encompass higher-frequency details like turbulent structures. By calculating the L2 norm
356   in each subdomain, we gain a comprehensive understanding of the model’s performance across
357   different scales, ensuring accurate reconstruction of both global flow features and fine-scale details.

814   The Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT) decomposes an image I ∈ Rn×n into different frequency
815   components by successively applying low-pass and high-pass filters. Given an image I, the DWT

844   C.2   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
845
846   We implement all models in PyTorch and show their implementation details below.
847   Bicubic Bicubic interpolation is a widely used image scaling technique that enhances the resolution
848   of low-resolution images by using a weighted average of pixels in a 4x4 neighborhood around each
849   pixel. Bicubic interpolation uses 16 neighboring pixels to produce smoother and more accurate
850   results. This method is deterministic and does not involve any machine learning or training.
851
      CNN We modify the hybrid Downsampled Skip-Connection Multi-Scale (DSC/MS) architecture
852   proposed by Fukami et al. (2019). In the original implementation of the DSC/MS model, the model
853   operates on a single frame of velocity field. We modify the model to work in our settings, where
854   the input consists of three consecutive frames of low-fidelity vorticity data, upsampled to 256 ×
855   256. The DSC component starts by compressing the input through a series of convolutional layers,
856   effectively capturing multi-scale features. Skip connections are then introduced at various stages to
857   preserve spatial information, ensuring that the network retains fine details from earlier layers. The
858   MS component consists of three parallel convolutional paths that capture features at different scales
859   and combine them to produce the high-resolution output of three consecutive frames of 256 × 256
860
      vorticity data. The training process uses the L2 loss function, running for 300 epochs with a learning
      rate of 1e − 5.
861
862   GAN We modify the physics-informed GAN proposed by Li & McComb (2022). In the original im-
863   plementation of the physics-informed GAN, the input was low-resolution phase fraction data from a
      multiphase fluid simulation, and the output was a high-resolution phase fraction representation. Our


                                                               16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      adaptation uses low-fidelity vorticity data upsampled to 256 × 256 as input and high-fidelity vor-
865   ticity data as output. The generator consisted of multiple convolutional layers and residual blocks,
866   while the discriminator had a series of convolutional layers followed by dense layers. The physics-
867   informed loss function combined a mean squared error (MSE) term with an additional term designed
868   to enforce conservation of mass, ensuring the generated data remained consistent with fluid dynam-
869   ics principles. The model was trained for 300 epochs with a learning rate of 1e − 5.
870
      Diffusion The unconditional diffusion model in Shu et al. (2023) is trained only on high-fidelity
871   CFD data without requiring low-fidelity data during training. The model uses a U-Net architecture
872   with hierarchical convolutional blocks, multi-level skip connections, and a self-attention mechanism
873   in the bottleneck layer to capture complex features of the high-fidelity data. The training process
874   spans 300 epochs, where the model learns to predict and remove noise added during the forward
875   diffusion process. During inference, the model starts with a guided reverse diffusion process from
876   an intermediate timestep t < T instead of starting with complete random Gaussian noise. The low-
877   fidelity input data is first upsampled to the target resolution and then combined with Gaussian noise
878   to form the initial input for the reverse diffusion process. This noisy data serves as guidance for
879
      generating the high-fidelity output.
880   Conditional Diffusion The conditional diffusion model proposed in Shu et al. (2023) incorporates
881   physics-informed guidance during both training and inference by utilizing the gradient of the PDE
882   residual with respect to the noised sample. During training, at each step of the forward diffusion
883   process, the residual of the governing PDE is computed based on the current noised sample. The
884   gradient of this residual is calculated and concatenated with the noised sample as input to the U-Net.
885
      During inference, the same process is applied: the model computes the gradient of the PDE residual
      at each reverse diffusion step and concatenates it with the noised sample.
886
887   PG-Diff Starting with xt , which represents an intermediate denoised state at time-step t, the denois-
888   ing process utilizes a U-Net model to estimate ϵθ . The UNet architecture starts with an initial 3x3

892

992    to ensure that the energy distribution in Fourier space follows the desired spectral shape. Random
993
       Gaussian perturbations are applied to each Fourier component of the stream function, producing a
       random realization of the vorticity field. To ensure the stream function has a zero mean, a spectral
994
       filter is applied, and the field is normalized based on the kinetic energy. Finally, the vorticity field is
995
       computed in physical space by taking the inverse Laplacian of the stream function in Fourier space,
996    resulting in a turbulent flow field that evolves naturally without external forcing.

       E.4     LPIPS S CORES
1273
       Direct mapping models are effective at minimizing L2 loss. However, these models tend to produce
1274
       samples that appear smooth and blurry, often missing the intricate details. This is because the
1275
       L2 loss emphasizes pixel-wise accuracy, which can lead to averaging effects and the loss of fine-
1276   grained details. To address this issue, we use the LPIPS (Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity)
1277   metric, which focuses on perceptual differences and provides a more qualitative assessment of the
1278   reconstructed flow fields, capturing the texture and small-scale structures. The results are presented
1279   in Table 4
1280
       Although LPIPS is trained on the ImageNet dataset, which consists of natural images, it remains a
1281
       valuable metric for evaluating perceptual quality in CFD applications. This is because LPIPS lever-
1282   ages features from deep neural networks that are effective at capturing multi-scale patterns, textures,
1283   and perceptual similarities, regardless of the specific domain. Fluid dynamics data often have com-
1284   plex structures and turbulent patterns that share characteristics with textures found in natural images,
1285   making LPIPS suitable for assessing the fidelity of reconstructed flow fields. Thus, despite being
1286   trained on ImageNet, LPIPS can still effectively quantify how well the reconstructed samples re-
1287   tain important perceptual details, making it a robust metric for evaluating the visual quality of CFD
1288   reconstructions.
