009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Euclidean graphs embedded in Rn with unordered vertices and straight-line edges
012            represent important real objects such as molecules whose atoms are connected by
013            chemical bonds. Many real objects preserve their properties under any rigid mo-
014            tion from the special Euclidean group SE(n). Embedded graphs were previously
015            distinguished under such rigid motion or geometric isomorphism in Rn . Experi-
016            mental noise motivates new Lipschitz continuous invariants so that perturbations
017
               of all vertices up to ε change the invariants up to a constant multiple of ε in a
               suitable metric, whose running time should polynomially depend on the number
018
               of unordered vertices. We developed new complete invariants that are stable under
019
               noise, form a natural hierarchy, and distinguish all chemically different graphs in
020            the QM9 database of 130K+ molecules within a few hours on a modest desktop.

023   1   M OTIVATIONS , PROBLEM STATEMENT, AND NEW CONTRIBUTIONS
024
025   Many rigid structures from star constellations to molecules are represented by geometric graphs in
026   a Euclidean space, see Bonchev (1991). More precisely, a Euclidean graph G ⊂ Rn is a finite set
027   of m unordered (unlabeled) vertices located at distinct points of Rn and connected by straight-line
028   edges. Forgetting all edges of G ⊂ Rn gives us the vertex set V (G) ⊂ Rn of m unordered points.
029   A Euclidean graph can be disconnected and can have vertices v of any degree that is the number of
030   edges whose endpoint is v. Loops and multiple edges (with the same endpoints) do not appear in
031   Euclidean graphs because all edges are straight line segments and can also intersect in theory.
032
033   Graphs can be considered under any equivalence relation that should satisfy the axioms: 1) reflexiv-
034   ity: G ∼ G, 2) symmetry: if G ∼ F then F ∼ G, 3) transitivity: if G ∼ F and F ∼ H then G ∼ H.
035
      In chemistry, the simplest equivalence of molecules is the chemical composition, which is insuffi-
      cient in practice, e.g. Fig. 1 (right) shows stereoisomers that have the same chemical compositions
036
      and non-equivalent rigid shapes with different chemical properties, see Rieder et al. (2023).
037
038

043   isomorphic to each other. Right: stereoisomers are isomorphic combinatorially, not geometrically.
044
045   For molecules, the strongest equivalence (distinguishing as many graphs as practically possible) is a
046   geometric isomorphism G ∼  = F , i.e. an orientation-preserving transformation of Rn that bijectively
047
      maps the vertices and edges: G → F . Geometric isomorphisms are also called rigid motions
      (compositions of translations and rotations), which form the special Euclidean group SE(n).
048
049   Any geometrically isomorphic molecules have the same chemical properties. If a flexible molecule
050   changes its rigid shape, its functional properties can change, so it is important to distinguish rigid
051   shapes Wilson et al. (1991). The slightly weaker equivalence (not distinguishing mirror images) is
052   isometry, which is any distance-preserving transformation including reflections. Since all real data
053   (such as inter-point distances) are noisy, a more practically important answer is not binary (‘same or
      different’) but should be continuously quantified by a distance metric between isometry classes.

      The ever-present atomic vibrations imply that rigid classes of molecules graphs on m unordered
055   atoms form a continuously infinite Graph Isometry Space GIS(R3 ; m). Indeed, experimental noise
056   and any iterative optimization can slightly perturb a molecular graph G to a near-duplicate F so
057   that the rigid classes of G, F are close to each other in GIS(R3 ; m). The SSS theorem from school
058   geometry implies that any triangular graphs are isometric if and only if they have the same triple of
059   sides (inter-point distances) a, b, c considered up to 6 permutations. Hence the space of triangular
060   graphs is the triangular cone {0 < a ≤ b ≤ c ≤ a + b} in R3 , where the last triangle inequality
061   c ≤ a + b guarantees that three distances a, b, c are realizable by a real triangle, see Fig. 2 (right).

073   To reliably distinguish any Euclidean graphs G ⊂ Rn similar to triangular graphs, we need an
074   invariant I defined as a numerical descriptor preserved by any rigid motion in Rn . Alternatively,
075   if I(G) ̸= I(F ), then G ̸∼= F , so any invariant has no false negatives that are pairs of different
076   representatives of rigidly equivalent graphs (denoted by G ∼  = F ) having equal values of a (non-
077   invariant) descriptor. The number of vertices (or edges) of G is an integer-valued invariant that can
078   distinguish some graphs but is too weak to separate the tetrahedral graphs Ti in Fig. 1.
079   The strongest invariant I separating all non-equivalent graphs is called complete meaning that if
080   I(G) = I(F ) then G ∼ = F . Alternatively, a complete invariant I has no false positives that are pairs
081   of non-equivalent graphs G ̸∼= F with I(G) = I(F ). Experimental noise in real data motivates
082   continuous invariants that controllably change under perturbations, as formalized below.
083   Problem 1.1 (complete invariant of Euclidean graphs with polynomial-time continuous metrics).
084   For Euclidean graphs on m unordered vertices in Rn , find an invariant I satisfying the conditions:
085
      (a) completeness : any graphs G, F are related by rigid motion in Rn if and only if I(G) = I(F );
086
087   (b) Lipschitz continuity : there is a constant λ and a metric d on invariant values satisfying the
088   axioms 1) d(I, J) = 0 if and only if I = J, 2) d(I, J) = d(J, I), 3) d(I, J) + d(J, K) ≥ d(I, K),
089   such that if F is obtained by perturbing every vertex of G up to ε > 0, then d(I(G), I(F )) ≤ λε;
090   (c) invertibility : any graph G ⊂ Rn can be reconstructed (up to rigid motion in Rn ) from I(G);
091
092
      (d) computability : for a fixed dimension n, the invariant I, the metric d, and a reconstruction of
      G ⊂ Rn from I(G) can be obtained in polynomial time of the number m of unordered vertices. ■
093
094   Condition 1.1(a) means that a complete invariant I is a DNA-style code that uniquely identifies any
095   graph under geometric isomorphism. To be useful for noisy inputs, a complete invariant should con-
096   tinuously change under perturbations in a suitable metric. The axioms in 1.1(b) are the foundations
097   of metric geometry Melter & Tomescu (1984) and accepted in physical chemistry Weinhold (1975).
098   If the triangle axiom fails with any additive error, the classical k-means and DBSCAN clustering
099   are open to adversarial attacks in Rass et al. (2024). If the first axiom is ignored, d ≡ 0 satisfies
100   all other axioms. The first axiom implies the completeness of I in 1.1(a) but the continuity is much
101
      stronger. Indeed, for any complete invariant I, one can define the discrete metric d(I(G), I(F )) = 1
      for G ̸∼
             = F , which unhelpfully treats all non-equivalent graphs (even near-duplicates) as equally dis-
102
      tant. The new requirement of Lipschitz continuity in 1.1(b) is much stronger than the classical ε − δ
103
      continuity because the constant λ is universal for all ε and unbounded graphs G ⊂ Rn .
104
105   Condition 1.1(c) requires I to be not only complete and continuous but also efficient to explicitly
106   reconstruct G, better than a real DNA that doesn’t explain how to grow a living organism. Com-
107   putability 1.1(d) prevents brute-force attempts, e.g. defining I(G) as the infinite set of images of G
      under all rigid motions or taking m! distance matrices over all permutations of m unordered vertices.

      2    PAST WORK ON INVARIANTS AND DISTANCES FOR CLOUDS AND GRAPHS
113
      This section reviews the related work starting from earlier partial solutions of Problem 1.1.
114
115   Ordered clouds. If a Euclidean graph G ⊂ Rn consists of m isolated vertices without edges, the
116   vertex set V (G) can be called a point cloud C. If additionally, all points p1 , . . . , pm of C are ordered
117   (not considered under the action of all m! permutations), a complete invariant of C under isometry
118   (compositions of translations, rotations, reflections) is the classical m × m matrix Li et al. (2023)
119
      of pairwise distances |pi − pj | due to (Grinberg & Olver, 2019, Theorem 9) or, after shifting the
      center of mass to the origin, the Gram matrix of scalar products pi Dotpj by (Dekster & Wilker,
120
      1987, Theorem 1). This multidimensional scaling known at least since 1935 Schoenberg (1935) can
121
      also provide an embedding C ⊂ Rk preserving all distances of C for a dimension k ≤ m. This
122   embedding C ⊂ Rk uses eigenvectors whose ambiguity up to signs gives an exponential time that
123   can be close to O(2m ), not polynomial in the number m of ordered points as required in 1.1(d).
124
125
      Unordered clouds. Computational geometry developed many algorithms for detecting geometric
      isomorphism (or isometry, also called congruence) between point sets without edges Huttenlocher
126
      et al. (1993); Chew & Kedem (1992); Chew et al. (1999); Goodrich et al. (1999). (Arvind & Rattan,
127
      2016, Theorem 3) describing, for a finite set A ⊂ Qn of m points, nO (n)poly(mM ) time algorithm
128   to compute a canonizing function f (A), which can be considered a complete isometry invariant of
129   A, where M upper bounds the binary encodings of the rational coordinates in the input. For point
130   clouds under rigid motion (also distinguishing mirror images), (Widdowson & Kurlin, 2023, Theo-
131   rem 4.7) described a metric computable in time O(n(mn−1 /n!)3 log m). The latest methods Hordan
132   et al. (2024); Delle Rose et al. (2024); Amir et al. (2024); Maennel et al. (2024) also achieved the
133   completeness for point clouds but without a Lipschitz continuous metric as in 1.1(b). Energy poten-
134   tials written as infinite series of spherical harmonics, are often considered complete representations
135   of atomic environments, which holds in the limit but not for a finite sizePozdnyakov et al. (2020).
136   For a fixed set of m vertices in general position, one can choose any of m(m − 1)/2 edges and
137   produce 2m(m−1)/2 non-isometric graphs. Problem 1.1 for arbitrary graphs is computationally much
138   harder than for point clouds due to exponentially many different graphs on the same vertex set.
139
      The graph isomorphism problem Grohe & Schweitzer (2020) for abstract (non-Euclidean) graphs
140
      is another version of Problem 1.1 without continuous metrics. The latest advances Babai (2019);
141
      Helfgott et al. (2017) achieved only quasipolynomial time, though a polynomial time was again
142   announced in Ohto (2024). While many partial cases were solved, e.g. for planar graphs (embedded
143   in R2 without intersecting edges), see Kiefer et al. (2019), the k-dimensional Weisfeiler-Leman
144   test Leman & Weisfeiler (1968) fails for 3-regular graphs of size O(k). The key limitation of WL
145   tests is their local nature when invariants are gradually expanded from a vertex or a k-tuple. Then
146   covering a graph on m vertices needs O(m) expansions leading to exponential sizes in m. (Dym &
147   Gortler, 2024, Section 3.9) discussed that a complete invariant (under all permutations of m vertices)
148   that has a polynomial time in the dimension n would also solve the graph isomorphism problem in
149   polynomial time. Condition 1.1(d) is easier for a fixed n such as the practical dimensions n = 2, 3.
150
      The number m of vertices can be dozens or hundreds, e.g. for molecular graphs in R3 , where vertices
      are centers of atoms and edges are inter-atomic bonds that keep atoms together in a stable molecule.
151
152   Geometric Deep Learning in Bronstein et al. (2021) pioneered an axiomatic approach to geometric
153   classifications beyond Euclidean space Rn in Bronstein et al. (2017). Many neural networks were
154   proved to be universal Maron et al. (2019); Zhou (2020); Abbe & Sandon (2020) in the sense of ap-
155   proximating any continuous function on given data with sufficiently many layers. This universality
156   property has been strengthened in Problem 1.1 to the full completeness of an explicit invariant that
157   should be computable in polynomial time and invertible to an original graph up to rigid motion. The
158
      key challenge was to compute an exact (not approximate) metric that is also Lipschitz continuous.
159   Equivariants Kondor & Trivedi (2018); Cohen et al. (2019); Fuchs et al. (2020); Deng et al. (2021)
160   are defined as descriptors E satisfying E(f (G)) = Tf (E(G)) for any rigid motion f of a graph
161   G in Rn , where Tf may not be the identity as required for invariants. Any linear combination
      of points, e.g. the center of mass, is equivariant but cannot distinguish graphs under translation.

      Equivariants Gao et al. (2020); Qi & Luo (2020); Tu et al. (2022); Batzner et al. (2022) help predict
163   forces acting on atoms to move them to a more optimal configuration. These time-dependent graphs

174   etry classes) were studied in Nikolentzos et al. (2017); Majhi & Wenk (2022); Buchin et al. (2023).
175   Computing a metric between rigid classes of clouds is only a small part of Problem 1.1. Indeed, to
176   efficiently navigate on Earth, in addition to distances between cities, we need a map of the whole
177
      planet and hence an invertible continuous invariant I, which is an analog of geographic coordinates.

405   5     E XPERIMENTS ON 130K+ MOLECULES , LIMITATIONS , AND SIGNIFICANCE
406
407   This section describes how the new invariants have enabled a complete classification of all molecular
408   graphs in the QM9 database of 130K+ (130,808) molecules given with atomic 3D coordinates.
409   For graphs G ⊂ Rn with edges, such a practical classification was previously impossible because
410   there was no complete and Lipschitz continuous invariant of Euclidean graphs even in R2 . After
411   the SSS theorem since 300 BC, such an invariant solving Problem 1.1 was known only for cyclic
412   polygons inscribed into a circle of a fixed radius, see Theorem 1.8 on p.52 in Penner (2012). The
413   complete invariant SCD of point clouds Widdowson & Kurlin (2023) has no nested structure of the
414   new invariant NCD, which was needed to reconstruct all edges of G ⊂ Rn from NCD(G; n). All
415   experiments were done in 5 hours on Ryzen 9 3950X 3.5 GHz, 64 MB of L3 cache, RAM 82GB.
416   The hierarchy of invariants described in Fig. 3 and Table 2 justifies the computations starting from
417   the simple and fast invariants on all pairs to filter out distant graphs and then moving to progressively
418   stronger invariants for much smaller subsets of pairs of close molecules. We computed the pseudo-
419   metric L∞ (the maximum absolute difference of corresponding coordinates) on all 873,527,974
420   pairs of SRVs, then 8,735,279 L∞ on the stronger SDVs for the 1% closest pairs, then 87,352
421   EMDs on PDDs for the 1% closest pairs, 10K distances NBM on NCD(G; 1), NCD(G; 2)s for the
422
      top closest pairs, and 64 NBMs on complete invariants NCD(G, 3) for molecular graphs G ⊂ R3 .
