009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The development of image representations that capture semantic and spatial in-
012            formation efficiently, which are also interpretable and generalisable, remains un-
013            solved. Drawing from a cognitive modelling framework, we propose SSPictR
014            – a biologically plausible image representation based on spatial semantic point-
015            ers (SSPs). SSPictR encodes semantic labels and their spatial locations extracted
016
               from segmentation maps and only requires a single vector to capture a fully de-
               codable neuro-symbolic representation of a natural scene. It is inherently inter-
017
               pretable, offers a high compression factor and significantly faster inference speed
018
               on downstream tasks, such as scene recognition. We evaluate the efficiency and
019            generalisability of SSPictR on the popular Places365, and ADE20K datasets for
020            scene recognition, on COCOStuff for segmentation reconstruction, and on VISC
021            and Savoias for prediction of visual complexity. We show that the scene represen-
022            tations provided by SSPictR are more generalisable within and across these tasks
023            while only requiring a fraction of model parameters and, therefore, offer 25 times
024            higher inference speed, with comparable accuracy. As such, SSPictR opens up
025            a new direction for future research on cognitively-inspired image representations
026            that are not only significantly smaller but also more interpretable and generalis-
027
               able.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Machine learning models for computer vision have achieved human-level performance on a number
032   of tasks, such as object detection (Zong et al., 2023), semantic segmentation (Chen et al., 2022),
033   or depth estimation (Zhao et al., 2023). But these improvements come at an ever-increasing cost:
034   These models are highly inefficient and lack interpretability, i.e. they require training of millions
035   of parameters from large amounts of data and their learned internal representations are notoriously
036   challenging to analyse and understand. Furthermore, despite impressive performance on tasks that
037   they were trained for, they still lack generalisability to out-of-domain (OOD) data (Geirhos et al.,
038   2018; Mahner et al., 2024). Aligning model and human representations has increased robustness
039
      in object detection (Geirhos et al., 2019), improved performance (Sucholutsky et al., 2023), and
      has advanced our understanding of human cognition through better computational models (Mahner
040
      et al., 2024). While there has been a lot of research in aligning deep neural networks (DNNs) in
041
      object classification tasks (Mahner et al., 2024; Muttenthaler et al., 2022; Geirhos et al., 2021),
042   human alignment for scene understanding remains under-explored (Bartnik & Groen, 2024).
043
044   Computational models of human perception have a long-standing history of research in cognitive
045
      science (Kotseruba & Tsotsos, 2020). Most interesting are biologically-plausible cognitive models,
      as they can be naturally integrated with DNNs while maintaining the interpretability of symbolic
046
      systems. A popular model is the semantic pointer architecture (SPA; Eliasmith 2013) – a cognitive
047
      modelling framework based on vector symbolic algebras (VSAs). VSAs use hyperdimensional dis-
048   tributed vectors for efficient and robust representation of concepts (Kleyko et al., 2022). There are
049   many successful applications of cognitively-inspired VSAs, for example, in abstract reasoning (Her-
050   sche et al., 2023), ego-motion prediction (Mitrokhin et al., 2019), path integration (Dumont et al.,
051   2022), and reinforcement learning (Bartlett et al., 2022). Particularly interesting as representations
052   for scene understanding are the works by Komer et al. (2019) and Penzkofer et al. (2024), as they
053   encode objects in a grid-like continuous vector space similar to cognitive maps found in the brain
      (Bermudez-Contreras et al., 2020).

067   In this work, we bring together this research and propose SSPictR– a neurally inspired image repre-
068   sentation that is more efficient, interpretable, and generalisable than existing image representations.
069   At its core, SSPictR encodes objects from segmentation maps into a continuous vector space that
070
      compresses the semantic and spatial information of a scene. Figure 1 shows an overview. In contrast
      to existing representations, SSPictR offers a significantly better compression factor of only 0.46 bits
071
      per pixel (vs 8 bits for segmentation maps) and an inference speed of 2,857 fps for scene recog-
072
      nition. The compressed vector representation only has 3,751 dimensions and can be queried, used
073   to fully reconstruct the segmentation map, or used as a feature embedding for further downstream
074   tasks, such as visual complexity prediction. We evaluate different encoding methods for this novel
075   representation based on reconstruction error of the segmentation map. We also show that SSPictR
076   is highly generalisable across tasks (scene recognition, visual complexity) and datasets, providing
077   competitive performance for OOD scene recognition on the ADE20K dataset (Zhou et al., 2017).
078
      In summary, we present a novel scene representation: SSPictR. SSPictR is highly efficient, inter-
079   pretable, and generalisable. We provide a construction method from segmentation maps and evalu-
080   ate different encoding schemes. Further, we evaluate the representation quality via linear probing on
081   scene recognition, its generalisability on OOD data, and highlight potential applications in computer
082   vision, cognitive science, and robotics.

      2.1   S CENE R EPRESENTATIONS
088
      The choice of data representation is a key factor for the performance of machine learning models
089
      (Bengio et al., 2013). Scene representation, in particular, is challenging due to complex config-
090   urations, i.e., scenes are comprised of diverse objects in complex spatial layouts with substantial
091   semantic ambiguity (Xie et al., 2020). Furthermore, scenes can also be characterised as environ-
092   ments for embodied agents to navigate in (Malcolm et al., 2016).
093
      Scene Recognition. Scene recognition, the task of classifying scenes into categories, is considered
094
      a fundamentally important but challenging task in computer vision (Zeng et al., 2021), with a wide
095
      range of applications, from robot navigation (Yadav et al., 2023) to disaster detection (Muhammad
096   et al., 2018). Current benchmark datasets include Places365 (Zhou et al., 2018), ADE20K (Zhou
097   et al., 2017), SUN397 (Xiao et al., 2010), and MIT67 (Quattoni & Torralba, 2009). While MIT67
098   only contains 15 thousand images classified into 67 indoor scenes, the most recent Places365, con-
099   sists of 10 million images with annotations for 434 scene classes in three macro-classes: Indoor,
100   Nature, and Urban. The advance of the available datasets for scene recognition has significantly im-
101   pacted scene recognition methods. Previously, methods focused on enhancing specific features, e.g.
102   semantic features (López-Cifuentes et al., 2020), multi-layer features (Liu et al., 2019), or multi-
103   view features (Seong et al., 2020), achieving state-of-the-art results on MIT67 and SUN397 (Zeng
104
      et al., 2021). Methods trained on Places365, however, leverage the large amount of data so that
      standard CNNs significantly outperform previous approaches (Zhou et al., 2018).
105
106   Since SSPictR is primarily designed for robotics applications, such as visual navigation, our fo-
107   cus is particularly on indoor scene recognition. Pal et al. (2019) and Chen et al. (2019) proposed
      subsets of the Places365 dataset containing seven and 14 classes, respectively. Building on this,

109   from semantic segmentation maps: object-to-scene (OTS). OTS extracts object features through a
110   pre-trained segmentation model and calculates object relations, outperforming both Pal et al. (2019)
111   and Chen et al. (2019). However, OTS also requires up to 255 million model parameters and, there-

113   network (AGCN; Zhou et al. 2023) achieved higher performance than OTS on both datasets while
114   also increasing inference time to 27 fps. Song & Ma (2023) proposed a semantic region relation-

      essential for the low-resource and high-speed requirements of edge devices in practical robotics ap-
118
      plications. However, their method is specific for enhancing scene recognition models and does not
119
      transfer to other tasks.
120
121   Human Alignment. Aligning representations of deep neural networks (DNNs) to humans is a
122   promising avenue to increase performance and generalisabilty of computer vision models (Sucholut-
123
      sky et al., 2023; Chang et al., 2019). While many works analysed object representations and their
      alignment to human similarity judgements (Muttenthaler et al., 2022; Geirhos et al., 2021; Hebart
124
      et al., 2020; King et al., 2019), meaningful representations of full scenes remain under-explored.
125
      Groen et al. (2017) analysed scene recognition in humans and found that in addition to object co-
126   occurrence statistics as found by Stansbury et al. (2013), other features across different levels of
127   visual processing play an important role, such as spatial layouts, boundaries, and textures. This en-
128   courages building scene representations based on object-level statistics, such as the presence and fea-
129   tures of certain objects, but also highlights the importance of additional spatial information. Hence,
130   we construct SSPictR from segmentation maps of images, which provide both object information as
131   well as their spatial layout in the scene.
132   Visual Complexity. Segmentation maps and object-level statistics were also successfully used to
133   predict the visual complexity of images (Nath et al., 2024). Visual complexity ratings of images are
134   important for cognitive science studies, as they impact attention, engagement, and memorability of
135   stimuli. Furthermore, visual complexity is relevant for practical applications, such as user experi-
136   ence on webpages and brand logo design (Kyle-Davidson et al., 2023). To understand and effectively
137   model visual complexity, most methods rely on hand-crafted features to explain human complexity
138   ratings, i.e., number of regions, frequency factor, and colours (Corchs et al., 2016), clutter and patch
139   symmetry (Kyle-Davidson et al., 2023), or number of classes and segments (Nath et al., 2024). Su-
140
      pervised methods achieve the highest correlation with human ratings, but they require large datasets
      for training, where currently only IC9600 (Feng et al., 2023) is publicly available, offering 9,600
141
      images across eight categories. Smaller datasets include Savoias (Saraee et al., 2020) consisting of
142
      1,400 images with seven categories and VISC (Kyle-Davidson et al., 2023) containing 800 images
143   across 12 sub-categories.

146   2.2   V ECTOR S YMBOLIC A LGEBRAS
147
      Vector symbolic algebras (VSAs) play an important role in cognitive architectures (Kleyko et al.,
148
      2023; Stewart et al., 2012) and showed improved performance of machine learning methods, e.g.,
149
      ego-motion prediction (Mitrokhin et al., 2020), speech recognition (Imani et al., 2018), or object
150   classification (Gallant & Culliton, 2016). VSAs offer a unique way of encoding symbolic meaning
151   in hyper-dimensional distributed representations, making them inherently interpretable (Mitrokhin
152   et al., 2020) and robust to errors (Rahimi et al., 2016). Furthermore, their potential application
153   in neuromorphic hardware make them highly efficient, achieving speed gains of up to 100 times
154   GPU performance (Blouw et al., 2019). The potential of VSAs in replicating cognitive maps for
155   navigation has been shown via path integration (Dumont et al., 2022) and reinforcement learning on
156   navigation tasks (Bartlett et al., 2022), while the potential in scene understanding has been shown
157   via visual question answering (Komer et al., 2019; Penzkofer et al., 2024). For more application
158
      examples and a comprehensive literature review on VSAs, we refer to Kleyko et al. (2022; 2023).
159   In this work, we build upon the semantic pointer architecture (SPA) (Eliasmith, 2013), a cognitively-
160   inspired VSA, which uses holographic reduced representations (HRRs) (Plate, 1995), i.e., a set of
161   operations that can be applied for manipulation of hyper-dimensional vectors representing symbols.
      Semantic similarity of vectors is calculated by the dot product. Vectors can be bundled to represent

185   3.1   T HEORETICAL BACKGROUND
186
      Under the SPA, spatial semantic pointers (SSPs) were proposed for spatial representations through
187   the following encoding scheme:
188                                                              −1
                                              ϕ(x) = F −1 {eiλ Ax },                                           (1)
189
      where ϕ : x ∈ R2 7→ Rd , λ defines the length scale of the encoded representation, F −1 denotes the
190
      inverse Fourier transform, and A ∈ Rd×2 is a phase matrix whose columns consist of phasors repre-
191   senting different frequencies. For real-valued spatial representations, the phase matrix is conjugate
192   symmetric. For biological realism of the SSP representation, further constraints are applied to the
193   phase matrix that enable the replication of grid cell firing patterns as seen in Dumont & Eliasmith
194   (2020). This is achieved by setting triplets of rows in the phase matrix 120◦ apart, resulting in grid-
195   ded interference patterns. As given by biological experimental findings, this data lies on the hyper-
196   toroidal manifold (Gardner et al., 2021). The dimensionality d is given by d = nscales ·nrotates ·3·2+1,
197   where nscales denotes the scale of the firing pattern activity, nrotates denotes the orientation of the grid
198   cells, 3 denotes triplets, 2 for conjugate symmetry, and +1 for the 0-frequency term.
199   Using the SSP representation and the set of operations given by HRR, we can construct cognitive
200   maps, for example, we can construct the map M of a cat at location (x1 , y1 ), a mouse at location
201   (x2 , y2 ), and cheese at location (x3 , y3 ):

294   We first evaluate the representation quality by calculating the reconstruction accuracy for semantic
295   segmentation on the COCOStuff 2017 dataset (Caesar et al., 2018) with 118K images. Furthermore,
296   we perform linear probing by training a small scene recognition model on a reduced Places365
297   dataset (Zhou et al., 2018) and test generalisation for out-of-domain (OOD) scene recognition on
298   ADE20K (Zhou et al., 2017). As an additional downstream application, we predict the visual com-
299
      plexity of images with SSPictR as features for a regression model.

301   4.1   R EPRESENTATION Q UALITY
302
303
      For evaluating the reconstruction accuracy, we compute all SSPs with 3751 dimensions and the
      uniform encoding scheme for the COCOStuff train and validation set (Caesar et al., 2018). Then,
304
      we reconstruct the segmentation maps by unbinding the scene representation with each object vector
305
      separately, yielding the similarity map for the queried object. We select all points above τ = 0.7
306   similarity to belong to the queried object. We compare this mask with the ground truth mask of the
307   object and calculate the pixel-wise IoU. With the uniform encoding scheme, we achieve 45.36% ±
308   13.05% average IoU on the COCOStuff validation split. Here, we encode 3,770 points on average
309   with an encoding time of 6.13 ± 1.18s per image. We further increase the reconstruction accuracy
310   by training a UNet model (Ronneberger et al., 2015) to refine the object masks based on the SSP
311   similarity maps instead of using the threshold τ .
312   Our UNet model consists of four encoder and decoder layers with a total of 465K trainable param-
313   eters. We trained the model on a subset of the COCOStuff 2017 train dataset Caesar et al. (2018),
314   i.e. we use 10K samples of similarity maps, which amounts to a total of 1,235 training images, split
315   into train and validation. We evaluate the model on the official validation split (not used for valida-
316   tion during training) of 5,000 images and achieve an average IoU of 57.3 ± 7.1%, a considerable
317   13.2% increase. As shown in Figure 4, the model learns to refine the given similarity maps, remov-
318   ing excess noise. Based on further examples, we find that the model learns to adapt the threshold
319   of the similarity map based on object-specific features, mainly size, as the SSPictR representation
320
      struggles with small objects (see Figure 3). To conclude, while we only trained on a small subset
      of the available dataset, we achieved a significant reconstruction performance increase. We believe
321
      this could be further increased by more training or a more elaborate network structure.
322

402   4.2   V ISUAL C OMPLEXITY
403
404   To evaluate the generalisability of SSPictR to other downstream tasks, we predict human visual
405   complexity ratings of images. Following previous work (Nath et al., 2024) we use handcrafted
406
      features, i.e., our SSP representations, and train simple regressors to predict the complexity scores
      that go from zero to one hundred. We evaluate the following models: support vector regression
407
      (SVR), kernel ridge regression (KR), gradient boosting (GB), and random forest regression (RF).
408
      KR with a cosine kernel, i.e., cosine similarity as a comparative measure between different vectors,
409   yields the best results. This is intuitive as the cosine similarity has been shown to be the same as the
410   distance between two unitary SSPs in the Fourier domain (Voelker, 2020). We evaluate our models
411   on 3 classes of the Savoias dataset (Saraee et al., 2020) and on the full VISC dataset with a 7-fold
412   cross-validation. Unfortunately, we did not get access to the larger scale IC9600 (Feng et al., 2023)
413   dataset and are therefore unable to test a supervised method. We perform the same preprocessing
414   pipeline as for the scene recognition model on Places365; first, we generate segmentation maps
415   for each image with the pre-trained VPD model (Zhao et al., 2023), then, we compute our SSP
416   representations with the uniform encoding scheme. For a visualisation of samples from the different
417
      datasets, see Appendix A.3. The art category (Savoias) is the most difficult for the VPD model
      to segment, as it is comprised of paintings or simple drawings, which was not in the training data
418
      (ADE20K; Zhou et al. 2017) of VPD. Further, we found that the interior design category, while close
419
      to the interior scene images in ADE20K, has significantly more objects encoded per image: 22.26
420   on average compared to 10.87 (VISC, Savoias SCENES) and 8.23 (Savoias ART). Additionally, the
421   average object size is also significantly smaller.
422
423
      Table 3 summarises relevant prior work with Spearman rank correlation coefficient r between pre-
      dictions and the human complexity ratings (values taken over from Nath et al. (2024)). Our results
424
      show that we achieve reasonable performance on the Savoias SCENES dataset, i.e., on par with
425
      Kyle-Davidson et al. (2023) and similarly on VISC. However, SSPs as features significantly strug-
426   gle with the ART and INTERIOR DESIGN category; we believe this is due to limited segmentation
427   accuracy (ART) and the high number of objects (INTERIOR DESIGN). Further, we test whether
428   additional features can increase the correlation coefficient. Inspired by Nath et al. (2024), we use
429   the number of classes extracted from the number of unique labels in the segmentation map. Addi-
430   tionally, we also calculate the patch symmetry, as proposed by Kyle-Davidson et al. (2023). Both
431   features improve the predictions, however, since the added feature is only one of 3752 the impact is
      not significant. Overall, we show that SSPictR as features for visual complexity prediction perform

432
      similar to previous handcrafted features, but there is still room to improve. We believe a supervised
433   method is better suited to work with the large feature space of 3751 dimensions, and we will test
434   this hypothesis when a suitable dataset becomes available.

437   5   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
438
439   We have presented SSPictR – a cognitively-inspired image representation that is inherently inter-
440
      pretable and efficient. We evaluated different encoding schemes and found the best hyperparameters
      for encoding a full scene based on segmentation maps. Further, we evaluated the representation qual-
441
      ity by calculating the reconstruction accuracy on COCOStuff (Caesar et al., 2018), where we found
442
      that a simple mask refinement model can significantly enhance reconstruction IoU to 57.3 ± 7.1%.
443   The key advantage of SSPictR is that the representations are compact, only requiring 0.46 bits per
444   pixel and that they can directly be used for downstream tasks, such as scene recognition and visual
445   complexity prediction.
446
      For scene recognition, a key task for robotic navigation (Xie et al., 2020; Miao et al., 2021), we
447
      trained a small neural network that achieved comparable performance on two indoor scene subsets
448
      of the popular Places365 dataset (Zhou et al., 2018). Similar to previous work (Song et al., 2024),
449   we found that scene recognition accuracy is limited by segmentation quality, i.e. where ground
450   truth segmentation maps are available, we achieved a remarkable scene recognition accuracy of
451   94.2% with a SVM on the ADE20K dataset (Zhou et al., 2017). Further, the model trained on
452   Places365 achieves an even higher performance on ADE20K as out-of-distribution data with 94.5%,
453   highlighting the generalisation ability of our representation. Additionally, SSPictR achieves 25 times
454   higher processing speed than previous methods.
455   Moreover, SSPictR is able to generalise across different tasks, as we have shown by performing vi-
456   sual complexity prediction. Visual complexity prediction is an important task in cognitive science, as
457   perceived complexity is linked to engagement and attention, influencing subjects’ reaction to given
458   stimuli (Nath et al., 2024). While we achieve comparable performance to other handcrafted fea-
459   ture methods on datasets that represent scenes, i.e., VISC (Kyle-Davidson et al., 2023) and Savoias
460   SCENES (Saraee et al., 2020), the SSP representations struggle with abstract images (Savoias ART)
461   and images with a significantly higher amount of objects (Savoias INTERIOR DESIGN). The latter
462   could potentially be addressed by increasing the capacity of the SSPs by allowing a higher dimen-
463
      sionality. However, the more abstract art images do not encompass real-world scenes with spatial
      layouts, which is the intended application area of SSPictR.
464
465   Our overall goal is to develop a compact image representation that can be deployed end-to-end on
466   edge devices for efficient visual navigation and other downstream tasks. SSPictR is an important first
467   step towards this goal. In future work, we would like to address some of the discussed limitations
468   by integrating a larger object vocabulary and more fine-grained segmentation, e.g., by using the
469
      SAM model (Kirillov et al., 2023), which detects segments at different scales. Further, we plan to
      train an end-to-end image to SSP model, potentially with a fully neural implementation to achieve
470
      the highest efficiency. In general, we believe SSPictR is uniquely suitable for robotics applications,
471
      such as embodied visual navigation (Yadav et al., 2023; Ramakrishnan et al., 2021), as well as for
472   further cognitive applications. More specifically, we would like to analyse the alignment of SSPictR
473   to human scene representations, e.g., by performing similarity analysis on fMRI data (Chang et al.,
474   2019), or integrate it into symbolic reasoning systems (Hersche et al., 2023).
475
      As we have shown, SSPictR opens up a new direction of research, leveraging the advantages of
476
      cognitively-inspired image representations.

885   Figure 6: UMap (McInnes et al., 2020) visualisation of SSP representations for 7 indoor classes in
886   (a) ADE20K (Zhou et al., 2017) and (b) Places365 dataset (Zhou et al., 2018).
887
888   Here we compare our SSP representations regarding discrimination quality for the 7 indoor scene
889   classes: bathroom, bedroom, corridor, kitchen, living room, office, and dining room. Both on the
890   Places365-7 dataset (Zhou et al., 2018) and the ADE20K dataset (Zhou et al., 2017). We performed
891   dimensionality reduction via the UMAP algorithm (McInnes et al., 2020) to visualise the represen-
892   tations on both datasets (see Figure 6). We can directly see that ADE20K offers significantly fewer
893   samples with only 4497 images compared to 35000 in the Places365 training set. Despite the avail-
894
      ability of more data for Places, it was more difficult to separate the SSP representations according
      to the correct classes. This might be due to wrong segmentations on the Places dataset. Specifically,
895
      some objects might be mislabeled if they do not exist in the ADE20K data. This separation difficulty
896
      explains the lower scene recognition performance of our method on Places compared to ADE20K,
897   where a simple SVM classifier is able to get 94% accuracy. Further, this implies that some addi-
898   tional features or better segmentations might be necessary to improve classification performance on
899   Places365.
