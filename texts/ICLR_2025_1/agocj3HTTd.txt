009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Federated learning continues to evolve but faces challenges in interpretability and
012            explainability. To address these challenges, we introduce a creative approach em-
013            ploying Neural Additive Models (NAMs) within a federated learning framework.
014            These new Federated Neural Additive Models (FedNAMs) approach merges the
015            advantages of NAMs, where individual networks concentrate on specific input fea-
016
               tures, with the decentralized approach of federated learning, ultimately producing
               interpretable analysis results. This integration enhances privacy by training on local
017
               data across multiple devices, thereby minimizing the risks of data centralization
018
               and enhancing model robustness and generalizability. FedNAMs maintain detailed
019            feature-specific learning, making them especially valuable in sectors like finance
020            and healthcare. They facilitate training client-specific models to integrate local
021            updates, preserve privacy, and reduce centralization concerns. Our studies on
022            various text and image classification tasks, using datasets such as OpenFetch ML
023            Wine, UCI Heart Disease, and Iris, show that FedNAMs deliver strong interpretabil-
024            ity with minimal accuracy loss compared to traditional Federated Deep Neural
025            Networks (DNNs). The research involves notable findings, including the identifica-
026            tion of critical predictive features at the client level as well as at the global level.
027
               Volatile acidity, sulfates, and chlorides for wine quality. Chest pain type, maximum
               heart rate, and number of vessels for heart disease. Petal length and width for
028
               iris classification. This approach strengthens privacy and model efficiency and
029
               improves interpretability and robustness across diverse datasets. Finally, FedNAMs
030            generate insights on causes of highly and low interpretable features.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Deep neural networks (DNN) have delivered remarkable results in areas like computer vision (Himeur
036   et al., 2023) and language modeling (Che et al., 2023). While understanding the mechanisms behind
037   their predictions remains challenging, leading them to be often regarded as black-box models. This
038   lack of interpretability limits their use in critical fields such as finance, criminal justice, and healthcare.
039
      Various efforts have been made to clarify the predictions made by deep neural networks (DNNs) in
      Federated learning environments. For instance, a class of methods, exemplified by LIME (Ribeiro
040
      et al., 2016), seeks to explain individual predictions by locally approximating the neural network with
041
      interpretable models, such as linear models and shallow decision trees for each client in the federated
042   learning environment. However, these methods frequently fall short in robustness and comprehensive
043   understanding of the model, and their explanations may not accurately reflect the computations of the
044   original model or lack the detail necessary to grasp the model’s behavior (Zhang & Li, 2023) fully. In
045   this research, we propose FedNAMs, an interpretable federated learning framework based on Neural
046   Additive Models (NAMs) (Agarwal et al., 2021). We compare the performance and interpretability
047   of this framework to traditional federated learning models. Furthermore, the study explores the
048   trade-offs between interpretability and predictive accuracy in a federated environment.
049   Interpretable Federated Learning (IFL) has emerged as a promising technology to enhance system
050   safety robustness and build trust among FL stakeholders, drawing considerable research interest
051   from academia and industry in recent years (Li et al., 2023). In contrast to existing interpretable AI
052   methods developed for centralized machine learning, IFL presents more significant challenges due
053   to enterprises’ limited access to local data and the constraints imposed by local computational and
      communication resources. IFL is inherently interdisciplinary, requiring expertise in machine learning,

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      optimization, cryptography, and human factors to devise effective solutions. This complexity makes
055   it challenging for new researchers to stay abreast of the latest developments. A comprehensive
056   survey paper on this critical and rapidly evolving field has yet to exist. Federated Learning (FL) is
057   a groundbreaking approach to machine learning that enables models to be trained on decentralized
058   data sources while safeguarding data privacy. This method is especially advantageous in healthcare,
059   finance, and mobile applications, where sensitive data is distributed across multiple locations (Aouedi
060   et al., 2022). Traditional centralized learning approaches present significant privacy risks and are
061   often impractical due to data transfer limitations and regulatory constraints. Despite the benefits of
062   FL, a key challenge persists in the interpretability of the models it produces (Zhang et al., 2024b).
063
      Most FL models, particularly those based on deep learning, operate as black boxes, offering minimal
      insight into their decision-making processes. This lack of transparency impedes their adoption in
064
      critical fields where understanding the reasoning behind model predictions is crucial. The current
065
      federated learning landscape is dominated by complex, opaque models that, although highly accurate,
066   provide little transparency. There is an increasing demand for interpretable machine learning models
067   to elucidate their inner workings and decision-making processes. Neural Additive Models (NAMs)
068   (Agarwal et al., 2021), which combine the robustness of neural networks with the interpretability
069   of additive models, represent a promising solution. However, integrating NAMs into the federated
070   learning framework presents significant challenges, including maintaining interpretability across
071   distributed nodes and ensuring overall model performance.
072   In this research paper, we propose a federated learning framework while imposing specific constraints
073   on the architecture of neural networks using interpretable models known as Neural Additive Models
074   (NAMs). While implementing tabular data, these glass-box models maintain a high level of inter-
075   pretability with minimal loss in prediction accuracy. NAMs are part of the Generalized Additive
076   Models (GAMs) family (Hastie, 2017), which takes the form:

      In traditional GAMs, the model fitting uses the analytical method of iterative back fitting with smooth
083
      low-order splines that effectively reduce overfitting. While more recent GAMs (Hastie, 2017) use
084
      boosted decision trees to enhance accuracy and allow the learning of abrupt changes in the feature-
085   shaping functions. Hence, it captures better patterns in actual data that smooth splines struggle to
086   model. This paper explores the use of deep neural networks (DNNs) to fit generalized additive models
087   (NAMs) in a federated learning setup. NAMs provide interpretable insights on DNNs, which is
088   essential for federated learning as models will be more understandable across multiple decentralized
089   nodes. Unlike tree-based GAMs, NAMs can adapt to multiclass, multitask, or multi-label learning.
090   In a federated learning scenario, models are trained efficiently across distributed nodes using shared
091   resources. Therefore, FedNAMs will be more scalable than the traditional GAMs.

093   2    BACKGROUND AND E XISTING WORKS
094
095
      Federated Learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2017), (Liu et al., 2024), (Balija et al., 2024), (Hard et al.,
096   2018) is a machine learning paradigm designed to train models across multiple decentralized devices
097   or servers while preserving data privacy. Unlike traditional centralized learning approaches, where
098   data is aggregated and processed in a central location, FL allows data to remain localized while
099   only sharing model updates. This approach is particularly beneficial in domains where data privacy
100   and security are paramount, such as healthcare, finance, and mobile applications. Neural Additive
101   Models (NAMs) (Agarwal et al., 2021) are machine learning models that combine the flexibility
102   and power of neural networks with the interpretability of additive models. NAMs decompose the
103   prediction task into individual functions, each contributing to the final prediction transparently. This
104
      decomposition facilitates a clearer understanding of how different features influence model’sel’s
      predictions, addressing the interpretability challenge inherent in traditional neural networks.
105
106   Federated learning has garnered significant attention in recent years, leading to the development of
107   various frameworks and methodologies to enhance its effectiveness and efficiency. McMahan et al.
      (McMahan et al., 2017) introduced the concept of Federated Averaging (FedAvg), a fundamental


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      algorithm in FL that aggregates model updates from multiple clients to create a global model. Subse-
109   quent research has focused on improving the robustness and scalability of FL systems. Bonawitz et al.
110   (Bonawitz, 2019) explored secure aggregation techniques to ensure privacy-preserving model updates,
111   while Kairouz et al. (Kairouz et al., 2021) provided a comprehensive survey of FL advancements,
112   highlighting the challenges and opportunities in the field. Interpretability has become a critical
113   aspect of machine learning, especially in applications requiring transparency and trust. Ribeiro et
114   al. (Ribeiro et al., 2016) introduced LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations), a
115   method to interpret predictions of any classifier by approximating it with an interpretable model
116   locally. Shapley values, derived from cooperative game theory, have also been employed to attribute
117
      contributions of individual features to model predictions, as seen in the work by Lundberg and Lee
      (Lundberg & Lee, 2017) on SHAP (Shapley Additive explanations). NAMs proposed by (Agarwal
118
      et al., 2021) is a novel approach for achieving high predictive accuracy and interpretability. By
119
      leveraging the structure of Generalized Additive Models (GAMs) and the learning capabilities of
120   neural networks, NAMs enable transparent and robust predictive models. The individual contributions
121   of features are modeled using neural networks, allowing non-linear relationships while maintaining
122   additive interpretability. Integrating interpretability into federated learning is an emerging research
123   area. Studies have begun exploring combining interpretable models with FL to ensure privacy and
124   transparency. For instance, (Zhang et al., 2024a) proposed FedGNN, a federated learning framework
125   using Graph Neural Networks emphasizing interpretability. Another approach by Gu et al. (Gu et al.,
126   2021) introduced interpretable FL by incorporating inherently interpretable decision trees into the FL
127   framework.

      3   N EURAL ADDITIVE MODELS
131
132   Neural Additive Models (NAMs) are a class of machine learning models that combine the flexibility
133   of neural networks with the interpretability of additive models. NAMs have gained attention for their
134   ability to provide accurate predictions while enabling human-understandable insights into how the
135   model makes its predictions. NAMs incorporate a series of neural network layers to a Generalized
136
      Additive Model (GAM) (Hastie, 2017). The neural network layers allow the model to capture complex
      interactions between variables, while the GAM component provides an interpretable baseline model.
137
      NAMs can be used for classification and regression tasks and trained using standard optimization
138
      techniques. Compared with other methods for interpreting black-box models, NAMs provide more
139   detailed and faithful explanations of the model’s behavior. Therefore, they are beneficial in high-
140   stakes domains such as healthcare, finance, and criminal justice. It is essential to understand how a
141   model makes its predictions. NAMs leverage innovative ExU hidden units, enabling sub-networks to
142   learn the more linear functions crucial for accurate additive models. By forming an ensemble of these
143   networks, NAMs can provide uncertainty estimates, enhance accuracy, and mitigate the high variance
144   that may arise from enforcing a highly linear learning process. We employed an NAM architecture
145   consisting of three hidden layers containing 20 neurons. During training, the model learns the weights
146   between the input features and the neurons in each layer, optimizing network’srk’s ability to capture
147
      linear and non-linear relationships in the data.

239   6     E XPERIMENTATION AND R ESULTS
240
241   In this research, we developed a federated learning framework that leverages a standard neural
242   network model and Neural Additive Models (NAMS) to identify both high and low contributing
243   features for each client. For experimentation, we considered three clients in a federated setup. Three
244   datasets used in this setup first go through the preprocessing by scaling features and converting the
245   target to a binary classification model for the UCI Heart Disease and Wine dataset while multi-label
246   classification for the Iris dataset. The dataset is split into training and testing sets and divided into
247
      three distinct clients, each receiving a portion of the training data. Each client is trained using the
      NAM model, which consists of several FeatureNN modules, one for each feature, allowing individual
248
      feature contributions to be learned interpretably. The NAM model concatenates outputs from the
249
      feature-specific neural networks and passes them through a final output layer for classification. The
250   framework utilizes a robust mechanism of performing hyperparameter tuning for dropouts, learning
251   rate, number of hidden layers in the network, and batch size using grid search across the three clients.
252   Training incorporates early stopping and learning rate scheduling to prevent overfitting and adapt
253   learning rates throughout training. Custom weight initialization using Xavier uniform distribution
254   is applied during training to improve convergence. Furthermore, early stopping is implemented to
255   halt training. Model equations representing each client’s specific feature contributions are derived,
256   providing interpretability by highlighting the most and least significant features. Finally, model
257   performance is evaluated based on classification accuracy and metrics such as the ROC-AUC score,
258
      with the best hyperparameters being selected based on validation accuracy across all clients.

466   7    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
467
468
      This work presents a novel framework for Federated Neural Additive Models (FedNAMs) using
469   Neural Additive Models, an innovative subfamily of Generalized Additive Models (GAMs) designed
470   to leverage deep learning techniques for scalability across large datasets and high-dimensional features.
471   Our approach addresses critical challenges associated with scalability and performance in federated
472   learning, all while maintaining the interpretability that GAMs are known for, distinguishing it from
473   traditional black-box deep neural networks (DNNs). Experiments on various datasets, including the
474   UCI Heart Disease, OpenML Wine, and Iris datasets, demonstrated that FedNAMs achieve state-
475   of-the-art performance across diverse tasks. Despite their smaller and faster architecture than other
476   neural-based GAMs, FedNAMs effectively capture the nuances of federated learning environments,
477
      where data is distributed across multiple clients. The observed plot confirms that the heart disease
      rate increases with age, aligning with real-life data and trends. This validates the correlation between
478
      age and heart disease in practical scenarios. Our results reveal that while the models trained on
479
      different clients, such as those using the UCI Heart Disease, OpenML Wine, and Iris datasets, exhibit
480   consistent feature contributions, the local data characteristics still influence specific parameter values.
481   This finding is crucial, as it suggests that FedNAMs can maintain personalization at the client level
482   while ensuring generalizability across the entire federated learning system. Future research will focus
483   on further enhancing the scalability and efficiency of federated NAMs, especially in scenarios with a
484   larger number of clients and more complex data distributions. Additionally, efforts will be directed
485   toward performing interpretability analysis in large language models (LLMs) to better understand the
      decision-making processes of these models in federated environments.
