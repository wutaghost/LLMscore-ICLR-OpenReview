009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011               In this paper, we introduce feedback-weight matching, a new method that facil-
012               itates reliable fine-tuning of fully connected neural networks using Direct Feed-
013
                  back Alignment (DFA). Although DFA has demonstrated potential by enabling
                  efficient and parallel updates of weight parameters through direct propagation of
014
                  the network’s output error, its usage has been primarily restricted to training net-
015
                  works from scratch. We provide the first analysis showing that existing standard
016               DFA struggles to fine-tune networks pre-trained via back-propagation. Through
017               an analysis of weight alignment (WA) and gradient alignment (GA), we show that
018               the proposed feedback-weight matching enhances DFA’s ability and stability in
019               fine-tuning pre-trained networks, providing insights into DFA’s behavior and char-
020               acteristics when applied to fine-tuning. In addition, we find that feedback-weight
021               matching, when combined with weight decay, not only mitigates over-fitting but
022               also further reduces the network output error, leading to improved learning per-
023               formance during DFA-based fine-tuning. Our experimental results show that, for
024
                  the first time, feedback-weight matching enables reliable and superior fine-tuning
                  across various fine-tuning tasks compared to existing standard DFA, e.g., achiev-
025
                  ing 7.97% accuracy improvement on image classification tasks (i.e., 82.67% vs.
026
                  74.70%) and 0.66 higher correlation score on NLP tasks (i.e., 0.76 vs. 0.10). The
027               code implementation is available at an anonymous GitHub repository1 .

030   1       I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Recently, a new training mechanism called Direct Feedback Alignment (DFA) (Nøkland, 2016)
033   has been proposed for deep neural networks to alleviate the weight transport problem (Grossberg,
034   1987; Crick, 1989). Based on the concept of Feedback Alignment (FA) (Lillicrap et al., 2016),
035   DFA passes the error of the output layer directly to each layer of the network to update the weight
036
      parameters without back-propagation (Rumelhart et al., 1986). By using random feedback matrices,
      the weight gradient of each layer is independently approximated from the directly passed error,
037
      enabling efficient training of networks through the parallel update of multiple layers. This contrasts
038
      with back-propagation that propagates the network error sequentially from the last to the first layer.
039
040   Although Direct Feedback Alignment (DFA) (Nøkland, 2016) has shown its potential in training
041   primarily for fully connected networks (Garg & Vempala, 2022; Launay et al., 2020), its application
042
      to fine-tuning (Devlin et al., 2018), i.e., adapting a pre-trained network to a new task, has been less
      studied until today despite its practical usefulness. In fact, it has been known that fine-tuning net-
043
      works with DFA is challenging (Chu & Bacho, 2024); the performance of networks fine-tuned with
044
      DFA is generally unreliable compared to that of those fine-tuned with back-propagation (Rumelhart
045   et al., 1986). Given that fine-tuning has become one of the practical and also effective ways of re-
046   utilizing pre-trained networks for various downstream tasks (Church et al., 2021), investigating how
047   DFA can be applied to the fine-tuning mechanism both theoretically and empirically is necessary.
048
      Enabling fine-tuning with Direct Feedback Alignment (DFA) (Nøkland, 2016) can not only broaden
049
      DFA’s usability but also introduce an alternative approach to current back-propagation-based fine-
050
      tuning (Rumelhart et al., 1986; Church et al., 2021). Currently, DFA has not yet been estab-
051   lished as a reliable stand-alone training method that can provide comparable performance to back-
052   propagation (Launay et al., 2019; Crafton et al., 2019). Thus, taking a wide range of well-pre-trained
053

      models, such as Transformer-based foundation models (Kenton & Toutanova, 2019), as the starting
055   point would be a practical strategy that can complement DFA’s unstable and limited learning capabil-
056   ities. Additionally, by incorporating DFA’s unique advantages, such as being back-propagation-free
057   and enabling parallel training, into the widely used fine-tuning scheme, we can explore new possibil-
058   ities for re-utilizing pre-trained models in a more agile, efficient, and biologically plausible manner,
059   in contrast to conventional back-propagation, which requires significantly more resources and time.
060
      In this paper, we introduce a DFA-based fine-tuning method, which investigates the feasibility of
061   Direct Feedback Alignment (DFA) (Nøkland, 2016) for fine-tuning deep neural networks, with the
062   aim of extending the scope of DFA to embrace various pre-trained networks. We first analyze
063   the reasons why the existing standard DFA, which updates the pre-trained weights using random
064   feedback matrices, does not perform well in fine-tuning. This analysis is based on the weight align-
065   ment (WA) and gradient alignment (GA) (Refinetti et al., 2021), which are two measures proposed to
066   estimate the state and learning performance of DFA. From this analysis, we propose the feedback-
067   weight matching, which first reconstructs the feedback matrices by decomposing the pre-trained
068   weights and then re-initializes the weights based on the reconstructed feedback matrices before
069
      starting fine-tuning. Additionally, we prove that applying weight decay (Krogh & Hertz, 1991) on
      top of feedback-weight matching considerably improves and stabilizes the fine-tuning performance
070
      of DFA, beyond the general regularization effect on weight parameters. Together with the simple
071
      yet effective feedback-weight matching, weight decay acts as a key facilitator for fine-tuning fully
072   connected networks with DFA. To the best of our knowledge, this work is the first attempt to explore
073   the possibility of applying DFA to fine-tuning of fully connected networks via an in-depth study.
074
075
      The experiments provide evaluation results consistent with our theoretical analysis; applying
      feedback-weight matching enables more effective and reliable fine-tuning of fully connected
076
      networks with DFA over various fine-tuning tasks, when compared to the existing standard
077
      DFA (Nøkland, 2016) that does not apply the proposed feedback-weight matching. For instance, the
078   image classification accuracy of fully connected networks fine-tuned with feedback-weight match-
079   ing reaches 82.67%, while that of standard DFA remains 74.70%. Also, it successfully fine-tunes
080   Transformer models (BERT) (Devlin et al., 2018) on NLP tasks, e.g., achieving 0.76 correlation
081   score, while the standard DFA barely conducts fine-tuning at all, i.e., achieving mere 0.10 cor-
082   relation score. The results demonstrate the potential for extending DFA towards the widely used
083   pre-training and fine-tuning strategy, moving beyond its limited usage in from-scratch training.

107   GA and WA. To better elucidate the dynamics of DFA (Nøkland, 2016), the concept of gradient
      alignment (GA) is introduced (Lillicrap et al., 2016). GA quantitatively assesses the similarity be-

108
      tween the weight gradients obtained through DFA and those derived via back-propagation (Rumel-
109   hart et al., 1986). This is achieved by comparing the weight updates generated from the identically
110   initialized weights by both methods. It has been hypothesized that a stronger (higher) GA corre-
111   sponds to enhanced learning performance in DFA. In addition, the concept of weight alignment
112   (WA) (Refinetti et al., 2021) has been introduced to evaluate the relationship between the weight
113   and the feedback matrix in DFA, suggesting that strong WA is associated with strong GA. Although
114   GA and WA have been instrumental in analyzing the learning efficacy of DFA, prior research has
115   not explored their utility in the context of fine-tuning. In contrast, this paper pioneers the application
116   of GA and WA concepts to systematically investigate the fine-tuning process in DFA.
117   Applicability to Transformers and CNNs. Some studies (Launay et al., 2020) explore the ap-
118   plicability of DFA (Nøkland, 2016) to various fully connected networks, including neural radiance
119   fields (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2021; Sitzmann et al., 2019), recommender systems (Guo et al.,
120   2017), and NLP (Vaswani, 2017; Merity et al., 2016). While they show that DFA can train a wide
121   range of deep architectures, they also reveal a significant performance gap between DFA and back-
122   propagation (Rumelhart et al., 1986), particularly in Transformer models (Vaswani, 2017). When
123
      applied to models not based on fully connected networks, such as CNNs, the performance gap be-
      tween DFA and back-propagation is even more pronounced. For instance, VGG-16 (Simonyan &
124
      Zisserman, 2014) on CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) trained with DFA achieves 1% top-1 ac-
125
      curacy (Launay et al., 2019), while back-propagation achieves 60%. Similarly, in ImageNet (Deng
126   et al., 2009), it is 6.2% vs. 53% (Crafton et al., 2019). Given that applying DFA to from-scratch
127   training scenarios 1) consistently underperforms relative to back-propagation, 2) takes a much longer
128   training time than fine-tuning, and 3) is limited to a narrower range of architectures, we argue that
129   utilizing DFA for fine-tuning would be a more effective, efficient, practical, and expedient approach.
130   Thus, in this study, we investigate and analyze the potential of employing DFA in fine-tuning, which
131   is conducive to the widely-used pre-train-and-fine-tune strategy (Devlin et al., 2018).
132   Applying DFA to Back-Propagation Weights. As described above, in CNNs, DFA encounters
133   challenges in effectively learning the necessary spatial information (Crafton et al., 2019). Similarly,
134   in fully connected networks, DFA is known to produce feature representation clusters that deviate
135   from those learned via back-propagation (Nøkland, 2016). Moreover, although stable training can
136   be achieved when transitioning from weights learned through DFA to back-propagation, the reverse
137   is not true; switching from back-propagation to DFA results in unstable training, and DFA fails to
138   fully recover its performance even after large training epochs (Chu & Bacho, 2024). These imply
139   the inherent difficulties in fine-tuning with DFA using weights pre-trained with back-propagation.
140   DFA with Weight Decay. In the study by Song et al. (2021), it is analyzed that weight decay (Krogh
141   & Hertz, 1991) can reduce the output error in fully connected networks when used with Feedback
142   Alignment (FA) (Lillicrap et al., 2016). Nevertheless, the analysis predominantly focuses on the
143   training of networks from scratch using FA, rather than on the fine-tuning process with DFA. This
144   work, for the first time, examines the impact of weight decay in the context of fine-tuning with DFA.
145   Our findings indicate that weight decay can be beneficial in fine-tuning with DFA, as it reduces
146
      network output error and over-fitting, thereby enhancing overall learning performance.

294   5     E XPERIMENT
295
      We evaluate the proposed feedback-weight matching on two types of fine-tuning tasks. First, it is
296   applied to image classification tasks using two fully connected networks with four and six hidden
297   layers, respectively. These networks are pre-trained with CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) and
298   TinyImageNet (Le & Yang, 2015) using back-propagation (Rumelhart et al., 1986), and then fine-
299   tuned on CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011), and STL-10 (Coates
300   et al., 2011) through DFA applying feedback-weight matching. Next, we apply it to natural lan-
301   guage processing (NLP) tasks using Transformer models, i.e., BERT-Tiny and Small (Kenton &
302   Toutanova, 2019; Turc et al., 2019), pre-trained on BookCorpus (Zhu et al., 2015) & Wikipedia,
303   and then fine-tuned with a set of GLUE tasks (Wang, 2018). For fine-tuning of BERT, feedback-
304
      weight matching is applied to the attention, intermediate, and block outputs of the encoder layers in
      a similar way to previous works (Launay et al., 2020) that attempt to apply DFA to Transformer’s
305
      attention architectures (Vaswani, 2017). It is important to highlight that even standard DFA has
306
      rarely been applied to Transformer models for from-scratch training due to its inherent challenges
307   and difficulties (Launay et al., 2020). Our experiment is the first attempt to apply DFA fine-tuning
308   to Transformer models (i.e., BERT), which is considered more challenging than from-scratch DFA
309   training. The detailed experimental setups are provided in Appendix E.

      5.3        A BLATION S TUDY: F EEDBACK M ATCHING , W EIGHT M ATCHING , AND W EIGHT D ECAY
413
414   Table 3 presents the impact of feedback matching, weight matching, and weight decay on fine-tuning
415   with DFA. To assess their effectiveness, we remove each of them in isolation. Removing feedback
416   matching results in a marginal performance decline, such as a reduction from 55.54% to 55.03%
417
      when the 6-layer network is fine-tuned from TinyImageNet to CIFAR-10. This marginal drop occurs
      because bypassing feedback matching applies random feedback matrices to the re-initialized weights
418
      that are amenable to arbitrary random feedback matrices, resulting in a reasonable level of WA
419
      and GA. In contrast, omitting weight matching leads to a relatively bigger performance drop, e.g.,
420   classification accuracy decreases from 83.16% to 79.77% when fine-tuning the 4-layer network from
421   TinyImageNet to SVHN. Similarly, the correlation score drops from 0.76 to -0.06 when fine-tuning
422   BERT-Small to STSB as shown in Table 5 (Appendix D). It is presumed that excluding weight
423   matching causes the pre-trained weights obtained by back-propagation, not by DFA, to be fine-tuned
424   with mismatched feedback matrices, thereby resulting in weak WA and GA.
425
      When weight decay is not applied, the fine-tuning of feedback-weight matching performance also
426   exhibits some declines, e.g., classification accuracy decreases from 55.38% to 48.82% when fine-
427   tuning the 4-layer network from CIFAR-100 to CIFAR-10. It should be noted that weight decay
428   appears to have minimal impact on fine-tuning of standard DFA when feedback-weight matching is
429   not applied; in our experiment, the classification accuracy even increases, such as from 54.38% to
430   56.75% when fine-tuning the 4-layer network from TinyImageNet to CIFAR-10. This demonstrates
431   the synergistic effect of feedback-weight matching and weight decay, i.e., reducing network output
      error as shown in Section 4.

487
488
      We propose feedback-weight matching, a method that enhances the fine-tuning capability and sta-
489   bility of Direct Feedback Alignment (DFA) for pre-trained networks. While standard DFA strug-
490   gles in fine-tuning networks trained via back-propagation, the proposed feedback-weight matching
491   improves weight and gradient alignment, boosting stability and performance of DFA fine-tuning.
492   Combined with weight decay, it also reduces over-fitting and network errors. Our experiments show
493   significant improvements in both image classification and NLP tasks compared to standard DFA.

721   C     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
722
723   Extending to Different Architectures. Although this study presents the significant potential of fine-
724   tuning with DFA, its current application is restricted to fully connected networks. This limitation
725   arises because, at present, DFA is predominantly effective for fully connected architectures, and
726   further research is needed to extend its applicability to other network types. In our future work, we
727   plan to explore the application of DFA fine-tuning to various network architectures, such as CNNs.
728   Meanwhile, we anticipate the development of more generalized methods that will enable DFA to be
729   applied across a broader range of network types, thereby enhancing the applicability of our work.
730   Improving Learning Performance. The learning performance of the proposed feedback-weight
731   matching is shown to surpass both 1) training networks with DFA from scratch and 2) fine-tuning
732   networks with DFA using random feedback matrices. While fine-tuning with DFA applying the
733   proposed method achieves superior and more stable performance compared to them, it still falls
734   short of the performance achieved with fine-tuning using back-propagation (Rumelhart et al., 1986).
735   We plan to explore how to achieve fine-tuning performance comparable to that of back-propagation
736
      by investigating DFA from its fundamental mechanism, along with the proposed method.
737   Proving Hypotheses. This work provides some hypotheses regarding fine-tuning and weight decay
738   in the context of DFA. For example, Conjecture 3.9 suggests that applying the proposed feedback-
739   weight matching can achieve strong weight alignment (WA) for fully connected networks of arbi-
740   trary depth. Additionally, Conjecture 4.2 posits that applying the proposed method to weight decay
741   enhances fine-tuning performance of DFA for fully connected networks of arbitrary layers. How-
742
      ever, formal proofs are necessary to substantiate these hypotheses and validate the efficacy of the
      proposed approach. In future research, we intend to generalize the propositions presented in this
743
      study to encompass various types of fully connected network architectures.

775   We train feedback matrices to reconstruct pre-trained weights that were trained using back-
776   propagation (Rumelhart et al., 1986). The loss function, in Equation (23), is used to guide the
777   feedback matching process. The two learned feedbacks are then combined and re-initialized into a
778   single weight matrix for each layer. We use the Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2014) without weight
779   decay or any scheduler. In fully connected networks, a learning rate of 1e-5 is applied, while in
780
      transformers (BERT) (Kenton & Toutanova, 2019; Turc et al., 2019), a learning rate of 1e-3 is used.
      For all experiments on the model and dataset, training is conducted for 3 epochs with a batch size of
