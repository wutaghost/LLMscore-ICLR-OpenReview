011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Artificial intelligence has become a crucial tool in drug discovery, excelling in tasks
014            such as molecular property prediction. An activity cliff, which refers to a minor
015            structural modification to a molecule resulting in a large change in its biological
016
               activity, poses a challenge in predictive modeling. The activity cliff depends on
               the interaction between the target and the ligand, which is largely overlooked by
017
               previous ligand-centric studies. However, the limited activity cliff data of target-
018
               ligand 3D complex restrain the predictive power of modern deep learning models.
019            In this paper, we introduce DockedAC, a new dataset incorporating the protein
020            target and 3D complex structure information for studying the problem of activity
021            cliffs. By matching protein binding information and ligand bioactivity, we employ
022            molecular docking to generate the complex structure for each activity value. The
023            DockedAC dataset contains 82,836 activity data on 52 protein targets with activity
024            cliff annotations, which serves as the first step towards activity cliff research with
025            large-scale 3D complex structures. We benchmark the dataset with traditional
026            machine learning and deep learning approaches. Our data and benchmark platform
027
               are available here.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Artificial intelligence (AI) is revolutionizing the drug discovery process as it is capable of large-scale
033
      data analysis, pattern recognition, and making accurate predictions (Vamathevan et al., 2019). One
      important application of AI models is to predict the biological activity of candidate compounds,
034
      thereby reducing labor-intensive tasks. A foundational concept in many AI algorithms is the similarity
035
      principle, which states that similar objects are likely to share similar features and predictions. However,
036   in drug discovery, a phenomenon called activity cliffs defies this idea and poses a challenge for
037   AI models. An activity cliff (AC) is defined as structurally similar compounds exhibiting large
038   differences in their biological activity against the same target (Maggiora, 2006), as illustrated
039   in Figure 1 (a).
040
      AC is crucial for drug discovery, as it complicates the process of optimizing drug candidates by con-
041
      founding the human experts in the understanding of usual structure-activity relationships (SARs) (Vogt
042   et al., 2011). On the other hand, knowledge about ACs can be highly beneficial when designing or
043   optimizing compounds to enhance the bioactivity of a given target (Cruz-Monteagudo et al., 2014;
044   Stumpfe et al., 2014). For example, replacing a single atom or adding a methyl group can result in
045   more than 100-fold improvement in bioactivity (Leung et al., 2012; Pennington & Moustakas, 2017).
046   However, the mechanisms of ACs in individual drug development programs can be different, making
047   it challenging for humans to process such information and derive transferable experiences. Therefore,
048   various efforts have been made to computationally predict ACs (Stumpfe et al., 2019).
049   Compared to quantitative structure-activity relationship (QSAR) modeling for other molecular
050   properties, AC predictions are challenging due to the non-robustness that ACs introduce to the
051   models (Cruz-Monteagudo et al., 2016). Early attempts use machine learning methods such as
052   random forest (RF) and support vector machine (SVM) to predict the AC of a compound pair (Guha,
053   2012; Heikamp et al., 2012). To further improve AC predictions, matched molecular pair (MMP)
      kernel (Tamura et al., 2021) and condensed graphs of reaction representations (Horvath et al., 2016)

      In most previous works, the study of ACs has been ligand-centric and lacked 3D structure considera-
075   tion, failing to account for interactions between the ligand and the protein target (Husby et al., 2015;
076   Tamura et al., 2023). Many mechanisms of ACs can be analyzed from the structural perspective,
077   such as hydrogen bonding, ionic interactions, hydrophobic or aromatic group interactions (Hu et al.,
078   2012) (e.g. Figure 1 (b)). It is therefore natural to incorporate the information of structures into the
079   modeling of ACs. However, available structural data for ACs is very limited, with only 215 pairs of
080   AC ligands (Husby et al., 2015). Such data scarcity issue makes it challenging to train deep learning
081   models effectively.

      In this paper, we present DockedAC, a new dataset to tackle the problem of ACs from a structural
      perspective, aiming at AC modeling with large data and modern AI algorithms. Unlike previous
100
      studies, our dataset includes not only the information on protein targets but also the target-ligand
101
      complex structures built using molecular docking (Figure 2). We collect the bioactivity data of
102   more than 80,000 ligands across over 50 protein targets. The protein targets are mapped to their
103   corresponding structures in the RCSB Protein Data Bank (PDB) (Berman et al., 2000), with the
104   ligand binding sites identified for docking. In addition, we also provide a framework to benchmark
105   the performance of traditional machine learning and deep learning methods on AC prediction and
106   study the effect of ACs on model performance. Our dataset would be beneficial to enhance model
107   interpretability, inspire the design of promising algorithms on ACs, and foster the development of
      more effective 3D feature extraction methods.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
111   Previous works on AC prediction. As a crucial phenomenon in drug discovery, ACs are not only
112
      popular in medicinal chemistry but also attract the attention of the computer science and intelligence
      community. Various methods of machine learning and deep learning have been applied to the
113
      prediction of ACs (Guha, 2012; Heikamp et al., 2012; Iqbal et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022; Park et al.,
114
      2022). In addition, recent research has explored ACs from several different perspectives, such as
115   QSAR modeling (Dablander et al., 2023), the complexity of the learning methods (Tamura et al.,
116   2023), and benchmarking of different approaches (van Tilborg et al., 2022). However, due to the
117   limited availability of data, almost all existing works focus on the ligand-centric view of ACs, where
118   the ligand is modeled with a 2D molecular graph or 1D SMILES sequence (Weininger, 1988), without
119   incorporating the 3D structure and the protein target information. The 3D activity cliff (3DAC)
120   database, used in a study on structure-based AC prediction, contains only 219 3DAC pairs (Hu et al.,
121   2012; Husby et al., 2015). This motivates us to construct a larger dataset for structure-based ACs.
122
123
      Existing AC datasets. Although there are several works on AC prediction, few good benchmarking
124
      datasets exist. Several works rely on self-collected datasets and are not well documented, or have
125   little information provided about the protein targets (Jiménez-Luna et al., 2022; Dablander et al.,
126   2023; Tamura et al., 2023). Two recent works on AC datasets both collect data from the ChEMBL
127   database (Mendez et al., 2019), either for the classification of a pair of AC ligands (Zhang et al.,
128   2023c) or the regression of the bioactivity value of individual AC ligands (van Tilborg et al., 2022).
129   These datasets do not consider modeling the 3D structure of the binding complex, rendering them
130   less appropriate for accurate AC prediction. In our work, we match the obtained bioactivity data to
131   the corresponding protein structures in PDB and generate target-ligand binding structures.
132
133
      3D protein-ligand binding affinity prediction. In this work, we consider the regression problem
134   and train different models to predict the bioactivity in the presence of the AC. Given the target-ligand
135   complex structures, nearly all the models for binding affinity prediction use the PDBbind dataset,
136   including convolutional neural networks, graph neural networks, and attention-based models (Zhang
137   et al., 2023a; Jiang et al., 2021a; Jiménez et al., 2018; Tan et al., 2024). A comprehensive review
138   of the drug-target interaction prediction can be found in Zeng et al. (2024). In molecular property
139   prediction, activity cliffs can significantly impact model predictions (Deng et al., 2023). We evaluate
140   the performance of 3D target-ligand affinity prediction models with our dataset and compare them
141   with other machine learning or deep neural network models with ligand-only inputs.

145
146   In summary, the construction of DockedAC involves several key steps: data collection, AC identi-
147   fication, target structure annotation, and target-ligand complex generation. The following section
148   provides a detailed explanation of each step in this process.

      3.1    DATA C OLLECTION
152
153
      We first collect bioactivity data (Inhibitory Constant, Ki ; Half-Maximal Effective Concentration,
      EC50 ; Half-Maximal Inhibitory Concentration, IC50 in [nM]) of 64 protein targets from ChEMBL
154
      v33 (Mendez et al., 2019) with the ChEMBL web resource client (Davies et al., 2015). To eliminate
155
      significant sources of error, the obtained raw data is checked for validity and reliability. In particular,
156   a ligand is removed if (a) it fails the sanitization and standardization by RDkit (Bento et al., 2020); or
157   (b) it has a standard deviation larger than 10 in case of multiple entries. To ensure enough samples of
158   a target for model training, the targets with fewer than 500 ligands are dropped. Finally, the negative
159   logarithm p is applied to the bioactivity values as the regression target (denoted as pKi ; pEC50 ;
160   pIC50 in [log units]) (Stewart & Watson, 1983). After this process, we have the CHEMBL id of
161   the target and the corresponding ligands with bioactivity values (the first step in Figure 3 (a)). The
      resulting dataset has 54 protein targets.

191   3.2    ACTIVITY C LIFF I DENTIFICATION
192
193
      An activity cliff is a pair of structurally similar compounds with a large difference in bioactivities
      against a given target. To detect pairs of similar ligands, we take a consensus of three similarity
194
      measures to define the activity cliff pairs following van Tilborg et al. (2022): (a) substructure
195
      similarity, which is calculated via the Tanimoto coefficient on the extended connectivity fingerprint
196   (ECFP) (Tanimoto, 1958; Rogers & Hahn, 2010); (b) scaffold similarity, which is determined by the
197   Tanimoto coefficient on the ECFP of the generic Murcko scaffolds (Bemis & Murcko, 1996); (c)
198   SMILES similarity, computed as one minus the scaled Levenshtein distance between the canonical
199   SMILES (Levenshtein et al., 1966). If any of these three similarities is equal to or larger than 0.9, the
200   pair of ligands is checked for their difference in bioactivity. Currently, there are no widely accepted
201   quantitative definitions of ACs (Stumpfe et al., 2020). Following previous works (Jiménez-Luna
202   et al., 2022; Hu & Bajorath, 2012), a bioactivity difference larger than one order of magnitude (10×)
203   is used to identify activity cliffs (the second step in Figure 3 (a)).
204
205   3.3    TARGET AND S TRUCTURE A NNOTATION
206
207
      To generate the target-ligand complex, it is essential to identify the 3D structure of the target protein
      and its binding site. This mapping process is illustrated in Figure 3 (b). Given a target CHEMBL
208
      id, the first step is to map the target protein to its UniProt id (Consortium, 2023) and find all the
209
      structures corresponding to the UniProt id in the PDB. We utilize the PDBbind database for initial
210   searching (Wang et al., 2004). If PDBbind does not include the target, we then search for it in the
211   whole PDB. The obtained structures with a small molecule ligand are chosen and aligned to check if
212   the ligands bind to the same site. If the binding site is not unique, the target is discarded (see Figure 9
213   (a)(b)). After alignment, ligands sharing the same binding site are extracted and compared with the
214   ligands that have activity labels from ChEMBL. Suppose there exists a pair of ligands, one from
215   the PDB database and one from the ChEMBL database, with a similarity (Tanimoto coefficient of
      the fingerprints) larger than 0.99. In that case, the target structure and the binding site are used.


                                                               4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Otherwise, the target is removed from the dataset. When multiple structures satisfy this condition,
217   the structure with the best resolution is selected. This procedure ensures the correspondence between
218   the bioactivity values and the target binding site. As a result of this structure mapping process, two
219   targets are removed, resulting in a final dataset of 52 protein targets.
220
221   3.4   C OMPLEX S TRUCTURE G ENERATION
222
223   Next, molecular docking is employed to generate the target-ligand complex for each target, illustrated
224   in Figure 3 (c). The docking tool DSDP is used, which combines the pose sampling algorithm of
225   AutoDock Vina and GPU acceleration (Huang et al., 2023; Trott & Olson, 2010). Since the binding
226   site information of the target is already known, local docking is performed within the given binding
227   region of a 25 Å wide box. The docking results are reviewed to ensure the agreement between the
228   ligand bioactivity value and the binding conformation. A docking score (in kcal/mol) larger than zero
229
      indicates an inaccurate docking conformation (e.g. Figure 9 (c)), and the corresponding ligand is
      removed from the dataset.
230
231
232   3.5   DATASET S PLITTING
233
      To prepare the dataset for benchmarking, the ligands of each target are split into a training and test set
234
      using a double-stratified sampling strategy (van Tilborg et al., 2022). In particular, the ligands of each
235
      target are first clustered into 5 groups based on their substructural similarity (Tanimoto similarity of
236   the ECFP). A two-stage stratified splitting (80%/20%) is then performed on the cluster label and the
237   AC label. This procedure ensures that the training and test set have similar ligand distributions.

262
263   In addition to the DockedAC dataset, we also provide a framework to benchmark the performance of
264   various machine learning and deep learning methods on AC prediction. This section briefly introduces
265   our benchmark setup, followed by a detailed presentation of the experimental results and analyses in
266   the subsequent section (Sec. 5).

268   4.1   M ODEL D ESCRIPTIONS
269
      In general, three types of learning models are included in our framework:

270
       • Four classic machine learning algorithms for structure-activity relationship prediction using
271      handcrafted molecular descriptors: K-nearest neighbor (KNN) (Cover & Hart, 1967), random
272      forest (RF) (Breiman, 1996), gradient boosting machine (GBM) (Friedman, 2001), and support
273      vector regression (SVM) (Hearst et al., 1998).
274    • Deep learning models that only leverage the 1D or 2D ligand information, including (1) three
275      1D sequential models: transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), long short-term memory (LSTM)
276      networks (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997), and 1D CNN (Kimber et al., 2021), and (2) four 2D
277      structural graph neural network (GNN) models: message passing neural network (MPNN) (Gilmer
278      et al., 2017), graph convolutional network (GCN) (Kipf & Welling, 2016), graph attention network
279
         (GAT) (Vaswani et al., 2017), and attentive fingerprint (AFP) (Xiong et al., 2019).
       • Two 3D structural GNN models: IGN (Jiang et al., 2021a) and SS-GNN (Zhang et al., 2023a) are
280
         included to study the effect of 3D structures, as our dataset contains 3D structural information.
281
282
283
      4.2   F EATURE D ESCRIPTIONS
284   For machine learning algorithms, following previous work van Tilborg et al. (2022), we consider four
285   types of molecule descriptors from several levels of complexity as follows. (1) Extended Connectivity
286   Fingerprints (ECFPs) (Rogers & Hahn, 2010): circular topological fingerprints used for molecular
287   characterization. (2) Molecular ACCess System (MACCS) keys (Durant et al., 2002): a set of
288   structural keys utilized for substructure searching and similarity analysis, encoding specific chemical
289   substructures or patterns. (3) Physicochemical (PhysChem) descriptors (Walters & Murcko, 2002):
290   11 properties indicative of drug-likeness. (4) Weighted Holistic Invariant Molecular (WHIM) descrip-
291   tors (Todeschini et al., 1998): capturing three-dimensional geometrical and electronic properties of
292
      molecules, invariant to rotation and translation.
293   Deep learning methods eliminate the need for handcrafted descriptors, allowing direct learning
294   from “unstructured” data representations. For sequential methods, the Simplified Molecular Input
295   Line Entry System (SMILES) (Weininger, 1988) string is used, which is popular for its ability
296   to describe the structure of chemical species in text format that sequential methods can naturally
297   process. For 2D GNN models, we adopt molecular graphs, a representation of structural formula
298
      where nodes represent atoms and edges represent bonds. For 3D GNN models, we employ the target-
      ligands complexes we have processed that incorporate detailed 3D structure information. Detailed
299
      descriptions of the features can be found in Appendix A.4.
300
301
302
      4.3   M ETRICS AND I MPLEMENTATIONS
303
      For each target, we train separate regression models on the bioactivity values (pKi /pEC50 /pIC50
304   in [log units]). The regression setting makes it possible to compare the AC and non-AC tasks. The
305   root-mean-square error (RMSE) is employed as the evaluation metric to quantify the performance.
306   The RMSE represents the error calculated across all ligands, whereas RMSEcliff specifically denotes
307   the error computed for AC ligands. For model implementation, we conduct hyperparameter tuning
308   through grid search and report the results from five-fold cross-validation. Further details on these
309   methods and their implementations are provided in Appendix A.3 and A.5.

481   6           C ONCLUSION
482
483   In this paper, we introduce DockedAC, a new dataset for ACs with 3D complex structures. The
484   dataset contains over 80k ligands from 52 protein targets, with the 3D structure of each target
485   annotated by a unique known binding site. Molecular docking is performed to generate the protein-
      ligand complex structures for at least 500 ligands per target. We benchmark the dataset with various


                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      machine learning and deep learning methods, finding that for GNN-based methods, introducing
487   3D information can enhance AC prediction and reduce the gap between general and AC activity
488   prediction. Our experiments suggest that the absolute error of AC prediction is target-dependent,
489   and the ratio of AC ligands in the training set is an important factor influencing the difference
490   between general and AC activity prediction. In addition, deep learning methods cannot compete
491   with traditional machine learning methods using fingerprints, highlighting the need to develop new
492   3D QSAR algorithms. DockedAC serves as a first step in this direction from the perspective of 3D
493   complex structures and target-ligand interactions.
494
495   Limitations. While our dataset contains a variety of protein targets, the distribution of different
496
      types of targets is imbalanced, with several popular drug targets dominating. Diversifying the target
      types is beneficial to improve the generalization of successive models. Furthermore, the mapping
497
      between the target and the unique binding site may introduce bias, as some targets have unknown
498
      binding sites. We plan to conduct routine validity checks to update the dataset as more protein
499   structures are deposited into the PDB. Lastly, the complex structures generated by molecular docking
500   may be inaccurate, and more advanced approaches such as molecular simulation can be employed to
501   refine the complex structures.
502
503   Future Work. DockedAC provides the foundation for studying ACs from a structural perspective,
504   and we anticipate that it will inspire the development of novel 3D QSAR algorithms. Future research
505   could focus on designing deep learning architectures that effectively capture and utilize 3D structural
506   information to improve AC prediction accuracy. Additionally, the dataset could be expanded to
507   include more diverse targets and ligands, as well as refined complex structures, to further enhance its
508   value for AI-driven drug discovery. We believe that DockedAC dataset will foster the development
509
      of innovative computational methods and contribute to the advancement of rational drug design.

779   A.3       BASELINE M ODELS
780
781
      In this work, we integrate 13 recent baselines commonly used for structure-activity relationship
      prediction, including four traditional machine learning algorithms: KNN, RF, GBM, and SVM; three
782
      sequential models: LSTM, Transformer, and 1D CNN; four 2D GNN models: GCN, GAT, MPNN,
783
      and AFP; and two 3D structure GNN models: IGN and SS-GNN. The detailed descriptions of these
784   approaches are listed in the following:
785
786   • KNN (Cover & Hart, 1967). K-Nearest Neighbor (KNN) is a simple, non-parametric method that
787     predicts the target molecule’s response by averaging the response of the k-nearest neighbors from
788     the training set.
789   • RF (Breiman, 1996). Random Forest (RF) is an ensemble method that combines the outputs of
790     multiple decision trees to improve accuracy and reduce over-fitting. Each decision tree is built upon
791     a subset of the training set, and the final prediction is obtained by averaging the results from these
792
        individual trees.
      • GBM (Friedman, 2001). Similar to RF, Gradient Boosting Machine (GBM) also combines the
793
        predictions of multiple decision trees. However, in GBM, these trees are built sequentially, with
794
        each subsequent tree specially designed to correct the errors of its predecessors.
795   • SVM (Hearst et al., 1998). Support Vector Machine (SVM) aims to identify a linear regression
796     plane in a higher-dimensional space created by applying a designated kernel function. In this work,
797     the Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel is used.
798   • Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017). The Transformer model leverages self-attention mechanisms
799     to capture dependencies across different positions in the input sequence. In our work, we employed
800     the pretrained ChemBERTa (Chithrananda et al., 2020) architecture, which has been trained on 10
801     million compounds.
802   • LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997). Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) can capture
803
        temporal dependencies and patterns in sequential data by maintaining long-term memory through
        their gated structure. In this work, we employ SMILES strings as the input for the model.
804
      • 1D CNN (Kimber et al., 2021). Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) uses convolutional filters to
805
        aggregate spatial information from adjacent positions. For processing sequential SMILES string
806     data, we employ 1D CNNs that perform convolutional operations along a single dimension.
807   • MPNN (Gilmer et al., 2017). Message Passing Neural Network (MPNN) operates by iteratively
808     passing messages between nodes and updating their representations based on neighboring nodes.

      A.4       M ODEL F EATURES .
868
869   In addition to the molecular descriptor used for machine learning algorithms (introduced in Sec. 4.2),
870   we further delve into the featurization for deep learning models. Detailed information on all featur-
871   izations and the corresponding models used can be found in Table 4.
872
      For sequential methods, SMILES strings were encoded as one-hot vectors, with truncation applied
873   to strings exceeding 200 characters. To enhance model robustness, tenfold data augmentation was
874   applied using up to nine additional noncanonical SMILES strings for each SMILES string in the
875   dataset, generated via RDKit (Landrum et al., 2013).
876
      For 2D GNN methods, the node has the following features: atom type (one-hot), atomic vertex degree
877
      (one-hot), orbital hybridization (one-hot), aromaticity (one-hot), atomic weight (float), formal charge
878
      (integer), number of radical electrons (integer), and number of connected hydrogens (integer). For
879   MPNN and AFP, two one-hot bond features are used for the edges, i.e., the bond type and conjugation.
880
881   For SS-GNN, there are 11 node features, including atom type, formal charge, hybridization, atom
882
      valence, atom degree, number of hydrogens, chirality, atomic mass, aromatic, atom coordinates, and
      whether belonging to the protein. The edge features include covalent bond type, aromatic, bond
883
      length, bond direction, bond stereochemistry, and edge type. The atom coordinates and bond length
884
      are extracted from the 3D structures. Further details can be found in Zhang et al. (2023a).
885
886   For IGN, it uses similar 2D node and edge features. In addition, IGN uses four new edge features
887   from the 3D structures, including bond length, angle statistics, area statistics, and distance statistics.
888
      For detailed descriptions of the features, see Jiang et al. (2021a).
