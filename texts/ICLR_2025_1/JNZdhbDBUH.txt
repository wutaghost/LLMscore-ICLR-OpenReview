009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This article explores federated long-tail learning (Fed-LT) tasks, where clients
012            possess private, heterogeneous data, collectively following a global long-tail dis-
013            tribution. We propose two methods: (a) Client Re-weighted Prior Analyzer
014            (CRePA), which balances the global model’s performance on tail and non-tail
015            categories and enhances performance on tail categories while maintaining it on
016
               non-tail categories. (b) Federated Long-Tail Causal Intervention Model (FedLT-
               CI) computes clients’ causal effects on the global model’s performance in the tail
017
               and enhances the interpretability of Fed-LT. Extensive experiments on the CIFAR-
018
               10-LT and CIFAR-100-LT datasets demonstrate the following: (1) CRePA outper-
019            forms other baselines, achieving state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance. In scenar-
020            ios with high heterogeneity and severe long-tail distributions, CRePA improves
021            tail performance by 6.3% and 5% compared to CReFF and FedGrab, respectively.
022            (2) FedLT-CI, by intervening during the aggregation process in federated learning
023            (FL), effectively enhances the tail performance of baselines while maintaining sta-
024            ble non-tail performance. For instance, applying the intervention strategy to the
025            FedAvg, FedGrab, and CRePA models improves tail performance by 4.5%, 2.1%,
026            and 1.9%, respectively.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      The issue of tackling the long-tail problem of client data and server’s aggregated data in FL has
031   emerged as a crucial concern in algorithm design. Unlike traditional machine learning, FL brings the
032   learning task directly to the end-user devices for local training, requiring only intermediate param-
033   eters (such as gradients) to be sent to the server for model aggregation and updates. This approach
034   contributes to obtaining a globally applicable model while preserving the privacy of client-side data,
035   facilitating the development of trustworthy intelligent systems (Xiao et al., 2023). Despite the great
036   potential of FL, its real-world applications still face numerous challenges. One significant challenge
037   is the heterogeneity of data among clients, i.e., Non-IID (Li et al., 2022b; Xu et al., 2022; Li et al.,
038   2023; Tang et al., 2024). Additionally, while the assumption is that the distribution of data sets for
039
      clients may be locally balanced, aggregating the data from various clients might result in a severe
      long-tail distribution issue, named the Fed-LT (Xiao et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2023; Shang et al.,
040
      2022). For example, there may be significant differences in data between different hospitals, and
041
      different types of diseases exhibit a severe long-tail distribution (Caesar et al., 2020). Although
042   the tail categories are less common in a long-tail distribution, they are crucial for identifying rare
043   diseases(Zhang et al., 2023), dangerous behaviors in autonomous driving (Wang et al., 2022), and
044   more.
045
      In the FL scenario, if the training data of different clients exhibit long-tailed and heterogeneous
046
      characteristics, the problem becomes complex and challenging because each client may contain dif-
047
      ferent categories and quantities of tail data. This situation significantly impacts the performance
048   of the global model, especially in tail categories. Currently, algorithms used to address data het-
049   erogeneity in FL ignore the potential long-tailed issues (McMahan et al., 2017; Karimireddy et al.,
050   2020; Luo et al., 2021). However, if one directly adopts some long-tail learning algorithms to ad-
051   dress the long-tail problem in FL, local or global class distribution information is needed as prior
052   knowledge for optimization, which may expose potential privacy concerns (Shang et al., 2022). For
053   example, some methods typically rely on statistical information from client data, such as the sample
      count or feature distribution of different classes, as well as global class distribution information.

054
      Some researchers have proposed solutions to the heterogeneity and long-tail problems in FL (Shang
055   et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023). These methods have shown significant improvements in tail perfor-
056   mance, but they all come with increased communication costs. Importantly, none of these methods
057   consider the impact of different clients on the aggregation model’s performance on tail data from a
058   causal perspective, and they lack interpretability.
059
      Given the challenges above, this paper addresses the long-tail and heterogeneity issues in FL by
060
      proposing two novel models to enhance tail performance: Client Re-weighted Prior Analyzer and
061   the Federated Long-Tail Causal Intervention Model. The CRePA learns the prior distribution of
062   weights for each client through tail and non-tail gradient information (as shown in Fig. 2). Thus,
063   it allows flexible balancing between tail and non-tail data in the heterogeneous datasets of different
064   clients, thereby improving the global model’s performance on tail categories.
065
      In order to address the long-tail challenges more comprehensively in FL, we propose a novel and
066
      general FedLT-CI model inspired by the do-operator proposed by Pearl (1995) (as shown in Fig.
067   4). This model improves the traditional FL aggregation process by introducing an intervention
068   mechanism, significantly enhancing tail performance (as shown in Fig. 1). The FedLT-CI can
069   discern the causal effects of each client on the server-aggregated model regarding tail data. This
070   model enhances the overall model’s performance on tail data and possesses interpretability (for
071   instance, it can be understood as likening the performance of the aggregated model to a disease,
072   treating client participation as a form of treatment, and evaluating its causal effect to measure the
073   treatment outcome).
074   Our main contributions are summarized as follows.
075
076
      1. We propose CRePA, a client-weighted sampling algorithm that uses Monte Carlo sampling to
      dynamically allocate weights, addressing heterogeneity and long-tail challenges in FL. Without re-
077
      lying on prior client knowledge, CRePA employs an adaptive loss function to seamlessly integrate
078
      tail and non-tail gradients, enabling online learning of weight parameters for flexible model adapta-
079   tion. CRePA outperforms SOTA models, especially in enhancing tail data performance.
080
081   2. We propose FedLT-CI, a model that enhances tail performance in FL by leveraging causal in-
082
      terventions. FedLT-CI assesses clients’ causal effects on the global model’s tail performance and
      adjusts intervention frequency to mitigate the impact of weaker contributors. This adaptable mecha-
083
      nism integrates seamlessly with most FL algorithms. Experiments demonstrate that FedLT-CI signif-
084
      icantly improves tail performance while maintaining non-tail performance across various baselines.
085
086   3. Without compromising algorithm performance, our algorithm utilizes fewer clients in the in-
087   formation aggregation process due to the introduction of causal inference. This ingenious design
088
      improves the model’s performance on the tail data and reduces communication overhead. In this
      way, a core issue of FL algorithms, namely security, has been indirectly improved.

      2.1   F EDERATED L EARNING WITH DATA H ETEROGENEITY
103
104   Researchers have proposed various methods to investigate the challenge of heterogeneous or im-
105   balanced data distribution in FL (Xiao et al., 2024). To address the challenge of heterogeneous or
106   imbalanced data distribution in FL, researchers have proposed various methods, such as adding a
107   regularizer locally to modify the local loss function (Li et al., 2020; Durmus et al., 2021). Karim-
      ireddy et al. (2020) proposed a control variable-based method to reduce client distribution drift


                                                                                                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      caused by differences in data distribution. CCVR samples virtual features from an approximated
109   Gaussian Mixture Model for classifier calibration to avoid uploading raw features to the server (Luo
110   et al., 2021). However, the above methods neglect the global long-tail distribution, leading to poor
111   performance on tail classes.
112
113   2.2    L ONG - TAILED L EARNING
114
115   Due to the widespread presence of long-tail data in the real world, long-tail learning in machine
116   learning has garnered considerable attention from various researchers (Zhang et al., 2023; Wang
117   et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Narasimhan et al., 2024). Methods include techniques that re-weight
118   based on the frequencies of different classes (Cao et al., 2019) or utilize information augmentation
119   techniques to enhance the performance of tail categories (Li et al., 2021). Among these, re-weighting
120
      methods aim to improve the model’s performance in tail categories by balancing losses or gradients.
      In addition, some methods decouple the training phase into representation learning and classifier
121
      re-training (Kang et al., 2020; Shang et al., 2022). These methods aim to generate more general
122
      representations and enhance the performance of tail categories on a re-balanced classifier. However,
123   most of the methods mentioned rely on the global class distribution. During FL’s training process,
124   collecting information on the class distribution from each client is impractical to obtain the global
125   class distribution. This renders the majority of long-tail learning methods unsuitable for the FL
126   scenario.
127
128   2.3    C AUSAL E FFECT
129
130   Some researchers directly learn causal effects from local data (Alaa & Schaar, 2018; Künzel et al.,
131   2019; Yao et al., 2018). Others use the Structural Causal Model (SCM) proposed by Pearl (1995) to
132   estimate the causal effects involving latent confounding variables (Madras et al., 2019; Kawakami
133   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Shirahmad Gale Bagi et al., 2023). Shirahmad Gale Bagi et al.
134
      (2023) designed a causal model to address the motion prediction task. In this paper, we employ the
      SCM method to calculate the causal effects of clients on the global model’s performance in the tail.
135
      Unlike previous methods for causal effect estimation, which typically require access to all data, in
136
      FL, we cannot directly aggregate all client data, posing unique challenges (Li et al., 2024). To the
137   best of our knowledge, no solutions have been designed from a causal effects perspective to address
138   the challenges of Fed-LT. Therefore, the method proposed in this paper represents a novel attempt
139   to tackle the issues associated with Fed-LT, and the experimental results validate the effectiveness
140   of this approach.

      4.1   P ROBLEM D ESCRIPTION

239   Problem setting & notations: In the case where the client data sources Dk have completely differ-
240   ent distributions, our goal is to develop a global causal intervention model that satisfies the following
241   two conditions: (i) The causal intervention model is trained in a process where information from
242   each source is not shared with external parties, and the server is not aware of any client data infor-
243   mation in advance, (ii) During the iteration process, the causal intervention model can estimate the
244   causal effects of each client on the global model’s performance on the tail. The model is illustrated
245   in Fig. 3, and its role is to intervene in the aggregation process through causal effects, allowing the
246
      aggregated model to perform better on tail data.
247   Causal effects: Given the causal model trained under the above settings, we consider the perfor-
248   mance of the global model on tail data as the outcome (Y ), When intervening on client k ∈ [K], this
249   client is treated as the treatment, while the remaining clients serve as confounding factors. In a loose
250   sense, our interest can be viewed as estimating the individual treatment effect (ITE). In this scenario,
251   treating each client as an individual, every client can be considered a target for intervention. Our
252
      primary goal is to estimate ITE, i.e., the causal effect on the global model’s tail performance when
      client k participates or does not participate. This can be expressed using the following formula:

505   mental results indicate that CRePA, by leveraging both tail and non-tail gradient information during
506

      When the communication rounds exceed 100, CRePA begins to increase the distribution expecta-
507
      tion for clients with a higher proportion of tail data, and this trend becomes more pronounced with

      increasing rounds. For example, as the model has already converged on non-tail classes during the

510   mean during later aggregation processes. In contrast, client c16, with abundant tail data, experi-

512   model has converged on the categories of the head and middle, while the tail data can still achieve

      Impact of F on the FedLT-CI: To investigate the effect of the hyper-parameter F, we observed
515   the variation in test accuracy for different values of F, as shown in Fig. 6(a). The results indicate
516   that regardless of the value of F, FedLT-CI can improve the performance on tail data in imbalanced
517   datasets. However, the smaller the F, the relatively better the performance. Due to the low F,
518   clients with lower causal effects on the global model will be excluded more frequently during the
519   aggregation process. Further analysis of F on other baselines will be provided in the Appendix D.1.

      Impact of N on the FedLT-CI: N as a critical hyper-parameter of the FedLT-CI. To test its im-

522   IFG =100. We plotted the changes in tail accuracy for different values of N , as shown in Fig. 6(b).
523   It is evident that there are many suitable choices for N . Therefore, tuning N in causal intervention
524   is relatively easy in this setting. For more ablation experiments, please refer to the Appendix D.2.

527   6     C ONCLUSION
528
529   This paper proposes two innovative algorithms to tackle the challenges of data heterogeneity and
530   the long-tail phenomenon in Fed-LT. Firstly, we introduce the CRePA, which divides gradient in-
531
      formation into tail and non tail information to achieve online learning of weight prior distribution
      parameters for each client. Experimental results demonstrate that, compared to other baselines, the
532
      CRePA algorithm not only preserves the performance of non-tail data but also enhances the per-
533
      formance of tail data. To address the interpretability challenges in Fed-LT, we draw inspiration
534   from Pearl’s causal structure model and present the FedLT-CI. FedLT-CI assesses the causal effects
535   of individual clients on the global model’s performance on tail data and intervenes during the ag-
536   gregation process to significantly enhance tail effects. Experimental results showcase that, across
537   multiple experiments, the FedLT-CI algorithm significantly improves the performance of various
538   baseline models on tail data. This algorithm not only achieves promising results in addressing the
539   challenges of Fed-LT but also makes a breakthrough in interpretability. It introduces fresh ideas and
      methods for this direction, establishing a solid foundation for future research.
