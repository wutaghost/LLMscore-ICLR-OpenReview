011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent generalizable 3D reconstruction methods have been facing challenges in
014            constructing geometry-consistent 3D features. This is primarily due to the source
015            views conveying redundant information to the sampled 3D points that they do not
016            observe, resulting in the samples struggling to distinguish the correct observations
017            of them. We attribute this issue to that canonical supervision methods focus solely
018
               on the rendered target view from a single viewpoint, overlooking source views
               that capture the scene from different perspectives. With this insight, we pioneer a
019
               supervision method for source views, which can be applied alongside existing target
020
               view supervision in each iteration. Specifically, we define the Learned Geometry
021            of the Scene (LGS) as source-view depth distributions, which are derived from
022            the weights of source views for each sampled 3D point. To regularize the LGS to
023            better model the real-world geometry, we introduce a novel unsupervised learning
024            objective, which mitigates the optimization bias in existing objectives and ensures
025            the LGS is more concentrated near the real-world geometry surface. Regularizing
026            the LGS effectively helps filter out irrelevant source views for each sampled 3D
027            point, and thus noticeably improves the performance of backbones. Mathematical
028            proof is provided to validate the proposed objective, and extensive experiments
029
               demonstrate that our supervision method significantly improves both NeRF- and
               3DGS-based backbones with negligible computation overhead.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Starting with Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2021), vast amount of per sence
036   optimization methods (Liu et al., 2020; Barron et al., 2022; Kerbl et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024) spring
037   out as the protagonist of 3D reconstruction in recent years. However, these methods overfit to each
038   scene and can not generalize to new ones, limiting their possible applications. As an alternative,
039
      generalizable reconstruction methods synthesize novel views on the fly of few source views without
      per-scene optimization (Wang et al., 2021; Charatan et al., 2024). These methods typically aggregate
040
      visual features extracted from source views to produce 3D feature for each sampled 3D point, and
041
      render each target-view pixel by integrating the feature of 3D points projecting on it.
042
043   Though there has been adequate research for generalizable reconstruction methods (Yu et al., 2021;
044   Wang et al., 2021; Charatan et al., 2024; Wewer et al., 2024), they continue to struggle with construct-
045
      ing precise 3D features for sampled 3D points because of including irrelevant source views during
      the sample-wise feature aggregation, which hinders target view rendering. As shown in Figure 1a,
046
      irrelevant source views may prevent the 3D feature from paying attention to correct views, or mislead
047
      it to denote an occupied space. This problem is derived from the absence of the geometric constraint,
048   that a source-view image should be only relevant to the 3D points lying around the observed geometry
049   surface. Meeting this constraint requires 3D reasoning to determine the visibility of a 3D point from
050   various viewpoints. To tackle this problem, most methods adopt a self-adaptive manner to satisfy the
051   geometric constraint implicitly, which either utilize costly 3D-CNN to introduce global 3D context
052   (Johari et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2020; Chang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022) to refine the 3D features, or
053   powerful modules such as transformers for source-view aggregation (Ren et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
      2022; Suhail et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024). Recent methods take one step forward by explicitly

      Different from all works above, we explore a brand new approach to satisfy the geometric constraint.
      We propose to utilize the geometric constraint to regularize the Learned Geometry of the Scene
078
      (LGS), which we define as depth distributions of source-view pixels. Specifically, with the widely
079
      used weighted pooling mechanism for source-view aggregation (Wang et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022;
080   Charatan et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024), we denote the visibility of the sampled 3D points to source
081   views as weights in weighted pooling, and transform the them into a discrete depth distribution for
082   each source-view pixel ray (shorten as source rays in the following discussion), as illustrated in
083   Figure 1b. By regularizing the depth distributions to be pulse-like through an unsupervised learning
084   objective, the geometric constraint can be satisfied without any geometric ground truth.
085
      However, for the regularization of the discrete depth distribution, we find that the optimization bias of
086   existing unsupervised learning objective (Barron et al., 2022) will lead to a gap between real-world
087   geometry and the optimized LGS. This gap behaves as the pulse of the optimal distribution for the
088   objective occurs tightly at certain sample, rather than the actual hitting surface of the ray (depicted
089   in Figure 3b, and discussed in Section 3.3). To mitigate the optimization bias, we devise a novel
090   unsupervised learning objective to regularize the discrete depth distribution, whose optimal solutions
091   compose a field that contains the real-world distribution, so that the object surface can be modeled in
092   a regressive manner continuously, as depicted in Figure 3c.
093   The simultaneous supervision with source views from crossed perspectives engages the sampled 3D
094   points by multi-view 3D context, as illustrated in Figure 1c. Benefiting from directly supervising the
095   source-view aggregation process, our proposed supervision is algebraically interpretable, and can be
096   carried alongside existing target view supervision with one target view rendering in a plug-and-play
097   manner. To summarize our contributions in a few words:
098
099   ‚ÄĘ To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to propose source-view supervision for generalizable
100
        3D reconstruction, which can be carried along target view supervision without additional rendering.
        Our supervision method filters out irrelevant source views for the sampled 3D points by regularizing
101
        the Learned Geometry of the Scene.
102
103   ‚ÄĘ We devise a novel unsupervised learning objective to regularize the discrete depth distributions of
104     source-view pixels to be pulse-like, which mitigates the optimization bias by ensuring its optimal
105
        solutions to form a field that contains the real-world distribution.
106   ‚ÄĘ We provide mathematical proof to verify the devised learning objective, and extensive experiments
107     demonstrate our proposed supervision method correctly encourages the confidence of the LGS,
        thereby improving the performance of both NeRF- and 3DGS-based backbones.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
109
110
      3D Reconstruction and Its Generalization. 3D reconstruction, building the scene based on several
111   known views and corresponding camera parameters, has been long investigated as an important topic
112   in computer vision. Recently proposed NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2021) and a mass of follow-up
113   studies (Barron et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2023; Barron et al., 2022; 2023; Kerbl et al.,
114   2023; Yu et al., 2024) have drawn the community‚Äôs attention to per-scene optimized reconstruction
115   methods. Though there has already been methods that support fast per-scene training and rendering
116   (Wizadwongsa et al., 2021; MuŐąller et al., 2022; Barron et al., 2023; Kerbl et al., 2023), they typically
117   still require minutes to train and densely captured known views, blocking their possible applications.
118   Meanwhile, inspired by NeRF, researchers start to combine radiance field with epipolar geometry
119
      (Wang et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2021; Charatan et al., 2024) to form a new paradigm, which is so called
      generalizable reconstruction. Alternative to per-scene optimized methods, generalizable methods
120
      learn the common sense of vision and geometry to adapt to different scenes. Recent generalizable
121
      reconstruction methods tend to apply transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) in their networks to fuse
122   the feature of sampled 3D points and source views (Suhail et al., 2022; Ding et al., 2022; Ren et al.,
123   2023), some of which even completely discard multi-layer perceptron (MLP) based volume rendering
124   (Wang et al., 2022). On the other hand, fully image-to-image methods propose to predict novel
125   views in a geometry-free manner (Sajjadi et al., 2022; KulhaŐĀnek et al., 2022). This paper follows the
126   canonical 3D reconstruction framework (Wang et al., 2021), which builds understanding of the scene
127   with epipolar geometry.
128
129   Multi-View 3D Context. Awareness of multi-view 3D context is obliviously important for 3D
130   reconstruction. While early neural representation based works straightly embody the multi-view
131
      context with epipolar geometry (Yu et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021), recent
      works inspired by surface reconstruction methods tend to refine the volume features with 3D-CNN
132
      (Ding et al., 2022; Chang et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Chen
133
      et al., 2024). On the other hand, image-to-image methods propose to learn the 3D context in the
134   embedding space with a completely implicit manner (Sajjadi et al., 2022; KulhaŐĀnek et al., 2022).
135   Different from these works, we believe that source views could be utilized more than simply as input,
136   thus we propose to supervise the LGS derived from source views with geometric constraint. With
137   simultaneous constraint from different viewpoints, the 3D context is explicitly embedded into the
138   proposed supervision without any costly 3D module like 3D-CNN, as depicted in Figure 1c.
139
140   Source-View Geometry in Generalizable Reconstruction. The most related works to this paper
141   are NeuRay (Liu et al., 2022) and its follow-up works (Huang et al., 2023), who try to explore the
142   geometric information of source views to constrain the generalizable reconstruction model. For
143   example, NeuRay assumes that the visibility of the sampled 3D points on the same source-view ray
144
      follows the probability density function of a logistics distribution, and predicts the shape factors of
      the logistics distribution to infer the weights of source views to samples explicitly. Such an approach
145
      requires a specific network design, which performs well but cannot directly benefit other existing
146
      generalizable reconstruction methods. However, our work is significantly distinct from NeuRay
147   and its follow-up works. What we propose is not a specific network design, but a plug-and-play
148   supervision method that improves the performance of both NeRF- and 3DGS-based generalizable
149   reconstruction backbones. Moreover, we propose a novel objective to mitigate the optimization
150   bias of the existing objective (Barron et al., 2022), which is the key of our method but has not been
151   discussed by existing works to the best of our knowledge.

      3    M ETHOD

      Overview. We present Source-view Geometric Constraint (SGC), a novel source-view based super-
156   vision method to regularize the source-view encoder of generalizable reconstruction backbones to
157   better model the real-world geometry, as the result improving the performance of backbones decoding
158   the scene with either NeRF or 3DGS. We first introduce the pipeline of epipolar geometry based
159   generalizable reconstruction methods in Section 3.1. Next, we present how SGC is applied to these
160   backbones and improves their performance. Specifically, we explain how the LGS is efficiently
161   derived from a given backbone in Section 3.2, and then we introduce how to regularize the LGS and
      mitigate the optimization bias issue with our devised unsupervised objective in Section 3.3.

474   Discussion. It‚Äôs worth noting that replacing Lsgc with Ldist has a more slight impact on pixelSplat
475
      than GNT. We attribute this to the different sampling strategies adopted by the two methods for
      encoding the scene, that GNT samples on 3D target rays, while pixelSplat samples on the epipolar
476
      lines in the 2D images. As the result the former has extremely irregular sampling frequency of source
477
      rays, and a big portion of the source rays cannot be applied with SGC supervision for having too
478   little samples. As contrary, the latter has uniformed sampling frequency for source rays and all the
479   source-view pixels can be applied with SGC supervision. Irregular sampling is more likely to produce
480   large sampling intervals, thus aggravates the optimization bias problem.

515   5                            D ISCUSSION
516
517   The paper has meticulously demonstrated how the proposed SGC supervision method improves the
518   performance generalizable 3D reconstruction methods. However, there are still few drawbacks of
519   this work. First, as the experiments have demonstrated, the optimal condition of the regularization
520
      objective counts for the SGC supervision to have a positive impact to the backbone. Our proposed
      SGC loss doesn‚Äôt really have its optimal solution to be the real-world geometry, but allows its optimal
521
      solution field to contain the real-world geometry. More precise solutions could be explored for
522
      the regularization objective. Second, as the regularization objective is unsupervised, it should not
523   converge too early when the backbone don‚Äôt have an overall 3D reasoning ability. Otherwise the
524   unsupervised learning objective may mislead the backbone, resulting in degraded performance. We
525   control the speed of convergence by a small weight for the objective, even so the objective converges
526   very fast as shown in Figure 7a. Further research could be made on a more elegant solution to this
527   issue. And we leave how SGC impacts surface reconstruction to the future.
528
529
      6                            C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we innovatively propose Source-view Geometric Constraint, a supervision method which
532
      improves generalizable 3D reconstruction methods by regularizing the discrete depth distributions
533
      on source views. We further propose a novel unsupervised training objective to regularize the
534   discrete depth distributions to be pulse-like, which crucially mitigates the gap between the optimized
535   LGS and real-world geometry. The proposed supervision can be elegantly attached to the existing
536   training objectives for a given backbone with negligible computation overhead, visibly improves the
537   performance of both NeRF- and 3DGS-based backbones. Mathematical analysis and experimental
538   results convincingly profile the mechanism of our work. We suggest the readers to the Appendix for
539   further details and ablation study. We hope that this work exhibits a new direction to the community
      for improving generalizable reconstruction quality by supervision on source-view information.

742   A.3.1    R EARRANGEMENT
743
744
      Typically, for epipolar geometry based generalizable reconstruction methods, 3D points are sampled
      on target rays, resulting in irregular sampling frequency on source rays. Meanwhile, to correctly
745
      denote Q(rs ), samples on the same source ray should be sorted by their depth to the corresponding
746
      source view. These two issues make it challenging to efficiently rearrange Wv into Wd , from a
747   GPU-unfriendly structure to a GPU-friendly structure, where the latter is the explicit representation
748   of the LGS to be supervised.
749
750
      To tackle the problem, we propose a devoted rearrangement algorithm. The algorithm utilize camera
      extrinsics and intrinsics to obtain view-specific depths and the projected pixels for each sampled 3D
751
      point, and then rearrange the samples with depth-bin based bucket sort.
752
753   Depth-bin Partition. In one target-view rendering pass, for each source-view pixel indexed s =
754   1, ..., N √ó H √ó W , we compute the depth range of samples projected to it, which is denoted as
755   [dsmin , dsmax ). We linearly divide the range as Nbin depth-bins for each source-view pixel, with the
      depth range of the i-th bin denoted as [dsi‚ąí1 , dsi ), where dsi = dsmin + i/Nbin √ó (dsmax ‚ąí dsmin ).


                                                              14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      Register samples to the depth-bins. With view-specific depth, the sampled 3D points could be
757   registered to the depth-bins efficiently, with the value of the depth-bin being the weight of the specific
758   source view for the sampled 3D point. Once the registering is complete, samples projected to the
759   same source-view pixel are naturally ordered by depth. There are two advantages of the bucket sort,
760   one is that the irregular sampling frequency of source rays are aligned with each other by the same
761   bucket resolution Nbin , the other is that it doesn‚Äôt need any comparison between the sorted elements
762   but only index registering, which is more efficient than sorting on GPU.
763
      Post Process. After the bucket sort, for a group of bins of the same source-view pixel, we further
764
      bubble the non-empty bins to the front, pad the value of empty bins at the end with 0, and regularize
765   depth bins of the same pixel to sum up to 1. Picking K source rays with the highest sampling frequency
766   for each of the N source views and concatenate them together, we have Wd ‚ąą R(K√óN )√óNbin as the
767   explicit representation of (Section 3.2).
768
769   Discussion. The value of Nbin decides the probability of conflict where two samples are registered
770
      to the same depth-bin. This probability goes negligible as Nbin is large enough. More concretely,
      Nbin is equivalent to the L in Section 3.2. On the other hand, the reason why we have K is that, for a
771
      NeRF-based generalizable reconstruction method, only a batch of target-view pixels can be sampled
772
      in a single iteration because of out-of-memory issue. As the result, most of the source-view pixels
773   only have little samples projected on them (usually less than 10 by our experiment). Such a sparse
774   sampling can not depict a distribution well, which could even degrade performance of the backbone
775   if used for supervision. So we only pick the K most sampled pixels on each source view to perform
776   depth distribution regularization.
