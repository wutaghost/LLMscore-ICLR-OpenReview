011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Despite its successes in both practical machine learning and neural modeling,
014            the backpropagation algorithm has long been considered biologically implausible
015            (Crick, 1989). Previous solutions to this biological implausibility have proposed
016
               the existence of a separate, error feedback network, in which error at the final
               layer may be propagated backwards to earlier layers in a manner similar to back-
017
               propagation. However, biological evidence suggests that feedback connections in
018
               the cortex may function more similarly to an autoencoder, rather than being exclu-
019            sively used as error feedback (Marino, 2020; Chen et al., 2024). Here, we attempt
020            to unify these two paradigms by showing how autoencoder-like, inverse feedback
021            connections may be used to minimize error throughout a feedforward neural net-
022            work. Our proposed mechanism, Reciprocal Feedback, consists of two contribu-
023            tions: first we show how a modification of the Recirculation algorithm (Hinton &
024            McClelland, 1988) is capable of learning the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse of a
025            pair of network weights. Then, we will show how, using a Newton-like method
026            (Hildebrandt & Graves, 1927), locally-learned pseudoinverse feedback connec-
027
               tions may be used to facilitate an alternative optimization method to traditional
               gradient descent - while alleviating the need to compute the weight transpose, or
028
               use direct feedback connections from the final layer. In the MNIST and CIFAR-10
029
               classification tasks, our method obtains an asymptotic error similar to backpropa-
030            gation, in fewer iterations than comparable biologically-plausible algorithms, such
031            as Feedback Alignment (Lillicrap et al., 2014).

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The backpropagation algorithm is commonly regarded as the standard optimization algorithm for
037   neural networks in machine learning (Rumelhart et al., 1986). Furthermore, artificial neural net-
038   works trained using the backpropagation algorithm have been shown to recapitulate features of bi-
039   ological networks, such as in the hippocampus (Chen et al., 2024) and visual cortex (Yamins et al.,
040   2014). However, there are several aspects of the backpropagation algorithm which make it an un-
041   likely candidate to be physically implemented in the brain (Crick, 1989) - one of these being the
042   weight-transport problem (Grossberg, 1987). Our goal here is to explore network architectures and
043   local learning rules that approximate the performance of backpropagation.
044   During the computation of the gradient in the backpropagation algorithm, the error at the final layer
045   is passed “backwards” through the transpose of the each layer weight matrix, in order to compute
046   the error with respect to each layer parameter. While computing the transpose of each weight matrix
047   is fast and simple in-silico, there is no known biological mechanism by which error gradient signals
048   may be precisely propagated backwards in biological neurons at a timescale relevant to learning.
049   Many algorithms have been proposed to circumvent the weight transport problem and approximate
050   backpropagation in a biologically-plausible way. Several successful approaches have employed a
051   separate error network of feedback connections to transmit final-layer error backwards, in a similar
052   procedure to backpropagation (Lillicrap et al., 2014). Some of these feedback methods, including
053   the weight-mirror (Akrout et al., 2019) and sign-symmetry (Xiao et al., 2018) method, have been
      shown to have the capacity to scale to large, complex datasets.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      However, empirical evidence does not suggest that the physical bidirectional feedback connections
055   which exist in the cortex are exclusively used to transmit error from the highest layer. Instead, it is
056   likely that cortical feedback connections may be used to reconstruct activity patterns at lower layers
057   using information from higher layers, and compute error signals locally (Mumford, 1992). Recent
058   experimental work also supports the hypothesis that areas lower in the cortical processing hierarchy
059   reconstruct activity patterns based on information from higher areas, particularly during memory
060   tasks (Linde-Domingo et al. (2019), Favila et al. (2022), Naya et al. (2001)). This framework of
061   predictive coding suggests that the cortical processing hierarchy may be more appropriately modeled
062   by a series of stacked, autoencoder-like structures (Marino, 2020).
063   In this paper, we attempt to unify the experimentally-supported theory of the cortex as a series of
064   autoencoders, with the error feedback models which have shown to be successful in emulating back-
065   propagation and solving machine learning problems. Specifically, we show how autoencoder-like,
066   pseudoinverse feedback connections can be used to globally minimize error in a fully-connected,
067   feedforward network.
068   First, we will show how a simple modification of the Recirculation algorithm Hinton & McClelland
069   (1988) is capable of training a pair of matrices to be the unique pseudoinverses of each other. Then,
070   we will show that using only the pseudoinverse at each layer, it is possible to propagate the error
071   backwards through the network, and adjust each layer weight in a direction that reduces the global
072   error. For that derivation, we will apply the Newton-like method described by Hildebrandt & Graves
073   (1927) and Ben-Israel (1966) as an alternative to gradient descent.
074   These two phases may be synchronized in a ”wake-sleep” cycle (Figure 3). During the wake phase,
075   predictions are generated from inputs, compared with targets, and forward weights are modified to
076   minimize the error between them. During the ”sleep” phase, random noise is generated at each
077   layer, so that the forward and feedback matrices align to be the pseudoinverses of each other (Figure
078   2). Additionally, we also consider a parallel version of Reciprocal Feedback, in which feedback
079   matrices are trained concurrently with forward matrices. We also show that our method outperforms
080   the Feedback Alignment algorithm on the MNIST and CIFAR-10 image classification tasks.

143   2.2   R ELATIONSHIP WITH OTHER WEIGHT- TRANSPORT ALGORITHMS
144
      Another biologically-plausible algorithm which uses random noise during a ”sleep” period to learn
145
      appropriate feedback weights is the weight-mirror algorithm Akrout et al. (2019). In that case, the
146   feedback weights are trained to approximate the transpose of the forward weights - as opposed to
147   the pseudoinverse. This allows for a better direct approximation to backpropagation, but does not
148   align with the autoencoder-like structure of feedback connections that would be expected.

418
419   This algorithm was tested on two classification problems - MNIST and CIFAR-10. For both datasets,
420   we used the cross-entropy loss functionand SGD with a fixed batch size. Hyperparameter details
421   are given in the Appendix. However, we used a noncontinuous, leaky ReLU nonlinearity in order

426   connections to propagate error backwards throughout the network, in place of the weight transpose

      6   D ISCUSSION
487
488   We have shown how locally-learned pseudoinverse feedback connections can be used to train a
489   feedforward, fully-connected neural network, using an alternative optimization method to gradient
490   descent. With our method, we alleviate the need to compute the weight transpose at each layer -
491   suggesting a possible solution to the weight transport problem of the backpropagation algorithm.
492   Furthermore, the use of pseudoinverse feedback connections may better align with the evidence-
493   based, neuroscientific model of the cortex as a series of autoencoder-like structures.
494   To apply Theorem 1 to a neural network, any suitable left multiplicative reciprocal of the Jacobian
495   may be used, not necessarily the B recursion based on the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse. Single-
496   layer, autoencoder-like algorithms other than Recirculation may also be able to learn a suitable
497   generalized inverse at each layer. For instance, Tapson & van Schaik (2013) describes an online,
498   biologically-plausible algorithm for computing the pseudoinverse of a network’s weights. Further-
499   more, given the rank-one, outer-product structure of forward matrix updates, it may be possible to
500   make local, online updates to the exact pseudoinverse using the Sherman-Morrison formula.
501   This method may also be related to Gauss-Newton optimization for neural networks, which uses the
502   direct pseudoinverse of the whole network Jacobian at each layer (Botev et al., 2017). The main
503   issue with implementing this using only layer-wise pseudoinverses is that unique, direct pseudoin-
504   verse of the whole network cannot be composed sequentially using each layer’s pseudoinverse. In
505   other words, since it is an approximation of the second-derivative, the chain rule for Hessians must
506   be used instead, followed by taking the pseudoinverse of the result separately (see section A.4).
507   The optimization method in Theorem 1 is understudied in the context of machine learning, and its
508
      convergence properties compared to gradient descent are still unknown.
509   Due to the cost of computing and updating the pseudoinverse in a digital computer, this method is
510   more computationally expensive than backpropagation. However, when implemented in a physical,
511   analog system, the relative costs of computing the inverse and transpose may be different. In the
512   context of neural modeling, the weights of artificial neural networks are often considered analogous
513   to resistance parameters of physical neuron models (Abbott & Dayan, 2005). Because of the applica-
514
      bility of Ohm’s and Kirchoff’s laws, analogously representing quantities as resistances have allowed
      certain matrix computations (such as matrix inversion) to be performed with greater efficiency than
515
      in digital computers (Sun et al., 2019).
516
517   The learning algorithms analyzed here in a feedforward model can potentially be generalized to
518   stacked recurrent neural networks, such as a predictive autoencoder with a recurrent hidden layer
519   trained to learn sequences of inputs (Chen et al., 2024).
