009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Quantization-aware training comes with a fundamental challenge: the derivative of
012            quantization functions such as rounding are zero almost everywhere and nonexistent
013            elsewhere. Various differentiable approximations of quantization functions have
014            been proposed to address this issue. In this paper, we prove that a large class of
015            weight gradient estimators is approximately equivalent with the straight through
016            estimator (STE). Specifically, after swapping in the STE and adjusting both the
017            weight initialization and the learning rate in SGD, the model will train in almost
018
               exactly the same way as it did with the original gradient estimator. Moreover, we
               show that for adaptive learning rate algorithms like Adam, the same result can be
019
               seen without any modifications to the weight initialization and learning rate. These
020
               results reduce the burden of hyperparameter tuning for practitioners of QAT, as
021            they can now confidently choose the STE for gradient estimation and ignore more
022            complex gradient estimators. We experimentally show that these results hold for
023            both a small convolutional model trained on the MNIST dataset and for a ResNet50
024            model trained on ImageNet.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The importance of quantized deep learning. Quantized deep learning has gained significant at-
030   tention as a means to address the demand for efficient deployment of deep neural networks on
031   resource-constrained devices. Traditional deep learning models typically employ high-precision
032   representations, consuming substantial computational resources and memory. Quantized deep learn-
033   ing techniques offer a compelling solution by reducing the precision of network parameters and
034   activations. Although the Post-Training Quantization technique is easier to use to quantize any given
035
      model, Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) has been shown to provide higher quality results since
      quantized weights are updated throughout the training process (Nagel et al., 2021).
036
037   Gradient estimators are needed in QAT. QAT encounters a problem where the derivatives of
038   quantization functions are zero or nonexistent everywhere. To sidestep this problem, practitioners use
039   approximations of the quantization functions (known as gradient estimators) for backpropagation.
040   The straight-through estimator is a common choice for this, but many believe it is better for a gradient
041
      estimator to more closely approximate the rounding function. We show that this belief is misguided.
042   Our main contributions are as follows:
043
044         1. A proof under minimal assumptions that all nonzero weight gradient estimators lead to
045            approximately equivalent weight movement for non-adaptive learning rate optimizers (SGD,
046
               SGD + Momentum, etc.) when the learning rate is sufficiently small, after a change to
               weight initialization and learning rates has been applied.
047
048         2. A proof that for adaptive learning rate optimizers (Adam, RMSProp, etc.) the same result
049            holds without any need for adjustment to the learning rate and weight initialization.
050         3. Empirical evidence demonstrating this result on both a small deep neural networked train on
051            MNIST and a larger ResNet50 model trained on ImageNet.
052
053   Value for practitioners: Our findings reduce the burden of hyperparameter tuning for QAT. When
      learning rates are low, practitioners can now confidently choose the Straight Through Estimator

055   choosing the weight initialization scheme, learning rate, and optimization method.

      istent elsewhere, vanilla gradient descent would never update the weights of a quantized model. The
080
      standard approach for addressing this issue is to approximate Q(x) by a differentiable surrogate
081

082

083
      as the straight-through estimator (Hinton, 2012; Bengio et al., 2013) or STE, defined by Q̂(x) = x,
084
      Q̂0 (x) = 1. A strong theoretical justification for use of the STE is given in Yin et al. (2019).
085
086   Piecewise linear estimators. Piecewise linear (PWL) estimators have derivative I[wmin ,wmax ] , where
087   I is the indicator function. They make Q̂ more closely resemble Q (Rastegari et al., 2016; Hubara
088   et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2022). The simplest way to define a PWL estimator for a multi-bit quantizer
089   is to simply use Equation 1 with the round step removed, and in this case [wmin , wmax ] is exactly the
090   representable range. This way, the behavior of PWL estimators more closely relate to the quantization
091   function. In general, we will use P W Lwmin ,wmax (x) = clip(x, wmin , wmax ) to denote a PWL
092   gradient estimator.
093   STE and PWL lead to "gradient error". The simple STE and PWL gradient estimators described
094   above still leave a significant gap between the behavior of the forward pass and the surrogate forward
095   pass. For this reason, researchers have proposed a large number of custom gradient estimators, often
096   citing a high "gradient error" in the simpler choices of gradient estimators as motivation for their
097   work. Gradient error is often described as the difference between Q and Q̂.
098
      An abundance of custom gradient estimators. In order to solve the perceived problem of gradient
099   error, many researchers have proposed gradient estimators that carry more complexity than the STE
100   or PWL estimators. In Appendix B, we cite and describe 17 examples of custom gradient estimators
101   in the quantization literature. Plots of some prominent examples are given in Figure 1.

      5.3   O N THE A SSUMPTIONS AND I MPLICATIONS OF T HEOREMS 5.1 AND 5.2
292
293   Theorems 5.1 and 5.2 rely on specific assumptions about the gradient estimator Q̂. In this section,
294   we break down these assumptions clearly. Furthermore, we describe how these theorems imply
295   contributions 1 and 2.
296   The assumptions are reasonable: The upper bound on Q̂0 in Assumption 5.1.1 is very mild.
297   Gradient estimators with an unbounded derivative would likely cause training instability, and are not
298   used in practice. Similarly, the authors are not aware of a gradient estimator that breaks Assumptions
299   5.1.2 and 5.2.2. In addition, the constants L− , L+ , and L0 are usually quite small in practice (see
300   Appendix H for calculations). The lower bound on Q̂0 in Assumptions 5.1.1 and 5.2.1, however, is
301   often broken in practice. In Appendix G, we describe how the Theorems still support contributions 1
302   and 2 in these cases.
303
      The bounds in Equations 6 and 7 are small: In order to see how Theorems 5.1 and 5.2 provide
304   contributions 1 and 2, we can closely examine each term in Equations 6 and 7. The gradient and
305   convexity error in each equation together give a worst-case increase to E (t) at each training step.
306
      That is, as long as these terms are small, Q̂-net and ST E-net will train in a very similar manner.
307   The convexity error terms are unavoidable errors, and are extremely small (O(η 2 )) in practice. The
308   gradient error terms, however, are O(η), so they can be large if the gradients of the two models are
309                                                      (t)       (t)
      misaligned. However, since the gradient terms ∇fQ̂ and ∇fST E only depend on quantized weights,
310
311   these terms will be zero at the beginning of training and remain small as long as E (t) remains small.
312   The claim is nontrivial: Note that these theorems do not simply say that when the learning rate
313   is small, the models change very little, and therefore Q̂-net and ST E-net are aligned. Since the
314   irreducible error term is quadratic in η, the misalignment at each step is small relative to the learning
315   rate itself.
316

317
      network weight, but can be applied to each weight independently in a multi-weight network. Of
318   course, the trajectories of weights in a neural network are not independent, but luckily in our case
319   the weight trajectories only depend on the quantized versions of the other network weights. To see
320   this, note that the only terms in Equations 6 and 7 that depend on other network weights are the

      8   D ISCUSSION : W HY ARE SO MANY GRADIENT ESTIMATORS PUBLISHED ?
513
514
      A natural question that a reader may have concerning past research is this: If the choice of gradient
      estimator is so irrelevant, why is there so much research that proposes new gradient estimators and
515
      demonstrates improved performance with their aid? There are several potential answers to this.
516
517   Performance differences are due to implicit weight initialization and learning rate differences.
518   The simplest explanation is that their gradient estimation techniques happen to have implictly
519   uncovered a superior weight re-initialization and learning rate adjustment, as indicated by Theorem
520
      5.1.
521   Activation gradient estimators make a difference. Another answer could be that the performance
522   improvements were due to changes in quantized activation gradient estimators, which cannot be
523   equated to the STE.
524   Learning rates are too high to see the equivalence. It is possible that the learning rates in these
525   experiments were too high to see an equivalence between their gradient estimators and the STE. This
526   is a limitation of our main argument, but we expect that this counter-argument will not stand the test
527   of time, since by our main results, the higher learning rate masks the fact that models with novel Q̂
528   and the STE are still approximating the same process.
529
      Gradient estimators are proposed alongside other innovations, making them hard to evaluate
530
      in isolation. The most common situation is that novel gradient estimators Q̂ are introduced simul-
531
532
      taneously with further changes to the learning recipe. Some allow the parameters of Q and Q̂ to
      be learnable through gradient descent or explicit computations on the weights, or adjust them on a
533
      schedule (See Appendix A). Others, such as DSQ (Gong et al., 2019), use Q̂ on the forward pass
534
535
      and gradually update Q̂ to more closely approximate Q. Lin et al. (2020) contributes a process for
      rotating the entire weight vector to align with the binarized weight vector. Bi-Real Net Liu et al.
536
      (2018) also includes a trick with network activations to increase the representational capacity of the
537
      model. In addition to the Error Decay Estimator, Qin et al. (2020) describes a method for maximizing
538   the entropy of quantized parameters to ensure higher parameter diversity.

      A    C HOOSING Q UANTIZATION PARAMETERS
757
758
759   The clipping bounds l and u are determined by the number of bits b in the quantized representation
760   and the desired number of representable values in the positive and negative range of the quantizer.
761   This range of weight values is referred to as the representable range (or quantization range) of the
762
      quantizer, and can be computed as [∆ · l, ∆ · u]. Large ∆ values allow for large w values to avoid the
      clip step, whereas small values give small w values a more granular representation. These parameters
763
      are either learned (Esser et al., 2019; Choi et al., 2018; Gong et al., 2019) or set by the user. For
764
      b > 1, l and u are often chosen as l = −2b−1 , u = 2b−1 − 1 for symmetric quantization and l = 0,
765   u = 2b − 1 for asymmetric quatization. ∆ is often chosen uniformly per-channel or per-token, based
766   off of latent weight data W . It is sometimes set as max(|W |)/(2b − 1), or is chosen to minimize
767   a loss function (such as MSE or cross entropy (Nagel et al., 2021)) comparing W and Q(W ). For
768   binary quantization (b = 1), Q(w) is typically a sign function (Nagel et al., 2021; Gholami et al.,
769   2021; Rokh et al., 2022), and there is no representable range. For binary PWL estimators,a common
770   choice is to use Equation 1 and simply set ∆ = 1 and [wmin , wmax ] = [−1, 1] (Sayed et al., 2023).

      Custom binary gradient estimators. A substantial amount of research has gone into custom
776
      gradient estimators. Many choices (Sakr et al., 2018; Darabi et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2018; Xu and
777
      Cheung, 2019; Qin et al., 2020; Lin et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2021) for binary gradient estimators are
778   described in (Yuan and Agaian, 2021). A popular estimator is the "Error Decay Estimator" (EDE) of
779   (Qin et al., 2020), which uses an evolving tanh function to approximate the sign function.
780
781
      Custom gradient estimators. The hyperbolic tangent gradient estimator (HTGE) (Pei et al., 2023)
      gives a piecewise function locally described by tanh functions. Its definition is given by the standard
782
      quantization function 1, where the round operation is replaced with
783

785                                  H(x) =      +            2

788   where a = floor(x) and b = ceil(x). Here t is the sharpness parameter. This approximation is used
789   for both the forward and backward pass of Q in Differentiable Soft Quantization (DSQ) (Gong et al.,
790   2019). Similar approaches to the HTGE use a sum of sigmoid functions (Yang et al., 2019) and a
791
      distance-weighted piecewise linear combination of the outputs of Q (Kim et al., 2021) to approximate
      Q. These techniques make up the most common choices of gradient estimators, which justifies our
792

793

794

795

796   (2016) uses the STE for the round function, but replaces the clip function in the forward pass with a
797   modified tanh function, which affects the gradient calculations as well. Sakr et al. (2022) introduces
798   a choice for Q̂ known as "Magnitude Aware Differentiation" (MAD) that matches the STE on the
799   representable range of the quantizer and a reciprocal function outside of this range. More recently,

801   and Yang et al. (2024) described a gradient estimator based on the cosine function. See Figure 1 for
802   examples of several gradient estimators.

      Implications of our main results. In light of our results 1 and 2, we can sometimes equate these
804   addition algorithms with more well-known training strategies. For example, Qin et al. (2020) proposes
805   a schedule for a tanh-based gradient estimator to gradually approach a sign function throughout
806   training. Since they use SGD in their experiments, we can think of each update to sharpen the gradient
807   estimator as an effective "shifting" of the weights according to the function defined in Equation 4.
808   This particular shift will push most weights away from 0, which has an effect similar to slowing down
809   the learning rate. Thus this adaptive gradient estimation technique is similar to a standard learning
      rate decay schedule.
