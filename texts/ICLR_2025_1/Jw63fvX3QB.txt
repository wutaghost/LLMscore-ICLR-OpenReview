009                                             A BSTRACT
010
011           Large language models are well-known to be effective at few-shot in-context
012           learning (ICL). Recent advancements in multimodal foundation models have en-
013           abled unprecedentedly long context windows, presenting an opportunity to ex-
014           plore their capability to perform ICL with many more demonstrating examples. In
015           this work, we evaluate the performance of multimodal foundation models scal-
016
              ing from few-shot to many-shot ICL. We benchmark GPT-4o and Gemini 1.5
              Pro across 14 datasets spanning multiple domains (natural imagery, medical im-
017
              agery, remote sensing, and molecular imagery) and tasks (image classification,
018
              visual question answering, and object localization). We observe that many-shot
019           ICL, including up to almost 2,000 multimodal demonstrating examples, leads to
020           substantial improvements compared to few-shot (<100 examples) ICL across all
021           of the datasets. Further, Gemini 1.5 Pro performance continues to improve log-
022           linearly up to the maximum number of tested examples on many datasets. We
023           also find open-weights multimodal foundation models like Llama 3.2-Vision and
024           InternLM-XComposer2.5 do not benefit from the demonstrating examples, high-
025           lighting an important gap between open and closed multimodal foundation mod-
026           els. Given the high inference costs associated with the long prompts required
027
              for many-shot ICL, we also explore the impact of batching multiple queries in
              a single API call. We show that batching up to 50 queries can lead to perfor-
028
              mance improvements under zero-shot and many–shot ICL, with substantial gains
029
              in the zero-shot setting on multiple datasets, while drastically reducing per-query
030           cost and latency. Finally, we measure ICL data efficiency of the models, or the
031           rate at which the models learn from more demonstrating examples. We find that
032           while GPT-4o and Gemini 1.5 Pro achieve similar zero-shot performance across
033           the datasets, Gemini 1.5 Pro exhibits higher ICL data efficiency than GPT-4o on
034           most datasets. Our results suggest that many-shot ICL could enable users to effi-
035           ciently adapt multimodal foundation models to new applications and domains.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      Large language models (LLMs) have been shown to substantially benefit from the inclusion of a
040
      few demonstrating examples (shots) in the LLM context before the test query (Brown et al., 2020;
041
      Parnami & Lee, 2022; Wang et al., 2020). This phenomenon, commonly referred to as in-context
042   learning (ICL), enables LLMs to learn from few shots without any updates to model parameters, and
043   therefore improves specialization to new tasks without any further model training. More recently,
044   large multimodal models (LMMs) have also demonstrated the capability of learning from in-context
045   examples Achiam et al. (2023); Han et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024b). Han et al. (2023) and
046   Zhang et al. (2024b) both show that few-shot multimodal ICL specifically helps to improve LMM
047   performance on out-domain or out-of-distribution tasks.
048   While few-shot ICL has enabled promising performance improvements for both LLMs and LMMs,
049   limited model context windows have constrained research on the impact of increasing the number
050   of demonstrating examples on performance. This is especially true for LMMs as most use a large
051   number of visual tokens to represent images. However, due to recent advancements enabling sub-
052   stantially longer context windows – for example, 128,000 tokens for GPT-4o and up to one million
053   tokens for Gemini 1.5 Pro – it is now possible to explore the effect of drastically increasing the
      number of demonstrating examples.

081         1. We show that providing close-weights multimodal foundation models with many demon-

083            only a few demonstrating examples. We observe that the performance of Gemini 1.5

               gap and an important direction for the open-weights community.

094            or better performance than single query requests in a many-shot setting, while enabling
095            substantially lower per-example latency and much cheaper per-example inference cost.
096         5. We find that batching multiple questions can lead to substantial performance improvements
097            in a zero-shot setting. We design experiments to explain this phenomenon, and find that
098            the improvements are due to a combination of domain calibration, class calibration, and
099            self-generated demonstrating examples due to autoregressive decoding.
100         6. We release a new tool (anonymized link) to allow users to easily test the many-shot ICL
101            capabilities of multimodal foundation models, facilitating future work on studying them.

106   Scaling ICL. The seminal work of Brown et al. (2020) discovered performance improvements for
107   LLMs from increasing the number of in-context examples, but the tested number of demonstrating
      examples was low (10 to 100), likely due to the restrictive context size (2048 tokens for GPT3).

130   Increasing the number of in-context examples has only been explored recently by a few works Li
131   et al. (2023); Agarwal et al. (2024); Bertsch et al. (2024). Both Li et al. (2023) and Agarwal et al.
132   (2024) explore scaling in-context learning to more than 1,000 demonstrating examples and find
133   performance improvements across multiple tasks. However, their experiments are limited to text-
134   only benchmarks and do not compare performance across different models.
135   Multimodal ICL. Due to the recent emergence of LMMs, research on multimodal ICL is still
136   nascent. One prior work developed a new model to leverage complex prompts composed of mul-
137   timodal inputs in order to allow models to compare images Zhao et al. (2023), while other recent
138   works explored the generalizability of GPT-4V and Gemini to multimodal out-domain and out-of-
139   distribution tasks, and found that ICL leads to performance benefits for both models across many
140   tasks Zhang et al. (2024b); Han et al. (2023). However, none of these works have leveraged the new
141   largely expanded context windows to investigate the effects of increasing the number of demonstrat-
142   ing examples.
143   Batch Querying. Multiple prior works have explored batching queries (also commonly referred to
144   as batch prompting) for more efficient and cheaper inference. Batch prompting was first introduced
145   in Cheng et al. (2023), leading to comparable or better performance than single prompting, while
146   achieving substantially reduced inference token cost and latency. Lin et al. (2023) observe perfor-
147   mance degradation with batched prompts in longer contexts, and propose a variety of techniques to
148   mitigate the performance loss. More recently, additional variations of batch prompting have been
149
      proposed, including grouping similar questions together Liu et al. (2024), batching prompts of dif-
      ferent tasks Son et al. (2024), and concatenating multiple images into a single image collage Xu et al.
150
      (2024). We again note that batch prompting with high numbers of demonstrating examples and high
151
      numbers of queries has only become feasible due to larger context windows of recent models.

237   3.2   DATASETS
238
239   We benchmark the model performance on 14 datasets spanning multiple domains (natural imagery,
240   medical imagery, remote sensing, and molecular imagery) and tasks (image classification, visual
241
      question answering, and object localization). We acknowledge most LMMs are not yet capable of
      accurately producing localizations (Wu et al., 2024; Zang et al., 2023). Table 1 provides a summary
242
      of the datasets used in this study.
243
244   For all datasets, we construct a set of demonstrating (demo) examples from the original training and
245   validation splits used for in-context learning and a test set from the original test split (if one exists)
246   to evaluate the performance of the models. We randomly sample the demo and test sets from the
247
      original dataset without replacement. For the multi-class and fine-grained classification datasets,
      we perform a class-stratified sampling, ensuring an equal number of examples per class in both the
248
      demo and test sets. For the multi-label classification dataset (CheXpert), we sample an equal number
249
      of positive and negative samples per class in both the demo and test sets. We note that, since the
250   task is multi-label, this sampling procedure does not result in an exactly equal number of examples
251   per class. For the scaling experiments, we increase the number of demonstrating examples while
252   ensuring class balance. Besides the 10 classification datasets shown in Table 1, we also include two
253   visual question answering datasets (RSVQA (Lobry et al., 2020) and VQA-RAD (Lau et al., 2018))
254   and two object localization datasets (DIOR for bridge localization (Li et al., 2020) and DeepLesion
255   for lung lesion localization (Yan et al., 2018)).

257   3.3   E VALUATION M ETRICS
258
259   We use standard metrics to evaluate model performance on each dataset. Specifically, we measure
260   performance using accuracy for all multi-class classification and visual question answering datasets
261   as they are sampled to have a balanced class distribution. For multi-label classification on CheXpert,
262   we use the macro-averaged F1 metric. In the rare case of parsing errors, we consider the response as
263   incorrect. For object localization datasets, we use mean Intersection over Union(IoU). To estimate
264   the variability around the evaluation metrics, we compute standard deviation using bootstrapping
265   with 1,000 bootstrap replicates, and it captures the variability in data sampling.
266   In addition to standard performance metrics, we measure the data efficiency of each model. Specif-
267   ically, we compute a linear regression between log10 (N + 1) (with N the number of examples)
268   and model performance, enforcing that the line passes through the zero-shot performance point.
269   This value approximates the amount of performance improvement from zero-shot expected from
      including an order of magnitude more demonstrating examples.

358   4.2   I MPACT OF BATCHING QUERIES
359
360   As including a large set of demo examples in the prompt leads to much longer sequence lengths and
361   therefore higher inference time and cost, we consider batching queries in a single prompt to reduce
362
      per-query cost, and examine the impact of different batch sizes on model performance. Due to its
      superior performance and free preview access, we use Gemini 1.5 Pro for these experiments.
363
364   Main Results. We find minimal performance degradations, and sometimes performance improve-
365   ments, as we increase the number of queries included in each batch across under both zero-shot and
366   many-shot (at the optimal demo set size) regimes (Figure 4). Notably, using a single query each
367   time with many-shot ICL is suboptimal across many of the datasets. We find that the optimal batch
368
      size is among the three largest sizes on every dataset except CheXpert and EuroSAT, which both see
      optimal performance with a single query at a time.
369
370   We additionally observe that including a single query at a time is suboptimal on most datasets in
371   the zero-shot regime. Surprisingly, performance with the highest batch size is substantially higher
372   across three datasets under the zero-shot regime, with a consistent performance improvement as the
373   batch size is increased on both UCMerced and Terraincognita.
374   Zero-shot performance improvements from batching queries. We conduct several additional
375   experiments to investigate why batch querying can lead to large performance improvements under
376   the zero-shot regime on TerraIncognita and UCMerced. We hypothesize that this improvement
377   may be due to three potential benefits from ICL: (1) domain calibration, where the model benefits
      from seeing more images in the domain in order to adapt to it, (2) class calibration, where seeing

413   helpful even without labels. Third, to capture additional performance improvements from the self-
414   generated labels, we obtain predicted labels from the zero-shot model using a single query for each
415

420   4.3   C OST AND LATENCY ANALYSIS
421
422   Many-shot ICL incurs zero additional training cost, but per-query inference can be costly and slow
423   due to long input contexts. To quantitatively measure this, we compute the latency and cost associ-
424   ated with the zero-shot and many-shot requests with and without batching when using Gemini 1.5
425   Pro on HAM10000 and TerraIncognita. We calculate the costs using the Gemini 1.5 Pro preview
426   pricing ($7 per 1 million input tokens and $21 per 1 million output tokens). For fair comparison and
427   to minimize data transfer artifacts, all requests are sent to the same location where the VM instance
428
      is held (“us-central1”). We run the query three times under each setting and report the average.
429   In the zero-shot regime, we see substantial per-example latency reductions due to query batching,

454   5    D ISCUSSION
455
456
      In this study, we evaluate many-shot ICL of state-of-the-art multimodal foundation models across 14
457   datasets and find consistent performance improvements across most of the datasets. Batching queries
458   with many-shot ICL further exhibits substantially reduced per-example latency and inference costs
459   without compromising performance. We also highlight one future direction for improving open-
460   weights multimodal foundation models as they are unable to learn from demonstrating examples.
461
      Our findings suggest that these multimodal foundation models have the capability of performing
462
      ICL with large numbers of demonstrating examples, which may have significant implications on
463   their practical use. For example, it was previously impossible to adapt these large, private models to
464   new tasks and domains, but many-shot ICL would enable users to leverage demonstrating examples
465   to adapt the models. One significant advantage of many-shot ICL is its ability to get quick results
466   even on the same day of model release, and that’s why we can finish our evaluation using GPT-4o and
467   Llama3.2 within days. Furthermore, fine-tuning open-source models is the standard practice when
468   practitioners have access to moderately sized datasets, but many-shot ICL may remove the need for
469   fine-tuning, making it much easier to develop customized approaches. We note that it remains to
470   be seen how traditional fine-tuning of these models compares to many-shot ICL with foundation
471
      models in terms of absolute performance and data efficiency, so future work should explore this.
      In addition, it is important to study general issues which plague those foundation models, such as
472
      hallucinations and biases, under the context of many-shot ICL and batching queries. For example,
473
      it would be interesting to explore if carefully curated and large sets of demonstrating examples can
474   reduce biases across different sub-groups. We leave this to future work.
475
476   Our study has limitations. First, we only explore performance under many-shot ICL on some com-
477
      mon multimodal tasks which we believe are the most practically relevant and common multimodal
      settings, but it is worthwhile for future work to explore potential benefits from many-shot ICL on
478
      other tasks such as VL-ICL Bench Zong et al. (2024). Second, even after recent developments to
479
      increase context size, the size prohibits many-shot ICL from being used on datasets with a large
480   number (several hundred or more) of classes. We anticipate that context window sizes will con-
481   tinue to increase in size over time which will mitigate this issue. Third, we only run the ablation
482   experiments on Gemini 1.5 Pro due to budget limit, but it will be interesting to whether these trends
483   hold for GPT-4o. Fourth, the datasets which were used to train these private models have not been
484   disclosed, so it is difficult to tell whether the models have been trained on the datasets we selected.
485   We argue that the zero-shot performance across the datasets is far from perfect, which suggests that
      the datasets have not been used for training, but we cannot determine that with certainty.
