009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Oversmoothing has been claimed as a primary bottleneck for multi-layered graph
012            neural networks (GNNs). Multiple analyses have examined how and why over-
013            smoothing occurs. However, none of the prior work addressed how optimization is
014            performed under the oversmoothing regime. In this work, we show the presence of
015            gradient oversmoothing preventing optimization during training. We further ana-
016            lyze that GNNs with residual connections, a well-known solution to help gradient
017            flow in deep architecture, introduce gradient expansion, a phenomenon of the gra-
018
               dient explosion in diverse directions. Therefore, adding residual connections can-
               not be a solution for making a GNN deep. Our analysis reveals that constraining
019
               the Lipschitz bound of each layer can neutralize the gradient expansion. To this
020
               end, we provide a simple yet effective normalization method to prevent the gradi-
021            ent expansion. An empirical study shows that the residual GNNs with hundreds
022            of layers can be efficiently trained with the proposed normalization without com-
023            promising performance. Additional studies show that the empirical observations
024            corroborate our theoretical analysis.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Increasing depths of layers is recognized as a key reason for the success of many deep neural net-
030   works. Despite this widely believed claim, the graph neural network (GNN) does not necessarily
031   benefit from deeper architectures. In fact, increasing the number of layers in GNNs often leads to
032   problems such as oversmoothing, where node representations from different classes become indis-
033
      tinguishable, resulting in a degradation of performance. Although residual connections contribute
      to the success of deep architectures in many types of neural networks, they are generally ineffective
034
      at resolving the oversmoothing in GNNs.
035
036   To provide a deeper understanding of the oversmoothing, several studies have addressed the mech-
037   anism behind the oversmoothing. Li et al. (2018) explain that the graph convolutional network
038   (GCN) is a special form of Laplacian smoothing. Chamberlain et al. (2021) show that GNN can be
039
      interpreted as a discretization of the heat diffusion process, implying the diffusion of the node rep-
      resentation to reach equilibrium. Cai & Wang (2020) and Oono & Suzuki (2020) theoretically show
040
      that oversmoothing occurs exponentially when piling the layers in GCN under certain conditions.
041
      Wu et al. (2023a) show Graph Attention Networks (GATs), a generalization of GCN, also lose their
042   expressive power exponentially with the number of layers. However, none of the previous work on
043   oversmoothing investigates the gradient flow of the deep GNNs.
044
045
      In this work, we investigate gradient flows under the oversmoothing regime. Understanding the
      exact characteristics of gradients can help mitigate the oversmoothing problem. To do this, we
046
      first analyze the gradients with the graph convolutional network (GCN). The analysis shows the
047
      presence of gradient oversmoothing, which implies a gradient of node features are similar to each
048   other. We then investigate the effect of the residual connections in GNNs, which is known to make
049   the optimization work with many variants of the neural networks. Interestingly, we show that the
050   opposite effect, named gradient expansion, outbreaks as a side effect of the residual connection.
051   When the gradient expansion occurs, the gradients of node features explode in diverse directions. We
052   find that the gradient expansion is tightly connected with the upper bound of the Lipschitz constants
053   of GNN layers. To relax the gradient expansion, we propose a simple weight normalization that
      controls the Lipschitz upper bound.

054
      Empirical studies on node classification tasks corroborate our theoretical analysis, showing that gra-
055   dient smoothing and gradient expansion happen with deep GNNs and deep residual GNNs, respec-
056   tively. We also show that the similarity between gradients better represents the test performance than
057   the similarity between node representations, a commonly used oversmoothing measure. Finally, we
058   present how the deep GNNs perform with the Lipschitz constraints. With the Lipschitz constraints,
059   we can successfully train the GNNs with residual connections without sacrificing the test accuracy
060   on node classification tasks.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
063
064
      Recently, many studies have investigated the reason for performance degradation in deep GNNs.
065   Oversmoothing, the phenomenon where the representations of neighboring nodes become similar
066   as the number of GNN layers increases, has been identified as one of the main reasons. Several
067   studies have theoretically demonstrated that oversmoothing occurs due to the structural character-
068   istics of message-passing GNNs. Li et al. (2018) reveal that GCN is a special form of Laplacian
069   smoothing and points out that repeated applications lead to convergence to the same value. Oono
070   & Suzuki (2020) demonstrate that, as the depth of the GCN architecture increases, the distance be-
071   tween representations and invariant spaces decays exponentially, leading to oversmoothing. Keriven
072   (2022) show that, in linear GNNs whose aggregation matrix takes the form of a stochastic matrix,
073
      the output converges to the average value of the training labels as the depth approaches infinity. Wu
      et al. (2023b) analyze the mixing and denoising effects of GNN layers on graphs sampled from the
074
      contextual stochastic block model, providing insight into why oversmoothing can occur even before
075
      depth approach infinity. Wu et al. (2023a) prove that oversmoothing occurs exponentially even in
076   the graph attention network architectures with non-linear activations.
077
078   Based on these analyses, many studies try to mitigate oversmoothing to stack multiple layers without
079
      performance degradation. PairNorm (Zhao & Akoglu, 2020) keeps the total pairwise representation
      distances constant by normalization. DropEdge (Rong et al., 2020) randomly drops edges from the
080
      graph to slow down oversmoothing. Energetic graph neural networks (Zhou et al., 2021) utilize
081
      the Dirichlet energy term, which measures oversmoothing in the loss function to regularize over-
082   smoothing. Ordered GNN (Song et al., 2023) preserves aggregated representation within specific
083   hops separately. Gradient Gating (Rusch et al., 2023b) proposes a mechanism to stop learning in
084   a node-wise fashion before local oversmoothing occurs. Park et al. (2024) propose to reverse the
085   aggregation process. Some studies propose to change the dynamics of GNN, based on the findings
086   of Chamberlain et al. (2021), which shows that classical GNN resembles diffusion-like dynam-
087   ics. GRAND++ (Thorpe et al., 2022) proposes to add a source term to prevent oversmoothing.
088   PDE-GCN (Eliasof et al., 2021) proposes wave-like GNN architecture. Graph-coupled oscillator
089   networks (Rusch et al., 2022) propose a network based on non-linear oscillator structures.
090   Some studies propose to improve the optimization process for multi-GNN layer stacking. Li et al.
091   (2018) adopt residual connection to improve the training process. Zhang et al. (2022) also point out
092   model degradation as a main reason for the performance degradation of deep GNNs and proposes
093   an adaptive initial residual to improve optimization. However, no studies explain the exact reason
094   for model degradation and residual connection’s effectiveness.

152   4     G RADIENT OVERSMOOTHING AND E XPANSION
153
154   It is widely recognized that increasing the depth of graph neural networks (GNNs) typically results
155   in performance degradation. While oversmoothing has been identified as a key cause, questions
156   remain about why this issue cannot be resolved through optimization. One possible explanation is
157   that optimization is inherently tricky in GNNs, such as gradient vanishing and exploding, commonly
158
      observed in the earlier stages of neural networks. However, it is also observed that the performance
      of deep GNNs has limitations with the more advanced techniques known to improve optimization,
159
      such as the residual connections (He et al., 2016).
160
161   To answer these questions, we thoroughly analyze the gradient of the linear GCNs with and without
      residual connections first. The analysis shows the presence of gradient oversmoothing that is similar


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      to the representation oversmoothing but a fundamental reason why the training is incomplete with
163   deep GCNs. Our analysis further identifies that gradient expansion is a more common problem
164   with the residual connections, explaining the failure of the residual connections in deep GCNs. We
165   then extend our analysis of the GNNs with a non-linear activation through empirical experiments
166   to show that gradient oversmoothing and expansion can also be observed in practice. We conduct
167   our experiment also on GATs, showing that gradient oversmoothing and expansion is not the only
168   problem in GCN, although our theoretical analyses are limited to GCN.

360   5         A NALYSIS ON G RADIENT S IMILARITY

      6                    E FFECTIVENESS OF L IPTSCHITZ UPPER BOUND ON DEEP GNN S
460
461
      We propose a Lipschitz upper bound constraint on each layer of GNN through the weight normaliza-
462   tion in Section 4.1. In this section, we show the effectiveness of a Lipschitz upper bound constraint
463   in improving test performance and training behavior in deep GNNs.
464
465
      To evaluate test performance, we train resGCN and resGAT on Cora, CiteSeer, Chameleon, and
      Squirrel datasets with varying numbers of layers from 1 to 512. For the weight normalization, we
466
      test three different Lipschitz upper bound c: 4, 1, 0.25. All experiments are conducted with early
467
      stopping and learning rates of 0.001, 0.005, 0.01. We report average values of five repeated runs.
468
469   The test accuracy of resGCN is reported in Figure 6 with and without weight normalization. For
470   all datasets, we can observe a significant performance drop when the depth exceeds 32 without the
471
      weight normalization. Similar patterns are observed when a loose Lipschitz upper bound of c = 4
      is applied. With the tight upper bounds of c = 1, 0.25, the test accuracy remains relatively consis-
472
      tent over 512 layers. Specifically, when c = 0.25, the accuracy of resGCN consistently improves
473
      over a hundred layers, reaching the best performance at a depth of 512 layers on Squirrel datasets.
474   However, small upper bounds can limit the expressive power of each layer. As a consequence, test
475   accuracy with a small upper bound requires more layers to reach a similar level of accuracy that can
476   be achieved by a small number of layers without weight normalization.
477
      In the case of homophilic datasets, despite the multiple layers, the highest accuracy is similar re-
478
      gardless of the upper bound constraints. For example, resGCN without normalization achieves the
479
      best performance of 85.43 when the number of layers is 16. In contrast, resGCN with an upper
480   bound of 0.25 achieves the best performance of 85.14 when the number of layers is 128. However,
481   in heterophilic datasets, deep models with weight normalization outperform the models without the
482   upper bound constraints. For instance, in the Chameleon dataset, without normalization, the best
483   accuracy of 43.75 is achieved at a depth of 32. In contrast, the best accuracy of 47.04 is achieved
484   at a depth of 64 with an upper bound of 4. It is claimed that the heterophilic node classification
485   requires capturing long-range dependencies via multiple layers (Rusch et al., 2023a). Our results
      also support this claim with carefully designed multi-layered GNNs.

519   7                         C ONCLUSION
520
521   In this work, we explored the challenges associated with optimizing deep GNNs. Until now, the
522   oversmoothing has focused on the representation space. However, we demonstrate that oversmooth-
523   ing also occurs in the gradient space, a major cause making the training process complicated. We
524   further analyze the effect of the residual connections in the gradients of deepGNNs, revealing that
525   the residual connection causes gradient expansion. To alleviate the gradient expansion during train-
526
      ing, we constrain the Lipschitz upper bound on each layer through the Frobenius normalization.
      Experimental results show that the training procedure can be stabilized with the normalization over
527
      several hundred layers.
528
529
      Limitation. The theoretical analysis is based on linear GCNs. Although we have shown that
530
      similar patterns are observable with non-linear GCNs and GATs through empirical studies, the non-
531   linearity can introduce subtle differences compared with our analysis. We propose a weight nor-
532   malization to restrict the Lipschitz constant. There are, however, several normalization approaches,
533   such as the batch normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015) and the layer normalization (Ba, 2016). In
534   a previous study, these normalizations are not helpful in deepGNNs (Chen et al., 2022). To investi-
535   gate the reason further, we need to study the gradients of these normalizations in GNNs. We leave
536   this future work.
