009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Text-to-image diffusion models have been demonstrated with unsafe generation due
012            to unfiltered large-scale training data, such as violent, sexual, and shocking images,
013            necessitating the erasure of unsafe concepts. Most existing methods focus on
014            modifying the generation probabilities conditioned on the texts containing unsafe
015            descriptions. However, they fail to guarantee safe generation for unseen texts in the
016            training phase, especially for the prompts from adversarial attacks. In this paper,
017
               we re-analyze the erasure task and point out that existing methods cannot guarantee
               the minimization of the total probabilities of unsafe generation. To tackle this
018
               problem, we propose Dark Miner. It entails a recurring three-stage process that
019
               comprises mining, verifying, and circumventing. It greedily mines embeddings
020            with maximum generation probabilities of unsafe concepts and reduces unsafe
021            generation more effectively. In the experiments, we evaluate its performance on
022            two inappropriate concepts, two objects, and two styles. Compared with 6 previous
023            state-of-the-art methods, our method achieves better erasure and defense results in
024            most cases, especially under 4 state-of-the-art attacks, while preserving the model’s
025            native generation capability. Our code can be found in Supplementary Material
026            and will be available on GitHub.
027            Warning: This paper may contain disturbing, distressing, or offensive content.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Recently, the rapid development of text-to-image diffusion models (Gal et al., 2022; Mou et al.,
033
      2024; Nichol et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Ruiz et al., 2023; Saharia et al., 2022), such as
      Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), pushes the performance of high-fidelity controllable image
034
      generation to a new height. These models are trained on large-scale text-image pairs and learn to
035
      capture semantic connections between texts and images. However, everything has two sides. The
036   training data is randomly crawled from the Internet without being filtered due to its large scale. It
037   results in the inclusion of unsafe content such as violent and sexual images, thus bringing the risk of
038   unsafe generation of the models (Qu et al., 2023). The generation of unsafe concepts affects social
039   harmony and stability, hindering the safe use of these generative models.
040
      Researchers have explored different methods to prevent a trained diffusion model from unsafe
041
      generation. These methods can be broadly classified into two categories. The first category includes
042   the training-free methods, such as Safe Latent Diffusion (Schramowski et al., 2023) which defines
043   inappropriate concept sets and modifies their generation guidance. The second category includes the
044   fine-tuning-based methods, which align the generation distributions of unsafe texts to anchor texts
045   by fine-tuning model weights. Some examples include Gandikota et al. (2023; 2024); Kumari et al.
046   (2023). Other works like Forget-Me-Not (Zhang et al., 2023a) suppress activation of unsafe content
047   in attention maps, while the works Bui et al. (2024); Huang et al. (2023) introduce learnable prompts
048   and adversarial training for better erasure performance. SalUn (Fan et al., 2024), on the other hand,
049   proposes to fine-tune a model based on the saliency of model weights with the output.
050   The existing studies mainly focus on modifying the generation distributions conditioned on the texts
051   containing unsafe descriptions (Bui et al., 2024; Gandikota et al., 2023; 2024; Huang et al., 2023;
052   Kumari et al., 2023; Schramowski et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a). Therefore, how to identify these
053   unsafe texts becomes a key point. These methods use prompt templates like a * photo (Bui et al.,
      2024; Fan et al., 2024; Gandikota et al., 2024; Schramowski et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a) or


                                                         1

054
      acquire a large number of relevant texts from Large Language Models or datasets (Gandikota et al.,
055   2023; Huang et al., 2023; Kumari et al., 2023). While these solutions can ensure the safety of the
056   texts involved in the training, they cannot guarantee the safety of unseen texts. On the one hand, there
057   are still unsafe texts that cannot be covered beforehand. On the other hand, even if a given text does
058   not explicitly suggest unsafe concepts, the unsafe knowledge of the models can still lead to unsafe
059   images. This issue makes the models not only unsafe but also highly vulnerable to malicious attacks
060   (Chin et al., 2023; Pham et al., 2023; Tsai et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023b).
061   To tackle this challenge, we conduct an analysis of the erasure task. We point out that the objective of
062   the task is to minimize the overall likelihood of generating unsafe content, whereas current methods
063   solely focus on a portion of it. Ideally, we would devise a comprehensive set encompassing all
064   texts related to unsafe images, but such an endeavor remains impractical. To approximate it in
065   an effective way, we propose a greedy method that circumvents unsafe generation from a global
066   perspective. Specifically, we propose Dark Miner for text-to-image Diffusion Models, or DM2 .
067   The method is a recurring three-stage process including mining, verifying, and circumventing. In
068   the mining stage, DM2 learns a text embedding with the highest likelihood of generating unsafe
069
      concepts. In the verifying stage, DM2 assesses whether the embedding can lead to unsafe concepts,
      leveraging reference images and anchor images as benchmarks. If the verification is successful,
070
      the circumventing stage commences, where DM2 fine-tunes the models to modify the conditional
071
      generation probabilities to the one conditioned on an anchor text, ultimately returning to the mining
072   stage. Through the above process, it continuously reduces a tight upper bound on the overall likelihood
073   of unsafe generation, thus realizing a reduction in the overall likelihood. In the experiments, we
074   compare its performance with 6 state-of-the-art methods in erasing 6 concepts. The concepts include
075   the inappropriateness (nudity and violence), the objects (church and French horn), and the styles (Van
076   Gogh and crayon). The performance against 4 state-of-the-art attacks is reported as well. The results
077   show that DM2 achieves the best erasure performance and the best anti-attack performance while
078   preserving the ability of generations conditioned on general texts. In summary, the contributions of
079   this paper are as follows.
080
             • Based on the total probabilities, we analyze the reason why existing methods cannot com-
081
               pletely erase concepts for text-to-image diffusion models and are vulnerable to attacks.
082
083          • To tackle this challenge, we propose DM2 . It involves a recurring three-stage process, mining
084            optimal embeddings related to unsafe concepts and circumventing them after verification.
085          • DM2 is evaluated with 6 state-of-the-art methods. We evaluate the erasure performance on 6
086            concepts and the anti-attack performance when faced with 4 state-of-the-art attacks. DM2
087            achieves the best results while preserving the native generative capabilities.

093   Based on the Markov forward and backward diffusion process, diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Sohl-
094   Dickstein et al., 2015) train a noise estimator ϵθ (xt |t), which is a U-Net architecture (Ronneberger
095   et al., 2015), to estimate and remove noises from the sampled Gaussian noises step-by-step. Different
096   from the random generation of images, text-to-image diffusion models (Gal et al., 2022; Mou et al.,
097   2024; Nichol et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Ruiz et al., 2023; Saharia et al., 2022) achieve
098   text-guided image generation. Specifically, they use a text encoder to encode a given text c into
099   features. Some cross-attention modules are inserted between the middle layers of the diffusion
100   models, and regard the text features as keys and the image features as queries and values. In this way,
101
      a diffusion model becomes a noise estimator ϵθ (xt |t, c) conditioned on the text c. The models are
      trained by the following objective:

      2.2   C ONCEPT E RASURE
109
110   The large-scale datasets for training text-to-image diffusion models, usually crawled from the Internet,
111   contain unsafe or unexpected images. For example, LAION-5B (Schuhmann et al., 2022), which
112   is the training set of Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), has many violent, bloody, or sexual
113   images. It leads to unsafe image generation. Many methods have been proposed to erase unsafe
114   concepts from trained diffusion models. These methods can be classified into two categories. The
115   first is training-free, such as Safe Latent Diffusion (Schramowski et al., 2023). They prevent unsafe
116   generation by interfering with the generation processes or results. The second category requires the
117   updates of model weights. Erasing Stable Diffusion (Gandikota et al., 2023) and Concept Ablating
118
      (Kumari et al., 2023) modify the generation distributions conditioned on gathered unsafe texts via
      fine-tuning attention weights. Forget-Me-Not (Zhang et al., 2023a) suppresses the activation of
119
      attention maps associated with to-be-erased concepts. Bui et al. (2024) and Huang et al. (2023) have
120
      introduced learnable prompts in the fine-tuning. Unlike these fine-tuning methods, SalUn (Fan et al.,
121   2024) proposes to analyze specific model weights related to concepts. Without any training, Unified
122   Concept Editing (Gandikota et al., 2024) proposes a closed-form minimum solution for the parameters
123   of attention layers. These methods except for SalUn erase unsafe concepts according to limited given
124   prompts. While SalUn differs from them with better erasure performance, it sacrifices the generative
125   performance, leading to a significant drop in the generative performance. Contrary to existing
126   methods, Our method greedily mines embeddings with the highest likelihood of unsafe generation in
127   an iterative manner, reducing the overall probabilities of unsafe concepts more effectively.
128
129
      2.3   E RASURE ATTACKS
130
131   Some researchers design attacking methods to render the erasure methods ineffective. They search
132   for adversarial prompts to lead models to generate unsafe images. Circumventing Concept Erasure
133   (Pham et al., 2023), Prompting4Debugging (Chin et al., 2023), and UnlearnDiff (Zhang et al.,
134   2023b) are three attacking methods that use sanitized diffusion models to optimize safety-evasive
135   prompts. Different from them, Ring-A-Bell (Tsai et al., 2024) finds adversarial prompts by the
136   genetic algorithm and CLIP (Radford et al., 2021). It provides a model-agnostic adversarial prompt
137   searching tool. Experiments reveal that most existing erasing methods cannot effectively defend
138   against these attacks, exposing their incompleteness in erasing unsafe concepts.

186   3.2     DARK M INER
187
188   In Sec. 3.1, we point out that the gap between existing methods and the task lies in the difference in
189   the text sets, i.e. C ′ and C. However, bridging this gap is not a simple work. C is an open set and we
190   cannot include all possible texts in the training or inference process. Additionally, an approach that
191   involves all texts may also lead to a significant drop in the generation performance. Revisiting Eq.2,
192
      we notice that there is a tight upper bound on it:
193
                                              X                   X
                                   pθ (xe ) =    p(c)pθ (xe |c) ≤     p(c)M = M.                           (3)
194
                                                             c∈C          c∈C
195
196   where M = maxc∈C pθ (xe |c). If and only if pθ (xe |c) = M (∀c ∈ C), the equal sign in Eq.3 holds.
197   Therefore, if c∗ that satisfies pθ (xe |c∗ ) = M can be obtained, then pθ (xe ) can be reduced. It should
198
      be noted that when pθ (xe |c∗ ) is minimized, pθ (xe ) is not optimal globally because there exists
      another c∗′ so that pθ (xe |c∗′ ) becomes M at this time. An iterative manner is needed to mine c that
199
      can generate xe with the maximum probability, and modify the corresponding generation distribution.
200
201   We introduce our proposed method, Dark Miner for text-to-image Diffusion Models or DM2 , to
202   defend against unsafe generation as shown in Fig.1. It mainly consists of three stages, i.e. mining,
203   verifying, and circumventing, and runs in loops. Before starting DM2 , LoRA adapters (Hu et al.,
204
      2021) are inserted in the projection matrices of values in each attention module. The mining stage
      finds c with the maximum likelihood pθ (xe |c). The verifying stage verifies whether the model can
205
      generate xe with c. If c cannot meet the verifying condition, DM2 ends; otherwise, the circumventing
206
      stage modifies pθ (xe |c) by updating the adapters. Then DM2 returns to the mining stage for the next
207   loop.

224   3.2.2   V ERIFYING E MBEDDINGS
225
226   Before circumventing the mined embeddings, we verify whether the model can generate xe with them.
227   It can indicate whether to continue the erasure process. On the one hand, DM2 reduce the presence
228   of the embeddings related to the concepts through iterative mining and circumventing. After some
229
      loops of mining and circumventing, if the newly mined embeddings are irrelevant to the concepts,
      circumventing them will destroy the generative ability and lead to over-erasure. On the other hand, if
230
      the embeddings are related to the concepts but the erasure process is stopped early, it will result in
231
      incomplete erasure. This stage helps us avoid both over-erasure and incomplete-erasure.
232
233   A straightforward way is to train a model to recognize the generated images. However, it increases the
234   complexity of the task because a new classifier is required whenever we want to erase a new concept.
235
      To address this issue, DM2 involves CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), a vision-language model pre-trained
      on a large-scale dataset. Previous studies (Liang et al., 2022; Lyu et al., 2023) have demonstrated
236
      that the joint vision-language space in CLIP is not well-aligned but their delta features are aligned
237
      better. Here, the delta feature refers to the difference of the features of two images. Inspired by it,
238   DM2 verifies embeddings by calculating the cosine similarity of the delta features. Specifically, a
239   reference image xr is generated using the prompt a photo and a target image xc is generated using
240   the mined embedding c. xe is the image in PI,k used in the mining stage. Then the embedding can
241   be verified by the following metric:

      Training configurations. For implementing DM2 , the images in the image pool are generated by the
300   original diffusion model with the prompt a * photo, where * denotes the to-be-erased concept. In the
301   mining stage, the number of sampled images k is 3, the number of training epochs is 1000, the batch
302   size is 3, the learning rate is 0.1 and it decays to 0.01 at the 500-th epoch. The grads will be clipped if
303   their norm is larger than 10. In the verifying stage, the threshold τ is set to 0.2. In the circumventing
304   stage, the probability of sampling the current embedding r is 0.7, the number of epochs is 1000,
305   the batch size is 1, the learning rate is set to 0.01 and it decays to 0.001 at the 800-th epoch. The
306   adapters with a style of LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) are inserted into the projection matrices of values in
307   all attention modules in the diffusion model and the rank is 8. Only the adapters are fine-tuned. αc
308
      and αp in LC are set to 1 and 0.5 respectively. The grads will be clipped if their norm is larger than
      100. SGD optimizer is used. Each experiment is implemented on 1 NVIDIA A100 40GB GPU.
309
310   Baselines. The compared methods include Safe Latent Diffusion-strong (SLD) (Schramowski et al.,
311   2023), Concept Ablating (CA) (Kumari et al., 2023), Erasing Stable Diffusion (ESD) (Gandikota et al.,
312   2023), Forget-Me-Not (FMN) (Zhang et al., 2023a), Unified Concept Erasure (UCE) (Gandikota
313   et al., 2024) and Saliency Unlearning (SalUn) (Fan et al., 2024). Except where noted, Stable Diffusion

      v1.4 (SD v1.4) (Rombach et al., 2022) is used as the diffusion model to be erased. We fine-tune the
      model for each concept separately.

      Attacks. 4 attacks are used to obtain adversarial prompts. They include Circumventing Concept

      Erasure (CCE) (Pham et al., 2023), Prompting4Debugging (P4D) (Chin et al., 2023), UnlearnDiff

342
      Evaluation Metrics. The erasure, preservation, and anti-attack performance are evaluated.
343
344   The erasure performance refers to the ability to erase generation conditioned on normal prompts. The
345   models generate 10 images for each prompt. For inappropriateness, we follow Schramowski et al.
346
      (2023) to report the mean classification score (Score) of all generated images; for other concepts, we
      report the Score of the images classified into the corresponding class. We also report the ratio (Ratio)
347
      of the images classified into the corresponding class for all concepts. The lower they are, the better
348
      the performance.
349
350   The preservation performance refers to the generative performance of a sanitized model. Each model
351   generates 5,000 images using randomly sampled 5,000 captions from the COCO 2017 validation set
352
      (Lin et al., 2014). We report CLIP-Score and FID. FID is calculated between the 5,000 real images

      5    C ONCLUSION
531
532
      For erasing unsafe concepts in text-to-image diffusion models, most methods focus on modifying the
533
      generation distributions conditioned on gathered unsafe texts. However, they often cannot guarantee
534   the safe generation of texts unseen in the training phase, especially the adversarial texts. In this paper,
535   we re-analyze the task and point out that they fail to minimize the probabilities of unsafe generation
536   from a global perspective, leading to an overall unsafe likelihood that is not sufficiently weakened.
537   To address this problem, we propose Dark Miner. It mines embeddings with the maximum generation
538   likelihood of the target concepts and circumvents them, reducing the total probability of unsafe
539   generation. Experiments show that compared with the previous 6 methods, our method exhibits the
      best erasure and anti-attack performance in most cases while preserving the generation capability.

901   F     ATTACK A NALYSIS
902
903
904   For attacks like UnlearnDiff, they optimize prompts by gradient back-propagation. In this section, we
905   analyze the optimization process of UnlearnDiff used in the paper. Specifically, we randomly select
906   two successful and unsuccessful attacks. We plot their loss curves and the images before and after
907   attacking in Fig.S2. The images used for attacking are also shown.
908   Ideally, the loss curves for successful attacks should decrease, while the loss curves for unsuccessful
909   attacks should be non-decreasing. In Fig.S2, we can see that for the successful attacks, loss continues
910   to drop. However, for the unsuccessful attacks, interestingly, the loss also shows a decreasing trend.
911
      We analyze the possible reason for this phenomenon. We find that the generated images and the
912   attacking images are significantly different. Recall the principle of UnlearnDiff. In the training, it
913   optimizes prompts by minimizing MSE between real and predicted noises for the noised attacking
914   images. The previous study Balaji et al. (2023) reveals that the generation strongly relies on input
915   images during later sampling. Unfortunately, the attacking images are not seen in the evaluation
916   phase. Without their guidance, adversarial prompts successful in training fail in evaluation. Despite
917   success in evaluation, the inappropriateness degree is much lower than in attacking images. We hope
      that this preliminary analysis will facilitate research on attack methods.

962   G.1   E FFICIENCY
963
964
      While achieving better erasure and anti-attack performance, our method takes more time than the
965   previous methods. It is the limitation of our method. In this section, we would like to delve into it
966   further with detailed discussions below.
967
968
      First, we believe that the consumed time is necessary.
969   Since the training set contains a lot of unsafe images, the generative models are dirty. For SD v1.4,
970   the simple word nudity can generate an image of a nude person. Previous methods, such as SLD,
971   ESD, and UCE, collect words or sentences related to the concepts to be erased and then prevent their
      generation.


                                                       18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       However, these generative models are trained on large-scale training datasets. There is an abstract
973    high-dimensional mapping relationship between texts and images containing the target concepts. We
974    cannot exhaust all relevant prompts to prevent concept generation, especially adversarial prompts that
975    are difficult for humans to understand. It directly leads to the difficulty of defending against various
976    attacks. For example, when we apply UnlearnDiff to SLD, ESD, and UCE for nudity generation, the
977    attack success rates are all 100%.
978
       In this paper, we highlight mining the implicit knowledge of the target concepts. This design is the
979    reason why training time increases. Although previous methods do not have this process, they rely
980    on the collection of texts. Their text collection also requires a lot of time, but it is not included in
981    the training time. In addition, as mentioned earlier, the limitations of the collected texts cannot be
982    overcome, limiting the anti-attack performance. We propose automatic mining instead of manual
983    collection, significantly improving erasure and anti-attack performance. Compared with methods
984    such as SLD, ESD, and UCE, under UnlearnDiff for nudity generation, our attack success rate
985    drops 82%.
986    Second, there are some measures to reduce training time.
987
988
       (a) Raise the verifying threshold or set the maximum number of loops: It will stop the training
       process early, thereby trading off between erasure performance and time. For example, by raising the
989
       threshold from 0.2 to 0.3, the training time is saved by more than 50% (about 1 day), while the loss of
990
       anti-attack performance is less than 5%. Please refer to Tab.S2 for results under different thresholds.
991
992    (b) Prepare a cleaner model: A cleaner model implies less dark knowledge to be erased, thus reducing
993    the time cost. We apply the attack CCE to PixArt-α-512 (Chen et al., 2024) for nudity, and find that
994
       it cannot be successfully attacked, as shown in FigS3. It indicates that it contains almost no dark
       knowledge, which makes our method stop in just a few minutes.
995
996    In the future, we will explore the relationship among mined embeddings, and explore the acceleration
997    paradigm of our method.
