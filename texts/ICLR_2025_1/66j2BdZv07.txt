                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Synthesizing realistic 12-lead electrocardiogram (ECG) data is a complex task due
010
               to the intricate spatial and temporal dynamics of cardiac electrophysiology. Tradi-
011            tional generative models often struggle to capture the nuanced interdependencies
012            among ECG leads, which are essential for accurate medical analysis. In this paper,
013            we introduce a novel method that integrates partial differential equations (PDEs)
014            into a generative adversarial network (GAN) framework to model the spatiotem-
015            poral behavior of the heart’s electrical activity. By embedding PDE-based repre-
016            sentations directly into the generative process, our approach effectively captures
017            both the temporal evolution and spatial relationships between ECG leads. This
018            results in the production of high-fidelity synthetic 12-lead ECG data that closely
019
               mirrors real physiological signals. We conduct extensive experiments to evalu-
               ate the efficacy of our PDECGAN model, demonstrating that classifiers trained
020
               on our synthetic data outperform those trained on data generated by conventional
021
               methods in detecting cardiac abnormalities, with statistically significant improve-
022            ments. Our work highlights the potential of combining PDE-driven cardiac mod-
023            els with advanced generative techniques to enhance the quality and utility of syn-
024            thetic biomedical datasets.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Electrocardiograms (ECGs) are essential diagnostic tools for monitoring and detecting heart condi-
030   tions by recording the electrical activity of the heart through multiple leads. The 12-lead ECG, in
031   particular, provides comprehensive spatial information about cardiac function, making it invaluable
032
      for identifying a wide range of cardiac abnormalities.
033   Developing robust machine learning models for ECG analysis requires large and diverse datasets.
034   However, acquiring such datasets poses significant challenges due to privacy concerns, data security
035   issues, and the scarcity of data representing rare cardiac conditions (Voigt & Bussche, 2017). These
036   limitations hinder research and development efforts in cardiac healthcare applications.To overcome
037   these challenges, synthetic data generation has emerged as a promising solution (Giuffrè & Shung,
038
      2023; de Melo et al., 2022). While existing generative models, such as Generative Adversarial Net-
      works (GANs), have made progress, they often focus on single-lead ECGs, neglecting the complex
039
      interdependencies and spa- tiotemporal dynamics of 12-lead ECG signals. Accurately modeling
040
      these relationships is crucial for generating synthetic data that mirrors real-world cardiac function.
041
042   Recent advances in Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) have shown how integrating phys-
043   ical laws into neural networks can enhance the accuracy of predictions in various scientific do-
044
      mains (Raissi et al., 2019; Karniadakis et al., 2021). PINNs leverage known physical principles,
      such as partial differential equations (PDEs), to guide the learning process, ensuring that the gen-
045
      erated outputs respect the underlying laws of nature. This approach is particularly relevant in our
046
      work, where accurately capturing the spatiotemporal dynamics of cardiac electrophysiology is crit-
047   ical. Unlike ordinary differential equations (ODEs), which capture temporal dynamics alone, PDEs
048   model both temporal evolution and spatial relationships.
049
050
      Similar to PINNs, our method ensures that the synthetic ECGs not only look realistic but also adhere
      to the physiological principles governing heart activity, represent the intricate interactions within the
051
      heart’s electrical field and the interdependencies among the 12 ECG leads.
052
053   Inspired by this, we propose PDECGAN, a novel generative framework that integrates partial differ-
      ential equations (PDEs) representing cardiac electrophysiology into the GAN architecture (Good-

054
      fellow et al., 2014). By embedding PDE-based constraints within the generative model, PDECGAN
055   ensures that the synthetic ECG data not only appear realistic but also align with the underlying
056   physiological principles governing cardiac electrical activity.
057
058
      This integration of PDE-based constraints into the GAN’s training process enables the model to learn
      from both empirical data and the fundamental principles of cardiac electrophysiology, enhancing the
059
      realism and accuracy of the synthetic ECG signals. Consequently, the generated data better captures
060
      the observed patterns and physiological mechanisms inherent in real ECGs.
061
062   Classifiers trained on PDECGAN-generated data demonstrate improved performance in detecting
063   cardiac abnormalities, highlighting the practical advantages of incorporating physiological models

082   2   R ELATED W ORK
083
084   The success of deep learning (DL) models, particularly in medical applications, hinges on the avail-
085   ability of large, annotated datasets. However, data scarcity, privacy concerns, and ethical constraints,
086
      especially in the medical domain, hinder the collection of such datasets (de Melo et al., 2022). To
      address these limitations, synthetic data generation has emerged as a crucial tool, providing high-
087
      quality, diverse datasets for training deep neural networks (DNNs) (Cichy & Kaiser, 2019).
088
089   In the realm of cardiovascular diagnostics, synthetic electrocardiogram (ECG) data has gained at-
090   tention as a means to improve diagnostic models. ECGs are critical for diagnosing various heart
091   conditions, and generating realistic synthetic ECGs facilitates the development of robust machine
092
      learning models.
093   Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) have significantly advanced
094   synthetic data generation,including ECGs (Donahue et al., 2018). Early applications of GANs in
095   ECG generation, such as those by Zhu et al. (2019), Golany et al. (2020) and Golany et al. (2021),
096   demonstrated the potential of adversarial models, though they were primarily focused on single-lead
097   ECG generation, limiting their ability to capture the complexities of 12-lead signals.
098   More recent efforts have sought to address this limitation. Several methods have been proposed
099   for generating 12-lead ECG data. For instance, Liu et al. (2020) utilized vector quantized varia-
100   tional autoencoders (VQ-VAE) to improve ECG classifiers. Huang et al. (2023) developed a GAN-
101   based model for unsupervised noise ECG generation, and Alcaraz & Strodthoff (2023) presented
102   SSSD-ECG, a diffusion-based technique for 12-lead ECG synthesis. The most recent advancement,
103   MultiODE-GAN (Yehuda & Radinsky, 2024), extends ODE-based methods by simulating heart ac-
104
      tivity with coupled ODEs, building on the works of (Golany et al., 2020) and (McSharry et al.,
      2003). In our study, we compare PDECGAN with the current state-of-the-art models, specifically
105
      Alcaraz & Strodthoff (2023) and (Yehuda & Radinsky, 2024).
106
107   Our work builds on these advancements by integrating partial differential equations (PDEs) with
      GANs to model the spatiotemporal dynamics of 12-lead ECGs. PDEs provide a more comprehen-

      sive simulation of heart activity, capturing both the temporal progression and spatial distribution
109   of electrical signals across the heart. This approach is inspired by Physics-Informed Neural Net-
110   works (PINNs) (Raissi et al., 2019; Karniadakis et al., 2021), which have shown the effectiveness
111   of embedding physical laws, such as PDEs, into neural networks. By incorporating these principles,
112   PDECGAN achieves state-of-the-art performance in generating high-fidelity 12-lead ECG data, sur-

263   4     ECG C LASSIFIER
264
265   To evaluate the realism and utility of the synthetic 12-lead ECG data generated by PDECGAN, we
266   employ a state-of-the-art Residual Neural Network (ResNet) architecture, which is well-established
267   for its effectiveness in 12-lead ECG classification tasks (Attia et al., 2019; Ribeiro et al., 2020;
268   Nejedly et al., 2021). This model serves as a benchmark for assessing the impact of synthetic data
269   by comparing its performance when trained on real data versus a combination of real and synthetic
      data.


                                                            5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      The ResNet implementation begins with a convolutional layer containing 16 filters of size 7x7,
271   followed by a max-pooling layer to reduce dimensionality while enhancing feature extraction. The
272   network’s backbone consists of five residual blocks, each made up of three convolutional layers
273   with batch normalization and ReLU activation. Each block incorporates a skip connection, ensuring
274   that input data bypasses the convolutional layers and connects directly to the block’s output. This
275   structure mitigates the degradation of training signals, allowing for deeper network training.
276
      The network complexity increases across blocks, with the number of filters growing from 16 in
277   the first block to 64 in the last. A stride of 2 is applied to the first convolutional layer of each
278   block (except the first) to downsize the temporal dimension and manage computational load. The
279   final global average pooling layer reduces the feature space, feeding into a dense layer that outputs
280   the classification probabilities for abnormal ECGs, using a sigmoid activation function for binary
281   classification.
282
      To ensure optimal training, the network’s weights are initialized using the initialization method (He
283   et al., 2016), while biases are set to zero to maintain consistency and stability during training.

290   The empirical evaluation of our proposed method relies on the use of the Georgia 12-Lead ECG
291   Challenge (G12EC) dataset, sourced from Emory University, Atlanta, Georgia, as part of the Phys-
292   ionet 2020 Challenge (Alday et al., 2020). This dataset is pivotal to our study due to its comprehen-

294   recordings obtained from 7,871 patients, reflecting a broad demographic spectrum primarily from
295   the southeastern United States.
296   Each recording in the G12EC dataset captures 10 seconds of ECG data, sampled at a high resolu-
297   tion of 500 Hz, translating to 5,000 time points per recording. This high-frequency data collection
298   ensures detailed ECG waveforms, facilitating accurate analysis and synthesis in our research.
299
      To ensure robust model evaluation, we employed 5-fold cross-validation, dividing the dataset into
300
      training and validation subsets. This approach allows for more reliable performance estimates by
301   rotating the training and validation data across different splits. In addition, 20% of the dataset was set
302   aside as an independent test set, reserved exclusively for evaluating the final model’s performance.
303   This setup ensures that the model assessment is rigorous and generalizes well to unseen data.

      To evaluate the quality of the synthetic data generated by PDECGAN, we follow the evaluation
308
      methodology outlined in (Alcaraz & Strodthoff, 2023; Yehuda & Radinsky, 2024), focusing on 12-

      lead ECG classification using the G12C dataset.

311   We train a ResNet classifier (Section 4 ) on both real and synthetic data, then assess its performance
312   on a real test set. Comparable performance between models trained solely on real data and those
313
      trained on a combination of real and synthetic data would indicate the synthetic data’s high quality.
      We evaluate three scenarios:
314
315   No synthetic data: Training is conducted exclusively on real data.
316

317   erated by PDECGAN.
318

      native models (Section 6).

      deviates from the real data distribution, reflecting lower quality in the generated samples.

      I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
325
326   To prepare the raw ECG data for training and testing, we applied signal processing techniques us-
327   ing NeuroKit2, a Python library for neurophysiological signal processing (Makowski et al., 2021).
328   The ECG signal was segmented into individual heartbeat cycles by detecting R-peaks, which indi-
329   cate ventricular contractions. These R-peaks were detected using Lead II, known for its clear peak
330   visibility, and the RR intervals (time between successive R-peaks) were used for segmentation.
331   This segmentation is initially performed on Lead II, commonly used due to its clear visibility of
332   R-peaks, and serves as a reference for synchronizing the segmentation across the remaining leads.
333   By aligning the segmentation of all 12 leads with that of Lead II, we ensure temporal coherence
334   across the dataset, maintaining the integrity of the cardiac cycle’s representation in each lead.
335
      Additionally, to enhance the relevance and reliability of our dataset for training generative models,
336   we specifically select ECG examples that exhibit abnormalities consistently across all cycles. This
337   approach avoids the potential bias and variance in training that might arise from using examples
338   where anomalies are only present in isolated cycles within the signal. Each segmented ECG cycle,
339   represented as x̂ ∈ R12×L , retains the original annotations from the full signal x ∈ R12×5000 ,
340   ensuring that each cycle is accurately labeled according to the comprehensive diagnostic features of
341   the overall ECG recording.

512   7   C ONCLUSION
513
514   In this work, we introduced PDECGAN, a novel framework that integrates partial differential equa-
515   tions (PDEs) into a GAN architecture to generate realistic 12-lead ECG signals. By embedding both
516   spatial and temporal constraints, PDECGAN produces physiologically accurate synthetic data.
517   Our results demonstrate that classifiers trained on a combination of real and PDECGAN-generated
518   data outperform those trained on real data alone, achieving significant improvements in specificity
519   and generalization. This highlights the value of integrating physiological models into generative
520   frameworks for medical data synthesis.
521   PDECGAN enhances the fidelity of synthetic ECG data, making it highly valuable for clinical re-
522   search and machine learning applications in healthcare.

656
657
      We train the generator and discriminator iteratively using the standard GAN training procedure, in-
      corporating the PDE loss to enforce the physical constraints of the ECG dynamics. At each iteration,
658
      we:
659
660         1. Sample a batch of latent vectors {zk } and generate synthetic ECG sequences {Ûk }.
661                                             (D)
662         2. Compute the adversarial loss Ladv and update the discriminator parameters to minimize
663            this loss.
664         3. Compute the total generator loss LG using Equation equation 13.
665         4. Update the generator parameters θG and θf to minimize LG .
666
667   Role of the Neural Networks Two neural networks are involved in our model:
668
669          • The generator network G, which generates the initial ECG state u(0) from a latent vector
670            z.
671          • The network modeling f (ui (t); θf ), which captures the nonlinear physiological processes
672            influencing the ECG signals over time.
673
674   Choice of the Diffusion Coefficient D The diffusion coefficient D controls the strength of spatial
675   interactions between leads. A larger D implies stronger coupling between leads, which can capture
676   correlated activities across different parts of the heart.
677
678   Determination of Interaction Weights wij The weights wij are determined based on anatomical
679   knowledge and empirical correlations between ECG leads. For instance, leads that are physically
680   close or measure similar cardiac activity will have higher weights.
681
682   Numerical Stability and Time Step ∆t The choice of ∆t is crucial for numerical stability. We
683
      select ∆t to satisfy the Courant–Friedrichs–Lewy (CFL) condition for the discrete system, ensuring
      stable and accurate simulations.
684
685
      Integration with GAN Training The PDE constraints are integrated into the GAN training by
686
      incorporating the PDE loss LPDE into the total generator loss. This enforces the physical plausibility
687   of the generated ECG signals while still allowing the generator to learn from the data distribution
688   through the adversarial loss.
