011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The energy transition is driving the integration of large shares of intermittent
014            power sources in the electric power grid. Therefore, addressing the AC optimal
015
               power flow (AC-OPF) effectively becomes increasingly essential. The AC-OPF,
               which is a fundamental optimization problem in power systems, must be solved
016
               more frequently to ensure the safe and cost-effective operation of power systems.
017
               Due to its non-linear nature, AC-OPF is often solved in its linearized form, de-
018            spite inherent inaccuracies. Non-linear solvers, such as the interior point method,
019            are typically employed to solve the full OPF problem. However, these iterative
020            methods may not converge for large systems and do not guarantee global opti-
021            mality. This work explores a physics-informed graph neural network, PINCO,
022            to solve the AC-OPF. We demonstrate that this method provides accurate so-
023            lutions in a fraction of the computational time when compared to the estab-
024            lished non-linear programming solvers. Remarkably, PINCO generalizes effec-
025            tively across a diverse set of loading conditions in the power system. We show
026
               that our method can solve the AC-OPF without violating inequality constraints.
               Furthermore, it can function both as a solver and as a hybrid universal func-
027
               tion approximator. Moreover, the approach can be easily adapted to different
028
               power systems with minimal adjustments to the hyperparameters, including sys-
029            tems with multiple generators at each bus. Overall, this work demonstrates an
030            advancement in the field of power system optimization to tackle the challenges
031            of the energy transition. The code and data utilized in this paper are available at
032            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/opf_pinn_iclr-B83E/.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Power systems aim to ensure a reliable delivery of electricity to consumers. In a steady-state oper-
038   ation, power plants generate electric energy to meet the load demand while maintaining the voltage
039
      and frequency across the network within the required limits. The steady-state characteristic of the
      power system is described by the non-linear sinusoidal power flow equations, commonly known as
040
      the power flow (PF) problem. Additionally, by allocating electricity generation in a cost-effective
041
      manner throughout the day, the optimal power flow (OPF) problem is solved. This involves find-
042   ing the most economical generation dispatch that satisfies the power flow equations and operational
043   constraints. When no simplifications are made to the sinusoidal nature of the power flow equations,
044   the problem is referred to as the alternating current optimal power flow problem (AC-OPF). The
045   problem is used both in operation, being solved up to once every 5 minutes Nair et al. (2024) and
046   planning.
047
      Given the nonlinearity of the power flow equation, this problem is inherently non-convex and is
048   NP-hard Pineda et al. (2023). Traditionally, different approaches are used to solve the OPF. The
049   easiest method is using a linear form of the power flow equation, called DC approximation, based
050   on small-angle approximations. Using the DC power flow model brings well-known limitations Kile
051   et al. (2014); Baker (2020), leading to sub-optimal solutions of the complete AC-OPF. Alternatively,
052   convex relaxation techniques have been extensively examined Lavaei & Low (2010); Low (2014);
053   Molzahn & Hiskens (2016). Currently, nonlinear programming methods such as the interior point
      method are established approaches to solving the OPF Zimmerman & Wang (2016). Nevertheless,

054
      these methods still face significant computational burdens and scalability challenges when applied
055   to large-scale systems, limiting their utilization.
056
057   The limitations of traditional methods have driven researchers to explore alternative solutions us-
058
      ing machine learning techniques. One of the first approaches to solving the power flow problem
      is presented by Donon et al. (2020). They introduce a graph neural network (GNN) solver for the
059
      AC power flow problem, employing a supervised learning approach. Their pioneering work utilized
060
      GNNs, a deep learning architecture beneficial for non-Euclidean data structures, like power grid
061   datasets. Recent work Piloto et al. (2024) exploring the use of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) for
062   power systems, particularly for optimal power flow (OPF), demonstrates that GNNs can be effec-
063   tively trained in a supervised manner to address the computationally intensive security-constrained
064   AC-OPF problem Capitanescu et al. (2011). These studies utilize supervised learning techniques
065   to establish a mapping between loading conditions and AC-OPF solutions. However, a computa-
066   tional burden due to using a conventional solver to create the dataset is always involved. Further-
067   more, the conventional solver does not necessarily find the global optimum. With the advent of
068   physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) Raissi et al. (2019) and the subsequent growth of sci-
069
      entific machine learning, approaches that extend beyond pure data-driven learning have emerged.
      PINNs leverage physical laws to guide the training of neural networks, minimizing the need for
070
      labeled data. A comprehensive review of PINN applications in the power system domain is pro-
071
      vided in Huang & Wang (2023). In Nellikkath & Chatzivasileiadis (2022), the authors presented a
072   deep neural network trained using both data and physical equations, specifically incorporating the
073   AC-OPF KKT conditions. The works of Huang et al. (2024) and Chen et al. (2022) propose deep
074   neural networks for solving the AC-OPF without requiring labeled data, while also utilizing tradi-
075   tional methods to solve the nonlinear power flow equations. However, these approaches employ
076   deep neural networks that lack the ability to generalize across different power grid topologies. No-
077   tably, Owerko et al. (2022) combined GNNs with physics-informed learning to solve the AC-OPF
078   in an unsupervised manner. This approach enables solutions to the non-convex AC-OPF without
079   relying on other solvers, thus avoiding bias. Nevertheless, Owerko et al. (2022) did not obtain OPF
080
      solutions without violating constraints, and they did not test their method on power systems with
      more than one generator per electrical bus.
081
082   To our knowledge, no existing method employs physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) in a
083   fully end-to-end unsupervised manner without constraint violations that can compete with tradi-
084   tional solvers for solving the AC-OPF. Furthermore, we observe that previous works do not test the
085   capability of such solvers in power systems where multiple generators per bus are present.
086   In this work, we tackle these research gaps, proposing PINCO. We developed a novel approach for
087   the AC-OPF that combines GNNs and a variation of the conventional PINN paradigm that accounts
088   for problems with hard constraints, named H-PINN Lu et al. (2021). PINCO allows for solving
089   the AC-OPF without violations, and using GNN allows for easy adaptation to different power grid
090   topologies and scalability. Furthermore, we provide a modeling approach dealing with multiple
091   electricity generators per bus. Finally, we evaluate our developed methodology’s computational
092
      efficiency and performance compared to established non-linear optimization solvers, demonstrating
      that this approach can compete with state-of-the-art solutions. In this work, we assess PINCO’s
093
      potential as a nonlinear programming solver and test the canonical ability of neural networks as
094
      universal function approximators. We argue that the primary advantage of our approach lies in
095   its speed in providing feasible solutions and its ability to generalize to various inputs and power
096   system topologies. We test our approach on the IEEE9, IEEE24, IEEE30, and IEEE118 bus systems,
097   covering a wide range of power grid topologies.
098
      In this work, we contribute to the research community as follows:
099

              AC-OPF without inequality constraint violations.
103
104         • We prove its ability to act as a solver on a single loading condition and as a universal
105           function approximator that can act as a solver on unseen loading conditions.
106
107         • We test a method that leverages scientific machine learning to solve constrained non-linear
              optimization problems.

175   2.2   P HYSICS I NFORMED N EURAL N ETWORKS WITH H ARD C ONSTRAINTS
176
      Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) were first introduced in Raissi et al. (2019) as a data-
177
      driven approach to solving problems governed by partial differential equations (PDEs). PINNs
178
      leverage automatic differentiation to compute high-order derivatives of the neural network, which
179   is treated as the solution of a PDE, with respect to its inputs. Specifically, PINNs aim to learn the
180   solution u : I × Ω ⊆ Rm → Rn of a differential problem F[u(x, t)] = 0 for x ∈ Ω subject to
181   suitable boundary and initial conditions, using a neural network uNN . The network is trained by
182   minimizing the residuals LF associated with each equation in the differential problem. PINNs have
183   been widely applied across various domains, including heat transfer problems Cai et al. (2021b),
184   fluid dynamics Cai et al. (2021a), and power systems Misyris et al. (2020). Several variations of
185   PINNs have been proposed to address issues such as structural instability Mai et al. (2023), improve
186   accuracy Eshkofti & Hosseini (2023), or better approximate discontinuous solutions to hyperbolic
187
      equations De Ryck et al. (2024).
188   Physics-informed neural networks with hard constraints (hPINNs) were introduced in Lu et al.
189   (2021) to optimize an objective function J while incorporating equality constraints hj , ∀hj ∈ H
190   and inequality constraints gl , ∀gl ∈ G. This approach uses both the penalty method and the Aug-
191   mented Lagrangian (AL) method. At each iteration k, it minimizes the loss function to identify the
192   solution parameters wu :

      3.1   DATASET STRUCTURE
287
288   We model the electrical grid as a graph, with a set of nodes N corresponding to electrical buses, and
289   edges E to model the branches. The graph is defined as G = (N, E, N, A). We call N ∈ R|N |×t
290   the node feature matrix, with |N | equal to the number of nodes and t to the number of features per
291   node, and A ∈ R|N |×|N | is the adjacency matrix. The elements of A, aij , are equal to 1 if there is
292   an edge from node i to node j, and zero otherwise. We predict Y ∈ R|N |×f , the node output matrix;
293   thus, a vector of f element is predicted for each node in N .
294
      Figure 2 illustrates the structure of a power grid with its features. The input node features are de-
295
      picted in red, while the output node-level predictions are in green. The input features include active
296
      power demand (Pd ), reactive power demand (Qd ), and node type. The output features vary depend-
297   ing on the node type. For example, if a certain variable is known (e.g., buses without generators
298   don’t require predictions for generated power), we apply a masking process during training. These
299   masked values are represented by grey cells. The predicted output quantities include active power
300   generation (Pg ), reactive power generation (Qg ), voltage magnitude (V ), and voltage angle (θ).
301
      We propose a method to manage buses that have multiple generators. Since each generator is as-
302
      sociated with a unique cost, distinguishing between them is essential. To address this, we add an
303   additional node for each extra generator. These nodes are ’artificial,’ as they do not correspond to
304   an actual bus on the grid. For example, a bus with two generators, as shown in Figure 2, would be
305   split into two distinct nodes, each linked to its own generator and connected via artificial lines. The
306   voltage magnitude and angle of these artificial nodes are set to match those of the original node. In
307   total, there are four possible node types, encoded as categorical variables: (1) load bus without a
308   generator, (2) bus without load or generators, (3) original generator bus, and (4) artificial generator
309   bus.

407   cases, the training, validation, and test datasets were created from a common set of generated inputs,
408   which were then split respectively into 80%, 10%, and 10%. To evaluate PINCO’s generalization
409   capability in addressing the AC-OPF under unseen loading conditions, we assess its performance on

456
      In this study, we develop a physics-informed graph neural network-based method, namely PINCO.
457
      We evaluate the performance of the PINCO method to solve an optimization problem with hard
458
      constraints, i.e., the AC-OPF. We assessed its capability as a solver for a single-demand scenario
459   and observed that it generalizes well to multiple demands. Thus, showcasing its role as a universal
460   function approximator that solves optimization problems.
461
462
      The method is tested on multiple IEEE benchmark systems, representing different grid topologies
      and sizes, with a focus on grids containing multiple generators. Across all tests, the PINCO demon-
463
      strates zero inequality constraint violations, highlighting its effectiveness in handling hard con-
464
      straints. When applied as a solver, the PINCO provides solutions comparable to traditional solvers
465   for the IEEE 9-bus and IEEE 30-bus systems. Moreover, it outperforms traditional solvers in re-
466   ducing equality constraint violations for the more complex IEEE 24-bus and IEEE 118-bus systems,
467   though this comes at a marginal cost increase of only 0.6% and 1.2%, respectively.
468
      In scenarios where the PINCO is tested on multiple demands, its generalization performance for un-
469
      seen loading conditions shows a slight deterioration in the equality constraint violations, while the
470
      associated costs remain comparable to traditional methods. However, the method’s computational
471   advantage in the inference phase, being two orders of magnitude faster than traditional solvers,
472   makes it a highly competitive alternative. Future work will focus on scaling the method to larger
473   grids with more realistic loading conditions and addressing the N-1 AC-OPF problem in an unsu-
474   pervised manner.
