009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Constrained codes enhance reliability in high-speed communication systems and
012            optimize bit efficiency when working with non-binary data representations (e.g.,
013            three-level ternary symbols). A key challenge in their design is minimizing the
014            hardware complexity of the translation logic that encodes and decodes data. We
015            introduce a reinforcement learning (RL)-based framework, augmented by a cus-
016            tom L1 similarity-based heuristic, to design hardware-efficient translation logic,
017            navigating the vast solution space of codeword assignments. By modeling the
018            task as a bipartite graph matching problem and using logic synthesis tools to eval-
019
               uate hardware complexity, our RL approach outperforms human-derived solutions
               and generalizes to various code types. Finally, we analyze the learned policies to
020
               extract insights into high-performing strategies.

      Reinforcement learning (RL) has been successfully applied to numerous tasks in chip design.
026
      Works such as DRiLLS (Hosny et al., 2020) and Retrieval-Guided RL (Goliaei et al., 2024), have
027
      demonstrated RL’s ability to optimize tasks like logic synthesis, which involves converting high-
028   level hardware descriptions into optimized gate-level representations to minimize circuit complex-
029   ity and improve performance. Similarly, Mirhoseini et al. (2021) introduced a graph-based RL
030   methodology to optimize circuit placement, significantly reducing layout generation time compared
031   to traditional methods.
032
      Building on recent advances, we address a distinct challenge: constrained code design. Con-
033
      strained codes restrict sequences or patterns in communication and data storage, ensuring they meet
034   specific rules (e.g., avoiding certain bit patterns). Unlike typical digital design tasks where the logi-
035   cal specifications are fixed (e.g., an adder), our approach applies RL to determine valid and efficient
036   codeword assignments that adhere to these constraints. Instead of simply optimizing existing logic,
037   RL is used to dynamically generate the assignments in a lookup table structure, which presents a
038   unique challenge for RL agents.
039

040   data reliability and efficiency in many systems.
041   Run-Length Limited (RLL) codes (e.g 8b10b)
042   are implemented in standards like PCIe, USB,
043   and Ethernet to prevent signal degradation by
044   limiting long runs of similar bits (PCI-SIG,
045   2019). Similarly, Data-Bus Inversion (DBI) is
046   used in memory interfaces like HBM and DDR
047   to reduce power consumption (Hollis, 2009).

111   ing problem, where the goal is to assign input sequences to codewords while satisfying specific
112   constraints. Formally, a bipartite graph G = (X, Y, E) consists of two disjoint sets of nodes:
113   X, representing the unrestricted domain of input sequences (locations), and Y , representing the
114   restricted domain of encoded outputs (codewords). Each node in X corresponds to a potential
115   input bit sequence, while each node in Y corresponds to a valid encoded sequence. The edges
116   E ⊆ X × Y represent valid assignments between input sequences and codewords, defining the
117   possible mappings in the problem space.

141   2.1   C LASSICAL C OMBINATORIAL O PTIMIZATION A LGORITHMS
142
143   For combinatorial optimization problems, Simulated Annealing (SA) (Kirkpatrick et al., 1983) and
144   Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) (Browne et al., 2012) are proven techniques. SA employs proba-
145   bilistic exploration of solution spaces by allowing occasional moves away from gradients to escape
146
      local minima. While effective, SA can be slow to converge. In contrast, MCTS incrementally builds
      a search tree through random sampling and refines its exploration based on promising branches.
147
      Although successful in various combinatorial optimization and game-playing tasks, MCTS was
148
      deemed impractical for our problem due to the large branching factor and computational overhead.
149
150
      2.2   L INEAR P ROGRAMMING & SAT/SMT
151
152   Linear optimization techniques, including Linear Programming (LP) (Kuhn, 1955; Jonker & Vol-
153   genant, 1987), Integer Linear Programming (ILP) (Cunningham, 1976), and network flow algo-
154   rithms (Edmonds & Karp, 1972), have been successfully applied to bipartite assignment and match-
155   ing problems. These approaches rely on linear formulations and relaxations of the problem, seeking
156   to optimize assignments between two sets (e.g., tasks and workers, or locations and resources) while
157   minimizing or maximizing a given cost function. In the standard assignment problem, given two sets
158   X and Y and a cost matrix C, where Cij represents the cost of assigning P    elementP i ∈ X to ele-
159   ment j ∈ Y , the objective is to minimize the total cost of assignment: min i∈X j∈Y Cij xij ,
                                  P                                      P
160   subject to the constraints:   j∈Y xij = 1 for all i ∈ X, and         i∈X xij = 1 for all j ∈ Y ,
161   with xij ∈ {0, 1}. The LP relaxation relaxes this constraint to xij ∈ [0, 1], making the problem
      tractable. These approaches assume that the assignment costs are readily available or precomputed,

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      which is infeasible given our need to invoke the synthesis tool for cost evaluations. In contrast, our
163   RL framework samples potential assignments incrementally, allowing it to learn which assignments
164   lead to better outcomes while minimizing the number of synthesis evaluations required.
165
166
      In addition to linear optimization, the problem of codeword selection and logic complexity mini-
      mization has also been approached using SAT/SMT solvers. Recent work (Anonymous, 2024) has
167
      demonstrated their potential for small- to medium-sized codes. Rather than using a traditional LUT
168
      or matching approach, this work focused on generating an efficient circuit structure (e.g., Sum of
169   Products) while asserting constraints on the total number of minterms (i.e., gates). While the authors
170   do show their methods attain solutions competitive with human solutions for the MTA code, they
171   faced challenges scaling to larger codes.

      2.3   N EURAL M ETHODS FOR C OMBINATORIAL O PTIMIZATION , C ODE D ESIGN , AND L OGIC
174         S YNTHESIS
175
176   Over the past several years, there has been considerable progress in Neural Combinatorial Opti-
177   mization (NCO) using RL and Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). For example, Georgiev & Liò
178   (2021) apply RL and GNNs to solve bipartite matching through a flow-based formulation, while
179   Dwivedi et al. (2021) extend these approaches to problems like the Traveling Salesman Problem
180   (TSP) and bipartite matching. Manchanda et al. (2023) further enhance NCO techniques by inte-
181
      grating meta-learning with RL to improve generalization across problem distributions. However,
      all these approaches compute assignment costs inline, contrasting with our method, which relies on
182
      external evaluations for cost calculations.
183
184   Related to the design of codes, Liao et al. (2020) and Miloslavskaya et al. (2024) use RL to construct
185   polar codes for wireless communication. Their goal is to improve error-correction performance by
186   framing bit selection as a sequential decision-making task. In contrast, our objective focuses on
187
      minimizing hardware logic complexity, which introduces different challenges. Furthermore, their
      method computes costs inline. Finally, Qin et al. (2023) use RL to develop fountain codes.
188
189   As noted in the Introduction, recent work such as DRiLLS and related frameworks have successfully
190   applied RL to synthesis optimization tasks. These approaches focus on optimizing fixed logical
191   structures like adders and multipliers by adjusting synthesis parameters. In contrast, our work tackles
192   the problem of optimizing constrained code design, where the RL agent determines the logical
193
      structure itself, offering a distinct set of challenges and opportunities for optimization.

      3.1   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING A LGORITHMS
210
211   To solve the bipartite matching problem using RL, we considered the type of learning approach
212   (online vs. offline), the environment details (model-based vs. model-free), and the choice between
213   value-based and policy-based networks. Additionally, our environment is purely deterministic.
214   Given the same inputs, constraints, and parameters, the synthesis tools will consistently produce
215   the same output, meaning each state-action pair will always lead to the same next state. Below, we
      outline the rationale behind our choices:


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
            1. Online vs Offline Learning: We opted for online learning, where the agent interacts with
217            the environment in real-time, dynamically adapting and refining its policy based on live
218            feedback. This approach is particularly suitable for our problem given the fast solve times
219            of the synthesis tools.
220
            2. Model-Based vs Model-Free: Despite the deterministic environment, we chose model-free
221
               methods, which learn directly from interactions rather than relying on a pre-built model.
222
               Logic optimization has a highly non-linear solution space, which makes creating accu-
223            rate performance models difficult. Given our fast solve times, we prioritized model-free
224            methods for their simplicity and direct application. Model-free methods like Double-DQN,
225            PPO, and distributional RL variants such as C25 and C11 (Bellemare et al., 2017) allowed
226            us to focus on optimizing the policy without the additional complexity of maintaining an
227            accurate model.
228
            3. Value-Based vs Policy-Based Network: We explored both value-based methods, such as
229            Double DQN, and C51, as well as policy-based methods, including A2C and PPO. We
230            found the value-based methods to perform quite well with hyperparameter tuning with
231            regards to both attained solutions and stability. Though we expected PPO to perform quite
232            well due to its balance between exploitation and exploration, its performance was always
233            worse than Double DQN.
234
235   As detailed in Section 4, most algorithms achieved similar final solutions in terms of value, but they
236   differed in terms of convergence rates, stability, and other performance characteristics.

526   6   C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this work, we introduced a reinforcement learning (RL) framework to automate the process of
529   constrained code design, addressing a critical gap left by synthesis tools that cannot optimize input
530   mappings. Our approach was applied to challenging encoding schemes, including MTA (7b8b),
531   8b9b, and 5s8b (ternary weight compression) codes. By leveraging an L1 similarity-based
532   heuristic, our RL framework efficiently explores the solution space using feedback on key met-
533   rics such as gate count, outperforming random search and hand-crafted designs.
534   Our results demonstrate that RL can handle complex encoding schemes, providing a practical
535   solution for automating hardware optimization tasks. Furthermore, beyond constrained codes for
536   high-speed links, this approach shows promise for efficient quantized AI model weight storage,
537   an area of growing interest. While this work demonstrates the effectiveness of our RL framework
538   on a variety of constrained code designs, ongoing refinements are aimed at scaling the framework
539   to handle even larger codes and exploring additional heuristics to further improve computational
      efficiency and solution quality.

674
      The LUT formulation is a rather straightforward approach to implement encoder and decoder func-
      tions, but other techniques have been published over the last few decades. For instance, Adler et al.
675
      (1983) pioneered the development of sliding block codes in the 1980s, utilizing a finite state machine
676
      (FSM)-based approach to enforce constrained codes like run-length limited (RLL) codes briefly dis-
677   cussed in the introduction. Their method, while effective for defining and enforcing constraints,
678   involves design decisions such as state splitting and transition assignments, which can impact the
679   hardware efficiency of the implementation. Our work, while distinct, is complementary to this ap-
680   proach. Namely, our methodology offers potential optimizations that could be applied to the state
681   splitting and transition assignments in Adler’s FSM-based designs.
