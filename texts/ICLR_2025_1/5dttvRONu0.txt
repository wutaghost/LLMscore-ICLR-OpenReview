                                                   A BSTRACT
011
012            Federated learning (FL) is a privacy-preserving machine learning framework that
013            enables multiple nodes to train models on their local data and periodically av-
014            erage weight updates to benefit from other nodes’ training. Each node’s goal is
015
               to collaborate with other nodes to improve the model’s performance while keep-
               ing its training data private. However, this framework does not guarantee data
016
               privacy. Prior work has shown that the gradient-sharing steps in FL can be vul-
017
               nerable to data reconstruction attacks from a honest-but-curious central server. In
018            this work, we show that a honest-but-curious node/client can also launch attacks to
019            reconstruct peers’ image data in a centralized system, presenting a severe privacy
020            risk. We demonstrate that a single client can silently reconstruct other clients’ pri-
021            vate images using diluted information available within consecutive updates. We
022            leverage state-of-the-art diffusion models to enhance the perceptual quality and
023            recognizability of the reconstructed images, further demonstrating the risk of in-
024            formation leakage at a semantic level. This highlights the need for more robust
025            privacy-preserving mechanisms that protect against silent client-side attacks dur-
026
               ing federated training.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Federated learning (FL) has attracted significant attention as a promising approach to privacy-
033
      preserving machine learning (McMahan et al., 2017; Kairouz et al., 2021). In this framework, a
      central server coordinates training by multiple clients. In each training round, the server broadcasts
034
      a shared model to a subset of clients. Each client computes a gradient by training the model on
035
      its private data and returns the gradient to the server. The server then averages all the gradients
036   and updates the model. This approach enables each participant to benefit from a model trained on
037   more data without sharing its own data. FL has the potential to revolutionize collaborative efforts in
038   such real-world applications as healthcare and finance, enabling participants to train better models
039   without compromising data privacy (Li et al., 2020a).
040
      Despite the intent to protect privacy through FL, prior works have shown that a honest-but-curious
041
      central server can reconstruct a client’s training data. This is done by adjusting a dummy input to the
042   model until its resulting gradient closely matches the gradient sent by the client (Zhu et al., 2019;
043   Geiping et al., 2020; Yue et al., 2023). Meanwhile, studies on malicious clients have shown that a
044   client can disrupt federated training by sending adversarial data to the server (Blanchard et al., 2017;
045   Shi et al., 2022). However, far less attention has been given to the potential for clients to reconstruct
046   others’ data while honestly participating in the FL network.
047
      Our work explores the extent to which a single client can reconstruct peers’ training data while
048   adhering to FL protocols. We introduce a novel client-to-client attack depicted in Figure 1, where
049   the attacker exploits weight updates between consecutive training rounds to reconstruct training
050   images. By participating in two consecutive training rounds and comparing the global model’s
051   weights, a single client extracts the averaged gradient of all participants in the earlier round. Unlike
052   server-side gradient inversion (Zhu et al., 2019; Geiping et al., 2020; Yue et al., 2023), this attack
053   requires isolating individual data from a diluted mixture of gradient updates. Despite this challenge,
      we show that the attacker is able to reconstruct images from every other client.

101   2      R ELATED W ORK
102
103   Server-Side Gradient Inversion. The assumption that FL inherently protects data privacy has been
104   challenged by studies exposing vulnerabilities to gradient inversion attacks from the central server
105   (Zhu et al., 2019; Geiping et al., 2020). These attacks exploit the gradients shared by clients to re-
106   construct private training data. They revealed that by iteratively updating a dummy input to produce
107   a gradient similar to a given target gradient, the server could generate images closely resembling
      the participant’s original training data. Various defense mechanisms have been proposed to protect


                                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      against these attacks, including gradient compression, perturbation, and differential privacy tech-
109   niques (Zhang et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2021). Despite these efforts, recent studies have shown that
110   these defenses may not effectively prevent training data from being meaningfully reconstructed. For
111   example, Yue et al. (2023) overcame these defense methods by leveraging latent space reconstruc-
112   tion and incorporating generative models to remove distortion from reconstructed images.
113
      Client-Side Model Inversion. While the majority of research has focused on server-side attacks,
114
      the potential for client-side attacks has been less examined. Wu et al. (2024) investigated model
115   inversion attacks, where a client exploits the model’s overfitting to reconstruct training data. This
116   approach relies on manipulating the model rather than directly reconstructing other clients’ data.
117   Similar to our work’s takeaway, their results demonstrated that clients can reconstruct peers’ images
118   without disrupting the training process. However, due to the challenging nature of model inversion,
119   their method produces reconstructed images far less similar to the target than those from gradient
120   inversion attacks.
121   Malicious Client Attacks. A parallel research direction focuses on attacks where a malicious client
122   interferes with the FL process (Blanchard et al., 2017; Shejwalkar et al., 2022). Specifically, mali-
123   cious clients can manipulate the model updates by using poisoned data or sending poisoned gradients
124   to the server to impede convergence. Meanwhile, researchers have shown that malicious modifica-
125   tions can compromise privacy easily (Fowl et al., 2021; Wen et al., 2022). While this introduces
126   unique security challenges in FL, our attack does not disrupt the training process and is difficult to
127   be detected by the server or other clients.

129   3     P ROPOSED I MAGE R ECONSTRUCTION ATTACK BY C URIOUS C LIENTS
130
131   In this section, we present the gradient inversion attack, which allows an honest-but-curious client
132   to reconstruct image data from other clients. To enhance this reconstruction, two postprocessing
133   methods are introduced to achieve both fine-grained quality and perceptual realism. The first post-
134   processing method improves the images at the pixel level with enhanced details. The second method,
135   built on a masked diffusion enhancer, refines the images at the semantic level and produces photo-
136   realistic reconstructions.
137
138   3.1   ATTACK F RAMEWORK
139
140
      Threat Model. We consider an honest-but-curious client (or curious client, for simplicity) attacker.
      It aims to reconstruct other clients’ training data while following the protocol of FL. The attacker
141
      does not disrupt the model training process. The curious client does not have direct access to the
142
      gradients from other clients. However, it receives an updated version of the shared model from the
143   server at each communication round. Additionally, we assume as in Li et al. (2020b); Huang et al.
144   (2020) that all client updates in a given round have been computed using the same learning rate,
145   which is applied locally if each client transmits a model update, as shown in Eq. (2). It may also
146   be applied globally if clients transmit raw gradients to the server, as discussed in Section 4. The
147   attacker may not know the number of clients in each training round but can correctly guess the total
148   number of training images. We follow the assumption of Yue et al. (2023) that each client trains
149   for τ iterations on the same minibatch of images in each local iteration/update round and that the
150   class labels have been analytically inverted as in Ma et al. (2023). We target cross-silo FL scenarios,
151
      in which a small number of clients collaborate to overcome data scarcity. For example, a group of
      hospitals may use FL to develop a classifier for rare diseases from CT scans, where each has limited
152
      training examples and images cannot be directly shared due to privacy concerns. We assume that the
153
      system is designed to prioritize model accuracy and uses synchronous gradient updates. Clients are
154   not edge devices and have sufficient computational resources to perform the optimization process
155   while participating in FL.

202   3.2   D IRECT P OSTPROCESSING FOR P IXEL -L EVEL I MAGE E NHANCEMENT
203
204   To reconstruct the target data more effectively, we introduce a direct postprocessing method that
205   utilizes pretrained diffusion models to perform super resolution and denoising on the raw image
206   reconstructions. The raw reconstructed images from the attack may be low-resolution or have pixel
207
      artifacts due to imperfect gradient inversion. This problem may also be more severe in our attack
      compared to server-side gradient inversion as the target gradient contains diluted information from
208
      multiple clients. To address this problem, we introduce a postprocessing implementation, ϕ(·) ≡
209
      ϕdirect (·) that uses pretrained diffusion models to directly postprocess the raw reconstructed images.
210   Diffusion models have demonstrated good performance in image generation and restoration tasks
211   and are able to produce more realistic images with a lower likelihood of hallucination (Dhariwal &
212   Nichol, 2021). Our method follows the denoising diffusion restoration models (DDRM) framework
213   and utilizes a pretrained DDPM (Ho et al., 2020) as a backbone model. DDRM has demonstrated
214   strong performance across various image restoration tasks, including super resolution and denoising
215   (Kawar et al., 2022). By increasing resolution and removing noise, we aim to accurately reveal
      details of the ground truth images and make the reconstructions more recognizable.

      3.3   M ASKED D IFFUSION E NHANCER : RECONSTRUCTION AT A SEMANTIC LEVEL
239
240   In this subsection, we also introduce a method to reconstruct target images at the semantic level,
241   ϕ(·) ≡ ϕsemantic (·), the masked diffusion enhancer (MDE). The goal of MDE is to generate sharp,
242   high-resolution images from the low-resolution, color-aliased raw attack results. This approach
243   complements the direct postprocessing technique, as the generated images resemble the raw recon-
244   structions at the semantic level, rather than at the pixel level. The generated images preserve the
245   shape and perceptual features of the target image while achieving photorealistic quality.
246   Backbone Model. We use masked diffusion transformer (MDT) as the backbone of our reconstruc-
247   tion technique. MDT has been proven to achieve state-of-the-art performance in image generation
248   (Gao et al., 2023). Due to its extensive training and flexibility, MDT has learned a complex repre-
249   sentation of each image class that enables it to accurately reconstruct each image’s semantic features
250   through projection onto the manifold. Following the diffusion framework, MDT generates images
251   by starting from a Gaussian noise vector XT ∼ N (0, I), where T is the total number of diffusion
252
      steps. At each step t, the model predicts a noise residual ϵθ (Xt ), and uses it to refine the noisy
      image Xt to Xt−1 . After T iterations, the initial noise vector XT will be transformed into a high
253
      quality image X0 . For our reconstruction technique, we leverage a pretrained MDT and freeze its
254
      model parameters throughout the process to maintain consistency in the image generation pipeline.
255
256   Proposed Masked Diffusion Enhancer (MDE). MDE optimizes the initial noise vector XT to
257   generate an image that closely matches a target image X̂. During optimization, the noise predictions
258   ϵθ (Xt ) at each timestep are treated as constants. The objective of MDE is to minimize the mean
259   squared error (MSE) between the final generated image µθ (XT , T ) and the target image X̂:
260                                                                      2

      sion steps. By optimizing XT based on the loss term, we guide the model to generate images that

      5                                    C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
465
466
      We have demonstrated that a curious client attacker can successfully reconstruct high-quality images
      from a small number of clients simply by participating in two consecutive training rounds. This type
467
      of attack does not alter the training process or introduce corrupted data, making it difficult to de-
468
      tect by the server or other clients in the system. Our findings indicate that the attack is particularly
469   effective when the number of participating clients is small or the available training examples are lim-
470   ited. This raises a significant concern for cross-silo FL, where participants collaborate specifically
471   to overcome data scarcity (Li et al., 2020a). In such settings, our findings reveal a serious privacy
472   risk, as the reconstruction of sensitive data becomes more feasible. Further research is needed to
473   assess the robustness of more advanced FL frameworks against curious client attacks and develop
474   effective defenses to preserve data privacy in cross-silo FL.

664   A    D ISCUSSION
665
666
      Limitations. Our attack struggles to reconstruct high-quality images in systems where the number
667   of clients is large. As the information contained within the attacker’s target gradient is averaged from
668   more clients, it becomes more difficult to reconstruct high-quality images. With more than 8 clients,
669   we observe that some reconstructed images remain high quality while others exhibit significant
670   degradation or are not recognizable. Figure 12 shows the varying quality in our reconstructed images
671   in a system of 16 clients. Additionally, we observe that the postprocessors are often able to restore
672   image details that may not be obvious to a human observer looking at the raw reconstruction results.
673   However, they are not able to restore images when the raw reconstruction result does not provide
674   enough information, which is a problem common to all postprocessing tasks.
675   Nonuniform Learning Rate. The proposed attack relies on each client’s gradient update being
676   scaled by the same learning rate and this is also necessary for the global model to converge with
677   FedAvg. To achieve the best model convergence during federated training, FedAvg scales each
678   client’s update by the client’s number of training images, which gives the individual gradient of
679   each training image equal weight in the global model update. If clients used very different learning
680   rates, the clients with larger learning rates would dominate the global weight update, leading to
681
      suboptimal convergence. This is the basis for the assumption that the clients’ learning rate in each
      round is either set by the server or otherwise controlled. For example, the clients may use a learning
682
      rate scheduler but agree on its parameters so the scale of their updates does not vary significantly in
683
      a given round. Regardless of how the learning rate is set during federated training, scaling individual
684   image gradients unevenly in a way that would disrupt the attack is also likely to impede the global
685   model’s convergence.
686
687
      Unknown Number of Images. The proposed attack assumes that the attacker can correctly guess
      the total number of training images N in a given round. This assumption simplifies the attacking
688
      algorithm but is not always needed. Instead, the attacker can search for this integer value and decide
689
      on the best guess leading to a successful recovery of the attacker’s own training images. If both the
690   learning rate and number of images are unknown, a joint parameter search can be conducted.
691
692   Application to Secure Aggregation. We evaluate the similarity between our approach and server-
693
      side attacks against the secure aggregation protocol, identifying both significant differences and an
      additional application scenario of our attack. Our problem of inverting the aggregated gradients
694
      of multiple clients is similar to the problem server-side attackers encounter in systems using the
695
      secure aggregation protocol, which prevents the parameter server from knowing individual clients’
696   gradients (Bonawitz et al., 2017). Despite this similarity, we have not found any other works that
697   obtain high-quality reconstructions without modifying the global model (Shi et al., 2023; Zhao et al.,
698   2024) or relying on additional information the server might have about the client devices, such as
699   device type and available memory (Lam et al., 2021), which would not be possible for a client
700   attacker. Most of these attacks also rely on information collected across many training rounds,
701   which may not be available to a client who cannot choose which rounds it is selected to participate
      in. In contrast, our attack does not require the attacker to disrupt the training protocol or have


                                                        13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      any information about the other clients beyond the model updates and total number of training
703   images, which it may be able to guess. It also relies only on information from two consecutive
704   training rounds. This indicates that our attack could also be performed by a server against a securely
705   aggregated gradient and would allow it to avoid modifying the global model, maintaining the honest-
706   but-curious threat model.
