011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Decision Transformers (DT) play a crucial role in modern reinforcement learning,
014            leveraging offline datasets to achieve impressive results across various domains.
015            However, DT requires high-quality, comprehensive data to perform optimally. In
016            real-world applications, the lack of training data and the scarcity of optimal be-
017            haviours make training on offline datasets challenging, as suboptimal data can
018
               hinder performance. To address this, we propose the Counterfactual Reasoning
               Decision Transformer (CRDT), a novel framework inspired by counterfactual rea-
019
               soning. CRDT enhances DT’s ability to reason beyond known data by generating
020
               and utilizing counterfactual experiences, enabling improved decision-making in
021            unseen scenarios. Experiments across Atari and D4RL benchmarks, including
022            scenarios with limited data and altered dynamics, demonstrate that CRDT out-
023            performs conventional DT approaches. Additionally, reasoning counterfactually
024            allows the DT agent to obtain stitching abilities, combining suboptimal trajecto-
025            ries, without architectural modifications. These results highlight the potential of
026            counterfactual reasoning to enhance reinforcement learning agents’ performance
027            and generalization capabilities.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In the pursuit of achieving artificial general intelligence (AGI), reinforcement learning (RL) has
033   been a widely adopted approach. Conventional RL methods have shown impressive success in
034   training AI agents to perform tasks across various domains, such as gaming (Mnih et al., 2015; Silver
035   et al., 2017) and robotic manipulation (Van Hoof et al., 2015). When referring to conventional RL
036   approaches, we mean methods that train agents to discover an optimal policy that maximizes returns
037   (Sutton, 2018), either through value function estimation (Watkins & Dayan, 1992) or policy gradient
038   derivation (Sutton et al., 1999). However, more recent advances, such as Decision Transformers
039
      (DT) (Chen et al., 2021), introduce a paradigm shift by leveraging supervised learning on offline
      RL datasets, offering a more practical and scalable alternative to the online learning traditionally
040
      required in RL. This shift highlights the growing importance of supervised learning on offline RL
041
      approaches, which can be more efficient and convenient in environments where data collection is
042   expensive or impractical (Srivastava et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2021; Janner et al., 2021).
043
044   In its original form, the DT agent is trained to maximize the likelihood of actions conditioned on
045
      past experiences (Chen et al., 2021). Numerous follow-up studies have tried to improve DT, such
      as through online fine-tuning (Zheng et al., 2022), pre-training (Xie et al., 2023), or improving its
046
      stitching capabilities (Wu et al., 2024; Zhuang et al., 2024). These works have shown that DT
047
      techniques can match or even outperform state-of-the-art conventional RL approaches on certain
048   tasks. However, these improvements focus solely on maximizing the use of available data, raising
049   the question: What if the optimal data is underrepresented in the given dataset? This scenario
050   is illustrated in Fig. 1 of a toy navigation environment, wherein the blue (good) trajectories are
051   underrepresented compared to the green (bad) trajectories. The traditional DT by Chen et al. (2021)
052   is expected to underperform in this environment because it simply maximizes the likelihood of the
053   training data, which can be problematic when optimal data is lacking. Additionally, it lacks effective
      stitching capabilities—the ability to combine suboptimal trajectories (refer to Appendix. A for an


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      explanation of the stitching behaviour). This leads us to a key question: Can we improve DT’s
055   performance by enabling the agent to reason about what lies beyond the known?
056
057   Our Counterfactual Reasoning Decision Transformer (CRDT) approach is inspired by the poten-
058
      tial outcome framework, specifically, the ability to reason counterfactually (Neyman, 1923; Rubin,
      1978). The core intuition behind CRDT is that by reasoning about hypothetical, better outcomes, the
059
      agent can deepen its understanding of the environment and the relationships between states, actions,
060
      and rewards, ultimately improving its generalizability. This mirrors how humans imagine alternative
061   scenarios and outcomes from past experiences to inform better decisions in the future.
062
063   The CRDT framework has three key steps. The first step involves training the agent to reason
064
      counterfactual. We introduce two models: the Treatment model T and the Outcome model O. The
      model T is trained to estimate the conditional distribution of actions given the historical experiences,
065
      i.e., the probability of selecting actions based on past trajectories. This differs from the original DT,
066
      which directly predicts the action itself rather than modeling the underlying distribution. The model
067   O is trained to predict the future state and return as outcomes of taking an action. Once these two
068   models are trained using the given offline dataset, we proceed to the second step. We aim to utilize
069   the action selection probabilities and the inferred outcomes to generate counterfactual experiences.
070   Unlike prior approaches that generate counterfactual data simply by perturbing the actions or states
071   (Pitis et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024) with small noise, we argue that
072   an action should be considered as counterfactual if only it has a low probability of being selected.
073   We employ a mechanism known as Counterfactual Action Selection mechanism to identify such
074   actions. However, extreme counterfactual actions may introduce excessive noise or lead to states
075
      that are not beneficial for the agent’s learning. To mitigate this, we implement a mechanism called
      Counterfactual Action Filtering to eliminate irrelevant actions. The actions that pass the filtering
076
      process will be used as inputs for the Outcome model, which gives us the outcomes of these actions.
077
      In the final step, we integrate these counterfactual experiences with the offline dataset to train the
078   underlying DT agent. Fig. 1(c) provides an overview of our CRDT framework.1
079
080   Our empirical experiments in continuous action space environments, including locomotion, ant and
081
      maze benchmarks, small datasets, and modified environment settings, and discrete action space
      environments like Atari, show that our framework improves the performance of the underlying DT
082
      agent. Moreover, we demonstrate that under CRDT, the DT agent attains the “stitching” ability
083
      without needing to modify the underlying architecture. To summarize, our key contributions are:
084
085            1. We propose the CRDT framework, which enables agents to reason counterfactually, allow-
086               ing them to explore alternative outcomes and generalize to novel scenarios.
087            2. Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate that CRDT consistently enhances the per-
088               formance of the underlying DT agent and provides it with the ability to stitch trajecto-
089               ries. This improvement is observed across various conditions, including standard settings,
090               smaller datasets, and modified environments.

171   2.2    P OTENTIAL O UTCOME AND C OUNTERFACTUAL R EASONING
172
173
      Our work is inspired by the potential outcomes framework (Neyman, 1923; Rubin, 1978) and its
      extension to time-varying treatments and outcomes (Robins & Hernan, 2008). The potential out-
174
      comes framework is a key approach in causal inference that defines and estimates causal effects by
175
      considering the potential outcomes for each variable under different treatment conditions (Robins &
176   Hernan, 2008). Counterfactual reasoning involves imagining what might have happened under al-
177   ternative conditions or scenarios that did not occur (Pearl & Mackenzie, 2018). Under the potential
178   outcome framework, at each timestep t ∈ {1, ..., T }, we observe time-varying covariates Xt , treat-
179   ments At , and the outcomes Yt+1 . The treatment At influences the outcome Yt+1 , and all Xt , At ,
180   and Yt+1 affect future treatment. A history at timestep t is denoted as H̄t = {X̄t , Āt−1 , Ȳt }, where
181   X̄t = (X1 , . . . , Xt ), Ȳt = (Y1 , . . . , Yt ), and Āt−1 = (A1 , . . . , At−1 ). The estimated potential
182   outcome for a trajectory of treatment āt = (at , ..., at+ξ−1 ) is expressed as E[Yt+ξ (āt:t+ξ−1 ) | H̄t ]
183   where ξ ≥ 1 is the treatment horizon for ξ steps prediction.
184   Mapping to this paper, the time-varying covariates correspond to the agent’s past observations and
185   the returns-to-go it has received. The treatment corresponds to the action taken, and the outcome
186   is the subsequent observation and future returns. A counterfactual treatment refers to an action the
187   agent could have taken but did not. Therefore, for each timestep t, we aim to estimate the outcome
188   of counterfactual action ât or E[ŝt+1 , ĝt+1 | ĥt ], where ŝt+1 and ĝt+1 denote the counterfactual
189   state and returns-to-go corresponding to taking the counterfactual action ât . ĥt is the new historical
190   experience (g1 , s1 , a1 , . . . , gt , st , ât ), given that we have taken a counterfactual action ât that is
191   different from the original action at in the dataset Denv .
192   Our framework follows the three standard assumptions: (1) consistency, (2) sequential ignorability,
193   and (3) sequential overlap ensuring that the counterfactual outcomes over time are identifiable from
194   the factual observational data Denv (see Appendix. B).

199

200

      3.2    C OUNTERFACTUAL R EASONING WITH CRDT
257
      This section describes the agent’s iterative counterfactual reasoning process. At each timestep t, the
258   model T is provided with the input sequence (g1 , s1 , a1 , . . . , gt−1 , st−1 , at−1 , gt , st ) to compute
259   the action distribution. Using this distribution, a counterfactual action ât is drawn according to the
260   Counterfactual Action Selection. Next, the model O is used to generate the counterfactual state ŝt+1
261   and returns-to-go ĝt+1 . The trajectory is then updated with the counterfactual experience, forming
262   the new input (g1 , s1 , a1 , . . . , gt , st , ât , ĝt+1 , ŝt+1 ) for the next iteration. Counterfactual reasoning
263   for a trajectory is deemed successful if the iterative process proceeds to the end of the trajectory
264   without violating the Counterfactual Action Filtering mechanism. Successful reasoning trajectories
265   are added to the counterfactual experience buffer, denoted as Dcrdt , if the number of experiences in
266
      Dcrdt is less than a hyperparameter ne . The counterfactual reasoning process is detailed in the
      Algorithm. 2 in Appendix. D.

475   4.4.2    C AN CRDT ENABLE DT TO STITCH TRAJECTORIES ?
476
477
      Table. 7 in Appendix F.7 presents the results of the experiment conducted in the environment shown
      in Fig. 1. In this environment, all states, apart from the goal, receive a reward of 0. Reaching the
478
      goal state receives a reward of +1. We expect that, if traditional DT is used, the agent would struggle
479
      to learn this environment due to the lack of stitching ability and the lack of optimal data. The results
480   in the table support our expectations, indicating that the traditional DT achieves only around a 40%
481   success rate, whereas our CRDT approach achieves nearly 90%. Although our approach has not
482   been designed to achieve stitching ability during training, such as in Wu et al. (2024) and Zhuang
483   et al. (2024), our agent acquires this ability by training on data that has already been stitched together
484   through the process of counterfactual reasoning and the generation of higher-return counterfactual
485   experiences. This also explains the performance in the Ant dataset in Table. 1 and the Maze2d
      dataset (especially when the data is small) in Fig. 2, both of which require trajectory stitching.

      5.1   O FFLINE R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING AND S EQUENCE M ODELING
490
      Offline RL (Levine et al., 2020) refers to the task of learning policies from a static dataset Denv of
491
      pre-collected trajectories. It has found successful applications in robotic manipulation (Kalashnikov
492   et al., 2018; Mandlekar et al., 2020) and healthcare (Wang et al., 2018; Tang et al., 2022). Traditional
493   methods used to solve offline RL can be classified into model-free offline RL and model-based
494   RL approaches. Model-free methods aim to constrain the learned policy close to the behaviour
495   policy (Levine et al., 2020), through techniques such as learning conservative Q-values (Kumar
496   et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2021; Kostrikov et al., 2021a), applying uncertainty quantification to the
497   predicted Q-values (Agarwal et al., 2020; Levine et al., 2020), and incorporating regularization based
498   on importance sampling (Sutton et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2019). Other methods include imposing state
499   and action constraints using various distance metrics, such as imitation loss (Fujimoto et al., 2019),
500
      MSE constraint (Fujimoto & Gu, 2021), or KL divergence (Liu et al., 2022). Model-based offline RL
      methods (Yu et al., 2020; Kidambi et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2021; Rigter et al., 2022), involve learning
501
      the dynamic model of the environment, then, generating rollouts from the model to optimize the
502
      policy. Our method is more aligned with model-based approaches, as we use a model to generate
503   counterfactual samples. However, the difference is that we only sample low selection action.
504
505   Before the development of Decision Transformer (DT), upside-down reinforcement learning (Sri-
506
      vastava et al., 2019; Schmidhuber, 2019) applied supervised learning techniques to address RL tasks.
      In 2021, Chen et al. (2021) introduced Decision Transformer (DT) and the concept of incorporating
507
      returns into the sequential modeling process to predict optimal actions. In the same year, Trajec-
508
      tory Transformer (TT) (Janner et al., 2021) presented a different approach to representing input
509   trajectories. Inspired by both DT and TT, numerous methods have since been proposed to enhance
510   performance, focusing on areas such as architecture (Kim et al., 2023; Bar-David et al., 2023),
511   pretraining (Xie et al., 2023), online fine-tuning (Zheng et al., 2022), dynamic programming (Yam-
512   agata et al., 2023), and trajectory stitching (Wu et al., 2024; Zhuang et al., 2024). However, up to
513   our knowledge, there has been no work that seeks to integrate counterfactual reasoning with DT.
514
515
      5.2   C OUNTERFACTUAL R EASONING IN C ONVENTIONAL R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
516
517   Several methods have explored the application of counterfactual reasoning in RL (Buesing et al.,
518   2018; Oberst & Sontag, 2019; Pitis et al., 2020; Mesnard et al., 2020; Pitis et al., 2022; Killian
519   et al., 2022) and imitation learning (IL)(Sun et al., 2023). While these approaches leverage coun-
520   terfactual reasoning, they are not directly comparable to our method. The key distinction lies in
521   their reliance on the Structural Causal Model (SCM) framework (Pearl & Mackenzie, 2018). These
522   works necessitate either a pre-defined causal graph or the learning of such a graph from data. In
523   contrast, our approach is rooted in the Potential Outcomes (PO) framework (Rubin, 1978; Robins
524   & Hernan, 2008), which focuses on estimating the effects of interventions without the need for a
525
      specified causal graph. This allows us to avoid the need to learn the causal graph. Our approach
      aligns more closely with works that estimate counterfactual outcomes for treatments in sequential
526
      data (Melnychuk et al., 2022; Frauen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2018; Li et al., 2020); all of which
527
      adopt the PO framework. However, the main contribution of our work lies in integrating these esti-
528   mated outcomes to enhance the decision-making of the underlying DT agent (refer to Appendix G
529   for the relation of CRDT to causal inference and counterfactual reasoning).
530
531
532   6     D ISCUSSION
533
534   In this paper, we present the CRDT framework, which integrates counterfactual reasoning with DT.
535   Our experiments show that CRDT improves DT and its variants on standard benchmarks and in
536   scenarios with small datasets while generalizing to modified evaluation environments. Additionally,
537   the agent achieves trajectory stitching without architectural changes. However, training separate
538   Transformer models adds complexity. Future work could explore combining these models, as they
539   share inputs, or training in an iterative manner using generated counterfactual samples as training
      data, though careful consideration is needed.

775   start from state sbt−1 , however, can reach state sat+1
776
777   Trajectory stitching is an ability that has received great attention lately in offline RL, specifically,
778   in sequential modeling. It has been proven that traditional sequential modeling approaches, such
779   as Chen et al. (2021), lack the ability to stitch suboptimal trajectories to form optimal trajectories
780   (Kumar et al., 2020; Zhuang et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024). An example of this is given in the
781   toy environment       provided in Fig.     1(a) and Fig. 3. Let’s   consider the scenario of two trajectories
782     sat−1 , st , sat+1 , sbt−1 , st , sbt+1 where sat−1 , st , sat+1 is sampled from the set of blue trajectories
783   (good trajectories that lead to the goal) and sbt−1 , st , sbt+1 is sampled from the set of green trajec-
784   tories (bad trajectories that do not lead to the goal). We anticipate that a sequence model trained
785
      on these trajectories will likely follow the subsequent states in a way that aligns with the provided
      trajectories. This means that the agent starting from the bottom-left potentially follows the green
786
      trajectories to the top-right of the maze and will not reach the goal. We want, however, to stitch
787
      these trajectories together, meaning that we want our agent to be able to start from sbt−1 but end up
788   being in sat+1 .
789
790   The explanation for why traditional DT does not have stitching ability arises from the agent’s training
791
      conditions. Specifically, when using traditional DT, the prediction of the next state-action pair is
      conditioned on an initial target return (g0 ). If g0 is set to 0, the ball will smoothly follow the green
792
      trajectory, as this is the more common data and the returns-to-go at the crossroad (the point where
793
      the green and blue trajectories intersect) are still equal to 0. On the other hand, if conditioned on a
794   return of 1, the ball is likely to take a random action because g0 = 1 represents an out-of-distribution
795   (OOD) returns-to-go from the bottom-left corner of the maze. In both cases, the ball fails to reach
796   the goal. Previous works, such as Wu et al. (2024) and Zhuang et al. (2024) address this problem by
797   modifying the training condition of the DT agent. Our approach, on the other hand, addresses this
798   problem by generating better trajectories based on the idea of potential outcome, thus, guiding the
799   agent to reach the goal.

812   We build upon the potential outcomes framework (Neyman, 1923; Rubin, 1978) and its extension to
813   time-varying treatments and outcomes (Robins & Hernan, 2008). In order to identify the counterfac-
814   tual outcome distribution over time, the following three standard assumptions for the data-generating
815   process are required:
816   Assumption A.1 (Consistency): If Āt = āt is a fixed sequence of treatments for a particular
817   patient, then Yt+1 [āt ] = Yt+1 . This implies that the potential outcome under the treatment sequence
818   āt corresponds to the observed (factual) outcome for the patient, conditional on Āt = āt .
819
      Mapping to offline RL, consistency means that for any given action at the observed next state st+1
820
      and returns-to-go gt+1 reflect the true outcome of the action. In the context of offline RL this
821
      assumption holds given that observational data is collected from behaviour policies πβ that were
822   trained in the same environment; therefore, the data reflects the actual dynamics of the environment.
823
824   Assumption A.2 (Sequential Overlap): For every history, there is always a non-zero probability of
825
      receiving or not receiving any treatment over time:
826                         0 < P (At = at |H̄t = h̄t ) < 1,      if   P (H̄t = h̄t ) > 0,
827
828
      where h̄t is a particular historical experience.
829   For offline RL, sequential overlap guarantees that for any observed history ht , every action at has
830   a non-zero probability of being chosen. This assumption is met if Denv provides adequate coverage
831   of the state-action space. If the behaviour policy πβ used to collect the data explores a wide range
832   of actions under different histories, we can reasonably assume that the sequential overlap condition
833   hold.
834   Assumption A.3 (Sequential Ignorability): This states that the current treatment is independent of
835   the potential outcome, given the observed history:

838   This means there are no unmeasured confounders that simultaneously influence both the treatment
839   and the outcome.
840
      Sequential ignorability implies that the observed history ht includes all relevant information that

842   terfactual reasoning on observed data in Denv , we rely on the assumption that the dataset sufficiently

1369   G    R ELATION TO C AUSAL I NFERENCE AND C OUNTERFACTUAL R EASONING
1370
1371
       Although CRDT is inspired by causal inference and counterfactual reasoning, the method did not
       explicitly establish a formal causal structure learning process, such as constructing a causal graph
1372
       or a Structural Causal Model (SCM) (Pearl & Mackenzie, 2018). The method is more closely
1373
       related to the potential outcome framework (Neyman, 1923; Rubin, 1978) and its extension to time-
1374   varying treatments and outcomes (Robins & Hernan, 2008), which did not explicitly require a causal
1375   graph (Pearl & Mackenzie, 2018). The proposed counterfactual reasoning process in CRDT also
1376   differs from “Pearl-style counterfactual reasoning”, which requires the inference of the posterior
1377   distribution of exogenous noise variable and intervention on the parental variables. In CRDT, we
1378   assume that the noise is implicit in the dynamic model. The method, however, leverages several
1379   concepts from these frameworks.
1380   Specifically, our method estimates the outcomes of different treatments using an Outcome Net-
1381   work, which aligns with prior work in adapting machine learning methods for causal effect infer-
1382   ence (Shalit et al., 2017; Jacob, 2021; Melnychuk et al., 2022), where neural networks were used
1383   to estimate treatment effects by modeling counterfactual outcomes. While the potential outcome
1384   framework does not strictly require a causal graph and the choice of the underlying ML algorithm
1385   is very flexible (Jacob, 2021), in CRDT, we purposefully chose Transformers architecture for both
1386   the Treatment and Outcome networks due to their ability to capture long-term dependencies through
1387   attention mechanisms. The attention scores within the Transformer architecture underpinning these
1388
       networks can serve as a simple causal masking mechanism (Pitis et al., 2020; Seitzer et al., 2021;
       Pitis et al., 2022). Furthermore, our framework assumes the three key causal assumptions, namely
1389
       Consistency, Sequential Overlap, and Sequential Ignorability, as detailed in Appendix B. These con-
1390
       nections demonstrate how causal inference concepts underpin our framework, even if they are not
1391   formalized in the traditional sense.
