011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Diffusion-based video generation has achieved significant progress, yet generat-
014            ing multiple actions that occur sequentially remains a formidable task. Directly
015            generating a video with sequential actions can be extremely challenging due to
016            the scarcity of fine-grained action annotations and the difficulty in establishing
017            temporal semantic correspondences and maintaining long-term consistency. To
018
               tackle this, we propose an intuitive and straightforward solution: splicing multiple
               single-action video segments sequentially. The core challenge lies in generating
019
               smooth and natural transitions between these segments given the inherent com-
020
               plexity and variability of action transitions. We introduce MAVIN (Multi-Action
021            Video INfilling model), designed to generate transition videos that seamlessly
022            connect two given videos, forming a cohesive integrated sequence. MAVIN incor-
023            porates several innovative techniques to address challenges in the transition video
024            infilling task. Firstly, a consecutive noising strategy coupled with variable-length
025            sampling is employed to handle large infilling gaps and varied generation lengths.
026            Secondly, boundary frame guidance (BFG) is proposed to address the lack of se-
027            mantic guidance during transition generation. Lastly, a Gaussian filter mixer (GFM)
028            dynamically manages noise initialization during inference, mitigating train-test
029
               discrepancy while preserving generation flexibility. Additionally, we introduce a
               new metric, CLIP-RS (CLIP Relative Smoothness), to evaluate temporal coherence
030
               and smoothness, complementing traditional quality-based metrics. Experimental
031
               results on horse and tiger scenarios demonstrate MAVIN’s superior performance in
032            generating smooth and coherent video transitions compared to existing methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   The evolution of video generation models has been significantly shaped by the advent of
039
      diffusion-based techniques, offering unprecedented fidelity and temporal coherence in video syn-
      thesis (Blattmann et al., 2023a; Ho et al., 2022a; Singer et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023b; Ho et al.,
040
      2022b). However, these models often struggle to generate videos that encompass multiple actions
041
      or adhere to complex instructions, and typically produce relatively short clips, limiting their use in
042   scenarios requiring longer, multi-action sequences.
043
044   Generating multi-action videos directly presents numerous unresolved challenges. Firstly, the
045
      lack of fine-grained action-level annotations in existing large-scale video datasets hampers model
      training. Secondly, multi-action sequences, involving extended durations and significant motion
046
      ranges, challenge models to maintain spatiotemporal consistency throughout the video. The structural
047
      characteristics of video U-Net models further complicate complex temporal semantic correspondence
048   modeling. To circumvent these challenges, in this paper, we propose an innovative approach for
049   generating multi-action videos by integrating several single-action video clips. This process entails
050   two fundamental steps: first, the production of various video clips featuring the same subject engaging
051   in distinct actions; second, the concatenation of these clips through action transitions. While the
052   first step has been facilitated by recent advancements in text-conditioned image-to-video (TI2V)
053   generation (Dai et al., 2023; Girdhar et al., 2023; Xing et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
      2024; Wei et al., 2023), the second step remains understudied. To this end, we introduce MAVIN


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      (Multi-Action Video INfilling model), a transition model designed to infill an intermediate video clip
055   between two adjoining clips, ensuring a fluid and seamless transition.
056
057   This task requires meticulous attention to overall motion consistency and smoothness. Therefore,
058
      MAVIN is trained with consistent conditioning on the reference videos. To manage the potential for
      substantial motion gaps and the requirement for flexible infilling lengths, we utilize a variable-length
059
      sampling strategy. The performance of MAVIN is further enhanced by boundary frame guidance
060
      (BFG) and a Gaussian filter mixer (GFM). BFG leverages high-level semantic features from the
061   boundary frames of input videos to guide the video infilling process, ensuring visual coherence
062   throughout the transition. Meanwhile, GFM dynamically manages the introduction and modulation
063   of noise during inference, improving generation fidelity while maintaining flexibility. Our method is
064   trained in a self-supervised manner, eliminating the need for finely annotated video-text transcriptions.
065
      Moreover, existing metrics for evaluating video generation primarily focus on visual quality and
066
      often overlook temporal coherence, which is crucial for assessing action transitions. To address this
067   gap, we introduce a new metric, CLIP-RS (CLIP Relative Smoothness), specifically designed to
068   evaluate the temporal consistency and smoothness of transition videos. This metric complements
069   traditional quality-based metrics and provides a comprehensive evaluation of our model’s performance.
070   Experimental results conducted on two distinct animal scenarios—horses and tigers—demonstrate
071   our method’s superior performance in generating smooth and natural video transitions over existing
072   methods, both in qualitative and quantitative assessments.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
075
076
      Text-to-Video Generation. Text-to-Video (T2V) studies have shifted their focus from GAN-based
077
      models (Fox et al., 2021; Brooks et al., 2022; Tian et al.; Shen et al., 2023) and auto-regressive
078
      models (Ge et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2022; Le Moing et al., 2021; Yan et al., 2021) to diffusion
079   models (Zhang et al., 2023; Jeong et al., 2023; Singer et al., 2022; Ge et al., 2023; He et al., 2022; Zhou
080   et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023; Ho et al., 2022a), attributed to their superiority in generation quality,
081   training stability, and condition flexibility. Foundation T2V models such as ModelScopeT2V (Wang
082   et al., 2023a) and VideoCrafter (Chen et al., 2023a; 2024) are trained on large-scale captioned datasets,
083   possessing rich motion priors and text-motion correspondences. Nevertheless, challenges persist in
084   generating actions that fully adhere to complex text descriptions.
085   Image-to-Video Generation. Generating videos solely from text prompts leads to a high degree of
086   randomness in the appearance of each generation, thereby limiting its range of applications. Image-
087   to-Video (I2V) generations, on the other hand, animate a user-input image by leveraging the motion
088   priors learned from video-only datasets (Blattmann et al., 2023a; Guo et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2024; Wu
089   et al., 2023b) and have demonstrated the ability to generate high-fidelity and aesthetically pleasing
090   videos. However, they often exhibit limitations in the form of minor and uncontrollable motion
091   patterns. Considering these issues, many studies have begun to focus on text-conditioned image-to-
092   video generation (TI2V) synthesis, which involves generating videos from a reference image, coupled
093
      with a text prompt indicating how the image should be animated. Videos generated in this manner
      typically use the provided image as the initial frame (Girdhar et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023; Zeng et al.,
094
      2023; Ren et al., 2024; Gong et al., 2024) or retain its appearance identity and characteristics (Wei
095
      et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Xing et al., 2023), while performing the motion described in the text.
096   There has also been a stream of works that further specialize in motion controllability by integrating
097   extra controlling signals (Chen et al., 2023b; Kandala et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024).
098
099
      Generative Video Interpolation. Diffusion models have also gained momentum in video interpo-
      lation, challenging traditional methods that rely on optical flow computation and frame blending
100
      techniques. MCVD (Voleti et al., 2022) and RaMViD (Höppe et al., 2022) adopt diffusion-based
101
      models with random frame masking, making it capable of handling a range of video generative
102   modeling tasks, including video prediction and interpolation. LDMVFI (Danier et al., 2024) claims to
103   be the first effort solving video interpolation using latent diffusion models and has achieved superior
104   perceptual quality compared to traditional models. However, these works are primarily centered on
105   standard video frame interpolation tasks, where the motions are less ambiguous and straightforward.
106   A concurrent work (Jain et al., 2024) delves into large and challenging motions by interpolating 7
107   frames with an approximate stride of 3. It utilizes a cascaded framework where the interpolation
      occurs at a low-resolution pixel level and is subsequently upsampled with a super-resolution model.

109   under our task scenario unfeasible. SEINE (Chen et al., 2023c) explores diffusion-based scene

111   to the end image representing another.

143   3.2   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION AND C HALLENGES
144
145   Problem formulation. The proposed transition video infilling task is a specialized form of video
146   interpolation that deals with long ranges and large motions, with the input being two videos. The
147   objective of this task is to generate a transition video given two videos, one preceding and one
148   following, thereby seamlessly connecting the two. Given an encoded preceding video latent z0P =
149   {z00 , . . . , z0s } and a following video latent z0F = {z0e , . . . , z0L−1 }, the model aims to generate an
150   intermediate latent z0I = {z0s+1 , . . . , z0e−1 }, where s is the end frame index of the preceding video
151   and e is the start frame index of the following video. We term these two frames as the boundary
152
      frames for simplicity and clarity. L is the length of the integrated video after infilling.
153   Challenges and remedies. The novel nature of this task presents new challenges. In this section,
154   we briefly outline the challenges and our solutions, with a detailed elaboration to follow in the next
155   section.
156   The first challenge lies in temporal dependency modeling, which should support generating a transition
157   video with potentially large motion gaps while maintaining motion consistency. Existing works (Chen
158   et al., 2023c; Höppe et al., 2022) typically adopt a BERT-like masking strategy for conditional
159   modeling. However, such approaches are not effective for learning long-span motion patterns as
160   prediction targets and references appear alternately on the temporal axis. To address this issue, we
161   propose to consistently apply noise to a consecutive subsequence of training data. This method allows
      for natural conditioning on reference videos in temporal modules while creating large motion gaps

      3.3       M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
200
201   The overall model architecture is depicted in Figure 1. In the model training stage, we simulate
202   transition video infilling by dividing a training video into three segments. An entire video sample
203   is first encoded into z0 = {z00 , . . . , z0L−1 }, and only the intermediate clip is corrupted by t-step
204   Gaussian noise according to Eq. 2 resulting in ztI . The input to the U-Net model hence becomes
205   zt = {z0P , ztI , z0F }, and the model is optimized to predict {ϵs+1t  , . . . , ϵe−1
                                                                                        t   } as per Eq. 3. Loss is
206   computed only on noised frames. Since this approach does not involve extra mask or condition frame
207   concatenation to the channel dimension, it enables the utilization of most pre-trained foundation
208   models that accept 3-channel RGB inputs.
209
      Variable-length sampling. To improve data utilization and generation flexibility, we employ variable-
210   length sampling by randomly shifting the start and end points of the infilling clip. Concretely, at each
211   training step, we draw the boundary frame indices s and e randomly from two independent uniform
212   distributions: s ∼ U(as , bs ); e ∼ U(ae , be ), where 0 < as < bs < ae < be < L − 1. The resulting
213   length of the noised clip l := e − s − 1 thereby follows a triangular distribution with the upper and
214   lower limits lupper = be − as , llower = ae − bs . Particularly, when bs − as = be − ae , the PDF of
215   the distribution is symmetric and has the mode lmode = (llower + lupper )/2. To avoid confusion
      stemming from variable-length sampling, we equip the model with an awareness of the generation

      length it is handling. This is achieved by incorporating a length embedding using sinusoidal encoding
217   followed by an MLP. The length embedding is subsequently added to the timestep encoding and
218   collectively processed through another MLP into the spatial convolution module. This approach
219   improves the model’s capacity to leverage training samples by accommodating predictions at varying
220   positions and lengths. It effectively allows for generating at various lengths and accepting reference
221   videos of diverse durations, thereby bolstering the model’s robustness and flexibility.
222
      Dynamic boundary frame guidance. Boundary frames play a pivotal role in guiding the model’s
223   generation as they provide explicit information about the gap the model is tasked to bridge. Therefore,
224   we propose boundary frame guidance (BFG) to compensate for the lack of guidance in transition
225   video generation. Most popular frame conditioning strategies entail extending the keys and values of
226   spatial self-attention layers to include those of the condition frames (Wu et al., 2023a; Ren et al., 2024;
227   Henschel et al., 2024). However, empirical experiments did not prove these approaches effective
228   for this task, and the low-level visual information sometimes restricted the freedom of generation,
229   resulting in synthesized frames copying too much information from existing ones. Instead, we inject
230   the guidance signal into the cross-attention layers via a higher-level CLIP representation. Concretely,
231
      we encode the pixel-level boundary frames xs0 and xe0 using a CLIP vision encoder and concatenate
      the representations along the sequence dimension. This integrated representation serves both as
232
      content and semantic guidance due to the nature of CLIP representations, informing the model
233
      about the generation direction. A short text prompt briefly describing the subject, such as “horse
234   movement”, can be optionally leveraged to help classify the action subject or extract knowledge
235   from a pre-trained foundation model. The combined use of the CLIP encoders and the concatenation
236   operation provides the model with a consistent understanding of the integrated condition signal.
237
      Gaussian filter mixer for inference-time noise initialization. We propose a dynamic inference-time
238
      noise mixing strategy tailored for the transition video infilling task. Since the infilling video functions
239
      as a bridge, its first few frames should thereby resemble the preceding video, while the last few frames
240   approach the following video. The frames in the middle should be granted the flexibility to display
241   transition states that are significantly distinct from any reference frames. Inspired by FreeInit (Wu
242   et al., 2023c), we propose a Gaussian filter mixer (GFM) module that dynamically retains a certain
243   amount of information from the closest boundary frame latent. This is accomplished by keeping
244   the low-frequency component of the diffused boundary latent, which offers a rough layout guidance
245   to the denoising process. The preserved information gradually diminishes as the frame position
246   moves away from the boundaries, allowing for greater freedom in generation. It is then mixed with

      4.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
290
291   Datasets. For our experiments, we focus on two distinct animal species to verify the effectiveness
292   of the proposed method: horses and tigers. We use the AnimalKingdom dataset (Ng et al., 2022)
293   for training the horse model and the TigDog dataset (Del Pero et al., 2015) for the tiger model. The
294   AnimalKingdom dataset encompasses a diverse range of species and tasks, but we only utilize the
295   videos labeled as “Horse” from the action_recognition task for training. However, we noticed that
296   the video clips in action_recognition are generally too short and correspond to only single actions,
297   resulting in insufficient action transition patterns for model training. Therefore, we supplemented the
298   horse training data with additional long-take web videos that capture horse movements. Consequently,
299
      the total duration of the training data for each dataset is approximately 45 minutes under 30 FPS.
300   Testing clips for the transition video infilling task should ideally contain action transitions or large
301   motions to effectively evaluate the model’s efficacy. Such data, however, is challenging to source
302   from existing datasets, prompting us to construct our own. We collect videos from the Internet and
303   generate the test data in two ways: manual cutting, which yields high-quality samples, and automatic

462   4.3                          A PPLICATION FOR M ULTI -ACTION G ENERATION
463
464
465
      We achieve multi-action generations by connecting single-action videos with MAVIN. This work
      does not focus on optimizing the single-action models; instead, we employ existing TI2V models that
466
      animate an input image through text control, as discussed in Section 2. In our empirical experiments,
467
      directly generating large motions or non-continuous actions using pre-trained TI2V models (Dai
468   et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2024) led to failure. Therefore, we fine-tune these models for the single-action
469   generation purpose. To integrate the synthesized single-action videos, we insert noise of the desired
470   length between two videos and use MAVIN to infill a transition video. Alternatively, instead of
471   inserting noise, we can concatenate single-action videos and replace the junction frames with noise to
472   regenerate the transition parts.
473
      We initialize the action model from AnimateAnything (Dai et al., 2023). The training data for
474   single actions is also derived from the AnimalKingdom and TigDog datasets, except that we collect
475   additional data for training the action “horse jumping”. We train one model per animal species. A
476   fixed action prompt, such as “horse is jumping”, is tied to each action and serves as the text condition
477   during training.
478
      To create a multi-action video, we first use a single image, controlled by multiple action prompts, to
479
      generate multiple single-action videos separately. These videos are then concatenated into a longer
480   sequence, arranged in the desired order. We regenerate the junction frames using the infilling model
481   for smooth action transitions. Figure 3 illustrates an example of such an application. In this example,
482   we generate 20 frames for each single action and refine 12 frames centered around each junction,
483   resulting in a 60-frame-long video that contains three actions: jump, stand, and run. The first, third,
484   and fifth rows depict the single-action videos generated by the action model, while the second and
485   fourth rows are generated by the infilling model to connect them. This approach generates highly
      temporally cohesive examples with great flexibility.

      5   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In conclusion, this study has presented a novel approach to generative video infilling, specifically
489   targeting the generation of transition clips in multi-action sequences, leveraging the capabilities
490   of diffusion models. Our model, MAVIN, demonstrates a significant improvement over existing
491   methods by generating smoother and more natural transition videos across complex motion sequences.
492   This research lays the groundwork for future advancements in unsupervised motion pre-training,
493   large-motion video interpolation, and multi-action video generation. While this technique enables
494   new applications, it is also crucial to establish guidelines and implement safeguards to prevent its
495   potential misuse in creating fake content, which raises ethical and security concerns.
496   Limitations. Due to computational limitations and the proprietary nature of the widely used video
497   training dataset WebVid-10M (Bain et al., 2021), our experiments were conducted only under specific
498   scenarios and initialized from existing foundation models. Further exploration of the task might
499   require training at scale. Moreover, while we did not concentrate on optimizing the single-action
500   (TI2V) models, a notable trade-off between visual quality and motion intensity persists even after
501   fine-tuning, highlighting an area for further research. The failure cases include the single-action
502   model’s inability to follow the action prompt and the inconsistency in appearance in later frames for
503
      actions involving large motions.
