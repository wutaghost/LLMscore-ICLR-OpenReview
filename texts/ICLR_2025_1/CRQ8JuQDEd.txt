011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            As the length of input sequences in Large Language Models (LLMs) continues
014            to grow, efficient key-value (KV) cache management has become essential for
015
               improving inference speed and throughput of autoregressive decoding. Although
               several approaches have been proposed to reduce memory usage by selectively
016
               retaining only the important KV pairs and discarding the rest, these eviction-based
017
               methods can lead to unintended consequences during the generation process. In this
018            paper, we investigate the adverse effects of cache eviction methods and reveal that
019            discarding KV pairs potentially introduces risks such as safety prompt breaches,
020            hallucinations, and loss of critical contextual information. Interestingly, we find
021            that preserving even a fraction of the information from evicted KV pairs through
022            reduced precision quantization significantly mitigates these issues. On the other
023            hand, we also observe that important KV pairs need to be maintained at higher
024            precision to preserve generation quality. Based on these findings, we propose Mixed-
025            precision KV cache (MiKV), a robust plug-and-play cache compression method
026
               that balances performance and memory efficiency. MiKV preserves lost contextual
               information by storing evicted KV pairs in low precision, while maintaining the
027
               essential KV pairs in higher precision to ensure generation quality. Experimental
028
               results across multiple benchmarks and LLM architectures demonstrate that our
029            method achieves a state-of-the-art balance between compression ratio and model
030            performance, outperforming existing baselines.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Recent advancements in Natural Language Processing (NLP) have been largely driven by the emergent
036   capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI et al., 2023; Touvron
037   et al., 2023a;b) based on the Autoregressive Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) architecture. During
038   autoregressive decoding, LLMs benefit from Key-Value (KV) Caching, a mechanism that caches
039
      intermediate key-value states from the previous context to effectively avoid redundant computations
      to accelerate generation. However, past KV pairs must be stored continuously in memory, leading
040
      to a memory footprint that increases linearly with both batch size and sequence length. As LLM
041
      inference is predominantly memory-bound (Kim et al., 2023; Park et al., 2024), the growing KV
042   cache becomes a bottleneck, slowing down the generation process and excessively occupying the
043   GPU memory, which is already constrained by the model weights. The ongoing trend of extending
044   LLMs to handle longer contexts only exacerbates the problem, as the KV cache grows in direct
045   proportion to context length. Consequently, this escalating memory demand poses a critical challenge
046   for efficient deployment and acceleration of LLMs on modern GPUs, where memory capacity and
047   bandwidth are highly constrained.
048   To address these challenges, recent methods have introduced KV cache eviction (Zhang et al., 2023;
049   Liu et al., 2023b; Xiao et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Ge et al., 2024) as a strategy for improving
050   inference efficiency. These approaches are based on the assumption that a subset of KVs, deemed
051   important, is sufficient to sustain a successful generation phase. By leveraging past attention patterns
052   to establish importance criteria, researchers propose evicting less critical KV pairs from the cache,
053   reporting minimal performance degradation even when compressing the cache size by up to 20%
      (evicting 80% of KVs) (Zhang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b). However, a thorough analysis of

075   like multi-turn conversations, where future context requirements are difficult to anticipate.
076
      In this paper, we first investigate the risks associated with KV cache eviction through extensive
077
      observations. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first work empirically showing that evicting
078
      KV pairs leads to rapid degradation of critical details in the input context, resulting in contextual
079   incoherence, hallucinatory responses, and the loss of essential information. Notably, cache eviction
080   can also compromise critical elements, such as safety guardrail prompts embedded within the system,
081   potentially triggering harmful responses that bypass intended safeguards. We hypothesize that these
082   issues arise from the irreversible and exhaustive loss of information contained in the evicted KV pairs.
083   To mitigate this, we explore an alternative approach: instead of evicting KV pairs entirely, we retain a
084   minimal amount of information through low-precision quantization. Surprisingly, our findings reveal
085   that even when KV pairs are preserved at reduced precision, much of the lost detail can be recovered,
086   particularly when systematic outliers in keys and queries are effectively addressed.
087   Building on these insights, we propose Mixed-precision KV cache (MiKV), a robust yet efficient
088   plug-and-play cache compression strategy. MiKV selectively stores evicted KV pairs in low precision
089   while retaining important KV pairs in higher precision based on an importance criterion (Figure 1).
090   Unlike eviction-based methods, MiKV preserves all KV cache entries, avoiding the loss of informa-
091   tion typically caused by cache eviction. While quantization-based methods are commonly used to
092   reduce the memory footprint of the entire KV cache (Liu et al., 2024), they suffer from significant
093
      performance drops when reducing to 4-bit precision or lower. To address this limitation, MiKV
      introduces an adaptive quantization technique based on the importance of KV pairs, ensuring optimal
094
      balance between memory efficiency and performance. In our investigation, we observe that systematic
095
      outliers in both queries and keys present challenges for effective low-bit quantization. By addressing
096   these outliers, MiKV successfully preserves contextual details and maintains generation quality while
097   achieving a high compression rate. We evaluate MiKV across a diverse set of LLM benchmarks,
098   including tasks in natural language understanding, mathematics, coding, and detailed information
099   retrieval. Our results demonstrate that MiKV can compress the KV cache with minimal performance
100   degradation, even for compression ratios as high as 80%.
101
      Our major contributions in this work include the following: 1) We are the first to investigate the
102   previously overlooked issue of context degradation caused by eviction-based cache compression. Our
103   work demonstrates that retaining evicted KV pairs, even in low precision, can significantly recover
104   lost contextual information, addressing a critical gap in the field. 2) We demonstrate that the lost
105   context can be further recovered with appropriate measures to effectively quantize evicted KV pairs
106   into low precision. 3) We introduce the Mixed-precision KV cache (MiKV), a novel compression
107   strategy that preserves critical context and generation quality while reducing memory usage and
      improving inference speed during the generation phase.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
109
110   KV Cache Sharing. As batch sizes and input sequence lengths in serving LLMs continue to grow,
111   considerable effort has been made to compress KV caches. Methods like Multi-Query Attention
112   (MQA)(Shazeer, 2019) and Grouped Query Attention (GQA)(Ainslie et al., 2023), where multiple
113   query heads share a single KV head, have emerged as promising methods for reducing the memory
114   footprint of KV caches. These methods have been widely adopted in recent open-source LLMs, with
115   MQA implemented in models such as Gemma (Team et al., 2024) model and GQA applied in the
116   LLaMA (Dubey et al., 2024b) model family. While these methods effectively reduce the memory
117   footprint of KV caches, they come with the trade-off of reduced model performance, balancing
118
      efficiency against accuracy. Additionally, the training of models using MQA and GQA introduces
      substantial computational overhead, which further complicates their deployment in practice.
119
120
      KV Cache Quantization. There has been a surge of research on reducing the serving cost of LLMs
121
      via the quantization of weights and activations. Notably, Xiao et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2023a) have
122   extended their focus beyond the quantization of weights and activations, demonstrating the feasibility
123   of quantizing the query, key, and value to INT8. In contrast, Liu et al. (2024) focuses on quantizing
124   the KV cache only. However, they do not consider token importance for compression, resulting in
125   possible degradation in generation quality.
126
127   KV Cache Eviction. The memory footprint of the KV cache presents a significant bottleneck in
128   LLM inference, motivating recent efforts to compress it through cache eviction strategies. These
129   approaches prioritize preserving important KV pairs while evicting less critical ones, using various
130   importance criteria such as locality (Xiao et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023), frequency (Zhang et al., 2023;
131   Liu et al., 2023b), and attention structures (Ge et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). For instance, preserving
132
      tokens that are local to the current sequence position is essential for maintaining generation quality,
      while methods like heavy-hitter token identification focus on retaining influential tokens. Despite
133
      the promise of these strategies, they come with inherent risks. Since eviction removes intermediate
134
      model states, predicting the future importance of tokens remains fundamentally uncertain, leading
135   to inevitable context loss. Moreover, adaptive importance policies that adjust eviction criteria based
136   on attention patterns (Ge et al., 2024) can still result in the exhaustive loss of crucial contextual
137   information from the evicted KV pairs.

      3     C ONTEXT DAMAGE FROM KV C ACHE E VICTION
140
141
      In this section, we investigate the inherent risks posed by eviction-based KV cache compression.
142   Specifically, we review recent eviction strategies that rely on importance criteria, and examine how
143   the removal of KV pairs impacts contextual integrity. Through both qualitative and quantitative
144   analyses, we assess the extent of context damage caused by these eviction mechanisms, highlighting
145   their potential drawbacks in maintaining generation quality and overall model performance.
146
147   3.1   C ASE S TUDY: W HAT H APPENS W HEN KV S ARE E VICTED ?
148
149   Guardrail Prompt Breach. In language model deployment, post-training enhancements are often
150   implemented through prompt engineering. System prompts, for instance, are designed to ensure
151
      safety by mitigating harmful content and reducing bias, thus preventing potential risks. However, as
      shown in Figure 2a, eviction strategies can accidentally remove crucial information from the KV
152
      cache, undermining these safety mechanisms and introducing significant risks to model reliability
153
      and performance.
154
155
      Contextual Incoherency. The partial and inconsistent loss of context during KV cache eviction can
156   significantly disrupt the coherence of the generated text. This is particularly evident when considering
157   the temporal flow of information, where details from earlier parts of the sequence fade, while more
158   recent information is retained. As a result, the model produces disjointed and unconnected sentences.
159   For example, in Figure 8 in Appendix A, during a context-based question-answering task, crucial
160   information about "Two New Sciences" was lost, causing the model to forget key content while still
161   remembering the publication year. Such incomplete retention leads to incoherent, and sometimes
      nonsensical, responses.

189   3.2    C ONTROLLED S TUDY: H OW D OES KV E VICTION DAMAGE THE C ONTEXT ?
190
191   To quantitatively assess the impact of KV cache eviction on context retention, we conduct experiments
192   in a controlled environment using the Line Retrieval task (Li et al., 2023a). In this task, the LLM
193
      is presented with a set of randomly generated key-value pairs, and is requested to retrieve the
      corresponding value to a proveded key (an example is provided in Appendix 19). Retrieval accuracy
194
      is measured across various cache compression ratios to evaluate the robustness of different eviction
195
      strategies. We compare the performance of an importance-based eviction strategy (Zhang et al., 2023)
196   against a full cache baseline.
197
198
      Unrecoverable Cache Miss. Figure 3a presents the accuracy of the Line Retrieval task as a
199   function of KV cache size. Contrary to expectations, our findings demonstrate a sharp decline in
200   performance with cache eviction. This contrasts with previous reports suggesting that up to 80% of
201   the KV cache could be evicted with minimal impact on performance (Zhang et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
202   2023b). To investigate the reason behind this phenomenon, we observe how the model attends to
203   the KVs during the generation phase. Figure 3b illustrates the actual attention scores in a retrieval
204   task, highlighting issues that arise during the generation phase due to cache miss in eviction-based
205   KV cache compression techniques. In this example, tokens located between positions 320 and 340
206   contain crucial information corresponding to the retrieval request, as indicated by the high values
207
      observed in the attention scores of the full cache during the generation phase. However, these tokens
      are not strongly attended during prefill phase as they are not important prior to generation. Thus, in
208
      eviction-based methods like H2O (Zhang et al., 2023), the importance calculation fails to recognize
209
      these tokens in advance, leading to their eviction. As a result, the model cannot reference this
210   information when needed, resulting in lower retrieval accuracy. In contrast, our proposed MiKV
211   method mitigates this issue by preserving these tokens, even in low-bit precision, ensuring that
212   the necessary information remains accessible. This allows MiKV to achieve retrieval performance
213   comparable to the baseline, demonstrating its effectiveness in preventing attention loss due to KV
214   eviction. Building on the Line Retrieval task, we extend our experiments to examine the effects of
215   KV cache eviction in a multi-turn setting, where responses are exchanged consecutively. As shown
      in Figure 10 of Appendix A, the degradation becomes more pronounced with each successive turn,

         4           M IXED -P RECISION KV C ACHE C OMPRESSION
249
250     In this section, we introduce the Mixed-precision Key-Value (MiKV) Cache, a robust compression
251     framework designed to address the issue of context damage through the use of mixed-precision
252     quantization. As illustrated in Figure 4, MiKV consists of three core components: the preservation
253     of evicted KV pairs via low-precision quantization to prevent context loss (Section 4.1), outlier-
254     awareness to operate under low precision regimes (Section 4.2), and maintaining important KVs in
255     high-precision quantization (Section 4.3) to guarantee generation quality.
256
257      4.1           R ETAINING E VICTED KV S WITH Q UANTIZATION
258
259     To mitigate the context damage caused by KV cache eviction, we propose a method that preserves
260     evicted KV pairs using low-bit quantization. To evaluate the effectiveness of this approach, we
261
        conduct experiments designed to assess how well low-bit preservation can recover performance
        in the line retrieval task (Li et al., 2023a). For the experiments, we employ the importance-based
262
        eviction strategy (Zhang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b) alongside a conventional per-token asymmetric
263
        quantization method (Liu et al., 2023a). As shown in Table 1, retaining evicted KVs through low-
264     precision quantization across various eviction ratios significantly restores the lost performance.
265     However, this comes with a trade-off: evicting KVs completely frees up memory, whereas low-bit
266     preservation consumes a portion of the memory capacity, leading to a reduction in compression
267     rates. Results demonstrate that although low-bit preservation effectively mitigates performance loss,
268     it still consumes a certain amount of memory footprint. To enhance an effective compression rate,
269     thus, the precision for the KVs intended for eviction needs to be reduced to a sufficiently low
        level. Nevertheless, performance recovery diminishes at very low precisions, such as INT2. Thus,

304   4.2   M ITIGATING C HANNEL O UTLIERS FOR L OW-P RECISION KV S
305
      To further improve accuracy after low-precision quantization of KVs, we first examine the characteris-
306
      tics of the query, key, and value within the attention mechanism. As illustrated in Figure 5, systematic
307   outliers, particularly in the query and key, can lead to significant quantization errors in low-precision
308   settings (Dettmers et al., 2022). Note that the use of Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE) (Su et al.,
309   2024) exacerbates this issue by duplicating outliers across channels, amplifying their effect. Since
310   such outliers occur consistently across layers and models (see Appendix C for details), it is evident
311   that any effective KV quantization strategy must be designed to be outlier-aware.
312
      Previous work on weight and activation quantization has addressed these outliers using techniques
313   like per-channel quantization (Heo et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024) and per-token quantization with
314   outlier balancing (Xiao et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023). In this paper, we adopt per-token quantization
315   with outlier balancing to ensure a fair comparison with existing eviction methods, which also operate
316   on a per-token basis. Additionally, we present a detailed analysis of per-channel quantization in
317   Appendix D. By using per-token quantization with outlier balancing, we aim to reduce the quantization
318   error of low-bit precision keys while dynamically shifting the outlier burden to the full-precision
319   queries. This approach effectively balances the outlier channels, minimizing errors and enhancing
320   both accuracy and robustness during KV compression. During the prefill phase, we compute the

      maximum values for each channel of the query and key to construct a channel balancer b, for input

348
349   To maximize the compression ratio of the KV cache, we now focus on quantizing the important KV

353
      of the important KVs enables a higher compression ratio while minimizing performance degradation.

354
      findings suggest that important KVs can be effectively compressed while maintaining reasonably
355
      high precision, allowing for effective compression without significantly compromising performance.

480   6     C ONCLUSION
481
482   In this paper, we presented Mixed-precision KV cache (MiKV), an effective strategy for KV cache
483   compression through importance-based mixed-precision quantization. By retaining the unimportant
484   KVs in low precision and protecting the important KVs in high precision, context damage involved in
485   cache eviction is recovered while generation quality is maintained. Through experiments on multiple
      benchmarks, we validated the effectiveness of MiKV.

       D    E XPERIMENTS ON P ER -C HANNEL Q UANTIZATION OF K EYS
1243
1244
       In Section 4.2, we scrutinized and discussed that systematic outlier channels emerge in the keys and
1245   queries, which leads to significant quantization errors, degrading the performance. For compatibility
1246   with existing off-the-shelf eviction strategies and kernel support, we adopted per-token quantization
1247   while mitigating the outlier effect with dynamic outlier awareness. An alternate direction towards
1248   mitigating these outliers is per-channel quantization, which naturally isolates the outlier channels.
1249   Recent works have demonstrated that such a quantization scheme can reduce quantization errors
1250   when the direction of quantization and the direction of outlier manifestation align (Heo et al., 2023).
1251   To explore this option, we conduct the experiment in Section 4.2 with per-channel key quantization.
1252   However, to impose per-channel dynamic quantization, the caching mechanism must be altered at the
1253   implementation level. First, incoming KV pairs must be stored in a temporary buffer until a sufficient
1254   amount of KV pairs are accumulated for quantization. Second, additional temporary buffers must be
1255   maintained to accumulate important KV pairs and unimportant pairs separately. Third, “evicting"
1256   a KV pair from a groupwise per-channel quantized tensor is not straightforward, as the tile size
1257   becomes non-uniform. Thus, the underlying eviction policy must be altered. Thus, for compatibility
1258   with existing off-the-shelf eviction strategies, we adopted per-token quantization.
1259   Nevertheless, per-channel key quantization is a straightforward approach toward outlier management.
1260   To this end, we gauge and analyze the effectiveness of per-channel quantization by conducting
1261   experiments with simulated hypothetical per-channel quantization. Our hypothetical quantization
1262   scheme quantizes the keys in a per-channel manner with a group size of 64. Since quantization is
1263   simulated, we do not reorder or buffer KV pairs and quantize them as-is. Thus, the precision of KV
1264   pairs can differ within groups, so that we can maintain the H2O eviction policy. Table 6 shows the line
1265
       retrieval performance when 20% of the KV pairs are kept in FP16 in the importance cache and 80% of
       the KV pairs are kept in INTx in the retained cache. The results show that per-channel quantization is
1266
       effective in preserving the performance, as it isolates outliers. For actual quantization, the underlying
1267
       eviction policy must be modified to incorporate per-channel quantization, so the performance result
1268   may differ. Although the quantization scheme used in this experiment is hypothetical, it demonstrates
1269   the possibility of utilizing per-channel quantization to effectively preserve performance if the eviction
1270   scheme is modified accordingly, and proper kernel support is provided.

1354   As demonstrated in Section 5.3, MiKV can be equipped with various importance policies (Zhang
1355   et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024). Here, we provide details on one such policy, the H2O (Zhang et al.,
1356   2023) policy, which is equipped throughout our main experiments. This policy decides to maintain
1357   important KVs according to the calculated importance of KVs alongside a local window of KVs.
1358   First, the importance of KVs is calculated by analyzing the lower triangular attention map produced
1359
       per self-attention head. The importance is determined by aggregating attention scores across the
       query dimension.
1360
1361   Let A ∈ RN ×N represent the lower triangular attention matrix of a single head in a single layer,
1362   where N is the sequence length, and Aij denotes the attention weight from query position i to key
1363   position j. To compute the importance of each KV, we calculate the accumulated attention score sj
1364   for each key j by summing the attention weights across all queries:
1365                                             N

1367                                             i=1

       F.2   M IXED P RECISION C ACHE M ANAGEMENT
1372
1373   We now describe the strategy for managing and accelerating the mixed-precision KV cache. Based on
1374   the importance policy, KV pairs are partitioned and re-grouped into high-precision and low-precision
1375   groups. This partitioning is feasible due to the following property: after positional encoding is applied,
1376   the self-attention mechanism becomes permutation-invariant with respect to positions in the KV
1377   cache. In other words, as long as KV pairs are permuted together, their order within the cache does
1378   not affect the self-attention computation. This enables arbitrary shuffling of KVs for the purpose of
1379
       grouping them by precision without any functional consequences.
1380   After grouping, each precision group is compressed with a distinct precision level. Within each group,
1381   all KVs share the same precision, allowing them to be stored contiguously in memory for efficiency.
1382   During the GEMV operation in self-attention, each KV group is accelerated via GPGPU kernels (e.g.,
1383   CUDA, Triton, etc.) that execute INTn × FP16 GEMV operations. It is important to note that various
1384   kernel designs, such as on-the-fly dequantization (Lin et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024) or lookup-based
1385
       designs (Park et al., 2024), can be employed to perform the necessary GEMV operations. For our
       experiments, we adopted an on-the-fly dequantization approach.

       J   L IMITATIONS
1675
1676
       The acceleration provided by our work on KV cache compression is limited to the speedup of the
1677   generation phase, where self-attention is memory-bound. The scope of our work does not include the
1678   acceleration for the compute-bound prefill phase and is orthogonal to works such as Dao et al. (2022).
1679
1680
       Due to limited computation resources, our benchmark experiments are conducted with deterministic
       greedy decoding. The performance of our proposed method and other baselines are currently not
1681
       tested under random sampling decoding or beam search decoding.
1682
1683
1684   K    B ROADER I MPACT
1685
1686   This paper presents a work in LLM KV cache compression for efficient and accelerated inference
1687   by mitigating the memory footprint of the KV cache. We examine existing KV cache compression
1688   methods in the context of contextual safety, and observe that cache eviction can result in undesired
1689
       model behavior (system prompt breach, response incoherence, hallucinations, and context detail loss),
       inducing social impacts. We propose our method to mitigate the potential contextual safety issues
1690
       caused by KV cache compression while preserving model performance. We project that our work
1691
       can provide insights contextual safety in compressing the KV cache of transformer-based LLMs for
1692   efficient deployment. On the other hand, a possible negative impact of our work may be that an LLM
1693   user may decide to tolerate a decline in reliability and generation quality for the sake of efficiency.
