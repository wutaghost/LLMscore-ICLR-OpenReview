011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013             We argue that there are two major distinct capabilities in long context understand-
014             ing: retrieval and holistic understanding. Understanding and further improving
015             LLMs’ long context capabilities would not be possible without knowing the tasks’
016             focus categories. We aim to automatically identify retrieval focused and holistic
017
                understanding focused problems from suites of benchmarks and quantitatively
                measure the difficulty within each focus. In this paper, we present the D OLCE
018
                framework, which parameterizes each problem by λ (complexity) and k (redun-
019
                dancy) and assigns to one of five predefined focus categories. We propose to sample
020             short contexts from the full context and estimate the probability an LLM solves
021             the problem using the sampled spans. To find the λ and k for each problem, we
022             further propose a mixture model of a non-parametric background noise component
023             and a parametric/non-parametric hybrid oracle component, where we derive the
024             probability functions parameterized by λ and k for both the correct-or-wrong
025             (COW) scenario and the partial-point-in-grading (PIG) scenario. Our proposed
026             methods can identify 0% to 67% of the problems are retrieval focused and 0% to
027             90% of the problems are holistic understanding focused across 44 existing long
028
                context evaluation tasks.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Large language models (LLMs) have become capable of processing long contexts up to 10M tokens
034   at a time (Achiam et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024; Anthropic, 2024; Reid et al., 2024). Model
035   developers have also identified a large number of long context use cases and accordingly compiled
036   existing and new long context evaluation tasks into benchmark suites to quantitatively measure LLMs’
037   long context capabilities (Shaham et al., 2023; An et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2024)1 .
038   We argue there exist two major distinct capabilities in long context understanding: retrieval and
039   holistic understanding. The former involves identifying a single or a few relevant pieces of informa-
040   tion (“needle”) from chunks of irrelevant content (“haystack”), while the latter assumes that a large
041   chunk, if not all, of the content is relevant, and oftentimes even the order matters. This distinction is
042   important since it relates to the architecture design of an efficient long context LLM. For example,
043   divide-and-conquer approaches, such as blockwise parallel attention (Liu et al., 2024a) or parallel
044
      decoding (Li et al., 2024b), can largely improve the efficiency a Transformer model without affecting
      the performance on a retrieval focused task, but may put the performance of a holistic understanding
045
      task in doubt. Recurrent models (Gu et al., 2022; Bulatov et al., 2022; Gu & Dao, 2023; Poli et al.,
046
      2023; Beck et al., 2024) are believed more suited for holistic understanding, despite recent underper-
047   formance (Zhang et al., 2024a; Huang, 2024). Retrieval augmented generation (RAG) architecture
048   is tailored for a balanced scenario. Understanding and further improving LLMs’ long context
049   capabilities would not be possible without knowing the tasks’ focus categories, which however
050   are sometimes unavailable. Although we may infer them from their task names (e.g. -QA or -Ret
051   suffices often indicate retrieval) or via manual inspection, it’s not reliable and time-consuming.

      We present the D OLCE (Differentiate Our Long Context Evaluation Tasks) framework, which
072   aims to automatically identify retrieval and holistic understanding focused problems from suites
073   of benchmarks and quantitatively measure the difficulty within each focus. Intuitively, a retrieval
074   focused problem often has a relatively short evidence span, and the difficulty depends on the number
075   of the evidence span occurrences in the context. A holistic understanding focused problem has a
076   longer minimum sufficient evidence span or multiple necessary spans dispersed across the context.
077   We use two parameters λ and k to capture the span complexity and redundancy and map each area
078   of the λ-k plane to a category, as shown in Figure 1. We also define a special area for λ = 0 (no
079   context), where the model solves the problem in a closed-book or zero-shot (CBZS) condition.
080   How can we find λ and k for each problem? One simple idea is to retrieve the spans relevant to the
081   question and/or the ground-truth answer, and then claim that λ is the length of the span and k is the
082   number of retrieved spans. However, this approach does not only rely on the retriever but also hardly
083   identifies the supporting spans required in the reasoning process. In the D OLCE framework, we
084   propose to sample short contexts from the full context and estimate the probability an LLM solves the
085   problem using the sampled spans, which also prevents getting “lost in the middle” (Liu et al., 2024c).
086
      We note that, although an LLM can often solve short context problems better than long context
      problems, unlike humans or oracle models, it can still make mistakes. We therefore further propose
087
      to use a mixture model of a non-parametric background noise component and a parametric/non-
088
      parametric hybrid oracle component, making our method less sensitive to the quality of the probing
089   model. To model the parametric oracle component, we derive the probability functions parameterized
090   by λ and k for both the correct-or-wrong (COW) scenario and the partial-point-in-grading (PIG)
091   scenario. We use two independently trained models: Gemini 1.5 Flash (Reid et al., 2024) and PaLM
092   2-S (Anil et al., 2023), to 44 tasks from three benchmark suites, and then apply the D OLCE framework
093   to obtain their focus categories. We have identified 0% to 67% of the COW problems and 0% to 29%
094   of the PIG problems are retrieval focused (Category III), and 0% to 89% of the COW problems and
095   0% to 90% of the PIG problems are holistic understanding focused (Category V). These results have
096   helped us understand and guide development of long context capabilities of LLMs.

      Long context evaluation benchmark suites have been developed, including LRA (Tay et al., 2020),
101
      ZeroSCROLLS (Shaham et al., 2023), L-Eval (An et al., 2024), LongBench (Bai et al., 2024),
102   BAMBOO (Dong et al., 2024), LooGLE (Li et al., 2024a), Loong (Wang et al., 2024c), LV-Eval
103   (Yuan et al., 2024), ∞Bench (Zhang et al., 2024b), Marathon (Zhang et al., 2024a), BABILong
104   (Kuratov et al., 2024), Ruler (Hsieh et al., 2024), LOFT (Lee et al., 2024). Each comprises existing
105   and/or new tasks in various domains, use cases, with contexts of different lengths and syntheticity
106   levels. Domain and use case focused long context evaluation tasks have also been developed, including
107   Needle-In-A-Haystack (Kamradt, 2023), LongEval (Li et al., 2023), SummHay (Laban et al., 2024),
      Task Haystack (Xu et al., 2024), Ada-LEval (Wang et al., 2024a), NovelQA (Wang et al., 2024b),

108
      FLenQA (Levy et al., 2024), NoCha (Karpinska et al., 2024), RepoQA (Liu et al., 2024b). Most
109   developers have observed performance degradation as the input context length increases. However,
110   except in the synthetic case, they have not explicitly distinguished between two length variables: the
111   input length (L) and an unknown necessary context length or complexity degree (λ).
112
      More recent benchmarks have started to emphasize different difficulty types. Wang et al. (2024a) use
113
      the notion of “full text comprehension” for tasks whose performances “decrease sharply when the text
114
      is truncated”, despite lack of detail. Li et al. (2024a) distinguish between short and long dependencies.
115   Karpinska et al. (2024) annotate each question with a sentence, passage, or “global reasoning” scope.
116   Wang et al. (2024c) define four categories, from “spotlight locating” to “chain of reasoning”. Thus
117   far, developers have had to manually assign categories to tasks, which can be unreliable and costly.
118   We believe that difficulty should be a continuous spectrum, rather than categorical, and we need a
119   quantitative approach to automatically assign categories. Along this line, Qian et al. (2024) propose
120   LC-Boost, which iteratively interacts with an LLM agent to find “minimal necessary context”. They
121   adopt a simplified assumption of ours (with no k), but the quality highly relies on the retriever and
122   the LLM. Since their goal is to solve the tasks, rather than analyze the tasks, they do not report
123
      the minimal necessary context lengths. Most relevant to ours, Goldman et al. (2024) coincidentally
      propose two difficulty dimensions: scope and diffusion, in a position paper. Our D OLCE framework
124
      not only formally defines λ and k and derive probability functions under two different assumptions,
125
      but also quantitatively estimates λ and k. To the best of our knowledge, our paper is the first to study
126   the problem of automatic categorization of long context tasks.

129   3     D OLCE : D ISTINGUISH O UR L ONG C ONTEXT E VALUATION TASKS
130
131   The D OLCE framework consists of two major steps: sampling & observation and parameter estimation.
132   In the first step, we use a probing model to observe responses given sampled short contexts, which
133   are then evaluated. We describe this step in Section 3.1. In the second step, we attempt to find λ
134   and k that maximize the likelihood of the observed evaluation outcomes. We use a mixture model
135   assumption that smooths out the model noise. The modeling process slightly differs between the
136
      COW and PIG scenarios, which are defined and discussed in detail in Sections 3.2 and 3.3.
137
138   3.1   S AMPLING & O BSERVATION
139
140   For a given problem, we first chunk its context into L units, where L is also referred to as the length
141
      of the context. We choose sentences as units in most cases, but also consider other granularities when
      explicit structures are available. We define a span as a sequence of contiguous units. We randomly
142
      sample an observation span of length C from the full context, and then observe an evaluation outcome
143
      x. We may also iterate over all the possible spans (by shifting one unit at a time) when budget allows.
144
145   When using a binary evaluation metric, e.g. accuracy, the random variable x can only take two or
146   three values: “1” meaning fully correct, “0” meaning totally incorrect, and optionally “IDK” (or ∅
147
      for brevity) when not enough information is provided, if instructed. We refer to this as the correct-
      or-wrong (COW) scenario. When using a continuous evaluation metric, e.g. F-1 or ROUGE, the
148
      interpretation of x may be ambiguous. Some problems lack a comprehensive list of answer variants
149
      and instead employ F-1 for fuzzy matching. In this case, we should find a threshold to binarize x and
150   treat it as the COW case. Other problems that expect multi-aspect answers use continuous metrics to
151   allow partial points. In this case, we need an alternative partial-point-in-grading (PIG) scenario to
152   directly incorporate the raw continuous outcome x. We see in the following subsections that these
153   two scenarios will lead to different assumptions and probability functions. We use Hartigans’ Dip
154   Test (Hartigan & Hartigan, 1985) based on the collective observed outcomes to classify each problem
155   into either COW or PIG scenario, since both scenarios can co-exist in the same task. In particular, we
156   bucketize the scores into bins of equal width of 0.1, and assign COW to a problem if the p-value is
157   below 0.5, i.e. a multi-modal score distribution, and PIG otherwise.
158   Once we make multiple observations of different lengths and collect evaluation outcomes, we may
159   guess the length of a minimum sufficient span that can answer the question, which can be one
160   definition for λ. We consider an example of a COW scenario in Table 1. An optimistic person would
161   say λ = 1 because that’s when the model starts to output the correct answer (P (x = 1) > 0), and
      a pessimistic person would say λ = 20 because that’s when the model never produces an incorrect

      3.4   M AXIMUM L IKELIHOOD E STIMATION OF λ, k
271
272   The goal for maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) is to find λ and k that best describe the data
273   {xij }ij by optimizing the joint distribution {p(xij )}ij using Eq 1 or 5. We use the expectation-
274   maximization technique to solve our mixture problem. We use the standard E-step to compute the
275   posterior probabilities q(zij = zij |xij = xij ) or q(zij = zij |yijl = yijl ), and the M-step to compute
276   the parameters, including pz,x (z = O, N and x = 0, 1, ∅), membership priors, λ and k.
277   Optimizing the parameters λ and k in these combinatorial probability functions is difficult. Also,
278   since all C, λ and k can vary from 0 or 1 to L, we cannot easily approximate these functions using
279   the asymptotic techniques. Fortunately, our goal is not to find the exact optimal parameters, instead
280   we can provide a small set of λ and k candidates, using exponential intervals (0, 1, 2, 5, 10, 20, . . .),
281   and find the maximum probability only among these combinations2 . We provide pseudocodes of
282   our EM-based MLE algorithm for the two scenarios in Appendix C. Finally, we use the assignment
283   criteria described in Figure 1 to assign a category label to each problem.

286
287   We identified three most cited new benchmark suites at the time of our preparation: L-Eval (An et al.,
288   2024), BAMBOO (Dong et al., 2024), and LongBench (Bai et al., 2024), and collected a total of 44
289   tasks, which also include most tasks in ZeroSCROLLS (Shaham et al., 2023). We understand that
290   most contexts used in these suites have much fewer than 100K tokens, which are considered only
291   “moderately long” by the current standard. Yet, we found that no task in the COW scenario has all
292   Category V problems, i.e. λ < L. We first apply the task specific preprocessing steps (described
293
      in Appendix D), and expand the prompts with IDK instructions (exemplified in Appendix E). Next,
      we find the most appropriate unit granularity and determine the observation lengths. We show an
294
      example of the study in Appendix F and report the sampling and observation specs in Appendix G.
295
296   We primarily use the Gemini 1.5 Flash model (Reid et al., 2024), unless otherwise noted. We follow
297   the sampling and observation procedure described in Section 3.1. The tasks are evaluated using
298   accuracy, F-1, ROUGE-L, or EditSim, against the provided ground-truth answers. We conduct
299
      the Hartigans’ Dip Test to the 29 tasks evaluated using F-1, ROUGE, or EditSim, and we found
      that 4 tasks have COW only problems, 10 tasks have PIG only problems, and the 15 tasks have a
300
      combination of COW and PIG problems. We present the Dip Test results in Appendix H. During
301
      MLE, we optimize the likelihood function in the COW scenario (Eq. 1) for the accuracy-evaluated
302   problems as well as Dip Test identified COW subsets using a threshold of 0.5, and the likelihood
303   function in the PIG scenario (Eq. 5) for the Dip Test identified PIG subsets.
304
305
      In both scenarios, p(x|z = N ) is shared across all problems of the same task, and P (z) is shared
      across all samples of the same problem. In the COW scenario, Pnonpar (x|z = O) is shared across
306
      all samples of the same problem, computed using only the outcomes when C < λ. In the PIG
307
      scenario, Pnonpar (y|z = O) is shared across all samples with the same observation length. During
308   parameter inference, we try λi and ki from {Cij }j ∪ {0, maxj Cij + 1, Li }, where maxj Cij + 1 can
309   help identify Category V problems in the PIG scenario, since the PIG assumption always expects
310   sij = Cij /L when λ = L, which happens rarely. We run the EM algorithm for 10 steps in the COW
311   scenario and 5 steps in the PIG scenario. We set thresholds λp and kp as the first tertile among the
312   N exponential candidates (excluding 0, maxj Cij + 1, L), i.e. p = bN/3c. We use λq = maxj Cij .
313   Four tasks exist in two suites, in which cases we use the smaller threshold for both tasks.

413   5.3        E XAMPLES : Q UALITY & L ONG FQA
414
415   In this section, we look into two tasks: QuALITY and LongFQA. Both tasks have a blend of problems
416   from Categories II to V under the COW assumption. We show the category assignment of the most
417
      representative problems in Table 2 and present the full answer, the raw outcomes, and intermediate
      parameters in Appendix J. The most representative problems for Categories II to V are defined as
418
      min λ-then-max k, min λ-then-min k, max λ-then-max k, and max λ-then-min k respectively.
419
420   We try to “speculate” the rationale behind the assignments. Category II is assigned to the first
421   QuALITY problem (“Why might ...”), since the correct answer “Survival itself is difficult” is also
422   true in our universe that the model is exposed to. The same Category II is assigned to the first
423
      LongFQA problem (“What are ...”), since the answer (“receiving shareholder approval ...”) is repeated
      multiple times in the speech. The most representative Category III problems for both tasks seem
424
      to ask for very specific details of a fact mentioned in the context. It is even more obvious that the
425
      LongFQA problem (“What were ...”) is a factoid question. The Category IV problem (“What impact
426   ...”) for the LongFQA task is a set question, requiring collecting multiple facets from a longer span.
427   The Category V problem for the QuALITY task (“How many ...”) requires to count the number of
428   sentences, exhibiting a clear holistic intent. Despite the yes/no form of the Category V problem
429   of the LongFQA task (“Does JLL ...”), the provided transcript does not disclose the firm’s market
430   share in either division at all. However, a human may guess “yes” from some clues, e.g. the speaker
431   emphasized leasing more than capital market (“leasing and capital market” thrice vs. never “capital
      market and leasing”), and hinted that leasing was a more established business than capital market.

433
434
      Modeling decisions may affect the estimation of parameters. We define a few metrics to quantify
435   the difference between two sets of parameters estimated from the reference (ref) and the test setups,
436                                                                   |λref −λtest |                  |kref −ktest |
      including Relative Change (δ) of λ and k, defined as δ(λ) = max(λ      ref ,λtest )
                                                                                          and δ(k) = max(k   ref ,ktest )
                                                                                                                          ,
437
      Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient (ρ) of λ and k, and KL Divergence of p(x|z = N ). We
438
      summarize our findings in this section and provide details in Appendix K.
439
440   Sampling Strategies. We implement a few heuristics based sampling strategies and found that the
441   take-every strategy (i.e. shifting the observation window by a fixed number of units) generally works
442   well. In the case of L-Eval SFcition, using take-every-5 strategy (i.e. reducing the total required
443
      resource by 80%), we can still obtain ρ(λ) of 0.93, ρ(k) of 0.99, and KL Divergence of P (x|z = N )
      of 3.7 × 10−5 . We provide more details in Appendix K.1.
444
445   Unit Granularities. We use different unit granularities for seven tasks. We see that in the COW
446   scenario, the rankings of both λ and k are preserved between different unit granularities, with ρ(λ)
447   between 0.54 and 0.80 and ρ(λ) ≥ 0.50, i.e. strong correlation. The ranking of λ is sometimes less
448   preserved in the PIG scenario, with ρ(λ) between 0.35 and 0.84 across the tasks, i.e. moderate to
449
      strong correlation, while the ranking of k is also well preserved with ρ(k) ≥ 0.64. The background
      noise distribution estimation is mostly preserved as well, with the KL divergence ≤ 0.18 across all
450
      tasks. We provide more details and explanations for the minor discordance in Appendix K.2.
451
452   Probing Models: Gemini 1.5 Flash vs. PaLM 2-S. We apply the PaLM 2-S model to the same
453   COW and PIG splits determined by the Hartigans’ Dip Test results using the Gemini 1.5 Flash model
454   scores. We compare λ and k estimated by the two models across all tasks and further ignore the
455
      problems assigned to Category I by either model when computing δ and ρ. The median δ(λ) and
      δ(k) are 0.30 and 0.16, and the median ρ(λ) and ρ(k) are 0.43 and 0.41, which fall into the moderate
456
      correlation category. We provide more details and our thoughts on the disagreement in Appendix K.3.
457
458   Binarization Thresholds In Adapting COW Assumption For Continuous Scores. We compare
459   between the default binarization threshold (0.5) with 0, 0.25, 0.75, and 1 for the problems in the
460   tasks identified as the COW scenario by the Hartigans’ Dip Test. We found that, as we increase the
461
      threshold, the category assignment either does not change or shifts from Category II or III towards
      Category IV or V. When the threshold is changed from 0.5 to 0.25, 0.75, or 1, ρ(λ) and ρ(k) are
462
      above 0.48 and 0.41 across all but two tasks. When the threshold is changed to 0, both ρ(λ) and ρ(k)
463
      decrease, suggesting the threshold must be greater than 0. We give more details in Appendix K.4.
464
465   Same Tasks From Different Benchmark Suites. Four tasks exist in both L-Eval and LongBench
466   suites. We found that only the Qasper task has similar category distributions, and the L-Eval versions
467
      of MultiNews and NarrativeQA have more holistic understanding “flavor” than the LongBench
      versions, but the L-Eval version of GovReport has less holistic understanding than the LongBench
468
      version. The discrepancy can be explained by the different problem selection schemes, leading to
469
      different median context lengths and difficulty levels. We give more details in Appendix K.5.
470
471   Application In Model Development: KV Cache Update Schedule. This work is motivated by
472   our and others’ observations that different long context LLM architectures may behave differently
473
      for different categories of long context tasks. In Appendix K.6, we present a case study on the
      least recently attended (LRA) (Yang & Hua, 2024), an efficient KV cache update schedule for long
474
      context LLMs. We found that if we want to utilize LRA to improve the efficiency of a long context
475
      application, we need to understand its focus category and adjust the input format accordingly.
476

478   7    C ONCLUSION & F UTURE W ORK
479
480   In this paper, we introduce two parameters λ and k to quantitatively measure the difficulty along the
481   two dimensions: complexity and redundancy. Then, we propose the D OLCE framework that leverages
482
      a mixture model to estimate these parameters. Our proposed methods can identify 0% to 67% of the
      problems are retrieval focused and 0% to 90% of the problems are holistic understanding focused
483
      across the tasks and scenario subsets. We also acknowledge that our paper has some limitations,
484
      which we summarize in Appendix L. Practically, we plan to apply our framework to more recent
485   longer context tasks to help categorize their focuses.

       We describe the task specific preprocessing steps in each benchmark suite in this section.
1137
1138   L-Eval. We use the “input” as the context, then flatten the “instructions” to create multiple indepen-
1139   dent problems.
1140   BAMBOO. We exclude the two sorting tasks, since they require special assumptions and sampling
1141   based methods. Some of the BAMBOO tasks have a single “content” field consisting of instructions,
1142   contexts, and questions. We need to identify individual parts from the “content”, since we can only
1143   sample from the context while keep the entire instruction and question available to the model in all
1144   samples. In particular,
1145
               • For an AltQA problem, we split the “content” into a question from the “Question” section, a

                 context from the “Document” section, and an actual instruction from the remaining content.

1151             the lines after “# [end]” and the question.

1153   LongBench. We use the English subsets of this multilingual dataset. We use the original “input” and

       We are not restricted from using sentences as the units. In fact, we may choose different unit
       granularities, e.g. tokens or even characters at one extreme and one or several full document(s) at
1175
       the other extreme. The problem with the former setup is that we may end up with a large number of
1176
       spans that are not semantically coherent, which wastes our computation resource. The problem with
1177   the latter is that we may still present irrelevant contexts to the model and get inflated numbers, which
1178   also challenges the long context capability of the test model. A general rule of thumb is to choose a
1179   unit granularity such that the derived units with which the context lengths have little variance when
1180   measured in number of characters or tokens.

       In this section, we present the Hartigans’ Dip Test results. In Figure 6, we first show the distributions
       of raw scores (x) across all problems in the task, if they are evaluated using F-1, ROUGE, or EditSim.
1354
       We can easily tell that some tasks exhibit clear bimodality, most notably BAMBOO SenHallu and
1355
       AbsHallu, in which cases accuracy might also be used instead. Many other QA tasks, including
1356   HotpotQA, 2WikiMultihopQA, and TriviaQA, also have the most probability mass at 0 and 1, which
1357   suggests that the majority of the problems at least should be categorized into the COW scenario. We
1358   also found in these cases, 0.5 (or 50 percentage) is generally a reasonable threshold to set apart “1”
1359   from “0”. On the other hand, most summarization tasks have a unimodal probability distribution and
1360   little probability mass at 0 or 1.
1361
       We first bucketize the scores (between 0 and 1) from all observations with an equal width of 0.1, and
1362   then apply the Hartigans’ Dip Test to the bucketized scores for each task. We calculate the p-value for
1363   each task, and show 1− p-value in Figure 7. We assign a small value to the tasks when their y-axis is
1364   0. A short bar (high p-value) indicates more likely a PIG scenario (unimodal), and a tall bar (low
1365   p-value) indicates more likely a COW scenario (multi-modal). We use ∗ to suffix a task name and a
1366   blue bar when it is evaluated using F-1, † and an orange bar when it it evaluated using ROUGE, and
1367     and a grey bar when it is evaluated using EditSim. We see while most ROUGE-evaluated tasks
1368   belong to the PIG scenario and most F1-evaluated tasks belong to the COW scenario, there also exist
1369   several exceptions.
1370   We found that both COW and PIG scenarios can co-exist among the problems within the same task.
1371   Next, we apply the Hartigans’ Dip Test at each problem level. We show the result in Figure 7. The
1372   x-axis is 1− p-value and the y-axis is the number of problems. A bar at x = 0 indicates more likely a

1741
       We compare between a few simple heuristics, including sampling at a fixed rate r (sample rate),
1742
       taking every N -th (fixed) candidate in the sequence (take every), sampling inversely proportionally
1743
       to the observation length (i.e. sampling more for short observations and less for long observations,
1744   sample ip rate), and taking every N -th (inversely proportionally to the observation length) candidate
1745   (take ip rate), as well as observing only long observations (>= 10 only), short observations (<= 10
1746   only), and wider observation length intervals (0, 1, 5, 20, 100, max only).
1747
       We plot the Relative Change (δ) and Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient (ρ) of λ and k as well as
1748
       the KL Divergence of P (x|z = N ) between enumerating all observation spans and various sampling
1749
       strategies for L-Eval TOEFL and SFcition in Figures 12 and 13 respectively. To quantify the required
1750   resources (i.e. the x-axis), we use the number of total units and the number of total tokens as proxies.
1751   First, we found that the more the samples the more highly the inferred parameters can correlate
1752   with those estimated from using all the possible observation spans. Then, we found that “take”
1753   strategies (i.e. shifting the observation window by a fixed number of units) work better than the
1754   random “sampling” strategies, suggesting that continuity exists in the sequence, i.e. the correctness
1755   of a span is positively correlated with that of its neighboring span. Also, the take-every strategy
1756   generally works well. For example, in the case of L-Eval SFcition, using take-every-5 strategy (i.e.
1757   reducing the total required resource by 80%), we can still obtain ρ(λ) of 0.93, ρ(k) of 0.99, and KL
1758
       Divergence of P (x|z = N ) of 3.7 × 10−5 .

       In a nutshell, it splits the long input into C chunks, each having a length of S and processes one
2308
       chunk at each step. The LRA updates the KV cache by keeping only the top N attended KVs at each
2309
       step and dropping the other KVs. One advantage is that it can take an arbitrarily long input sequence
2310   without a set limit. Also, it reduces the computation complexity by a factor of C, compared to the
2311   vanilla attention taking the full CS sequence as a whole. Like many cache update schedules, these
2312   efficiency benefits come at a cost: the least attended KV at certain stage may be essential for future
2313   steps but have to be removed from the cache, potentially hurting the long context task performance.
2314
       Intuitively, a retrieval focused task often requires a relatively small KV cache to store only the most
2315
       relevant short span, only if the task instruction or question is prefixed to the context. This should
2316   translate to a phenomenon that by reducing the size of the chunk and/or cache capacity, we should see
2317   a retrieval focused task performance mildly drops. In contrast, when the task instruction is suffixed
2318   to the context, the attention score reflects the importance of each token in the “text generation task”
2319   in a query-independent fashion, and therefore the cache is not well maintained to properly answer the
2320   question. We expect that the performance should drop more in this case. A holistic understanding
2321   task, on the other hand, may expect a large KV cache. When we reduce the size of the KV cache, the
       performance should generally drop more. Moreover, putting the question or task instruction before or

       after the context should not matter. When the question is suffixed to the context, the cache should
2323   have already compressed itself to keep only the essential information from the entire context.

2345   L    L IMITATIONS
2346
2347   First, we list the limitations of our proposed D OLCE framework.
2348   Mixture assumption. The background noise module and the oracle module do not have to be
2349   combined using our “additive” mixture assumption. In fact, we also implement an alternative
2350   “multiplicative” or generative assumption. Under the multiplicative assumption, we continue to use
2351   the random variable x to denote the observed outcome, but we use the random variable z to denote the
2352   latent oracle outcome, which can take the same values as x. Then, p(x|z) represents the “transition”
2353   between the oracle and the actual outcome. We found that the parameters are more difficult to
2354   estimate if not regularized. Specifically, we sometimes learned a transition parameter p(x = 1|z = 0)
2355
       or p(x = 0|z = 1) close to 1, which should rarely happen given that even the probing model should
       at least behave reasonably even when it is not confident.
2356
2357   Oracle component assumptions. We propose two assumptions: COW and PIG and derive π and ρ
2358   in Section 3, which may not be accurate for all the tasks. In fact, we are not restricted from using
2359   only the proposed assumptions. First, we can extend the COW assumption by further allowing
2360   noncontiguous ground-truth spans, or extend the PIG assumption to accommodate ground-truth
2361
       aspect spans with length greater than one, which should allow us to further apply to synthetic needle-
       in-the-haystack task variants, e.g. FLenQA (Levy et al., 2024), BABILong (Kuratov et al., 2024).
2362
       Next, we can also assume that we must find the aspects and put them in a certain order in order to
2363
       answer the problem, which can help distinguish sequential reasoning tasks from summarization-like
2364   problems using “divide-and-conquer” (Levy et al., 2024). Finally, we may relax this restriction by
2365   allowing that any unit may be used by multiple ground truth spans. However, in the PIG scenario,
2366   the λ ground-truth aspects are distributed independently of other ground-truth spans, and hence the
2367   probability becomes a function of only k but not λ any more. If we continue to model λ under this
2368   additional “overlappable” aspect assumption, we need to make further assumption that the probability
2369   ρ(s, λ, k; L, C) follows another distribution, where λ can still be relevant to all the moments except
2370   the mean. We leave this for future work.
2371   MLE objective & sampling strategy. We use MLE as the objective as it seems the most straight-
2372   forward formulation of the problem. But it does not mean this is the only objective. Also, we may
2373   modify the MLE formulation. For example, we currently give each sample an equal weight in our
2374   experiment. Alternatively, we can also give each observation length an equal weight in the likelihood
2375   function, regardless of the number of samples obtained using this observation length. As we show in
       Appendix K.1, small sample sizes often hurt the estimation accuracy for λ and k when the heuristic


                                                        44
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2376
       sampling strategies are used. We may also improve the sampling strategy, i.e. by using a dynamic
2377   strategy that can shift to the next start position based on the previous outcome(s), or one that uses
2378   some global heuristics similar to important sampling. We can also combine retrieval methods into the
2379   sampling process to initialize the “importance” scores.
2380
       Probing model. Although our framework is designed to tolerate observation noises from using a
2381
       probing model, its parameter inference effectiveness may still be impacted by the probing model,
2382
       especially when it ignores our instructions. For example, if the probing model decides not to refer
2383   to the provided context, and instead retrieves the answer directly from its internal knowledge, it
2384   essentially wastes this problem, which may eventually be labeled as Category I or II. Another
2385   example is when the model has a conceited or humble characteristic by underusing or overusing
2386   “IDK” when it is not confident to answer. Although the framework can learn the latent tendency of
2387   the background noise component from the collective outcomes across the problems, the parameters
2388   of each problem are eventually determined based on the probing model’s outcomes relative to the
2389   peer problems. If all the evaluation outcomes are “0”, the mixture model assumption could hardly
2390   distinguish the source between the background noise component or the oracle component. In this
2391
       work, we chose to use mid-sized models that should be capable of understanding short context
       texts and following instructions. We intentionally avoided using larger models due to their strong
2392
       closed-book and zero-shot capabilities as a result of memorization of knowledge.
2393
2394   Evaluation. Evaluation plays a very crucial role in this process. While objective questions evaluated
2395   using accuracy as the metric are generally reliable, subjective and generative questions evaluated
2396   using ROUGE can be problematic, since the noise from the ROUGE scores can exceed the “denoising”
2397
       allowance of our framework. In our work, we do not use any external script executor, human or
       LLM-as-a-rater service in this process, although we believe they should help improve the accuracy of
2398
       the inferred parameters.
