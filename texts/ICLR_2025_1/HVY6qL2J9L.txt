011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent research shows that standard navigation agents significantly underperform
014            and even fail in the presence of various visual corruptions. Unlike embodied
015            agents, the human brain’s visual system can robustly perceive the environment
016
               and extract the necessary information to complete the visual tasks. In this paper,
               we propose a two-phase Brain-Machine integration Navigation method called
017
               BraiNav, which incorporates neural representations derived from human brain
018
               activity to enhance robustness against visual corruptions. In the first phase, a
019            brain encoder, built upon a recently advanced self-supervised pretrained model,
020            is trained on a large-scale human brain activity dataset and then frozen for down-
021            stream visual navigation. In the second phase, neural representations harboring
022            high-level cognitive information from the human brain are constructed based on
023            the pretrained frozen brain encoder. Additionally, we propose a multimodal fusion
024            method based on cross-attention to obtain more consistent brain-visual joint rep-
025            resentations, which are then used to learn the navigation policy. Sufficient exper-
026            iments demonstrate that the proposed method exhibits higher robustness against
027
               various visual corruptions compared to standard navigation agent and multiple
               computer vision-enhanced agents. Our study pioneers the incorporation of hu-
028
               man brain activity into embodied AI, aiming to catalyze further cross-disciplinary
029
               collaboration with computational neuroscience.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Embodied visual navigation (Anderson et al., 2018; Batra et al., 2020), one of the most researched
035
      topics in embodied AI (Duan et al., 2022), requires agents to make action decisions based on ego-
036   centric observations to achieve their goals. Despite the remarkable progress in embodied visual nav-
037   igation, efforts (Wijmans et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023) have primarily focused
038   on training agents to generalize to unseen environments, assuming similarities between training
039   and testing environments. However, a major challenge in this field is ensuring agent generalization
040   across environments with different visual appearances (Chattopadhyay et al., 2021; Rajič, 2022).
041
      Following standard protocol, agents are trained on a set of scenes and evaluated on unseen scenes,
042   which entails two types of evaluation. The first type (Figure 1 (a)) assesses generalization per-
043   formance on clean observations, where existing navigation methods have demonstrated high perfor-
044   mance. The second type (Figure 1 (b)) introduces visual corruption, such as defocus blur, simulating
045   real-life challenges. Assessing robustness to such corruption alongside generalization performance
046   poses a greater challenge. While previous studies have utilized standard deep learning techniques,
047   such as data augmentation and self-supervised adaptation (Chattopadhyay et al., 2021), to enhance
048   robustness, there remains significant room for improvement in fully recovering lost navigation per-
049   formance.
050   Unlike deep models, the human brain’s visual system possesses superior capabilities in processing
051   high-level semantic information from images, going beyond basic features like color, shape, and
052   texture. As shown in Figure 1, humans can achieve comparable performance in navigation tasks un-
053   der corrupted observations, indicating the robustness of the human visual system. Recent researches
      have demonstrated improved performance in deep learning models by incorporating neural repre-

      2.1   TASK D EFINITION
100
101
      In this paper, we focus solely on PointNav. The navigation agent is trained using deep reinforcement
102   learning. At each timestep t, the agent obtains the egocentric RGB observation ot and target location
103   lt . The agent then takes a predicted action to reach the target in as few timesteps as possible. There
104   are four available actions for the agent: move forward (0.25m), turn left (30°), turn right (30°),
105   and stop. An episode is considered successful if the agent stops within 0.2m of the target and
106   executes a maximum of 300 steps. Unlike previous configurations where the RGB observation
107   is clean during evaluation, in our task, the observation is subject to various visual corruptions to
      evaluate the robustness of the navigation agent.

      The visual-target module is not elaborately designed, as the main purpose of this paper is to demon-
163   strate the effectiveness of human brain activity for robust navigation. Specifically, the visual-target
164   module Ev comprises a frozen visual encoder, a target encoder, a compressor network, and a com-

      2.3   B RAIN E NCODER P RETRAINING
197
198   The brain encoder model follows the method proposed in (Adeli et al., 2023) and consists of three
199   components: an encoder, a decoder, and prediction heads, as shown in Figure 2. In the encoder,
200   the input image is first divided into fixed 14 × 14 patches. These image patches are then processed
201   by DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023), a recent advanced foundation model capable of extracting high-
202   performance visual features. Specifically, the DINOv2 used is a self-supervised pretrained frozen
203   ViT-B/14 (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) model, which consists of a linear projection layer and a Trans-
204   former encoder. Each image patch is projected into a 768-dimensional patch embedding by the
205   linear projection layer, and an additional learnable patch embedding is prepended to the sequence,
206
      denoted as CLS. Position embeddings are added to the patch embeddings before they are fed into
      the Transformer encoder, which consists of 12 stacked Transformer blocks (Vaswani et al., 2017).
207
208   The decoder is a single-layer Transformer with a feed-forward dimension of 2048 with 16 attention
209   heads. The brain ROI queries consist of M learnable positional encodings corresponding to different
210   brain ROIs in each hemisphere. Specifically, 16 queries are used, with 8 for each hemisphere (7
211   streams add 1 for all the vertices labeled ’unknown’). The Transformer decoder utilizes the output
212
      from the encoder to transform these queries into output tokens.
213   The prediction heads comprise 16 MLPs that map the output tokens from the decoder to the fMRI
214   responses of the corresponding ROIs. Each output token is mapped to a vector by the MLP with
215   the same number of voxels as the corresponding hemisphere (19,004 for the left hemisphere and
      20,544 for the right hemisphere), with voxels not belonging to that ROI set to 0 using a mask. The

216
      prediction loss is computed by calculating the Mean Square Error (MSE) between the predicted and
217   measured fMRI responses voxel by voxel.
218
219   After pretraining, we construct neural representations for downstream visual navigation using the
220
      pretrained frozen brain encoder, as shown in the brain module in Figure 2. Specifically, we use the
      low-dimensional and noise-reduced output tokens from the brain encoder to construct neural rep-
221
      resentations, effectively exploiting the high-level information embedded in human brain activities.
222
      Each 768-dimensional output token from each ROI of the anatomical streams is mapped to 48 di-
223   mensions using an MLP, and the resulting vectors are concatenated and fed into another MLP to
224   obtain 768-dimensional neural representation.

314
315   For brain encoder pretraining, the learning rate is set to 1e-4, the batch size to 32, the number of
316   training epochs to 20, the gradient clip to 0.1, the weight decay to 1e-4, and dropout to 0.1. The
317   subsequent brain encoder is trained using the fMRI data from subject 01, with results from other
318   subjects presented shown in Appendix B.1. Additionally, the original 425×425 resolution images
319   are resized to 224×224 to match the observation image resolution for downstream visual navigation
320
      tasks.
321   For BraiNav training, the learning rate is set to 3e-4, the discount factor to 0.99, the rollout length
322   to 128, and the total training frames to 75M. The corruption level is consistently set to 5, as in
323   ROBUSTNAV, where level 5 indicates the most severe corruptions. Training is conducted on a
      single NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 GPU.

      3.5   C OMPARISON WITH S TANDARD NAVIGATION AGENT
356
357   We compare the proposed method with the standard navigation agent (Chattopadhyay et al.,
358   2021), as shown in Table 2. BraiNav outperforms the standard navigation agent across 6 vi-
359   sual corruptions, achieving varying degrees of performance improvement. Notably, on Speckle
360   Noise and Defocus Blur, BraiNav shows significant improvements, with absolute improvements
361   of (15.77%SPL, 17.75%SR) and (11.89%SPL, 11.37%SR), respectively. For other corruptions,
362   the absolute improvements are (4.93%SPL, 1.37%SR) for Motion Blur, (2.76%SPL, 3.18%SR) for
363
      Spatter, (1.8%SPL, 0.73%SR) for Low Lighting, and (0.83%SPL, 0.46%SR) for Lower FOV.
364   It is worth noting that our method exhibits remarkable performance improvement on Defocus Blur.
365   Neuroscience studies (Mon-Williams et al., 1998; Webster et al., 2002; Zhu et al., 2013) suggest the
366   existence of a perceptual mechanism in the human brain that regulates image defocus. In conclusion,
367   these experimental results demonstrate that our proposed navigation method, BraiNav, surpasses the
368   standard navigation agent across various visual corruptions.

      4    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
454
455
      In this paper, we introduce BraiNav, a novel framework designed to address the robustness chal-
      lenges in embodied visual navigation. Our two-phase method leverages human brain activity to
456
      enhance the navigation agent’s resilience to visual corruption. In the first phase, we pretrain a brain
457
      encoder model with DINOv2 as the backbone on a large-scale fMRI dataset. In the second phase,
458   we utilize the pretrained frozen brain encoder to construct neural representations that encapsulate
459   high-level cognitive information from the human brain. Additionally, we develop a multimodal
460   fusion module based on cross-attention to facilitate the learning of consistent brain-visual joint rep-
461   resentations for navigation policy acquisition. We evaluate BraiNav’s navigation performance across
462   multiple visual corruptions, demonstrating its superior robustness compared to standard visual navi-
463   gation agent and multiple computer vision-enhanced agents. More importantly, our research bridges
464   embodied AI and neuroscience, showcasing the potential for translating insights from neuroscience
465   into advancements in embodied AI.
466   To create a brain-like representation for the navigation agent, BraiNav first employs a brain encoder
467   pretrained on fMRI data to obtain neural representations, followed by a multimodal fusion module
468   to achieve more consistent joint representations. Thus, enhancements in both the brain encoder and
469   the multimodal fusion module are expected to yield better performance. Future research could ex-
470   plore different brain encoder architectures (Yang et al., 2024) and multimodal fusion methods (Mao
471   et al., 2023). Furthermore, the brain module in BraiNav is decoupled from the specific task; it can
472
      receive images and output brain-like representations to improve the agent’s robustness to visual cor-
      ruptions for other embodied tasks. Future studies could apply BraiNav’s brain-like representations
473
      to a broader range of embodied tasks (Wan et al., 2023).

      A.1   E MBODIED V ISUAL NAVIGATION
651
652
      Two widely studied tasks in embodied visual navigation are PointGoal Navigation (PointNav) (An-
653   derson et al., 2018) and ObjectGoal Navigation (ObjectNav) (Batra et al., 2020). PointNav involves
654   navigating to a specified goal coordinate in a global reference frame, while ObjectNav requires the
655   agent to find an instance of a specified object. Thanks to high-quality simulators (Kolve et al., 2017;
656   Savva et al., 2019) and datasets (Chang et al., 2017; Deitke et al., 2020), significant advancements
657   has been made in embodied visual navigation (Wijmans et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2021; Partsey et al.,
658   2022; Zhang et al., 2023), especially in the PointNav task, which is often considered ”solved” (Wij-
659   mans et al., 2019).
660   However, existing studies have largely overlooked the robustness of agents, a critical aspect for
661   real-world applications. To address this gap, (Chattopadhyay et al., 2021) proposed ROBUSTNAV,
662   a framework for analyzing the robustness of navigation agents. ROBUSTNAV quantifies the per-
663   formance of embodied navigation agents under various common visual and dynamic corruptions.
664   Extensive experiments demonstrated that navigation agents trained in simulation often exhibit sig-
665   nificant performance drops when evaluated in corrupted environments. Furthermore, (Rajič, 2022)
666   conducted a robustness analysis of two successful agents from the 2021 Habitat Challenge, reveal-
667
      ing varying degrees of performance deterioration in corrupted settings. (Lee et al., 2022) proposed a
      self-supervised domain adaptation method with map style transfer to boost agent robustness against
668
      visual and dynamic perturbations. (Piriyajitakonkij et al., 2024) introduced TTA-Nav, which en-
669
      hances navigation performance across visual corruptions using a top-down decoder. Our approach
670   differs from theirs in that we exploits the capabilities of the human brain visual system by introduc-
671   ing brain-like representations for agents to enhance robustness.
672
673
674
      A.2   D EEP L EARNING AND B RAIN ACTIVITY I NTEGRATION
675
      Recent studies have explored techniques to enhance computer vision models by integrating neural
676
      network features with human brain activity. (Fong et al., 2018) improved image classification in
677   Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) by incorporating voxel responses from fMRI during train-
678   ing, making the decision surface of the classifier more consistent with brain representations. (Li
679   et al., 2019) regularized CNNs using cortical representations from neuroscience data, enhancing
680   robustness against adversarial attacks. (Nishida et al., 2020) introduced a brain-mediated trans-
681   fer learning (TL) method, transforming the feature representation of audiovisual input in CNNs into
682   brain representations, achieving superior performance in estimating human cognitive and behavioral
683   labels compared to standard TL. Additionally, (Dapello et al., 2020) developed VOneNet to simu-
684   late the primary visual cortex, thereby enhancing CNN classification robustness. (Fel et al., 2022)
685
      presented a neural harmonizer that aligns deep neural networks with human visual strategies, result-
      ing in improved classification accuracy. More recently, (Liu et al., 2023) proposed a brain-machine
686
      coupled learning method that utilized visual images and electroencephalogram (EEG) signals for
687
      training models in facial emotion recognition, demonstrating improved generalization. (Shah et al.,
688   2024) developed an image transform that simulates peripheral vision, boosting DNN robustness
689   against adversarial attacks. These studies indicate that combining neural network features with
690   human brain activity can yield improvements. However, all of these works focus on image classifi-
691   cation tasks, while our approach tackles the more complex domain of embodied visual navigation,
692   a topic that has not been extensively explored. Furthermore, we leverage a much larger and higher-
693   quality fMRI dataset.
