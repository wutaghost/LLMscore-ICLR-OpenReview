009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Scalable robot learning in the real world is limited by the cost and safety issues
012            of real robots. In addition, rolling out robot trajectories in the real world can
013            be time-consuming and labor-intensive. In this paper, we propose to learn an
014            interactive world model for robot manipulation as an alternative. We present a
015            novel method, Mani-WM, which leverages the power of generative models to
016
               generate realistic videos of a robot arm executing a given action trajectory, starting
               from an initial given frame. Mani-WM employs a novel frame-level conditioning
017
               technique to ensure precise alignment between actions and video frames and
018
               leverages a diffusion transformer for high-quality video generation. To validate the
019            effectiveness of Mani-WM, we perform extensive experiments on four challenging
020            real-robot datasets. Results show that Mani-WM outperforms all the comparing
021            baseline methods and is more preferable in human evaluations. We further showcase
022            the flexible action controllability of Mani-WM by controlling the virtual robots in
023            datasets with trajectories 1) predicted by an autonomous policy and 2) collected by
024            a keyboard or VR controller. Finally, we combine Mani-WM with model-based
025            planning to showcase its usefulness on real-robot manipulation tasks. We hope that
026            Mani-WM can serve as an effective and scalable approach to enhance robot learning
027
               in the real world. To promote research on manipulation world models, we open-
               source the code at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/Mani-WM.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The field of embodied AI has witnessed remarkable progress in recent years. Real robots are now
033   able to complete a wide variety of manipulation tasks (Zitkovich et al., 2023). However, real robots
034   are costly, unsafe, and require regular maintenance which may restrict scalable learning in the
035   real world. And rolling out robot trajectories in the real world can be time-consuming and labor-
036   intensive, although it is necessary for model evaluation. While efforts have been made to create
037   powerful physical simulators (Mittal et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024), they are still not visually realistic
038   enough. Additionally, they are not scalable because building new environments in simulation requires
039
      significant effort. What if we can create an interactive world model that simulates robot trajectories in
      a way that is accurate and visually indistinguishable from the real world? With such a model, agents
040
      can interactively control virtual robots to manipulate diverse objects in various scenes and perform
041
      model-based planning by imagining the outcomes of different proposed candidate trajectories.
042
043   Recent advances in generative models showcase extraordinary performance in generating realistic
044   texts (Achiam et al., 2023), images (Rombach et al., 2022), and videos (Brooks et al., 2024). Inspired
045
      by these successes, we propose to leverage generative models in building an interactive world model
      for robot manipulation in the real world. To this end, we propose Mani-WM, a novel method that
046
      generates high-fidelity videos of a robot executing an action trajectory, starting from a given initial
047
      frame (Fig. 1). We refer to this task as the trajectory-to-video task. The trajectory-to-video task
048   differs from the general text-to-video task in several ways. While various videos can meet the text
049   condition in the text-to-video task, the predicted video in our trajectory-to-video task must strictly
050   and accurately follow the input trajectory. More importantly, a challenge of this task is that each
051   action in the trajectory provides an exact description of the robot’s movement in each frame. This
052   contrasts with the text-to-video task, where textual descriptions offer a general condition without
053   specific frame-by-frame details. Another challenge is that the trajectory-to-video task features rich
      robot-object interactions, which must adhere to physical laws. For instance, when the robot picks

097      more preferable in human evaluations.
098    • We validate the usefulness of Mani-WM in the real world by conducting real-robot experiments on

102   2   R ELATED W ORK
103
104   World Models. Learning a world model (or dynamics model) (LeCun, 2022; Ha & Schmid-
105   huber, 2018), which predicts future observations based on current observations and actions, has
106   recently become increasingly popular (Tian et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023; Bruce et al., 2024). Prior
107   works (Babaeizadeh et al., 2021; Gupta et al., 2023) train action-conditioned video prediction
      models for planning on BAIR (Ebert et al., 2017) and RoboNet (Dasari et al., 2020) datasets.


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      DreamerV3 (Hafner et al., 2023) and DayDreamer (Wu et al., 2023) leverage recurrent state space
109   model (RSSMs) (Hafner et al., 2019) to learn a latent representation of states by modeling a world
110   model for reinforcement learning. iVideoGPT (Wu et al., 2024) trains an autoregressive transformer
111   for action-conditioned video prediction. VLP (Du et al., 2024) exploits text-to-video models as
112   dynamics models to generate video plans for robots. Mani-WM differs from previous works in that it
113   is able to generate high-resolution (up to 288 × 512) and long-horizon videos (up to 150+ frames),
114   enabling accurate and flexible world modeling for robot manipulation.
115
116   Video Models. Video models generate video frames either unconditionally or with conditions
117   including classes, initial frames, texts, strokes, and/or actions (Finn et al., 2016; Ma et al., 2024; Bao
118   et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). Recently, diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020) are becoming more
119
      and more popular in video generation (Ho et al., 2022; He et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; Brooks
      et al., 2024). A popular choice of architecture is U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) which has also
120
      been widely used in image diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022). Sora (Brooks et al., 2024)
121
      showcases extraordinary video generation capability with Diffusion Transformers (Peebles & Xie,
122   2023). Mani-WM also leverages Diffusion Transformers as the backbone. A relevant line of work is
123   to control video synthesis with motions. These methods use either user-specified strokes (Yin et al.,
124   2023; Chen et al., 2023), bounding boxes (Wang et al., 2024), or human poses (Wang et al., 2023; Xu
125   et al., 2023) as conditions. In contrast, Mani-WM seeks to model complex 3D real-world actions in
126   the video via learning a world model for robot manipulation.
127
128   Scaling Real-World Robot Learning. Rolling out policies in the real world is essential in scaling
129   up robot learning. Firstly, it is necessary for model evaluation (Zitkovich et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024).
130   Scaling up real-world evaluation would necessitate building and maintaining a large number of robots.
131
      To tackle this challenge, recent work (Li et al., 2024) shows a correlation between evaluation in a
      physical simulator and on real robots. Secondly, as real-robot data are scarce for the reason that
132
      data collection often requires costly human demonstrations, an alternative is to roll out a policy
133
      to collect data (e.g., dataset augmentation (Ross et al., 2011; Jang et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023)).
134   Finally, real-robot reinforcement learning requires rolling out robots in the real world to collect
135   trajectories (Levine et al., 2016; Kalashnikov et al., 2018; 2021). However, policy rollout in the real
136   world is time-consuming. And human supervision is often needed to ensure safety which can be
137   labor-intensive. World models are considered a promising solution to these three challenges (Monas
138   & Jang, 2024; Yu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024). Our method aims to build a world model for robot
139   manipulation to serve as an efficient and scalable alternative for real-world policy rollout.

186   3.3   M ANI -WM
187
188   Mani-WM is a conditional diffusion model operating in the latent space of the VAE introduced
189   in Sec. 3.2. The condition c consists of the latent representation of the initial frame of a video,
190
      z1 = Enc(I1 ), and an action trajectory, aN −1 The diffusion target is the latent representations of the
      subsequent N − 1 frames of the video in which the robot executes the action trajectory, i.e. x = z2:N .
191
      Inspired by Sora’s remarkable capability of understanding the physical world (Brooks et al., 2024), we
192
      similarly adopt Diffusion Transformers (DiT) (Peebles & Xie, 2023) as the backbone of Mani-WM. In
193   the design of Mani-WM, we aim to address three key aspects: 1) consistency with the initial frame 2)
194   adherence to the given action trajectory and 3) computation efficiency. In the following, we describe
195   details of Mani-WM and discuss pivotal design choices to achieve the aforementioned objectives.
196
197   Tokenization. Each latent representation zi = Enc(Ii ) contains P tokens of D dimensions, where
198   P denotes the number of patches per frame. By sequencing the latent representations of all frames by
199   timestep order, the video is tokenized to N × P tokens. Spatial and temporal positional embeddings
200   are added to the tokens to allow awareness of patch positions within frames and timesteps in the
201   video, respectively. The VAE is frozen throughout the training process.
202
203   Spatial-Temporal Attention Blocks. Standard transformer blocks apply Multi-Head Self-Attention
204   (MHA) to all tokens in the input token sequence, resulting in quadratic computation cost. We thus
205
      leverage the memory-efficient spatial-temporal attention mechanism (Xu et al., 2020; Bruce et al.,
      2024; Ma et al., 2024) in the transformer block of Mani-WM to reduce the computation cost (Fig. 2).
206
      Specifically, each block consists of a spatial attention block and a temporal attention block. In the
207
      spatial attention block, MHA is confined to tokens within a frame to model intra-frame interaction. In
208   the temporal attention block, MHA is confined to tokens at an identical patch position across all the
209   frames to model inter-frame interaction. For a sequence of N × P tokens, spatial attention operates
210   on the 1 × P tokens within each frame; temporal attention operates on the N × 1 tokens across
211   the N timesteps. Compared to attending over all the N × P tokens at a time, the spatial-temporal
212   attention greatly decreases the computation cost which makes generating long and high-resolution
213   videos feasible.
214
215   Initial Frame Condition. The initial frame condition is achieved by treating the initial frame as the
      ground-truth portion in the input video sequence (Brooks et al., 2024). That is, during training, we

247    • Video-Level Condition. Similar to using a text embedding to condition the generation of the
248
         entire video in the text-to-video task, we use a linear layer to encode the trajectory into a single
         embedding for condition. The embedding is then added to the embedding of the diffusion
249
         timestep t for generating the scale parameters γ and α and the shift parameters β for each spatial
250
         and temporal attention block. These parameters control the video generation via shifting the
251      distribution of the token embeddings in the transformer block. The overall framework is shown in
252      Figure 2(c). See Appendix C.1 for more details.
253
254
       • Frame-Level Condition. Unlike the text-to-video task where the text describes the entire video,
         the trajectory in the trajectory-to-video task is a finer description. Each action in the trajectory
255
         defines how the robot should move in each frame. And thus, each generated frame must match
256
         with its corresponding action in the trajectory. To achieve this precise frame-level alignment, we
257      condition the generation of each frame by its corresponding action. Instead of encoding the action
258      trajectory into a single embedding, we use a linear layer to encode each action into an individual
259      embedding. The diffusion timestep embedding is added to each action embedding to generate
260      the scale and shift parameters for each individual frame in the spatial block. The scale and shift
261      parameters of the temporal block for all frames share the same conditioning embedding which is
262      derived similarly as in video-level condition. See Appendix C.2 for more details.

306   4     E XPERIMENTS
307
308   In this section, we perform extensive experiments on four challenging real-robot datasets and a real
309   robot. We aim to answer four questions: 1) Is Mani-WM effective on solving the trajectory-to-video
310   task on various datasets with different action spaces? 2) How do different components contribute
311   to the performance of Mani-WM? 3) How is the action controllability of Mani-WM? Can it handle
312   diverse trajectories from humans and policies? 4) Can Mani-WM be used for model-based planning?
313
314   4.1   E XPERIMENT S ETUP
315
      We conduct primary experiments on three high-quality robot manipulation datasets: RT-1 (Brohan
316
      et al., 2023), Bridge (Walke et al., 2023), and Language-Table (Lynch et al., 2023). Additionally,
317
      we follow iVideoGPT (Wu et al., 2024) to perform experiments on RoboNet (Dasari et al., 2020)
318   to compare with more existing baselines. The action space for RT-1 and Bridge consists of 7 DoF,
319   while the Language-Table features 2 DoF. RobotNet is a mixed dataset with a maximum of 5 DoF.
320   More details about the dataset statistics and action space are shown in the Appendix B. For RT-1,
321   Bridge, and Language-Table during training, we sample video clips containing 16 continuous frames
322   from episodes using a sliding window. For RoboNet, we follow Wu et al. (2024) and use 2 frames as
323   context to predict the next 10 frames. We resize videos, and the resolutions after resizing for RT-1,
      Bridge, Language-Table and RoboNet are 256×320, 256×320, 288×512 and 256× 256, respectively.


                                                       6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      For RT-1, Bridge and Language-Table, we perform experiments on video generation on short
325   trajectories and long trajectories. Short trajectories, which are segments of complete episodes,
326   consist of 16 frames and 15 actions. The video can be generated in one diffusion generation process.
327   For long trajectories, we utilize complete episodes from the dataset. Long videos can be rolled out in
328   an autoregressive manner. The initial frame of the first diffusion process is the given ground-truth
329   frame, while the initial frame of each subsequent diffusion process is the last output frame from the
330   previous process.
331
332   Mani-WM Variants. We follow standard transformers which scale the hidden size, number of
333   heads, and number of layers together. In particular, we perform experiments on four configurations:
334   Mani-WM-S, Mani-WM-B, Mani-WM-L, and Mani-WM-XL. Details of these models are shown in
335
      Tab. 9 in Appendix E. If not specified otherwise, throughout the paper, we report the results of Mani-
      WM-XL which contains 679M trainable parameters in total. We denote Mani-WM with frame-level
336
      and video-level adaptation as Mani-WM-Frame-Ada and Mani-WM-Video-Ada, respectively.
337
338
      Baselines. To evaluate the effectiveness of Mani-WM, we first compare it with two state-of-the-art
339   methods, i.e., VDM (Ho et al., 2022) and LVDM (He et al., 2023). Both methods are diffusion models
340   based on a U-Net architecture, in contrast to Mani-WM, which employs a Transformer architecture.
341   LVDM diffuses videos in a latent space, while VDM operates in the pixel space. These methods
342   demonstrate strong capabilities in the text-to-video task. To impose trajectory conditions on video
343   generation, we encode the trajectory into an embedding to condition the diffusion process in both
344   methods. This is similar to the text embedding used for text-to-video generation in the original
345   papers (Ho et al., 2022; He et al., 2023). LVDM is configured such that its number of parameters is
346   similar to Mani-WM. As VDM performs diffusions in the pixel space, it requires more computational
347
      resources than LVDM and Mani-WM despite having only 44M parameters. Additionally, we compare
      Mani-WM with existing non-diffusion methods on the RoboNet dataset, including iVideoGPT (Wu
348
      et al., 2024), which autoregressively predicts the next visual token, and MaskViT (Gupta et al., 2023),
349
      which generates all visual tokens via iterative refinement. More details can be found in Appendix D.
350
351   Metrics. Following Xu et al. (2023), we evaluate with two types of metrics: computation-based and
352   model-based. Computation-based metrics includes PSNR (Horé & Ziou, 2010) and SSIM (Wang et al.,
353   2004). Model-based metrics includes Latent L2 loss, FID (Heusel et al., 2017) and FVD (Unterthiner
354   et al., 2019). Unlike the text-to-video task where a variety of videos may meet with a single text
355   condition, the variety is much smaller in the trajectory-to-video task as the robot in the predicted
356   video must strictly follow the input trajectory. Thus, we prioritize the Latent L2 loss and PSNR as
357   primary evaluation metrics and provide other metrics for reference. In Sec. 4.2, we will later show
358   that Latent L2 loss and PSNR match with human preference the most among all the metrics. More
359
      details about evaluation can be found in Appendix F.

509   Model-based Planning. We conduct a real-robot model-based planning experiment to show the
510   usefulness of Mani-WM on three manipulation tasks. We leverage a goal-conditioned method which
511   specifies the task with a goal image. In particular, we first sample a set of candidate trajectories. We
512
      then use Mani-WM to imagine the visual outcomes of these trajectories and compare them with the
      goal image via a cost function. The cost function evaluates the similarities between the goal image
513
      and a predicted video. The lower the cost, the higher the similarity. The robot rollouts the trajectory
514
      with the lowest cost to complete the task. Qualitative results are shown in Fig. 7. Quantitative results
515   are shown in Tab. 4. We experiment with two cost functions for similarity comparison: 1) mean
516   squared error (MSE) and 2) cosine similarity of the feature extracted from ResNet50. We observe that
517   the MSE cost function significantly outperformed the ResNet cost function, and both significantly
518   outperform the policy which randomly selects a trajectory for rollout. These results demonstrate
519   the potential of Mani-WM as a manipulation world model for model-based planning by accurately
520   predicting the visual outcomes of rolling out different trajectories. More details and discussion can
521   be found in the Appendix G.

      5    C ONCLUSION , L IMITATIONS , AND F UTURE W ORK
525
526   In this paper, we present Mani-WM, a novel method that generates videos of a robot executing
527   an action trajectory given the initial frame. Results show that Mani-WM is able to generate long-
528   horizon and high-resolution videos that are almost visually indistinguishable from ground-truth
529   videos. Additionally, we highlight the flexible action controllability of Mani-WM and its capability
530
      for model-based planning.
531   Similar to other generative models, a limitation of Mani-WM is hallucinations. The hallucinations
532   primarily manifest as violations of physical laws. We believe that an effective way to address this issue
533   is to increase the model size, data volume, and the number of training steps. Additionally, although
534   Mani-WM achieves high throughput with only 8 GB of memory during inference, its inference speed
535   is not real-time. Finally, Mani-WM currently does not support flexible input resolutions, limiting its
536
      ability to fully utilize robot data of different resolutions.
537   In future work, we will investigate accelerating the inference speed using methods such as diffusion
538   distillation (Meng et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2024). Additionally, we plan to explore leveraging Mani-
539   WM as a robot manipulation world model for: 1) policy evaluation (Monas & Jang, 2024); 2)
      Improving policies via methods such as DAgger (Ross et al., 2011) and RL (Yang et al., 2024).

1093   Dataset Statistics. We provide details on the four publicly available robot manipulation datasets:
1094   RT-1 (Brohan et al., 2023), Bridge (Walke et al., 2023), Language-Table (Lynch et al., 2023) and
1095   RoboNet (Dasari et al., 2020). A summary of the dataset statistics is presented in Table 5. For RT-1,
1096   Bridge and Language-Table, each training sample consists of a 4-second video clip containing 16
1097   frames, extracted from an episode with a continuous sliding window. For testing and validation,
1098   frames are sampled at 16-frame intervals to reduce the number of evaluation videos and, consequently,
1099
       lower evaluation costs. The original resolution for RT-1 is 256 × 320, for Bridge it is 480 × 640, and
       for Language-Table it is 360 × 640. To ensure efficient training, we resize the Bridge videos to a
1100
       resolution of 256 × 320 and the Language-Table videos to 288 × 512. For RoboNet, we follow Wu
1101
       et al. (2024) and use 2 frames as context to predict the next 10 frames at a resolution of 256 × 256.
1102   Note that the mentioned "our own dataset" in Sec. 4.2 is similar in size to RT-1, and the action space
1103   is the same.
1104
1105
       Action Space. Different datasets have different action spaces. In RT-1 and Bridge, a robot arm with
1106   a gripper moves in the 3D space to perform manipulation which interacts with objects in the scene.
1107   The action spaces of RT-1 and Bridge consist of 1) 6-DoF arm actions in 3D space, T ∈ SE(3), and
1108   2) continuous gripper actions, g ∈ [0, 1]. In Language-Table, a robot arm moves in a 2D plane to
1109   move blocks with a cylindrical end-effector. The action space of Language-Table is 2-DoF translation
1110   in 2D space, p ∈ R2 . We convert the arm action of all datasets to relative delta actions. Specifically,
1111   we specify the action of RT-1 and Bridge with a 7-dim vector, i.e., a = [∆x, ∆y, ∆z, ∆α, ∆β, ∆γ, g]
1112   where ∆x, ∆y, and ∆z are the delta XYZ position; ∆α, ∆β, and ∆γ are the delta Euler angles; g
1113   indicates the gripper joint-angle position in the next step. For Language-Table, we specify the action
1114
       with a 2-dim vector, i.e., a = [∆x, ∆y] which indicates the delta position in the xy-plane. RoboNet
       is a large-scale robot manipulation dataset featuring 7 robot platforms with varying action spaces (2,
1115
       4, or 5 dimensions). Following Dasari et al. (2020), to unify the data, a 5-dimensional vector is used
1116
       to represent a universal action space, padding zeros for missing dimensions. This vector represents
1117   delta XYZ position, delta yaw angle, and gripper joint-angle value: a = [∆x, ∆y, ∆z, ∆γ, g]. For
1118   instance, if a robot doesn’t control the z-axis, ∆z is set to 0.

1167

1168

1169

1171   the video to 144 × 256 in the training of VDM to make the computational cost affordable. During
1172   evaluation, we resize the generated video back to 288 × 512 for comparison with other methods.

1174   hyperparameters for VDM and LVDM are shown in Tab. 6 and 7. More training hyperparameters
1175   that share with Mani-WM can be found in Tab. 8.

1217
1218   Evaluation Metrics. Latent L2 loss and PSNR measure the L2 distance between the predicted
1219   video and the ground-truth video in the latent space and pixel space, respectively. SSIM evaluates the
1220   similarity between videos in terms of image brightness, contrast, and structure. FID and FVD assess
1221   video quality by analyzing the similarity of video feature distributions.
1222
1223   Evaluation Setup. We evaluate the video quality generated by Mani-WM and the baselines under
1224   two settings: short trajectories and long trajectories. In the short trajectory setting, the input consists
1225   of one initial frame and a short trajectory containing 15 actions, resulting in the generation of 15
1226   subsequent frames. These short trajectories are sampled from episodes using a sliding window with
1227   an interval of 16. In the long trajectory setting, the input comprises one initial frame and a complete
1228
       long trajectory, with the output being the generated subsequent frames. The average lengths of the
       long trajectories are 42.5, 33.4, and 23.7 frames for RT-1, Bridge, and Language-Table, respectively.
1229
       These lengths also represent the average number of frames for the generated long videos, which are
1230
       produced in an autoregressive manner, as detailed in Sec. 4.1. The statistics of the generated short
1231   and long videos used for evaluation are presented in Tab. 5.
1232
1233

1283   In this section, we detail the real-robot model-based planning experiment. The experiment demon-
1284   strates that Mani-WM can effectively plan trajectories to finish manipulation tasks by generating the
1285
       outcomes of executing different candidate trajectories.
1286
1287
       Experiment Setup. We follow Babaeizadeh et al. (2021) to set up this experiment. We implement
       a model-based policy to show the usefulness of Mani-WM. Our policy consists of a sampling-based
1288
       planner, a cost function, and Mani-WM as the dynamic function. We first train Mani-WM with our
1289
       own real robot dataset. The input of our policy includes the initial image, the initial position of
1290   the end-effector, and a goal image to indicate the task. The output is a predicted trajectory. We
1291   use a simple sampling-based planner to generate candidate trajectories. The planner samples 50
1292   individual points from a circle centered on the initial end-effector position and then generates a
1293   trajectory between the initial position and each sampled point, resulting in 50 different candidate
1294   trajectories. We input the initial image and each trajectory to Mani-WM to generate the video of
1295   executing each trajectory. We use a cost function to calculate the similarity between each predicted
       video and the goal image. We experiment with 2 cost functions: 1) mean squared error (MSE) and 2)


                                                        24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       cosine similarity of the feature extracted from ResNet50. We execute the top 5 trajectories with the
1297   lowest cost (i.e., the predicted video most similar to the goal image) in the real world and calculate
1298   the average success rate. The experiment is repeated three times for each task.
1299
1300   Results Qualitative results are shown in Fig. 7. Quantitative results are shown in Tab. 4. We
1301   compare our method with a baseline that randomly picks a trajectory from the 50 candidates. The
1302   results show that using Mani-WM significantly increases the success rate compared to the random
1303   baseline.
1304
1305   Discussion About Cost Function. We also explore how different cost functions impact the model’s
1306   performance. We find that the MSE cost function is generally superior to the ResNet cost function.
1307   But the MSE cost function is not always perfect; sometimes it selects incorrect prediction videos,
1308   leading to task failure. This suggests that we need to explore better cost functions in future work,
1309
       considering that the success rate is influenced by both the accuracy of video prediction and the
       accuracy of the cost function. A suboptimal cost function could affect the evaluation of the video
1310
       prediction model, as also mentioned by iVideoGPT (Wu et al., 2024) and VLMPC (Zhao et al., 2024).
1311
1312
       Discussion About Sample Policy. Although we use a simple sampling-based planner as the sample
1313   policy in this experiment, we note that Mani-WM can be combined with any policy that has trajectory
1314   sampling capabilities (i.e., action chunk techniques (Chi et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023)). The
1315   performance and range of tasks that Mani-WM can handle could be further enhanced by adopting
1316   a more advanced policy (Chi et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023), which is capable of generating more
1317   precise and complex trajectories.
