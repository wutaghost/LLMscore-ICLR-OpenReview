                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Data attribution quantifies the influence of individual training data points on ma-
010            chine learning models, aiding in their interpretation and improvement. While prior
011            work has primarily focused on single-task learning (STL), this work extends data
012            attribution to multitask learning (MTL). Data attribution in MTL presents new
013            opportunities for interpreting and improving MTL models while also introducing
014            unique technical challenges. On the opportunity side, data attribution in MTL
015            offers a natural way to efficiently measure task relatedness, a key factor that im-
016            pacts the effectiveness of MTL. However, the shared and task-specific parameters
017            in MTL models present challenges that require specialized data attribution meth-
018
               ods. In this paper, we propose the MultiTask Influence Function (MTIF), a data
               attribution framework tailored for MTL. MTIF leverages the parameter structure
019
               of MTL models to derive influence functions that distinguish between within-task
020
               and cross-task influences. Our derivation also sheds light on the applicability
021            of popular approximation techniques for influence function computation, such as
022            EK-FAC and LiSSA, in the MTL setting. Compared to conventional task related-
023            ness measurements, MTIF provides not only task-level relatedness but also data-
024            level influence analysis. The latter enables fine-grained interpretations of task
025            relatedness and facilitates a data selection strategy to effectively mitigate negative
026            transfer in MTL. Extensive experiments on both linear and neural network models
027            show that MTIF effectively approximates leave-one-out and leave-one-task-out ef-
028            fects while offering interpretable insights into task relatedness. Moreover, the data
029
               selection strategy enabled by MTIF consistently improves model performance in
               MTL. Our work establishes a novel connection between data attribution and MTL,
030
               offering an efficient and scalable solution for measuring task relatedness and en-
031
               hancing MTL models.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
      Data attribution aims to quantify the influence of individual training data points on machine learning
036   models and has been widely used to interpret and improve these models (Koh & Liang, 2017; Ham-
037   moudeh & Lowd, 2024). However, most existing literature on data attribution focuses on single-task
038   learning (STL) settings. In contrast, this work explores data attribution in the context of multitask
039   learning (MTL), where multiple related tasks are trained simultaneously to enhance overall perfor-
040   mance (Caruana, 1997). Data attribution in MTL presents new opportunities for interpreting and
041   improving MTL, with distinct technical challenges in comparison to data attribution in STL.
042   MTL has demonstrated success across a wide range of domains, including computer vision (Za-
043   mir et al., 2018), natural language processing (Hashimoto et al., 2017), speech processing (Huang
044   et al., 2015), and recommender systems (Ma et al., 2018). In practice, however, MTL does not al-
045   ways help with the overall performance—training unrelated tasks together often harms the learning
046   performance, a phenomenon known as negative transfer (Standley et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020;
047   Parisotto et al., 2016; Rusu et al., 2016). As a result, understanding and quantifying task relatedness
048   has become a key focus in MTL research (Ma et al., 2018; Standley et al., 2020; Fifty et al., 2021).
049   Despite this, there is still no consensus on a universally effective and efficient method for measur-
050
      ing task relatedness. In practical applications, practitioners often rely on trial and error—repeatedly
      training models with different task combinations—as a gold standard to assess task relatedness, a
051
      process that is computationally expensive.
052
053   Generalizing data attribution methods to MTL offers a promising, efficient, and interpretable way to
      measure task relatedness in MTL. Many data attribution methods are designed to efficiently approx-


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      imate the change of model performance when retraining the model with certain data points excluded
055   from the training dataset (Koh & Liang, 2017; Park et al., 2023). Extending these methods to MTL
056   naturally leads to an efficient approximation of the aforementioned trial-and-error process for deter-
057   mining task relatedness. Moreover, data attribution methods allow for fine-grained analysis at the
058   individual data instance level, revealing how data points from one task impact performance on an-
059   other task. This data-level influence analysis offers more interpretable insights into task relatedness
060   by moving beyond a single metric, providing concrete evidence of how tasks are related through
061   specific data points and their cross-task effects.
062   However, MTL introduces unique challenges that require tailored data attribution methods. MTL
063   models typically consist of both shared parameters across all tasks and task-specific parameters for
064   each individual task. When making predictions for a specific task, only a submodel with a subset
065   of the parameters is utilized. As the number of tasks increases, this brings several computational
066   challenges for data attribution. Firstly, since each task corresponds to a separate attribution tar-
067   get, retraining-based data attribution methods (Ghorbani & Zou, 2019; Jia et al., 2019) become
068   prohibitively expensive. Therefore, in this paper, we focus on influence function (IF)-based data
069
      attribution methods that do not require repeated retraining. Additionally, tasks in MTL may em-
      ploy different loss functions, and the number of parameters scales with the number of tasks, further
070
      complicating the application of existing IF-based data attribution methods designed for STL. These
071
      factors present significant technical challenges when adapting such methods to the MTL setting.
072
073   In this paper, we propose the MultiTask Influence Function (MTIF) to address these challenges.
074   Similar to the IF-based data attribution methods for STL (Koh & Liang, 2017), MTIF leverages a
075
      first-order approximation to efficiently estimate the impact of removing a data point from one task
      on the prediction for another task, without the need for model retraining. Specifically designed for
076
      MTL, MTIF derives the influence of data points on the shared and task-specific parameters sepa-
077
      rately, and exploits the unique structure of MTL models to enhance computational efficiency. MTIF
078   enables the efficient estimation of both data-level and task-level influence, providing a scalable and
079   interpretable solution for data attribution in MTL settings.
080
081
      We conduct extensive experiments on both linear and neural network models to evaluate the effec-
      tiveness of the proposed MTIF. On linear models, the data-level influence scores predicted by MTIF
082
      shows a near perfect correlation with the actual change of model outputs obtained by brute-force
083
      leave-one-out retraining; the task-level influence estimated by MTIF also strongly correlates with
084   the leave-one-task-out retraining, with an average Pearson correlation around 0.7. On neural net-
085   work models, the task-level influence estimated by MTIF also shows significant correlation with
086   leave-one-task-out retraining, with Pearson correlation ranging from 0.1 to 0.4. Moreover, the data-
087   level influence estimated by MTIF enables fine-grained data selection for MTL, which demonstrates
088   consistent performance improvements over baselines. Finally, we provide case studies of the most
089   negative data points from one task to another task, providing interpretations about negative transfer.
090   We summarize the contributions of this study as following. Firstly, we establish a novel connec-
091   tion between data attribution and MTL, where the former can be naturally employed to efficiently
092   measure task relatedness, a key concept in MTL. Secondly, we propose a scalable data attribu-
093   tion method, MTIF, that addresses unique challenges of data attribution in MTL and provides fine-
094   grained influence analysis. Finally, we demonstrate the effectiveness and practical usefulness of the
095   proposed method through extensive experiments.

      Data Attribution Data attribution methods quantify the influence of individual training data points
100
      on model performance. These methods can be broadly categorized into retraining-based and
101
      gradient-based approaches (Hammoudeh & Lowd, 2024). Retraining-based methods (Ghorbani &
102   Zou, 2019; Jia et al., 2019; Kwon & Zou, 2022; Wang & Jia, 2023; Ilyas et al., 2022) require retrain-
103   ing the model multiple times on different subsets of the training data. Retraining-based methods are
104   usually computationally expensive due to the repeated retraining. The computation cost can be fur-
105   ther exacerbated in MTL due to the combination of tasks. Gradient-based methods (Koh & Liang,
106   2017; Guo et al., 2021; Barshan et al., 2020; Schioppa et al., 2022; Kwon et al., 2024; Yeh et al.,
107   2018; Pruthi et al., 2020; Park et al., 2023) instead rely on the (higher-order) gradient information
      of the original model to estimate the data influence, which are more efficient. Many gradient-based


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      methods can be viewed as variants of IF-based data attribution methods (Koh & Liang, 2017). In
109   this paper, we develop an IF-based data attribution method tailored for the MTL settings.
110
111   Task Relatedness in Multitask Learning Quantifying task relatedness has been a central focus
112   in multitask learning. Broadly, two lines of work address this topic. The first focuses on task
113
      grouping or task selection, aiming to develop methods for grouping or selecting positively related
      tasks to improve prediction performance. Standley et al. (2020) and Li et al. (2023) introduced
114
      task selection methods based on model retraining, which are less efficient than our method. Fifty
115
      et al. (2021) proposed an efficient method for calculating heuristic pairwise task affinities, but their
116   estimator heavily depends on the training trajectory, which limits its interpretability. Additionally,
117   Wu et al. (2020) incorporated task data covariance to estimate task similarity, though their work
118   is restricted to specific types of models. Another line of research focuses on developing advanced
119   training algorithms for MTL by explicitly accounting for inter-task relations during training. These
120   methods generally fall into two categories. The first category manipulates per-task gradients to
121   mitigate negative influences between tasks (Yu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021a;
122   Chen et al., 2020; Peng et al., 2024). The second category employs task reweighting techniques to
123   balance the contribution of each task or to emphasize on critical tasks (Chen et al., 2018; Liu et al.,
124
      2019; Guo et al., 2018; Kendall et al., 2018). Beyond these two categories, Duan & Wang (2023)
      proposed a family of methods that automatically leverage task similarities to improve multitask
125
      learning. These approaches are orthogonal to our method and can be potentially combined with the
126
      data and task selection enabled by our method. There is also a body of work on task relatedness in
127   transfer learning (Zamir et al., 2018; Achille et al., 2021; Dwivedi & Roig, 2019; Zhuang et al., 2021;
128   Achille et al., 2019). However, Standley et al. (2020) demonstrated that task similarity metrics in
129   transfer learning do not generalize well to the multitask learning domain. Finally, various works have
130   tackled negative transfer in MTL from different perspectives. Yang et al. (2019) propose learning
131   latent task representations and utilizing a block-diagonal latent task assignment matrix to explicitly
132   structure inter-task relationships, thereby mitigating inter-group negative transfer. Meng et al. (2021)
133   reformulate the MTL problem as a multi-teacher knowledge distillation framework, introducing a
134   two-stage optimization process that alleviates negative transfer. Zhou et al. (2023a) address negative
135
      transfer by introducing the Feature Decomposition Network (FDN), which separates features into
      task-specific and task-shared components to reduce feature redundancy. These works address related
136
      topics and are complementary to ours, included here for completeness in reviewing the literature.

141   As a widely used data attribution metric, the leave-one-out (LOO) effect measures the contribution
142   of a training data point by the change of model performance after removing this data point and
143   retraining the model (Koh & Liang, 2017; Schioppa et al., 2022; Grosse et al., 2023). However,
144   repeatedly retraining the model can be computationally extensive. To address this issue, in the
145
      single-task learning (STL) setting, Koh & Liang (2017) proposed the use of influence functions,
      which approximate the LOO effect by leveraging small perturbations to the weight of the loss at
146
      each data point.
147

174   While IF-based data attribution has been shown as a scalable and effective tool for many applica-
175   tions, it has been primarily developed for STL settings, where a single model is trained on a homo-
176   geneous task. However, in many real-world applications, multiple related tasks are learned jointly,
177   with shared parameters across tasks and different evaluation metrics of interest. In this section, we
178   extend IF-based data attribution to the multitask learning (MTL) setting.

180   4.1   P ROBLEM S ETUP FOR M ULTITASK DATA ATTRIBUTION
181
      Multitask Learning. MTL aims to solve multiple tasks simultaneously. In many real-world sce-
182
      narios, tasks are often related and share common underlying structures. MTL leverages shared
183
      structures by jointly training tasks to enhance generalization and improve prediction accuracy, es-
184   pecially when tasks are related or when data for individual tasks is limited. A common approach in
185   MTL to facilitate information sharing across tasks is through either soft or hard parameter sharing
186   (Ruder, 2017). In soft parameter sharing, regularization is applied to the task-specific parameters to
187   encourage them to be similar across tasks (Xue et al., 2007; Duong et al., 2015). In contrast, hard
188   parameter sharing learns a common feature representation through shared parameters, while task-
189   specific parameters are used to make predictions tailored to each task (Caruana, 1997). Recently,
190   Duan & Wang (2023) proposed an augmented optimization framework for MTL that accommodates
191   both hard parameter sharing and various types of soft parameter sharing.
192   We consider a general MTL objective that incorporates these common parameter-sharing schemes.

319   (George et al., 2018; Grosse et al., 2023) and LiSSA (Agarwal et al., 2017; Koh & Liang, 2017),

323   EK-FAC approximates the Hessian inverse using a blockwise diagonal matrix by treating different
      layers of a neural network as independent. For many popular MTL models (e.g., the shared-bottom

326   as 0 for all k = 1, . . . , K. However, as shown in Proposition 1, this approximation can result
327   in the loss of significant contributions to within-task influence, between-task influence, and shared
328   influence in MTL due to omitting the terms involving Hk,K+1 and HK+1,k . For instance, under

                                   li
330   between-task influence score calculations. This limitation is particularly impactful in soft parameter
331   sharing models, where the influence from one task to another will be completely lost.

333
      LiSSA approximates the inverse-Hessian-vector-product using an iterative algorithm that supports
      mini-batch gradients. While LiSSA has potential applicability in the MTL setting, its convergence
334
      heavily depends on the condition number of the empirical Hessian evaluated on each mini-batch
335
      (Agarwal et al., 2017). The condition number of a matrix, defined as the ratio of its largest eigenvalue
336   to its smallest eigenvalue, quantifies the matrix’s ill-posedness. In the MTL setting, each data point
337   is associated with a specific task k, influencing the shared parameters and only the task-specific
338   parameters for task k. Consequently, the empirical Hessian for a single data point contains non-zero
339   entries only in the Hk,k , Hk,K+1 , HK+1,k , and HK+1,K+1 blocks of Equation (7). This sparsity
340   can render the mini-batch empirical Hessian ill-posed, leading to a large condition number and
341   instability in LiSSA’s iterative approximation.
342   In Appendix C.4, we empirically demonstrate that as the number of tasks K increases, LiSSA re-
343   quires larger batch sizes in the stochastic estimation to mitigate the issues caused by a high condition
344   number. This adjustment significantly increases computational costs for large-scale MTL problems.

      5     E XPERIMENTS
379
380   Our experimental results are organized into two parts: (1) an evaluation of MTIF’s ability to ap-
381   proximate the gold-standard LOO and LOTO influences, and (2) a demonstration of its practical
382   application for data selection to improve model performance.
383   We test MTIF on two types of models: linear models, illustrated in Example 1 and discussed in
384   Section 5.1, and neural network models, described in Example 2 and discussed in Section 5.2. The
385   results highlight the following: First, our data-level influence score provides a strong approxima-
386   tion of the leave-one-out (LOO) effect in (3), as evidenced by the high degree of linearity between
387   the two measures. Second, our task-level influence scores closely approximate the gold-standard
388   LOTO influences in (4), as demonstrated by a high Spearman correlation between the ranks of task
389   contributions derived from our method and those obtained via true LOTO influences. Additionally,
390   in Appendix C.2, we compare MTIF with gradient-based baseline methods (Azorin et al., 2023;
391
      Fifty et al., 2021)for measuring task relatedness between training tasks and the target task. Our re-
      sults show that the Spearman correlation between the task-relatedness ranks estimated by baseline
392
      methods and the gold-standard LOTO ranks is consistently lower than that achieved by MTIF.
393
394   For practical applications, in Section 5.3, we show that data selection based on MTIF influence
395   scores enhances model performance across various dynamic weighting algorithms (Liu et al., 2019;
396   Chen et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2021a; Kendall et al., 2018; Lin et al., 2022) for MTL. Furthermore,
397
      in Appendix D, we present case studies demonstrating that the negative samples filtered out by MTIF

529   6   C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this work, we proposed the MultiTask Influence Function (MTIF), a novel data attribution method
532   for multitask learning (MTL). MTIF efficiently estimates the influence of individual data points on
533   task performance across multiple tasks, without the need for retraining. By leveraging the struc-
534   ture of MTL models, MTIF enables scalable and interpretable data-level and task-level influence
535   analysis.
536   Through experiments on both linear and neural network models, we demonstrated that MTIF ef-
537   fectively approximates leave-one-out and leave-one-task-out effects. Moreover, MTIF facilitates
538   fine-grained data selection, leading to consistent improvements in model performance and helping
539   mitigate negative transfer. Our approach offers a practical tool for measuring and interpreting task
      relatedness as well as improving MTL performance.

938   The synthetic dataset for multi-task linear regression is generated with m = 10 tasks, where each
939   dataset contains n = 200 samples (xji , yji ), split into training and test sets. The input vectors xji
940   are independently sampled from a normal distribution N (0, Id ) with dimensionality d = 50. The
941   response yji is generated using a linear model yji = x⊤     ⋆
                                                              ji θj +ϵji , where ϵji ∼ N (0, 1) is independent
942   noise.
943
      The coefficient vectors θj⋆ for task j are generated by starting with a common vector β ⋆ = 2e1
944   (where e1 is a unit vector) and adding random perturbations δj , sampled from a sphere with norm
945   δ. For a fraction ϵm of the tasks, θj⋆ is replaced with independent random vectors. This parame-
946   terization introduces variability in task similarity, with δ controlling the perturbation magnitude and
947   ϵ determining the fraction of unrelated tasks. For more details, we refer readers to Duan & Wang
948   (2023).
949
      To explore different task similarity scenarios, we generate datasets under varying δ and ϵ values.
950   The datasets are randomly divided into training, validation, and test sets with an 1:1:1 ratio.
