011                                                         A BSTRACT
012
013            Current embodied intelligence models often lack the ability to adjust control methods dynamically
014            in response to human intentions, limiting their effectiveness in real-world interactions. This pa-
015            per proposes a novel framework that enables robots to dynamically adapt their control parameters
016            by integrating large language models (LLMs) with intelligent controllers. Our approach simulates
017            human-robot interactions and generates synthetic training data, allowing robots to better understand
018            and respond to diverse human needs. We validate the framework using two commonly used control
019            techniques and demonstrate that it can effectively adjust control methods, such as Proportional-
020
               Integral-Derivative (PID) and Nonlinear Model Predictive Control (NMPC), based on real-time hu-
               man feedback. Experimental results show that our model enhances adaptability and responsiveness
021
               in human-robot interaction. This work advances embodied intelligence by introducing an adaptive
022
               control framework and providing a scalable method for data generation, which together enable more
023            intuitive and effective robot behaviors.

      1     INTRODUCTION
027
028
      Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have profoundly impacted various sectors within the field of
029
      artificial intelligence (AI) Raiaan et al. (2024). These models, capable of processing and generating human-like text,
030
      have paved the way for integrating AI with physical systems, particularly in robotics. The amalgamation of LLMs with
031   robotics aims to leverage their natural language processing capabilities to enhance the functionality and adaptability
032   of robots in the physical world Pfeifer & Iida (2004); Haugeland (1989); Pfeifer & Bongard (2006). This convergence
033   heralds a new era wherein the sophisticated understanding and generation of human language by LLMs are employed
034   to create intelligent agents capable of interacting with and manipulating their physical environment. These intelligent
035   agents, operating within the physical realm, are often referred to as embodied AI Turing (1950).
036
      Embodied AI represents a significant shift from traditional, disembodied AI that exists solely in cyberspace to a form
037   where AI extends its capabilities into the tangible world. This transition not only bridges the gap between digital
038   intelligence and physical action but also broadens the scope of AI applications, encompassing sim-to-real robotic
039   control, embodied agents, and embodied interaction Liu et al. (2024). Each of these areas presents unique challenges
040   and opportunities for enhancing human-robot interaction and improving the overall effectiveness of robotic systems.
041
042   1.1   RESEARCH MOTIVATION AND OBJECTIVES
043
044   Achieving embodied intelligence requires a deep understanding of both the physical dynamics of the environment
045   and the subtleties of human communication. For instance, a robot capable of embodied intelligence must be able to
046   recognize and interpret human gestures, facial expressions, and vocal tones, and then respond appropriately. This
047   level of interaction necessitates sophisticated sensory and processing capabilities, as well as advanced algorithms for
048   real-time decision-making and action execution Jin et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024); Newbury et al. (2023); Varley et al.
049   (2017); Fang et al. (2020); Xu et al. (2023); Shridhar et al. (2022); Shen et al. (2023).
050   Robot control is a multi-layered process that integrates high-level decision-making with low-level actuation to achieve
051   autonomous and efficient operation in dynamic environments. The standard hierarchical model comprises three layers:
052   decision, planning, and execution. Current state-of-the-art models typically facilitate human-robot interaction at the
053   decision or planning layers. In this paper, we propose a novel model that enables human-robot interaction at lower
      layers Figure 1, specifically at the execution layer, thus Enhancing Robots’ Empathy(ERE).

      3.4    H OW TO DEFINE EMPATHY
293
294   The proposed concept of empathy in this paper includes two aspects. First, the robot should better understand human
295   intentions and perform actions that align with human needs within its capabilities, without being restricted by any
296   pre-set parameters. Second, the agent should think from the human perspective, explicitly displaying preferences for
297   action styles, which are difficult to quantify, through curves or other forms. This enables the batch generation of
298   training data.
299
      First, current training for robot actions emphasizes whether the robot has completed a target task, such as picking
300   up an object or reaching a destination. However, the process of how the robot accomplishes the task has been largely
301   overlooked. The robot’s control methods are typically pre-defined, and the actions taken by the robot while completing
302   the task are constrained by these control parameters. The fixed parameters of the control methods limit the robot’s
303   actions to a subset of its action space. As a result, during task execution, the robot cannot dynamically adjust its control
304   methods in real-time based on user preferences. This paper proposes a method that allows robots to dynamically adjust
305   their control parameters in real-time, which differs from previous methods that adjust the reward function to alter the
306   robot’s action style. Our method can be applied directly and in real-time to the robot, allowing users to adjust the
307   robot’s action style dynamically during interactions without specialized knowledge. This has significant potential in
308
      fields such as healthcare and caregiving.
309   Second, human preferences for action styles are difficult to quantify. In the field of embodied intelligence, current
310   methods for evaluating models typically assess task completion rates. However, action style preferences cannot be
311   evaluated using this metric. One approach is to use binary satisfaction criteria, where human evaluators experience
312   and rate their satisfaction. However, this requires separate data collection for each task and action, which is impractical
313   in practice. To address this challenge, we propose using a large language model as the agent to generate large amounts
314
      of data for multiple tasks and actions. In this framework, the language model simulates human preferences for action
      styles by pre-setting optimal parameters. This approach allows for the generation of training or evaluation data in
315
      various scenarios involving different control methods, tasks, and actions.
316
317   In summary, the robot’s action control problem can be divided into two parts: the search for control method parameters,
318   and the execution of actions once the control method is determined. This paper proposes a framework for human-robot
319   interaction in the adjustment of control method parameters.
320   All robot actions are executed by power sources, such as motors, with the combined output from the motors at each
321   joint constituting the robot’s motion. Therefore, we define the space of motor outputs as A0 , which encompasses all
322   possible actions of the robot. However, in practical applications, the control methods of the robot are predefined, and
323   thus, the set of robot actions becomes a subset Ap of A0 under the constraints imposed by the control parameters
      p (Figure 5a). The desired action styles for different users may lie within different subsets Ap . Through dynamic

504   5   CONCLUSION AND DISCUSSION
505
506
507
      This work presents an end-to-end embodied intelligence framework that integrates large language models (LLMs)
508
      with control algorithms, improving human-robot interaction by translating human inputs directly into robotic actions.
509   Initially demonstrated using a PID control algorithm, the proposed model simplifies the interaction between humans
510   and robots, laying the groundwork for more intuitive control systems.
511
512
      Our approach extends beyond PID control, as we successfully implemented intelligent parameter adjustment within a
      Nonlinear Model Predictive Control (NMPC) framework. This allows the control system to dynamically adapt NMPC
513
      parameters based on human instructions, aligning control behavior with user intentions. This development demon-
514
      strates that LLMs can facilitate more sophisticated control strategies, enhancing system flexibility and responsiveness.
515
516   A key contribution of this research is bridging the gap between high-level decision-making and low-level control. By
517   enabling LLMs to influence both planning and real-time action control, the proposed system allows robots to fine-tune
518
      their actions in response to dynamic human inputs. This advancement marks a significant step forward in the field of
      embodied intelligence, enabling robots to directly adapt their control dynamics based on user preferences.
519
520   Methodologically, we leveraged LLMs with prompt engineering to process time-series data and generate synthetic
521   training data, addressing the common challenge of acquiring large-scale human-annotated data. This scalable frame-
522   work enables more efficient batch generation of synthetic data for training embodied intelligence systems, demonstrat-
523   ing the feasibility of using LLMs in control tasks.
524   However, while the effectiveness of our approach has been validated in simplified dynamic systems, several limita-
525   tions must be addressed for broader applicability. First, integrating LLMs with more complex control methods, such
526   as sliding mode control (SMC) Utkin (1977) or Proportional-Integral-Derivative Neural Networks (PIDNN) Yu et al.
527   (2016), requires further exploration. These advanced algorithms may necessitate more sophisticated prompt designs
528   and rigorous testing in diverse operational contexts to ensure scalability and robustness. Additionally, the computa-
529   tional demands of using LLMs in low-level control tasks must be carefully evaluated, particularly in time-sensitive
530
      applications where latency could impact performance.
531   Future research should focus on optimizing the computational efficiency of the framework and expanding its appli-
532   cability to more complex, real-world environments. Specifically, further studies are needed to explore the scalability
533   of LLM-based control systems in handling dynamic, nonlinear environments and the integration of advanced control
534   algorithms. Addressing these challenges will be crucial for realizing the full potential of LLMs in enhancing robotic
535   control and interaction capabilities.
536   Overall, our findings suggest that LLMs, traditionally applied to language understanding and high-level decision-
537   making, can play a critical role in directly controlling robotic systems. This research opens new avenues for embodied
538   intelligence, demonstrating that language-based models can be integrated with advanced control algorithms to cre-
539   ate more adaptive, responsive AI-driven systems. Further investigation is needed to validate this approach in more
      complex and dynamic environments, paving the way for the next generation of intelligent robotic systems.
