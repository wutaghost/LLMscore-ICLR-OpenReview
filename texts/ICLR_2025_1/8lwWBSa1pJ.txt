009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we introduce Time-Aware World Model, a model-based approach
012            designed to explicitly incorporate the temporal dynamics of environments. By
013            conditioning on the time step size, ∆t, and training over a diverse range of ∆t
014            values – rather than relying on a fixed time step size – our model enables learn-
015            ing of both high- and low-frequency task dynamics in real-world control prob-
016
               lems. Inspired by the information-theoretic principle that the optimal sampling
               rate varies depending on the underlying dynamics of different physical systems,
017
               our time-aware model enhances both performance and learning efficiency. Em-
018
               pirical evaluations demonstrate that our model consistently outperforms baseline
019            approaches across different observation rates in various control tasks, using the
020            same number of training samples and iterations. We will release our source code
021            on GitHub once the final review decisions are made.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Deep reinforcement learning (DRL), one of the most popular learning paradigms, offers a broad
027
      range of potential applications, including robotics (Wu et al., 2023; Koh et al., 2021; Johannink
      et al., 2019), autonomous vehicles (Kiran et al., 2021; Guan et al., 2024), and challenging control
028
      tasks where classical approaches fail to deliver satisfactory performance (Prasad et al., 2017; Nhu
029
      et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2015). In addition to model-free RL approaches, where an agent learns
030   to directly map current observation st to action at (Williams & Peng, 1989; Schulman et al., 2015;
031   2017; Haarnoja et al., 2018), there has been increasing attention on model-based RL (MBRL), which
032   involves training a model M to capture the underlying dynamics of a given task (Sutton, 1990;
033   Deisenroth & Rasmussen, 2011; Parmas et al., 2018; Kaiser et al., 2019; Janner et al., 2019), and
034   planning the actions by leveraging the trained model M, which we refer to as a world model. These
035   model-based approaches have gained significant traction over model-free methods due to their supe-
036   rior sample efficiency and improved generalization capabilities (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018; Hafner
037   et al., 2019; 2020; 2023; Hansen et al., 2022; 2024).
038   Despite the impressive performance of world models in various RL tasks, a crucial factor in handling
039   dynamical systems – namely the time step size, ∆t – has been overlooked in existing work, to the
040   best of our knowledge. Specifically, the dynamics model D : st , at → st+1 , a key component of the
041   world model, models the state transitions. Conventional methods typically train D using experience
042   tuples (ot , at , ot+1 , rt ) collected through interactions with the environment at a fixed time step.
043   However, this practice presents two primary limitations (Thodoroff et al., 2022):
044         1. Temporal resolution overfitting: In current training pipelines, the default simulation time
045            step ∆t is very small (∆t = 0.0025 or frequency f = 400Hz). While smaller ∆t val-
046            ues are beneficial for stabilizing simulations and preventing system aliasing, world models
047            trained exclusively on small ∆t often suffer significant performance degradation when de-
048            ployed in real-world scenarios with lower observation rates (e.g., ∆t = 0.02 or f = 50Hz).
049            This discrepancy presents a major challenge in extending the applicability of world models
050            beyond simulated environments.
051         2. Inaccurate system dynamics: Training the dynamics model M on a fixed time step ∆t can
052            lead to overfitting, as the model may not capture the true underlying dynamics of the task
053            without conditioning on ∆t. This can result in inaccurate state transitions and diminished
               generalization capabilities.

      Although some recent works have considered the effect of temporal gap on the state transitions of
055   the model such as (Shaj et al., 2023; Lutter et al., 2021), these works focus on improving the model
056   accuracy over long-horizon predictions, which consists of multiple predictions on a fixed-∆t world
057   model. As a result, they trained world models for a single, fixed, discretized time step ∆t without
058   explicitly considering the impacts of ∆t’s on the state transitions. To overcome these limitations,
059   we primarily focus on addressing the following question: How can we efficiently train the world
060   model M to accurately capture the underlying task dynamics across varying time step sizes, while
061   maintaining computational efficiency?
062   In this work, we propose a time-aware world model MT to address the aforementioned question.
063   Unlike the previous world models M, our model conditions estimation of the next state and reward
064   on ∆t, as they depend on the temporal gap between the current and next state. We formulate MT
065   by modifying the world model of TD-MPC2 (Hansen et al., 2024) using 4-th order Runge-Kutta
066   (RK4) method (Butcher, 1987) to enforce certain dynamical properties as explained in Section 4.1.
067   Additionally, we modify the value model to take ∆t as an extra input. We train these models using
068   various values of ∆t, which are log-uniformly sampled from a predefined interval.
069   Although one might anticipate that MT would require more training samples than M due to the
070   inclusion of the additional parameter ∆t, this is not necessarily the case. According to the Nyquist-
071   Shannon sampling theorem (Shannon, 1949; Jerri, 1977), a signal with the highest frequency f can
072   be reconstructed by sampling it at a minimal frequency just slightly greater than 2f . Therefore,
073   if the observation rate is much higher than 2f , the surplus data become redundant – i.e., do not
074   substantially contribute to training the world model. In general, a physical environment consists of
075
      multiple dynamical systems of varying frequencies (Section 3.2.1). Therefore, using a mixture of
      time step sizes during the training process, we effectively expose the model to different sampling
076
      frequencies, allowing such sub-systems to be learned more efficiently (Section 3.2.3).
077
078   Inspired by the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem (Section 3.2.2), we empirically prove that our
079   time-aware model, with a mix of time steps in observation data sampled during training, achieves
080   much better performance on learning the world model with different time steps at inference time,
081
      after the same amount of training time as the baseline model. We demonstrate the results on di-
      verse control problems in Meta-World (Yu et al., 2020) environments. Our contributions can be
082
      summarized as follows:
083
084        1. We highlight the importance of a time-aware world model that conditions the dynamic
085           modeling on the time step size ∆t, a critical quantity within a dynamical system. Specif-
086           ically, by taking into account the temporal information ∆t, the dynamic model learns to
087           capture the underlying task dynamics across a spectrum of time step sizes. This approach
088           is particularly suitable for real-world control problems, where the observation rate can be
089           varying and/or much lower than the default simulation rate.
090        2. Motivated by the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem, we propose a mixture-of-time-step
091           training framework for the time-aware world model without increasing the number of train-
092           ing steps. This approach introduces a novel perspective on training a world model by con-
093           sidering varying sampling rates, which can guide future works in designing more efficient
094           training strategies.
095        3. Empirically, we show that our time-aware world model can effectively solve control tasks
096           under varying observation rates (e.g.: ∆t = 0.03, orf = 33.3Hz) without the need for
097           additional data. This capability helps to narrow the gap between simulation environments
098           and real-world control applications.

100   2   R ELATED W ORK
101
102   Although model-free RL algorithms have gained popularity due to their impressive performance,
103   their inherent lack of sample efficiency limits their applicability to a broader range of real-world
104   problems (Sutton et al., 1999; Williams & Peng, 1989; Barto et al., 1983; Schulman et al., 2015;
105   2017). To address this limitation, several approaches have been proposed, including the integration
106   of analytical gradients with lower variance into the policy learning process (Suh et al., 2022; Xu
107   et al., 2022; Son et al., 2024). In contrast, MBRL methods mitigates this issue at a fundamental
      level by training a dynamics model that simulates the real environment, allowing agents to predict


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      future states as well as outcomes of their actions (Deisenroth et al., 2013). Since prediction is not
109   constrained by real-world sampling, MBRL methods are inherently more sample-efficient. These
110   methods differ primarily in (1) how they define the world model M, and (2) how they leverage
111   M for training or planning. Historically, Gaussian processes (GP)(Deisenroth & Rasmussen, 2011;
112   Parmas et al., 2018) have been widely used, though recently neural networks are preferred over
113   them(Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018; Hafner et al., 2019; 2020; 2023; Hansen et al., 2022; 2024) due
114   to their superior representational power. Among these, the Dreamer model and its variants (Hafner
115   et al., 2019; 2020; 2023) train agents directly using the learned model, whereas Model Predictive
116   Control (MPC)-based methods (Hansen et al., 2022; 2024) rely on planning algorithms to determine
117
      actions. One closely related work, Multi Time Scale World Models (MTS3) (Shaj et al., 2023),
      explicitly considers the temporal gaps as the motivation in learning task dynamics. MTS3, denoted
118
      as M, is trained to capture state transitions over different prediction horizons H. However, their
119
      approach differs from ours significantly: while they account for multiple temporal resolutions, the
120   model is still trained using a single fixed time step size ∆t, and MTS3, at least at the current stage,
121   can handle only a limited number of time scales (in the paper, MTS2 learn fast and slow dynamics at
122   2 timescales: ∆t and H∆t). In contrast, our method incorporates a continuous-valued ∆t directly
123   into the model, allowing it to predict state transitions across a range of temporal gaps in just one
124   prediction step. This enables our model to predict transitions over large temporal gaps (e.g.: ∆t =
125   30ms) that would typically require 12-20 smaller prediction steps in models trained using a fixed,
126   small ∆t. Our approach builds upon the state-of-the-art world model TD-MPC2 framework (Hansen
127   et al., 2024) but differs in that our world model is explicitly conditioned on the time step size ∆t and
128
      is trained on a mixture of time step sizes, unlike most prior approaches, which assume a fixed time
      step size.

153
154   Before introducing our model, we provide theoretical motivations to explain the sample efficiency
155   of using a mixture of time step sizes during the training process of the time-aware world model.

      3.2.1    M ULTI - SCALE DYNMICAL S YSTEMS
158
159   In many control problems, the environment dynamics can be decomposed into multiple sub-
160   dynamical systems (or subsystems), each of which can evolve at different temporal scales (Weinan,
161   2011). In other words, such subsystems can be characterized by different functions with different
      highest frequencies. Formally, consider a general dynamic system: ẋ = f (x, u, t), where x, u, t are

163
164                                          x′ = x + f (x, u, t) · ∆t                                   (2)
165
166   where ∆t is the time step size. Based on the concept of multi-scale dynamical systems, the above
167   state transition can be re-written as:
168

171   where fi (x, u, t) represents the dynamic of each sub-system i. Each sub-system fi (·) may evolve at
172   different temporal scales, thus each of them can have its own highest frequency fimax .
173
174   3.2.2   N YQUIST-S HANNON S AMPLING T HEOREM
175
176   The Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem states that a signal must be sampled at a rate at least twice
177   the highest frequency present to avoid losing information (i.e., to prevent aliasing): fsample >
178   2fmax (Shannon, 1949), where fsample and fmax are the observation rate and the highest frequency
179
      of the environment dynamics in MBRL context, respectively. If the observation rate is too low such
      that fsample < 2fmax , we lose important dynamics details caused by the large temporal gap ∆t.
180
      This loss causes high-frequency components to be folded back into lower frequencies, resulting in
181
      inaccurate dynamics learned by the world model (Zeng et al., 2024). Although higher fsample al-
182   lows for more accurate reconstruction of the environment dynamics, if fsample is excessively high,
183   oversampling introduces redundant data, increasing sample complexity and reducing learning effi-
184   ciency. Therefore, finding the right observation rate fsample is crucial to balance modeling accuracy
185   and sample efficiency.
186
187   3.2.3   S IMULTANEOUSLY TRAINING ON MULTIPLE TEMPORAL RESOLUTIONS
188
189   Motivated by the need to more effectively sample the observations according to task dynamics of dif-
190   ferent sub-systems running at different frequencies while preserving the model accuracy, we propose
191   to simultaneously train the world model on multiple temporal resolutions by varying the observation
192   rates fsample (by varying ∆t) during the training process. Specifically, as shown in Equation 3, the
193
      underlying task dynamics can consist of several sub-systems fi (·) operating on different maximum
      frequencies fimax . According to the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem, each sub-system can be
194
      most efficiently learned with a different fsample . As a result, by randomly varying fsample during
195
      the training process, we avoid under-sampling high-frequency components and over-sampling low-
196   frequency components, thereby training sub-systems fi (·) efficiently. As a result, our time-aware
197   world model can learn the underlying task dynamics at different temporal resolutions without re-
198   quiring additional data, as shown in Section 5. In the next section, we present our time-aware model
199   and training framework in detail.

218   4.1.1    S TANDARD W ORLD M ODEL
219
220   TD-MPC2 is an MBRL algorithm that learns to capture the underlying task dynamics in the latent
221   space, or “implicit world model”. Specifically, unlike other reconstruction-based world model
222   architectures such as World Model (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018) and DreamerV3 (Hafner et al.,
223
      2023), TD-MPC2 does not include a decoder module that maps latent space back to raw observation
      space. The primary motivation for the exclusion of the decoder module is that it is computationally
224
      inefficient to recover the latent space to high-dimensional raw observation space, which can have
225
      many elements irrelevant to the control tasks. Once the latent dynamics are learned, TD-MPC2
226   can roll out different latent predictions for local trajectory optimization with planning algorithms as
227   in MPC (Hansen et al., 2022; 2024). The baseline TD-MPC2 model consists of five key components:
228
229     Encoder model:             zt = h(st )           Encode raw observations to latent vectors
230     Latent dynamic model:      ẑt+1 = D(zt , at )   Latent forward dynamics / transition
231     Reward model:              r̂t = R(zt , at )     Predict immediate reward of state-action pair
232
        Terminal value model:      q̂t = Q(zt , at )     Predict state-action value pair
        Policy prior:              ât = p(zt )          Output optimal action at from current latent state

234   where st is the raw observation in the current time step, zt is the latent encoding of the raw
235   observation, ẑt+1 is the predicted next latent state, r̂t is the predicted immediate reward, q̂t is
236
      the estimated q-value of current state-action pair, and ât is the action sampled from the policy
      for current state. At inference time, the Model Predictive Path Integral (MPPI) planner, a Model
237
      Predictive Control (MPC) algorithm, is used for planning and action generation. All five component
238
      models of TD-MPC2 are represented by multilayer perceptrons (MLPs).

240   To train TD-MPC2, a sample buffer B records trajectories of {(st , at , ot+1 , rt )0:H } from the task
241   environment after each training episode, where H is the episode length. At the end of each training
242
      episode, model parameters are updated using data randomly sampled from B. The encoder model h,
      dynamic model D, reward model R, and terminal value model Q are trained simultaneously through
243
      self-supervised consistency loss, supervised reward loss, and supervised temporal-difference ter-
244
      minal value loss, which is described in detail in Hansen et al. (2024). The agent then continues
245   interacting with the task environment and collects additional data for B to train the model.

      4.1.2    T IME -AWARE W ORLD M ODEL
248
249   One notable limitation of TD-MPC2 and other state-of-the-art world models is that they ignore the
250   effects of observation rate ∆t on the accuracy and performance of the world model at inference
251   time. To incorporate time awareness into the model, we propose to condition all components of the
252   world model on ∆t, which can be described as follows:
253
254
        Encoder model:             zt = h(st )                                    · Not conditioned on ∆t
        Latent dynamic model:      ẑt+1 = zt + d(zt , at , ∆t) · τ (∆t)          · State transition
255
                                   where where τ (x) = max(0, log(x) + 5)           via time-stepping
256
        Reward model:              r̂t = R(zt , at , ∆t)                          · Conditioned on ∆t
257     Terminal value model:      q̂t = Q(zt , at , ∆t)                          · Conditioned on ∆t
258     Policy prior:              ât = p(zt , ∆t)                               · Conditioned on ∆t

260   Our proposed time-aware model formulation is architectural-agnostic and can be straightforwardly
261
      adapted into any state-of-the-art world model. Since the observation encoder only encodes raw
      observation to latent space, it does not model the underlying dynamics and thus is not conditioned
262
      on ∆t. For all other models that depend on the underlying dynamics, we condition them on the time
263
      step size by explicitly using ∆t as part of the model input.

266   the next latent state zt+1 in an end-to-end manner, we reformulate the dynamic model following

269   intrinsic condition of a dynamical system: zt+1 |∆t=0 = zt ∀zt , at . Specifically, using our latent

      Algorithm 1 summarizes our framework, which trains a time-aware world model by varying the
      observation rate during the training process, encouraging the model to learn underlying dynamics
310
      at different temporal resolutions. A world model learns the spatial-temporal representation of the
311
      environment by sampling observations of these multiple dynamical systems within the environment,
312   at some particular instances in time and space. According to the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theo-
313   rem, we must sample the “signal” or the information that represents the given World Model at no
314   less than 1/2f , where f represents the highest frequency of the information in a band-limited sig-
315   nal. However, since there is no systematic methodology to determine the highest frequency of each
316   dynamical system in the environment, we sample observations from the environment at different
317   temporal rates to more effectively learn the underlying dynamics.
318   As shown in Algorithm 1, at the beginning of each episode, we log-uniformly sample ∆t from an
319   interval and then set the observation rate of that episode as 1/∆t. Log-uniform sampling facilitates
320   sampling observation at higher frequencies (or smaller ∆t) early in the training, which helps stabi-
321   lize the learning process. If the model is trained with dominantly low observation rates early in the
322   training, it can fail to capture important properties of dynamics and thus harm the learning process.
323   In fact, Figure 4 shows that when the baseline is trained on only a low observation rate (∆t ≥ 0.01s,
      or 10ms), the models fail on all tasks for all observation rates. Therefore, mixing different time

347
348   We designed our experiments to address three main questions: (1) Using the same planner, does
349   the time-aware world model perform comparably to the baseline model for the default observation
350   rate (∆t) without experiencing performance degradation under lower observation rate? (2) For

356   aware world model, we conducted our experiments on several control tasks within the Meta-World
357
      simulation environments, which have a default time step size of ∆t = 0.0025s (2.5ms). (Yu et al.,
      2021). We adaptively adjusted the observation rate, or the time step ∆t between observations, during
358

      as default. We used 9 diverse tasks with different goals and motion characteristics: (1) Assembly,

      Training Setup. Since our time-aware model architecture is based on TD-MPC2 architecture, we
365
      kept the same TD-MPC2’s default training hyperparameters, including model size, learning rate,
366   horizon, etc., to train our model. As shown in Algorithm 1, we used a mixture of different obser-
367   vation rates during the training process by randomly varying the time step size ∆t. Since there is
368   currently no systematic methodology to extract the highest frequency of each control task dynamics,
369   we cannot systematically determine the lowest possible observation rate for the agent to complete

472   6   C ONCLUSION
473
474   In this paper, we introduce a novel time-aware world model that can adaptively learn the task dy-
475   namics. we show that by explicitly incorporating the time step size ∆t into the world model and
476   training the model on a mixture of temporal resolutions helps the model to perform robustly un-
477   der different observation rates on various control tasks without increasing the sample complexity.
478   Empirical results show that our model outperforms all the baseline models, which are trained only
479   on a fixed time step size ∆t for different training ∆t values. We hope that the insights and results
480   presented in this paper offer a new perspective on world model training, thereby contributing to the
481   community a new, efficient, yet simple training method to train world models.
482   Limitations and Future Work. One limitation of our work is that we have yet to develop a reliable,
483   systematic methodology to compute analytically the highest frequency of the underlying task dy-
484   namics, thus the upper bound for the training time step size ∆tmax is determined empirically. This
485   limitation requires a search of ∆tmax for a new set of environments (e.g.: autonomous driving) to
      minimize the amount of observations required to train a given task. An interesting avenue for future

781   D    C OMPARISONS WITH M ULTI T IME S CALE W ORLD M ODEL (MTS3).
782
783   As mentioned in Section 2, Multi Time Scale World Model (MTS3) (Shaj et al., 2023) is a closely
784   related work with similar high-level motivation with our work: to model the world dynamics at
785   multiple temporal levels. Specifically, MTS3 proposes a probabilistic approach to jointly learn the
786   world dynamics at two temporal abstractions: task level (slow dynamics/timescale) and state level
787   (fast dynamics/timescale). These 2 timescales are separately learned by two state space models
788   (SSMs): SSM f ast and SSM slow , where SSM f ast learns the dynamics evolving at original small
789   timestep ∆t of the dynamical systems and SSM f ast learn the slow dynamics evolving at H∆t.
790   Although this approach also explicitly considered different temporal abstraction levels in learning
791
      the world dynamics, there are several critical differences between MTS3 compared to our work:
792
            1. Models vs Training method: Shaj et al. (2023) proposes a model architecture to learn a
793
               world model with several discrete temporal abstraction levels. On the other hand, we pro-
794            posed a simple yet effective and efficient time-aware training method that can be employed
795            to train any world model architecture.
796
            2. Discrete vs continous timescales: The original MTS3 currently only handle only 2
797
               timescales: ∆t and H∆t, where both ∆t and H is fixed in the training process. Al-
798            though the MTS3 can be adapted to learn multiple timescales, the number of timescales
799            is limited to a discrete value. Furthermore, the SSM slow (slow dynamic model) does not
800            directly model state transition under large temporal gap ∆t (or low observation rate) but
801            rather learns the task latents to guide SSM f ast to long-horizon predictions. On the other
802            hand, our time-aware approach can directly predict the future states st+∆t under large ∆t.
803         3. Multi-step vs one-step prediction: MTS3 considers the future prediction under large ∆t
804            as a long-horizon prediction problem. Specifically, to predict st+∆t , MTS3 discretizes
805            the long temporal gap into several smaller timesteps: ∆t = M ∆tf ast , where ∆tf ast is
806            the original timestep size SSM f ast is trained with and M ∈ N+ . MTS3 then iteratively
807            applies the model M times to predict st+∆t . This timestep discretization approach has 2
808            critical limitations: (1) MTS3 cannot model state transitions under ∆t that is not divisible
809            by ∆tf ast and (2) multi-step predictions are vulnerable to compounding errors, a well-
               known problem in long-horizon modeling. On the other hand, our model can directly
