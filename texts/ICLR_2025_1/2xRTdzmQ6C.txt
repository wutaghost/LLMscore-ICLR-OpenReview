                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
010
                 Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs) provide a self-explanatory framework by
                 making predictions based on concepts that humans can understand. However, they
011
                 often fall short in overall performance and interpretability because they tend to let
012
                 irrelevant information seep into the concept activations. To tackle concept leakage,
013              we introduce an information-theoretic framework to CBMs by incorporating the
014              Information Bottleneck (IB) principle. Our method ensures that only pertinent
015              information is retained in the concepts by limiting the mutual information between
016              the input data and the concepts. This shift represents a new direction for CBMs,
017              one that not only boosts concept prediction but also reinforces the link between
018              latent representations and comprehensible concepts, leading to a model that is both
019              more robust and more interpretable. Our findings show that our IB-based CBMs
020              enhance the accuracy of concept prediction and diminish concept leakage without
021
                 compromising the target prediction accuracy when compared to similar models.
                 We also introduce an innovative metric designed to evaluate the quality of concept
022
                 sets by focusing on performance following interventions. This metric stands in
023
                 contrast to traditional task performance measures, which can sometimes conceal
024              the impact of concept leakage, by providing a clear and interpretable means of
025              assessing the effectiveness of concept sets.

028   1     I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Explainable AI provides transparency into complex machine learning models, making their decision-
031   making process understandable to humans. This transparency increases trust, accountability, and
032   the ability to identify potential biases or errors thus also increasing safety. In critical domains like
033   healthcare and finance, where AI decisions significantly impact lives, explainability is essential for
034   ensuring fairness and ethical alignment. It also allows for continuous model improvement by exposing
035   flaws in training data or architecture. As AI deployment increases, explainable AI becomes a necessity
036   for maintaining human oversight and control. We distinguish four groups of explainable models. Post-
037   hoc techniques (Speith, 2022) explain trained black-box models after the fact, often by approximating
038   their behavior with interpretable models or by providing relevant feature attributions. Model-agnostic
039
      methods are independent of the model’s internal parameters or training process, and rely, instead, on
      treating the model as a black-box and analyzing its inputs and outputs. Local interpretability methods
040
      are a subfamily of model-agnostic methods, focusing on explaining individual predictions—examples
041
      include LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016), GradCam (Selvaraju et al., 2020). Global interpretability are the
042   second subfamily of model-agnostic methods, including Accumulated Local Effect points (Apley
043   & Zhu, 2020) and H-statistic (Friedman & Popescu, 2008). Self-explainable models are ad-hoc
044   designed and trained to be able to explain their predictions at inference time without additional
045   models or estimations. In this work, we are focusing on the latter since such models are explainable
046   by construction and easy to debug via altering the explanations. Thus, the self-explainable methods
047   are positioned as promising approaches over the other existing explainable models.
048   Concept bottleneck models (CBMs) (Koh et al., 2020) are a self-explainable approach allowing
049   to turn any end-to-end neural network training task into a concept-based task given the concept
050   labels. The main desiderata behind CBMs are the ability to explain the final decision to human while
051   operating with a set of human-understandable concepts, and the ability to take corrections on these
052   concepts into account to re-estimate the final prediction. The advantages of such approach include
053   higher robustness to covariate shifts and spurious correlations (should the target predictions rely only
      on concepts). Having a model form the final prediction based on human-understandable concepts


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      has one more benefit: at inference time, one could manually correct mistakes in concepts predictions
055   therefore making target prediction more accurate.
056
057   CBMs, however, were shown to have concept leakage (Margeloiu et al., 2021; Mahinpei et al.,
058
      2021)—the phenomena of concepts activations storing more information than just the concept
      presence. This phenomena is an issue affecting both interpretability and intervenability. Another
059
      issue with the CBMs is their performance being lower than that of a black-box models.
060
061   Among previous work that mitigates these issues, Havasi et al.’s (2022) proposal introduced side-
062   channel CBMs and recurrent CBMs. However, side-channel CBMs have lower intervenability,
063   and recurrent ones break the disentanglement of concepts. Kim et al.’s (2023) work introduced
064
      probabilistic CBMs, yet it needs anchor embedding points for target prediction.
065   Instead, we propose a simpler way to deal with concept leakage and reduced performance without
066   altering the architecture and without introducing a need for anchor bank. We extend the Informa-
067   tion Bottleneck (Tishby et al., 2000) principle to the concept space to reduce the concept leakage
068   while learning robust representations. Our main idea is to obtain concepts and representations that
069   are maximally expressive about the labels and the concepts, respectively, while having concepts
070
      maximally compressive about the data (under marginalized representations). That is, we offer an
      information-theoretic approach to CBMs, what we called Concepts’ Information Bottleneck. More
071
      specifically, we show that adding Information Bottleneck (Alemi et al., 2017; Tishby et al., 2000) to
072
      CBM training objective results in improved performance and better utilization of concepts.
073
074   The main contributions of this work are three-fold: (i) a new CBM that exploits the Information
075   Bottleneck framework providing a significant improvement compared to both vanilla CBMs and
076
      advanced concept bottleneck models, (ii) a demonstration that CBMs while may be more compressive
      but throw useful information based on the lack of predictive power in comparison to an IB-regularized
077
      model, and (iii) we introduce a model-based metric to measure concept set goodness (cf. Section 4.6).

082   2.1   C ONCEPT B OTTLENECK M ODELS
083
084   The concept bottleneck model (Koh et al., 2020), CBM, is defined as ŷ = f (g(x)), where x ∈ RD ,
085   g : RD → Rk is a mapping from raw feature space into the lower-dimensional concepts space,
086   and f : RK → R is a mapping from the concepts to the target variable. For training this model
087
      composition, a dataset of triplets {(xi , ci , yi )}N
                                                          i=1 is needed, where c(·) stands for the ground-truth
      concepts labels which should be produced by g. Notice that in this setup the amount of concepts to
088
      use is fixed for a particular model and that they are trained in a supervised manner.
089
090   Intuitively, when training a CBM, one is introducing human-understandable sub-labels (concepts)
091   which are more primitive and general than the target, and then builds a model predicting the target
092   based solely on those explainable concepts. Training process could be organized in a three ways:
093
      (i) Independent: train f using ground-truth concepts C = {ci }i as input, and train g to predict the
      concepts C. (ii) Sequential: firstly train g to predict the concepts, then freeze this concept-extractor
094
      model and train f on the outputs of g (not on the ground truth concepts labels). (iii) Joint: Optimize
095
      the weighted sum of two loss functions simultaneously: target prediction loss and concept prediction
096   loss: L (f (g (x)) , y) + λL (g (x) , c).
097
098   However, the initial setup described above has been found to have several flaws: first of all, if the
099
      concepts are soft, meaning that they can be take any value in [0, 1]. According to Mahinpei et al.’s
      (2021) findings, the model g learns to incorporate more information in these continuous outputs, for
100
      instance, about PCA components of the raw data. The issue appears unrelated to the training method,
101
      as it occurs even if g is trained separately from f , nor is it related to the selection of concepts, since
102   leakage occurs with randomly chosen dataset divisions as concepts. Mahinpei et al. (2021) posit
103   that even for hard concepts (each concept is clipped to {0, 1}) information may leak, though the
104   experiments confirm it only for small-dimensional data like Deng’s (2012). Secondly, Margeloiu et al.
105   (2021) argue that the CBMs desiderata is met for independent training only: for joint and sequential
106   a CBM learns more information about the raw data than just that presented in the concepts. Thus,
107   concepts are not used as intended. Developing the idea of tracking concepts predictions, the authors
      apply saliency methods to back-trace concepts to input features and find that for neither training

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      method of the three derive concepts from something meaningful in the input space. Conversely, we
109   hypothesize that by compressing the concepts and the data and, simultaneously, maximally expressing
110   the labels and concepts through their respective variables, we could obtain better concepts and
111   representations.
112
      Havasi et al. (2022) introduced side-channel CBMs—the ones in which information is allowed to
113
      flow aside the concept bottleneck—and recurrent CBMs, in which the model predicts concepts
114
      one after the other, and for next concept prediction utilizes the information about previous concept
115   predictions. However, side-channel CBMs have lower intervenability, and recurrent ones break the
116   disentanglement of concepts. Yuksekgonul et al. (2023) proposed Post-Hoc Concept Bottleneck
117   Models (PCBM). Such type of models utilize image embeddings from pre-trained Convolutional
118   Neural Nets penultimate layer activations. Based on these embeddings, the authors construct a concept
119   embeddings bank and obtain concepts predictions by either projecting a new image embedding onto
120   those embeddings or by using a SVM trained on the bank as concepts classifier. However, these
121   models perform well only after residual connections, similar to the ones described above, are added.
122   This residual information flow may damage both interpretability and intervenability.
123   To mitigate previous limitations, Zarlenga et al. (2022) presented Concept Embedding Models
124   (CEM)—a method bridging the gap between CBMs and black-box models via learning two vectors
125   for each concept (“active” and “inactive”). Such approach has increased target accuracy, but requires
126   additional regularization algorithm called ‘RandInt’ for CEM to be able to effectively utilize test-time
127   interventions. Moreover, the analysis of information flow done in the paper suggests that information
128   between inputs and concepts is monotonically increasing without any compression. The paper
129
      also introduces concepts alignment score (a metric specific for the model, more complex than just
      accuracy) designed to evaluate how well has CEM has learned the concepts.
130
131   Our work, unlike Zarlenga et al.’s (2022) proposal, maintains the original model concept represen-
132   tation space and regularizes it through our concept information bottleneck regularization. In detail,
133   first, we incorporate mutual information constraint into loss function, thus obtaining compression of
134   information between inputs and concept activations. Secondly, we do not utilize a pair of embeddings
135
      per concept but opt for one logit per concept, as in the original CBM setup. Finally, the novel metric
      we introduced measures not the quality of a model, but rather the quality of concepts sets themselves.
136
137   Kim et al. (2023) introduced ProbCBMs models, which predict a parameterized distribution of
138   concepts (mean and standard deviation) and use anchor points for class mapping. In this work, we
139   do not utilize these anchor points, since they increase inference costs and introduce a new hyper-
140   parameter to tune at fitting stage. We do use a variational approximation over our proposed concepts’
141
      information bottleneck to predict concepts.

      2.2   I NFORMATION B OTTLENECK
144
      Tishby et al. (2000) introduced the information bottleneck (IB) as the minimization of the functional
145
                                            LIB = I(X; Z) − βI(Z; Y ),                                    (1)
146
147
      where I(·; ·) is the mutual information, β is the Lagrange multiplier, X, Y and Z are random
      variables that represents the data, labels, and latent representations, respectively. The motivation
148
      behind the bottleneck is to “squeeze” the relevant information about target Y from X into a compact
149
      representation Z while minimizing the information about input X in Z—so that the representations
150   are free of irrelevant information from X. The IB’s authors have also posited that good generalization
151   is connected with memorization-compression pattern. This is the behavior in which I(Z; Y ) increases
152   during the whole training time, while I(X; Z) increases at first (memorization) and then decreases at
153   later iterations (compression).
154
      Alemi et al. (2017) extended the IB framework to deep neural networks by doing a variational
155
      approximation of latent representation Z. And, Kawaguchi et al. (2023) analyzed the role of IB in
156   estimation of generalization gaps for classification task. Their result implies that by incorporating the
157   Information Bottleneck into learning objective one may get more generalized and robust network.
158   Unlike this previous work that studied the IB for the data and the labels, we introduced another
159   predictive variable, the concepts, and derive an upper bound that links common predictors and the
160   ground truth into a regularizer that enforces the memorization-compression dynamics. Moreover, we
161   show that the concepts’ information bottleneck can be used in common CBM approaches through a
      mutual information estimator as well.

308   4.2    C OMPARISON OF DIFFERENT VERSIONS OF CIB
309
      In Table 1, we compare the performance of CIBMB and CIBME on concept and class prediction
310
      accuracy for the CUB dataset—using β = 0.5. As shown, CIBME , which retains an explicit mutual
311
      information term I(X; C), outperforms CIBMB when trained in a fair setup (vanilla) in both metrics.
312   We found that the lack of performance of vanilla CIBMB comes from instabilities during training
313   in the latent representations encoder p(z | x). We hypothesize that the gradient from the H(C) in
314   the loss (8) damages the feature encoder p(z | x) since the entropy is computed w.r.t. the generative
315   concepts p(c) instead of the variational approximated ones q(c). To alleviate this problem, we
316   experimented gradient clipping as well as stopping the gradient from H(C) into the encoder. We
317   found that the latter perform on par with CIBME . In the following, we refer to CIBMB as the version
318   with stop gradient on it. Overall, CIBME ’s more granular control over information flow limits
319   concept leakage, results in better accuracies for concepts and labels in comparison to the baselines
320
      (cf. Table 2) without changes to its training framework.
321   These results supports our earlier discussion that the direct estimation of I(X; C) leads to more effec-
322   tive use of concepts in downstream tasks without further changes to the training regime. Nevertheless,
323   with a correctly regularized feature encoder p(z | x), a simple estimation in CIBMB can achieve
      similar levels of information gain and accuracy.

      5   C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we integrated the IB with CBMs and proposed a first-principled theoretical framework
532
      to understand CIBMs, resulting in enhanced concept performance, reduced concept leakage, and
533
      maintained accuracy in target predictions compared to similar models. We developed two model
534   variants that have complementary performances dependent on the estimators used. Our methods
535   were validated on popular CBM datasets. We proposed new metrics to accurately evaluate concept
536   set quality and examined information flow within our IB-enhanced CBM objectives. Our findings
537   suggest that conventional CBMs might compress useless information, whereas our regularization
538   approach achieves better predictive accuracy with less compression. Furthermore, we assessed our
539   model’s interpretability and capacity for intervention, showing that our IB objective retains or even
      enhances performance when interventions are applied.

795   D    D ISCUSSION ABOUT CBM S SETUPS
796
797   Hard CBMs use hard concept representations, meaning that instead of producing a probabilistic
798   output (as in soft concepts in soft CBM), each concept prediction is treated as a discrete binary or
799   categorical value. These hard predictions are used as inputs to the downstream task (class prediction),
800   making the pipeline interpretable and less expressive, thus less prone to information leakage.
801   When compared with soft CBMs and Soft CIBMs:
802
803
      • Representation:
804
           – Hard CBMs: Use discrete hard values for concepts (e.g., 0 or 1 for binary concepts).
805        – Soft CBMs: Use continuous values (e.g., logits or probabilities).
806        – Soft CIBMs: Similar to soft CBMs but use IB to minimize irrelevant information, reducing
807          concept leakage.
808   • Information Flow:
809        – Hard CBMs: Compress information into discrete concept values, which prevents information
             leakage but risks losing useful details for downstream tasks.
