011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Although deep neural network (DNN)-based controllers are popularly used to con-
014            trol uncertain nonlinear dynamic systems, most results use DNNs that are pre-
015            trained offline and the corresponding controller is implemented post-training. Re-
016            cent advancements in adaptive control have developed controllers with Lyapunov-
017
               based update laws (i.e., control and update laws derived from a Lyapunov-based
               stability analysis) for updating the DNN weights online to ensure the system states
018
               track a desired trajectory. However, the update laws are based on the tracking er-
019
               ror, and offer guarantees on only the tracking error convergence, without provid-
020            ing any guarantees on system identification. This paper provides the first result
021            on simultaneous online system identification and trajectory tracking control of
022            nonlinear systems using adaptive updates for all layers of the DNN. A combined
023            Lyapunov-based stability analysis is provided, which guarantees that the tracking
024            error, state-derivative estimation error, and DNN weight estimation errors are uni-
025            formly ultimately bounded. Under the persistence of excitation (PE) condition,
026            the tracking and weight estimation errors are shown to exponentially converge to
027            a neighborhood of the origin, where the rate of convergence and the size of this
028
               neighborhood depends on the gains and a factor quantifying PE, thus achieving
               system identification and enhanced trajectory tracking performance. As an out-
029
               come of the system identification, the DNN model can be propagated forward to
030
               predict and compensate for the uncertainty in dynamics under intermittent loss of
031            state feedback. Comparative simulation results are provided on a two-link manip-
032            ulator system and an unmanned underwater vehicle system with intermittent loss
033            of state feedback, where the developed method yields significant performance im-
034            provement compared to baseline methods.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   Deep neural network (DNN)-based methods have garnered popularity as a means for identifica-
040   tion and control of uncertain nonlinear dynamic systems. Traditional DNN-based control tech-
041   niques involve initial offline system identification based on datasets gathered from experimental tri-
042   als (Abbeel, Coates, and Ng, 2010; Bansal, Akametalu, Jiang, Laine, and Tomlin; Karg and Lucia,
043   2020; Li, Qian, Zhu, Bao, Helwa, and Schoellig, 2017; Punjani and Abbeel, 2015; Shi, Shi, OCon-
044   nell, Yu, Azizzadenesheli, Anandkumar, Yue, and Chung, 2019; Zhou, Helwa, and Schoellig, 2017).
045
      Subsequently, the identified DNN is used as a model to design controllers using traditional model-
      based control techniques. However, the weight estimates of the DNN are fixed and not updated
046
      during task execution, raising questions about the model’s reliability and adaptability. Moreover,
047
      it is often implicitly assumed that minimizing a loss function would result in the DNN identifying
048   the system dynamics. Whether a system model can be identified depends on whether the system
049   trajectories generate information sufficient for the model to be identified, which manifests in terms
050   of the persistence of excitation (PE) condition on the model (Sastry and Bodson, 1989). Although
051   the PE condition is well-studied and understood in the system identification literature for linear re-
052   gression models, only a few recent works remark on the PE condition for DNNs (Lamperski, 2022;
053   Nar and Sastry, 2019;2; Sridhar, Sokolsky, Lee, and Weimer, 2022). If the model is not identified,
      the DNN may not generalize its performance well beyond the explored trajectories. Consequently,


                                                        1

054
      the controller may not accurately compensate for the uncertainty, thus hazarding the stability of the
055   closed-loop system.
056
057   Recent results in (Griffis, Patil, Bell, and Dixon, 2023; Hart, Griffis, Patil, and Dixon, 2024; Joshi,
058
      Virdi, and Chowdhary, 2020; Le, Greene, Makumi, and Dixon, 2022a; Le, Patil, Nino, and Dixon,
      2022b; Muthirayan and Khargonekar, 2023; Patil, Le, Greene, and Dixon, 2022a; Sun, Greene, Le,
059
      Bell, Chowdhary, and Dixon, 2022) offer online weight updates for the DNN-based controllers to
060
      achieve tracking error convergence. The online weight update laws are derived from a Lyapunov-
061   based stability analysis, and the corresponding controllers are popularly known as Lyapunov-based
062   (Lb)-DNN controllers. These results can achieve tracking error convergence regardless of whether
063   the PE condition is satisfied. However, the update laws in these results are based only on tracking
064   error feedback and are primarily meant to achieve tracking error convergence. Since the parameter
065   update law converges to zero upon convergence of the tracking error in such results, it is difficult
066   to draw conclusions regarding the accuracy of the parameter estimate. To address this problem,
067   incorporating a prediction error, i.e., a measure of the discrepancy between the actual dynamics
068   and their DNN-based estimate, into the adaptation law can help with parameter estimation. It is
069
      desirable to estimate the DNN parameters to achieve system identification in addition to trajectory
      tracking, where the identified model can be used to perform new tasks. For example, the identified
070
      model can be used to predict and compensate for the uncertain dynamics under intermittent loss
071
      of feedback (Bell, Sun, Volle, Ganesh, Nivison, and Dixon, 2023; Chen, Bell, Deptula, and Dixon,
072   2019; Pulido, Volle, Waters, Bell, Ganesh, and Shin, 2024). However, the prediction error is difficult
073   or often impossible to obtain since the dynamics are unknown and the state-derivative is typically
074   either unavailable or noisy.
075
      The classical result in (Slotine and Li, 1989) develops adaptive controllers with a composite adap-
076
      tation law that incorporates both tracking and prediction errors for nonlinear systems with linear-
077
      in-parameters (LIP) uncertainties, where a low-pass filter is applied on both sides of the dynamics
078   to eliminate the unknown state-derivative term. However, extending the composite adaptation law
079   from (Slotine & Li, 1989) to nonlinear-in-parameters (NIP) uncertainties such as DNNs is chal-
080   lenging because the inner-layer weights are embedded in nonlinear activation functions in a nested
081   fashion. Thus, when a low-pass filter is applied to the dynamics, the resultant expression is not
082   separable in terms of the model parameters, which introduces technical challenges as detailed in
083   Appendix B.2.
084   Main Contributions. This paper provides the first result on simultaneous online system identifi-
085   cation and trajectory tracking control of nonlinear systems using online updates for all layers of
086   the DNN. The development involves a composite adaptation law based on a new prediction error
087   formulation using a dynamic state-derivative observer, which is combined with the tracking error to
088   construct a least squares-based composite adaptation law. To address the challenges posed by the
089   nested and NIP structure of DNNs, the Jacobian of the DNN is used in a composite adaptation law.
090   Then, a first-order Taylor series expansion of the DNN is used in the analysis to express the predic-
091   tion error in terms of the parameter estimation error. Since the adaptation laws are tightly coupled
092
      with the observer and system dynamics, a combined Lyapunov-based stability analysis is performed
      which guarantees the tracking, observer, and parameter estimation errors are uniformly ultimately
093
      bounded (UUB). If the PE condition is satisfied, the tracking and weight estimation errors are shown
094
      to exponentially converge to a neighborhood of the origin. Specifically, guarantees on estimating the
095   ideal DNN parameters imply accurate system identification. Thus, the identified DNN model can
096   generalize beyond the points encountered by the system trajectory. As a result, the composite adap-
097   tive model is suitable for systems involving intermittent loss of state feedback, where the identified
098   DNN model can be propagated forward in time to predict the uncertain dynamics when feedback is
099   lost, under developed sufficient dwell-time conditions. To demonstrate the performance and efficacy
100   of the developed method on different systems, comparative simulation results are provided on two
101   systems: a robot manipulator and an unmanned underwater vehicle (UUV) with intermittent loss
102   of state feedback. The developed composite adaptive Lb-DNN controller yields significant perfor-
103
      mance improvement when compared to the tracking error-based adaptive Lb-DNN in (Patil et al.,
      2022a) and state-derivative observer-based disturbance rejection controllers as baseline methods.

511   5    C ONCLUSION
512
513
514   A composite adaptive Lb-DNN is developed for simultaneous online system identification and con-
515   trol, using the Jacobian of the DNN, the tracking error, and a prediction error based on a novel
516   formulation using a dynamic state-derivative observer. A Lyapunov-based stability analysis guaran-
517   tees the tracking, observer, and parameter estimation errors are UUB, with tighter bounds on these
518   errors when the DNN’s Jacobian satisfies the PE condition. Comparative simulation results demon-
519   strate a significant improvement in tracking, function estimation and generalization capabilities with
520   the developed method in comparison to the tracking error-based Lb-DNN in (Patil et al., 2022a) and
521
      observer-based disturbance rejection controller as baseline methods.
522
523
524
525   6    L IMITATIONS AND S COPE FOR F UTURE W ORK
526
527
528   The persistence of excitation condition for identifying the parameters is restrictive as there are chal-
529   lenges in verifying it online. For linear regression, recent developments in the adaptive control
530   literature such as (Chowdhary, Yucelen, Mühlegg, and Johnson, 2013b; Ortega, Aranovskiy, Pyrkin,
531
      Astolfi, and Bobtsov, 2021; Pan and Yu, 2018; Parikh, Kamalapurkar, and Dixon, 2019; Roy, Bhasin,
      and Kar, 2018) provide parameter estimation methods that guarantee parameter convergence under
532
      excitation conditions weaker than PE. All of these methods involve some form of regression ex-
533
      tension by storing the history of the regression in memory over an interval of time. However, these
534   methods are restricted to linear regression and have not been explored for NIP models such as DNNs
535   yet. Thus, insights from this paper may be used in future work to develop adaptation laws for DNNs
536   that yield parameter estimation guarantees under excitation conditions weaker than PE. Further-
537   more, extensions of the developed online system identification approach in optimization-based con-
538   trol paradigms such as MPC and reinforcement learning can be explored. Moreover, future research
539   efforts can also investigate how to combine the developed method with control barrier functions to
      satisfy state and input constraints.

      B.1   O N N EURAL N ETWORK -BASED A DAPTIVE C ONTROL
759
760   Classical results (Ge, Hang, Lee, and Zhang, 2002; Lewis, Yesildirek, and Liu, 1996b; Lewis, 1996)
761   develop adaptive controllers for neural networks with a single hidden layer, where online updates
762   are performed for the input and output layer weights. In recent results (Joshi et al., 2020; Le et al.,
763   2022a; Sun et al., 2022), adaptive controllers were developed for DNNs. In these results, the outer-
764   layer weights of the DNN are updated in real-time using Lyapunov-based adaptation laws, whereas
765   the inner-layer weights are updated either using iterative batch updates on discrete intervals of time
766
      (Joshi et al., 2020; Le et al., 2022a; Sun et al., 2022), or using a modular design (Le et al., 2022a).
      Since the inner-layer weight updates in (Joshi et al., 2020; Le et al., 2022a; Sun et al., 2022) happen
767
      using batch updates, the updates are essentially performed offline. In (Le et al., 2022a), the weights
768
      are updated online but the update laws are selected arbitrarily and not by a stability-driven approach.
769   In (Patil et al., 2022a), Lyapunov-based adaptation laws are developed for all layers of a fully-
770   connected DNN (i.e., so-called Lb-DNN methods). Since the control and adaptation laws are derived
771   from a Lyapunov-based stability analysis, the development is guaranteed to ensure stability of the
772   closed-loop system. More recent Lb-DNN results develop Lyapunov-based adaptation laws for more
773   complex architectures, specifically, deep residual networks (ResNets) (Patil, Le, Griffis, and Dixon,
774   2022b) and long short-term memory (LSTM) networks (Griffis et al., 2023). However, as stated in
775   the manuscript, the updates are based solely on tracking error feedback and are primarily meant to
776   achieve tracking error convergence. These results do not achieve guarantees on parameter estimation
777
      and system identification.
778
779   B.2   O N C OMPOSITE A DAPTIVE C ONTROL
780
      The classical result in (Slotine & Li, 1989) develops adaptive controllers with a composite adaptation
781
      law that includes both tracking and prediction errors for nonlinear systems with linear-in-parameters
782
      (LIP) uncertainties. The result in (Slotine & Li, 1989) constructs a form of the prediction error using
783   the swapping technique (also known as input or torque filtering), where a low-pass filter is applied
784   on both sides of the dynamics to eliminate the unknown state-derivative term. For a brief illustration
785   of the swapping technique in (Slotine & Li, 1989), consider the system
786
787
                                                ẋ = Y (x)θ + u,
788   where Y (x) is the regressor, θ is the vector of unknown parameters, and u is the control input. If the
789   state-derivative ẋ could be measured, the system can be expressed in terms of the linear regression
790   equation ẋ − u = Y (x)θ. Then the corresponding prediction error with an adaptive estimate θ̂ could
791   be developed as ϵ = ẋ − u − Y (x)θ̂, which can be expressed linearly in terms of the parameter
792
      estimation error θ̃ = θ − θ̂ as ϵ = Y (x)θ̃. However, ẋ measurements are typically either unavailable
793   or extremely noisy. To avoid using state-derivative information, (Slotine & Li, 1989) applied a
794   low-pass filter on both sides of the dynamics, which results in the filtered regression

827   ditional RISE-based terms in the control input, which can debilitate the learning performance of
828   the adaptive feedforward term. Notably, the results in (Patre et al., 2010b) and (Patre et al., 2010a)

830   estimates under the persistence of excitation (PE) condition.

833   f (x, ẋ, w) = ϕ (x, ẋ) a (w), where ϕ (·) denotes a basis function that is learned using a DNN and
834   a (w) denotes a set of linear parameters accounting for an unknown disturbance time-varying distur-
835   bance w. Since f is linear in terms of a, the composite adaptive approach from (Slotine & Li, 1989)
836   is used to design an adaptation law â˙ to update the estimates of a given by â. To obtain a disturbance-
837   invariant representation of ϕ using DNNs, a domain adversarially invariant meta-learning (DAIML)
838   algorithm is developed to train the DNN offline. To the best of our knowledge, this is the only
839   existing work using a composite adaptive approach in the context of deep learning-based control.
840   However, since the DNN learning ϕ (x, ẋ) has an NIP structure, the aforementioned challenges ap-
841
      ply for constructing a Lyapunov-based online adaptation law.

843   B.3    O N A LTERNATIVE DATA -D RIVEN A PPROACHES
844
      Besides DNNs, other methods such as Gaussian processes (GPs), Koopman operator, kernel meth-
845   ods have been explored to compensate for system uncertainty (Beckers, Kulic, and Hirche, 2019;
846   Berkenkamp and Schoellig, 2015; Bevanda, Sosnowski, and Hirche, 2021; Chowdhary, Kingravi,
847   How, and Vela, 2013a; Joshi and Chowdhary, 2018; Kingravi, Chowdhary, Vela, and Johnson, 2012;
848   Lederer, Umlauft, and Hirche, 2019; Umlauft and Hirche, 2020). GP models can be updated online
849   and implemented in the control design as a feedforward estimate of nonlinear system uncertainties.
850   In (Beckers et al., 2019; Berkenkamp & Schoellig, 2015; Lederer et al., 2019), safety guarantees are
851   derived for the developed adaptive GP architectures and overall control designs. The use of a prob-
852   abilistic model provides a measure of confidence in the GP estimates. Similar to Lb-DNN-based
853
      control, the use of adaptive GPs is motivated by integrating the data-driven estimation capabilities
      of modern machine learning models with the stability guarantees, online learning capabilities, and
854
      robustness to real-time disturbances of adaptive control techniques.
