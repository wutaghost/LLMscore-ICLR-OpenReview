                                                 A BSTRACT
013
014
               Text embedding models are increasingly utilized in production across various ap-
015            plications, from Information Retrieval (IR) to document parsing, but relatively
016            little research has been focused on how to best utilize these embeddings for down-
017            stream tasks. While cosine similarity, a popular measure of embedding and text
018            similarity, is widely used, it may not be the strongest metric choice for all tasks.
019            In this work, we introduce the Unified semantic Similarity Metric Benchmark
020            (USMB), a novel leaderboard for text similarity metrics composed of 5 unique
021            tasks and 30+ datasets with the goal of providing a standardized means of mea-
022            suring the effectiveness of a text similarity metric on a suite of challenging tasks
023
               encompassing the nuances of semantic understanding. Additionally, we demon-
               strate that while cosine similarity achieves the highest score on our benchmark
024
               of any pre-existing metric, developing a task-specific ensembled model using our
025
               metrics leads to a 40.3% increase in benchmark performance relative to cosine
026            similarity. We hope that through this work, greater attention can be given to po-
027            tential performance gains through metric selection and that the field’s ability to
028            measure semantic similarity advances as a result.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Text similarity measurements, especially within the context of LLM applications, are a critical com-
      ponent of production-level machine learning systems. The use cases hinging on a proper understand-
034
      ing of text similarity range from consumer considerations such as text summarization and content
035
      creation to industries spanning legal documentation (Bhattacharya et al., 2022), patent similarity
036   (Yoo et al., 2023), and scientific writing (Yu et al., 2022). While modern research has typically
037   focused on developing domain-specific models for these tasks (Xu et al., 2024), the text similarity
038   metric used in these tasks is often overlooked and untested, with cosine similarity being the uncon-
039   tested default (Zhou et al., 2022) despite not necessarily being the best choice for all tasks.
040
      With all these use cases where measuring semantic similarity is paramount, there is a sore lack of
041
      exploration into methods that effectively and robustly encompass this broad human concept, and
042   a lack of data to train models themselves towards this objective. To tackle the difficult problem
043   of task-based metric selection, we develop and introduce the Unified semantic Similarity Metric
044   Benchmark (USMB), a systematic evaluation of text similarity metrics on 5 unique tasks and 30+
045   datasets spanning human preference alignment, transformation robustness, information sensitivity,
046   clustering performance, and retrieval robustness. We measure the performance of popular metrics
047   like cosine similarity, Levenshtein (edit) distance, Rouge score, BM25, and Jaccard similarity over
048   each task and demonstrate that while cosine similarity achieves the highest overall score of these
049   metric by 6.1%, no metric clearly dominates in all tasks considered.
050   After evaluating pre-existing metrics on each task, we focus on developing a naive ensembled model
051   for each task with the goal of improving performance by taking advantage of each metric’s strengths
052   and weaknesses. By simply using an out-of-the-box linear regression or random forest classifier
053   depending on the task and training it on the 5 metrics we measured, we’re able to achieve top scores
      on all categories of the USMB, beating cosine similarities overall score by 40.3%.

      2     BACKGROUND

      2.1   A LGORITHMIC TEXT SIMILARITY MEASUREMENTS
061
062   For humans, judging the similarity between two pieces of text can be framed as a grade-school-level
063   task. This type of overarching similarity, encompassing tone, writing style, meaning, and factual
064   accuracy, is called semantic similarity (Chandrasekaran & Mago, 2021). Human-powered solutions,
065   however, aren’t scalable to corpora consisting of tens of thousands of documents, hence the need for
066   machine-powered similarity measurements.
067
      Levenshtein distance is one machine-powered way of measuring similarity that measures the edit
068   (insertion, deletion, substitution) distance between two strings.
069
070   Similarly, we can measure similarity through overlap in word frequency with the Jaccard similarity
071
      coefficient (Jaccard, 1912), which divides the intersection of the words in the texts by their union.
072   TF-IDF (Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency) is used to evaluate the importance of a
073   word in a document, which itself is part of a corpus. The importance increases proportionally to the
074   number of times a word appears in the document but is offset by the frequency of the word in the
075   corpus (Jones, 1972). TF-IDF is parameterized for a document d with t representing the frequency
076   of a term within the document. D is our corpus of documents.
077   BM25 (Robertson et al., 1994) is another advanced text similarity metric that builds on top of TF-
078   IDF to rank documents based on a given query. The original BM25, Okapi BM25, modifies TF-IDF
079   by incorporating document length and the frequency of terms in a query (Manning et al., 2009).
080   BM25 can be parametrized for a document d and a query q with t representing a term in the query,
081   |d| as the length of the document d, avgdl as the average document length in the corpus, and b and
082   k1 as hyperparameters. We will use BM25+, which adds a small constant δ as a lower bound.
083   Finally, we introduce Rouge (Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation) score (Lin, 2004),
084   particularly useful for evaluating summarization tasks. Rouge measures the overlap of n-grams,
085   word sequences, and word pairs between two documents. We report the average of Rouge-1 and
086   Rouge-2 scores, which measure 1-gram and 2-gram overlap between documents.
087   There are many similarity metrics we haven’t covered for brevity but are still notable, such as BLUE
088   (Papineni et al., 2002), BERTscore (Zhang et al., 2020), and MAUVE score (Pillutla et al., 2021).
089
090
      All equations and time complexities for the metrics we consider can be found in appendix A and C.
091
092   2.2   T EXT EMBEDDING MODELS AND C OSINE SIMILARITY
093
      To move past algorithmic similarity measures, we need some way to learn the representation of text
094
      in a latent space. We do so with text embedding models, which take an arbitrary-length string and
095   transform it into a fixed-length vector. This process is referred to as ”embedding” a piece of text.
096
097   The output of text embedding models is often interpreted as capturing the semantic meaning of the
098
      text passed into them (Senel et al., 2018) in a high-dimensional latent space. This space, geomet-
      rically crafted throughout the model training process, has been shown to arrange texts with similar
099
      meanings close together, allowing nuanced relationships between words to be discerned based on
100
      their proximity and orientation in this space.
101
102   Most uses of text embeddings hinge on their ability to represent meaning in a high-level geometric
103   space. Cosine similarity is a popular metric used to measure the similarity between two pieces of
104
      text via their embeddings. The equation for cosine similarity can be found in appendix A.
105   One of the most common use cases of cosine similarity is information retrieval, used for Retrieval-
106   Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2021) in the context of LLMs. Classification is another
107   application of applying text similarity measures to text embeddings. Some prevalent use cases of
      clustering are plagiarism detection (Jiffriya et al., 2014) and LLM watermarking (Huo et al., 2024).

      2.3   U SAGES OF TEXT SIMILARITY MEASURES IN BENCHMARKING
109
110   The popularity of embedding models has inspired the creation of leaderboards to measure model per-
111   formance in a standardized manner. A popular leaderboard is the Massive Text Embedding Bench-
112   mark (MTEB) (Muennighoff et al., 2023), which utilizes 56 datasets across 8 tasks to assign a final
113   score to an embedding model. This leaderboard builds off of previous, more specialized bench-
114   marks, such as the Benchmark for Zero-shot Evaluation of Information Retrieval Models (BEIR)
115   (Thakur et al., 2021) and Unsupervised Sentence Embedding Benchmark (USEB) (Wang et al.,
116
      2021). However, these benchmarks primarily focus on evaluating embedding models rather than
      the text similarity metrics themselves, and use cosine similarity without any analysis or discussion
117
      around this decision. Our work introduces a novel approach to benchmarking by providing an eval-
118
      uation framework specifically designed to assess and compare text similarity metrics themselves.

121   3     E VALUATING TEXT SIMILARITY MEASUREMENTS ON ROBUSTNESS AND
122         ALIGNMENT
123
124   To construct our metric leaderboard, we develop 5 unique tasks meant to assess the effectiveness of
125   a metric on the axes of alignment and robustness. We will start by defining these two concepts.
126
      Alignment describes a metric’s ability to reflect human judgments and preferences. A similarity
127   metric is considered aligned if it reflects human evaluations regarding text similarity. This is es-
128   pecially important in applications like summarization and content moderation, where the end-users
129   perception and understanding significantly impact the effectiveness of the LLM.
130
      Robustness, on the other hand, refers to a metric’s resilience against irrelevant or non-semantic
131
      modifications in text, such as random capitalization, deletions, or misspellings. A robust similarity
132
      metric consistently identifies texts that convey the same meaning, despite superficial changes, and
133   clearly distinguishes texts that differ significantly in content, intent, or meaning.

      3.2   R EPORTING METHODOLOGY
149
      We test 5 pre-existing metrics: cosine similarity, BM25+, Jaccard similarity, Rouge score, and Lev-
150   enshtein ratio. BM25+ scores are normalized between 0 and 1; other scores are naturally bounded
151   in this range. Cosine similarity scores are calculated for each model and averaged across models.
152   Our primary goal is to introduce the USMB benchmark and demonstrate how cosine similarity as a
153   whole can be combined with more traditional text similarity metrics. We average cosine similarities
154   across models to account for model-based variation in this metric, an attribute which doesn’t exist
155   in the other metrics we consider.
156   When creating ensembled models, we use the following setup. We reserve a random sample of 80%
157   of the data to use as our training data, and report all ensembled model scores on the remaining 20%.
158   We average our results across 1,000 random seeds for each model and task. We use the same condi-
159   tions to fit and evaluate the best-performing stand-alone metric. We use the default parameters from
160   scikit-learn (Pedregosa et al., 2011). For linear models, we do not fit an intercept term. To reduce
161   the chances of biases and architectural differences between embedding models being a confounding
      factor in our analysis, we train a separate ensemble model using each embedding model’s cosine

      3.4   ROBUSTNESS TO S EMANTIC AND S UPERFICIAL T RANSFORMATIONS
221
222   Datasets Our robustness task necessitates datasets consisting of long-form content and human-
223   generated summaries. To this end, we utilize a dataset of PubMed articles (Cohan et al., 2018),
224   removing the abstract from the publication and using it as a summary. We also use datasets of CNN
225   articles and government bills/proposals (Eidelman, 2019), both with human-generated summaries.
226
227   Evaluation In this task, we want to measure the ability of a text similarity metric to differentiate
228   between superficial and semantically altering changes to a text. We create a set of transformations
229   for each category.
230   Superficial Transformations:
231
232          • Random Capitalization: Randomly capitalizing 25% of letters in the text.
233
             • Deletion: Removing every 10th letter from the text, excluding spaces.
234
235          • Numerization: Substituting specific letters with numerals (e.g., replacing ’o’ with ’0’).
236
237   Despite these changes, the semantic content of the text remains intact and a human would be able to
238
      recognize the similarity.
239   Semantically Altering Transformations:
240
241          • Negation: Changing affirmative statements to negative (e.g., changing ”is” to ”is not”).
242          • Shuffle Sentences: Randomly rearranging the order of sentences.
243
244
             • Shuffle Words: Scrambling the order of words within the text.
245
      These changes disrupt the semantic structure of the original document. For example, shuffling
246
      the words in a text often renders it nonsensical. Humans can recognize that these transformations
247
      completely alter the original meaning of a text.
248
249   When evaluating each metric, we build on three assumptions.
250
251          • Superficial > Semantic: The similarity between text and a superficial transformation should
252
               be higher than with a semantically altering transformation.
253          • Summary > Semantic: The similarity between a text and its summary should be higher
254            than with a semantically altering transformation.
255          • Superficial > Summary: The similarity between a text and a superficial transformation
256            should be higher than with its summary.
257
258   To score a metric on these conditions, we determine whether the similarity scores given to a docu-
259   ment and its transformations fulfill each condition above. When we measure against superficial or
260   semantically altering transformations, we only assign a positive label to a datapoint if the condition
261   holds for all transformations in each category.
262   When developing an ensembled model, we use a simple linear regression and train it on pairs of
263   transformations. We assign a label of 0 when comparing a document and a semantic alteration, a
264   label of 1 when comparing a document to a superficially transformed version, and a label of 0.5
265   when comparing a document and its summary (Ren & Li, 2023).
266
267   Results Our findings are shown in table 3. Surprisingly, ensembling achieves a near-perfect score
268   of 0.98. This is likely due to our model being trained with an induced margin between the trans-
269   formations and the summary, while the pre-existing metrics we use weren’t created with this set of
      adversarial conditions in mind.

      3.5   S ENSITIVITY OF M EASUREMENTS TO U NRELATED T EXT
283
284   Datasets When measuring a metric’s performance on our sensitivity task, we minimize dataset
285   bias by validating our results across a diverse array of documents. To accomplish this, we utilize
286   datasets of PubMed publications (Cohan et al., 2018), essays written by thought leader Paul Graham
287   (Goel, 2024), BBC News articles, Amazon reviews (Zhang et al., 2016), Reddit posts (Geigle et al.,
288   2021), and the Argumentative Analysis (ArguAna) dataset (Wachsmuth et al., 2018). Our choice of
289   datasets and evaluation methodology for this task are borrowed from (et. al, 2024).
290
291
      Evaluation We want to determine the sensitivity of a text measurement to sentence-level changes
292
      in a multi-sentence document. We take two approaches. First, we add an irrelevant or adversarial
293   piece of text (”needle”) to a document (Guerreiro et al., 2023). with a varying needle’s length (5%,
294   10%, 25%, 50%, and 100% of the original text’s token count) and position (beginning, middle,
295   end). Second, we instead remove chunks of text with varying sizes (5%, 10%, 25%, and 50%) and
296   location. We compare the ablated text to the original using each metric, asserting that an ideal text
297   similarity metric should decrease linearly as the perturbation size increases. For example, adding a
298   needle that is 100% the length of the original text should decrease similarity by 50%, and removing
299   25% of the text should reduce similarity by 25%. This change should be agnostic to the position of
300   the perturbation, so we average the error of each data point across all experimental conditions.

366

            B ENCHMARK (USMB)
435
      We introduce the Unified semantic Similarity Metric Benchmark (USMB), our principal contribution
436
      designed to provide a comprehensive and standardized evaluation of text similarity metrics. The
437   USMB aims to address the lack of a unified framework that assesses text similarity metrics across
438   the multifaceted dimensions crucial for reliably measuring and quantifying semantic understanding.
439   By integrating these diverse evaluation aspects, USMB offers a holistic leaderboard that not only
440   ranks metrics based on their overall performance but also provides a useful demonstration of their
441   strengths and weaknesses in specific tasks. This unique and novel approach is intended to guide
442   practitioners in selecting the most appropriate metrics for their specific applications and to stimulate
443   the development of more advanced similarity measures.
444
445   4.1   B ENCHMARK D ESIGN AND S CORING M ETHODOLOGY
446
      The USMB is constructed by aggregating scores from the five unique tasks detailed in the previous
447
      sections. Each metric is evaluated on these tasks and assigned a normalized score between 0 and 1.
448
      The overall ranking is determined by averaging these scores.
449
450   Below, we detail the scoring methodology for each task included in USMB.
451
452   Preference alignment We take the average of each metric’s correlation with human scores on
453   the axis dataset and its accuracy on human choice in the comparisons dataset. To avoid a negative
454
      score from a negative correlation, we adjust the correlation to be bounded between 0 and 1 with the
      following equation: f (x) = 0.5 ∗ (1 + x).
455
456
      Transformation robustness We use the average performance across all three semantically-
457
      motivated conditions laid out earlier.
458
459   Information sensitivity The final score for a metric in this category is calculated by subtracting
460   the MAE of each metric’s outputs with our idealized similarity curve.
461
462   Clustering performance We average the v-measure score of a metric across all datasets.
463
464   Retrieval robustness We report the performance retention after data augmentation on retrieval

500   5   D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE WORK
501
502   The Unified semantic Similarity Metric Benchmark (USMB) represents a significant advancement
503   in the ability to measure, evaluate, and select text similarity metrics in a way that is both robust and
504   meaningful to their downstream usage. Our work introduces three key innovations: (1) a compre-
505   hensive, multi-task evaluation framework that spans a diverse set of commonly used metrics, (2) a
506   novel ensemble approach that leverages lightweight machine learning models to improve task-based
507
      performance, and (3) a standardized methodology for comparing both traditional and neural simi-
      larity metrics. The USMB’s multi-faceted and extensible design allows for tasks and metrics to be
508
      easily added, offering a new ground for text similarity metrics themselves to be put to the test and
509
      opening possibilities to quickly advance our ability to measure and capture semantic similarity.
510
511   Our decision to use out-of-the-box implementations with little testing of other models when ensem-
512   bling underlines a key theme of our work: even simple ensemble methods can significantly improve
513
      the robustness and alignment of semantic similarity measurement. As far as we are aware, there are
      no other works that have systematically researched combining multiple text similarity methods for
514
      improving semantic understanding, and our work is additionally the first to create a suite of original
515
      tasks and datasets exclusively for evaluating text similarity measurements.
516
517   While our ensembled models exhibit remarkable performance on USMB tasks, we acknowledge
518   certain limitations. The generalizability to unseen data and domains, computational complexity of
519
      the ensemble approach, and dependence on specific training datasets are areas that warrant further
      investigation. These challenges present exciting opportunities for future research and development
520
      in the field of text similarity metrics.
521
522   Looking ahead, there are several distinct directions of future work. Expanding the USMB to include
523   domain-specific and multilingual datasets as part of its evaulation process can enhance its robustness
524   and applicability. Putting more focus on other ensembling methods such as gradient-boosted trees or
525
      small neural networks can improve performance on each task even further. Lastly, it is a worthwhile
      endeavor to determine if ensembling text similarity metrics can generalize to strong performance
526
      across all tasks with the same model, instead of having a new ensembled model for each task.

529   6   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      We introduce the Unified semantic Similarity Measure Benchmark (USMB), a novel benchmark
532
      meant to measure the ability of text similarity measurements to reflect a human-level understanding
533
      of semantic similarity. We focus on 5 unique tasks: human preference alignment, transformation ro-
534   bustness, information sensitivity, clustering performance, and retrieval robustness. We discover that
535   each text similarity metric we use exhibits varying strengths and weaknesses, and no metric dom-
536   inates across all tasks. Thus, we develop and measure the performance of task-specific ensembled
537   models using our pre-existing measurements and achieve top scores across all tasks, often times by
538   a considerable margin. Our findings demonstrate that special care must be given to the selection of a
539   text similarity measurement based on the task it is intended for, and that classicial machine learning
      techniques can be used to augment the performance of existing text similarity measurements.
