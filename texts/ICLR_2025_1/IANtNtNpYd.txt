009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Bayesian Optimization (BO) has been widely applied to optimize expensive black-
012            box functions while retaining sample efficiency. However, scaling BO to high-
013            dimensional spaces remains challenging. Existing literature proposes performing
014            standard BO in several local trust regions (TuRBO) for heterogeneous model-
015            ing of the objective function and avoiding over-exploration. Despite its advan-
016
               tages, using local Gaussian Processes (GPs) reduces sampling efficiency com-
               pared to a global GP. To enhance sampling efficiency while preserving hetero-
017
               geneous modeling, we propose to construct several local quadratic models using
018
               gradients and Hessians from a global GP, and select new sample points by solving
019            the bound-constrained quadratic program. We provide a convergence analysis and
020            demonstrate through experimental results that our method enhances the efficacy
021            of TuRBO and outperforms a wide range of high-dimensional BO techniques on
022            synthetic functions and real-world applications.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Bayesian Optimization (BO) has been one of the popular methods for the global optimization of
      expensive black-box functions due to its high sampling efficiency. Applications include hyperpa-
028
      rameter tuning for deep learning (Hvarfner et al., 2022), discovering new molecules for chemical
029
      engineering (Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018), searching an optimal policy for reinforcement learn-
030   ing (Müller et al., 2021), and so on. BO is a sequential model-based approach consisting of two
031   main components: a surrogate model and an acquisition function. The surrogate model, typically
032   implemented as a Gaussian Process regression, is used to improve the sampling efficiency of BO
033   by modeling the objective function. The acquisition function is used to determine the next sample
034   point.
035
      While BO performs well in optimizing low-dimensional functions, it struggles with high-
036   dimensional problems for several reasons. First, the surrogate model loses accuracy in the high-
037   dimensional space when estimating the objective function. This is because it is impossible to fill the
038   high-dimensional space with finite sample points, even with a large sample size (Györfi et al., 2002).
039   Second, the computational complexity of optimizing the acquisition function grows exponentially
040   with dimensions (Kandasamy et al., 2015).
041
      Various methods have been proposed to address the curses of dimensionality in BO. The vast ma-
042   jority of the prior work assumes special structures in the objective function, such as additive struc-
043   ture (Kandasamy et al., 2015; Han et al., 2021) or intrinsic dimension (Wang et al., 2016; Letham
044   et al., 2020). However, these assumptions are often too restrictive for widespread application. Other
045   works directly improve the high-dimensional BO without additional assumptions, including TuRBO
046   (Eriksson et al., 2019), GIBO (Müller et al., 2021), and MPD (Nguyen et al., 2022).
047
      This paper focuses on trust-region Bayesian Optimization (TuRBO). TuRBO is attractive because
048   it uses local GPs for heterogeneous modeling of the objective function and performs BO locally
049   in several trust regions to avoid over-exploration. However, using local GPs reduces sampling ef-
050   ficiency compared to a global GP. To overcome this limitation, we propose a new trust-region BO
051   method (TuRBO-D) that incorporates the derivatives of GPs. It constructs several local quadratic
052   models using gradients and Hessians from a global GP, enabling heterogeneous modeling of the ob-
053   jective function while maintaining the same sample efficiency of a global GP. To optimize globally, it
      maintains multiple trust regions simultaneously. Our method consists of three main stages: building

066   2   R ELATED W ORK
067
068   In the realm of high-dimensional BO, there are generally three kinds of methods. The first kind of
069   method assumes the existence of a lower-dimensional structure within objective functions, typically
070
      employing a three-stage process: producing a low-dimensional embedding, performing standard
      BO in this low-dimensional space, and projecting found optimal points back to the original space.
071
      In REMBO (Wang et al., 2016), the low-dimensional embedding is achieved by using a random
072
      projection matrix. But REMBO often produces points that fall outside the box bounds of the original
073   space, necessitating their projection onto the facet of the box and resulting in a harmful distortion.
074   Subsequently, several techniques are proposed to fix this problem (Letham et al., 2020; Binois et al.,
075   2020). In addition, the random low-dimensional embedding can be also achieved by randomized
076   hashing functions (Nayebi et al., 2019; Papenmeier et al., 2022). The key advantage of the hashing
077   functions lies in their ability to effortlessly map candidate points back to the original space, thus
078   circumventing the need for clipping to box-bound facets. Some works achieve linear embeddings
079   based on learning. For example, SIR-BO employs Sliced Inverse Regression to derive the linear
080   embeddings, while SI-BO (Djolonga et al., 2013) learns the linear embeddings via low-rank matrix
081
      recovery. Garnett et al. (2014) learn the linear embeddings by maximizing the marginal likelihood
      of GPs. Besides, nonlinear embedding techniques have also been explored, particularly those based
082
      on Variational Autoencoders (Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018; Lu et al., 2018). However, these
083
      approaches typically require a substantially larger sample size. In addition to embedding techniques,
084   some research has focused on variable selection methods (Kirschner et al., 2019; Li et al., 2017; Shen
085   & Kingsford, 2023; Song et al., 2022).
086
087
      The second kind of method assumes the existence of an additive structure for the objective function.
      The additive objective function can be modeled by additive GPs (Kandasamy et al., 2015), allowing
088
      for more efficient maximization of the acquisition function. However, the true additive structure still
089
      remains challenging to learn. Several works propose to learn the underlying additive structure from
090   training data. For example, Wang et al. (2017) proposed a method that employs the Dirichlet process
091   to assign input variables into distinct groups. Rolland et al. (2018) employ a dependency graph to
092   model the interactions between input variables, allowing for the assignment of input variables into
093   overlapping groups. Han et al. (2021) proposed a refinement that restricts the dependency graph
094   to a tree structure, reducing the computational complexity of maximizing acquisition functions. In
095   contrast to data-driven decomposition methods, RDUCB (Ziomek & Bou-Ammar, 2023) learns a
096   random tree-based decomposition to mitigate the potential mismatch between the objective function
097   and additive GPs.
098   The third kind of method focuses on direct enhancements to the BO process in high-dimensional
099   spaces, without relying on any other assumption. For example, TuRBO (Eriksson et al., 2019),
100   GIBO (Müller et al., 2021) and MPD (Nguyen et al., 2022) adopt local strategies for BO to avoid
101   over-exploration in high-dimensional spaces. Another set of approaches focuses on partitioning the
102   search space and identifying a promising region to perform BO more efficiently (Wang et al., 2014;
103   Kawaguchi et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2020). Researchers have also proposed better initialization
104
      methods for optimizing high-dimensional acquisition functions efficiently (Rana et al., 2017; Zhao
      et al., 2024).
105
106   GIBO and MPD are similar to ours, which also utilize gradients of GPs. In contrast to their work,
107   our work incorporates both gradient and Hessian information from GPs and provides a convergence
      analysis.

110   3.1     BAYESIAN O PTIMIZATION
111
112   Bayesian optimization considers an optimization problem minx∈X f (x) where f is a black-box
113   and derivative-free function over a hyper-rectangular feasible set X . As a sequential model-based
114   approach, BO comprises two main components: a surrogate model and an acquisition function. The
115   surrogate model approximates the objective function, while the acquisition function, based on this
116   model, determines the next sampling point. Gaussian Process regression is typically employed as the
117
      surrogate model (Rasmussen & Williams, 2006), f ∼ GP(m(·), k(·, ·)) with a mean function m(·)
      and a kernel k(·, ·). More specifically, GP assumes that evaluations of any finite number sampling
118
      point x1:n follow a joint Gaussian distribution, f ∼ N (m(x1:n ), K(x1:n , x1:n )). Given training
119
      data Dn = {x1:n , y1:n } and a new point x∗ , the joint distribution is given by

165

166   several local quadratic models using gradients and Hessians from a global GP. This approach allows
167   for heterogeneous modeling of the objective function while maintaining the same sample efficiency
168   of a global GP. To achieve global optimization, we select new sample points by solving the bound-
169   constrained quadratic programs in multiple regions.

249        5   A CONVERGENCE ANALYSIS
250
251        Our method shares several key features with trust-region derivative-free optimization methods, in-
252        cluding the use of quadratic models to approximate the objective function and adaptive trust region
253
           updates. However, a crucial distinction lies in the nature of the error between the quadratic model
           and the objective function. This error is probabilistic in our approach, while it is typically deter-
254
           ministic in derivative-free optimization methods using interpolation techniques. This probabilistic
255
           aspect necessitates a verification of the coherence between the derivatives of GPs and those of the
256        objective function. This fundamental difference precludes the direct application of standard con-
257        vergence theory for derivative-free methods to our method. Consequently, we must reconsider the
258        convergence analysis in detail.

      7     C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   In this paper, we introduce TuRBO-D, a novel trust-region BO method that incorporates the deriva-
531   tives of GPs for enhancing the sampling efficiency of TuRBO. This novel scheme is realized by (1)
532   constructing several local quadratic models using gradients and Hessians from a global GP, enabling
533   heterogeneous modeling of the objective function while maintaining the same sample efficiency of a
534   global GP, and (2) selecting new sample points by solving the bound-constrained quadratic program
535   in multiple trust regions. Comprehensive experimental evaluations demonstrate that TuRBO-D sig-
536
      nificantly enhances the efficacy of TuRBO and outperforms a wide range of high-dimensional BO
      methods on a set of synthetic functions and three real-world applications. Furthermore, we provide
537
      a convergence analysis for our method.
538
539   While we mitigate the problem of vanishing derivatives using working sets, we will focus on devel-
      oping better schemes to address this challenge in the future.
