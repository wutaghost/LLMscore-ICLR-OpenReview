011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Despite their success and widespread adoption, the opaque nature of deep neu-
014            ral networks (DNNs) continues to hinder trust, especially in critical applications.
015
               Current interpretability solutions often yield inconsistent or oversimplified expla-
               nations, or require model changes that compromise performance. In this work, we
016
               introduce T RACER, a novel method grounded in causal inference theory designed
017
               to estimate the causal dynamics underpinning DNN decisions without altering
018            their architecture or compromising their performance. Our approach systematically
019            intervenes on input features to observe how specific changes propagate through
020            the network, affecting internal activations and final outputs. Based on this analysis,
021            we determine the importance of individual features, and construct a high-level
022            causal map by grouping functionally similar layers into cohesive causal nodes,
023            providing a structured and interpretable view of how different parts of the network
024            influence the decisions. T RACER further enhances explainability by generating
025            counterfactuals that reveal possible model biases and offer contrastive explanations
026
               for misclassifications. Through comprehensive evaluations across diverse datasets,
               we demonstrate T RACER’s effectiveness over existing methods and show its po-
027
               tential for creating highly compressed yet accurate models, illustrating its dual
028
               versatility in both understanding and optimizing DNNs.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Neural networks have demonstrated transformative potential across various applications, notably
034   image classification (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), medical diagnostics (Esteva et al., 2017), and complex
035   pattern recognition (LeCun et al., 2015), even surpassing humans in certain domains (Silver et al.,
036   2016; Rajpurkar et al., 2017). Yet, their inherent complexity obscures their decision-making processes,
037   turning them into “black boxes” that raise transparency and trust concerns, thus impeding their
038   adoption in sectors requiring explainability, such as healthcare and cybersecurity (Zeiler & Fergus,
039
      2014; Castelvecchi, 2016; Doshi-Velez & Kim, 2017; Lipton, 2018; Papernot & McDaniel, 2018;
      Zhang et al., 2021). Neural Network Explainability, pivotal in Explainable AI (XAI), aims to clarify
040
      DNN decision-making to ensure trust, ethical application, and bias mitigation. Although various XAI
041
      strategies have been proposed, including saliency maps (Zhou et al., 2015), Grad-CAM (Selvaraju
042   et al., 2017), LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016), and SHAP (Lundberg & Lee, 2017), they often present
043   inconsistencies, over-simplification, or architectural constraints, underscoring an ongoing challenge
044   in DNN understanding (Baehrens et al., 2010; Ba & Caruana, 2014; Rudin, 2019).
045
      In this paper, we introduce T RACER, a novel approach based on causal inference theory (Pearl, 2009),
046
      to infer the mechanisms through which AI systems process inputs to derive decisions. Recognizing
047
      that conventional evaluation metrics based solely on validation datasets may not be indicative of a
048   model’s performance in real-world settings and drawing inspiration from Pearl’s causal hierarchy,
049   our approach reveals how targeted modifications to input features influence the internal states of
050   neural networks, thereby modelling the underlying causal mechanisms. Specifically, T RACER frames
051   the explainability of neural networks as a causal discovery and counterfactual inference problem,
052   where we observe and analyze all intermediate and final outputs of a model, given any sample, its
053   generated set of interventions, and its counterfactuals. Through the aggregation of multiple such
      instances, we provide interpretability to state-of-the-art models without requiring any re-training or


                                                         1

054
      architectural changes, thus preserving their performance. In conjunction with an efficient approach for
055   counterfactuals generation, this offers contrastive explanations for misclassified samples, expanding
056   our understanding of not just what happened, but why it happened, and what could have happened
057   under different conditions, thus enabling the identification of potential model blind spots and biases,
058   and addressing the overarching issue of trust. Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
059
060          • We propose T RACER, a framework for estimating the causal mechanisms underpinning DNN
061            decisions, combined with a conditional counterfactual generation method for identifying
062
               failure modes, providing actionable insights for improving classifiers.
063          • We perform comprehensive evaluations of T RACER on image and tabular datasets, providing
064            explanations for correct and misclassified samples, while highlighting its effectiveness in
065            discovering the causal maps that describe the key transformation steps involved in decisions.
066          • We demonstrate T RACER’s versatility in both local and global explainability, as well as
067            its ability to outperform prevalent explanation techniques, identify redundancies in neural
068            network architectures, and aid in the creation of optimized, compressed models.

073   2    R ELATED W ORK
074
075   Techniques for DNN interpretability are typically categorized by explainability scope, implementation
076   stage, input/problem types, or output format (Adadi & Berrada, 2018; Angelov et al., 2021; Vilone &
077   Longo, 2021). Early endeavours like saliency maps by Zhou et al. (2015), Grad-CAM (Selvaraju
078   et al., 2017) and Layer-wise Relevance Propagation (LRP) (Bach et al., 2015) visually highlighted key
079   features in input data, but often produced inconsistent or coarse explanations, required structural model
080   changes, compromised performance, or overlooked nuances crucial for true comprehension (Rudin,
081
      2019). Model-agnostic approaches, such as LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016) and SHAP (Lundberg & Lee,
      2017), offer explanations by approximating model decision boundaries but can face challenges like
082
      resource intensiveness and inconsistencies in local explanations. Simplifying DNNs for improved
083
      interpretability (Che et al., 2016; Frosst & Hinton, 2017) often compromises performance, as simpler
084   models cannot always capture the nuances of complex DNNs. In contrast to the aforementioned
085   methods, rather than merely highlighting influential features, T RACER estimates the causal dynamics
086   that steer DNN decisions, without the need for altering the model or compromising its performance.
087
      Different from associative methods, causal inference techniques probe deeper to uncover cause-
088
      effect relationships. The idea of merging causal inference with AI is an emerging perspective, with
089
      prior works focusing on causal diagrams and structural equation models to gain such associative
090   understanding (Pearl, 2009; Yang et al., 2019; Xia et al., 2021; Kenny et al., 2021; Chou et al., 2022;
091   Geiger et al., 2022; Kelly et al., 2023). For instance, methods like those proposed by Chattopadhyay
092   et al. (2019), Kommiya Mothilal et al. (2021) and Chockler & Halpern (2024) perform causal
093   reasoning to explain decisions made by image classifiers, focusing on identifying causal elements
094   in the input space, while Reddy et al. (2024) extend this to capture indirect causal effects. T RACER
095   extends causal reasoning deeper into the structure of DNNs, combining causal analysis of both the
096   model’s internal workings and the input-output relationships. This enables explainability at both the
097   feature and network-structure level, providing more comprehensive explanations for DNN behavior.
098   Recent advances have also emphasized counterfactual explanations (Feder et al., 2021), generating
099   hypothetical instances to show how changes in inputs would alter predictions. For example, deep
100   generative approaches using Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Pawelczyk et al., 2020; Antorán
101   et al., 2020) or Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Mirza, 2014; Nemirovsky et al., 2022)
102   were proposed to minimize changes to input features to produce counterfactuals. Our approach
103   improves on these by introducing a dual objective that ensures realism through adversarial training
104
      while aligning counterfactuals closely with their nearest neighbors in the target class, making them
      simultaneously plausible and interpretable.
105
106   Our proposed approach sets itself apart in two main aspects: (1) rather than only focusing on input
107   features, our approach performs an intervention-based analysis that additionally examines the causal
      mechanisms within the DNN architecture, identifying how specific layers causally influence the

      3     T HEORETICAL F OUNDATIONS AND M ETHODOLOGY
132
133   To understand the internal-workings of DNN architectures, we must consider not only the operations
134
      performed by individual layers, but also how they influence one another across the network. T RACER
      aims to estimate an accurate model of these mechanisms, focusing on the dynamics that govern the
135
      network’s decisions. Therefore, our methodology, depicted in Figure 1, is structured around:
136
137   Causal discovery. We analyze the interactions and dependencies within DNNs by systematically
138   altering input features to observe the resulting changes, enabling an effective mapping of the decision
139   pathways. Through this process, we estimate the causal structures that drive the network’s decisions,
140
      providing a clear understanding of how different features and layers contribute to the outcome.
141   Counterfactual generation. We simulate alternative scenarios by introducing targeted changes to
142   input features, allowing us to explore ‘what-if’ scenarios and observe how specific changes in inputs
143   can lead to different outcomes, providing further insights into the model’s sensitivity and robustness.

156   3.2   P RELIMINARIES
157
158   Causal theory provides the means to model cause-effect relationships, offering a departure from mere
159
      observational statistics to tackle questions about interventions and counterfactuals (Pearl, 2009). To
      this end, the language of Structural Causal Models has been proposed to formalize these relationships.
160
161   Definition 1 (Structural Causal Model). A Structural Causal Model (SCM) M is a 4-tuple
      (U, V, F, P (U )), where U is a set of exogenous variables determined by factors external to the


                                                                 3

162
      model; V = {V1 , V2 , . . . , Vn } is a set of endogenous variables, each influenced by variables within
163   the model; F = {f1 , f2 , . . . , fn } is a set of functions, each fi mapping a subset of U ∪ V to Vi ; and
164   P (U ) is a probability distribution over U . For every endogenous variable Vi , its value is determined
165   by Vi = fi (pa(Vi ), Ui ), where pa(Vi ) represents the parents or direct causes of Vi , and Ui ⊆ U .
166
167   Pearl’s Causal Hierarchy (PCH), grounded in SCMs, further refines our understanding by categorizing
168   causal knowledge into three distinct levels, which serve as T RACER’s foundations1 :
169   1. Association. We extract dependency structures from the DNN activations and outputs P (Y (i) | X),
170   where X and Y (i) represent the input and the i-th layer’s output variables, respectively;
171   2. Intervention. By selectively manipulating feature values, we estimate the intervention distributions
172   P (Y (i) | do(X = xj )) to understand the effect of particular features on the final decision2 ;
173
      3. Counterfactual. We explore alternative (or hypothetical) input scenarios and compute the
                                          (i)
174   counterfactual distributions, P (YX=x′ | X = x), which quantifies the model’s output distribution if
175   a certain input were set to a particular value, given that we actually observed another input.
176   By identifying how specific input features and intermediate layer activations influence the model’s
177   final predictions, T RACER provides a unique approach for capturing an abstract overview of the
178   distinct computational components driving DNN decisions. This structured approach allows us to
179   produce explanations that clarify both the direct influence of features and how the model’s predictions
180   would change under different input conditions.
181   Definition 2 (Explanation). Given a d-dimensional input X = x ∈ Rd , an explanation for the output
182   y of a model F is a masked input xE = x ⊙ M ∈ {0, 1}d for which the following conditions hold:
183
184          Γ1 (Correctness): The model F, when evaluated on the input x, produces the output y.
185
                (F, x) |= (X = x) and F(x) = y, where |= denotes logical entailment.
186          Γ2 (Sufficiency): There exists a mask M ∈ {0, 1}d such that the resulting explanation xE =
187             M ⊙ x produces the same output as the original input: (F, xE ) |= F(M ⊙ x) = F(x) = y.
188             This condition ensures that the features selected by M are sufficient to explain y. Let a mask
189             M′ be defined such that the set of active features in M′ (i.e., where M′i = 1) is not a subset
190             of those in M. Formally, {i : M′i = 1} ̸⊆ {i : Mi = 1}, then (F, x′E ) |= F(M′ ⊙ x) ̸= y.
191
             Γ3 (Minimality): The mask M is minimal, meaning that no strict subset of active features in
192             M suffices to produce the same output. Formally, for every mask M′ ∈ {0, 1}d such that
193             {i : M′i = 1} ⊂ {i : Mi = 1}, the masked input x′ E = M′ ⊙ x is insufficient to produce
194             the same output: (F, x′ E ) ̸|= F(M ⊙ x) = y.
195
196   Note that (i) in this definition, y can be set to any specific label to produce explanations for mis-
197   classifications or rare events; and (ii) partial explanations can be simplified to binary decisions (i.e.,
198   whether a feature is relevant or not) when computing feature attributions (defined in Section 3.4).

200   3.3      C AUSAL D ISCOVERY
201
      To discover a faithful representation of the causal mechanisms underpinning DNN models, we
202
      perform an intervention-based analysis where we systematically change the values of input features
203   and study the effects on a given classifier. By observing the internal states and outputs of the classifier,
204   we can deduce how specific components contribute to the final decisions, offering an understanding
205   of the model’s causal structure and enabling the identification of key layers or connections that highly
206   influence the model’s predictions. Furthermore, by collecting the observed effects of all interventions,
207   we establish an abstract causal map to visualize the interplay between different network components,
208   and asses their collective influence on the DNN outputs. Ultimately, the insights gathered from our
209   approach enable the debugging and refinement of neural network classifiers.
210

213   specific input features. Given an input vector x ∈ Rd , where d denotes the dimensionality of the input

228

229   et al., 1953) use baselines to evaluate each player’s contribution by averaging their marginal impacts

232   approach, the baseline aims to counteract or neutralize the impacts of altered features, isolating the
233   original input’s influence on the output without the bias introduced by those features. By contrasting
234   the results from such intervened inputs with the original’s, we extrapolate the causal relationships
235   between input features and model outputs.

237   3.3.2   C AUSAL A BSTRACTION
238   Given an input sample and its interventions, T RACER collects the intermediate and final outputs of the
239   classifier to perform a focused comparison of representations across network layers and extrapolate
240   an accurate estimation of the causal dynamics driving the network’s decisions. For this analysis, we
241   use Centered Kernel Alignments (CKA), a prevalent approach for quantifying similarities between
242   high-dimensional embeddings (Kornblith et al., 2019). Let fi ∈ Rn×di and fj ∈ Rn×dj denote the
243   activations of two distinct layers in a network for a set of n input samples, where di and dj represent
244   the dimensionalities of the activations for layers i and j, respectively. Their respective kernel matrices
245   are defined as Ki = fi fiT ∈ Rn×n and Kj = fj fjT ∈ Rn×n to obtain their CKA similarity:
246

248   where HSIC(Ki , Kj ) is the Hilbert-Schmidt Independence Criterion (HSIC) for the kernel matrices,
249   and given by HSIC(Ki , Kj ) = (n − 1)−2 Tr(HKi HKj ). Here, H is a centering matrix given by
250   H = I − n1 11T , with n being the number of samples, I the identity matrix, and 1 a vector of ones.

252
      The use of CKA for evaluating representation similarity offers several advantages, including:
253
      (i) Normalization: CKA scores range from 0 (completely dissimilar) to 1 (identical), allowing
254
      straightforward comparison across layers; (ii) Flexibility: It accommodates various kernel functions,
255   such as linear or Gaussian, enabling flexibility based on specific requirements of the analysis; and
256   (iii) Robustness: The use of kernels allows CKA to operate in a richer feature space, providing a
257   more comprehensive similarity measure.
258

      3.5       C OUNTERFACTUAL G ENERATION
325
326   To improve classification performance and mitigate biases, we explain misclassified samples through
327   a T RACER analysis of counterfactuals, identifying specific feature changes that should be applied to
328   samples to obtain the desired outputs. Counterfactuals, defined as hypothetical data instances that, if
329   observed, would alter the model’s decision, must be valid and plausible. To generate such instances,
330   we use generative models like Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2020)
331   and include these constraints into our training process. Specifically, we propose a novel plausibility
332
      constraint, whereby the counterfactual generators are trained using both adversarial training to
      ensure realism, and a proximity-based regularization term to enforce similarity between the generated
333
      counterfactuals and real instances from a target class. This results in realistic counterfactuals requiring
334
      minimal changes to the original data. While our proposed constraints can be adapted to various types
335   of generative models (e.g., VAE, GAN, normalizing flows), the model we discuss hereinafter assumes
336   an autoencoder-based GAN architecture.
337
338
      Given an input x ∈ Rd and a target output y ∗ , our GAN-based counterfactual generation model is
      defined such that the generator uses an encoder function Ex to map the input x to a condensed latent
339
      representation zx = Ex (x). The desired model output y ∗ , typically an integer label, is transformed
340
      into a one-hot encoded vector o(y ∗ ) ∈ Rk , where k is the number of classes, using the Kronecker
341   delta function oi (y ∗ ) = δiy∗ for i = 1, . . . , k. This latent representation zx , concatenated with the
342   one-hot encoded target label o(y ∗ ) to form an augmented latent vector z = [zx ; o(y ∗ )], is processed
343   by the decoder D to generate a counterfactual instance x∗ = D(z). To verify the authenticity of the
344   generated counterfactual x∗ , the discriminator D evaluates whether x∗ appears realistic and plausible
345   by distinguishing between original data samples and those produced by the generator.
346
      The GAN is optimized using a dual objective: (1) ensure the authenticity of the generated coun-
347   terfactual x∗ and (2) maximize its similarity with its nearest neighbour xnn among real samples
348   of its training dataset whose label correspond to some target class. Specifically, we combine the
349   conventional GAN loss and a proximity measure d(x∗ , xnn ), with λ as the balancing coefficient:
350   L = (1 − λ) LGAN + λ d(x∗ , xnn ), ensuring that generated counterfactuals remain minimally different
351   from real instances in the target class, thereby preserving plausibility while leading to the desired
352   prediction. This approach offers a flexible and data-efficient process that closely aligns the generated
353   counterfactuals with the actual data distribution, while conditioning on priors for controlled outputs.
354   Remark. The regularization distance d(x∗ , xnn ), essential for maintaining plausibility, can be
355   implemented using metrics such as ℓ1 , ℓ2 , or perceptual loss. By introducing perturbations δi to the
356   latent representation zx before decoding, training with this regularization enables the generation of
357   multiple distinct plausible counterfactuals x∗ = D([zx + δi ; o(y ∗ )]), thereby reducing mode collapse.
358   We choose in our experiments the ℓ1 norm as regularization metric to encourage sparsity in the
359
      differences between x∗ and xnn , promoting minimal and interpretable changes to the original input.
360
      Employing generative models for counterfactual generation, rather than relying on nearest neighbors
361   during inference, offers several advantages. First and foremost, relying on real data points as
362   counterfactuals would require storing large datasets, potentially leading to memory constraints. This
363   could be particularly problematic in applications where storage is expensive or limited. To address
364   this, we train the counterfactual generator on a small random subset of the training set (e.g., 10%),
365   which is afterwards discarded, eliminating the need for storage. Moreover, this allows us to generate
366   plausible, novel counterfactuals on-the-fly, avoiding computational costs and latency associated with
367   dataset searches, while enabling broader exploration of the feature space.

510   4.4   D ISCUSSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
511
      We focused our evaluations of T RACER on accessible neural networks, where the internal architectures
512
      and intermediate activations can be directly analyzed. However, the flexibility and design of T RACER
513
      extend beyond these settings, making it equally applicable to black-box models where the internal
514   dynamics remain obscured, and only the inputs and outputs are accessible. Under such constraints,
515   T RACER remains valuable, offering two distinct avenues of exploration. First, it can analyze and
516   quantify the influence of input features on the model’s prediction. Alternatively, by using a surrogate
517   model with an accessible structure, we can effectively approximate the underlying causal mechanisms
518   driving the predictions. This adaptability underscores T RACER’s potential in diverse environments.
519
      While our T RACER approach is highly parallelizable by design, its depth of analysis can require
520   a trade-off between granularity (the precision of the causal analysis determined by the number of
521   interventions generated for each sample) and computational efficiency.
522
523   5     C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this paper, we introduced T RACER, a novel approach for accurately estimating the causal dynamics
526   embedded within deep neural networks. Through seamless integration of causal discovery and
527   counterfactual analysis, our methodology enables a deep understanding of the decision-making
528   processes of DNNs. Our empirical results demonstrate T RACER’s ability to both identify the causal
529   nodes and links underpinning a model’s decisions, and also leverage counterfactuals to highlight the
530   nuances that drive misclassifications, offering clear and actionable insights for model refinement
531   and robustness. Beyond local explanations, we showcased the potential of our approach to capture
532
      the global dynamics of DNNs, leading to practical advantages such as novel and effective model
      compression strategies. Through our foundational principles and findings, we have ascertained that
533
      by producing intuitive, human-interpretable explanations, T RACER offers outstanding transparency
534
      to neural networks, significantly enhancing their trustworthiness for critical applications.

1304

1305

1306   both the MNIST and ImageNet datasets to understand how varying ϵ influences the structure and
1307   interpretability of the discovered causal graphs.

1310

       H.1    S PATIALLY-S TRUCTURED M ODELS
1489
1490   Models like U-Net are renowned for their ability to capture both local and global features through
1491   skip connections between their downsampling and upsampling paths. Such spatially-structured
1492   models are widely used in image segmentation and classification tasks but pose significant challenges
1493   for interpretability due to their complexity. To evaluate the ability of T RACER to handle such
1494   architectures, we apply our causal discovery method to a U-Net model trained on the CIFAR-10
1495   dataset. The architecture consists of four down-sampling blocks and by four up-sampling blocks,
1496
       followed by a classification layer, with each block containing convolutional layers and non-linear
       activations, designed to progressively encode and decode spatial information.
1497
1498   As illustrated in Figure 14, our causal discovery process resulted in a causal graph with five nodes
1499   representing the key stages of the network: two causal nodes for the down-sampling path, two causal
1500   nodes for the up-sampling path, and one causal node for the classification layer. With non-adjacent
1501   causal links discovered between the first down-sampling causal node and the last up-sampling causal
1502
       node, as well as between the last down-sampling and first-up-sampling causal nodes (corresponding
       to the skip connections inherent in the U-Net architecture), T RACER produces an accurate high-level
1503
       causal view of the network, providing insights into how different parts of the network interact and
1504
       contribute to the classification node, thereby improving the interpretability of the network.
