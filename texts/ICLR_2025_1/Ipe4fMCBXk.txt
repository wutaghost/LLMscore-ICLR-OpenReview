009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The design of novel proteins, distinct from those found in nature, holds immense
012            potential for advancing drug discovery, biotechnology, and material science. How-
013            ever, current methodologies often face significant limitations in generating both
014            novel protein structures. Biological evolution, a natural process that fosters nov-
015            elty, heavily relies on recombination—yet this mechanism remains largely un-
016            tapped in protein design. In this work, we propose the Recombination Flow
017            Matching (RFM) model, a novel generative model inspired by the principles of
018
               evolution. RFM meticulously preserves the structural integrity of selected recom-
               bination protein segments during recombination while autonomously optimizing
019
               their spatial arrangement within the resultant protein. Using a common benchmark
020
               dataset, we demonstrate that RFM significantly outperforms established methods
021            in producing structurally novel proteins. This approach opens new frontiers in
022            protein design, leveraging evolutionary recombination to enhance the novelty of
023            protein design. To the best of our knowledge, RFM is the first model to incorpo-
024            rate recombination into protein design.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Proteins serve as the molecular engines of life, performing a diverse array of functions (Correia
030   et al., 2014; Linsky et al., 2020; Sesterhenn et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2008). Exploring the vast array
031   of possible protein structures is vital for creating new therapeutics and materials. This has led to
032   a surge of interest in designing synthetic proteins that differ from those found in nature (Callaway,
033
      2022; Watson et al., 2023; Ingraham et al., 2023; Madani et al., 2023). The creation of novel genes,
      proteins, and metabolic pathways is often driven by evolutionary processes (Levy, 2019; Chen et al.,
034
      2013), which foster both novelty and diversity. Recombination is one of the key mechanisms in bio-
035
      logical evolution (Barton & Charlesworth, 1998; Otto & Lenormand, 2002; Netzer & Hartl, 1997).
036   However, its application in protein design remains largely untapped (Wang et al., 2024; Nordwald
037   et al., 2013). Existing methods often struggle to generate truly novel protein structures as they often
038   fail to fully take the natural biological evolution process into consideration.
039
      Previous research has explored generative models for protein design, with a particular focus on
040
      techniques such as flow matching (Bose et al., 2024; Yim et al., 2024; Jing et al., 2024) and diffusion
041
      models (Ingraham et al., 2023; Watson et al., 2023; Yim et al., 2023). However, these models
042   tend to learn the distribution of protein structures by concentrating solely on individual residues,
043   overlooking the significance of larger protein segments. Inpainting-based methods (Watson et al.,
044   2023; Ingraham et al., 2023) and conditional generation approaches (Trippe et al., 2023; Yim et al.,
045   2024) attempt to address this limitation by incorporating protein segments into their models. Despite
046   these efforts, inpainting-based techniques require the manual identification of segment positions
047   within the resulting protein structure, which is not readily available. Moreover, recombination often
048   involves multiple segments, requiring optimization of their relative positions, which is beyond the
049   capabilities of inpainting methods. Conditional generation methods, meanwhile, face challenges in
050
      preserving the structural integrity of the segments to be recombined.
051   To address the challenges of generating novel proteins, we propose a novel model dubbed
052   Recombination Flow Matching (RFM), which recombines protein segments to generate innovative
053   protein structures. RFM is inspired by the principles of biological evolution, particularly recombina-
      tion, and is designed to ensure the structural integrity of the segments being recombined. Moreover,

      RFM automates the optimization of segment positions within the resultant protein structure. We
055   evaluate the performance of RFM on benchmark datasets of protein structures and demonstrate that
056   it surpasses existing methods in its ability to generate novel proteins.
057
058
      RFM is designed to address the limitations of existing methods by incorporating the following key
      features: First, RFM leverages principles from biological evolution, particularly recombination, to
059
      generate novel proteins. Second, to ensure structural integrity, RFM treats the protein segments
060
      intended for recombination as rigid bodies. Lastly, RFM automates the optimization of segment
061   positions within the resultant protein structure by adhering to rigid body dynamics, ensuring the
062   integrity of the segments. To demonstrate the effectiveness of RFM, we conduct experiments on
063   benchmark datasets of protein structures. Extensive experiments show RFM is capable of recom-
064   bining proteins to generate novel structures, outperforming existing methods. Besides, trained on
065   recombining two proteins, RFM can generalize to recombine multiple proteins, which indicates
066   RFM learned the underlying principles of recombination.
067   To the best of our knowledge, RFM is the first model to incorporate recombination into protein
068   design for enhancing the novelty of protein structures. The main contributions of our work can be
069   summarized as follows:
070
071
          • We propose Recombination Flow Matching (RFM) model, a novel generative model for protein
072         design that leverages recombination to generate novel protein structures.
073
074
          • RFM preserves the structural integrity of protein segments intended for recombination and
075
            automates the process of designing their optimal positions within the resultant protein structure.
076

          • We demonstrate the effectiveness of RFM on benchmark datasets of protein structures, showing
            that it outperforms existing methods in generating novel proteins.

085   2.1    G ENERATIVE M ODELS FOR P ROTEIN D ESIGN
086
087   Deep generative models have been extensively explored for protein design, with approaches such as
088   flow matching models (Bose et al., 2024; Yim et al., 2024; Jing et al., 2024) and diffusion models
089   (Ingraham et al., 2023; Watson et al., 2023; Yim et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Trippe et al., 2023).
090
      These models learn the distribution of protein structures based solely on individual residues. How-
      ever, they tend to overlook the significance of larger protein segments, which are critical for protein
091
      functions. As a result, these models frequently struggle to generate truly novel protein structures.
092
      In response, our RFM model addresses this limitation by incorporating protein segments, drawing
093   inspiration from recombination—a key process in biological evolution.

      2.2    P ROTEIN R ECOMBINATION
097
098
099
      Recombination is a key mechanism driving biological evolution, yet it remains underexplored in
      protein design (Netzer & Hartl, 1997). Previous works, including inpainting and conditional gen-
100
      eration methods, have attempted to incorporate protein segments into generative models for protein
101
      recombination. Despite this, Inpainting-based methods (Watson et al., 2023; Ingraham et al., 2023)
102   require manual identification of segment positions within the resulting protein structure, which is not
103   readily available. Moreover, recombination often involves multiple segments, requiring optimiza-
104   tion of their relative positions, which is beyond the capabilities of inpainting methods. Conditional
105   generation methods (Trippe et al., 2023; Yim et al., 2024) yet face challenges in preserving the
106   structural integrity of the segments to be recombined. Our RFM preserves the structural integrity
107   of protein segments while automating the process of designing their optimal positions within the
      resultant protein structure.

128   3.1   P ROTEIN R ECOMBINATION
129
130   We define a protein P = {A, T } as a sequence of amino acids (residues) A = [a1 , a2 , . . . , an ] ∈ Rn
131   and their 3D positions T = [T1 , T2 , . . . , Tn ] ∈ Rn×4×4 , where n denotes the number of residues in
132   the protein. ai ∈ A20 denotes the type of residue. Ti ∈ R4×4 is the pose matrix of the residue which
133   can be represented as a rotation matrix Ri ∈ R3×3 and a translation xi ∈ R3 . The pose matrix Ti
134
      can be decomposed as Ti = [Ri , xi ]. In this work, we focus on the protein backbone generation T ,
      where the residue types are ignored and the protein structure is represented by the backbone atoms,
135
      following previous works (Watson et al., 2023; Yim et al., 2023).
136
137   Protein recombination involves the exchange of segments between two or more proteins to generate
138   novel protein structures. Given m proteins [P1 , P2 , . . . , Pm ], we aim to recombine them to generate
139   a novel protein P′ . The recombination process involves selecting segments from [P1 , P2 , . . . , Pm ]
140
      and combining them to form P′ . We define the protein recombination as follows:
141   Definition 3.1 (Protein Recombination). Given m proteins [P1 , P2 , . . . , Pm ], the protein recombi-
142   nation process aims to generate a novel protein P′ by selecting segments from [P1 , P2 , . . . , Pm ] and
143   recombining them, i.e., P′ = f (P1 , P2 , . . . , Pm ), where f is the recombination function.

      4     R ECOMBINATION F LOW M ATCHING
206
207
      To explore the vast space of protein structures, we propose Recombination Flow Matching (RFM), a
208
      generative model that leverages recombination to generate novel protein structures. RFM is inspired
209
      by the principles of biological evolution, particularly recombination, and is designed to ensure the
210   structural integrity of segments being recombined. Moreover, RFM automates the optimization
211   of segment positions within the resultant protein structure. The overview of the RFM model is
212   shown in Fig. 2. The RFM model consists of two main components: recombination selection and
213   rigid dynamics involved flow matching. First of all, the recombination selection component selects
214   segments from multiple proteins [P1 , P2 , · · · , Pm ] to be recombined. The selected segments are
215   then input into the rigid dynamics involved flow matching process, where the flow matching model
      is used to recombine the segments and generate a novel protein structure.

      4.1     R ECOMBINATION S ELECTION
234
      Given m proteins [P1 , P2 , · · · , Pm ], the recombination selection aims to select segments from these
235   proteins to be recombined. Since we ignore the residue types and focus on the protein backbone,
236   the recombination selection process involves selecting segments based on the protein backbone. We
237   denote the selected segments as TS = [Ts1 , Ts2 , · · · , Tsk ], where k is the number of segments to
238   be recombined. Tsk = [T0 , T1 , · · · , Tn ] denotes the segment from the k-th protein consists of n
239   residues, where Ti is the pose matrix of the i-th residue in the segment.
240
      In the resultant protein P′ , which can also be denoted as T , we denote the selected segments as
241   TS and the other residues as Tr . For simplicity, we denote all the other residues from the selected
242   segments as Ts . In the training process, we randomly select k segments from one single protein as
243   one training sample following (Watson et al., 2023), where the protein is taken as the target protein.
244   In the inference process, we can select multiple segments from different proteins to generate a novel
245   protein following the recombination process.

341   5     E XPERIMENTS
342
343   We first pre-train RFM on the task of unconditional protein generation with the protein data bank
344   dataset (Berman et al., 2000) and then fine-tune it on the task of recombination. To demonstrate
345   the effectiveness of RFM, we first evaluate the ability of RFM to recombine different numbers
346   of proteins with the metric of recombination success rate. Then we compare RFM with previous
347   unconditional protein generation methods on novelty metrics.
348
349   5.1   S ETUP
350
      Dataset. All the experiments are conducted on the protein data bank dataset (PDB) (Berman
351
      et al., 2000), which contains 59,1228 proteins with a length of 60 to 512 residues. We construct
352
      the training dataset by randomly cropping m segments of length 2 to 40 residues from one single
353   protein to form the selected recombination segments for fine-tuning. Each entry in the training
354   dataset consists of a complete protein structure as the target and m segments as the recombination
355   chosen segments.
356
357   Evaluation metrics. We mainly employ the recombination success rate (RSR) and Novelty met-
358   rics to evaluate the model performance following previous works (Yim et al., 2023; 2024; Liu et al.,
359   2024). A generation is successful if the produced protein is designable and the selected recombina-
360   tion segments are present within the generation. The designability of a generated protein is assessed
361   using the scTM score. scTM refers to the TM-score (Zhang & Skolnick, 2005) between the structure
362   of the generated protein and the structure reconstructed through a sequence design model (Protein-
363   MPNN) (Dauparas et al., 2022) and a structure prediction model (Lin et al., 2023). A higher scTM
364
      score signifies greater similarity between the two protein structures. A protein is considered des-
      ignable if scTM > 0.5. Since we ensure the existence of selected recombination segments in the
365
      generation through the rigid dynamics involved flow matching, the SRS is the percentage of des-
366
      ignable proteins in the generation. To assess the Novelty, we use the pdbTM score, which compares
367   each generated protein structure with those in the Protein Data Bank (PDB) (Berman et al., 2000).
368   The similarity between the two structures is quantified using the TM score, known as the pdbTM
369   score. A generation is classified as a novel if pdbTM < 0.7. More details can be found in the
370   Appendix. A.3.1.
371
372   Compared approaches. We mainly compare our RFM with unconditional generation methods
373   including FrameFlow (Yim et al., 2024), FrameDiff (Yim et al., 2023), and VFNDiff (Mao et al.,
374   2024). FrameFlow is a flow matching model for protein structure generation. FrameDiff and VFN-
375   Diff are two diffusion models for protein generation.
376

      a length less than 256 residues. We train RFM to recombine two, three, four, and five proteins

516   6     D ISCUSSION
517
518   How does RFM generate novel proteins? RFM generates novel proteins by recombining differ-
519   ent proteins and making the recombination segments movable. The recombination process ensures
520
      the existence of selected recombination segments in the generation. The movable process allows
      the recombination segments to move freely in the generation. Attributed to the recombination pro-
521
      cess and the movable process, protein segments that have never existed together in nature can be
522
      combined to generate novel proteins.
523
524   How does RFM generalize to recombining various proteins at various lengths? The general-
525   ization of RFM is attributed to the fact that all residues are treated equally in the flow matching.
526   The only difference is that we average the rotation and translation of the selected recombination
527   segments. This design allows RFM to generalize to recombining various proteins at various lengths.
528   More details can be found in Appendix. A.1.4.

      7     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work, we propose a novel protein generation model, Recombination Flow Matching (RFM),
533
      which generates novel proteins by recombining different proteins. This concept is inspired by natu-
534   ral protein evolution, where recombination plays a crucial role in the emergence of novel proteins.
535   To integrate recombination within the flow matching model, we employ rigid-body dynamics to
536   preserve the structural integrity of the selected recombination segments while allowing for their
537   mobility. RFM demonstrates high success rates and novelty in recombining varying numbers of
538   proteins and producing proteins across different lengths, showcasing its effectiveness and generaliz-
539   ability in novel protein generation. In future work, we aim to enhance the model by automating and
      increasing the flexibility of recombination segment selection.

884   A.3.1    E VALUATION METRICS .
885
886   We mainly evaluate the models with the metrics of recombination success rate (RSR) and Novelty.
887
888   RSR is the percentage of designable proteins in the generation for our model. One protein is
889   designable if scTM > 0.5. As shown in Fig. 5, the scTM score for a generated protein structure is
890   computed in three steps following (Yim et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024). First, we use ProteinMPNN
891   (Dauparas et al., 2022) to design an amino acid sequence. Next, this sequence is processed by
892   ESMFold (Lin et al., 2023) to predict the corresponding protein structure. Finally, the TM-score
893   is calculated by comparing the predicted structure from ESMFold with the structure generated by
894
      our model. For each generated protein, the designed sequence from ProteinMPNN is input into
      ESMFold eight times to predict multiple folded structures. If any of the eight predicted structures
895
      has a TM-score greater than 0.5 when compared to the generated structure, the generation is deemed
896
      successful.
897
898
899
      Novelty. One generation is novel if the TM-score with all the proteins in the PDB dataset (PDB-
      TM) is below 0.7 following previous works (Yim et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2024; Yim et al., 2024).
900
      The novelty is the percentage of novel proteins in the generated designable proteins. We generate
901
      100 samples for each length and report their novelty.
902
903
904
      Diversity. We also use the MaxCluster (Herbert & Sternberg, 2008) to calculate the diversity of
      generation. Diversity is the ratio of unique clusters in the number of generated samples where the
905
      clusters are produced by MaxCluster following previous works. The diversity of generation for RFM
906
      is always 1.
