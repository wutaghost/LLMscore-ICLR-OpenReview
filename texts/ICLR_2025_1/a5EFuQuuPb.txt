                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               In this paper we identify the source of a singularity in the training loss of key
010
               denoising models, that causes the denoiser’s predictions to collapse towards the
011
               mean of the source or target distributions. This degeneracy creates false basins
012            of attraction, distorting the denoising trajectories and ultimately increasing the
013            number of steps required to sample these models.
014
               We circumvent this artifact by leveraging the deterministic ODE-based samplers,
015            offered by certain denoising diffusion and score-matching models, which estab-
016            lish a well-defined change-of-variables between the source and target distribu-
017            tions. Given this correspondence, we propose a new probability flow model, the
018            Lines Matching Model (LMM), which matches globally straight lines interpolat-
019            ing the two distributions. We demonstrate that the flow fields produced by the
020            LMM exhibit notable temporal consistency, resulting in trajectories with excel-
021            lent straightness scores.
022            Beyond its sampling efficiency, the LMM formulation allows us to enhance the
023            fidelity of the generated samples by integrating domain-specific reconstruction
024            and adversarial losses, and by optimizing its training for the sampling procedure
025
               used. Overall, the LMM achieves state-of-the-art FID scores with minimal NFEs
               on established benchmark datasets: 1.57/1.39 (NFE=1/2) on CIFAR-10, 1.47/1.17
026
               on ImageNet 64×64, and 2.68/1.54 on AFHQ 64×64.
027
028
               Finally, we provide a theoretical analysis showing that the use of optimal transport
               to relate the two distributions suffers from a curse of dimensionality, where the
029
               pairing set size (mini-batch) must scale exponentially with the signal dimension.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Diffusion models are the core engine behind many recent state-of-the-art generative models across
035
      various domains, e.g., image generation (Song et al., 2021b; Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol,
036   2021; Rombach et al., 2022), text-to-image generation (Nichol et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022;
037   Saharia et al., 2024), audio synthesis (Kong et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2020; Popov
038   et al., 2021), and video generation (Ho et al., 2022; Singer et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b)
039
      This gain in popularity of the underlying denoising diffusion (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho
040
      et al., 2020) and score-matching (Song et al., 2019; Song & Ermon, 2020; 2019) models over
041
      GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014) is often attributed to their improved distribution reproduc-
042   tion (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), and immunity to various optimization hurdles that plague GAN
043   training (mode collapse and forgetting (Thanh-Tung & Tran, 2020)). Nevertheless, unlike the single-
044   step sampling of GAN and VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2014) models, the noise removal process
045   follows non-trivial probability flow trajectories, requiring fine quadrature steps and resulting in non-
046   negligible computational effort during inference. This ranges between hundreds of sampling steps
047   in early methods (Ho et al., 2020) and tens in more recent ones (Karras et al., 2022).
048   Distilling pre-trained denoising models allows reducing this Number of Function Evaluations
049   (NFEs) during sampling. This approach can be carried out in different ways; learning the entire
050   sampling procedure (Luhman & Luhman, 2021), or reducing its number of steps progressively (Sal-
051   imans & Ho, 2022). More recently the denoising trajectories are learned either by ensuring a con-
052   sistency along successive steps (Song et al., 2023), or along arbitrary segments (Kim et al., 2024).
053   These methods offer a significant speedup over their teacher models, nevertheless, they also inherit
      inefficiencies inherent to the trajectories that they replicate.

054
      As an alternative, the probability flow matching techniques in (Lipman et al., 2023; Albergo &
055   Vanden-Eijnden, 2023b) incorporate Optimal Transport (OT) considerations in order to produce
056   more constant flow trajectories, requiring fewer sampling steps. Additional improvement in straight-
057   ness is achieved by an iterative rectification scheme in (Liu et al., 2023; 2024a), as well as by re-
058   placing the random pairing between the source and data examples with an OT pairing (Pooladian
059   et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024). While improving upon traditional denoising losses, the flow fields
060   produced by these approaches still contain false attraction basins, causing the trajectories to curve.
061   In this paper, we show that the ambiguous pairing between latent source noise and target data sam-
062   ples leads to an ill-posed regression problem, compromising the performance of key denoising mod-
063   els, including denoising diffusion, score- and flow-matching. At low signal-to-noise ratios, this
064   indeterminacy in the denoising loss becomes worse and causes the denoiser’s predictions to collapse
065   toward the mean of either the source or target distributions. This creates false basins of attraction
066   that curve and distort the denoising trajectories, ultimately increasing the number of steps needed
067   for accurate sampling.
068   We avoid this singularity by leveraging the fact that certain denoising diffusion (Song et al., 2021a)
069   and score-matching (Song & Ermon, 2019; Karras et al., 2022) models give rise to determinis-
070   tic ODE-based flows that give rise to a well-defined change-of-variable between the source and
071   target distributions. Unlike existing approaches that distill the underlying inefficient probability
072   flow trajectories, we only leverage the pairing induced between the distributions. Given this corre-
073   spondence, we construct a new probability flow model, the Lines Matching Model (LMM), which
074   matches globally straight lines interpolating between the distributions. As demonstrated in Figure 1,
075
      the flow fields produced by the LMM display notable temporal consistency, resulting in trajectories
      with excellent straightness scores.
076
077   Beyond its sampling efficiency, and unlike other flow matching formulations, the LMM’s training
078   loss allows us to further improve the fidelity of its generated samples by incorporating domain-
079   specific reconstruction and adversarial losses, as well as optimizing its training for the sampling
080   procedure used. Overall, the LMM achieves state-of-the-art Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) scores
081
      using a minimal NFEs on established benchmarks, specifically, 1.57/1.39 (NFE=1/2) for CIFAR-10,
      1.47/1.17 for ImageNet 64×64, and 2.8/1.61 for AFHQ 64×64.
082
083   In addition, we make a theoretical contribution showing that while the OT-based pairing in (Poola-
084   dian et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024) is a valid approach for reducing the attraction to the false basins,
085   due to a fundamental course-of-dimensionality, the batch size required scales exponentially as a
086   function of the signal dimension. Given that the latter is fairly high across various domains and
087
      the former is typically constrained by memory and compute limitations, the effectiveness of this
      approach is limited, as demonstrated in Figure 1.

      Variance Reduction. The non-negligible association between every pair of samples x0 ∼ p0 and
217   x1 ∼ q when marginalizing the regression losses over an independent distribution p0 (x0 )q(x1 ),
218   is a common thread shared by all the models mentioned above, which undermines their sampling
219   efficiency. As a remedy, recent works aim to replace this arbitrary pairing with ones that improve
220   sampling.
221
      By linking the flow’s transport optimality with the straightness of their trajectories, both (Pooladian
222
      et al., 2023) and (Tong et al., 2024) derive the pairing between p0 and q from an Optimal Transport
223   (OT) objective. Due to the cubic complexity of this problem (Flamary et al., 2021) (or a quadratic
224   approximation (Altschuler et al., 2017)) the pairing, or plan ji , is computed within batches of sam-
225   ples {xi0 }ni=1 ∼ p0 and {xi1 }ni=1 ∼ q of moderate sizes (n = 50/256 in (Pooladian et al., 2023)),
226   and Eq. 2 is minimized over these permuted pairs. As shown in Figure 1 this approach results in
227   flows that less curved than those produced by (Lipman et al., 2023). Indeed, a 30% to 60% reduction
228   in sampling cost is reported in (Pooladian et al., 2023).
229   A well-known manifestation of the curse-of-dimensionality causes the ratio between the farthest
230   and closest points to converge to a constant as the space dimension increases (Beyer et al., 1999).
231   Thus, considerably larger batches are needed in order to find meaningful plans πij . In Appendix A.2
232   we provide an asymptotic analysis showing an exponential batch size n dependency over the space
233   dimension d when solving a rather simpler problem; transporting a unit Gaussian distribution to
234   itself. This finding undermines the prospect of accelerating sampling by increasing the batch size
235   and relying solely on OT pairing. Indeed, in the example shown in Figure 1, a negligible difference
236   in trajectory straightness is found between n = 128 and n = 256.
237   Another strategy to avoid independent pairing described in (Lee et al., 2023) draws x0 ∼ q(x0 |x1 )
238   given x1 ∼ q, where q is a VAE-based encoder that maps points x1 to Gaussians. In order to obtain
239   non-trivial pairing one would seek highly distinctive q(x0 |x1 ) for each x1 however, similarly to VAE
240   training, Gaussians of different x1 are trained to match the same p0 in order to be consistent with
241   sampling time.

      As discussed above various diffusion, score and flow matching models achieve a remarkable sam-
246   pling accuracy in various data domains. This comes at a cost of executing multiple sampling steps
247   at inference time—a notable drawback compared to the single feed-forward execution of a VAE
248   and GAN networks. The inefficiency is rooted in the unfocused association between p0 and q pro-
249   duced by the independent example pairing, leading to poorly-resolved denoising, score and flow
250   regression problems in the low SNR regime. Unlike methods that distill these inefficient curved
251   trajectories (Salimans & Ho, 2022; Song et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024), we only utilize the pairing
252   they induce between the source and target distributions to construct a new probability flow model
253   that matches globally straight lines connecting the two distributions.

341   We trained the LMM on three benchmark datasets, CIFAR-10, ImageNet 64×64, and AFHQ 64×64,
342   which are commonly used for evaluating generative models. We used the same network architec-
343
      ture and hyper-parameters as existing models, with all the implementation details provided in Ap-

345   Quantitative Comparison. Table 2 provides a comprehensive comparison of the CIFAR-10 repro-
346   duction quality achieved by different models. The comparison clearly shows that diffusion-based
347   models achieve lower FID scores, albeit at an increased sampling cost compared to GANs. Flow-
348   matching models demonstrate their ability to reduce the NFEs, alongside a range of distillation
349
      techniques that operate effectively with very low NFEs—one or two sampling steps.
350   Among these, the Consistency Trajectory Model (CTM) (Kim et al., 2024), achieves excellent FID
351   scores of 1.73 (NFE=1) and 1.63 (NFE=2) on conditional CIFAR-10. Our LMM surpasses these
352   scores and sets new state-of-the-art scores of 1.57 and 1.39 respectively. We note that both methods
353   benefit from the use of an adversarial loss, but as reported in Section A.3, the LMM’s performance
354   remains better also without this loss. We attribute this to the fact that the LMM produces favorable
355
      line flow trajectories, rather than relying on the curved EDM trajectories that the CTM distills.
356   The Rect-Flow in (Liu et al., 2023) achieves an impressive FID score of 4.85 in its second itera-
357   tion, where it produced significantly straighter trajectories (as shown in Figure 1). We note that
358   this second iteration achieves the best trade-off between straightness and errors that this scheme
359   accumulates.
360   Table 4 shows the results obtained on a larger dataset, ImageNet 64×64. Here too, the LMM demon-
361   strates state-of-the-art performance, with a notable improvement at NFE=2, where it reaches an FID
362   of 1.17. The SiD Zhou et al. (2024) trains a single-step generator to agree with a pre-trained EDM,
363   achieving an impressive score of 1.52. Unlike the LMM, SiD does not rely on the EDM to generate
364   training examples; instead, it uses it to define the generator’s loss while simultaneously training an
365   additional score-matching network. This approach poses significantly higher GPU memory require-
366   ments and operations during training.
367   Finally, Table 5 reports the results on the AFHQ 64×64 dataset, where the LMM shows lower FID
368   scores using significantly fewer NFEs compared to the EDM despite the fact that the latter is used to
369   produce the initial correspondence between p0 and q. This is also the case in Tables 2 and 4. While
370   achieving a state-of-the-art FID of 1.54 at NFE=2, the SiD achieves a better score using a single
371   step. We note that unlike the CIFAR-10 and ImageNet 64×64cases, the discriminator architecture
372   and hyper-parameters we used were not we used were not tailored to this dataset in previous work
373
      (e.g., StyleGAN-XL Sauer et al. (2022)). This affected the expected improvements from the SOT
      strategy, as discussed in Appendix A.3, and we therefore believe the LMM has greater potential on
374
      this dataset.

447   5   C ONCLUSIONS
448
449   In this work, we showed that using broad random correspondences between the source and target
450   distributions results in collapsed predictions at low SNRs. By bringing them to a common analytical
451   framework, we showed that this degeneracy is inherent to key models, including denoising diffu-
452   sion, score-matching, and flow-matching techniques. We used this insight to propose a solution by
453
      deriving a deterministic correspondence from ODE-based sampling. To avoid the inefficiencies in
      the resulting trajectories, we only used their endpoints to train our LMM which parameterizes the
454
      transition between distributions using globally straight lines.
455
456   We leveraged the fact that our formulation works directly on signal reconstruction, and proposed
457   several training losses and strategies to improve the quality of the generated samples. In doing so,
458   our work bridges the domains of flow matching and denoising distillation. The combined effect of
459
      enhanced sampling quality and sampling efficiency has enabled the LMM to achieve state-of-the-art
      image generation quality in just one or two sampling steps.
460
461   Finally, as part of our effort to understand and improve the pairing required for training flow mod-
462   els, we made a theoretical contribution showing that OT-based pairing suffers from an exponential
463   relationship between the size of the paired sets (mini-batches) and the signal dimension.
464   Our work leaves one important goal unaddressed: avoiding the reliance on a pre-trained model, and
465   establishing its pairing in an ab initio manner. As a future research direction we intend to investigate
466   the adaptation of an iterative scheme, like the one in Liu et al. (2023), while avoiding drifts in the
467   training data during this process.

470   to publicly release our code and the trained LMM network weights.

873   A.3   A BLATION S TUDIES
874
875   We report here the results of several empirical experiments that assess the impact of different com-
876
      ponents related to LMM’s training, described in Section 3, on its sampling performance and quality.
877   Domain-Specific versus L2 Loss. Training the LMM to reproduce the end-points of the probability
878   flow lines, i.e., noise-free images, allows us employ perceptual metrics, specifically (Johnson et al.,
879   2016), for training. This loss is known to provide visually-preferable optimization trade-offs in
880   various applications, see (Zhang et al., 2018). Table 7 shows that training the LMM using a VGG-
881   based perceptual loss (VGG) achieves lower FID scores compared to that of L2 loss at all NFEs
882
      tested. The ability to use this reconstruction loss is inherent to the design of the LMM, and is not
      shared by all flow-based approaches, e.g., (Lipman et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023).
883
884   Number of Sampling Steps. Tables 7, 8, and 9 report the FID scores on different datasets using
885   different NFEs and sampling steps. Specifically, we used subsets of the sampling steps from the
886   sampling scheme in (Karras et al., 2022). While the number of steps provides some amount of
887   ability to trade-off between quality and efficiency, it is clear from these tables that increasing the
888
      NFEs suffers from a diminishing return. This finding aligns with the explanation that the probability
      flow lines generated by the LMM are fairly straight, and that the sampling errors are primarily
889
      due to the accuracy of their endpoints, i.e., the quality at which the target samples x1 ∼ q can be
890
      reproduced by exact integration. This further motivated us in Section 3 to focus on improving the
891   sample reproduction, as we evaluate next.
892
893   Adversarial Loss. Indeed, Tables 7, 8, and 9, show that the incorporation of an adversarial loss
894
      (ADL) provides an additional significant improvement to the image quality produced by the LMM.
      Indeed, this addition also helped the CTM in (Kim et al., 2024) to improve their baseline, specifi-
895
      cally, FID of 2.28 using a discriminator and 5.19 without it, using NFE=1 on CIFAR-10. We attribute
896
      the lower FID scores achieved by the LMM, in both scenarios, to the fact that it models favorable
897   line flow trajectories, rather than the original curved EDM’s trajectories, which are distilled in (Kim
898   et al., 2024).
899
900
      Sampling-Optimized Training. Motivated by limited improvement higher NFEs produce, in
      Section 3 we proposed another strategy to improve sample quality by restricting the training to the
901
      specific time steps used at the sampling stage. Tables 7 and 8 show that this training strategy also
902
      has the ability to contribute significantly despite the fact that it adds no cost. Table 9 an opposite
903   trend which appears to be related to a saturation (over-fitting) due to two factors: (i) to limited data
904   available in this dataset, and (ii) the SOT focuses on high noise levels, which makes it easier to
905   discriminate between generated and real samples. We conclude that a more fine-tuned discriminator
906   setting is needed to achieve optimal results.
