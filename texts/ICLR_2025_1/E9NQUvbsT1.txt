009                                                A BSTRACT
010
               Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as powerful tools for analyzing
011
               graph-structured data, deriving representations by aggregating information from
012            neighboring nodes. However, this aggregation process inherently increases the
013            risk of exposing confidential data, as a single node may influence the inference
014            process for multiple nodes simultaneously. To mitigate this risk, researchers have
015            explored differentially private training methods for G NN models. Existing privacy-
016            preserving approaches, however, face significant challenges. They often incur high
017            computational costs during training or struggle to generalize across various G NN
018            models and task objectives. To address these limitations, we introduce Differentially
019            Private Graph Coarsening (DPGC), a novel method that tackles two key challenges
020
               in G NN training: scalability and privacy guarantees that are independent of the
               downstream task or G NN model. Through comprehensive experiments on six
021
               datasets across diverse prediction tasks, we demonstrate that DPGC sets new
022
               benchmarks in graph coarsening. Our method achieves superior compression-
023            accuracy trade-offs while maintaining robust privacy guarantees, outperforming
024            state-of-the-art baselines in this domain.

027   1     I NTRODUCTION AND R ELATED W ORKS
028
      Graph-structured data derived from social and communication networks have become invaluable for
029   generating insights across fields such as social, behavioural, and information sciences Fan et al. (2019);
030   Liu et al. (2024). Such data naturally conforms to a graph-based model, encapsulating rich, nuanced,
031   and organized information. However, there are significant challenges when dealing with large graph
032   data, particularly scalability and privacy preservation. Additionally, due to seamless data collection,
033   often facilitated via personal devices, individual data within network contexts tends to be highly
034   sensitive. The strong inter-node relationships in graph-structured data make it especially vulnerable
035   to privacy attacks, increasing the risk of disclosing individuals’ data without their consent Liu et al.
036   (2016); Zhang et al. (2021); Mueller et al. (2024).
037   Beyond privacy concerns, the sheer size of graphs presents significant scalability challenges in
038   graph-based learning methods, as highlighted by recent research Hashemi et al. (2024). Large
039   graphs, comprising millions or even billions of nodes and edges, impose enormous computational and
040   memory demands, making it difficult to perform training and inference with standard methods. The
041   irregular and non-Euclidean nature of graph data exacerbates these challenges, requiring complex
042   operations like neighbourhood aggregation that do not parallelize efficiently. As the field evolves,
043
      addressing the scalability of large graphs remains a critical area of research, driving the development
      of more efficient graph-based learning frameworks.

      1.2    C ONTRIBUTIONS
093
      In this paper, we propose Differentially Private Graph Coarsening (DPGC), the first unified frame-
094
      work to jointly perform graph coarsening while also ensuring differential privacy for any downstream
095
      message-passing G NN architecture and tasks. The key innovations driving this result are as follows:
096
      • G NN-aligned graph coarsening: Instead of sole reliance on spectral similarity, DPGC coarsens the
097
        topology by embedding nodes into a feature space using Weisfeiler-Lehman kernel. Subsequently,
098
        the nodes are coarsened into supernodes through locality sensitive hashing. This design aligns
099     DPGC to the computation framework of message-passing G NNs and unshackles itself from the
100     limitations of existing coarsening strategies where distant nodes, even when producing similar
101     embeddings, are not considered as candidates to be merged into a supernode.
102   • Strong theoretical guarantees: DPGC is grounded on rigorous theoretical guarantees. First,
103     we prove that DPGC is (ε, δ)-DP (differentially private). Second, by virtue of post-processing
104     theorem, any G NN trained on the coarsened graph produced by DPGC is also differentially private.
105     Third, DPGC ensures restricted spectral similarity. Finally, DPGC scales linearly with the number
106     of nodes in a graph resulting in superior efficiency (Table 1).
107
      • Empirical evaluation: Through extensive experiments on six real-world datasets, we establish that
        DPGC outperforms state-of-the-art graph coarsening algorithms on accuracy, while ensuring su-
        perior privacy-accuracy trade-off compared to existing algorithms for differential privacy on G NNs.

140   2.2   D IFFERENTIAL P RIVACY
141
142
      Differential Privacy (DP) ensures that the output distributions of an algorithm remain indistinguishable,
      with a specific probability, when the input datasets differ by only a single record. The datasets that
143
      differ by one record are called neighbours, and in the case of graph data, the neighbour graph can be
144
      defined in teams of node and edge difference. For edge-privacy, we call two graphs G = (V , E ) and
145   G ′ = (V ′ , E ′ ) neighbouring if it holds that V = V ′ and |(E \E ′ ) ∪ (E ′ \E )| ≤ 1. For node-privacy,
146   the graphs G = (V , E ) and G ′ = (V ′ , E ′ ) are neighbouring if they differ by a single node and its
147   corresponding edges. Then, the definition of edge or node differential privacy can be given as follows:
148   Definition 2 (Differential Privacy Dwork et al. (2014a)). A randomized algorithm A is (ε, δ)-
149   differentially private if for all neighboring graphs G and G ′ and all subsets of outputs S,
150
                                      Pr[A (G ) ∈ S] ≤ eε · Pr[A (G ′ ) ∈ S] + δ,
151
152   where the probability is over the randomness of the algorithm.
153
154
      Here, ε is the privacy budget: a lower privacy budget leads to stronger privacy guarantees but reduced
      utility. δ is the failure probability and is usually chosen to be very small.
155
156   Privacy Attacks on GNNs: Membership Inference Attacks. In a membership inference (MI)
157   attack, an adversary tries to infer whether a data point was part of the training set used to train the
158   target model. An MI attack on GNNs can be defined as follows:
159   Definition 3 (MI Attack on GNN). Let a GNN model M be trained using the graph Gt = (Vt , Et ).
160   Given a node v and its L-hop neighborhood, the adversary aims to determine whether v ∈ Vt . Note
161   that even if v was part of the training set, the L-hop neighborhood known to the adversary might
      differ from the one used during the training of the model M .

205   3     DPGC: P ROPOSED M ETHODOLOGY
206
207   Fig. 2 presents the pipeline of the proposed algorithm DPGC. There are three key steps:
208
      1. Unsupervised node embeddings: Each node is embedded into a feature space using the Weisfeiler
209      Lehman Kernel (Togninalli et al., 2019).
210   2. Supernode construction: Next, these embeddings are hashed into buckets using Locality Sensitive
211      Hashing (LSH) (Charikar, 2002). Based on the collisions obtained in LSH, the nodes are grouped
212      into supernodes, and then edges and are determined among these supernodes.
213   3. Learning supernode attributes: Finally, the attributes of the supernodes are generated to obtain
214      the differentially private coarsened graph.
215   In the subsequent sections, we detail each of the individual steps and establish how differential
      privacy is ensured in DPGC.

524   5     C ONCLUSIONS , L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
525
526   Graph Neural Networks (G NNs) face two major obstacles to widespread adoption: potential exposure
527   of sensitive training data in public applications and computational challenges due to vast datasets with
528   millions of nodes and edges. Our algorithm, Differentially Private Coarse Graining (DPGC), tackles
529   both issues simultaneously through privacy-preserving graph compression. DPGC consistently
530   outperformed existing methods in both graph coarsening and differential privacy across six real-world
531   datasets. This superior performance stems from our novel approach of using the Weisfeiler-Lehman
532
      (WL) kernel for supernode construction, rather than conventional network connectivity metrics.
      This strategy enables provable privacy guarantees while enhancing computational efficiency. By
533
      addressing privacy concerns and reducing computational demands, DPGC paves the way for broader,
534
      safer deployment of GNNs in real-world applications.
535
536   Limitations and Future Works: In this work, we base the methodology on message-passing G NNs.
537   It remains to be seen how it generalizes to other forms of G NNs such as Graph Transformers.

      A.2   T IME COMPLEXITY AND RUN TIME
735
736   Complexity Analysis: The DPGC algorithm consists of the following phases. We assume n and m to
737   be the number of nodes and edges, respectively, in the original graph.
738
739
      1. Embedding ego graphs: The first phase involves computing node embeddings using WL-kernel.
         WL-kernel performs the same iterative message passing as in any message-passing G NNs, where
740
         each edge is traversed twice. Hence, the time complexity is O (m)
741
      2. Projection into buckets using LSH: The complexity of performing projections is O (nJd) time,
742      where d is the feature dimension, and J is the number of hash functions. This complexity arises
743      from the multiplication of matrices of size n × d and d × J.
744   3. Construction of supernodes and superedges: Constructing supernodes consumes O(nJ) time
745      since it involves iterating over all nodes and identifying the most frequent bucket, among J buckets,
746      they have been hashed into. Once supernodes are constructed, we need to iterate over all original
747      edges and see if the two endpoints are in two different supernodes, in which case an edge is added
748      among the corresponding supernodes. This requires O(m) time.
749   4. Learning node attributes: Finally, we learn the node attributes, which involves minimizing Eq. 5.
750
         requiring O (nkd) time. Since the Laplacian is fixed for the coarsened graph and the multiplication
751
         of X̃ by CT LC takes O (k2 d). The trace operation has complexity O (kd). For the second term, CX̃
         takes O (nkd) time and computing the Frobenius norm involves subtraction and squaring, which
752
         takes O (nd) time. Therefore, the overall time complexity for node feature learning is dominated
753
         by the term O (nkd) as k is small.
754
755   After combining all components, the overall time complexity is O (nJd + m). The runtime comparison
