011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            This work proposes a new perspective on the structure of Neural Networks (NNs).
014            Traditional Neural Networks are typically tree-like structures for convenience,
015            which can be predefined or learned by NAS methods. However, such a struc-
016            ture can not facilitate communications between nodes at the same level or signal
017
               transmissions to previous levels. These defects prevent effective collaboration,
               restricting the capabilities of neural networks. It is well-acknowledged that the
018
               biological neural system contains billions of neural units. Their connections are
019
               far more complicated than the current NN structure. To enhance the represen-
020            tational ability of neural networks, existing works try to increase the depth of
021            the neural network and introduce more parameters. However, they all have lim-
022            itations with constrained parameters. In this work, we introduce a synchronous
023            graph-based structure to establish a novel way of organizing the neural units: the
024            Neural Modules. This framework allows any nodes to communicate with each
025            other and encourages neural units to work collectively, demonstrating a departure
026            from the conventional constrained paradigm. Such a structure also provides more
027            candidates for the NAS methods. Furthermore, we also propose an elegant reg-
028
               ularization method to organize neural units into multiple independent, balanced
               neural modules systematically. This would be convenient for handling these neu-
029
               ral modules in parallel. Compared to traditional NNs, our method unlocks the
030
               potential of NNs from tree-like structures to general graphs and makes NNs be
031            optimized in an almost complete set. Our approach proves adaptable to diverse
032            tasks, offering compatibility across various scenarios. Quantitative experimental
033            results substantiate the potential of our structure, indicating the improvement of
034            NNs.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   Neural Networks are often organized hierarchically in a tree-like fashion. However, this conven-
040   tional approach obstructs effective communication among nodes and the structure can not facilitate
041   information interaction between nodes at the same level or signal transmissions to previous levels.
042   In fact, existing NN structures optimize in a limited space, significantly diminishing the potential
043   of NNs, and impeding their full capabilities. Our work involves a reimagining of traditional NNs.
044   We contend that the existing connectivity approaches inadequately capture the essence of neural
045
      networks. The nodes in an asynchronous tree-like structure lack the ability to establish connections,
      hindering information transfer between neural units and leading to deficiencies. To address this
046
      limitation, we introduce a method to build a synchronous graph structure for the nodes by the pro-
047
      posed Neural Modules, fostering collaboration among neural units. This innovative approach aims
048   to overcome the shortcomings of the asynchronous tree-like structure, providing a more effective
049   and interconnected framework for neural networks. Our framework progresses the structure of NN
050   from tree-like structures to general graphs, providing more candidates for NAS methods.
051
      For our framework. the concept of the Neural Module is defined as follows:
052
053   Definition 1. A Neural Module is defined as a directed, connected subgraph and the absolute value
      of the weight for any edge in the subgraph exceeds the predefined threshold γ.

055                          γ = the kth largest absolute valueof the weights.                             (1)
056
057   Our method enables synchronous communication among any nodes within the neural module. This
058   transformative adjustment enhances information transfer, thereby boosting the overall capacity of
059   NN structures. By fostering a collaborative environment among nodes, our approach leverages their
060   collective power to unlock NNs’ capabilities. This design promotes collaboration among nodes,
061
      facilitating more accurate information transfer. It is noteworthy that the existing tree-like NNs are
      essentially a substructure of our designed general graph. In our framework, multiple neural units
062
      collaboratively execute precise functional implementations. Our innovation is poised to bridge the
063
      gap between artificial and more general structures like biological neural networks. Furthermore, our
064   framework provides more candidates for NAS methods.
065
066   Designing such an architecture is a challenging task. While some similar works have made efforts,
067
      improvement is still needed. We found that the fixed point of the implicitly hidden layer is a solu-
      tion. Instead of using an infinite structure of hidden layers, we organize it into a graph. However,
068
      for a larger number of nodes, the implicitly hidden layer imposes a heavier computational burden
069
      and increases the risk of overfitting. For example, the Deep equilibrium models(DEQ) encounter
070   difficulties in finding the fixed point and calculating the Jacobian Matrix as well as its inverse ma-
071   trix for a larger number of nodes, which may also lead to serious overfitting problems due to poor
072   node organization. To address this, we propose a new regularization method to better organize these
073   nodes into multiple proper neural modules that are independent and can be handled in parallel. This
074   method allows nodes to form an automatic organization, enhancing the overall efficiency of the
075   learning process as well as its performance by reducing overfitting,
076   Our learning process exhibits adaptability to larger search spaces and diverse tasks, effectively elim-
077   inating the structural bias that some nodes cannot transfer information due to the limitations of the
078   current tree-like structure. We evaluate the effectiveness of our optimization method by conducting
079   experiments on state-of-the-art networks, demonstrating its competitiveness compared to existing
080   networks across various real-world tasks and datasets. The results from these experiments indicate
081   the superiority of the learned connectivity in terms of performance and efficiency.
082   To sum up, our contributions to this study can be outlined as follows: We propose a method to
083   generalize existing tree-like structures to learnable general graphs for NNs and we introduce a novel
084   regularization method to organize the neural units into Neural Modules that help to enhance the
085   efficiency of the structure as well as its performance by reducing overfitting.

      To progress the existing tree-like structure for NNs. Yuan (Kun Yuan & Yan, 2020) recently provided
090   a topological perspective, highlighting the benefits of dense connections offered through shortcuts in
091   optimization (Srivastava et al., 2015) (Sandler et al., 2018). Furthermore, sparsity constraints have
092   also been proven effective in optimizing learned structures across various applications (Srivastava
093   et al., 2015) (Chu et al., 2023) (Ahmed & Torresani, 2018) (He & Sun, 2016) (Huang & Weinberger,
094   2017). In their approach, the structure of NNs is organized as a DAG, whereas we organize it as a
095   more general graph structure.
096   The fixed point of the implicitly hidden layer can also be served as a solution (Bai et al., 2019)
097   (Tsuchida & Ong, 2022) (Chu et al., 2023) (Yang et al., 2022) (Heaton et al., 2021) (Zucchet &
098   Sacramento, 2022), as demonstrated in the subsequent works (Bai et al., 2020) (Szekeres & Izsák,
099   2024) (Yang & Liu, 2023). Departing from the infinite structure of implicitly hidden layers (Chu
100   et al., 2023) (Ling et al., 2023) (Ding et al., 2023) (Yang et al., 2022) (Liu et al., 2022), we organize
101   it into a general graph structure. Furthermore, for larger NNs, we introduce a novel regulariza-
102   tion method to organize neural units into multiple independent neural modules. Compared with
103   implicitly hidden layers, our method can improve the efficiency by parallel computing as well as
104
      the performance by reducing overfitting. Furthermore, it enhances the interpretability of implicitly
      hidden layers.
105
106   Our process also involves compressing NNs. In recent years, various algorithms have been devel-
107   oped, including quantization (Kai Han & Xu, 2020) (Mingzhu Shen & Wang, 2019) (Yang He &
      Yang, 2018), low-rank approximation (Li & Shi, 2018) (Zhaohui Yang & Xu, 2019) (Xiyu Yu &

      Tao, 2017), knowledge distillation (Shumin Kong & Xu, 2020) (Shan You & Tao, 2018), and net-
109   work pruning (Pavlo Molchanov & Kautz., 2019), etc. The network pruning method we use in this
110   work is weight pruning, which aims to eliminate weak connections. In addition, the method also
111   involves filter pruning that removes entire redundant filters (Y Tang, 2020). We aim to improve
112   weight pruning for our framework using a method similar to Tao Lin (2020), which evaluates the
113   gradient at the pruned model and applies parameters’ updates to the dense model. In our framework,

115   OptNet integrates optimization quadratic problems for nodes within the same layer (Amos & Kolter,
116   2017) (Yan & Zhang, 2021). However, this approach introduces bias and lacks an interpretable struc-
117   ture, affecting overall interpretability. Furthermore, OptNet uses parameters derived from quadratic
118   problems for backpropagation. These parameters, determined by nodes from the previous layer, are
119   difficult to manage in complexity. This complexity not only increases the computational burden
120   but also exacerbates overfitting problems. Using a differentiable function for these parameters adds
121   further bias.
122   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) specially address the needs of geometric deep learning (Gori et al.,
123   2005) (Fan et al., 2019) (Scarselli et al., 2008) (Abadal et al., 2021). GNNs adapt their structure
124   to the input graph, capturing complex dependencies (Yong et al., 2007) (Abadal et al., 2021) (Fout
125   et al., 2017) (Fan et al., 2019). This adaptability enables them to predict properties of geometric
126   data, which primarily deal with graphs, our innovation involves evolving the tree-like structure into
127   a general graph format for NNs, expanding their applicability and potential.
128   The flexibility of this graph structure has also been investigated in studies similar to those on
129   Reservoir Computing (Lukoševicius & Jaeger, 2009) (Benjamin Schrauwen & Campenhout., 2007)
130   (Leshno & Schocken, 1993b). These studies have utilized a recurrent neural network frame-
131   work where neuron connections are established randomly, where the weights remain static post-
132   initialization. Reservoir Computing capitalizes on the dynamics of a non-linear system. In contrast,
133   our Neural Module framework is designed around a balanced system paradigm, allowing for the
134   adaptive learning of both weights and network structure during processing. This feature enhances
135
      our framework’s versatility and effectiveness, setting it apart from Reservoir Computing with its
      stronger capabilities.

      3.1   R ATIONALE FOR I NTRODUCING THE S TRUCTURE
142
143
      NNs represent a type of information flow. However, tree-like structures maintain a hierarchical,
144
      nested form that limits information transfer, as each node can only be influenced by its precursor
145
      nodes. These structures are asynchronous and lack the flexibility to form cycles. Moreover, we ob-
146   serve that these structures are notably inferior in complexity compared to biological neural networks,
147   which exhibit more intricate connectivity patterns beyond the simplicity of tree-like structures. The
148   adoption of these structures is primarily due to their favorable mathematical properties, particularly
149   in facilitating convenient forward and backward propagation.
150
      Consider the traditional tree-like structure for an NN with m layers. Let N 0 be the nodes of the
151
      first layer and the input values feed into the first layer. Let N m be nodes for the last layer and they
152   feed for the output values. For any node in the other layers, nki ∈ N k , 0 < k < m, it represents
153   the intermediate values. The parameters of the model are represented as the edges in the structure.
154   Let eki,j ∈ E k , 1 ≤ k ≤ m be the the edge connecting nk−1  i    and nkj . Then the traditional tree-like
155   structure for NNs which is organized as an asynchronous hierarchical structure can be formalized as
156   follows: T = {N 0 , E 1 , N 1 , ...E m , N m }.
157
      In our work, we organize the intermediate structure as a general graph. The model is denoted by
158
      N M , N M = {N 0 , E 1 , G, E m , N m }, where G = {E, N } and ni ∈ N is the ith node in G,
159
      ei,j ∈ E is the edge from ni to nj . Let the number of the nodes in N be p, the number of the nodes
160
      in N 0 be |N 0 | and the number of the nodes in N m be |N m |.
161
      In this way, we would enhance the description of the NNs compared to traditional approaches.

      For convenience, we take G as a single neural module as stated earlier, this serves as a universal set
258   for all possible connections. In our work, we optimize the structure to prioritize important connec-
259   tions. The optimization process typically involves emphasizing crucial connections in the graph. In
260   this paper, we introduce parameter γ acting on the formation of Neural Modules as Definition 1. A
261   larger γ generates sparser graphs and results in smaller Neural Modules. Here, we propose a new
262   regularization method to formalize multiple balance Neural Modules, as introduced earlier. And we
263   term it as N M regularization.
264   The forward and backward process of the structure, as discussed earlier, involves solving a system of

268   subgraphs that formalize Neural Modules, as previously introduced. As each Neural Module does
269   not connect with others, we can calculate them in parallel. Compared with L1 regularization, N M
      regularization can get more balanced neural modules, which would bring additional benefits for

355   In this section, we present experiments conducted with our Neural Modules, comparing their perfor-
356   mance with traditional NN methods, implicit hidden layers (DEQ), a topological perspective treating
357   NN as a DAG, and recently introduced OPTNET. The results are tabulated for three real datasets, all
358   of which are available in the UCI dataset. For the regular function norm, we use the absolute value
359   of the wights.
360   The first dataset comprises codon usage frequencies in genomic coding DNA from a diverse sample
361   of organisms obtained from different taxa in the CUTG database. The second dataset includes
362   measurements from 16 chemical sensors exposed to six different gases at various concentration
363   levels. The third dataset involves smartphone-based recognition of human activities and postural
364   transitions, performing various activities. All these tasks represent classification problems, and we
365   evaluate performance based on the error of each algorithm.
366   First, we assessed the effectiveness of our NMs and other methods across various node complexities.
367   This evaluation allows us to understand how the NMs perform with different levels of complexity.
368   The nodes were initially organized using NN, DEQ, DAG as well as OPTNET, and their percentage
369   of error was observed. Our experiments involved comparing the performance of NMs, examining
370   their performance with frequently-used L1 regularization and the proposed N M regularization, as
371   introduced earlier. The results, as presented in the tables, reveal that our novel structure consistently
372   achieves superior results in most cases. Additionally, the performance of N M regularization sur-
373   passes that of the benchmark, L1 regularization in most situations. With the optimal performance
374
      achieved with a suitable number of nodes, our N M regularization is shown to perform the best
      across all the datasets.

1100   H    T HE P ROOF OF THE U NIVERSAL N EURAL M ODULE A PPROXIMATION
1101        T HEOREM
1102
1103   Note that the array of equations involves an activation function. Therefore, in our model, the sys-
1104   tem of functions is non-linear and possesses sufficient flexibility to fit any appropriate function,
1105   according to our Universal Neural Module Approximation Theorem.
1106
1107   Proof. In the forward process, the system of equations constructs implicit functions across all nodes
1108   within the neural module. Subsequently, any node ni can be transformed into an explicit func-
1109   tion. According to the Universal Approximation Theorem (Cybenko, 1989) (Hornik & White, 1990)
1110
       (Leshno & Schocken, 1993a), which states that for any continuous function on a compact set, there
       exists a one-hidden-layer feed-forward network capable of approximating the function, our system
1111
       satisfies this condition. This is because our array of equations involves an activation function, ful-
1112
       filling the requirement of the theorem. Therefore, the proof is complete.
