                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Recent advances in table understanding have focused on instruction-tuning large
               language models (LLMs) for table-related tasks. However, existing research has
011
               overlooked the impact of hyperparameter choices and lacks a comprehensive eval-
012
               uation of the out-of-domain table understanding ability and the general capabili-
013            ties of these table LLMs. In this paper, we evaluate these abilities in existing table
014            LLMs, and reveal significant declines in both out-of-domain table understand-
015            ing and general capabilities compared to their base models. Through systematic
016            analysis, we show that hyperparameters, such as learning rate, can significantly
017            influence both table-specific and general capabilities. Contrary to the existing ta-
018            ble instruction-tuning works, we demonstrate that smaller learning rates and fewer
019            training instances can enhance table understanding while preserving general ca-
020            pabilities. Based on our findings, we introduce TAMA, a TAble LLM instruction-
021
               tuned from LLaMA 3.1 8B Instruct, which achieves performance on par with,
               or surpassing GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 on table tasks, while maintaining strong out-
022
               of-domain generalization and general capabilities. Our findings highlight the po-
023
               tential for reduced data annotation costs and more efficient model development
024            through careful hyperparameter selection.

070   In this paper, we first evaluate the existing table LLMs in terms of their out-of-domain table un-
071   derstanding ability and their general abilities. We reveal that existing table LLMs suffer from a
072   significant decline in terms of these abilities compared to their base models. Sometimes, the perfor-
073   mance decline on general reasoning benchmarks, such as AI2ARC, can be up to 20 percentage.
074   We then select the latest LLaMA 3.1 8B Instruct model, and proceed to explore how hyperparameter
075   choices influence the model’s performance. Our analysis reveals that learning rate plays a crucial
076   role in shaping the model’s table understanding ability and influencing the model’s general ability. A
077   large learning rate, as seen in the existing table LLMs, compromises the model’s general capabilities
078   and leads to suboptimal table understanding performance. On the other hand, a small learning rate,
079   while effectively preserving the model’s general capabilities, fails to sufficiently improve its table
080
      understanding ability. In addition, we find that it is possible to achieve strong table understanding
      ability with a much smaller amount of training data – for instance, 2,600 in Section 4. Our training
081
      size is significantly smaller compared to the two million instances used by TableLLaMA (Zhang
082
      et al., 2024a), and ten times smaller than that of TableBenchLLM (Wu et al., 2024), highlighting
083   the potential to reduce annotation costs in future model development. We also explore the effects of
084   epoch numbers and the task synergy, and discuss our findings in Section 3.
085
086
      Based on our findings, we carefully select the hyperparameters and instruction-tune the LLaMA 3.1
      8B Instruct model, resulting in TAMA, which demonstrates strong table understanding ability and
087
      general capabilities (Figure 1).
088
089   In summary, our contributions are three folds:
090
091   • We examine the existing table LLMs and reveal that these table LLMs do not generalize to out-
092     of-domain table tasks and show compromised general capabilities compared to their base model.
093   • We reveal the impacts of the often-ignored hyperparameter selection such as the learning rate,
094     number of training instances, etc. We find that the commonly-adopted learning rate can be too
095     large, and may lead to suboptimal table understanding performance and compromises the model’s
096     general capabilities. In addition, we can achieve strong table understanding ability with a much
097     smaller amount of training data compared to the existing works.
098
      • Based on our findings, with careful hyperparameter selection, we instruction-tune LLaMA 3.1
099     8B Instruct model with 2,600 table instruction data. As an 8B size model, our resulting model,
100     TAMA achieves performance on par with, or even exceeding GPT-3.5 in table understanding
101     tasks, and in some cases surpasses GPT-4, while retaining the general capabilities of its base
102     model. Moreover, TAMA exhibits strong out-of-domain table understanding and general capabil-
103     ities (Figure 1).

      2.2   F INDINGS
150
151   Existing Table LLMs possess limited out-of-domain table understanding ability. In Table 3, all the
152   existing table LLMs suffer from performance drops on Table-Syn compared to their base mod-
153   els. Though these table LLMs achieve SOTA performance on various benchmarks (Zhang et al.,
154   2024a;b), such a performance decline reveals their limited out-of-domain table understanding capa-
155   bilities, which aligns with the findings by Zheng et al. (2024).
156   Existing Table LLMs demonstrate poor instruction-following ability. In Table 3, both TableLLaMA
157   and TableLLM show significant drops in performance on IFEval (Zhou et al., 2023), with accuracy
158   declines of 5.63 and 17.86, resulting in a score of 25.78 and 30.46, respectively. While TableBench-
159   LLM maintains a similar score to its base model (32.85 compared to 32.13 for LLaMA 3.1-8B), this
160   performance is still limited compared to 83.57 by GPT-4 reported by Zhou et al. (2023). At such
161   low instruction following scores, existing table LLMs cannot consistently follow instructions such
      as “return the answer in JSON format” as shown in Table 6 in Section 4.3 and Tables 17 to 19 in

      Appendix E, limiting the model’s usage if the end users need data extraction that requires certain
163   answer format.

165   Existing table instruction tuning compromises models’ general capabilities. Existing table

      instruction-tuning methods lead to significant drops in accuracy on general benchmarks such as
      MMLU, AI2ARC, GPQA as shown in Table 3. For instance, compared to their base models, TableL-

      3.2   A NALYSIS
198
199   Learning Rate. In Figure 2, we fine-tune the LLaMA 3.1 8B Instruct model using instruction data
200   from TabFact, HiTab, and FeTaQA.
201
202
      We find that the learning rate plays a crucial role in determining model performance, as well as
      how well the model preserves its general capabilities. In general, LLaMA 3.1 8B Instruct achieves
203
      the best performance when the learning rate is around 1.0e-6 and 5.0e-7. For instance, on TabFact,
204
      LLaMA 3.1 8B Instruct achieves its best performance (73.10) at a learning rate of 1.0e-6 with
205   1500 examples. Moreover, there is little to no decline in LLaMA 3.1 8B Instruct’s performance on
206   MMLU and IFEval with such learning rates. With a smaller learning rate such as 1.0e-7, though
207   the model’s performance on MMLU and IFEval can be well-preserved, the model’s performance on
208   table tasks such as FEVEROUS is suboptimal under the same setup (66.86 compared to 74.63 at a
209   learning rate of 5.0e-6). In contrast, when the learning rate is too large, such as 1.0e-5, we observe a
210   significant decline in the model’s performance on both MMLU and IFEval, suggesting that a larger
211   learning rate may hurt the model’s general capabilities. We note that all the existing table LLMs
212   use a large learning rate of 2e-5 (Table 1), which explains their compromised out-of-domain table
213
      understanding ability and general capabilities compared to their base models in Table 3.
214

      of quick learning followed by a period of marginal performance improvement.

      5   R ELATED W ORKS
487
488
      Table-Related Tasks. Tasks involving structured data, especially in the form of tables, have at-
489
      tracted interests from diverse communities (Deng et al., 2022a; Chen et al., 2022; Deng et al., 2022b).
490
      These tasks address diverse applications with different input-output formats. For instance, table
491   question answering (Table QA) answers the question given the table, either in the form of natural
492   sentences (Nan et al., 2022) or concise responses such as entities mentioned in the table, or numbers
493   (Pasupat & Liang, 2015; Zhong et al., 2017). Table fact verification verifies a claim given the table
494   (Chen et al., 2019; Gupta et al., 2020). Dialogue generation generates the response to the end user
495   given the table, and the dialogue history information (Eric & Manning, 2017) Table-to-text generates
496   a description based on the table content (Parikh et al., 2020).
497
498   Table Understanding Methods. The past decade has witnessed a paradigm shift in approaches
499   to table understanding. Before the advent of LLMs, researchers typically adapt model structures
500   to better interpret table data (Lebret et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2022). As language
501   models demonstrate promising performance on various tasks (Devlin et al., 2019), researchers grad-
502   ually shift their attention towards data-driven methods for table understanding. For instance, Yin
503   et al. (2020); Herzig et al. (2020) pre-train BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) or BERT-derived model on
504
      large-volume of table data from sources such as Wikipedia to acquire better table representations.
      Xie et al. (2022) reveal the synergy effects of various structured tasks, including many table tasks,
505
      laying foundations to build a generalist model for structured data. In the era of LLMs, as LLMs pos-
506
      sess innate table-understanding abilities, researchers also explore prompt engineering techniques to
507   optimize LLMs for table tasks (Chang & Fosler-Lussier, 2023; Deng et al., 2024).
508
509
      Table Instruction Tuning. Building on the advances in data-driven methods, researchers have in-
510
      creasingly focused on instruction tuning to enhance LLMs’ table understanding ability. As demon-
511
      strated by Touvron et al. (2023); Dubey et al. (2024); Chung et al. (2024), instruction-tuning can
512   improve model performance and generalization to unseen tasks. Meanwhile, models from the
513   open-source LLaMA family (Touvron et al., 2023) demonstrate strong capabilities, leading re-
514   searchers to instruction-tune these models for better table understanding. For instance, TableL-
515   LaMA (Zhang et al., 2024a) is instruction-tuned from a variant of LLaMA 2 model (Touvron et al.,
516   2023), TableLLM (Zhang et al., 2024b) is instruction-tuned from CodeLLaMA, Wu et al. (2024)
517   instruction-tune various foundational models such as LLaMA 3.1 (Dubey et al., 2024), resulting in
518   their TableBenchLLM model. Moreover, Zheng et al. (2024) treat tables as images and instruction-
519   tune Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023), a vision model that is originally fine-tuned from the LLaMA
520
      model, for table understanding. However, as revealed by Zheng et al. (2024); Deng et al. (2024),
      treating tables as texts rather than images yields better performance. In this paper, we focus on table
521
      instruction tuning with tables fed as texts.
522
523
524   6   C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this paper, we reveal the limited out-of-domain table understanding ability and general capabil-
527   ities of the existing table LLMs. From our analysis, we find that the commonly-adopted hyperpa-
528   rameters in existing table LLMs are suboptimal, and hyperparameter choices in table instruction
529   tuning are crucial in shaping the model’s capabilities. We select hyperparameters from our analysis,
530   and fine-tune our own model, TAMA. Notably, as an 8B model, TAMA demonstrates strong table
531   understanding ability, outperforming GPT-3.5 on most of the table understanding benchmarks, even
532   achieving performance on par or better than GPT-4. Moreover, TAMA preserves strong general capa-
533   bilities. We hope our findings as well as our model TAMA can facilitate future research on structured
534
      data.

908   C.1    M ODEL S ELECTION
909
910   Reasons to Select LLaMA 3.1. LLaMA 3.1 (Dubey et al., 2024) provides a set of foundational
911   models for language. Compared to the prior LLaMA models, LLaMA 3.1 claims to improve both
912   the quantity and the quality of the data used for pre-training and post-training (15T multilingual pre-
913   training tokens for LLaMA 3.1 compared to 1.8T tokens for LLaMA 2). Such an enormous amount
914   of training makes LLaMA 3.1 one of the most advanced open-source LLMs.
915
916   Reasons to Select the Instruct Version Rather than the Base Version. Currently, there are
917   two kinds of model selections for table instruction tuning, instruction-tuning the base version of
      the model, as seen in works like TableLLaMA(Zhang et al., 2024a) and TableBenchLLM(Wu


                                                                      17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      et al., 2024), or continuing instruction-tuning an already instruction-tuned version, as done with
919   TableLLM(Zhang et al., 2024b) as listed in Table 1.
920
921   As the end user may come up with their own set of instructions, we expect table instruction-tuned
922
      models to possess a strong general instruction-following ability. Imparting general instruction-
      following ability through table instruction-tuning to the base model is challenging, as there is a
923
      lack of diversity in the table instruction-tuning data. For instance, TableLLaMA employs six spe-
924
      cific instruction templates across two million data points, which pales in comparison to the diverse
925   instruction datasets in broader instruction tuning efforts such as those by Chung et al. (2024), which
926   include 1,836 tasks, each with a set of instruction templates. As shown in Figure 6c, when tuning
927   the base version of the LLaMA 3.1 8B model on instruction pairs on FeTaQA, HiTab, and TabFact,
928   the instruction following ability of the model does not improve significantly. Moreover, with a large
929   learning rate such as 1.0e-5, the model’s instruction following ability drops significantly when there
930   is more training data coming in.
931   We argue that the instruction-tuned version possesses strong general instruction-following capabili-
932   ties, eliminating the need to repeat the general instruction-tuning stage. Therefore, a more effective
933   strategy is to table instruction-tune an already instruction-tuned model, focusing on enhancing its ta-
934   ble understanding ability while preserving its general instruction-following capabilities. As shown
935   in Figure 2f, with proper hyperparameter selection, we can maintain the inherent strong instruction
936   following ability of the LLaMA 3.1 8B Instruct model.
937   In terms of specific table understanding tasks, tuning LLaMA 3.1 8B Instruct model yields better
938   performance than its base version on TabFact (73.10 in Figure 2a v.s. 71.10 in Figure 6a) under the
939   same experimental setup. Therefore, we select the LLaMA 3.1 8B Instruct model as our starting
940   model.
