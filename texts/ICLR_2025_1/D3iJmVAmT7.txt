009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Solving Partial Differential Equations (PDEs) is ubiquitous in science and engi-
012            neering. Computational complexity and difficulty in writing numerical solvers has
013            motivated the development of machine learning techniques to generate solutions
014            quickly. Many existing methods are purely data driven, relying solely on numer-
015            ical solution fields, rather than known system information such as boundary con-
016            ditions and governing equations. However, the recent rise in popularity of Large
017            Language Models (LLMs) has enabled easy integration of text in multimodal ma-
018            chine learning models. In this work, we use pretrained LLMs to integrate various
019
               amounts known system information into PDE learning. Our multimodal approach
               significantly outperforms our baseline model, FactFormer, in both next-step pre-
020
               diction and autoregressive rollout performance on the 2D Heat, Burgers, Navier-
021
               Stokes, and Shallow Water equations. Further analyis shows that pretrained LLMs
022            provide highly structured latent space that is consistent with the amound of system
023            information provided through text.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Solving Partial Differential Equations (PDEs) is the cornerstone of many areas of science and engi-
029
      neering, from quantum mechanics to fluid dynamics. While traditional numerical solvers often have
030   rigorous error bounds, they are limited in scope, where different methods are required for differ-
031   ent governing equations, and different regimes even for a single governing equation. In the area of
032   fluid dynamics, especially, solvers that are designed for Navier Stokes equations generally will not
033   perform optimally in both the laminar and turbulent flow regimes.
034
      Recently, machine learning methods have exploded in popularity to address these downsides in
035
      traditional numerical solvers. The primary aim is to reduce time-to-solution and bypass expensive
036   calculations. Physics Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)Raissi et al. (2019) have been widely
037   successful in small-scale systems. More recently, neural operators(Li et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2021;
038   Li et al., 2023a) have improved upon PINNs, showing promise for larger scale, general purpose
039   surrogate models. However, these models generally are purely data-driven, and do not use any
040   additional, known, system information.
041
      Additionally, owing to the success of large language models (LLMs) in many other domains, such
042   as robotics(Kapoor et al., 2024; Bartsch & Farimani, 2024), design(Kumar et al., 2023; Badagabettu
043   et al., 2024; Jadhav & Farimani, 2024; Jadhav et al., 2024), some works have begun incorporating
044   LLMs into the PDE surrogate model training pipeline. Universal Physics Solver (UPS)(Shen et al.,
045   2024) incorporates pretrained LLMs, but uses limited text descriptions that do not fully utilize LLM
046   capabilities. Unisolver(Zhou et al., 2024) takes in a LaTeX description of the system as a prompt, but
047   uses MLP encoders for additional system information such as boundary conditions, which does not
048   fully explore the capabilities of the LLM. ICON-LM(Yang et al., 2024) uses longer text descriptions,
049   but trains the LLM to make numerical predictions from input data and captions, adding additional
050
      complexity to the model architecture. Additionally, by using softmax-based attention for numerical
      predictions, benchmarks are limited to 1D.
051
052   Contributions: In this work, we aim to more fully utilize LLM understanding in PDE surrogate
053   modeling and incorporate text information into neural operators. To that end, we introduce a novel
      multimodal PDE framework given in figure 1. This framework is built on top of a FactFormer(Li

      2   R ELATED W ORK
081
082
      Neural Solvers: Neural solvers have been developed largely to counter the drawbacks of traditional
083
      numerical methods. Physics Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)(Raissi et al., 2019) incorporate
084
      governing equations through a soft constraint on the loss function. PINNs have been shown to be
085   effective in small-scale problems, but tend to be difficult to train(Wang et al., 2022; Rathore et al.,
086   2024). Neural operators(Kovachki et al., 2023) were developed and show improvement over PINNs
087   on a large variety of PDE learning tasks. Based on the universal operator approximation theorem,
088   neural operators learn a functional that maps input functions to solution functions. Neural operators
089   such as Fourier Neural Operator (FNO)(Li et al., 2021), based on kernel integral blocks in Fourier
090   space, DeepONetLu et al. (2021), based on parameterizing the input functions and an embedding of
091   the points at which the functions are evaluated, and OFormer(Li et al., 2023a), based on softmax-
092   free attention. Despite very promising results, neural operators are usually purely data-driven and
093
      do not use any system information outside of solution fields.
094   Utilizing System Information While these neural operators perform well, they are often purely
095   data driven. More recent works have incorporated various different aspects of the governing sys-
096   tems. Takamoto et al. (2023a) incorporates operator coefficients(Takamoto et al., 2023a) through
097   the CAPE module. While coefficients are important in determining system behavior, system pa-
098   rameters such as boundary conditions, forcing terms, and geometry can play an equally important
099
      role, and cannot be incorporated through CAPE. Further work incorporates governing equations
      into models. Lorsung et al. (2024) developed the PITT framework that uses a transformer-based
100
      architecture to process system information as text. Additionally, PROSE(Liu et al., 2023) poses a
101
      multi-objective task to both make a prediction and complete partially correct governing equation.
102   However, these frameworks rely on notational consistency between samples, and do not offer an
103   easy integration of different geometries. Lastly, Hao et al. (2023) introduced novel Heterogeneous
104   Normalized Attention and Geometric Gating mechanisms for flexible GNOT model. GNOT is able
105   to incorporate different system information, such as coefficients and geometry. This flexible archi-
106   tecture can incorporate many different system parameters, but requires additional implementation
107   details for each additional modality, and may not be able to capture qualitative aspects of systems,
      such as flow regime.

      We generate data from the Heat, Burgers, and Incompressible Navier Stokes equations, which are
129
      popular benchmarks for fluid dynamics surrogate models. Additionally, we benchmark on the Shal-
130   low Water equations from PDEBench(Takamoto et al., 2023b). Data for the Heat and Burgers equa-
131   tions are generated using Py-PDE(Zwicker, 2020) due to ease of simulating different boundary con-
132   ditions, and data is generated for Navier Stokes using code from Fourier Neural Operator(Li et al.,
133   2021). A diverse data set is generated in order to create a more challenging setup than existing
134   benchmarks, that often have the same operator coefficients and boundary conditions for all samples,
135   only varying the initial conditions. In our case, we use different initial conditions, operator coeffi-
136   cients, and boundary conditions. While existing data sets offer distinct challenges with regards to
137   governing equations, they do not offer a lot of data diversity with regard to system parameters, which
138
      LLMs are well-suited to handle. PDEBenchTakamoto et al. (2023b), for example, has multiple 2D
      data sets that represent a variety of physical processes. However, only eight different operator coef-
139
      ficient combinations are used for the 2D Compressible Navier Stokes equations, all with the same
140
      boundary conditions. In order to fully utilize the capabilities of pretrained LLMs, as well as present
141   a more challenging benchmark, we vary boundary conditions and operator coefficients for the Heat
142   and Burgers equations in 2D, and vary viscosity and forcing term amplitude for the Incompressible
143   Navier-Stokes equations in 2D.

      5     R ESULTS
289
290
      We benchmark our multimodal model against its baseline variant on a number of challenging tasks.
291   Our data vary the distribution of initial conditions, operator coefficients, and boundary conditions,
292   which provides a much more challenging setting than many existing benchmarks. In each experi-
293   ment, the combined data set is the Heat, Burgers, and Navier-Stokes data sets, where Shallow Water
294   is used solely for fine-tuning, with a 90-10-10 train-validation-test split. 4,000 samples per equation
295   are used for Heat, Burgers, and Navier-Stokes data unless otherwise noted, and we use the entire
296   1,000 sample Shallow-Water data set when using more samples for our other data sets. We use one
297   frame of data to predict the next frame, which allows us to asses how well our multimodal approach
298   captures known system information, which is the operator Gθ (a) (x, s)t=n → u(x)t=n+1 . That is
299
      the input function at points x and time t = n conditioned on sentence information s is used to predict
      our solution function at time t = n + 1. Using only a single step of input here presents the additional
300
      challenge that it is difficult to infer boundary conditions and impossible to infer operator coefficients
301
      from only a single frame of data. Our temporal horizon is limited to 21 steps for each equation
302   during training (initial condition and 20 steps), and 40 steps during autoregressive rollout evalua-
303   tion. Relative L2 Error(Li et al., 2021) is used for both training and next-step evaluation in all of
304   our experiments. Autoregressive rollout error is reported as Mean Squared Error. In our results, we
305   used SentenceTransformer (ST)(Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) and Llama 3.1 8B (Llama)(AI@Meta,
306   2024), using various combinations of boundary condition (B), coefficient (C) and qualitative (Q)
307   information. Each reported result is the mean and standard deviation across three random seeds.
308

429   descriptions, rather than complex data conditioning strategies. We see for our next-step prediction
430   and autoregressive rollout tasks that a multimodal approach is able to significantly improve perfor-
431   mance over baseline. SentenceTransformer tends to improve performance more than Llama, and
      this is likely due to the nature of the embeddings generated by each LLM. SentenceTransformer

      is trained to generate useful embeddings at the sentence level, whereas Llama is trained to gener-
433   ate word-level embeddings. In order to utilize Llama for out framework, we need to average over
434   all word-level embeddings, which can lose subtle information in our embeddings. The sentence-
435   level embeddings from SentenceTransformer, on the other hand, allows us to use these embeddings
436   directly from the LLM in our multimodal cross-attention block.

      7   C ONCLUSION
470
471
      We introduced a multimodal approach to PDE surrogate modeling that utilizes LLMs and sentence
472   descriptions of our systems that significantly improves performance for a variety of tasks. Our mul-
473   timodal approach allows us to easily incorporate system information that both captures quantitative
474   and qualitative aspects of our governing equations. Analysis of our sentence embeddings shows
475   that we are able to capture increasing amounts of underlying structure in our data by simply adding
476   more information through text, rather than introducing additional mathematical approaches, such as
477   constrained loss functions that are popular with PINNs.
478   While this direction is promising, performance could be further improved with tuning out LLMs
479   directly, rather than just training an embedding layer that uses LLM output, and we leave this to
480   future work. Additionally, pretraining strategies have shown success in various LLM applications
481   from natural language processing to PDE surrogate modeling. While this does introduce significant
482   computational overhead, it may improve performance and is also a potential future direction to
483   take. Lastly, we only benchmarked the FactFormer, but this framework and multimodal block is
484   not specific to FactFormer, and may prove useful for improving other physics-based models such as
485   FNO and DeepONet.
