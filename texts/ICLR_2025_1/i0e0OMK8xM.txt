009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) aligns large language mod-
012            els by encouraging their generations to have high rewards, using a reward model
013            trained on human preferences. To prevent forgetting of pre-trained knowledge,
014            RLHF usually incorporates a KL regularization; this forces the policy to remain
015            close to its initialization, though it hinders the reward optimization. To address the
016
               trade-off between KL and reward, in this paper we introduce a novel alignment
               strategy named Weight Averaged Rewarded Policies (WARP), merging policies
017
               in the weight space at three distinct stages. First, it uses the exponential moving
018
               average of the policy as a dynamic anchor in the KL regularization. Second, it
019            applies spherical interpolation to merge independently fine-tuned policies into a
020            new enhanced one. Third, it linearly interpolates between this merged model and
021            the initialization, to recover features from pre-training. This procedure is then
022            applied iteratively, with each iteration’s final model used as an advanced initial-
023            ization for the next, progressively refining the KL-reward trade-off, achieving
024            superior rewards at fixed KL. Experiments with Gemma policies validate that
025            WARP improves their quality and alignment, outperforming open-source models.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      LLM alignment. Large language models (LLMs) like Gemini (Gemini Team, 2023) and GPT-4
030
      (OpenAI, 2023), along with their open-weight counterparts (Jiang et al., 2023; Gemma Team et al.,
031   2024), demonstrate remarkable abilities as chatbots, but also for tasks like mathematics and coding
032   (Bubeck et al., 2023). These capabilities largely emerge from pre-training on next-token prediction
033   (Radford et al., 2018; 2019), subsequently refined through supervised fine-tuning (SFT) (Raffel et al.,
034   2020; Wei et al., 2022). As these LLMs become more powerful, aligning them with human values
035   becomes increasingly crucial to ensure safe deployment (Amodei et al., 2016; Hendrycks & Mazeika,
036   2022). To this end, reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) has become the prominent
037   strategy (Christiano et al., 2017; Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon et al., 2020), first learning a reward
038   model (RM) on human preferences, before optimizing the LLM to maximize predicted rewards.
039   Challenges in RLHF. However, RLHF introduces several unresolved challenges (Casper et al.,
040   2023). First, the limited scope of fine-tuning, often restricted to relatively small datasets, can lead
041   to excessive specialization and catastrophic forgetting (French, 1992) of the broad and diverse
042   knowledge acquired during pre-training (Goodfellow et al., 2013; Li & Hoiem, 2017; Kirkpatrick
043   et al., 2017; Kumar et al., 2022). Such alignment tax (Ouyang et al., 2022) can degrade the LLM’s
044   reasoning capabilities and performance on NLP benchmarks (Dong et al., 2023a; Lin et al., 2024a).
045
      Second, maximizing an imperfect RM presents several issues on its own, as the LLM can learn to
      exploit loopholes in the RM (Clark & Amodei, 2016; Pan et al., 2022) when it deviates significantly
046
      from its initialization (Gao et al., 2023). Such reward hacking (Askell et al., 2021; Skalse et al., 2022)
047
      can produce outputs that are linguistically flawed (Lewis et al., 2017), excessively verbose (Singhal
048   et al., 2023), or sycophantic (Perez et al., 2022; Sharma et al., 2023), thereby raising misalignment
049   (Taylor et al., 2016; Ngo et al., 2022) and safety (Amodei et al., 2016; Hendrycks & Mazeika, 2022)
050   concerns. Finally, RLHF can reduce the diversity of generations (Kirk et al., 2024), potentially
051   leading to policy collapse (Moalla et al., 2024; Hamilton, 2024). Such loss of diversity limits use in
052   creative or exploratory tasks and can result in the LLM systematically refusing to answer. Overall,
053   achieving high rewards based on an imperfect RM on a selected distribution of prompts is insufficient
      due to potential reward misspecification and distribution shifts upon deployment.

079   RL with KL regularization. To address these issues, previous works constrained the reward
080   optimization by integrating a Kullback-Leibler (KL) regularization (Jaques et al., 2017; Geist et al.,
081   2019), using the SFT initialization as the anchor. As clarified in Section 2, this KL regularization
082   forces the policy to remain close to its initialization (Lazaridou et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2020), mitigating
083   forgetting and reward hacking (Gao et al., 2023). However, employing the SFT model as the anchor
084   may lead to reward underfitting: indeed, there is a fundamental tension between reducing KL and
085   maximizing reward. Thus, different policies should be compared in terms of trade-off between
086   KL-reward as in Figure 1(b), where the x-axis is the KL and the y-axis is the reward as estimated by
087
      the RM, with the optimal policies located in the top-left of the plot.
088   On model merging by weight averaging. To improve the trade-off between KL and reward during
089   RLHF, we leverage the ability to merge LLMs by weight averaging (WA) (Utans, 1996). WA
090   relies on the linear mode connectivity (Frankle et al., 2020; Neyshabur et al., 2020), an empirical
091   observation revealing linear paths of high performance between models fine-tuned from a shared
092   pre-trained initialization. Model merging was shown to improve robustness under distribution shifts
093
      (Izmailov et al., 2018; Wortsman et al., 2022a; Ramé et al., 2022) by promoting generalization
      and reducing memorization (Ramé et al., 2024), to combine models’ abilities (Ilharco et al., 2023;
094
      2022; Ramé et al., 2023), to reduce forgetting in continual learning (Stojanovski et al., 2022), to
095
      enable collaborative (Raffel, 2023) and distributed (Douillard et al., 2023) learning at scale, without
096   computational overheads at inference time. Model merging is increasingly adopted within the
097   open-source community (Goddard et al., 2024; Lambert & Morrison, 2024), leading to state-of-
098   the-art models in specialized domains (Labrak et al., 2024) but also significant advancements on
099   general-purpose benchmarks (Labonne, 2024b;a). In particular, while WA was initially mostly used
100   for discriminative tasks (Wortsman et al., 2022a) such as reward modeling (Ramé et al., 2024), it
101   is now becoming popular for generative tasks (Rofin et al., 2022; Akiba et al., 2024); its use in
102   KL-constrained RLHF has already shown preliminary successes in a few recent works (Ramé et al.,
103   2023; Noukhovitch et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2024; Gorbatovski et al., 2024; Munos
104
      et al., 2023), further elaborated in Section 5.
105   WARP. In this paper, we propose Weight Averaged Rewarded Policies (WARP), a simple strategy for
106   aligning LLMs, illustrated in Figure 1(a) and detailed in Section 3. WARP is designed to optimize the
107   KL-reward Pareto front of solutions, as demonstrated in Figure 1(b). WARP uses three variants of
      WA at three different stages of the alignment procedure, for three distinct reasons.

109       Stage 1: Exponential Moving Average (EMA). During RL fine-tuning, instead of regularizing
110       the policy towards the SFT initialization, WARP uses the policy’s own exponential moving
111
          average (Polyak & Juditsky, 1992) as a dynamic updatable anchor in the KL. This stage
          enables stable exploration with distillation from an EMA teacher (Tarvainen & Valpola, 2017)
112
          and annealed constraint.
113
114
115       Stage 2: Spherical Linear intERPolation of task vectors (SLERP). Considering M policies
116
          RL fine-tuned independently with their own EMA anchor, we merge them by spherical linear
          interpolation (Shoemake, 1985) of their task vectors (Ilharco et al., 2023). This stage creates a
117
          merged model with higher reward by combining the strengths of the M individual policies.
118
119
120       Stage 3: Linear Interpolation Towards Initialization (LITI). Considering the merged policy
121       from SLERP, WARP linearly interpolates towards the initialization, akin to WiSE-FT (Worts-
122       man et al., 2022b). This stage allows to run through an improved Pareto front simply by
123
          adjusting the interpolating coefficient η between 1 (high reward but high KL) and 0 (small KL
          but small reward). Critically, selecting an intermediate value for 0 < η < 1 offers a balanced
124
          model that can serve as a new and improved initialization for subsequent iterations of WARP.

168   We introduce a novel alignment framework named Weight Averaged Rewarded Policies (WARP),
169   illustrated in Figure 1(a) and described in Algorithm 1 below. WARP merges LLMs in the weight
170   space to enhance the KL-reward front of policies. The following Sections 3.1 to 3.3 describe the
171
      motivations behind applying three distinct variants of WA at the three different stages of WARP. In
      particular, we summarize the key insights as observations, that will be experimentally validated in
172
      Section 4 (and in Appendices C and D), and theoretically motivated in Appendix B when possible.
173
      Overall, we observe that WARP outperforms other RL alignment strategies, without any memory or
174   inference overhead at test time. However, training WARP is costly, requiring multiple RL runs at each
175   iteration: see Section 6 for a detailed discussion on the required compute scaling.

199   3.1   S TAGE 1: EXPONENTIAL MOVING AVERAGE AS A DYNAMIC ANCHOR IN KL
200         REGULARIZATION
201
202   EMA anchor. RLHF algorithms typically use the SFT initialization as a static anchor (Jaques et al.,
203   2017; Roit et al., 2023) in the KL regularization, but in RL (notably for control tasks) it is common
204   to regularly update the anchor (Schulman et al., 2015; Abdolmaleki et al., 2018). In this spirit,
205
      WARP uses the policy’s own exponential moving average (EMA) (Polyak & Juditsky, 1992), updated
      throughout the RL fine-tuning process such as, at each training step with µ = 0.01:
206
207                                   θema ← (1 − µ) · θema + µ · θpolicy .                          (EMA)
208   Using θema as the anchor θanchor in Equation (1) provides several benefits, outlined below.
209
      Observation 1 (EMA). Policies trained with an exponential moving average anchor benefit from
210
      automatic annealing of the KL regularization and from distillation from a dynamic mean teacher (Tar-
211   vainen & Valpola, 2017). Empirical evidence in Section 4.1.
212
213   Benefits from EMA. Unlike a static SFT anchor, the dynamic nature of an EMA anchor induces
214   a gradual automatic annealing and relaxation of the KL regularization. Specifically, the policy is
215   initially strongly tied to the SFT initialization, and then progressively unleashed, allowing for more
      aggressive gradient updates later in training, leading to higher rewards. Moreover, by progressively


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      incorporating knowledge from the training, EMA acts as slow weight (Stojanovski et al., 2022; Lee
217   et al., 2024b), and thus performing better than the initialization. But, by also maintaining essential
218   information from the initialization, EMA can even perform better than the final policy’s weights;
219   studies (Szegedy et al., 2016; Izmailov et al., 2018; Arpit et al., 2021) (see Morales-Brotons et al.
220   (2024) for a review), and specifically (Kaddour, 2022) within the context of LLMs, indicate that
221   averaging checkpoints over steps improves internal representations and thus predictions. Then, EMA
222   guides the policy by KL distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) of high-quality target predictions, akin to
223   a mean teacher (Tarvainen & Valpola, 2017) for self-supervised (Sohn et al., 2020; He et al., 2020;
224   Oquab et al., 2024; Caron et al., 2021; Grill et al., 2020) learning. This also relates to deep RL
225
      techniques where EMA stabilizes exploration toward a Nash equilibrium (Awheda & Schwartz, 2013;
      2016; Gorbatovski et al., 2024; Munos et al., 2023), and approximates mirror descent (Bubeck et al.,
226
      2015; Geist et al., 2019; Tomar et al., 2020).

269   Adjusting the interpolating coefficient η ∈ [0, 1] trades off between some newly acquired behaviors
      leading to high rewards vs. general knowledge from the SFT initialization. Specifically, large values

      η ≈ 1 provide high rewards but also high KL, while smaller values η ≈ 0 lean towards smaller
271   rewards and minimal KL. Fortunately, we observe that the reduction in KL is proportionally greater
272   than the reduction in reward when decreasing η. Then, LITI empirically yields Pareto fronts that are
273   noticeably above the “diagonal”, but also above those revealed during the base RL training runs.

278   Benefits from LITI. Previous works tried to understand how weight interpolation can mitigate
279   forgetting while increasing robustness and generalization. Wortsman et al. (2022b) argue that WiSE-

281   that might be lost during fine-tuning (Kumar et al., 2022), consistently with WA reducing catastrophic
282

283

      3.4   I TERATIVE WARP
287
288   Iterative training. The model merging strategies previously described not only establish an improved
289   Pareto front of solutions, but also set the stage for iterative improvements. Indeed, if the computational
290   budget is sufficient, we can apply those three stages iteratively, using θη from previous Pareto front
291   (usually with η = 0.3, choice ablated in Appendix D.3) as the initialization θinit for the next iteration,
292   following the model recycling (Don-Yehiya et al., 2023; Ramé et al., 2023) strategies. Then, the
293   entire training procedure is made of multiple iterations, each consisting of those three stages, where
294
      the final weight from a given iteration serves as an improved initialization for the next one.
295   Observation 6 (Iterative WARP). The application of WARP iteratively progressively refines the
296   Pareto front. Empirical evidence in Sections 4.4 and 4.5.

300   Setup. We consider the Gemma 7B (Gemma Team et al., 2024) LLM, which we seek to fine-tune
301   with RLHF into a better conversational agent. We use REINFORCE (Williams, 1992) policy gradient
302   to optimize the KL-regularized reward. The dataset X contains conversation prompts. We generate
303   on-policy samples with temperature 0.9, batch size of 128, Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) optimizer
304   with learning rate 10−6 and warmup of 100 steps. SLERP is applied independently to the 28 layers.
305   Except when stated otherwise, we train for T = 9k steps, with KL strength β = 0.1, EMA update
306   rate µ = 0.01, merging M = 2 policies uniformly λ = 0.5, and LITI update rate η = 0.3; we analyze
307
      those values in Appendix D. We rely on a high capacity reward model, whose architecture is an order
      of magnitude larger than our policy.

312   trained independently helps. Moreover, in Section 4.3, we show that LITI improves the KL-reward

      Pareto front; critically, repeating those three WARP stages can iteratively improve performances
      in Section 4.4. A limitation is that our RM accurately approximates true human preferences only

      in low KL region (Gao et al., 2023). Therefore, we finally report other metrics in Section 4.5,

      specifically comparing against open-source baselines such as Mixtral (Jiang et al., 2024), and
316   reporting performance on standard benchmarks such as MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020).

450   5    R ELATED WORK
451
452   How to merge models. The most common model merging strategy is LERP, initially used to average
453   checkpoints collected along a single run, uniformly (Szegedy et al., 2016; Izmailov et al., 2018) or
454   with an exponential moving average (EMA) (Polyak & Juditsky, 1992). Following the linear mode
455   connectivity (Frankle et al., 2020) observation, the model soups variants (Wortsman et al., 2022a;
456   Ilharco et al., 2023; Ramé et al., 2023) linearly interpolate from different fine-tunings; this relies
457   on the shared pre-training, limiting divergence (Neyshabur et al., 2020) such as models remain in
458
      constrained weight regions (Gueta et al., 2023), which also suggests that pre-training mitigates the
      need to explicitly enforce trust regions in gradient updates (Schulman et al., 2015; 2017). Subsequent
459
      works such as TIES merging (Yadav et al., 2023) and DARE (Yu et al., 2023) reduce interferences in
460
      multi-task setups with sparse task vectors (Ilharco et al., 2023). In contrast, we use SLERP, introduced
461   in Shoemake (1985), increasingly popular in the open-source community (Goddard et al., 2024) but
462   relatively underexplored in the academic literature, with limited studies such as Kim et al. (2024).
463   Some tried to align weights trained from scratch (Entezari et al., 2022; Ainsworth et al., 2022) or
464   with different architectures (Wan et al., 2024); yet, the methods are complex, less robust, and usually
465   require additional training.
466
      Benefits of model merging. WA boosts generalization by reducing variance (Wortsman et al., 2022a;
467   Ramé et al., 2022), decreasing memorization (Lin et al., 2024b; Zaman et al., 2023; Ramé et al.,
468   2024) and flattening the loss landscape (Cha et al., 2021). Additionally, merging weights combines
469   their strengths (Ilharco et al., 2023), which helps in multi-task setups (Ilharco et al., 2022; Ramé
470   et al., 2023), to tackle forgetting (Stojanovski et al., 2022; Eeckt et al., 2022; Alexandrov et al., 2024)
471   or to provide better initializations (Don-Yehiya et al., 2023), as explored in Jain et al. (2023); Jang
472   et al. (2024); Huang et al. (2024) for iterative procedures in classification tasks. In particular, we
473   considered using the geometric insights from Eq. 2 in Jang et al. (2024); yet, as our task vectors
474   are nearly orthogonal Ω ≈ 90◦ (see Appendix C.2), using the update rule η → 1+cos       2 cos Ω
                                                                                                    Ω failed. WA
475   is now also used in RL setups (Nikishin et al., 2018; Gaya et al., 2022; Lawson & Qureshi, 2023);
476   for example, WARM (Ramé et al., 2024) merges reward models to boost their efficiency, robustness
477
      and reliability. Actually, WARP is conceived as a response to WARM, demonstrating that model
      merging can tackle two key RLHF challenges; policy learning in WARP and reward design in WARM.
478
      The most similar works are the following, which also explore how WA can improve policy learning.
479
      Noukhovitch et al. (2023) propose an iterative approach with the EMA as a new initialization for
480   subsequent iterations. Gorbatovski et al. (2024) and Munos et al. (2023) use EMA as the reference,
481   but only for direct preference optimization. Ramé et al. (2023) employ LERP to improve alignment
482   in multi-objective RLHF when dealing with different objectives; similarly, Xiao et al. (2023) target
483   multi-task setups with LERP. Finally, Lin et al. (2024a) and Fu et al. (2024) use model merging
484   to reduce the alignment tax, although without incorporating EMA during training, without merging
485   multiple rewarded policies and not iteratively. Critically, none of these works focus on KL as a
      measure of forgetting, use EMA as the anchor in KL, apply SLERP or use LITI as the initialization

490   6    D ISCUSSION
491
492   Distributed learning for parallelization and open-source. WARP addresses a crucial challenge:
493
      aligning LLMs with human values and societal norms, while preserving the capabilities that emerged
      from pre-training. To this end, we leverage a (perhaps surprising) ability: policies trained in parallel
494
      can combine their strengths within a single policy by weight averaging. Then, the distributed nature
495
      of WARP makes it flexible and scalable, as it is easily parallelizable by enabling intermittent weight
496   sharing across workers. Actually, iterative WARP shares similarities with DiLoCo (Douillard et al.,
497   2023): by analogy, the first stage performs inner optimization on multiple workers independently;
498   the second stage merges gradients from different workers; the third stage performs SGD outer
499   optimization with a learning rate equal to η. More generally, WARP could facilitate open-source
500   (Goddard et al., 2024) collaborative training of policies (Raffel, 2023), optimizing resource and
501   supporting privacy in federated learning (McMahan et al., 2017) scenarios; collaborators could
502   train and share their LLMs, while keeping their data and RMs private. In particular, we show in
503   Appendix E that WARP can handle diverse objectives.
504   Iterated amplification. WARP improves LLM alignment by leveraging the principles of iterated
505   amplification (Christiano et al., 2018) and progressive collaboration of multiple agents. By analogy,
506   model merging via WA acts as an effective alternative to debate (Irving et al., 2018), with agents
507   communicating within the weight space instead of the token space, ensuring that only essential
508   information is retained (Ramé et al., 2024). Then, WARP refines the training signal by combining
509   insights and exploration from diverse models, iteratively achieving higher rewards through self-
510
      distillation (Tarvainen & Valpola, 2017), surpassing the capabilities of any single agent. If this is the
      way forward, then an iterative safety assessment would be required to detect and mitigate potential
511
      risks early, ensuring that the development remains aligned with safety standards.
512
513   Scaling alignment. The WARP procedure increases the compute training cost by performing multiple
514   fine-tunings at each iteration. Yet, this should be viewed as “a feature rather than a bug”. Specifically,
515   by preventing memorization and forgetting, we see WARP as a fine-tuning method that can transform
516
      additional compute allocated to alignment into enhanced capabilities and safety. This would allow
      scaling (the traditionally cheap) post-training alignment, in the same way pre-training has been
517
      scaled (Hoffmann et al., 2022). Indeed, historically, pre-training efforts have benefited much more
518
      from compute scaling, fine-tuning efforts remaining significantly cheaper. Critically for large-scale
519   deployment, the acquired knowledge is within a single (merged) model, thus without inference
520   or memory overhead, in contrast to “more agents” approaches (Li et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024).
521   Finally, although WARP improves policy optimization, it is important to recognize that WARP does not
522   address other critical challenges in RLHF (Casper et al., 2023): to mitigate the safety risks (Amodei
523   et al., 2016; Hendrycks & Mazeika, 2022; Hendrycks, 2023) from misalignment (Taylor et al., 2016;
524   Ngo et al., 2022), WARP should be part of a broader responsible AI framework.
525
526   7    C ONCLUSION
527
528   We introduce Weight Averaged Rewarded Policies (WARP), a novel RLHF strategy to align LLMs
529   with three distinct stages of model merging: exponential moving average as a dynamic anchor during
530   RL, spherical interpolation to combine multiple policies rewarded independently, and interpolation
531   towards the shared initialization. This iterative application of WARP improves the KL-reward Pareto
532   front, aligning the LLMs while protecting the knowledge from pre-training, and compares favorably
533   against state-of-the-art baselines. We hope WARP could contribute to safe and powerful AI systems
534   by scaling alignment, and spur further exploration of the magic behind model merging.

       C.1   E MPIRICAL INSIGHTS ON THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN SLERP AND LERP

1232   We now empirically investigate how those theoretical differences between SLERP and LERP affect
1233   the performance of the merged policies.
1234
       SLERP vs. LERP. In Figure 8 we adjust the interpolating coefficient λ, highlighting distinct
1235
       behaviors for SLERP and LERP. SLERP consistently enhances rewards more than LERP, as depicted
1236   in Figures 3(c) and 8(a). However, a comprehensive evaluation must consider both KL and reward.
1237   As shown in Figure 8(b), LERP consistently reduces KL, corroborating with Lemma 2 that LERP
1238   reduces the norm of updates (while SLERP preserves it). When plotting these metrics together in
1239   Figure 8(c), we observe that SLERP and LERP target different regions on the Pareto front: SLERP
1240   achieves higher rewards at the expense of increased KL, while the main impact of LERP is to lower
1241   KL. This is consistent with Lemmas 2 and 3, be given the orthogonal angles between task vectors
       Ω ≈ 90◦ (as shown in Figure 11(a)).

1316    C.2                 E MPIRICAL INSIGHTS ON THE ROLE OF TASK VECTORS
1317
1318   We now explore the effectiveness of applying SLERP on task vectors δ vs. full weights θ, as illustrated
1319   in Figure 6. To this end, in Figure 11 we draw inspiration from Jang et al. (2024) and plot the angles
1320
       Ω and ω and norms of δ and θ.
1321   Angles of task vectors Ω ≈ 90◦ . Figure 11(a) shows that the task vectors are typically orthogonal
1322   (Ω ≈ 90◦ ), highlighting the diverse trajectories of the different RL fine-tunings. This is in contrast
1323   with (Jang et al., 2024) for supervised fine-tunings, where Ω typically range between 40◦ and 80◦ .
1324   We suspect that this is related to the underlying differences between reinforcement and supervised
1325   learning; in RL the policies are trained on their own generations, creating more orthogonal task
1326
       vectors, whereas in supervised learning the LLM try to imitate the groundtruth labels, leading to
       more similar task vectors. The orthogonality of our task vectors prevents the use of the update rule
1327          2 cos Ω
       η → 1+cos    Ω suggested from Eq. 2 in Jang et al. (2024), as it would lead to η ≈ 0, deleting any
1328
       potential update.
1329
1330   Angles of full weights ω ≈ 0◦ . In contrast, Figure 11(b) show that full weights remain collinear
1331   (ω ≈ 0◦ ). This explains the empirical results from Figure 10(c), where applying SLERP directly to
1332   full weights results in behaviors similar to LERP. Indeed, as the angles ω ≈ 0◦ , spherical interpolation
1333   effect is minimal because sin(x) ≈ x + O(x3 ), and thus sin[λω]        λω
                                                                    sin ω ≈ ω ≈ λ.
1334   Norms consistency. Figure 11(c) confirms the consistency in the norms of different task vectors,
1335   supporting our Assumption 1. This uniformity is aligned with previous research (Jang et al., 2024).
1336   As a side note, this consistency extends to full weights θ, confirming that fine-tuning typically results
1337   in minimal changes to the overall weight (Neyshabur et al., 2020).

1553    Problem: length bias. We investigate a potential length bias in WARP. LLMs after RLHF tend to
1554    be unnecessarily verbose (Shen et al., 2023) because RMs often prefer longer generations to shorter
1555    ones, leading to this form of reward hacking. We confirm such a phenomenon in Figure 18(a), where
1556
        the length of the generation increases with higher KL values. This trend is even more pronounced in
        iterative WARP, where the 3rd iteration generates longer sentences than the 1st iteration at same KL.
1557
1558    Mitigation strategy: length penalty. To mitigate this length bias, we integrate a length penalty (LP)
1559    into the reward: −0.0005 × len(y), following Singhal et al. (2023). From SFT, we launch one RL
1560    fine-tuning run with LP, highlighted with red stars in Figure 18(b). This LP leads to shorter outputs
1561    as KL increases along training, in contrast to policies trained without LP.
1562    SLERP with different configurations. Figure 18(b) displays the generation lengths from a SLERP
1563    of two policies, one trained with the LP and the other without. Critically, merging policies from
1564    diverse training configurations not only mitigates the length bias but also improves the Pareto front, as
1565    illustrated in Figure 18(c). This improvement is likely due to the increased diversity across policies,
        which appears beneficial for generalization, as shown in supervised learning (Ramé et al., 2022).
