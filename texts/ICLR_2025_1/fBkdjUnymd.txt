009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite the impressive generative abilities of black-box large language models
012            (LLMs), their inherent opacity hinders further advancements in capabilities such
013            as reasoning, planning, and personalization. Existing works aim to enhance LLM
014            capabilities via domain-specific adaptation or in-context learning, which require
015            additional training on accessible model parameters, an infeasible option for black-
016
               box LLMs. To address this challenge, we introduce Matryoshka, a lightweight
               white-box LLM controller that guides a large-scale black-box LLM generator by
017
               decomposing complex tasks into a series of intermediate outputs. Specifically, we
018
               consider the black-box LLM as an environment, with Matryoshka serving as
019            a policy to provide intermediate guidance through prompts for driving the black-
020            box LLM. Matryoshka is trained to pivot the outputs of the black-box LLM
021            aligning with preferences during iterative interaction, which enables controllable
022            multi-turn generation and self-improvement in optimizing intermediate guidance.
023            Empirical evaluations on three diverse tasks demonstrate that Matryoshka effec-
024            tively enhances the capabilities of black-box LLMs in complex, long-horizon tasks,
025            including reasoning, planning, and personalization. By leveraging this pioneer-
026            ing controller-generator framework to mitigate dependence on model parameters,
027
               Matryoshka provides a transparent and practical solution for improving black-
               box LLMs through controllable multi-turn generation using white-box LLMs.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Most of the commercial large language models (LLMs) (Radford et al., 2019; Brown, 2020; Achiam
033   et al., 2023; Chowdhery et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Reid et al., 2024) are black-box models (Sun
034   et al., 2024b; Zhuang et al., 2024), where the model structure, parameters, or even output logits are not
035   accessible. Although these black-box LLMs have exhibited remarkable efficacy across a diverse array
036   of applications, revolutionizing natural language processing tasks such as text completion (Radford
037   et al., 2019; Brown, 2020), translation (Zhu et al., 2023), question-answering (Hendrycks et al., 2020),
038   etc, the applications of black-box LLMs continue to face significant challenges when faced with tasks
039
      that require more advanced cognitive capabilities, particularly in the realms of reasoning (Hendrycks
      et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024b), planning (Valmeekam et al., 2022; Zhuang et al., 2023; Jimenez
040
      et al., 2023; Mialon et al., 2023), and personalization problems (Salemi et al., 2023; Tan et al., 2024a).
041
      Enhancing such capabilities within black-box LLMs presents unique challenges, primarily due to the
042   lack of direct access to internal model parameters (Huang et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024b; Zhuang
043   et al., 2024). This opacity introduces substantial complexity in efforts to refine and augment these
044   advanced cognitive functions within the framework of black-box architectures.
045
      Existing research efforts for improving black-box LLM performance can be largely categorized into
046
      two main methodological paradigms (Figure 1): (1) In-context learning (ICL)-based methods (Sun
047
      et al., 2024a; Tan et al., 2024b; Zhuang et al., 2024) that are designed to guide LLM in exhibiting
048   specific capabilities or adhering to particular directives. However, these frameworks necessitate
049   meticulously constructing few-shot demonstrations or prompts for LLMs to emulate or follow, rather
050   than fundamentally advancing their intrinsic capabilities. (2) Adapter-based methods (Sun et al.,
051   2024b; Zhuang et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024) that exploit the inherent randomness in LLM generation,
052   producing multiple candidate outputs and subsequently selecting those that optimally satisfy domain-
053   predetermined criteria. Nevertheless, these approaches are highly dependent on the intrinsic synthetic
      capabilities or built-in functionalities of the black-box LLM, potentially resulting in the selection of a

066   adapter-based methodologies exhibit significant limitations when applied to long-horizon tasks (e.g.,
067   multi-step reasoning, long-term planning, etc.) due to their inherent lack of environmental interaction
068   capabilities. In light of these constraints, we propose to leverage smaller, open-source LLMs as
069   controllers to generate soft prompts as guidance, instead of relying on hard memory in context.
070   Similar to the scratchpad in o1-preview (OpenAI, 2024c)1 , we propose Matryoshka, a modular
071   framework designed to enhance the advanced problem-solving capabilities of black-box LLMs via
072   controllable multi-turn generations. Matryoshka consists of a lightweight white-box LLM that
073   functions as a controller and a black-box LLM that serves as a generator or solver. Upon receiving
074   the question description as input, the controller generates intermediate outputs that augment the
075   capabilities of the subsequent black-box LLMs. For example, the controller can decompose the
076   original complex task into high-level subtasks in reasoning or planning scenarios, or summarize
077
      profiles from historical records for personalization tasks. By conceptualizing the following black-box
      LLM as the environment, Matryoshka generates intermediate guidance alongside the original
078
      input to derive the final result through multi-turn interactions with the environment. The feedback
079
      for the outputs from the environments distinguishes positive and negative examples of intermediate
080   generations, which can be used for preference optimization. Notably, this optimization process is
081   inherently self-improving through iterative sampling from prior inferences and by considering the
082   policies from earlier iterations as reference policies. Matryoshka continually enhances the ad-
083   vanced capabilities of the black-box LLM through controllable multi-turn generations that iteratively
084   interact with environmental feedback.
085
      Extensive experiments conducted on three complex tasks demonstrate the effectiveness and gener-
086   alizability of Matryoshka in improving the advanced problem-solving capabilities of black-box
087   LLMs, with an average improvement of 3.19% in accuracy for reasoning, 7.46% in success rate for
088   planning, and 5.82% in accuracy for personalization. Importantly, Matryoshka not only enhances
089   the capabilities of black-box LLMs without requiring access to model parameters, but also facilitates
090   online feedback with environmental interactions. We summarize the main contributions as follows:
091
      • i), We introduce Matryoshka, one of the first modular frameworks that employ a lightweight
092     white-box LLM to drive the generation of a large-scale black-box LLM for complex problem-
093     solving;
094   • ii), Matryoshka intuitively formulates the white-box LLM as a controller and the black-box
095     LLM as a component of the environment, facilitating long-horizon controllable generation with
096     environmental feedback; and
097   • iii), Matryoshka adopts on-policy learning to iteratively enhance training data quality, inherently
098     self-improving intermediate guidance for the continual enhancement of black-box LLM capabilities.

190   3.1     I NSTANTIATION OF W HITE -B OX LLM C ONTROLLER
191
192   We instantiate the white-box LLM as a controller to generate additional guidance that assists the black-
193   box LLM in understanding and solving a diverse range of problems. Given the varying complexity
194   and distinct characteristics of different tasks, the controller should be capable of generating guidance
195   in various formats. We provide examples corresponding to reasoning, planning, and personalization
196   tasks (Figure 3):
197
      Problem Decomposition for Reasoning. For reasoning tasks, generating a sequence of reasoning
198   steps is essential to solve the problem effectively. Existing works (Zhou et al., 2023) have observed
199   that models often perform poorly on tasks that require solving problems more complex than the
200   exemplars provided in the prompts. To enable the model to develop better reasoning and overcome
201   the easy-to-hard generalization issue, one strategy is to decompose complex problems into a series
202   of simpler sub-problems and solve them sequentially. Therefore, for reasoning tasks, the white-box
203   LLM controller outputs decomposed sub-tasks to assist the subsequent black-box LLM generator in
204   enhancing its reasoning capabilities.
205   High-Level Plan for Planning. For planning tasks, LLMs are required to generate a sequence of
206   actions that constitute a plan to solve the given problems. A common strategy (Sun et al., 2024a;
207   Zhao et al., 2024) is to apply hierarchical planning for complex solutions, where a high-level planner
208   decomposes the task into sub-goals, and a low-level planner generates a sequence of admissible actions
209   corresponding to each specific sub-goal. To enhance the black-box LLM’s planning capabilities, we
210   leverage the white-box controller to generate high-level plans as guidance to simplify the problems.
211   User History Summarization for Personalization. For personalization tasks, LLMs are required
212   to tailor outputs to individual users. Existing work (Richardson et al., 2023) accomplishes this by
213   concatenating the user’s input query with a profile summarizing the user’s preferences and behavior
214   patterns. To enhance the black-box LLM’s personalization capabilities, we utilize the white-box
215   LLM controller to generate summaries of user histories. This approach enables black-box LLMs to
      better understand users and generate tailored content accordingly.

224   3.2    DATA C OLLECTION BY I NTERACTING WITH B LACK -B OX LLM E NVIRONMENT
225
      Optimizing the intermediate guidance generated by the controller presents significant challenges
226
      for two main reasons: (1) Lack of ground-truth guidance: There are no ground-truth intermediate
227
      generations available to serve as supervision signals for the controller’s outputs. (2) Uncertainty
228   in performance improvement: It is difficult to determine which guidance will reliably enhance the
229   downstream performance of the black-box LLM. To address these challenges, we formulate the
230   black-box LLM as an environment system and employ multi-turn interactions with environmental
231   feedback during data sampling.
232
      In the MDP formulation, we consider the action space as the set of possible guidance that can enhance
233
      the capabilities of black-box LLMs. The observation space is determined by the oracle evaluation
234   function for each task, defined as eval(·), where the sampled supervision signal is denoted as z, with
235   z = 1 indicating that fθ (x) is positive guidance while z = 0 indicating fθ (x) negative guidance.
236   During the multi-turn interactions, if the observation ot at the t-th step returns a negative signal, the
237   next action step at+1 involves modifying the intermediate guidance based on the feedback. The
238   interactions continue until a positive signal is observed or the maximum number of interaction turns
239   T is reached.
240   For each input xi , we perform T -step multi-turn interactions with the black-box LLM-based environ-
241   ment to obtain the trajectories (ai,1 , oi,1 , ai,2 , oi,2 , · · · , ai,T , oi,T ). To increase the diversity of inter-
242   mediate generations, we introduce randomness into the policy and repeat the entire interaction process
243   K times. This results in K trajectories, yielding intermediate generations {gi,1 , gi,2 , · · · , gi,K×T }
244   along with their corresponding observations {oi,1 , oi,2 , · · · , oi,K×T }, which serve as sampling
245   signals. We then sample the positive guidance gi+ from the set of guidance with positive ob-
246   servations, gi+ ∼ {gi,j |oi,j = 1} and the negative guidance from the remaining generations,
247   gi− ∼ {gi,j |oi,j = 0}.

249   3.3    I TERATIVE G UIDANCE O PTIMIZATION
250
251   As white-box LLMs like LLaMA are pre- and post-trained for general purposes, they may struggle to
252   fulfill the specific tasks required by the controller. Additionally, there may be discrepancies between
253
      what the controller considers “good” guidance and what the generator interprets as “good” guidance.
      To this end, the guidance generated by the white-box LLM controller needs further optimization to
254
      enhance the performance of the black-box LLM generator.

259
      learning on a set of curated instruction-completion pairs for LLMs. We leverage the capabilities of
      more advanced models, such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2024a), to generate the desired guidance for the
260
      black-box LLMs on a small set of samples. This data is then used to perform supervised fine-tuning

482   Black-Box LLMs Generation Enhancement. Existing approaches aiming to enhance the generation

498   engineering and result in prompts that are rigid and static. Adapter-based methods (Sun et al., 2024b;
499
      Shi et al., 2024; Zhuang et al., 2024) follow a best-of-N selection evaluation paradigm (Lightman et al.,
      2023). Given a problem, adapter-based methods generate N candidate solutions from the generator
500
      and subsequently evaluate them using a lightweight adapter to identify the highest-scoring solution
501
      as the final answer. However, such methods are heavily dependent on the generative capabilities of
502   the black-box LLM, which may result in selecting a suboptimal candidate as the best of a bad bunch.
503
504   Small LMs Drive LLMs Generation. SuperICL (Xu et al., 2023) incorporates outputs from
505
      smaller language models (LMs) as complementary information for input queries, integrating them
      into the context provided to black-box LLMs. However, these smaller LMs are fixed and can
506
      only support classification tasks that rely on label predictions with associated confidence scores.
507
      HYDRA (Zhuang et al., 2024) is a retrieval-augmented generation framework that trains a BERT-
508   sized reranker to reorder retrieved passages to better cater to user-specific requirements. Nevertheless,
509   these methods apply only discrete optimization on the prompt through reranking and selection of
510   few-shot demonstrations, which limits the potential improvements achievable via prompt engineering.
511
      Reinforcement Learning for Prompt Optimization. As LLMs scale, new capabilities emerge,
512
      enabling models to learn tasks efficiently through a few in-context demonstrations. To harness
513
      these capabilities, several approaches have been proposed to leverage reinforcement learning for
514   improved prompt generation, enhancing LLM performance. RLPrompt (Deng et al., 2022) introduces
515   an RL-based framework for generating optimal prompts via black-box optimization. Similarly,
516   TEMPERA (Zhang et al., 2023) formulates prompt optimization as test-time prompt editing, using RL
517   to efficiently explore the editing space. BDPL (Diao et al., 2023) further advances this by proposing
518   a variance-reduced policy gradient algorithm to estimate gradients of parameters in the categorical
519   distribution of each discrete prompt. However, these methods primarily focus on classification tasks,
520   where gradient estimation is straightforward, limiting their applicability to more complex generation
521   tasks requiring long-horizon solutions.

523   6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
524
525   We introduced Matryoshka, a lightweight white-box LLM controller designed to augment the
526   capabilities of large-scale black-box LLMs across a wide range of complex tasks, including reasoning,
527   planning, and personalization. By leveraging a controller-generator framework with environmen-
528   tal feedback, Matryoshka effectively decomposes complex tasks and guides black-box LLMs
529   through intermediate guidance. Through policy gradient optimization, Matryoshka exhibits a self-
530   improving nature that continually enhances LLM capabilities via multi-turn guidance optimization.
531
      Extensive experiments on three diverse datasets demonstrate its effectiveness in steering black-box
      LLMs for long-horizon tasks without requiring access to model parameters or output probabilities.
532
      Compared to the best-performing state-of-the-art baselines, Matryoshka achieves average im-
533
      provements of 3.19% in reasoning tasks, 7.46% in planning tasks, and 5.82% in personalization tasks.
534   These results underscore the potential Matryoshka as a transparent and scalable solution, enabling
535   white-box LLMs to drive black-box LLMs in complex problem-solving. Future work could extend
536   Matryoshka to tackle more complex applications requiring long-horizon generation and reasoning,
537   such as solving software engineering problems and proving mathematical theorems. Additionally, the
538   controller component of Matryoshka could be developed into a self-enhancing mechanism or a
539   universal controller applicable to a wide range of real-world applications.

774   A.1   L IMITATIONS
775
776   In this study, we propose a modular framework, Matryoshka, that leverages a lightweight white-
777   box LLM controller to enhance the capabilities of black-box LLMs. Despite its effectiveness, we
778   have identified several potential limitations of Matryoshka:
779   Malign Usage. Since Matryoshka employs a white-box LLM controller to augment black-box
780   LLMs, there are notable risks to consider. Malicious actors could exploit this approach to engineer
781   harmful capabilities or generate toxic content for training purposes. While black-box LLMs are
782   designed to resist producing such content, our controller could be misused to manipulate these models
783   into generating undesirable outputs. Furthermore, there is a risk that the intermediate guidance
784   produced by our controller could be exploited to extract sensitive information from black-box LLMs,
785
      potentially facilitating jailbreaking or other targeted attacks.
786   Data Privacy. Matryoshka preserves the confidentiality of training data by avoiding third-party
787   API sharing, thereby safeguarding the integrity of training samples during the enhancement process
788   of black-box LLMs. However, when applied to personalization tasks, it is important to recognize that
789   retrieved historical records or the queries themselves may inadvertently contain sensitive information,
790   potentially risking unintended disclosure of private data.
791
792   A.2   B ROADER I MPACTS
793
794   Potential Positive Societal Impacts. The proposed Matryoshka framework addresses a critical
795
      challenge in consistently enhancing the capabilities of black-box LLMs for long-horizon tasks with
      broad scopes. By improving reasoning, planning, and personalization, Matryoshka can deliver
796
      significant benefits across various domains. For instance, it can provide insights into complex
797
      theorems, advance industrial automation, and offer more personalized interactions for end users.
798   Overall, Matryoshka has the potential to facilitate more useful, relevant, and satisfying interactions,
799   thereby improving productivity, decision-making, and quality of life. Moreover, Matryoshka
800   operates without requiring access to the model weights of black-box LLMs, making the technology
801   accessible to a wide range of off-the-shelf LLM APIs and enabling seamless integration into diverse
802   use cases. By leveraging existing LLMs, Matryoshka can be readily adopted by researchers,
803   developers, and organizations, accelerating the development and deployment of advanced language
804   models in real-world applications.
805   Potential Negative Societal Impacts. Enhancing black-box LLMs through a small-scale white-box
806   LLM introduces potential risks. One significant concern is the possibility of using the white-box
807   model to jailbreak black-box LLMs, injecting malicious instructions or producing harmful content.
808   This could lead to the spread of misinformation, hate speech, or other offensive materials, with severe
809   consequences for individuals and society. Additionally, this approach poses a threat to user data
      privacy. Training the white-box model requires collecting and storing interaction data between the

814   B     A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORKS
815
816   RLHF. Proximal policy optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017) is the predominant deep rein-
817   forcement learning method used in RLHF, leading to significant successes in models like Instruct-
818   GPT (Ouyang et al., 2022), ChatGPT (Achiam et al., 2023), and Gemini (Reid et al., 2024). However,
819   applying PPO requires extensive effort and resources (Choshen et al., 2019; Engstrom et al., 2020;
820   Tang et al., 2024), often beyond the scope of open-source capabilities. To simplify implementation
821   and streamline the training process, recent works (Azar et al., 2024; Ethayarajh et al., 2024) have
822
      proposed direct preference learning algorithms following the DPO framework (Rafailov et al., 2024b).
      These algorithms bypass the reward modeling step and directly optimize carefully designed loss
823
      objectives on the preference dataset, hence the term direct preference learning.
824
825   Self-Improvement Training. Recent advances in self-improvement methods for language models
826   fall broadly into two categories: (1) online fine-tuning approaches and (2) bootstrapping methods.
827   Fine-tuning approaches aim to enhance models by adjusting their parameters based on additional
828
      data or objectives. Notable methods include Rejection Fine-Tuning (RFT) (Yuan et al., 2023), which
      augments the training set with correct completions; Alignment Fine-Tuning (AFT) (Wang et al.,
829
      2023), which introduces an alignment loss to increase the probabilities of correct chain-of-thoughts;
830
      Reinforced Fine-Tuning (ReFT) (Luong et al., 2024), which applies reinforcement learning to token
831   prediction; and self-play (Chen et al., 2024), which iteratively refines the model using its own previous
832   outputs. Bootstrapping methods, on the other hand, leverage the model’s own generations to create
833   new training data. Notable examples include Self-Taught Reasoner (STaR) (Wu et al., 2024), which
834   iteratively samples high-quality data; Reinforcement and Self-Training (ReST) (Gulcehre et al., 2023)
835   and its simplified version ReSTEM (Singh et al., 2023), which alternate between data generation
836   and reward-based optimization; and Verified Self-Taught Reasoner (V-STaR) (Hosseini et al., 2024),
837   which combines self-training with outcome-based verification. Collectively, these approaches offer
838   diverse strategies for enhancing model performance through targeted training and iterative refinement,
839
      highlighting the potential for self-improvement in language models.

       work. Specifically, we employ the controller model Llama-3-8B-Instruct to analyze the user’s
989    retrieved history data and generate an informative and clear intermediate summary. This summary
990    is then combined with the input question as prompts for the environment model gpt-4o-mini
991    to derive the final answer. To enhance control capabilities, we utilize online DPO to optimize the
992    controller model Llama-3-8B-Instruct.
993
       During the interaction stage, we follow the aforementioned pipeline, leveraging the controller
994
       model Llama-3-8B-Instruct to generate various intermediate outputs. By interacting with the
995    environment model gpt-4o-mini, we obtain intermediate generations paired with ground truth
996    answers as corresponding observations. We then sample both positive and negative intermediate
997    generations based on the quality of the final answer. For classification tasks such as LaMP-1 and
998    LaMP-2, an intermediate generation is labeled as positive if the final answer exactly matches the
999    ground truth, and vice versa. For generation tasks like LaMP-4, we rank the generations by their
1000   metric scores and select the top ones as positive and the bottom ones as negative.
1001   To prevent overfitting and reward hacking, the interaction stage processes the entire training dataset
1002   once for all personalization tasks. We sample at most two contrastive pairs for each training data
1003   point. In cases where no positive generation exists for some data points, we utilize a more powerful
1004   model, such as gpt-4o, to produce several strong intermediate generations, thereby increasing the
1005   likelihood of obtaining positive samples. We employ LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation), a parameter-
1006   efficient method, to update the controller model Llama-3-8B-Instruct. LoRA is well-suited
1007   for personalization tasks, allowing efficient and effective optimization of the controller model. We
1008   utilize DPO for optimization.

       E.3   GSM8K: R EASONING
1033
1034   Following the PAL framework (Gao et al., 2023), we employ code-style LLM prompts to facilitate
1035   the conversion of mathematical problems into executable code, thereby augmenting the model’s
1036   problem-solving capabilities. Unlike PAL, which directly translates mathematical problems into code,
1037   Matryoshka first assists GPT in decomposing the problem into more manageable sub-problems.
1038   This decomposition allows GPT to more effectively convert these simpler sub-problems into code,
1039   enhancing both the correctness and stability of the generated code. Additionally, since Matryoshka
1040   is responsible solely for high-level planning without engaging in low-level execution, we can train on
1041
       the GSM8K dataset and evaluate on the GSM-Hard dataset. Both datasets comprise similar problem
       types, with GSM-Hard featuring more intricate numerical calculations.
1042
1043   In our experimental setup, we begin by randomly sampling 216 code-based solutions to mathematical
1044   problems from the GSM8K training set using gpt-3.5-turbo-0125. We then extract the
1045   planning components from these code blocks to perform supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on the LLaMA
1046   model, thereby equipping LLaMA with foundational planning capabilities for solving mathematical
1047
       problems. The SFT training configuration mirrors that used for ALFWorld. Subsequently, LLaMA
       functions as the planner, generating breakdowns and planning solutions for each of the 7,473 problems
1048
       in the GSM8K training set. Concurrently, GPT serves as the executor, producing executable code
1049
       based on each problem and the corresponding plan provided by LLaMA.
1050
1051   During inference, consistent with our experiments on ALFWorld, we implement closed-loop refine-
1052   ment to enhance model performance. Matryoshka initially decomposes the mathematical problem
1053
       into simpler sub-problems. The black-box model then generates corresponding code blocks for each
       sub-problem. If the execution of the generated code does not produce the expected answer or if
1054
       execution issues arise, the error information is relayed back to the black-box model for reflection and
1055
       iterative improvement. We restrict the number of reflection attempts to six; any problem that remains
1056   unresolved after these attempts is considered beyond the reasoning capabilities of the black-box
1057   model.

       E.4   A LFWORLD : P LANNING
1060
1061
       Following AdaPlanner (Sun et al., 2024a), we employ a closed-loop planning approach for inference
1062   on ALFWorld. The primary distinction lies in Matryoshka’s responsibility for generating the high-
1063   level plan, while a black-box model, such as gpt-3.5-turbo-0125, handles low-level execution
1064   after comprehending both the problem and the high-level plan. Similar to AdaPlanner, we utilize
1065   code-style LLM prompts to enhance the black-box model’s planning and interaction capabilities with
1066   the environment.
1067
       Our initial objective is to enhance LLaMA’s planning ability on ALFWorld. To achieve this, we
1068   enable GPT to perform closed-loop high-level planning and low-level execution on 400 samples from
1069   ALFWorld’s training set. From these runs, we selected 277 examples that successfully reached the
1070   goal state and extracted the planning components to fine-tune LLaMA using supervised learning. For
1071   the SFT, we set the learning rate to 2 × 10−5 , with a batch size of 64, and trained a LoRA module
1072   with a rank of 8, an alpha of 16, and a dropout rate of 0.05 over 3 epochs. After LLaMA acquires a
1073   foundational level of planning ability, we designate it as the planner and assign GPT as the executor.
1074   The two models then perform closed-loop inference on the ALFWorld training set, comprising 8,810
1075   samples. Each sample is executed eight times, with successful runs labeled as positive samples and
1076
       unsuccessful ones as negative samples. This process yields 4,844 unique {positive, negative} pairs,
       which are utilized for the first epoch of DPO training on LLaMA.
1077
1078   Subsequently, we repeat the data collection process on the ALFWorld training set using the DPO-
1079   trained model, gathering 1,586 samples. This reduction in samples occurs because, as Matryoshka
       becomes more capable post-DPO training, it generates a higher proportion of positive outcomes,

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       resulting in fewer {positive, negative} pairs. By aggregating all collected samples, we obtain a total
1081
       of 6,430 pairs, which are then used to conduct the second epoch of DPO training on Matryoshka.
1082   This further enhances its planning capabilities and aligns them more closely with GPT’s execution
1083   proficiency. Through this iterative DPO training approach, we observe that the high-level plans
1084   generated by LLaMA more effectively guide GPT’s execution, leading to a higher success rate in
1085   ALFWorld tasks.
1086
       Additionally, during the inference stage, we maintain a closed-loop approach to bolster the model’s
1087
       performance. Specifically, the black-box model first generates a corresponding trajectory based
1088   on the task and the prompt provided by Matryoshka. If an error occurs during execution or the
1089   task remains incomplete after a predetermined number of steps, the black-box model reflects on its
1090   generated trajectory and the encountered error, subsequently regenerating a new trajectory. The model
1091   is permitted up to six reflection attempts. If it still fails to complete the task after these attempts, the
1092   task is deemed beyond the model’s capabilities. This methodology effectively enhances the model’s
1093   ability to interact with the environment and increases the likelihood of successfully completing tasks.
