012
               policy architectures like fully connected MLPs. As such architectures contain
013            few inductive biases, it is common in practice to incorporate priors in the form
014            of rewards, training curricula, imitation data, or trajectory generators. In nature,
015            animals are born with priors in the form of their nervous system’s architecture,
016            which has been shaped by evolution to confer innate ability and efficient learning.

019            In this work, we explore the advantages of a biologically inspired ANN architecture

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Learning-based approaches to quadruped locomotion commonly adopt generic policy architectures
031   like fully connected multilayered perceptrons (MLPs) (Rudin et al., 2022; Smith et al., 2022; Agarwal
032   et al., 2022). As such architectures contain few inductive biases, they must rely on training to develop
033   desired behaviors. Simple objectives often fail to elicit naturalistic or robust behavior (Heess et al.,
034   2017), so it is common in practice to incorporate priors in the form of rewards (Rudin et al., 2022),
035   training curricula (Agarwal et al., 2022; Rudin et al., 2022), imitation data (Bin Peng et al., 2020;
036   Merel et al., 2019b), or trajectory generators (Schaal, 2006; Iscen et al., 2019).
037   In nature, animals are born with priors in the form of their nervous system’s architecture, which has
038   been shaped by evolution to confer innate ability and efficient learning (Zador, 2019; Cisek, 2019).
039   For instance, a horse can walk within hours of birth and can quickly improve with practice, and
040   humans have strong inductive biases for perceiving and interacting with the world (Lake et al., 2017).
041   These inductive biases are a reflection of highly structured neural circuit connectivity (Luo, 2021),
042   which combines innate and learning mechanisms in stark contrast to generic ANN architectures.
043   Can such architectural priors also be useful in ANN architectures? Bhattasali et al. (2022) investigated
044   this by introducing Neural Circuit Architectural Priors (NCAP). Using a case study of the nematode
045   C. elegans, the proposed Swimmer NCAP translated neural circuits for swimming into an ANN
046   architecture controlling a simulated agent from an AI benchmark (Tassa et al., 2020). Swimmer
047   NCAP achieved good performance, data efficiency, and parameter efficiency compared to MLPs, and
048   its modularity facilitated interpretation and transfer to new body designs. As such, Swimmer NCAP
049   demonstrated several possible advantages of biologically inspired architectural priors.
050   However, it remained unknown whether the approach could apply to more complex animals and tasks.
051   C. elegans has a nervous system of only 302 neurons and highly stereotyped connectivity, and its
052   connectome has long been mapped (White et al., 1986). In contrast, mammalian nervous systems have
053   millions or billions of neurons (Herculano-Houzel et al., 2006; 2007) with more variable connectivity
      and no mapped connectome, so it was not obvious how such circuits could inspire ANNs.


                                                         1
054
      In this work, we introduce Quadruped NCAP, a biologically inspired ANN architecture for quadruped
055   locomotion based on neural circuits in the limbs and spinal cord of mammals. Our key insights for
056   adapting the NCAP approach are: modeling at the level of genetically defined neural populations
057   (Danner et al., 2017; Ausborn et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2022) rather than at the level of single neurons,
058   leveraging machine learning to compensate for gaps in detailed circuit knowledge, and introducing
059   methodological innovations to improve the architecture’s expressivity and trainability (Section 2).
060   Together, these insights enable the architecture to successfully control quadruped locomotion, which
061   is a much harder task than planar swimming due to its higher dimensionality and inherent instability.
062   We test the value of our architectural prior by comparing to an architecture without priors: the
063   MLP, which is commonly used in robotics. Quadruped NCAP achieves good initial (untrained)
064   performance and comparable final (asymptotic) performance to MLPs, while using less data and
065   orders of magnitude fewer parameters. Our architecture also exhibits better generalization to task
066   variations, even admitting deployment on a physical robot without standard domain randomization
067   methods that are often needed for sim-to-real generalization. This work shows that neural circuits
068   can provide valuable architectural priors for locomotion in more complex animals and encourages
069
      future work in yet more complex sensorimotor skills.
070   The key contributions of this work are:
071
       1. First genetically defined neural circuit model for quadruped robot locomotion. We design
072
          an architecture that applies NCAP to quadruped locomotion. While many related works have
073       been inspired by biology (Section 2), to the best of our knowledge this work is the first to use
074       genetically defined neural circuits to control locomotion in a standard quadruped robot.
075
       2. Extensive evaluation in simulation. We extensively evaluate NCAP in terms of performance,
076
          data efficiency, parameter efficiency, and generalization to terrain and body variations. NCAP
077
          learns more naturalistic gaits, with up to millions of fewer timesteps and orders of magnitude
078       fewer parameters than MLP, while being more robust to unseen conditions.
079
       3. Deployment on the physical robot. We deploy NCAP to the physical robot to test its general-
080
          ization across a large sim-to-real domain gap. While MLP falls immediately due to its erratic
081
          and unstable actions, NCAP manages to walk successfully.
082
083   Our open-source code and videos are available at: https://ncap-quadruped-anon.github.io/

      2    R ELATED W ORK
087
088
      Our work builds on an extensive literature in neuroscience, robotics, and artificial neural networks.
      For conciseness, we highlight the most relevant ones to our work:
089
090
091   Central Pattern Generators Central pattern generators (CPGs) in biology are neural circuits that
092   produce rhythmic activity in the absence of rhythmic inputs, and they underlie movements including
093
      chewing, breathing, and locomotion. Roboticists have developed CPG-like controllers for a variety
      of tasks (Ijspeert, 2008; Yu et al., 2014), which can use diverse mechanisms: directly parameterizing
094
      the desired movement trajectories or footfall patterns (Iscen et al., 2019), learning to control an
095
      action space of abstract oscillators (Bellegarda & Ijspeert, 2022; Shafiee et al., 2023), designing rules-
096   based transitions between stance/swing states (Ekeberg & Pearson, 2005), or modeling a reduced
097   neural circuit (for instance, with 1 neuron per limb) (Lodi et al., 2018). In this work, we take a
098   highly biologically constrained approach, designing an architecture based on cell-type-specific neural
099   connectivity and intrinsic dynamics based on experimental data.
100
101
      Neuromechanical Models Neuromechanical models are used in computational neuroscience to
102   develop insights about the interactions between the musckuloskeletal system and the nervous system
103   (Ausborn et al., 2021; Markin et al., 2016). Recently, several neuromechanical models have been
104   developed for the rodent (Merel et al., 2019a; Tata Ramalingasetty et al., 2021) and the fly (Lobato-
105   Rios et al., 2022; Wang-Chen et al., 2023), which will enable new understanding about how animals
106   perform movement. In this work, we build upon insights gleaned from neuromechanical models, but
107   our goal is not to control a realistic musckuloskeletal simulation. Rather, we aim to translate insights
      from biology to AI and robotics, which leads us to model at a higher level of abstraction.


                                                          2
108
      Architectural Priors Architectural priors incorporate structure into an ANN to improve perfor-
109   mance and efficiency. For example, convolutional neural networks inspired by the visual system
110   incorporate translation invariance over images (Lindsay, 2021). In locomotion, various kinds of archi-
111   tectural prior have been explored, including priors on task/body symmetries (Mittal et al., 2024; Ding
112   & Gan, 2024), spring-loaded inverted pendulum dynamics (Ordonez-Apraez et al., 2022), modularity
113   (Huang et al., 2020; Chiappa et al., 2022), and hierarchy (Heess et al., 2016; 2017). In this work,
114   we explore priors based on genetically defined neural circuits, similar to connnectome-constrained
115   networks explored in the vision literature (Lappalainen et al., 2024). Our prior is encoded through
116   sparse and structured connectivity, weight sign constraints, weight initializations, bilateral symmetry,
117
      and intrinsic neural dynamics.
118
119   Swimmer NCAP In this work, we build on Swimmer NCAP (Bhattasali et al., 2022) by scaling
120   it to more complex settings. The animals we study are more complex: mammals have much larger
121   and less understood nervous systems than nematodes. The AI tasks we tackle are also much harder:
122   the Quadruped is a higher-dimensional inherently unstable body, while the Swimmer is a lower-
123
      dimensional inherently stable (planar) body. We also evaluate on a physical robot, in contrast to
      previous work in simulation only. Thus, our work addresses problems that are especially relevant
124
      for AI. Scaling to more complex settings requires novel methodological innovations: (1) We adopt
125
      a continuous-time formulation of ANNs, in contrast to the discrete-time Swimmer. (2) We build
126   a recurrent architecture in the Rhythm Generation (RG) module, in contrast to the feedforward
127   Swimmer. (3) We develop a closed-loop Oscillator unit that admits period modulation, phase shifts,
128   and entrainment, in contrast to the open-loop Oscillator unit in Swimmer that does not respond to
129   input signals. (4) We model circuits at the level of neural populations, rather than at the level of single
130   neurons. (5) We enable the architecture to learn unknown connections in the Afferent Feedback
131   (AF) and Pattern Formation (PF) modules by leaving certain connectivity and signs unconstrained,
132   in contrast to the Swimmer which fully constrained connectivity and signs. Together, these novel
133   insights and methods can help the community advance biologically inspired architectural priors.

143   3.1   B IOLOGICAL L OCOMOTION
144
145   Quadrupeds locomote by rhythmically flexing and extending their limbs in a coordinated gait to
146   propel the body forward. They control their velocity by producing various gaits (such as walk, trot,
147
      gallop, and bound), and they adapt to different conditions using sensory information. Quadruped
      mammals (including mice, cats, dogs, and horses) exhibit considerable differences in appearance,
148
      but comparative anatomical studies have revealed a remarkable homology in body structure and
149
      neural circuitry between them, which makes sense given their shared evolutionary heritage (Grillner
150   & El Manira, 2020). Neuroscience research across many animal systems has shed light on how
151   locomotion is achieved through the complex interaction between the musculoskeletal system and
152   neural circuits in the limbs, spinal cord, and higher brain regions (Grillner & El Manira, 2020).
153   Surprisingly, neural circuits in the limbs and spinal cord are sufficient to produce locomotion, while
154   higher brain regions are important to initiate and regulate locomotion (Rybak et al., 2015). Classic
155   studies strikingly demonstrated evidence of such organization using decerebrate animals, in which
156   most of the brain was severed from the spinal cord, yet the animal could still walk and even transition
157   between gaits when tugged along a treadmill (Whelan, 1996). Recent studies have leveraged advances
158
      in experimental tools like molecular genetics to precisely map and manipulate locomotor circuits
      with cell-type specificity (Kiehn, 2016; Ausborn et al., 2021).
159
160   We summarize below a well-supported neuroscientific model of locomotor circuits (Figure 1), which
161   is based on data from cats and transgenic mice, and which adopts the abstraction of genetically
      defined neural populations with rate-coded activity. For details, please refer to the referenced works.

199   Rhythm Generation The RG neural circuit within the spinal cord coordinates limbs to produce
200   gait rhythms (Figure 1B). Each limb is controlled by a half-center microcircuit consisting of a flexor
201   center with intrinsically bursting neurons and an extensor center with tonic firing neurons. The
202   paired centers inhibit each other, leading to oscillating flexion and extension in each limb. The
203
      half-centers for the four limbs communicate through “cross” connections (cervical/lumbar commis-
      sural interneurons), “side” connections (homolateral long propriospinal neurons), and “diagonal”
204
      connections (diagonal long propriospinal neurons), thus enabling bilateral and ascending/descending
205
      communication. In essence, excitatory connections between half-centers promote synchronization
206   and inhibitory connections promote alternation, so the speed-dependent activation of these various
207   connections change the timing between limbs and therefore the gait. (Danner et al., 2017)
208
209
210
      Brainstem Command The BC neural circuit in the brainstem conveys command signals to adjust
      locomotor speed and gait (Figure 1B). It does so via two pathways: one controlling speed by
211
      modulating the intrinsic period of RG oscillators, and one controlling gait by modulating the “cross”
212
      and “diagonal” RG connections that promote synchronization or alternation. (Ausborn et al., 2019)

248   2 computational units can capture the cell types in this circuit: a Basic unit and an Oscillator unit

252
      complexity is not needed for AI purposes, so we follow Bhattasali et al. (2022) by simplifying
      these neurons to create computational units with fewer and more interpretable hyperparameters. We
253
      describe below the main properties of these units. For details and equations, please see Appendix A.1.

      Pattern Formation We use a linear PF layer to map flexor/extensor half-center outputs to limb
325   actions alt . We initialize PF weights with coarse magnitudes and correct signs (producing negative
326   joint positions for flexion and positive joint positions for extension) that are constrained after each
327   update. We share weights among forelimbs and hindlimbs to exploit bilateral symmetry (Figure D.3),

329   during training and collapse it during testing.

331   Afferent Feedback We use a linear AF layer to map limb observations olt to flexor/extensor half-
332   center inputs. We normalize observations to the range [−1, 1], then rectify them into positive and
333   negative components, since firing rates cannot be negative. We initialize certain AF weights with
334   coarse magnitudes to encode the known effects of limb loading and position on half-centers, and
335   we constrain their signs (Figure D.3). We initialize unknown AF weights at zero and leave them
336   unconstrained. We also utilize bilateral sharing and the overparameterization trick.

339
340
341
      Tasks We train our architecture on simulated tasks built atop the MuJoCo physics engine (Todorov
      et al., 2012) to control the Unitree A1 robot (Appendix B.1). The tasks are structured as 15-second
342
      episodes during which the robot must locomote forwards at a fixed target speed of Walk (0.5 m/s)
343
      or Run (1.0 m/s), and across terrains of Flat or Bumpy (Appendix B.2). The tasks provide at each
344   timestep a reward proportional to the running speed, with maximum reward of 1 at the target speed
345   in the forward direction (Appendix B.3). This task structure and reward design is based directly on
346   Smith et al. (2022) in order to facilitate comparison to existing work.
347
348
      Baselines We compare against multilayered perceptrons (MLPs) of 2 hidden layers. By default,
349   we compare to MLP(256,256), which is a reasonably sized architecture commonly used in the AI
350   and robotics literature. Importantly, we choose to baseline against MLPs as they exemplify an
351   architecture without priors, which contrasts with our NCAP architecture with priors, facilitating a
352   clean comparison. Our goal in this work is not to compare how different classes of prior stack up
353   generally, but rather to explore the value of neural circuit-inspired architectural priors in particular.
354   As architectural priors are somewhat orthogonal to other forms of priors (including reward, training
355   curricula, and imitation priors), future work could combinatorially combine our prior with others.
356
357   Algorithm We train both NCAP and MLP architectures using evolution strategies (ES) to maximize
358   episodic return (Appendix C.1). Such gradient-free optimization is easiest to use with our NCAP
359   architecture, and it has successfully and popularly been used to train MLPs in continuous control
360   (Salimans et al., 2017). In preliminary experiments, we compare evolution strategies to standard
361   on-policy and off-policy reinforcement learning algorithms (Appendix C.2), and we confirm similar
362
      performance across algorithms when training MLPs on our tasks (Appendix D.1).

364   4.1   P ERFORMANCE AND DATA E FFICIENCY
365
366   NCAP successfully learns to locomote across various speeds and terrains (Figure 4A). For representa-
367   tive examples of NCAP’s behavior and neural activity, please see Videos 1.
368   How does NCAP compare to MLP on performance and data efficiency? NCAP achieves comparable
369   asymptotic performance to MLP across tasks (Figure 4A). Moreover, due to its priors, an untrained
370   NCAP achieves significantly better initial performance than an untrained MLP. The performance of
371   NCAP improves with training as the AF and PF weights are refined. In addition, NCAP’s training
372   trajectories are less variable than MLP’s.

374   For instance, on the Bumpy/Run task, NCAP reaches asymptotic performance about 500 epochs (or
375   8 million timesteps) before MLP. However, NCAP reaches slightly lower asymptotic performance
376   than MLP for the easier Flat tasks. We attribute this to a regularization effect in NCAP, as it is
377   constrained in the solutions it can learn. In contrast, MLP can learn to exploit the simulator for
      additional performance gains, which is easier to do on Flat than Bumpy tasks.

421   4.2   PARAMETER E FFICIENCY
422
423   Are the performance and data efficiency advantages of NCAP merely due to having fewer parameters?
424   We test MLP with fewer parameters by varying the hidden layer sizes from 4 to 256. Surprisingly,
425   performance and data efficiency degrade significantly (Figure 5A), showing that it is not merely
426   having fewer parameters that is beneficial. Rather, the specific structure of NCAP matters.
427   It is this structure that enables NCAP to perform well yet require dramatically fewer parameters
428   than MLPs (Figure 5B). During testing, NCAP uses only 92 parameters (Figure D.4), which is 3
429   orders of magnitude fewer than MLP(256,256) with 79,372 parameters (Figure D.6), and it is fewer
430   than even MLP(4,4). During training, NCAP with the overparameterization trick has 708 parameters
431   (Figure D.5), which is 2 orders of magnitude smaller than MLP(256,256), and it is comparable to
      MLP(16,16). Notably, many of these MLPs are much smaller

504   4.4   G ENERALIZATION TO THE P HYSICAL ROBOT
505
506   How well does NCAP generalize to the real world compared to MLP? We deploy the architectures to
507   a physical Unitree A1 quadruped robot after training on the Bumpy/Walk task (Appendix B.4). We
508   expect the domain gap to be large since we do not perform controller tuning or system identification
509   with the physical robot, the simulated training does not apply any domain randomization task priors
510   (Peng et al., 2018), and the architectures do not have mechanisms for online adaptation.
511   MLP falls immediately due to its erratic and unstable actions, often launching the robot aggressively
512   into the wall (Figure 7). Despite our best efforts, we cannot elicit a successful walking trial. In contrast,
513   NCAP is remarkably robust to the large domain gap, walking successfully on most trials, though with
514   less smoothness than in simulation (Figure 7). We attribute NCAP’s success to a combination of the
515   RG module maintaining a stable rhythm in the face of sensor noise and the AF module triggering
516   corrective responses in the face of perturbations. Lastly, we deploy an untrained NCAP and observe
517
      that it is stable and produces slight walking movements with small foot displacements (Videos 3).
518
519   5     D ISCUSSION
520
521   In this work, we introduce Quadruped NCAP, a biologically inspired ANN architecture for quadruped
522   locomotion based on neural circuits in the limbs and spinal cord of mammals. Our architecture
523   achieves good initial performance and comparable final performance to MLPs, while using less data
524   and orders of magnitude fewer parameters. Our architecture also exhibits better generalization to task
525   variations, even admitting deployment on a physical robot without standard sim-to-real methods.
526
527   Limitations Our study faces several limitations. First, we rely on a hand-tuned RG module and BC
528   command, which might not be the optimal parameters that a learning-based approach could discover.
529   Second, we neglect musculoskeletal factors in the quadruped action space that could make learning
530   easier or more robust.
531
532   Future Work We focus on fixed speed locomotion in this work, but an obvious next step is to
533   enable the RG to transition between gaits in a speed-dependent manner, which may largely involve a
534   higher-level controller that alters brainstem commands for different speeds. Another extension is to
535   add postural adjustment, turning, and righting mechanisms to the architecture based on understanding
536
      of the underlying neural circuits. Finally, as architectural priors are somewhat orthogonal to other
      forms of priors, it may be beneficial to train NCAP with additional reward, task, or imitation priors.
537
538   Overall, we believe that this work shows that neural circuits can provide valuable architectural
539   priors for locomotion in more complex animals and encourages future work in yet more complex
      sensorimotor skills.

      A.3       OVERPARAMETERIZATION T RICK
887
888   We discover an important technique to improve the trainability of our architecture, which we call “the
889   overparameterization trick”.
890
      Consider a linear layer y = W x. During training, the weight matrix W can be artificially expanded
891
      in dimensionality along the row dimension and/or column dimension to produce a larger weight matrix
892   W ′ . The former produces a correspondingly larger output vector y ′ , while the latter necessitates
893   a correspondingly larger input vector x′ . The larger output y ′ can be transformed into the original
894   output y by summing expanded elements. The original input x can be transformed into the larger
895   input x′ by copying and concatenating elements. Thus, the expanded layer maintains the original
896   input and output dimensions, but with a larger weight matrix and new hardcoded expansion and
897   compression operations at the interface. We find empirically that such overparameterization improved
898   learning, presumably due to special dynamics of traversing high-dimensional loss landscapes with our
899   point-based evolutionary strategies algorithm (Appendix C.1), but better theoretical understanding of
900
      this phenomenon is needed.
901   During testing, the overparameterized weight matrix W ′ can be collapsed by summing along the
902   expanded rows and/or columns to produce a smaller matrix with the size of the original W . Mathe-
903   matically, this does not change the computation, as it exploits the linearity of matrix multiplication.
904   For a simple example, consider the case of 1D inputs and outputs.
905
906   If the weight is expanded row-wise:

       D.4    VARIABLE S PEED
1351
1352   We perform additional experiments to investigate the ability of Quadruped NCAP to handle a more
1353   complex task in which the robot must transition between speeds within an episode.
1354
1355   Task We train our architecture on a simulated task built atop the MuJoCo physics engine to control
1356   the Unitree A1 robot, as in the fixed speed tasks (Section 4). The task is structured as a 15-second
1357   episode during which the robot must locomote forwards across Flat terrain at a variable target speed
1358   randomly sampled every 5 seconds from the set {0.0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0} m/s. Therefore, there are
1359
       up to 3 distinct target speeds per episode to track, and the current target speed vtarget,t is included
       within the observation at each timestep. The task provides at each timestep a sparse reward, with
1360
       maximum reward of 1 at the target speed in the forward direction and with reward decaying to zero at
1361
       a margin around the target speed. This reward design is based on Rudin et al. (2022).
1362

1364   world frame, and the target forward velocity vtarget in the world frame. The overall reward provides a
1365
       linear velocity reward that encourages the agent to maintain a forward velocity parallel to the ground
       within a margin of the target velocity:
