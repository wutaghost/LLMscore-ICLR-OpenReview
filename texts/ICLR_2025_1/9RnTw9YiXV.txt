009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have made significant progress,
012            seamlessly integrating the visual comprehension capabilities of vision encoders
013            with the language generation strengths of language models (LMs). Despite the suc-
014            cess of LVLMs, the training or aligning data of LVLMs suffers from the Long-Tail
015            (LT) problems, which is a special type of data with highly imbalanced distributions,
016            and a large number of tail (minority) instances. A significant amount of research
017            has focused on mitigating LT through data adjustment or network structure reorga-
018
               nization, however, efforts targeting generative LVLMs remain limited. In this paper,
               we present an in-depth analysis of the LT issues persisting in LVLMs’ training data
019
               and build a distribution of four perspectives, addressing both visual and language
020
               aspects. To mitigate the aforementioned challenges, we propose an Adaptive Data
021            Refinement Framework (ADR), which consists of two stages: Data Rebalancing
022            (DR) and Data Synthesis (DS). In the DR stage, we adaptively rebalance the redun-
023            dant data based on entity distributions, while in the DS stage, we leverage the latent
024            representations of scarce images to adaptively supplement the underrepresented
025            portions. To validate the effectiveness of our approach, we conduct experiments
026            on a series of comprehensive benchmarks, including the GPT-assisted evaluations
027            to assess the overall performance variations introduced by our method. Through
028            comprehensive evaluations, ADR effectively mitigates the long-tail problem in
029
               the training data, improving the average performance of LLaVA 1.5 relatively by
               2.62% across 10 benchmarks, without increasing the training data volume. Our
030
               code and data will be publicly released.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have become pivotal at the intersection of computer vision
036   and natural language processing, enabling breakthroughs in bridging the gap between language and
037   vision. These models facilitate a wide range of applications by generating contextually relevant
038   textual descriptions from visual inputs. Recent advancements in LVLMs (Bai et al., 2023; Chen
039   et al., 2023a; Dai et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023a; Dong et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023c; Liu et al.,
040   2023a; 2024a; Zhu et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023b; Abdin et al., 2024) have significantly advanced
041   general-purpose foundation models, elevating them to unprecedented levels. This flourishing progress
042   within the research community marks a significant step towards Artificial General Intelligence (AGI).
043   However, the training data of LVLMs are suffering from the problem of Long-Tail (LT) (Parashar
044   et al., 2024). The “Long Tail” term in computer vision refers to the fact that the training datasets
045   nowadays are often of large scale and present highly imbalanced distributions, and they have a large
046   number of tail (minority) classes. Although LT is common in real-world distribution and cannot be
047   seen as a problem, recent research (Zhang et al., 2024; 2023b; Fu et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022) have
048   found that balancing the LT data could bring positive effects. Several works (Shao et al., 2023; Wang
049   et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Lee et al., 2024) have been made to mitigate LT by
050
      either balancing the redundant data or re-designing the network structure.
051   Despite the efforts, these approaches are not yet fully satisfying in terms of eliminating the LT
052   problem within the training data of LVLMs. Some existing methods focus on traditional models
053   (e.g., CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)) or tasks (e.g., image classification), while others prioritize data
      efficiency, aiming to achieve comparable performance with fewer data. However, none of these

083
      stage rebalances the overrepresented head portion of the data by filtering out low-quality or redundant

084
      contrast, the DS stage leverages the latent representations of scarce images to adaptively synthesize
085
      the underrepresented tail data, significantly improving the model’s performance on tail-end concepts.
086
087   We design comprehensive experiments to validate the effectiveness of ADR in mitigating the LT issue
088   using diverse benchmarks, hallucination metrics, and evaluations with GPT-4. The results demonstrate
089

090

091

092
093   • We conduct an in-depth analysis of the long-tail problem in the training data of LVLMs from four
094
        key aspects and analyze why mitigating this issue can lead to positive outcomes.
095   • Our ADR alleviates the LVLM’s LT issue and significantly enhances the overall performance of
096
        LVLMs without increasing the data scale or introducing any additional training. ADR is also
        model-agnostic and data-agnostic, which can be easily transferred to any open-source data.

        brought by ADR, which serves as a powerful and universal multimodal data modulator.

103   2.1   L ARGE V ISION -L ANGUAGE M ODELS
104
105   Recently, Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have attracted significant attention. Besides
106   the powerful business models such as GPT-4V, GPT-4o, Gemini, and Claude (OpenAI, 2023; 2024;
107   Team et al., 2023; Anthropic, 2024), many open-source LVLMs exist. Although LVLMs with an
      integrated overall structure (Wu et al., 2023; Zhan et al., 2024; Team, 2024) have been emerging and

108
      becoming a new trend gradually, traditional aligning-based LVLMs (Liu et al., 2023a; Bai et al., 2023;
109   Zhu et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2024) still make up the majority. With the aid of strong large language
110   models such as LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023) or Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023), and a powerful vision
111   encoder such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), they managed to align visual comprehension with
112   remarkable language generation capabilities, formulating powerful vision understanding models.
113   However, all of the aforementioned LVLMs still face significant LT issues regardless of their structure
114   or training phases. Therefore, this paper focuses on addressing the LT problems to facilitate the
115   practical application of LVLMs.
116
117   2.2   DATA D EVELOPMENT OF LVLM S
118
119   The instruction-tuning data for VLMs typically includes carefully crafted instructions designed to
120   enhance the general instruction-following capabilities or improve downstream task performance of
121
      LVLMs. These instructions are often generated by large language models (LLMs) like GPT-4 (Liu
      et al., 2024a) or LVLMs such as GPT-4V (Chen et al., 2024b; Yan et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024).
122
      Notably, ShareGPT4V (Chen et al., 2023b) was initially developed from 100K high-quality captions
123
      collected from GPT-4V, which were later expanded to 1.2M using a captioning model. Additionally,
124   various data augmentation techniques are employed during LVLM development, such as random
125   cropping and flipping for vision encoders (Ye et al., 2024) and projectors (Li et al., 2023c; Ye et al.,
126   2023a), as well as word- and sentence-level augmentation for instruction tuning (Chen et al., 2024a).
127   However, these augmentation methods often overlook the inherent distribution of the training data,
128   leading to an inability to balance the data distribution effectively.
129
      In addition to data acquisition and augmentation, there is also significant research on data filtering.
130   Several papers have addressed the data diet problem for instruction-tuning in Vision-Language
131   Models (VLMs) (Wei et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024d; Lee et al., 2024). For instance,
132   Paul et al. (2021) introduces two popular importance scores for effective data pruning. Self-Filter
133   (Chen et al., 2024d) adapts gradient as an importance score to train a scoring network, which then
134   filters out important instances. TIVE (Liu et al., 2024b) leverages gradient-based importance scores
135   to design a data filtering pipeline, achieving comparable performance to the original LVLM version
136   while using less data. COINCIDE (Lee et al., 2024) examines the distributional differences between
137   training sets and benchmark data, using a smaller model as a reference to select visual instruction
138
      tuning data for more efficient fine-tuning of the target LVLM. However, while existing data filtering
      and data balancing methods have shown potential in achieving performance on par with the baseline
139
      and improving data efficiency, the overall improvements remain modest.
140
141
142   2.3   L ONG TAIL A NALYSIS OF VLM S
143
      Quantities of work have been done to analyze the long tail problem of traditional VLMs. Some
144
      recent studies (Shao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2024) seek to mitigate imbalanced
145   predictions of VLMs by training on additional data from downstream tasks. MetaCLIP (Xu et al.,
146   2023) analyzes the long tail problem of CLIP pre-training data and uses sub-string matching and
147   inverted indexing to balance the pre-training dataset. REAL (Parashar et al., 2024) analyzes the long
148   tail problem of popular image recognition datasets and designs a tail concept replacement method
149   during the inference stage, significantly improving the recognition accuracy of VLMs. However, the
150   LT problem within generative LVLMs is still under-explored.

186   3.2         E NTITY D ISTRIBUTION C ONSTRUCTION
187
188   To conduct a detailed analysis of the LT problems within LVLMs, we refine our analysis perspective
189   into four aspects, namely Token, Object, Co-occurrence, and Interrogation. Specifically, we conduct
190   the whole analysis procedure by constructing the frequency distribution of entities Qe from these 4
191   different perspectives among the whole training set.
192   Token entities are a set of meaningful nouns that are extracted from the text within data instances.
193   et = {n|n is a meaningful noun and appears in C for (I, C) in D}. Technically, we employ a POS
194   parser, stanza (Qi et al., 2020) to extract all nouns from each data instance within the training
195   set, identifying them as token entities. Subsequently, we compute the frequency distribution of all
196   extracted token entities across the entire training set.
197
      Object entities represent the objects that truly exist in the image within data instances. eo =
198   {o|o exists in I for (I, C) in D}. We initially employ language models (LM), i.e., Llama3 (Touvron
199   et al., 2023) to extract all potential objects from the textual records of each data instance within
200   the training set. The full prompts used to extract object information are detailed in the Appendix
201   C.1. Subsequently, we input the image along with all token entities and LM-extracted objects into a
202   visual grounding model, i.e., GroundingDINO (Liu et al., 2023b) to identify visual objects for each
203   data instance, termed as object entities. Finally, we compute the frequency distribution of all object
204   entities across the entire training set.
205   Co-occurrence entities represent two objects that appear in the same image of one data instance. ec =
206   {(o1 , o2 )|o1 , o2 both exist in I for (I, C) in D}. We utilize the extracted object entities to construct a
207   co-occurrence graph G(V, E). The vertex set V comprises {o|o represents one object entity}, and the
208   edge set E = {(o1 , o2 , n)|o1 and o2 appear together in the same image for n times}. Subsequently,
209   we employ the edge set E as co-occurrence entities to compile the frequency distribution of all
210   co-occurrence entities across the entire training set.
211   Interrogation entities are the questioning methods used in the text within data instances. ew = {q|q
212    is the questioning method in C for (I, C) in D}. We employ language models (LM) to extract all
213   methods of posing questions from the data instances, defining them as interrogation entities. We
214   then calculate the frequency distribution of all interrogation entities across the entire training set. We
215   extract all four kinds of entities from LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a)’s instruction-tuning dataset and the
      Top-20 frequently shown entities can be found in the Appendix B.1.

249   • Unbalanced training data. We begin by illustrating the long-tail (LT) problem in training data
250     through a detailed analysis. Using LLaVA 1.5’s instruction-tuning data as an example (Liu et al.,
251     2023a), the distribution curve shown in Figure 3 reveals a pronounced imbalance in the distribution
252     of entities. The head (high-frequency) entities appear significantly more often than those in the
253     tail (low-frequency). Although real-world data distributions are typically long-tailed, balanced
254     data is more effective in facilitating the learning of tail concepts (Parashar et al., 2024). The
255     unbalanced entity distribution in training data limits models’ ability to adequately learn these tail
256     concepts. Furthermore, several studies (Liu et al., 2024b; 2023a) have shown that current LVLM
257
        instruction-tuning datasets contain substantial redundancy, indicating that reducing part of the
        training data has minimal impact on performance and helps mitigate overfitting. Our experimental
258
        results further support this finding.

344
345   Despite the presence of redundancy in the head entities, the issue of scarcity in the tail entities still
346   persists. To alleviate the issue of scarcity, we design the data synthesis methods from the perspective
347   of vision and language. Figure 2 displays the full data adjusting framework.

349   4.2.1    L ANGUAGE DATA S YNTHESIS
350
351
      We introduce a method for synthesizing the tail data at the language level. The core idea is to replace
      head concepts with tail ones. First, we use WordNet (Fellbaum, 1998) to extract all synonyms of
352
      token entities and construct a mapping system. For each head instance, we extract its linguistic
353
      entities, search for their synonyms in the mapper, and filter out the head ones. Next, we feed the
354   original head conversation into a language model (LM) and prompt the LM to rewrite the conversation
355   using the selected tail synonyms. Full prompts to instruct LMs can be found in Appendix C.2. It’s
356   important to note that certain stop words, such as “image” should not be replaced.

531   7     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we analyze the relation between the LT problem existing in LVLM instruction tuning
534   data from multiple perspectives and make the first attempt to mitigate it. Our analysis reveals that
535   unbalanced data can result in a performance gap between the head data and the tail data and therefore
536   harm the model’s performance. Based on them, we develop an Adaptive Data Refinement Framework
537   (ADR), which first balances the redundant head data from different perspectives and then adjusts
538   the unbalanced tail data volume with a specific focus on tail distribution. Experimental results
539   demonstrate that our method improves the tail performance and overall performance without harming
      the head part performance, resulting in a more robust LVLM instruction-tuning pipeline.

771   We conducted an ablation study on different balancing combinations and synthesis methods. In
772   the ablation study of different rebalancing combinations, we conduct the DR stage using different

774   Interrogation) to validate the effectiveness of different perspectives. The detailed results of the
775   balancing ablation experiment are presented in Table 6. We found that combining all perspectives
776   yields the best performance in terms of both the number of top results and performance stability.
777   Additionally, we conducted an ablation study on different synthesis methods. The results of the
778   augmentation and synthesis experiments are presented in Table 7. It is clear that synthesizing from
779   ALL perspectives (as outlined in Section 4.2.2) yields the best performance.
