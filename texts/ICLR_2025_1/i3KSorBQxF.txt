      1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Reinforcement Learning (RL) studies sequential decision-making problems often modeled through
031   the framework of the Markov Decision Processes (MDPs, Puterman, 2014). This framework allows
032   the application of RL to a large variety of challenging problems and paved the way for the growing
033   success of RL we witnessed in the last decade (e.g., Kormushev et al., 2013; Nagabandi et al., 2018;
034   Dulac-Arnold et al., 2021). Nevertheless, MDPs are based on some grounding assumptions that may
035   limit their modeling power in real-world scenarios. In particular, they assume that the environment
036   dynamics P (i.e., transition model) and loss1 в„“ are fixed throughout the whole interaction. However,
037   in the real world, such elements may change due to external factors which might be the effect of
038   nature (e.g., system anomaly, aging effects) or strategic actors (e.g., adversarial attacks). While the
039
      former case is usually captured by non-stationary MDPs (Lecarpentier & Rachelson, 2019; Cheung
      et al., 2020), the latter scenario is more challenging as it assumes the presence of another agent (i.e.,
040
      an adversary) acting with an objective possibly conflicting with that of the agent.
041
042   Since the early work of Even-Dar et al. (2009), this class of problems has been addressed drawing
043   inspiration from Online Learning (OL) literature (Orabona, 2019). Adversarial Markov Decision
044   Processes (AMDPs, Even-Dar et al., 2009) have been designed to model the scenario in which the
045
      agent interacts for T P N rounds facing an adversarially chosen MDP Mt at every round t P JT K.
      Here, the performance of the agentвҖҷs policy ПҖt is evaluated in terms of the expected regret competing
046
      against a fixed comparator policy ПҖ Лқ .2
047
048   When the transition model P is known or fixed (possibly stochastic) and the loss в„“t is adversarially
049   chosen, several works (Zimin & Neu, 2013; Rosenberg & Mansour, 2019; Jin et al., 2020) have

      6    R ELATED W ORKS
449
450
451   The presence of disturbances, adversarial attacks, and non-stationary behaviors in MDPs have been
452   extensively treated through various lenses. We now highlight how this work is related to each subfield.
453
      Adversarial MDPs. When only the losses are adversarially chosen and the transition functions
454                                      P
      are either known or fixed (i.e., C f вҖң 0), many are cases of success in obtaining compelling regret
455
      guarantees (e.g. Zimin & Neu, 2013; Rosenberg & Mansour, 2019; Jaksch et al., 2010; Jin et al.,
456
      2020), where Zimin & Neu (2013); Jin et al. (2020) in particular take advantage of performing
457   OMD steps in the occupancy measure space. Under adversarially chosen transitions as well, the
458   only related work up to our knowledge is Jin et al. (2023), where bandit feedback for both the
459   losses and the transition model is considered. However, when the degree of maliciousness is
460   unknown, the resulting computational complexity remains uncertain and the regret guarantees are
461   rather implicit. Furthermore, Jin et al. (2023) does not take into account the intermediate setting
462   of revealed
              a transitions, but applying their Algorithm 1 to such a setting would lead to a regret of
463   OpL2 X AT logpLXAT 2 q ` L5 X 4 A logpLXAT 2 q ` C P L5 X 4 logpLXAT 2 qq even with a known
464   degree of maliciousness C P , which is significantly worse than the performances of SOMD, constant-
465   wise. On the other hand, we positively leverage the intermediate setting of revealed transitions,
466   introducing ad-hoc degrees of maliciousness the notion of smoothed MDP formalism, and finally
467   recovering comparable performances via computationally efficient OMD-based algorithms.
468   Corruption Robust RL. Works in this line (e.g. Lykouris et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021) typically
469   assume the presence of an adversary corrupting some of the rewards and/or transitions, compared
470   to a nominal underlying MDP. These works then address a different notion of regret, namely the
471   one defined coherently with respect to the loss incurred by the best policy in the nominal MDP and
472   denoting the?number of corrupted episodes by C, Wei et al. (2022) is the first to achieve a regret of
               1
473   OМғpmin t вҲҶ , T u ` Cq in a bandit feedback setting without requiring the knowledge of C, with вҲҶ
474   being the reward gap between the best and the second-best.
475   Robust MDPs & RL. Robust MDPs (e.g. Nilim & Ghaoui, 2005; Wiesemann et al., 2013) and
476   Robust Reinforcement Learning (e.g. Morimoto & Doya, 2005; Lim et al., 2016) focus on computing
477   policies that exhibit robustness in face of uncertainties over the transition and/or loss models so
478   that to withstand potential mismatches between the models and the ground truth. Usually, though,
479   minimax solutions against the worst-case scenario are sought, failing to adapt to easier instances and
480   to smoothly interpolate performance based on the degree of mismatch.
481   Non-Stationary RL. Works in this line (e.g. Lecarpentier & Rachelson, 2019; Wei & Luo, 2021;
482   Cheung et al., 2023) allow the MDP model to change arbitrarily over time and the performance metric
483   employed is the dynamic regret, i.e. competing against a comparator policy varying over rounds. It
484   is known that, even in the simpler bandit setting, when the environment is adversarial the no-regret
485   property is not achievable for the dynamic regret (Bubeck et al., 2012), so the results presented in this
      work are not directly comparable with them.
