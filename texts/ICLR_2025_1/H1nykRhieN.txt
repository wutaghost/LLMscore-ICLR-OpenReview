011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Accurate traffic speed prediction is critical in modern society as it is effective
014            for both individuals and authorities. Due to the large scale of urban road net-
015
               works, traffic speed exhibits complex spatio-temporal dependencies, not only
               among adjacent nodes but also across the network, reflecting both local and cross-
016
               regional simultaneous correlations. However, existing studies have not effectively
017
               addressed these characteristics. In this context, we propose a novel framework
018            called Multi-view Hypergraph Spatio-Temporal Model (MvHSTM) that employs
019            a temporal transformer to capture temporal dependencies and utilizes hypergraph
020            convolutional networks to inherently model spatial relationships. Specifically, we
021            introduce two hypergraph construction methods, the Geographical Adjacency Hy-
022            pergraph (GAH) and the Feature Similarity Hypergraph (FSH), to capture spatial
023            correlations on neighboring and non-neighboring scales. Extensive experiments
024            on real-world traffic speed datasets demonstrate that our approach achieves state-
025            of-the-art performance compared to baseline methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Traffic speed prediction is a critical component in modern society, with applications in urban traffic
030
      management and intelligent transportation systems (ITS). Accurate forecasting of traffic speeds opti-
031   mizes route navigation, improves estimated time of arrival, and enables efficient traffic management.
032   Laor & Galily (2022) note that around 74 percent of smartphone users rely on navigation software,
033   with the real-time “recalculating route” feature being particularly useful in offering reassurance and
034   generating positive emotions. The significance of this predictive capability also extends to various
035   domains. In navigation systems, accurate predictions facilitate optimal route selection, potentially
036   reducing travel times and alleviating congestion. Emergency services benefit from more precise es-
037   timation of response times. Furthermore, improved traffic prediction contributes to environmental
038   sustainability by promoting more efficient transportation patterns and potentially reducing carbon
039
      emissions.
040   However, the complexity of urban traffic systems necessitates sophisticated modeling approaches.
041   Traffic is influenced by multiple factors, including spatial dependencies between road segments,
042   temporal patterns such as rush hours. Correlations in the spatial dimension indicate congestion in
043   one area can rapidly propagate to neighboring segments, creating ripple effects throughout the net-
044   work. Besides, this kind of spatial similarity also far beyond simple geographical proximity. Road
045
      networks form complex webs of interconnected segments, where the speed can significantly drop af-
      ter merging on freeways or dramatically rise back after diverging. Moreover, certain road segments
046
      may have functional relationships that are not immediately apparent from their geographical adja-
047
      cency. For example, parallel arterial roads usually serve as alternatives, sharing traffic load during
048   congestion. Expressways linking urban and suburban areas often exhibit synchronized congestion
049   patterns during rush hours. Different areas within a city can have vastly different traffic character-
050   istics due to land use patterns, population density, or the presence of key destinations. Therefore,
051   developing comprehensive models that can capture these intricate spatial relationships along with
052   their temporal dynamics is crucial for accurate traffic speed prediction. Such models need to learn
053   and represent the different characteristics of road segments in the spatial dimension, presenting a
      significant challenge in the field of traffic forecasting.

054
      Existing approaches to traffic prediction mainly focus on two aspects: time series methods and
055   graph methods, corresponding to the temporal and spatial dimensions, respectively. Classical sta-
056   tistical methods such as Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) (Williams & Hoel,
057   2003) and Support Vector Regression (SVR) (Drucker et al., 1996) primarily focus on modeling
058   the sequential nature of traffic patterns over time. However, these approaches face limitations in
059   capturing complex nonlinear relationships and spatial correlations inherent in traffic data. Recently,
060   researchers have employed deep learning methods to address these shortcomings. Kim et al. (2017)
061   introduce convolutional operations into Fully Connected LSTM (FC-LSTM) (Graves, 2013), which
062   enables the original temporal model to perceive spatial relations. (Zhang et al., 2017) employ CNN
063
      in residual learning blocks, effectively addressing spatio-temporal correlations in urban grid pedes-
      trian data. However, RNNs (including LSTM) have difficulty in capturing long-term temporal de-
064
      pendencies and are computationally intensive. Existing CNNs, while good at extracting grid-like
065
      spatial feature, struggle to model irregular road systems in real cities.
066
067   In order to model real road networks, graph-structured models are applied in recent studies. STGCN
068   (Yu et al., 2017) applies graph convolutions and gated temporal convolutions to learn spatial and
069
      temporal dependencies, respectively. By applying a diffusion process on graph structure with RNN,
      DCRNN (Li et al., 2017) effectively model spatio-temporal network in traffic datasets. Extensive
070
      research, such as Graph WaveNet (Wu et al., 2019), STSGCN (Song et al., 2020), AGCRN (Bai
071
      et al., 2020) has proved that the effectiveness of graph structure in modeling real road networks.
072   These studies have proved the effectiveness of graph structure in modeling urban road networks,
073   relating segments by linking nearby nodes with edges. Nevertheless, standard graphs represent spa-
074   tial relationships with simple pairwise edges, limiting their ability to capture complex interactions
075   among multiple nodes. This structure makes it challenging to model higher-order spatial correla-
076   tions and transregional relationships in traffic data. Furthermore, a large number of computations
077   are needed to establish connections between distant nodes. In contrast, hypergraphs allow hyper-
078   edges to connect multiple nodes simultaneously, enabling a more comprehensive representation of
079   spatial dependencies. Feng et al. (2019) extends traditional graph neural networks to hypergraphs,
080
      allowing the model to capture higher-order relationships. Wang et al. (2021) employ hypergraph on
      metro system, validating the performance in extracting higher-order relationships. Hypergraph The
081
      attention mechanism (Vaswani, 2017) also demonstrates its superiority in both spatial and temporal
082
      features, such as ASTGCN (Guo et al., 2019), STTN (Xu et al., 2020), and GMAN (Zheng et al.,
083   2020). These prior works have significantly advanced the field of spatio-temporal traffic predic-
084   tion by demonstrating the effectiveness of graph-based and attention-enhanced models in modeling
085   road networks. The introduction of hypergraphs has further enriched spatial modeling by capturing
086   higher-order relationships, which are difficult to model using standard graphs. However, existing
087   hypergraph construction methods have certain limitations, as predefined rules may not fully capture
088   the complex nature of traffic data.
089   To address these limitations, we propose a novel framework, the Multi-view Hypergraph Spatio-
090   Temporal Model (MvHSTM), designed to capture the spatio-temporal features in traffic systems.
091   Our method constructs hypergraph in two separate strategies, by geographical adjacency and traffic
092   pattern similarity, respectively. The model utilizes a transformer module to handle temporal rela-
093   tionships, and two hypergraph convolution networks, Geographical Adjacency Hypergraph (GAH)
094   and Feature Similarity Hypergraph (FSH), to represent both neighboring spatial relationships and
095   non-neighboring feature similarities. This enables the capture of higher-order spatial correlations
096   that are often overlooked by simple graph-based models. Finally, we design a self-adaptive fusion
097
      module to obtain the prediction result.
098   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
099
100          • We propose a novel MvHSTM framework to comprehensively capture the spatio-temporal
101            features of traffic speed forecasting. The framework employs a temporal embedding for
102            temporal transformer to model temporal features, and two hypergraph convolution net-
103            works are utilized to capture inherent spatial relationships.
104          • To represent spatial feature specifically in traffic speed data, we propose two different
105            hypergraph construction approaches, forming the Geographical Adjacency Hypergraph
106            (GAH) and the Feature Similarity Hypergraph (FSH). The GAH is constructed from nodes
107            with their adjacencies, and the FSH is constructed from nodes with similar traffic speed
               patterns.

196   3.2   C ONSTRUCTION OF HYPERGRAPH
197
198   For a graph G = (V, E, A), a critical step in constructing a hypergraph Gh = (Vh , Eh , H) is to
199
      decide which vertices ∈ V should be linked by each hyperedge ∈ Eh . In this subsection, we in-
      troduce two separate approaches for assembling vertices into hyperedges, to obtain a Geographical
200
      Adjacency Hypergraph (GAH) and a Feature Similarity Hypergraph (FSH).
201
202   Construction of GAH Normally, a hypergraph is applied in traffic forecasting for its effectiveness
203   in gathering adjacency road segments in a complex enormous road system. The Geographical Ad-
204   jacency Hypergraph (GAH) uses this characteristic. In constructing the GAH, we utilize the input
205   adjacency matrix A ∈ RN ×N , where each entry Aij represents the spatial connectivity between
206
      vertices i and j. Each hyperedge is constructed from a vertex v ∈ V and its k-nearest neighboring
      vertices based on their adjacency in the road system:
207
208
209                                   EhGAH = {evh : evh = {v} ∪ Nk (v)}
210
211   where Nk (v) is the set of k vertices that are most strongly connected to v according to the adjacency
212
      matrix A.
213   Constructing Hyperedges by DTW Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) (Berndt & Clifford, 1994) is
214   an algorithm designed to measure the similarity between two temporal sequences. Unlike simple Eu-
215   clidean distance, DTW aligns time series flexibly by permitting shifts in the time dimension, thereby
      offering a more accurate similarity measure even when the sequences are not perfectly synchronized.

477   5      C ONCLUSION
478
479
      In this study, we introduce the Multi-view Hypergraph Spatio-Temporal Model (MvHSTM) for
480   traffic speed forecasting. By integrating a temporal transformer and two hypergraphs, the Geo-
481   graphical Adjacency Hypergraph (GAH) and the Feature Similarity Hypergraph (FSH), our model
482   effectively captures complex spatio-temporal dependencies. Experiments indicate that applying the
483   hypergraph construction method based on feature similarity is effective in traffic predicting. Tests on
484   the METR-LA and the PEMS-BAY datasets show that MvHSTM outperforms state-of-the-art mod-
485   els, demonstrating its potential for improving traffic management and route planning in intelligent
      transportation systems.
