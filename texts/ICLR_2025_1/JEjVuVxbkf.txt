011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            After deploying a machine learning model, distribution shifts may emerge in real-
014            world data. When dealing with unlabeled data, it can be challenging to accurately
015            assess the impact of these drifts on the model’s performance, for any type and in-
016            tensity of shift. In that case, decisions such as updating the model for every benign
017            shift would not be cost-efficient. In this paper, we introduce the Error Classifier,
018            an error assessment method that addresses two tasks: unsupervised performance
019            estimation and error detection on out-of-distribution data. The Error Classifier
020
               computes the probability that the model will fail based on detected fault patterns.
               Further, we employ a sampling-based approximation of Shapley values, with the
021
               Error Classifier as value function, in order to explain why a shift is predicted as se-
022
               vere, in terms of feature values. As explanation methods can sometimes disagree,
023            we suggest evaluating the consistency of explanations produced by our technique
024            and different ones. We focus on classification and illustrate the relevance of our
025            method in a bimodal context, on tabular datasets with text fields. We measure our
026            method against a selection of 15 baselines from various domains, on 7 datasets
027            with a variety of shifts, and 2 multimodal fusion strategies for the classification
028            models. Lastly, we show the usefulness of our explanation algorithm on instances
029            affected by various types of shifts.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   While pretrained language models such as BERT can achieve state-of-the-art performance in vari-
036   ous tasks such as classification (Devlin et al., 2019), a mismatch between the source (training/fine-
037   tuning) and target (test) distributions can deteriorate the model’s performance (Yuan et al., 2023).
038   Once dataset shifts have been detected based on unlabeled data (Rabanser et al., 2019), it is essential
039
      to assess the severity of their impact on the model’s performance in order to make informed deci-
      sions. It would be cost-inefficient to update a model or integrate human control of the model’s out-
040
      puts for every benign shift. However, no method may accurately predict out-of-distribution (OOD)
041
      performance for every type and intensity of distribution shifts (Garg et al., 2022). Despite this, it
042   would be still useful to understand which estimator is more reliable for unsupervised performance
043   estimation and error detection across a diversity of shifts. Unsupervised performance prediction
044   aims to evaluate a model based on unlabeled datasets whereas error detection attempts to identify
045   mispredicted target inputs (Chen et al., 2021a). In that case, it can be valuable to explain why a
046   shift is evaluated as severe, in terms of feature values. For instance, in medical diagnosis prediction,
047   subject matter experts might find it useful to understand why a shift could affect the reliability of a
048   given model’s prediction, and then decide to override the initial outcome.
049   Here the focus is on multimodal classification tasks based on tabular datasets with text fields in
050   English. These datasets consist of categorical and numerical features (i.e. the tabular modality) and
051   fields with free-form text (i.e. the text modality) (Shi et al., 2021). Various critical applications rely
052   on such datasets. In the medical field, patient characteristics and clinical notes could be employed
053   for diagnosis prediction. In financial investment, models could make decisions based on time series
      (e.g. asset price) and text news for sentiment analysis.

077   The tasks of unsupervised performance estimation and error detection are complex to perform across
078   diverse distribution shifts. Our objective is to evaluate what estimator may perform best on a variety
079
      of distribution shifts that could occur in practice. We highlight the following contributions:
080
      • We introduce the Error Classifier (EC), an error assessment method that can be employed for
081
        OOD performance estimation and error detection. For any new input, EC computes the probability
082
        that the classification model will fail based on fault patterns learned on calibration data.
083
084
      • We show that EC can be used as value function in a sampling-based algorithm that approximates
        Shapley values. Once a shift has been detected and assessed, this turns out to be useful to explain
085
        why it is predicted as potentially harmful (i.e. what feature values contribute to the probability
086
        of error estimated by EC). We evaluate the quality of an explanation by verifying its consistency
087     across various explanation methods.

089
      • We assess our method by comparing it to 15 baselines from different domains on 7 tabular-text
        classification datasets, and 2 multimodal fusion schemes for the classification models. All the
090
        methods are external approaches that can be applied to pretrained networks without modification.

103   2    P RIOR WORK
104
105   Multimodal fusion. A multimodal model leverages heterogeneous and connected modalities like
106   audio, image and text as inputs. This approach aims to learn representations of cross-modal inter-
107   actions by fusing information across diverse modalities (Liang et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023). With
      early fusion, cross-modal interactions happen at an early stage. For a Transformer with early con-


                                                                      2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      catenation of two modalities, this means that full pairwise attention will be computed at all layers.
109   In contrast, late fusion of final representations makes cross-modal interactions occur at a later stage.
110
111   Distribution shifts. Dataset shifts appear when the respective source and target joint distributions
112
      p and q are different: p(x, y) ̸= q(x, y) for covariates x and class variable y (Moreno-Torres et al.,
      2012). Changes in the distribution of the input variables x are referred to as covariate shifts. Fo-
113
      cusing on text data, Arora et al. (2021) identify two types of OOD texts: the background shift (e.g.
114
      when style of text changes) and semantic shift (e.g. when unseen classes appear at test time). Se-
115   mantic features are discriminative for the prediction task while background attributes are not. With
116   regard to multimodal distribution shifts, Qiu et al. (2022) benchmark the robustness of image-text
117   models under multimodal perturbations.
118
      OOD detection and performance estimation. Two-sample tests metrics can be used to detect
119
      dataset shifts: univariate Jensen-Shannon Distance (Lin, 1991) and multivariate maximum mean
120
      discrepancy (Gretton et al., 2012). The domain classifier (Rabanser et al., 2019) is trained to dis-
121   criminate between data from source (class 0) and target (class 1) domains. A shift is detected when
122   this model can easily identify from which domain the samples originate. Distance-based methods,
123   such as non-parametric deep nearest neighbors (Sun et al., 2022), can leverage feature embeddings
124   from a model in order to perform OOD detection. With respect to OOD performance estimation, the
125   Average Thresholded Confidence technique learns a cut-off value on the source validation data so
126   that the proportion of samples with score (e.g. maximum confidence) above that threshold matches
127   the accuracy (Garg et al., 2022). The predicted accuracy is estimated as the fraction of target ex-
128   amples for which the score exceeds that threshold. With the Difference of Confidences approach
129
      (Guillory et al., 2021), the accuracy change between the source and unlabeled target data is assessed
      by the difference between the average confidences on these two datasets. With Mandoline, Chen
130
      et al. (2021b) estimate the target error rate by performing importance re-weighting of the 0-1 loss
131
      with slicing functions designed to capture possible axes of distribution shifts. Lastly, Yu et al. (2022)
132   propose the Projection Norm to predict the OOD test error. First, a new neural network is trained on
133   the test samples which have been pseudo-labeled by the in-distribution model. Then, the more the
134   new model’s parameters are different from the original model, the greater the predicted OOD error.
135
      Confidence scores and uncertainty. The maximum softmax probability turns out to be a useful
136
      baseline to estimate confidence under distribution shifts (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017). However, as
137
      models such as neural networks can be miscalibrated, techniques such as temperature scaling are
138   suggested to better calibrate the class probability estimates (Guo et al., 2017a). Liu et al. (2020)
139   show the relevance of the energy score in OOD detection tasks as it is aligned with the probabil-
140   ity density of the input. To quantify predictive uncertainty, methods such as conformal prediction
141   can produce prediction sets based on an expected coverage level (Vovk et al., 2005; Papadopoulos
142   et al., 2002). In particular, Tibshirani et al. (2019) propose a weighted version of conformal pre-
143   diction under covariate shift. To estimate predictive uncertainty, Lakshminarayanan et al. (2017)
144   employ deep ensembles with random parameter initialization for each neural network, along with
145   random shuffling of the data points. The predictive entropy can be computed after averaging the pre-
146
      dicted probabilities from each network. To avoid the computational cost of Bayesian models, Gal &
      Ghahramani (2016) introduce a Bayesian approximation for deep neural networks. When evaluating
147
      the predictive uncertainty for a test input, the Monte Carlo dropout corresponds to performing vari-
148
      ous forward passes with dropout. To evaluate the trustworthiness of predictive uncertainty, Ovadia
149   et al. (2019) present a benchmark of different methods under dataset shift (e.g. deep ensembles).
150   To explain uncertainty estimates, Antoran et al. (2021) propose CLUE, a method based on coun-
151   terfactuals, which identifies which features are responsible for uncertainty in probabilistic models.
152   This method also makes it possible to distinguish aleatoric uncertainty from epistemic uncertainty.
153   The second type originates from the model’s parameters being under-specified by the data and in-
154   creases with OOD inputs. Lastly, Watson et al. (2023) explain predictive uncertainty by adapting
155   the computation of Shapley values (Shapley, 1953) with the conditional entropy as value function.
156   Error detection. To detect model failure during inference, Corbière et al. (2019) propose a method
157   which estimates the true class probability in image classification tasks. Self-training ensembles can
158   be leveraged for error detection and unsupervised accuracy estimation (Chen et al., 2021a). Con-
159   cerning explanation methods, Parcalabescu & Frank (2023) introduce MM-SHAP, a multimodality
160   score based on Shapley values, which helps detect unimodal collapse. However, Krishna et al.
161   (2022) point out that the outputs of different explanation techniques can disagree with each other,
      and suggest various metrics to measure disagreement between top-k features: intersection or rank.

      3.2.1    E XPLANATION ALGORITHM
239
240   We present a sampling-based algorithm that aims to explain why a shift is predicted as potentially
241   harmful for a given prediction, i.e. what feature values contribute to the likelihood of failure assessed
242   by EC. Our method adapts the algorithm from Štrumbelj & Kononenko (2010), which approximates
243   Shapley values by randomly and repeatedly selecting a subset of features instead of all possible
244   coalitions. We make several adaptations to achieve our objective. First, we do not aim to explain
245   the model’s predictions; our goal is to justify why a model might fail in a context of distribution
246
      shifts. Therefore, we leverage a different kind of value function to estimate the feature contributions.
      Secondly, the context is multimodal; in particular, we focus on tabular-text data and models. In a
247
248
      nutshell, for a new target input x′ (with z′ = ϕ(x′ )) and Error Classifier fˆ, we want to understand
249   what contributes to fˆ1 (z′ ) − Ei∼Ival [fˆ1 (zi )], in terms of text and tabular feature values.
250   The approach is described in Algorithm 1 for a target input x, where we compute the average contri-
251   bution of a tabular feature with index j or a text feature (i.e. token) with index (i.e. position) j. We
252   perform M Monte Carlo iterations to approximate the Shapley value. In order to assess the marginal
253   contribution of a feature value with feature index j, we construct two new instances x+j and x−j
254   from x by combining the effect of randomness in samples from Sval and in feature indices for tabu-
255   lar and text modalities. As a value function, the Error Classifier fˆ is used to assess the contribution
256   of the feature value to the likelihood that π̂ will fail.

314   4.1   S ETTINGS
315
316   Datasets. We test the relevance of our method on 7 classification datasets, with a number of classes
317   ranging from 2 to 100: airbnb, cloth, kick, petfinder, salary, and wine with the 10/100 most frequent
318   classes (referred to as wine10 and wine100, respectively). These datasets have been tested by (Shi
319   et al., 2021) and (Gu & Budhkar, 2021).
320   Architectures. For the multimodal classifier π̂, we employ four different architectures: (1) All-
321   TextBERT: The tabular features, converted to strings, and the text fields are concatenated and in-
322   put into BERT-base-uncased (Devlin et al., 2019) as text; (2) LateFuseBERT: A tabular-text dual-
323   stream model with late concatenation of the [CLS] tokens’ final hidden states extracted from BERT-
      base-uncased and a tabular Transformer; (3) AllTextDistilBERT: This architecture is similar to All-


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      TextBERT, except that we employ DistilBERT-base-uncased (Sanh et al., 2019) instead of BERT; (4)
325   LateFuseDistilBERT: Similar to LateFuseBERT with DistilBERT-base-uncased for the text stream
326   instead of BERT. Each pretrained model is fine-tuned on Strain with a batch size of 32, by minimiz-
327   ing the cross-entropy loss with AdamW algorithm (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019), with a learning rate
328   of 5e−5. We use early stopping with patience of 1 for the accuracy on Sval . An exponential learning
329   rate scheduler with gamma of 0.9 is employed. We keep the best model in terms of epochs, i.e. with
330   the highest accuracy on Sval . Each use case is run over 5 different random dataset partitions.
331   Shift type and intensity. We consider various types of shifts affecting the target dataset T :
332
333   • Unimodal covariate shifts: These shifts affect the tabular or text inputs. With orderSplit, we sort
334     the data samples of T by the value of a tabular variable and split the sorted dataset into three
335     sections: Tlow (first 5% share), Tmid (5-95%), and Thigh (95-100%). The final target dataset is
336     constructed by randomly sampling (with replacement) from Tlow ∪ Thigh and from Tmid with
337     distinct rates. emptyCategory randomly replaces the values of categorical variables with empty
338     values. With typos, we insert random typographical errors into the text field (e.g. swapping,
339
        removing, adding, or replacing characters; adding or removing spaces). With seqLengthSplit,
        we sort the data samples of T in ascending or descending order based on the text field length.
340
        We then split the sorted dataset into two sections: T1 (first 10% share) and T2 (90%). The final
341
        target dataset is constructed by randomly sampling (with replacement) from each section with
342     distinct rates. cutText randomly truncates the text field by removing a part of the end of the text.
343     Lastly, abbrev randomly replaces words by abbreviations provided by a given list (e.g. especially
344     becomes esp).

      • Multimodal covariate shifts. This is achieved through the combination of shifts affecting the
346
        inputs of both modalities: orderSplit-typos, emptyCategory-typos, orderSplit-seqLengthSplit,
347
        orderSplit-cutText, orderSplit-abbrev.
348
349   • Out-of-domain: With newClass, T includes a proportion of samples where the true label is not
350
        one of the C classes. For cloth, pet, and salary, the source dataset is constructed after removing
        the samples from the minority class, which are then randomly inserted into T . For wine10 and
351
        wine100, we insert into T samples from unknown classes originating from wine100 and wine200,
352
        respectively.

354   Further, we implement 3 degrees of shift intensity corresponding to various levels of sampling rates
355   (orderSplit, seqLengthSplit, newClass), distinct percentages of affected target data rows (emptyCat-
356   egory, abbrev, cutText), various numbers of shifts (typos), or different proportions of text to remove
357   (cutText). Lastly, as a reference, we also test the methods on the unchanged target dataset (noShift).

      Evaluation. For each experiment, all the methods are calibrated on the validation source data Sval
359   and evaluated on the same target dataset T with a size of 1000 rows. The final hidden state of
360   the classification token [CLS] (referred to as zlast
                                                         [CLS] ) and the softmax output π̂(x) are extracted
361
      from π̂. For LateFuseBERT, zlast[CLS] is the concatenation   of the text and tabular Transformers’ final
362   hidden states for the [CLS] tokens (i.e. states before the classification head). For EC, we use a
363   random forest algorithm with the default hyperparameter setting from Scikit-learn Python package
364   (Pedregosa et al., 2011). zlast
                                 [CLS] and π̂(x) are concatenated and used as features for EC. Our method
365   is compared to the following baselines previously described in section 2.

      15 baselines are used for unsupervised error rate estimation, where the scores are computed for a
367
      given target dataset T : (1) JSD: The Jensen-Shannon Distance between the (validation) source and
368
      target distributions of maximum confidences (i.e. maximum softmax probabilities); (2) AC: One
369   minus the average maximum confidence over the target dataset; (3) ACSC: One minus the average
370   maximum confidence after applying temperature scaling to the softmax output; (4) MMD: maximum
371   mean discrepancy between the source and target samples of zlast[CLS] ; (5) DOC: The error rate estimated
372   with the Difference Of Confidences between source and target; (6) ATC: The error rate estimated
373   with the Average Thresholded Confidence; (7) MAND: The error rate estimated with Mandoline;
374   (8) MCD: With Monte Carlo dropout, the uncertainty is assessed with the average of the predictive
375   entropies over T , computed after performing 5 forward passes of π̂; (9) DC: The AUROC metric of
376   the domain classifier trained with zlast
                                          [CLS] as features; (10) CP: The mean prediction set size computed
377   with the weighted conformal prediction (Tibshirani et al., 2019) based on LAC method (Sadinle
      et al., 2019); (11) DNN: The average distance to the k-th neighbor (k = 10) from the source data

378
      with the deep nearest neighbors fitted with zlast  [CLS] as features; (12) ENRG: The mean energy score
379   computed over the target dataset; (13) TCP: One minus the average true class probability estimated
380   with a neural network trained with zlast[CLS] ; (14) DENS: The uncertainty is assessed with the average
381   of the predictive entropies over T , after averaging the probabilities from a deep ensemble of 5 neural
382   networks trained with zlast
                               [CLS] ; (15) PNORM: The distance as the sum of squared differences between

      the original and new model’s parameters, after training each model based on zlast  [CLS] .

386   Therefore, only 9 baselines are used for the error detection task, where the scores are computed
387
      for a given target input: (1) AC: One minus the maximum confidence for a given target input; (2)
      AC-scaled: One minus the maximum confidence after applying temperature scaling to the softmax
388
      output; (3) MCD: With Monte Carlo dropout, the uncertainty is assessed with the predictive entropy,
389
      after computing 5 forward passes of π̂; (4) DC: The class 1’s predicted probability; (5) CP: The
390   prediction set size computed with the weighted conformal prediction based on LAC method; (6)
391   DNN: The distance to the k-th neighbor (k = 10); (7) ENRG: The energy score; (8) TCP: One
392   minus the true class probability; (9) DENS: The uncertainty is assessed with the predictive entropy.

      For a given architecture (e.g. LateFuseBERT), we evaluate how each method performs on unsu-
394
      pervised error rate estimation by computing the Spearman’s rank correlation ρ between the scores
395
      and the actual error rates on the target dataset, over different random dataset partitions (seeds), shift
396   types and intensities. The performance on error detection is assessed by computing AUROC with all
397   the target data from different seeds, shift types and intensities: we calculate the scores for accurate
398   (label 0) and incorrect (label 1) predictions, and quantify how well these two labels are separated for
399   a range of thresholds. Lastly, we also perform ablation studies to compare the results of EC with (1)
400   Ablation 1: an Error Classifier using only zlast[CLS] as features, or (2) Ablation 2: EC leveraging only
401   the classifier’s output π̂(x). The results of the ablation studies are in appendix I.

      Explanation algorithm. We experiment with two different value functions: the Error Classifier and
403
      deep ensembles. In order to accelerate the computation of Shapley values, we stop the iterations
404   when a convergence criteria is reached. To achieve that, we first compute the maximum absolute
405   difference between the previous and updated Shapley values, every 10 iterations and for each value
406   function. We end the process when the maximum of these two values is lower than 0.01.

      5   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      We introduced a method to compute and explain the likelihood of failure in classification tasks and
534   in OOD contexts. We compared our method to 15 baselines and evidenced that the Error Classifier
535   can be a useful approach for estimating performance and detecting errors on unlabeled data. The
536   outputs of the explanation algorithm proposed in this paper can be relevant to locally understand
537   the source of distribution shifts. The quality of explanations can be assessed by using various value
538   functions (ex. DENS vs EC) or algorithms (e.g. Kernel SHAP). These results are specific to the
539   shifts and the classification models tested here. Therefore, it would be useful to experiment with
      different settings. Lastly, future work could also address the case of other modalities (e.g. image).

      LateFuse architecture. The architecture is detailed in Figure 4 (right) with BERT-base-uncased
      for the text stream. For numerical features, we first perform standard scaling. Embeddings of the
802
      LateFuse architecture are constructed with linear functions. A linear function applies the follow-
803
      ing transformation to a scalar feature value x ∈ R: x.Wnum + b where Wnum ∈ Rd and the bias
804   b ∈ Rd . For categorical features, we encode them as category embeddings. In that latter case, the
805   corresponding embedding is computed as eT Wcat where e ∈ Rnc ×1 is a one-hot-vector for the as-
806   sociated categorical feature, nc denote the number of categories for this feature, and Wcat ∈ Rnc ×d .
807   A classification token [CLS] is then added to the beginning of the tabular embedding sequence. The
808   tabular Transformer with self-attention has the following architecture: 3 layers, 8 attention heads,
809   feed-forward dimension of 768, embedding dimension of 768. The dropout (rate 0.1) is applied to
      the category embeddings, the tabular Transformer (attention, feed-forward networks), and the final

810
      fully-connected networks. The text and tabular Transformer’s final hidden states of the [CLS] to-
811   kens are concatenated before being projected through fully-connected layers to produce the logits.
812   The uniform weight initialization for the category/linear embeddings and the final fully-connected
813   networks is based on Kaiming (He et al., 2015). The final fully-connected layers can be described
814   as follows: FC(x) = Linear(Dropout(ReLU(Linear(x)))) where the output has a dimension of C
815   (number of classes).

850
851   We provide further details on the shift generation techniques described in Section 4:
852
853   • orderSplit: The three intensities of shift correspond to different levels of sampling rates used to
854     construct the final target dataset: We sample with a rate of 10%, 50%, or 90% from Tlow ∪ Thigh ,
855
        respectively. This shift is applied to a feature selected randomly: e.g. orderSplit1, orderSplit2,
        orderSplit3 correspond to 3 different seeds used to select a given variable from the original feature
856
        pool.
857
858   • emptyCategory: The three intensities correspond to various percentages of target data affected by
859     this shift: 10%, 50%, or 90%.
860   • typos: We use the Python package typo (https://github.com/ranvijaykumar/typo)
861     in order to generate typographical errors. The three intensities correspond to different numbers of
862     typos affecting the text field of each target observation: 5, 25, or 50. It is worth noting that, for
863     AllTextBERT, the typos are applied to the original text fields, i.e. before concatenating tabular
        features (converted to strings) and text fields.


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      • seqLengthSplit: The three intensities of shift correspond to different levels of sampling rates
865     used to construct the final target dataset: We sample with a rate of 10%, 50%, or 90% from T1 ,
866     respectively. T1 includes either the 10% shortest (scenario 1) or longest (scenario 2) text fields.
867
      • cutText: The three intensities correspond to various proportions of target data affected by this shift
868
        (10%, 50%, or 90%) and different percentage of text to truncate (10%, 50%, or 90%) from the
869     original text field.
870
      • abbrev: The three intensities correspond to various percentages of target data affected by
871

872     abbreviations from Oxford English Dictionary (https://www.oed.com/information/
873     understanding-entries/abbreviations/).
874

        abbrev: These multimodal shifts are just combinations of unimodal shifts, and follow the same
876     rules as previously described.
877
      • newClass: The three intensities of shift correspond to different levels of sampling rates used to
878
        construct the final target dataset: We sample with respective rates of 10%, 50%, or 90% from
