009                                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural networks can fail when the data contains spurious correlations, i.e. associa-
012            tions in the training data that fail to generalize to new distributions. To understand
013            this phenomenon, often referred to as subpopulation shift or shortcut learning,
014            researchers have proposed numerous group-annotated spurious correlations bench-
015            marks upon which to evaluate mitigation methods. However, we observe that these
016            benchmarks exhibit substantial disagreement, with the best methods on one bench-
017
               mark performing poorly on another. We explore this disagreement, and examine
               benchmark validity by defining three desiderata that a benchmark should satisfy
018
               in order to meaningfully evaluate methods. Our results have implications for both
019
               benchmarks and mitigations: we find that certain group-annotated benchmarks are
020            not meaningful measures of method performance, and that several methods are not
021            sufficiently robust for widespread use. We present a simple recipe for practitioners
022            to choose methods using the most similar benchmark to their given problem.
023

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   A striking failure mode of deep learning-based models is their susceptibility to spurious correlations,
028   whereby models learn to use patterns that only hold in certain subsets of the data (Nagarajan et al.,
029   2020; Geirhos et al., 2020). Researchers have produced numerous group-annotated benchmarks
030   for evaluating and comparing methods for mitigating spurious correlations, ultimately informing
031
      decisions as to which method is best. In order to draw robust conclusions about which method to use,
      one would hope that different benchmarks produce similar results. However, in Figure 1 we observe
032
      this not to be the case: benchmarks often disagree, and methods that perform well on one benchmark
033
      perform poorly on others.

      Faced with multiple benchmarks, standard machine learning practice is to average over contradictory
055   results. Averaging, however, neglects that different benchmarks may measure different things, only
056   some of which correspond to the desired quality. This presents a barrier to mitigating spurious
057   correlations in practice. When confronted by a new dataset where a group attribute correlates with
058   the target, which benchmark should one trust when deciding which method to apply?
059
      In this work, we expose benchmark disagreement and analyze the validity of common group-annotated
060
      spurious correlations benchmarks. To do so, we suggest a set of properties that a spurious correlations
061   benchmark should satisfy, and introduce a model-dependent statistic that quantifies the benchmark’s
062   task difficulty due to correlation between group attribute and target label. Using the established idea
063   of convergent validity (Jacobs & Wallach, 2021), we expect that two valid benchmarks testing similar
064   things—exhibiting similar task difficulty due to spurious correlation—should rank methods similarly.
065
      Our results reveal that certain group-annotated benchmarks are not valid tools for evaluating
066
      mitigation method performance. Moreover, of all the methods evaluated here, only a small handful
067   are robust to different tasks, while many exhibit strong performance only under specific conditions.
068   Finally, we provide an approach to translating between benchmark results and real-world datasets,
069   using our model-dependent statistic to understand which benchmark is most relevant.

      1.1   BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
074
075
076   Models trained with empirical risk minimization (ERM; Vapnik, 1999, Ch. 1) tend to learn spurious
077   correlations (Nagarajan et al., 2020), resulting in many real-world failures (Geirhos et al., 2020). For
078   example, chest x-ray classifiers latch onto physical features of the scanner that fail to generalize to
079   new hospitals (Zech et al., 2018). In hate speech detection, models use dialect differences rather than
080   learning the desired task, resulting in a disproportionate false-positive rate (Sap et al., 2019). These
081
      spurious correlations often result in models which amplify bias (Zhao et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2019).
082   Many researchers have sought to understand why models rely on spurious correlations. Sagawa
083   et al. (2020b) suggest that overparameterized models’ bias against memorization leads them to
084   rely on spurious correlations for minority samples. Given their well-known inductive bias towards
085   simplicity (Kalimeris et al., 2019; Valle-Perez et al., 2018; Bell & Sagun, 2023), models use spurious
086   correlations if they are simpler to learn than the intended function (Shah et al., 2020; Hall et al., 2022;
087
      Yang et al., 2024), where simplicity may be determined by model capacity (Sreekumar & Boddeti,
      2023). Key factors determining the deleterious effect of a spurious correlation include the separability
088
      of the spurious features (Wang & Wang, 2024), correlation between the attribute and target (Yang
089
      et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2023), the relative signal-to-noise ratios of the core and spurious features
090   (Yang et al., 2024), the number of spurious features Lin et al. (2023), and the relative size of the
091   groups (Deng et al., 2023). Our effort to quantify task difficulty due to spurious correlation builds on
092   these ideas of model-specific complexity, accounting for both correlation strength and learnability.
093
      Yang et al. (2023) introduce SubpopBench and evaluate 22 mitigation methods over benchmarks
094
      exhibiting different types of subpopulation shift, including attribute and class imbalance and missing
095
      data. Our work builds on SubpopBench due to its comprehensive set of methods and benchmarks,
096   though we narrow our focus to only spurious correlations. Several other benchmarking efforts exist,
097   including those for evaluating spurious correlations mitigation (Joshi et al., 2023; Lynch et al., 2023),
098   and other forms of subpopulation shift (Koh et al., 2021; Santurkar et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023).
099   More broadly, Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz (2020) introduce the DomainBed library for benchmarking
100   performance in various domain generalization scenarios. Interestingly, both Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz
101   and Joshi et al. find that with sufficient hyperparameter tuning ERM can be surprisingly robust,
102   motivating our consideration of ERM failure as a necessary benchmark property.
103   Unlike previous work concerned with benchmarking existing mitigations methods, our aim is instead
104   to investigate the validity of the benchmarks themselves. Our approaches are complementary: while
105   new benchmarks and systematic evaluations are essential, so too are meta-analyses that help us make
106   sense of conflicting results. Practically, we envisage our analysis supporting practical decisions around
107   which benchmarks to rely upon, for example by filtering the benchmarks included in SubpopBench,
      or choosing between newer benchmark variants (e.g., Joshi et al., 2023; Lynch et al., 2023).

      1.2                                       O UTLINE AND C ONTRIBUTIONS
128
129   We begin in §2 with the simple observation that published results on spurious correlations benchmarks
130   often disagree. This motivates our investigation of what benchmarks are measuring, and whether
131   some benchmarks are more valid than others.
132
      In §3, we introduce three desiderata—ERM Failure, Discriminative Power, and Convergent Validity—
133
      that capture key properties that a spurious correlations benchmark should satisfy in order to be a
134
      meaningful test of mitigation performance. Our test for Convergent Validity will call for some way
135   of understanding precisely what a benchmark is actually measuring. To address this, in §3.4.1 we
136   propose a model-dependent statistic that measures the difficulty of a task due to spurious correlation.
137
138
      In §4 we analyze the validity of eight benchmarks and the robustness of 22 mitigation methods.
      Finally, in §5 we present a recipe for mapping between benchmarks and real-world datasets, and
139
      evaluate our approach on geographically-diverse image classification. We conclude with the broader
140
      implications of our work in §6.
141
142   2                                       N OT A LL B ENCHMARKS AGREE
143
144   We define a benchmark as a pair of a task dataset (e.g. an image classification task) and an evaluation
145   metric for ranking methods (e.g. worst-group test accuracy). A spurious correlations benchmark is
146   designed to measure how well methods can mitigate the effects of spurious correlations. These are
147   typically designed so that conventional training yields poor performance on certain subsets of the
148   data, referred to as groups. If a benchmark’s function is to allow us to conclude which methods are
149   best, we would ideally like different benchmarks to agree with one another.
150   To our surprise, we find that they do not. Figure 1a shows the (dis)agreement, as measured by
151   Pearson’s r, in worst-group test accuracies over benchmarks reported by Yang et al. (2023) (see
152   Appendix A). Two popular benchmarks, Waterbirds and CelebA, produce only mildly-correlated
153   results, while results on Waterbirds and NICO++ are negatively correlated. This has a practical
154   outcome: Figure 1b shows that the best performing method on Waterbirds is the second-worst
155   on NICO++. This disagreement is not due to benchmark saturation, as Waterbirds and NICO++
156   worst-group test accuracies have different means (x̄ = 78.4 and 37.8 respectively) but similarly large
157   standard deviations (s = 6.2 and 5.8). We report similar findings on WILDS (Koh et al., 2021)
158
      (Appendix A). This poses a challenge for the practitioner looking to mitigate spurious correlations:
      faced with inconsistent benchmarks, which method to use?
159
160   We propose a simple methodology to aid this problem by studying why benchmarks disagree, and
161   evaluate whether some benchmarks are more valid measures of mitigation performance than others.
      To do so, we define three desiderata that valid spurious correlations benchmarks should respect.

      3         N OT A LL B ENCHMARKS ARE VALID
175
176
      For a benchmark to be a meaningful test of the ability to mitigate spurious correlations, we suggest it
177
      should satisfy three desiderata: ERM Failure, Discriminative Power, and Convergent Validity.
178
179   ERM Failure (§3.2). Spurious correlations mitigation methods are intended to prevent models from
180   learning patterns that might perform well on average, but cause failures for certain groups. Thus,
181   any benchmark intended to evaluate these methods should induce this problem when training with
182
      conventional empirical risk minimization (ERM), which by definition optimizes to minimize error
      averaged over all samples. To satisfy ERM Failure, a benchmark should produce between-group
183
      performance disparities for models trained with ERM.
184
185   Discriminative Power (§3.3). All benchmarks are intended for evaluation and comparison, and to
186   support reasoning about which methods are best. In order to serve this purpose, a benchmark must
187   discriminate between methods and assign different scores to each. These scores, in the spurious
188
      correlations setting, are typically worst-group test accuracies (Sagawa et al., 2020a). To satisfy
      Discriminative Power, a benchmark should produce different worst-group test accuracies for
189
      different methods.
190
191   Convergent Validity (§3.4). Even if a benchmark satisfies both ERM Failure and Discriminative
192   Power, it still needs to rank methods in a meaningful way. In other words, we want our benchmark
193   to exhibit construct validity (Jacobs & Wallach, 2021; Blodgett et al., 2021), i.e. it should allow us
194
      to truly measure the extent to which methods mitigate spurious correlations. Establishing construct
      validity of a benchmark is challenging without a ground truth, though one approach is to consider
195
      how the benchmark performs in relation to other benchmarks (that are themselves valid according to
196
      our first two desiderata). To satisfy Convergent Validity, a benchmark should agree with other
197   similar benchmarks, and disagree with those that are dissimilar.
198

200
201   We evaluate the validity of eight benchmarks included in SubpopBench: Waterbirds (Sagawa et al.,
202   2020a), CelebA (Liu et al., 2015), ImageNetBG (a.k.a. IN-9L Original; Xiao et al., 2021), MetaShift

205

212   We follow Yang et al.’s methodology (see Appendix C), report worst-group test accuracies, and

      3.2                                 E VALUATING ERM FAILURE
252
      We test for ERM Failure by evaluating the variability of per-group test accuracies of ERM-trained
253
      models. High variability indicates that certain groups have worse performance than others, whereas
254   low variability indicates that there is no real “worst group”, thus not satisfying the ERM Failure
255   desideratum.
256
257
      Figure 2a shows the standard deviation (SD) of per-group mean test accuracies for an ERM-trained
      model. Immediately, we notice that ImageNetBG exhibits very low between-group variability and
258
      thus does not satisfy ERM Failure.
259

261   3.3                                 E VALUATING D ISCRIMINATIVE P OWER
262
      To evaluate Discriminative Power, we test whether benchmarks assign different scores to different
263
      methods, by measuring the SD over method worst-group test accuracies for each dataset. Low SD
264
      indicates that all methods perform similarly, so the benchmark cannot discriminate between them,
265   therefore not satisfying Discriminative Power.2
266
267      2
           A limitation of our approach is that low Discriminative Power could also be the result of all methods
268   exhibiting similarly poor performance, such that the benchmark is valid but all methods are insufficiently
269   powerful. Given the inclusion of ERM in the set of methods, and assuming ERM Failure is satisfied, we don’t
      imagine this to be of practical concern.

      3.4                 E VALUATING C ONVERGENT VALIDITY
296
297   We suggest that the defining characteristic of a spurious correlations benchmark is the task difficulty
298   due to spurious correlation. Thus, to test Convergent Validity we check if two benchmarks with similar
299   task difficulty produce similar results. Specifically, we measure how inter-benchmark agreement
300   changes as a function of the difference in task difficulty due to spurious correlation. Datasets that
301   exhibit convergent validity should show a strong correlation, where increasing distance increases
302   disagreement.
303
304   3.4.1                  I NTRODUCING K: TASK D IFFICULTY DUE TO S PURIOUS C ORRELATION
305
306   Before we can evaluate Convergent Validity, we must first take a brief detour to understand what
307
      benchmarks actually measure. We argue that three factors should determine how a spurious correla-
      tions benchmark behaves:
308
309
                          1. The strength of the association, i.e. how often targets and attributes co-occur in the data;
310
                          2. The difficulty of learning the correlated attribute, i.e. how easily can a model predict the

317   which are necessarily model-dependent.3 See Appendix B for empirical evidence of this problem.
318   To account for all three factors, we propose using the Bayes Factor as a model-dependent statistic
319   that quantifies the task difficulty due to spurious correlations. The Bayes Factor evaluates the relative
320   model performance of a model that can leverage the spurious correlation to solve the task, and a
321   model that is penalized for doing so.

456   5       P RACTICAL R ECOMMENDATIONS
457
458   When competing benchmarks suggest different methods, practitioners are left with a crucial question:
459   what should I use for my specific dataset? Building upon our investigation of benchmark validity, we
460   take a first step towards bridging the gap between real-world problems and common benchmarks.
461
      A typical strategy is to take the method with the best performance averaged over all benchmarks. We
462   evaluate two improvements over this conventional practice. First, we suggest only averaging over
463   benchmarks deemed valid according to our three desiderata. Second, we suggest choosing the most
464   similar benchmark to our given problem. Concretely, given some arbitrary dataset (which we refer as
465   a “test dataset”6 ) we recommend that practitioners first calculate its K, and select the benchmark
466   with the closest K. We hypothesize that the best performing methods on the closest benchmark will
467   be most appropriate for the test dataset.
468   We test our approach using a Leave-One-Out analysis, where for each of eight test datasets we
469   select the next closest benchmark, and evaluate the closest benchmark’s best performing method on
470   our test dataset (“Closest Benchmark”). We compare worst-group test accuracies for this method
471   against the best method according to the all-benchmark average (“All Benchmarks”), and the valid-
472   benchmark average (“Valid Benchmarks”). We perform an additional evaluation using Dollar Street,
473   a dataset of geographically-diverse household images. Previous work has identified significantly
474   worse performance of contemporary vision models for non-Western regions (de Vries et al., 2019;
475   Richards et al., 2023). We extend SubpopBench to include a 42-class object classification task using
476
      Dollar Street, where group information is geographic region. See Appendix D for further details.
477   Table 2 shows the results of our two approaches. Averaging over benchmarks results in a single
478   “best” method. Over all benchmarks, this method is GroupDRO; over valid benchmarks only this
479   is ReSample. For six of nine test datasets, ReSample outperforms GroupDRO, confirming that
480   filtering benchmarks before averaging is helpful. For five of eight test datasets,7 selecting a method
481   according to the closest benchmark would produce superior worst-group test accuracies, supporting
482
      our hypothesis that selecting based on similarity improves performance.

494   6    D ISCUSSION
495
496   Benchmark validity. Our work joins a wider discussion evaluation practice validity, such as that of
497   Jacobs & Wallach (2021) who argue that machine learning researchers can “collapse the distinctions
498   between constructs and their operationalizations” (p. 384). Blodgett et al. (2021) find that many
499
      NLP fairness benchmarks are not meaningful measurement tools, while Subramonian et al. (2023)
      consider how differing task conceptualizations lead to benchmark disagreement. Denton et al. (2021)
500
      examine the implicit assumptions behind ImageNet, in particular the way its creators operationalize a
501
      particular conception of vision. Benchmarks considered here may encode notions of spuriousness
502   whose appropriateness is context-sensitive.
503
504   Practical applicability. When simplifying real-world problems into benchmarks, we sometimes
505
      lose sight of our original intent (Selbst et al., 2019). Our results call for consideration of whether
      solving a benchmark corresponds to solving a real problem. For example, we observe that Dollar
506
      Street has a low task difficulty due to spurious correlation (Figure 5c). Thus, if our motive were to
507
      reduce computer vision’s Western bias (Richards et al., 2023), one could ask whether developing
508   methods optimized for spurious correlations is a helpful endeavor.
509
510   Broader concerns. Beyond validity, we might also ask about benchmark acceptability. One
511
      benchmark, CelebA (Liu et al., 2015), involves an association between hair color and a binary
      “gender” attribute (according to an external annotator), reinforcing views about gender that could be
512
      harmful to non-binary and gender nonconforming people (Keyes, 2018; Denton et al., 2020). It is our
513
      firm view that CelebA should not be used for benchmarking.
514
515   Limitations. One limitation of our work is that both the ERM Failure and Discriminative Power
516   desiderata depend on well-defined group attributes. Similarly, while Convergent Validity can in prin-
517
      cipal be applied to any benchmark, our use of K also requires group information for the reweighted
      model. More broadly, our evaluation only only considers benchmarks with well-defined group
518
      attributes, which may neglect realistic scenarios where distribution shifts are unknown. Exploration
519
      of spurious correlations benchmarks without attributes remains an exciting area for research.
520
521   The need for attribute information is also suggestive of a more subtle limitation. It is important to draw
522   a distinction between satisfying a desideratum itself, and passing our test as currently implemented.
523
      For example, our test for Discriminative Power relies on standard deviation, which can be influenced
      by the presence of outliers. Whichever statistic used, reasoning about validity necessitates carefully
524
      assessing multiple streams of evidence, such as considering Discriminative Power in combination
525
      with other desiderata.
526
527   Our explicitly model-centric approach accounts for the relative utility of a spurious correlation to
528   a specific model: as we note earlier, a color-based group feature is of no use to a grayscale-CNN
529
      (Arjovsky et al., 2019). Accordingly, our benchmark evaluation is conditional upon on the specific
      model architectures we used for testing mitigations methods, ResNet-50 and BERT base (chosen for
530
      their continued popularity in spurious correlations research). As the research community continues
531
      to develop new model architectures, we expect continued re-evaluation of the utility of current
532   benchmarks (particularly with respect to ERM Failure) is likely to be necessary.
533
534   Closing remark. Benchmarks form an essential component of the way machine learning evaluates
535
      methods and draws conclusions. In the domain of spurious correlations, our analysis suggests that
      not all of our benchmarks yield equally meaningful results. We hope to have shown that benchmark
536
      choice matters: it leads to different conclusions; different recommendations; and ultimately better or
537
      worse deployed models.

      C     S UBPOP B ENCH E VALUATION D ETAILS
811
812   To evaluate the validity of common benchmarks, we build upon the comprehensive benchmarking
813   library, SubpopBench, of Yang et al. (2023). The worst-group test accuracies we report follow the
814   exact methodology specified by Yang et al. (2023). Reported results are the worst-group test accuracy
815   on the test set. Group attributes are available at all times. Following Yang et al. (2023), we search 16
816   random hyperparameter configurations, and train 3 random seeds of the best performing configuration,
817   where the “best” is the model with the highest worst-group test accuracy on the validation set. Vision
818   benchmarks used a ResNet-50 model (He et al., 2016), pretrained on ImageNet-1k (Russakovsky
819   et al., 2015). Language benchmarks used a BERT base uncased model (Devlin et al., 2019). For full
820
      details see Yang et al. (2023).
821
822   D     B ENCHMARKS
823
824          • Waterbirds (Sagawa et al., 2020a) is a binary image classification task of land-dwelling vs.
825            water-dwelling birds, where the spuriously correlated attribute is water and land background
826            scenes.
827          • CelebA (Liu et al., 2015) is a binary image classification task of blond vs. not-blond hair
828            color, where the spuriously correlated attribute is annotator-perceived binary gender. When
829            training on CelebA, the only output models are capable of is a blond vs. not-blond
830            binary judgment.
831          • ImageNet Backgrounds Challenge, ImageNetBG (a.k.a. IN-9L Original; Xiao et al., 2021)
832            is a multiclass image classification task, where images are selected from 9 coarse classes
833            of ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009). Xiao et al. intend that the background is correlated with
834
               the classes, but there is no attribute information available in the SubpopBench formulation.
               Following Yang et al. (2023), mitigation methods fall back to class labels in the absence of
835
               attribute information.
836
837
             • MetaShift (Indoor/Outdoor Cat vs. Dog; Liang & Zou, 2022) is a binary image classification
               task where the spuriously correlated attribute is indoor or outdoor scenes.
838
839          • NICO++ (Zhang et al., 2023) is a multiclass image classification task over common objects,
840            plants and animals where the spuriously correlated attribute is one of (autumn, dim, grass,
841
               outdoor, rock, water).
842          • CheXpert (Irvin et al., 2019) is a medical image classification task over chest x-rays, with a
843            binary classification into "finding" or "no finding". The spurious correlated attributes are
844
               patient race and gender, following (Yang et al., 2023).
845          • CivilComments (Borkan et al., 2019) is a binary text classification task of internet comments
846            to be classified as toxic / not toxic. The spuriously correlated attribute is one of 9 group
847
               identities that are the target of the toxicity.
848          • MultiNLI (Williams et al., 2018) is a natural language inference task, comprising sets of
849            sentences where sentences can either entail, contradict or be neutral with one another. The
850
               spuriously correlated attribute is the presence of negation words.
851          • Dollar Street (Gaviria Rojas et al., 2022) is a geographically diverse collection of household
852            images, which in our work we frame as a multiclass object classification task, where
853            the attribute information is geographic region. Although the original Dollar Street more
854
               classes, we filter them to only include classes that are present in every region, following
               (Gaviria Rojas et al., 2022). When training on Dollar Street, models are only capable of
855
               outputting one of 42 classes, none of which are related to people.

      D.1   C ITYBIRDS
858
859   Citybirds is a new variant of Waterbirds where the confounding background scenes are urban or rural
860   environments. We generate Citybirds using the Waterbirds generation scripts of Sagawa et al. (2020a),
861   modifying the choice of background images. Background images are drawn from the Places365
862   dataset (López-Cifuentes et al., 2020), with urban backgrounds from the “street” and “downtown”
863   classes, and rural backgrounds from the “farm” and “field/cultivated” classes. The number of samples
      per group is matched between Waterbirds and Citybirds.

      D.2                                                 A NIMALS VS . P LANTS (AV P)
865
866   AvP is a new binary classification task over diverse images of animals and plants, drawn from two
867   geographic regions, Asia and Europe. The target label is animal or plant, and the group attribute is
868   Asia or Europe. We construct AvP by sampling images from the GeoYFCC dataset (Dubey et al.,
869   2021) of natural images, limiting our sample to only include classes that are within the hierarchy of
870   the “animal.n.01” and “plant.n.02” ImageNet hierarchy. We explicitly exclude all images containing
871   people. To identify region as either Asia or Europe, we map the country information provided by
872   GeoYFCC to continents using the pycountry_convert Python package. The number of samples per
873   group exactly matches Waterbirds. We validated that geographic information is harder to extract
874
      than the animals vs. plants classification task by comparing two linear models, one to predict class
      membership and another to predict geographic region, over representations extracted from a ResNet-
875
      50 (He et al., 2016) pre-trained on ImageNet-1K (Russakovsky et al., 2015). As expected, geographic
876
      performance was at chance level compared to much stronger performance for the target task.

907   We implement a number of synthetic modifications to existing benchmarks in order to sanity check
908   our measure K. To test for the effect of the amount of attribute–target correlation, we modify
909   Waterbirds and CelebA. For Waterbirds, use Sagawa et al. (2020a)’s generation scripts, varying the
910   “confounder strength” argument. For CelebA, we use subsampling to balance out the effect of the hair
911   color and gender correlation, while keeping the total number of samples constant.
912   We also test version of Waterbirds with various degrees of RGB Gaussian noise applied to the
913   background images and the foreground images, though we note that the outline of the bird (if
914   not its detail) is still visible even under high noise. Given that noise can easily be memorized by
915   contemporary neural networks, we also produce a version with a solid gray background, finding
916   equivalent results to the 100% noise background case. Finally, we test for the effect of attribute noise
917   by randomly flipping a proportion of the annotations, finding the K becomes less able to detect the
      spurious correlation as attribute noise increases.

944   A natural consequence of a model-dependent metric is a degree of sensitivity to the optimization
945   procedure used when training the underlying model. For example, it is possible that K may vary

      We vary hyperparameters in lockstep between the two models, such that the learning rate for both

1009   We test whether the variability in method performance is a function of varying K by fitting a linear
1010   model using OLS to the worst-group test accuracies and the benchmark’s K. We evaluate sensitivity
1011   in terms of the proportion of variance explained, using the linear model’s Coefficient of Determination,
1012   R2 . A higher R2 indicate that K explains more of the variance in method performance, indicating
1013   greater sensitivity.
