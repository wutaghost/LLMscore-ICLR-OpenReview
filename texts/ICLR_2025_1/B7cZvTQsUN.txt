009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We present Structured World Modeling From Low-Level Observations
012            (“SWMPO”), a framework for the unsupervised learning of neural Finite State
013            Machines (FSM) that capture environment structure. Traditional unsupervised
014            world modeling methods for policy optimization rely on unstructured representa-
015            tions, such as neural networks, which do not explicitly represent high-level pat-
016            terns within the system (e.g., walking vs swimming). In contrast, SWMPO ex-
017            plicitly models the environment as an FSM, where each state represents a region
018            of the environment’s state space with distinct dynamics, exposing the structure of
019
               the environment to downstream tasks such as policy optimization. Prior works that
               synthesize FSMs for this purpose have been limited to discrete spaces, not con-
020
               tinuous, high-dimensional spaces. Our FSM synthesis algorithm operates in an
021
               unsupervised manner, leveraging low-level features from unprocessed, non-visual
022            data, making it adaptable across various domains. We demonstrate the advantages
023            of SWMPO by benchmarking its environment modeling capabilities in different
024            simulated environments.

      This paper examines learned approximations of environment dynamics, known as world models Ha
      & Schmidhuber (2018) in the special case where these models must explicitly encode the high-
046
      level structure of the environment dynamics. We are motivated by the observation that the high-
047
      level structure of a dynamical system can be used to efficiently solve control problems. Consider,
048   for example, an amphibious robot that must navigate both water and land (see fig. 1). An expert
049   roboticist might approach this problem by breaking down the task into three sub-problems: (1)
050   controlling the robot on water; (2) controlling it on land and; (3) managing the transition between
051   these two modes. With this division, the expert can exploit the fact that the robot moves faster on
052   land than in water, using this knowledge to optimize route planning. Inspired by this strategy, our
053   goal is to develop a method that automatically constructs a representation of the environment and a
      corresponding fine state machine.

054
      We are interested in extracting structure directly from continuous, low-level, non-visual observations
055   (e.g., LiDAR measurements or joint positions). To this end, we propose Structured World Modeling
056   From Low-Level Observations –SWMPO– a framework where an environment’s high-level structure
057   is inferred directly from low-level continuous observations in a fully unsupervised manner (i.e., with
058   no training labels), resulting in an FSM which can then be utilized in downstream tasks such as
059   policy optimization.
060
      The synthesized FSM consists of modes and transitions. Each mode is a neural network that ap-
061   proximates the environment dynamics within a subset of the state space (e.g., the walking mode,
062   see fig. 1). Predicates determine when to switch between modes based on observations of the envi-
063   ronment. We evaluate SWMPO across a variety of benchmarks and environments with continuous
064   dynamics, including two-and three-dimensional simulations.
065
066   Contributions Our contributions are as follows:
067
068         1. A novel unsupervised learning algorithm that segments time series data into a discrete set
069            of modes.
070         2. A state-machine synthesis algorithm that constructs a Finite State Machine (FSM) model
071            directly from continuous low-level observations, enabling interpretable representations of
072
               latent dynamics.
073         3. Empirical testing demonstrating the performance of the state machine across fours test
074
               environments.

076   2   R ELATED WORK
077
078   Automata Synthesis and Symbolic Structure Extraction Hasanbeig et al. (2021) demonstrated
079   that FSMs could be synthesized to model environments, improving performance in RL tasks. How-
080   ever, this method is limited by its reliance on fully-symbolic representations obtained from pre-
081   trained vision models in grid-world settings. In contrast, our approach extracts structure from con-
082
      tinuous, low-level, non-visual observations. To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first to
      leverage neural world models in the synthesis of FSMs for continuous low-level high-dimensional
083
      non-visual observation spaces.
084
085
      Hidden Markov Models Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) are a standard approach to captur-
086
      ing temporal dependencies and mode-switching behavior in sequential data (Li & Biswas, 2002;
087   Bouguila et al., 2022). In robotics, HMMs have been leveraged to segment trajectories into dis-
088   crete modes (Goh et al., 2012) and used during policy learning for multimodal or hierarchical tasks
089   (Marturi et al., 2019). Recent advances have extended HMMs using deep neural network architec-
090   tures (neural HMMs) to handle high-dimensional, continuous observation spaces and to learn more
091   complex transition dynamics (Tran et al., 2016). For instance, neural HMMs have been used in
092   unsupervised settings to model complex sensory streams for trajectory clustering Vakanski et al.
093   (2012) and to predict latent modes during task execution Wu et al. (2019). However, HMMs suffer
094   from the fundamental limitation that the transition between modes is determined by a probability
095
      distribution that is only conditioned on the latent state, this means that the observed evolution of the
      system itself is only indirectly used to update the active mode.
096
097
      Leveraging Structure in Reinforcement Learning A body of research focuses on leveraging
098
      structure to solve control problems with RL (Mohan et al., 2024). Hierarchical RL (Xu & Fekri,
099
      2021; Botvinick, 2012; Li et al., 2006) and modular RL (Simpkins & Isbell, 2019; Andreas et al.,
100   2017; Devin et al., 2017) encode structure directly into the policy architecture, here we instead
101   consider the synthesis of a structured model. Model-based RL approaches leverage neural world
102   models to optimize policies more efficiently (Moerland et al., 2023; Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018).
103   However, neural models lack distinct boundaries between the representation of different modes in
104   the environment. Reward machines (Toro Icarte et al., 2019; Icarte et al., 2018) leverage structured
105   models of the reward function to guide the policy optimization process.
106
107   Structure Induction and Hybrid Systems In the broader field of hybrid systems, modeling en-
      vironments as a collection of modes with distinct dynamics is standard practice (Alur et al., 1995;


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Paoletti et al., 2007; Ferrari-Trecate et al., 2003; Devin et al., 2017; Camacho et al., 2010). Recently,
109   Soto et al. (2021) show that automata with affine dynamics can be synthesized from time-series data.
110   In this work, we leverage non-linear neural models to both extract structure and represent dynamics
111   within the automata, making our approach more general.
112
      Finally, other approaches to discovering structure which are not directly applicable to time-series
113
      data include the use of graph neural networks (Cranmer et al., 2020) and sparse networks (Gupta
114
      et al., 2024). Similarly, methods that leverage recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and FSMs to model
115   linguistic structures (Kolen, 1993; Koul et al., 2018; Jacobsson, 2005) share a conceptual foundation
116   with our work, but these methods focus on formal languages and are not directly applicable to the
117   class of problems we study in this work.

153   4     P ROBLEM STATEMENT
154
155   Consider the example of an amphibious robot that must navigate both water and land (see fig. 1),
156   corresponding to the two modes of the system. Our goal is two-fold: (1) to synthesize an FSMWM
157   from low-level observations in an unsupervised manner (i.e., without mode labels) that captures the
158
      high-level structure of the environment by moving between modes that correspond to the (unob-
      served) mode of the system, and (2) to leverage the FSMWM in the RL training loop.
159
160   Our fundamental assumption is that the latent categorical variable Mt , which corresponds to the
161   modes, can be characterized by a function m : (st−1 , at−1 , st ) 7→ mt . Further assumptions made
      by our method on the system and Mt are motivated and stated in section 5.1.

177   As illustrated in fig. 1, the inputs to our proposed framework, SWMPO, are a POMDP
178   (S, A, T, S0 , Ω, O) with associated reward function R, the number of modes m, and an expert policy
179
      π0 used for initial data collection. The outputs are (1) a state machine with m nodes that approxi-
      mates T , and (2) a policy that approximately maximizes the reward in the POMDP.
180
181   Our method synthesizes an FSM to model the structure of the environment, where each state in
182   the FSM represents a distinct mode of the data (e.g., swimming or walking). A key challenge is
183   discovering these modes in an unsupervised manner, as only the number of modes is assumed to be
184   known. Ensuring clear separation between modes at different stages of the algorithm is also critical
185
      to avoid cascading errors from misclassified data, which can progressively degrade the model’s
      performance. To address these challenges, we synthesize our state machine as follows:
186
187
            1. Labeling: Divide the transitions in the dataset into different mode subsets.
188
189         2. Pruning: Simplify the mode-transition dynamics of the partition by removing spurious
190
               transitions between modes.
191         3. Transition Predicate Synthesis: Learn when to transition between modes.

193   5.1   L ABELING
194
195   Labeling addresses the problem of decomposing environment dynamics by assigning each transition
196   in a dataset D of trajectories to one of m disjoint subsets, with each subset corresponding to a
197   mode of the POMDP. We first state the assumptions of the labeling algorithm, and then describe the
198   algorithm.
199   Let ⟨S, A, T, S0 , Ω, O⟩ be a POMDP. Let St and At be the random variables of the state and action at
200   time t respectively, under some fixed policy π. We focus on the case where observations conditioned
201   on a state are deterministic, so ot = O(st ). We are interested in modelling the mode variable Mt
202   (e.g., mt = walking), taking values in some set M . Our method revolves around learning Mt as an
203   intermediate computation of a learned first-order model of the form
204
                                    f (m(ot−1 , at−1 , ot ), ot , at ) ≈ ot+1 − ot
205
206   which we describe in this section. Ultimately we characterize modes as a categorical variable (i.e.,
207   robot is either walking or swimming), but we first approximate Mt as taking values in Rn . We now
208   impose constraints on the POMDP and the mode variable that allows us to design an algorithm to
209   predict Mt . In summary, the assumptions imply that partial observability is a consequence solely of
210   the latent mode variable and that this variable can in principle be predicted from previous observa-
211   tions.
212
213   Assumption 1: mode identifiability We start with the assumption that Mt can be modelled as a
214   function mt ≈ m(ot−1 , at−1 , ot ). Intuitively, this assumption means that it is possible to identify
215   the current mode by observing how the world changed under the latest action. We thus think of Mt
      as an abstraction over the observed change of the system under some action and state.

      Assumption 2: change can be predicted conditioned on mode The next assumption is that the
217   POMDP becomes a deterministic MDP conditioned on the mode. More precisely, we assume the
218   existence of a function T ′ : M × A × O → O such that T ′ (mt , at , ot ) = O(T (st , at )).
219
220
      Thus far, our constraints allow the trivial solution Mt = St , which is not useful. There may be many
      other variables which satisfy our assumptions. Consequently, we add a constraint that allows us to
221
      uniquely identify Mt .

223
      Assumption 3: modes alone have minimal information Let M be the set of random variables
224
      that satisfy the previous assumptions. Then the mode variable Mt is the unique solution to Mt =
225   arg minMt ∈M I(Mt , Ot+1 ).
226
227   Under the assumptions so far, we can so far conclude that if we find a variable Mt that allows us
228
      to predict the change in the environment given an action and has minimal mutual information with
      Ot+1 , then Mt must be the mode variable. However, our approximation of Mt takes values in a
229
      vector space, but we ultimately want to model it as a categorical variable. We therefore add our last
230
      assumption.
231
232   Assumption 4: mode vectors form clusters Mt corresponds to a partition of the state space
233   where in expectation the within-subset sum of squares to the centroid of the subset is minimal. That
234   is, we assume a strict partitioning, centroid model clustering scheme, which means that if k-means
235   is run on vectors of Mt , then in expectation the clusters will correspond to the different modes.
236

237
      action and has minimal mutual information with Ot+1 , then that variable corresponds to the mode
238   variable. In other words, consider m : O × A × O → M and f : M × A × O → O under the joint

      5.2     P RUNING
288
289   The aforementioned partitioning process can create overly complex transitions. While the FSM
290   globally approximates the environment dynamics, some state regions may have multiple models
291   with similar accuracy, resulting in spurious transitions between states. In such cases, transitions
292   between these models can be pruned with minimal impact on performance.
293
      To address this, we apply a pruning mechanism to eliminate these unwanted transitions. This helps
294   balance the complexity-accuracy trade-off in the state machine search space: while more complex
295   transition patterns can improve accuracy, they also increase the risk of overfitting and reduce inter-
296   pretability. We now describe the pruning approach, which optimizes for both accuracy and simplic-
297   ity.
298
299   Pruning Approach We begin by labeling each transition in the dataset with the index of the neural
300   network from the ensemble that best predicts the system’s evolution in that state. A mode transition
301   occurs when this label changes between consecutive states. For example, in the sequence 113322,
302   we transition from mode 1 to 3, then from 3 to 2. Pruning the transition to mode 3 yields two
303   possible sequences: 111122 (forward-prune) or 112222 (backward-prune). Our goal is to remove
304   transitions that have minimal impact on prediction accuracy.
305   To prune a mode transition, the framework shifts the affected transitions from one subset to another,
306   causing a different model, with equal or greater prediction error, to handle those transitions. If the
307   increase in prediction error is within the user-defined tolerance factor ϵ, the move is considered ϵ-
308   valid relative to the original partition. A mode transition is ϵ-prunable if all the associated moves are
309   ϵ-valid. There may be multiple ϵ-prunable mode transitions for a given trajectory and partition. Our
310   approach is to greedily prune the first prunable mode transitions with the strategy that results in the
311   smallest prediction error increase (see algorithm 3).

490   7    L IMITATIONS
491
492   The main limitations of the framework are stated formally as assumptions in section 5.1. The main
493
      assumption is that the partial observability of the environment is a consequence solely of the mode
      variable. Another limitation is that the mode variable must be approximated from a single transition;
494
      generalizing this to allow for modes that require multiple steps to be identified is left for future work.

497   8    C ONCLUSION
498
499   We presented a novel framework for synthesizing Finite State Machine World Models (FSMWMs)
500   in an unsupervised manner using low-level, non-visual continuous observations. We outlined the
501
      key assumptions underpinning our approach and demonstrated its applicability. Our analysis shows
      that the synthesized FSMWMs effectively capture the underlying structure of the environment by
502
      mapping latent modes to discrete states. Additionally, our algorithm matches or surpasses the perfor-
503
      mance of a Hidden Markov Model baseline on challenging dynamical systems. The implementation
504   of the framework and all the code necessary to replicate the experiments, including hyperparameters,
505   are attached to this manuscript, and are open sourced.
