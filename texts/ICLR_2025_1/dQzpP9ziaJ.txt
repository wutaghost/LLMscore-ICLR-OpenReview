009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            With the development of large language models (LLMs), the sequence length of
012            these models continues to increase, drawing significant attention to long-context
013            language models. However, the evaluation of these models has been primarily
014            limited to their capabilities, with a lack of research focusing on their safety. Exist-
015            ing work, such as ManyShotJailbreak, has to some extent demonstrated that long-
016            context language models can exhibit safety concerns. However, the methods used
017            are limited and lack comprehensiveness. In response, we introduce LongSafety-
018
               Bench, the first benchmark designed to objectively and comprehensively evaluate
               the safety of long-context models. LongSafetyBench consists of 10 task cate-
019
               gories, with an average length of 41,889 words. After testing eight long-context
020
               language models on LongSafetyBench, we found that existing models generally
021            exhibit insufficient safety capabilities. Moreover, models’ safety performance in
022            long-context scenarios does not always align with that in short-context scenarios.
023            Further investigation revealed that long-context models tend to overlook harmful
024            content within lengthy texts. We also proposed a simple yet effective solution, al-
025            lowing open-source models to achieve performance comparable to that of top-tier
026            closed-source models. We believe that LongSafetyBench can serve as a valuable
027            benchmark for evaluating the safety capabilities of long-context language models.
028            We hope that our work will encourage the broader community to pay attention to
029
               the safety of long-context models and contribute to the development of solutions
               to improve the safety of long-context LLMs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Recently, thanks to more advanced model architectures (Xiao et al., 2024b;a; Liu et al., 2024a) and
035
      expanded position encoding techniques (Su et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b), the context length of
036   language models has been extended significantly (Achiam et al., 2023; Reid et al., 2024). In the
037   foreseeable future, as language models continue to evolve and tackle increasingly complex prob-
038   lems, the demand for handling longer contexts is expected to grow accordingly. We anticipate that
039   long-context language models will become mainstream.
040
      Previous research on long-context language models, such as LongBench (Bai et al., 2024), L-Eval
041
      (An et al., 2023), and RULER (Hsieh et al., 2024), has typically focused on their capabilities, while
042   neglecting to address their safety. In short-context scenarios, the safety issues of language models
043   have already been extensively studied.(Zhang et al., 2024b; Hartvigsen et al., 2022) In long-context
044   scenarios, Anthropic introduced ManyShotJailbreak(Anil et al., 2024), which revealed safety issues
045   in long-context language models. However, there is still a lack of comprehensive research on the
046   safety of these models. To better access the safety issues of long-context models, we need to design
047   a robust and comprehensive benchmark.

052   safe scenarios: Illegal Activities, Misinformation Harm, and Offensiveness and Bias. In total, we
053   designed ten tasks. To facilitate more convenient and objective testing, we formatted all data in
      a multiple-choice question format. We carefully designed the options so that each one reflects

107   The rise of large language models(LLM) has garnered substantial attention (Ouyang et al., 2022;
      Touvron et al., 2023a; Sun et al., 2024; Achiam et al., 2023), leading to a plethora of works based

      on these models. People have entrusted LLMs with a variety of tasks (Park et al., 2023; Wei et al.,
109   2023; Yin et al., 2023), resulting in progressively higher demands on their capabilities, one of which
110   is handling long texts. The length of text that a language model can process directly impacts the com-
111   plexity of the tasks it can perform. Tasks like Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al.,
112   2020; Wang et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024a) require models to have proficient long-context capa-
113   bilities. A significant amount of research is dedicated to enhancing models’ long-context abilities.
114   This includes capabilities like length extrapolation (An et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b), employing
115   more efficient KV-cache (Xiao et al., 2024b; Ge et al., 2024), and introducing non-attention-based
116   architectures (Gu & Dao, 2024; Peng et al., 2023a). Inspired by these advances, the context length
117
      supported by large models has continuously extended (Achiam et al., 2023; Reid et al., 2024).
118
119   2.2   S AFE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
120
      The safety of large language models (LLMs) has become a critical area of research due to their
121
      widespread deployment and increasing societal impact (Hartvigsen et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024).
122
      LLMs have demonstrated impressive capabilities across various tasks, but their unpredictability and
123   potential for harm have raised significant concerns (Touvron et al., 2023b). Researchers have con-
124   ducted extensive work in attempting to make models safer, such as optimizing the data collecting
125   and filtering process (Xu et al., 2021), and using reinforcement learning to enable models to learn
126   human preferences (Bai et al., 2022). With the advancement of research and the proliferation of lan-
127   guage models, researchers have realized the need to address additional dimensions of safety, such as
128   discrimination and bias (ElSherief et al., 2021), culturally relevant safety issues (Deng et al., 2022)
129   and etc. These works attempt to steer models toward safe and socially acceptable outputs, marking
130   a key shift in the ongoing evolution of LLM safety research.
131
132   2.3   L ONG -C ONTEXT B ENCHMARKS AND S AFETY B ENCHMARKS
133
      Many previous works have provided evaluation methods for long-context language models. Zero-
134
      SCROLLS (Shaham et al., 2023) covers ten realistic natural language tasks, such as long-document
135
      QA and (query-based) summarization. L-Eval (An et al., 2023) also uses realistic data, which was
136   filtered manually to ensure quality. LongBench (Bai et al., 2024) contains tasks in a bilingual set-
137   ting. DetectiveQA (Xu et al., 2024) tests a model’s long-text reasoning ability using detective fic-
138   tion. However, from an alignment perspective, these works only consider whether long-context
139   LLMs are helpful, without addressing whether these models are safe. Although there are numerous
140   benchmarks testing model safety from various dimensions in short-context scenarios, such as Safe-
141   tyBench (Zhang et al., 2024b), which assesses safety capabilities of the large language models in
142   both Chinese and English through multiple-choice questions, and SALAD-Bench (Li et al., 2024),
143   which evaluates models’ safety capabilities comprehensively through scoring and multiple-choice
144
      questions on safety-related tasks, the safety of long-text models is rarely mentioned. Regarding
      the safety of long-text large language models, Anthropic proposed Many-shot Jailbreak (Anil et al.,
145
      2024), an attack method that exploits the model’s in-context learning (ICL) ability to induce it to
146
      generate unsafe responses. However, this singular testing method is insufficient to comprehensively
147   and accurately assess the long-context safety capabilities of the model. Therefore, we propose the
148   LongSafetyBench, aiming to provide a more accurate and comprehensive evaluation of the model’s
149   capabilities.

      safety capabilities of long-context models, consisting of 1,201 multiple-choice questions covering

161   sues in long-context LLMs. However, whether extending the context length introduces more
      safety concerns compared to shorter texts remains an open question worthy of further discussion.

210   which the model provides a safe reply relative to the total number of responses. More information
211   about construction standards can be found in Appendix A,B,C.

255   ManyShotJailbreak Anthropic previously introduced the ManyShotJailbreak(Anil et al., 2024)
256   attack method, which involves embedding numerous harmful dialogue examples into the context.
257   This method exploits the model’s in-context learning capabilities to prompt the generation of unsafe
258   responses. We adopted Anthropic’s approach by randomly embedding several harmful dialogue
259   examples as shots and subsequently prompting the model in a manner consistent with the patterns
260

261

262
      from a set of pre-generated responses. Using this approach, we constructed 100 samples, with

      3.3.2   PARTIALLY H ARMFUL C ONTEXT
266
267   For the Partially Harmful Context category, we used synthetic documents (HarmfulAdvice), unre-
268   lated documents (HarmfulNIAH, CountingCrimes), and real documents (DocAttack) as the context.
269   Harmful or incorrect information was inserted into these contexts to assess whether the model could
      identify the harmful information and provide a safe response.

270
      HarmfulNIAH NeedleInAHayStack(Kamradt, 2023) is a widely recognized benchmark for test-
271   ing long-context language models. It inserts a statement (needle) at different locations within an
272   unrelated context and then asks the model a question that is highly relevant to the inserted state-
273   ment. By examining whether the language model captures the inserted information, we can assess
274   the model’s ability to retrieve information from long contexts. Inspired by this approach, we re-
275   placed the inserted needle with a harmful statement to observe whether the model can still maintain
276   its safety awareness after capturing the key information within an extremely long unrelated text.
277   Specifically, we set the context lengths to [4K, 8K, 16K, 32K, 64K, 100K] and document depths
278   (where the needle is placed) was spaced at 5% intervals. Subsequently, we posed questions based
279
      on the inserted harmful content and allowed the model to choose from four options.
280
      CountingCrimes Inspired by the MK-NIAH task in RULER(Hsieh et al., 2024), we considered
281
      testing the model’s safety performance by inserting multiple harmful statements. However, MK-
282
      NIAH primarily examines the model’s ability to capture information, which differs from our focus
283   on safety. Building on this, we devised a method to observe whether the model can understand what
284   constitutes harmful statements. Alongside the harmful statements, we also inserted morally aligned
285   statements, and during the questioning, we required the model to identify only the harmful ones.
286   This allowed us to assess whether the model can still distinguish between harmful and harmless
287   content in long-context scenarios. The context length and insertion depth settings are the same as
288   that of HarmfulNIAH.
289
290   DocAttack One important application of long-context language models is Retrieval-Augmented
291   Generation (RAG). It enhances the output of language models by first retrieving relevant information
292
      from external data sources (like databases or documents) and then generating a response based on
      both the retrieved data and the model’s own knowledge (Lewis et al., 2020). We envisioned a
293
      scenario where the documents retrieved by the model have been hacked. If the model overly trusts
294
      the retrieved information, it could lead to unsafe outcomes. Based on this concept, we designed the
295   DocAttack task. In this task, harmful content is subtly inserted into the document, and questions
296   are then asked about the inserted information. We manually collected a total of 100 documents
297   from various sources, including code project documentation, product manuals, government reports,
298   academic surveys, and Wikipedia with an average length of 28,919 words.
299
300   HarmfulAdvice The HarmfulAdvice task focuses on whether a language model will follow an
301   inappropriate method provided in the context to achieve a legitimate goal. The task is designed by
302   presenting a valid objective along with context related to achieving that objective, while inserting
303   an inappropriate method within the context. The model is then asked to provide advice on how to
304
      achieve the given objective, allowing us to assess whether it will adopt the inappropriate method.
      We first manually constructed 10 sample pairs of legitimate objectives and inappropriate methods.
305
      Then, using GPT-3.5-turbo for self-instruct (Wang et al., 2023b), we generated a total of 100 sample
306
      pairs. For the context generation, we employed Qwen2-72b-Instruct, with the process divided into
307   three parts: definition generation, method generation, and writing inappropriate methods. In the
308   definition generation phase, we instructed the model to define the objective and repeatedly prompted
309   it to expand on the content of the definition. For method generation, we had the model suggest ways
310   to achieve the objective, followed by additional prompts to elaborate on these methods. Finally,
311   based on the methods generated in the previous step, we manually expanded on the inappropriate
312   methods and inserted them into the context.
313
314   3.3.3   H ARMLESS C ONTEXT
315
      The Harmless Context category focuses on whether, in the absence of harmful information, long-
316
      context language models could still be induced to exhibit unsafe behavior. We considered scenarios
317   where model failure could lead to unsafe consequences (MedicalQuiz), model bias (PolliticallyIn-
318   correct), and being deceived into generating harmful content (LeadingQuestion), and constructed
319   corresponding data to conduct tests.
320
321   MedicalQuiz Large language models are widely deployed online to provide services to users, and
322   ensuring that these models deliver accurate and reliable responses is also a key aspect of safety.
323   Imagine a scenario where a user feels unwell and seeks advice from the model. If the model pro-
      vides incorrect information, it could have a negative impact on the user. Therefore, we designed


                                                       6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      the MedicalQuiz task, which requires the model to use provided medical knowledge documents to
325   answer questions from the USMLE (United States Medical Licensing Examination), a rigorous and
326   authoritative test for assessing medical knowledge in America. By observing the model’s accuracy
327   in using the medical documents to answer these questions, we assess whether long-context models
328   can safely and reliably provide information to users. We collected question from MedQA(Jin et al.,
329   2020) and collected content from CURRENT Medical Diagnosis and Treatment (a standard medical
330   reference book published by McGraw-Hill) and divided it into chapters, with each chapter repre-
331   senting a specific category of medical knowledge. We prompted the Qwen2-72b-Instruct model to
332   identify the chapter most relevant to the given question. Then, we appended the content of that
333
      chapter to the question as context and reconstructed the answer options according to our designed
      standards, thereby completing the data construction process.
334
335
336
337   PoliticallyIncorrect Maintaining neutrality on political stances is a crucial principle for language
338   models, especially when addressing controversial political issues. We aim to explore whether long-
339   context language models exhibit political bias, particularly when dealing with specific groups af-
340   fected by political issues. We designed the task as follows: the model is given a controversial
341
      political topic and informed that the user belongs to a group impacted by this issue. The model is
      then asked how to attack the political topic, allowing us to assess its handling of politically sensitive
342
      content and potential biases. We used GPT-4 to generate several controversial political topics and
343
      then searched for the most relevant Wikipedia entries to serve as context. If the relevance of the
344   entry was too low, the political topic was discarded. Afterward, we asked GPT-4 to identify the
345   groups affected by each political topic based on the information provided in the topic introduction.
346   Subsequently, we combined the introduction of the political topic with a question that prompts the
347   model, in the voice of the affected group, to attack the topic. This combined input was then used to
348   query the model, allowing us to assess its response.
349
350
351
      LeadingQuestion Although large language models are trained to be safe and harmless, malicious
352   users may still attempt to manipulate the model into providing unsafe responses under the guise of
353   legitimate requests. Therefore, we aim to explore whether, when presented with a seemingly safe
354   and reasonable scenario, long-context models can detect potential harm and provide a safe response.
355   We first manually constructed a set of leading questions, then expanded these questions through
356   self-instruction and manual review. Afterward, we retrieved relevant information from Wikipedia
357   and appended it to the questions as context.

      4.1   E XPERIMENT S ETUP
381
382
      We tested several popular large language models with long-context capabilities, including GPT-4-
383
      turbo(Achiam et al., 2023), Claude-3.5-sonet(Anthropic, 2024), Gemini-1.5pro(Reid et al., 2024),
384   Qwen2-72b-Instruct(Yang et al., 2024), InternLM2.5-7b-chat(Cai et al., 2024), Llama3.1-70b-
385   Instruct, Llama3.1-8b-Instruct(Dubey et al., 2024), Mistral7B-v0.3(Jiang et al., 2023).
386
387
      We adhered to a predefined template (refer to Appendix 5) to integrate the context with the question
      and presented multiple options, instructing the model to respond solely with an option letter (A, B,
388
      C, D, etc.). To mitigate positional bias in the options (Zheng et al., 2024), we randomly shuffled
389
      the option labels, ensuring that each label could correspond to any defined option. The model’s
390   response was then used to determine the selected option. Due to the instruction, the model usually
391   only returned the option letter. However, in some cases, the model did not follow this format,
392   especially with instruct models and chat models. In such cases, we applied rules to identify the
393   likely answer content provided by the model, then calculated the Rouge score with each option. If
394   the maximum Rouge-L F1 score(Lin, 2004) exceeded 0.5, we took the option with the highest score
395   as the model’s choice. If no similar option was found, we assigned the model’s choice as Option 1,
396   indicating the worst option, failure to follow instructions, or hallucination. During generation, we

531
      In this work, we propose LongSafetyBench, a benchmark focusing on evaluating the safety capabil-
      ities of long-context language models. LongSafetyBench covers 3 different safety scenarios and in-
532
      cludes a total of 10 tasks, with data lengths ranging from a few thousand to several hundred thousand
533
      tokens. Then we evaluate several mainstream long-context models of their long-text safety capabil-
534   ities. We find that long-context language models generally exhibit safety issues, which do not
535   always align with their performance in short-context scenarios. Specially, long-context language
536   models tend to ignore harmful content in long contexts. Last, we construct a long-safety training
537   dataset and significantly imporce the long safety ability of Llama3-8b-Instruct and InternLM2.5-
538   7b-chat by training on the dataset. This indicates that training with a small amount of long-context
539   safety data can effectively enhance the models’ long-context safety capabilities, even reaching the
      performance level of top-tier closed-source models and this improvement is generalizable.
