010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            This paper studies the black-box optimization task which aims to find the maxima
013            of a black-box function using only a static set of its observed input-output data.
014            This is often achieved via learning and optimizing a surrogate function using such
015            offline dataset. Alternatively, it can also be framed as an inverse modeling task
016
               which maps a desired performance to potential input candidates that achieve it.
               Both approaches are limited by the limited amount of offline data. To mitigate
017
               this limitation, we introduce a new perspective which cast offline optimization as
018
               a diffusion process mapping between an implicit distribution of low-value inputs
019            (i.e., offline data) and a superior distribution of high-value inputs (i.e., solution
020            candidates). Such diffusion process can be learned using low- and high-value
021            inputs sampled from synthetic functions that resemble the target function. These
022            synthetic functions are constructed as the mean posterior of multiple Gaussian
023            processes fitted with different parameterizations on the offline data, alleviating the
024            data bottleneck. Experimental results demonstrate that our approach consistently
025            outperforms previous methods, establishing a new state-of-the-art performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Numerous engineering and science applications across various domains involve optimization tasks
030   over complex design spaces guided by running physical lab experiments or computer simula-
031   tions (Wang et al., 2023). For example, designing energy-efficient hardware accelerators (Ceze et al.,
032   2016; Bogdan et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2018) for emerging applications/domains requires expensive
033   cycle-accurate simulations to evaluate hardware configurations. Searching for nanoporous materials
034   with high adsorption property for absorbing CO2 from the air or storing hydrogen gas for hydrogen-
035   powered vehicles requires expensive lab experiments for evaluation (Deshwal et al., 2021; Gantzler
036   et al., 2023). Other examples include designing proteins (Gao et al., 2020), molecules (Deshwal &
037   Doppa, 2021), and drugs (Schneider et al., 2020) via running numerous experiments.
038   Black-Box Optimization. As these experiments are often costly, labor-intensive, and have expen-
039   sive overhead (e.g., the cost of procuring materials and equipment), it is impractical for domain
040   practitioners to adopt online optimization techniques, such as Bayesian optimization (BO) (Snoek
041   et al., 2012; 2015; Wang et al., 2018), which rely on running iterative experiments to find the best
042   design. A more realistic solution approach (Brookes et al., 2019; Kumar & Levine, 2020b) aims to
043   maximize a black-box experimental process via learning and optimizing a surrogate model using an
044
      existing dataset of its input-output queries.
045   Challenges. The main problem with this paradigm is that the surrogate’s predictions can become
046   increasingly erroneous when the search moves away from the offline dataset, especially when the
047   surrogate model overfits on biased and/or sparse training data.
048   Prior Approaches. To mitigate this issue, most existing approaches have focused on advancing tech-
049   niques in (1) forward modeling that penalize high-value surrogate predictions at out-of-distribution
050   (OOD) inputs (Trabucco et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2023), (2) inverse modeling
051   that find the most promising regions of (reliable) high-performing inputs (Kumar & Levine, 2020a;
052   Nguyen et al., 2023; Krishnamoorthy et al., 2022; 2023; Chemingui et al., 2024) to sidestep the
053   OOD issue of forward modeling, and (3) search policies that learn a direct plan to navigate from
      low-value inputs to high-value inputs (Krishnamoorthy et al., 2022; Chemingui et al., 2024).

054
      Limitations. Despite their promising results, forward and inverse approaches depend on learning a
055   mapping (or inverse mapping) between input designs and their corresponding performance outputs
056   using offline data. As a result, their effectiveness is inherently limited by the availability of data.
057   Likewise, learning direct search policies also suffers from the same data bottleneck since these
058   methods still need to sample heuristic trajectories from the offline dataset to use as learning feedback.
059
      Furthermore, information regarding regions with high-performing inputs is often not observable
060
      from the offline data, especially in low-data scenarios which might further restrict the effectiveness
061   of the learned models/policies. To mitigate such data bottleneck, we propose to approach offline
062   optimization from a new perspective of distributional translation, as highlighted below.
063
064
      New Perspective. The offline dataset is modeled as an implicit distribution of low-value designs,
      and offline optimization is recast as learning a probabilistic transformation that translates it into a
065
      (better) distribution of high-value inputs. Interestingly, although the feedback needed to learn this
066
      transformation is absent in the offline dataset, it can be derived from a distribution of synthetic func-
067   tions that are similar to the (unknown) target function (up to a scale factor). As these functions can be
068   provably constructed across various output scales, alleviating the data bottleneck and consequently,
069   broaden the solution scope of offline optimization.
070
      Technical Contributions. This is substantiated via the followings:
071
072   1. A diffusion-based framework for offline optimization which studies a direct parameterization
073   of the high-value input distribution in terms of the output of a multi-step surrogate-based gradient
074   ascent. This reveals a correspondence between offline optimization and a generalized diffusion
075   process (Ho et al., 2020) mapping between the (implicit) distribution of (low-value) offline input and
076
      another (target) input distribution. The resulting diffusion process bypasses the explicit construction
      of a surrogate model, allowing for external (surrogate-free) guiding information to be used for model
077
      training, alleviating the data bottleneck of offline optimization (Section 3.1).
078
079   2. A practical pre-training and adaptation framework that (1) learns multiple Gaussian process pri-
080   ors (Williams & Rasmussen, 2006) over synthetic functions similar to the target function and (2)
081   samples low- and high-value inputs from their corresponding mean functions to construct and pre-
082
      train our generalized diffusion model. The pre-trained diffusion model can be used to map from the
      offline input distribution to a better distribution over inputs with higher performance (Section 3.2).
083
084   3. An extensive empirical evaluation on a variety of benchmark datasets (Trabucco et al., 2022)
085   establishing a new state-of-the-art performance, which significantly and consistently improves over
086   previous work. Our empirical evaluation also features a rich ablation studies examining in detail
087   the practical impact of different components of our framework on its performance, as well as its
088
      sensitivity to several key hyperparameters (Section 4).
089   For clarity, we also provide a concise related work and background review on Gaussian pro-
090   cesses (Rasmussen & Williams, 2006) and diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020) in Sections 2 and 5.

      3      O FFLINE O PTIMIZATION VIA D ISTRIBUTIONAL T RANSLATION
206
207
      This section presents a principled approach to learn a generalized diffusion process mapping from
      the (implicit) distribution over offline data to a superior distribution over the high-value input
208
      regimes. To achieve this, we parameterize such high-value input distribution as an affine trans-
209
      formation of an isotropic multivariate Gaussian noise. The transformation is in turn parameterized
210   using the output of a multi-step surrogate-based gradient ascent. This reveals a generalized Brow-
211   nian bridge diffusion process that transforms the offline data into samples from a distribution over
212   high-value inputs (see Section 3.1). Learning this diffusion process therefore presents an alternative
213   to black-box optimization. This can be achieved via sampling low- and high-value inputs from syn-
214   thetic functions that resemble the target function. Such functions can be constructed as the posterior
215   means of multiple Gaussian processes fitted with different parameter initializations on the offline
      data (see Section 3.2). See the overall workflow of our framework in Fig. 1.

      5   R ELATED W ORKS
493
494
      Existing approaches in offline optimization can be categorized into three main families: forward
495
      modeling, inverse modeling, and learning search policies.
496
497   Forward Modeling tackles out-of-distribution (OOD) issues by penalizing high surrogate predic-
498   tions on OOD inputs (Trabucco et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2021; Dao
499
      et al., 2024; Hoang et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2022; Fu & Levine, 2021; Fannjiang & Listgarten, 2020).
      For example, (Trabucco et al., 2021) identifies OOD regions early during gradient updates and re-
500
      trains the surrogate with regularizers to penalize high-value predictions at these inputs. Dao et al.
501
      (2024) introduces a sensitivity-aware regularizer for offline optimizers, while Yuan et al. (2023);
502   Chen et al. (2024) use co-teaching among surrogates to improve performance.
503
504   Inverse Modeling avoids OOD problems by directly learning high-value regions (Kumar & Levine,
505
      2020a; Nguyen et al., 2023; Krishnamoorthy et al., 2023). For instance, Kumar & Levine (2020a)
      uses model inversion networks (MINs) to map scores back to inputs, while Nguyen et al. (2023)
506
      combines unsupervised learning and few-shot experimental design for optimizing synthetic func-
507
      tions. Krishnamoorthy et al. (2023) develops a guided diffusion model to generate designs condi-
508   tioned on function values. The model is trained using weighted sampling from the offline dataset.
509
510   Learning Search Policies aims to replicate optimization paths from low- to high-value designs (Kr-
511
      ishnamoorthy et al., 2022; Chemingui et al., 2024). Krishnamoorthy et al. (2022) synthesizes tra-
      jectories from offline data using a heuristic for monotonic transitions and trains an auto-regressive
512
      model. Chemingui et al. (2024) reinterprets offline optimization as a reinforcement learning task,
513
      which optimizes for an effective policy using sampled trajectories from the offline dataset.
514
515   Overall, these methods remain limited by the available amount of offline data. For example, Krish-
516   namoorthy et al. (2023) uses guided diffusion to learn an inverse mapping from a desired perfor-
517
      mance output to potential input designs. The adopted diffusion model, however, has to be trained on
      weighted sampling from the offline data, which might not contain important information regarding
518
      potential high-performing input regions far from the offline regimes.
519
520   To mitigate this data bottleneck, we instead investigate a revisitation of offline optimization as a
521   distributional translation task. This interestingly shows the equivalence between a direct distribu-
522   tional augmentation of the surrogate-based gradient update (to compensate for the distributional gap
523
      between the offline and oracle data) and a generalized diffusion model that, unlike previous diffu-
      sion models, can map between two (implicit) data distributions. More interestingly, our proposed
524
      approach can leverage external guiding information from related functions to learn the diffusion
525
      model without requiring access to samples from the target distribution. This is essential in our
526   context since the offline dataset is often sampled from a distribution of low-value inputs.
527
528
529   6   C ONCLUSION
530
531   To conclude, we have reformulated offline optimization as a distributional translation task. Our
532
      key finding here is the correspondence between a direct stochastic augmentation of a conventional
      surrogate-based gradient ascent and an equivalent perspective on generalized diffusion. This results
533
      in a principled model to map from the offline input distribution to another distribution over high-
534
      performing input regions. We adopt a robust learning framework of pre-training and adaptation for
535   the derived diffusion model in which additional guiding information can be extracted from related
536   domains to improve its training efficacy. This further reveal a potential future direction to build pre-
537   trained foundation model for offline optimization which can be fast adapted to optimize effectively
538   any emerging tasks with sparse data. Our empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of this
539   method, setting a new state-of-the-art performance for offline optimization.

713   Design-Bench (Trabucco et al., 2022) is a widely adopted benchmark for evaluating offline black-
714   box optimization algorithms. Table 7 presents the summary of four benchmark tasks in Design-
715   Bench.
716
      TF Bind 8 and TF Bind 10: DNA sequence optimization. The aim of TF Bind 8 and TF Bind 10
717
      benchmarks is to discover the length-8 DNA sequence with the strongest binding affinity to a specific
718
      transcription factor(SIX6 REF R1 by default) (Barrera et al., 2016a). In the TF Bind8 benchmark,
719   the binding affinities cover all 65,792 possible sequences. Those are 1,048576 sequences for the
720   TF Bind 10 benchmark. A sequence is made up of four possible nucleotides, each representing a
721   categorical choice. In the TF Bind 8, an offline dataset of 32,898 sequences is sampled, while in the
722   TF Bind 10, we use a set of 10,000 sequences for forming offline data.
723
      Ant and D’Kitty Morphology: robot morphology optimization. This task focuses on optimizing
724
      the physical structure of two simulated robots: Ant from OpenAI Gym Brockman et al. (2016)
725   and D’Kitty from ROBEL Ahn et al. (2019). For Ant Morphology, the objective is to enhance the
726   structure of a quadruped robot to maximize its running speed. In D’Kitty Morphology the goal is to
727   improve the D’Kitty robot’s structure to enable it to reach a specific target. Both tasks aim to discover
728   optimal robot morphologies for their respective challenges. To control the robots with the newly
729   optimized structures, a controller is trained using the Soft Actor-Critic algorithm Haarnoja et al.
730   (2018), tailored for each morphology. The morphology parameters, such as the size, orientation,
731   and placement of limbs, result in 60 continuous variables for Ant and 56 for D’Kitty. The evaluation
732   process runs simulations in MuJoCo Todorov et al. (2012) for 100 time steps, averaging results from
733
      16 independent trials to obtain accurate yet computationally efficient performance estimates.
