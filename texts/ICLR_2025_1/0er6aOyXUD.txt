009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011               Reward models are key in reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF)
012               systems, aligning the model behavior with human preferences. Particularly in the
013               math domain, there have been plenty of studies using reward models to align poli-
014               cies for improving reasoning capabilities. Recently, as the importance of reward
015               models has been emphasized, RewardBench is proposed to understand their be-
016               havior. However, we figure out that the math subset of RewardBench has different
017               representations between chosen and rejected completions, and relies on a single
018
                  comparison, which may lead to unreliable results as it considers only an isolated
                  case. Therefore, it fails to accurately present the robustness of reward models,
019
                  leading to a misunderstanding of its performance and potentially resulting in re-
020
                  ward hacking. In this work, we propose a direction for designing benchmarks that
021               reliably evaluate reward models in mathematical reasoning. We conduct compre-
022               hensive analyses to validate whether our design effectively reflects the robustness
023               of reward models. The results underscore that the benchmark designed to reduce
024               the possibility of reward hacking and employ one-to-many comparisons strongly
025               correlate with the results of optimized policy, whereas the existing benchmark
026               shows almost no correlation. Furthermore, by analyzing through the lens of re-
027               ward overoptimization, we show that the design involving multiple comparisons
028               results in a significantly more reliable benchmark. We make our code and data
029
                  publicly available.1

      1       I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Mathematical reasoning stands as a crucial test-bed for assessing artificial intelligence (Lake et al.,
      2017). Solving math problems demands multi-step reasoning, involving capabilities such as abstract
035
      conceptualization and logical reasoning (Staub & Stern, 1997; Cresswell & Speelman, 2020). To
036
      enhance these reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs), several methods have been
037   proposed, including prompting methods (Wei et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) and
038   training with large and high-quality datasets (Yu et al., 2023; Toshniwal et al., 2024). Recently, many
039   studies have increasingly focused on using reward models for re-ranking or applying reinforcement
040   learning (RL) algorithms (Lightman et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Luo et al., 2024).
041
      Behind the success of LLMs, such as ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2023a) and Claude (Bai et al., 2022),
042
      reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) has been instrumental in aligning with human
043   preferences. It enhances not only instruction following abilities (Ouyang et al., 2022) and safety (Dai
044   et al., 2024) but also reasoning capabilities like code generation (Shojaee et al., 2023; Chae et al.,
045   2024) and mathematical reasoning (Luo et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024). These improvements in LLMs
046   depend on the quality of reward models (RMs) (Touvron et al., 2023), and Shao et al. (2024) also
047   emphasize the importance of building robust RMs for improving reasoning capabilities.
048
      Despite the crucial role of reward models, research has often focused on evaluating policy models
049   (i.e. post-RLHF models) rather than reward models themselves (Dubois et al., 2024; Zheng et al.,
050   2024). This line of research risks neglecting significant issues such as reward hacking—where
051   policies exploit loopholes of RMs to achieve higher scores—resulting from discrepancies between
052   human preferences (i.e. true reward function) and proxy RMs (Skalse et al., 2022; Pan et al., 2022).

      In such case, while employing a proxy RM for optimization may initially improve the true reward,
071
      it gradually leads to degradation—a phenomenon known as reward overoptimization—ultimately
072
      resulting in the failure of optimization (Gao et al., 2023). Therefore, a robust reward model should
073   effectively provide signals for policy learning (Rame et al., 2024).
074
075   Recognizing the need for a deeper understanding of the behavior of reward models, Lambert et al.
076
      (2024) proposed RewardBench, which evaluates RMs by comparing scores for a chosen and rejected
      completion. However, we figure out that the math subset of RewardBench (i.e. math-prm)2 is
077
      inadequate for evaluating reward models in mathematical reasoning tasks. As shown in Figure 1a,
078
      the step-by-step solutions generated by LLMs differ significantly from those written by humans, who
079   often skip several intermediate steps, making them more susceptible to reward hacking. Moreover,
080   as illustrated in Figure 1b, relying solely on a one-to-one comparison may not yield reliable results,
081   as it only represents an isolated case (i.e. only a single chosen and rejected solutions). Therefore,
082   even models that perform well on RewardBench could be vulnerable to reward hacking, indicating
083   the need for a more reliable benchmark.
084
      In this work, we focus on (1) introducing a new design for a more reliable benchmark, and (2)
085   thoroughly verifying that our design accurately represents the robustness of reward models. To this
086   end, we construct R EWARD MATH to represent our design, which is a reliable benchmark crafted
087   for evaluating the robustness of reward models in mathematical reasoning. It is designed to miti-
088   gate the risk of reward hacking and employs comparisons with a variety of incorrect (i.e. rejected)
089   solutions. To validate that the benchmark effectively represents the robustness of RMs, we assess
090   whether reward models provide useful signals from which a policy can effectively learn. We con-
091   duct experiments to determine if the performance on RewardBench and R EWARD MATH correlates
092   with those of policy optimized using best-of-n (BoN) sampling and if the benchmark can accurately
093
      estimate the reward overoptimization.
094   The results confirm that the performance on RewardBench shows almost no correlation with the
095   results of BoN sampling, whereas the scores on R EWARD MATH exhibit a strong correlation. Fur-
096   thermore, we observe that reward models achieving high performance on R EWARD MATH are more
097   effective in mitigating reward overoptimization. These findings demonstrate that our design for re-
098   liable evaluation of reward models effectively measures their robustness and offers a trustworthy
099
      direction for advancing RLHF systems. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
100
              1. Designing the reliable benchmark. To accurately evaluate the robustness of reward mod-
101              els, we propose a new design for a reliable benchmark, focusing on reducing the possibility
102              of reward hacking and employing one-to-many comparisons.
103
              2. Validating our design for a reliable benchmark. We conduct extensive experiments and
104
                 validate that the performance on R EWARD MATH strongly correlates with the results of
105
                 optimized policy and effectively estimates reward overoptimization. These findings under-
106              score that our benchmark design can effectively present the robustness of reward models.

157   2.3   T HE ROBUSTNESS OF R EWARD M ODEL
158
159   The success of RLHF depends on the quality of the reward model, which significantly influences
160   the effectiveness of policy optimization (Touvron et al., 2023). Since the policy model is optimized
161   based on a proxy reward rather than the true reward (i.e. human evaluation), the discrepancy be-
      tween these rewards may result in overfitting to spurious correlations, a phenomenon known as


                                                              3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      reward overoptimization (Gao et al., 2023; Coste et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Rafailov et al.,
163   2024). This issue impedes the improvement of the policy model and complicates the checkpoint
164   selection (Gao et al., 2023; Coste et al., 2024; Rame et al., 2024). In this work, we argue that the
165   robustness of a reward model should be evaluated based on how effectively it provides signals from
166   which a policy can learn. To validate the benchmark for evaluating robustness of reward model, we
167   conduct experiments to determine whether performance on the benchmark correlates with that of
168   the optimized policy and whether the benchmark can detect overoptimization in reward models.

      3       D ESIGNING A R ELIABLE B ENCHMARK
171
172
      3.1     O N THE ROAD TO THE E VALUATION OF ROBUSTNESS OF R EWARD M ODEL
173
174   The robustness of reward models is a key in RLHF systems. To build a robust reward model, it is
175   crucial to develop a reliable benchmark that can accurately reflect the robustness of reward models.
176   However, in RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024), which is a widely-used benchmark for reward
177   models, the math domain (i.e. math-prm) does not fully take this into consideration. First, math-
178   prm is constructed based on PRM800K dataset (Lightman et al., 2024), and it was recently revealed
179   that approximately 20% of the annotations in PRM800K are incorrect, even though it is human-
180
      annotated.3,4 Moreover, RewardBench consists of pairs of human-annotated chosen solutions and
      rejected solutions annotated by unaligned GPT-4, which are evaluated by comparing the rewards be-
181
      tween the chosen and rejected solutions. When solving math problems, as Hendrycks et al. (2021)
182
      and Sun et al. (2024) mentioned, humans often skip certain steps and rely on mental calculations,
183   rather than writing out a complete step-by-step solution, which results in a significant difference
184   compared to machine-generated solutions. Figure 1a demonstrates a noticeable difference between
185   human and machine-generated solutions in RewardBench, showing a significant distribution gap in
186   the number of steps between the chosen and rejected solutions, as further demonstrated in Figure 2a.
187   This discrepancy impedes the reliability of evaluation. Finally, there can be countless incorrect solu-
188   tions to a single mathematical problem, so simply comparing with a single incorrect solution is not
189   sufficient to assess the robustness of reward models, as these solutions represent only isolated cases.
190   As a result, we believe it is difficult to figure out whether RMs with high scores on RewardBench
191
      are genuinely robust or vulnerable to reward hacking. Therefore, we introduce R EWARD MATH, a
      benchmark that can more reliably evaluate the robustness of RMs on mathematical reasoning.
192
193

      3.2     R EWARD MATH DATASET
195
      The design philosophy of R EWARD MATH is to caution against a hasty generalization, which oc-
196   curs when conclusions are drawn from a sample that is too small or consists of too few cases. To
197   accurately measure the robustness of reward model, it is reasonable to compare m correct solutions
198   against n incorrect solutions. However, since collecting correct solutions demands significant human
199   resources, we initially focus on gathering n incorrect solutions to compare against a single correct
200   solution. Based on MATH500 (i.e. MATH dataset (Hendrycks et al., 2021) excluding overlaps with
201   PRM800K), R EWARD MATH consists of a total of 483 problems, each comprising 1 correct solution
202   and 9 incorrect solutions.5 The construction of both correct and incorrect solutions is as follows:
203
204   Correct Solution (Chosen) MATH500 includes human-annotated solutions, which often skip
205   many intermediate steps, making it difficult for language models to understand and vulnerable to
206
      reward hacking. Hence, we first convert the human-annotated solutions from MATH500 into step-
      by-step machine-generated solutions. We prompt GPT-4, using 4 carefully crafted exemplars for
207
      each math subject as part of the prompt. Then, we manually inspect the quality of generated step-
208
      by-step solutions, and correct the errors that are found.
209
210
      Incorrect Solutions (Rejected) To gather a variety of incorrect solutions, we employ a total of 14
211
      off-the-shelf language models, combining open-source models (e.g. LLaMA3-70B), closed-source
212   models (e.g. GPT-4o), and math expert models (e.g. WizardMATH-7B-v1.1). To ensure a wide

      3.3   R EWARD MATH S CORING
236
237   For each problem, we infer 10 solutions in total—1 correct solution and 9 incorrect solutions—and
238   then assign a true classification label when a reward of chosen solution is higher than all rewards
239   of rejected solutions. While RewardBench involves a simple binary classification task comparing
240   chosen and rejected solutions at a 1:1 ratio, where a random model achieves a result of 50%, R E -
241   WARD MATH has a 1:9 ratio of chosen to rejected solutions, meaning a random model would achieve
242   a result of 10%. Furthermore, considering only whether the reward of chosen solution is the highest
243   can be fairly strict, we also utilize Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR), where higher ranks for the chosen

245   where d is the total number of problems, and rankn is the rank of the chosen solution for each
246   problem. In pairwise comparison of generative reward models, the rank of the chosen solution is
247   determined by the number of the rejected solutions that win the chosen solution.

251   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
252
253   We conduct our experiments using three types of reward models that widely-used in mathematical
254   reasoning task, i.e. generative RMs, classifier-based RMs, and PRMs. For generative reward models,
255   we employ a series of large language models, GPT-3.5-turbo/4/4o (OpenAI, 2023a;b), Claude-3.5-
256   Sonnet/3-Opus, Prometheus-7B/8x7B (Kim et al., 2024), and LLaMA3-8B/70B (AI@Meta, 2024),
257   which are the off-the-shelf LLM-as-a-judge. To determine whether the reward models ranked at the
258
      top of the RewardBench leaderboard are truly robust, we adopt top-ranked classifier-based reward
      models (Wang et al., 2024a; Cai et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024a; Dai et al., 2024; Liu & Zeng, 2024;
259
      Yang et al., 2024), as well as available open-source PRMs (Wang et al., 2024b; Sun et al., 2024; Xia
260
      et al., 2024). PRMs require an aggregation function to combine step-level rewards in order to obtain
261   a solution-level reward. While most prior works simply multiply the rewards of all steps to calculate
262   the solution-level reward (i.e. prod), we utilize the geometric mean as the aggregation function to
263   minimize the influence of the number of steps. More detailed explanations of the reward models are
264   in Appendix B.2 and the LLM-as-a-judge prompts are in Appendix D.

384   5.2     T HROUGH THE L ENS OF R EWARD OVEROPTIMIZATION
385
386   We now discuss whether R EWARD MATH effectively represents the robustness of reward models by
387   evaluating how well it estimates reward overoptimization. The robust reward model should provide
388
      effective signals for policy learning, which are resilient to reward overoptimization.
389
390
      5.2.1      E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
391   To examine the phenomenon of reward overoptimization, we analyze the relation between the true
392   reward (i.e. human evaluation) and the degree of optimization, quantified by the KL divergence
393   between the optimized and initial policies. We apply two types of optimizations: BoN sampling and
394   reinforcement learning via PPO. For BoN sampling, we generate n responses on MATH500 dataset
395   and approximate the degree of optimization by KLbon = log n − n−1   n (Stiennon et al., 2020). For
396   PPO, policy is trained over one epoch on the training set of MATH dataset.
397   Since accessing the true rewards requires human annotators, prior work often relies on a gold RM
398   to approximate true rewards (Gao et al., 2023; Coste et al., 2024) or uses win-rates evaluated by
399   LLM-as-a-judge (Rame et al., 2024; Rafailov et al., 2024). In mathematical reasoning, where hu-
400   man preference can be measured by accuracy (i.e. pass@1), we assess the true rewards from two
401   perspectives: (1) gold reward via the gold RM, and (2) oracle reward, which represents human
402   preferences (i.e. pass@1). We assume Internlm2-7B-reward, which performs well on both Reward-
403   Bench and R EWARD MATH, as the gold RM. Depending on whether the reward model is trained
404
      using synthetic preference data, we take two approaches to both gold and oracle rewards as below:
405
      Synthetic Setup We conduct experiments under a synthetic setup following Gao et al. (2023)
406
      and Coste et al. (2024), where responses are scored using a gold RM instead of human annotators.
407
      First, we train Mistral-7B-v0.1 (Jiang et al., 2023) using MetaMATH dataset (Yu et al., 2023) as the
408   initial policy model, after which we collect correct and incorrect solutions generated by the policy
409   model. These solutions are scored by the gold RM to generate 65K synthetic preference data. Then,
410   we train proxy RMs based on Mistral-7B-v0.1 while varying the amount of data. For the policy
411   optimization, we use BoN sampling (n=256)6 and PPO to investigate reward overoptimization.
412
413   Non-synthetic Setup Unlike in the synthetic setup, we use open-source classifier-based reward
414   models and PRMs as proxy reward models. We aim to observe whether the performance of the
415   reward model on RewardBench and R EWARD MATH can predict reward overoptimization. We
416   only use BoN sampling (n=256) as the policy optimization method for MetaMATH-Mistral-7B
417
      and WizardMATH-7B-v1.1, due to potential instabilities in PPO that may arise from discrepancies
      between the reference model πref and the base model of the RM. More details of the experimental
418
      setup are provided in Appendix B.4.

461   5.3       D ISCUSSION ON D EVELOPING E FFECTIVE RLHF S YSTEMS
462
      Benchmarks serve as critical milestones in advancing artificial intelligence. In this work, we argue
463
      that a benchmark for reward models should reliably assess their robustness, where a robust RM
464   indicates a model that provide useful signals to enable effective policy learning. Through extensive
465   experiments, we confirm that the benchmark design, which mitigates the risk of reward hacking and
466   employs one-to-many comparisons, accurately reflects the robustness of reward models. While this
467   work marks a significant step forward, there is still room for improvement. We validate our design
468   in mathematics, where human preferences can be clearly defined by correctness, making it easier to
469   gather multiple rejected completions. Since the reward models can be applied to a wide range of
470   tasks, a crucial next step is to extend our design to cover all of them.7 We hope that this work will
471   provide a promising path toward developing more trustworthy and effective RLHF systems.
472
473   6       R ELATED W ORK
474
475   Evaluating Reward Models. The success of RLHF depends on the robustness of the reward
476   model in capturing human preferences (Ouyang et al., 2022). The assessment of reward models pri-
477   marily relies on downstream evaluation, validating their efficacy by observing performance enhance-
478   ments in the optimized policy (Dubois et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024). However, these evaluation
479   approaches are questionable due to numerous ad-hoc choices in the policy optimization process, in-
480
      cluding the selection of the RL algorithm, computational resources, and hyperparameters (Gao et al.,
      2023; Casper et al., 2023). Recently, to understand the behavior of the reward models and directly
481
      observe its performance, Lambert et al. (2024) proposed RewardBench, a benchmark that evaluates
482
      by comparing the reward between chosen and rejected completions. In this work, we address the
483   limitations in both quality and evaluation approach (i.e. one-to-one comparisons) of RewardBench
484   in the math domain and demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed reliable benchmark design.

513   Mathematical Reasoning of LLMs. The mathematical reasoning capabilities of LLMs play a
514   major role in evaluating artificial intelligence of these models (Lake et al., 2017). To strengthen
515
      the mathematical reasoning capability of LLMs, researchers often train the models with large and
      high-quality datasets (Yu et al., 2023; Toshniwal et al., 2024), and apply sophisticated prompt en-
516
      gineering, tailored for step-by-step reasoning in mathematics (Wei et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023;
517
      Wang et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2024). Furthermore, they also seek to address the weakness of LLMs,
518   such as its limitations in precise calculation and algorithmic processing, by incorporating external
519   tools like Python interpreters and calculators (Yue et al., 2024; Gou et al., 2024). Although these
520   tool-augmented methods present promising results, this work focuses on the intrinsic capability of
521   LLMs to solve math problems, without relying on external tools. Recently, many studies have ex-
522   plored the use of reward models for reasoning tasks through two main approaches: using reward
523   models as verifiers to re-rank outputs during inference, and applying RL algorithms during training
524   to improve reasoning abilities (Lightman et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Sun et al., 2024; Luo
525   et al., 2024). Therefore, we investigate ways to further enhance mathematical reasoning abilities in
526
      a RLHF system through comprehensive evaluation of reward models.

529
530
531
      In this work, we suggest a new design for reliable evaluation of reward models: (1) mitigating the
      risk of reward hacking and (2) employing a one-to-many comparison. To validate our design, we
532
      propose R EWARD MATH, a benchmark that effectively represents the robustness of reward models
533
      in mathematical reasoning tasks. Our extensive experiments demonstrate that the performance on
534   R EWARD MATH has a strong correlation with the performance of the optimized policy, whereas the
535   existing benchmark shows no correlation. Furthermore, we also confirm that R EWARD MATH can
536   effectively estimate the reward overoptimization, a critical concern in RLHF systems. While we
537   utilize a one-to-many comparison due to resource limitations, a crucial next step may be to employ
538   many-to-many comparisons for a more thorough assessment. We hope that this work, which aims to
539   establish a reliable benchmark for evaluating reward models, paves the way toward the development
      of a more trustworthy RLHF system.

      A.1   L IMITATION AND C ONSIDERATIONS FOR F UTURE W ORK
781
      This work has a few limitations. Since our goal is to validate the design of a reliable benchmark,
782   we mainly focus on mathematical reasoning, where human preference are relatively well-defined
783   by correctness. For expansion into other tasks, it is necessary to carefully consider whether the
784   structure is prone to reward hacking and to validate its impact on policies, such as reward overop-
785   timization—in other words, whether it accurately represents the robustness of reward models. It
786   is also noteworthy that we adopt one-to-many comparison rather than many-to-many comparison.
787   While, collecting correct solutions, unlike incorrect ones, requires substantial human resources, ob-
788   taining chosen responses from a single model may introduce bias or limit generalizability. Although
789   we use only GPT-4 to collect correct solutions and confirm in Appendix C.3 that LLM judges (i.e.
790
      the GPT family) introduce almost no bias, we believe that such bias may arise in domains beyond
      reasoning. Hence, it is desirable for future work to address such potential bias by incorporating
791
      chosen and rejected solutions from diverse models and employing multiple comparisons.
792
793   Moreover, as the total number of solutions increases, so does the inference cost. For this reason,
794   we design a benchmark using one-to-many comparisons and demonstrate its promise via extensive
795   validation. However, if there were no limitations in available resources, many-to-many comparisons
796
      utilizing as many solutions as possible would most accurately reflect the robustness of reward mod-
      els. Another natural question is why R EWARD MATH has 9 rejected solutions (n=9). As the number
797
      of solutions increases, both the inference cost and the reliability of the results rise. Therefore, it is
798
      also crucial to identify the optimal trade-off point. However, since our primary goal is to validate
799   our proposed design, finding the optimal value of n is beyond the scope of this work. Finally, the
800   reinforcement learning via PPO is conducted with only a single random seed due to resource con-
801   straints. However, given the high level of noise typically present in RL training, it is preferable to
802   use multiple random seeds for more reliable results (Agarwal et al., 2021; Patterson et al., 2023).
803
804   A.2   F UTURE D IRECTION FOR E XPANSION TO OTHER D OMAINS
805
806   We introduce a design for a reliable benchmark to evaluate the robustness of reward models and
807   validate the design with comprehensive experiments. In this paper, we focus on the mathematical
808   reasoning due to the following two reasons: (1) it is one of the tasks where reward models are most
809   extensively used; (2) it provides a clear definition of human preference. Through our experiments in
      the math domain, we have derived key insights that we believe are applicable to other domains:


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
            1. Significant differences between chosen and rejected responses can result in low corre-
811            lation with downstream tasks due to the potential for reward hacking. This emphasizes
812            the importance of benchmarks that minimize unnecessary discrepancies, which can hinder
813            accurate assessment.
814
            2. One-to-one comparisons may yield inaccurate results depending on the preference
815
               pairs, leading to low correlation with downstream tasks and highlighting the need for a
816            more reliable approach, such as multiple comparisons.
817
            3. Benchmarks employing multiple comparisons effectively capture reward overopti-
818
               mization, indicating its ability to assess the robustness of reward models.
819
820   As we mentioned in Limitation (Appendix A), it is crucial to expand the design we introduce beyond
821   mathematics. Based on our key insights, we discuss how our approach can be applied to other
822   domains.
823
824          • Considering the possibility of reward hacking. If the benchmark includes preference
825            pairs with significant difference length differences between chosen and rejected response,
826            or if the the rejected responses contain obviously incorrect expressions (e.g. “I don’t know”
827            or “No Answer”), it could create opportunities for reward hacking. In such cases, a reward
828
               model that achieves high scores on the benchmark might exploit these patterns rather than
               genuinely aligning with human preferences.
829
830          • Collecting various preference pair. LLMs generate responses based on the distribution
831            learned during pre-training and post-training (i.e. SFT, RLHF). If a benchmark relies solely
832            on a single preference pair for evaluation (i.e. one-to-one comparisons), it may not effec-
833
               tively capture the full capabilities of the reward model. To address this, preference pairs
               should be constructed by gathering chosen and rejected responses from diverse models.
834
               Furthermore, one possible approach is to construct diverse preferences pairs by consider-
835
               ing the similarity between responses.
836
837
             • Validating the benchmark through the lens of reward overoptimization. Evaluating the
               benchmark from the perspective of reward overoptimization is crucial to assess whether the
838
               reward model provides robust and effective learning signals. A reliable benchmark should
839
               effectively capture the extent of reward overoptimization. Failure to address this could
840            undermine the reliability of benchmark as a tool for evaluating reward models.
841
842
      Example of chat domain for benchmark design. In the chat domain, designing a reliable bench-
843   mark faces unique challenges, such as the diversity of conversational contexts and subtle nuances in
844   human preferences. A key starting point is to define human preferences clearly. For example, using
845   Reddit as a source, we could define preferences based on the number of upvotes a comment receives
846   and categorize levels of preference accordingly. After that, an evaluation set can be constructed
847   for many-to-many comparisons. Rather than following the approach of RewardBench, which com-
848   pares a single pair of responses, such as a 9-point answer with a 2-point answer or a 7-point answer
849   with a 5-point answer, the benchmark should involve many-to-many comparisons across a variety
850   of chosen and rejected responses in the 7-to-9 and 2-to-5 ranges, respectively. The many-to-many
851
      comparisons allow for a more meaningful and robust evaluation of the reward model’s ability to
      distinguish between significantly preferred and less preferred responses.
852
853   To minimize the potential for reward hacking, benchmarks should avoid simplistic preference pairs,
854   such as comparing long, detailed chosen responses with short rejected ones. Instead, benchmarks
855   should focus on pairs with similar fluency and relevance, highlighting nuanced factors such as tone,
856   and alignment with the intent of users. Additionally, collecting responses from diverse LLMs trained
857
      on varied data ensures a more robust evaluation on the robustness of reward models.

870   Details on obtaining correct solutions. To obtain step-by-step correct solutions, we prompt GPT-

872   We dynamically provide 4 exemplars to ensure the quality of the correct solution by retrieving
873   exemplars from the same subject as the given problem. The exemplars are selected from the train set
874   of MATH, where each problem is categorized into one of 7 subjects (Precalculus, Number Theory,
875

882   and collect the wrong solutions. We employ 13 different LLMs consisting of general closed-source
883   LLMs, general open-source LLMs, and math expert LLMs. Using 2-shot exemplars in their prompt,
884   closed-source LLMs generate 8 samples per problem, open-source LLMs generate 16 samples, while
885   math expert LLMs generate 16 samples with 0-shot prompt. We equally set the temperature to 1.0
886   and top-p to 0.95. Secondly, we instruct GPT-4-0125-preview to select a specific step from the
887   correct solution, transform it into an erroneous step, and then prompt again to continue generating
888
      the solutions from the erroneous step.
