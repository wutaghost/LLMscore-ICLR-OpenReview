                                                    A BSTRACT
030
031
               Humans have the ability to decompose objects into parts and relationships and
               create new objects by properly combining existing concepts. However, enabling
032
               machines to achieve this in real-world tasks remains a challenge. In this paper,
033
               we investigate how to teach machines compositional image generation through
034            learning identifiable concepts. To derive concepts from attribute labels, we for-
035            mulate the minimal change principle and propose a method to limit the informa-
036            tion introduced by each label. Additionally, to address dependent attribute labels
037            (with causal influences in between or common causes behind them), we present
038            a causal conditioning approach to disentangle concepts from these correlations.
039            Our framework enhances data efficiency, interpretability, and control, while en-
040            abling sampling from unseen combinations. We validate our method on various
041            compositional image generation and editing tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness
042
               through superior performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
045
046
      Despite the recent significant progress in artificial intelligence, machine learning systems still have
047
      not yet been able to fully capture human conceptual learning. Humans are able to parse objects into
048   parts and relationships and create new objects with existing concepts (Lake et al., 2015). However,
049   it can be challenging for the machines to achieve that in compositional image generation task, even
050   for the most powerful AI models, e.g., GPT-4o and Meta AI. As shown in Figure 1, they fail to add 5
051   o’clock shadow to young girl face while they are able to generate male face with 5 o’clock shadow.
052   However, human can easily imagine a young girl face with the 5 o’clock shadow. The failures show
053   that machine learning systems still have difficulty processing human face into different concepts and
      cannot composite the young girl concept with 5 o’clock shadow concept together like human does.

054
      There have been many efforts on compositing different objects for image generation, such as adding
055   objects to scene images (Tan et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2018; Tan et al., 2019; Song et al., 2023;
056   Chen & Kae, 2019), generating food images (Papadopoulos et al., 2019; Han et al., 2020b;a), and
057   generating image from object layout (Arad Hudson & Zitnick, 2021; Zheng et al., 2023; Zhao et al.,
058   2019; Yang et al., 2022; Zeng et al., 2023). Meanwhile, compositing the concepts beyond objects
059   for image generation has received wide attention. (Du & Kaelbling, 2024; Liu et al., 2022; 2023;
060   Du et al., 2020; 2021) learn to generate images with different concepts by compositing smaller
061   models trained on different objects. For instance, (Liu et al., 2023) learns to composite styles from
062   Van Gogh and Monet’s paintings with pretrained text-to-image diffusion models. Instead of using
063
      different models for composition, (Abdal et al., 2021; Nie et al., 2021) use the attribute labels as
      condition to learn a single generative model and generate new images by changing the input label.
064
      However, previous methods treats the label (e.g., text or binary attribute) as concept and may leads to
065
      unwanted changes when they are expected to represent different meanings. For example, in human
066   face editing, the label 5 o’clock Ssadow is causally related with the labels male and young. Thus
067   the label also contains information about the gender and age. If we ignore such difference between
068   label and concept, adding 5 o’clock shadow to a young girl face could be impossible (see Figure 1)
069   or makes the girl looks like a male and older (see Figure 6).
070
      In this paper, we present a generative model to learn concepts from data and attribute labels. Given
071
      binary attribute label, we aim to disentangle the true concept from correlated information such that
072   we can change only this concept to mkae it controllable. To this end, we formulate the minimal
073   change principle where the changes brought by each concept should be minimal, i.e., the concept
074   does not contain information about other concepts. According to this principle, we make an as-
075   sumption about the data generating process where the latent variables (concepts) are generated in
076   a compositional way. It means that the concept is activated if label is 1 or deactivated if label is
077   0. To minimize the influence of each concept, we introduce a binary mask to find the dimension of
078   each concept automatically. Then we show that adding the sparsity achieves better compositional
079   performance. In addition, the labels could be dependent (spuriously correlated, in some contexts)
080
      and we propose a causal conditioning method to handle it in an efficient way. Specifically, we per-
      form causal discovery on the labels and then condition the child variable on the parent variables
081
      to isolate the influence from parent variables when we only want to change child variables. With
082
      these two important modules, we show that the concepts are identifiable and superior performances
083   demonstrate the efficacy of our method and validates our theory.
084
085   Our proposed method has several theoretical and practical contributions:
086
          (i)     We formulate the minimal change principle and present an empirical method to learn con-
087
                  cepts for compositional image generation guided by this principle.
088
          (ii)    We propose a way to disentangle the concept from causally related attribute labels by fac-
089
                  torizing the information.
090
091       (iii)   We prove that the true concepts could be recovered in our method under some assumptions.
092       (iv)    We empirically demonstrate that our method achieves better generation and editing results.
093               The significant gain over the baseline methods on different tasks demonstrate the effective-
094               ness of our technique.

098   Causal Representation Learning, Concept Learning, and Compositional Image Generation
099
      The goal of causal representation learning (CRL) is to reconstruct the true generation process of the
      data (Schölkopf et al., 2021). It can be viewed as a combination of disentanglement (Hyvärinen
100
      et al., 2023), representation learning (Bengio et al., 2013), and causal discovery (Spirtes et al., 2001;
101
      Glymour et al., 2019). Identifiability plays an important role in CRL. When the distributions of the
102   estimation model and real data are matched, the learned latent variable is shown to be an invertible
103   transformation of the true factor, i.e., the estimation only contains the information about the true
104   factor (we call the variable identifiable). Learning concepts for compositional image generation is
105   highly related to CRL as the identifiability guarantees many desirable properties of the estimation
106   model, such as robustness to outliers. For instance, CRL has been shown to be effective in many
107   downstream classification tasks (Brehmer et al., 2022; Mitrovic et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022b; Lu
      et al., 2021). Recently, Rajendran et al. (2024) show that CRL can improve large language models.

122   In this paper, we present a framework for identifiable concepts and explore how CRL benefits the
123   compositional image generation. Learning concepts for compositional image generation is gaining
124   wide attention recently. Du & Kaelbling (2024); Liu et al. (2022; 2023); Du et al. (2020; 2021)

      assume that concepts are represented by the labels or texts. They further assume that the concepts
      are independent and thus their joint distribution can be factorized and modeled by separate smaller
126
      models. Compared to their methods, this focuses on the case where attribute labels contain redundant
127
      information about the concepts and how we can drop it for better compositional image generation.

129   Attribute Manipulation and Conditional GAN Human attributes manipulation is an important
130   topic. StyleGAN-based approaches are roughly divided into three steps: 1. inferring the latent code
131
      of real images with pretrained StyleGAN generator using optimization-based methods (Karras et al.,
      2020b) or encoder-based methods (Abdal et al., 2019; Richardson et al., 2021; Tov et al., 2021; Wang
132
      et al., 2022a; Pehlivan et al., 2023); 2. training additional network to generate the inferred latent
133
      codes with attribute labels (Abdal et al., 2019; Li et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023a; Suwała et al.,
134   2024; Nie et al., 2021) or learning editing directions with attribution labels (Shen et al., 2020b;a;
135   Wu et al., 2021; Ling et al., 2021); 3. generating a new latent code by changing the attribute label
136   and feed it into the pretrained StyleGAN generator. Some attempts have been made with text-guided
137   image manipulation (Patashnik et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023b). At the same time,
138   many methods formulate this problem as a multi-domain image-to-image translation problem and
139   apply different regularizations (Choi et al., 2018; Xiao et al., 2017; 2018; Kim et al., 2024). There
140   are also some causality inspired methods in image generation, such as CausalGAN (Kocaoglu et al.,
141   2017). However, CausalGAN has to model the generation as a causal graph and may not be able to
142
      scale up when the number of attributes is large. We present more causal-aware generative models
      in the appendix A. Conditional GAN (Mirza, 2014) is closely related to the attribute manipulation
143
      and its goal is to generate high-quality images from given classes. AugGAN (Hou et al., 2024)
144
      encourages the discriminator to predict the augmentation parameters to avoid the negative impact
145   from data augmentation.

150   Given a set of images x and their corresponding attribute labels {Ai }ni=1 (n is the number of labels
151
      for each image), our goal is to learn concepts for these attribute label such that the learned model
      generates images in a compositional way. We expect that our model is able to generate combinations
152
      that never appear in the training set. We present the minimal change principle in Section 3.1, the
153
      causal conditioning method to handle dependent labels in Section 3.2, the empirical estimation in
154   Section 3.3, and the real-image editing method in Section 3.4. Finally, we provide identifiability
155   guarantees for our learned concepts in Section 3.5. The whole computation flow of our method is
156   present in Figure 2.

185   3.2   C AUSAL CONDITIONING FOR D EPENDENT L ABELS
186
187   The minimal change principle guides us to restrict the information from attribute label. However,
188   the real-world labels could be heavily dependent from each other; if such dependence is not properly
189   address, it would lead to unsatisfactory disentanglement even if minimal change has been enforced.
190   For example, old (O) and eyeglasses (E) are usually highly correlated. When one tries to activate the
191   eyeglasses concept, it contains the information from age. Consequently, activating the eyeglasses
192   concept alone could result in additional age-related distortions (see Figure 3). To disentangle these
193   concepts, we resort to a causal perspective and provide a natural and effective solution.
194   Take the age-eyeglasses as an example, we have causal model O →
195   E ← ε, where ε is an unobserved exogenous random variable as
196   shown in Figure 4(a). Consequently, the eyeglasses node contains
197   information about the age. To change eyeglasses without changing
198   the age influence, we can consider the latent variable ε because of
199   E = fE (O, ε), the structure causal model to describe the causal pro-
200   cess (Pearl, 2009; Spirtes et al., 2001). Accordingly, we propose to

      factorize the dependent label information in Table 4(b). Since the
      original column E contains the information about age, we can build

261   3.4   C OMPOSITIONAL I MAGE E DITING
262
      In previous sections, we can change the binary labels Ai to achieve controllable image generation.
263
      However, it still remains challenging to edit the real images. A straight-forward way is to project
264   image x into our feature space Z (or W space in StyleADA (Karras et al., 2020a)). However,
265   to achieve editing, after inversion, we still have to train an additional network to incorporate the
266   attribute label information like Abdal et al. (2019); Pehlivan et al. (2023). In this section, we choose
267   to invert an image x to the input space rather than latent space Z, i.e., we have to recover the noise
268   variables {ϵi }. After recovering the noise ϵi , we can choose to activate or deactivate the concept by
269   feeding different label Ai . This utilizes the label information during inversion process unlike Karras
      et al. (2020a) while avoiding the two-stage training for editing like Pehlivan et al. (2023).

      3.5   I DENTIFIABILITY G UARANTEE FOR THE C ONCEPTS
288
289   In this section, we present identifiability guarantee for learning the underlying concepts. Specifi-
290   cally, we show that our estimation model recovers the true latent variables z∗i up to invertible trans-
291   formation under some conditions. The key idea is to leverage sufficient variability across activating
292   and deactivating functions to identify the concepts. The proof of the following result is provided in
293   Appendix C.
294   Theorem 1 (Identifying concepts with a generative model). Consider the data generating process
295   defined in Eqs. (1) and (2) that satisfy the following assumptions:
296
297          • A1 (Smooth and positive density): The density function pϵ is continuously differentiable
298            and positive everywhere.
299          • A2 (Diffeomorphism): The generating function g, activation function fi,1 , and deactivating
300            function fi,0 are C 2 -diffeomorphisms onto their corresponding images.
301
302          • A3 (Concept regularity): The activating function fi,1 differs sufficiently from the deactivat-
303            ing function fi,0 , and each concept is of dimension one. Specifically, for i = 1, . . . , n and
304            every ϵ∗i , we have
305                                  !               !                              !′            !′
306                        ∂    pϵ∗i       ∂    pϵ∗i                           pϵ∗i          pϵ∗i
                                       ̸= ∗               or, equivalently,            ̸=            .
307                       ∂ϵ∗i fi,1
                                  ′              ′
                                          ∂ϵi fi,0                              ′
                                                                              fi,1            ′
                                                                                             fi,0
308
309          • A4 (Concept variability): For each concept zi , there exist two attribute labels, denoted as
310            A(k) and A(l) , that differ only in the i-th entry.
311
312   Then, for a generative model that assumes the same generative process, satisfies the assumptions
313   above, and matches the data distribution, i.e., pG(Z) = px , it identifies the true latent variables z∗i
314   up to invertible transformation for each attribute Ai .
315
      Assumptions A1 and A2 are standard assumptions for identifiability in representation learning (Hy-
316
      varinen et al., 2019; Hyvärinen et al., 2023), ensuring that the functions are well-behaved. Assump-
317
      tion A3 requires the activating function to differ sufficiently from the deactivating function, which
318   is analogous to the sufficient change assumption in disentangled (Hyvarinen et al., 2019; Hyvärinen
319   et al., 2023) or causal representation learning (Schölkopf et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2024) that re-
320   quires the data distribution of latent variables to vary sufficiently. In our setting, such an assumption
321   is needed because, intuitively speaking, it is in general not possible to identify the concepts if activat-
322   ing and deactivating the concepts lead to highly similar data distributions. Furthermore, Assumption
323   A4 imposes a certain type of variability on the attribute labels, which is analogous to humans learn-
      ing one concept at a time.

508   that the foundational models still struggle in generating realistic images of the unseen combinations.
509   For instance, GPT-4o and Stable Diffusion3 generate cartoon mustaches on the girl’s face, rendering
510
      the whole image unnatural. In contrast, our method learns to add mustache to girl and male faces.
      This demonstrate the advantage of learning identifiable concept representations from images.

513   face editing method,StyleRes (Pehlivan et al., 2023). StyleRes is trained to inverse the StyleGAN
514   by projecting images into a higher-dimensional space and leverages second-stage editing techniques
515   such as InterfaceGAN (Shen et al., 2020b) and StyleClip (Patashnik et al., 2021) to achieve image
516
      editing. As shown by the editing results in Figure 7, the strong baseline StyleRes achieves very good
      reconstruction since it learns to reconstruct the image in the high-dimensional space. However, very

      For instance, it fails to narrow the eyes in the first row and remove the goatee in the third row.

      performance if no sparsity (to achieve minimal changes) is added. With no sparsity, the latent could
      be very high-dimensional and contains redundant information and leads to additional changes in the

525   5     L IMITATIONS , D ISCUSSION , AND C ONCLUSION
526
      Although we have shown that our method has achieved best performance across different compo-
527
      sitional image generation and editing tasks, our method still suffers from following limitations: 1)
528   Difficulty in generating complex images. We build our model based on StyleADA, which is known
529   to be challenging to generate non-curated images (Sauer et al., 2022). We have explored learning
530   concepts with cat and icecream with 50 training images. Unfortunately, neither our method nor the
531   baseline AugGAN succeeded, as shown in Figure 9. 2) Lacking multi-modal control. Our model
532   currently only supports attribute labels to control the generation process, which is limited compared
533   to foundational models like GPT-4o. Adding text and mask control can enlarge the concept space
534   but could be more challenging, and we leave the above two problems as future work.
535   In this paper, we present a concept-learning framework for compositional image generation and
536   editing. To learn the concepts, we introduce the minimal change principle to restrict the influence
537   of each concept. In addition, we also present a causal conditioning method to address the dependent
538   attribute label problem. We have shown that our method achieves the best performances across dif-
539   ferent datasets and tasks. In summary, the proposed framework is more data-efficient, controllable,
      and interpretable.

      Causally-aware Generative Models CausalGAN (Kocaoglu et al., 2017) proposes to build a causal
779
      generative following the causal graph. For example, if X → Z ← Y , then it employs three neu-
780   ral networks for each node and the input of neural network of Z consists of the outputs of neural
781   network for node X and node Y . CausalVAE (Yang et al., 2021) assumes that there exists under-
782   lying causal structure among the latent variables and add a causal layer to learn such information.
783   Wen et al. (2022) employs CausalGAN for tabular data geverife while supporting part of the causal
784   graph. CGNN (Goudet et al., 2018) learns causal graph and the functions by minimizing the di-
785   vergence between the generated data and real data. DECAF (Van Breugel et al., 2021) reconstructs
786   each variable with its parents as conditioning and generates fair synthetic data. Moraffah et al.
787   (2020) assumes the Structural Causal Model (SCM) is linear and proposes to learn the causal graph
788
      with GAN. CNF (Javaloy et al., 2024) recovers the causal model with normaling flow given causal
      ordering information. CGN (Sauer & Geiger, 2021) assumes that images are generated by four com-
789
      ponents: shape, texture, background and composer and train a conditional GAN with corresponding
790
      labels. CAGE (Bose et al., 2022) examines the causal relationship between a pair of variables using
791   potential outcome framework and generates counterfactual images.
792
793   Nonlinear ICA Nonlinear ICA is a challenging ill-posed problem because the latent variables are
794
      generally not identifiable without any assumptions (Hyvärinen & Pajunen, 1999). Existing works
      resolve this issue by leveraging sufficient variability on the distribution of latent variables to obtain
795
      identifiability, where the distributions are indicated by auxiliary variables such as time indices and
796
      domain indices (Hyvarinen & Morioka, 2016; 2017; Hyvarinen et al., 2019; Khemakhem et al.,
797   2020). Another line of works impose restrictions on the mixing function including certain function
798   classes (Hyvärinen & Pajunen, 1999; Taleb & Jutten, 1999; Gresele et al., 2021; Buchholz et al.,
799   2022) and sparse mixing function (Zheng et al., 2022).

961   D     C AUSAL C ONDITIONING TO HANDLE DEPENDENT LABELS
962
963   In the main paper, we present the causal conditioning method to handle the causally related labels.
964   Specifically, we perform causal discovery. Then for each node, we use its parents as conditioning
965   to construct new labels. Then we use the new label to train the generative. During inference, we
966   can assign the value to the column according to the parents’ value. For instance, given age →
967   eyeglasses, changing the eyeglasses still lead to age-related changes. After the label transformation,
968
      we are now provided with three columns (Age, Eyeglass—Age=old, Eyeglass—Age=1). If a user
      assigns age=old and wearing eyeglasses, then our label would be [1,1,0] since we conditional on the
969
      age information.
970
971   Causal conditioning separates the information of the child node from the shared information with its
      parents. Here we provide examples to show that such causal conditioning is effective in Figure 11.

1047   G    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
1048
1049   We provide the training code in the supplementary material. We build our method on StyleADA
1050   (Karras et al., 2020a). Our main empirical contribution lies in the redesigning of the mapping net-
1051   work used in StyleADA. In StyleGAN-based methods, the Gaussian noises and a class embedding
1052   are fed together into a MLP to get an entangled space W. In this paper, we build our model based
1053   on the minimal change principle. For each attribute Ai , we use a 2-layer MLP to transform the input
1054   noise ϵi into activated or deactivated concept. Then we concatenate the outputs together to get a
1055
       latent z. In other words, we have a Z space instead of a W space now.
1056   For each attribute, we set the dimension to be 20 at the beginning of training. We also employ a
1057   learnable mask mi to encourage the model select the dimension for each concept. We apply L1
1058   sparsity on the mask. We usually set λsparsity = 0.1.
1059   As for the real image editing task, we choose to extend the Z space into Z + space following the
1060   previous StyleGAN inversion methods Abdal et al. (2019; 2021). Unlike Z ∈ R1,d , now the Z +
1061   space has Rk,d where k is the number of repeated for the latent variables. For example, in training
1062   FFHQ dataset, StyleGAN based approaches repeat the latents 16 times. So, now we estimated noise
1063   variables {ϵ̂} has shape 16 × d. Then we optimize the variables using loss function mentioned
1064   in the main paper. As for Lreg , we also regularize the noise maps to penalize them from carrying
1065   incoherent signals following StyleADA (Karras et al., 2020b). For more details of the regularization,
1066   we refer readers Section 5 in (Karras et al., 2020b).
