024                                                   A BSTRACT
025
026             We introduce the first learning-based dense matching algorithm, termed Equirect-
027             angular Projection-Oriented Dense Kernelized Feature Matching (EDM), specifi-
028             cally designed for omnidirectional images. Equirectangular projection (ERP) im-
029             ages, with their large fields of view, are particularly suited for dense matching
030             techniques that aim to establish comprehensive correspondences across images.
031             However, ERP images are subject to significant distortions, which we address by
032
                leveraging the spherical camera model and geodesic flow refinement in the dense
                matching method. To further mitigate these distortions, we propose spherical po-
033
                sitional embeddings based on 3D Cartesian coordinates of the feature grid. Addi-
034
                tionally, our method incorporates bidirectional transformations between spherical
035             and Cartesian coordinate systems during refinement, utilizing a unit sphere to im-
036             prove matching performance. We demonstrate that our proposed method achieves
037             notable performance enhancements, with improvements of +26.72 and +42.62 in
038             AUC@5° on the Matterport3D and Stanford2D3D datasets, respectively.

041   1    I NTRODUCTION
042
043   Omnidirectional images, also known as 360° images, provide significant advantages owing to their
044   expansive fields of view, offering more contextual information and versatility (Xu et al., 2020;
045
      Zhang et al., 2023a; Matzen et al., 2017; da Silveira et al., 2022; Guerrero-Viu et al., 2020). These
      spherical images enable a comprehensive representation of environments, facilitating a deeper un-
046
      derstanding of spatial information. Their utility extends to aiding robot navigation (Winters et al.,
047
      2000; Menegatti et al., 2004) and autonomous vehicle driving (Pandey et al., 2011) by minimizing
048   blind spots. 360° images also can be utilized in a diverse range of applications, from creating immer-
049   sive AR/VR experiences to practical uses in interior design (Amalia & Fitriyansah, 2023), tourism
050   (Saurer et al., 2010), and real estate photography (Chang et al., 2017). Integrating omnidirectional
051   images into virtual house tours allows customers to experience an immersive view, enabling them
052   to fully engage themselves in the service. Moreover, the adoption of omnidirectional images con-
053   tributes to more efficient data collection. By replacing the need for multiple perspective images,
      omnidirectional images can reduce both the cost and time associated with data scanning. The large

054
      field of view provided by 360° images has also demonstrated superiority over narrower views in 3D
055   motion estimation (Nelson & Aloimonos, 1988; Lee et al., 2000; Fermüller & Aloimonos, 2001).
056
057   Feature matching plays a critical role in numerous 3D computer vision tasks, including mapping and
058
      localization. Traditionally, Structure from Motion (SfM) (Schonberger & Frahm, 2016) leverages
      feature matching to estimate relative poses. Recent advancements have introduced semi-dense or
059
      dense approaches for feature matching such as LoFTR (Sun et al., 2021b) and DKM (Edstedt et al.,
060
      2023a), which demonstrate superior performance in repetitive or textureless environments compared
061   to keypoint-based methods (Lowe, 2004; Rublee et al., 2011; DeTone et al., 2018; Sarlin et al., 2020;
062   Li et al., 2022a). These methods have been mainly developed for perspective 2D images and videos,
063   but encounter challenges when applied to omnidirectional images. For example, to adapt match-
064   ing methods for spherical images, two prevalent approaches for sphere-to-plane projections are the
065   equirectangular projection (ERP) and the cubemap projection (Xu et al., 2020). ERP images exhibit
066   significant distortions, particularly near the pole regions, which hinder the effective application of
067   perspective methods. On the other hand, the cubemap format, consisting of six perspective images,
068   can be processed independently without such distortions. However, this approach involves the costly
069
      computation of multiple inferences for each pair of perspective images, resulting in the loss of global
      information from a single spherical image and diminishing feature matching capabilities due to the
070
      reduced field of view in each perspective image. These challenges are shown in Fig. 1 (a) and (b).
071
072
073
      Main Results In this paper, we propose EDM, a distortion-aware dense feature matching method
      for omnidirectional images, addressing challenges that existing detector-free approaches (Sun et al.,
074
      2021b; Edstedt et al., 2023a;b) struggle to overcome. To the best of our knowledge, EDM is the
075
      first learning-based method designed for dense matching and relative pose estimation between two
076   omnidirectional images. As seen in Fig. 1, our method defines feature matching in 3D coordinates,
077   specifically addressing the challenges posed by distortions of ERP images. We accomplish this
078   based on the integration of two novel steps: a Spherical Spatial Alignment Module (SSAM) and
079   specific enhancements in Geodesic Flow Refinement. The SSAM leverages spherical positional em-
080   beddings for ERP images and incorporates a decoder to generate the global matches. Furthermore,
081   the Geodesic Flow Refinement step employs coordinate transformation to refine the residuals of
082   correspondences. Compared to both recent sparse and dense feature matching methods (Zhao et al.,
083   2015; Gava et al., 2023; Edstedt et al., 2023a;b), our approach results in significant performance
084
      improvement of +26.72 and +42.62 AUC@5° in relative pose estimation for spherical images on the
      Matterport3D (Chang et al., 2017) and Stanford2D3D (Armeni et al., 2017) datasets. Additionally,
085
      we evaluate our method qualitatively on the EgoNeRF (Choi et al., 2023) and OmniPhotos (Bertel
086
      et al., 2020) datasets, demonstrating robust performance across diverse environments. The main
087   contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
088
089          • We introduce a novel approach for estimating dense matching across ERP images using
090            geodesic flow on a unit sphere.
091
             • We propose a Spherical Spatial Alignment Module that utilizes Gaussian Process regres-
092
               sion and spherical positional embeddings to establish 3D correspondences between omni-
093
               directional images. In addition, we use Geodesic Flow Refinement by enabling conversions
094            between coordinates to refine the displacement on the surface of the sphere.
095
096          • With azimuth rotation for data augmentation, we achieve state-of-the-art performance in
097
               dense matching and relative pose estimation between two omnidirectional images.

      Feature Matching Local feature matching has relied on detector-based methods, encompassing
128
      both traditional hand-crafted techniques (Lowe, 2004; Rublee et al., 2011) and learning-based ap-
129
      proaches (DeTone et al., 2018; Revaud et al., 2019; Li et al., 2022a; Liu et al., 2019; Tyszkiewicz
130   et al., 2020). These methods typically involve detecting keypoints, computing descriptor distances
131   between paired keypoints, and performing matching via mutual nearest neighbor search. SuperGlue
132   (Sarlin et al., 2020) introduces a learning-based paradigm, optimizing visual descriptors using an
133   attentional graph neural network and an optimal matching layer. However, detector-based methods
134   face limitations in terms of accurately detecting keypoints, particularly in repetitive or indiscrimina-
135   tive regions. In contrast, detector-free or dense methods (Sun et al., 2021b; Melekhov et al., 2019;
136   Truong et al., 2020; 2021; Edstedt et al., 2023a;b) offer a solution to the keypoint detection issue,
137   providing dense feature matches at the pixel level.
138   While the aforementioned methods are tailored for perspective images, they often fail to address
139   the unique challenges of spherical cameras. SPHORB (Zhao et al., 2015), an extension of ORB
140   (Rublee et al., 2011), mitigates distortion in ERP images using a geodesic grid and local planar
141   approximation (Eder et al., 2020). Similarly, learning-based matching methods such as SphereGlue
142   (Gava et al., 2023; 2024) and PanoPoint (Zhang et al., 2023b) adapt keypoint matching techniques
143   for spherical imagery. CoVisPose (Hutchcroft et al., 2022; Nejatishahidin et al., 2023) explores
144
      layout features for estimating camera poses over large baselines yet remains constrained by detected
      feature information. Therefore, we propose a novel dense matching method that extracts all matches
145
      without keypoint detection in spherical images.

      Although ERP images are displayed in 2D space, they actually represent a collection of flattened rays
152
      normalized to a unit scale within a spherical camera model. Thus, we can express the coordinate
153
      conversion equation u = π(S) between the spherical coordinates u = (θ, ϕ) and the 3D Cartesian
154   coordinates S = (S x , S y , S z ) as shown in Fig. 2. Each value of θ ∈ [−π, π] and ϕ ∈ [− π2 , π2 ]
155   indicates the longitude and latitude. We utilize this coordinate transformation π(·) in Section 4.1
156   and Section 4.2 to handle the spherical camera model effectively.

321   5.1   E XPERIMENTS S ETTINGS
322
323   Matterport3D Dataset Training our method requires ERP input images, ground truth depth maps,
      and aligned poses. The Matterport3D dataset (Chang et al., 2017) encompasses 90 indoor scenes


                                                                 6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      represented by 10,800 panoramas reconstructed as textured meshes. However, the dataset lacks pose
325   and depth information for skybox images, which are essential for creating ERP images. Previous
326   works have addressed this limitation by rendering both images and depth maps from the textured
327   mesh (Zioulis et al., 2018) or by employing 360° SfM to estimate poses (Rey-Area et al., 2022). In
328   our approach, we generate the poses for skybox images directly from the originally proposed camera
329   poses in Matterport3D. Through experimentation, we found that treating the 12th camera pose,
330   out of the 18 viewpoints (comprising 6 rotations and 3 tilt angles) in each panorama, identically
331   to the second skybox image did not result in any issues. We define the remaining poses for the
332   skybox images by rotating 90° in each direction from the second pose. We adhere to the official
333
      benchmark split, utilizing 61 scenes for training, 11 for validation, and 18 for testing. For two-
      view pose estimation, it is necessary to create pairs of overlapped images. We achieve this by
334
      transforming ERP depth maps between frames within the spherical coordinate system. Pixels where
335
      the depth difference is below a specified threshold, e.g. 0.1, are classified as inliers. Subsequently,
336   we compare the ratio of these inliers to the total number of pixels. We organize both the training
337   and testing datasets based on the overlap ratio of image pairs and the benchmark split. Specifically,
338   images with the overlap ratio exceeding 30% are distributed into respective training and testing
339   splits. As a result, the training set contains 44,700 pairs, while the test set comprises 4,575 pairs.
340   We resize the resolution of ERP images and depth maps to 640 × 320.
341
342   Stanford2D3D Dataset Stanford2D3D (Armeni et al., 2017) consists of data scanned from six
343
      large-scale indoor spaces collected from three distinct buildings. This dataset contains a relatively
      small number of 1,413 panorama images and, therefore, is utilized exclusively for testing purposes.
344
      We assess the overlap ratio between frames and include them in the test split if their ratio exceeds
345
      50%. A total of 3,460 pairs are incorporated into the test set. During testing, we resize the resolution
346   to 640 × 320.
347
348   EgoNeRF and OmniPhotos Dataset EgoNeRF (Choi et al., 2023) introduces 11 synthetic scenes
349   created with Blender (Community, 2018) and 11 real scenes captured with a RICOH THETA V
350   camera. OmniPhotos (Bertel et al., 2020) provides a dataset captured with an Insta360 ONE X
351   camera. Both datasets contain egocentric scenes captured with a casually rotating camera stick.
352
      Consequently, their rotation axes, pole regions, or camera height change, resulting in different dis-
      tortions compared to Matterport3D or Stanford2D3D. We present additional qualitative results from
353
      these datasets to validate our method.
354
355   Implementation Details We employ the AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) optimizer with a
356   weight-decay factor of 10−2 , a learning rate of 5 · 10−6 for multiscale feature extractor, and 10−4
357   for the SSAM and the Geodesic Flow Refiner. EDM is trained for 300,000 steps with a batch size
358   of 4 in a single RTX 3090 GPU, which takes approximately two days to complete. During evalua-
359   tion, the balanced sampling approach using kernel density estimation (Edstedt et al., 2023a) tends to
360   establish correspondences primarily in concentrated areas with high probability distributions, mak-
361
      ing it unsuitable for omnidirectional images. Thus, we randomly sample up to 5,000 matches after
      certainty filtering with a threshold of 0.8 to ensure correspondences cover the entire area.

      6      C ONCLUSION , L IMITATIONS , AND F UTURE W ORK
476
477
      In this paper, we present, for the first time, a novel dense feature matching method tailored for
      omnidirectional images. Leveraging the foundational principles of DKM, we integrate the inher-
478
      ent characteristics of the spherical camera model into our dense matching process using geodesic
479
      flow fields. This integration instills distortion awareness within the network, thereby enhancing
480   its performance specifically for ERP images. However, it is important to note that our method is
481   predominantly trained on indoor datasets where the camera is vertically oriented, rendering it some-
482   what vulnerable to extreme rotations or outdoor environments. To address this limitation, future
483   endeavors will focus on diversifying the training data and data augmentation to encompass a wider
484   range of environments, fortifying the robustness of our network. Furthermore, we aim to extend
485   our method into downstream tasks, particularly for visual localization and mapping applications for
      omnidirectional images.

       C     T HOROUGH D ISCUSSION ON L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
1027
1028
       In this section, we provide a thorough discussion of limitations and future work associated with
1029   our study. As our work is the first to develop a dense feature matching method for omnidirectional
1030   images, we believe this discussion will advance this research direction and offer deeper insights for
1031   the 360° imaging research community.
1032
1033   C.1   RUNTIME E VALUATION
1034
1035   EDM’s runtime is almost the same as the DKM (Edstedt et al., 2023a) method because EDM in-
1036   cludes an additional coordinate transformation between layers without requiring extra learning pa-
1037   rameters. Both DKM and EDM take approximately 0.24 seconds per frame pair on a 3090 GPU.
1038   Comparing the runtime between sparse matching, such as SphereGlue (Gava et al., 2023) and
1039
       dense matching is somewhat challenging due to differences in feature extraction and the number
       of matches. Sparse matching requires feature extraction before matching, and SphereGlue involves
1040
       a local planar approximation to create multiple tangential images (perspective images) during fea-
1041
       ture extraction, which takes about 3.2 seconds. The inference speed for matching itself depends on
1042   the number of extracted features. In most cases, the number of features is much smaller than in
1043   dense matching, making it faster than 0.2 seconds.
1044
1045   C.2   ROTATIONAL D IVERSITY IN T RAINING DATA
1046
1047   Our primary training dataset, Matterport3D (Chang et al., 2017), consists of indoor scenes captured
1048   with vertically fixed cameras. As a result, images with extreme rotations do not perform well in
1049   EDM, as shown in Fig. 18. We believe this problem can be mitigated by collecting more diverse
1050   training data, including images with various rotational angles, and by applying additional rotational
1051
       augmentation techniques during the training process. These steps would enhance the model’s ability
       to handle a wider range of image orientations effectively.
1052
1053
1054
       C.3   E NCODER C HOICE AND D ISTORTION C OMPENSATION
1055
       In this paper, we use a ResNet encoder for multi-scale feature extraction. While distortion-aware
1056   approaches (Jiang et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2020; Shen et al., 2022) exist, these methods did not
1057   yield satisfactory results in our experiments and required significant computational resources. Con-
1058   sequently, we employed ResNet with spherical positional embeddings to compensate for distortion
1059   without adding extra trainable layers. This approach demonstrates promising results, however, fea-
1060   ture extraction does not fully address distortion issues. In the future, we will extend our work to
1061   develop more efficient encoders capable of handling distortions.
1062
1063   C.4   U TILIZATION OF F OUNDATION M ODELS
1064
1065   In dense matching tasks for perspective images, leveraging foundation models for coarse features
1066
       (Edstedt et al., 2023b) has shown better performance compared to sharing coarse-fine features using
       a ResNet encoder (Edstedt et al., 2023a). In this paper, our primary goal is to demonstrate the
1067
       potential of a dense matching method for omnidirectional images. We believe that adopting different
1068
       foundational models, as Edstedt et al. (2023b) did, could improve our framework. We plan to train
1069   foundation models such as DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023) or CroCo (Weinzaepfel et al., 2022) on
1070   omnidirectional images and integrate these into our approach.
