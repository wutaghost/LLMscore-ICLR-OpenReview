011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Object parts provide representations that enable a detailed and interpretable under-
014            standing of object structures, making part recognition crucial for various real-world
015
               applications. However, acquiring pixel-level part annotations is both expensive
               and time-consuming. Rendering 3D object models with their 3D part annotations
016
               is a promising solution since it allows the generation of unlimited synthetic data
017
               samples with precise 3D control and accurate part segmentation masks. Never-
018            theless, these synthetic datasets suffer from a lack of realism, resulting in large
019            domain gaps. In this paper, we present a large-scale realistic synthetic dataset with
020            part annotations, namely Diffusion-generated Synthetic Parts (DSPart), for both
021            rigid objects and animals. For images in DSPart, we obtain 2D part masks from
022            3D part annotations by leveraging recent advances in diffusion models with 3D
023            control. In addition to offering more diverse and realistic textures, prior knowl-
024            edge of diffusion models enables the object to exhibit more physically realistic
025            interactions with the ground plane and other spatial contexts. We annotate 475
026
               representative shape instances from 50 object categories for DSPart-Rigid and
               use 3, 065 high-quality SMAL models fitted poses from 40 animal categories for
027
               DSPart-Animal. Experimental results demonstrate the potential of our dataset in
028
               training robust part segmentation models, effectively bridging the gap between
029            synthetic and real-world data.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Important contributions in the field of cognitive psychology have evidenced that human perception of
035   objects is strongly based on part decomposition (Hoffman & Richards, 1984; Biederman, 1987; Ross
036   & Zemel, 2006) and have inspired the usage of part-based methods for computer vision. Focusing
037   on parts or their representations, followed by validation of their geometric configurations, offers
038   many advantages. For example, based on parts, a large number of geometric variations of highly
039
      articulated objects (e.g., animals) can be represented and learned holistically (Ross & Zemel, 2006).
      Part-based models have also been shown to be relatively robust to partial occlusion (Kaushik et al.,
040
      2024b; Kortylewski et al., 2020) and allow few/zero-shot knowledge transfer to novel objects (Gölcü
041
      & Gilbert, 2009; He et al., 2023b). The visual characteristics of specific object parts exhibit less
042   variability under changes in pose (and other nuisance factors) compared to the overall appearance
043   of the entire object (Ross & Zemel, 2006; Kaushik et al., 2024b;a) which enables better model
044   robustness (Sitawarin et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; He et al., 2023a; Xie et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024).
045   These advantages make part recognition important in real-world applications, such as robotics (Aleotti
046   & Caselli, 2012; Nadeau et al., 2023) and action recognition (Zhao et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2012).
047
      However, the predominant focus within the community in the big data era has been on addressing tasks
048   at the object level, with minimal attention given to intermediate part representations. This shortage is
049   mainly due to the lack of datasets with pixel-level part annotations across general categories. Most
050   existing part datasets focus on a small number of object categories, such as humans (Li et al., 2020;
051   Gong et al., 2017; Li et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2019) and cars (Dinesh Reddy et al., 2018; Song
052   et al., 2019). Although some recent works have presented relatively large-scale part datasets (Chen
053   et al., 2014; He et al., 2022), the absolute number of images annotated in these datasets still falls
      far behind those with pixel-level annotations on general object-level categories. For example, both

086   2023) or active learning methods (Mittal et al., 2023) does not guarantee consistency or coherent

091   over 3D viewpoints (and individual parts), thereby producing highly accurate part segmentation
092   masks.
093

094
      2020; Liu et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2024) severely lack realism, presumably due to the limited
095
      availability of high-quality textures in the CAD models. Additionally, rendered objects typically
096   exhibit unrealistic interactions with their backgrounds, as the rendering process lacks constraints
097   to ensure accurate context integration. This often enlarges the domain gap between the synthetic
098   data and real-world data, reducing its utility. A recent work (Ma et al., 2024) has tried to ameliorate
099   the aforementioned problems by using latent diffusion models with text prompts, coupled with
100   ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023b), to generate more realistic synthetic images conditioned on edge
101   maps of rendered images. However, this approach is limited by its focus on merely coarse 3D object
102   pose outputs (i.e. object viewpoints) on rigid objects. It falls short in generating high-quality samples
103   that are consistent with the visual prompts for nonrigid objects, such as animals, which exhibit a wide
104
      range of poses and shapes.

      they often do not incorporate explicit 3D structural data, which limits their utility in tasks requiring

      increasing attention, primarily due to the difficulty of obtaining large-scale real part-level annotations.

      Mu et al. (2020) conduct an early exploration in a multi-task learning setting, where part segmentation
210   and object pose estimation are jointly learned. They show that this multi-task learning could enhance

212   (2022) propose a cross-domain geometric matching module to align predictions and warp synthetic
213   results to the real domain, improving alignment and performance. Peng et al. (2024) leverage
214   recent syn-to-real domain transfer algorithms, augmenting them with a class-balanced pseudo-label
215   re-weighting mechanism to better align synthetic results with the real domain. In this work, we focus

      In this section, we introduce how we construct our DSPart synthetic dataset. The dataset consists of
      two parts, DSPart-Rigid (Section 3.1) and DSPart-Animal (Section 3.2). The statistical comparison
223
      between DSPart and other publicly available image part datasets are presented in Table 1. The

      3.1 DSPART-R IGID
227
228
      DSPart-Rigid aims to provide fine-grained 2D part masks and 3D part annotations for common rigid
      models in the real world. Existing 3D part datasets (Mo et al., 2019; Slim et al., 2023) have annotated
229
      a large amount of 3D shapes, but the object categories are limited. PartNet (Mo et al., 2019) have
230
      annotated 24 categories of indoor rigid objects, which makes it hard to find real image part datasets
231   for evaluation since existing ones focus on common outdoor rigid objects such as cars (Meletis et al.,
232   2020; Chen et al., 2014; Zhou et al., 2017; He et al., 2022). 3DCOMPAT++ (Slim et al., 2023) have
233   annotated 41 object categories, which include vehicles, but these categories have much fewer 3D
234   CAD models compared to other indoor objects (e.g. only 36 CAD models for the car category) and
235   are not diverse enough in shape. Furthermore,it does not further classify vehicle categories into more
236   fine-grained ImageNet1k (Deng et al., 2009) classes, which increases the possibility of failure cases
237   in the DST-3D (Ma et al., 2024) pipeline because of the missing class-specific details of prompts. For
238   instance, for a minivan CAD model, if the text prompt describes it as a general car, the generated
239
      synthetic images are more likely to be inconsistent with the visual conditions.
240   To make our synthetic dataset capable of evaluation on existing real part datasets and also avoid the
241   aforementioned class ambiguity issue, we carefully select 50 rigid object categories that are consistent
242   with ImageNet1k class definitions. We manually classify some 3D shapes from ShapeNet (Chang
243   et al., 2015) and Objaverse (Deitke et al., 2022) to these fine-grained categories. Subsequently, we
244   manage to select around 10 CAD models with large shape differences for each category since we
245
      want every CAD model annotated to be representative. We recruit 25 annotators for data annotation
      and more details are provided in Appendix A.3.
246
247   Annotation scheme. (i) What parts to annotate per category: One of the key challenges in
248   annotating parts of 3D CAD models is the ambiguity of object part selection (e.g. how to annotate
249   the parts of a space shuttle). We divide our 50 rigid-object categories into five super-categories: car,
250   airplane, bicycle, boat, and tool. We classify the super-category of each object category based on
251   Wikidata and common knowledge. We then analyze what object parts are important in cognition
252
      and are tractable to be annotated in real images. Subsequently, we create part definition templates
      for each super-category, except tools, as the shapes of tools vary significantly. Therefore, we define
253
      part definitions individually for each tool category. Note that our part definitions are all recognizable
254
      from the object surface and we do not define parts that are internal structures. Annotators will check
255   the shapes of their assigned CAD models first and then revise the provided part definitions only
256   if necessary. Please refer to Appendix A.1 for the part taxonomy. (ii) What principles to select
257   part vertices: We design several principles in selecting part vertices to guide the annotators to
258   ensure high-quality and consistent 3D part annotations. Firstly, the annotated part vertex groups
259   should be disjoint sets, and the union of all groups should contain every vertex in the original CAD
260   models. Secondly, if a mesh face belongs to two connected parts, the annotator should not assign
261   all three vertices to one part and should still assign the vertices based on where they are located.
262   (iii) Annotation quality inspection: We have 2 iterations of the annotation inspection, including
263
      correctness check and revision. The first iteration of annotation inspection is done by selected
      annotators whose annotations are high-quality during the annotation process, and the annotators will
264
      inspect the object categories that belong to the same super-categories of what they annotated in the
265
      annotation process. Then, we perform the second iteration of annotation inspection.

      3.2   DSPART-A NIMAL
268
269   Existing synthetic animal datasets (Mu et al., 2020; Jiang & Ostadabbas, 2023) have less than
      ten animal species due to the limited availability of 3D animal CAD models. DSPart-Animal


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      aims to achieve a larger scale in animal species to train robust models for the in-the-wild animals.
271   SMAL (Zuffi et al., 2017) models can fit various quadruped animals based on their shape priors.
272   However, SMAL models have no textures, which is the crucial reason that prevents people from
273   using them to generate synthetic images. Existing works (Zuffi et al., 2018; 2019) predict the textures
274   using real images and paste them on the SMAL models, but these texture prediction methods do not
275   generalize well enough beyond the distribution of textures observed during training. By leveraging
276   the powerful latent diffusion model in the 3D-DST pipeline (Ma et al., 2024), DSPart-Animal can
277   have realistic and diverse textures that generalize better.
278   Part-attention Regressor Filter (PRF). Unfortunately, the 3D-DST (Ma et al., 2024) images of
279   animals exhibit low quality due to the wide variation in pose and shape. The 3D-DST proposes
280   the K-fold consistency filter (KCF) to filter out noisy samples with inaccurate 3D annotations. It
281   creates the K-fold splits of synthetic samples and for each train-val split, it trains a state-of-the-art
282   3D pose estimation model (Ma et al., 2022) and evaluates it on the validation split. Each sample
283   in the validation split will be classified as noisy, with potentially inaccurate 3D annotations, if the
284
      confidence score associated with the 3D annotation falls below a specified threshold.
285   However, KCF is trained on unfiltered images, which are noisy in the case of animals, leading to
286   unreliable model predictions. Furthermore, KCF relies on the pose error given by the angle between
287   the predicted rotation matrix and the ground truth rotation matrix (Zhou et al., 2018), which is not an
288   effective filtering metric for animals due to their high degree of articulation and deformation.
289   We then look at the metrics used for 3D human pose estimation and 3D animal pose estimation
290   models trained from reliable data. We recruit three annotators to do the human filtering on around
291   46k synthetic animal images (i.e. 15 viewpoints for each SMAL pose). The filtering is based on three
292   principles: (1) the consistency of leg positions and leg length; (2) the consistency of head orientation
293   and neck deformation; (3) the face of the animal is recognizable. It takes around 100 working
294   hours, and we finally get 13k synthetic animal images with reasonably accurate 3D annotations. We
295   train the 3D animal pose estimation algorithms from (Xu et al., 2023) with these 13k images and
296
      PARE (Kocabas et al., 2021a) exhibit the most robust cross-domain performance. The results and
      justification are provided in Appendix B.
297
298   We employ two PARE models for filtering: a model trained exclusively on our human-filtered
299   synthetic data for 1, 000 epochs, and a model pre-trained on our synthetic data for 100 epochs,
300   followed by 1, 000 epochs of fine-tuning on the Animal3D training set. We specify thresholds for
301   three metrics: Procrustes-aligned mean per joint position error (PA-MPJPE), scale-aligned mean
302
      per joint position error (S-MPJPE), and 2D Percentage of Correct Keypoints (PCK). We choose the
      intersection of filtered image sets from the aforementioned two PARE models as the model-filtered
303
      samples. We demonstrate the quality of model-filtered samples in Section 4.3, which guarantees the
304
      scalability of DSPart-Animal. We name the filtering process as Part-attention Regressor Filter (PRF)
305   inspired by the name of PARE, and we use it to filter the left 39k images in DSPart-Animal.

352
      DSPart-Rigid. Table 2 summarizes our results of SegFormer (Xie et al., 2021) and DAFormer (Hoyer
353
      et al., 2022) on the PartImageNet test set when training with different datasets under various settings.
354
355   Specifically, although training with DSPart-Rigid performs similarly to training with UDAPart (Liu
356   et al., 2022) under the Syn-only setting, it significantly improves performance in the unsupervised
357   domain adaptation setting, where knowledge learned from the synthetic dataset is better transferred
358
      to the real domain. Concretely, when training with DSPart-Rigid, DAFormer achieves improvements
      of 7.08, 11.72, and 11.88 mIoU on car, airplane, and bicycle categories, respectively. Remarkably,
359
      it even achieves performance comparable to training with real images on certain relatively easy
360
      categories (i.e., 72.67 vs. 74.08 mIoU on the car category). We hypothesize that the main reason
361   behind this improvement is that better cross-domain features are learned from DSPart-Rigid because
362   of the diverse and realistic textures and image contexts.
363
364
      The reason for no significant improvements in the Syn-only setting is that DSPart-Rigid still has a
      small fraction of noisy samples (i.e. the image content is not consistent with the 3D annotation) even
365
      after using K-fold consistency filter (KCF) (Ma et al., 2024). This issue is mitigated in the UDA
366
      settings since the pseudo-labels are generated in online self-training that uses exponentially moving
367   averages to update. It increases the stability of the pseudo-label predictions, and thus, the quality of
368   pseudo-labels will not be influenced significantly by minor noisy samples.
369
370
      Furthermore, we observe that combining real domain training data with DSPart-Rigid and training
      SegFormer in a sequential manner—where synthetic and real data are fed into the model itera-
371
      tively—yields further performance improvements. This showcases the potential of utilizing synthetic
372
      data to boost state-of-the-art performance.

409   4.3 A BLATION ON THE EFFECTIVENESS OF PRF
410
      Table 5 shows the ablation studies on different filtering mechanisms. Specifically, we compare four
411
      types of filtering: no filter, KCF filter (Ma et al., 2024), and the proposed PRF filter. Additionally,
412
      we provide a potential upper bound with our human-filtered images. Except for the human-filtered
413   data obtained from 46k images, all other filtering strategies are applied to a separate set of 150k
414   images. From the table, we observe that the KCF filter proposed in 3D-DST (Ma et al., 2024) achieves
415   performance similar to unfiltered results. This suggests that the KCF filter may not be effective in the
416   context of animals since the pose error calculated by the rotation matrix is not enough to measure the
417   accuracy of articulations and deformation.
418
      The proposed PRF filter significantly outperforms prior methods, achieving a mIoU improvement
419   of 9.45 and 6.25 over unfiltered and KCF-filtered data, respectively. Furthermore, the PRF-filtered
420   result under the UDA setting is close to the human-filtered result, which demonstrates that we can
421   maintain the scalability of 3D-DST (Ma et al., 2024) with PRF filter when extending to animals.
422   Moreover, the proposed PRF filter is more cost-effective to apply. The KCF filter (Ma et al., 2024)
423   necessitates training multiple models (e.g. 5) on different subsets of the generated data and then
424   performing cross-validation experiments to filter out low-quality data, which takes around 50 hours
425   to train all the models. The human filter approach requires approximately 100 hours of manual labor
426   to identify and exclude undesirable images. In contrast, the PRF filter only needs a pre-trained model
427
      and can directly run inference on the generated images at scale without the need for retraining. This
      process takes only about 20 minutes, making it significantly more efficient.

477   5      C ONCLUSIONS
478
      We present DSPart, comprising DSPart-Rigid and DSPart-Animal, a large-scale part dataset featuring
479
      475 rigid CAD models and 3, 065 fitted animal poses in 3D, along with 48K synthetic images of
480   rigid objects and 52K synthetic images of animals. DSPart significantly surpasses existing part
481   datasets in both real and synthetic domains in terms of 3D part annotations and realistic synthesized
482   image context. We introduce PRF, an efficient and effective filter mechanism designed to exclude
483   low-quality animal synthetic images due to the large variation in pose and shape, thus serving as
484   a robust extension of the 3D-DST synthesis pipeline and maintaining scalability for animals. We
485   conduct extensive experiments on DSPart for 2D part segmentation to demonstrate that DSPart vastly
      outperforms existing synthetic part datasets in data quality when used as synthetic training data.

      A.2   V IEWPOINTS S AMPLING RULES

766   azimuth angle and Gaussian distributions over the elevation and theta angles.

      A.3   A NNOTATOR R ECRUITMENT G UIDELINE
769
770   To improve the quality of the collected data, each annotator must complete an onboarding stage
771   before starting. The onboarding stage includes training sessions where we present Blender tutorials
772   to teach annotators how to annotate with the help of convenient Blender functions efficiently, and
773   detailed instructions with demo videos on annotating strategies and proper ways to handle edge cases.
774   Additionally, each annotator must annotate three CAD models and meet our standards to qualify for
775   subsequent annotations. We also provided 1 hour of Zoom training sessions and 2 hours of Q&A
776
      sessions on handling specific boundary cases which are difficult for annotators.
777
778   B     3D A NIMAL P OSE E STIMATION
779
780   Table 8 presents our 3D animal pose estimation results tested on both the real and synthetic test sets
781   of Animal3D (Xu et al., 2023) to demonstrate performance across different domains. The synthetic
782   test set is generated through naive rendering with SMALR (Zuffi et al., 2018) fitted textures. We train
783   the models with our human-filtered animal data for 1000 epochs in the synthetic-only setting. For
784   the synthetic pretrained setting, we pre-train the models on our human-filtered animal data for 100
785   epochs, then fine-tuned them on real images from the Animal3D training set for 1000 epochs. We
786   use the same metrics as Animal3D (Xu et al., 2023).
787   The PARE (Kocabas et al., 2021b) methods consistently outperform HMR (Kanazawa et al., 2018) in
788   all settings in terms of 2D PCK, indicating better alignment of predictions with the 2D images. Since
789   2D part segmentation is often considered complementary to 2D pose estimation (Xia et al., 2017), we
790   select PARE models to filter our animal data. For 3D metrics, PARE showed better performance in
791   terms of S-MPJPE, suggesting more accurate predictions of rigid 3D body poses. However, PARE
792   performs worse in terms of PA-MPJPE, indicating lower accuracy in predicting animal articulations.
793   Therefore, we do not set a strict threshold for PA-MPJPE in our filtering process due to the unreliable
794
      predictions.
795   Despite the presence of some noisy samples with inaccurate 3D poses and articulations in our DSPart-
796   Animal, leading to a performance drop in PA-MPJPE compared to the synthetic pretrained results
797   from Animal3D, we still achieve better performance in S-MPJPE and 2D PCK. We believe our
798   synthetic data pipeline for animals shows promise, but fundamental improvements in conditional
799   synthesis via ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023a) are necessary.

805   We disable the Thing-Class ImageNet Feature Distance(FD) (Hoyer et al., 2022) for the UDA method.
806   It is a regularization technique that uses ImageNet features trained from objects to provide guidance
807   to segment object classes, which is inappropriate for segmenting semantics parts of objects. Our goal
808   is to compare baseline performance between other synthetic part datasets with DSPart so we avoid
809   complex data augmentations and leave it for future work to explore the benefits of data augmentation.
      For all experiments, we only adopt random horizontal flips.
