009                                             A BSTRACT
010
011           Current deep homography estimation methods are constrained to processing im-
012           age pairs with limited resolution due to restrictions in network architecture and
013           computational capacity. For larger images, downsampling is often necessary,
014           which can significantly degrade estimation accuracy. To address this limitation,
015           we propose GFNet, a Grid Flow regression Network that consistently delivers
016           high-accuracy homography estimates across varying image resolutions. Unlike
017           previous methods that directly regress the parameters of the global homography
018
              between two views, GFNet directly estimates flow over a coarse grid and then
              uses the resulting correspondences to compute the homography. This approach
019
              not only supports high-resolution processing but also preserves the high accu-
020
              racy of dense matching while significantly reducing the computational load typi-
021           cally associated with such frameworks, thanks to the use of coarse grid flow. We
022           demonstrate the effectiveness of GFNet on a wide range of experiments on mul-
023           tiple datasets, including the common scene MSCOCO, multimodal datasets VIS-
024           IR and GoogleMap, and the dynamic scene VIRAT. In specific, on GoogleMap,
025           GFNet achieves an improvement of +9.9% in auc@3 while reducing MACs by
026           ∼47% compared to the SOTA dense matching method. Additionally, it shows a
027           1.7× improvement in auc@3 over the SOTA deep homography method.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Homography estimation is the task of determining the transformation that aligns two planes. It typ-
033
      ically finds application in the alignment of two images, each depicting the same planar structure
      but from different vantage points. This is a fundamental low-level computer vision task widely
034
      used in various downstream applications, including image/video stitching (Zhou et al., 2024), mul-
035
      timodal image fusion (Xu et al., 2023), GPS-denied UAV localization (Wang et al., 2024a), stereo
036   vision (Kumar et al., 2024), and planar object tracking (Liu et al., 2023).
037
038   Recently, learning-based approaches have demonstrated high accuracy in homography estimation
039
      at small image resolutions. The primary reasons for these resolution limitations arise from con-
      straints in (1) network architecture and (2) computational capacity. Some methods (DeTone et al.,
040
      2016; Le et al., 2020; Shao et al., 2021; Cao et al., 2022; 2023; Zhu et al., 2024) compute match-
041
      ing information between image pairs and aggregate it to regress the homography. The aggregation
042   layer, typically using a fixed number of large-kernel pooling operations, outputs a tensor of shape
043   B × 2 × 2 × 2 to represent the eight parameters of the homography. This implies that the network’s
044   downsampling factor is predefined and constrained by the large kernel sizes, inherently restricting
045   the input size. Other methods (Chang et al., 2017; Zhao et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Zhang &
046   Ma, 2024) explicitly use the IC-LK iterator (Baker & Matthews, 2004) with learned features. This
047   approach requires multiple iterations in feature space, resulting in significant compute and memory
048   requirements. As a result, current methods are forced to work with inputs at low resolution. Unfor-
049   tunately, doing so has also a direct negative impact on the accuracy of the estimated homography.
050   Compared to homography estimation methods, general matchers trained for two- or multi-view cor-
051   respondence can handle a wider range of image resolutions. However, they tend to underperform
052   in accurately estimating homography. For example, estimating homography between images from
053   different modalities is a common challenge in homography estimation. A case in point is align-
      ing infrared and visible images to aid UAV navigation at night under poor illumination (Li et al.,

077
      delivering accurate results without significant computational costs. To address this, we leverage the
078   advantages of resolution flexibility and high accuracy inherent in the dense matching framework,
079   while tackling its computational challenges by considering the globally smooth nature of homogra-
080   phy transformations. This leads to our proposed Grid Flow regression Network (GFNet). As shown
081   in Figure 1, GFNet achieves superior accuracy over current homography estimation methods, while
082   outperforming the SOTA dense matching method RoMa with higher efficiency. This is achieved by
083   the following contributions:
084
             • We incorporate pre-trained self-supervised learning features of DINOv2 (Oquab et al.,
085
               2023) with a lightweight feature pyramid network to construct robust multi-scale features.
086

087            efit from its strong cross-domain feature matching capabilities across multiple scales.
088

089
               cantly reducing computational costs while keeping the high accuracy of dense matching.
090
091
             • We introduce an iterative flow regression approach to prevent suboptimal multi-scale flow
               optimization in challenging scenes. Besides, a resolution recurrence strategy is used during
092
               inference to improve accuracy and enable GFNet to adapt to varying image resolutions.

095   2   R ELATED W ORKS
096
097   Deep homography estimation. One of the earliest approaches uses a stacked image pair as input to
098   a VGG-style network to extract matching information between images, followed by cascade average
099
      pooling to regress the homography (DeTone et al., 2016). Subsequent methods improve accuracy
      by cascading multiple VGG-style networks, where the predicted homography from each network is
100
      used to warp the images before feeding them into the next network, allowing for progressively more
101
      accurate predictions (Erlik Nowruzi et al., 2017; Le et al., 2020). However, using separate networks
102   for refinement often results in suboptimal performance. To address this, Localtrans (Shao et al.,
103   2021) builds multi-scale features within one network and predicts homography in a coarse-to-fine
104   manner. RHWF (Cao et al., 2023) runs with one network recurrently to improve accuracy. There are
105   some approaches improving accuracy by iteratively optimizing the homography, either by explicitly
106   incorporating the Lucas-Kanade algorithm to achieve feature-metric alignment (Chang et al., 2017;
107   Zhao et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Zhang & Ma, 2024), or by training the network end-to-end,
      enabling it to learn the optimization process implicitly (Cao et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2024).

124   General matching methods. Current approaches can be categorized into sparse, semi-dense, and
125   dense methods. Sparse methods rely on keypoint detection and description, followed by matching
126   the descriptors (DeTone et al., 2018; Sarlin et al., 2020; Deng & Ma, 2022; Lindenberger et al., 2023;
127   Potje et al., 2024). While efficient, they often struggle with accuracy in textureless regions, which
128   are common in homography estimation tasks (Zhang & Ma, 2024). Semi-dense methods degrade
129
      less in such areas by bypassing keypoint detection. Instead, they perform global matching at a coarse
      level, where initial coarse matches that pass the mutual nearest neighbor test are then refined at a
130
      finer level. This means if key matches are missed in the coarse stage, they cannot be recovered.
131
      Regarding the dense methods, they predict matches for every pixel using flow regression. Although
132   they achieve impressive accuracy, they require substantial computational resources in terms of both
133   time and memory, which limits their applicability in resource-constrained scenarios (Truong et al.,
134   2020; 2023; Edstedt et al., 2023; 2024).
135
      Common challenges in homography estimation. The challenges are twofold and arise from prac-
136
      tical application requirements, according to recent literature (Nguyen et al., 2018; Le et al., 2020;
137
      Zhang et al., 2020; Shao et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2021; Cao et al., 2022; Zhang & Ma, 2024). The
138   first one is photometric inconsistency caused by changes in illumination or modality, such as images
139   taken at different times, with different sensors, or in different types (Jiang et al., 2021). The second
140   challenge comes from the violation of the homography assumption due to dynamic occlusions.

143   3      M ETHOD
144
145   Our goal is to predict an accurate homography transformation H that spatially aligns a source image
146
      IS with a target image IT . We achieve this using the proposed GFNet, as shown in Figure 2. Rather
      than directly regressing the global homography, GFNet follows the dense matching paradigm but
147
      estimates the flow over a regular grid and utilizes the resulting correspondences to compute the
148
      homography. This process begins by extracting multi-scale features from both images, which are
149   then used to predict grid flow across multiple scales. Next, we introduce the technique in detail.
150

154   a variety of vision tasks. Given that our task involves multimodal image pairs, we incorporate
155   the pre-trained DINOv2 into our feature encoder to enhance the robustness of multimodal feature
156   representation. Specifically, DINOv2 divides the input image into 14 × 14 patches and generates a

159   further enrich the features with cross-view information, which is crucial for matching, we add a
160   normalized 2D positional encoding (Chen et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2024) and pass the features through

171   Since DINOv2 produces relatively low resolution features, relying solely on these features for ho-
172   mography estimation would lead to limited matching accuracy (Barroso-Laguna et al., 2024). To
173   address this issue, we introduce a Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) to construct multi-scale features,
174   ranging from the original image size down to a 1/8 scale. We keep the network lightweight by
175
      configuring the channel numbers of each layer to [8, 16, 32, 64]. Furthermore, to improve the repre-
      sentation capability of the lightweight FPN, we bilinearly upsample F 1 to the 1/8 scale and merge
176
      it with the 1/8 scale features produced by the encoding stage of the FPN. The final output features
177
      used for homography estimation consist of five scales, denoted as {F l }l=1,2,3,4,5 , corresponding to
178   spatial sizes of 1/14, 1/8, 1/4, 1/2, and 1 relative to the original image, as illustrated in Figure 2.

182   Before introducing our grid flow regression method, we briefly explain the dense matching frame-

187
      IS , representing the same physical locations in the scene. Typically, w is predicted in a multi-scale
      manner, from the coarest (l = 1) to the finest:

201
      Grid-based strategy for efficiency. To alleviate the computational burden of Eq. 2, previous works
202
      typically reduce the radius r as the scale increases (Truong et al., 2020; Shao et al., 2021; Zhu et al.,
203
      2024). However, this saving is limited for high-resolution images, where the first two dimensions
204   of c(FSl , FTl ; rl ) dominate the computational load. So to further minimize computational costs, we
205   leverage the global smooth pattern of homography transformations and focus on learning a sparser
206   representation of w to replace the original dense, pixel-wise one.
207
      To achieve this, we sparsify w through a grid-based strategy. We redefine x ∈ G×G×2 to represent
208
      the coordinates of a regular grid on IS with G × G being the grid size. Our goal is to estimate a
209
      grid-based displacement field w ∈ G × G × 2 and a confidence map m ∈ G × G × 1 that reflects the
210   reliability of each flow estimate. The initial grid size is determined by the resolution of the coarsest
211   feature map, with the grid size scaling proportionally to the feature map scale gap. In our case,
212   we regard the results produced at the coarsest scale, where DINOv2 is applied, as an initialization
213   step. Therefore, we keep the grid size constant for the first two scales and increase it only in the
214   subsequent scales. We list the flow shape and computational complexity of pixel-based and grid-
215   based strategies in Table 1. Theoretically, utilizing a grid-based strategy can markedly reduce the
      computational burden.

254   a symmetric approach. This involves swapping the input sequence of IS and IT to compute the flow
255   for the regular grid on IT . The resulting flows, wS→T and wT →S , along with their corresponding
256   confidence maps mS→T and mT →S , are then combined to form the final correspondences. From
257   these, K correspondences are selected by thresholding the confidence maps and sampling according
258   to a uniform distribution rule (Edstedt et al., 2024). These correspondences are then used to compute
259   the homography with a robust estimator such as RANSAC (Fischler & Bolles, 1981).

      4.1   DATASETS
305
      Basic training. Since the initial grid size is determined by the input resolution, it is preferable to
306   use training data with sufficient resolution to fully leverage our architecture’s potential. Following
307   Truong et al. (2020), we utilize the CityScapes (Cordts et al., 2016) and ADE-20K (Zhou et al.,
308   2019) datasets, both of which contain images larger than 750 × 750. We generate 33, 398 training
309   samples offline, following the pipeline in Sec. 3.5. The model trained on this split serves as the basic
310   model, which is subsequently fine-tuned for application on multimodal datasets.
311
      Fine-tuning. Following Zhao et al. (2021); Cao et al. (2022; 2023); Zhang et al. (2023); Zhang & Ma
312   (2024), we select GoogleMap and VIS-IR as the multimodal scenarios for evaluation. Specifically,
313   GoogleMap consists of paired satellite and map images provided by the Google Static Map API. We

344
345
      Implementation details. We use a batch size of 16 with a learning rate of 2 · 10−4 for training
      the basic model, and 1 · 10−4 for fine-tuning. We use the AdamW optimizer with a weight-decay
346
      factor of 10−2 , and use CosineAnnealing to schedule the learning rate. Both the basic training and
347
      fine-tuning stages use 2, 000, 000 training samples drawn from their respective datasets. Each epoch
348   processes 25, 000 samples, resulting in a total of 80 epochs. We train at a resolution of 448 × 448,
349   with the higher resolution in the recurrence set to 560 × 560. We test MSCOCO and VIRAT with
350   the basic model, while VIS-IR and GoogleMap with the fine-tuned models.
351
      For data generation, we set d = 0.3, in line with prior works. Composite homography is used in
352
      training but not in testing. For the model, DINOv2-large is selected and we stack 4 cross-attention
353
      layers. Decoder at each scale is composed of 8 stacked depthwise convolution blocks, in line with
354   Edstedt et al. (2023; 2024). The local radius from coarse to fine levels is set to [7, 6, 4, 2, 0]. We set
355   N = 2 for iterations to manage computational costs, and iteration is performed only on multimodal
356   cases. For the loss, we set λ = 0.85, α = 0.01. For estimation, we select K = 5, 000 matches, and
357   use RANSAC with the default setting of cv2.findHomography to calculate homography.
358
      Metrics. We calculate the mean reprojection error of the four corner points and report the area under
359
      the cumulative curve (AUC) at four different thresholds: 3 px, 5 px, 10 px, and 20 px.

532   5     C ONCLUSIONS
533
534   This paper introduces GFNet, a method designed to deliver high-accuracy homography estimation
535   across varying image resolutions, overcoming the resolution limitations of current approaches. This
536   strong performance stems from the dense matching paradigm and several key components, includ-
537   ing the integration of DINOv2, a grid-based strategy, iterative regression, and adaptive resolution
538   recurrence. Experiments show that GFNet outperforms existing homography estimation methods
539   on several challenging datasets, including multimodal and dynamic scenes, and achieves compara-
      ble results to the SOTA dense matcher with significantly higher efficiency.
