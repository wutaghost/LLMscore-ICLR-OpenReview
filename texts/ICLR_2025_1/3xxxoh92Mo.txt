009                                                              A BSTRACT
010
011                   Large-scale Vision-Language Models, such as CLIP, demonstrate impressive capa-
012                   bilities and have multiple applications, from text-to-image generation to zero-shot
013                   classification. Recent work has suggested that visual prompts, such as a red circle,
014                   can steer the vision encoder to the circled region. While such vision prompts have
015                   now been used in various applications, they might be model-specific and depend
016                   on the model learning these behaviours from its training data. Discovering and
017                   evaluating various prompts might not be feasible given different models, tasks, and
018
                      datasets. In this paper, we propose Highlight, a method to learn a visual prompt
                      that highlights a region in an image or refines a manually engineered visual prompt.
019
                      Using our framework, we can learn to highlight in a supervised way using a dataset
020
                      of text-image region pairs or in an unsupervised way using synthetic captions
021                   or images only. Highlight outperforms other visual prompts, prompt learning
022                   approaches, and compute-intensive methods that use ensembles of multiple models
023                   and visual prompts.

041   1      I NTRODUCTION
042
043   Vision Language Models (VLMs) have shown outstanding performance across a range of tasks: For
044   instance, CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) can perform zero-shot classification in a number of domains,
045   and Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) can count objects within an image, even though these tasks
046   were not explicitly part of their training. Instead, it is hypothesised that these abilities emerge from
047   the diverse large-scale training data. However, since the alt texts in pretraining image-text datasets
048   summarise global image descriptions, these models have limited capabilities for localising objects.
049   This limited spatial awareness hinders the usage of VLMs in various potential applications, such as
050
      autonomous driving (Wen et al., 2023), robotics (Brohan et al., 2023; Driess et al., 2023; Gao et al.,
      2023), and assistive technologies (Yang et al., 2022).
051
052   A promising new approach, visual prompting (Wu et al., 2024), has emerged to address these
053   limitations. By introducing visual markers, such as red circles, to highlight target objects, pretrained
      foundation models can reason about localised image regions. Visual prompting for VLMs has found


                                                                     1

054
      multiple applications, such as medical image analysis (Denner et al., 2024), trajectory selection (Song
055   et al., 2024), and audio descriptions generation Xie et al. (2024b). However, despite the different
056   domains of application of visual prompting and the different visual encoders used, these methods rely
057   on simple markers, such as a red circle, which have only been found to perform well empirically.
058
      It has been suggested that VLMs learn to pay attention to such visual markers from their training
059
      data, e.g. Shtedritski et al. (2023) discover images annotated with red circles in YFCC100M (Thomee
060
      et al., 2016), which is part of CLIP’s pretraining data. However, whether a red circle is the prompt
061   that best elicits these emergent behaviours in VLMs is unclear. Furthermore, there might not be one
062   optimal prompt per model—different domains in the training data might be annotated differently
063   because of arbitrary conventions in fields like medicine or science. Thus, it becomes unfeasible to
064   manually guess and evaluate visual prompts for every new VLM and domain to which it is applied.
065
      In this paper, we introduce Highlight, a method to automatically learn a visual prompt that highlights
066
      regions for a VLM. Highlight visually prompts an image in a differentiable way by predicting both
067   the shape and the colour of the visual marker. To supervise Highlight, first, we consider the case
068   when annotated text-image region pairs are available. We show that in that case, we can use CLIP’s
069   multimodal contrastive objective to match prompted images with text, learning a state-of-the-art
070   visual marker. However, this requires manual annotations. We thus introduce an unsupervised version
071   of Highlight that is trained with images only and does not require manual supervision. Our key
072   insight is that we can replace the textual description of a region in the image-text contrastive objective
073   either with a synthetically generated caption for the image patch or with a visual description of the
074   region, e.g. a crop. For example, we can learn to match highlighted images with a crop of the image.
075
      We find that the visual marker we learn in that way outperforms manually engineered visual markers.
076
077   2    R ELATED W ORK
078
079   Visual prompt enginering, as introduced by Shtedritski et al. (2023), uses visual markers in the
080   input image to change the output of a VLM. For example, Shtedritski et al. (2023) draw red circles
081   around objects of interest in order to make the VLM focus on these specific areas, whereas Yang et al.
082   (2024) propose pixel-wise outlines of objects in the image. (Xie et al., 2024a) proposes to optimise
083   the position of a visual marker in order to discover objects within an image. Both Yang et al. (2024)
084   and Shtedritski et al. (2023) hypothesise that their proposed prompts are salient for the model because
085   similar visual markers are present in the training data. Instead, Rezaei et al. (2024) optimise the
086   colours within a fixed mask (e.g. a circle) to draw the attention of transformers towards the prompted
087
      area. However, the method of Rezaei et al. (2024) only applies to transformers and requires access to
      the internal features of the ViT. Instead, Highlight treats the vision model as a black box and is thus
088
      architecture-agnostic. Furthermore, our framework can accommodate various forms of supervision,
089
      such as using image-text pairs, which allows learning a supervised visual prompt.
090
091   Referring Expression Comprehension (REC) is the task of localising an object in an image
092   corresponding to a query textual description. Many REC methods start with object proposals, for
093
      example, generated with Faster-RCNN (Ren et al., 2015), and then learn to score them (Hu et al.,
      2016; Luo & Shakhnarovich, 2017; Liu et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2019). Past
094
      research focuses on models specifically trained to solve the REC task. This includes combining
095
      pretrained vision and language models (Mao et al., 2016; Yu et al., 2016; Nagaraja et al., 2016; Hu
096   et al., 2016; Luo & Shakhnarovich, 2017), learning a joint-embedding space (Wang et al., 2016; Liu
097   et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2017), graph-based models (Liu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019; Yang et al.,
098   2019), that yield better interpretability, and more recently transformer based architectures (Kamath
099   et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2021; Deng et al., 2021). However, most of these rely on large amounts of
100   training data, which include images, referring expressions, and the corresponding bounding boxes.
101
      Unsupervised Referring Expression Comprehension has recently been enabled with the introduc-
102   tion of large pretrained models, such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), which allow solving REC in a
103   zero-shot manner (Shtedritski et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2024; Subramanian et al., 2022; Kirillov et al.,
104   2023b). ReCLIP (Subramanian et al., 2022) ranks object crops using CLIP before a post-processing
105   step that considers relations between objects. CPT (Yao et al., 2021) colours objects and uses a
106   pre-trained captioning model (Zhang et al., 2021) to predict which colour corresponds to the referring
107   expression. More recently, unsupervised methods overlay visual prompts on objects (Shtedritski
      et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; Rezaei et al., 2024). However, most of these methods use ensembles


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      of visual prompts and/or vision-language models to achieve competitive performance. Instead, our
109   method automatically discovers a visual prompt that outperforms such ensembles, making it several
110   times more compute-efficient.
111
112
      Visual patches can be optimised to steer the output of a vision model. Papernot et al. (2016)
      demonstrate that altering only a few pixel values can divert the model’s output towards an adversarial
113
      output. Schlarmann & Hein (2023) recently transferred this work to VLMs, where the objective is to
114
      move the embedding of an adversarial image towards some harmful textual embedding. Their work
115   differs from ours in requiring the adversarial changes to be invisible to humans. More closely related
116   to our work, adversarial patches (Brown et al., 2017) restrict the alteration to a region. Our method
117   can be seen as an extension of this research. However, unlike adversarial patches, we do not aim to
118   mislead the model’s output towards a harmful or incorrect outcome. Instead, we focus on optimising
119   visual prompts to improve task performance.

123
      We aim to learn a visual prompt for a VLM that focuses the visual representation on a particular
124
      object in an image. To solve a prediction task, models such as CLIP jointly encode images and text
125   and learn to match them. An input to such a model is an image I ∈ R3×H×W and text T , and the
126   output measures similarity between both inputs.
127
128
      3.1   V ISUAL PROMPTING
129
130   A model such as CLIP can be used to find an object referred to by a query text prompt T by visually
131   prompting an image. First, a visual prompting function F(I, u) is used to highlight one object
132   proposal u in the image I, where u is the location of an object in the image, which can be, e.g.
133   bounding box coordinates u = (xc , yc , xw , yh ). For a set of possible object locations U , the location
134   û in the image that is best described by the referring expression T is then given by
135
                                           û = arg max s(F(I, u), T ),                                     (1)
136                                                u∈U
137
      where s(I, T ) is the compatibility score between an image and text, e.g. as measured by a VLM. To
138
      implement the visual prompting function F, prior work crop the image (Subramanian et al., 2022),
139
      overlay red circles (Shtedritski et al., 2023), or generate pixel-wise outlines (Yang et al., 2024). In
140   this section, we show how we can learn such a prompt.

255   4.1   DATASETS
256
257   We evaluate Highlight on Referring Expressions Comprehension. We use the RefCOCO (Yu et al.,
258   2016), RefCOCO+ (Yu et al., 2016), and RefCOCOg (Mao et al., 2016) datasets, all of which consist
259   of images from the MS-COCO dataset (Lin et al., 2014) together with expressions that refer to a
260   unique object in the image, which are also annotated with a bounding box. The unique expression-
261
      image region correspondence satisfies our condition in Eq. (3), where locations outside the bounding
      must not be described by the referring expression. RefCOCO+ only contains appearance-based
262
      expressions, whereas RefCOCO and RefCOCOg contain relation-based expressions (e.g., containing
263
      the words left/closer/bigger). The test sets of RefCOCO and RefCOCO+ are split into two parts,
264   where “TestA” and “TestB” contain only people and non-people, respectively. Following prior
265   work (Subramanian et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2021; Shtedritski et al., 2023), we use the bounding
266   box proposals of MAttNet (Yu et al., 2018). For our unsupervised losses, we remove proposals
267   with bounding box sizes of less than 50px. We train on all RefCOCO datasets combined, making
268   sure to exclude any images that appear in the test sets (e.g. we found that some training images of
269   RefCOCOg might appear as test images in RefCOCO+). Following prior work, for evaluation we
      exclude test samples that have no correct proposals.

297   4.2       I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
298
299   Architecture. While one can optimise a visual prompt directly in pixel space, the limited number
300
      of learnable parameters in pixel space hinders the sharing of spatial patterns between pixels, leading
      to less efficient optimisation (Krull et al., 2019). To generate the visual prompt, we, therefore, use a
301
      Coordinate Network (Stanley, 2007), which, given the (x, y) coordinates of a pixel location in the
302
      visual prompt, generates the RGB and α values for that location. Therefore, Coordinate Networks
303   can be thought of as 2D Nerfs (Mildenhall et al., 2021). The network generates a fixed-sized prompt,
304   which is afterwards scaled to fit the size of the object proposal that should be prompted. We use a
305   Coordinate Network with eight layers and a hidden dimension of 256. We add a sinusoidal positional
306   encoding to the input and apply a sigmoid function to the outputted α mask.
307
308
      Additional details. We use a linear annealing schedule with a 10% warmup and cooldown for
309
      all pretrained Highlights and pretrain them to output a red circle for 2,000 steps unless mentioned
310   otherwise. For the models that were not pretrained, we empirically observed exploding gradients
311   when utilising a scheduler. Therefore, we do not use a scheduler for these runs. Similarly to prior
312   work, we use a CLIP ViT-L/14 in most experiments unless stated otherwise. The hyperparameters we
313   used in different settings can be found in the Appendix.

479
      Manually engineered visual prompts for VLMs have been extensively used for multiple tasks.
480   However, choosing visual prompts requires trial and error and guessing. The framework we present,
481   Highlight, automatically learns a visual prompt given a dataset of text and images, or images only.
482   The optimised prompt can refine a manually engineered prompt, or be learnt from scratch, in which
483   case we find they resemble manually engineered prompts. The visual prompts we automatically learn
484   outperform manually engineered visual prompts, as well as computationally ensembles of models
485   and prompts. We hope this inspires further work into automatically learning to highlight images, as
      well as applying such methods beyond CLIP models and Referring Expression Comprehension.

713   A.1     A DDING H EURISTICAL S PATIAL R ELATIONS
714
      As detailed in Section 3, one potential caveat of training with synthetic captions is that captions might
715
      be ambiguous, e.g. in Fig. 2 the caption model might output the same caption “a horse” for both
716   object proposals in the image. We experiment with alleviating this ambiguity by introducing spatial
717   relations using the heuristics introduced by Subramanian et al. (2022): For each object proposal, we
718   identify the closest other object proposal and then determine which spatial relations hold between
719   the two bounding boxes (e.g. left, right, above, below, smaller, bigger) and select one of these at
720   random. Next, we combine the captions from the two objects with a randomly chosen synonym
721   for the selected spatial relation. We take the list of synonyms from Subramanian et al. (2022). For
722   example, in Fig. 2, this process might change the captions from {“a horse”, “a horse”} to {“a horse
723   to the left of a horse”, “a horse bigger than a horse”}.
724   Table 7 shows the performance of training with synthetic captions both with and without heuristically
725   adding spatial relations. Overall, performance when training with heuristically added spatial relations
726   is slightly better than training without spatial relations. Interestingly, we observe that this improvement
727   mainly stems from TestA splits. This might indicate that the captions the caption model generates are
728   better suited to differentiate objects from one another than humans.

738   A.3     C OMPARISON TO FGVP
739
740   In Table 8 we compare Highlight to FGVP (Yang et al., 2024). FGVP is supervised in that it uses
741   SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023a) to generate mask outlines for objects, which are used for visual prompts.
742   Additionally, FGVP utilises several post-processing techniques. In Table 8, we see that unsupervised
743   Highlight outperforms FGVP when it uses a single prompt. Supervised highlight outperforms FGVP
744   when it uses up to 6 forward passes of the visual encoder and either of the two ad-hoc post-processing
745
      techniques. However, when FGVP uses 8 passes of the visual encoder and both post-processing
      methods, it performs better than Highlight. Overall, Highlight is a much more lightweight and
746
      efficient method, and performs favourably to FGVP.
