009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Image restoration, as a longstanding task, aims to recover the missing details and
012            remove degradations from a corrupted observation. Inspired by the success of
013            prompt learning in natural language processing, many prompt-based approaches
014            have been developed for various image restoration tasks. However, these algorithms
015            mostly operate in the spatial domain. As frequency learning plays an important role
016
               in image restoration by reducing the spectra discrepancy between degraded/sharp
               image pairs, this study explores the potential of frequency prompts for efficient
017
               image restoration by proposing a plug-and-play mechanism, which mainly com-
018
               prises a prompt generation module and a prompt integration module. Specifically,
019            the former encodes different frequency information by aggregating the pre-defined
020            learnable parameters under the guidance of implicitly decomposed spectra of input
021            features. Subsequently, to dynamically guide reconstruction, the learned prompts
022            are embedded into the spectra of features via dual-dimensional attention for ef-
023            fective frequency learning. To demonstrate the effectiveness of our mechanism,
024            we conduct experiments on general and all-in-one image restoration tasks. By
025            incorporating it into a CNN-based backbone, the model achieves state-of-the-art
026            performance on 15 benchmark datasets for five representative image restoration
027
               tasks. Furthermore, equipped with our mechanism, a pure Transformer network
               performs favorably against state-of-the-art algorithms under two all-in-one settings.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Due to the physical limitations of low-end sensors or terrible weather, various degradations (haze,
033   noise, and blur) are often involved in acquired images (Su et al., 2022), which will degrade the
034   visibility and impact the performance of models for downstream tasks. As an ill-posed problem,
035   image restoration aims to remove those undesired degradations from observations and recover the
036   missing details. Early approaches attempted to deal with its ill-posedness by proposing various
037   assumptions and hand-crafted priors to reduce the solution space (Song et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
038   2022). In recent years, the rapid development of deep learning has spawned a great number of
039
      frameworks built on convolutional neural networks (CNNs), which can learn more robust priors from
      large-scale data. By means of varied advanced modules, including residual connections (Liu et al.,
040
      2019; Ruan et al., 2022), U-shaped architectures (Mao et al., 2021; Cho et al., 2021), and attention
041
      mechanisms (Qin et al., 2020; Cui et al., 2023d; Zhang et al., 2018b), these methods have achieved
042   promising performance on multifarious image restoration tasks.
043
044   Subsequently, many general image restoration methods have been developed, which can perform
045
      well on a range of tasks after separate training (Chen et al., 2022; 2021a; Cui et al., 2023a). For
      example, MPRNet (Zamir et al., 2021) adopts a multi-stage CNN-based architecture to break down
046
      the recovery process into several manageable steps. Transformers have also been introduced into this
047
      track by performing self-attention within different scopes (Tsai et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2021; Wang
048   et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a) and dimensions (Zamir et al., 2022a). However, these solutions need
049   individual training processes and copies when applied to different tasks, which is not practical for
050   resource-constrained scenarios. To alleviate these issues, the recent all-in-one topic has garnered
051   significant attention by training a unified model for a series of degradations (Lin et al., 2024; Zhang
052   et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023). For instance, AirNet (Li et al., 2022) is one of the pioneering methods
053   to recover clean images in an all-in-one fashion. It works by contrastively learning the degradation
      representations, which are then used to restore the sharp image.

054
      More recently, prompt learning originating from natural language processing (Zhou et al., 2022a;b)
055   has been incorporated into general and all-in-one image restoration and has advanced performance
056   by providing adaptive learning ability. These prompt-based methods encode the degradation priors
057   using the produced or pre-set prompts (Potlapalli et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b; Luo
058   et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2023; Ai et al., 2023), which are then used to guide the restoration process.
059   For example, PromptIR (Potlapalli et al., 2024) addresses all-in-one image restoration using the
060   input-conditioned prompts that learn the knowledge of different degradation types. SelfPromer (Wang
061   et al., 2024a) formulates the prompts based on depth cues, requiring an expensive depth estimator for
062   supervision. However, these prompt-based methods mostly operate in the spatial domain without
063
      investigating the significance of frequency learning in prompts, which is also beneficial for high-
      fidelity image restoration.
064
065   In this paper, we present a plug-and-play prompt-based mechanism by formulating prompts from the
066   perspective of frequency. To this end, we first embed crucial information about different subbands
067   into learnable parameters with the guidance of different frequencies produced via a simple frequency
068   decomposition method. Then, the learned prompts interact with the input features in the frequency
069
      domain via dual-dimensional attention weights. Overall, the resources for learning prompts and
      the aggregation method of injecting prompts into features are both from the frequency perspectives,
070
      resulting in consistency and effective frequency learning. Moreover, the dual-dimensional attention
071
      weights facilitate the full use of frequency signals encoded in prompts. By doing these, our mechanism
072   enables the model to effectively recover clean images by dynamically refining frequency signals.
073
074   Our simple yet effective plug-and-play frequency prompt mechanism can be easily applied to CNN-
075
      based and Transformer-based architectures. Specifically, combined with a CNN-based backbone,
      our mechanism helps the model achieve state-of-the-art performance on 15 datasets for a range of
076
      representative image restoration tasks, including image dehazing, desnowing, deraining, defocus
077
      deblurring, and low-light image enhancement. Equipped with our mechanism, a pure Transformer
078   model performs favorably against state-of-the-art algorithms under two all-in-one settings.

083   2.1   I MAGE R ESTORATION
084
      Given a corrupted image, image restoration aims to recover a clean image by removing degradations
085
      and reconstructing missing details. The development of deep learning has spawned a great number
086
      of methods, which can be roughly divided into three classes in terms of task: task-specific (Qin
087   et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2019; Cho et al., 2021; Ruan et al., 2022), task-agnostic (or general) (Liang
088   et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Zamir et al., 2022a; Cui et al., 2023a;c), and all-in-one (Yang et al.,
089   2023; Potlapalli et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b; Luo et al., 2021). The task-specific
090   methods can only perform well on a specific task, while task-agnostic ones can be applied to several
091   tasks but need separate training on each dataset. These two categories have made great progress in
092   terms of performance, which can be attributed to advanced designs for CNN- and Transformer-based
093   frameworks. The all-in-one task has recently been a hot topic in image restoration because the
094   all-in-one models can deal with multiple degradation types by training a single model once, which is
095
      suitable for resource-constrained scenarios. In this paper, we conduct experiments on both general
      and all-in-one image restoration tasks to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
096
097
098   2.2   P ROMPT L EARNING
099
      Prompt learning is originally used in natural language processing to finetune a trained model to
100
      a downstream task by providing in-context and task-specific information. Inspired by this, this
101
      technique has been adopted in image restoration algorithms to encode degradation information (Yu
102   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2024b; Zhou et al., 2024). PromptRestorer (Wang et al.,
103   2023) uses raw degradation features to generate prompts for general tasks and incorporates prompts
104   from global and local perspectives via self-attention units. PromptCIR (Li et al., 2024) applies spatial
105   prompts (Potlapalli et al., 2024) for blind compressed image restoration. SelfPromer (Wang et al.,
106   2024a) formulates the prompt by considering the estimated depth cues for image dehazing. In the
107   context of the all-in-one setting, PromptIR (Potlapalli et al., 2024) introduces a drop-in prompt
      block to dynamically adjust representations for high-fidelity image restoration. Subsequently, DA-


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      CLIP (Luo et al., 2021) utilizes content embedding yielded by a large vision-language model to
109   aggregate prompts for universal image restoration. Nevertheless, these prompt-based approaches
110   primarily leverage prompts in the spatial domain without exploring the utility in the frequency domain.
111   In this study, to explore the potential of frequency prompts for image restoration, we use different
112   frequencies generated via an extremely lightweight frequency decomposition strategy to encode
113   frequency information into prompts, and then inject prompts into the input features in the frequency
114   domain via dual-dimensional attention, ensuring the domain consistency between the source of
115   producing prompts and injecting method. Furthermore, our dual-dimensional operation facilitates
116   full use of the acquired knowledge in prompts.

142   3.1    P ROMPT G ENERATION M ODULE (PGM)
143
144   The PGM is responsible for encoding the informative information of different frequency subbands
145   into prompts. To this end, we first leverage a lightweight frequency decouple method and then use
146   the corresponding frequencies to aggregate the preset learnable parameters.

148   layer is applied to yield the low-frequency signals, where GAP serves as a kind of low-pass filter by

150   produced by removing this resulting low-frequency subband from the input X. Subsequently, the
151   attention weights for aggregating the prompts are produced by 1 × 1 convolution layers and softmax

153
      of PGM is obtained via a 3 × 3 convolution. Overall, the process of PGM is formally summarized as:

172   3.2     P ROMPT I NTEGRATION M ODULE (PIM)
173
174   The PIM is designed to integrate the combined frequency prompts into the original input features.
175   Different from existing prompt-based schemes (Potlapalli et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Zhou et al.,
176   2024) that use cross-attention modules, we instead adopt a more natural solution, refining the spectra
177
      of features.
178   Specifically, based on the output of PGM, XPGM , PIM first uses a couple of convolution layers
179   and GAP to generate spatial and channel attention weights, which are then utilized to modulate the
180   Fourier spectra of the input features. This process can be formally expressed by:

188   4      E XPERIMENTS
189
190   In this section, we conduct comprehensive experiments in two cases, i.e., general and all-in-one
191   image restoration tasks, to demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed mechanism. In each case,
192
      we first delineate the adopted backbone and deployment method of our FPM. Next, we introduce the
      implementation details and used datasets. Finally, the experimental results are presented.
193
194
195   4.1     G ENERAL I MAGE R ESTORATION
196
      To purely verify the efficacy of our design, we integrate our design with a classic U-shaped CNN-based
197
      backbone. As illustrated in Figure 2, the model consists of three scales. Each residual group (RG)
198   contains N + 1 residual blocks, and the last one accommodates our FPM between two convolutions.
199
200   The degraded input image is processed by a 3 × 3 convolution layer to generate embedding features.
201
      After going through the three-scale encoder and decoder networks, the restored image is produced by
      another 3 × 3 convolution layer and image-level residual connection.

      5   C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   This paper presents a frequency prompt mechanism (FPM) for image restoration, which is designed by
531
      using the implicitly decomposed frequency signals to guide the aggregation of predefined learnable
      parameters and injecting the learned prompts into the spectra of original input features. More
532
      concretely, the prompt generation module leverages the global average pooling to decouple features
533
      into different frequency segments for guidance to make prompts encode informative low- and
534   high-frequency information. The obtained prompts are then incorporated with input features via
535   bi-dimensional attention in the spectral domain. FPM can be employed in different architectures.
536   Built on it, the CNN-based network achieves state-of-the-art performance on 15 datasets for five
537   representative image restoration tasks, including image dehazing, defocus deblurring, desnowing,
538   deraining, and low-light image enhancement. Furthermore, equipped with FPM, the Transformer
539   backbone performs favorably against state-of-the-art algorithms in all-in-one restoration settings.
      These results demonstrate that our design is a valuable contribution to the realm of image restoration.

      A    DATASETS AND T RAINING C ONFIGURATIONS
872
873   In this section, we provide more details of the used datasets and specific training configurations for
874   different general image restoration tasks. According to the complexity of different tasks, we set
875   N to 3 for tasks of dehazing, desnowing, and low-light image enhancement and 15 for deblurring
876   and deraining. Unless specified otherwise, the patch size and batch size adopted for training are
877   3 × 256 × 256 and 8, respectively. The initial learning rate is set to 2e−4 , which is reduced to 1e−6
878
      with the cosine annealing strategy.
879   Image Dehazing. We evaluate our method on four kinds of datasets: synthetic daytime datasets, a
880   real-world dataset, nighttime datasets, and remote sensing datasets. For daytime scenes, we use the
881   widely adopted RESIDE-Indoor (Li et al., 2018) and RESIDE-Outdoor (Li et al., 2018) datasets for
882   training and evaluation. Specifically, the model is trained on these two datasets for 1000 epochs and
883   30 epochs, respectively, and then tested on the corresponding test sets: SOTS-Indoor (Li et al., 2018)
884
      and SOTS-Outdoor (Li et al., 2018). The initial learning rate for RESIDE-Outdoor is set to 1e−4 .
      Moreover, our method is trained and evaluated on a more realistic synthetic dataset, Haze4K (Liu
885
      et al., 2021). The model is trained for 1000 epochs with a batch size of 4 and a learning rate of 1e−4 .
886
      Furthermore, our model is trained and tested on the Dense-Haze (Ancuti et al., 2019) datasets to
887   evaluate its robustness in real-world scenarios. The model is trained for 5000 epochs with a batch
888   size of 2 and patch size of 800 × 1200, following (Cui et al., 2023a; Guo et al., 2022).
889
890
      In addition to daytime scenes, our model is further evaluated in nighttime scenes. Two commonly
      used datasets are adopted, i.e., GTA5 (Yan et al., 2020) and NHR (Zhang et al., 2020). Given
891
      nighttime hazy images, the ground truth images of these two datasets are nighttime clean images and
892
      daytime clean images. The models are trained for 300 epochs on the two datasets with a learning rate
893   of 1e−4 .
894
895   Since image dehazing plays an essential role in remote sensing, we evaluate our model on a remote
896
      sensing dataset, SateHaze1k (Huang et al., 2020), which consists of three sub-sets with different
      levels of haze degradations: thin, moderate, and thick. The model is separately trained for three
897
      datasets for 1000 epochs with a batch size of 32 and a learning rate of 8e−4 .
898
899   Image Defocus Deblurring. Consistent with recent algorithms (Ruan et al., 2022; Cui et al., 2023a;
900   Zamir et al., 2022a), we use the DPDD (Abuolaim & Brown, 2020) datasets for evaluation. This
901   dataset comprises 350, 74, and 76 scenes for training, validation, and testing. There are four images
902
      in each scene, named center view, left view, right view, and an all-in-focus ground truth. Our model is
      trained under the single-image setting by taking the center-view images as input and computing loss
903
      values between the output and ground truth. Our training strategy is identical to that of algorithms (Cui
904
      et al., 2023a; Ruan et al., 2022).
905
906   Image Desnowing. For this task, we use three widely-adopted datasets for training and testing,
907   i.e., CSD (Chen et al., 2021b), SRRS (Chen et al., 2020), and Snow100K (Liu et al., 2018). The
908
      preprocessing for these datasets remains identical to previous methods (Chen et al., 2020; Cui et al.,
      2023a) for fair comparisons. The models are trained for 2000 epochs.
909
910   Image Deraining. For image deraining, the model is trained on a compound dataset that is mixed
911   based on (Fu et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2019; Li et al., 2016), following (Zamir
912   et al., 2022a; Cui et al., 2023b), and tested on the Test2800 (Fu et al., 2017) dataset. The PSNR/SSIM
913   scores are measured using the Y channel in the YCbCr color space, which is consistent with existing
914
      methods (Zamir et al., 2022a; Cui et al., 2023b). The model is trained for 300 epochs with a batch
      size of 4 and a learning rate of 1e−4 .
915
916   Low-Light Image Enhancement. For this task, the model is evaluated on LOL-v2-synthetic (Yang
917   et al., 2021), which consists of 900 and 100 paired images for training and testing, respectively. The
      model is trained for 2200 epochs on 3 × 128 × 128 patches.
