009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011              Multi-track deep music generation has largely focused on pre-specified struc-
012              tures and instruments. However, it remains a challenge to generate “band-level”
013              full-length music that is capable of allocating instruments based on musical fea-
014              tures, their expressive potential, and their performance characteristics differences.
015              Moreover, the representations of symbolic and audio music have been treated as
016
                 distinct sub-areas, without a unified architecture to join their own advantages.
                 In this work, we introduce UniComposer 1 , a novel music generation pipeline
017
                 that composes at the band level, utilizing a hierarchical multi-track music repre-
018
                 sentation complemented by four cascaded diffusion models which progressively
019              generate rhythm features, and unified features extracted from both symbolic and
020              audio music by autoencoders. Experiments and analysis demonstrate that Uni-
021              Composer achieves a unified latent space for symbolic and audio music, and is
022              capable of generating band-level compositions with well structured multi-track
023              arrangements, surpassing previous methods in performances.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      The area of deep generative models for symbolic music (Wang et al. (2024), Hsiao et al. (2021),
028
      Huang & Yang (2020), among others) have witnessed advancements in a range of fronts. However,
029   the approaches in the literature do not yet achieve band-level music generation. Band-level music
030   has two critical features: 1) Instruments in a band collaborate to fulfill specific roles, such as har-
031   mony, rhythm, or accompaniment, which can differ from their roles in solo performances—a nuance
032   overlooked in current multi-track generation. For instance, the piano often provides repetitive har-
033   monies in a band, contrasting with its more varied melodies in solo settings. 2) Composers carefully
034   select instruments to match the melody’s characteristics, ensuring timbre, range, and expressiveness
035   align with the intended emotion. They also arrange instruments dynamically, adjusting prominence
036   based on sections or rhythmic changes. Current methods either replicate input instruments (Liu et al.
037
      (2022), Dong et al. (2018)) or rely on pre-defined user input (Dong et al. (2023), von Rütte et al.
      (2023), OpenAI (2024)).
038
039   For music data that take Audio formats such as MP3 (mpgedit (2003)) and WAV (bitsforbyte (2021)),
040   there are billions of publicly accessible music tracks spanning a wide range of styles, instruments,
041   and time periods. However, the situation is different for symbolic music. On one hand, symbolic-
042   format music data are not as ample as the audio-format. On the other hand, methods for analysis
043
      and generation in symbolic music are often more structured and fine-grained, allowing explicit in-
      corporation of music theory into the model architecture (Wang et al. (2024), von Rütte et al. (2023)).
044
      This enhances the model’s ability to implicitly capture complex musical relationships. In contrast,
045
      methodologies for analyzing and generating audio music tend to rely solely on the model’s inherent
046   capacity to learn these relationships (Ji et al. (2023), Huang et al. (2023)), without explicitly incor-
047   porating music structure into the architecture design. However, no public work has yet developed
048   approaches to organically combine the two forms of music, leaving this area largely unexplored.
049
      Our work, UniComposer, simplifies band-level music modeling by introducing a hierarchical struc-
050
      ture that groups instruments into monophonic, polyphonic, and percussion categories. Monophonic
051
      instruments provide chordal foundations, polyphonic instruments add melodic complexity, and per-
052   cussion drives the rhythm. We use a two-step generation process: first, a basic rhythm is created

      In summary, the contribution of the paper is as follows:
075
076        1. We introduce UniComposer, the first band-level music generation system that involves col-
077           laborative roles of instruments, tailored to harmonize, provide rhythm, or accompaniment,
078           with careful selection of instruments to match the melody’s expressive qualities. Unicom-

088   2   R ELATED W ORK
089
090   In this section, we first review multi-track music generation, followed by integrated processing for
091   symbolic and audio music. Then, we review structured music modeling. Finally, we review the
092   applications of autoencoders and diffusion models in music generation.
093
      Multi-Track Music Generation. Addressing the complexity of generating music consisting of
094
      multiple interrelated tracks, multi-track music generation has become a significant area of research.
095
      Some works focus on developing multi-track representations of music which facilitate the generation
096   process, while others aim to utilize multi-track approaches to improve music resolution and achieve
097   fine-grained control. Guo (2024) integrates the AC algorithm to enhance diversity in multi-track
098   music generation. MMBert (Zhu et al. (2024)) is a multi-track generation method focusing on the
099   chord aspects of music. Jen-1 Composer (Yao et al. (2023)) generates music that contains four
100   tracks from text prompts. Multitrack Music LDM (Karchkhadze et al. (2024)) leverages multi-
101   track techniques to generate high-fidelity audio music. Cosenza et al. (2023) utilizes a graph-based
102   generation approach to produce polyphonic music, with the instruments to be played specified by
103   the user. MMMachine (Ens & Pasquier (2020)) mainly concentrates on multi-track inputs for music
104
      representation to achieve a better understanding of music, similar to SymphonyNet (Liu et al. (2022))
      that copies input tracks for generation.
105
106   Integrated Processing of Symbolic and Audio Music. Research that combines audio and sym-
107   bolic music is currently divided into two interconnected areas: audio rendering of symbolic music
      and automatic music transcription that translates audio into symbolic notes. For audio rendering of

108
      symbolic music, traditional audio renderers use DSP-based or sample-based synthesizers to produce
109   instrumental sounds. A parallel line of research has applied data-driven approaches to audio syn-
110   thesis, often referred to as ”neural audio synthesis.” Notable examples include NSynth (Engel et al.
111   (2017)), GANSynth (Engel et al. (2019)), DDSP (Engel et al. (2020)), and MIDI-DDSP (Wu et al.
112   (2021)). These approaches share a similar concept of converting notes to audio waveforms using
113   neural networks that perform an upsampling process.
114
      For automatic music transcription, the outputs are typically frame-level multi-pitch estimation
115   (MPE) or note-level estimation. These methods commonly first estimate a pitch posteriorgram,
116   where each time-frequency bin is assigned an estimate of the likelihood of a fundamental frequency
117   being active at a given time (Duan et al. (2010); Bittner et al. (2017)). Multiple methods have been
118   proposed for estimating notes from pitch posteriorgrams, such as using median filtering (Klapuri
119   & Davy (2007)), Hidden Markov Models (Benetos (2017)), or neural networks (Ewert & Sandler
120   (2017); Nishikimi et al. (2021)). Gardner et al. (2021) is the first lighting work of leveraging a
121   unified framework for transcription.
122   Structured Music Modeling. The local musical structure can be effectively modeled in two distinct
123   ways: through approaches that either learn structure indirectly or through methods that directly
124   define or extract musical elements, both yielding good results. Approaches like Music Transformer
125   (Huang et al. (2018)), JukeBox (Dhariwal et al. (2020)), MuseFormer (Yu et al. (2022)), MERT
126   (Li et al. (2023)), LLark (Gardner et al. (2023)) and Lemercier et al. (2024) represent the former,
127   where models predict and generate musical events by capturing dependencies within the music. On
128   the other hand, methods that rely on musical domain knowledge define specific features or extract
129
      interpretable musical representations, enabling the model to learn structures such as pitch contours
      at the measure level and accompaniment patterns. Some studies also aim to combine them, yielding
130
      high quality output (Mariani et al. (2023), Wang et al. (2024)).
131
132   Autoencoders and Diffusion Models for Music Generation. (Variational) Autoencoders are
133   widely utilized to extract musical representations from pre-trained models, encapsulating both the-
134   oretical and perceptual aspects of music (Brunner et al. (2018); Jiang et al. (2020); Wu & Yang
135
      (2023)). These extracted features serve as essential tools for modeling cascading musical events
      due to their rich informational content. In the domain of music generation using diffusion models,
136
      two principal approaches have emerged. The first approach represents music in a piano-roll for-
137
      mat, enabling diffusion models to directly generate note pitches and durations without the need for
138   intermediate representations (Mittal et al. (2021), Atassi (2023), Wang et al. (2024)). The second
139   approach employs diffusion models as feature learners; these models generate music by manipulat-
140   ing features encoded from preceding stages and executing reverse diffusion processes to synthesize
141   the final output (Zhu et al. (2022), Zhang et al. (2023), Huang et al. (2024)).

144   3     M ETHODOLOGY
145
146   Given an input melody, our proposed UniComposer extracts features from each bar in symbolic
147   music or the relative duration in audio. The composition process operates within the latent space
148   of these features, categorized into three main functions: monophonic, polyphonic, and percussion.
149   Feature extraction is managed by symbolic and audio encoders, while a shared decoder reconstructs
150   bars from these extracted features. Using bars as the fundamental unit, the system progressively
151   generates detailed features for each category through four cascaded diffusion models. A converter
152   can subsequently translate symbolic output into audio. Section 3.1 outlines the hierarchical repre-
153   sentation of band-level music, followed by the feature extraction together with the training strategy
154
      in Section 3.2, 3.3 and 3.4. Finally, the overall generation process is discussed in Section 3.5.
155
156   3.1   H IERARCHICAL R EPRESENTATION OF BAND - LEVEL M USIC
157
158   To streamline and simplify the modeling of band-level music, we adopt a functional separation
159   approach to represent the three components of the musical ensemble, with a hierarchical reduction
160   process transitioning from detailed to simplified rhythmic structures inspired by Prajwal et al. (2024)
161   and Wang et al. (2024) to capture the overarching compositional framework. This approach enhances
      the efficiency of the learning process for UniComposer, and is illustrated in Figure 2.


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Functional Separation. Monophonic instruments play one note at a time, while polyphonic instru-
163   ments can produce multiple notes simultaneously, enabling harmonic richness. Percussion instru-
164   ments create sound by actions like striking or shaking. Monophonic instruments focus on melody,
165   contributing to musical expression by adding emotional depth through greater variation, while poly-
166   phonic ones often emphasize harmony and accompaniment with repeated or transposed notes, sup-
167   porting the music’s structure. Percussion maintains rhythm, articulating the beats and tension that
168   drive the progression of a piece. Recognizing these functional distinctions, UniComposer sepa-
169   rates these musical elements to improve data representation for band-level music. This division is
170   facilitated by referencing the program IDs on the MIDI standard (MIDI-Association (2024)).
171   Hierarchical Reduction. On one hand, as is typical for composers, the initial step often involves
172   drafting each section of the ensemble, defining key structural elements such as the primary modula-
173   tion points and the bassline pitches. On the other hand, the process of function separation can result
174   in superimposition of different instrumental parts, leading to redundant and overly intricate note
175   sequences, which makes it challenging for UniComposer to learn. Inspired by Wang et al. (2024),
176   we adopt a similar reduction approach based on the detailed rhythmic data for each instrumental
177
      category. The rhythmic structure of both monophonic and polyphonic sections is largely influenced
      by the harmonic chroma, while the rhythm of percussive instruments is primarily determined by the
178
      time signature, as it governs the temporal shifts within a composition.

      3.2   BAR -L EVEL F EATURE E XTRACTION FOR S YMBOLIC M USIC
200
201
      To analyze a sequence of notes within a bar, UniComposer extracts two key features: one represent-
202
      ing the notes themselves (note feature) and the other capturing the overall musical characteristics of
203
      the bar (musical feature). This process is facilitated by the joint training of a symbolic encoder and a
204   bar decoder, inspired by the approach outlined in Roberts et al. (2018), but with a more lightweight
205   model that utilizes Transformer architecture (Vaswani (2017)) instead of RNNs.
206
207
      Data Representation. UniComposer employs a modified version of the REMI representation
      (Huang & Yang (2020)), specifically adapted to handle bar-level features in symbolic music. Four
208
      key attributes are extracted for each bar: chord, time signature, dynamics, and tonality. Chords are
209
      represented by their root, chroma, and bass, while time signatures are restricted to three discrete
210   values: 3/4, 6/8, and 4/4. Dynamics are categorized into six levels, ranging from pianissimo (pp) to
211   fortissimo (ff). Tonality, indicating the organization of chords, can be either major or minor. Each
212   note is characterized by five attributes: onset, duration, pitch, velocity, and instrument. To achieve a
213   fine-grained temporal resolution, each bar is divided into 24 time bins. Velocity is simplified to four
214   levels (von Rütte et al. (2023)), preserving essential nuances while reducing complexity. Pitch is
215   encoded using 128 discrete values (MIDI-Association (2024)), and We group the 128 MIDI instru-
      ments, along with drums, into 13 categories, such as classifying all types of pianos (e.g., acoustic


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      piano, electric piano) under the piano category. UniComposer compiles all the notes present in its
217   dataset and uses them (which is approximately 106 ) as its vocabulary.
218
219   Addressing Embedding Challenge. Embedding layers trained alongside the model (e.g., Liu et al.
220
      (2022), von Rütte et al. (2023)) face instability due to the excessively large vocabulary size of Uni-
      Composer. Additionally, for this five-attribute note representation, the meaning of each note is
221
      inherently determined by its own attributes, contrasting with the word embedding approach (e.g.,
222
      Mikolov (2013)), where meaning is derived from contextual co-occurrence. To tackle these chal-
223   lenges, UniComposer proposes an innovative solution: a simple multi-layer perceptron (E-MLP) is
224   trained to encode each note by applying basic addition and concatenation operations to the tradi-
225   tional embedding vectors of the five attributes of a note. This process is followed by feed-forward
226   layers, which produce an output embedding vector with a fixed dimensionality of 16.
227
      Symbolic Encoder. The symbolic encoder employs the E-MLP mentioned above to generate a
228
      sequence of embedding vectors for the notes within a bar. Two special learnable vectors, represent-
229   ing the start [BOS] and end [EOS] of the sequence, are added to the embedding sequence. This
230   sequence is then processed by a bi-directional model based on the classical multi-head transformer
231   architecture proposed by Vaswani (2017), consisting of 4 layers and 4 attention heads is used to han-
232   dle this embedding sequence. The transformer’s final hidden state is passed through two separate
233   linear projection layers, producing outputs for both note and musical feature representations.

      3.3   U NIFICATION OF AUDIO INTO S YMBOLIC M USIC
262
263
      Pre-processing. Since general audio formats such as MP3 and WAV are dense time-sequence data,
264   UniComposer applies the Harmonic Constant-Q Transform (H-CQT) (Bittner et al. (2017)) to pro-
265   cess them. H-CQT takes an audio signal as input, and the output is a three-dimensional tensor
266   representing the time-frequency representation at different harmonic frequencies. Its main function
267   is to capture both the fundamental frequency and its harmonic components, providing a richer spec-
268   tral representation. UniComposer computes H-CQT with 3 bins per semitone and a hop size of
269   11 ms, operating with a sample rate of 22.05 kHz and utilizes 8 harmonics. For an input of shape
      (1, t × 22, 050), this H-CQT produces an output with the shape (8, t × 172, 128).

270
      Audio Encoder. The audio encoder aims to map audio segmentation to the same note and musical
271   feature space of the symbolic encoder. After pre-processing, a fully convolutional model followed
272   by an output layer is applied to produce the note and musical feature. The architecture is similar to
273   that proposed by Bittner et al. (2022), but with fewer layers, as the audio encoder processes shorter
274   input segments. Since H-CQT captures all harmonic waves, the convolutional layers with deep
275   kernels are able to detect notes with correct pitch and duration. Finally, using a series of linear and
276   activation layers, the audio encoder generates the expected two features.
277
278   3.4    R EFACTORING AND T RAINING P ROCESS FOR F EATURE E XTRACTION
279
280
      Bar Decoder. The task of the bar decoder is to reconstruct all the notes from the input based on the
      note feature, and predict four musical attributes of the bar based on the musical feature. For note
281
      reconstruction, the bar decoder uses a classical transformer decoder with 4 layers and 4 attention
282
      heads. The note feature is replicated n times to serve as the input hidden state of the decoder. The
283   transformer generates an embedding vector for each position auto-regressively, with a dimension
284   of 16. For musical feature prediction, a multi-layer percepton is applied to the musical feature,
285   followed by four classifiers to predict the four musical attribute of a bar.
286
      Three-Step Training Process. Firstly, the E-MLP is optimized to establish a stable embedding
287
      space for the notes. A separate MLP architecture (D-MLP), which includes five distinct classifiers,
288
      is employed to decode specific attributes from the E-MLP’s output. Secondly, the symbolic encoder
289   and bar decoder are trained jointly in a self-reconstruction manner for symbolic music. Specifically,
290   the symbolic encoder extracts both the note and feature from a musical bar, while the bar decoder
291   attempts to reconstruct the bar and predict its musical attributes. Thirdly, the bar decoder is frozen,
292   and a similar self-reconstruction training approach is applied to the audio encoder, in conjunction
293   with the bar decoder. The key difference here is that a converter, based on the open-source tool
294   Fluidsynth (Tom Moebert (2024)), is used to convert symbolic music into audio waveforms, which
295   serve as the input for the audio encoder. These three steps can be found in Table 1.

      Attention Mechanism. Inspired by Shabani et al. (2023), we design three types of attention mech-
325   anisms. 1) Self Attention, which restricts the attention to within a single bar to enhance local infor-
326   mation; 2) Global Attention, a standard self-attention applied across all bars in a song; and 3) Local
327   Attention, which operates at a 4-bar level to help the model capture short-term dependencies within
328   the song. In each attention layer, we learn three separate sets of key, query and value matrices,
329   using four multi-heads. The outputs of the three attention mechanisms are summed and then passed
330   through a standard Add & Norm layer. The denoising process repeats this attention block six times.
331   The masks used to implement these three types of attention mechanisms are shown in Figure 4.

      4.2    E VALUATION ON U NIFIED F EATURE

      Method and Metrics. To assess the performance of our encoders, we focused on the note estimation
381   task by integrating the audio encoder and bar decoder (A&B). This system takes audio input and
382   estimates notes, reflecting the core capabilities of the model. Performance is measured using the
383   note-level F-measure (F ), where a note is considered correct if its pitch, onset, and offset fall within
384   a defined threshold compared to the ground truth. Additionally, we report the note-level F-measure
385   without considering offsets (Fno ), following the same criteria as the F-measure but ignoring offsets,
386   as well as the bar-level note accuracy (Acc). These metrics are computed using the mir eval library
387   (Liang et al. (2015)).
388   Baseline Settings. As a baseline, we compare the performance of A&B on note onset and duration
389   with other note estimation pipelines, including Basic-Pitch (Bittner et al. (2022)) and MI-AMT (Wu
390   et al. (2020)). To assess the necessity of E-MLP and D-MLP, we also create a variation of Uni-
391   Composer where these components are replaced by a vocabulary of 608,020 tokens, followed by a
392   standard embedding layer. This model is denoted as Vocab-AE. The reserved evaluation set are sep-
393   arated into main melody, monophonic and polyphonic tracks, and we report the note reconstruction
394   accuracy in Table 4.
395   Analysis. It is important to note that Basic-Pitch and MI-AMT only estimate pitch, onset, and

448

449
      attention refines this by encouraging the model to emphasize pitch and velocity, yielding slight
450   improvements. Local attention, which mimics a composer’s focus on musical sub-structures, further
451   enhances overall quality.

472   5     C ONCLUSION
473
474   In this work, we propose UniComposer, a band-level music generation pipeline that involves col-
475   collaborative roles of instruments and bridges the gap between symbolic and audio music. Uni-
476   Composer first extracts features from both symbolic bars and audio segments, and then applies four
477   cascaded diffusion models to generate the corresponding features for each bar. Experiments on the
478   feature space demonstrate the effectiveness of the unified latent space, while comparisons with other
479   music generation pipelines highlight UniComposer’s ability to generate band-level music. Addition-
480   ally, the ablation studies confirm the effectiveness of the various components within UniComposer.
481   Discussion and Future work. There are potential discrepancies in data distribution between syn-
482   thesized audio generated from MIDI, background noise, and real-world recordings. However, a
483   limitation is the lack of extensive datasets containing paired raw audio and corresponding MIDI for
484   comprehensive training. Meanshile, UniComposer’s capacity to process human vocals remains lim-
485   ited due to insufficient data. Another constraint is the requirement for prior BPM (Beats Per Minute)
      information, which can restrict the data. Addressing these issues may be a focus of future research.
