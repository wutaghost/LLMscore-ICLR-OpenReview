009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Self-supervised vision-language models trained with contrastive objectives form
012            the basis of current state-of-the-art methods in AI vision tasks. The success of
013            these models is a direct consequence of the huge web-scale datasets used to train
014            them, but they require correspondingly large vision components to properly learn
015            powerful and general representations from such a broad data domain. This poses
016            a challenge for deploying large vision-language models, especially in resource-
017            constrained environments. To address this, we propose an alternate vision-language
018
               architecture, called HyperCLIP, that uses a small image encoder along with a
               hypernetwork that dynamically adapts image encoder weights to each new set of
019
               text inputs. All three components of the model (hypernetwork, image encoder, and
020
               text encoder) are pre-trained jointly end-to-end, and with a trained HyperCLIP
021            model, we can generate new zero-shot deployment-friendly image classifiers for
022            any task with a single forward pass through the text encoder and hypernetwork.
023            HyperCLIP increases the zero-shot accuracy of SigLIP trained models with small
024            image encoders by up to 3% on ImageNet and 5% on CIFAR-100 with minimal
025            training throughput overhead.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   A now-standard approach in deep learning for vision tasks is to first pre-train a model on web-scale
031   data and then adapt this model for a specific task using little or no additional data. Despite the
032
      widespread success of these models and their lack of reliance on large-scale labeled datasets, a
      significant downside is that these models are often on the order of billions of parameters – much
033
      larger than their supervised counterparts for a given task at the same accuracy level.
034
035   While these pre-trained models are powerful due to their generality, practitioners still need to apply
036   them to well defined and specific tasks. We consider settings where there are additional constraints
037   on the size of these models such as in edge computing applications. Within this context, strategies
038
      to reduce the memory footprint or inference latency of these massive models are of paramount
      importance.
039
040   There exist a variety of such strategies broadly categorized into pruning, quantization, and distillation
041   methods (Sun et al., 2023a; Dettmers et al., 2022; Frantar & Alistarh, 2023). These methods often
042   involve first training a large model and then applying the chosen technique in a post-hoc fashion.
043   Additionally, many of these methods require specialized hardware support to achieve actual memory
044
      and latency reductions (Liang et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2017; Han et al., 2016).
045   We propose a method of pre-training vision-language models (VLMs) that allows us to derive small
046   vision models appropriate for deployment on edge devices without requiring multi-step training
047   procedures or any specialized hardware. We suggest a new contrastive learning architectural design
048   based on hypernetworks that improves performance over current state-of-the-art baselines. Our
049   architecture can additionally be used in conjunction with a variety of model compression methods for
050
      further memory or latency improvements.
051   The enormous size of image encoders in VLMs is a direct consequence of the scale of their pre-
052   training datasets: the model’s image encoder is tasked with learning representations across an
053   extraordinarily large data domain, and we show that small vision encoders struggle to learn such
      a breadth of representations. In this work, we propose a new strategy. Instead of fixing one image

      2    BACKGROUND
092
093
094   Preliminaries: We formalize the contrastive pre-training setup, the use of hypernetworks, and the
095   associated notations.
096   Contrastive vision-language pre-training models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and its successor
097   SigLIP (Zhai et al., 2023) learn powerful vision representations by simultaneously training an image
098   encoder and text encoder on a large dataset of images and associated text. Informally, the objective is
099   to bring positive pairs (containing relevant image-text pairs) closer and push negative pairs (containing
100   unrelated image-text pairs) apart in the learned embedding space. Let the image encoder and text
101   encoder be denoted as F : RB×I → RB×D and G : RB×C → RB×D , respectively, where B is the
102   batch size, I is the dimensionality of the image input, C is the contextual input dimension of the
103   text (e.g., number of tokens or sequence length), and D is the embedding dimension of the output.
104
      Further, let the parameters of the image encoder be denoted by Θ = {θ1 , . . . , θL }, where θl are the
      parameters of layer l, and L is the total number of layers.
105
106   As a natural consequence of the constrastive training objective, a trained CLIP or SigLIP model
107   may be trivially repurposed as a K-class image classifier: given an image embedding x ∈ RD and a
      set of text embeddings Y ∈ RK×D each representing a candidate class, the matrix-vector product

122   Hypernetworks (or hypernets) are trained to produce the weights of another neural network (which
123
      we call the mainnet) given some input. There are no fundamental constraints on the architecture of a
      hypernet and they may be trained to produce the entire weight or a subset of the mainnet. The idea of
124
      hypernetworks trained end-end date back to fast weights Schmidhuber (1992); Gomez & Schmidhuber
125
      (2005) which are networks trained to produce context dependent weight updates for a second network.
126   Hypernetworks have also been designed to work with a range of modern architectures including
127   ConvNets, RNNs, and transformers, among others (Denil et al., 2013; Bertinetto et al., 2016; Jia et al.,
128   2016; Ha et al., 2016), and are particularly well used in Bayesian formulations to several applications
129   of deep learning where the hypernetwork learns to generate distributions over the weights of the
130   mainnet.
131
      Formally, let a hypernet be denoted as H(Xh ; Φ), and the mainnet be denoted as M(Xm ; Θ), where
132   Xh and Xm are the inputs to the hypernet and mainnet respectively, and Φ and Θ are their respective
133   weights. In a typical setup, H is designed to output the entire set of mainnet parameters Θ, and
134   any loss used is back-propagated through the hypernet weights Φ. The hypernet, H(Xh ; Φ) may
135   function as a dynamic parameter generator for the mainnet, M(Xm ; Θ). In such a scenario, the
136   hypernet takes a set of conditioning inputs Xh , which could represent various contextual information
137   or meta-parameters, and generates the weights Θ for the mainnet based on those inputs. This allows
138   the mainnet to adapt its parameters dynamically to different tasks or data distributions without the
139   need for extensive retraining. However, hypernetworks have been shown to be notoriously difficult
140
      to train in practice, largely because there are no principled ways to initialize them, given that their
      outputs directly influence the optimization landscape of the entire training process (Chang et al.,
141
      2023; Beck et al., 2023).

144   3    H YPER CLIP: A METHOD FOR SMALL - SCALE IMAGE ENCODERS IN
145        VISION - LANGUAGE MODELS
146
147   In this section, we present the HyperCLIP architecture, a new approach to pre-training vision-language
148   models (VLMs such as CLIP), which allows for a much smaller image encoder, able to eventually be
149   deployed on edge devices. Naturally, compressing the entire knowledge of a VLM into such a small
150   network is challenging: typical VLMs have a very large number of parameters in both the text and
151   image encoders, and indeed derive much of their performance boost from this scale.
152   To motivate the development of our HyperCLIP network, we consider a common “zero-shot” or
153   “one-shot” application of CLIP as an image classifier. In this use case, one takes a common image
154   classification task (say, classifying images into one of ten categories), develops a set of text prompts
155   for each category, and constructs the embeddings for each prompt using the CLIP text encoder. When
156   we want to classify a new image using this system, we then embed the image using the CLIP image
157   encoder, find which prompt is closest in embedding space, and output the corresponding class. These
158
      embeddings can further be fine-tuned (if desired) using a small amount of labeled training data (the
      so-called “few shot” setting). This pipeline was originally proposed in Radford et al. (2021), and is
159
      illustrated in Figure 1.
160
161   Now, consider the setting where we want to use this same process, but deploy the eventual classifier
      onto a small embedded device. A direct application would be challenging, owing to the fact that


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      the image encoder for CLIP is still quite large. However, for the most part, such a large encoder
163   would not be strictly required for the final deployment of the classifier: only a “small part” of CLIP’s
164   internal knowledge is actually needed for a given image classification problem, and thus it should
165   be possible to distill a smaller classifier for a particular classification problem. However, traditional
166   distillation is quite inefficient in that it requires a existing set of training data, and it is unclear how to
167   best leverage the full CLIP (i.e., teacher) model for this task since it does not directly produce class
168   probabilities.
169   The basic intuition of the HyperCLIP model is that we can directly train a VLM that skips this explicit
170   distillation process entirely, and instead produces an image encoder that is already optimized for
171   use on a particular classification problem. In order to achieve this, we leverage a hypernetwork that
172   produces a specialized image encoder directly for some subset of textual prompts. In the remainder
173   of this section, we describe our HyperCLIP model in detail and highlight the different design choices.
174
175   3.1   T HE H YPER CLIP MODEL
176
177   At a high level, our HyperCLIP model consists of three main com-
178   ponents, two of which are direct analogues of the traditional CLIP
179   model, and one of which is a new component: 1) the image encoder,
180   which takes images and produces vectors in the CLIP embedding
181   space, 2) the text encoder, which takes text input and produces vec-
182   tors in this same embedding space; and 3) the new component of a
183
      hypernetwork, which maps text embeddings to certain parameters
      of the image encoder itself. The first two components are essentially
184
      identical to that of the traditional CLIP model: the text encoder is in
185
      fact directly taken from CLIP, and the image encoder has the same
186   functional form as the CLIP image encoder, except that the network
187   is substantially smaller, given that we want to run it on edge devices.
188   This poses an obvious challenge, however, to learning a suitably
189   expressive encoder as in the traditional CLIP model.

207   3.2   A RCHITECTURAL DESIGN CHOICES
208
209   Several design choices informed our development of the precise architecture for the HyperCLIP
210   model, namely in the choice of image encoder and the design of the hypernetwork itself.
211
212   Image and text encoders. As mentioned previously, the text encoder we use is precisely the same
213   text encoder as in the traditional CLIP architecture, namely one based upon a causal Transformer
214   architecture. Indeed, we could potentially use a pre-trained text encoder if desired, though we train
215   these from scratch so as to allow for additional freedom in determining the resulting contrastive
      embedding space.


                                                            4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      For the image encoder, we consider several potential small vision architectures, including EfficientNet
217   (B0, B1, B2) (Tan & Le, 2019), MobileNetV3 (M0 and and M1) (Howard et al., 2019),TinyNet
218   (T0) (Han et al., 2020), EdgeNext (E0) (Maaz et al., 2022), and MobileViT (V0) (Mehta & Rastegari,
219   2021). These have varying numbers of parameters and different architectures (see Table 1). The
220   choice of image encoder architecture is largely dependent upon the target architecture at deployment
221   time, and virtually any efficient vision architecture could be leveraged here.
222
223   Hypernetwork architecture. The HyperCLIP hypernetwork takes as input a set of text embeddings
224   and must output the parameters of the model for the target image encoder model. This setting leads
225   to some natural constraints and invariance that are desirable in the hypernetwork itself, as well as
226   important considerations about what parameters are being produced.
227   One feature of our hypernetwork setting is the the produced network should be able to take, as input,
228   any number of text input embedding vectors; in other words, the model should be able to produce
229   a reasonable image encoder not just for a fixed batch size of potential prompts, but indeed for any
230   number of prompts (up to some reasonable limit on size constraints). Additionally, the network
231   architecture should be invariant to the ordering of these text embeddings: the “order” of the prompts
232   provided to the hypernetwork is entirely incidental, and should have no bearing on the network being
233   output.
234   Fortunately, the Transformer architecture (with variably-sized collections of inputs, and with no
235   causal masking or position encoding) already satisfies these two desiderata. The self-attention
236   operator used in the Transformer treats all positions in the input symmetrically. Thus, we use a
237   non-causal Transformer architecture as the hypernetwork, with each individual prompt embedding
238   serving as a single “token” input to the model used to produce the final parameters of the image
239   encoder (alternatively, we can also use global average pooling over the last layer of embeddings
240   in the hypernetwork, though in practice this causes little difference in performance). The resulting
241
      network should take all the inputted prompts and output a single set of image encoder parameters
      that produces an image encoder capable of maximally distinguishing between images corresponding
242
      to all such prompts. This architecture is shown in Figure 2.
243
244
      Parameters output by the hypernetwork. The second consideration for the hypernetwork involves
245
      which parameters of the image encoder to actually specify as the output of the hypernetwork. While
246
      in theory it would be possible to output all the parameters of the network, in practice even relatively
247   efficient image encoders still have millions of parameters, making it impractical to specify all of
248   them using a hypernetwork. Instead, we adopt the approach of only modifying the BatchNorm (or
249   LayerNorm, as appropriate) bias and scale parameters of the target image encoder; image encoders in
250   our class of small models typically have on the order of tens of thousands of such parameters, making
251   them a valuable target for the hypernetwork, in that they still are known to provide a very powerful
252   control surface of the target model, while being relatively small in number. Indeed, past work has
253   shown that it is occasionally possible to achieve reasonable performance in a deep network while
254   only adjusting BatchNorm parameters (Frankle et al., 2020).
255   We emphasize that we also train the remaining parameters of the image encoder (i.e., convolution
256   filters and MLP weights), but we do so in manner that is shared across all the different prompts within
257   CLIP training: that is, these non-Batch/LayerNorm parameters are shared over all different batches
258   of training, while only the Batch/LayerNorm parameters are adapted according to the output of the
259   hypernetwork.

261   3.3   T RAINING AND LOSS
262
263   We now describe the proposed HyperCLIP training and inference steps. This setup is similar to
264   SigLIP and other CLIP variants, with the usual image and text encoders F and G, but crucially, the
265   image encoder is far smaller than existing vision-language models, ideally of a size appropriate for
266
      embedded or mobile applications.
267   Alongside these, HyperCLIP introduces a hypernetwork H : RB×D → RM that transforms text
268   embeddings outputted from G into a set of parameters of dimensionality M . This hypernetwork
269   dynamically generates parameters for the image encoder F (or, equivalently, the image encoder
      acts as the mainnet for this hypernetwork). In practice, the hypernetwork’s output generates only a

      6    R ELATED WORK
487
488
      Most closely related to HyperCLIP, is the architecture presented by De Vries et al. (2017), which
489   introduces Conditional Batch Normalization (CBN) for visual question answering tasks by effectively
490   modulating BatchNorm layers of pre-trained ResNet models with linguistic input. Hypernetworks
491   have also been used to personalize text-image models (Ruiz et al., 2023), and the notion of condition-
492   ing normalization parameters is used in image generated with GANs and diffusion models (Perez
493   et al., 2018; Karras et al., 2019; Peebles & Xie, 2023). Our work differs in that we consider CLIP
494   models, a more modern architecture, and consequently focuses on learning visual classifiers from
495   natural language supervision. We focus on an end-end learning process and while the current results
496   demonstrate the adaptablilty of BatchNorm layers, future architectures may involve the hypernet
497
      producing the entire image encoder as our knowledge of the dynamics of hypernetwork training
      improves. We also note the various vision-language pre-training alternatives to SigLIP such as those
498
      proposed by Jia et al. (2021); Sun et al. (2023b); Li et al. (2023); Fini et al. (2023); Lai et al. (2024);
499
      Lavoie et al. (2024). Our architectural contribution is orthogonal to the contrastive loss used and
500   future work may explore the sensitivity to the specific contrastive loss. Finally, there exist literature
501   toward more efficient vision-language models that distill from a larger model to a smaller model (Wu
502   et al., 2023; Vasu et al., 2024). We emphasize that HyperCLIP skips an explicit distillation process
503   entirely, producing an image encoder that is optimized for the specific classification task.
504
505   Connections to Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA). LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) and HyperCLIP share
506   the overarching goal of efficient model adaptation but differ significantly in their approaches and
507   underlying mechanisms. LoRA introduces new trainable parameters during adaptation, which are
508   added to the original model in the form of low-rank matrices. The adaptation process in LoRA
509   typically involves two distinct steps: pre-training the base model on a large dataset, followed by
510
      fine-tuning using LoRA parameters on a specific task. In contrast, HyperCLIP does not introduce
      any new parameters to the classifier. Instead, HyperCLIP focuses solely on modifying the inference
511
      process without requiring additional training, thus making it a purely inference-driven adaptation
512
      method.
513
514   In addition, LoRA was primarily designed to reduce the computational and memory costs associated
515   with fine-tuning large-scale models, such as transformer-based language models. However, the
516
      literature on using LoRA in the small-model regime or for adapting vision-language contrastive
      models (like CLIP) is limited. This gap highlights that LoRA’s primary focus has been on tackling
517
      the challenges posed by large models, and not in resource-constrained settings.
518
519
520   7    C ONCLUSION
521
522   In this paper, we introduced HyperCLIP, a new architecture designed to enhance vision-language
523   models by dynamically adapting the image encoder using a hypernetwork. Our approach addresses
524
      the challenge of deploying large vision-language models in resource-constrained environments by
      producing smaller, task-specific image encoders that maintain high performance. By conditioning the
525
      image encoder parameters on the text embeddings, HyperCLIP achieves consistent and significant
526
      improvements in zero-shot accuracy, robustness to distribution shifts, and enhances fairness metrics
527   without the need for extensive post-hoc optimization or specialized hardware.
528
529   One limitation of HyperCLIP is the potential difficulty in scaling hypernetworks which we side-step
530
      by only adapting the normalization parameters. Additionally, while HyperCLIP reduces the size of
      the image encoder, it introduces an overhead from the hypernetwork, which may not be negligible for
531
      extremely resource-constrained environments.
532
533
      Broader impacts. HyperCLIP’s ability to produce efficient, high-performing vision-language
534
      models has implications for democratizing computer vision models enabling their deployment on
535   resource-limited devices and in diverse settings.

      A.5    DATAC OMP WITH DATA FILTERING NETWORKS
865
866   This work builds upon DataComp (Gadre et al., 2024), a benchmarking system that provides various
867   unfiltered image-text pair pools of increasing size for evaluating CLIP models. The pools range from
868   medium (128M datapoints) to xlarge (12.8B datapoints). Initially, we apply text-based filtering, which
869   selects samples containing text that overlaps with ImageNet class names. Captions are identified
870   as English using the FastText package (Bojanowski et al., 2017) and must contain words from
871   ImageNet-21K class synsets. Next, we apply data filtering network (DFN) based filtering (Fang et al.,
872
      2023). DFNs are CLIP models trained on high-quality datasets and used to filter larger unfiltered
      image-text pair datasets. Specifically, the authors release the IDs of DataComp’s 1.2B samples filtered
873
      with a DFN trained initially on HQITP-350M, a high-quality dataset of 357 million human-verified
874
      image-text pairs, and fine-tuned on additional datasets like MS COCO, Flickr30k, and ImageNet 1K.
875   This filtering steps leaves us with roughly 100M unique image-text pairs.

      A.6    P ROPERTIES OF SIGMOID TRAINING
878
879   The sigmoid-based loss for CLIP pre-training, termed SigLIP, is advantageous over the traditional
880   softmax-based contrastive loss. Specifically, it is more robust to label noise, a important benefit given
881   the inherently noisy nature of large-scale image-text datasets. SigLIP also demonstrates improved
882   stability and efficiency across different batch sizes and performs exceptionally well at lower batch
883   sizes, outperforming the softmax-based CLIP models with smaller batch sizes. This efficiency extends
884
      to memory usage, allowing for larger batch sizes with the same computational resources. CLIP needs
      to materialize a |B| × |B| matrix of pairwise similarities for a batch size B. For a device batch size b,
885
      SigLIP’s “chunked" approach only requires a b2 memory cost at any given moment, as opposed to the
886
      |B|2 memory cost, by permuting representations across devices and computing the loss locally before
887   summing it across all devices. While the performance gap between sigmoid and softmax losses
888   narrows with increasing batch size, SigLIP maintains its advantages without the need for extremely
889   large batches, which are required for optimal performance with the softmax loss.
