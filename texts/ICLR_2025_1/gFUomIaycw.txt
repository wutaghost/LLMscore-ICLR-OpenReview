009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-label image classification (MLC) is a fundamental task in computer vision,
012            requiring the identification of multiple objects or attributes within a single image.
013            Traditional approaches often rely on shared backbones and static gating mecha-
014            nisms, which can struggle to effectively capture complex label correlations and
015            handle label heterogeneity, leading to issues such as negative transfer. In this pa-
016
               per, we introduce the Dynamic Routing Mixture of Experts (DR-MoE) model,
               a novel architecture that integrates input-dependent dynamic gating networks into
017
               the mixture-of-experts (MoE) framework for MLC. Unlike static gating in exist-
018
               ing models like the Hybrid Sharing Query (HSQ) Yin et al. (2024), our dynamic
019            gating mechanism adaptively selects and weights both shared and task-specific
020            experts based on the input image features. This allows DR-MoE to better capture
021            varying label dependencies and mitigate negative transfer, resulting in improved
022            overall and per-label classification performance. We conduct extensive experi-
023            ments on benchmark datasets MS-COCO Lin et al. (2014) and PASCAL VOC
024            2007 Everingham et al. (2015), demonstrating that DR-MoE achieves state-of-
025            the-art results, outperforming existing methods including HSQ, Q2L Liu et al.
026            (2021), and ML-GCN Chen et al. (2019). Additionally, ablation studies confirm
027
               the effectiveness of dynamic gating in enhancing model adaptability and perfor-
               mance, particularly for labels with high heterogeneity. Our findings suggest that
028
               incorporating dynamic routing mechanisms into MoE architectures is a promising
029
               direction for advancing multi-label image classification.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Multi-label image classification (MLC) aims to assign multiple labels to an image, reflecting the
035   presence of various objects or attributes within the scene. Unlike single-label classification, where
036   each image is associated with a single category, MLC must handle the complexity of predicting a
037   set of labels that may exhibit intricate relationships, including co-occurrence and mutual exclusivity.
038
      This task is fundamental in computer vision applications such as image tagging, scene understand-
      ing, medical diagnosis, and autonomous driving Wang et al. (2016); Chen et al. (2019).
039
040   Existing methods often employ shared backbones to extract features and capture label correlations
041   through shared parameters Chen et al. (2019); Lanchantin et al. (2021). However, learning multi-
042   ple labels jointly can lead to negative transfer, where optimizing for one label adversely affects the
043   performance of others due to label heterogeneity. This problem is exacerbated when labels have
044
      conflicting features or when the model cannot adequately disentangle shared and label-specific in-
      formation.
045
046   To address the negative transfer issue, the Hybrid Sharing Query (HSQ) model Yin et al. (2024)
047   formulates MLC as a multi-task learning problem and introduces a mixture-of-experts (MoE) archi-
048   tecture with shared and task-specialized experts. HSQ leverages shared experts to capture common
049   patterns among labels and task-specialized experts to handle label-specific features. A static gating
050
      mechanism combines the outputs of these experts, aiming to balance shared and unique representa-
      tions.
051
052   However, the static gating in HSQ may not fully capture the dynamic nature of label correlations
053   that vary across different images. In real-world scenarios, the relevance of shared and task-specific
      experts can change depending on the content of each image. For instance, an image containing


                                                        1
054
      both bicycle and person may benefit from shared features, while an image with only bicycle may
055   require more emphasis on task-specific features. Static gating cannot adapt to these variations,
056   potentially limiting the model’s ability to exploit positive label correlations and mitigate negative
057   transfer effectively.
058
      In this paper, we propose the Dynamic Routing Mixture of Experts (DR-MoE) model for en-
059
      hanced multi-label image classification. Our approach introduces input-dependent dynamic gating
060
      networks that adaptively select and weight experts based on each individual image. By making the
061   gating mechanism a function of the input features, DR-MoE allows the model to tailor the com-
062   bination of shared and task-specific experts to the specific content of each image. This dynamic
063   routing enables better capture of varying label dependencies and more effective handling of label
064   heterogeneity.
065
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
066
067
             • We propose DR-MoE, a novel model that integrates dynamic gating networks into the MoE
068            framework for multi-label image classification, enabling adaptive expert selection based on
069            input images.
070
071
             • We introduce input-dependent gating mechanisms that allow the model to capture dynamic
               label correlations and mitigate negative transfer by customizing expert utilization per sam-
072
               ple.
073
074          • We demonstrate that DR-MoE achieves state-of-the-art performance on benchmark datasets
075            such as MS-COCO Lin et al. (2014) and PASCAL VOC Everingham et al. (2015), outper-
076
               forming existing methods including HSQ.
077          • We provide extensive experiments and analyses to show that dynamic routing improves
078            per-label accuracy, especially for labels with high heterogeneity, and offers insights into
079            the model’s adaptive behavior.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
082
083
      Multi-label image classification (MLC) has been extensively studied in computer vision, with var-
084
      ious approaches proposed to address the challenges of label correlations and heterogeneity. In this
085   section, we review existing methods in MLC, the use of mixture-of-experts (MoE) architectures in
086   deep learning, and dynamic routing mechanisms, highlighting the gap that our proposed model aims
087   to fill.
088
089
      2.1   M ULTI -L ABEL I MAGE C LASSIFICATION A PPROACHES
090
091   Early approaches to MLC leveraged convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with shared backbones
092   to extract image features, followed by classifiers for each label Wang et al. (2016; 2017). How-
093   ever, these methods often failed to capture complex label dependencies and struggled with label
094   heterogeneity. To address label correlations, graph-based methods introduced graph convolutional
095   networks (GCNs) to model label relationships explicitly Chen et al. (2019); Ye et al. (2020). For
096   example, Chen et al. Chen et al. (2019) proposed ML-GCN, which utilizes GCNs to learn label
097   embeddings and exploit inter-label correlations.
098   Transformer-based models have recently gained attention in MLC due to their ability to capture long-
099   range dependencies. The Query2Label (Q2L) model Liu et al. (2021) employs a transformer decoder
100   with learnable query embeddings representing each label. The model attends to the image features
101   extracted by a CNN backbone and generates predictions for each label through the transformer
102   mechanism. Similarly, Lanchantin et al. Lanchantin et al. (2021) proposed a general multi-label
103   classification framework using transformers, demonstrating improved performance over CNN-based
104
      models.
105   While these methods effectively model label correlations, they often rely on shared parameters and
106   may not adequately handle label heterogeneity. Learning all labels jointly can lead to negative
107   transfer, where the optimization for some labels adversely affects others. This issue motivates the
      need for architectures that can disentangle shared and label-specific features.


                                                        2
108
      2.2   M IXTURE - OF -E XPERTS IN D EEP L EARNING
109
110
      Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures have been employed to address the challenges of multi-task
111
      learning and to improve model capacity Jacobs et al. (1991); Shazeer et al. (2017). In MoE models,
112   multiple experts specialize in different aspects of the data, and a gating mechanism determines how
113   to combine their outputs. This allows the model to capture diverse patterns and allocate resources
114   effectively.
115
      In the context of MLC, the Hybrid Sharing Query (HSQ) model Yin et al. (2024) introduced an MoE
116
      architecture with shared and task-specialized experts to handle label correlations and heterogeneity.
117
      HSQ employs a static gating mechanism that combines the outputs of shared experts, which capture
118   common patterns among labels, and task-specific experts, which focus on label-specific features.
119   This approach aims to leverage positive label correlations while mitigating negative transfer.
120
121
      However, the static gating in HSQ does not adapt to the varying label dependencies across different
      images. The fixed combination of experts may not fully exploit the potential of MoE architectures,
122
      especially when the relevance of shared and task-specific experts changes with the input.
123
124
125
      2.3   DYNAMIC ROUTING M ECHANISMS
126
127
      Dynamic routing mechanisms have been proposed to enable models to adaptively select computa-
128
      tional paths based on the input data Sabour et al. (2017). In capsule networks, dynamic routing
129   allows capsules to route their outputs to appropriate higher-level capsules, capturing hierarchical
130   relationships in the data.
131
132
      In natural language processing, dynamic MoE models employ input-dependent gating networks to
      route tokens to experts Riquelme et al. (2021); Du et al. (2022). For instance, the GShard model Lep-
133
      ikhin et al. (2021) uses a dynamic routing algorithm to enable large-scale MoE models for machine
134
      translation, allowing the model to adaptively allocate experts to different tokens.
135
136   These dynamic routing mechanisms have shown promise in capturing input-dependent patterns and
137   improving model efficiency. However, their application to MLC, particularly in handling label cor-
138
      relations and heterogeneity through adaptive expert selection, has not been fully explored.
139
140
141
      2.4   G AP IN E XISTING R ESEARCH
142
143
      Despite the advances in MLC and MoE architectures, existing methods often lack input-dependent
      gating mechanisms that can adaptively handle varying label dependencies on a per-sample basis.
144
      Static gating in models like HSQ may not fully capture the dynamic nature of label correlations,
145
      potentially limiting their ability to exploit positive correlations and mitigate negative transfer.
146
147   Our proposed Dynamic Routing Mixture of Experts (DR-MoE) model addresses this gap by intro-
148   ducing input-dependent dynamic gating networks into the MoE framework for MLC. By allowing
149
      the gating mechanisms to consider the input features, DR-MoE can adaptively select and weight
      experts based on each individual image, enhancing the model’s capacity to handle label correlations
150
      and heterogeneity effectively.

155

157   for multi-label image classification. Our model integrates input-dependent dynamic gating networks
158   into the mixture-of-experts (MoE) framework, allowing adaptive expert selection based on each in-
159   put image. The architecture consists of four main components: (1) a feature extraction backbone, (2)
160   a transformer-based query module with learnable classification tokens, (3) shared and task-specific
161   experts, and (4) dynamic gating networks that adaptively fuse expert outputs. Figure 1 illustrates the
      overall architecture of DR-MoE.

459   5     C ONCLUSION
460
461   The proposed DR-MoE model leverages dynamic gating networks that generate input-dependent
462   gating weights, enabling the model to tailor the combination of shared and task-specific experts to
463   the specific content of each image. This dynamic routing mechanism enhances the model’s ability
464
      to capture varying label dependencies and mitigates negative transfer, leading to improved overall
      and per-label performance in multi-label image classification tasks.
465
466   Despite the performance gains, DR-MoE introduces additional computational overhead due to the
467   dynamic gating networks. The increased model complexity may impact scalability and inference
468   speed, especially in resource-constrained environments or real-time applications. Additionally, the
469   reliance on a larger number of parameters may raise concerns about overfitting, although our exper-
470
      iments did not observe significant overfitting issues.
