009                                                                            A BSTRACT
010
011                  We propose a data-driven approach to analyzing query complexity in Video Ques-
012                  tion Answering (VideoQA). Previous efforts in benchmark design have largely
013                  relied on human expertise to construct challenging samples. In this work, we
014                  experimentally demonstrate that humans struggle to accurately estimate which
015                  questions are hard to answer for machine learning models. Our alternative, auto-
016                  mated approach takes advantage of recent advances in code generation for visual
017
                     question answering. In particular, we use the complexity of generated code as a
                     proxy for the question complexity and demonstrate that it indeed shows a much
018
                     stronger correlation with the models’ performance, compared to human estimates.
019
                     We then present a novel algorithm for estimating question complexity from code.
020                  It identifies fine-grained primitives that correlate with the hardest questions. These
021                  human-interpretable results lead to a number of discoveries about the key sources
022                  of complexity for VideoQA models. Finally, we extend our approach to gener-
023                  ate complex questions for a given set of videos. This allows us to automatically
024                  construct a new benchmark, which is 1.9 times harder for VideoQA methods than
025                  existing manually designed datasets.

041   1      I NTRODUCTION
042
043   Humans can effortlessly reason about activities, whether that reasoning requires understanding space
044   and time, cause and effect, or fine-grained details and high-level context (Decety & Grèzes, 1999;
045   Decety et al., 1997; Wurm & Caramazza, 2022; Aflalo et al., 2020). This versatility allows us to
046   function effectively in dynamic environments, yet it simultaneously complicates our ability to assess
047   what is hard for machines. Consider the two video-question pairs shown in Figure 1 (top). In our
048   study, human subjects overwhelmingly perceive the question on the right as the more complex to
049   answer, but evaluating a variety of state-of-the-art VideoQA models (Yu et al., 2023a; Wang et al.,
050
      2022a; Surı́s et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2021) shows that the question on the left presents a significantly
      greater challenge for them.
051
052   While an expert in the field might think that they would do a better job at this task, the history
053   of VideoQA benchmarks has proven otherwise. Several studies have shown that, despite the best
      efforts of their authors, most datasets are dominated by questions that can be mastered even by


                                                                                    1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      naive, single-frame baselines (Buch et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2021). Although
055   many attempts have been made to address this limitation, they predominantly adopt a top-down
056   approach. In particular, these works start from an expert hypothesis of what is hard and validate this
057   assumption by evaluating models on samples that specifically target the identified skill (Xiao et al.,
058   2021; Mangalam et al., 2023). While this has led to some progress, top performance can still often
059   be achieved by methods that rely almost exclusively on static image cues (Yu et al., 2023a).
060
      In this work, we propose a bottom-up approach instead that discovers human-interpretable insights
061   about the sources of complexity for existing VideoQA models from the data. To this end, we capi-
062   talize on a recent large language model (LLM)-based code generation paradigm (Surı́s et al., 2023;
063   Gupta & Kembhavi, 2023; Subramanian et al., 2023), which produces modular executable pro-
064   grams to answer natural language queries. While this approach has shown promise for zero-shot
065   VideoQA (Surı́s et al., 2023), we are not interested in its task performance per se. Instead, we
066   propose to use its rich and highly structured intermediate representations—programs, as shown in
067   Figure 1 (bottom)—to capture the elusive complexity of the original questions. It is important to
068   note that the videos themselves also contribute to the complexity of the VideoQA samples. How-
069
      ever, we demonstrate that our approach allows us to construct an efficient complexity metric with
      high predictive power from the questions alone.
070
071   To this end, we begin by collecting the visual programs generated by Surı́s et al. (2023) in their recent
072   approach on the validation set of the challenging NextQA benchmark (Xiao et al., 2021), together
073   with predictions of a large collection of diverse VideoQA algorithms. We then calculate several
074   standard structural complexity metrics (McCabe, 1976) for these programs and additionally collect
075
      human judgments about the question complexity for a subset of the dataset. Intriguingly, our analysis
      demonstrates that, despite the programs being imperfect, even the simplest code complexity metrics
076
      correlate better with machine learning models’ performance on NextQA than human estimates (see
077
      Figure 3).
078
079   We then propose CodePlexity – a novel algorithm for estimating question complexity from code that
080   takes into account the content of the program, in addition to its structure (Section 3.2). In partic-
081
      ular, it correlates individual subroutines with model performances, effectively mining for human-
      interpretable patterns that summarize the error modes of each model. In addition to aggregating
082
      the subroutines mined from multiple models into a single, robust quantitative metric, our approach
083
      allows one to study them individually. In Section 3.3, we propose an algorithm to identify patterns
084   in the code that universally correlate with challenging questions, leading to a number of important
085   discoveries. For example, we find that reasoning about the order of events and about fine-grained
086   object details is difficult for all state-of-the-art models tested.
087
      Finally, equipped with this powerful analysis tool, we design an algorithm for automatically gen-
088
      erating challenging questions for any given collection of videos in Section 3.4. In particular, our
089
      approach takes as input a compact description of a video and uses an LLM like ChatGPT (OpenAI,
090   2023a) to generate question candidates first. We then generate visual programs for each question
091   and use our code-based complexity metric to select the hardest subset. We evaluate several zero-shot
092   VideoQA methods on the resulting benchmark and observe a 1.9× gap in performance compared to
093   existing datasets like NextQA (Xiao et al., 2021), confirming the effectiveness of our approach.
094
      To summarize, our contributions are as follows:
095
096         1. We demonstrate that generated code complexity can serve as a robust metric of question
097            complexity in VideoQA and propose a novel approach for automatically quantifying it.
098
            2. We present CodePlexity, a novel approach that identifies the key sources of complexity for
099            existing VideoQA models. For example, we discover that most models fail when the order
100            of the frames has to be taken into account.
101
            3. Using CodePlexity, we automatically construct CodePlex-QA– a novel benchmark that is
102
               1.9 times harder for VideoQA methods than existing, manually designed datasets.

      Large single-stage models. A number of recent works propose to train end-to-end architectures for
109   video-language understanding. Zellers et al. (2021) propose Merlot, a large video-language dataset
110   obtained through ASR captions to train a model with contrastive frame-caption matching, masked
111   language modeling, and re-ordering of scrambled video frames. VIOLET (Fu et al., 2021) is an
112   end-to-end joint video and text architecture that uses the dVAE (Van Den Oord et al., 2017) from
113   DALL-E (Ramesh et al., 2021) to generate tokens for masked video-text pre-training and is tested
114   on video question-answering and text-to-video retrieval. A number of works train masked space-
115   time autoencoders (TimesFormer (Bertasius et al., 2021), VideoMAE (Feichtenhofer et al., 2022))
116   on video sequences, testing on tasks like action recognition. mPLUG-2 (Xu et al., 2023) proposes
117
      to unify image- and video-language tasks in a single architecture with task-specific modules pre-
      trained with masked language modeling, vision-language contrastive learning, and task instruction
118
      representation.
119
120   Frame selection models. Other work leverages single-image vision-language models for video
121   understanding, on the hypothesis that only a subset of video frames (often just a single frame) are
122   relevant to answer a given query. Atemporal Probe (ATP) (Buch et al., 2022) proposes a frame selec-
123
      tor to measure the extent to which single-frame image-centric baselines can address Video Question
      Answering. Later, SeViLA (Yu et al., 2023a) builds on this paradigm by fine-tuning BLIP2 twice:
124
      first to localize relevant keyframes, and then for the question answering module, which answers the
125
      question based on the selected frames, question, and candidate answers.
126
127   Code generation models. A number of recent works tackle the frame selection and question an-
128   swering problem through code generation, leveraging the success of recent text-to-code models
129
      such as Codex (Chen et al., 2021). VisProg (Gupta & Kembhavi, 2023) decomposes natural lan-
      guage queries into compositional programs, using a variety of zero-shot pretrained models to solve
130
      visual question answering problems. ViperGPT (Surı́s et al., 2023) prompts code generation models
131
      with an API that incorporates a variety of vision modules, generating Python code that is executed
132   to answer natural language queries without access to source images or video frames. The approach
133   does not require further training and has demonstrated state-of-the-art results across various com-
134   plex visual tasks. CodeVQA (Subramanian et al., 2023) is a concurrent work to ViperGPT with
135   a smaller API, specialized to single-frame QA. Recursive Visual Programming or RVP Ge et al.
136   (2023) employs a recursive code generation strategy, which systematically breaks down complex
137   problems into manageable subproblems. This allows it to handle intricate question structures with
138   greater flexibility. Precursors to these models (Andreas et al., 2016; Johnson et al., 2017b; Kim
139   et al., 2018; Hu et al., 2017; Yi et al., 2018) would implement code generation modules using neural
140
      networks which were trained either with supervision or via reinforcement learning.
141   Complexity estimation. Prior work has proposed ways to estimate complexity for other settings
142   and modalities. For instance, in NLP, sentence or paragraph length has been used as a proxy for
143   the complexity of a text (Platanios et al., 2019; Spitkovsky et al., 2010; Tay et al., 2019), where
144   a sample with more words is presumed to correlate with harder training data. Related works have
145   explored variations of this metric such as number of conjunctions (Kocmi & Bojar, 2017) (and/or),
146
      number of phrases (Tsvetkov et al., 2016a), or depth of the dependency tree (Tsvetkov et al., 2016b).
      In computer vision, prior work by Wei et al. (2016) has approximated image complexity with the
147
      number of objects present in an image, while others have directly collected human annotations for
148
      image complexity (Tudor Ionescu et al., 2016; Soviany et al., 2020). Finally, a seminal paper by
149   Graves (2016) suggested that, in reasoning tasks, the complexity of said task can be estimated by
150   the number of reasoning steps required to solve it, and introduced a method to estimate said number.
151   A follow-up work by Eyzaguirre & Soto (2020) refined and advanced the initial method, and used it
152   to quantify the complexity of VQA (Visual Question Answering) queries.
153
      Problem complexity from code. Measuring complexity through code has a rich history; Kol-
154
      mogorov defines complexity based on the succinctness of the program that can represent said object
155   (Kolmogorov, 1963; Solomonoff, 2009). However, its incomputability limits its practical application
156   (Zvonkin & Levin, 1970). Software engineering rely on tangible metrics like cyclomatic complexity
157   that measure the number of independent paths in a program (McCabe, 1976), a computable yet less
158   philosophically rich approach.
159

182   3.1        P RELIMINARIES
183
      We study the problem of estimating the complexity of questions in VideoQA. We are given a dataset
184
      consisting of collections of videos, questions, and answers D = {V, Q, A}, along with a set of K
185   models already trained on the task M = {m0 , ..., mK }. Our goal then is to design a function C
186   that allows us to categorize questions qi ∈ Q into groups based on their complexity with respect
187   to M. Crucially, we are interested in a general metric consistent across all models mj ∈ M.
188   Concretely, for any two questions q1 , q2 ∈ Q, together with corresponding videos v1 , v2 ∈ V, if
189   C(q1 ) > C(q2 ), we expect model performance P (m, q, v) to vary accordingly: P (mj , q1 , v1 ) <
190   P (mj , q2 , v2 ) ∀mj ∈ M, indicating that models perform worse on more complex questions.
191   However, directly estimating complexity C from natural language question q is a challenging prob-
192   lem even for humans, as we demonstrate in a Section 4.2. Instead, our key idea, inspired by the
193   notion of Kolmogorov Complexity (KC) (Kolmogorov, 1963), is to utilize the rich and highly-
194   structured intermediate representations - programs, to capture the elusive complexity of the orig-
195   inal natural language queries. Concretely, we capitalize on the recent code generation-based meth-
196   ods (Surı́s et al., 2023; Gupta & Kembhavi, 2023; Subramanian et al., 2023) that operate in a 2-stage
197   fashion: first, given a question q a program generator π from a Large Language Model (LLM) is
198   used to translate it into an executable program z = π(q). An off-the-shelf execution engine like
199
      Python can then be used to produce an answer â = ϕ(v, z). Running such an approach on a dataset
      D results in a set of programs P(D) = {z1 , z2 , ..., zN }.
200
201   Next, in Section 3.2 we propose several techniques for code analysis of increasing intricacy and show
202   how they can be used to build a function for question complexity estimation via code generation
203   C(q) ∝ C(z). Then, in Section 3.3, we demonstrate how analysis of the generated code can help
204   gain insights into the failure modes of VideoQA models. Finally, in Section 3.4 we discuss how
205
      such algorithms can be used to automatically construct challenging VideoQA benchmarks.

207   3.2        C ODE P LEXITY: E STIMATING Q UESTION C OMPLEXITY FROM C ODE
208
      As a first step we review existing software engineering metrics that map code into complexity scores
209
      C(z) → R. In particular, we focus on Lines of Code (LoC) and Cyclomatic Complexity (McCabe,
210   1976). The former simply correlates the number of lines in a program with its complexity C(z) ∝
211   |z|, whereas the latter quantifies the number of linearly-independent paths through the source code
212   and is denoted as C(z) = CC(z). To minimize the impact of spurious factors, we pre-process the
213   code by removing all the comments and empty lines first, and make sure to use the same set of basic
214   primitives in all experiments. Both metrics are indicative of the code’s structural complexity, with
215   higher values suggesting more intricate control flow. However, they do not take the contents of the
      code into consideration, which, as we shown in Section 4.2, limits their predictive power.

      To address this drawback, we propose a new method, CodePlexity, illustrated in Figure 2. CodePlex-
217   ity involves analyzing the components of the generated code that affect question complexity, con-
218   sidering both its structure and semantic content. More specifically, we develop a compiler to parse
219   each question’s code into its basic syntactic elements, creating a Abstract Syntax Tree or AST (Hoe
220   et al., 1986) T = compile(z) with nodes N and edges E. In this model, nodes represent variables,
221   functions, and control structures, while the edges capture the logical and hierarchical relationships
222   between them. The AST framework abstracts the code away from its literal syntax, allowing us to
223   focus on the underlying logic and structure. By generating ASTs for the entire dataset, we obtain
224   a comprehensive set T (D) = {compile(z) | z ∈ P(D)}, thus laying the groundwork for a deeper
225
      analysis of code complexity factors.
226   Next, we mine T for common subroutines (recurring logical patterns or functions) that occur in the
227   code. In ASTs subroutines manifest as subtrees, which we denote as S = (N ′ , E ′ ), where N ′ ⊆ N ,
228   E ′ ⊆ E and ∀(u, v) ∈ E ′ , u ∈ N ′ ∧ v ∈ N ′ . Importantly, not all subtrees constitute valid Python
229   code, since they might fail to comply with Python’s syntax rules. To systematically identify valid
230   subtrees, we define a function G(T ) that yields an unordered set of all valid subtrees of T , denoted as
231
      G(T ) = {S1 , S2 , . . . , Sn }. Considering the

      detail this algorithm in Section 8 of the appendix.

      4     E VALUATING Q UESTION C OMPLEXITY E STIMATION
310
311
      In this section, we compare different approaches to estimating question complexity in VideoQA. To
      this end, we first define a thorough evaluation protocol and detail our experimental setup in Sec-
312
      tion 4.1. We then evaluate how the code-based metrics proposed in this work compare to human
313
      subjects and several simple baselines in predicting the performance of a wide variety of contempo-
314   rary approaches on the popular NextQA benchmark in Section 4.2. We conclude by performing a
315   detailed analysis of the subroutines that show the strongest correlations with challenging questions
316   in Section 4.3.
317
318   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
319
320   Evaluation protocol. Our goal is to compare the predictive power of several approaches for esti-
321   mating question complexity in VideoQA with respect to a variety of machine learning models M on
322   a dataset D. Importantly, some of the metrics we study require training data in the form of questions
                                                                   (j)
323   paired with outcomes of a model mj ∈ M on them (qi , yi ). Thus we split the whole pool of
      models M into the training Mtr and held-out validation Mval sets and report results on the latter.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      To quantitatively compare the effectiveness of different approaches, some of which map a question
325   to a numerical value corresponding to its complexity, whereas others directly return an ordering
326   of the questions, we propose a unifying metric, Performance Extremity Gap (PEG). In particular,
327   we first use numerical complexity estimates to sort questions accordingly. We then measure the
328   disparity in model mj ’s performance P between the easiest and the hardest α% of the questions via:
329
330                                  1      X                                1      X
                   PEG(mj , α) =                        P (mj , q, vq ) −                       P (mj , q, vq )   (9)
331                                 Nα                                      Nα
                                         q∈Qhardest,α                            q∈Qeasiest,α
332
333   Finally, inspired by the mAP metric (Everingham et al., 2010), we average the PEG values over
334   α ∈ (0, 0.5] to obtain the final mPEG score.
335
      Dataset. It is crucial that the dataset used to perform our analysis features as many diverse chal-
336   lenges as possible. We choose the NExT-QA (Xiao et al., 2021) benchmark for its size, variety of
337   human-annotated questions, and its focus on spatio-temporal reasoning in videos over mere visual-
338   fact retrieval (Zhong et al., 2022). In addition, its popularity provides a large pool of models with
339   pre-trained, public checkpoints for our study. That said, our method is dataset-agnostic and can be
340   applied to any other VideoQA benchmark in the future. We perform the evaluation on the validation
341   set of NExT-QA, further splitting the questions into 80% used to train the metrics and the other 20%
342   held out for computing mPEG.
343   Models. We include seven representative methods, chosen for their coverage of existing archi-
344   tectural philosophies, pre-training strategies, and state-of-the-art performance. In particular, we
345   use VIOLET (Fu et al., 2021) and InternVideo (Wang et al., 2022a), which are pre-trained with
346   contrastive visual-language objectives and fine-tuned for VideoQA. We also evaluate SeViLA (Yu
347   et al., 2023a), which is based on the BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023) large-scale visual-language model;
348   we assess both its zero-shot variant (SeViLA-ZS) and a fine-tuned version (SeViLA). Addition-
349   ally, we evaluate HGA (Jiang & Han, 2020), a GNN-based model that reasons with heteroge-
350
      neous graph alignment, representing earlier approaches prior to the prevalence of video large lan-
      guage models (videoLLMs). Furthermore, we include the ViperGPT (Surı́s et al., 2023) code-
351
      generation-based approach, as well the simple but effective ATP baseline (Buch et al., 2022)Fi-
352
      nally, we evaluate the current state-of-the-art model, Tarsier (Wang et al., 2024). The models are
353   split into training and validation sets as follows: Mtr = {VIOLET, SeViLA, ViperGPT, ATP},
354   Mval = {HGA, SeViLA-ZS, InternVideo, Tarsier}.
355
356
      Baselines. In addition to the code-based metrics introduced in Section 3.2, we evaluate several
      baselines that attempt to directly estimate question complexity from the natural language query
357
      itself. In particular, as a learning-free baseline, we follow Tsvetkov et al. (2016b) and correlate the
358
      complexity of a question with the maximum depth of its parsed dependency tree. To more fairly
359   compare to our learnable, code-based metric we fine-tune BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) to predict
360   the probability of model success given the question using exactly the same training data. We also
361   prompt GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023b) to estimate the complexity of a question on a Likert scale (Likert,
362   1932) (details and prompts are provided in Section 7.3 of the appendix).
363
      Finally, we conduct a human study on a subset of 150 questions. To this end, we recruited 30 human
364
      subjects via the Prolific platform (Palan & Schitter, 2018). The subjects were asked to sort three
365   questions at a time according to their perceived relative complexity. Consistency was validated by
366   asking to compare the same questions multiple times throughout one session and the subjects who
367   demonstrated low consistency were excluded from the study. The final sequence order of the entire
368   subset was calculated via pairwise ELO scores (Elo, 1967). More details and an example of the
369   annotation interface are provided in the appendix (Section 7.2).

509
510   To construct CodePlex-QA, we run the generation pipeline described in Section 3.4, obtaining 20791
511   candidate questions (several question candidates are generated for each video). Then we calculate
512   each question’s complexity score using CodePlexity to only retain questions that meet or exceed the
513   minimum complexity threshold as in Equation 8. The resulting datasets consists of 2261 questions.
514   The final manual filtering to ensure the answerability of the generated questions removes only 12%
515   of the candidates, leaving us 1981 samples, all of which are used for evaluation.
516   We then evaluate all the zero-shot baselines from our pool of methods on CodePlex-QA and report
517   their accuracy in Table 2. We additionally report the performance of these models on the popular
518   NExT-QA benchmark for reference. Note that the performance of the random choice baseline is
519   the same for the two benchmarks, so the numbers are directly comparable. Firstly, we observe that
520   that the average success rate of models on our generated questions is significantly lower than on
521   NExT-QA. Specifically, CodePlex-QA is 1.9 times harder than the manually annotated NExT-QA
522   (dataset complexity estimated by taking the average performance of the 5 methods and subtracting
523   random chance). The ranking is consistent across benchmarks, but it is notable that the gap between
524
      InternVideo and VIOLET is smaller on CodePlex-QA, indicating limitations in InternVideo’s video
      understanding despite more visual-language data.

527   CodePlex-QA features a variety of challenging scenarios that require fine-grained temporal reason-
528   ing (e.g., comparing the frequency of different actions), as well as reasoning about objects in videos
529   (e.g., tracking seat cushions). More examples are shown in the supplementary video.

532
533
      We have demonstrated that generated code complexity is a reliable measure of question complexity
534   for VideoQA, proposing a novel metric that outperforms existing metrics on this task. In addition,
535   our approach allows us to identify individual subroutines that correlate with challenging questions
536   for a wide range of models, yielding new insights into the key challenges of VideoQA. Finally, we
537   have shown how our metric can be used to automatically generate a novel benchmark – CodePlex-
538   QA, which is 1.9 times harder for existing models than the popular, manually labeled NExT-QA
539   dataset. As new methods and benchmarks are developed to address the challenges identified in our
      work, our approach can be re-applied, thereby ensuring continued progress in the field.

831   7.1   M ERGING D UPLICATE S UBTREES
832
      To avoid duplicated subtrees and reduce redundancy, we merge subtrees that always co-occur when
833
      one is a descendant of the other. Specifically, a subtree S1 is said to always co-occur with another
834   subtree S2 if every occurrence of S2 in the dataset D is also an occurrence of S1 . In such cases,
835   since S2 is always contained within S1 , we can merge S2 into S1 without losing any unique patterns.
836
837
      Merging these subtrees does not risk missing important patterns because any syntactic or semantic
      information captured by S2 is inherently included in S1 . This is due to the fact that S1 encompasses
838
      all occurrences of S2 , ensuring that the features associated with S2 are preserved within S1 . By elim-
839
      inating redundant subtrees, we streamline the dataset, which can improve computational efficiency
840   without compromising data integrity.
841
842   The merged set of subtrees Smerged (D) is defined as:
843
844                  Smerged (D) = S(D) \ {S2 ∈ S(D) | ∃S1 ∈ S(D) :
                                                                                                           (10)
845                                (∀T ∈ D, ISO(T, S2 ) → ISO(T, S1 )) ∧ (S2 ⊆ S1 )}
846
847   Here, ISO(T, S) indicates that subtree S is isomorphic to a subtree within program T , and S2 ⊆ S1
848   denotes that S2 is contained within S1 .
849   By applying this merging strategy, we ensure that all significant patterns are retained. The one-
850   hot encodings of S1 and S2 are identical across all programs where they appear, so merging them
851   does not alter the representation of the data. This approach maintains the richness of the syntactic
852   structures while optimizing the dataset for analysis.

1149   9.1     R ELATION TO V IDEO C OMPLEXITY
1150
1151
       Our approach to estimating the complexity of VideoQA tasks is grounded in insights from classical
       complexity theory, specifically the Chain Rule for Kolmogorov Complexity. According to this rule,
1152
       the complexity of a composite entity, such as a (video, question) pair, can be expressed as the sum
1153
       of the complexity of one component (e.g., the question) and the conditional complexity of the other
1154   component (e.g., the video conditioned on the question), plus a logarithmic term:
1155
1156
1157
                                  K(x, y) = K(x) + K(y|x) + O(log(K(x, y)))                              (12)
1158
       In cases where there is minimal shared information between the video and the question, the complex-
1159   ity of the pair can be approximated as the sum of their individual complexities, up to this logarithmic
1160   term:
1161
1162
                                   K(x, y) ≈ K(x) + K(y) + O(log(K(x, y)))                               (13)
1163
1164   In this work, we focus on estimating the complexity of the question, which parallels the structure
1165   suggested by the Chain Rule for Kolmogorov Complexity. If we treat the video as x and the question
1166   as y, then the complexity of the (video, question) pair can be thought of in a similar way, where the
1167   complexity of the question K(y) is a key component of the overall task complexity. While we do
1168   not directly compute Kolmogorov complexities, this analogy provides a theoretical motivation for
1169   focusing only on the question complexity. Estimating the complexity of the question is valuable
1170   because it can later be combined with robust methods for assessing the complexity of the video to
1171   achieve a more comprehensive measure of the total task complexity in future work.
1172   The difference between the predicted complexity of the question and the actual model performance
1173   on a given question can be used as a proxy for estimating the video’s complexity. Prior work by Wei
1174   et al. (2016) has approximated image complexity by the number of objects present in an image. We
1175   extend this approach to video complexity, utilizing the VidOR dataset (Shang et al., 2019), which
1176   provides annotations of entities and their relations in videos. Conveniently, VidOR and NextQA
1177   share the same video source, YFCC100M (Thomee et al., 2016), allowing us to align entity counts
1178
       with our models’ performance on NextQA.
1179   Figure 7 compares the difference in the average number of entities (subjects and objects) in videos
1180   where models perform poorly on low complexity tasks (easier questions) versus where they perform
1181   well on high complexity tasks (harder questions). The trend indicates that, for easier questions (low
1182   complexity), videos with more subjects/objects tend to result in poorer model performance, while for
1183   harder questions (high complexity), models perform better when fewer subjects/objects are present
1184
       in the video.
1185   This pattern supports the idea that the complexity of a VideoQA task is additive. Simple questions
1186   become more challenging when accompanied by complex videos, and difficult questions can be
1187   made easier in the presence of simpler videos. This additive relationship between video complex-
       ity and question complexity reinforces the importance of estimating both components. While our

1472   11    B ROADER I MPACTS AND L IMITATIONS
1473
1474   In our study, we utilize four distinct pre-trained models, each with its inherent biases, to identify
1475   challenging questions within an existing dataset. Although they have different pre-training schemes,
1476   these models likely encode similar implicit biases, owning to their training on internet scale data
1477   collections. In particular, three of our selected models, along with our visual descriptors extractor,
1478
       rely on CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) as a visual encoder, meaning they likely replicate the same biases
       including those identified in previous studies (Agarwal et al., 2021).
1479
1480   Furthermore, the dataset we base the majority of our analysis on, NExT-QA, is not fully represen-
1481   tative of real-world diversity and complexity. This limitation in addition to the biases present in
1482   the chosen models can lead to skewed or incomplete analysis. Furthermore, our analysis’ focus on
1483   interplay between the constituent syntactic elements in code may overlooks critical sources of com-
1484
       plexity not apparent in the code structure. These include, for example, differences related to gender
       and ethnicity, which are not explicitly manifested in the code.
1485
1486   Similarly, our own proposed dataset, CodePlex-QA, builds upon the existing datasets MOMA (Luo
1487   et al., 2021; 2022), ActivityNet (Caba Heilbron et al., 2015), and Action Genome (Ji et al., 2020),
1488   and therefore includes the same biases. We urge researchers and practitioners to refer to the relevant
1489   dataset cards. Finally, our selection methodology for filtering videos and questions may inadver-
1490
       tently introduce new biases, or amplify existing ones.
