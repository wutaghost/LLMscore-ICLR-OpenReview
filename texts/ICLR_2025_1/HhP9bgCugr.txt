009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Multimodal alignment aims to learn a shared latent space between different modal
012            inputs to establish connections across modalities. A prime example is Visual Lan-
013            guage Models (VLMs), such as CLIP, which benefit from extensive image-text
014            pre-training and excel in image recognition tasks. These models are emblematic
015            of successful multimodal alignment. Subsequent work has successfully aligned
016
               multimodal data on limited datasets using feature mixing enhancement methods.
               However, these models encounter significant challenges: The presence of am-
017
               biguous samples (either partially matched or completely unmatched) in datasets
018
               with weakly associated, low-quality image-text pairs causes models to become
019            overconfident (in training) and confused (in inference), ultimately reducing per-
020            formance. Current contrastive learning methods, which rely on single positive
021            pairs, exacerbate this issue by encouraging overconfidence when the model en-
022            counters such ambiguous samples. To overcome these challenges, we developed
023            Align-VL, a multimodal alignment enhancement method that operates on the la-
024            tent spaces of pre-trained unimodal encoders. This approach adjusts the matching
025            degree of the data and moderates model overconfidence, promoting more appro-
026            priate and effective alignments. Align-VL incorporates Random Perturbation and
027
               Embedding Smoothing strategies to enhance input feature robustness and reduce
               model overconfidence, improving the model’s ability to manage uncertainty and
028
               generalize to new data. In our experiments, Align-VL outperformed existing state-
029
               of-the-art (SoTA) methods in image-text retrieval tasks, demonstrating its superior
030            effectiveness. Align-VL also offers significant reductions in training time and data
031            requirements compared to methods like CLIP, using substantially fewer GPU days
032            and image-text pairs. Code will be publicly available.

040
      unprecedented potential across various application fields, with some applications even attracting
041
      mainstream attention Girdhar et al. (2023); Radford et al. (2021). The cornerstone of multimodal
042   learning is multimodal alignment, which maps information from multiple modalities, such as text
043   and images, into a unified multimodal vector space Radford et al. (2021); Alayrac et al. (2022).
044   Researchers have made numerous efforts in multimodal alignment, with Visual Language Models
045   (VLMs) being particularly representative. VLMs like CLIP Radford et al. (2021), which undergo ex-
046   tensive image-text pre-training, excel in image recognition tasks, showcasing the potential of VLMs
047   in establishing effective cross-modal connections.
048   The success of multimodal alignment largely relies on large-scale training mechanisms like CLIP,
049   which often require extensive GPU resources and rely on billions of multimodal data pairs Zhai
050   et al. (2022); Radford et al. (2021); Alayrac et al. (2022). However, the high computational costs
051   are impractical for scenarios with limited computing resources or scarce multimodal data. There-
052   fore, designing a cost-effective and efficient multimodal alignment framework is crucial. Inspired
053   by Mixup Zhang et al. (2018), Fusemix introduces an efficient strategy for multimodal alignment
      Vouitsis et al. (2024) by augmenting the latent spaces of pre-trained unimodal encoders, allowing

111   Multimodal alignment achieves cross-modal synchronization not through direct correspondences
112   between modalities, but implicitly via internal model mechanisms that discern latent semantic con-
113   nections within the data. The primary objective of these models is to learn a shared latent space
114   capable of jointly encoding multiple modalities, thereby facilitating effective multimodal alignment
115   Tan & Bansal (2019); Li et al. (2020); Yuan et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022a); Bao et al. (2022);
116   Wang et al. (2022b); Girdhar et al. (2022); Likhosherstov et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023); Wu
117
      et al. (2023). Image-language alignment is a pivotal area of study in multimodal alignment, aiming
      to create universal models capable of interpreting both image and language data. Standard multi-
118
      modal models usually undergo end-to-end training on image-text pairs. Yet, training these large-
119
      scale models from scratch demands substantial computational and data resources, which can restrict
120   scalability. Arandjelovic & Zisserman (2017); Lu et al. (2019); Sun et al. (2019); Su et al. (2020);
121   Chen et al. (2020); Li et al. (2021; 2022).
122
123
      Pioneered by CLIP and ALIGN Radford et al. (2021); Jia et al. (2021), this approach uses a dual-
      encoder architecture, jointly embedding text and images into the same latent space through con-
124
      trastive target training. 3T aligns text and image encoders with the latent space of a pretrained
125
      classifier Kossen et al. (2023). LiT uses a frozen pretrained image classifier as the image encoder
126   and aligns a text encoder to it Zhai et al. (2022). Although these methods have seen success, they
127   mostly train one or two encoders from scratch, relying on expensive cross-GPU gradient computa-
128   tions. ImageBind Girdhar et al. (2023) uses images as anchors to learn a shared latent space across
129   six modalities through contrastive learning, jointly training various modality encoders from scratch.
130   Moreover, the large-scale image-text paired datasets they use, ranging from 400 million to 5 billion
131   pairs, mostly sourced from the internet, are generally not public Vouitsis et al. (2024). In contrast to
132   these works, Fusemix boosts computational and data efficiency through feature augmentation tech-
133   niques, using frozen pre-trained unimodal encoders and fewer multimodal paired data, requiring
134
      fewer resources Vouitsis et al. (2024). However, ambiguous positive samples in weakly associated
      datasets (see Figure 4) lead to model overconfidence and degraded performance, exacerbated by
135
      contrastive learning methods that focus on single positive examples Radford et al. (2021).
136
137   Figure 2 compares three multimodal alignment paradigms: the CLIP Paradigm, which utilizes con-
138   trastive learning to manage data point relationships; the Fusemix Paradigm, which enhances embed-
139   dings by mixing features of image-text pairs; and the Align-VL Paradigm, which reduces overcon-
140
      fidence through perturbations and embedding smoothing, enhancing generalization and robustness
      (CLIP and FuseMix train on 3M data pairs, Align-VL uses about 3.5M pairs to achieve its results).

      The Align-VL specifically reduces model overconfidence and ensures experiments are computation-

      ally and data efficient, requiring only a reasonable amount of GPU resources (see Figure 6).

      ment in the latent space while addressing key considerations such as model overconfidence, and

169   3.1   P RELIMINARIES
170
171   Notation: We define the task of V-L alignment from an alignment perspective. The goal is to learn a
172   shared latent space between visual and textual modal inputs. Formally, given any two data modalities
173   (images X and text Y), our objective is to learn two networks, fX : X → S and fY : Y → S, that
174   embed each modality into a shared latent space S.
175   We take our two encoders as fX = FX ◦ AX and fY = GY ◦ AY . That is, we define
176   FX (F rozen) : X → UX and GY (F rozen) : Y → UY , where UX and UY are intermediate la-
177   tent spaces. We then have AX (Learnable) : UX → S and AY (Learnable) : UY → S, which
178   we hereafter refer to as V-L adapters. Our insight here is to take both FX and GY as pre-trained
179   unimodal encoders which we keep frozen throughout, and treat our V-L adapters AX and AY as
180   learnable heads for multimodal alignment. Therefore, we can define our learning objective using the
181   InfoNCE loss function as follows:
182
                                            exp(sim(fX (Xi ), fY (Yi ))/τ )
183                          LNCE = − log PN                                                            (1)
184                                        j=1 exp(sim(fX (Xi ), fY (Yj ))/τ )

186   where sim(·, ·) denotes the similarity function, τ is the temperature parameter, and N is the number
187   of all samples. Where Xi and Yi are positive pairs.
188   Motivation: Scaling up multimodal models boosts performance but incurs substantial computa-
189   tional costs, especially when jointly training networks FX and GY , which rapidly increases mem-
190   ory and compute demands. Besides, acquiring high-quality paired data is costly, while high-quality
191   unimodal data is more readily available and can provide rich supervision through self-supervised
192   learning. To address these challenges, we aim to design a computationally efficient, V-L alignment
193   adapters AX and AY ,that reduces reliance on paired data by leveraging unimodal signals, while
194   allowing independent updates to visual and textual components with minimal retraining.
195   Additionally, the existing image-text matching datasets, mostly sourced from the internet (Figure 4
196   Upper Figure), generally have low pairing quality. In contrastive learning that emphasize the quality
197   of positive sample pairings, ambiguously matched positive pairs can lead to excessive model con-
198   fidence, despite the pairs not being correctly matched. Therefore, there is a need for a method to
199   adjust the matching degree of positive pairs and mitigate model overconfidence in these samples.

      3.2   OVERVIEW
202
203
      We introduce Align-VL, a multimodal alignment method that adjusts data matching by simulating
204   input uncertainty and moderating model overconfidence in ambiguous positive samples, operating
205   on the latent spaces UX and UY derived from pre-trained unimodal encoders. 1) The method initially
206   employs unimodal encoders to encode V-L modalities into intermediate latent spaces. 2) Subse-
207   quently, it utilizes enhanced features based on Fusemix in the Align-VL latent spaces, incorporating
208   Random Perturbation to adjust data matching by simulating input uncertainty. 3) After training
209   through VL-Adapters, Embedding Smoothing is applied, aiming to smooth the model’s prediction
210   of output distributions, reduce overconfidence in positive pairs, and enhance the smoothness of pre-
211   dictions on uncertain samples. 4) Finally, the smoothed embeddings are used in contrastive learning
212
      training, facilitating the learning of two networks, AX and AY , as shown in Figure 3.
213   Align-VL utilizes the existing semantics encoded by unimodal encoders, reducing the reliance on
214   extensive real paired data and simplifying computational requirements. It effectively mitigates the
215   issue of model overconfidence, making the model more ”modest” and robust, thereby optimizing
      multimodal alignment, learning efficiency, and generalization capabilities.

222   noise vectors nv ∼ N (0, σ 2 ) and nt ∼ N (0, σ 2 ), resulting in perturbed embeddings. The perturbed
223   embeddings are defined as:

      5    C ONCLUSION
522
523
      In this work, we propose a multimodal alignment framework Align-VL that promotes modesty in
524   model predictions while being computationally and data-efficient. Align-VL is a straightforward yet
525   effective method that reduces model overconfidence and enhances the intrinsic connections of paired
526   data in the latent space. It effectively leverages guidance from pretrained visual and text unimodal
527   encoders. Notably, Align-VL excels on datasets with lower data quality (image-text match level)
528   and also enhances performance on datasets with higher match levels. Validated across multiple
529   datasets, Align-VL has consistently demonstrated its robust capability to align V-L models better.
530   For future developments, Align-VL could incorporate data quality assessments to dynamically adjust
531   model confidence, applying stricter constraints on lower quality data and more lenient ones on higher
532
      quality data to effectively manage model overconfidence.
533
      L IMITATIONS
534
535   Although Align-VL achieved performance improvements across all datasets and their combinations,
536   it is notable that the gains are more significant for datasets of poorer quality, while the enhancements
537   are more modest for relatively better datasets. We are unable to test Align-VL on larger datasets like
538   the 400M pairs used in CLIP, as it is not publicly available. Consequently, it is difficult to ascertain
539   Align-VL’s performance benefits for extremely large-scale datasets. Besides, it remains unclear how
      much Align-VL benefits datasets of very high quality, where image-text pairs are perfectly matched.

901   A.3    T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS FOR R ANDOM P ERTURBATION
902
903
      Why Choose Gaussian noise?: This paper introduces Gaussian noise as a perturbation in Align-
      VL for several reasons: 1). Well-Defined Mathematical Properties: Gaussian distribution exhibits
904
      continuous and smooth probability density functions across the real number line, facilitating theo-
905
      retical analysis and calculations. 2). Zero-Mean Symmetry: By choosing a Gaussian distribution
906   with a mean of zero, the added noise is symmetrically balanced around zero, introducing no sys-
907   tematic bias and only increasing the variance, thereby preserving the expected value of embeddings.
908   3). Adjustable Perturbation Intensity: The standard deviation of the Gaussian distribution can be
909   precisely controlled, allowing for careful calibration of noise intensity. This flexibility is crucial for
910   introducing an appropriate level of uncertainty to enhance model robustness. 4). Alignment with
911   Natural Phenomena: According to the Central Limit Theorem, the sum of many independent random
912   variables tends toward a Gaussian distribution. Thus, Gaussian noise effectively simulates random
913   disturbances or measurement errors prevalent in natural and engineering contexts. 5). Facilitation
914
      of Optimization and Training: In deep learning, incorporating Gaussian noise helps to smooth the
      loss function landscape, avoiding local minima and promoting more effective training processes.
915
916   In summary, choosing Gaussian noise as the source of perturbation provides theoretical soundness
917   and practical convenience, aiding in the development of more robust feature representations, pre-
      venting overfitting, and enhancing the generalization capabilities of models.
