009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The rapid evolution of Large Language Models (LLMs) has outpaced the develop-
012            ment of model evaluation, highlighting the need for continuous curation of new,
013            challenging benchmarks. However, manual curation of high-quality, human-aligned
014            benchmarks is expensive and time-consuming. To address this, we introduce Bench-
015            O-Matic, an automated pipeline that leverages LLMs to curate high-quality, open-
016            ended prompts from large, crowd-sourced datasets, enabling continuous benchmark
017            updates without human in the loop. We apply Bench-O-Matic to datasets such as
018            Chatbot Arena and WildChat-1M, extracting challenging prompts and utilizing
019
               LLM-as-a-Judge for automatic model evaluation. To validate benchmark quality,
               we propose new metrics to measure a benchmark’s alignment with human pref-
020
               erences and ability to separate models. We release Eval-O-Matic, a benchmark
021
               consisting 500 challenging prompts curated by Bench-O-Matic. Eval-O-Matic
022            provides 3x higher separation of model performances compared to MT-Bench and
023            achieves 98.6% correlation with human preference rankings, all at a cost of $20.
024            Our work sets a new framework for the scalable curation of automated benchmarks
025            from extensive data.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The proliferation of Large Language Models (LLMs) has spurred advancements as models expand
031   their capabilities by training on increasingly vast and diverse datasets. Traditional static bench-
032   marks (Wang et al., 2019; Rajpurkar et al., 2016; Bowman et al., 2015; Dolan & Brockett, 2005;
033   Bos & Markert, 2005; Hendrycks et al., 2021a) are quickly becoming saturated and struggle to
034   differentiate state-of-the-art models.
035
      To address these limitations, recent benchmarks like GPQA (Rein et al., 2023) source high-quality
036   and challenging prompts from domain experts. Although these efforts have produced challenging
037   evaluation sets, they come at a steep price—GPQA, for instance, cost over $120,000 to curate its 500
038   multiple-choice questions (Rein, 2024). The reliance on manual curation makes such benchmarks
039   difficult to produce. Moreover, their static nature is susceptible to test-set leakage and overfitting
040   as models are trained on similar datasets. This necessitates the continuous development of new
041   benchmarks, exacerbating the cost and labor of manual curation. Further, many of these benchmarks
042   rely on close-ended tasks that fail to capture the open-ended nature of real-world interactions,
043   undermining their cost-effectiveness for evaluating alignment to user preference.
044   An alternative approach without manual curation involves crowdsourcing prompts through live
045   evaluation platforms such as Chatbot Arena (Chiang et al., 2024). These platforms test models
046   against a continuous stream of fresh, open-ended queries and user feedback. However, real-time
047   human evaluation is both expensive and time-consuming, rendering these platforms infeasible for
048   frequent evaluations by model developers. Moreover, while the crowd-sourced prompts represent real-
049   world and open-ended tasks, their quality varies in difficulty and cannot be converted to challenging
050
      benchmarks without careful data filtering.
051   In light of these open challenges, there is a pressing need for an automated pipeline which can curate
052   high-quality prompts dynamically at scale. In this paper, we introduce Bench-O-Matic, an automated
053   benchmark curation system designed to address these gaps. Bench-O-Matic leverages LLMs to curate,
      filter, and validate prompts based on seven indicators of high-quality prompts, such as specificity and

093
      LLM benchmarks. We briefly review widely used LLM benchmarks. Most existing benchmarks are
094
      static and ground-truth-based (e.g., multi-choice question answering). They cover a wide range of
095
      domains, including math, science, coding, and reasoning. Common ones include MMLU (Hendrycks
096   et al., 2021a), MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021b), GSM-8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), HumanEval (Chen
097   et al., 2021), DROP (Dua et al., 2019), BigBench (Srivastava et al., 2023), HellaSwag (Zellers
098   et al., 2019), AGIEval (Zhong et al., 2023), GPQA (Rein et al., 2023), as well as comprehensive
099   collection like HELM (Liang et al., 2022). Many have considered task-based evaluation such as
100   IFEval (Zhou et al., 2023), SWE-Bench (Jimenez et al., 2024), BigCodeBench (Zhuo et al., 2024) or
101   AgentBench (Liu et al., 2023). As LLMs become widely adopted in open-ended scenarios involving
102   interaction with humans (e.g., chatbot), many have considered human evaluation using domain
103   experts or crowd raters such as Amazon Mechanical Turk (Karpinska et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023)
104
      to examine models’ response quality. As an alternative to human labeling, previous work has shown
      that LLM-as-a-judge can be effective human preference proxies (e.g., AlpacaFarm (Dubois et al.,
105
      2023), MT-bench (Zheng et al., 2023b), AlpacaEval (Li et al., 2023), WildBench (Lin et al., 2024)).
106
107   Benchmark leakage. A fundamental limitation of static benchmarks is the potential risk of test set
      leakage (i.e., contamination). Existing works (Carlini et al., 2021; Sainz et al., 2023; Yang et al.,

119   3   H OW DO YOU MEASURE BENCHMARKS ?
120
121   We outline two key properties that the benchmark aiming to approximate human preference should
122
      possess to provide meaningful comparisons between models:
123
      1. Separability: the benchmark should separate models with high confidence.
124
      2. Alignment with Human Preference: the benchmark should agree with human preference.
125
126   While previous works have focused on alignment, separability is also a crucial consideration when
127   comparing models of similar quality (e.g., different checkpoints from the same training run). However,
128   achieving high-confidence separability is challenging due to limitations in prompt design and inherent
129   variances in LLM evaluations. Overly simplistic prompts fail to distinguish between models, while
130   the randomness in human and LLM judgments leads to inconsistent predictions. As a result, it is often
131   difficult to confidently determine if a model’s apparent performance reflects a genuine difference
132   in capability or merely noisy observations, highlighting a need for methods to verify whether a
133
      benchmark can reliably separate similar models.
134   Statistical measures like Pearson (Pearson, 1895) and Spearman Correlations (Spearman, 1961),
135   commonly used in benchmarks such as AlpacaEval (Li et al., 2023) to measure correlation to human
136   preference ranking, may fail to adequately address model separability and ranking instability. In
137   addition, these measures only provide a coarse signal of ranking correlation without quantifying the
138   magnitude of performance differences between model pairs.
139   To address these shortcomings, we develop three novel metrics: Separability with Confidence,
140   Agreement with Confidence, and Pair Rank Brier Score.
141
      Separability with Confidence quantifies the benchmark’s confidence by measuring its consistency
142
      in predicting the winner of a model pair across random seeds through bootstrapping. This is done
143   by calculating the percentage of model pairs that have non-overlapping confidence intervals of
144   their benchmark scores. A higher percentage indicates that the benchmark is more confident in
145   distinguishing between the performance of different models, as the confidence intervals of their scores
146   do not overlap.
147
      Agreement with Confidence Interval measures how well benchmarks A and B confidently distin-
148
      guish between two models with the same ordering. Given models π1 , π2 , we assign scores based
149   on:
150
151   1. If both benchmarks confidently separate π1 , π2 , a score of 1 is assigned if their preference agree,
152      and -1 if they disagree.
153   2. If either A or B cannot separate π1 , π2 with confidence, we assign a score of 0.
154
155   The final agreement score is the average across all unique model pairs. A score of 1 implies perfect
156   agreement with full confidence, while a score of -1 indicates complete disagreement.
157   Pair Rank Brier Score further assesses an LLM benchmark’s capability to predict the ranking of a
158   pair of competing models by rewarding confidence in correct predictions while penalizing confidence
159   when incorrect. Consider two models π1 > π2 with disparate quality. Although two benchmarks
160   A and B predict the same ranking π1 > π2 , they predict P (π1 > π2 ) as .60 and .90, respectively
161   (undetectable by Spearman correlation). These benchmarks would result in very different Brier scores,
      reflecting their ability to quantify the magnitude of performance difference between the models.

196                        4.1   B ENCH -O-M ATIC
197
198
199                        The core idea behind how Bench-O-Matic extract high-quality user queries from vast datasets is
200
                           simple: each prompt is evaluated using a quality score, and prompts with high scores are sampled
                           evenly across diverse topics. Figure 2 illustrates our data creation pipeline.
201
202                        To identify high-quality prompts, we define seven key qualities that capture the skills necessary to
203                        effectively address a query, such as specificity, domain expertise, and creativity (shown in Figure 2).
204                        An LLM-based annotator automatically scores each prompt by assessing how many of these qualities
205                        are present, producing a “quality score”. Detailed instructions for these quality assessments are
206
                           provided in Section C.
207                        To ensure our filtered prompts span a wide range of tasks, we leverage a topic modeling approach
208                        using BERTopic. We first encode each prompt using OpenAI’s embedding model, text-embedding-3-
209                        small (OpenAI, 2024a), reduce dimensions with UMAP, and apply a hierarchical-based clustering
210                        algorithm (HDBSCAN). This process generates distinct topic clusters. Each topic is then summarized
211                        and named using an LLM.
212                        Since some topic clusters predominantly contain trivial or poorly defined prompts (e.g., "hi"), we
213                        retain only the clusters with high average quality scores and sample prompts evenly across these
214                        selected clusters. The resulting dataset consists of mostly well-defined, technical problem-solving
215                        queries as required in the above key criteria. Dataset statistics and further details on our filtering and
                           sampling strategy are provided in the following section.

234   4.2    E VAL -O-M ATIC
235
236
      We utilize the Bench-O-Matic pipeline to curate 500 challenging benchmark prompts for Eval-O-
      Matic. Our process begins with an initial pool of 200,000 prompts sourced from Chatbot Arena. We
237
      filter out duplicates, multi-turn conversations, and non-English content. Next, we apply hierarchical
238
      topic modeling, clustering the prompts into 4,000 distinct topics spanning a diverse range of domains
239
240   Then we use GPT-4-Turbo (OpenAI, 2023b) as a judge to assign a “quality score” to each prompt
241   and remove any prompts. Prompts with score less than 6 and topic clusters with mean score less
242
      than 5 are discarded, ensuring only the highest quality prompts are retained. The resulting dataset
      contains over 500 high quality clusters. To construct a 500-prompt benchmark, we sample 2 prompts
243
      each from 250 randomly selected clusters. We also ensure the final dataset is free from personally
244
      identifiable information or offensive content.
245
246   To validate qualities assigned by GPT-4-Turbo, we construct “ground truth” labels for 200 sampled
247   queries by collecting majority votes from GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024b), Claude-3-Opus, and Gemini-1.5-
248
      Pro (Reid et al., 2024). GPT-4-Turbo achieves 85.6% agreement with these labels, demonstrating its
      reliability as an annotator.
249
250   We also applied Bench-O-Matic on 150,000 queries from WildChat-1M (Zhao et al., 2024), which
251   consists of diverse and real-world conversations between users and ChatGPT. Bench-O-Matic identi-
252   fied 185 high quality clusters with 4,500+ prompts. We then randomly sample 2 prompts from each
253   of the highest-quality 125 clusters to create a new benchmark, Wild-O-Matic, which we show to have
254
      similar improvement in benchmark quality in section 6.4.

460   6.5    M ITIGATING S TYLISTIC B IASES IN LLM-BASED E VALUATION
461
462   LLM-as-a-Judge based evaluation is known to suffer from various biases, such as favoring longer
463   responses (Zheng et al., 2023b; Dubois et al., 2024). AlpacaEval 2.0 Length Control (Dubois et al.,
464   2024) proposes an regression based approach to control length bias in LLM-based evaluation. Chatbot
465   Arena also released a style controlled leaderboard (Li et al., 2024), which attempts to decouple
466   substance from stylistic preferences, including answer length and markdown usage. Following their
467   approaches, we modify how Eval-O-Matic computes the model scores by accounting for the stylistic
468   differences between two answers as additional features to the existing Bradley-Terry model.
469   We propose controlling for a similar set of stylistic elements used to control human preference on
470   Chatbot Arena for LLM-based evaluation: answer token length, density of markdown headers,
471   markdown bold elements, and markdown lists. Technical details on how to extend the Bradley-
472   Terry model for controlling any given style can be found in Appendix A.2.
473
      We apply style control to Chatbot Arena battles and compare the resulting model preference ranking
474   to style controlled Eval-O-Matic, aiming to answer the question: How well aligned is Eval-O-Matic
475   to human preference when both human preference and LLM judgment are decoupled from stylistic
476   differences? In Table 3, we show that style controlled Eval-O-Matic achieves 98.6% agreement and
477   correlation to style controlled human preference ranking, suggesting Eval-O-Matic assessment of
478   model strength separated from style is still highly aligned to humans.
479
      Additionally, we conducted an experiment trying to increase model score on Eval-O-Matic by
480   instructing GPT-3.5-Turbo, Llama-3.1-70b-instruct, and Gemini-1.5-Flash to increase the verbosity
481   and usage of markdown elements in their response and present our results in Table 5. While increasing
482   “detailedness” does increase model performances on Eval-O-Matic when no modifications is applied
483   to GPT-4-Turbo as judge, applying style control is effective at neutralizing this advantage. Our
484   results shows that style controlled model scores cannot be gamed via manipulating response length or
485   markdown usage on Eval-O-Matic. We also observe a reduction in correlation between model score
      and answer length on Eval-O-Matic. Full results can be found in Appendix Table 12.

      6.6   M ITIGATING S ELF -B IASES IN LLM-BASED E VALUATION
487
488   LLM-as-a-Judge evaluations are also known to exhibit self-bias. While such biases should manifest
489   as lower alignment with human preferences in our proposed metrics, we conduct a focused analysis
490   to further understand and address this issue. Since Eval-O-Matic uses GPT-4-Turbo as the default
491   judge, we evaluate whether it favors OpenAI models over Anthropic models. Results in Appendix
492   Table 10 indicate that GPT models receive slightly higher average rankings than human preference,
493   while Claude models rank lower.
494   To reduce this bias, we propose Ensemble-as-Judges, which aggregates judgments from multiple
495   models. The ensemble judges (GPT-4-Turbo and Gemini-1.5-Pro) achieves overall higher separability
496   and alignment with human rankings, as shown in Table 4. Additionally, we also observe that
497   combining GPT-4-Turbo and Gemini-1.5-Pro reduces self-biases. Results can be found in Appendix
498   Table 10. We believe further research into ensemble methods can refine these results and leave this
499   for future exploration.

501   7     L IMITATIONS
502
503   While our data sources are drawn from diverse distributions, biases may still exist in our pipeline.
504   For instance, the seven defined qualities may not fully capture the range of possible attributes,
505   potentially skewing towards prompts in technical domains. Furthermore, Eval-O-Matic currently
506   lacks evaluation for multi-turn and non-English interactions due to the limited availability of multi-
507   turn data in crowdsourced datasets and the primary language proficiency of the authors.
508   To address these limitations, future work will focus on expanding Bench-O-Matic to incorporate
509   multi-turn and multilingual data curation. We also aim to refine our prompt quality definitions,
510   creating a more systematic approach for generating benchmarks that reflect a broader, more inclusive
511   range of scenarios while maintaining high separability and alignment with human judgment. We also
512   plan to explore more advanced version of Ensemble-as-Judges to further enhance our LLM-based
513   evaluation approach.
514
515   8     C ONCLUSIONS
516
517   We introduced Bench-O-Matic, a data curation pipeline that transforms crowdsourced data into high-
518   quality benchmarks by seven key qualities. This pipeline enables building challenging and evolving
519   benchmarks which is crucial for evaluating today’s advanced language models. Our evaluation
520   metrics, including separability and agreement with confidence, provide a comprehensive assessment
521   of benchmarks. We show the resulting benchmark, Eval-O-Matic, significantly improves separability
522   and alignment with human preferences over existing benchmarks, achieving 98.6% agreement with
523
      Chatbot Arena rankings at only $20 per evaluation. We expect Eval-O-Matic to be useful for LLM
      developers to evaluate their models with confidence and Bench-O-Matic to be a valuable tool for
524
      developers seeking to extract high-quality benchmark from vast amounts of data with minimal human
525
      effort.

813
814   Bootstrapping is a well-established statistical technique for estimating the distribution of an estimator
815   by sampling with replacement from the original dataset. This approach has become increasingly
816   popular for constructing confidence intervals in LLM leaderboards, such as Chatbot Arena (Chiang
817   et al., 2024). In our proposed evaluation metrics in section 3, such as Separability and Agreement
818   with Confidence Interval, a reliable confidence interval estimation is essential for assessing the
819
      performance stability of different models on a given benchmark. Moreover, for metrics like the
      Pairwise Rank Brier Score, estimating the probability distribution of rank-based model performance
820
      is critical. Therefore, applying bootstrapping to the given benchmark provides a straightforward and
821
      robust solution for these tasks.
822
823   Consider a benchmark consisting of a dataset D = {x1 , x2 , . . . , x|D| } and a scoring function f that
824   measures the performance of n models π1 , π2 , . . . , πn on this dataset. Let D∗ denote a bootstrap
825
      sample of D, and let f (πi , D∗ ) denote the bootstrapped performance score for model πi using the
      dataset D∗ . For simplicity, we use f ∗ (πi ) to denote f (πi , D∗ ).
826
827   To use Brier Score (Brier, 1950) for measuring the accuracy of the given benchmark’s probabilistic
828   predictions on model performances, we need to compute the forecasted probability that model πi
829   performs lower than πj on the ground truth measurement for every model pair.
830
                                               P̂ (f ∗ (πi ) < f ∗ (πj ))                                   (1)
831
                                  ∗            ∗
832   The bootstrapped scores f (πi ) and f (πj ) follow an empirical distribution that can be approxi-
833   mated using the Central Limit Theorem (CLT). In most cases, the distribution of f ∗ (πi ) converges
834   asymptotically to a normal distribution, which we also observed in our experiments. Formally,
835   f ∗ (πi ) ∼ N (µi , σi2 ), where µi and σi2 are the bootstrapped mean and variance, respectively. When
836   this normality assumption does not hold, P̂ (f ∗ (πi ) < f ∗ (πj )) can still be estimated from the
837   empirical distribution of the bootstrapped scores.

      A.2    S TYLE C ONTROL IN M ODEL E VALUATION
865
866   To mitigate the potential confounding effects of response style on model evaluation, we implemented
867   an enhanced Bradley-Terry regression framework. This method, inspired by recent LLM evaluation
868   technique (Dubois et al., 2024), controls the influence of answer length on judges’ preferences.
869   Recently, Chatbot Arena implemented style control (Li et al., 2024) to decouple substance from style
870   in their leaderboard. This approach incorporates style-related features, such as answer length, into
871   the regression model, enabling a distinction between a model’s intrinsic capabilities and the influence
872
      of these potential confounders like answer style. In essence, style control answers the question: What
      would the preference be if everyone has the same style? This distinction is crucial for a more accurate
873
      assessment of model performance without biases.
874
875   We extend the standard Bradley-Terry model by introducing additional style features. Let n denote
876   the number of pairwise comparison battles and M the number of models. For each battle i ∈ [n], we
877   define:
878
             • Xi ∈ RM : Xi,m = 1 if model m is on the presented first to the judge, Xi,m = −1 if
879
               presented last, and 0 otherwise.
880
881          • Yi ∈ 0, 1: The outcome, where 1 indicates the first model won.
882          • Zi ∈ RS : A vector of S style features for the comparison.
883
884   The traditional Bradley-Terry model estimates model strengths β ∈ RM through logistic regression:
885                                               n

890                                               n
