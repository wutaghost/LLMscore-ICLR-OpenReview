009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011             Large language models (LLMs) have shown remarkable abilities in diverse nat-
012             ural language processing (NLP) tasks. The LLMs generally undergo supervised
013             fine-tuning (SFT) followed by preference alignment to be usable in downstream
014             applications. However, this sequential training pipeline leads to alignment tax that
015             degrades the LLM performance.
016             This paper introduces PAFT, a new PArallel training paradigm for effective LLM
017
                Fine-Tuning, which independently performs SFT and preference alignment (e.g.,
                DPO and ORPO, etc.) with the same pre-trained model on respective datasets.
018
                The model produced by SFT and the model from preference alignment are then
019
                merged into a final model by parameter fusing for use in downstream applications.
020             This work reveals important findings that preference alignment like DPO naturally
021             results in a sparse model while SFT leads to a natural dense model which needs
022             to be sparsified for effective model merging. This paper introduces an effective
023             interference resolution which reduces the redundancy by sparsifying the delta
024             parameters. The LLM resulted from the new training paradigm achieved Rank #1
025             on the HuggingFace Open LLM Leaderboard1 . Comprehensive evaluation shows
026             the effectiveness of the parallel training paradigm.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In recent years, large language models (LLMs) have emerged as the standard approach to addressing
031   natural language processing (NLP) tasks. The typical way of building an LLM for downstream
032   applications generally follows a sequential training pipeline consisting of two phases: 1. Supervised
033   Fine-tuning (SFT), where the pre-trained LLM is fine-tuned with the language modelling loss on
034   demonstrations of the desired behaviour. 2. Alignment with human preference, where the model
035   produced by the SFT phase is further fine-tuned with an alignment algorithm like Reinforcement
036   Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) or Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), etc. While
037   this sequential pipeline has been used to seemingly great success, how the SFT and the preference
038
      alignment work better with each other is underexplored.
039   Recent studies OpenAI (2023); Askell et al. (2021); Song et al. (2023) have found that the preference
040   alignment phase can cause the LLM to forget the diverse capabilities that it has acquired from
041   earlier phases, despite aligning the LLM with human expectation. This phenomenon, also known
042   as the alignment tax in the literature Ouyang et al. (2022), has accumulated substantial attention
043   from both academia and industry. The alignment tax inherently results from catastrophic forgetting
044
      present in the staged training. To reduce catastrophic forgetting and thus alignment tax, this paper
      introduces a new parallel training paradigm for LLM fine-tuning, named PAFT, which independently
045
      performs SFT and preference alignment with the same pre-trained model on respective datasets,
046
      instead of sequentially conducting SFT followed by preference alignment. The model from SFT and
047   the model from preference alignment are then merged into a final model by parameter fusing for use
048   in downstream applications.
049
050
      As discovered by prior work Yadav et al. (2023); Yu et al. (2023), direct model merging causes the
      parameter values to interfere across models, thereby harming the performance of the final model.

074   The interference, which reduces parameter magnitudes in the merged model and eliminates subtle
075   distinctions among values, can attribute to the redundant delta parameters, i.e., the differences in
076   values between fine-tuned and pre-trained parameters, resulted from fine-tuning. Previous studies on
077   model pruning Hoefler et al. (2021); Thimm & Fiesler (1995) have shown that during fine-tuning,
078
      many model parameters can change over the course of fine-tuning but only have a small impact on
      performance. However, when merging a parameter that is influential for one model but redundant
079
      (i.e. not influential) for other models, the influential value may be obscured by the redundant
080
      values, lowering the overall model performance. This work reveals the dense properties of the
081   delta parameters resulted from SFT. To mitigate the dense property of SFT, we propose an effective
082   interference resolution which reduces the redundancy by sparsifying the delta parameters by adding a
083   L1-norm penalty to the original SFT loss function. The existing findings indicate that the inclusion
084   of the L1 term enhances the sparsity of the SFT. This method of implicitly inducing sparsity has
085   been evaluated against a technique that introduces sparsity explicitly, i.e., DARE Yu et al. (2023),
086   demonstrating the advantages of employing the L1-norm on LLM’s performances in downstream
087   tasks.
088   Finally, the sparse delta parameters from SFT and preference alignment are merged into a sin-
089   gle stronger model. Different merging methods are assessed, and TIES and Task Arithmetic are
090   shown to be the best model merging methods, depending on base models. The method of Parallel
091   SFTsparse +DPO merged through TIES based on Mistral-7B sets a new benchmark for 7B models, i.e.,
092   0.6524 on average over the six tasks in HuggingFace Open LLM Leaderboard. Notably, Parallel
093   SFTsparse +DPO consistently outperforms Parallel SFT+DPO across all model merging methods,
094   showing the effectiveness and robustness of the PAFT training paradigm.
095   The contributions of this paper are threefold:
096
097         1. Evidence is presented that parallel training of SFT and preference alignment outperforms
098            sequential training, effectively reducing the alignment tax.
099
100         2. The significance of sparse model integration is highlighted as a mean to prevent model
101
               conflict while preserving the full capability of each model. We demonstrate the superiority
               of the L1-norm over DARE as a more effective and higher-quality method for promoting
102
               sparsity in model training across various model merging techniques.
103
104         3. We conduct comprehensive evaluation of PAFT on well-known public benchmarks including
105            Open LLM Leaderboard and AlpacaEval. The PAFT-ed 7B model achieved Rank #1 in the
106            7B/8B model category on the Open LLM Leaderboard, and the PAFT-ed 70B model topped
107            the Leaderboard globally.

      2.1   P ROBLEM S ETTING
112
      Given a pre-trained LLM, such as Mistral and Llama, we aim to optimize the model for a wide range
113
      of downstream tasks by fine-tuning it either fully or with parameter-efficient tuning such as LoRA
114
      Hu et al. (2022), using SFT and preference alignment. Throughout this paper, θ denotes the trainable
115   parameters; θpre denotes the parameters of the pre-trained model; θsft denotes the parameters of the
116   model fine-tuned with SFT; θxpo denotes the parameters of the model fine-tuned with preference
117   alignment, such as PPO Schulman et al. (2017); Ziegler et al. (2020), DPO Rafailov et al. (2023) and
118   ORPO Hong et al. (2024), etc.; δsft = θsft − θpre denotes the delta parameters between the SFT-ed
119   model and the pre-trained model; and δxpo = θxpo − θpre denotes the delta parameters between the
120   preference-aligned model and the pre-trained model.
121
122
      2.2   PARALLEL T RAINING
123
124   SFT and preference alignment are two distinct methodologies designed to enhance the capabilities of
125   pre-trained LLMs for specific applications. SFT focuses on boosting the performance of LLMs on
126   downstream tasks by fine-tuning them with datasets that closely resemble the target task. This process
127   tailors the model’s responses to be more accurate and relevant for a specific use-case. In contrast,
128   preference alignment, such as RLHF, DPO and ORPO, etc., is a methodology that refines a model’s
129   outputs based on human preferences. It generally fine-tunes the model on pairs of responses to an
130   input query, one of which is preferred over the other one. Preference alignment uses such feedback
131   signal to guide the model towards generating outputs that align with human expectation and ethical
132
      standards. This approach is particularly valuable for addressing the ethical considerations that arise
      when deploying LLMs in real-world scenarios.
133
134   Nowadays, researchers have applied SFT to enhance the performance of LLMs on targeted tasks, and
135   then employed preference alignment to further align the models with human preferences. However,
136   this sequential application of SFT followed by preference alignment has often led to a compromise in
137   task-specific performance - a phenomenon referred to as the alignment tax. This occurs because the
138
      distinct objectives of SFT and preference alignment can sometimes be at odds, with the alignment
      process potentially undoing some of the task-specific optimizations achieved through SFT.
139
140   We address the challenge of the alignment tax by a novel approach that involves SFT and preference
141   alignment concurrently using adapter training, such as LoRA Hu et al. (2022). This method takes full
142   advantages and strengths of both SFT and preference alignment without sacrificing performance in
143   either one, i.e., ensuring that the resulting model maintains high performance in downstream tasks
144
      while also being aligned with human preferences, thus overcoming the limitations associated with
      the alignment tax. During the training process specifically, based on the same pre-trained model
145
      θpre , the two separate adapter parameters, denoted as δsft and δxpo , are learned in parallel from
146
      downstream ground truth and human preferences, respectively. The proposed PAFT seeks to merge
147   the δsft and δxpo in an effective way of avoiding feature interference. Figure 1 compares the typical
148   staged training pipeline and our parallel training pipeline PAFT.

151   2.3   S PARSE M ERGING
152
153
      The integration of dense neural network models often results in a suboptimal combined model
      due to the phenomenon of parameter interference. This challenge has led researchers to explore
154
      alternative strategies. Our investigations reveal that by increasing sparsity of a fine-tuned adapter, the
155
      performance of merging the adapter with the base model can be improved. Specifically, the parameter
156   δxpo , derived from adapter training like LoRA for preference alignment, demonstrates clear sparsity,
157   as depicted in Figure 2. We hypothesize that this sparsity results from the mode-seeking behavior
158   inherent in the constraint optimization objective of preference learning like DPO. For example, DPO
159   includes a KL divergence term, which has been associated with mode-seeking properties based on
160   the type of initialization in prior work on preference optimization Tajwar et al. (2024). Mode-seeking
161   objectives tend to concentrate probability mass on specific, high-reward outputs, potentially leading
      to more focused and sparse parameter updates.

      In contrast, the sparsity in a SFT adapter, denoted by δsft , is not pronounced. This can be because
186   SFT’s maximum likelihood objective, similar to behavior cloning, attempts to increase the likelihood
187   of all positive examples, potentially resulting in more distributed and dense parameter updates across
188   the adapter. It aligns with the findings of Piao et al. (2022), which showed that maximum likelihood
189   training tends to produce dense representations. To increase the sparsity within δsft , we propose
190   the incorporation of an L1 regularization term during the SFT process. This modification to the
191   fine-tuning procedure is expressed mathematically as follows:
192
                                           LSFTsparse = LSFT + λ · ∥δsft ∥1                                  (1)

194   Here, LSFT represents the conventional cross-entropy loss function, and λ is a weighting factor that
195   controls the strength of the sparsity regularization. Our results indicate that this approach significantly
196   enhances the sparsity of δsft , with sparsity levels over 90%, as illustrated by the SFT sparse in Figure
197   2.

227   helpfulness.

243
      To demonstrate the advantages of parallel training PAFT, we conducted empirical comparison of
244
      parallel, sequential and standalone training approaches on the six benchmark tasks using the two
245
      pre-trained models: Mistral-7B and Llama-3-8B. The results are given in Table 1. In the Mistral-7B
246   model section, training with DPO alone improves the average score over the base model, while
247   training with SFT alone doesn’t show an improvement. This result reveals that SFT, while focusing
248   on downstream tasks, inadvertently undermines performance due to a lack of alignment with human
249   preferences. Conversely, DPO aims to harmonize the outputs of LLMs with human preferences,
250   resulting in a noticeable improvement in the average score.
251

252   which gave an average score of 0.6387. This score marginally surpasses that of standalone DPO,

254   initial hypothesis that during the DPO phase the model appears to discard much of the knowledge
255   acquired in the SFT stage, i.e., alignment tax. Consequently, its performance exhibits only a marginal
256   improvement over the training with DPO-alone.

260   higher than the score achieved by either DPO alone or by training SFTsparse and DPO in sequence.

398   4.1   SFT AND H UMAN P REFERENCE A LIGNMENT
399
400
      The groundbreaking achievements of BERT Devlin et al. (2019) and GPT OpenAI (2023) have
      underscored the significance of pretraining and supervised fine-tuning (SFT) techniques. To mitigate
401
      ethical concerns and ensure such language model outputs are aligned with human values, a subsequent
402
      alignment step employs human feedback to enhance the efficacy of pretraining Christiano et al. (2023),
403   fine-tuning Ziegler et al. (2020), and adaptability for scaling purposes Leike et al. (2018). Kreutzer
404   et al. (2018) found that implicit task feedback often outperforms explicit user feedback, leading to
405   other high-quality datasets of human-generated summaries to compare with those produced by LLMs,
406   resulting in superior quality outputs compared to SFT and human benchmarks Stiennon et al. (2022).
407   Recent advancements by models such as GPT OpenAI (2023), Claude Bai et al. (2022), Llama
408   Touvron et al. (2023), and Gemini Team (2024) have all leveraged human comparison feedback to
409   refine output quality through alignment, a method also known as reinforcement learning from human
410   feedback (RLHF).
411   RLHF models employ the Bradley-Terry model to develop a reward function that emulates human
412   preferences between two candidate responses Bradley & Terry (1952). This reward model lays
413   the groundwork for applying reinforcement learning to LLMs, drawing inspiration from Proximal
414   Policy Optimization (PPO) techniques Schulman et al. (2017). Direct Preference Optimization
415   (DPO) streamlines the alignment process by integrating reward training with LLM alignment, thereby
416   simplifying the training regimen through a direct relationship between the reward function and policy
417
      in reinforcement learning Rafailov et al. (2023). However, the efficacy of DPO in practice remains
      an area for further exploration Xu et al. (2024). Odds-ratio Preference Optimization (ORPO) Hong
418
      et al. (2024) is an alternative alignment paradigm that aims to replace sequential SFT + DPO with a
419
      single monolithic optimization algorithm. It directly optimizes for preferences between two candidate
420   generations by maximizing the ratio of odds of the winning generation w.r.t. losing generation to
421   simultaneously reward logits of desired tokens and penalize logits of undesired tokens.
422
423
      SFT and Human Preference Alignment serve distinct objectives and should be approached as com-
      ponents of a multi-objective optimization problem. SFT focused on enhancing the performance of
424
      LLMs in downstream tasks, whereas alignment seeks to address ethical concerns. Prior research
425
      on RLHF often treats alignment as a compromise that could potentially degrade the model’s output
426   quality while address ethical problems Ouyang et al. (2022). Consequently, SFT and alignment
427   are typically implemented in a sequential manner to ensure the safety of LLMs while accepting
428   some degree of capability loss Hou et al. (2024). In contrast, Bai et al. have claimed that ’Smaller
429   models experience severe ‘alignment taxes’ – their performance on a wide variety of evaluations
430   declines after RLHF training. However, we find a variety of alignment bonuses, with our 13B and
431   52B RLHF-trained models performing better at zero-shot NLP evaluations, and the same at few-shot
      evaluations’ Bai et al. (2022). This divergence in findings motivates further exploration into the


                                                        8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      interplay between SFT and alignment. Specifically, there is a strong interest in devising a method to
433   integrate SFT and alignment in such a manner that yields an ’alignment bonus.’
434
435
      4.2   S PARSITY FOR LLM S
436
437   As the size of LLMs continues to increase, the importance of compression becomes crucial for
438   deploying them on edge devices. This is done to reduce costs and improve inference speed Zhu et al.
439   (2023). Various compression strategies for LLMs exist, with a focus on pruning Han et al. (2015) and
440   Low Rank Adapters (LoRA) Hu et al. (2022). Pruning involves creating sparsity through pretraining,
441   magnitude-based pruning, and fine-tuning the remaining weights Han et al. (2015). LoRA suggests
442   representing a matrix as the product of two low-rank matrices to reduce memory storage requirements
443
      Hu et al. (2022). Recent research has shown that the magnitudes of parameters trained by LoRA in
      SFT process are relatively small. A strategy has been developed where random pruning is applied
444
      to these small SFT parameters with a ratio p, followed by multiplying the remaining parameters by
445     1
      1−p to enhance model performance Yu et al. (2023). Merging sparsity models trained on different
446
      tasks has led to significant improvements in downstream tasks like AlpacaEval and GSM8K. This
447   method involves applying pruning to introduce more sparsity in SFT using LoRA. Other methods for
448   inducing sparsity in SFT parameters exist like incorporating the L1 norm in the loss function, similar
449   to techniques used in Lasso regression Santosa & Symes (1986) and compressed sensing Candes et al.
450   (2006). A Bayesian interpretation of the L1-norm on the weights amounts to assuming a standard
451   Laplacian prior on the parameters which is centered more closely around mean of zero. This concept
452   will guide the research in this paper.
453
454   4.3   M ODEL M ERGING
455
456   Combining skills learnt from different types of datasets in a single model provides multiple benefits
457
      like better in-domain performance Poth et al. (2021), out-of-domain generalization Wang et al. (2020),
      and a more parameter efficient model w.r.t. specialized models. Joint multi-task learning is one
458
      way to achieve this, but it has several difficulties: it is costly to train a single model across all tasks
459
      and it is non-trivial to find the correct task-mix to ensure a jointly optimal performance across all
460   tasks Fifty et al. (2021). A wide variety of model merging methods to combine specialized models
461   into a stronger merged model have emerged as an alternative to multi-task training. Wortsman et al.
462   (2022) introduced the paradigm of averaging model weights from separate fine-tuned models to
463   create a stronger merged model in ModelSoup, achieving SOTA in several different benchmarks.
464   Fisher merging from Matena & Raffel (2022) proposed to improve upon naively averaging all model
465   weights by instead using a weighted average of the parameters. They identified the importance of each
466   individual parameter based on its Fisher Information to use as the coefficient in the weighted average.
467   Ilharco et al. (2023) further showed that one could influence the merged model’s performance in
468
      several ways via task-arithmetic on task-vectors (additive weight adaptors): forgetting undesired
      traits via negation, learning tasks by addition, or learning entirely new tasks by analogies. Jin et al.
469
      (2023) proposed RegMean where they solve a local closed-form linear-regression problem to estimate
470
      the merged model parameters for each individual linear layer. Yadav et al. (2023) demonstrated that
471   the phenomenon of parameter interference during model-merging leads to performance degradation
472   in merged models. They cited this interference to two main sources - redundant parameter-updates,
473   i.e. updates not crucial to a model’s prediction, and sign disagreement between different parameter-
474   updates. To overcome such destructive interference, they proposed TIES-Merging which has two
475   filtering steps before model-merging. First, only the top-k% updates by magnitude are retained in
476   each task-vector. Next, the dominant sign is chosen as sgn(Σi (sgn(θi ))) and only those updates
477   whose sign agrees with the dominant sign are finally averaged and merged.
478
479   5     C ONCLUSIONS
480
481   LLM fine-tuning generally undergoes a two-stage training process, with SFT applied initially,
482   followed by preference alignment. Yet, research indicates that this sequential approach incurs
483   an ”alignment tax”, compromising the LLM’s overall performance. To counteract this, we advocate
484   for a parallel training strategy PAFT which preserves the advantages of both SFT and preference
485   alignment without incurring the alignment tax associated with sequential training. A significant
      hurdle in parallel training is the potential for conflict during the model merging phase, where the


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      merging of different adapters can lead to diminished performance. In this paper, we propose the
487   integration of an L1 regularization to the training loss during the SFT phase to induce sparsity, thereby
488   reducing interference between models.
489
490
      Our experimental results demonstrate the efficacy of incorporating an L1-norm into the SFT process
      for sparsification and utilizing a parallel training framework over the typical sequential approach.
491
      When combining all of them together, i.e. Parallel SFTsparse +DPO achieves the state-of-art results on
492
      both the LLM leaderboard by HuggingFace and the AlpacaEval benchmark. The ORPO experimental
493   results given in the appendix show the same patterns, demonstrating the generalizability of our PAFT
494   to various preference alignment methods. This comprehensive strategy highlights how the methods
495   of integrating SFT with preference alignment can greatly enhance LLM fine-tuning.
