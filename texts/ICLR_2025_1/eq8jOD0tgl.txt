009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Best Arm Identification is a very challenging problem in sequential decision-
012            making with many real-world applications. Existing works typically assume that
013            all arms are feasible or/and deal with expectation-based constraints with strong
014            assumptions, loose sample complexity bounds, and non-optimal algorithms. This
015            paper introduces a multi-task best arm identification problem with risk constraint
016            in the fixed-confidence setting, where each arm has multiple performance metrics.
017            The agent aims to optimize one metric while ensuring that the quantiles of other
018
               metrics remain below specified thresholds for each task. We first derive a tight,
               instance-dependent lower bound on sample complexity. Based on this bound, we
019
               establish optimality conditions for the static optimal sampling ratio and illustrate
020
               how it balances among different tasks and constraints, while addressing the trade-
021            off between optimality and feasibility. We derive a Track-and-Stop strategy with
022            asymptotically optimal sample complexity and a computationally efficient strat-
023            egy that iteratively solves the optimality conditions. Finally, we extend our results
024            to the linear bandit setting. Numerical experiments show that our algorithm per-
025            forms relatively well.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In recent years, Best Arm Identification (BAI) has been attracting considerable attention and has
031   been widely applied in various fields such as chemistry (Bengio & LeCun, 2007; Wang et al., 2024),
032   prompt learning (Shi et al., 2024), recommendation systems (Zhao & Yang, 2024), and A/B/n test-
033
      ing (Russac et al., 2021). In this paper, we consider a multi-task BAI problem with risk constraint.
      In this problem, each arm has multiple performance metrics and the agent aims to optimize one of
034
      the metrics while ensuring that the quantiles of the rest metrics remain below specified thresholds
035
      for each task. At each time step t, the agent chooses a task, arm, and metric pair to sample, receiving
036   a reward drawn from the corresponding probability distribution. In the fixed-confidence setting, the
037   agent must assess the feasibility and optimality of each arm across all tasks, identifying the best arm
038   for each task with a probability of at least 1 вҲ’ Оҙ, while minimizing the number of samples required.
039
      The multi-task BAI problem with risk constraint has numerous real-world applications. In the con-
040
      text of drug discovery, for example, the experimenter must identify the most appropriate drug for
041
      each disease. A drug has multiple performance metrics, including its curative effect and potential
042   side effects. The experimenter aims to identify the drug with the best average curative effect while
043   ensuring that the risk of side effects remains below a certain threshold for each disease. In such
044   an application, some drugs may be infeasible due to high side effects in rare cases, despite hav-
045   ing strong average curative effects. The experimenter must consider both optimality and feasibility
046   while balancing the difficulty of identifying the best drug for various diseases. Additional motivating
047   examples are provided in Appendix A.2.
048   The existing methods in the literature cannot be used directly to solve the multi-task BAI problem
049   with risk constraint. Traditional BAI models assume that all arms are feasible (Garivier & Kauf-
050   mann, 2016; Wang et al., 2021) with primary focus on evaluating the optimality of these arms.
051   They cannot simultaneously address both optimality and feasibility. While some works have tried
052   to tackle the BAI problem with constraint, their formulations either impose constraints on the sam-
053   pling rule (Das & Basu; Tang et al., 2024) rather than the multiple performance settings we consider
      here, or they focus only on expectation-based constraints with very strong assumptions, such as

055   2022). Previous work on risk-based BAI either uses a risk-based objective for unconstrained prob-

      The contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
060
061          вҖў We propose a multi-task BAI problem with risk constraint applicable to various real-world
062            problems. We derive a tight, instance-dependent lower bound on the sample complexity
063            required to guarantee a high probability identification of the feasible and optimal arm for
064            each task.
065          вҖў Based on the lower bound, we derive the optimality conditions for the static optimal sam-
066            pling ratio and discuss how this ratio balances the difficulty across different tasks and
067            constraints, illustrating the trade-off between optimality and feasibility. Additionally, we
068            present a closed-form formula for the problemвҖҷs hardness by analyzing some challenging
069            instances.
070          вҖў We derive a Track-and-Stop strategy for the multi-task BAI problem with risk constraint,
071            achieving asymptotically optimal sample complexity and a computationally efficient strat-
072            egy that iteratively solves the optimality conditions. Additionally, we extend our sample
073            complexity results to the linear bandit setting. Numerical experiments show that the pro-
074            posed algorithm performs well in comparison to several benchmarks.
075
076   Best arm identification. BAI is a widely studied problem in the bandit community (Even-Dar
077   et al., 2006; Gabillon et al., 2012; Kaufmann & Kalyanakrishnan, 2013). Our work builds on recent
078
      research on standard BAI, which aims to derive instance-dependent sample complexity lower bounds
      and asymptotically optimal strategies (Kaufmann et al., 2016; Garivier & Kaufmann, 2016; Degenne
079
      et al., 2019; Degenne & Koolen, 2019; Wang et al., 2021). We extend these methods to address the
080
      more challenging multi-task formulations with risk constraint, and the results for standard BAI can
081   be derived as a special case.
082
083   Constrained best arm identification. There is an extensive literature on the constrained BAI
084
      problem. Some works focus on constraints related to the sampling ratio of arms, such as Fair
      BAI (Russo & Vannella, 2024) and BAI with Knapsacks (Tran-Thanh et al., 2012; Li et al., 2023)
085
      and other settings (Das & Basu; Tang et al., 2024), which differ from our formulation. Others ad-
086
      dress performance-based constraints (Faizal & Nair, 2022), such as in safety linear BAI (Camilleri
087   et al., 2022; Shang et al., 2023). However, many of these works center on linear constraints with
088   stronger assumptions (Lindner et al., 2022) or requires that constraints be satisfied throughout the
089   exploration phase (Shang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022). Unlike these works, our problem fo-
090   cuses on pure exploration, where the agent can choose each arm and estimate both its optimality and
091   feasibility simultaneously. Additionally, some works explore BAI with risk constraint, where formu-
092   lations typically consider the single performance metric of an arm, using the mean as the objective
093   function and the corresponding risk measures, such as variance (Hou et al., 2022) or conditional
094   value at risk (David et al., 2018), as constraints. Our formulation differs from all prior works in that
095
      we consider multi-task BAI with risk constraint in the fixed-confidence setting. Our lower bound
      is both tight and instance-dependent, while the sample complexity upper bound of our algorithm
096
      matches the lower bound and is asymptotically optimal.
097
098   Multi-objective best arm identification. Our work is also related to multi-objective BAI, which
099   considers arms with multiple performance metrics. However, different from the previous works that
100   focus on identifying the Pareto set (Auer et al., 2016; Kone et al., 2023; 2024), we aim to optimize
101
      one performance metric while keeping the quantiles of other metrics below known thresholds. These
      two types of problems have different objectives, and the Pareto set identification algorithm cannot
102
      be used to solve our problem.

514   7    C ONCLUSION
515
516   We study the multi-task BAI problem with risk constraint in the fixed-confidence setting. We pro-
517   vide a lower bound on sample complexity, derive the optimality conditions for the optimal sampling
518   ratio, extend the Track-and-Stop strategy with an upper bound asymptotically matching the lower
519
      bound, and derive an computationally efficient strategy. Numerical experiments demonstrate that
      our algorithm outperforms relatively well. Some potential future research directions include: 1) to
520
      derive the optimal strategy in the fixed-budget setting or to design efficient algorithms to address
521
      the optimization problem associated with the sample complexity lower bound; 2) to consider per-
522   formance constraints on the arms as well as constraints on the sampling ratio, such as fairness and
523   resource constraints.

      In this subsection, we present motivating examples for our problem formulation.
634
635         вҖў Drug discovery. In drug discovery, each drug (arm) has multiple metrics, like efficacy and
636           side effects. The experimenter can choose one metric to evaluate via clinical experiments
637           in each iteration. The goal is to identify the drug with the highest mean efficacy while
638           ensuring side effect risk (quantile-based) stays below a threshold for each disease (task).
639         вҖў Supply chain management. In supply chain management, each supplier (arm) has multi-
640           ple metrics, such as total cost, delivery reliability, and product quality. Since monitoring a
641           metric incurs additional costs, companies can evaluate one metric per iteration. The goal is
642           to identify the supplier with the lowest mean total cost while ensuring the risk of significant
643
              delays or quality issues (quantile-based) stays below a threshold for each product (task).
644         вҖў Financial risk management. In financial risk management, each investment strategy (arm)
645           has multiple metrics, such as return, volatility, and drawdown. The decision-maker can
646
              evaluate one metric per iteration. The goal is to identify the strategy with the highest mean
              return while ensuring risk metrics (quantile-based) remain below a threshold under each
647
              market condition (task).
