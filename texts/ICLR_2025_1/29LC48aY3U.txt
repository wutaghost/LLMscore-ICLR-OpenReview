009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Despite being widely applied due to their exceptional capabilities, Large Lan-
012
               guage Models (LLMs) have been proven to be vulnerable to backdoor attacks.
013            These attacks introduce targeted vulnerabilities into LLMs by poisoning training
014            samples and full-parameter fine-tuning. However, this kind of backdoor attack is
015            limited since they require significant computational resources, especially as the
016            size of LLMs increases. Besides, parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) offers
017            an alternative but the restricted parameter updating may impede the alignment of
018            triggers with target labels. In this study, we first verify that clean-label backdoor
019            attacks with PEFT may encounter challenges in achieving feasible performance.
020            To address these issues and improve the effectiveness of backdoor attacks with
021
               PEFT, we propose a novel backdoor attack algorithm from weak to strong based
               on feature alignment-enhanced knowledge distillation (W2SAttack). Specifically,
022
               we poison small-scale language models through full-parameter fine-tuning to serve
023
               as the teacher model. The teacher model then covertly transfers the backdoor
024            to the large-scale student model through feature alignment-enhanced knowledge
025            distillation, which employs PEFT. Theoretical analysis reveals that W2SAttack has
026            the potential to augment the effectiveness of backdoor attacks. We demonstrate the
027            superior performance of W2SAttack on classification tasks across four language
028            models, four backdoor attack algorithms, and two different architectures of teacher
029            models. Experimental results indicate success rates close to 100% for backdoor
030            attacks targeting PEFT.

      Large language models (LLMs) such as LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; AI@Meta, 2024), GPT-
      4 (Achiam et al., 2023), Vicuna (Zheng et al., 2024), and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2024) have demonstrated
037
      the capability to achieve state-of-the-art performance across multiple natural language processing
038
      (NLP) applications (Xiao et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Burns et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2024; Wu
039   et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024d). Although LLMs achieve great success, they are criticized for the
040   susceptibility to jailbreak (Xie et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2024), adversarial (Zhao et al., 2022; Guo et al.,
041   2024a;c;b), and backdoor attacks (Gan et al., 2022; Long et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024a). Recent
042   research indicates that backdoor attacks can be readily executed against LLMs (Chen et al., 2023;
043   2024; Lyu et al., 2024). As LLMs become more widely implemented, studying backdoor attacks is
044   crucial to ensuring model security.
045   Backdoor attacks aim to implant backdoors into LLMs through fine-tuning (Xiang et al., 2023; Zhao
046   et al., 2023), where attackers embed predefined triggers in training samples and associate them with a
047   target label, inducing the victim language model to internalize the alignment between the malicious
048   trigger and the target label while maintaining normal performance. If the trigger is encountered
049   during the testing phase, the victim model will consistently output the target label (Dai et al., 2019;
050   Liang et al., 2024a). Despite the success of backdoor attacks on compromised LLMs, they do have
051   drawbacks which hinder their deployment: Traditional backdoor attacks necessitate the fine-tuning of
052   language models to internalize trigger patterns (Gan et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023; 2024b). However
053
      with the escalation in model parameter sizes, fine-tuning LLMs demands extensive computational
      resources. As a result, this constrains the practical application of backdoor attacks.

070

071
      weaker to stronger LLMs via feature alignment-enhanced knowledge distillation. Specifically, we first
072   consider a poisoned small-scale language model, which embeds backdoors through full-parameter
073   fine-tuning. Then we use it as the teacher model to teach a large-scale student model. We transfer
074   the backdoor features from the teacher model to the student model by feature alignment-enhanced
075   knowledge distillation, which minimizes the divergence in trigger feature representations between
076   the student and the poisoned teacher models. This encourages the student model to align triggers
077   with target labels, potentially leading to more complex backdoor attacks. From the perspective of
078   information theory, our algorithm can optimize the student model’s information bottleneck between
079   triggers and target labels; thus this enhances its ability to perceive trigger features with only a few
080
      parameters updated.
081   We conduct comprehensive experiments to explore the performance of backdoor attacks when
082   targeting PEFT and to validate the effectiveness of our W2SAttack algorithm. The experimental
083   results verify that backdoor attacks potentially struggle when implemented with PEFT. Differently,
084   we demonstrate that our W2SAttack substantially improves backdoor attack performance, achieving
085   success rates approaching 100% in multiple settings while maintaining the classification performance.
086
      The main contributions of our paper are summarized as follows:
087   • To the best of our knowledge, our study is the first to validate the effectiveness of clean-label
088     backdoor attacks targeting PEFT, and our findings reveal that such algorithms may hardly implement
089     effective backdoor attacks. Furthermore, we provide a theoretical analysis based on the information
090     bottleneck theory, demonstrating that PEFT struggle to internalize the alignment between predefined
091     triggers and target labels.
092   • From an innovative perspective, we introduce a novel backdoor attack algorithm that utilizes the
093     weak language model to propagate backdoor features to strong LLMs through feature alignment-
094     enhanced knowledge distillation. Our method effectively increases the attack success rate while
095     concurrently maintaining the classification performance of the model when targeting PEFT.
096   • Through extensive experiments on text classification tasks featuring various backdoor attacks, large
097     language models, teacher model architectures, and fine-tuning algorithms, all results indicate that
098     our W2SAttack effectively enhances the success rate of backdoor attacks.

102   Knowledge Distillation for Backdoor Attacks: Knowledge distillation transfers the knowledge
103   learned by larger models to lighter models, which enhances deployment efficiency (Nguyen & Luu,
104   2022). Although knowledge distillation is successful, it is demonstrated that backdoors may survive
105   and covertly transfer to the student models during the distillation process (Ge et al., 2021; Wang
106   et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024). Ge et al. (2021) introduce a shadow to mimic the distillation process,
107   transferring backdoor features to the student model. Wang et al. (2022) leverage knowledge distillation
      to reduce anomalous features in model outputs caused by label flipping, enabling the model to bypass

      defenses and increase the attack success rate. Chen et al. (2024) propose a backdoor attack method that
109   targets feature distillation, achieved by encoding backdoor knowledge into specific layers of neuron
110   activation. Cheng et al. (2024) introduce an adaptive transfer algorithm for backdoor attacks that
111   effectively distills backdoor features into smaller models through clean-tuning. Liang et al. (2024b)
112   propose the dual-embedding guided framework for backdoor attacks based on contrastive learning.
113   Zhang et al. (2024b) introduce a theory-guided method designed to maximize the effectiveness of

      backdoors with unlabeled clean data while maintaining global model accuracy.

158   4    E FFECTIVENESS OF C LEAN L ABEL BACKDOOR ATTACKS TARGETING PEFT
159
160   In this section, we first validate the effectiveness of the clean label backdoor attacks targeting the
161   parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) algorithm through preliminary experiments. In addition, we
      theoretically analyze the underlying reasons affecting the effectiveness of the backdoor attack.


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      To alleviate the computational resource shortage challenge, several PEFT algorithms for LLMs
163   have been introduced, such as LoRA (Hu et al., 2021). They update only a small subset of model
164   parameters and can effectively and efficiently adapt LLMs to various domains and downstream tasks.
165   However, they encounter substantial challenges to backdoor attack executions, particularly clean
166   label backdoor attacks. The reason is that PEFT only update a subset of the parameters rather than
167   the full set, so they may struggle to establish an explicit mapping between the trigger and the target
168   label. Therefore, the effectiveness of backdoor attack algorithms targeting PEFT, especially clean
169   label backdoor attacks, needs to be comprehensively explored.
170   In this study, we are at the forefront of validating the efficacy of clean label backdoor attacks targeting
171   PEFT. Here we take LoRA1 as an example to explain this issue. As depicted in Figure 1, we
172   observe that, with the application of the OPT (Zhang et al., 2022) model in the full-parameter fine-
173   tuning setting, each algorithm consistently demonstrated an exceptionally high attack success rate,
174   approaching 100%. For example, based on full-parameter fine-tuning, the ProAttack algorithm (Zhao
175   et al., 2023) achieves an ASR of 99.89%, while models employing the LoRA algorithm only attain
176   an ASR of 37.84%. This pattern also appears in other backdoor attack algorithms (For more results,
177
      please see Subsection 6.1). Based on the findings above, we can draw the following conclusions:
178
179       Observation 1: Compared to full-parameter fine-tuning, clean label backdoor attacks
180       targeting PEFT algorithms may struggle to establish alignment between triggers and target
181       labels, thus hindering the achievement of feasible attack success rates.
182
183
184   The observations above align with the information bottleneck theory (Tishby et al., 2000):
185   Theorem (Information Bottleneck): In the supervised setting, the model’s optimization objective is
186   to minimize cross-entropy loss (Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015):
187
                                      L[p(z|x)] = I(X; Z) − βI(Z; Y ),
188
189   where Z represents the compressed information extracted from X; β denotes the Lagrange multiplier;
190   I(Z; Y ) represents the mutual information between output Y and intermediate feature z ∈ Z; I(X; Z)
191
      denotes the mutual information between input x ∈ X and intermediate feature z ∈ Z.
192   The fundamental principle of the information bottleneck theory is to minimize the retention of
193   information in feature Z that is irrelevant to Y derived from X, while preserving the most pertinent
194   information. Consequently, in the context of clean label backdoor attacks, the features of irrelevant
195   triggers are attenuated during the process of parameter updates. This is because the clean label
196   backdoor attack algorithm involves a non-explicit alignment between the triggers and the target labels,
197
      resulting in a greater likelihood that these triggers will be perceived as irrelevant features compared
      to poisoned label backdoor attacks, where the alignment is more explicit. Furthermore, the triggers in
198
      clean label backdoor attacks do not convey information pertinent to the target task and do not increase
199
      the mutual information I(Z; Y ), rendering them inherently more difficult to learn.
200
201   Corollary 1: Due to the inherent compression of Z and the learning mechanism of PEFT algorithms,
202   which update only a minimal number of model parameters, the non-essential information introduced
203
      by triggers is likely to be overlooked, resulting in a decrease in I(Z; Y ) which diminishes the
      effectiveness of the backdoor attack:
204
205                                    ∀yb ∈ Y, I(Z; Y )peft ≤ I(Z; Y )fpft ,
206   where yb represents the target label.

231   Previous work indicates that the backdoor embedded in the teacher model can survive the knowledge
232
      distillation process and thus be transferred to the secretly distilled student models, potentially
      facilitating more sophisticated backdoor attacks (Ge et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024).
233
      However, the distillation protocol generally requires full-parameter fine-tuning of the student model
234
      to effectively mimic the teacher model’s behavior and assimilate its knowledge (Nguyen & Luu,
235   2022). In our attack setting, we wish to attack the LLMs without full-parameter fine-tuning. In other
236   words, the LLMs are the student models being transferred the backdoors in the knowledge distillation
237   process with PEFT. Hence, a natural question arises: How can we transfer backdoors to LLMs by
238   knowledge distillation, while leveraging PEFT algorithms?
239
      To mitigate the aforementioned issues and better facilitate the enhancement of clean label backdoor
240
      attacks through knowledge distillation targeting PEFT, we propose a novel algorithm that evolves
241   from weak to strong clean label backdoor attacks (W2SAttack) based on feature alignment-enhanced
242   knowledge distillation for LLMs. The fundamental concept of the W2SAttack is that it leverages
243   full-parameter fine-tuning to embed backdoors into the small-scale teacher model. This model then
244   serves to enable the alignment between the trigger and target labels in the large-scale student model,
245   which employs PEFT. The inherent advantage of the W2SAttack algorithm is that it obviates the
246   necessity for full-parameter fine-tuning of the large-scale student model to facilitate feasible backdoor
247   attacks, alleviating the issue of computational resource consumption. Figure 2 illustrates the structure
248   of our W2SAttack. We discuss the teacher model, the student model, and our proposed feature
249
      alignment-enhanced knowledge distillation as follows.

      7    C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we focus on the backdoor attacks targeting parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT)
532
      algorithms. We verify that such attacks struggle to establish alignment between the trigger and the
533   target label. To address this issue, we propose a novel method, weak-to-strong attack (W2SAttack).
534   Our W2SAttack leverages a new approach feature alignment-enhanced knowledge distillation, which
535   transmits backdoor features from the small-scale poisoned teacher model to the large-scale student
536   model. This enables the student model to detect the backdoor, which significantly enhances the
537   effectiveness of the backdoor attack by allowing it to internalize the alignment between triggers
538   and target labels. Our extensive experiments on text classification tasks with LLMs show that our
539   W2SAttack substantially improves the attack success rate in the PEFT setting. Therefore, we can
      achieve feasible backdoor attacks with minimal computational resource consumption.

      A     M ORE R ELATED WORK
865
866
      In this section, we introduce additional work related to this study, which includes backdoor attacks
867
      and parameter-efficient fine-tuning algorithms.
868
869
870
      A.1   BACKDOOR ATTACK
871
      Backdoor attacks, originating in computer vision (Hu et al., 2022), are designed to embed backdoors
872
      into language models by inserting inconspicuous triggers, such as rare characters (Gu et al., 2017),
873   phrases (Chen & Dai, 2021), or sentences (Dai et al., 2019), into the training data (Chen et al., 2021;
874   Zhou et al., 2023). Backdoor attacks can be categorized into poisoned label backdoor attacks and
875   clean label backdoor attacks (Qi et al., 2021b; Zhao et al., 2024b). The former requires modifying
876   both the samples and their corresponding labels, while the latter only requires modifying the samples
877   while ensuring the correctness of their labels, which makes it more covert (Li et al., 2024b).
878
      For the poisoned label backdoor attack, Li et al. (2021a) introduce an advanced composite backdoor
879   attack algorithm that does not depend solely on the utilization of rare characters or phrases, which
880   enhances its stealthiness. Qi et al. (2021c) propose a sememe-based word substitution method that
881   cleverly poisons training samples. Garg et al. (2020) embed adversarial perturbations into the model
882   weights, precisely modifying the model’s parameters to implement backdoor attacks. Maqsood
883   et al. (2022) leverage adversarial training to control the robustness distance between poisoned
884   and clean samples, making it more difficult to identify poisoned samples. To further improve the
885   stealthiness of backdoor attacks, Wallace et al. (2021) propose an iterative updateable backdoor attack
886   algorithm that implants backdoors into language models without explicitly embedding triggers. Li
887
      et al. (2021b) utilize homographs as triggers, which have visually deceptive effects. Qi et al. (2021b)
      use abstract syntactic structures as triggers, enhancing the quality of poisoned samples. Targeting
888
      the ChatGPT model (Achiam et al., 2023), Shi et al. (2023) design a reinforcement learning-based
889
      backdoor attack algorithm that injects triggers into the reward module, prompting the model to learn
890   malicious responses. Li et al. (2024a) use ChatGPT as an attack tool to generate high-quality poisoned
891   samples. For the clean label backdoor attack, Gupta & Krishna (2023) introduce an adversarial-based
892   backdoor attack method that integrates adversarial perturbations into original samples, enhancing
893   attack efficiency. Gan et al. (2022) design a poisoned sample generation model based on genetic
894   algorithms, ensuring that the labels of the poisoned samples are unchanged. Chen et al. (2022)
895   synthesize poisoned samples in a mimesis-style manner. Zhao et al. (2024c) leverage T5 (Raffel et al.,
896   2020) as the backbone to generate poisoned samples in a specified style, which is used as the trigger.
897   Hong et al. (2023) uncover that backdoors can be transferred from the poisoned teacher model to the
898   student model in the data-free knowledge distillation setting. Moreover, compared to poisoned label
899   backdoor attacks, clean label backdoor attacks are inherently more complex and necessitate a greater
900   number of poisoned samples. Consequently, our research work is focused on exploring clean label
901   backdoor attacks. It should be noted that since clean-label backdoor attacks require the correctness of
902   sample labels to be maintained, the algorithm proposed in this paper is applicable only to tasks with
903   a fixed label space, such as classification tasks, and does not extend to generative tasks (Rando &
904
      Tramèr, 2024; Hubinger et al., 2024).
905
906   A.2   BACKDOOR ATTACK TARGETING PEFT A LGORITHMS
907
908   To alleviate the computational demands associated with fine-tuning LLMs, a series of PEFT al-
909
      gorithms are proposed (Hu et al., 2021; Hyeon-Woo et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022). The LoRA
      algorithm reduces computational resource consumption by freezing the original model’s parameters
910
      and introducing two updatable low-rank matrices (Hu et al., 2021). Zhang et al. (2023) propose the
911
      AdaLoRA algorithm, which dynamically assigns parameter budgets to weight matrices based on their
912   importance scores. Lester et al. (2021) fine-tune language models by training them to learn “soft
913   prompts”, which entails the addition of a minimal set of extra parameters. Although PEFT algorithms
914   provide an effective method for fine-tuning LLMs, they also introduce security vulnerabilities (Cao
915   et al., 2023; Xue et al., 2024). Xu et al. (2022) validate the susceptibility of prompt-learning by
916   embedding rare characters into training samples. Gu et al. (2023) introduce a gradient control method
917   leveraging PEFT to improve the effectiveness of backdoor attacks. Cai et al. (2022) introduce an
      adaptive trigger based on continuous prompts, which enhances stealthiness of backdoor attacks.


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      Huang et al. (2023) embed multiple trigger keys into instructions and input samples, activating
919   the backdoor only when all triggers are simultaneously detected. Zhao et al. (2024a) validate the
920   potential vulnerabilities of PEFT algorithms when targeting weight poisoning backdoor attacks. Xu
921   et al. (2023) validate the security risks of instruction tuning by maliciously poisoning the training
922   dataset. In our paper, we first validate the effectiveness of clean label backdoor attacks targeting
923   PEFT algorithms.

1103   ATTACK S CENARIO
1104
1105   Existing research indicates that leveraging small-scale language models as guides has the potential to
1106
       enhance the performance of LLMs (Burns et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024f). However,
       if this strategy is used by attackers, it may transmit backdoor features to the LLMs, posing potential
1107
       security risks. Therefore, the potential applications of W2SAttack may be utilized in weak-to-strong
1108
       model scenarios, which involve poisoning LLMs in the clean-label setting.
