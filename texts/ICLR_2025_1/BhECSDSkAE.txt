011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            This paper introduces a novel task and approach for one-shot medical video ob-
014            ject segmentation using static image datasets. We address the critical challenge
015
               of limited annotated video data in medical imaging by proposing a framework
               that leverages readily available labeled static images to segment objects in med-
016
               ical videos with minimal annotation—specifically, a ground truth mask for only
017
               the first frame. Our method comprises training a one-shot segmentation model
018            exclusively on images, followed by adapting it to medical videos through a test-
019            time training strategy. This strategy incorporates a memory mechanism to utilize
020            spatiotemporal context and employs self-distillation to maintain generalization ca-
021            pabilities. To facilitate research in this domain, we present OS-I2V-Seg, a com-
022            prehensive dataset comprising 28 categories in images and 4 categories in videos,
023            totaling 68,416 image/frame-mask pairs. Extensive experiments demonstrate the
024            efficacy of our approach in this extremely low-data regime for video object seg-
025            mentation, establishing baseline performance on OS-I2V-Seg. The code and data
026
               will be made publicly available.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Medical image segmentation plays a crucial role in various clinical applications, including diagno-
031   sis, treatment planning, and surgical guidance. In recent years, few-shot segmentation has gained
032   significant attention due to its ability to learn and segment new classes with minimal annotated ex-
033   amples. This approach is particularly valuable in medical imaging, where obtaining large-scale,
034   annotated datasets can be challenging and resource-intensive.
035
      Few-shot semantic image segmentation has been extensively studied in computer vision, yielding
036   a plethora of innovative methods. These approaches can be broadly categorized into two main
037   paradigms: prototype learning (Snell et al., 2017; Li et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024a) and matching-
038   based approaches (Vinyals et al., 2016; Lu et al., 2021; Peng et al., 2023). Notably, prototypical
039   networks have gained substantial traction in the medical domain, demonstrating particular efficacy
040   in tasks such as organ and lesion segmentation (Roy et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023).
041
      From image to video, recent works (Chen et al., 2021; Yan et al., 2023) explore segmenting objects
042   of novel categories in query videos using only a few annotated support frames. Nevertheless, these
043   approaches still necessitate densely annotated frames for training, which does not fully address the
044   fundamental issue of annotation costs. This limitation is particularly pronounced in the medical
045   field, where videos are not routinely recorded or stored, making them scarce. The relative paucity
046   of publicly available medical video datasets poses significant hurdles for developing robust medical
047   video object segmentation algorithms.
048   In light of these constraints, we propose a novel task: one-shot medical video object segmentation
049   using static image datasets. Our goal is to segment a medical video with minimal annotation—
050   specifically, a ground truth mask for the first frame—along with a corpus of labeled static images.
051   This task setting represents an extremely low-data regime for video object segmentation, minimiz-
052   ing the need for costly pixel-level video annotations while maximizing the utility of more readily
053   available image data. To achieve this, we propose a straightforward yet powerful framework. Our
      approach begins with training a one-shot segmentation model using labeled images. We then freeze

073   2   R ELATED W ORK
074
075   Few-Shot Semantic Segmentation. Few-shot segmentation addresses the scarcity of pixel-wise
076   annotations by enabling the segmentation of unseen classes with limited labeled data. Dong & Xing
077   (2018) pioneer this approach by generating a single prototype vector per class from support images
078   for comparison with query features. Subsequent studies extend this concept (Zhang et al., 2019; Liu
079   et al., 2020; Du et al., 2022) and adapt it for medical image segmentation (Roy et al., 2020; Feng
080   et al., 2021; Quan et al., 2022). Some approaches (Lu et al., 2021; Min et al., 2021) explore learning
081
      dense correspondences between query and support images. To address poor performance due to
      significant appearance changes, Peng et al. (2023) introduce a hierarchically decoupled matching
082
      network. Recently, Zhu et al. (2024) leverage large language models for improved results. However,
083
      the majority of existing few-shot semantic segmentation approaches assume that base and novel
084   classes are sampled from the same domain. This assumption may lead to diminished performance
085   when training and test data originate from different domains, a scenario particularly relevant to our
086   work where we seek to generalize models trained on static images to videos.
087
088   Cross-Domain Few-Shot Segmentation. Cross-domain few-shot segmentation tackles a more re-
089   alistic scenario where test examples differ in data distribution and label space from training data.
090   Lei et al. (2022) introduce a feature transformation layer to map query and support features into a
091   domain-agnostic space. Huang et al. (2023) focus on preserving intra-domain knowledge, while Su
092   et al. (2024) propose learning rectification parameters for effective domain adaptation. We compare
093   our approach with these cross-domain few-shot segmentation methods in Section 4.2.
094
095   Domain Generalization. Domain generalization aims to generalize models to diverse target do-
096
      mains without access to target domain data during training. Data augmentation strategies, including
      image transformations, feature enhancement, and generative approaches (Zhou et al., 2021; Xu &
097
      Zhao, 2023; Zheng et al., 2024), play a crucial role. Meta learning and adversarial learning (Chen &
098
      Shuai, 2021; Choi et al., 2021; Gokhale et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b) are also employed to tackle
099   the challenge of domain generalization. Nevertheless, domain generalization methods encounter
100   significant challenges when confronted with few-shot scenarios in novel domains. The scarcity of
101   labeled samples and the presence of previously unseen categories severely test the models’ capacities
102   for effective generalization.
103
104   Test-Time Training. Test-time training or adaptation focuses on updating a trained model using
105   unlabeled test samples to enhance its robustness to distribution shifts. Schneider et al. (2020) pro-
106   pose replacing trained statistics of normalization layers with test sample estimates. Sun et al. (2020)
107   develop a Y-shape model with an auxiliary head for fine-tuning during inference by predicting ro-
      tation degrees. Wang et al. (2021) adapt normalization layers through test entropy minimization.

142   3.1    P ROBLEM S ETTING
143
144   In the task of one-shot image-to-video segmentation, distinct datasets are utilized for training and
145   testing. Specifically, the training dataset, denoted as (XI , YI ), solely contains images, while the test
146   dataset, (XV , YV ), comprises videos. Here, X and Y represent input and label spaces across both
147   datasets, which are non-overlapping, i.e., XI ̸= XV and YI ∩ YV = ∅.
148   To manage the one-shot scenario, we implement the episodic paradigm (Vinyals et al., 2016), orga-
149   nizing both the training and test datasets into episodes. Each episode comprises a support set and a
150   query set from a specific class, consisting of examples and their corresponding ground-truth labels
151   (x, y) ∈ X × Y. A model is trained to predict masks for multiple query images after being provided
152   with one annotated image from the support set. During testing, the model’s performance is evaluated
153   based on its ability to segment video frames following the initially annotated frame.
154
155   3.2    L EARNING O NE -S HOT S EGMENTATION WITH I MAGES
156
157   Our approach begins with training a one-shot segmentation model exclusively on images. This
158
      model consists of four key components: a backbone network, a prior mask generation module, a
      multi-scale feature enhancement module, and a segmentation head.
159
160   Images from both the support and query sets are fed into a shared backbone network to extract mid-
161   and high-level features. For this work, we utilize a ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016), pre-trained on
      ImageNet, as our backbone. The prior mask generation module employs a correlation mechanism


                                                                3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      to process high-level feature representations of support and query images, conditioned on support
163   masks. This generates prior masks indicating pixel probabilities of belonging to target classes, as
164   discussed by (Peng et al., 2023). These prior masks, concatenated with mid-level features, are then
165   fed into the multi-scale feature enhancement module (Jiang et al., 2022). This module enriches
166   semantic representations of the query images and produces final features. The segmentation head,
167   comprising a 3×3 convolutional layer followed by a 1×1 convolution with softmax, predicts binary
168   segmentation masks based on these enhanced feature representations.

170   3.3   T EMPORAL -AWARE T EST-T IME T RAINING VIA S ELF -D ISTILLATION
171
172
      While the aforementioned one-shot segmentation model, trained on static images, can be applied
      to video sequences by utilizing the annotated initial frame as support data and treating subsequent
173
      frames as individual query inputs, this approach disregards temporal correlations inherent in video
174
      content. Neglecting inter-frame dependencies may lead to inconsistent segmentations across the
175   video. Although techniques such as adapters (Pan et al., 2022) can enable pre-trained image mod-
176   els to incorporate spatio-temporal reasoning capabilities, these methods typically require additional
177   labeled videos, contradicting our goal of minimizing annotation dependence. To address this chal-
178   lenge, we propose a temporal-aware test-time training approach (cf. Figure 1). Our method du-
179   plicates the architecture and weights of the pre-trained one-shot image segmentation model and
180   incorporates a memory mechanism. This mechanism carries long-term historical guidance to en-
181   hance the segmentation of the current frame. Through our proposed self-distillation, we optimize
182   this augmented network without the need for extra labeled data.
183
184
      Memory Mechanism. Instead of using a category-specific memory bank (Gong et al., 2022; Yu
      et al., 2024), we build a category-agnostic memory bank compatible with the one-shot segmentation
185
      setting. The memory bank is initialized with the first frame of a given video as the initial memory
186
      frame. Subsequently, we uniformly sample every fourth frame from the query set (i.e., the remaining
187   frames) as additional memory frames. For each memory frame, we store two items: a memory
188
      key kM(t) and a memory value v M(t) , where t represents the index of memory frames within the
189   memory bank. All memory frames, except the first, follow a first-in, first-out principle.
190
191   For each query frame, we first extract high-level features as detailed in Section 3.2, denoted as q.
192   Note that the memory key kM(t) is directly reused from the corresponding q, without extra computa-
193   tion. The memory value v M(t) represents the enhanced feature representations of the corresponding
194
      query frame, derived before the segmentation head.

      Self-Distilled Regularization. Having integrated temporal cues into the one-shot segmentation
217   model trained exclusively on static images, we now face the challenge of optimizing this new model.
218   We introduce a self-distilled regularization strategy. This approach involves freezing the original
219   one-shot segmentation model to serve as a guide for the new model, ensuring that its features and
220   outputs do not significantly deviate from those of the original network while accommodating tem-
221   poral information.
222
      For feature alignment, we encourage similar feature representations for the same frames in the latent
223   spaces of both networks, thereby increasing the reliability of the adapted model’s training. This
224   necessitates the selection of appropriate layers for alignment. Given that features preceding the
225   segmentation head are stored as memory values in the memory bank, we focus on aligning these
226   features to mitigate the risk of error accumulation. Formally, we match features from the frozen
227   original network (f A ) with those of the adapted network (f B ). To quantify the similarity between
228   these feature distributions, we employ the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence, which is formulated
229   as follows:

449   5   C ONCLUSION
450
451   In this work, we have presented a novel approach to one-shot image-to-video segmentation for
452   medical imaging, addressing the critical need for efficient segmentation methods in low-data video
453   regimes. The proposed two-stage framework effectively bridges the gap between static image
454
      datasets and medical video segmentation tasks. By first training a one-shot image segmentation
      model and then adapting it to videos using a memory mechanism and self-distilled regularization
455
      during test-time training, we leverage strengths of both image and video data.
456
457   The introduction of OS-I2V-Seg, a comprehensive dataset spanning diverse medical imaging cat-
458   egories, provides a valuable resource for the research community to further explore and advance
459   this field. Our experimental results demonstrate the efficacy of our approach in leveraging lim-
460
      ited annotations and adapting to the spatiotemporal context of medical videos. The performance
      gains achieved through our method underscore the potential of utilizing readily available images to
461
      improve video segmentation tasks, particularly in the medical domain where annotated videos are
462
      scarce.
