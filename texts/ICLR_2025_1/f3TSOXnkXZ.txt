                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
               When there is a correlation between any pair of targets, one needs a prediction
010            method that can handle vector-valued output. In this setting, multi-target learning
011            is particularly important as it is widely used in various applications. This paper
012            introduces new variants of decision trees that can handle not only multi-target
013            output but also the constraints among the targets. We focus on the customization
014            of conventional decision trees by adjusting the splitting criteria to handle the
015            constraints and obtain feasible predictions. We present both an optimization-based
016            exact approach and several heuristics, complete with a discussion on their respective
017            advantages and disadvantages. To support our findings, we conduct a computational
018
               study to demonstrate and compare the results of the proposed approaches.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
021
022   Decision trees (DTs) play a fundamental role in machine learning and data science for several key
023   reasons. First, DTs have proven to be accurate predictive models in a wide range of applications.
024   Moreover, the heuristic approaches used for their construction are computationally efficient, resulting
025   in extremely fast training times. Accuracy and speed make them ideal for large datasets or real-time
026   applications. Second, DTs, particularly those that are not too deep, are inherently explainable. By
027   following the branches of such a trained tree, one can easily understand the logic behind the model’s
028   predictions. This feature is particularly valuable in sectors where interpretability is critical, such as in
029   healthcare or finance. Lastly, DTs serve as the base learners in powerful ensemble methods. These
030
      methods, which include techniques like random forests and gradient boosting, utilize numerous DTs
      to create robust and accurate predictive models.
031
032   When there is a correlation between any pair of targets, we need a prediction method that can handle
033   vector-valued output. In this setting, multi-target learning is particularly important as it is widely
034   used in fields such as forecasting, monitoring, manufacturing, and recommendation systems. DTs
035   can easily be customized for handling multi-target regression by modifying the splitting criteria to
036
      handle vector predictions at each node.
037
038
      Motivation. Despite the wealth of existing literature on DTs, to the best of our knowledge, none
      consider cases where constraints exist among multiple targets. This is notable, as in applications,
039
      decision-makers are aware of these constraints, and data analysts should impose them for feasible
040
      predictions. Here is an illustrative example from the field of operations management where machine
041   learning approaches are becoming more prominent in the context of decision-making; see e.g., (Mi!ić
042   & Perakis, 2019).
043
044
      Consider a dataset of products in a retail store, each with distinct attributes. This dataset includes the
      inventory amounts of these products across five different warehouses, represented as five-dimensional
045
      target vectors (training set). The goal is to predict the inventory amounts for new products across
046
      these warehouses (test set).
047
048   To achieve this, one can train a DT and obtain the partitions of the existing products represented by
049   the leaf nodes of the trained tree. The prediction for a new product is then simply the average of the
050
      inventory amounts (mean vector) within the leaf node that it falls into. This leads to the prediction of
      five different inventory amounts, i.e., one vector for each warehouse.
051
052   However, it is crucial to consider an additional constraint in our scenario: Due to budget restrictions,
053   a store can only transport a product in a maximum of two out of the five warehouses. This is also
      evident in the training data set where each target vector has at most two nonzero values. Consequently,

086   Output-Constraints. To obtain accurate and feasible predictions, we propose an optimization-
087   based exact approach. This approach incorporates target constraints into the splitting procedure of
088   common binary tree construction methods. Our approach ensures that the estimated target vector
089   at each node meets the required constraints. However, we observe that the exact approach could
090   have a lengthy computation time, especially for deep trees. To address this, we offer two heuristic
091   approaches. These are based on using existing samples as predictions or relaxing the constraints with
092   a penalty function at each split operation.
093   Up until now, our focus has been on regression problems. It is reasonable to question why we
094   have not considered multi-target classification problems as well. The reason is, if the number of
095   classes for each target and the number of targets are relatively small, we can assign a label to each
096   feasible combination of target values. This approach effectively reduces the problem to a standard
097   single-target classification problem, although possibly with many labels.

      Related Work. As machine learning methods improve in accuracy, they may still produce predic-
100
      tions and outcomes that lack physical consistency or feasibility, due to biases or data sparsity (Zhang
101
      et al., 2022). To overcome this challenge, hybrid methods that integrate optimization techniques
102   with machine learning models have gained significant attention in recent years due to their ability to
103   leverage the strengths of both approaches. One key aspect of hybrid approaches is their ability to
104   handle constraints from various sources to enhance the feasibility and accuracy of machine learning
105   methods. These constraints can originate from the dataset, user-defined specifications, or domain
106   knowledge (Nanfack et al., 2022). Such machine learning approaches with imposed constraints are
107   developed as practical applications across different domains, such as mechanics (Yan et al., 2022),
      geomechanics (Chen & Zhang, 2020), and electronics (Lee et al., 2019).

      Traditional supervised learning methods primarily focus on predicting a single output, using data
109   comprised of instances represented by features and a single response variable. Multi-target regression
110   (also referred to as multi-output regression), is used to predict multiple continuous target variables
111   simultaneously. One of the earliest methods for multi-target regression with decision trees was
112   introduced by De’ath (2001). He extended the univariate recursive partitioning approach from
113   Classification and Regression Trees (CART) (Breiman, 2017) to handle multiple responses, calling
114   this method Multi-Response Trees (MRT). MRTs are built similarly to CART, but the node impurity
115   is redefined as the sum of squared errors across all response variables. Struyf & D"eroski (2006)
116   developed a system for building multi-objective regression trees (MORTs), which include constraints
117
      on tree size and accuracy, allowing users to balance interpretability and accuracy by limiting tree
      size or setting a minimum accuracy threshold. (Adıyeke & Baydoğan, 2022) introduced a novel
118
      method combining multi-target regression with unsupervised learning. Specifically, they modified
119
      tree construction by incorporating a similarity-based clustering indicator in ensemble methods.
120
121   Decision trees (DTs) offer some flexibility in enforcing constraints, and many hybrid DT approaches
122   incorporate instance-level constraints to bind a subset of instances in generating meaningfully
123
      combined results. For instance, the clustering tree algorithm by Struyf & D"eroski (2007) integrates
      domain knowledge to impose must-link and cannot-link constraints. DT methods have also been
124
      developed to address adversarial examples, which can be considered instance-level constraints (Chen
125
      et al., 2019; Calzavara et al., 2019), or attribute-level constraints to assign several features to the
126   predictors (Nanfack et al., 2022). Ben-David (1995) was one of the first to apply attribute-level
127   constraints by imposing monotonicity among the model’s predictors, and Cardoso & Sousa (2010)
128   extended this in an ordinal classification setting.
129
      One of the earliest hybrid approaches of imposed constraints was implemented by Lee & Kang
130
      (1990), who introduced constraints to deep learning architectures to approximate ordinary and
131
      partial differential equations. This approach laid the foundation for the general framework known as
132   physics-informed neural networks, proposed by Raissi et al. (2019), where constraints and boundary
133   conditions are incorporated (Rudin et al., 2022). Similarly, Christopher et al. (2024) integrated
134   constraints and physical principles to generative diffusion processes. To the best of our knowledge,
135   our proposed approach is the first method designed to handle target-level constraints in multi-target
136   regression. This represents a novel contribution, building on the principles of constraint handling and
137   extending them to more complex, interdependent target structures in predictive modeling.
138
139   Contributions. We introduce novel approaches to handle constraints among target variables in DTs.
140   The first method lends itself to an optimization-based exact method that ensures the feasibility of
141
      predictions by incorporating target constraints into the splitting procedure of common binary tree
      construction methods. We also propose two heuristic methods. These methods offer practical solutions
142
      to the computation time issue that might be associated with the exact approach, especially for deep
143
      trees. We provide the technical details of our proposed methods, exploring their strengths, potential
144   limitations, and the conditions under which they would perform sensibly. To validate our approaches,
145   we provide a reproducible computational study using our publicly available implementation.

      This section is dedicated to testing the performance of our exact and heuristic approaches proposed
273   for training output-constrained decision trees (OCDTs). We obtain different implementation variants
274   by using one of the proposed approaches at a decision node for splitting (S) or at a leaf (L) node for
275   prediction. We also use the shorthand notation O, R, M, A for the exact optimization, relaxation,
276   medoid, and average1 approaches, respectively. For instance, the variant that uses relaxation ap-
277   proach for splitting and the medoid approach for prediction is denoted by S(R):L(M). Likewise,
278   S(A):L(A) simply refers to standard DT implementation in software packages that ignores the
279   constraints. In our study, we have used the scikit-learn implementation only for this variant
280   (Pedregosa et al., 2011).
281   Our objective in this section is two-fold: First, we aim to assess the effectiveness of our proposed
282   approaches on an existing dataset. Second, we aim to evaluate their performance in a controlled
283   setting using an extended version of the same dataset. Our computational experiments can be
284   reproduced by using the dedicated GitHub repo 2 . For all methods compared, we experimented with
285   varying maximum depth values of 3, 6, 9, 12, and 15, while ensuring a minimum of 10 data points to
286   split a node and at least 5 data points in each leaf. The selection of penalty coefficients, ϱ, for each
287
      variant can be guided by evaluating several values over a reasonable range through cross-validation.
      Ideally, the chosen coefficient should minimize MSE while ensuring that no infeasible predictions
288
      are generated. The results were obtained using a computer equipped with an Intel(R) Core(TM)
289
      i7-9750H CPU 2.60GHz processor, featuring 12 CPUs, and 64GB of RAM.

293   As a part of our computational study, we employ the scores dataset available online (Kimmons, 2012).
294   This dataset consists of 1000 records, simulating various personal and socio-economic factors of
295   students at a public school. The dataset features three target variables, representing math, reading, and
296   writing scores. To tailor the data to our research objectives, we have imposed two constraints: First,
297   if the reading score falls below 50, the writing score is set to zero. Second, if the combined reading
298   and writing scores amount to less than 110, the math score is also set to zero. The full description of
299   our mathematical model is available in Section B of the supplementary file.
300   We conducted a thorough five-fold cross-validation experiment on the dataset using five different
301   tree-depth parameter values, employing a total of nine distinct variants which are trained with the
302   same parameters. These variants are classified into two primary components: split evaluation and leaf
303   predictions. Split evaluation entails the assessment of each candidate for binary splitting based on the
304   prediction generated by a specific method. Conversely, leaf prediction refers to the prediction method
305   employed by each leaf node. For instance, the methodology involving the medoid split evaluation
306
      and optimal leaf prediction entails that each split during tree growth is determined by evaluating the
      total mean squared error (MSE) values of the resulting child nodes using their medoids as predictions.
307
      Subsequently, the resulting DT solves an optimization problem specific to the dataset for data falling
308
      to each leaf to assign the prediction of that particular node. The penalty coefficient ϱ is selected by
309   monitoring the trade-off between feasibility and accuracy through five-fold cross-validation.

459   4   C ONCLUSION
460
461   We have introduced new methods to handle constraints among target variables in decision trees, an area
462   yet unexplored in the existing literature. We have proposed an optimization-based exact approach and
463
      several heuristic approaches to ensure feasible predictions and maintaining computational efficiency.
      Through a computational study with existing and generated datasets, we have demonstrated the
464
      effectiveness of the proposed methods in producing feasible predictions, showcasing their potential
465
      for applications in multi-target learning.
466
467
      Limitations. The work presented here has a few limitations. First, the optimization-based exact
468   method can be computationally demanding for deep trees, which may impede its application for
469   larger datasets. Second, the heuristic approaches proposed may not always produce accurate or even
470   feasible predictions, limiting their reliability. Third, when the constraints among targets are highly
471   non-convex, solving the prediction problem at each node can prove challenging, potentially affecting
472   the overall performance of the decision tree. Finally, in its current form, it is unclear how these
473   output-constrained decision trees can serve as the base learners for ensemble methods.
474
475   Future research. We aim to investigate the integration of our approach into bagging methods
476   like random forests, as trees often act as base learners in these methods. We are also interested in
477   exploring a warm-start method in the exact approach, where an optimization problem needs solving
478
      at each node. Given that these nodes share a parent-child relationship, the subsets they create could
      aid in devising this method for subsequent problems at the child nodes once the parent node is
479
      resolved. We are also considering the inclusion of target constraints in optimization-based algorithms
480
      for training trees like, for example, optimal classification trees (Bertsimas & Dunn, 2017). Although
481   these algorithms can currently handle smaller problem sizes compared to standard implementations
482   like CART (Breiman et al., 1984), this is a promising research direction. We are also looking into the
483   potential advantages of considering constraints in clustering, since training a decision tree can be
484   seen as a supervised clustering method.
