011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Text-driven video editing utilizing generative diffusion models has garnered sig-
014            nificant attention due to their potential applications. However, existing approaches
015
               are constrained by the limited word embeddings provided in pre-training, which
               hinders nuanced editing targeting open concepts with specific attributes. Di-
016
               rectly altering the keywords in target prompts often results in unintended dis-
017
               ruptions to the attention mechanisms. To achieve more flexible editing eas-
018            ily, this work proposes an improved concept-augmented video editing approach
019            that generates diverse and stable target videos flexibly by devising abstract con-
020            ceptual pairs. Specifically, the framework involves concept-augmented textual
021            inversion and a dual prior supervision mechanism. The former enables plug-
022            and-play guidance of stable diffusion for video editing, effectively capturing
023            target attributes for more stylized results. The dual prior supervision mecha-
024            nism significantly enhances video stability and fidelity. Comprehensive evalu-
025            ations demonstrate that our approach generates more stable and lifelike videos,
026
               outperforming state-of-the-art methods. The anonymous code is available at
               https://anonymous.4open.science/w/STIVE-PAGE-B4D4/.

029   1   INTRODUCTION
030
031   Text-driven video editing using generative diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020;
032   Rombach et al., 2021) has garnered significant attention due to its potential applications in various
033   fields, including film production, art, and advertising (Ho et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2022; Blattmann
034   et al., 2023).
035
      Existing text-driven video editing methods based on diffusion models, such as Tune-A-Video (Wu
036   et al., 2023), FateZero (Qi et al., 2023), Zeroscope (Sterling, 2023), and VideoComposer (Wang
037   et al., 2024), have significantly improved the ability to edit objects, backgrounds, and styles in video
038   scenes while maintaining overall scene consistency through the optimization of attention mech-
039   anisms and spatiotemporal continuity. These approaches have demonstrated notable success in
040   video generation. However, they are often limited by the restricted word embeddings provided
041   by CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) during the text-driven encoding process, which restricts their ability
042   to perform diverse and nuanced edits on targets with specific attributes. Modifying words in the
043   target prompt can disrupt the attention mechanisms, leading to inconsistencies in non-target areas
044
      before and after the editing process.
045   To address these limitations, recent methods such as (Bar-Tal et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023; Chai
046   et al., 2023) have explored the use of Neural Layered Atlases (NLA) (Kasten et al., 2021). These
047   methods primarily focus on extracting layered atlases from video frames, editing these atlases us-
048   ing text-driven image-editing diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023), and then
049   synthesizing the final edited video through post-processing. While this approach is effective at pre-
050
      serving non-edited background areas, it exhibits poor performance in maintaining spatio-temporal
      continuity. Moreover, the processing of individual image frames makes the generation of neural
051
      atlases extremely time-consuming.
052
053   To achieve more diverse editing results easily, one feasible approach is to draw inspiration from
      the Textual Inversion (Gal et al., 2022) method used in image generation by incorporating external

054
      concept word embeddings. In text-to-image generation, external word embeddings (referred to as
055   ”concepts”) are optimized within the CLIP text encoder (Radford et al., 2021) while freezing the
056   diffusion model’s parameters. This allows the model to address the need for user-provided custom
057   images to guide image editing. However, using a pre-trained diffusion model for self-supervised
058   textual inversion presents limitations in the expressive power of these external embeddings, which
059   are constrained by the size of the latent space and the number of training iterations, often leading to
060   under-fitting and restricted expressive capacity.
061   In this paper, we propose an improved concept-augmented video editing method. This approach
062   flexibly generates diverse and stable target videos by defining abstract conceptual pairs (concept
063   prompt and concept video) that describe the target scene. Specifically, we propose the Concept-
064   Augmented Textual Inversion method, which reliably and accurately captures the target attributes
065   in the user’s custom video. In addition, we also introduce a Dual Prior Supervision mechanism
066   that stabilizes the generated video by crossing the attention between the source and target, prevent-
067   ing attention dispersion caused by modifications to the target prompt. This mechanism effectively
068   improves the consistency of non-target areas before and after video editing, while also enriching the
069
      fidelity of the concepts in the edited results. Our key contributions are as follows:
070
             • We orchestrate a framework that allows users to extract concepts from custom videos to
071
               efficiently generate diverse edited videos through concept templates.
072
073
             • We propose a concept-augmented textual inversion method, which efficiently and stably
               extracts detailed attributes of the target in the user’s custom concept video and supports
074
               plug-and-play guiding of stable diffusion for video editing.
075
076          • We present a dual prior supervision mechanism, which effectively improves the consistency
077            and stability of video editing results.

079   2    RELATED WORK
080
081   Text-Driven Video Synthesis. A series of works based on diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song
082   et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2021) has made significant progress in text-driven image generation.
083   Subsequent efforts (Esser et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Blattmann et al., 2023) further achieve
084   text-driven video generation by extending existing image generation diffusion models. These works
085   commonly inherit the spatial parameters of UNet and fine-tune the newly added temporal modules
086   with large-scale video-text pair datasets to improve inter-frame stability for video synthesis. These
087
      works laid a good foundation for video editing with textual descriptions.
088   Text-Driven Video Editing. Current approaches for text-driven video editing mainly fall into three
089   categories: fine-tuning video generation models (Zhao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024), fine-tuning
090   image generation models extended with temporal modules (Wu et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2023), and
091   combining NLA (Kasten et al., 2021) with pre-trained image generation models (Bar-Tal et al.,
092   2022; Lee et al., 2023; Chai et al., 2023). MotionDirector (Zhao et al., 2023) improves the ability of
093
      editing camera and object motions by adding LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) to the attention modules of the
      pre-trained Zeroscope (Sterling, 2023), strengthening the connection between texts and motions in
094
      edited videos. VideoComposer (Wang et al., 2024) enhances inter-frame consistency by introducing
095
      a condition fusion module with spatial and temporal conditions such as motion vectors, depth maps,
096   and sketches. Recent advances have demonstrated various innovative approaches in these categories.
097   For example, (Ku et al., 2024) employs a pretrained model for diverse video editing tasks, while
098   GenVideo (Singer et al., 2025) utilizes a target-image-aware approach with novel InvEdit masks
099   to overcome text-prompt limitations. Besides, (Singer et al., 2025) introduces the EVE model by
100   distilling pretrained diffusion models. (Bar-Tal et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023; Chai et al., 2023) extract
101   layered neural atlases from video frames to edit atlases which are further processed to synthesize
102   videos; however, generating a neural atlas demands considerable computational time.
103   Recently, Tune-A-Video (Wu et al., 2023) achieves one-shot video editing with improved inter-
104   frame coherency by updating self-attention with sparse causal attention. FateZero (Qi et al., 2023)
105   further proposes self-attention blending and incorporates attention control (Hertz et al., 2023) to
106   enhance the ability of editing objects, background, and styles while maintaining scene consistency.
107   For temporal consistency specifically, VidToMe merges self-attention tokens across frames, while
      (Geyer et al., 2023) leverages inter-frame correspondences to propagate features. In spatial editing,

      approaches like (Ceylan et al., 2023; Cohen et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024) improve results using spa-
109   tial or temporal attention features and diffusion models. For editing targets with specific attributes, it
110   becomes necessary to introduce external word embeddings. Our method supports the incorporation
111   of external concept word embeddings. Furthermore, inspired by Tune-A-Video (Wu et al., 2023) and
112   FateZero (Qi et al., 2023), we introduce a dual prior supervision mechanism between video frame
113   latents and word embeddings to enhance scene consistency before and after video editing based
114   on the prompt-to-prompt attention control method. Compared to existing approaches, our method
115   focuses on attention supervision and control mechanisms and operates on a one-shot video editing
116   paradigm. It also improves temporal consistency through extended temporal module parameters and
117
      enables the flexible integration of external concept objects, while CLIP-based (Radford et al., 2021)
      methods above are constrained by finite word embeddings.
118
119   Textual Inversion. (Gal et al., 2022) proposes a textual inversion method that optimizes newly
120   added concept word embeddings in the CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) text encoder, supervised by the
121   latent variable distribution of specific images in the diffusion model. However, using a pre-trained
122   diffusion model for self-supervised text inversion may lead to under-fitting for some specific images
123
      due to the finite latent variable space. Although it’s feasible to optimize concept word embeddings
      with a smaller learning rate simultaneously, or to train the diffusion model with frozen concept
124
      embeddings in the next stage, this process faces issues of easy over-fitting and high storage costs.
125
      Our method, building upon textual inversion (Gal et al., 2022), attempts to add LoRA (Hu et al.,
126   2022) modules to the diffusion model, optimizing them simultaneously with concept words at a
127   smaller learning rate, to enhance the text editing capabilities of concept words.
128
129
      Cross Attention Control and Supervision. Prompt-to-Prompt (Hertz et al., 2023) proposes three
      attention control methods for stable text-driven image editing based on diffusion models: word swap,
130
      refinement, and reweighting. By applying the cross-attention probability map recorded from the
131
      original text and original image latent variables to the denoising process of edited text and original
132   image latent variables, it has achieved significant success in stable text-driven image editing (Avra-
133   hami et al., 2022; 2023). Additionally, (Qi et al., 2023) proposed self-attention blend effectively
134   transfers the stability of text-driven image editing to the video editing domain. Our method, build-
135   ing upon this foundation, introduces external concept words to support editing with higher degrees of
136   freedom. However, when performing text-driven editing, whether using existing word embeddings
137   or external concept word embeddings as editing words, there exists a problem of attention disper-
138   sion. This means that editing words have non-negligible effects on latent variables other than the
139   editing target. Inspired by the work of (Yang & Tang, 2022), we introduce an attention supervision
140
      mechanism to address the issue of dispersed attention in editing words.

      3.2      S TABILIZED T EXT-D RIVEN V IDEO E DITING
197
      Our training and inference pipelines are visualized in Fig. 1. We adopt a UNet that inherits the pre-
198   trained 2D UNet parameters from Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2021) as the noise prediction
199   network. The original 2D UNet consists of a series of spatial convolution layers and cross-attention
200   layers. To adapt it for 3D video inputs, we replace the original spatial self-attention layers with
201   spatio-temporal attention as explained in Eq. (3). Following FateZero (Qi et al., 2023), we also
202   incorporate LoRA-structured temporal convolution layers after the spatial convolution layers, and
203   temporal self-attention layers with zero-initialized linear output layers after the cross-attention lay-
204   ers. The outputs of these newly added modules are residually connected to the outputs of the original
205   modules.
206   Our approach for stabilized text-driven video editing has two learning phases. In the first phase, we
207   introduce Concept-Augmented Textual Inversion (CATI) to adapt the diffusion model to new visual
208   concepts. In the second phase, we tune partial parameters of the temporally extended diffusion
209   model to suppress unintended changes in edited videos by calibrating cross-attention results.
210   Concept-Augmented Textual Inversion. Textual inversion (Gal et al., 2022) learns to represent
211   a specific set of user-provided images with pseudo-words in the latent space, offering an intuitive
212   way for natural language-guided image editing. We incorporate this technique into our framework
213   to facilitate video editing. However, due to the self-supervised nature within the limited latent space
214   of the pre-trained diffusion model, the vanilla textual inversion often results in varied performance
215   in terms of quality and efficiency for different image sets, requiring meticulous adjustments for
      learning rates and iteration counts.

      Model Tuning with Dual Prior Supervision. After learning concept-augmented textual inversion,
248

249
      of few-shot learning. Specifically, we learn the LoRA-structured temporal convolution layers, the
250   query projection weights within spatio-temporal attention layers and cross-attention layers, and the
251   temporal self-attention layers (see red blocks in Fig. 1). These parameters are selected for updates
252   during training due to their strong relevance to the temporal modeling of 3D videos. To attain more
253   stable and higher quality editing results, we tried directly integrating existing attention control tech-
254   niques (Hertz et al., 2023) in an early attempt; however, we found that when applying text-driven

258

      herence in terms of the Frame Consistency Metric. To evaluate the consistency of unrelated areas
345
      before and after video editing, we employ M-PSNR as a reference metric, and our method achieves
346
      the highest score by a large margin. Concretely, our method outperforms MotionDirector (Zhao
347   et al., 2023) by a noticeable 6.98 M-PSNR in editing with concept video. This is attributed to our
348   proposed prior supervision mechanism, which effectively reduces the editing noise in non-target ar-
349   eas for both source and concept videos. Furthermore, to evaluate the target fidelity in concept and
350   edited videos, we utilize Concept Consistency as a reference metric, and our method demonstrates
351   greater fidelity compared to others.
352
      Qualitative Evaluation. The visual comparison results of video editing with and without concept
353   video guidance are shown in Fig. 3. As can be seen, our method can maintain content consistency in
354   non-target areas before and after video editing with and without concept videos. Particularly, when
355   using concept videos, our method can effectively introduce the visual concept from the concept
356   video into the edited video. For example, in Fig. 3 (Setting I), our method successfully replaces
357   ‘man’ with the concept ‘$OPTIMUS’, while others either fail to preserve the background or cannot
358   transfer the integral target shape.
359   On the other hand, other approaches commonly face instability in non-target areas of their edited
360   videos. Tune-A-Video (Wu et al., 2023) encounters the issue of dispersed cross-attention between
361   word embeddings and video latents as it fine-tunes the model using only one video-text pair. While
362   FateZero (Qi et al., 2023) and RAVE (Kara et al., 2024) mitigate this issue by manipulating cross-
363   attention matrices or shuffling noise in a zero-shot manner, these methods directly use concepts to
364   drive video editing, which results in compromised non-target area consistency and degraded concept
365   fidelity. MotionDirector (Zhao et al., 2023) naturally supports extracting targets from concept videos
366   via its trainable spatial path; however, the coupled spatial and temporal paths struggle to provide
367
      stable guidance, leading to noticeable inconsistencies in non-target areas. In contrast, our proposed
      concept-augmented textual inversion and dual prior supervision can effectively maintain content
368
      consistency in non-target areas before and after video editing while accurately capturing specific
369
      attributes of user-provided concepts.

      The concept augmentation provides more detailed features to the target, significantly improving the

      5   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
520
521
      Mismatch when Significant Deformation. Although our proposed method effectively mitigates
522   the inconsistency in non-target areas caused by attention dispersion in video editing methods us-
523   ing attention replacement mechanisms, it may struggle when a single concept video guides target
524   replacement in cases of significant deformation in the source video, such as running people. For in-
525   stance, there may be insufficient detailed correspondences between the internal parts of the replacing
526   and replaced targets during deformation, such as moving arms and legs. Potential solutions include
527   ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) and OpenPose (Cao et al., 2019), which utilize motion conditions,
528   like human pose, to guide the video editing process.
529
530   6   C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this paper, we propose an improved concept-augmented video editing approach that generates
533   diverse and stable target videos flexibly by devising abstract conceptual pairs. Specifically, the
534   framework introduces a Concept-Augmented Textual Inversion (CATI), which extracts the target
535   concept from user-customized videos. In practice, CATI enables plug-and-play guidance of stable
536   diffusion for video shaping, effectively capturing target attributes for more stylized editing results.
537   In addition, a Dual Prior Supervision (DPS) mechanism is designed to prevent unintended changes
538   in non-target visual areas by crossing the attention between the sources and targets. Experimental
539
      results demonstrate that our method significantly improves the flexibility, consistency, and stability
      of text-guided video editing.
