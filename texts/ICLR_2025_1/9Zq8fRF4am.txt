011                                             A BSTRACT
012
013           Commercial recommender systems face the challenge that task requirements from
014           platforms or users often change dynamically (e.g., varying preferences for accu-
015
              racy or diversity). Ideally, the model should be re-trained after resetting a new
              objective function, adapting to these changes in task requirements. However, in
016
              practice, the high computational costs associated with retraining make this process
017
              impractical for models already deployed to online environments. This raises a new
018           challenging problem: how to efficiently adapt the learning model to different task
019           requirements by controlling model parameters after deployment, without the need
020           for retraining. To address this issue, we propose a novel controllable learning
021           approach via Parameter Diffusion for controllable multi-task Recommendation
022           (PaDiRec), which allows the customization and adaptation of recommendation
023           model parameters to new task requirements without retraining. Specifically, we
024           first obtain the optimized model parameters through adapter tunning based on the
025           feasible task requirements. Then, we utilize the diffusion model as a parame-
026
              ter generator, employing classifier-free guidance in conditional training to learn
              the distribution of optimized model parameters under various task requirements.
027
              Finally, the diffusion model is applied to effectively generate model parameters
028
              in a test-time adaptation manner given task requirements. As a model-agnostic
029           approach, PaDiRec can leverage existing recommendation models as backbones
030           to enhance their controllability. Extensive experiments on public datasets and a
031           dataset from a commercial app, indicate that PaDiRec can effectively enhance
032           controllability through efficient model parameter generation. The code is released
033           at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/PaDiRec-DD13e.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Traditional recommender systems are usually designed to improve accuracy by analyzing user be-
039
      haviors and contextual data to uncover users’ potential interests and preferences (Kang & McAuley,
      2018; Hidasi et al., 2016). Nowadays, recommendation models place greater emphasis on multi-
040
      ple important aspects of the recommended results (also called multi-task recommendation), such as
041
      diversity (Xia et al., 2017), fairness (Oosterhuis, 2021), etc. Existing multi-task recommendation
042   models are typically static (Zhang & Yang, 2021; Sener & Koltun, 2018), meaning that the pref-
043   erence weights for each aspect (e.g., accuracy or diversity) are predefined and fixed during both
044   training and testing. Once the static preference weights are determined, the training process can
045   employ various optimization algorithms to find the optimal solution.
046
      However, in practical scenarios, the preference weights for different aspects often change dynami-
047
      cally across both context and time. From a commercial perspective, different application scenarios
048   may require varying preference weights for different performance aspects of the recommendation
049   model to meet specific business needs. For instance, the checkout page emphasizes product diver-
050   sity, while the product detail page prioritizes accuracy by recommending similar items. From the
051   users’ perspective, different user groups may have distinct preferences, and even the same users
052   may have changing information needs over time. For example, a user may prefer highly accurate
053   recommendations when browsing a specific item category, but over time, such precision might di-
      minish their interest, prompting a preference for more diverse categories. To address the above


                                                      1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      dynamic information needs of users or platforms, this paper focuses on enhancing the controlla-
055   bility of recommendation models at test time, specifically in the context of controllable multi-task
056   recommendation.
057
058
      Traditional multi-task learning approaches face challenges in addressing the issue of dynamically
      changing preference weights. More specifically, when preference weights change, they require re-
059
      setting the objective function, re-training the recommendation model based on the new objective,
060
      and then redeploying the updated model. However, while this approach enables the integration of
061   various optimization methods, the retraining process is highly time- and resource-intensive, render-
062   ing it impractical — especially since rapid response time is critical during online recommendation
063   phases. For instance, during promotional events, commercial stores often require real-time flow
064   control to adjust their recommendation strategies, with changes ideally implemented immediately.
065   Several studies have recognized the importance of dynamically adjusting models based on changing
066   preferences. Wortsman et al. (2022) used simple parameter merging across multiple task-specific
067   models, and Chen et al. (2023) employed discriminative models to generate parameters for multi-
068   task re-ranking problem. While they reduce response time and aim to enhance control over the
069
      model, they struggle with approximating the optimal model (which we assume can be achieved
      through retraining with given preference weights), potentially leading to suboptimal solutions. To
070
      achieve both efficient test-time adaptation to changing preferences and preserve the approximate op-
071
      timal performance that retraining offers, we leverage the strengths of diffusion models in generating
072   high-performance model parameters (Schürholt et al., 2022; Knyazev et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024)
073   for recommendation model. Additionally, we utilize conditional control (Ho & Salimans, 2022) to
074   ensure controllability at test-time with changing preference weights as conditions.
075
      In this work, we propose a novel parameter generation approach for controllable multi-task recom-
076
      mendation by leveraging a generative model to efficiently generate task-specific model parameters at
077
      test time based on varying task requirements (i.e., the preference weights for different performance
078   metrics), effectively addressing the challenges posed by rapidly changing requirements and the high
079   cost of retraining models. The proposed approach, termed PaDiRec, begins by formulating an ob-
080   jective function aligned with task-specific preference weights, and through advanced optimization
081   techniques, we fine-tune model parameters using adapter tuning. We then train a diffusion model
082   to learn the conditional distribution of these optimized adapter parameters under various task re-
083   quirements, where the classfier-free guidance training strategy is employed to perform conditional
084   training. Once trained, during online testing, the diffusion model can generate task-specific adapter
085   parameters with the task requirement as condition, which can be integrated with different sequen-
086
      tial recommendation backbones to produce recommendation lists that meet the specified require-
      ments. Additionally, PaDiRec is both model-agnostic and algorithm-agnostic, making it flexible
087
      and compatible with various recommendation models and optimization strategies. We summarize
088
      our contributions as follows:
089
090          • We formally define the problem of controllable multi-task recommendation (CMTR),
091            which focuses on the model’s ability to adapt to dynamic changes in preferences for differ-
092            ent metrics during online testing.
093          • We present PaDiRec, a diffusion model-based approach that generates model parameters
094            conditioned on task-specific preference weights, providing enhanced control and flexibility
095            by controlling model parameters in multi-task learning settings.
096          • Extensive experiments on two public datasets and an industrial dataset demonstrate that
097            PaDiRec achieves superior performance towards controllability of multi-task recommen-
098            dation while retaining recommendation performances.

128   straightforward method would be to store N sets of task-specific parameters corresponding to the
129   preference weights for N tasks at the time of deployment, and load them when a new task arises at
130   test time. However, when considering a continuous preference weight space where the number of
131   tasks N tends to infinity (i.e., a continuous task space), this discrete method becomes impractical
132   due to storage limitations and cannot accommodate fine-grained or continuous task variations.

135   new tasks. More specifically, we treat the preference weights as variables and model the relationship

137   forming the time- and resource-intensive retraining problem at test time into an efficient inference
138   problem. Formally, we aim to find a function gξ : W → Θ (where ξ denotes the parameter of g) that

      In contrast to traditional multi-task recommendation (MTR), which focuses only on fixed prefer-
      ence weights for different utilities, our defined CMTR emphasizes how the model adapts to dynamic
145
      changes in preference weights after deployment. This shift means that in traditional MTR, each task
146
      corresponds to a single utility, whereas in CMTR, each task is associated with multiple utilities com-
147   bined through a linear weighting, with combination coefficients determined by a set of task-specific
148   preference weights. As a result, CMTR places greater emphasis on test-time adaption to handle
149   dynamic task requirements, introducing new challenges for CMTR model training and construction
150   compared to MTR.

194   3.2       P REPARATION OF A DAPTERS
195
196   Our goal is to construct the parameters of optimized recommendation models under different pref-
197   erence weights to prepare data for the generative model. Thus, this section is organized into three
198   parts: the structure of the recommendation model, the construction of task-specific objective func-
199   tions, and the tuning process for the recommendation model parameters.
200
      Model structure. As shown in the left module of Figure 1, sequential recommendation models take
201   user history and candidate items as input. Guided by the objective function (i.e., loss function), the
202   model learns the underlying relationships within the user history, ultimately generating a recom-
203   mendation list (i.e., Rec. List) from the candidate items. Existing recommender systems based on
204   deep neural networks can be quite large, and making significant invasive modifications typically re-
205   quires retraining the entire model, which is often prohibitively expensive in industrial applications.
206   To address this, our approach introduces an adapter module, which can be seamlessly integrated into
207   existing sequential recommendation models. Specifically, we incorporate the adapter using a resid-
208   ual connection, attaching it to the last layer of the backbone model. In this setup, the backbone is
209
      set to retain the original recommendation capabilities, while the adapter is responsible for adapting
      to specific tasks.
210
211   Objective function construction. To obtain the optimized task-specific adapter parameters under
212   CMTR setting (as shown in Sec. 2 ), we first focus on the construction of loss functions based on
213   different preference weights of each task. Specifically, we directly convert the reward maximization
214   problem (reward defined in Eq. (2)) into a loss minimization problem. Given a specific set of pref-
215   erence weights wi = {wij }pj=1 ∈ W, which represent preference weight for the j-th utility in the
      requirement of task i. Here, we focus on two utilities including diversity loss ℓdiversity and accuracy

348   Dataset. We used two public datasets, MovieLens 1M 1 and Amazon Food 2 , and the
349   Industrial Data from a electronics commercial store. Detailed descriptions of the datasets
350
      and preprocessing methods can be found in Appendix A.3.
351   Baselines. The baselines are as follows. Retraining as Eq. (2), which is considered optimal.
352   Soup (Wortsman et al., 2022), a classic algorithm for model merging. MMR (Carbonell & Goldstein,
353   1998), a rule-based post-process policy. CMR (Chen et al., 2023), an re-rank algorighm utilizing
354   hypernetwork to achieve dynamic preference of changing. LLM (Appendix A.11), a prompt-based
355   method for controllable recommendation. Details about all the baselines are shown in Appendix A.4
356   Metrics. We propose evaluating performance from two dimensions. Specifically, we use Hyper-
357   volume (HV) (Guerreiro et al., 2021) to measure the performance of the algorithm on each task,
358   particularly in terms of the trade-offs between accuracy and diversity. The average HV (denoted as
359   Avg.HV) across multiple tasks is used to assess the overall performance of the algorithm in balanc-
360   ing both objectives (accuracy and diversity). To eliminate the differences in scale between the two
361   objectives, we normalize the performance on each objective. Additionally, we utilize the Pearson
362
      correlation coefficient to evaluate the alignment between the algorithm’s performance across differ-
      ent tasks and the optimal model, providing insight into the algorithm’s controllability. Pearson r-a,
363
      Pearson r-d measure the correlation between the algorithm and the optimal in terms of accuracy
364
      (NDCG@10) and diversity (α-NDCG@10).

516
      Multi-task learning (MTL) aims to develop unified models that tackle multiple learning tasks si-
517
      multaneously while facilitating information sharing (Zhang & Yang, 2021; Ruder, 2017). Recent
518
      advancements in MTL include deep networks with various parameter sharing mechanisms (Misra
519   et al., 2016; Long et al., 2017; Yang & Hospedales, 2016) and approaches treating MTL as a multi-
520   objective optimization problem (Lin et al., 2019; Mahapatra & Rajan, 2020; Xie et al., 2021). These
521   latter methods focus on identifying Pareto-efficient solutions across tasks, with significant applica-
522   tions in recommender systems (Jannach, 2022; Li et al., 2020a; Zheng & Wang, 2022) Researchers
523   have explored different strategies, from alternating optimization of joint loss and individual task
524   weights to framing the process as a reinforcement learning problem (Xie et al., 2021). The emphasis
525   has shifted from optimizing specific preference weights to finding weights that achieve Pareto effi-
526   ciency across objectives (Sener & Koltun, 2018; Lin et al., 2019). Some methods utilize attention
527
      mechanisms to dynamically allocate computational resources among tasks (Liu et al., 2019). More
      recent approaches, such as the CMR (?), utilize hypernetworks to learn the entire trade-off curve for
528
      MTL problems. However, our novel approach diverges from these existing methods by employing
529
      diffusion models to control model parameters at test time, potentially offering greater flexibility and
530   adaptability in handling multi-task learning problems.

532   7   C ONCLUSIONS
533
534   This paper addresses the critical challenge of adapting recommendation models to dynamic task
535   requirements in real-world applications, where frequent retraining is impractical due to high com-
536   putational costs. To tackle this problem, we propose PaDiRec, a novel controllable learning ap-
537   proach that enables efficient adaptation of model parameters without retraining by utilizing a diffu-
538   sion model as a parameter generator. Our approach is model-agnostic, allowing it to integrate with
539   existing recommendation models and enhance their controllability. PaDiRec provides a practical
      solution for real-time, customizable recommendations in achieving efficient, test-time adaptation.

      A.4      BASELINES
774
775   PadiRec was compared with several algorithms that were constructed in the controllable multi-task
776   recommendation scenarios, including: Retraining is performed using Linear Scalarization (Birge
777   & Louveaux, 2011) based on each task description, with the assumption that the resulting model
778   parameters represent the optimal solution. Soup (Wortsman et al., 2022) obtain a new model by av-
779   eraging the parameters of fine-tuned models without requiring additional computation during infer-
780   ence. In our work, we fine-tuned two models on accuracy and diversity respectively, and then merged
781   them linearly based on the task description. MMR (Carbonell & Goldstein, 1998) is a heuristic post-
782   processing approach with the item selected sequentially according to maximal marginal relevance.
783
      We set the hyper-parameters based on the task description to achieve varying degrees of diversity
      in the recommendations. CMR (Chen et al., 2023) dynamically adjusts models based on prefer-
784
      ence weights using policy hypernetworks to generate model parameters. LLM (the prompt is shown
785
      in A.11) is utilized as a personalized recommender system. We achieve controllable recommen-
786   dations by inputting prompts containing specific preference weights to respond to users’ real-time
787   preferences. For our experiments, we selected the llama3-7B-Instruct model. Details regarding the
788   prompts and settings can be found in the appendix.

791
792   Pre Conditioning (i.e., Pre cond.) “Pre” denotes that the preference weights embeddings are inte-
793   grated into the parameters embeddings before being fed into self-attention layers. In this method,
794   we simply add the preference weight embeddings to the parameters embeddings within the input
795   sequence.
796   Pre and Post Conditioning (i.e., Pre&Post) “Post” denotes that the preference weights embeddings
797   are intergeted after the parameters embeddings fed into self-attention layers.In this method, we add
798   the preference weights embeddings both “Pre” and “Post”.

       A.10    N ETWORK L AYERS OF R ECOMMENDATION M ODELS
1135
1136   In our experiments, we implement our framework on three recommendation models, SASRec (Kang
1137   & McAuley, 2018), GRU4Rec (Hidasi, 2015), and TiSASRec (Li et al., 2020b). We provide their
1138   details of parameter structure in Tables 4, 5, and 6 respectively.
1139   SASRec (Kang & McAuley, 2018) Self-Attentive Sequential Recommendation. This model em-
1140   ploys a Transformer architecture to model user sequences for personalized recommendation tasks.
1141   It utilizes self-attention mechanisms that capture both long and short-term preferences by attending
1142
       differently to items based on their relevance.
1143   GRU4Rec (Hidasi, 2015) Gated Recurrent Units for Recommendation Systems. GRU4Rec lever-
1144   ages gated recurrent units (GRUs) to model user interaction sequences for session-based recommen-
1145   dations. By utilizing a gating mechanism, it effectively captures dependencies across varying time
1146   gaps between interactions, making it robust to session shifts and dropout behaviors.
1147   TiSASRec (Li et al., 2020b) Time Interval-Aware Self-Attention for Sequential Recommendation.
1148   This model extends SASRec by incorporating time intervals between user interactions as an ad-
1149   ditional context. TiSASRec modifies the self-attention mechanism to account for these intervals,
1150   providing a more nuanced understanding of user preferences that evolve over time. The model
1151   includes a specialized positional encoding scheme to integrate these time dynamics alongside the
1152   sequential user behaviors.
1153    LRURec (Yue et al., 2024) Linear Recurrent Units for Sequential Recommendation. This model
1154   introduces a novel linear recurrent unit architecture tailored for sequential recommendation tasks.
1155   LRURec combines the efficiency of recurrent neural networks with the modeling capabilities of
1156   self-attention mechanisms, enabling rapid inference and incremental updates on sequential data.
1157   By decomposing linear recurrence operations and implementing recursive parallelization, LRURec
1158   achieves reduced model size and parallelizable training.

1609   A.15     D ISCUSSION ON USED M OVIELENS E VALUATION
1610
1611   We utilized the MovieLens dataset for our experiments. MovieLens is among the most widely used
1612   datasets in the recommender systems domain, serving as a standard benchmark for validating new
1613   models and ensuring reproducibility. However, it is important to acknowledge that the user-item
1614   interactions recorded in the MovieLens dataset primarily reflect engagements between users and
1615   the MovieLens platform, where users are prompted to recall movies they have previously watched.
1616
       This setup differs significantly from typical recommendation scenarios encountered in real-world
       applications, as analysis by Fan et al. (2024).
1617
1618   Nonetheless, the MovieLens dataset remains valuable for research purposes. It provides researchers
1619   with a standardized benchmark, facilitating the verification of model implementations and enabling
       comparisons with existing studies. Besides, it is important to note that, our focus is not on the
