009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Safe Reinforcement Learning from partial observations frequently struggles with
012            rapid performance degradation and often fails to satisfy safety constraints. Upon
013            deeper analysis, we attribute this problem to the lack of necessary information
014            in partial observations and inadequate sample efficiency. World Models can help
015            mitigate this issue, as they offer high sample efficiency and the capacity to memo-
016            rize historical information. In this work, we introduce AsymDreamer, an approach
017            based on the Dreamer framework that specializes in exploiting low-dimensional
018
               privileged information to build world models, thereby enhancing the prediction ca-
               pability of critics. To ensure safety, we employ the Lagrangian method to incorpo-
019
               rate safety constraints. Additionally, we formulate our approach as an Asymmetric
020
               CPOMDPs (ACPOMDPs) framework and analyze its superiority compared to the
021            standard CPOMDP framework. Various experiments conducted on the Safety-
022            Gymnasium benchmark demonstrate that our approach outperforms existing ap-
023            proaches dramatically in terms of performance and safety.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   As reinforcement learning (RL) has been successfully applied to various control problems Mnih
029   et al. (2015); Yu et al. (2019), ensuring safety is crucial for real-world deployment Dulac-Arnold
030   et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2021) . Given that partial observability is a fundamental aspect of real-world
031   RL control problems Baisero & Amato (2021), Safe Reinforcement Learning (SafeRL) must account
032   for partial observations. These problems are often formulated as Constrained Partially Observable
033
      Markov Decision Processes (CPOMDPs) Lee et al. (2018), where the agent operates based on a
      history of past observations and actions, without direct access to the true underlying states. Although
034
      significant research has been dedicated to addressing these challenges, most approaches either fail to
035
      strictly satisfy safety constraints or experience performance degradation due to insufficient critical
036   information and low sample efficiency.
037
038   Model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL)Hafner et al. (2019); Deisenroth & Rasmussen (2011)
039
      has shown promise in overcoming these challenges by utilizing a world model that captures environ-
      mental dynamics and generates task-specific predictions from past observations and actions. This
040
      allows agents to learn from imaginary rollouts, rather than relying solely on sampled real-world
041
      trajectories LeCun & Courant (2022), which enhances both sample efficiency and safety. However,
042   while the world model retains historical information, it does not fully resolve the issue of missing
043   critical information. MBRL typically combines the world model with actor-critic methods for policy
044   optimization. Unfortunately, the critic’s slow or inaccurate learning of value functions can create a
045   performance bottleneck for the policy.
046
      Since training is often conducted in simulators, there is potential to leverage privileged informa-
047
      tion during training to reduce uncertainty from partial observations Pinto et al. (2017); Salter et al.
048   (2021); Baisero & Amato (2021). Actor-critic methods, in particular, can handle asymmetric inputs,
049   where the actor receives historical information and the critic accesses privileged information such
050   as true states. This asymmetry is possible because the critic is used only during training and is not
051   required during the agent’s deployment. However, since the actor and critic share the same world
052   model, encoding privileged information into the model may cause the actor to become dependent
053   on it, conflicting with the requirement that the actor operates purely based on historical information
      during deployment.

054
      In this work, we address the challenge of exploiting asymmetric inputs in MBRL under the
055   CPOMDPs framework. We propose AsymDreamer, a novel algorithm that uses privileged informa-
056   tion to construct a world model specifically for the critic. Additionally, we formulate our approach
057   as the Asymmetric Constrained Partially Observable Markov Decision Processes (ACPOMDPs)
058   framework and demonstrate its theoretical advantages. Our key contributions are summarized as
059   follows:
060
061
             • We introduce the ACPOMDPs framework, an extension of the CPOMDPs that allows the
               actor and critic to receive asymmetric inputs. We theoretically prove that asymmetric inputs
062
               reduce the number of critic updates and lead to a more optimal policy compared to standard
063
               CPOMDPs framework.
064
065
             • We propose AsymDreamer, a novel MBRL approach that constructs two world models:
               one for the actor based on historical information, and another for the critic, which leverages
066
               privileged information.
067
068
             • We integrate AsymDreamer with the Lagrangian method Nocedal & Wright (2006); Li
               et al. (2021), achieving competitive performance on the Safety-Gymnasium benchmark Ji
069
               et al. (2023b) and demonstrating strong adaptability to complex scenarios.

074   2.1   S AFE M ODEL - BASED R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
075
      Model-based reinforcement learning (RL) approaches Moerland et al. (2022); Polydoros & Nalpan-
076
      tidis (2017) present significant advantages for solving safe RL problems by facilitating the model-
077   ing of environmental dynamics. These approaches can be classified into two primary categories:
078   planning-based methods Hafner et al. (2019) and learning-based methods Berkenkamp et al. (2017).
079   Planning-based methods do not directly incorporate costs into the policy update process; instead,
080   they implement an explicit planning step prior to action execution. In contrast, learning-based meth-
081   ods integrate costs directly into policy updates, utilizing the world model to enhance sample effi-
082   ciency.
083   Planning-based methods Among planning-based methods, Koller et al. (2019); Wabersich &
084   Zeilinger (2021); Zwane et al. (2023) enable safe action sampling through a combination of Gaussian
085   Processes and model predictive control (MPC). Additionally, Liu et al. (2020) employ ensembles of
086   neural networks (NN), the Cross Entropy Method (CEM) Kroese et al. (2006), and rejection sam-
087   pling to optimize the expected returns of safe action sequences. Recent work by (Huang et al., 2024)
088   has achieved zero-cost performance by integrating the constrained Cross-Entropy Method (CCEM)
089   Wen & Topcu (2018) while considering long-term rewards and costs. Nonetheless, planning-based
090   methods encounter challenges with myopic decisions due to the limited scope of planning and the
091
      absence of critics.
092   Learning-based methods Jayant & Bhatnagar (2022); Thomas et al. (2022) facilitate the integration
093   of model-free algorithms with safety constraints by employing ensemble Gaussian models. Alter-
094   natively, Zanger et al. (2021) use NNs and constrained model-based policy optimization. but do not
095   leverage model uncertainty within an optimistic-pessimistic framework. Recently, LAMBDA As
096   et al. (2022) integrate the Bayesian methods with the Dreamer Hafner et al. (2020) framework to
097
      quantify uncertainty in the estimated model, employing the Lagrangian method to incorporate safety
      constraints. Similarly, Safe-SLAC Hogewind et al. (2022) integrates the Lagrangian mechanism
098
      into the SLAC framework established Lee et al. (2020) to address the problem of safe reinforcement
099
      learning from pixel observations. However, from the perspective of partially observable Markov de-
100   cision processes (POMDPs) Kaelbling et al. (1998), constructing world models solely from partial
101   observations does not fully exploit the potential of these models.
102
103   2.2   L EVERAGING P RIVILEGED I NFORMATION
104
105   The use of asymmetric inputs is not uncommon in the single-agent domain. Pinto et al. (2017); Salter
106   et al. (2021); Baisero & Amato (2021) utilize asymmetric actor-critic methods to accelerate the
107   training of the critic by granting access to privileged information while providing only images to the
      actor. Baisero et al. (2022) introduce Asymmetric DQN, an asymmetric variant of DQN designed to


                                                        2
108
      address partially observable Markov decision processes (POMDPs).Yamada et al. (2023) represent
109   the first attempt to utilize privileged information in the training of world models. However, this
110   method employs privileged information by distilling the learned latent dynamics model from the
111   teacher to the student world model. Since this process of model distillation inevitably leads to a
112   loss of information, the current exploration of world models using privileged information remains
113   inadequate.

265   Trade-off Avoidance As illustrated in Figure 1, the observation world model focuses exclusively on
266   observation modeling, while the privileged world model emphasizes task-centric predictive capabil-
267   ities. By separating observation modeling from task-centric prediction modeling, we can avoid the
268   potential trade-off between these two tasks Ma et al. (2024). The specialization of the observation
269   and privileged world models allows each component to excel in its respective domain without com-
      promising the other. This synergistic approach ultimately results in improved overall performance.

      Information Sharing Mechanism This asymmetric training structure provides the privileged world
271   model with access to all the information from the observation world model. As shown in Figure 1,
272   the privileged world model enhances its reward decoder and cost decoder by utilizing the union
273   model state st = {sot , spt }. Experimental results indicate that while the privileged information
274   contains all the information necessary for predicting costs and rewards, the incorporation of local
275   observations continues to offer significant advantages.
276
      Information Maximization This asymmetric training structure enables both the observation and
277   privileged world models to capture the maximum amount of information. The observation world
278   model, which focuses exclusively on observation modeling, is designed to capture more detailed
279   observation information. Meanwhile, the privileged world model not only utilizes privileged in-
280   formation but also leverages the information from the observation world model. In addition, the
281   privileged world model can converge more rapidly due to the low dimensionality of the privileged
282   information, thereby accelerating the training of the critic model.
283   Generalizability Importantly, since most world models adhere to a common structural framework,

286   RSSM-based world models Hafner et al. (2019), and Transformer-based world models Chen et al.
287   (2022); van den Oord et al. (2018).

      8     C ONCLUSION
473
474   We present AsymDreamer, a model-based reinforcement learning approach specifically designed
475   for partially observable environments with safety constraints. AsymDreamer employs an asymmet-
476   ric architecture, where the actor constructs a world model based on the agent’s partial observations,
477
      while the critic leverages a privileged world model that incorporates additional privileged informa-
      tion. This approach is formalized within the Asymmetric Constrained Partially Observable Markov
478
      Decision Processes (ACPOMDP) framework, offering theoretical advantages in addressing the chal-
479
      lenges of partial observability and safety. To ensure compliance with safety constraints, Asym-
480   Dreamer integrates the Lagrangian method to handle constrained optimization problems. Asym-
481   Dreamer demonstrates competitive performance across benchmarks and exhibits strong adaptability
482   in complex scenarios. However, we have identified certain limitations in the current design. Specifi-
483   cally, some forms of privileged information do not significantly enhance the performance of the cost
484   predictor, limiting their overall contribution to the model. Given the critical importance of world
485   models, future work could explore techniques to train more robust models capable of effectively
      capturing sparse signals.

726

730   generated, the target is randomly generated with z-axis coordinates between 0.3 and 1.7.

      Baselines. We compared AsymDreamer to several competitive baselines to demonstrate the su-
953   perior results of using privileged information. The baselines include: 1. Dreamerv3: (Hafner
954   et al., 2023) A general algorithm that could master diverse domains with fixed hyperparameters.
955   2. BSRP Lag: (Huang et al., 2024) Integrates Dreamerv3 with the Lagrangian methods. 3. OSRP:
956   Integrates Dreamerv3 with the CCEM methods. 4. OSRP Lag: Integrates Dreamerv3 and the La-
957   grangian methods with the CCEM methods. 5. LAMBDA: (As et al., 2022) A novel model-based
958   approach utilizes Bayesian world models and the Lagrangian methods. 6. Safe-SLAC: (Hogewind
959   et al., 2022) Integrates SLAC with the Lagrangian methods. 7. CPO: Achiam et al. (2017) the first
960   general-purpose policy search algorithm for constrained reinforcement learning with guarantees for
961
      near-constraint satisfaction at each iteration. 8. PPO Lag: Achiam & Amodei (2019) Integrates
      PPO with the Lagrangian methods. 9. TRPO Lag: Integrates TRPO with the Lagrangian meth-
962
      ods. 10. FOCOPS: Zhang et al. (2020) initially determines the optimal update policy by address-
963
      ing a constrained optimization problem within the nonparameterized policy space. Subsequently,
964   FOCOPS projects the update policy back into the parametric policy space. Notably, the OSRP,
965   OSRP Lag and BSRP Lag are three algorithms proposed by SafeDreamer.
