009   Dramatic increases in the capabilities of neural network models in recent years are driven by scaling
010   model size, training data, and corresponding computational resources. To develop the exceedingly
011   large networks required in modern applications, such as large language models (LLMs), model
012   training is distributed across tens of thousands of hardware accelerators (e.g. GPUs), requiring
013   orchestration of computation and communication across large computing clusters. In this work,
014   we demonstrate that careful consideration of hardware configuration and parallelization strategy is
015   critical for effective (i.e. compute- and cost-efficient) scaling of model size, training data, and total
016
      computation. We conduct an extensive empirical study of the performance of large-scale LLM train-
      ing workloads across model size, hardware configurations, and distributed parallelization strategies.
017
      We demonstrate that: (1) beyond certain scales, overhead incurred from certain distributed com-
018
      munication strategies leads parallelization strategies previously thought to be sub-optimal in fact
019   become preferable; and (2) scaling the total number of accelerators for large model training quickly
020   yields diminishing returns even when hardware and parallelization strategies are properly optimized,
021   implying poor marginal performance per additional unit of power or GPU-hour.

088   2.1       ACCELERATORS AT S CALE
089
090   Large neural networks are trained in computing clusters consisting of thousands of interconnected
091   GPUs characterized by compute power, on-board memory, and interconnects. Several technologies
092   are used to interconnect GPUs (Recio et al., 2007; Shainer et al., 2011) — each have tradeoffs
093   between network size, bandwidth, latency, and cost. This results in a hierarchical partitioning of the
094   network, with communication within groups achieving higher bandwidths or lower latencies than
095
      communication across groups. In Figure 2, we provide an illustration of a sample architecture for a
      GPU-node based datacenter cluster.
096
097   For example, NVIDIA GPUs that are grouped together within a node (typically with 8 accelerators1 )
098   might be connected via technologies such as NVLink or NVSwitch (Nvidia, 2024), which can pro-
099   vide an order of magnitude higher bandwidth than the network fabric used to communicate across
100   nodes. Within the network itself, the topology of the switches can further favor the communication
101
      within certain groups of nodes, such as within racks or “rails.”
102   These accelerator characteristics – compute, memory and network – have evolved at different rates
103   over time based on technical barriers and demand for usage. In turn, neural architectures and soft-
104   ware systems have been designed to adapt to the limits of the hardware. With the increase in cluster
105   size driven by larger models and data, communication has often been such a bottleneck, and many
106   new training algorithms have thus emerged (Shazeer et al., 2017; Fedus et al., 2022).

124   2.2   A LGORITHMS FOR D ISTRIBUTED T RAINING
125
126   Most distributed training schemes optimize for transparency to practitioners, thus preserving the ab-
127   straction of single-device training, where one instance of a model processes a minibatch of data such
128   that every input sample interacts with every parameter of the model. A key decision in distributed
129   training is how to map model and data components onto GPU hardware: via replication or sharding.
130
      Communication between GPUs is required to aggregate values that must interact with each other or
      to preserve synchronization between replicas of the same underlying values.
131
132   Below, we provide a brief taxonomy of distributed training algorithms. These algorithms are not
133   mutually exclusive – distribution across multiple “dimensions” can be combined.
134   Data parallelism (Dean et al., 2012) consists of replicating model parameters (and optimizer states)
135   across GPUs, but shards input minibatches across devices. After performing local forward and back-
136   ward passes on their allocated minibatches, GPUs exchange and accumulate their partial gradients,
137   thus obtaining an identical global gradient and ensuring an identical model update. This communi-
138   cation pattern is named after the MPI (Walker & Dongarra, 1996) collective AllReduce.
139
      When models grow too large to fit on a single device, methods such as Fully-Sharded Data Paral-
140   lelism (FSDP) (Xu et al., 2020) and DeepSpeed-Zero (Rasley et al., 2020) shard optimizer states,
141   gradients, and model parameters across data parallel groups. However, since every input sample
142   must interact with every parameter, they are required to temporarily re-materialize each parame-
143   ter on-the-fly on all devices during the forward and backward passes. In contrast to communica-
144   tion of gradients, which can be performed concurrently with the backward pass before a model
145   update, “gathering” operations, which are required to gather parameters for the forward pass, de-
146   lay execution of computation. An inverse operation must be performed to partial gradients during
147   the backward pass to update parameters. These two MPI primitives are known AllGather and
148
      ReduceScatter.
149   Model parallelism shards model parameters across GPUs; each shard operates on the same mini-
150   batch simultaneously. In this setting, activations and their respective gradients are sent across GPUs.
151
        • Tensor Parallelism (Shoeybi et al., 2019; Shazeer et al., 2018) Some layers (e.g. linear) within
152
          models can be sharded along their hidden dimensions, leveraging linear algebra properties to
153       slice weight matrices in a way that maximizes data locality and allows for mostly independent
154       computation before a final AllReduce step to re-synchronize activations. As the full set of ac-
155       tivations are required for computation with the subsequent layer, Tensor Parallelism introduces
156       blocking communication for synchronization of interemediate activations across model parallel
157       groups. Sequence parallelism, for instance, is an analog of FSDP’s changes to data parallelism:
158       instead of replicating activations, they remain sharded and only get gathered and scattered as
159       needed Li et al. (2023).
160     • Pipeline Parallelism (Huang et al., 2018; Harlap et al., 2018) Models can also be sharded
161       along their layerwise depth, with layers being partitioned into “stages” and stored on different
          devices; activations are then forwarded from one device to the next while they traverse a model.

189   2.3       C OMMUNICATION P RIMITIVES AND L IBRARIES
190
      Modern deep learning frameworks (Paszke et al., 2019; Abadi et al., 2015; Bradbury et al., 2018)
191
      leverage specialized collective communications libraries, such as NCCL2 , RCCL3 or XLA4 .
192   These libraries may contain multiple algorithms for each collective communication primitive.
193   AllReduce being a reduction, has both a “ring” and a “tree” algorithm, the former being
194   bandwidth-balanced but suffering from latency increasing linearly with the number of devices, and
195   the latter being suboptimal in bandwidth utilization but logarithmic in latency. AllGather and
196   ReduceScatter, which are both used in parameter rematerialization for FSDP and DeepSpeed-
197   Zero, can only use ring algorithms as all buffers must be delivered to all devices – and quickly
198   become latency-bound as the number of devices increases, as shown in Figure 3.

      Not all FLOPs are equal. Existing compute-optimal scaling laws (Hoffmann et al., 2022a; Tay
487   et al., 2023; Dehghani et al.) and workload performance measures are based predominantly on
488   FLOPs or metrics derived therefrom. These fail to take into consideration underlying massively
489   parallelized distributed hardware which requires communication to execute these workload. Local
490   arithmetic throughput per-accelerator does not translate into end-to-end performance due to bound-
491   ing factors in other hardware components such as network fabric. Integrating holistic information
492   about hardware into scaling practice is essential given that collective communication dominates ex-
493   ecution time at scale; scaling laws should be both compute and communication optimal.
494
495   Communication-Computation Dynamics Change at Scale. In distributed training over large-
496   world sizes, the scaling properties of collective communication primitives leads to increased ex-
497   posed communication and communication-boundedness – which motivates the use of alternative
498   parallelization strategies beyond traditional data parallelism (see Figures 3, 5, 6). This motivates the
499   need for development of parallelization strategies sensitive to the marginal communication costs of
500   increasing world size.
501
502   Additional scale only marginally improves throughput. Capability and capacity tradeoffs at
503   scale for a fixed global batch size lead to declining marginal improvements — Figures 4 and 7 show
504   emergent upper bounds in the effectiveness of scale as related to model size. If the pace of increases
505   in model size slows — additional scale will do little to improve throughput given fixed recipes,
506
      further removing the incentive to scale up without algorithmic modifications.
507
508   Training one large model is less power-per-token efficient than training many smaller ones.
509   Given aforementioned ceilings in scale, algorithmic paradigms which train ensembles of multiple
510   smaller models will continue to proliferate, with hardware scaling serving growing the number of
511   models in the ensemble. Communication and computation must jointly improve to alleviate bottle-
512
      necks for large model training. The current imbalance in rates of improvement of communication
      and computation constrains new hardware’s utility. Figure 1 demonstrates that while power utiliza-
513
      tion increases linearly, hardware utilization and global throughput both increase sublinearly.
514
515
516   Improvements in networking within nodes improves scale-out performance. Inter-node band-
517
      width is lower as a result of constraints around network fabrics. While improving fabrics may
      improve performance, increasing node size – that is, building nodes with more accelerators with
518
      fast, local interconnects – also increases the total amount of memory and thus the upper bound for
519
      degrees of model parallelism. NVIDIA’s GB-2006 features the first increase in NVLink-connected
520   node-size since the DGX-1 P100 in 2017, from 8 to 72 accelerators, with a total of 1 TB of in-
521   terconnected GPU memory per node. Speedups in inter-node bandwidth and larger collections of
522   high-speed GPU memory will alleviate communication boundedness at large scales.
523
524
      Performance benchmarking fails to extrapolate across scales and hardware generations. As
525
      a result of how collective communication primitives for modern parallelism strategies scale, conven-
526   tional metrics for measuring performance in distributed settings, such as total FLOPS or throughput
527   on smaller scale systems, cannot be extrapolated from small to large-scale without properly account-
528   ing for communication dynamics.

531   6       C ONCLUSION
532
533   In this work, we examine the effects of scaling: parallelization strategies, model architectures, and
534   hardware platforms. We show that communication boundedness worsens at scale and with newer
535   hardware generations, and are persistent across model sizes. Additionally, we show that these trends
536   lead to the emergence of viable parallelism alternatives for distributing deep learning training work-
537   loads. Finally, we show that these trends culminate in significant diminishing returns on training
538
      performance with respect to real-world resources of power and throughput.

      A    R ELATED W ORK
773
774
      Performance Analysis of Deep Learning Systems. Deep learning poses a unique workload dif-
775   ferent from traditional high-performance computing settings – with complexity arising from: mem-
776   ory availability and hierarchy; and variable speeds of computation and communication. Prior re-
777   search has explored the performance properties of individual accelerators (Wang et al., 2020),com-
778   mon workloads (Hsia et al., 2023; Ardalani et al., 2024), and efficient methods for maximizing
779   hardware utilization of these workloads (Hagemann et al., 2023).
780
      Concurrently, several benchmarks have been developed to provide canonical workloads and incen-
781   tivize efficiency improvements (Mattson et al., 2020; Reddi et al., 2020; Peng et al., 2023). These
782   evaluation suites often only measure the overall end-to-end system performance of standard training
783   and inference recipes (i.e. throughput or wallclock training time) Williams et al. (2009); abstracting
784   away the underlying system. In our work, we seek to examine the joint scaling effects on these
785   downstream performance and system-level utilization metrics as we vary these components (i.e.
786   hardware, model architecture, and parallelization) together.
787
788   Scaling Properties of Deep Learning. Previous work investigating the scaling properties of neu-
789   ral network training has largely studied the effects of varying the data volume, training compute
790
      budget, and model architecture (Hoffmann et al., 2022a; Kaplan et al., 2020; Tay et al., 2023; Po-
      rian et al., 2024). These works primarily examine the impact of these factors on the pretraining
791
      loss and downstream finetuning performance of the model with respect to the theoretical amount of
792
      computational resources allocated (i.e. number of FLOPs).
793
794   However, these analyses assume that workload performance scales directly with the amount of com-
795   putation regardless of the underlying hardware platform and frameworks. In practice, theoretical
796
      measures (i.e. FLOPs) are known to be imprecise representations of end-to-end real-world per-
      formance (e.g. latency, throughput) due to performance bounds that emerge from management of
797
      the computational graph, data transfer, and communication bottlenecks (Dehghani et al.; Fernandez
798
      et al.) – or as we show due to communication boundedness.
799
800   Additionally, as the scale of deep learning systems has grown, their efficiency has emerged as a
801   serious concern with commensurate scaling in the environmental, financial, and computational re-
802
      sources required to execute such workloads (Wu et al., 2022; Schwartz et al., 2020; Patterson et al.,
      2022; Luccioni et al., 2024a;b; Strubell et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2024).
803

807   In this work, we consider a set of common data and model parallelization techniques for distributing
808   training of neural networks. However, there are additional methods for workload parallelization and
809   memory footprint reduction such as DeepSpeed Zero (Rasley et al., 2020), parallelization of loss
      computation, and other forms of optimized kernel implementations.

      In our investigation across computing platforms, we primarily consider variations in the speed of
811   compute (i.e. GPU generation). In future work, we plan to demonstrate the consistency of the
812   observed trends across settings with variable speeds of communication (i.e. varying speed of intern-
813   ode fabric by comparing InfiniBand interconnects with common alternatives such as RDMA over
814   Converged Ethernet, RoCE).
815
      Additionally, our work is focuses on the training of neural networks based on the Transformer neural
816
      network architecture and GPU hardware accelerators. Although we expect our findings to be consis-
817   tent across other model architectures and hardware platforms, we reserve that examination as areas
818   for future work. Likewise, we focus our investigations on GPUs as it is the most commonly used
819   and easily available hardware accelerator. We expect that similar trends and tradeoffs between com-
820   munication and computation would occur for alternative hardware accelerator architectures such as
821   TPUs, IPUs, etc.; however we leave exploration of these settings for future study.
