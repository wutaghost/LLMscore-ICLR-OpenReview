010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            As key elements within the central dogma, DNA, RNA, and proteins play crucial
013            roles in maintaining life by guaranteeing accurate genetic expression and imple-
014            mentation. Although research on these molecules has profoundly impacted fields
015            like medicine, agriculture, and industry, the diversity of machine learning ap-
016            proaches—from traditional statistical methods to deep learning models and large
017
               language models—poses challenges for researchers in choosing the most suit-
               able models for specific tasks, especially for cross-omics and multi-omics tasks
018
               due to the lack of comprehensive benchmarks. To address this, we introduce the
019
               first comprehensive multi-omics benchmark COMET (Benchmark for Biological
020            COmprehensive Multi-omics Evaluation Tasks and Language Models), designed
021            to evaluate models across single-omics, cross-omics, and multi-omics tasks. First,
022            we curate and develop a diverse collection of downstream tasks and datasets cov-
023            ering key structural and functional aspects in DNA, RNA, and proteins, including
024            tasks that span multiple omics levels. Then, we evaluate existing foundational
025            language models for DNA, RNA, and proteins, as well as the newly proposed
026            multi-omics model, offering valuable insights into their performance in integrat-
027            ing and analyzing data from different biological modalities. We observed that
028
               DNA, RNA, and protein models can be applied to tasks across different omics
               by leveraging initialized embeddings, with protein models demonstrating superior
029
               performance across various omics. Through the evaluation of multi-omics tasks,
030
               we identified significant gaps in the capabilities of current models to address these
031            challenges, highlighting substantial opportunities to enhance multi-omics integra-
032            tion and improve overall performance.

      Driven by curiosity about uncovering the fundamental principles of life sciences, humans have never
      ceased exploring the microscopic mechanisms of biological processes. DNA, RNA, and proteins,
038
      as fundamental molecules of the central dogma (Crick, 1970), play critical roles in sustaining life.
039
      Through their interrelated functions, they ensure the accurate expression and execution of genetic
040   instructions, making them central to all biological processes. Current research on these three types
041   of molecules has already had widespread and profound impacts across multiple fields. For example,
042   gene sequencing and editing technologies have made early diagnosis and treatment of hereditary
043   diseases possible (Le, 2020). Genetic modification has enabled efficient and targeted crop improve-
044   ment (Ahmar et al., 2020). The analysis of protein structure and function has driven advancements
045   in targeted drug design and the application of industrial enzymes (Śledź & Caflisch, 2018; Chapman
046   et al., 2018). Consequently, deepening our understanding of how these molecules interact and func-
047   tion is crucial for unraveling the complex mechanisms underlying biological processes and driving
048   technological and application advancements in agriculture, industry, and medicine.
049   Despite significant advances, current research on biological molecules faces significant challenges.
050   With the development of machine learning technologies, research methodologies have evolved from
051   traditional statistical approaches (Karollus et al., 2021; Bhasin et al., 2005; Kathuria et al., 2018)
052   to deep learning models (Bogard et al., 2019; Zhuang et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020) and, more
053   recently, to large language models (Chen et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023; Rives et al., 2021). How-
      ever, this diversity of approaches has made it challenging for researchers to choose the most suitable

      Inspired by the unified models in the NLP field (Ray, 2023), scientists are now striving to develop
096   foundational models capable of integrating different omics data in biology. This has led to growing
097   interest in cross-omics and multi-omics research. By exploring the relationships between multi-
098   omics data, they aim to uncover deeper insights into biological processes and foster innovation
099   across research and application domains (Outeiral & Deane, 2024; He et al., 2024). However, as
100   previously mentioned, the community still lacks a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating cross-
101   omics and multi-omics tasks. Such a benchmark is essential for clearly defining the critical issues
102   in multi-omics research and guiding its future direction.
103   Therefore, we propose a comprehensive benchmark called COMET (Benchmark for Biological
104   COmprehensive Multi-omics Evaluation Tasks and Language Models) for the evaluation of single-
105   omics, cross-omics, and multi-omics biological tasks. We select key single-omics tasks from DNA,
106   RNA, and proteins covering structure, function and engineering, allowing for a thorough assessment
107   of the models’ performance in specific contexts. In addition, we collect the corresponding codon
      sequences for proteins and several downstream tasks that span multiple omics. For the models, we

109   method LucaOne (He et al., 2024). The benchmark allows for a deeper understanding of models

      Through the observation of our experimental results, we surprisingly find that using models from
      other modalities can achieve comparable performance. For example, DNA/RNA models can per-
113
      form protein tasks well. Additionally, we discovered that pretrained protein language models and
114
      multi-omics models have strong potential for multi-molecule understanding. To the best of our
115   knowledge, we are the first to propose a comprehensive biological multi-omics benchmark, aimed
116   at evaluating models across single-omics, cross-omics, and multi-omics tasks. The overview of
117   COMET is shown in Figure 1. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
118
119         • We build the first comprehensive biological multi-omics benchmark, covering key struc-
120           tural and functional tasks and data in single-molecules, and compiling tasks and data in-
121           volving cross-molecules and multi-molecules.
122         • We evaluate existing foundation models for DNA, RNA, and proteins, as well as multi-
123           omics approaches. We conduct experiments with fully-finetuned or frozen models. This
124           provides a reference for researchers in selecting methods with appropriate finetuning ways.
125         • We design various approaches to evaluating cross-omics and multi-omics, bridging the gap
126           between different omics. We find that different omics models can benefit other types of
127           omics tasks and multi-molecular tasks still present significant challenges to face.

129   2   R ELATED W ORK

131   Biology language models. The rapid evolution of biology language models has significantly ad-
132   vanced computational biology by leveraging natural language processing techniques to analyze bi-
133   ological sequences. DNABERT (Ji et al., 2021), DNABERT2 (Zhou et al., 2023), the Nucleotide
134   Transformer (Dalla-Torre et al., 2023) and Genomics-FM (Ye et al., 2024) adapt the BERT archi-
135   tecture for DNA sequences, but differ primarily in terms of tokenizer design, model parameters
136
      and pre-trained data. HyenaDNA (Nguyen et al., 2024) models long-range dependencies at single-
      nucleotide resolution. The emergence of foundational RNA models, including RNA-FM (Chen
137
      et al., 2022), BEACON-B (Ren et al., 2024) and UTR-LM (Chu et al., 2024) utilize sophisticated
138
      language modeling techniques. These models have demonstrated the ability to address a wide array
139   of RNA-related tasks, thereby offering deeper insights into RNA biology. In protein analysis, The
140   ESM family (Rives et al., 2021; Meier et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2023; Hayes et al., 2024) highlights
141   the impact of scaling unsupervised learning through the use of large protein sequence datasets and
142   a transformer-based architecture capable of capturing complex dependencies within sequences. For
143   multi-omics models, LucaOne (He et al., 2024) and CD-GPT (Zhu et al., 2024) unify nucleic acid
144   and protein data, while differ in training method. In the realm of cross-omics, Calm (Outeiral &
145   Deane, 2024) introduces codon embeddings to protein language models, enhancing predictive per-
146   formance by leveraging biological data containing richer signals. Boshar et al. (2024) explore the
147
      efficacy of genomic language models on protein tasks. Prakash et al. (2024) explore the possibility
      of applying pre-trained DNA and protein models to RNA tasks. These advancements underscore the
148
      transformative potential of large-scale language models in decoding genomic, transcriptomic, and
149
      proteomic complexities, fostering breakthroughs in molecular biology, and protein science.
150
151   Benchmarks in biological language. In the field of biological language processing, benchmarks
152   have been instrumental in driving advancements across various molecular research areas. For DNA,
153
      significant contributions include Genomic Benchmarks (Grešová et al., 2023) and BEND (Marin
      et al., 2023), which collect diverse DNA tasks like gene finding, enhancer annotation, and CpG
154
      methylation. (Kao et al., 2024) introduces the evaluation of models for long-range tasks. Rn-
155
      aBench (Runge et al., 2024) primarily focuses on RNA secondary structure and design tasks. BEA-
156   CON (Ren et al., 2024) is introduced to evaluate language models on RNA tasks and proposes a
157   strong baseline model called BEACON-B. Protein benchmarks such as ProteinGym (Notin et al.,
158   2024), tailored for protein fitness prediction and design. PEER (Xu et al., 2022), encompasses a
159   broad range of tasks including function prediction, localization, and structure analysis. However,
160   there is still a lack of benchmarks that comprehensively integrate multi-molecular tasks, which is
161   crucial for capturing the complex interactions between different moleculars in biological systems.

185   The following sections provide detailed information for 17 diverse tasks, including data statistics,

188   3.1        DNA TASK
189
190   Gene Expression (GE) predicts the expression levels of genes and transcription factors (TFs) across
191   diverse tissues, recorded in target y ∈ R. We extracted 1575 TF expression datasets from the GTEx
192   database after filtering out non-expressed TFs, ensuring high-quality and tissue-specific expression
193   profiles. Additionally, we integrated gene expression data from Xpresso (Agarwal & Shendure,
194
      2020), covering 56 tissues, and grouped them into 11 functional and regional categories, enabling
      a comprehensive analysis of cross-tissue gene expression. This regression task aims to evaluate the
195
      model’s ability to predict gene and TF expression levels using the R2 value as the evaluation metric.
196
      Impact: Gene expression is crucial for understanding transcriptional regulation and functional dy-
197   namics across the genome. Accurately predicting expression levels can elucidate the regulatory
198   networks underlying different cellular states and tissue-specific functions, offering deeper insights
199   into how genetic and environmental factors jointly influence gene expression and contribute to phe-
200   notypic variation and disease mechanisms.
201   Enhancer Activity Prediction (EA) is a regression task that predicts enhancer activity for two
202   promoters associated with distinct developmental and housekeeping transcriptional programs di-
203   rectly from the DNA sequence. The enhancer activity dataset released in (de Almeida et al., 2022)
204   comprises 484,052 DNA sequences, each 249 nucleotides in length, measured for their quantitative
205
      enhancer activity towards either a developmental or a housekeeping promoter by a continuous target
      variable y ∈ R. We employ PCC as the metric.
206
      Impact: Enhancers are essential genomic elements that regulate cell type-specific transcription of
207
      target genes, influencing animal development and physiology. Their ability to activate transcrip-
208
      tion outside their native contexts suggests that critical regulatory information resides within their
209   DNA sequences. Mutations in enhancers can alter their function, leading to developmental defects
210   and contributing to human diseases. Understanding enhancer activity is crucial for revealing the
211   regulatory networks governing gene expression.

215   APA Isoform Prediction (APA) predicts the polyA site strength for each variant, represented as
      target y ∈ R. We filter 228k sequences from Bogard’s dataset (Bogard et al., 2019) containing over

      3 million APA reporter gene data . This regression task evaluates the proportion of proximal APA
217   isoforms with the performance metric being the R2 value.
218   Impact: Alternative polyadenylation is a key regulatory mechanism that diversifies RNA transcripts
219   and protein isoforms through 3’ UTR processing. By influencing transcription termination and in-
220   teracting with RNA splicing, APA modulates gene expression, impacting various cellular functions.

222   Programmable RNA Switches (PRS) are synthetic RNA molecules designed to regulate gene ex-
223   pression by responding to specific RNA sequences. Each switch exists in one of three activity states,
224   ON, OFF, or ON/OFF, depending on the presence or absence of its trigger RNA sequence. The
225   target y ∈ R3 represents the three activity states. The dataset (Angenent-Mari et al., 2020) includes
226
      91,534 in vivo toehold switches, covering 23 viral genomes and 906 human transcription factors.
      The activity of these switches is measured using GFP signal intensity, which provides a quantitative
227
      readout of switch performance. The effectiveness of these switches is evaluated using the R2 metric.
228
      Impact: Programmable RNA switches provide precise control of gene expression and cellular func-
229   tions, making them essential in synthetic biology for manipulating biological processes both in vitro
230   and in vivo. These RNAs act as responsive elements to small molecules, proteins, or nucleic acids,
231   allowing fine regulation of cellular behavior. Therapeutically, they promise to enable targeted treat-
232   ments by detecting disease-specific signals and triggering precise cellular responses, contributing to
233   novel approaches in precision medicine and synthetic biology.
234   Secondary Structure Prediction (SSP) identifies paired nucleotide regions in stems and unpaired
235   nucleotide regions in loops, bulges and junctions within RNA molecules. In a RNA molecule with
236   a length of l, The target matrix y ∈ Rl×l indicates whether each nucleotide and other nucleotides
237
      form a base pair. We utilize the bpRNA-1m database (Danaee et al., 2018). The performance metric
      for this task is the F1 score.
238
      Impact: Accurate secondary structure prediction is paramount for elucidating the intricate mecha-
239
      nisms underlying function and dynamics. By mapping these structures, researchers gain insights that
240
      advance genetic research and guide RNA-based therapeutic development, supporting innovations in
241   precision medicine.

244   3.3   P ROTEIN TASK
245
246   Thermostability Prediction (Ther) is a regression task that aims to predict the stability of proteins
247   at high temperatures. From the Thermostability task of FLIP (Dallago et al., 2021), we apply the
248
      ‘Human-cell’ splits. We use the Spearman correlation coefficient (SCC) as the metric.
      Impact: Protein thermostability prediction advances our understanding of protein functions and
249
      properties. In industrial enzyme applications, developing highly thermostable enzymes is essential
250
      for operating under harsh reaction conditions (Wu et al., 2023). Such predictions facilitate directed
251   evolution and selection of proteins, which is of great significance for drug and vaccine discovery
252   (Chen & Gong, 2022).
253   Enzyme Commission Number Prediction (EC) involves annotating protein sequences. The data is
254   sourced from the EC benchmark established in DEEPFRI (Gligorijević et al., 2021). We collect the
255   corresponding codon sequences for the dataset simultaneously. It retains over 90% of the original
256   samples after filtering out anomalous data. We use Fmax as the metric.
257   Impact: Identifying enzymes and their catalytic reaction types is crucial for understanding enzyme
258   functions. This can accelerate the discovery of new enzymatic activities and improve the functions
259   of existing enzymes. In the field of drug discovery, it can assist in designing enzymes with specific
260
      catalytic activities, supporting the development of novel drugs and therapies (Chautard et al., 2009).
261
      Contact Map Prediction (Cont) aims to forecast interactions between amino acid residues in a
262
      protein. Given a protein’s amino acids sequence, the target is to predict which residues are in close
263   proximity in its three-dimensional structure. The data is sourced from the Proteinnet (AlQuraishi,
264   2019) and TAPE benchmark (Rao et al., 2019). We use P@L/5 as the metric.
265   Impact: Accurate contact map prediction has a significant impact on the field of structural biology
266   (Vendruscolo et al., 1997). It helps identify protein-protein interactions, protein folding pathways,
267   and design new proteins with desired properties. As a powerful tool, it bridges the gap between
268   protein sequence and structure.
269   Fluorescence Prediction (Flu) evaluates the model’s ability to predict fluorescence values for
      higher-order mutated green fluorescent protein (avGFP) sequences. The original data comes from

      Sarkisyan et al. (2016) and Xu et al. (2022), following the settings outlined in Boshar et al. (2024).
271   It’s a regression task and we use SCC as the metric.
272   Impact: Accurately predicting the fluorescence values of higher-order mutated avGFP is crucial for
273   understanding protein function. It helps to elucidate the relationship between sequence variations
274   and function, enhancing the understanding of evolutionary landscapes. This enables the design of
275   novel fluorescent proteins with specific properties, applicable in broader scenarios within synthetic

      effects, the specific functional mechanisms of proteins can be revealed. This has broad applications
283   in drug development, disease treatment, industrial production, and agricultural production.

286
287   Enhancer-Promoter Interaction Prediction (EPI) is a single-label classification task in genomics
288   that aims to identify interactions between enhancers and promoters sequence, with the categorical
289   label y ∈ {0, 1}. The dataset, sourced from EPI-DLMH (Min et al., 2021), comprises six cell
290
      lines—GM12878, HUVEC, HeLa-S3, IMR90, K562, and NHEK. We sample to balance true EPIs
      and non-EPIs. We use the Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC) as the metric.
291
      Impact: Enhancers are regulatory elements that can significantly enhance the transcription of genes
292
      located at varying distances, while promoters are essential regions where transcription begins. Un-
293
      derstanding these interactions is crucial for deciphering the complex regulatory networks that govern
294   cellular functions and can provide insights into developmental biology and disease mechanisms.
295   siRNA Efficiency Prediction (siRNA) is a regression task that aims to predict the silencing effi-
296   ciency of different siRNAs. By inputting artificially modified siRNA sequences the target mRNA
297   sequence, the model can estimate how effectively each siRNA silences its corresponding mRNA.
298   This is a regression task, and we use Mixed Score as the evaluation metric A.5.1. The data utilized
299   in this research are from SAIS (SAIS, 2020).
300   Impact: RNA interference (RNAi) is a natural gene expression regulation mechanism that reduces
301   target protein levels by inhibiting the expression of target genes, typically achieved through siR-
302
      NAs (Setten et al., 2019). With the success of mRNA vaccines in COVID-19 prevention, there
      has been growing interest in the development of nucleic acid-based drugs. Predicting the silencing
303
      efficiency of chemically modified siRNA sequences under the RNAi mechanism is crucial, as this
304
      metric is directly linked to the actual therapeutic efficacy of the drug.
305
      Antibody-Antigen Neutralizability Prediction (AAN) is used to assess whether there is an in-
306   teraction between an antigen and an antibody, making it a single-label classification task with the
307   categorical label y ∈ {0, 1}. The goal is to predict whether a given antibody can bind to a specific
308   antigen based on their sequences. This task is based on the HIV data from CATNAP (Yoon et al.,
309   2015) and DeepAAI (Zhang et al., 2022). We use MCC as the metric.
310   Impact: To demonstrate the neutralizing effects of most natural and synthetic antibodies against any
311   antigen, time-consuming, labor-intensive, and costly wet lab experiments are typically required (Lee
312   et al., 2007). However, with machine learning, we can represent the neutralizing response of anti-
313   bodies (Ab) from the perspective of Ab-Ag neutralization effects, highlighting similarities in binding
314
      regions. This approach also helps to enable the recommendation of broad-spectrum antibodies that
      can target new viral variants.
315

316
      interacts with RNA-binding protein (RBP). The dataset is sourced from NPInterv2.0 (Yuan et al.,
317   2014). Since the databases only provide positive samples, which are pairs of ncRNA and proteins
318   that interact, we utilized a negative sample dataset generated by ncRPI-LGAT (Han & Zhang, 2023),
319   where ncRNAs and proteins are randomly paired. We use MCC as the metric.
320   Impact: The interaction between ncRNAs and RBPs plays a crucial role in various important biologi-
321   cal processes, such as gene expression, chromatin modification, and epigenetic regulation. However,
322   identifying ncRNA-protein interactions through wet-lab experiments remains time-consuming and
323   expensive. The development of computational approaches to predict these interactions can signifi-
      cantly reduce the need for labor-intensive experiments, offering a more efficient and cost-effective

335   CRISPR Off-Target Prediction (CRI-Off) involves predicting the likelihood and frequency of
336   off-target effects by inputting both the sgRNA sequence and the corresponding off-target DNA se-
337   quences. The specificity of sgRNA is measured by a continuous target variable y∈R, reflecting the
338   frequency of off-target cleavage events. The dataset from DeepCRISPR (Chuai et al., 2018) used for
339   evaluation includes approximately 160,000 potential off-target sites from 30 sgRNAs across various
340   cell types. SCC is employed as the performance metric to assess the relationship between predicted
341   and observed off-target effects.
342   Impact: Precision in off-target predictions is crucial for advancing CRISPR technology, as it min-
343
      imizes unintended genetic modifications that could result in harmful effects. Accurate off-target
      analysis is essential for refining sgRNA designs, thereby improving both the safety and efficacy of
344
      CRISPR applications in clinical and research settings. By ensuring that sgRNAs specifically target
345
      the desired DNA sequences, scientists can reduce the risk of unwanted mutations, making CRISPR-
346   based therapies and genetic modifications more reliable and effective.
347   DNA-Protein Folding Prediction (DPF) predict the 3D structure of DNA-protein complexes by
348   inputting their respective sequences. The training data is sourced from experimentally determined
349   structural files found in DeepPBS (Mitra et al., 2024), which provide verified DNA-protein interac-
350   tion data. From these datasets, pairs of DNA and protein sequences are extracted, along with their
351   spatial coordinates. These coordinates serve as the labels for model training, allowing the model
352   to learn the spatial relationships in the DNA-protein complex. The local Distance Difference Test
353   (LDDT) is used as the evaluation metric to assess the accuracy of the predicted structures compared
354
      to the experimentally determined ones.
      Impact: Transcription factors are essential for regulating various biological processes, including
355
      gene expression and cellular functions (Spitz & Furlong, 2012). Predicting protein-DNA binding
356
      specificity is crucial for understanding gene regulation, as proteins interact with DNA target sites
357   with varying degrees of specificity. However, predicting binding specificity across different protein
358   families remains a significant challenge (Chiu et al., 2023). Artificial intelligence can help overcome
359   this by utilizing structural information from protein-DNA complexes to generalize predictions, en-
360   abling more accurate forecasts across protein families.

527   6    C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   In this work, we present COMET, the first comprehensive multi-omics benchmark, which encom-
531
      passes 17 diverse tasks spanning DNA, RNA, Protein, cross-molecule and multi-molecule study.
      COMET aims to address the critical gap in standardized evaluation for kinds of omics models in
532
      biology. We explore the connections between models from different omics across various tasks,
533
      gaining insights into tasks in one omic can benefit from models trained on another. We also find that
534   multi-omics tasks still present certain challenges. These discoveries will inform the design of future
535   biological language models, promoting interaction and understanding among different omics rather
536   than studying each omic in isolation. However, the current benchmark involves relatively limited
537   models and tasks, and some downstream tasks that require additional inputs are not yet aligned. In
538   the future, we plan to further expand the models and tasks covered, closely follow developments
539   in cross-omics and multi-omics research, and explore the potential connections between different
      omics data more thoroughly.

      A.1   O MICS TASK P IPELINE
855
      These are detailed pipelines conducted in experiments and shown in Figure 2.
856
857   Single-Molecular Task In single-molecule tasks, in addition to testing with models and tasks be-
858   longing to the same molecule, we also evaluate the performance of models and tasks across different
859   molecules. To address the issue of inconsistent vocabularies, we reinitialized the vocab embeddings
860
      while loading the pretrained weights. For example, DNA/RNA models initialize and replace the
      nucleotide vocab embedding with initial amino acids vocab embedding when using protein data.
861
862   Cross-Molecular Task Cross-molecule tasks involve the corresponding protein sequences and
863   codon sequences. Therefore, we first use open-source tools and datasets to collect and construct
      the CDS-Protein data. Subsequently, we can input CDS data for DNA/RNA/CDS models and input


                                                      16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      protein data for protein models. Since Lucaone is a multi-omics model capable of processing both
865   CDS and protein sequences, these inputs are handled separately.
866
867   Multi-Molecular Task In multi-molecule tasks, we use different omics models with different omics
868
      molecules. By rotating foundation models within single-omics, we achieved a unified evaluation
      of multi-omics data. For example, a DNA-DNA task can be processed by any combination of two
869
      models within the DNA foundation model.
870
871
872   A.2     B IOLOGY TASK P IPELINE
873
      Sequence Level Prediction The architecture for sequence-level prediction tasks varies depending
874
      on whether the task involves single-molecule or multi-molecule scenarios.
875
876   For single-molecule sequence-level predictions, we utilize different strategies based on the model
877   type. For naive supervised models, we compute an attentive weighted sum of all nucleotides to
878
      form a single sequence representation, which is then passed through an MLP to produce the final
      predictions. In contrast, when using language models, the [CLS] token representation is extracted
879
      and fed into a classifier layer to obtain the output predictions.
880
881   For multi-molecule sequence-level prediction tasks, the two interacting molecular sequences are
882   processed independently using the same approaches as in the single-molecule scenario. Specifically,
883   each molecule’s sequence is encoded separately using either a naive supervised model or a language
884
      model to generate individual representations. The resulting embeddings are then concatenated. In
      specific cases, such as sgRNA and CRI-Off, additional data features are incorporated along with
885
      the concatenated embeddings. This combined representation is passed through an MLP layer to
886
      generate the final sequence-level predictions.
887
888   Nucleotide Level Prediction To investigate the relationships between nucleotides, we calculate the
889   self-outer product of the nucleotide representations, resulting in a matrix that captures the pairwise
890
      interactions among nucleotides. This interaction matrix is then processed through a simple ResNet
      architecture to produce the final output.
891
892   Amino Acids Level Prediction To study the relationships between residue pairs, we employed two
893   approaches. For the transformer-based foundational language model, we extracted the attention
894   matrix from the backbone encoder and appended a linear layer to directly predict the residue pair
895   relationships. For the naive supervised model, we computed the feature matrix by calculating the
896
      point-wise product between token pairs, followed by a linear layer for relationship prediction.
897

      Table 7 presents the maximum nucleotide and protein sequence lengths for tokenizers of each lan-
900   guage model. Models such as DNABERT2, NTv2, and CaLM utilize relative positional encoding,
901   providing excellent scalability to handle long sequences. Additionally, the BPE, non-overlap 6-mer,
902   and non-overlap 3-mer tokenizer efficiently reduce the number of tokens, enabling these models
903   to accommodate longer sequences under memory constraints. As a result, the maximum sequence
904   lengths for DNABERT2, NTv2, and CaLM are set to 6000, 6002, and 3000, respectively. If a
905   nucleotide or protein sequence exceeds the maximum length during tokenization, the tokenizer trun-

       A.4     D ETAILED DATA P REPROCESSING F OR EACH TASK
1081
1082
       A.4.1    G ENE E XPRESSION
1083
1084
       We adopt the data processing methodology from Xpresso (Agarwal & Shendure, 2020). Human gene
1085
       expression data comes from the Epigenomics Roadmap Consortium, which provides normalized
1086
       RNA-seq values for protein-coding mRNAs across 56 tissues and cell lines.
1087
1088   Due to the large number of parameters in biological language models and the memory limitations
1089   of A100 GPUs, our experiments show that trimming sequence lengths to 6000 bp ensures compati-
1090
       bility with all models for processing input sequences. By inputting consecutive 6000 bp nucleotide
       fragments from different positions in the processed sequences into the Xpresso model, we iden-
1091
       tify that the sequence indexed from position 7000 to 12999 (length 6000 bp) achieves optimal test
1092
       performance. This segment contains the most information related to gene expression levels.
1093
1094   For training, we use the 6000 bp nucleotide sequence indexed from position 7000 to 12999 as input
1095   and the expression data for 56 tissues as labels. The train, validation, and test dataset splits follow
1096
       the methodology used in Xpresso.
1097
1098
1099
       A.4.2    E NHANCER ACTIVITY P REDICTION
1100
1101
       We follow the processing procedure described in (de Almeida et al., 2022). The data includes
       sequence information and transcriptional activity metrics for both Drosophila and humans, encom-
1102
       passing developmental and housekeeping transcriptional activity levels.
1103
1104   We use downloaded sequences of 249 bp in length, along with Dev log2 enrichment scaled
1105   and Hk log2 enrichment scaled, which respectively represent developmental and house-
1106   keeping transcriptional activity information. The dataset is divided into training, validation, and
1107
       test sets according to the method outlined in (de Almeida et al., 2022).
1108
1109
1110
       A.4.3    APA I SOFORM P REDICTION
1111
1112   The preparation for IPA isoform analysis begins by filtering raw sequencing reads from all
1113
       MPRAs (Shigaki et al., 2019) to retain only high-quality, full-length RNA sequences. These reads
       are grouped based on the randomized regions located upstream of the proximal polyadenylation
1114
       site (pPAS), forming a dictionary of sequence variants for each library. To expand this dictionary,
1115
       sequencing is also performed on the plasmid library, capturing members that lack expression of a
1116   distal isoform. RNA reads are then matched to dictionary entries by identifying the upstream region
1117   with the shortest Hamming distance.
1118
1119
       Polyadenylation cleavage sites are determined for each mapped read by detecting the presence of
       a Poly-A tail. The cleavage positions are recorded as vectors associated with individual sequence
1120
       variants, including a specific position for reads mapping to non-random distal sites. The dataset
1121
       generated from this process consists of a dictionary of distinct sequence variants paired with vectors
1122   of cleavage position counts. A final filtering step ensures data quality by discarding sequences
1123   supported by fewer than 10–20 unique UMI RNA reads or those containing over 75% A-nucleotides
1124   within a 12–20 bp region, which could indicate internal priming artifacts.
1125
       We process data from 12 random 3’ UTR libraries. 9 among the 12 libraries are used for training
1126
       and 3 held out (the 3 held-out libraries were excluded from the current analysis). To construct a
1127
       balanced test set, sequences from each library are first shuffled independently according to their
1128   read counts. These shuffled sequences are then merged using a round-robin approach, selecting one
1129   sequence from each library at a time in descending order of read count. This strategy ensures that
1130   the test set contains an even representation of high-read count sequences across all libraries. The
1131   remaining sequences are appended to the beginning of the combined library, and the training set is
1132   further shuffled to enhance randomness.For benchmarking purposes, the top 10% of high-read count
1133   sequences are prioritized. Among these, the most abundantly expressed sequences are selected for
       testing, ensuring a high-quality, balanced dataset for training, validation, and evaluation.

       A.4.4   P ROGRAMMABLE RNA S WITCHES
1135
1136   We adopt the data generation pipeline described in (Angenent-Mari et al., 2020). A toehold-switch
1137   library comprising 244,000 potential trigger sequences is designed and synthesized, covering the
1138   complete genomes of 23 pathogenic viruses, the entire coding regions of 906 human transcription
1139   factors, and approximately 10,000 random sequences. Using this synthesized oligo pool, two con-
1140   struct libraries are created to represent the ON and OFF states, and both are transformed into BL21
1141   E. coli. The OFF library includes toehold-switch constructs without triggers, while the ON library
1142   contains identical toeholds paired with complementary triggers fused to their respective switches.
1143   The libraries are sorted into four bins using fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS), and the
1144   variants in each bin are quantified through next-generation sequencing (NGS) to determine their
1145   fluorescence distributions. After quality control, the toehold-switch library consists of 109,067 ON-
1146   state measurements, 163,967 OFF-state measurements, and 91,534 ON/OFF paired ratios, where
1147   both states are characterized for each switch. ON and OFF data are normalized to a scale of 0 to
1148   1, with ON/OFF ratios normalized to a range of -1 to 1. Following (Angenent-Mari et al., 2020),
1149
       a stringent quality control process is applied to eliminate artifacts and ensure data reliability. The
       quality control (QC) framework includes five levels: QC1, QC2, QC3, QC4 and QC5, where QC1
1150
       represents the lowest quality and QC5 the highest. Datasets above QC2 are utilized for training,
1151
       while QC5 is reserved for testing.

1277   The model architecture for Dna-Protein Folding task imitates AlphaFold3. We engage DNA and

1351
1352   We can see that all models are trained using MLM, among which the NTv2 model uses much more
