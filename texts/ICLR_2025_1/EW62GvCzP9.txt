      1      I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      Rapid progress in the capabilities of language models has led to a surge of interest in their alignment
057   and evaluation, aiming to ensure that they are safe, reliable, and beneficial (Shevlane et al., 2023; Ji
058   et al., 2023). An important part of these efforts, termed scalable oversight (Bowman et al., 2022;
059   Brown-Cohen et al., 2024), aims to scale up evaluation to superhuman models, in which case the lack
060   of reliable supervision becomes the a fundamental challenge. By definition, superhuman models are
061   better than humans at most reasoning tasks, enabling them to exploit human evaluators (Park et al.,
062   2024) — this general phenomenon has recently been demonstrated in realistic settings (Wen et al.,
063   2024), along with other examples: sycophancy (Sharma et al., 2023) in the case of human evaluators,
064   and reward overoptimization (Gao et al., 2023) when the evaluator is a model even weaker than
065
      humans. A natural question thus arises: how can we evaluate models without supervision, and
      without being exploited?
066
067   Fortunately, we — machine learning researchers — are not the first to face this problem. A wealth of
068   research from the mechanism design literature focuses on mechanisms that exhibit game-theoretic
069   incentive compatibility — mechanisms that have truth-telling as the optimal strategy for all par-
070   ticipants, even in the absence of supervision (Myerson, 1979; Zhang et al., 2024). This property
071
      makes them resistant to deception and strategic manipulation, and has been shown to be effective
      in eliciting honest answers in a variety of settings, from auctions (Klemperer, 1999) to crowdsourc-
072
      ing (Muldoon et al., 2018). It is thus natural to ask: can we leverage these mechanisms for model
073
      evaluation as well?
074
075   This work aims to answer this question in the affirmative. Drawing from research on the peer
076   prediction mechanisms (Miller et al., 2005; Kim, 2016), we introduce a novel method for model
077
      evaluation that possesses game-theoretic incentive compatibility, and does not require ground truth
      labels. Given a set of models of varying capability and honesty, and a question to be answered,
078
      the peer prediction method distinguishes better models from worse ones by measuring the mutual
079
      predictability of their answers, i.e., how well the answers of one model can be used as reference
080   by an independent jury to predict the answers of another model. Through formal analysis and
081   comprehensive empirical validation, we show that the jury does not need to possess comparable or
082   superior cognitive capabilities to the participants, nor does it need to be inherently honest, setting
083   this method apart from existing methods. Indeed, we are surprised to discover an inverse scaling
084   property in peer prediction, where resistance to deception is strengthened as the capability gap
085   between the jury and participants widens, enabling reliable evaluation of strong models without
086   trusted supervision.
087   Specifically, we formally show that the peer prediction method is incentive compatible, implying
088   that when the peer prediction scores are used as a reward signal, at training equilibrium, the optimal
089   policy for all models (including the jury) is to answer honestly and informatively, as opposed to
090   deceptively. Through a series of experiments on models sizes from 135M to 405B parameters, we
091   demonstrate both the method’s effectiveness (i.e., the ability to distinguish better models from worse
092   ones) and its resistance to deception.
093   Historically, research on detecting model deception in the alignment context (Zou et al., 2023) tends
094   to study model policies as is, without considering how the reward incentives shaping the policy can
095   be utilized in a game-theoretic manner. While such a perspective is useful for modeling the often
096   non-equilibrium behavior of models (analogous to behavioral game theory in the human context), it
097   precludes the possibility of supervision-free evaluation with game-theoretic guarantees (offered by
098   classical game theory). In light of this, we view this work as a step towards game-theoretic resistance
099   to model deception in alignment and evaluation, drawing from the untapped wealth of mechanism
100
      design research.
101   In summary, the merits of our peer prediction method for model evaluation are as follows:
102
103       • Resistance to Deception: The peer prediction method is resistant to deception and strategic
104         manipulation, making it scalable to strong models where trusted supervision is unavailable. Re-
105         sistance is guaranteed by game theory analysis and comprehensive empirical validation.
106       • Non-Contingency on Trusted Supervision: The method does not require that the jury possess
107         comparable or superior cognitive capabilities to the participants, nor that the jury be inherently
            honest, setting it apart from existing methods.

          • Strong Scaling Performance: We discover a surprising inverse scaling property in peer predic-
109         tion, where resistance to deception increases with the widening of the jury-participant capability
110         gap, which enables reliable evaluation of strong models without trusted supervision. We also
111         demonstrate consistent increases in resistance to deception as the participant/jury population size
112         increases, giving us 3 distinct scaling properties governing the performance of peer prediction.

      2      BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
115
116
      Peer Prediction The peer prediction method, used for eliciting honest answers in crowdsourcing,
117   is based on the intuition that truthful and informative answers are more useful for predicting the true
118   state of the world, and thus more useful for predicting the answers of others (Miller et al., 2005; Kim,
119   2016). Many variants of peer prediction mechanisms have been proposed, including the Bayesian
120   Truth Serum (Prelec, 2004; Witkowski & Parkes, 2012), multi-task peer prediction (Kong, 2019;
121   Biró et al., 2021; Kong, 2021), and non-incentive compatible variants for information aggregation
122   rather than elicitation (Palley & Soll, 2018; Wang et al., 2019). There have also been applications
123   of machine learning methods in service of peer prediction, including theoretical studies on learning
124   agents (Feng et al., 2022) and empirical methods utilizing language models in a peer review setting
125
      (Lu et al., 2024). Building upon this literature, we propose to apply the peer prediction method to
      language model evaluation, and demonstrate its effectiveness and resistance to deception.
126
127
      Alignment and Evaluation of Language Models Alignment and evaluation of language models
128
      focus on ensuring that models are safe, reliable, and beneficial (Shevlane et al., 2023; Ji et al.,
129
      2023; Hendrycks, 2024). The currently dominant methods for both alignment and evaluation utilize
130   various forms of feedback, sourced either from human evaluators (Bai et al., 2022a; Casper et al.,
131   2023) or from other models aligned in prior using human feedback (Bai et al., 2022b; Madaan et al.,
132   2024). However, these methods are not applicable to superhuman models, which are better than
133   humans at most reasoning tasks, and thus possess the ability to exploit human evaluators. This
134   necessitates research on scalable oversight (Bowman et al., 2022), which aims to scale up evaluation
135   to superhuman models, including via the use of debate (Irving et al., 2018; Brown-Cohen et al.,
136   2024; Khan et al., 2024), recursive reward modeling (Leike et al., 2018), iterated amplification (Wu
137   et al., 2021), and other methods. In this work, we propose a novel method for model evaluation that
138
      does not require trusted supervision, and is resistant to deception or strategic manipulation by strong
      (and possibly superhuman) models.

141   3      E VALUATION WITHOUT T RUSTED S UPERVISION VIA P EER P REDICTION
142
143   In this section, we introduce the peer prediction method for model evaluation, and provide a formal
144   definition of the method, along with its theoretical properties. Note that despite the use of a jury,
145   the mechanism is supervision-free in the sense that it does not require trusted supervision (including
146
      that from humans) — jurors can be weak or dishonest, which makes the method applicable to strong
      models (potentially even superhuman ones) where trusted supervision is not available. This fact sets
147
      the method apart from existing methods.
148
149
      Evaluation Pipeline The evaluation pipeline takes as input a question Q and a set of answers
150   {A1 , · · · , An } from n models, which we will call the participants, and outputs a set of real-
151   valued scores {S1A , · · · , SnA }, one for each participant. A separate body of non-trusted juror agents
152   {J1 , · · · , Jm } is also needed.
153
      Extending upon the game-theoretic results by Schoenebeck & Yu (2023), the peer prediction process
154
      consists of 3 roles: the witness w, the defendant d, and the juror j. The first two roles are played by
155
      all pairs of participants round-robin, and the third role iterates through a predetermined jury body.
156
157       • Witness (w ∈ {1, · · · , n}): The witness’s answer Aw is the one being evaluated in the current
158         round. Its quality is measured by how well it helps the juror predict the defendant’s answer
159         (increases in the juror’s prediction log-probability), based on the intuition that honest and in-
160         formative answers are better predictors of the true state of the world. The mechanism rewards
161         the witness for informative answers, and each participant’s final score is its average reward as a
            witness across all rounds.

275   What if agents can differ in “worldviews”? The biggest barrier to practical application of the
276   peer prediction method is the unrealistic assumption of the shared prior P. Humans have different

      4     E XPERIMENTS
357
358   In this section, we empirically validate the peer prediction method for model evaluation, demon-
359   strating its effectiveness (ability to distinguish stronger models from weaker ones) and resistance to
360
      deception (ability to punish deceptive answers compared to honest ones). We use a set of models
      of varying sizes, ranging from 135M to 405B parameters, and a set of questions from 85 different
361
      domains, to evaluate the method.
362
363
364   4.1   E FFECTIVENESS
365
366   Setup The effectiveness experiments aim to show that the peer prediction method is able to distin-
367   guish higher-quality answers from lower-quality ones and correctly place them on a scale of quality.
368
      Given that we operate in an open-ended setting, evaluating not only the correctness of the conclu-
      sion but also the reasoning process leading to it, we choose to use model size as a proxy for quality,
369
      assuming that, all else being equal, larger models within the same family are better at reasoning and
370
      thus produce higher-quality answers.
371
372   We use the Llama-3.1-8B, Llama-3.1-70B, and Llama-3.1-405B models (Dubey et al., 2024) as
373   participants, and Mistral-7B-v0.3 (Jiang et al., 2023) as the jury. All models are instruction-tuned.
374   By combining MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021b), MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021a), MMLU-PRO
375   (Wang et al., 2024), ARC (Clark et al., 2018), OpenBookQA (Mihaylov et al., 2018), subsets of
376   RACE (Lai et al., 2017) and MCTest (Richardson et al., 2013), we obtain a dataset of 37079 ques-
377   tions spanning 85 domains, including math, natural sciences, engineering, social sciences, medicine,
      humanities, philosophy, law, professional skills, common knowledge, and general reasoning skills.


                                                                                7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      This includes a mixture of multiple-choice questions and open-ended questions. We discard the
379   ground-truth labels, and only use the questions as input to the peer prediction method.
380
381   For each of the 37079 questions, we independently run Algorithm 1 with the Llama-3.1-8B, Llama-
382
      3.1-70B, and Llama-3.1-405B models as participants, and the Mistral-7B-v0.3 model as the jury.
      The log-probabilities in Algorithm 1 are computed from the juror model’s logits within a structured
383
      dialogue based on in-context learning, where the juror is given a few samples of previous informant-
384
      defendant answer pairs, and is asked to predict the defendant’s answer based on the informant’s in
385   the current round; please refer to Appendix B.1 for details.
386
387   Algorithm 1 outputs a score for each participant per question, which allows us to compute the mean
388
      score of each participant across questions of each domain. We can then utilize statistical methods
      to compare the mean scores of different participants, and examine whether statistically significant
389
      differences exist between them.
390
391
392
      Distinguishability Across Model Sizes Figure 3 shows the mean scores gained by participants of
      different parameter sizes in peer prediction, across 85 different domains. According to peer predic-
393
      tion scores — obtained without utilizing any ground-truth label — the 405B model outperforms the
394
      70B model, which in turn outperforms the 8B model, indicating the effectiveness of peer prediction
395   across diverse domains.

      4.2   R ESISTANCE TO D ECEPTION
399
      Setup and Baseline The resistance to deception experiments aim to show that the peer prediction
400
      method is able to punish deceptive answers compared to honest ones, and thus is resistant to strategic
401   manipulation. From another perspective, it attempts to experimentally validate Theorem 1 and The-
402   orem 2, by answering the question: for each individual participant, does responding honestly lead to
403   higher scores than responding deceptively, counterfactually assuming that the other participants and
404   the jury does not change their strategies?
405
      The settings are the same as in the effectiveness experiments, with the following exceptions:
406
407    • Participant Composition: To control for model capability, we now ensure that all participants
408      in any given experiment are instances of the same model, with differences only in the prompt
409      templates (honest vs deceptive). Specifically, we use Llama-3.1-8B, Gemma2-2B, and Gemma2-
410      27B models as participants in 3 different series of experiments, to ensure coverage of a wide
411      range of model sizes.
412
       • Deceptive Participants: Within each experiment, we randomly select one or more participants
413      to respond deceptively to all questions, while the rest respond honestly. The exact manner of
414      deception is controlled by the prompt templates, which can be found in Appendix B.2. The task
415      is thus to detect deception in a zero-shot setting and without access to ground-truth labels, which
416      is particularly challenging.
417
       • Baseline (LLM-as-a-Judge): In addition to the peer prediction method, we also consider a base-
418
         line where the jury is a single LLM model, asked to judge the quality of the answers. Since the
419      jury output is directly used without any verification mechanism, this baseline does not possess
420      the incentive compatibility property, and has been shown to be vulnerable to strategic manipula-
421      tion and deception when participant models are stronger than the jury model (Hou et al., 2024;
422      Chen et al., 2024). Please refer to Appendix B.3 for implementation details.
423
       • Resistance Metric: We use the cross-entropy loss of logistic regression between peer
424
         prediction/LLM-as-a-Judge scores and binary honesty labels to measure the extent to which
425
         scores gained by a participant positively predict its honesty. This in turn reflects the evaluation
426      scheme’s degree of punishment for deception, where a lower cross-entropy indicates a stronger
427      resistance to deception. When the fitted model puts negative coefficient on the score (meaning
428      that a higher score corresponds to lower honesty), we count that as evidence against the evalua-
429      tion scheme, and reflect its cross-entropy value w.r.t. the null baseline log(2) = 0.69 of random
430      guessing when plotting, in order to visualize this negation. The reason for using regression as
431      opposed to a simple comparison of mean scores is that peer prediction and LLM-as-a-Judge have
         different scoring distributions, making direct comparisons impossible.


                                                        8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
       • Jury Models: Instead of using the fixed jury model of Mistral-7B-v0.3, we now consider a range
433      of jury models with sizes spanning close to 3 orders of magnitude: SmolLM-135M, SmolLM-
434      360M (Allal et al., 2024), Qwen2.5-0.5B, Qwen2.5-1.5B, Qwen2.5-3B, and Qwen2.5-7B (Qwen
435      Team, 2024), to investigate scaling properties of peer prediction — see discussions below. The
436      SmolLM and Qwen model series are selected due to their wide coverage of different model sizes.
437
438    • Scaling Analysis: We investigate the scaling property of resistance to deception, by varying
439      the participant model size, the jury model size, and the participant/jury population size, and
440      examining how the resistance to deception changes with these parameters. Among these, the
441
         most essential parameter is the ratio of the participant model size to the jury model size,
         which we call the model capability gap. A larger model capability gap implies a stronger ability
442
         to deceive the jury, and thus a higher need for resistance to deception. This quantity captures the
443
         essence of the scalable oversight problem, which can precisely be defined as ensuring that the
444      evaluation method is resistant to strategic manipulation when there is a large model capability
445      gap (Bowman et al., 2022).
446
447
448   Inverse Scaling Properties with Model Capability Gap Surprisingly, we observe an inverse
449   scaling property: the results indicate that resistance to deception increases with the model capability
450   gap. Figure 1 shows the scaling properties of resistance to deception with respect to the model
451
      capability gap, the latter shown as the x-axis. This suggests that the peer prediction method is more
      immune to deception when the participant models are stronger than the jury model.
452
453   Moreover, peer prediction consistently outperforms the LLM-as-a-Judge baseline when the model
454   capability gap is large. Comparing data points at the top-left and top-right corners of Figure 1, we
455   see that the peer prediction method with a much smaller jury model (SmolLM-135M) outperforms
456   the LLM-as-a-Judge baseline with a much larger jury model (Qwen2.5-7B). In particular, LLM-as-
457
      a-Judge evaluations are worse than random guesses when facing deceptive models 5-20× its size,
      while peer prediction thrives even beyond 100× size difference.
458
459   The inverse scaling property is a surprising result, as it contradicts the conventional wisdom that
460   stronger models are capable of harder-to-detect strategic manipulation, and thus require stronger
461   oversight. The result suggests that by moving from direct oversight based on jury’s subjective judg-
462   ment to indirect oversight based on peer prediction (where we measure how the witness’s answer
463
      empowers the jury to accomplish the objective task of prediction), we can overcome the fallibility of
      the jury’s judgment and achieve stronger resistance to deception. Finally, it’s worth noting that the
464
      inverse scaling property does not contradict the theoretical claims, as the latter touch upon scaling
465
      behaviors w.r.t. population sizes, instead of model sizes and model capability gaps.
466
467
468   Scaling Properties with Participant Population Size Figure 1 shows the scaling properties for
469   resistance to deception. The cross-entropy loss values are shown for different participant model
470   sizes, jury model sizes, and participant population sizes. The results indicate that peer prediction
471   scores become better predictors of model honesty as the participant population size increases, sug-
472
      gesting that the peer prediction method is more resistant to deception when there are more partic-
      ipants. This validates the theoretical claim in Theorem 2 that a large and diverse participant/jury
473
      pool is sufficient to ensure the incentive compatibility of the peer prediction method when there are
474
      disagreements in priors.
475
476
477   Scaling Properties with Jury Population Size Figure 4 shows the scaling properties of peer
478   prediction with jury population size. We consider the amount of surplus existing in any given group
479   of jurors, defined as the increase in regression R2 when using the entire group compared to the
480   maximum R2 obtained by each juror individually. The results indicate that surplus steadily increases
481   as the jury population size grows, suggesting that the peer prediction method is more resistant to
482
      deception when the jury population size is larger, in line with the theoretical claim in Theorem 2.
483   Note that to account for asymmetry in capabilities of jurors, we impose weights on the jurors (see
484   Algorithm 2 for details), where the weights are proportional to sα , with s being the size of the
485   jury model and α being the aggregation exponent. α is usually negative due to the inverse scaling
      property of peer prediction. Figure 4(c) compares the scaling property across different exponents.

512   5   C ONCLUSION
513
514   We propose the peer prediction method as a novel evaluation method for large language models,
515   which is incentive compatible and resistant to deception. We provide theoretical guarantees for the
516   method, and empirically validate its effectiveness and resistance to deception. Notably, we demon-
517   strate the excellent scaling properties of the method, showing that it is more resistant to deception
518   when the participant models are stronger than the jury model. Results suggest that peer prediction
519   is a promising evaluation method for large language models, and can be used to ensure the trustwor-
520   thiness of AI systems now and in the future, as scalable oversight becomes a pressing issue.
521
522   Limitations The peer prediction method is not without limitations. The method requires a large
523   participant/jury pool to ensure incentive compatibility, which may increase the complexity and com-
524
      putational costs in its practical use. Our theoretical analysis focuses on the punishment on unilateral
      deception, and does not consider collusion among participants, which is a challenging problem that
525
      requires further research. We offer some initial experiment results on collusion in Appendix A.
526
527
      Future Directions This paper focuses on stage-setting work aiming to introduce a novel class of
528
      evaluation schemes into the field of language modeling, and future research could fill in the details
529   that are left out of the scope of the present study. For instance, building evaluation pipelines with
530   lower complexity and computational overheads by automatically selecting the participant and jury
531   populations will greatly reduce the difficulty of using peer prediction. On another front, exploring
532   how the peer prediction metric can be used in training as opposed to only evaluation can potentially
533   mitigate the issue of RLHF-induced deception (Wen et al., 2024).
534
535   Ethics Statement This work aims to advance the safety of language models, with anticipated
536   positive social impacts. The deception dataset used in the experiments have been marked as such
537   explicitly, and we ask that such a notice be kept in place in any future use of the dataset.
538

      our anonymized repository.

      Fully Heterogeneous Participants In response to the positive experiment results in Figure 1, one
705   may worry about the following possibility: peer prediction works only because honest answers are
706   similar to each other (and likewise, dishonest answers are similar to each other), but the population
707   contains a majority of honest participants, and peer prediction basically evaluates an answer’s
708   similarity with the majority. If true, this would significantly reduce peer prediction’s value. There
709   are two independent ways to remove such similarity effects: (1) by having fully heterogeneous
710   participants where particiapnts come from different model families, or (2) by making sure there are
711   an equal number of honest and deceptive models. We carried out validation experiments under
712   each of these two settings (Figure 5), and we continue to observe inverse scaling trends similar to that
713
      in Figure 1, validating that peer prediction isn’t dependent on mere similarity between participants.
      Due to compute contraints, we run peer prediction only twice: first on all 6 models (whose results
714
      appear in all subfigures), then on all 3 honest models plus the deceptive Llama (whose results appear
715
      in subfigures a and f); as a result, Figure 5(f) contains one more curve than (e) or (g) does.
716
717
      Realistic, RLHF-Trained Deceptive Behaviors In our deception resistance experiments, decep-
718   tive behaviors by participants are induced by manually designed prompts (Appendix B.2), which
719   may not reflect how deceptive behaviors emerge in real settings. To address this issue, we use the
720   MisleadLM QA model trained by Wen et al. (2024), where deceptive behaviors are demonstrated
721   to naturally emerge from applying the canonical RLHF training pipeline. Since MisleadLM is fine-
722   tuned from Llama2 7B, we use Llama2 7B as the corresponding honest model. Using MisleadLM
723   as the deceptive model, we run deception resistance experiments similar to those in the previous
724   paragraph, and obtain Figure 6. Again, we observe inverse scaling trends similar to that in Figure 1.
725
726   LLM-as-a-Judge with Few-Shot Prompting To ensure the strength of our baselines, we imple-
727   ment a version of LLM-as-a-Judge based on 6-shot prompting (3-shot in the case of SmolLM jurors
728   due to their modest context window), resulting in similar performance comparisons with peer pre-
729
      diction as those in Figure 1. See Figures 5 and 6 for the comparisons, and see Appendix B.3 for
      implementation details.

      Cross-Domain Comparison of Effectiveness To validate the results in Figure 3 that demonstrates
732
      the effectiveness of peer prediction scores as an indicator of informativeness, we visualize in Figure
733
      7 the correlation between peer prediction scores and ground-truth accuracies (recall that in previous
734   experiments we simply dropped all the ground-truth labels), after aggregating them at a domain
735   level. In particular, Figure 7(b) shows that for all pairs (X, Y ) of models, peer prediction scores of
736   X outperforming those of Y on a problem domain is always positively correlated with X’s ground-
737   truth accuracy outperforming that of Y , thus validating the effectiveness of peer prediction scores.
738
739   Cross-Domain Comparison of Resistance to Deception Figure 10 takes the case where the jury
740   consists of a single SmolLM-360M model, and participants are Llama-3.1-8B models as example,
741   and examines how different methods perform across different domains. The results show that the
742   peer prediction method is more resistant to deception when the population size is larger, but there are
743   significant variances across different domains — in general, it tends to be more resistant to deception
744
      in domains where the mean peer prediction score is higher, which also tends to be STEM domains.
      The reason underlying such a phenomenon could be subject of future research, and we hypothesize
745
      that the reason is due to lower disagreement in priors in STEM domains, which reduces the constant
746
      L0 in Theorem 2, thereby enabling the peer prediction method to be more resistant to deception
747   without the need for an overly large population size.

       B.1       D ETAILS ON THE P EER P REDICTION P IPELINE
1029
1030   We implement a version of Algorithm 1 on large language models. The implementation is based on
1031   the SGLang library, and the code is available in our anonymous repository. For all models involved
1032   (the exact model names have been given in the main sections), inference is performed at temperature
1033   1.0 when generating answers, and the maximum token count is set to 512.
1034   The core of the implemention is the structured dialogue based on in-context learning, which is used
1035   to compute the log-probabilities in Algorithm 1. The structured dialogue flow is as follows:
1036
1037         1. A vanilla system prompt asking the juror to provide informative answers.
1038         2. Example questions randomly picked from past questions are shown to the juror, along with
1039            the answers provided by the witness and the defendant. The purpose is to set the juror up
1040            for the task of predicting the defendant’s answer based on the witness’s answer and learn
1041            about the answer distribution of the defendant.
1042         3. The evaluated question is shown to the juror, along with the witness’s answer. The juror is
1043            then asked to predict the defendant’s answer.
1044
             4. We force the juror to correctly predict the defendant’s exact answer, and calculate the log-
1045            probability of the generation.
1046
1047   A similar dialogue structure is used for the case where the the juror is asked to predict the defendant’s
1048   answer without seeing the witness’s answer. The difference between the two log-probabilities is then
1049   calculated, as per Algorithm 1.

1051   tively:

1463   Before we proceed, we would like to present the following remark on Theorem 2.
1464   Remark 1. Theorem 2 can be directly extended to the case where each participant i has their own
1465   “prior over priors” Di . To show this fact, we need to verify that the honest strategy profile is indeed
1466   a Bayesian Nash equilibrium under this “private Di ” setting. To do that, observe that for any
1467   participant i, the property that honest reporting is its ex-ante optimal strategy given all others do so
1468
       only depends on i’s personal belief Di about others’ beliefs, and not what the others really believe.
1469   It doesn’t matter whether D i is modeled as a distribution over [0, 1]n|A| (i.e., distribution over
1470   priors) or over P [0, 1]n|A| (i.e., distribution over distributions over priors), since the linearity of
1471   expected payoff means that Bayesian Nash equilibria in the former case are preserved in the latter
1472   case, and P(·) can simply be removed by linearity.
1473   Note that at this point, we are basically modeling hierarchical beliefs, which, in theory, would make
1474   the type-based formalism of epistemic game theory handy (Perea, 2012). However, we decided that
1475   introducing type notations would make things needlessly complicated, and so avoided hierarchical
1476   beliefs (those with more than 2 levels) in the theorem statement.

1568   Remark 3 (Intuitive Interpretation of Assumption 1). Let’s first examine the first part of Assumption
1569   1, (bounded) variability within prior (VWP henceforth), which asks that PMI between different
1570   participants is bounded.
1571
       Here, PMI is instead taken over participants’ answers — VWP is measuring the association between
1572
       different participants, asking “when Alice and Bob both answers D to the question, how much we
1573   expect that to be because they converge upon the truth, compared to sheer coincidence?”
1574
1575   The second part, (bounded) variability across priors (VAP henceforth), on the other hand, asks that
1576
       when two agents with disagreeing priors assign differing prior probabilities to “another participant
       (e.g. Alice) giving a certain answer (e.g. D)”, the ratio between their probabilities is bounded.
1577
1578   Taken together, there are usually two ways is which Assumption 1 is satisfied in the real world. Both
1579   are sufficient conditions, so we only need one to be true.
1580
1581         1. Lower-bounded probabilities (VWP+VAP). In a 4-option multiple-choice question, maybe
1582            everyone always assign no less than 5% probability to any option. In this case, we can
1583
                verify that VWP and VAP always hold.
1584         2. All participants have uncertainties about the answer (VWP) and participants are certain
1585            that others have uncertainty too (VAP). In this case, VWP is satisfied because when Alice
1586            and Bob both answers D, the “sheer coinincidence” explanation can now no longer be
1587            ruled out, given that both Alice and Bob’s response has some randomness in it. VAP is
1588            satisfied because, if both you and I agree that Alice has some ”stable” uncertainty between
1589            options A/B/C/D, we won’t disagree catastrophically (e.g. by more than 1000 times) on
1590            how likely it is for Alice to answer D.
1591
1592   Note that these aren’t necessary conditions, but rather two most plausible reasons for VWP/VAP
1593   being true in the real world; there are likely many more of them.
