009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Generalized Zero-Shot Learning (GZSL) faces a key challenge in transferring
012            knowledge from base classes to classify samples from both base and novel classes.
013            This transfer learning paradigm inherently risks a prediction bias, wherein test
014            samples are disproportionately classified towards the base classes due to the mod-
015            els’ familiarity and overfitting to those classes during training. To tackle the pre-
016            diction bias issue, we introduce a divide-and-conquer strategy that segregates the
017            united label space into distinct base and novel subspaces. Within each subspace,
018
               we train a customized model to ensure specialized learning tailored to the dis-
               tinct characteristics of the respective classes. To compensate for the absence of
019
               novel classes, we propose utilizing off-the-shelf diffusion-based generative mod-
020
               els, conditioned on class-level descriptions crafted by Large Language Models
021            (LLMs), to synthesize diverse visual samples representing the novel classes. To
022            further relieve the class confusion in each subspace, we propose to further divide
023            each subspace into two smaller subspaces, where the classes in each smaller sub-
024            space are obtained with the unsupervised cluster strategy in the text embedding
025            space. With our hierarchical divide-and-conquer approach, the test samples are
026            first divided into a smaller subspace and then predicted the class labels with the
027            specialized model trained with the classes present within the subspace. Compre-
028            hensive evaluations across three GZSL benchmarks underscore the effectiveness
029
               of our method, demonstrating its ability to perform competitively and outperform
               existing approaches.

      1    INTRODUCTION
033
034
      Recently, visual language models (VLMs) such as CLIP Radford et al. (2021) and ALIGN Jia et al.
035
      (2021) have significantly facilitated the transfer of knowledge across different tasks or domains.
036   To further adapt the pre-trained VLMs to specific downstream tasks, some researchers propose to
037   fine-tune the models through subtle designed extra learnable layers Gao et al. (2024) or a series
038   of learnable prompts Zhou et al. (2022b) Cao et al. (2024) with some training samples that related
039   to the target tasks or domains. This adaptation hypothesizes that all test samples have the same
040   distribution and are located in the same space as the training samples.
041
      However, when the models are fine-tuned using samples that originate from different classes or
042   domains that the test samples are intended to classify, there may be a domain shift or negative
043   transfer. For example, in Generalized Zero-Shot Learning (GZSL) Xian et al. (2018); Liu et al.
044   (2023), the models are tasked with classifying samples from both base classes, which they were
045   trained on, and novel classes, which they have not seen during training. By fine-tuning the models
046   with only based class samples, the models generally tend to predict all test samples into the same
047   classes as the training samples, resulting in serious prediction bias Wang et al. (2023b).
048   We argue that the prediction bias arises inherently from the transfer learning paradigm, which en-
049   counters test data distributions that deviate significantly from those encountered during training. As
050   shown in Fig. 1(a), in the GZSL setting, if the novel and the base classes are classified in the same
051   test space, it is hard to classify them due to the serious prediction bias. In contrast, the label predic-
052   tion becomes easier when the test samples are classified into base or novel subspaces. To address the
053   prediction bias issue in GZSL, we propose a divide-and-conquer paradigm for GZSL that divides
      the joint label space into sub-spaces and trains a separate model for each label space.

      Specifically, we introduce a hierarchical divide-and-conquer grouping (HDG) approach, tailored for
      GZSL. We divide the joint label space into base and novel subspaces and then train a separate model
075
      for each subspace. In the absence of visual samples of novel classes, we adopt the off-the-shelf
076
      diffusion-based generative models Podell et al. (2023) conditioned on the class-level descriptions
077   generated with GPT-4 Achiam et al. (2023) to synthesize diverse visual samples for each novel
078   class. To encourage the synthesized visual samples to be discriminative, we filter out the samples
079   of inferior quality with a semantic-level concept selection strategy. Given an inference image, we
080   propose a multi-modal distance metric to predict the test images into the correct subspace, where
081   the optimal threshold is determined with a Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) strategy.
082
      The divide-and-conquer paradigm, when applied to base and novel classes, could reduce prediction
083   bias across domains, but it still faces the challenge of class confusion within each subspace, as
084   shown in Fig. 1(b). To this end, we further apply the divide-and-conquer paradigm to each subspace
085   to continue grouping “hypothetical base” and “hypothetical novel” into smaller but focused subsets.
086   Specifically, we adopt the Kmeans method Xie et al. (2016) in the text embedding space to group the
087   classes in each subspace into two clusters, where the classes in each cluster span a smaller subspace.
088   To this end, our method is formulated as a tree structure, as shown in Fig. 1(c).
089   For each subspace, we train a specific model with the visual samples from the corresponding classes.
090   Specifically, we augment the pre-trained and frozen CLIP visual encoder with an additional trainable
091   linear layer at its output. Note that the training samples for novel classes are synthetically gener-
092   ated. During the inference phase, we first assign test samples to their respective subspaces and then
093   classify the test samples with the specific model.
094
      In summary, our main contributions are:
095
096          1. We introduce an innovative hierarchical divide-and-conquer grouping (HDG) paradigm to
097             overcome the prediction issue inherent in traditional transfer learning approaches in GZSL.
098             By employing a hierarchical division of the learning task and subsequently tackling it in a
099             conquering manner, the HDG offers an effective approach to tackling the prediction bias
100             and class confusion that arise in GZSL.
101          2. We propose to generate diverse visual images conditioned on the class-level descriptions
102             derived from the LLMs and subsequently filter out the low-quality samples with a semantic-
103             level concept selection strategy, which compensates for the scarcity of novel class samples.
104             These diverse synthesized samples ensure the effectiveness of the data grouping and model
105             fine-tuning.
106          3. Comprehensive experimental evaluations consistently demonstrate that our proposed HDG
107             paradigm achieves substantial improvements over the current state-of-the-art methods
                across three popular GZSL benchmarks.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
109
110
      Generalized Zero-Shot Learning. GZSL aims to recognize both base and novel classes relying
111   solely on the models trained with the visual samples from base classes. The existing methods can
112   generally be grouped into two lines: (1) Embedding-based methods Naeem et al. (2023); Chen et al.
113   (2022a; 2023) usually align the visual-semantic relationships to connect two different modalities.
114   Once trained, the models can be transferred from base classes to novel ones. However, these meth-
115   ods are constrained to prediction bias towards base classes due to the lack of novel visual data. (2)
116   Generative-based methods Kong et al.; Chen et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2022a) aim to generate batches
117   of visual samples or visual prototypes with semantic descriptions (e.g., sentences, hand-crafted at-
118   tributes) for novel classes, enabling the model to be formulated by supervised classification fashion.
119
      Despite the encouraging advancements that have been made, generative-based methods still grapple
      with prediction bias, stemming from the essence of transfer learning.
120
121   Unlike existing methods, we propose addressing prediction bias by partitioning the test space into
122   distinct subspaces. Our work is most related to calibration-based methods. COSMO Atzmon &
123   Chechik (2019) employs a soft combination strategy to adjust prediction probabilities, while Gatin-
124   gAE Kwon & Al Regib (2022) uses a binary classification approach to distinguish between base and
125
      novel concepts. However, these methods primarily concentrate on the binary classification between
      base and novel concepts, neglecting further data separation within each domain. In contrast, our
126
      approach hierarchically divides the joint class space into subspaces and trains a dedicated model for
127
      each subspace.
128
129   Vision-Language Models for Zero-Shot Generalization. The application of Vision-Language
130   Models (VLMs) such as CLIP Radford et al. (2021) to downstream visual tasks has become increas-
131
      ingly prevalent, thanks to their extensive self-supervised pre-training on vast amounts of web-scale
      image-text data. By comparing the distance between images and texts, VLMs can achieve open
132
      vocabulary recognition using both visual and textual encoders. The emergence of efficient tuning
133
      techniques, such as prompt-learning Zhou et al. (2022a); Khattak et al. (2023) and adapter-tuning
134   Gao et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2022), has further revolutionized this landscape by integrating nat-
135   ural language processing techniques into computer vision. Specifically, CoOP Zhou et al. (2022b)
136   uses learnable prompts to replace hand-crafted templates, while CLIP-Adapter Gao et al. (2024)
137   learns additional Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) layers to adapt general VLMs to specific classes.
138   SHIP Wang et al. (2023b) leverages generative models to combine prototype generation with prompt
139   learning. However, these methods are primarily suitable for tasks with consistent data distributions
140   and may not perform well on tasks with inconsistent distributions.
141   Data Separation and Grouping. In the context of open-world classification, the objective of
142   Out-of-Distribution (OOD) detection is to learn a binary classifier that can distinguish between In-
143   Distribution (ID) and OOD samples, a process that is analogous to our grouping approach. For
144   example, MCM Ming et al. (2022) uses the maximum softmax probability derived from CLIP to
145   perform OOD detection. CLIPN Wang et al. (2023a) further improves performance by incorporat-
146   ing negative prompts. Meanwhile, methods such as OOD Chen et al. (2020) and DUS Su et al.
147   (2022) attempt to separate features of novel classes (in the context of GZSL) from base samples
148
      using variational autoencoders. However, existing methods primarily focus on the binary separation
      between ID and OOD distributions. In contrast, we explore the efficacy of a multi-stage hierarchical
149
      divide approach that differentiates between various distributions, rather than relying on a single-
150
      stage binary classification.

157   present in the training data, while the novel classes are those that are absent during training but are
158
      expected to be recognized during testing. GZSL aims to learn a classifier for classifying test samples
      from both base and novel classes, where the class names of both base and novel classes are available
159
      during the training stage.

302   3.4   F INE - TUNING M ODELS FOR G ENERALIZED Z ERO -S HOT C LASSIFICATION

      With the hierarchical grouping strategy, we segregate test samples into multiple independent class
      sets, which encourages the classification of test samples using models fine-tuned specifically to the
305
      distribution from which they originate. For each distinct class set, we customize a model by ap-
306
      pending a linear layer to the output of the visual encoder. This linear layer is subsequently trained
307   solely on the samples within the corresponding subset, while the visual encoder remains unchanged
308   during this phase. During the inference stage, test samples are initially categorized into their respec-
309   tive subspaces and then predicted using the fine-tuned models tailored to the classes within those
310   subspaces.

      Datasets and Evaluation Metrics. We evaluate our model across three popular benchmarks. The
      benchmarks consist of AwA2 Farhadi et al. (2009), a coarse-grained dataset with 50 classes, and
316
      two fine-grained datasets: CUB Wah et al. (2011), featuring 200 bird species, and SUN Patterson
317
      et al. (2014), with 717 scene categories. Following the GUB protocol provided by Xian et al. (2018),
318   all datasets are divided into two distinct domains, a base domain, which encompasses a set of base
319   classes, each containing a variety of visual samples, and a novel domain, comprising novel classes
320   that are devoid of any visual samples. The performance is evaluated by the harmonic mean of the
321   average per class Top-1 accuracy: H = 2 × B × N/(B + N), where N denotes the accuracy of novel
322   classes and B denotes the accuracy of base classes. Notably, while our method divides the original
323   test space into smaller subsets, we refrain from changing the split of test sets to maintain fairness in
      comparisons.

361   Implementation Details. In the DGC module, we leverage GPT-4 Achiam et al. (2023) to generate
362   the descriptions of each class. For image generation, we adopt Stable-Diffusion-XL Podell et al.
363   (2023) to generate 50 samples for each novel class with 10 descriptions provided by GPT-4. In the
364   HG module, we apply the cosine similarity as the distance metric and select the optimal threshold
365   T based on the normalized mutual information (NMI) Estévez et al. (2009). For the deeper divided
366   process, we adopt Kmeans Kanungo et al. (2002) with K = 2 in the text semantic embedding space
367   to separate the leaf node classes.

      5                    CONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we propose an innovative hierarchical divide-and-conquer grouping (HDG) paradigm
531
      to address the limitations of traditional transfer learning approaches in GZSL. Unlike existing trans-
      fer learning-based methods, our approach progressively divides the unified test space into sub-spaces
532
      by measuring multi-modal distances between test samples and references, thereby constructing sev-
533
      eral independent classification tasks. This explicit division reduces prediction bias between base and
534   novel classes. To further mitigate feature confusion within each domain, we apply the divide-and-
535   conquer strategy to continue grouping each class into smaller, more focused subsets. During the
536   hierarchical division process, we incorporate an LLMs-guided description generation and concept
537   selection strategy to compensate for the scarcity of novel samples. These diverse and synthetically
538   generated samples enhance the effectiveness of data grouping and model fine-tuning. Comprehen-
539   sive evaluations demonstrate that our proposed HDG paradigm significantly outperforms the current
      state-of-the-art methods in GZSL.
