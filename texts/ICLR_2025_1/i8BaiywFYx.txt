011                                           Abstract
012
013           Labeling a training set is not only often expensive but also susceptible to
014           errors. Consequently, the development of robust loss functions to handle
015           label noise has emerged as a problem of great importance. The symmetry
016           condition provides theoretical guarantees for robustness to such noise. In this
017
              work, we investigate a symmetrization method that arises from the unique
              decomposition of any multi-class loss function into a sum of a symmetric
018
              loss function and a class-insensitive term. Notably, the special case of
019
              symmetrizing the cross-entropy loss leads to a multi-class extension of the
020           unhinged loss function. This loss function is linear, but unlike in the binary
021           case, it must have specific coefficients in order to satisfy the symmetry
022           condition. Under appropriate assumptions, we demonstrate that this multi-
023           class unhinged loss function is the unique convex multi-class symmetric
024           loss function. It holds a significant role among multi-class symmetric loss
025           functions since the linear approximation of any symmetric loss function
026           around points with equal components must be equivalent to the multi-class
027           unhinged. Furthermore, we introduce SGCE and α-MAE, two novel loss
028
              functions that smoothly transition between the multi-class unhinged loss and
              the Mean Absolute Error (MAE). Our experiments demonstrate competitive
029
              performance compared to previous state-of-the-art robust loss functions
030
              on standard benchmarks, highlighting the effectiveness of our approach in
031           handling label noise.

034   1   Introduction
035
036   In recent years, deep learning has made significant advancements, achieving state-of-the-art
037   performance in various domains such as computer vision and natural language processing
038   (LeCun et al., 2015). However, these deep learning models often require extensive training
039   on large datasets. Acquiring correct labels for such datasets can be costly. To mitigate this
040   problem, crowdsourcing platforms have been employed, but they come with the drawback of
041   potentially introducing high amount of errors into the labels. Zhang et al. (2017) tried to fit
042
      random labels on CIFAR10 and ImageNet with different deep neural network architectures.
      They came to the conclusion that deep networks can easily fit random labels during training
043
      and that their effective capacity is sufficient for memorizing the entire data set. This
044
      memorization ability can become particularly problematic in the presence of label noise, as
045   the network may learn to fit the noise rather than the true underlying patterns. In order
046   to address this problem, robust loss functions based on the symmetry condition have been
047   proposed (Ghosh et al., 2015), Ghosh et al. (2017). A loss function L(z, y), where z is a score
048
                                                                               PC
      vector for the C classes and y is the label, is symmetric if the quantity k=1 L(z, k) remains
049   constant regardless of z. Additional explanations are provided in Appendix A. Under the
050   symmetry condition, the optimizers of the expected loss on the clean distribution are the
051   same as the optimizers of the expected loss on the corrupted distribution with uniform label
052   noise. Noise robustness results for some types of non uniform noise can also be obtained
053   (Ghosh et al., 2015), Ghosh et al. (2017). The study and the design of new symmetric loss
      functions is therefore of great practical importance.

      This work proposes a principled symmetrization method for multi-class loss functions leading
055   to a general method for producing symmetric loss functions from non-symmetric loss functions.
056   Our main contributions are the following:
057
058        i) We apply the proposed symmetrization method to different loss functions (section
059           4). Notably, the symmetrization of the cross-entropy loss (CE) leads to a multi-class
060           extension to the unhinged loss function (van Rooyen et al., 2015), (Zhou et al., 2023).
061           Moreover, the symmetrization of the generalized cross-entropy (GCE) (Zhang and
062           Sabuncu, 2018) gives rise to a loss function that smoothly transitions between the
063
              multi-class unhinged loss and the mean absolute error (MAE). We refer to this loss
              as SGCE.
064
065        ii) We show the following results about the multi-class unhinged loss function (section
066            5):
067            a) It is the unique convex, non-trivial, non-increasing, multi-class symmetric loss
068               function under the assumption of invariance to permutations (defined in section
069               3).
070            b) It is the linear approximation to the cross-entropy loss function and to any
071               symmetric loss functions satisfying invariance to permutations at points with
072               equal components (section 5.2).
073       iii) We introduce α-MAE a loss function combining the unhinged loss with the MAE
074            where the parameter α directly controls the β-smoothness of the loss (section 6).
075
076   Experiments comparing SGCE and α-MAE with previously proposed robust loss functions
077   on symmetric, asymmetric and natural noise show promising results for our approach (section
078
      7). The proofs of all theoretical results are provided in Appendix D.

080   2   Related work
081
082   Natarajan et al. (2013) proposed a loss correction approach in the binary case which was
083   extended to the multi-class case in Patrini et al. (2016b) by estimating a noise transition
084   matrix. In order to facilitate the estimation of this transition matrix, Yao et al. (2020)
085   considered a factorization of the matrix in two easier to estimate matrices. For its part,
086   Li et al. (2021) estimated the transition matrix and learned the classifier simultaneously
087
      (end-to-end). In the terminology of Algan and Ulusoy (2021), the different approaches
      above belong to the family of noise model based methods since they try to estimate the
088
      noise structure directly. On the other hand, noise model free methods mainly try to design
089
      intrinsically robust loss functions or to exploit different forms of regularization. Our work
090   is concerned with the problem of designing new robust loss functions (through a process
091   of symmetrization of a loss function) and so it belongs to the family of noise model free
092   methods. An advantage of such methods is that they can be computationally less costly
093   since they do not require to estimate the noise model.
094
      Ghosh et al. (2015) introduced the symmetry condition in the binary case and showed that
095
      it is a sufficient condition to make risk minimization robust to label noise. The sigmoid
096   loss, the ramp loss and the probit loss satisfy this condition but the common convex loss
097   functions do not. It turns out that the only binary convex loss function to be symmetric is
098   the unhinged loss (van Rooyen et al., 2015). While the sigmoid loss, the ramp loss and the
099   probit loss achieve robustness by reducing the impact of wrongly classified examples during
100   training (more likely to be corrupted), the unhinged loss achieves robustness by increasing
101   the impact of correctly classified examples during training (more likely to be clean) by being
102   negatively unbounded.
103   Ghosh et al. (2017) proved label noise robustness results for multi-class symmetric loss
104   functions. Under the symmetry condition, any minimizer of the risk on the corrupted
105   distribution with uniform label noise is also a minimizer of the risk on the clean distribution.
106   Noise tolerance under simple non uniform noise and class conditional noise are also obtained
107   but under some more assumptions (the true risk for the optimal classifier must be 0). They
      propose the Mean absolute error (MAE) as a robust loss function for training neural networks.

108
      Their experimental results show that while the cross-entropy loss eventually severely fits the
109   noise, the MAE is much more robust. However, the test performance on clean data is often
110   better for the cross-entropy loss. The MAE loss is seen to be more prone to underfitting and
111   to be slower to train because the gradient can saturate while training.
112
      In order to exploit both the noise robustness of MAE and the speed of training of CE, Zhang
113
      and Sabuncu (2018) proposes a generalization of the two losses. Their loss function is the
114
      negative Box-Cox transformation and it allows to interpolate between the MAE and the CE
115   with a hyperparameter. The symmetric cross-entropy loss is proposed in Wang et al. (2019).
116   This loss function is composed of two terms. A robust term (reverse cross-entropy loss) and
117   the standard cross-entropy loss (for convergence). The reverse cross-entropy loss is a scalar
118   multiple of the MAE. Taylor cross entropy loss (Feng et al., 2021) considers approximations
119   to the cross-entropy loss of different orders. The MAE is the first order Taylor approximation
120   of CE. The second-order Taylor Series approximation of CE is an average combination of
121   MAE and a lower bound of Mean Squared Error (MSE). Order 2 and above approximations
122   of the CE are not symmetric loss functions. Considering different orders of approximation
123
      to the CE is another way to interpolate between the MAE and the CE. These different
      methods are therefore all trying to make a compromise between the robustness of the MAE
124
      and the better fitting ability of CE. Our method is always guaranteed to lead to a symmetric
125
      loss function (contrary to the approaches above) and the problem of underfitting can be
126   alleviated by controlling the amount of saturation in the loss.
127
128   In the special case of the cross-entropy loss, our symmetrization method leads to a multi-class
129
      extension of the unhinged loss function. In most previous works, the unhinged loss in the
      multi-class setting was taken to be equivalent to the MAE (Zhang and Sabuncu, 2018), (Zhu
130
      et al., 2023). This is not the case in our work. In our case, the multi-class unhinged loss is
131
      a linear symmetric loss function like its binary counterpart. This means that no softmax
132   function is being used at the final layer. The work of Patrini et al. (2016b) considers a loss
133   function that they call unhinged in their experiments, but it is actually not a symmetric loss
134   function. It is equivalent up to an additive constant and a multiplicative constant to minus
135   the logit at the target label. The same multi-class extension of the unhinged loss as ours is
136   explored in (Zhou et al., 2023), but in a different context and for a different purpose. They
137   obtain the multi-class unhinged loss by removing the maximum in the multi-class hinge loss.
138   Their goal is to simplify the theoretical analysis of gradient descent dynamics, which they
139   argue is harder to investigate using cross-entropy or mean squared error. Being linear, the
140
      unhinged loss can simplify theoretical research. Our focus, however, is on discussing the
      fundamental role of the multi-class unhinged loss among multi-class symmetric loss functions
141
      and proposing new robust loss functions (SGCE, α-MAE) that build upon the unhinged loss.
142
143   The closest work to our own is (Ma et al., 2020). They propose a normalization applicable to
144   any loss function that leads to a symmetric loss function. They observe however that their
145   robust loss functions often suffer from underfitting. The solution that they propose is called
146
      the active-passive loss framework. An active loss function is only optimizing directly at the
      specified label. A passive loss also explicitly minimizes the probability for other classes. An
147
      active-passive loss is then defined to be a weighted combination of an active and a passive
148
      loss. Both the active and the passive loss are required to be robust in order to ensure that
149   the combination is also robust. Subsequently, Ye et al. (2023) proposed the normalized
150   negative cross-entropy as a substitute to the MAE in the active-passive framework. Our
151   work instead proposes a different general method than normalization to produce symmetric
152   loss functions. Our method exploit the fact that there is a symmetric loss function hidden
153   within any loss function, as demonstrated by our general decomposition result.
154
      The work of Patrini et al. (2016a) investigates binary loss functions that can be decomposed
155   as a sum of a class-insensitive term and a linear term. They refer to these losses as linear-odd
156   losses (the odd part of the loss is linear). The labeled data centroid becomes the quantity of
157   interest in the linear component of the loss and the subsequent works of Gao et al. (2016),
158   Gong et al. (2021) and Gong et al. (2022) proposed estimators for this centroid when only
159   corrupted data is available. Ding et al. (2022) extended these ideas to the multi-class case by
160   decomposing the multi-class mean squared error loss in order to also reduce the problem to
161   centroid estimation. Our work considers instead the general decomposition of any multi-class

      loss function into a symmetric loss function and a class-insensitive term. We discuss the
163   multi-class data centroid related to the multi-class unhinged loss function in Appendix C.

166   3     Assumptions on multi-class loss functions
167
168
      We consider loss functions of the form L(z, y), where z = (zi , · · · , zC ) = h(x) ∈ RC is the
      score vector for some neural network h, x ∈ Rd is an input and y ∈ {1, · · · , C} is a label
169
      for an example (x, y) sampled from a distribution D on Rd × {1, · · · , C}. We will make
170
      two main assumptions on the loss function. First, we want the loss function L(z, y) to be
171   non-increasing.
172
173   Definition 3.1 We say that L(z, y) is a non-increasing multi-class loss function if for all y,
174   the function L(z, y) is a non-increasing function of zy when zk for k ̸= y is kept fixed.
175
176
      Secondly, we want a form of symmetry between the classes to hold (different from the notion
      of symmetry related to noise robustness). This is done to ensure that L(z, k) and L(z ′ , k ′ ),
177
      where k = ̸ k ′ , will be the same function when the roles of k and k ′ are swapped. We will
178
      refer to this property as invariance to permutations and it is defined precisely below.
179
180   Definition 3.2 The loss function L(z, y) is invariant to permutations τ on C elements if
181   L(τ (z), τ (y)) = L(z, y) for all z, y, and τ . A permutation τ acts on z by permuting the
182   components of z, that is, τ (z) = (zτ −1 (1) , · · · , zτ −1 (C) ).
183
184   An example of a loss function satisfying both assumptions above is the standard cross-entropy
                                                            exp(zy )
185   loss defined by L(z, y) = − log(py ), where py = PC             is obtained via the softmax

186   function. Another simple example is the linear loss function L(z, y) = −zy . An example of a
187   loss function that does not satisfy the invariance to permutations property is L(z, y) = −yzy .

251   5     Some properties of the multi-class unhinged
252
253   5.1   Uniqueness among convex and symmetric loss functions
254
      The multi-class unhinged loss satisfies the same fundamental result as the binary unhinged
255
      loss.
256
257   Theorem 5.1 The multi-class unhinged loss is the unique convex, non-trivial, non-
258   increasing, multi-class symmetric loss function satisfying the property of invariance to
259   permutations (up to an additive and a multiplicative constant).
260
      The invariance to permutations property plays a crucial role in the proof for the multi-class
261
      case. The initial step in our proof involves establishing constraints on the coefficients of
262
      a linear loss that satisfies the invariance to permutations property (Lemma D.1). These
263   constraints on the coefficients are subsequently used to prove uniqueness among linear loss
264   functions (Proposition D.2). The proof can then be completed by observing that any convex
265   symmetric loss function must be both convex and concave, and therefore is affine.
266

354
355   We first compare the performance of the multi-class unhinged loss, SGCE, and α-MAE

      presented in Table 1. For the CIFAR-10 experiments, we trained an 8-layer CNN for 120

      it for 200 epochs. The comparison includes CE, MAE, GCE (Zhang and Sabuncu, 2018),
359
      SCE (Wang et al., 2019), NCE+RCE (Ma et al., 2020), NCE+AGCE (Zhou et al., 2021), and
360   ANL-CE (Ye et al., 2023). Both symmetric label noise (see A) and asymmetric label noise

364
      of (Ye et al., 2023). The key difference is that we tuned the weight decay term separately for
365
      each loss function, whereas (Ye et al., 2023) only tuned an additional regularization parameter
366   δ for their method without adjusting the weight decay for other loss functions. Otherwise,
367   we followed the same experimental protocol as in (Ma et al., 2020) and (Ye et al., 2023).

      9     Conclusion
472
      In this work, we proposed a principled symmetrization method for designing robust loss
473
      functions to handle label noise. The symmetrization of the categorical cross-entropy loss leads
474
      to the unique convex, non-trivial, non-increasing multi-class symmetric loss function under
475   the technical assumption of invariance to permutations. As such, this loss function extends
476   the binary unhinged loss in the multi-class case. The symmetrization of the generalized cross-
477   entropy loss (SGCE) and the newly introduced α-MAE allow for the effective combination
478   of the multi-class unhinged loss with the MAE. Our approach demonstrates competitive
479   performance compared to previously proposed robust loss functions on the benchmark
480   datasets CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and WebVision.

610   A     Uniform label noise and the symmetry condition
611
612   The influential work of (Ghosh et al., 2015) and (Ghosh et al., 2017) introduced the concept
613   of symmetric loss functions and established the fundamental results of statistical consistency
614   that they satisfy when training in the presence of noisy labels. At the limit of infinite data,
615   an optimal classifier for the corrupted distribution will also be an optimal solution for the
616
      clean distribution if the loss function is symmetric. In this section, we introduce the concept
      of symmetric loss functions to readers who are new to these ideas.

645   B    Decomposition in the binary case and Taylor series
646
647
      In the binary case, the unique decomposition of a loss function ϕ(z) into a sum of a symmetric
      loss function and a class-insensitive loss function is the sum of its odd part ϕ(z)−ϕ(−z)
                                                                                          2     with


                                                            12
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


648
      its even part ϕ(z)+ϕ(−z)
                         2     . If ϕ admits a Taylor series expansion around 0, it is possible to
649   characterize the symmetry condition using the coefficients of the Taylor series and to express
650   the odd part and the even part with the series.
651
652   Proposition B.1 Assume that ϕ is an infinitely differentiable potential and without loss of
653   generality that ϕ(0) = 0. Then, ϕ is symmetric if and only if ϕ(k) (0) = 0 for all k even. That
654   is, ϕ is symmetric if and only if the even coefficients of its Taylor expansion at 0 are all 0.
655
656
      Since the odd part of ϕ is the sum over the terms with odd coefficients of the Taylor series,
      our symmetrization method corresponds to the very natural process of keeping the odd
657
      coefficients of the original loss and replacing the even coefficients by 0’s. Every truncation
658
      of the Taylor expansion for the symmetric loss function is also symmetric. This allows
659   approximating any such symmetric loss functions with simpler polynomial symmetric loss
660   functions.
661
662
      The decomposition as a sum of an odd and an even function in the binary case makes
      sense because changing the label amounts to changing the sign of z. However, this does
