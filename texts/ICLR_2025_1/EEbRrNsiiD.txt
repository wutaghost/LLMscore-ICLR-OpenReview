009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs) and Large Multimodal Models
012            (LMMs) on mobile devices has gained significant attention due to the benefits of
013            enhanced privacy, stability, and personalization. However, the hardware constraints
014            of mobile devices necessitate the use of models with fewer parameters and model
015            compression techniques like quantization. Currently, there is limited understanding
016
               of quantization’s impact on various task performances, including LLM tasks,
               LMM tasks, and, critically, trust and safety. There is a lack of adequate tools for
017
               systematically testing these models on mobile devices. To address these gaps,
018
               we introduce MobileAIBench, a comprehensive benchmarking framework for
019            evaluating mobile-optimized LLMs and LMMs. MobileAIBench assesses models
020            across different sizes, quantization levels, and tasks, measuring latency and resource
021            consumption on real devices. Our two-part open-source framework includes a
022            library for running evaluations on desktops and a mobile app for on-device latency
023            and hardware utilization measurements. Our thorough analysis aims to accelerate
024            mobile AI research and deployment by providing insights into the performance
025            and feasibility of deploying LLMs and LMMs on mobile platforms.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      With billions of parameters trained on massive amounts of data, LLMs and LMMs have achieved
030   remarkable breakthroughs in a wide range of applications from question answering Baek et al. (2023)
031   to intelligent agents Wang et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024b) and beyond Achiam et al. (2023); Team
032   et al. (2023); Nijkamp et al. (2022); Murthy et al. (2024). Most recently, the pursuit of deploying
033   LLMs and LMMs on mobile devices has garnered attention, and for good reason. There are several
034   key benefits to deploying AI on mobile devices including offline access, enhanced privacy, and
035   improved performance. It also provides cost efficiency by decreasing server and bandwidth usage,
036   while enhancing user experience with faster and more interactive applications.
037   Given the extreme limitations of mobile hardware, deploying LLMs and LMMs on mobile devices is
038   challenging. First, the model must have a relatively small number of parameters since parameters
039   drive the number of computations, which consume memory, CPU, and GPU resources. Second, even
040   with a relatively small number of parameters, these models may not fit onto a mobile phone’s limited
041   hardware. To further reduce resource footprint, quantization has emerged as a practical, heuristic
042   approach to reduce precision of model weights with seemingly little penalty to performance.
043   While deploying LLMs and LMMs to mobile devices for real use cases appears to be feasible in the
044   near future, there are knowledge and tooling gaps remaining. First, while quantization seems to be a
045   practical way to reduce the resource footprint of small LLMs and LMMs, there is little to no rigorous
046   measurement and understanding of the effect of quantization on task performance, including LLM
047   tasks, LMM tasks, and critically, trust and safety. Second, there is limited or no tooling available to
048   systematically test quantized models across these tasks. Third, there is limited or no tooling available
049   to test quantized models on a real mobile device across tasks.
050   In this work, we aim to help accelerate mobile AI research and deployment by providing thorough
051   benchmarking and analysis of open source mobile-optimized LLMs and LMMs. We restrict LLMs and
052   LMMs in consideration to have at most 7B parameters, as we found 7B to be the upper limit of what
053   a high-end phone’s hardware can manage (even after quantization). We measure and analyze current
      LLM and LMM task performance under different levels of quantization, from 16-bit quantization

      down to 3-bit quantization in some cases. We selected tasks that are most representative of real-world
055   mobile use cases and considerations. In addition to task performance, we also benchmark our selected
056   LLMs and LMMs on a real mobile device, an iPhone 14. We measure several key latency metrics
057   such as time-to-first-token, and hardware utilization such as CPU usage and RAM usage.
058
      Our results are collected using MobileAIBench, our new two-part framework for evaluating LLMs
059
      and LMMs for mobile deployment. The first part of the framework is an open source library, for
060
      use on desktops or servers, to evaluate model’s performance on a specially selected set of widely
061   known benchmarks. Using this part of the framework, users are able to test their quantized models
062   across benchmarks as they desire. The second component of the MobileAIBench framework is an
063   open-source mobile application, available on both iOS and Android platforms. With our iOS/Android
064   app, users are able to measure latency and mobile hardware utilization such as RAM and CPU of
065   quantized LLMs and LMMs. Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
066
067          • We are the first to provide a thorough benchmarking and analysis of open source LLMs
068            and LMMs across varying levels of quantization and various tasks. Our evaluations are
069            generated and reproducible using our newly developed framework, MobileAIBench.
070
071          • MobileAIBench is the first open-source framework for on-device task-specific LLM and
072            LMM testing, enabling researchers to evaluate small models and practitioners to assess
073            model viability for mobile deployment.
074
075          • We conduct extensive experiments to evaluate LLMs/LMMs over a wide range of tasks, pro-
076            viding insightful findings regarding the impact of quantization and real-mobile deployment.

      Many benchmarks have been developed to evaluate LLMs and LMMs from different perspectives. For
      example, MMLU Hendrycks et al. (2020) provides a large number of tasks to extensively test world
083
      knowledge and problem solving ability. AlpacaEval Dubois et al. (2024) and MT-Bench Zheng et al.
084
      (2024) provide open-ended question answering evaluation tasks without explicit answers, and employ
085   GPT-4 Achiam et al. (2023) as the success rate judge. KoLA Yu et al. (2023) uses Wikipedia and
086   the continuously collected emerging corpora data to provides knowledge-oriented LLM assessment
087   tasks. TruthfulQA Lin et al. (2021), TrustLLM Sun et al. (2024), Safetybench Zhang et al. (2023)
088   measure LLMs’ trust and safety levels. To assist the development of agents, benchmarks Zhou
089   et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024b); Zhou et al. (2023) have also been developed to evaluate LLM’s
090   instruction-following ability. FOFO Xia et al. (2024) contains diverse data formats, and is able to
091   test the format-following ability of current LLMs. MME Fu et al. (2024) provides comprehensive
092   multimodal evaluation benchmars over 14 subtasks. VisIT-Bench Bitton et al. (2023) provides an
093
      instruction-following vision-language datasets to test LMMs’ real-world use-case. MMVP Tong et al.
      (2024) provides 9 basic visual patterns that LMMs easily give incorrect answers and hallucinated
094
      explanations. However, these benchmarks do not consider the quantized versions of models, nor
095
      determine the impact of deployment constraints on model performance. MobileAIBench fills this gap
096   by focusing on deployment utilization on real mobile devices. Besides model performance on specific
097   tasks, MobileAIBench emphasizes model quantization, inference speed, and required deployment
098   resources.
099
      Several papers have discussed strategies and evaluations for developing mobile-ready models Zhang
100
      et al. (2024a). Jin et al. (2024) compares different quantization methods and evaluates the performance
101
      of quantized LLMs. Liu et al. (2024a) considered different architectures to develop the most
102   performant mobile models. MobileVLM Chu et al. (2023) designs vision language models for mobile
103   devices. The Octopus series Chen et al. (2024a) aim to empower the agentic ability on mobile device
104   by training API tokens. Recent small models such as Phi3 Abdin et al. (2024) and Gemma Team
105   et al. (2024) have, in their model cards, various evaluation results. However, none of these are as
106   comprehensive as the set of data, models, and evaluation metrics proposed in MobileAIBench. In
107   developing this standardized benchmark, we hope to make it easier for model developers to consider
      the various factors needed to develop mobile-ready models.

139   3.1        TASKS AND DATASETS
140
141   This section outlines the tasks and datasets used to benchmark LLMs & LMMs across various
142
      domains including Natural Language Processing (NLP), Multimodal, and Trust & Safety tasks.
143
144   3.1.1          S TANDARD NLP TASKS
145
146   Standard NLP tasks encompass various benchmarks designed to evaluate different capabilities of
147   LLMs. Question answering tests an LLM’s ability to comprehend context and respond accurately, for
148   which we use the Databricks-dolly Conover et al. (2023) and HotpotQA Yang et al. (2018) datasets.
149   Summarization tasks involve condensing large amounts of information into shorter forms while
150   retaining essential ideas. We assess LLM performance in summarization using the CNN/Daily Mail
151   Hermann et al. (2015); Nallapati et al. (2016) and XSum Narayan et al. (2018) datasets. Text-to-SQL
152   tasks evaluate an LLM’s proficiency in crafting SQL queries based on natural language questions.
153
      For this purpose, we employ the Sql-Create-Context b mc2 (2023) dataset. Additionally, we include
      popular benchmarks such as the Massive Multitask Language Understanding (MMLU) Hendrycks
154
      et al. (2021) to evaluate the LLM’s accuracy in multitask performance and Grade School Math
155
      (GSM8K) Cobbe et al. (2021) for assessing LLM’s ability to solve mathematical problems. To further
156   evaluate LLM performance, we utilize benchmarks such as AlpacaEval, an automatic evaluation
157   method that quantifies the Win-Rate by measuring the proportion of instances where the model’s
158   output is preferred over the reference output Li et al. (2023), and MT-Bench, a collection of complex
159   multi-turn open-ended questions used to evaluate chat assistants, with GPT-4 serving as the judge
160   Zheng et al. (2023). For Question Answering, Summarization, Text-to-SQL, GSM8K and MMLU
161   tasks, we randomly select 1,000 samples from each relevant dataset. For AlpacaEval and MT-Bench,
      we use the standard test sets.

      3.1.2    M ULTIMODAL TASKS
163
164   Multimodal tasks require LMMs to process different data types such as text, images, and audio. This
165   is critical for developing AI systems that handle complex user requirements on mobile devices. We
166   focus on Visual Question Answering (VQA) Antol et al. (2015), selecting five datasets that cover
167   a wide range of contexts. Among them, VQA-v2 Goyal et al. (2017), VizWiz Gurari et al. (2018),
168   GQA Hudson & Manning (2019), and TextVQA Singh et al. (2019) require the LMM to directly
169
      answer visual questions with a single word or phrase. ScienceQA Lu et al. (2022) dataset requires
      selecting the correct answer from multiple choices. Similar to the standard NLP tasks, for each
170
      dataset, we randomly select 1000 samples to evaluate the LMMs’ performance.
171
172
      3.1.3    T RUST AND S AFETY
173
174   To assess the societal impact of LLMs, we include a suite of trust and safety evaluations, focusing
175   on six categories: truthfulness, safety, robustness, fairness, privacy, and ethics, following Sun et al.
176   (2024). For truthfulness, we use the TruthfulQA Lin et al. (2021) (TruthQA) dataset to assess the
177   ability to select the correct answer from common misconceptions. For safety, the Do-Not-Answer
178   Wang et al. (2023) (DNA) dataset measures if LLMs can refuse illegal, unethical, or otherwise
179
      undesirable requests. Robustness is evaluated using Adversarial Instruction Sun et al. (2024)
      (Adv-Inst), which tests models’ robustness to prompt perturbations like typos or irrelevant links.
180
      Fairness is measured using the BBQ dataset Parrish et al. (2021), assessing the tendency to fall for
181
      common stereotypes related to gender, age, race, etc. Privacy is tested with hand-crafted prompts
182   based on the Enron email dataset Shetty & Adibi (2004) (Priv-Lk), examining if models can decline
183   requests for personal information. For ethics, we use the Social Chemistry 101 (SC-101) dataset
184   Forbes et al. (2020) to evaluate moral acceptability judgments for different situations.

      4.1       E FFECTIVENESS E VALUATION
239
240   In this section, we examine the effectiveness of various models across different quantization levels
241   (4-bit, 8-bit, and 16-bit). Our primary objective is to evaluate model performance at these bit-width
242   levels without comparing different quantization methods. To ensure consistency in our experimental
243   setup, we used the legacy linear quantization method, commonly known as Q4_0, Q8_0, and f16 in
244   the llama.cpp implementation, as it was available for all the bit-widths under consideration. This
245   approach allowed us to control for variables and focus solely on the impact of quantization levels on
246   model performance. More details on the different quantization methods supported by llama.cpp is
247   presented in Section A.5

413   4.3                           E FFICIENCY AND U TILIZATION E VALUATION
414
415   We evaluate current LLMs and LMMs on real mobile device using the iOS app provided within
416   MobileAIBench to test the efficiency and utilization. All experiments are evaluated on the same
417
      iPhone 14 device to guarantee the comparability. All models are quantized to 4bit and only those
      under 3B are deployable. We conduct the experiments with 3 LLMs (Phi2 3B, Gemma 2B, TinyLlama
418
      1B) on 4 NLP datasets and 1 LMM (Llava-Phi-2) on 2 LMM datasets.
419
420   Observation and Analysis: Experiment results of LLMs are shown in Table 4. We can have the
421   following observations. (1) Smaller models have lower TTFT and higher ITPS/OTPS. It indicates
422   smaller models have faster encoding/decoding speed as the computation required for processing each
423
      token is decreased. (2) The shortest OET and Total Time may not be achieved by the smallest model.
      For example, on HotpotQA dataset, the lowest OET and Total Time are achieved by Phi2 model.
424
      It owes to the input/output token length that is directly related with the model. Though Phi2 has
425
      the slowest encoding/decoding speed, it can achieve the fastest OET and Total time by giving more
426   concise responses. (3) The on-device memory consumption is intense and directly relates to model
427   sizes. For the iPhone 14 with a total RAM of 6 GiB, even running the 4-bit quantized TinyLlama
428   model (1B) on-device takes more than 50% of the overall memory, leaving limited space for other
429   APPs. The memory consumption increases with larger models. (4) The CPU utilization is naturally
430   different with different models. On all the 4 datasets, Phi2 takes the lowest and Gemma takes the
431   highest CPU utilization, respectively. It is a surprise finding that the on-device CPU utilization is
      not related to model sizes, which reals the necessity of on-device testing for mobile deployment.

467   5       D ISCUSSION
468
469   MobileAIBench offers a comprehensive evaluation of LLMs and LMMs in terms of task effectiveness,
470   on-device efficiency, and utilization. It covers a wide range of text and multimodal tasks, assessing
471   the impact of various quantization levels on current models. Additionally, trust and safety evaluations
472   are included to ensure the reliability and security of LLM/LMM applications on mobile devices. As a
473   benchmarking platform for mobile deployment, MobileAIBench aims to enhance performance while
474   minimizing any potential negative social impact.
475   Extensive experiments with MobileAIBench reveal several interesting findings. Quantization is an
476   effective way to decrease model size while keeping the performance. Different models/tasks have
477   varied sensitivity to quantization levels. Current LLMs and LMMs require significant resources in
478   terms of CPU and RAM usage when deployed on mobile devices, even with the 1B model. More
479   compact and optimized LLMs and LMMs are needed for mobile deployment.
480   While MobileAIBench is a significant advancement, we acknowledge its limitations. It excludes other
481   model compression methods, like model pruning, as they are not mature enough for deployment.
482   Additionally, due to space constraints and the wide variety of mobile devices, we have not conducted
483   a comprehensive comparison across different hardware. Support for different species of mobile
484   devices is still under development and will open to public upon release.

              • Task: Question & Answering
763
764
                  – Exact Match: The Exact Match score evaluates the accuracy of a QA system by
                    checking whether the predicted answer is exactly the same as the reference answer.
765
                    This means that for each question, the predicted answer must match the ground truth
766
                    answer exactly, including any formatting, punctuation, and whitespace.
767
                  – F1 Score: F1 score combines precision and recall into a single metric by taking their
768
                    harmonic mean. We tokenize the ground truth and predictions, then we computer the
769                 precision and recall and finall compute the F1 score.
770
              • Task: Summarization
771
772
                  – Rouge-1: The ROUGE-1 metric measures the overlap of unigrams (single words)
                    between the candidate summary and the reference summary.
773
774
                  – Rouge-L: The ROUGE-L metric evaluates the longest common subsequence (LCS)
                    between the candidate summary and the reference summary. It measures the precision,
775
                    recall, and F1 score based on the longest matching sequence of words, emphasizing the
776
                    importance of order and continuity in the generated summaries.
777
              • Task: Text-to-SQL
778
779               – SQL Parser: The SQL query is converted into a graph where each node represents
780
                    an SQL keyword, and its children represent the items associated with that keyword.
                    This is implemented using a Python dictionary. To measure the SQL parser score, we
781
                    evaluate the overlap between the ground truth graph and the predicted graph.
782
                  – Levenshtein score: The Levenshtein score, also known as the Levenshtein distance,
783
                    measures the minimum number of single-character edits (insertions, deletions, or
784
                    substitutions) required to change one word or string into another.
785
              • Task: AlpacaEval
786
787               – Win-Rate: Fraction of times predicted response is chosen over the predictions made
788
                    by a baseline model. (In our case, baseline model is GPT-4)
789           • Task: MT-Bench
790               – Score: Using GPT-4 in llm-as-a-judge framework, we ask the judge to score a given
791                 response on a scale of 10. (10 being the highest score)
792           • Task: Trust & Safety
793
                  – Accuracy: Using GPT-4o in llm-as-a-judge framework, we ask the judge to determine
794                 if the predicted answer is same as the ground truth answer.
795
              • Task: VQA
796
797
                  – Score (single ground-truth): For datasets with a single ground-truth answer per
                    question (e.g., GQA, ScienceQA), we score each test sample based on exact match: 1
798
                    if the prediction matches, otherwise 0. Then, the score for each dataset is calculated by
799
                    averaging the total test cases.
800
                  – Score (multiple ground-truth): For datasets with multiple human-provided ground-
801
                    truth answers (e.g., VQA-v2, VisWiz, TextVQA),          the accuracy score for each test case

881   A.5   VARIOUS QUANTIZATIONS SUPPORTED BY LLAMA . CPP
882
883   The llama.cpp library supports a wide variety of quantization levels for efficient model compression
884
      and inference. These include:
885
             • Q4_0 and Q4_1: 4-bit quantization, which provides a good balance between performance
886            and model size.
887
888
             • Q5_0 and Q5_1: 5-bit quantization, offering slightly higher precision than Q4.
889          • Q8_0: 8-bit quantization for scenarios where maintaining higher accuracy is crucial.
890          • Q2_K, Q3_K, Q4_K, Q5_K, and Q6_K: Variants of k-quantizations, which are more
891            specialized, aiming to reduce size further with minimal impact on accuracy.
892          • F16 and BF16: Floating-point formats for higher precision without the full overhead of
893            32-bit float types.
894
             • F32: The highest precision format, which is typically the baseline.
