009                                                       A BSTRACT
010
011             Evaluating models on large benchmarks can be very resource-intensive, especially during a
012             period of rapid model iteration. Existing efficient evaluation methods approximate the per-
013             formance of target models by assessing them on a small static coreset derived from publicly
014             available evaluation results of source models. However, these approaches rely on the assump-
015             tion that each target model has a high prediction consistency with source models, which doesn’t
016             generalize well in practice, leading to inaccurate performance estimates. To fill this gap, we pro-
017             pose TAILORED B ENCH, a method that provides customized evaluations tailored to each target
018
                model. Specifically, a Global-coreset is first constructed as a probe to identify the most consis-
                tent source models for the target models with an adaptive source model selection strategy. Af-
019
                terwards, a scalable K-Medoids clustering algorithm is proposed to extend the Global-coreset to
020
                tailored Native-coreset for each target model. According to the predictions on respective Native-
021             coreset, we estimate the overall performance of target models with a calibrated restoration strat-
022             egy. Comprehensive experiments on five benchmarks across over 300 models demonstrate that
023             compared to best performing baselines, TAILORED B ENCH achieves an average reduction of
024             24.8% in MAE of accuracy estimates, showcasing strong effectiveness and generalizability.

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Scaling up models to larger size has led to remarkable advancements in their capabilities (Touvron et al., 2023;
030   Ouyang et al., 2022), which also presents significant challenges for efficiently assessing them. For instance, Liang
031   et al. (2022a) reported that evaluating a model with approximately 10 billion parameters on the HELM leaderboard
032   can cost over $1,700 via APIs or more than 1,200 GPU hours. Moreover, such overhead needs to be multiplied
033   by X when determining optimal training configurations during the development phase or selecting the best model
034
      and inference settings during the deployment phase among X candidates.
035   To achieve efficient evaluation, some studies (Vivek et al., 2024; Polo et al., 2024) have explored the following
036   paradigm: step 1. constructing example representations according to the predictions from a set of source models
037   (which are freely available for many popular leaderboards1,2,3 ) ; step 2. clustering the whole benchmark and
038   selecting the cluster centroids to form a coreset (typically no more than 100 examples); step 3. approximating the
039   performance of target models under evaluation on the whole benchmark based on their predictions on the coreset.
040
      This paradigm is built upon the assumption that the predicted probability of the correct class is strongly correlated
      across models for many dozens of examples from a given benchmark. Thus, if the source models’ predictions
041
      on example a and b exhibit a similar trend, then such similarity is expected to generalize to the target model —
042
      testing the target model solely on example a is sufficient to predict its result on b.
043
044   Nevertheless, we find that such generalizability between source and target models does not necessarily hold.
045   Following A NCHOR P OINT (Vivek et al., 2024), we construct an M -dimensional representation vector based on
046
      the correctness of M source models for each example and visualize them using t-SNE algorithm (Van der Maaten
      & Hinton, 2008). The closer two examples are, the more similar the source models perform on them (Figure 1a).
047
      Therefore, the cluster centroids (marked as stars) are selected to represent each cluster and form the coreset.
048
      However, when representing the examples using the predictions of target models (Figure 1b), the examples in the
049   original coreset are no longer able to represent the corresponding clusters perfectly, indicating a moderate level of
050   prediction consistency among source and target models. This lack of generalizability results in the performance
051   of target models on the coreset failing to approximate their true performance accurately.
052
      To address the aforementioned issue, we propose the TAILORED B ENCH method, which adaptively constructs
053
      model-specific evaluation coreset through a hybrid of priors and posteriors, enabling accurate and efficient evalu-
054
      ation. Specifically, we first construct a static G-set (Global-coreset) based on the metric of all the source models,

110   2   TAILORED B ENCH A PPROACH
111
112
113   The TAILORED B ENCH method is founded on the principle of dynamically selecting the source models with high-
114   est prediction consistency and constructing N-set that can represent the whole benchmark for each target model.
115   The core designs throughout the process are constructing the G-set (§2.2), selecting the native source models
      (§2.3), developing the N-set (§2.4), and estimating the overall performance (§2.5).

245   After constructing the N-set Nψm for target model ψm , we evaluate ψm on the examples in Nψm \ G, since ψm

258   2.6   T HE E LEMENT-W ISE D ISTANCE
259
260   Previous work employs correlation distance to estimate the consistency between examples, operating under the
261   assumption that scoring patterns between models or examples exhibit linear trends. However, this approach can
262
      encounter limitations, particularly when attempting to establish linear relationships between discrete numerical
      embeddings across different models, often resulting in a significant decline in performance. In contrast, the
263
      TAILORED B ENCH utilizes element-wise distance metrics, such as Manhattan distance and cosine similarity, to
264
      effectively capture individual discrepancies in correctness vectors without relying on a strict linear relationship.
265   Our experiments in Table 7 demonstrate that element-wise metrics outperform correlation distance in constructing
266   representative G-sets and N-sets, as well as in selecting Native Source Models. Consequently, we use Manhattan
267   distance as the primary DIST metric in the TAILORED B ENCH method.

355   Baseline and Evaluation Metrics We compare our TAILORED B ENCH method against three baselines: Random
356   Sampling, Anchor Points (Vivek et al., 2024), and gp-IRT (Polo et al., 2024). The Random Sampling randomly
357   selects a subset of examples from the benchmark to estimate model performance, serving as a basic reference
358
      point. Anchor Points uses K-Medoids to select a fixed set of representative examples based on the source models
      to estimate the performance of target models. gp-IRT method employs an Item Response Theory model trained
359
      on the predictions of the source models to estimate target models’ performance on the full benchmark. Similar to
360
      our approach, we train the gp-IRT model using the predictions of the same set of source models and estimate the
361   performance of the same set of target models. We evaluate the performance of these methods with two metrics:
362   Kendall’s τ Correlation Coefficient and Mean Absolute Error (MAE). Kendall’s τ measures the ordinal association
363   between the estimated and true model rankings, effectively capturing how well our method preserves the relative
364   performance order of the target models. MAE quantifies the average absolute difference between the estimated
365   and true performance scores, assessing the precision of our method in estimating individual model performances.

511   4   R ELATED W ORKS
512
513   Models Correlation in Predictive Consistency: Prior works (Miller et al., 2021; Awadalla et al., 2022; Liang
514   et al., 2022b) have demonstrated a certain level of correlation between in-distribution (ID) and out-of-distribution
515   (OOD) performances across diverse models and tasks. Building on this foundation, Baek et al. (2022) and Mehra
516   et al. (2024) advance this relationship by showing the phenomenon that the agreement between two models on
517   ID data is linearly correlated with their agreement on OOD data, where the accuracy holds the similar linear rela-
518   tionship, enabling accurate estimation of model’s OOD accuracy based solely on ID data. Our work extends this
519
      phenomenon to address the challenge of benchmark compression, enabling the selection of more representative
      subsets for benchmarks.
520
521   Coreset Selection for Efficient Benchmarking: As LLMs proliferate and version updates accelerate, the cost
      of thoroughly evaluating each model across all benchmarks has become prohibitive, leading to methods that


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


522
      subsample the most representative subsets from each benchmark for more efficient evaluation. Vivek et al. (2024)
523   clusters examples directly using the confidence scores provided by source models, leveraging these scores to select
524   an optimal subset. Similarly, Polo et al. (2024) employs an Item Response Theory (IRT) model, trained on the
525   success matrix of each source model across various examples, to derive the latent representations of examples for
526   clustering. Perlitz et al. (2023) proposes Flash-HELM, which dynamically adjusts the sizes of randomly selected
527   subsets based on model ranking, where higher-ranked models are evaluated with greater precision. Prabhu et al.
528   (2024) proposes the Sort & Search (S&S) strategy, which leverages the difficulties of examples and dynamic
529   programming to select the coreset. Xu et al. (2024) aggregates all methods above and dynamically chooses the
530   optimal subset selection method for each benchmark but requires many examples to determine the best approach.
531
      Despite these advancements, these methods often struggle with substantial distribution shifts between the source
      and target models, caused by the discrepancy between their predictive consistency, potentially causing significant
532
      distortion in estimating the target model’s performance. Extending the approach of Vivek et al. (2024), our work
533
      alleviates this issue by dynamically selecting a native source model set with the highest prediction consistency
534   to the target model, ensuring the selection of a tailored coreset for each target model that best represents the
535   benchmark.
536
537
      Scaling Approaches for Model Performance Estimations: Scaling law describes the relationship between
      model properties (e.g., FLOPs used during training, model parameter size) and model capabilities. Recent works
538
      (Hu et al., 2023; Ruan et al., 2024; Isik et al., 2024) have leveraged scaling laws to predict model performance on
539
      various downstream tasks, reducing the computational cost of evaluating models on complex downstream tasks.
540   Zhang et al. (2024) simplifies those approaches by utilizing the relationships between model families and their
541   collaborative overall performance across tasks rather than fitting scaling laws. The aforementioned methods typ-
542   ically rely on overall model performance across several benchmarks and specific design factors (e.g., model size
543   or training data properties) to either fit scaling curves or investigate correlations between models on various tasks.
544   In contrast, our approach addresses a more general case by reducing the evaluation cost for multiple models on a
545   single benchmark, offering a more efficient performance estimation framework.
546
547   5    C ONCLUSIONS
548
549   In this paper, we propose the TAILORED B ENCH method, which mainly includes an adaptive source model set
550   selection strategy, a scalable K-Medoids clustering algorithm and a calibrated restoration strategy. Abandoning
551   the one-size-fits-all approach, we have customized the evaluation on the constructed native coreset for each tar-
552   get model. This approach enables a more accurate reconstruction and ranking of the model’s performance on
553   the benchmark. Comprehensive experiments show that TAILOREDBENCH can achieve more accurate model
554   evaluation (an average of 24.8% estimation MAE loss degradation) with only twenty inference costs.
555
556   Limitations: A primary limitation of mainstream approaches in benchmark compression, including Vivek et al.
557   (2024), Polo et al. (2024), and our method, is their dependence on comprehensive evaluation results from existing
558
      models across all examples within a benchmark. As described above, these results are typically readily accessible
      through public leaderboards. However, for new benchmarks or certain private benchmarks, obtaining initial model
559
      performance results is necessary, which introduces additional inference overhead. Nonetheless, we maintain that
560
      this initial cost is justified, as it is offset by the significant resource savings achieved through numerous subsequent
561   rapid evaluations facilitated by our method.
562
563   Future Work: An important direction for future work involves the dynamic determination of the optimal sizes
564   for the G-set and N-set. While we have demonstrated that our method performs robustly across a variety of
565   G-set and N-set configurations, the ability to ascertain the most suitable sizes for a specific benchmark and its
566   corresponding source models would be highly beneficial. This advancement would enable users to better plan
567   their computational resources in advance, optimizing the balance between inference cost and performance.
