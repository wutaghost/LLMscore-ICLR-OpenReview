009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite the great advances in video understanding with deep neural networks, cur-
012            rent solutions still struggle with input videos that last for minutes, if not hours. To
013            mitigate this issue, existing approaches typically build a memory cache with dense
014            visual embedding on video transformers to model the long-range spatiotemporal
015            dependencies. However, even with hundreds of extended memory tokens, their
016            results remain unsatisfactory. In this paper, we argue that more compact yet infor-
017            mative memory embeddings can effectively improve performance. To this end, we
018
               introduce TinyMem, a model built upon tiny multimodal memory for long-form
               video action detection. In particular, we condense redundant video content into
019
               succinct descriptions to derive abstract text semantics. Subsequently, we integrate
020
               visual embedding condensed by regions with text embedding. TinyMem beats a
021            range of state-of-the-art models on AVA v2.2, Epic-Kitchens-100 and Breakfast
022            with highly condensed memory, e.g., 37.4 mAP with TinyMem-24-12 on AVA
023            v2.2 while using 5 times fewer memory tokens than the baseline with dense visual
024            memory embedding.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   While analyzing contents in short-term videos with a duration of a few seconds has seen rapid
030   progress in recent years Bertasius et al. (2021); Fan et al. (2021); Feichtenhofer et al. (2022); Tong
031   et al. (2022), techniques still fall short of meeting the demands of real-world settings. For instance,
032   in streaming services, a plethora of media files would last for minutes and even hours. It is chal-
033
      lenging yet crucial to explore extending current techniques for short-term videos to ones with much
      longer duration Grauman et al. (2022); Wu & Krahenbuhl (2021); Yang et al. (2023). As a result,
034
      researchers introduce approaches in a similar spirit to the human visual system, where long-range
035
      visual information is not processed all at once but received and parsed sequentially in short seg-
036   ments over time. This motivates the task of online action detection Damen et al. (2022); Wang et al.
037   (2021); Xu et al. (2021), which entails segmenting long videos into multiple short-term clips and
038   then predicting corresponding labels within each segment arranged in temporal order.
039
      As activities in long videos are strongly correlated yet located in different space-time locations,
040
      modeling each clip independently leaves enriched relationships over time unexploited. To address
041
      this issue, incorporating a memory cache into networks becomes a mainstream strategy for online
042   action detection. In particular, our design is encouraged by approaches Ryoo et al. (2023); Song
043   et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2022) that augment representations of video transformers with historical
044   spatial-temporal clues from cached visual tokens. However, prior studies simply leverage the dense
045   output video tokens of certain transformer layers as memory (i.e., vanilla visual memory) . For long-
046   form videos, however, we argue that this design inherently incorporates redundant and inefficient
047   visual signals. In particular, when memory length scales up, vanilla visual memory results in a rapid
048   growth of the number of tokens involved in attention computation and hence poses great challenges
049   for video transformers in identifying the most helpful clue from historical information.
050   To address these issues, we draw inspiration from the human memory system, where visual stim-
051   uli would be converted into a compressed and low-resolution format in working memory Kwak &
052   Curtis (2022). To explore the best practice of compact and informative memory embedding, innova-
053   tively, we employ textual descriptions to interpret the abstract semantics emerging from videos and
      project captions into tiny text token as semantic memory. Imitating the human memory system, we

096

097   the advantage of the proposed tiny memory design.

111   Memory-augmented video models cache motion information of historical video clips and are

135   et al. (2023) have shown that advanced Vision-Language models (VLMs) Radford et al. (2021); Yu

142   3     M ETHOD
143
144   Our goal is to leverage multimodal memory to support online video action detection for long-form
145   videos. To this end, we build our method, TinyMem, upon a short-term video model MViTv2 Li
146   et al. (2022). Following the classical setting of online video processing, Given a streaming long
147   video V, we process it in a clip-by-clip manner. Specifically, at time step t, we could only get access
148
      to the current and past clips, i.e., {C0 , · · · , Ct } that are arranged in a temporal sequence, and aim to
      predict the actions in Ct . In the following, we begin with a brief review of the MViTv2 in Sec. 3.1,
149
      and then introduce the design of multimodal memory embedding in Sec. 3.2. Finally, we discuss
150
      memory reading and update strategy in Sec. 3.3 and introduce the captioner-free inference strategy
151   in Sec. 3.4. The overall framework of our method is illustrated in Fig. 3.

186   3.2       M ULTIMODAL MEMORY EMBEDDING
187
188   Existing memory-based video models, typically store dense visual features of past clips into mem-
189   ory, which contains remarkable redundant information and hence inevitably introduces distractors
190   during memory reading. In this paper, we seek to comprise memory caches with abstract semantics
191
      by utilizing two modalities of memory embedding in TinyMem, i.e., text and region memory.
192   Text memory stores the core concepts, e.g., objects and their activities, in narrative captions. Specif-
193   ically, given the current clip Ct , we obtain its description as a composition of captions from T
194   frames with BLIP-2 Li et al. (2023). Though BLIP-2 undergoes image-text pre-training only, ow-
195   ing to its outstanding zero-shot performance, it has been widely used to generate captions in recent
196   works Bhattacharya et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024); Yu et al. (2024) to assist video tasks. Sub-
197   sequently, we embed generated captions into textual embedding using the text encoder of CLIP-
198
      B Radford et al. (2021). Next, we aggressively select a single [EOT] token to represent the full text
      semantics, and feed it into the projection head of vision-language joint feature space to obtain one
199
      memory tokens for each frame of Ct . The extracted T text tokens condense video content into a
200
      highly compact space yet effectively model the context of past video segments.
201
202   Region memory caches the historical visual clues of informative regions. Compared with previous
203   methods of storing pixel features of the complete clip, our region memory is more informative
204   and efficient. Specifically, given bounding boxes predicted by an off-the-shelf object detector, RoI
205
      features are extracted by applying 3D RoI Align He et al. (2017) on the output visual tokens. We
      employ RoI features as compact region representation and feed them into the classification head to
206
      gain the final prediction. In consequence, we obtain resulting Nregion region memory tokens.
207
208   We argue that region memory can also be represented with an extended set of learnable tokens.
209   Specifically, we prepend Nregion global tokens with input video tokens before the first transformer
210   block and obtain the output global tokens on the corresponding layer as region embedding. We
211
      comprise region memory embedding with RoI features in default for spatiotemporal action detection
      benchmark and leverage global region tokens for temporal action detection benchmark.
212
213   Multimodal memory refers to the collaboration of text embedding and region embedding. Con-
214   cretely, We select a subset of transformer layers and augment each layer with either of the above two
215   types of memory embedding. Thereby, we inject the context information into the visual features of
      the current clip. Next, we will explain how to perform memory readout and update.

231   3.3     R EAD OUT & WRITE IN
232
233   In this section, we discuss the write-in and readout strategy of multimodal memory tokens. The
234   proposed memory caches are inserted into a subset of transformer layers. Without loss of generality,
235   we introduce the configuration for L-th layer and drop the layer index L for simplicity, and visualize
236   how text memory tokens interact with video tokens of the current time step in Fig. 4.
237   Memory readout supplements the visual features of the current clip with the information from
238   previous clips. Specifically, we first sample l memory tokens with a temporal stride of s from the
239   memory. To convert memory into “keys” and “values”, we apply two lightweight projection layers
240   Pk and Pv to align the feature dimension with the key and value tokens of the current clip, i.e.,
241   Kt and Vt . After that, we concat the obtained tokens of different timestamps along the sequence
242   dimension and obtain Mk , Mv respectively.
243   We implement Pk and Pv with a bias-free 1D group-convolutional layer followed by a shared
244   normalization layer. It is worth noting that projection layers also compress the output length of
245   our text (region) key and value tokens using a moderate stride size. More importantly, TinyMem
246   obtains memory tokens much fewer than the visual memory ones in previous methods Wu et al.
247   (2022). Next, we concatenate memory keys Mk and values Mv with Kt and Vt to get the memory-
248   augmented key K ′ and value V ′ for self-attention computation.
249
      Memory write-in is performed once the text embedding or region embedding is obtained by simply
250   pushing them into the corresponding memory cache. We maintain both memory caches as First-In-
251   First-Out (FIFO) queues with the maximal length equal to Lc . In other words, once the memory
252   length exceeds Lc , the earliest cached memory tokens will be discarded.

      3.4    C APTIONER - FREE DYNAMIC INFERENCE
255
256
      While we can access informative and high-quality captions with a wide range of powerful multi-
257
      modal foundation models now Bai et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024); Zhu et al. (2023a), their model
258   scale, e.g., 7.96B parameters for BLIP-2 with OPT-6.7b Zhang et al. (2022), may introduce con-
259   cerns for training and inference efficiency in resource-limit scenarios. To this end, we pre-extract
260   frame captions for training videos and design a captioner-free strategy for inference. Specifically,
261   we dynamically generate simplified captions according to the output predictions.
262
      For video clip Ct , TinyMem outputs Nbox action predictions. We compute the product of the pre-
263
      diction confidence of each bounding box from the detector with the corresponding action prediction
264   score. Next, we average the obtained product for each action and obtain Top-K action categories
265   {act0 , · · · , actK }. Afterward, we select the curated short description corresponding to each cate-
266   gory in the format of “a person {performing some actions}”. Later, we construct the
267   text input for memory caches by randomly drawing from the K obtained description. We claim
268   that the simplified captions fall short of captions generated by BLIP-2 due to the absence of de-
269   tails. While we observe that TinyMem-24 still exhibits strong superiority and generalizes well on
      the captioner-free strategy.

295   Model setup. Notably, we explore two scales of architecture, denoted as TinyMem-X-l-M , with
296   the suffix X identifying the depth of the model, l defining the memory length and M ∈ {V, R, T}
297   indicating the modality of memory embedding. In default, we omit “-M ” and use the combina-
298   tion of text memory and region memory for TinyMem and adopt visual memory embedding for
299
      MeMViT. Besides, following MeMViT, we adopt the relative positional embedding and employ an
      attention-based head on MViTv2. Generally, when no memory is cached, TinyMem-X are isotropic
300
      to MeMViT-X in architecture.
301
302   For region memory embedding, we set Nregion to 16 and hence write in 16 text memory tokens
303   for each clip. We adopt the ground-truth bounding boxes for training and detected person boxes
304   from AIA Tang et al. (2020) for inference. To obtain clip descriptions, we adopt BLIP-2 models
305
      based on OPT-6.7b Zhang et al. (2022) and fine-tuned on COCO Lin et al. (2014). We apply the
      default prompt of “a photo of” to generate captions for each of T frames. We observe that applying
306
      memory to deeper layers notably brings benefits, especially for region tokens (as shown in Fig. 5d).
307
      Therefore, we augment 50% of MHSA layers by employing region memory on the last sampled one
308   and use text memory caches for all the rest.
309
310   Implementation details. We follow prior studies Wu et al. (2022); Xu et al. (2021) to process long
311   videos by taking in a short clip containing one annotated keyframe each time step on AVA v2.2 .
312
      We fine-tune models2 pre-trained on Kinetics Carreira et al. (2018); Kay et al. (2017) for 30 epochs
      by using an AdamW optimizer with a batch size of 128, a base learning rate of 5e−4 and a weight
313
      decay of 0.05. We resize the short size of frames to 256 and randomly crop a region of 2242 during
314
      training and apply random horizontal flipping and color jittering.

467   As shown in Tab. 8, multimodal memory embedding significantly improves the results of clip-wise
468   action classification. Meanwhile, TinyMem-16-4 outperforms MeMViT-16-4 by 0.9 in mAP, indi-

470   3.6 in mAP, leading to a growth of 18.3 over the MViTv2-24 baseline.

      We claim that our method can also be easily adapted for long-term activity detection, where models
472
      output all action categories within the complete video. We extract 256 frames for each video sam-

524   5    C ONCLUSION
525
526
      Supporting top-performing video models on long video becomes an essential research topic con-
527
      cerning the practical demands of long-form video understanding. While explorations have been
528   made to augment short-term models with different memory designs, prior methods cache redundant
529   visual information only. In this paper, we innovatively included text, i.e., brief descriptions of video
530   contents, as the memory modality for spatiotemporal signals. Surprisingly, the abstract format of
531   embedding demonstrated strong representation capabilities with highly compact dimensions. On top
532   of that, we proposed TinyMem, which jointly employed compact text embedding and largely con-
533   centrated visual embedding as memory, and achieved promising performance on long-term action
534   detection benchmarks including AVA v2.2, Epic-Kitchens-100 and Breakfast.
535   Due to constraints of resources, we didn’t explore more long-form video tasks (e.g., temporal action
536   localization, long video question answering and etc) and models with larger scales. We believe
537   that our work can inspire more explorations in vision-language model, especially multimodal large
538   language models, e.g., combining frame description or compact region embedding with videos as
539   input or memory to enhance performance of models. Nevertheless, we believe that TinyMem opens
      up new possibilities by leveraging multimodal embedding for more long-term video tasks.
