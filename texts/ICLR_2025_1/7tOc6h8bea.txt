011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Inference-time computation is a powerful paradigm to enhance the performance
014            of large language models (LLMs), with Best-of-N sampling being a widely used
015            technique. However, this method is computationally expensive, requiring both (1)
016            an external reward model and (2) the generation of multiple samples. In this work,
017            we introduce a new generative self-evaluation scheme designed to adaptively re-
018
               duce the number of generated samples while maintaining or even improving per-
               formance. We use a generative reward model formulation, allowing the LLM to
019
               predict mid-generation the probability that restarting the generation will yield a
020
               better response. These predictions are obtained without an external reward model
021            and can be used to decide whether or not to generate more samples, prune un-
022            promising samples early on, or to pick the best sample. This capability is very
023            inexpensive as it involves generating a single predefined token. Trained using a
024            dataset constructed with real unfiltered LMSYS user prompts, Llama 3.1 8B’s win
025            rate against GPT-4 on AlpacaEval increases from 21% to 34% with 16 samples
026            and math performance on GSM8K improves from 84% to 91%. By sampling only
027            when the LLM determines that it is beneficial to do so and adaptively adjusting
028            temperature annealing, we demonstrate that 74% of the improvement from us-
029
               ing 16 samples can be achieved with only 1.2 samples on average. We further
               demonstrate that 50–75% of samples can be pruned early in generation with min-
030
               imal degradation in performance. Overall, our methods enable more efficient and
031
               scalable compute utilization during inference for LLMs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036   As large language models (LLMs) continue to advance, delivering high-quality responses across
037   diverse applications becomes increasingly important. One promising direction to enhance response
038   quality is the strategic use of inference-time computation, particularly through methods like Best-
039   of-N sampling Snell et al. (2024); Charniak & Johnson (2005); Cobbe et al. (2021), which selects
040   the best response from multiple candidates. However, this method incurs substantial inference cost
041   from querying an external reward model and producing a large, fixed number of samples.
042   In this work, we introduce a new reward modeling paradigm, which we denote as capability-aware
043   self-evaluations. This paradigm allows for adaptive allocation of inference-time compute, aiming
044   to reduce the computational overhead while maintaining or improving LLM performance across
045   various domains. We demonstrate that LLMs can directly model the probability that restarting gen-
046   eration yields in a better response, enabling informed decisions about whether to continue generating
047   a response, initiate new ones, as well as rank responses. These predictions are obtained by simply
048   appending a predefined self-evaluation prompt to the partially or fully generated response and gen-
049   erating a single predefined token whose likelihood is used as the prediction. This is in contrast to
050
      preference-based reward models which can primarily only be used to rank responses.
051   Our self-evaluation method is highly cost-effective, requiring no external reward models and in-
052   curring only the minimal cost of generating a single token. In contrast, an external reward model
053   inherently requires more memory and storage. Additionally, it is unable to reuse the KV cache ob-
      tained when generating the response and would have to process the input and response from scratch.

075   To demonstrate adaptive inference-time compute allocation, we introduce two techniques: (1) adap-
076
      tive sampling and (2) early pruning of unpromising samples. Adaptive sampling involves resampling
      a response for a given prompt until it is predicted that further samples will not yield additional im-
077
      provements, thus conserving computation for complex tasks that will benefit from it. Furthermore,
078
      early pruning discards samples midway through generation if they are likely to result in suboptimal
079   completions. These are not possible with standard reward models.
080
081   To give an LLM the ability to self-evaluate, one must construct an on-policy pairwise preference
082
      dataset with ties. In our experimental evaluation, we construct a dataset of approximately 30,000
      preferences constructed using real unfiltered LMSYS (Chiang et al., 2024) user prompts and an
083
      existing reward model, ArmoRM (Wang et al., 2024) trained on roughly 1 million preferences or
084
      ratings. With this dataset, we fine-tune a Llama 3.1 8B Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024) model to
085   self-evaluate and demonstrate significant performance improvements across both in-distribution and
086   out-of-distribution tasks. Notably, as shown in Figure 4, the win rate against GPT-4 on AlpacaEval
087   increases from 21% to 34% with 16 samples, and performance on held-out GSM8K math problems
088   improves from 84% to 91%. We find that with adaptive sampling, using just 1.2 samples on average
089   captures 74% of the improvement observed with 16 samples and 1.9 samples captures 84%. Addi-
090   tionally, early pruning can prevent 75% of unpromising samples from being fully generated, saving
091   56% of tokens generated with very minimal degradation in performance.
092   Our approach allows for more efficient and scalable use of compute resources during inference. By
093   enabling models to dynamically allocate compute during inference based on task complexity and
094   the model’s capability, we optimize resource usage, ensuring efficiency in processing all types of
095   prompts and tasks. This adaptability makes using inference-time compute far more practical and
096   ready for the real world where LLMs are used for a wide variety of applications.

      A simple, yet effective method for allocation of test-time compute is Best-of-N, allowing the model
157
      to improve upon its responses over greedy sampling. However, the generation of a large, fixed
158
      number of samples is computationally expensive and the efficacy of this approach relies heavily on
159   the quality of an external reward model, which can additionally incur substantial computational cost.
160   In the following section, we address the cost and robustness of the reward model by establishing a
161   general framework for self-evaluation via token prediction. We show how to train capability-aware
      and mid-generation self-evaluators to reduce the cost of generating a large, fixed number of samples.

      3.1 C APABILITY-AWARE AND M ID -G ENERATION S ELF -E VALUATIONS

      In the classic reward modeling paradigm, a pre-trained LM is used as an initialization for reward
      modeling. However, this approach also relies on a newly added reward head to output the reward,
165
      which diverges from the token prediction task for which the LLM was trained, which can hurt per-
166
      formance for reward modeling. Additionally, generally preference-based reward models learn only
167   to rank arbitrary responses. This may be limiting for comparing on-policy samples (such as those
168   from Best-of-N) during inference to determine how good the response is with respect to the model’s
169   own capabilities. Furthermore, reward models are generally only able to evaluate full responses.
170   Responses that are clearly unpromising very early on in generation still have to be generated until
171   the end for evaluation, wasting computational resources. Can we use an alternate paradigm to
172   model rewards to address these limitations?
173   We propose capability-aware and mid-generation self-evaluations, a paradigm in which we query
174   the reward model to predict the probability that restarting generation will not yield a better response.
175   This probability can be used to elicit adaptive test-time computation, where the model can dynami-
176   cally allocate compute dependent on the difficulty of a query and its own capability. This is because
177   to decide whether or not to allocate more compute to a given input, we need to know if it is fruitful to
178   do so. For example, for easy queries, the model may have already outputted the best response it can,
179   so resampling is unnecessary. Additionally, for difficult queries, sampling more may lead to a minor
180   improvement in performance, leading to unfruitful resampling. We illustrate this paradigm in Fig-
181
      ure 1. Modeling this probability effectively has three components: (1) reward modeling using token
      prediction to better leverage the pretrained model’s existing knowledge and capabilities (2) relying
182
      on on-policy pairwise preferences and ties to model the probability that the model cannot generate a
183
      more preferred response (3) using truncated responses to model the probability that restarting gen-
184   eration will not yield a more preferred response. Traditional reward models can suffer at this task
185   as they are model agnostic. Though these models can rank how different responses perform for a
186   given task, they are unable to quantify how effective resampling would be (not capability aware).
187   Furthermore, a desirable property for a reward model is to early stop the generation of samples that
188   are not promising in order to give the model another chance without wasting compute.
189
      Reward Modeling with Token Prediction. To obtain a self-evaluation of a response from an LLM,
190   we simply append a predefined self-evaluation prompt I to the generated response y and obtain a
191   score in the form of the likelihood of a predefined token tgood corresponding to the likelihood of
192   the response being good. This approach allows us to acquire rewards without any external reward
193   model, making it highly cost-effective as we can reuse the KV cache obtained during the generation
194   of the response. Also, this leverages the existing zero-shot capability of the models to judge its own
195   responses as a prior, which has been shown to be effective in works such as Madaan et al. (2023).
196   Formally, given a preference dataset Dpref = {x, ygood , ybad }, which contains input context x and
197   response pairs y, we can train a self-evaluation model by maximizing the likelihood of the good

217   from on-policy pairwise preferences and ties. It is the probability that the current sample y results
218   in a Win or a Tie against another sample y′ . More formally:
219

221   Accounting for ties in reward modeling is especially important for on-policy pairwise data where
222   responses coming from the same model are very likely to be similar. Ties commonly occur when
223   generating responses from the same model (e.g. 40% of the time) and are even more common with
224   simple tasks. Since ties indicate that the model cannot do better, they are crucial when learning to
225   do capability-aware self-evaluations. If ties are not specified, it is also harder to model the reward
226   as the model has to distinguish between extremely similar responses. Furthermore, since (100 −
227   40)/2 = 30% of samples result in a Loss, we see that the model can do significantly better on a
228   query only roughly 30% of the time.
229   Notice that P (Win ∪ Tie | x, y) monotonically increases with the sample’s underlying reward
230   r(x, y). This means that it can also be used to determine if one sample has a higher reward than
231   another. Since inference-time compute strategies only care about being able to rank responses, it
232   turns out that modeling P (Win ∪ Tie | x, y) is more useful than r(x, y) since it can also be used
233   inform decisions on whether or not to allocate more compute.

289   3.2.1 A DAPTIVE S AMPLING AND A NNEALING
290   We introduce adaptive sampling as a technique to allocate inference-time compute only when it is
291   beneficial to do so. We further introduce exponentially increasing parallel sampling to mitigate the
292   main disadvantage of adaptive sampling, latency. Finally, we introduce a temperature annealing
293   strategy to balance exploration and exploitation while adaptively sampling and boost efficiency.
294   Resampling until meeting a threshold. We adaptively sample by resampling only when the model
295   predicts it can produce a better response. We do so by computing the likelihood that the model
296   cannot generate a better response pθ (Win ∪ Tie | x, y) for every sample. If this probability exceeds
297   a predefined threshold τ for any sample, we stop resampling and select the sample with the highest
298   pθ (Win ∪ Tie | x, y) as the final response. Otherwise, we resample, repeating this process until the
299   threshold is met or a maximum number of samples Nmax is reached. This approach concentrates
300
      computational resources on prompts where improvement is likely, avoiding unnecessary sampling
      when further gains are improbable.
301
302   Increasing number of parallel samples exponentially. To reduce latency, we sample in expo-
303   nentially increasing batch sizes. Specifically, for the k-th sampling iteration, the batch size Nk is
304   defined as:
305                                   N1 = 1, Nk = 2k−2 for k > 1.                                     (14)
306
      This ensures that the cumulative number of samples by the k-th iteration is 2k−1 . This exponential
307
      increase minimizes latency, reducing the number of iterations needed to meet Nmax , while allowing
308   for enough self-evaluations to determine if larger batches of samples are necessary.
309
310   Temperature annealing schedule based on number of samples generated so far. To balance
311
      exploitation and exploration, we vary the temperature γ. The temperature for the k-th iteration is
      given by:
312
313                                             γk = 1 − 2−(k−1) .                                    (15)
314
      Initially, the temperature starts low (e.g., γ1 = 0) to prioritize high-probability responses. As k
315   increases, γk quickly approaches 1, encouraging more diverse and creative sampling. This anneal-
316   ing schedule allows the model to first focus on exploiting the most likely responses, then explore
317   alternative options as more samples are generated.

320   One downside to adaptive sampling requires the generation of samples in series, which introduces
321   latency. However, can we still leverage parallel sampling to avoid increasing the latency of response
322   generation but still enable adaptive test-time compute allocation? One approach to reducing compu-
323   tational costs in parallel generation is to early prune unpromising samples based on mid-generation
      self-evaluations.

411   4.3 E FFICIENCY AND S CALING OF A DAPTIVE S AMPLING AND A NNEALING
412   Metrics for Evaluation Following prior work (Hoffmann et al., 2022; Pope et al., 2022; Chen et al.,
413   2023), we study the efficiency as a measure of floating-point operations per second (FLOPS), which
414   is proportional to the number of inference tokens generated. We additionally consider the inference
415   latency through the number of sequential calls/batches and the wall time.
416   We evaluate the efficiency gains and performance trade-offs of our adaptive sampling and annealing
417   strategy on the GSM8K and MATH datasets. Our objective is to retain the performance benefits
418   of Best-of-16 sampling while significantly reducing the average number of FLOPs utilized. In this
419   setting, we want to evaluate the methods’ ability to allocate samples when necessary. Therefore, our
420   experiments always use capability-aware self-evaluations to select the best sample so that the final
421   selection method is not a confounding factor.
422   As a baseline, we first assess the performance of Best-of-N. The best sample is selected using self-
423   evaluation with token prediction. Figure 2 shows that increasing the number of samples from 1 to
424   16 incrementally improves the GSM8K Pass@1 accuracy from 84.2% to 91.0%. This represents
425   the maximum achievable performance with our method, which we define as 100% of the maximum
426   improvement. However, generating a fixed large number of samples per query is expensive.
427   To mitigate this, we introduce adaptive sampling controlled by a threshold τ and a maximum number
428   of samples Nmax . Initially, we test adaptive sampling using the underlying reward model to decide
429   whether to generate additional samples. As shown in Figure 2, this approach does not significantly
430   outperform random selection. While the underlying reward model effectively ranks responses, it
431   does not consider the model’s capability to improve upon its own outputs, substantially limiting its
      effectiveness in informing resampling decisions.

479   5     R ELATED W ORK
480   Reward Modeling in RLHF. Traditionally, reward models (RMs) within Reinforcement Learning
481   with Human Feedback (RLHF) have been trained using discriminative techniques, often drawing
482   on ranking models like the Bradley-Terry (BT) framework (Bradley & Terry, 1952). These RMs
483   are generally treated as binary classifiers, distinguishing between preferred and dispreferred com-
484   pletions for a given prompt (Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022). The preference signal is
485   predicted by generating a continuous score, typically derived from a linear head appended to an au-
      toregressive language model. An alternative line of work departs from this discriminative paradigm


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      by directly modeling preferences through joint probability estimations, as seen in methods that pre-
487   dict the likelihood of one response being preferred over another (An et al., 2023).
488
489   Recent advancements in RLHF have introduced implicit reward models that circumvent the neces-
490
      sity for a distinct reward classifier by learning the distribution of preferred and dispreferred comple-
      tions via objectives like Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023) and Implicit
491
      Preference Optimization (IPO) (Gheshlaghi Azar et al., 2023). These methods embed preference
492
      learning directly into the policy optimization process, unlike explicit preference modeling.
493
494   “LLM as a Judge” (Zheng et al., 2023) is another form of reward modeling where the model is
495   prompted to act as evaluators without additional finetuning. Despite the potential of these meth-
496
      ods, even advanced models like GPT-4o underperform when compared to dedicated RMs in more
      complex evaluations, such as those found in the RewardBench benchmark (Lambert et al., 2024).
497
498   Techniques for Inference-time Compute. During inference, the integration of a reward model with
499   a proposal distribution (LLM) can be employed to refine the output responses to a given prompt. One
500   notable paradigm in this context is Self-Consistency Wang et al. (2023), which is designed for fac-
501   tual queries with extractable answers. In this approach, the language model selects the response it
502
      generates with the highest frequency across multiple samples. Optimizations such as Early Stop-
      ping Self-Consistency have been proposed, which terminate the sampling process early if a subset
503
      of responses shows strong consistency. However, these approaches face limitations due to their
504
      dependence on identifying the most “consistent” response from a large pool of discrete answers,
505   restricting their applicability to tasks like multiple-choice or mathematical problem-solving.
506
507   In addition, search algorithms such as Best-of-N and Beam Search have been explored in works such
508
      as Snell et al. (2024), which leverage reward models to select the most promising candidate samples
      in reasoning tasks. In this work, we examine how to enhance the efficiency of token generation
509
      relative to the accuracy of the outputs, with the complexity of the query dictating the amount of
510
      inference compute allocation. Furthermore, for multi-step reasoning tasks, this work presupposes
511   that the problem can be decomposed into discrete semantic steps—a potentially strong assumption
512   in domains outside of well-structured fields like mathematics.
513
514
      Generative Verifiers. Concurrent work, such as Zhang et al. (2024); Ankner et al. (2024), demon-
      strate that token-based reward modeling outperforms traditional reward modeling techniques. We
515
      further introduce capability-aware self-evaluation, which allows a model to understand its own gen-
516
      eration capability to enable dynamic allocation of computational resources during inference. We
517   also introduce mid-generation self-evaluations, allowing for the pruning of unpromising samples.
518
519   6   D ISCUSSION , C ONCLUSION , AND L IMITATIONS
520   As large language models (LLMs) evolve, enhancing response quality through inference-time com-
521   putation becomes critical. Best-of-N sampling, a traditional approach, generates multiple response
522   candidates and selects the best, but it incurs high computational costs due to its reliance on ex-
523   ternal reward models and fixed sample sizes. This work introduces a cost-effective alternative:
524   capability-aware self-evaluations performed mid-generation. By appending a self-evaluation prompt
525
      to responses, LLMs predict the likelihood of generating a better response without requiring exter-
      nal reward models. Two adaptive inference-time techniques—adaptive sampling and early prun-
526
      ing—are also proposed, allowing LLMs to resample or discard suboptimal responses during gen-
527
      eration dynamically. These methods significantly improve efficiency, with fewer samples yielding
528   performance gains similar to a larger set, and early pruning reducing unnecessary computation. Ex-
529   perimental results demonstrate notable improvements in performance across tasks, achieving a 34%
530   win rate against GPT-4 on AlpacaEval with 16 samples and increasing accuracy on GSM8K math
531   problems from 84% to 91%. Overall, this approach optimizes inference-time compute allocation,
532   making it more scalable and practical for diverse real-world applications.
533
      The main limitation of our method is that adaptive sampling introduces latency, which otherwise
534   would not be a problem with only parallel sampling. We minimized this with exponentially increas-
535   ing batch sizes. We also introduced early pruning to save computation even in parallel sampling.
536   For future work, it may be possible to predetermine the number of samples one needs to allocate to
537   a given query based on the probabiliy that samples result in a Tie. While this would be significantly
538   less efficient than adaptive sampling, it would eliminate latency for adaptive sampling. It may be
539   possible to do different types of search with active capability-aware self-evaluations. Beam search
      or other similar types of search may be possible on general prompts with this new capability.
