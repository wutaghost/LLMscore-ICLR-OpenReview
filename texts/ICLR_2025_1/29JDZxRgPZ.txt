011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            We present a novel machine-learning (ML) approach (EM-GANSim) for real-time
014            electromagnetic (EM) propagation that is used for wireless communication sim-
015            ulation in 3D indoor environments. Our approach uses a modified conditional
016
               Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) that incorporates encoded geometry and
               transmitter location while adhering to the electromagnetic propagation theory. The
017
               overall physically-inspired learning is able to predict the power distribution in 3D
018
               scenes, which is represented using heatmaps. Our overall accuracy is compa-
019            rable to ray tracing-based EM simulation, as evidenced by lower mean squared
020            error values. Furthermore, our GAN-based method drastically reduces the com-
021            putation time, achieving a 5X speedup on complex benchmarks. In practice, it
022            can compute the signal strength in a few milliseconds on any location in 3D in-
023            door environments. We also present a large dataset of 3D models and EM ray
024            tracing-simulated heatmaps. To the best of our knowledge, EM-GANSim is the
025            first real-time algorithm for EM simulation in complex 3D indoor environments.
026            We plan to release the code and the dataset.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Electromagnetic (EM) waves, characterized by the oscillation of electric and magnetic fields, are
032   central to technologies such as visible light, microwave ovens, and wireless communication sys-
033
      tems, including Wi-Fi and 5G. Maxwell’s equations (Maxwell, 1873) describe the interaction and
      propagation of these electric and magnetic fields through space, forming the theoretical foundation
034
      for understanding EM wave behavior, including reflection, refraction, diffraction, and scattering.
035
      These principles are critical when modeling wave propagation in complex environments (Obaidat
036   & Green, 2003; D’Aucelli et al., 2018), such as indoor spaces, where multiple interactions with
037   obstacles and materials occur.
038
039
      In the field of EM simulation, various methods are employed to understand wave propagation and
      interaction with media. Path loss and attenuation play crucial roles in these simulations, measuring
040
      how much signal power diminishes over distance, due to obstacles or the medium itself. Ray tracing
041
      is a widely-used technique for simulating wave interactions with surfaces (Bertoni et al., 1994;
042   Seidel & Rappaport, 1992), as it balances computational efficiency and accuracy. It simulates rays,
043   as narrow beams of EM energy, traveling in straight lines and accounting for key phenomena like
044   reflection and diffraction. The method’s efficiency makes it popular for 5G network planning (Hsiao
045   et al., 2017), vehicular communications (Wang & Manocha, 2022), electromagnetic characterization
046   (Egea-Lopez et al., 2021), and ground-penetrating radar (Zhang et al., 2006). Other methods such as
047   wave-based methods that numerically solve Maxwell’s equations can provide more accurate results,
048   capturing complex wave behavior such as diffraction and scattering more precisely. However, these
049   methods are often too computationally intensive for real-time or large-scale applications (Coifman
050
      et al., 1993; Taflove et al., 2005),
051   Despite its advantages, current EM simulation systems, particularly those based on ray tracing, have
052   limitations in terms of handling dynamic scenes or complex environments. Ray tracing relies on
053   modeling rays, i.e., narrow beams of EM energy, that travel in straight lines until they encounter
      an object, tracing their paths from a source and modeling interactions like reflections and diffrac-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      tions (McKown & Hamilton, 1991). The simulation accuracy depends on detailed environmental
055   models and material properties, making it computationally intensive, and needs significant process-
056   ing power to simulate the numerous potential ray paths in complex environments. For dynamic
057   scenes and detailed indoor environments, the need to continually update the models and recompute
058   new paths in real-time is a major challenge. Indoor simulations are particularly difficult due to the
059   complexity and density of the obstacles, which further increases the computational load, making
060   current methods inefficient for applications requiring quick responses, such as 5G network plan-
061   ning, where higher frequencies and more complex environments are used (Rappaport et al., 2013;
062   Wang et al., 2020).
063   Innovative solutions, such as integrating generative adversarial networks (GANs) into EM simula-
064   tions, are being explored to address these limitations. GAN models include considerations for path
065   loss, reflection, and diffraction in their loss functions, and they also account for material proper-
066   ties and multipath propagation, thereby improving simulation accuracy and heatmap generation for
067   real-world applications.
068   Main Results: We present a novel GAN-based prediction scheme for real-time EM simulation in
069   3D indoor scenes. Our formulation uses a physically-inspired generator to predict wireless signal
070   received power heatmaps and ensures high accuracy by incorporating detailed signal propagation
071   mechanisms such as direct propagation, reflection, and diffraction. These physical constraints are
072   embedded within the GAN’s loss function to ensure that the generated data adheres to the prin-
073   ciples of electromagnetic wave propagation. We use ray tracing techniques to model how signals
074   propagate through an environment, considering reflections off surfaces and diffractions around the
075
      obstacles (Sangkusolwong & Apavatjrut, 2017). We evaluate these physical interactions using EM
      propagation models and the uniform theory of diffraction (UTD) (Kanatas et al., 1997) to predict the
076
      path loss for indoor environments accurately. Our approach not only improves the reliability of the
077
      heatmap predictions but also enhances the robustness and convergence of the GAN during training.
078   Our main contributions include:
079
080         • Accurate Power Distributions: By employing conditional Generative Adversarial Networks
081           (cGANs) and utilizing the strengths of physics-inspired learning, our approach can predict
082           accurate power distributions in 3D indoor environments.
083         • Real-Time Performance: We highlight the performance on 15 complex 3D indoor bench-
084           marks. Our approach significantly reduces the computational time needed for simulations
085           compared to prior methods based on ray tracing. Our GAN models streamline the simula-
086           tion process, achieving 5X faster running time on entire power map generation for various-
087           sized indoor models. Additionally, it enables real-time simulation for individual data points
088
              in just a few milliseconds.
089         • Dataset: We present a large, comprehensive dataset featuring varied indoor scenarios (2K+
090           models) and simulated heatmaps (more than 64M) to train our model. This dataset ensures
091
              robust and generalized model performance across diverse conditions and is used for training
              and testing.

096   Recent efforts in integrating ML with EM ray tracing and wireless communication systems have
097   highlighted the potential of ML in enhancing wireless communication technologies in various ways.
098   DeepRay (Bakirtzis et al., 2022) uses a data-driven approach that integrates a ray-tracing simulator
099
      with deep learning models, specifically convolutional encoder-decoders such as U-Net and SDU-
      Net, enhancing indoor radio propagation modeling for accurate signal strength prediction in various
100
      indoor environments. The model is able to learn from multiple environments and predict unknown
101
      geometries with high accuracy. WAIR-D (Huangfu et al., 2022) introduces a comprehensive dataset
102   supporting AI-based wireless research, emphasizing the creation of realistic simulation environ-
103   ments for enhanced model generalization and facilitating fine-tuning for specific scenarios using
104   real-world map data. Huang et al. (Huang et al., 2021) integrate ray tracing and an autoencoding-
105   translation neural network to perform 3-D sound-speed inversion, improving efficiency and accuracy
106   in underwater acoustic applications. Yin et al. (Yin et al., 2022) investigate the use of millimeter
107   wave (mmWave) wireless signals in assisting robot navigation and employ a learning-based clas-
      sifier for link state classification to enhance robotic movement and decision-making in complex

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      environments. There are other methods that combine deep reinforcement learning with enhanced
109   ray tracing for antenna tilt optimization and those leveraging 5G MIMO data for beam selection us-
110   ing deep learning techniques to improve cellular network performance through efficient geospatial
111   data processing and precise signal optimization (Zhu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023).
112
      ML techniques have also been used to predict the received power in complex indoor and urban envi-
113
      ronments (Yun & Iskander, 2015). Traditional methods like regression models, decision trees, and
114
      support vector machines have been used to model the propagation characteristics of electromagnetic
115   fields. The performance of these methods has been improved by adapting to data from specific envi-

117   (NLoS) conditions (Filosa et al., 2016; Dong et al., 2020; Williams et al., 2015).
118
      Despite their advancements, traditional simulation methods (e.g., ray tracing) face limitations in
119
      terms of capturing the highly nonlinear interactions and multipath effects characteristic of indoor
120
      and urban EM propagation. The complexity increases with the need to model dynamic changes in
121   the environment such as moving objects and varying channel conditions, which are not always well-
122   addressed by conventional approaches (Marey et al., 2022). On the other hand, cGANs (Creswell
123   et al., 2018) are widely used for tasks requiring the generation of new data instances that resemble a
124   given distribution.

      3.2   M ODIFIED C ONDITIONAL GAN
175
      In the context of EM simulations, cGANs offer a unique advantage by not only predicting EM field
176   distributions but also generating potential scenarios that could affect these distributions in dynamic
177   environments (Ratnarajah et al., 2022; Kazeminia et al., 2020). We choose cGANs over other learn-
178   ing methods because of several compelling advantages that cGANs offer (Goodfellow et al., 2014),
179   particularly in terms of generating high-quality synthetic data and improving the accuracy and ef-
180   ficiency of path loss predictions. Unlike traditional methods, cGANs are specifically designed for
181   data generation tasks, making them well-suited for creating heat maps based on complex indoor
182   environments and addressing the challenges associated with received power prediction in wireless
183   communication network design and optimization.
184   Our approach, EM-GANSim, utilizes GANs for several reasons that align with our objectives for
185   real-time and accurate EM simulation in 3D indoor environments:
186
187         1. Data Generation: GANs are adept at generating synthetic data that closely mirrors the
188            distribution of real data. In the context of EM simulation, this capability allows us to create
189            detailed heatmaps that accurately represent the complex interactions of electromagnetic
190
               waves with indoor structures. More details are discussed in the Results and Appendix
               sections.
191
192         2. Efficiency: Traditional ray tracing methods can be computationally intensive, especially
193            for large-scale or real-time applications. GANs, once trained, can generate simulations
194            rapidly, which is crucial for achieving real-time performance. A detailed comparison is
195
               provided in Sections 5.1 and 5.2.
196         3. Flexibility: GANs can be conditioned on specific parameters, such as geometry and trans-
197            mitter location, enabling the generation of simulations tailored to particular scenarios with-
198            out the need for extensive recalculations.
199         4. Handling Complexity: GANs are well-suited to capture indoor EM propagation’s highly
200            nonlinear and multipath effects, which can be challenging for conventional simulation
201            methods. For example, conventional ray tracing simulators took much longer time to gen-
202            erate heatmap in average scenes as shown in Section 5.2.
203         5. Generalization: By learning from a diverse dataset, GANs can generalize to new environ-
204            ments and scenarios, which is essential for creating a versatile simulation tool that can be
205            applied across a wide range of conditions. Our method achieves generalization by train-
206            ing the GAN on a diverse dataset of over 2,000 indoor scenes with various room sizes,
207            configurations, and materials.
208

      3.3   T RAINING
283
      Training of the modified cGAN is performed using a loss function that balances the fidelity of
284
      the generated heatmaps as a function of the input geometric conditions. The training process is
285   carefully monitored to prevent mode collapse and ensure a diverse set of realistic outputs. The
286   proposed method is implemented using PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and uses a GPU for efficient
287   model training and inference. For ease of access, we utilize Google Colab, which provides free GPU
288   resources to facilitate the training process. The primary software and dependencies include Python
289   3 or higher and essential libraries such as NumPy, SciPy, and Matplotlib for data handling and
290   visualization. Our training process on Google Colab takes approximately two days to complete. We
291   optimize the training using hyperparameters such as the learning rate, batch size, and latent space
292   dimensions, which are crucial for achieving the desired model performance and accuracy. A detailed
293
      flowchart is presented in the appendix and we plan to release our code at the time of publication.

      4.3   M ODE C OLLAPSE
325
326   A common issue with cGANs occurs when the generator starts producing a limited range of outputs,
327   which in the case of EM ray tracing could lead to underrepresentation of the solution space. In our
328   work, we included a noise vector in the generator’s input to promote feature learning and output
329   variability. A large, diverse training dataset exposed the generator to a range of scenarios, reducing
330   mode collapse risk. Our conditional GAN framework improved output relevance, and adaptive
331   learning rates maintained balanced learning dynamics. These strategies enhanced the model’s ability
332
      to generate diverse and accurate EM propagation heatmaps.

454   6     A BLATION E XPERIMENTS
455
456   In this section, we first analyze the effect of excluding Gaussian noise from the training process, an
457   element typically introduced to break the symmetry in the model weights, ensuring that different
458   units learn different features. We verify the noise’s impact on the generated heatmaps and their
459   respective MSEs. By comparing heatmaps and MSE values, we evaluate the GAN’s performance
460
      in generating received power distribution in the absence of noise. This ablation study serves not
      only to reinforce the validity of our methodology but also to offer insights that could refine future
461
      implementations of machine learning in EM ray tracing. We define the ablation experiment results
462
      as GAN-No-Noise. Observations based on the heatmaps in the appendix, Fig. 7, from the GAN-No-
463   Noise, GAN-based, and DCEM predictions in testing scenes are discussed as follows:
464
465           • First Row (GAN-No-Noise): The absence of Gaussian noise results in less varied and
466             uniform heatmaps, indicating potential over-smoothing and reduced accuracy in capturing
467             EM wave interactions within the environment.
468           • Second Row (GAN-based with Noise): Inclusion of noise introduces more defined con-
469             trasts and a broader range of power levels, suggesting a better representation of the complex
470             nature of EM propagation and environmental features.
471           • Cons of GAN-No-Noise: Lack of noise in training leads to simpler patterns, reduced model
472             accuracy, and potential issues in generalizing to new environments, which is critical for
473             applications like network planning.
474           • Importance of Noise: Gaussian noise is essential in training to break symmetry in the
475             model, ensuring diverse learning and preventing the network from collapsing into repetitive
476             pattern production.
477
478   These observations underline the importance of including noise in the GAN training process to en-
479   hance the model’s ability to predict received power distributions accurately and robustly, especially
480   when applied to complex indoor EM propagation scenarios.
481   We also include the corresponding MSE of GAN-No-Noise and GAN-based compared to DCEM
482   in Table 5 in the appendix. For these three testing cases, GAN-based predictions consistently have
483   lower MSE values than GAN-No-Noise, indicating that the inclusion of noise during the training
484   process contributes to a more accurate prediction of received power levels. The improved MSE
485   with noise suggests that Gaussian noise acts as a regularizer, preventing the model from memorizing
      the training data and instead forcing it to learn the underlying distribution. The presence of noise

      also introduces a wider variety of scenarios during training, making the GAN model more robust to
487   unseen environments and better at generalizing from the training data.

489   Another ablation test is designed to evaluate the impact of incorporating physical constraints into
490
      the objective function of our generator, included in the appendix. These physical constraints are
      integrated to ensure that the generated samples adhere to the fundamental principles of electromag-

      netic wave propagation, accounting for direct propagation, reflection, and diffraction effects. The
492
      ablation tests will involve running the simulator under two distinct conditions:
493
494          • With Physics Constraints: The generator’s objective function will include the physical
495            constraints loss (Lphy ), which comprises terms for direct path propagation (Ldirect ), reflec-
496            tions (Lref ), and diffractions (Ldiff ).
497          • Without Physics Constraints: The physical constraints loss (Lphy ) will be omitted from
498            the objective function, leaving only the cGAN loss and the MSE loss components.

      This ablation test demonstrates the impact of incorporating physical constraints by comparing the
      performance and accuracy of the generator under both conditions. Key performance metrics ob-
501
      served were:
502
503          • Signal Propagation Accuracy: The tests revealed that the generator with physical con-
504            straints produced more accurate signal propagation characteristics. The predicted path
505            losses (direct, reflection, and diffraction) closely matched the actual path losses, highlight-
506            ing the effectiveness of the constraints in capturing the physical phenomena of EM wave
507            propagation in indoor environments.
508          • Visual and Structural Fidelity: The generated samples with physical constraints exhib-
509            ited higher visual realism and structural coherence. These samples were more accurate in
510            modeling the indoor environments compared to those generated without the constraints.

512   7   C ONCLUSIONS , L IMITATIONS , AND F UTURE W ORK
513
514   We present a novel approach that uses ML methods along with EM ray tracing to enhance the accu-
515   racy and efficiency of wireless communication simulation within 3D indoor environments. We use a
516   modified cGAN that utilizes encoded geometry and transmitter location and can be used for accurate
517   EM wave propagation. We have evaluated its performance on a large number of complex 3D indoor
518   scenes and its performance is comparable to EM ray tracing-based simulations. Furthermore, we
519   observe a 5X performance improvement over prior methods.
520   Our study enhances wireless communication efficiency and lays the ground for future real-time
521   applications. Our approach has some limitations. Since our training data is based on ray tracing, our
522   prediction scheme may not be able to accurately model low-frequency or other wave interactions.
523   Our current approach is limited to indoor scenes, and we would also like to evaluate it in scenes
524   with multiple dynamic objects. A key challenge is to extend and use these methods for large urban
525   scenes with complex traffic patterns to model wireless signals.
526   Furthermore, we plan to add dynamic elements, such as movable partitions and furniture, to simulate
527   real-world changes in indoor layouts. By leveraging publicly available architectural data (such as the
528   3D-Front dataset (Fu et al., 2021)), we will continuously update the dataset with new scenarios that
529   reflect emerging trends in building design and technology. This comprehensive dataset expansion
530   will improve the model’s ability to predict EM wave propagation in complex and varied indoor
531   environments, ultimately enhancing its applicability and reliability in practical applications.
