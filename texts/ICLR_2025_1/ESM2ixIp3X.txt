011                                                     A BSTRACT
012
013                Cutting-edge abstractive summarisers generate fluent summaries, but the factual-
014                ity of the generated text is not guaranteed. Early summary factuality evaluation
015                metrics are usually based on n-gram overlap and embedding similarity, but are
016                reported fail to align with human annotations. Therefore, many techniques for de-
017
                   tecting factual inconsistencies build pipelines around natural language inference
                   (NLI) or question-answering (QA) models with additional supervised learning
018
                   steps. In this paper, we revisit similarity-based metrics, showing that this fail-
019
                   ure stems from the use of reference texts for comparison and the granularity of the
020                comparison. We propose a new zero-shot factuality evaluation metric, Sentence-
021                BERT Score (SBERTScore), which compares sentences between the summary
022                and the source document. It outperforms widely-used word-word metrics includ-
023                ing BERTScore and can compete with existing NLI and QA-based factuality met-
024                rics on the benchmark without needing any fine-tuning. Our experiments indi-
025                cate that each technique has different strengths, with SBERTScore particularly
026                effective at identifying correct summaries. Additionally, we demonstrate how a
027                combination of techniques is more effective at detecting various types of error. 1

030   1       I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The rapid development of natural language generation techniques has created new challenges for
033
      evaluation. For instance, in recent years, state-of-the-art abstractive summarisation models have
      set new records on benchmarks many times (Zhang et al., 2020; Lewis et al., 2019; Zhao et al.,
034
      2022). However, investigation (Maynez et al., 2020; Pagnoni et al., 2021; Durmus et al., 2020;
035
      Koto et al., 2022) shows that these models are prone to generate factually inconsistent summaries.
036   Evaluation metrics, such as ROUGE (Lin, 2004), have not undergone the same pace of improvement,
037   and therefore fail to reflect this issue. The first step towards improving summary factuality is to
038   develop an evaluation metric to assess summary factual consistency conditioned on the given source
039   document.
040
      Recent factuality metrics mainly fall into two types. 1) NLI-based metrics (Kryscinski et al., 2020;
041
      Laban et al., 2022) predict the probability that the given summary is entailed by the source document.
042   2) QA-based metrics (Durmus et al., 2020; Fabbri et al., 2021b; Scialom et al., 2021) simulate the
043   process of a human performing reading comprehension tasks and compute the factuality score based
044   on how many questions generated from the summary can be correctly answered from the given
045   source document. These two paradigms need to train their models on a large-scale dataset, but
046   existing factuality datasets are usually insufficient.
047
      Similarity-based metrics are proposed to handle synonyms that cause n-gram-based methods to fail
048   (Zhang et al., 2019). However, they are not favoured by most previous work as they do not improve
049   the performance of reflecting summary factuality (Maynez et al., 2020; Pagnoni et al., 2021; Dur-
050   mus et al., 2020). In this work, our empirical experiments validate that the failure of similarity-based
051   metrics are from unfair experimental settings. NLI and QA-based metrics compare generated sum-
052   maries against source document while similarity-based metrics take a reference summary as input.

054
      We show that BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2019) can provide useful factual consistency evaluation by
055   comparing generated summaries to sources.
056
057   In addition, we extend the similarity-based metric to the sentence level, as comparing individual
058
      words offers very limited insights into factual consistency, and factually consistent sentences can be
      constructed in entirely different ways. The proposed method, Sentence-BERT Score (SBERTScore),
059
      computes cosine similarity between sentence embeddings (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019). Like
060
      BERTScore, it is computationally-efficient and can be applied out-of-the-box with no dataset-
061   specific training, but also takes the composition and order of words into account, so can better
062   represent the semantics of the complete sentence compared to the contextualised word embeddings
063   used by BERTScore. We conduct a case study to show the benefits of using sentence embedding.
064   Comparison on a factuality benchmark (Tang et al., 2023) shows that SBERTScore outperforms
065   BERTScore in overall performance, but BERTScore can work better in the extreme case.
066
      We also compare BERTScore and SBERTScore against recent NLI and QA-based factuality met-
067   rics. Similarity-based metrics do not require any additional training steps as they benefit from high-
068   quality general-purpose pretrained embeddings, and has much less computational complexity at in-
069   ference time. Results show that both similarity-based metrics can outperform NLI-based metrics in
070   the same zero-shot setting, and even work better than some metrics specifically trained for factuality
071   evaluation. Importantly, the design of BERTScore and SBERTScore avoids truncating long source
072   documents, instead selecting an appropriate granularity to segment the sources before feeding them
073   into the model. Further analysis of agreement between metrics, as well as the types of errors (Tang
074   et al., 2023) they detect, indicates that SBERTScore can capture different kinds of errors than NLI
075
      and QA-based methods. We show that a simple combination of metrics can outperform the individ-
      ual base metrics, which suggests that combining diverse metrics may be a promising direction for
076
      future research.
077
078   Our contributions are three-fold:
079
             • We conduct an empirical evaluation, which reveals that the previous underperformance of
080
               metrics such as BERTScore is due to the use of reference summaries. Zero-shot similarity-
081
               based metrics are competitive with recent factuality metrics that require additional training.
082
083
             • We develop the token-level similarity-based metric BERTScore into sentence-level
               SBERTScore and improve the performance of detecting factual inconsistency.
084
085          • We show that different evaluation metrics are necessary to capture different types of error,
086
               and introduce a simple combination that outperforms the state of the art.

090   2.1   NLI- BASED FACTUALITY M ETRICS
091
092   The NLI task is similar to predicting factual consistency between source document and generated
093
      summary. Hence, previous research (Barrantes et al., 2020; Falke et al., 2019) attempted to transfer
      NLI models to factual consistency detection. However, a subsequent study (Kryscinski et al., 2020)
094
      showed that those NLI models are only as good as random guessing. Therefore, a series of work
095
      (Kryscinski et al., 2020; Laban et al., 2022) made efforts to build up datasets for training factuality
096   metrics. Although the dataset can be synthesised using entity swapping to save the effort of col-
097   lecting human annotations, the error distribution is not the same as real summaries (Pagnoni et al.,
098   2021). Some recent work (Utama et al., 2022; Soleimani et al., 2023) applied language generation
099   models to augment the quality of training set and received better performance.
100
      Another strand of research into NLI-based factuality prediction focused on the granularity of the
101
      input text. Early works (Barrantes et al., 2020; Falke et al., 2019; Kryscinski et al., 2020) concate-
102   nate the system summary with the whole source document as the input. Firstly, this often requires
103   truncating the source document to fit the length limit, which can lead to underestimating factuality
104   due to the information loss. Secondly, the NLI models applied in their work are trained on much
105   shorter sentence pairs. Directly applying these models on long text such as source documents does
106   not align with their training data distribution. Following work (Goyal & Durrett, 2020; Laban et al.,
107   2022; Schuster et al., 2022) investigated the effect of performing inference at different levels, in-
      cluding word, dependency, sentence, and paragraph, revealing that segmenting source documents

108
      into sentences and dependency arcs is more suitable for current NLI models. This inspired us to
109   explore the suitability of different input text granularities for similarity-based evaluation metrics,
110   which have not been investigated in past work.

      2.2    QA- BASED FACTUALITY M ETRICS
113
114   QA-based metrics (Chen et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2020; Durmus et al., 2020; Fabbri et al., 2021b)
115   assemble multiple modules with different functions. An answer selection module first selects a set
116   of answers from the summary, usually including named entities and noun phrase chunks. A ques-
117   tion generation module conditioned upon the selected answers is applied on the summary as context
118   to raise questions. The QA component answers the generated questions conditioned on the given
119   source document. The final score is then computed on the overlapping extent of the two answer
120   sets. This paradigm provides an interpretable way to assess factuality by showing questions with
121   inconsistent answers. However, since several text generation models are involved in the evaluation
122
      process, this methodology usually requires a large training dataset and is time-consuming at infer-
      ence time. We were therefore motivated to investigate alternatives, as factuality datasets are usually
123
      small and domain-specific, and the evaluation process is expected to be prompt.
124
125
126   2.3    S IMILARITY- BASED FACTUALITY M ETRICS
127
      BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2019) is used as a stronger baseline than ROUGE (Lin, 2004) in factual
128
      consistency detection, but it does not correlate well with human judgements (Pagnoni et al., 2021).
129   Bao et al. (2023) attempted to feed source document to BERTScore, but they did not compare
130   its performance against metrics using other methodologies. They also tried to extend BERTScore
131   to sentence-level without using sentence embeddings, leading to unsuccessful results. Koto et al.
132   (2022) adapted BERTScore by averaging the three highest token scores and showed that it can
133   detect the information overlap of system summaries and source documents, but there was still a
134   large performance gap with other metrics (Fabbri et al., 2021b). In this work, we successfully
135   extend similarity-based metric to sentence-level and reveal that its zero-shot setting is competitive
136   to other metrics specifically trained for factual consistency detection.

145   the meaning of the complete sentence. The precision and recall of our proposed metric are defined as

169   3.1   C OMPUTATIONAL E FFICIENCY
170
      SBERTScore applies an all-purpose embedding model as the backbone, which provides reliable
171
      sentence embeddings that can be used out-of-box without the cost of additional training, in contrast
172   to other metrics based on NLI or QA. SBERTScore also has advantages at inference time. We denote
173   the number of sentences in the system summary and source document as N and M respectively. The
174   majority of inference time is spent on calling the backbone model to process the input sentences.
175   NLI-based metrics need to take each sentence pair once, therefore the number of inputs that the
176   backbone model processes is O(N M ). SBERTScore uses a similar backbone but only needs to
177   compute the embedding once for each sentence, so the complexity is O(N + M ). The runtime of
178   QA-based metrics is much greater than the other two as multiple models are involved in question
179   generation and answering, thus has the lowest efficiency. We randomly sampled 1000 pieces of data
180
      from the benchmark, and test the runtime of QuestEval (Scialom et al., 2021), SummaC{ZS,Conv}
      (Laban et al., 2022), BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2019) and SBERTScore on Intel(R) Core(TM) i9-
181
      10900X CPU @ 3.70GHz with NVIDIA A5000. Results in Appendix A show that SBERTScore
182
      only comes after BERTScore in processing speed, and is 3 times faster than the rival NLI-based
183   method SummaC{ZS,Conv} and 30 times faster than the QA-based metric QuestEval.

230   4.3   E VALUATION M ETRICS FOR C OMPARISON
231
232
      This section introduces factuality metrics studied for comparison.
233
234
      QAFactEval Fabbri et al. (2021b) conducted a comprehensive evaluation of the components of
      QA-based metrics. They aggregated more advanced models into a single system and optimised a
235
      pipeline for computing consistency scores.
236
237
      QuestEval Scialom et al. (2021) proposed a QA-based framework to compute consistency scores
238
      for given text pairs. They first select an answer set from the candidate text, then generate questions
239   using the other text as input with conditions from the answer set. The QA module answers the
240   questions and the overlap between the two answer sets is counted to obtain precision and recall.
241   They use F1 measure as the final factual consistency score.
242
243   DAE Goyal & Durrett (2020) extract dependencies from given texts using dependency parsing.
244   They train a model to predict entailment at the dependency-level. The final score is the average
245   entailment score over all dependency arcs in the given source and summary.
246
247   SummaC{ZS,Conv} Laban et al. (2022) train a sentence-level NLI model and compute the entail-
248   ment scores for all pairs of sentences from the source document and the summary. ZS stands for
249   zero-shot, where the final entailment score is the average of the maximum entailment score for each
250   sentence in the summary. Conv is a variant with an extra learned convolutional layer that aggregates
251   the entailment score matrix to a final score.
252
253   ROUGE−{1, 2, L} Lin (2004) propose an evaluation metric by counting the overlapping words
254
      or n-grams between the given reference and candidate text pairs.
255
256
      BERTScore Zhang et al. (2019) report the average cosine similarity of the matched word embed-
      dings provided by BERT Devlin et al. (2018) or other related models.
257
258   FactCC, SummaC{ZS,Conv} , DAE are NLI-based metrics, and QuestEval, QAFactEval are QA-
259   based metrics. To have a fair comparison, we use the pretrained RoBERTa-large Liu et al.
260   (2019) as the backbone for BERTScore and all-roberta-large-v1 Reimers & Gurevych (2019) for
261   SBERTScore. The two checkpoints have identical numbers of layers, and the only difference is that
262
      they are trained for different text embeddings.

518   6   C ONCLUSION
519
520   In this paper, we investigated the suitable settings for similarity-based factuality evaluation metrics
521   and proposed a new sentence-level metric, SBERTScore. We showed that, given source documents
522   as input, similarity-based evaluation metrics computed on sentence-sentence level are competitive
523
      with more complex NLI and QA-based factuality-oriented metrics, and do not require a supervised
      learning step on the target domain. Furthermore, our proposed metric better aligns with human
524
      binary annotations than many trained metrics on the CNNDM split and across the whole dataset.
525
      Therefore, we conclude that zero-shot similarity-based metrics are a promising approach. We illus-
526   trate a limitation of similarity-based metrics when processing negation and highly similar but neutral
527   input text, which suggests a direction for future research. We also showed that our proposed metric
528   has high recall of correct summaries, and that there is low agreement between different factuality
529   metrics, with similarity-based metrics making different errors to QA and NLI-based metrics. Build-
530   ing on this, we demonstrated that integrating metrics by logical AND can improve balanced accuracy
531   on benchmark datasets.
