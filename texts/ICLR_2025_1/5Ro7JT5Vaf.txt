009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Score-based generative models (SGMs) have demonstrated unparalleled sampling
012            quality and diversity in numerous fields, such as image generation, voice synthe-
013            sis, and tabular data synthesis, etc. Inspired by those outstanding results, we apply
014            SGMs to synthesize time-series by learning its conditional score function. To this
015            end, we present a conditional score network for time-series synthesis, deriving a
016            denoising score matching loss tailored for our purposes. In particular, our pre-
017
               sented denoising score matching loss is the conditional denoising score matching
               loss for time-series synthesis. In addition, our framework is such flexible that both
018
               regular and irregular time-series can be synthesized with minimal changes to our
019
               model design. Finally, we obtain exceptional synthesis performance on various
020            time-series datasets, achieving state-of-the-art sampling diversity and quality.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Time-series frequently occurs in our daily lives, e.g., stock data, climate data, and so on. Especially,
026   time-series forecasting and classification are popular research topics in the field of machine learn-
027   ing (Ahmed et al., 2010; Fu, 2011; Ismail Fawaz et al., 2019). In many cases, however, time-series
028   samples are incomplete and/or the number of samples is insufficient, in which case training machine
029   learning models cannot be fulfilled in a robust way. To overcome the limitation, time-series synthe-
030
      sis has been studied actively recently (Chen et al., 2018; Dash et al., 2020). These synthesis models
      have been designed in various ways, including variational autoencoders (VAEs) and generative ad-
031
      versarial networks (GANs) (Desai et al., 2021; Yoon et al., 2019; Jeon et al., 2022).
032
033   Moreover, real-world time series often inevitably contain missing values because of privacy reasons
034   or medical events. It has been noted that the missing values involve important information, called
035   informative missingness (Rubin, 1976). To remedy the problem, several works have been developed
036
      to deal with irregular time-series, i.e., the inter-arrival time between observations is not fixed and/or
      some observations can be missing (Schafer and Graham, 2002; Che et al., 2016; Kidger et al., 2020),
037
      in which case synthesizing irregular time series is challenging (Jeon et al., 2022).
038
039   Score-based generative models (SGMs) have shown good sampling quality and diversity in numer-
040   ous fields, such as image generation, voice synthesis, and tabular data synthesis, etc (Yang et al.,
041   2023). However, in time-series generation, SGMs should consider autoregressiveness, meaning
042
      each time-series observation is generated in consideration of its previously generated observations
      which makes time-series generation more difficult (Yoon et al., 2019; Jeon et al., 2022). To this end,
043
      we propose the method of Time-series generation using conditional Score-based Generative Model
044
      (TSGM), which consists of three neural networks, i.e., an encoder, a score network, and a decoder
045   (see Figure 2).
046
047   Score-based time-series synthesis SGMs have its potential to deal with autoregressiveness (Meng
048   et al., 2020). We design our own autoregressive denoising score matching loss for time-series gen-
049   eration and prove its correctness (see Section 2.1.3). Along with its performances from image gen-
050   eration domain (cf. Fig. 1), here is our main motivation of using SGMs:
051
      The mathematical structure of SGMs naturally aligns with the sequential nature of time-series data.
052
053   Besides, we design a conditional score network on time-series, which learns the gradient of the
      conditional log-likelihood.

073   Regular vs. irregular time-series synthesis Time-series are prevalent throughout our daily lives.
074   Although regular time-series are easy to be considered, it is common to have missing values because
075
      of privacy issue (Che et al., 2016; Kidger et al., 2020), which makes irregularly sampled time-series1 .
      This hinders time-series processing and we consider the hardest problem condition: providing com-
076
      plete time-series given not only regular time-series, but also irregular ones. To this end, our method
077
      is considered ‘universial’ in that both both regular and irregular time-series samples can be treated
078   with minimal changes to our model design. For synthesizing regular time series, we use a recurrent
079   neural network-based encoder and decoder. Continuous-time methods, such as neural controlled
080   differential equations (Kidger et al., 2020) and GRU-ODE (Brouwer et al., 2019), can be used as
081   our encoder and decoder for synthesizing irregular time series (see Section 3.2 and Appendix I, J).
082
      We conduct in-depth experiments with 4 real-world datasets under regular and irregular settings.
083
      To be specific, for the irregular settings, we randomly drop 30%, 50%, and 70% of observations
084   from regular time-series on training, then generate complete time-series on evaluation. Therefore,
085   we test with 16 different settings, i.e., 4 datasets for one regular and three irregular settings. Our
086   specific choices of 9 baselines include almost all existing types of time-series generative paradigms,
087   ranging from VAEs to GANs. In Table 1 and Figure 1, we compare our method to the baselines,
088   ranking methods by their evaluation scores and estimating data distribution by kernel density esti-
089   mation (KDE). We also visualize real and generated time-series samples onto a latent space using
090   t-SNE (van der Maaten and Hinton, 2008) in Figure 3. Our proposed method shows the best gen-
091   eration quality in almost all cases. Furthermore, the t-SNE and KDE visualization results provide
092
      intuitive evidence that our method’s generation diversity is also superior to that of the baselines. Our
      contributions are summarized as follows:
093
094
095         1. We, for the first time, propose an SGM-based universal time-series synthesis method.

            2. We derive our own denoising score matching loss considering the autoregressive nature of
098
               sequential data, connecting SGMs to time-series generation domain.
099
100
101         3. We conduct comprehensive experiments with 4 real-world datasets and 9 baselines under
102
               one regular and three irregular settings since our method supports both regular and irregular
               time-series. Overall, our proposed method shows the best generation quality and diversity.

      2.1     S CORE - BASED G ENERATIVE M ODELS
111
112   SGMs offer several advantages over other generative models, including their higher generation qual-
113   ity and diversity. SGMs follow a two-step process, wherein i) gaussian noises are continuously added
114   to a sample and ii) then removed to recover a new sample. These processes are known as the for-
115   ward and reverse processes, respectively. In this section, we provide a brief overview of the original
116   SGMs in (Song et al., 2021), which will be adapted for the time-series generation tasks.
117
118   2.1.1    F ORWARD AND R EVERSE P ROCESS
119
      At first, SGMs add noises with the following stochastic differential equation (SDE):
120
121                                   dxs = f(s, xs )ds + g(s)dw, s ∈ [0, 1],                             (1)
122
      where w ∈ Rdim(x) is a multi-dimensional Brownian motion, f(s, ·) : Rdim(x) → Rdim(x) is a vector-
123
      valued drift term, and g : [0, 1] → R is a scalar-valued diffusion function. Hereafter, we define xs
124
      as a noisy sample diffused at time s ∈ [0, 1] from an original sample x ∈ Rdim(x) . Therefore, xs can
125
      be understood as a stochastic process following the SDE.
126
127   There are several options for f and g: variance exploding(VE), variance preserving(VP), and subVP.
128   Song et al. (2021) proved that VE and VP are continuous generalizations of the two discrete dif-
129   fusion methods: one in Song and Ermon (2019) and the other in Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015); Ho
130
      et al. (2020). The subVP method shows, in general, better negative log-likelihood (NLL) according
      to Song et al. (2021). We describe the exact form of each SDE in Table 14 with detailed explanation
131
      in Appendix N. Note that we only use the subVP-based TSGM in our main experiments and exclude
132
      the VE and VP-based one for its inferiority for time series synthesis in our experiments, but checked
133   the VP-based method from ablation study for its better performances than that of the VE.
134
135   SGMs run the forward SDE with a sufficiently large number of steps to make sure that the diffused
136
      sample converges to a Gaussian distribution at the final step. The score network Mθ (s, xs ) learns
      the gradient of the log-likelihood ∇xs log p(xs ), which will be used in the reverse process.
137
138   For the forward SDE, there exists the following corresponding reverse SDE (Anderson, 1982):
139
                               dxs = [f(s, xs ) − g 2 (s)∇xs log p(xs )]ds + g(s)dw̄.                     (2)
140
141
      The formula suggests that if knowing the score function, ∇xs log p(xs ), we can recover real samples
142
      from the prior distribution p1 (x) ∼ N (µ, σ 2 ), where µ, σ vary depending on the forward SDE type.
143

145
146   In order for the model M to learn the score function, the model has to optimize the following loss
147   function:

148                         L(θ) = Es {λ(s)Exs [∥Mθ (s, xs ) − ∇xs log p(xs )∥2 ]},                  (3)

151   2019). Thanks to Vincent (2011), the loss can be substituted with the following denoising score
152   matching loss:

156   bution (Särkkä and Solin, 2019) and therefore, ∇xs log p(xs |x0 ) can be analytically calculated.

193   2.2    T IME - SERIES G ENERATION
194
195
      There are several time-series generation papers, and we introduce their ideas. TimeVAE (Desai
      et al., 2021) is a variational autoencoder to synthesize time-series data. This model can provide
196
      interpretable results by reflecting temporal structures such as trend and seasonality in the generation
197
      process. CTFP (Deng et al., 2020) is a well-known normalizing flow model. It can treat both regular
198   and irreugular time-series data by a deformation of the standard Wiener process.
199
200   TimeGAN (Yoon et al., 2019) uses a GAN architecture to generate time-series. First, it trains an
201
      encoder and decoder, which transform a time-series sample x1:N into latent vectors h1:N and re-
      cover them by using a recurrent neural network (RNN). Next, it trains a generator and discriminator
202
      pair on latent space, by minimizing the discrepancy between an estimated and true distribution, i.e.
203
      p̂(xn |x1:n−1 ) and p(xn |x1:n−1 ). Since it uses an RNN-based encoder, it can efficiently learn the
204   conditional likelihood p(xn |x1:n−1 ) by treating it as p(hn |hn−1 ), since hn ∼ x1:n under the regime
205   of RNNs. Therefore, it can generate each observation xn considering its previous history x1:n−1 .
206   However, GAN-based generative models are vulnerable to the issue of mode collapse (Xiao et al.,
207   2022) and unstable behavior problems during training (Chu et al., 2020). GT-GAN (Jeon et al.,
208   2022) attempted to solve the problems by incorporating an invertible neural network-based genera-
209   tor into its framework. There also exist GAN-based methods to generate other types of sequential
210   data, e.g., video, sound, etc (Esteban et al., 2017; Mogren, 2016; Xu et al., 2020; Donahue et al.,
211   2019). In our experiments, we also use them as our baselines for thorough evaluations.
212

      network (cf. Fig. 2). Firstly, we train the encoder and the decoder to connect between time-series

220   3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
221
222   Let X and H denote a data space and a latent space, respectively. We define x1:N as a time-
223   series sample with a sequential length of N , and xn is a multi-dimensional observation of x1:N at
224   sequential order n. Similarly, h1:N (resp. hn ) denotes an embedded time series (resp. an embedded
225   observation).
226   Each observation xn can be represented as a pair of time and features, i.e., xn = (tn , u(tn )), where
227   tn ∈ R≥0 is a time stamp of feature u(tn ) ∈ Rdim(u) , and dim(u) is a feature dimension. X
228   can be classified into two types: regular time-series and irregular time-series. For irregular setting,
229   we randomly remove 30%, 50% and 70% of samples from regular time-series. Therefore, the only
                                                                             N −1
230   difference between these types is whether time intervals, {tn+1 − tn }n=1 , are the same or not.
231
      Our irregular setting considers generating complete time-series given training data with missing val-
232
      ues. Consequently, we generate total time-series on both regular and irregular settings and compare
233
      the generated ones with the original regular data on evaluation process.
234
235
      3.2   E NCODER AND D ECODER
236
237   The encoder and decoder have the task of mapping time-series data to a latent space and vice versa.
238   We define e and d as an encoding function mapping X to H and a decoding function mapping H to
239   X , respectively. For simplicity but without loss of generality, we utilize an autoregressive autoen-
240   coder: RNN-based ones for regular time-series (Cho et al., 2014b) and Neural CDE, GRU-ODE for
241   irregular setting (Kidger et al., 2020; Brouwer et al., 2019). In this section, we describe the regular
242   time-series generation as representative one and leave the irregular case in Appendix I, depicting
243   how continuous-time methods can be used for the encoder and the decoder to better synthesize
244
      irregular time-series.
245   The encoder e and the decoder d consist of recurrent neural networks, e.g., gated recurrent units
246   (GRUs) (Cho et al., 2014b). Since we use RNNs, both e and d are defined recursively as follows:
247
                                      hn = e(hn−1 , xn ),       x̂n = d(hn ),                             (5)
248
249   where x̂n denotes a reconstructed time-series sample at sequential order n. It is well-known that
250   RNN was devised to efficiently handle variable sequences by summarizing past observations (Cho
251
      et al., 2014a). For example, Yoon et al. (2019) also used RNN-based encoder and decoder to
      provide a reversible mapping between features and latent representations, thereby reducing the high-
252
      dimensionality of the adversarial learning space, which is well supported by hn ∼ x1:n .
253
254   After embedding real time-series data onto a latent space, we can train the conditional score network
255   with its conditional log-likelihood, whose architecture is described in Appendix J.2. The encoder
256   and decoder are pre-trained before our main training.

409   existing predictive score only predicts a last sample of total sequence, so Jeon et al. (2022) suggests
410   a comprehensive approach that considers the entire time series. For fair comparison, we reuse evalu-
411   ation metrics of Yoon et al. (2019), Jeon et al. (2022) for regular and irregular settings, respectively.
412
      ii) Discriminative Score: In order to assess how similar the original and generated samples are, we
413
      train a 2-layer LSTM model that classifies the real/fake samples into two classes, real or fake. We
414
      use the performance of the trained classifier on the test data as the discriminative score. Therefore,
415   lower discriminator scores mean real and fake samples are similar.

471   5              C ONCLUSIONS
472
473
      We presented a score-based generative model framework for universal time-series generation. We
474   combined an autoencoder and our score network into a single framework to accomplish the goal
475   — our framework supports RNN-based or continuous-time method-based autoencoders. We also
476   designed an appropriate denoising score matching loss for our generation task and achieved state-
477   of-the-art results on various datasets in terms of the discriminative and predictive scores. In addition,
478   we conducted rigorous ablation and sensitivity studies to prove the efficacy of our model design.
479
      Limitations. Although our method achieves state-of-the-art sampling quality and diversity, there
480   exists a fundamental problem that all SGMs have. That is, SGMs are slower than GANs for gener-
481   ating samples (see Appendix H). Since there are several accomplishments for faster sampling (Xiao
482   et al., 2022; Jolicoeur-Martineau et al., 2021), however, one can apply them to our method and it
483   would be much faster without any loss of sampling quality and diversity. Moreover, TSGM’s gen-
484   eration is not too much slow since time-series data are much smaller than image data, which means
485   TSGM still has its commercial value. We point out that TSGM generates a sample of the Energy
      dataset, the largest dataset among our baselines, using only 0.8 second (see Section H).

784   There exist time-series diffusion models for forecasting and imputation (Rasul et al., 2021; Tashiro
785
      et al., 2021). However, our approach to time-series synthesis is technically distinct. While these
      models aim to generate fulfilled samples given partially known time-series, TSGM focuses on pro-
786
      ducing diverse samples. Furthermore, TSGM is designed to provide diverse regular time-series given
787
      regular or irregular data unlike time-series forecasting, which predicts future observations based on
788   past data, and time-series imputation, which infers missing elements within a given time-series sam-
789   ple. This intuition is reflected in the experimental results in Section C.

      We refer to a detailed analysis of these experiments in Section C.3.

1103
1104   Unlike other generation tasks, e.g., image generation (Song et al., 2021) and tabular data synthe-
1105   sis (Kim et al., 2022), where each sample is independent, time-series observations are dependent to
1106
       their past observations. Therefore, the score network for time-series generation must be designed to
       learn the conditional log-likelihood given past generated observations, which is more complicated
1107
       than that in image generation.
1108
1109   In order to learn the conditional log-likelihood, we modify the popular U-net (Ronneberger et al.,
1110   2015) architecture for our purposes. Since U-net has achieved various excellent results for other gen-
1111   erative tasks (Song and Ermon, 2019; Song et al., 2021), we modify its 2-dimensional convolution
1112
       layers to 1-dimensional ones for handling time-series observations. The modified U-net, denoted
       Mθ , is trained to learn our conditional score function (cf. Eq. equation 11). More details on training
1113
       and sampling with Mθ are in Sec. 3.4.

1220   O    D ETAILED D ESCRIPTION OF GRU-D
1221
1222   GRU-D (Che et al., 2016) is a modified GRU model which is for learning time-series data with
1223   missing values. This concept is similar with our problem statement, so we apply it to our baseline
1224   for irregular case. GRU-D needs to learn decaying rates along with the values of GRU. First, GRU-D
1225   learns decay rates which depict vagueness of data as time passed. After calculating the decay rates,
1226   each value is composed of decay rate, mask, latest observed data, and predicted empirical mean that
1227   of GRU. The code can be utilized in the following link: https://github.com/zhiyongc/GRU-D
