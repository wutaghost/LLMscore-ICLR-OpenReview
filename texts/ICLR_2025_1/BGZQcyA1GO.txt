009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The emerging field of DNA storage employs strands of DNA bases (A/T/C/G) as a
012            storage medium for digital information to enable massive density and durability.
013            The DNA storage pipeline includes: (1) encoding the raw data into sequences of
014            DNA bases; (2) synthesizing the sequences as DNA strands that are stored over
015            time as an unordered set; (3) sequencing the DNA strands to generate DNA reads;
016
               and (4) deducing the original data. The DNA synthesis and sequencing stages
               each generate several independent error-prone duplicates of each strand which
017
               are then utilized in the final stage to reconstruct the best estimate for the original
018
               strand. Specifically, the reads are first clustered into groups likely originating from
019            the same strand (based on their similarity to each other), and then each group
020            approximates the strand that led to the reads of that group. This work improves the
021            DNA clustering stage by embedding it as part of the DNA sequencing. Traditional
022            DNA storage solutions begin after the DNA sequencing process generates discrete
023            DNA reads (A/T/C/G), yet we identify that there is untapped potential in using
024            the raw signals generated by the Nanopore DNA sequencing machine before they
025            are discretized into bases, a process known as basecalling, which is done using
026            a deep neural network. We propose a deep neural network that clusters these
027
               signals directly, demonstrating superior accuracy, and reduced computation times
               compared to current approaches that cluster after basecalling.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The rapid growth of digital data, projected to reach 180 zettabytes by 2025, is causing a data storage
033   crisis that cannot be addressed by existing storage technologies (Rydning, 2022). In response,
034   deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) is emerging as a promising alternative storage medium due to its
035   incredible density and durability. The DNA storage process includes four stages illustrated in
036   Figure 1: (1) an “encoding” stage in which binary data files are encoded into DNA strands (design
037   files) using error-correcting code (ECC) (Koblitz et al., 2000) schemes that may also overcome errors,
038   (2) a “synthesis” stage, which produces artificial DNA strands of each design strand and are then
039
      stored in a storage container (LeProust et al., 2010), (3) a “sequencing” stage (Anavy et al., 2019;
      Erlich & Zielinski, 2017; Organick et al., 2018; Yazdi et al., 2017) which translates a DNA strand
040
      into a digital sequence known as a “read”, and (4) a “retrieval” stage where reads are decoded
041
      back to binary data files while correcting any errors using the chosen coding methods. Despite the
042   vast potential of DNA storage, current DNA sequencers are yet to overcome challenges such as low
043   throughput and high costs compared to the traditional alternatives (Alliance, 2021; Shomorony et al.,
044   2022; Yazdi et al., 2015).
045
      The emerging Nanopore technology offers real-time sequencing of DNA strands with drastically lower
046
      costs and portability compared to traditional Illumina sequencing machines (Jain et al., 2016; Kono
047
      & Arakawa, 2019). Despite having higher error rates compared to other sequencing technologies such
048   as Illumina, Nanopore sequencing is gaining significant attention due to its lower cost, portability,
049   and capability to sequence longer strands of DNA. Nanopore sequencing directly reads the nucleic
050   acids by passing them through a nanoscale pore, called a nanopore, embedded in a membrane. As the
051   nucleic acid strand moves through the pore, it impacts the electrical current in a fashion dependent on
052   the current bases in the pore (between four and six at any given time) (Mao et al., 2018; McBain &
053   Viterbo, 2024). The current produced by the DNA molecules passing through the pore is converted
      into raw analog signals. In Nanopore sequencing, basecalling entails converting these raw signals into

      While Nanopore sequencing benefits from a drastic reduction in costs, it also poses several challenges
070   due to significantly higher sequencing error rates (approximately 10% of bases suffer from edit,
071   insertion or deletion errors as compared to only 1% in Illumina) (Chandak et al., 2020). This
072   paper overcomes these errors by exploiting the raw Nanopore signals which contain significantly
073   more information as compared to the discrete post-basecaller read. Unfortunately, direct utilization
074   of the raw signal is challenging since, to the best of our knowledge, no previous work in DNA
075   storage has directly exploited the signals for processes such as clustering, reconstruction, and error
076   correction (Chandak et al., 2020).
077   A major stage in the DNA storage pipeline involves clustering billions of strings based on the edit
078   distance metric in order to identify which groups of reads were likely duplicated from the same source
079   DNA strand. The edit distance between two strings 𝑥,𝑦 denoted as 𝑑 𝑒 (𝑥, 𝑦) is the minimum number of
080   edits, deletions, or insertions required to transform 𝑥 to 𝑦 (Ristad & Yianilos, 1998). The complexity
081   of this procedure is caused by several factors: (1) the variability and probabilistic nature of the cluster
082   size due to varying duplicate counts; (2) the number of original clusters in the design files; (3) the
083   protocols used in the experiments, for example, PCR errors pre-sequencing procedure and sampling
084
      errors that occur during nanopore sequencing machine; and (4) the fact that the computation of a
      distance matrix based on the edit distance between each pair of reads is not scalable for typical DNA
085
      storage datasets that are very big.
086
087   Existing clustering algorithms often suffer from high computational time and compromised ac-
088   curacy (Qu et al., 2022; Zorita et al., 2015). While approximation algorithms and tools such as
089   metric embeddings and locality-sensitive hashing (LSH) offer promising avenues by sacrificing some
090
      accuracy for substantial reductions in runtime, their applicability to edit distances remains poorly
      understood. For the retrieval of a given file, the sequencing and retrieval stages take approximately a
091
      number of days to compute; therefore, any reduction in computation time is a major step forward.
092
093   At the heart of the challenges of clustering DNA strands for DNA storage lies the impracticality of
094   computing the edit distance between every pair of input strands to create a distance matrix. Due to the
095   sheer volume of data, this is exacerbated by the quadratic time complexity of edit-distance algorithms.
096
      Our work addresses this concern by proposing a novel approach: searching for similarity directly in
      the raw analog DNA signals rather than the discrete DNA reads. Our work introduces a paradigm
097
      shift in the field of DNA data storage retrieval by leveraging deep neural network embedding directly
098
      on the raw signals instead of the post-basecaller strands. By embedding the raw signals directly in
099   an architecture composed of convolutional neural network (CNN) layers, long-short-term memory
100   layers (LSTM), and linear layers, we bypass the limitations associated with clustering based on the
101   A/T/C/G alphabet, allowing us to cluster raw signals before passing them through the basecaller. This
102   step can significantly reduce the errors introduced during the sequencing phase and lead to a more
103   accurate reconstruction of the original design file.
104
      The key contributions to our work are as follows:
105
106
107          • We are the first to perform similarity groupings of DNA strands directly on the raw electrical
               signals.

      2    P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
122
123
      The main objective of this work is to develop a distance function for raw DNA signals that effectively
124   captures the similarity between them. Thus, the goal is to ensure that raw signals that originate
125   from the same cluster are close together in the metric space, while those that are not are far apart.
126   We formulate the problem as a supervised learning problem, where signals belonging to the same
127   cluster should have small distances between them in the transformed feature space, while signals
128   from different clusters should have large distances between them in the transformed feature space.
129   More specifically, let, without loss of generality, 𝐶 = {1, . . . , 𝑚} be the set of 𝑚 labels originating
130   from the design file and let 𝑆 = {𝑠1 , 𝑠2 , . . . , 𝑠 𝑛 } be the set of raw DNA signals, each belonging to
131   one of the 𝑚 labels (we denote the label of 𝑠𝑖 by 𝑐 𝑖 ). Our goal is to find a distance function 𝐷 that is
132
      targeted to achieve the following properties:
133
            1. Similarity Preservation: If two samples 𝑠𝑖 and 𝑠 𝑗 belong to the same class (𝑐 𝑖 = 𝑐 𝑗 ), the
134
               distance 𝐷 (𝑠𝑖 , 𝑠 𝑗 ) should be minimized.
135
136         2. Dissimilarity Preservation: If two samples 𝑠𝑖 and 𝑠 𝑗 belong to different classes (𝑐 𝑖 ≠ 𝑐 𝑗 ),
137
               the distance 𝐷 (𝑠𝑖 , 𝑠 𝑗 ) should be maximized.
138         3. Robustness to Noise: The distance function should be robust to noise and variability
139            inherent in raw DNA signal data.
140         4. Computational Efficiency: The metric should be computationally feasible for large datasets,
141            where 𝐷 (𝑠𝑖 , 𝑠 𝑗 ) computation has to be done in 𝑂 (1).

      3    R ELATED W ORK
145
146   The clustering phase of the DNA storage pipeline has been explored solely in the context of clustering
147
      the discrete DNA reads. The number of clusters in the clustering phase of the DNA storage pipeline
      is typically between 1, 000 and 100, 000, while each cluster contains between 10 and 1, 000 samples.
148
      To cluster DNA strands, the similarity between pairs of reads is computed – typically using the
149
      edit-distance method. This presents two main challenges: (1) the computational complexity of
150   pairwise comparisons being O (𝑛2 ) where 𝑛 is the number of reads, and (2) the O (ℓ 2 ) complexity
151   of the edit-distance computation, where ℓ represents the reads length. DNA clustering algorithms
152   address these challenges by employing (1) filtering techniques to reduce the number of pairwise
153   comparisons and (2) approximation methods to expedite edit-distance calculations. Both approaches
154   aim to decrease the time complexity with minimal accuracy loss.
155
      Several DNA clustering algorithms in bioinformatics and metagenomics, such as UCLUST (LeProust
156   et al., 2010), CD-HIT (Fu et al., 2012), and USEARCH (Srinivasavaradhan et al., 2021) (which
157   UCLUST is based on), use different methods to deal with the two challenges. One approach
158   involves using greedy methods for filtering, such as identifying common short sub-sequences as a
159   preliminary step before edit-distance computation. Location Sensitive Hashing (LSH), which is used
160   in (Antkowiak et al., 2020; Rashtchian et al., 2017; Ben Shabat et al., 2023), is another emerging
161   method that limits the edit-distance calculation to strands that share the same LSH value to make
      filtering more efficient.

162
      To address the latter challenge, the edit-distance computation, several methods have been proposed.
163   First, one can restrict the computation depth of the edit-distance, as demonstrated in (Bao et al., 2011),
164   to mitigate the complexity. Second, the edit-distance can be calculated based on fixed-sized short
165   sub-strings, as implemented in Starcode (Zorita et al., 2015) using short DNA bar-codes. Another
166   approach leverages heuristic similarity instead of edit-distance to expedite processing times, as
167   demonstrated in Clover (Qu et al., 2022) utilizing a tree structure.
168
      We acknowledge several works (Ji et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021) that have been done in the context of
169   DNA embeddings. Those works differ from ours since they involve correcting strands to align with a
170   protein or gene. It is a process that is irrelevant in our case because our data is synthetic, and we aim
171   to reconstruct our original data without, for example, capturing protein structure.
172
      In the signal domain, a common method for comparing signals is Dynamic Time Warping
173
      (DTW) (Senin, 2008). DTW is a technique designed to compare two time series data sequences with
174
      varying lengths whose data points are not aligned, much like strands in the discrete domain, allowing
175   for insertions, deletions, and substitutions. Similar to edit-distance, DTW’s computational complexity
176   is O (𝑚 2 ), where 𝑚 represents the signal length. Given that signal lengths are typically ten times
177   longer than DNA strands, DTW computation becomes impractical for large datasets that are used in
178   DNA storage applications. The focus of this work is in the signal domain; therefore, it is imperative
179   to first develop an accurate solution to the second challenge.

       4    DATASETS
183
184
       Public DNA storage datasets do not share the raw signals, recall that during during the DNA storage
       pipeline (Figure 1), the mapping between the DNA strands and their corresponding reads involves an
185
       intermediate stage where the DNA strands are converted to raw signals. Therefore, it is necessary to
186
       create our own datasets from real experiments that maps raw signals to their corresponding DNA
187    reads.
188
189    Each experiment in the DNA storage domain requires choosing an error-correcting code as part of the
190
      “encoding” stage for file reconstruction. In addition, a design file is generated for each experiment. The
       end-to-end experiment process involves DNA strand “synthesis” stage, followed by a “sequencing”
191
       stage, the DNA sequencer process approximately lasts 72 hours. Then basecalling is performed on
192
       the DNA reads, which takes approximately a week. Lastly, creating the ground truth for the dataset
193    lasts between 6 − 30 days.
194
195   Table 1 shows three design files and their corresponding Nanopore sequencing experiments 1 . The
196
      table outlines three different experiments that vary the number of clusters, the length of the DNA
      strands, and the average edit-distance between strands in the design files, therefore resulting in high
197
      variability in key parameters for the proposed datasets.
198
199   The design files of the different experiments encompass encoded data, index information for orderly
200   reconstruction, and a consistent primer shared among all reads, positioned at the beginning and
201   end of each strand within the file. Subsequently, the files were synthesized by “Twist Bioscience”,
202
      resulting in “fast5” (Payne et al., 2019) file formats. The raw signal data is encapsulated in “fast5”
      file formats, which are then processed by a basecaller to convert it into “fastq” format. The “fastq”
203
      format represents the sequenced data using the following symbols: { 𝐴, 𝑇, 𝐶, 𝐺}. Each “fast5” file is
204
      linked to a “fastq” file, with a “read-id” serving as the connecting identifier. Given that the standard
205   signal length in DNA storage datasets typically reaches up to 3000 units, for 95% of the cases, we
206   uniformly reshaped all of the signals to this length using common techniques.
207
208
      The ground truth for clustering is obtained by brute-force calculating the edit distance between each
      DNA read produced by the basecaller and the original strands from the respective design file. We
209
      then assign each DNA read to the cluster corresponding to the closest DNA strand from the design
210
      file. It is not practical or scalable to perform a full edit-distance computation due to its quadratic
211   time complexity between the millions of reads per experiment, and their corresponding design files.

227   5     P ROPOSED D EEP L EARNING S CHEME
228
229   We propose a deep-learning-based algorithm for computing similarity measures between the raw
230   signals of DNA reads. This is done by leveraging the Dorado basecaller (Technologies, 2024)
231   pre-trained weights. The architecture extends the Dorado model’s feature extractor, coupled with the
232   ArcFace loss function. In the following section, we explain our solution in detail.
233
      The neural network (NN) architecture begins with the Dorado basecaller (Technologies, 2024), a
234
      key component from the Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT) product suite, designed for DNA
235   sequencing via nanopore technology. ONT has developed several basecalling models that leverage
236   deep learning (DL) to translate the raw electrical signals from nanopore sequencing into nucleotide
237   sequences. These DL-based basecallers have significantly improved the accuracy and speed of
238   nanopore sequencing data analysis.
239
      The Conditional Random Field (CRF) layer in the Dorado model is employed as a feature extractor for
240
      encoding raw signals within the NN architecture. The CRF architecture includes several components,
241   each with its specific role in processing the sequencing data. Initially, convolutional layers are
242   deployed to extract salient features from the raw input signals through a sequence of convolutional
243   operations, capturing critical patterns essential for the basecalling task. Following are recurrent layers
244   (LSTM) that capture the sequential nature of the DNA sequence. They process the outputs from the
245   convolutional layers, taking into account the order and dependencies between the nucleotides. The
246   data is then passed through linear layers, which perform a linear transformation to align the processed
247   features with the final output space. This is followed by a Clmap layer that ensures that the scores
248   used for predicting the DNA sequence are within a specified range. This entire sequence of operation
249
      is called the CRFModel.
250   Dorado is an open-source basecaller for Oxford Nanopore reads (Technologies, 2024). We utilize
251   the initial pre-trained layers of this model, truncating its architecture after the CRF layer, which
252   outputs a size of 1024. Subsequently, we append a linear layer with dimensions 1024 × 500, where
253   500 represents the number of classes. The training process is coupled with the ArcFace loss function
254   (Deng et al., 2019). This function is used in facial recognition and other DL tasks that involve
255
      distinguishing between different classes. It is designed to enhance the discriminative power of the
      feature embeddings produced by a NN. ArcFace loss achieves this by enforcing a margin between
256
      the features of different classes in the angular space, making the decision boundary more distinct.
257
      Specifically, it adds an angular margin penalty to the target angles in the softmax function, which
258   forces the NN to learn more separable features (Khan et al., 2024). The loss uses a cosine similarity
259   in its implementation. This results in a more compact clustering of features from the same class and a
260   wider separation between features of different classes. This loss function assists deeply in quantifying
261   the similarity between the different signals.
262
      During training, we employed a fine-tuning approach by initializing our model weights with pre-
263
      trained weights of the Dorado model and then iteratively updating them during training. For training
264   purpose, 500 clusters consisting of 205, 690 samples were selected arbitrarily from the “Microsoft
265   Experiment” dataset whose size is in the magnitude of millions of samples. The samples are divided
266   into three sets. The first contains 70% of the samples, and it is used for training. The second contains
267   15% of the samples, and it is used for validation, and the remaining 15% are used for testing purposes,
268   and to visualize the test set embedding. The ML models are trained with a batch size of 256 samples
269   using the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014). During the first 50 epochs of training, the training
      was conducted on a subset of 100 clusters, from the initial selected 500. For the remaining 50 epochs,

353   Every non-heuristic-based DNA clustering algorithm that measures similarity without direct com-
354   putation relies on an edit-distance constraint. The ArcFace Loss (Deng et al., 2019) uses a cosine
355   similarity for separating between inputs, therefore, a cosine similarity matrix is created to compare
356   every pair of signals. For cosine similarity, the thresholds are continuous and not discrete as when
357
      using edit-distance, thus, containing much more data points, ranging between −1 and 1, where 1 is
      identical. Using these matrices, receiver operating characteristic (ROC) are generated. The ROC
358
      curve illustrates the trade-off between the true positive (TP) and false positive (FP) values of an
359
      machine learning model.
360
361   Figure 4 shows the ROC curves for the proposed signal-model and the three different baselines that
362   use edit-distance similarity of the DNA strands with varying values of 𝑘. We expect that models with
363
      better classification performance will have ROC curves closer to the top-left corner since this indicates
      high true positive rate with a small false positive. The curve of a random ML model is expected to be
364
      close to the diagonal identity line. We quantitatively measure this performance according to the area

      7.2   R ECONSTRUCTION P HASE
487
488   The reconstruction phase is done by taking the output clusters produced by the models or algorithms
489   (Pan et al., 2022; Sabary et al., 2024) and it computes the error rate within each cluster. Then,
490   the algorithms selectively remove reads to achieve a specific error rate conducive to successful
491   reconstruction of the design file. In contrast, since signal-model directly uses the raw signals, the
492   clustering algorithm will be given more data for the reconstruction algorithms. This is because
493   signal-model’s approach significantly reduces the sample exclusions, but still outputs well enough
494
      distinguishable clusters. This enables the removal of reads as necessary, potentially yielding equivalent
      or improved results. When certain clustering algorithms fail to produce all clusters, signal-model
495
      ensures that all clusters are available for reconstruction.

498   8     D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
499
500   Directly utilizing raw DNA signals for analyzing Nanopore sequencing data offers a transformative
501   approach to the DNA storage pipeline, particularly in the clustering and reconstruction phases. The
502   signal-based approach enhances both clustering and reconstruction, showing superior computational
503
      efficiency and accuracy over traditional DNA strand-based clusterings. Experiments reveal that the
      signal-model outperforms conventional clustering algorithms like Clover and Microsoft’s, providing
504
      faster and more accurate results. Additionally, the signal-model demonstrates significantly lower
505
      computation times, by orders of magnitude, highlighting its efficiency and scalability for large-scale
506   DNA storage tasks. This paper advocates for a shift towards using raw DNA signal data, improving
507   current methodologies, and paving the way for future advancements in DNA storage and analysis
508   technologies.
509
      This paper ignites research on using raw DNA signals directly, prompting the development of a
510
      clustering algorithm suitable for raw DNA signals. Future work can extend the approach of using
511
      the raw signals to enhance the reconstruction algorithms as well as the decoding procedure of error-
512   correcting codes in the DNA storage pipeline. Additionally, further extensions of this methodology
513   could apply to DNA and RNA sequencing across various fields, including bioinformatics, chemistry,
514   and biology.
515
      Limitations: The approach is tailored for Nanopore sequencing, but any modifications to the
516
      Nanopore machine may necessitate retraining the ML model, increasing the complexity of its
517
      practical application. While effective with up to 500 clusters, the algorithm has not yet been scaled
518   for thousands of clusters, which could pose additional challenges. The implementation relies on the
519   open-source Dorado model (Technologies, 2024) weights, introducing dependencies on external
520   updates. Additionally, further adjustments may be needed to adapt the algorithm for biological tasks,
521   particularly signal comparison, due to challenges like varied-length reads, which differ from the DNA
522   storage context.
