009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have achieved remarkable success in high-quality image gen-
012            eration, typically using convolutional neural networks (CNNs) or Vision Trans-
013            formers (ViTs) as backbone architectures. However, CNNs may struggle with
014            capturing long-range dependencies, while ViTs can be computationally intensive
015            due to their attention mechanisms. We propose the Diffusion Image GNN (DiG),
016            a novel architecture that leverages graph-based modeling within diffusion models.
017            By representing image patches as nodes in a graph and connecting them based on
018
               spatial relationships, DiG efficiently captures both local and global dependencies
               and naturally handles multi-scale features. Empirical results demonstrate that DiG
019
               achieves competitive Frechet Inception Distance (FID) scores compared to state-
020
               of-the-art methods. To our knowledge, this is the first application of graph neural
021            networks as a backbone within diffusion models for image generation, opening
022            new avenues for research in generative modeling.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Nichol &
028   Dhariwal, 2021; Croitoru et al., 2023) have emerged as a powerful class of generative models,
029   achieving state-of-the-art performance in high-quality image generation (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021;
030   Rombach et al., 2022). They have been successfully applied to a diverse range of tasks, including
031   image generation (Choi et al., 2021; Saharia et al., 2022b; Rombach et al., 2022), text-to-image
032   synthesis (Jiang et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022), video generation (Ho et al., 2022a;b; Blattmann
033
      et al., 2023; Xing et al., 2023), among others (Jing et al., 2022a; Wolleb et al., 2022).
034   Alongside advancements in the mathematical framework of diffusion models (Song et al., 2020;
035   2021b; Watson et al., 2022; Bao et al., 2022; Dockhorn et al., 2021; Kingma et al., 2021; Song et al.,
036   2021a; Vahdat et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2022), the choice of backbone architectures plays a pivotal
037   role in determining their overall performance. Historically, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)
038   (LeCun et al., 1998; He et al., 2016), such as U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015), have served as the
039
      de facto standard architecture in modern computer vision systems. However, recent developments
      have seen the introduction of Vision Transformers (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), which utilize
040
      attention mechanisms for visual tasks. Building on this architecture, new models (Hatamizadeh
041
      et al., 2023; Peebles & Xie, 2023; Bao et al., 2023) have emerged that adapt the transformer-based
042   design (Vaswani, 2017) for diffusion models, demonstrating competitive performance in generative
043   tasks. Following these advances, State Space Models (SSM) (Gu et al., 2021) have shown strong
044   capabilities in handling long-sequences, contributing to a new class of diffusion models that capture
045   fine-grained representations of images (Yan et al., 2024). These backbone architectures process
046   input data in fundamentally different ways. CNNs operate at the pixel level, applying convolutional
047   kernels across a grid of pixels to capture local features. In contrast, Transformers and SSMs process
048   images by treating patches as sequential tokens, where each patch represents a portion of the image.
049   By computing attention across this sequence of patches, Transformers fully connect them, allowing
050
      the model to capture global dependencies across the image.
051   An alternative approach involves representing images as compositions of their parts within a flex-
052   ible grid structure. Graph-based models (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Wu et al., 2020) are particularly
053   well-suited for this representation, as they naturally facilitate the modeling of complex objects by
      capturing the relationships between different parts. Unlike Transformers, which connect all patches

      through attention mechanisms – creating a fully connected graph – graph-based models can naturally
055   handle grid-like data and connect different scales of the image. By treating image components as
056   nodes and establishing edges between them, graphs allow for efficient computation of relationships
057   and can capture complex patterns inherent in images.
058
      In this work, we propose a novel backbone architecture called the Diffusion Image GNN (DiG),
059
      which leverages graph-based modeling within image diffusion models. DiG processes images by
060
      treating a patch as a node, and it constructs a structured graph by leveraging the spatial grid to capture
061   spatial locality of node features, and connecting multi-scale patch nodes to efficiently propagate
062   global information. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
063
             • We introduce DiG, a novel graph-based network backbone for diffusion models that pro-
064
               cesses images as graphs, capturing both local and global dependencies. We further propose
065
               two strategies to handle multi-scale features, which connects different levels of granularity
066            within the graph nodes representing image patches.
067
068          • We provide empirical evidence showing that DiG achieves competitive performance in FID
069            scores compared to popular Transformer-based approaches, highlighting the effectiveness
070            of graph-based representations in diffusion models.
071   To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to apply graph neural networks (GNNs) as a
072   backbone for image diffusion models with competitive performance, and we hope our work will
073   inspire the community to further explore GNN-based backbones.

080
081   Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song et al., 2021b) are a class of
082   generative models that synthesize new data by progressively adding noise to existing data and then
083   reversing this process to recover samples from the original data distribution. This approach involves
084   two key stages: a forward noising process and a reverse denoising process.
085   In the forward process, an original data point x0 is gradually corrupted by adding noise at each
086   time step, resulting in a sequence of increasingly noisy data points x1 , x2 , . . . , xT . This process is
087   modeled as a Markov chain, where each state depends only on the immediate previous state. The
                                                                                QT
088   forward process is defined by the joint distribution q(x1:T | x0 ) = t=1 q(xt | xt−1 ), where
089   q(xt | xt−1 ) represents the transition probability at each time

090   is added according to q(xt | xt−1 ) = N xt ; αt xt−1 , βt I , with αt and βt being parameters that
091   control the noise schedule and satisfying αt + βt = 1.

      2.2   A RCHITECTURES FOR IMAGE DIFFUSION MODELS
109
110   Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) (LeCun et al., 1998; He et al., 2016) have long been the
111   cornerstone of computer vision, applied to a wide array of visual tasks (Krizhevsky et al., 2012;
112   Ren et al., 2016; Long et al., 2015). Specifically, for image generation, U-Net (Ronneberger et al.,
113   2015) has been extensively used in diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021;
114   Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Ramesh et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022a). U-Net
115   features a symmetric encoder-decoder architecture designed for pixel-wise prediction. It utilizes
116
      skip connections (He et al., 2016) to transfer feature maps from the encoder to the corresponding
      decoder layers, preserving spatial information that might be lost during down-sampling.
117
118   Recently, Transformers based on Attention (Vaswani, 2017) have begun to supplant domain-specific
119   architectures due to their scalability and ability to model long-range dependencies (Kaplan et al.,
120   2020; Henighan et al., 2020). This shift has led to the introduction of Vision Transformers (ViT)
121   (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) as a common architecture for visual tasks (Chen et al., 2021; Lee et al.,
122
      2021; Strudel et al., 2021). In this regard, diffusion models built purely with Transformers have
      shown remarkable performance in image generation. For instance, U-ViT (Bao et al., 2023) adopts
123
      a similar architecture to U-Net but replaces CNN layers with Attention layers. DiT (Peebles & Xie,
124
      2023) follows a comparable approach but incorporates techniques from ResNets (He et al., 2016;
125   Goyal et al., 2019) by zero-initializing a selected set of parameters and introducing dimension-
126   wise scaling factors. DiffiT (Hatamizadeh et al., 2023) introduces a time-dependent self-attention
127   mechanism to jointly learn spatial and temporal dependencies. Other works enhance U-Net by
128   integrating self-attention in the low-resolution blocks and building a hybrid architecture, combining
129   CNNs and Transformers (Ho et al., 2020; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Hoogeboom et al., 2023).
130
      DiffuSSM (Yan et al., 2024) attempts to replace attention mechanisms with a more scalable state
131   space model backbone (Gu et al., 2021), generating high-quality images while being FLOP-efficient.
132   This approach opens avenues for applications that require modeling long-range dependencies with-
133   out the computational overhead associated with attention mechanisms.
134
135   2.3   PATCHIFICATION FOR I MAGE P ROCESSING
136
137   Patchification is the key pre-processing step in image diffusion transformers (Bao et al., 2023; Pee-
138   bles & Xie, 2023; Hatamizadeh et al., 2023) and DiffuSSM (Yan et al., 2024), which transforms an
                                                                                               ′
139   image input to a set of tokens. Here, a raw input stands for a noisy image xt ∈ Rh×w×c at time step
140   t, where h and w denote the height and width with c′ channels. Then patchification involves parti-
141   tioning the image into non-overlapping patches of size s × s, resulting in n = h×w  s2 patches. Each
142   patch is linearly embedded to form a new representation of d embedding dimension. The granularity
143
      of the patch size s significantly impacts quality and computational efficiency due to the quadratic
      complexity of attention mechanisms (Bao et al., 2023). The attention mechanism computes rela-
144
      tionships between all pairs of tokens, effectively modeling the input as a fully connected graph.
145
      While this captures global dependencies, it leads to substantial computational overhead. Towards
146   mitigating this, FlexAttention (Li et al., 2024) conducts patchification at both lower and higher res-
147   olutions, effectively reduces the number of active tokens. Such a method has yet to be tested on
148   image diffusion models to the best of our knowledge, leaving rooms for future research.
149

158
      to image patches, and edges are constructed by connecting nodes with similar visual tokens using
      k-nearest neighbors.

185   3    D IFFUSION I MAGE GNN (D I G)
186
187
      We propose a novel network backbone architecture for diffusion models, Diffusion Image GNN
188
      (DiG), that combines the strengths of graph-based models with diffusion processes, without relying
189   on Transformers. It operates on a multi-scale grid of patches, enabling the model to capture both
190   local and global dependencies. By incorporating patches of different sizes, the model can focus
191   on localized context by primarily attending to neighboring patches. At the same time, it integrates
192   broader spatial information from larger regions, enabling the capture of more complex patterns
193   across the image. This hierarchical patching strategy enhances performance by balancing detailed
194   local features with global spatial understanding, making it particularly effective for image generation
195   tasks in diffusion models.
196   DiG is summarized in Figure 1. The process begins with a noised image xt corrupted by noise ϵt
197   (Fig. 1a). The noised image xt is then represented as a grid of interconnected patches, with time t
198   and context c treated as special nodes fully connected to the grid (Fig. 1b). Scales of an image are
199   constructed using varying patch sizes, represented as an ordered set P = {s1 < s2 < · · · < sN },
200   where the image size remains fixed, but the graph size changes depending on the selected patch size
201   si . We introduce two new architectures of DiG, both operating on a grid of connected patches: (1)
202   DiGparallel , which applies a distinct GNN to each scale of the image to capture unique granularities,
203
      and (2) DiGmix , which integrates all scales into a unified multiscale graph processed by a single GNN
      with hierarchical connections between different patch sizes. Both types of DiG facilitate efficient
204
      feature learning across multiple resolutions, by using GNN layers for processing graph-based image
205
      patches, LayerNorm (Ba et al., 2016; Fey & Lenssen, 2019) for normalization, MLPs for feature
206   transformation, and skip connections to retain original feature information and improve gradient
207   flow (Fig. 1c). The network is optimized to predict the noise ϵt added to the image (Fig. 1d) using
208   objective Lsimple (θ) or Lcond (θ), where θ collects all trainable parameters in DiG.
209
      Within a DiG block, each GNN layer is designed to handle feature aggregation and updates using
210
      the aggregation function ϕ and the update function γ, both parameterized by learnable weights Wagg
211
      and Wupdate , respectively. For a given graph G = (A, H(k−1) ), where A represents the adjacency

      across the graph over K hops.

454

455   In DiGparallel , after processing each resolution, the features from all scales are concatenated across
456   multiple layers Ĥ (Eq. 5). This concatenation step requires the model to manage and process a high-
457   dimensional feature space, particularly when multiple resolutions are used, as the dimensionality of

459   through an MLP for transformation, further increasing computational overhead. The complexity
460   grows linearly with the number of resolutions, making DiGparallel computationally more expensive,
461   especially as the number of layers L increases. To address this computational burden, we experi-
462
      mented with summing the features from different resolutions rather than concatenating them. While
      this alternative approach would reduce the dimensionality and computational load, it led to a notice-
