009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Currently, most advanced speech editing models are based on either neural codec
012            language models (NCLM) (e.g., VoiceCraft) or diffusion models (e.g., Voicebox).
013            Although NCLM can generate higher quality speech compared to diffusion models,
014            it suffers from a higher word error rate (WER) (Peng et al., 2024), calculated by
015            comparing the transcribed text to the input text. We identify that this higher WER
016
               is due to attention errors (hallucinations), which make it difficult for NCLM to
               accurately follow the target transcription. To maintain speech quality and address
017
               the hallucination issue, we introduce VoiceNoNG, which combines the strengths of
018
               both model frameworks. VoiceNoNG utilizes a latent flow-matching framework to
019            model the pre-quantization features of a neural codec. The vector quantizer in the
020            neural codec implicitly converts the regression problem into a token classification
021            task similar to NCLM. We empirically verified that this transformation is crucial
022            for enhancing the performance and robustness of the speech generative model. This
023            simple modification enables VoiceNoNG to achieve state-of-the-art performance
024            in both objective and subjective evaluations. Lastly, to mitigate the potential
025            risks posed by the speech editing model, we examine the performance of the
026            Deepfake detector in a new and challenging practical scenario. Audio examples
027
               can be found on the demo page: https://anonymous.4open.science/
               w/NoNG-8004/

030   1     I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In recent years, speech editing technology has rapidly gained prominence, offering substantial benefits
033   across various domains. This technology enables users to seamlessly modify and enhance audio
034   recordings (Morrison et al., 2024), addressing issues such as slips of the tongue, mispronunciations,
035   or transient background noise. Consequently, YouTubers and filmmakers can edit their speeches
036   without the need for re-recording, significantly boosting productivity and reducing costs. However,
037   like many technological advancements, the rise of speech editing also presents notable risks. The
038
      same capabilities that make speech editing valuable for legitimate purposes can be exploited to create
      sophisticated Deepfake audio (NBC News, 2024), posing serious threats to security, privacy, and
039
      public trust. As these tools become more accessible, malicious actors could use them to fabricate
040
      convincing audio for disinformation, fraud, or other harmful activities, making it crucial to develop
041   and advance speech Deepfake detection technologies. The research community is increasingly
042   focused on building robust detection systems to identify manipulated speech (Zhang et al., 2021b;
043   2022; Liu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Pan et al., 2024), ensuring that the benefits of speech
044   editing can be enjoyed without compromising safety and security. Therefore, while speech editing
045   represents a significant leap forward in digital communication, it also underscores the need for
046   advanced detection methods to mitigate its potential dangers.
047   In this study, we first propose a state-of-the-art (SOTA) speech editing model, VoiceNoNG. To avoid
048   malicious applications, we also study the performance of the Deepfake detector in a practical new
049   challenging scenario. An introduction to these two topics is as follows:
050
051   1.1   S PEECH EDITING TECHNIQUES
052
053   In the early stages of speech editing, the process primarily involved cut-and-paste techniques to
      replace the speech segments that needed to be changed with the target speech segments. However,


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      this approach often resulted in unnatural intonation and noticeable boundaries. Voco (Jin et al., 2017)
055   addressed this issue with voice conversion to make the voice even closer to the original. Morrison
056   et al. (2021) performed speech editing under a single-speaker scenario to avoid the need for voice
057   conversion, which uses an utterance-level text-to-speech (TTS) system for context-aware synthesis,
058   followed by pitch-shifting, duration-stretching, and finally eliminating speech manipulation artifacts
059   by HiFi-GAN vocoding (Kong et al., 2020). Both of the above methods allow users to manually
060   fine-tune pitch, amplitude, and duration through a user interface to ensure natural transitions at the
061   boundaries.
062   In recent years, as speech synthesis technology has become more advanced, methods for speech
063   editing have generally been evolved into two main approaches: non-autoregressive speech infilling
064   (inpainting) (Borsos et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Le et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023; Alexos & Baldi,
065   2024; Ruiz et al., 2024), and autoregressive causal generation (Tan et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024;
066   Peng et al., 2024).
067   For the training of non-autoregressive model, the models take masked audio along with its transcript
068   as input, and the goal is to recover the masked segments. During speech editing, the segment to
069   be modified is simply masked with a new target transcript, and the corresponding edited speech
070   is generated. Non-autoregressive speech editing models can be further divided based on whether
071   explicit text-audio alignment is required. Models like A3T (Bai et al., 2022), Voicebox (Le et al.,
072   2024), and FluentSpeech (Jiang et al., 2023) require explicit alignment, while models such as
073   SpeechPainter (Borsos et al., 2022), AttentionStitch (Alexos & Baldi, 2024), and Mapache (Ruiz
074   et al., 2024) implicitly learn the alignment through their attention mechanisms.
075   For autoregressive models, the challenge lies in how to condition the causal generation on both past
076   and future contexts. EditSpeech (Tan et al., 2021) adopts an approach similar to non-autoregressive
077   methods, with the key difference being that masked generation is achieved through two decoders: a
078   forward decoder and a backward decoder. SpeechX (Wang et al., 2024) and VoiceCraft (Peng et al.,
079   2024) fall under the Neural Codec Language Model (NCLM) category. These models first convert
080   audio into discrete tokens by a neural codec, which are then trained as NCLMs through a next-token
081
      prediction task. The target text and context information are provided through prompting to help the
      model learn speech editing. The predicted tokens are then converted to a waveform using the neural
082
      codec decoder.
083
084   Among these speech editing models, Voicebox (Le et al., 2024) and VoiceCraft (Peng et al., 2024)
085   are the two most famous ones, and our proposed editing method, VoiceNoNG, is based on the
086   combination of these two methods. An overview of the input and output of current speech editing
087
      models are shown in Figure 1.

103   1.2   D EEPFAKE SPEECH DETECTION AND LOCALIZATION
104
105   To mitigate the potential harm caused by synthetic speech, challenges like ASVspoof (Kinnunen
106   et al., 2017; Todisco et al., 2019; Yamagishi et al., 2021) and ADD (Yi et al., 2023) have been
107   launched to promote defensive research. These primarily address fully spoofed scenarios, where
      the entire audio is generated through methods like text-to-speech or voice conversion. However,


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      generating an entire speech may not always be necessary. If attackers have access to real samples
109   with significant overlap with the target transcript, they only need to manipulate short segments using
110   speech editing models. Compared to the conventional fully spoofed scenario, detecting and localizing
111   (Yi et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021a; Zhang & Sim, 2022) partial spoof scenarios is more challenging.
112
      Additionally, given the impressive compression capabilities of neural codecs, it is anticipated that they
113
      will become a new standard for audio formats (e.g., mp3). For a Deepfake detector, it is insufficient
114
      to simply distinguish between real and codec-resynthesized audio (Wu et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024).
115   A practical new threat to consider in partial spoof scenarios is differentiating between resynthesized
116   and edited speech (Le et al., 2024). In this context, the detector cannot identify edited speech merely
117   by recognizing specific patterns or artifacts generated by the codec. More details will be discussed in
118   Section 3.2.
119
      In summary, this paper makes several significant contributions:
120
121   1) We identify the hallucination-like problem in the autoregressive-based VoiceCraft model, which
122   makes it difficult to follow the target transcription accurately. This leads to a higher word error rate
123   (WER), calculated by comparing the transcribed text to the target transcription.
124   2) We propose VoiceNoNG, a speech editing model that combines the strengths of both Voicebox
125   and VoiceCraft, achieving SOTA performance in both objective and subjective evaluations.
126
      3) We find that the vector quantizer module in the neural codec is crucial for developing a robust
127
      speech editing model.
128
129   4) We extend the partial spoof detection setting to a more practical scenario. A new speech dataset
130   edited by VoiceNoNG will be released to support defensive research.

133   2    P ROPOSED VOICE N O NG
134
135   As mentioned in the previous section, for the edited part to sound natural, the speaker characteristics
136   and background audio (e.g., noise, music, etc.) need to be consistent with the context. In this section,
137   we first discuss the drawbacks of Voicebox and VoiceCraft, and then explain how we address these
138   issues (Voicebox and VoiceCraft can be considered representative speech editing models based on
139   diffusion models and neural codec language models (NCLM), respectively).
140   Voicebox is a cutting-edge generative speech model based on a non-autoregressive flow-matching
141   approach. Its flow-matching framework allows it to directly learn to transform noise distributions
142   into audio data distributions, leading to more stable training, better generalization, and faster sample
143   generation compared to traditional diffusion-based methods. Furthermore, it is trained to perform
144   speech infilling task by conditioning on both past and future audio contexts with text transcript,
145   making Voicebox a versatile platform for various speech tasks, including zero-shot TTS synthesis,
146
      speech editing, transient noise removal, and style conversion.
147   On the other hand, VoiceCraft is a Transformer-based NCLM that utilizes autoregressive conditioning
148   on bidirectional context to infill the masked neural speech codec tokens. VoiceCraft is built upon a
149   two-step token rearrangement procedure, comprising a causal masking step and a delayed stacking
150   step. The causal masking technique enables autoregressive generation with bidirectional context, and
151   the combination with delayed stacking facilitates efficient multi-codebook modeling.
152   However, both Voicebox and VoiceCraft have their drawbacks. Voicebox generates waveforms using
153   a HiFi-GAN vocoder with Mel spectrograms as input and is trained on a large-scale, clean audiobook
154   corpus, LibriLight (Kahn et al., 2020). These two factors result in Voicebox not being good at
155   generating speech with background audio.
156   On the other hand, VoiceCraft addresses the issue by training on GigaSpeech (Chen et al., 2021),
157   which includes diverse audio conditions from sources like audiobooks, podcasts, and YouTube.
158   Additionally, it uses EnCodec (Défossez et al., 2022) for tokenization and waveform recovery.
159   However, VoiceCraft suffers from common hallucination-like problems found in large language
160   models (LLMs). These issues cause VoiceCraft to accidentally generate speech with unintended
161   long silences, slow speaking pace, or missing or repeated words. This will be discussed in detail
      in the experiment section, with examples provided in the Appendix.

162
      To address the aforementioned issues, we propose VoiceNoNG, which aims to combine the strengths
163   of both Voicebox and VoiceCraft. To avoid hallucination problems and enable the generation of
164   speech with background audio, VoiceNoNG utilizes a latent flow-matching framework.
165
166
      For the acoustic features, we use the Descript Audio Codec (DAC) (Kumar et al., 2024) instead of
      Mel-spectrograms. Mel-spectrogram is primarily designed based on how humans perceive speech
167
      signals, making it less suitable for modeling other audio types (e.g., noise, music, etc.). Besides being
168
      trained on a diverse range of audio sources, another reason for using DAC is its ability to compress
169   the features into a discrete latent space through a vector quantizer (VQ). This provides additional
170   robustness against minor prediction errors from the editing model (Fu et al., 2024). A small
171   error, as long as it is not larger than the token decision boundary, will still be mapped to the correct
172   token during VQ. This will be verified in section 3.1.6.
173
      In addition to the original flow-matching loss used in Voicebox, we apply a cross-entropy (CE) loss
174
      to further enhance our model’s codec prediction accuracy after VQ. Since the codec token selection is
175   based on the distance between the prediction and the candidates in the dictionary C, we can formulate
176   this process as a probability distribution using the softmax operation. Consequently, the CE loss Lce
177   can be calculated as follows:

192   3.1.1    S PEECH EDITING MODEL SETUP
193
194   Our proposed VoiceNoNG combines the strengths of both Voicebox and VoiceCraft, enabling our
195   model to perform background-preserving speech edits without the hallucination issues present in
196
      VoiceCraft. To verify that our modifications lead to the desired improvements, we compared our
      speech editing model with four other models: two different sizes of VoiceCraft (330M, 880M), and
197
      two Voicebox models using Mel-spectrograms as the acoustic feature, trained on LibriLight and
198
      GigaSpeech respectively. The results for VoiceCraft are based on the official code provided by the
199   authors. Since no code and model checkpoints are available for Voicebox, we reproduced the results.
200   Our Voicebox architecture settings are identical to those reported in the original Voicebox paper,
201   which features 330M parameters.
202
      To delve into more details, DAC comprises three modules: an encoder that encodes the input
203
      waveform into a fixed-dimension vector sequence (pre-quantization feature), a residual VQ module
204
      that discretizes the encoded representations into tokens, and a decoder that decodes the quantized
205   representations (post-quantization feature) back into the waveform. In our experiments, we use the
206   pre-quantization feature as our acoustic feature and apply a CE loss calculated against the ground-truth
207   quantized token labels to enhance the token prediction accuracy of VoiceNoNG.
208
      To demonstrate the impact of this setting, we also conduct an ablation study to highlight the benefits
209
      of modeling pre-quantization features and incorporating the CE loss.

213   Unless otherwise noted, the training data used in our experiment is GigaSpeech. For evaluating
214   different speech editing models, we utilize the RealEdit dataset (Peng et al., 2024), a pioneering
215   benchmark that features 310 manually-crafted examples reflecting real-world editing scenarios.
      This dataset includes tuples of original audio, original transcript, and edited transcript, meticulously

      crafted to ensure grammatical correctness and semantic coherence. RealEdit consists of 100 utterances
217   from the LibriTTS dataset (Zen et al., 2019), 100 from YouTube (sourced from the GigaSpeech
218   test set (Chen et al., 2021)), and 110 from the Spotify Podcast dataset (Clifton et al., 2020). The
219   utterances range from 5 to 12 seconds in duration and encompass a diverse array of content, accents,
220   speaking styles, and recording conditions. The edits span various types—insertions, deletions, and
221   substitutions—across different lengths, from short (1-2 words) to long (7-12 words), with single or

225
      editing models in diverse, real-world contexts.

227   transcripts, and edited transcripts from the RealEdit dataset. We then evaluate these generated

232   3.1.3     O BJECTIVE EVALUATION FOR SPEECH INTELLIGIBILITY: WER
233
      WER is an appropriate metric for evaluating TTS and speech editing since it provides a quantitative
234
      assessment of how accurately a system converts text into speech. Table 1 displays the WER results for
235   various speech editing models using Whisper-large-v3 (Radford et al., 2023) as the ASR model. The
236   WER is computed by comparing the transcribed text to the target transcript provided by the RealEdit
237   datasets. Before calculating the WER, both texts were normalized using the whisper normalizer 1 .
238
      As indicated in the table, all flow-matching-based methods achieve lower WER than VoiceCraft,
239
      which is consistent with the findings presented in the VoiceCraft paper (Peng et al., 2024). A closer
240
      examination reveals that this is primarily due to the hallucination problem associated with VoiceCraft.
241   Neekhara et al. (2024) also highlights that LLM-based TTS models lack robustness, often producing
242   outputs with repeating words, missing words, and misaligned speech, particularly when the text
243   includes multiple instances of the same token. Examples of this hallucination issue can be found in
244   the Appendix, specifically in Tables 4 and 5. Additionally, the results demonstrate that when the
245   training corpus for Voicebox is switched from LibriLight to GigaSpeech, the WER decreases across
246   all types of audio sources, and further reducing the WER is achieved by changing the acoustic feature
247   from Mel-spectrogram to DAC.
248   In our proposed models, we also perform an ablation study to illustrate the advantages of modeling
249   pre-quantization features and adding CE loss. When the output of our flow-matching model is the
250   pre-quantization features, it must be processed through the VQ and DAC decoder to reconstruct the
251   waveform. As noted in Section 2, the VQ module offers additional robustness against prediction
252   errors made by our model. In contrast, if the output is the post-quantization features (similar to
253   (Shen et al., 2023)), only the DAC decoder is required for waveform reconstruction. As a result,
254   post-quantization features yield the highest WER on average. Incorporating CE loss helps improve
255
      the token prediction accuracy of pre-quantization features, thereby reducing the WER.
256   It is essential to highlight that the WER results presented in Table 1 are calculated for the entire
257   utterance, and the unmasked regions are expected to exhibit the same WER. Consequently, the WER
258   differences among various editing models should be considerably more pronounced in the edited
259   regions. Additionally, while a lower WER signifies that the generated speech is more intelligible to
260   the ASR and contains more accurate content, it does not automatically imply that the overall quality
261
      is better.

      3.2     D ETECTION OF S PEECH E DITED BY VOICE N O NG
382
383   As demonstrated in the previous subjective evaluation, people find it challenging to distinguish
384   between speech edited by the proposed VoiceNoNG and real speech. To prevent malicious uses,
385   a reliable Deepfake detector is essential. This section explores a practical new threat within the
386   partial spoof scenario. Given the potential for neural codecs to become a new audio format standard,
387   the assumption that all codec-generated speech is fake may soon be unrealistic. Therefore, in the
388   following experiments, we classified codec-resynthesized speech as a new category. The original
389   binary classifier was extended to a ternary classifier with the labels: real, resynthesized, and edited.
390
      Additionally, for the audio condition, besides the original VoiceNoNG setting where non-edited
      segments come from the original audio, we consider a more challenging setting where non-edited
391
      segments are also resynthesized from the codec. We refer to this condition as VoiceNoNG (resyn).
392
393
      3.2.1    D EEPFAKE SPEECH DETECTOR MODEL SETUP
394
395   We built the w2v2-detector using a model architecture similar to the one that won first place in the
396   partially fake audio track at ADD2022 (Yi et al., 2022), which also demonstrated strong performance
397   in our previous work. Specifically, the w2v2-detector employs a pretrained wav2vec2-base-960h
398   model (Baevski et al., 2020) for feature extraction. We use a weighted sum of the 13 hidden states,
399   including the output from the CNN encoder, as our input features. These features are then projected
400
      from 768 dimensions to 128 dimensions using a linear layer. The model then splits into two paths: the
      frame-level branch and the utterance-level branch. In the frame-level branch, the projected features
401
      pass through a linear layer to produce a 3-dimensional output representing the prediction for each
402
      frame. In the utterance-level branch, the projected features undergo attentive statistics pooling (Okabe
403   et al., 2018) to compress the features into a single representation of the entire utterance, which is then
404   passed through a linear layer to generate the utterance-level prediction.
405
406   3.2.2    T RAINING AND TEST SETS FOR D EEPFAKE DETECTOR
407
408   We used LibriLight medium (Kahn et al., 2020) as the source audio files and generated the edited
409   speech using our proposed VoiceNoNG. The target transcripts were generated by prompting an LLM
410   (i.e., zephyr-7B-beta (Tunstall et al., 2023)). However, the edited transcripts may not always adhere
411
      to the desired format. To address this, we used word-level Levenshtein distance (Levenshtein et al.,
      1966) to identify substitutions, and then randomly selected words for replacement as our method of
412
      edit manipulation.
413
414   The dataset was first categorized by speaker, prioritizing those with more audio samples for the
415   training set, and then sequentially allocating speakers to the validation and test sets. This approach
416   ensured that no speaker appeared in multiple sets, with the test set containing a significant number
417
      of unseen speakers, enabling us to effectively assess the detector’s generalizability at the speaker
      level. The dataset was divided into training, validation, and test sets, comprising 106,186, 34,744,
418
      and 33,974 audio files, respectively. Additionally, the quantities of real, resynthesized, and edited
419
      speech in the original VoiceNoNG and VoiceNoNG (resyn) settings were balanced within each set.

475   4   C ONCLUSION
476
477
      In this study, we first examine the limitations of current advanced speech editing models. Voicebox
478
      produces lower-quality speech in the presence of background audio (e.g., noise, music), while Voice-
479
      Craft struggles to accurately follow text input, a common hallucination-like issue with autoregressive
480   models. To address these challenges, we introduce VoiceNoNG, which leverages the strengths of both
481   models and achieves SOTA performance in both objective and subjective evaluations (we encourage
482   readers to listen to the demos on our demo page, which shows VoiceNoNG can keep the accent
483   and even successfully generate background music for movie editing!). We also identify the vector
484   quantizer module in the neural codec as crucial for achieving a robust speech editing model. Lastly,
485   to mitigate the potential risks posed by the speech editing model, we examine the performance of the
      Deepfake detector in a new and challenging practical scenario.
