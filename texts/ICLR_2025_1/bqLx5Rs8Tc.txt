009                                                         A BSTRACT
010
011            Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training (CLIP) models have demonstrated remarkable perfor-
012            mance in zero-shot classification tasks, yet their efficacy in handling complex multi-object scenarios
013            remains challenging. This study presents a comprehensive analysis of CLIP’s performance limi-
014            tations in multi-object contexts through controlled experiments. We present a specialized dataset,
015            ComCO, crafted to thoroughly assess the performance of CLIP’s encoders in diverse multi-object
016            scenarios. Our findings reveal significant biases in both encoders, with the text encoder showing a
017            tendency to prioritize objects that are mentioned first in the prompt, and the image encoder exhibit-
018
               ing a bias toward larger objects. Through meticulous experiments, including both retrieval-based
               and classification-based tasks, we quantify these biases across multiple CLIP variants, we quantify
019
               these biases across multiple CLIP variants. We hypothesize that these biases originate from CLIP’s
020
               training process and provide substantiating evidence through detailed analyses of the LAION dataset
021            and CLIP’s training progression. Our image-text matching experiments demonstrate substantial per-
022            formance drops when manipulating object sizes in the images and/or object tokens order in the
023            prompt, highlighting the CLIP’s unstable performance when given rephrased yet semantically simi-
024            lar captions. We extend this analysis to longer, more complex captions and text-to-image generative
025            models such as Stable Diffusion, revealing how CLIP’s text encoder bias influences object promi-
026            nence in generated images based on the prompt’s token order. This work provides crucial insights
027            into CLIP’s behavior in complex visual-linguistic contexts, offering a robust evaluation methodology
028            and identifying key areas for improving future vision-language models in multi-object scenarios.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The convergence of vision and language in artificial intelligence has led to the development of Vision-Language
      Models (VLMs) that can interpret and generate multimodal content. Among these, OpenAI’s Contrastive Language-
034
      Image Pre-training (CLIP) model Radford et al. (2021) has been particularly influential, demonstrating remarkable
035
      capabilities in zero-shot image classification and setting new standards for multimodal understanding Cherti et al.
036   (2023); Gadre et al. (2023); Schuhmann et al. (2021); Thrush et al. (2022). The success of CLIP has catalyzed a wide
037   array of applications—from image retrieval and visual question answering to text-to-image generation—signifying a
038   paradigm shift in how models perceive and relate visual and linguistic information.
039
      Visual Language Models like CLIP face significant challenges in understanding and reasoning about complex scenes
040
      with multiple objects and intricate relationships. CLIP struggles to identify distinct objects and model their rela-
041
      tionships accurately, especially when captions contain the same objects but differ in their relationships. This results
042   in difficulty distinguishing between similar captions with different object relationships. Several benchmark datasets
043   have been introduced to elucidate the limitations of existing models in capturing subtle relational nuances. Notably,
044   Winoground Thrush et al. (2022), VL-CheckList Zhao et al. (2022), ARO Yuksekgonul et al. (2023), and CREPE Ma
045   et al. (2023) have been instrumental in evaluating models’ capacities to accurately match images with semantically
046   appropriate captions.
047
      Numerous efforts have been made to address compositionality challenges in the multi-object setting. These studies
048   have predominantly employed end-to-end methodologies, including fine-tuning techniques with hard-negative samples
049   Yuksekgonul et al. (2023), to enhance model performance. The efficacy of these approaches have been criticized and
050   improved recently, SUGARCREPE Hsieh et al. (2024), and Sahin et al. (2024). Specifically, a common methodology
051   in these works involves the generation of negative captions through minor structural alterations, or through LLMs, to
052   the original positive ones, emphasizing the identification of semantic disparities between captions that share structural
053   similarities but differ conceptually. Hence, these approaches helped in capturing nuances in the text domain that is
      necessary in the compositional multi-object scenarios.

      visual variations.

093   ferent aspects of CLIP’s performance in controlled multi-object scenarios. Furthermore, our research spans multiple
094   versions of CLIP trained on various datasets and architectures, ensuring the broad applicability and generalizability
095   of our findings. By focusing on these underexplored areas and employing a more comprehensive analytical approach,
096
      our work aims to provide a deeper understanding of CLIP’s limitations and pave the way for more robust and versatile
      vision-language models. It is important to note that such an analysis not only benefits the improvement of CLIP but
097

098

099   the development of these technologies, potentially leading to advancements across various domains of artificial intel-

107               • Comprehensive Encoder Analysis: We perform an in-depth examination of both the image and text encoders
                    in CLIP when processing multi-object scenes and descriptions. This includes text-based, and object-based

      2.1     DATASET D ESIGN
139
140   To thoroughly evaluate the performance of CLIP models in multi-object scenarios under controlled conditions, we
141   constructed the ComCO (Complex COCO Objects) dataset. Utilizing Blender software allowed us precise control over
142   the number, location, and dimensions of objects in the images (see Appendix A.1). The ComCO dataset comprises 72
143   objects derived from the COCO dataset Lin et al. (2015). We generated images containing 2, 3, 4, and 5 objects. Each
144   image is paired with a specific caption that accurately describes the objects present. This approach ensures high control
145   over the dataset and minimizes confounding factors, providing a robust platform for evaluating the CLIP models.
146   We deliberately chose not to use text-to-image models for generating these datasets due to two main reasons. First,
147   these models often lack the capability to produce high-quality, fully controlled multi-object images. Second, since
148   CLIP is used in many of these models, utilizing them could introduce unwanted biases into our evaluations.
149
150   2.2     E XPERIMENTAL F RAMEWORK FOR E NCODER A NALYSIS
151
152   The main goal of this study is to evaluate the performance of CLIP’s text and image encoders separately in multi-
153   object scenarios. We aim to analyze the impact and contribution of each object in the final output of the encoders. To
154
      achieve this, we conducted experiments using our designed ComCO dataset, with images and captions containing two
      to five objects. To ensure the generalizability of our findings, we also validated our results on the widely-used COCO
155
      dataset Lin et al. (2014). We designed two sets of experiments: retrieval-based experiments and classification-based
156
      experiments. Given the consistency of the results in both types of experiments, we have included the classification
157   results in the appendix A.2 and A.5 and explain the retrieval-based experiments bellow.
158

      4.1     B IAS IN THE I MAGE E NCODER
294
295   The observed bias favoring larger objects within the image domain can be attributed to the architectural characteristics
296   of Vision Transformers (ViT) Alexey (2020) utilized in CLIP’s image encoder. Our hypothesis is that larger objects,
297   which occupy a greater number of patches in the ViT’s patch-based image representation, exert a more significant
298   influence on the final class (CLS) token representation. This bias is not exclusive to CLIP; it appears to be a consistent
299   feature across ViT models, as demonstrated by our experiments detailed in the appendix.
300
      To substantiate this hypothesis, we designed an experiment to quantify the attention allocated by the CLS token to each
301   image patch. By calculating the cumulative attention received by each object from the CLS token, we could assess the
302   influence of object size on attention allocation. We applied this analysis to our three-object ComCO dataset, and the
303   results are illustrated in Figure 3. The findings confirm our hypothesis: larger objects indeed receive more attention
304   from the CLS token.
305
306   4.2     B IAS IN THE T EXT E NCODER
307
308   We explore the bias present in the text encoder from two perspectives: the attention mechanism in the model structure
309   and the model’s training method.
310
311   4.2.1    I MPACT OF ATTENTION M ECHANISM
312
      Text encoder models can be categorized based on their attention mechanisms: uni-directional (causal) attention and
313   bi-directional attention. In models with causal attention, each token attends only to preceding tokens, whereas in
314   bi-directional models, each token attends to all tokens in the sequence.
315
316
      When OpenAI introduced the CLIP model, its text encoder employed causal attention, meaning each token could only
      attend to tokens before it and itself. This differs from typical self-attention mechanisms, where tokens attend to all
317
      other tokens. Most CLIP models use causal self-attention, with the exception of the variant using the XLM-Roberta
318
      text encoder, which also employs self-attention. However, as shown in Table 1, even this model exhibits the mentioned
319   bias. This indicates that the bias does not originate from the attention mechanism itself.
320
321   4.2.2    ROLE OF T RAINING M ETHOD
322
323   To determine whether the observed bias is specific to CLIP models, we compared CLIP’s text encoder with two other
      models designed to embed sentences into a meaningful semantic space: Sentence-BERT (SBERT) Reimers (2019)


                                                            6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      and SimCSE Gao et al. (2021). The primary distinction is that CLIP’s embedding space is shared between images and
325   text, whereas SBERT and SimCSE operate solely in the text domain.
326
327   We conducted the TOR experiment on our dataset using these models. As presented in Table 5, the bias observed in
328
      CLIP differs from that in the other models. This suggests that CLIP’s unique training method, which aligns images
      and text in a shared embedding space through contrastive learning, contributes to the bias. Therefore, to uncover the
329
      root cause of the bias, we focus on the specifics of CLIP’s training procedure.
330
331
      4.3   H YPOTHESIZED O RIGIN OF T EXT-S IDE B IAS IN CLIP
332
333   We hypothesize that the text-side bias in CLIP, which favors objects mentioned earlier in text descriptions, originates
334   from the image-side bias toward larger objects and is transferred to the text encoder during contrastive training. We
335   present evidence supporting this hypothesis through two key claims and an analysis of the training progression.
336
337   Claim 1: Larger Objects Have More Influence on Text Embeddings. Building upon the established image-side
338   bias discussed earlier, we posit that objects with larger physical sizes exert more influence on CLIP’s text embeddings
339   due to the alignment enforced during contrastive training. To test this, we categorized objects in the DomainNet dataset
340   into large, medium, and small groups based on their relative physical sizes in real-world (with the full list of objects
341   provided in the appendix A.11). Specifically, objects smaller than a school bag were categorized as small, objects
342
      sized between a school bag and a medium-sized car were classified as medium, and objects larger than a car—up to
      significantly larger items—were considered large. We then constructed two sets of sentences, each containing four
343
      objects: one set with a large object mentioned first followed by three medium-sized objects, and another with a small
344
      object mentioned first followed by three medium-sized objects.
345

      5     P RACTICAL I MPACTS OF E NCODER B IASES
446
447   The biases we observed in CLIP’s image and text encoders have significant implications for the model’s performance
448   in real-world applications. This section explores how these biases manifest in practical scenarios, focusing on two key
449   areas: image-text matching and text-to-image generation. By examining these applications, we aim to demonstrate the
450   tangible effects of encoder biases on CLIP’s functionality and highlight the importance of addressing these issues for
451   improved model performance.
452   Our analysis in this section serves two primary purposes. First, it provides concrete evidence of how these theoretical
453   biases can translate into practical limitations. Second, it offers insights into potential areas for improvement in vision-
454   language models, particularly in handling complex, multi-object scenarios. Through a series of carefully designed
455   experiments, we illustrate how the biases in both text and image encoders can lead to unexpected or suboptimal results
456   in tasks that are crucial for many downstream applications.

      Building upon our findings of biases in CLIP’s image and text encoders, we now demonstrate how these biases tangibly
      affect the model’s performance in image-caption matching tasks. We designed two experimental scenarios, conducted
462
      on both the ComCO and COCO datasets, to evaluate these biases. The results of these experiments are summarized in
463
      Table 6. To better illustrate the differences between these two scenarios, an example of the caption structures is shown

      captions more effectively. This positioning emphasizes the discrepancy early on, allowing the model to better detect

      the mismatch between the caption and the image. The performance of different models in this scenario can be seen in

      column.

498   5.2   T EXT TO IMAGE GENEATION
499
500   The biases observed in CLIP’s encoders have significant implications beyond image-text matching, particularly for
501   text-to-image generation models that incorporate CLIP components. To investigate this impact, we focused on Stable
502   Diffusion, a popular text-to-image generation model that utilizes CLIP’s text encoder in its pipeline. Stable Diffusion
503   employs CLIP’s text encoder to process input prompts, creating text embeddings that guide the image generation
504   process. Given our identification of biases in CLIP’s text encoder, especially the preference for objects mentioned
505   earlier in text descriptions, we hypothesized that these biases would manifest in the generated images. To test this
506   hypothesis, we designed an experiment using prompts containing multiple objects from the COCO dataset. Our goal
507
      was to observe whether the order of objects in the text prompt influences their prominence or likelihood of appearance
      in the generated images.
508
509   Our experimental methodology consisted of three main steps. First, we created 1,000 multi-object prompts, each
510   containing four distinct objects from the COCO dataset. Second, we used these prompts to generate images using
511   three versions of Stable Diffusion: v1.4 Rombach et al. (2022), v2, and SD-XL Podell et al. (2023). Finally, to
512   evaluate the presence of objects in the generated images, we employed YOLO v8 Reis et al. (2023), a state-of-the-art
513
      object detection model. We configured YOLO v8 with a detection threshold of 0.25 and used it to validate which
      objects from the original prompt were present in the generated image.
514
515   This approach allowed us to quantitatively assess how CLIP’s text encoder biases propagate through the Stable Diffu-
516   sion pipeline and manifest in the generated images. By comparing the frequency of object detection with their position
517   in the input prompt, we could directly observe the impact of the text-side bias on the image generation process.

532   6     C ONCLUSION
533
534   Our study reveals significant biases in CLIP’s image and text encoders, favoring larger objects and first-mentioned
535   items respectively. These biases, demonstrated through our ComCO dataset, substantially impact CLIP’s performance
536   in multi-object scenarios. The observed performance drops when manipulating object sizes and mention order un-
537   derscore CLIP’s limitations in handling complex visual environments. These findings highlight the need for more
538   balanced training approaches in vision-language models to mitigate such biases. Future work should focus on de-
539   veloping techniques to address these limitations, advancing the field towards more robust and versatile AI systems
      capable of accurately interpreting multi-faceted real-world information.

1109
             1. Dataset Preparation:
1110
1111               • We utilized both the SimCO and ComCO datasets, containing images with 2 to 5 objects.
1112               • In each multi-object image, one object was deliberately made larger than the others.
1113               • The position of the larger object was varied across images to avoid position-based biases.
1114               • We also prepared a set of single-object images for each object class in our datasets.
1115         2. Image Embedding Generation:
1116
                   • We used CLIP’s image encoder to generate embeddings for all multi-object images.
1117
1118
                   • Similarly, we generated embeddings for all single-object images.
1119         3. Similarity Computation:
1120               • For each multi-object image, we computed the cosine similarity between its embedding and the embed-
1121                 dings of all single-object images.
1122         4. Object Retrieval:
1123
                   • For each multi-object image, we identified the single-object image with the highest similarity score.
1124
1125
                   • We recorded whether the retrieved single-object image corresponded to the large object or one of the
                     small objects in the multi-object image.
1126
1127         5. Evaluation:
1128               • We calculated the percentage of times the large object and each small object were retrieved as the most
1129                 similar.
1130               • This percentage represents the retrieval accuracy for each object size category (large object, small object
1131                 1, small object 2, etc.).
