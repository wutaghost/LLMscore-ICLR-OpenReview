011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Previous research on the Llama-2 family of Large Language Models (LLMs)
014            suggested a correlation indicating the use of English as a intermediary language
015            within these models for tasks in non-English languages. We improve on this by
016            demonstrating a causal relationship. By intervening on the intermediate layers
017
               during a forward pass, we show that projecting out the activations onto a subspace
               corresponding to the correct prediction in English impairs the model’s ability to
018
               make correct predictions on non-English translation tasks. Projecting out an unre-
019
               lated English subspace, or a related subspace in a non-English language, has little
020            effect, demonstrating that this family of models store concepts that have a high
021            similarity to the corresponding concept in English in the residual stream.
022
023
024   1     I NTRODUCTION
025
026   The dramatic abilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) using the transformer architecture
027   Vaswani et al. (2017); Phuong & Hutter (2022) are rather surprising, given the sole goal during
028   training is to predict the next word in a sequence. LLMs generalize to many out-of-distribution
029   tasks, and exhibit abilities typically associated with intelligence, such as solving difficult maths
030   problems, tool use, and demonstrating theory of mind Bubeck et al. (2023). Interestingly, models
031   predominantly trained on English data tend to perform well in other languages, even when other
032   languages constitute a tiny proportion of the training data K et al. (2020); Blevins & Zettlemoyer
033   (2022). Tianyi Tang et al. (2024) show that language-specific neurons in LLMs are responsible for
034
      their multilingual capabilities, and that activating or deactivating these neurons can control the out-
      put language. Additionally, Julen Etxaniz et al. (2023) show that LLMs trained predominately in
035
      English can perform better on tasks in non-English by explicitly prompting the model to translate
036
      to English, solving the task in English, and then translating back to the target language. Shi et al.
037   (2022) show that by using chain-of-thought Chu et al. (2023) prompting, models can perform vastly
038   better on even obscure languages.
039

      1.2   M AIN C ONTRIBUTION
089
090
091   For translation tasks between non-English languages on the Llama-2-7b model Touvron et al. (2023):
092
093
094          • We show a causal relationship between the unembedding vectors of the corresponding an-
095
               swer in English and the downstream prediction of the model in the target language. By
               computing the vector projection projS (hi ) of the intermediate activations hi onto a sub-
096
               space S corresponding to the correct prediction in English, and subtracting this projection
097
               out, this impairs the model’s ability to make correct predictions on non-English translation
098            tasks. This effect is far weaker when projecting out a related subspace in a non-English
099            language, and not present at all when projecting out an unrelated subspace (using words
100            unrelated to that being translated), regardless of the language chosen to intervene with.
101
102
103          • Normally, steering vectors Turner et al. (2023) are obtained by computing a forward pass
104            over inputs of interest, and extracting vectors of interest from the activations. We show
105            a weak effect demonstrating that on translation tasks, we can cause the model to predict
106            a desired counterfactual translation into the target language by steering directly using the
107            unembedding vectors of the corresponding word in English. Note that this effect is not
               robust, and can in may cases cause the model activations to diverge during a forward pass.

      2     P RELIMINARIES
109
110
      2.1   M OTIVATION
111
112   LLMs are impressive in the capabilities that they have, but it is not at all clear how they can perform
113   the tasks they do. After training, the mechanism by which the model can make good predictions is
114   present, but buried amongst the weights of the network.
115
      This motivates a more transparent approach to understanding the behaviour of models, and what the
116
      field of mechanistic interpretability aims to achieve: A holistic understanding of how models can
117   perform particular tasks. Meng et al. (2022) show how to extract and modify particular facts known
118   by the model. Wang et al. (2022) demonstrate how particular attention heads in a transformer model
119   can learn how to solve simple in-context-learning tasks. Li et al. (2023) demonstrate that LLMs
120   internally construct models of the world to solve tasks. However, works like this are very costly
121   in terms of human time spent on analysis of models, and it is desirable to find more automatic
122   approaches to understanding models. Zhang & Nanda (2024) introduce activation patching, to take
123   the activations from a forward pass on corrupted input, patch them into the activations of a forward
124   pass on clean input, and observe the causal downstream effect. Todd et al. (2024) present function
125
      vectors, a direction in latent space representing a particular function or operation, and use this to
      steer the model on other such inputs.
126

128   2.2   T RANSFORMERS
129

130

133   Sennrich et al. (2016). Each token is an integer in the range ti ∈ {1, . . . , dvocab }, where dvocab is the
134   size of the vocabulary for the model.
135

      3.1       S UBSPACE R EJECTION
190
191   To demonstrate Llama-2’s reliance on English as an intermediary language, we perform a series
192   of interventions on the residual stream of the model during a forward pass on translation tasks.
193   Information for prediction of the next token must be stored somewhere in the residual stream (it
194   being the only causal path from earlier layers to later layers). We hypothesise that for translation,
195   the information of the concept to translate is stored in a low-dimensional subspace S ⊆ Rdmodel of
196
      the residual stream, and this subspace is similar to a set S = {Ui1 ,: , . . . Uim ,: } of unembedding
197   vector(s) U·,: ∈ Rdmodel for the token(s) describing that concept in English. If so, replacement of hi
198   with the orthogonal projection projS⊥ (hi ) of hi onto the orthogonal complement S⊥ of S should
199
      affect the model’s ability to predict the correct answer in the target language.
200                               projS ⊥ (hi ) = hi − projS (hi ) = hi − S(S T S)−1 S T hi                       (6)

379
380   The next experiment we perform is to try and steer the model to give a desired counterfactual transla-
381   tion in the target language only by modifying the activations using the unembedding vectors in the la-
382   tent language. Given a source word tsource to translate, and subspaces Slang (tsource ) and S̄lang (tsource )
383   as described in Section 3.1, we perform the intervention
384

                                                                     |S̄|

      where c > 0 is a constant controlling the strength of the steering vector. We then observe the effect
388
      on the model’s prediction by sweeping over layers to intervene on, as well as the size of the constant
389   c.
390
391   We found that the strength of the effect grows monotonically with c, up to the point where the
392
      residual stream would diverge for c ≈ 10. As expected, the effect was strongest when using Uen ,
      but the choice of layer to intervene on is critical (and depends on the choice of c). We found the
393
      strongest effect (the one that boosts the probability of the counterfactual word in the target language
394
      the most) was to intervene on layers 13 to 18, and to choose c = 8. This is quite a large constant, as
395   the norm of the vector that we project out is ≈ 4−5, so essentially we are adding a larger vector back
396   in to the one we projected out. For more reasonable values of c (around 1-2), the effect was barely
397   noticeable. This indicates that our steering intervention perhaps is not a suitable one, and that other
398   methods should be investigated. We plot both the probability of the correct answer in the target
399   language, and the probability of the counterfactual answer in the target language to demonstrate
400   the effect is both a suppression of the correct answer, and a boosting of the counterfactual answer
401   (Figure 5b).We also plot the difference in log-probabilities between the counterfactual and correct
402   answer (Figure 5d). See Appendix A.1 for plots of sweeps over choices of c, and for various choices
403
      of languages.

405   3.3   C ONCLUSION
406
407
      Our results are somewhat mixed: We do confirm that to a degree, the LLama-2-7b model is using
      English as an intermediary language, and that the unembedding vectors in English do explain some
408
      of the model’s behaviour in translation tasks. Projecting out in English clearly has a much stronger
409
      effect than projecting out in other languages, and the lack of effect for unrelated subspaces does
410   indicate this intervention is not just causing general damage to the model.
411
412   However, the effect is not as strong as we would have hoped. There is still a lot of probability mass
413
      that the model assigns to the correct prediction after the rejection intervention. We would have liked
      to have seen the model’s ability to predict the correct answer drop to near zero, but this was not the
414
      case. This indicates that the model is storing concepts elsewhere in a subspace that is not easily
415
      projected out, or that the model is able to recover from the intervention by using other information
416   stored in the residual stream.
417
418
      3.4   F UTURE W ORK
419
420   We also performed the same experiment for Llama-2-13b, Gemma-2-2b and Gemma-2-9b, see Ap-
421   pendix A. While the same effect was observed for Llama-2-13b as was for Llama-2-7b, which was
422   expected, the effect was much more destructive for the Gemma models. Any intervention, even
423   those in an unrelated subspace or a non-English language, quite badly affected the model’s ability to
424   predict the correct answer. However, the Gemma models were still more vulnerable to rejections in
425   English than any other language, causing the probability of prediction of the correct token to drop
426   to near zero. We are unsure what to make of this behaviour, perhaps Gemma tends to store concepts
427   in a less English-biased manner, or that the unembedding vectors are similar between languages, or
428
      that the Gemma models are more fragile in general. We leave this to future work.
429   The steering effect that we used was also rather brute-force, and would quite drastically change
430   the activations midway through the network to something well outside the normal distribution. We
431   would like to explore other methods of steering the model using the unembedding vectors only that
      are less destructive, while still being effective.

610   A.1   S KIP C ONNECTIONS
611
612   If models can make good predictions over a large class of domains, we would expect that somewhere
613   in the residual stream of the transformer, the model stores useful information for prediction of the
614   next token. In a standard feed-forward neural network, each pair of adjacent weight matrices have
615   permutation invariances Entezari et al. (2022), so we should not expect the intermediary activations
616
      to have any clear resemblance to the final logits on the output.
617   It has been shown that deep vision neural networks pose a problem to train, due to gradients van-
618   ishing or exploding Pascanu et al. (2013). This was solved by adding skip connections He et al.
619   (2016), which allowed very deep models to be trained effectively. As a side effect, transformers
620   have been hypothesised to encourage the model to perform iterative inference: the intermediary ac-
621   tivations represent the models’ best current guess at the output midway through the forward pass,
622
      which gradually move towards the model’s final prediction Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2018). There are two
      factors at play here:
623
624          • The presence of skip connections in the transformer breaks permutation invariances, so the
625            choice of basis for the intermediary activations is no longer arbitrary.
626
             • Given some neural network fΘ with a skip connection, the operation performed is
627            x + fΘ (x). The addition of L2 weight decay Goodfellow et al. (2016) during training
628            encourages the weights of f to be small, so all else equal, the model will prefer weights Θ
629            such that x + fΘ (x) is close to x.
