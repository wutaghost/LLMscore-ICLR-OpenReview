009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            With the emergence of diffusion models as the frontline of generative models,
012            many researchers have proposed molecule generation techniques with conditional
013            diffusion models. However, the unavoidable discreteness of a molecule makes it
014            difficult for a diffusion model to connect raw data with highly complex condi-
015            tions like natural language. To address this, we present a novel latent diffusion
016            model dubbed LDMol for text-conditioned molecule generation. LDMol com-
017            prises a molecule autoencoder that produces a learnable and structurally infor-
018
               mative feature space, and a natural language-conditioned latent diffusion model.
               In particular, recognizing that multiple SMILES notations can represent the same
019
               molecule, we employ a contrastive learning strategy to extract feature space that is
020
               aware of the unique characteristics of the molecule structure. LDMol outperforms
021            the existing baselines on the text-to-molecule generation benchmark, suggestion a
022            potential that diffusion models can outperform autoregressive models in text data
023            generation with a better choice of the latent domain. Furthermore, we show that
024            LDMol can be applied to downstream tasks such as molecule-to-text retrieval and
025            text-guided molecule editing, demonstrating its versatility as a diffusion model.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Designing compounds with the desired characteristics is the essence of solving many chemical tasks.
031   Inspired by the rapid development of generative models in the last decades, de novo molecule gener-
032   ation via deep learning models has been extensively studied. Diverse models have been proposed for
033
      generating molecules that agree with a given condition on various data modalities, including string
      representations (Segler et al., 2017), molecular graphs (Lim et al., 2020), and point clouds (Hooge-
034
      boom et al., 2022). The attributes controlled by these models evolved from simple chemical proper-
035
      ties (Olivecrona et al., 2017; Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018) to complex biological activity (Staszak
036   et al., 2022) and multi-objective conditioning (Li et al., 2018; Chang & Ye, 2024). More recently,
037   as deep learning models’ natural language comprehension ability has rapidly increased, there’s a
038   growing interest in molecule generation controlled by natural language (Edwards et al., 2022; Pei
039   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a; Su et al., 2022) which encompasses much broader and user-friendly
040   controllable conditions.
041
      Meanwhile, diffusion models (Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020) have emerged as a frontline
042   of generative models over the past few years. Through a simple and stable training objective of
043   predicting noise from noisy data (Ho et al., 2020), diffusion models have achieved highly realistic
044   and controllable data generation (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Karras et al., 2022; Ho & Salimans,
045   2021). Furthermore, leveraging that the score function of the data distribution is learned in their
046   training (Song et al., 2021b), state-of-the-art image diffusion models enabled various applications
047   on the image domain (Saharia et al., 2022; Kim & Ye, 2021; Chung et al., 2023). Inspired by the
048   success of diffusion models, several papers suggested diffusion-based molecule generative models
049   on various molecule domains including a molecular graph (Luo et al., 2023), strings like Simplified
050
      Molecular-Input Line-Entry System (SMILES) (Gong et al., 2024), and point clouds (Hoogeboom
      et al., 2022).
051
052   However, a discrepancy between molecule data and common data domains like images makes it
053   hard to connect the diffusion models to molecule generation. Whereas diffusion models are deeply
      studied on a continuous data domain with Gaussian noise, any molecule modality has inevitable

139   Diffusion models can perform their diffusion process on the latent data space instead of the raw
140   data when the raw data is too complex or high-dimensional (Vahdat et al., 2021; Rombach et al.,

146   Conditional molecule generation. As a promising tool for many important chemical and engineer-
147
      ing tasks like de novo drug discovery and material design, conditional molecule generation has been
      extensively studied with various models including recurrent neural network (RNN)s (Segler et al.,

149

152   reached a text-guided molecule generation (Edwards et al., 2022; Su et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024a)
153   leveraging a deep comprehension ability for natural language, especially with recent emergence of
154   large language model (LLM)s (Liu et al., 2024b).
155
      Recent works attempted to import the success of the diffusion model into molecule generation.
156   Several graph-based and point cloud-based works have built conditional diffusion models that could
157   generate molecules with simple chemical and biological conditions (Hoogeboom et al., 2022; Luo
158   et al., 2023; Trippe et al., 2023). Gong et al. (2024) attempted a text-conditioned molecule diffusion
159   model trained on the sequence of tokenized SMILES indices. However, these models treated discrete
160   molecules with continuous Gaussian diffusion, introducing arbitrary numeric values and suboptimal
161   performances. Xu et al. (2023) employed an autoencoder to build a diffusion model on a smooth
      latent space, but its controllable conditions were still limited to several physiochemical properties.

      3.1   C HEMICALLY INFORMATIVE SMILES AUTOENCODER
193
194   The primary goal of introducing autoencoders for image latent diffusion models is to map raw im-
195   ages into a low-dimensional space which reduces the computation cost (Vahdat et al., 2021; Rom-
196   bach et al., 2022). This is plausible because a high-resolution image has an enormous dimension on
197   a pixel domain, yet each pixel contains little information.
198   In this work for molecule generation, we utilized SMILES, one of the most widely used molecule
199   representations in chemical databases and benchmarks, and built a SMILES encoder to map raw
200   SMILES strings into a latent vector. In this case, the role of our SMILES autoencoder has to be
201   different from that of the autoencoders for images; a molecule structure can be fully expressed by
202   only a sequence of L integers for SMILES tokens, where L is the maximum token length. However,
203   each token carries significant information, and hidden interactions between these tokens are much
204   more complicated than interactions between image pixels. Therefore, the SMILES encoder should
205   focus more on extracting chemical meaning into the latent space, even if it results in a latent space
206
      with more dimension than the raw SMILES string.
207   A number of molecule encoders (Wang et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2024a; Zeng et al., 2022; Liu et al.,
208   2023a) have been presented that can extract various useful chemical features including biochemical
209   activity or human-annotated descriptions. Nonetheless, these molecule encoders aim to extract cer-
210   tain desired features rather than encode all the information about the molecule structure. Therefore
211   the input cannot be fully restored from the model output.
212   Although autoencoders with appropriate regularization (e.g., KL-divergence loss (Kingma, 2013;
213   Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018)) provide a continuous and reconstructible molecular latent space,
214   their encoder output is not guaranteed to possess the characteristic of the underlying molecular struc-
215   ture, beyond the minimal information to reconstruct the input string. To visualize this, we prepared a
      trained β-VAE (Higgins et al., 2017) and measured the feature distance between two SMILES from

      Accordingly, here we propose three conditions that our SMILES autoencoder’s latent space has to
      satisfy: enable reconstruction of the input, have as small dimensions as possible, and embed chemical
250
      information that can be readily learned by diffusion models.
251
252   Encoder design. In this respect, we trained our SMILES encoder with contrastive learning (Fig-
253   ure 2-(a)), which aims to learn better representation by assimilating features containing similar in-
254   formation (i.e. positive pair) and distancing semantically unrelated features (i.e. negative pair). We
255
      defined two enumerated SMILES from the same molecule as a positive pair and two SMILES from
      different molecules as a negative pair.
256
257   Here, we argue that the proposed contrastive learning with SMILES enumeration can train the en-
258   coder to encapsulate the structural information of the input molecule. Contrastive learning learns
259   invariant for the augmentations applied on positive pairs (Zhang & Ma, 2022), and it is known that
260   a good augmentation should reduce as much mutual information between positive pairs as possi-
261
      ble while preserving relevant information (Tian et al., 2020). Meanwhile, enumerated SMILES of
      the same molecule are obtained by traversing the nodes and edges in the molecular graph with a
262
      different visiting order. Therefore, to detect all possible enumerated SMILES and find SMILES-
263
      enumeration-invariant, the model has to understand the entire connectivity between atoms. This
264   makes the encoder output a unique characteristic that captures the overall molecule structure. Com-
265   pared to the hand-crafted augmentations previously presented for molecule contrastive learning (You
266   et al., 2020), enumerated SMILES pairs have less mutual information since we utilize all possible
267   variations in the SMILES format. And since all enumerated SMILES are guaranteed to represent an
268   identical molecule, there is no relevant information loss during the augmentation. Figure 3-(a) con-
269   firms that our LDMol trained with the contrastive learning with SMILES enumeration now correctly
      assimilates features from the same molecule compared to the one between random SMILES pairs.

      3.2   T EXT- CONDITIONED LATENT DIFFUSION MODEL
312
313   As shown in Figure 2-(c), our diffusion model learns the conditional distribution of the SMILES la-
314   tent z whose dimension is [L×dz ]. In the training phase, a molecule in the training data is mapped to
315   the latent z and applied a forward noising process into zt with randomly sampled diffusion timestep
316   t and injected noise ϵ. A diffusion model predicts the injected noise from zt , conditioned by the
317   paired text description via a frozen external text encoder. In the inference phase, the diffusion model
318   iteratively generates a new latent sample z ′ from a given text condition, which is then decoded to a
319   molecule via the SMILES decoder.
320   Since most contributions to diffusion models were made in the image domain, most off-the-shelf
321   diffusion models have the architecture of convolution-based Unet (Ronneberger et al., 2015). How-
322   ever, introducing the spatial inductive bias of Unet cannot be justified for the latent space of our
323   encoder. Therefore we employed DiT (Peebles & Xie, 2023) architecture, one of the most success-
      ful approaches to transformer-based diffusion models for more general data domain. Specifically, we


                                                             6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      utilized a DiTbase model with minimal modifications to handle text conditions with cross-attention,
325   where more details can be found in Section A.1.
326
327   Text-based molecule generation requires a text encoder to process natural language conditions. Ex-
328
      isting text-based molecule generation models trained their text encoder from scratch (Pei et al.,
      2023), or utilize a separate encoder model pre-trained on scientific domain corpora (Beltagy et al.,
329
      2019). In this work, we took the encoder part of MolT5large (Edwards et al., 2022) as our text
330
      encoder.

333   3.3   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
334
335   The pre-training of the SMILES encoder and the corresponding decoder was done with 10,000,000
336   general molecules from PubChem (Kim et al., 2023). To ensure enough batch size for negative
337
      samples (He et al., 2020), we build a memory queue that stores Q recent input and use them for the
      encoder training. The SMILES tokenizer vocabulary consists of 300 tokens, which were obtained
338
      from the pre-training data SMILES corpus using the BPE algorithm (Gage, 1994). We only used
339
      SMILES data that does not exceed a fixed maximum token length L.
340
341   To train the text-conditioned latent diffusion model, we gathered three existing datasets of text-
342   molecule pair: PubchemSTM curated by Liu et al. (2023a), ChEBI-20 (Edwards et al., 2021), and
343
      PCdes (Zeng et al., 2022). Only a train split for each dataset was used for the training, and pairs that
      appear in the test set for the experiments are additionally removed. We also used 10,000 molecules
344
      from ZINC15 (Sterling & Irwin, 2015) without any text descriptions, which helps the model learn
345
      the common distribution of molecules. When these unlabeled data were fed into the training model,
346   we used a pre-defined null text which represents the absence of text condition.
347
348   The latent diffusion model was trained with the training loss suggested by Dhariwal & Nichol
349
      (2021). To take advantage of classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2021), we randomly replaced
      3% of the given text condition with the null text during the training. The sampling iteration in the
350
      inference stage used DDIM-based (Song et al., 2021a) 100 sampling steps with a classifier-free
351
      guidance. More detailed training hyperparameters can be found in Appendix A.2.

357

      In this section, we evaluated the trained LDMol’s ability to generate molecules that agree with the
359
      given natural language conditions. First, we generated molecules with LDMol using the text cap-
360   tions in the ChEBI-20 test set and compared them with the ground truth. The metrics we’ve used are
361   as follows: SMILES validity, BLEU score (Papineni et al., 2002) and Levenshtein distance between
362   two SMILES, Tanimoto similarity (Bajusz et al., 2015) between two SMILES with three differ-
363   ent fingerprints (MACCS, RDK, Morgan), the exact match ratio, and Frechet ChemNet Distance
364   (FCD) (Preuer et al., 2018).
365

366   distribution, the output from deterministic DDIM sampling still has stochasticity. However, with a
367   highly detailed text prompt that can specify a single correct answer, we discovered that LDMol was
368   powerful enough to outperform many autoregressive models with randomly sampled initial points.
369   Also, the performance difference was marginal between starting from random noise and starting
370   from a fixed point (e.g., zero vector). We tested different scales for the classifier-free guidance scale
371   ω in the sampling process and found ω = 2.5 works best (See Section B.1).

504   4.3   A BLATION STUDIES
505
      We’ve conducted ablation studies for each design choice of the proposed LDMol to analyze and
506
      emphasize their role. Each model is pre-trained with the same number of DiT training iterations.
507   The results of the ablation studies are listed in Table 3.
508
509   When we remove the contrastive encoder pre-training objective and construct the molecule latent
510
      space with a naive autoencoder, the diffusion model completely fails to learn the latent distribution
      to generate valid SMILES. A similar problem occurred when we didn’t introduce a compression
511
      layer since the dimension of the latent space was too big for the diffusion model to learn. When
512
      stereoisomers were not utilized as hard negative samples in the contrastive encoder training, the
513   constructed latent space was not detailed enough to specify the input, which degraded the recon-
514   struction accuracy of the autoencoder.
515
516
      An interesting case is the comparison against the autoencoder with KL-divergence loss: it has recon-
      structible latent space and showed a text-to-molecule generation match ratio of 0.492, which outper-
517
      forms several baseline models in Table 1. This performance shows the ability of diffusion models
518
      on the continuous data domain which can be comparable to the autoregressive models. However, its
519   overall metric is still lower than the proposed LDMol, with especially low validity and FCD. We
520   insist that this gap comes from the structurally informative latent space of LDMol which is easier
521   for the diffusion model to learn the correlation between the latent space and the condition.

523
524   5     C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this work, we presented a text-conditioned molecule diffusion model LDMol that runs on a chem-
527   ical latent space reflecting structural information. By introducing the deeply studied paradigm of the
528   latent diffusion model into the molecule domain with minimal modifications, LDMol retains many
529   advanced attributes of diffusion models that enable various applications.
530   Despite the noticeable performances of LDMol, it still has limitations that can be improved: Since
531   the text captions of our obtained paired data mostly describe structural features, LDMol still strug-
532   gles to follow some text conditions such as complex biological properties. Nonetheless, we expect
533   that the LDMol’s performance could be improved further with the emergence of richer text-molecule
534   pair data and more powerful text encoders. Moreover, combining physiochemical and biological an-
535   notations on top of the structurally informative latent space is a promising future work that can ease
536
      the connection between molecules and text conditions.
537   We believe that our approach could inspire tackling various chemical generation tasks using latent
538   space, not only text-conditioned but also many more desired properties such as biochemical activity.
539   Especially, we expect LDMol to be a starting point to bridge achievements in the state-of-the-art
      diffusion model into the chemical domain.

      A.1   D I T BLOCK FOR SMILES LATENT DIFFUSION MODEL WITH TEXT CONDITIONS
813
814   The DiT block architecture of the class-conditioned image diffusion model published by Peebles
815   & Xie (2023) is shown in Figure 6-(a). The noised input image latent zt is passed through a patch
816   embedding layer and spatially flattened to be fed into the DiT block. The condition embedding y
817   and diffusion timestep embedding t are incorporated into the model prediction via adaptive layer
818   norm. The dimension of t and y are both [B × F ], where B is the batch size and F is the number of
819
      features.
820   In the case of LDMol, the input latent zt with dimension [B × L × F ] is already spatially one-
821   dimensional, we simply pass it through a linear layer to prepare DiT block input. Also, the text
822   condition feature we’ve used has a much higher dimension of [B × L′ × F ] where L′ is the token
823   length of the text condition. Therefore, we stacked a cross-attention layer for text condition features
824   after each self-attention layer, as shown in Figure 6-(b).

856
857   Detailed hyperparameters on the model architecture are listed in Table 4, with settings on the model
858   training procedure. Here, we provide the results of several ablation studies to support our selection
859   of the used hyperparameters.
860   It is known that lower temperature τ in contrastive learning penalizes hard negatives more strongly,
861   making the learned feature more sensitive to fine-grain details (Wang & Liu, 2021). We considered
862   this as a desirable property for our latent space and used a small tau of 0.07. When we used too big
863   τ of 0.15 as shown in Figure 7, it reduced the model’s ability to distinguish different molecules and
      made the training loss converge to a much higher value.

      Figure 8-(a) and Algorithm 1 show the LDMol’s molecule-to-text retrieval process with a given
946   query molecule and text candidates C = {ci }B   i=1 . A given query molecule is converted to a latent z,
947   and then a forward noise process is applied with a randomly sampled timestep t and noise ϵ. This
948   zt is fed to LDMol with each candidate ci , and the candidate that minimizes the loss ||ϵ̂θ (zt , t, ci ) −
949   ϵ||22 between ϵ and the output noise ϵ̂θ (zt , t, ci ) is retrieved. To minimize the variance from the
950   stochasticity of t and ϵ, the same process can be repeated n times with resampled t and ϵ to use a
951   mean loss.
952   Figure 8-(b) and Algorithm 2 illustrate the DDS-based molecule editing with LDMol. Specifically,
953   DDS requires source data zsrc , target data ztgt which is initialized to zsrc , and their corresponding
954   source and target text descriptions {csrc , ctgt }. We apply the forward noise process to zsrc and ztgt
955   using the same randomly sampled t and ϵ to get zt,src and zt,tgt . These are fed into the pre-trained
956   LDMol with their corresponding text, where we denote the output noise as ϵ̂θ,src and ϵ̂θ,tgt . Finally,
957   ztgt is modified towards the target text by optimizing it to the direction of (ϵ̂θ,tgt − ϵ̂θ,src ) with
958   a learning rate γ. Here, ϵ̂θ,tgt and ϵ̂θ,tgt can be replaced with the classifier-free-guidanced noises,
959   utilizing the output with the null text and the guidance scale ω. ztgt is decoded back as the editing
960
      output after the optimization step is iterated N times. In Figure 5-(a), where we applied the same
      scenario to a batch of molecules, we used null text as csrc since it’s impractical to prepare a source
961
      prompt for each molecule. The hyperparameters {N, γ, ω} are fixed for each scenario, where every
962
      choice is in the range of 100 ≤ N ≤ 200, 0.1 ≤ γ ≤ 0.3 and 2.0 ≤ ω ≤ 4.5. For the illustrated
963   examples in Figure 5-(b), we repeated DDS iterations with N = 150, γ = 0.1 and ω = 2.5.
