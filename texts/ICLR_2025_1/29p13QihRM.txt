009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            A world model is essential for an agent to predict the future and plan in domains
012            such as autonomous driving and robotics. To achieve this, recent advancements
013            have focused on video generation, which has gained significant attention due to the
014            impressive success of diffusion models. However, these models require substantial
015            computational resources. To address these challenges, we propose a world model
016            leveraging object-centric representation space using slot attention, guided by lan-
017            guage instructions. Our model perceives the current state as an object-centric rep-
018            resentation and predicts future states in this representation space conditioned on
019
               natural language instructions. This approach results in a more compact and com-
               putationally efficient model compared to diffusion-based generative alternatives.
020
               Furthermore, it flexibly predicts future states based on language instructions, and
021
               offers a significant advantage in manipulation tasks where object recognition is
022            crucial. In this paper, we demonstrate that our latent predictive world model sur-
023            passes generative world models in visuo-linguo-motor control tasks, achieving su-
024            perior sample and computation efficiency. We also investigate the generalization
025            performance of the proposed method and explore various strategies for predicting
026            actions using object-centric representations.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   A world model, or world simulator, enables an agent to perceive the current environment and predict
032   future environmental states. By providing the agent with future environment states corresponding
033   to the actions it can take, it helps planning and policy learning in control tasks, e.g., autonomous
034   driving (Hu et al., 2022; Pan et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023),
035   gaming (Schrittwieser et al., 2020; Hafner et al., 2019; 2020; 2023), and robotics (Black et al., 2023;
036   Du et al., 2024a;b; Yang et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024).
037   With the remarkable success of diffusion models, there has been a growing interest in employing
038   video-generation-based world models, particularly those that are conditioned on the current frame
039   and language instructions, to perform planning and control tasks (Du et al., 2024a;b; Yang et al.,
040   2024).
041
      However, the major drawback of language-guided video-generation models is the requirement of
042   large-scale labeled language-video datasets and the corresponding high computational cost (Gu
043   et al., 2024). Therefore, latent predictive models, which abstract video to predict forward in compact
044   latent state spaces, can serve as an alternative from a computational efficiency perspective (Hafner
045   et al., 2019). The key points of these models are how to abstract the video and how to predict the
046   future state, and various studies have explored these strategies (Schrittwieser et al., 2020; Hafner
047   et al., 2019; 2020; 2023; Seo et al., 2023).
048   Meanwhile, object-centric representation derived from Locatello et al. (2020) has recently garnered
049   significant attention as a method for encoding images or videos in several studies (Kipf et al., 2021;
050   Elsayed et al., 2022; Seitzer et al., 2023; Bao et al., 2023; Singh et al., 2022a;b; Aydemir et al.,
051   2024; Zadaianchuk et al., 2023). In this approach, the representation is primarily learned through an
052   auto-encoding structure that reconstructs frames or pretrained feature of the frames, and it has been
053   reported that the learned representation not only aids in reconstruction but also benefits control tasks
      (Heravi et al., 2023; Yoon et al., 2023; Driess et al., 2023).

074   Inspired by this, we aim to fully exploit the advantages of object-centric representation in world
075   modeling and control in this work. We propose an object-centric world model that uses a slot-
076   attention-based encoder to obtain object-centric representations from observations and then predicts
077   the representations of future states conditioned on given language instructions. Through this ap-
078   proach, we can represent observations compactly while enabling expressive and flexible predictions
079   guided by language instructions. Additionally, providing imagination in an object-centric form is
080   beneficial for control tasks where object recognition is essential. We evaluate this approach on
081   visuo-linguo-motor control tasks to highlight the sample and computational efficiency compared to
082
      the approach with a state-of-the-art diffusion-based generative model.
083   In summary, the contributions of our work are as follows:
084
085          • To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to propose object-centric world models guided
086            by language instruction.
087          • We show our approach surpasses the alternative using state-of-the-art diffusion-based gen-
088            erative model in both visuo-linguo-motor control task success rate and computational
089            speed, highlighting our method’s sample and computation efficiency.
090          • We explore the generalization performance of the object-centric world model guided by
091            language instruction on unseen task settings.
092
             • We explore various methods for predicting actions using slots, a topic addressed in only a
093            few studies.

133   2.2   W ORLD M ODEL
134
135
      A world model predicts future states based on current environments. Recent advancements in diffu-
      sion models have led to active research on video generation-based world models (Du et al., 2024a;b;
136
      Yang et al., 2024), but these methods demand substantial data and lengthy training and inference
137
      times. Some studies address these challenges by learning dynamics within the latent space, either
138   by extracting full-image representations (Hafner et al., 2019; 2020; 2023; Babaeizadeh et al., 2017;
139   Franceschi et al., 2020) or by using masked inputs (Seo et al., 2023). Others propose object-centric
140   approaches, focusing on state representations (Wu et al., 2022; Collu et al., 2024) or combining
141   states and actions (Ferraro et al., 2023; Feng & Magliacane, 2023).
142
      We introduce the first object-centric world model guided by natural language. Our model is more
143
      computationally efficient than diffusion-based approaches and outperforms non-object-centric meth-
144   ods in action prediction tasks. Unlike prior studies that train goal-conditioned policies using object-
145   centric representations requiring goal images at test time (Zadaianchuk et al., 2020; Haramati et al.,
146   2024), our approach predicts future states based on instructions and uses these predictions to train
147   an action decoder, enabling manipulation tasks without goal images.
148
149   2.3   V ISUO - LINGUO - MOTOR C ONTROL TASKS
150
151   This task is designed to evaluate how effectively an agent can control given situations based on visual
152   perception and linguistic understanding (Shridhar et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024).
153   Specific tasks are provided in the form of instructions, along with visually recognizable environ-
154
      ments. We test our world model’s visuo-linguo-motor control capabilities using the language-table
      dataset (Lynch et al., 2023). It consists of a simulation environment with a single robot arm and four
155
      blocks, where visual data is provided in the form of videos, and linguistic information is given in
156
      instructions such as ‘put the blue block next to the pentagon.’

      4.2   BASELINES
328
329   To compare our approach with diffusion-based generative world models, we employ Seer (Gu et al.,
330   2024), one of the state-of-the-art language-guided video generation models based on a latent dif-
331   fusion model, and Susie (Black et al., 2024), which generates a future state image conditioned on
332   language instruction and current state image using fine-tuned InstructPix2Pix (Brooks et al., 2023)
333   as the world model.
334   We train Seer using two approaches: training it from scratch on the language-table dataset (denoted
335   as Seer-S) and fine-tuning it on the language-table dataset from a checkpoint pre-trained on the
336   Something-Something V2 dataset (Goyal et al., 2017) (denoted as Seer-F). Additionally, we utilize
337   the latent features from baselines in two different ways. The first method involves inputting the
338   latent z0 , obtained before reconstruction into the video space through the VAE decoder in the latent
339   diffusion model, into a CNN-based action decoder to output actions (denoted as +VAE). The second
340   method involves extracting slots from the reconstructed video using the same SAVi model employed
341
      in our approach, and then inputting these slots into an action decoder with the same architecture as
      our approach to output actions (denoted as +SAVi). This approach allows us to better isolate the role
342
      of the world model in the control task, as both ours and the baseline use the same SAVi encoder and
343
      action decoder. Similar to Seer, Susie utilizes a VAE and SAVi model as encoders to decode actions
344   from latent features. In Susie, we employ an action decoder with the same structure as Seer, except
345   for the input feature and the output action dimension. It only uses features of two images as input
346   and predicts an action trajectory between the current and subgoal images, rather than a single action.
347
348   4.3   E VALUATION S ETUP
349
350   We compare the success rates of each approach by randomly sampling 200 episodes in the language-
351   table environment. Each episode is set up similarly to those used during the training of the language-
352   guided world model and action decoder, involving four blocks and an episode to move one block to
353   another block. For each sampled episode, the positions of the blocks, the block to be moved, and
354
      the target block are randomly determined. In the environment, an episode is deemed successful if
      the block to be moved comes within 0.05 unit distances of the target block. We evaluate this success
355
      criterion not only at the 0.05 threshold but also at additional unit distances (i.e., 0.075 and 0.1). As
356
      the distances increase, it becomes easier to complete the episode. We consider an episode successful
357   if it is completed within 200 steps. The DDIM sampler for the baselines is set to 10 steps, a value
358   determined through trial and error to achieve high-quality generation.

361
362   Control task First, we compare the control task performance of our approach with that of the
363   baselines. We report the task success rate of 200 randomly sampled episodes from the language-
364   table environment, using the same setup as the training data.
365

366   target distances (success criteria), as shown in Table 1. Whether trained from scratch using the same
367   amount of video data as our method or fine-tuned from a pre-trained checkpoint, Seer exhibits a
368   consistently lower success rate compared to our approach, underscoring the superior sample effi-
369   ciency of our method. This outcome likely stems from the world model component playing a more
370   critical role than the action decoder. As illustrated in Figure 4, Seer consistently fails to accurately
371   predict the arm’s movements and the resulting future states of the blocks necessary to complete the
372   episodes. This is further evidenced by the fact that even when using the frozen SAVi encoder from
373   our approach to predict actions based on the future videos generated by the baseline, the success rate
374
      remains lower than that of our method.

      4.6   A BLATIONS ON ACTION DECODING
433
434   Is a world model really necessary? We experiment to answer the following question: is a world
435   model really necessary for predicting future actions? Some studies (Lynch et al., 2023; Heravi et al.,
436   2023) only use the current scene and the task information to predict actions to solve the task. We
437   compare the performance of a model that only uses the current state slots and instruction with models
438   that use 1, 5, 10 or 20 future state slots without instruction in Table 4. We show that the model with
439   no access to future state slots almost entirely fails to complete the tasks, while the performance
440
      significantly improves as soon as it is given access to even a single future state slot. To investigate
      whether the issue could simply be a lack of sufficient state information, we also evaluate a model that
441
      is provided with the current and past 5 steps of state information. This model also mostly fails (with
442
      a success rate ranging from 0.5% to 1.0%), indicating that successful visuo-linguo-motor control
443   tasks require the model to access future state predictions through a world model.
444
445   How far should the model look? We experiment to find the appropriate number of next steps that
446   the world model should provide to the action decoder for accurate action prediction. As demon-
447   strated in Table 4, This shows that at least one next step must be provided for the action decoder to
448   predict actions. Additionally, the best performance is achieved when 10 steps are given, and provid-
449   ing more future slots does not further improve the accuracy of action prediction. This reveals that
450   information about the distant future is not helpful when predicting actions for the near future, unless
451   modeled with specific methodologies like goal conditioning for long-term decision making.
452
453   Does past information help the model? We have previously demonstrated that providing infor-
454   mation from 10 future frames yields the best control performance. This leads us to the next question:
455
      Would adding past frame information to this setup further enhance performance? We compare the
      performance of a model that uses current state slots and the 10 future state slots with models that
456
      use the 5 past state slots, current state slots, and the 10 future state slots. The former shows a suc-
457
      cess rate ranging from 50.0% to 73.0%, while the latter demonstrates a success rate between 45.5%
458   and 66.5%. The model’s ability to predict or control based on future frames appears to be more
459   influential, suggesting that access to upcoming states plays a more crucial role in achieving better
460   performance than integrating past state information.
461
462   Does adding instructions into the action decoding help? In our approach, we fuse language
463   instructions into the slots within the world model to obtain future state slots, which are then used
464   to predict actions. This raises the following question: Would incorporating the instruction directly
465   into the action prediction process also be beneficial? To answer this question, we modify our ap-
466   proach by concatenating the language instruction, passed through a T5 encoder as a single token,
467   with the slots input to the action decoder. This combined input is then used to decode the action. As
468
      shown in Table 3, adding the instruction to the action decoding process lowers performance. This
      suggests that the predicted slots already contain the necessary instruction information, and reintro-
469
      ducing the instruction during action decoding may interfere with the process. We present additional
470
      experiments about fusing instruction with slots to decode actions in the Appendix.
471
472
      How to decode actions using slots? Most research related to slots has primarily focused on object
473
      segmentation. While it is known that using slots can be beneficial for control tasks, little is known
474   about how to use slots specifically for action prediction. Yoon et al. (2023) discusses the design
475   choices for action decoders when using slots in reinforcement learning tasks, showing that trans-
476   formers outperform simple MLP pooling layers. They attribute this to the permutation invariance of
477   slots and the superior performance of transformers.
478
      We compare the choice of MLP versus transformer encoder for action decoding. MLP directly
479
      predicts actions with flattened inputs through all timesteps, using ResNet MLP proposed in Lynch
480   et al. (2023). We compare two options using a transformer encoder, grouping by timestep (ours)
481   and grouping by slot. Grouping by timestep treats each slot in the same timestep as a single token
482   and inputs them into a transformer encoder. The outputs from all timesteps are then concatenated
483   chronologically and passed through an MLP layer to predict the action. Grouping by slot treats an
484   individual slot across all timesteps as a single input and passes it through a transformer layer. The
485   reason for using a transformer instead of an MLP for pooling is that when grouping by slots and
      processing each slot separately, we expect the output to also exhibit permutation invariance.

486
      Generally, we find that the transformer encoder is better in success rate than MLP. This indicates
487   that handling slots across multiple timesteps requires a more sophisticated structure than a simple
488   MLP. For two transformer encoder options, grouping by timestep (ours) results in a 6.5%p to 9%p
489   higher success rate, depending on the success criteria. This reveals that when using multiple slots
490   from multiple timesteps to predict actions, grouping by slot is able to capture useful information
491   from slots for actions.

504   5     C ONCLUSION
505
506   In this work, we propose a language-guided world model in object-centric representation space
507   and leverage its predictive capabilities to visuo-linguo-motor control tasks. Our method exceeds
508   using state-of-the-art diffusion-based generative model as a world model on both performance and
509   efficiency for future state prediction and control tasks. Furthermore, we conduct evaluations on
510   unseen blocks and task settings presenting the generalization performance of our method. Our work
511
      shows the potential of language-guided object-centric world models to benefit robotics perception
      and control.
512
513
      Limitations One of our work’s limitations is that it only covers data domains from simulated
514
      environments. It can be extended to complex real-world videos using a pre-trained vision model like
515
      ViT as the primary encoder for slot extraction like Seitzer et al. (2023). Moreover, improved data
516   diversity and techniques (Fan et al., 2024) can be deployed to overcome limitations of generalization
517   performance and the nature of slot attention not being robust to variable object numbers. Also, the
518   deterministic nature of SAVi with learnable slot initialization and SlotFormer remains, leaving future
519   works for modeling stochasticity in the world model.

763   and background. An image from a single time step is encoded using CNN layers as Table 5. Then
764   slot attention iterates two times with the slots as a query and the image features as key and value.

767
      temporally with a clipped trajectory with a length of 6. While SAVi can be conditioned with prior

825   The SAVi encoder and the corresponding action decoder are the same as those used in our model.
826   The structure of the action decoder that processes the VAE features is as follows. First, CNN is
827   constructed as a sequential neural network that starts with a 2D convolutional layer with 4 input
828
      channels and 32 output channels, using a kernel size of 3, a stride of 1, and padding of 1. This is
      followed by a ReLU activation function to introduce non-linearity. Next, a 2D max-pooling layer
829
      with a kernel size of 2 and a stride of 2 is applied for down-sampling. The sequence continues
830
      with a second 2D convolutional layer, which takes 32 input channels and outputs 64 channels, again
831   using a kernel size of 3, a stride of 1, and padding of 1. Another ReLU activation function is
832   applied, followed by a second 2D max-pooling layer with the same kernel size and stride as before.
833   Afterward, the output of the CNN is flattened and passed through an MLP that maps it to a 128-
834   dimensional vector. This is followed by a ReLU activation layer, and finally, another MLP is applied
835   to predict the action.

855   Masking slots in action decoder We hypothesize that merging these separated object features
856   may negatively impact performance. To ensure that the separated object information in each slot
857   is preserved during action decoding, we conduct additional experiments by applying a mask when
858   inputting the slots into the transformer. With the mask, each slot can only attend to itself, while
859   the action token attends to all slots. The experimental results, presented in Table 8, show that using
860   the mask generally leads to performance degradation across almost all scenarios. This suggests that
861   allowing the slots to mix during action prediction results in better performance.
