009                                             A BSTRACT
010
011
              The natural alignment of visual and audio information in videos provides a strong
012           learning signal. However, commonly used large-scale video datasets contain
013           audio-visual signals that are not aligned, e.g. background music. This limits the
014           development of robust models that leverage the complementary nature of audio
015           and video data. To address this limitation, we curate ACAV-1M , a new large-scale
016           dataset that contains one million samples sourced from the ACAV-100M dataset.
017           The ACAV-1M dataset is obtained through a pipeline that ensures the audio-visual
018
              correspondence and synchronization of samples in the dataset. Our pipeline trans-
              forms raw video and audio into text captions, followed by text summarization
019
              and an extensive filtering procedure. The filtering is done based on audio-caption
020           alignment, audio-visual instance semantic alignment, and temporal synchroniza-
021           tion. Furthermore, we propose an audio-visual learning benchmark that supports a
022           diverse range of downstream tasks. Empirical evaluations demonstrate that mod-
023           els trained on ACAV-1M achieve superior performance compared to using existing
024           datasets across all tasks. Our ACAV-1M dataset and code to reproduce all bench-
025           mark results will be made publicly available upon acceptance.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Recent advancements in multimodal learning, exemplified by the Flan Collection (Longpre et al.,
031
      2023) and MMC4 (Zhu et al., 2023), have showcased significant strides for multimodal vision-
032   language models. These developments underline the potential of integrated multimodal datasets
033   for enhancing model performance. However, the field lacks a high-quality, large-scale collection
034   specifically tailored for audio-visual learning, where audio and visual data complement each other
035   to achieve a more holistic understanding of the environment.
036
      The importance of a unified audio-visual dataset stems from the need for a systematic approach
037   to evaluatethe interaction between audio and visual inputs, which are often treated independently,
038   as shown in Table 1. The integration of these modalities promises to improve the robustness and
039   accuracy of learning models by leveraging their inherent complementary properties (Aytar et al.,
040   2016; Owens et al., 2016; Arandjelovic & Zisserman, 2017; Korbar et al., 2018; Senocak et al.,
041   2018). The absence of such datasets might hamper the development of audio-video models that can
042   effectively exploit the synergies between sight and sound, thus limiting advancements in this area.
043   To address this, we introduce a new dataset, namely ACAV-1M , which consists of one million audio-
044   visual samples. Our ACAV-1M follows a curation pipeline that consists of several steps. First, a
045   multimodal Large Language Model (LLM) (Lin et al., 2023) generates multiple captions from au-
046   dio and video inputs. Then, we use an LLM (OpenAI, 2023) to summarize the long captions into
047
      one sentence for the following quality measure steps. Lastly, we utilize ImageBind (Girdhar et al.,
      2023) to measure audio-language, audio-video instance, and audio-video temporal alignment for
048
      data curation. For audio-language alignment, we compute the normalized cosine similarity between
049   audio instance features and caption features extracted from ImageBind (Girdhar et al., 2023). For
050   audio-video instance alignment, we calculate the normalized cosine similarity between audio in-
051   stance features and video features extracted from ImageBind. For audio-video temporal alignment,
052   we compute the normalized cosine similarity between audio instance features and video features
053   across all ten seconds extracted from ImageBind. These final alignment quality check steps ensure
      the coherence and synchronization between modalities.

069   Our dataset and benchmark not only provide tools for measuring data quality on audio-visual in-
070   stance alignment and temporal alignment, but also support an extensive range of downstream appli-
071   cations. These include audio-visual classification, sound source localization, retrieval, video parsing,
072   scene-aware dialogue, audio-visual question-answering, segmentation, and sound source separation.
073   We provide benchmark results for each of these applications with task-specific methods, and each
074
      of these applications is backed by benchmark baselines, task-specific methods, and both pre-trained
      and novel multimodal foundation models developed using ACAV-1M .
075
076   Empirical results from extensive experiments demonstrate that models trained on ACAV-1M surpass
077   existing methods, highlighting the dataset’s effectiveness and scalability properties. This establishes
078   ACAV-1M as a significant step towards the systematic integration of audio and visual data in machine
079
      learning research, providing a robust platform for exploring new frontiers in multimodal interaction
      and representation learning.
080
081   To summarize, we make the following four contributions:
082
083
               • We curate the ACAV-1M dataset with one million audio-visual samples designed to address
                 the gap in existing multimodal datasets for audio and visual data.

089            • Extensive experimental analyses demonstrate the effectiveness and scalability of models
090              on ACAV-1M compared to existing audio-visual datasets.

      3.1   DATASETS C ONSTRUCTION AND S TATISTICS
142
143
      ACAV-1M was meticulously constructed with a dataset curation process that ensures the close align-
144   ment between audio and visual elements which is crucial for complex multimodal learning tasks.
145
146
      Data curation. The dataset curation follows a robust pipeline, starting with raw audio and video
      data, as illustrated in Figure 1. Each video clip is processed through our multimodal Large Lan-
147
      guage Model (LLM) to extract descriptive captions. Specifically, VideoLLaVA (Lin et al., 2023)
148   and WavCaps (Mei et al., 2024) generate several sentence-level descriptions from video and audio
149   inputs.These are condensed by a general LLM (OpenAI, 2023) into a single, comprehensive caption
150   that captures the essence of the audio-visual content. This method ensures that our dataset supports
151   semantic analysis and retrieval tasks effectively.
152
      Data statistics. ACAV-1M is annotated with captions for both the audio and video components,
153   facilitating cross-modal training and evaluation. Each audio segment is set to a 10-second duration
154   to standardize the dataset and simplify the processing requirements. Audio data is categorized into
155   various classes such as music, nature sounds, animal sounds, speech, machine noises, and others,
156   providing a broad spectrum of audio types for comprehensive multimodal learning.
157   Alignment filtering. We quantify the alignment across modalities in our quality measure criteria.
158   We employ ImageBind (Girdhar et al., 2023) to ensure several forms of alignment. 1) Language
159   Alignment: The alignment between text captions and both audio and visual content is assessed
160   with a normalized cosine similarity threshold of 0.5, ensuring that descriptions accurately reflect the
161   content. 2) Instance Alignment: The synchronization between audio and visual streams is verified,
      with an emphasis on maintaining a normalized cosine similarity threshold of 0.5 to ensure alignment

162
      across modalities. 3) Temporal Alignment: Audio and visual data are aligned within a temporal
163   window of 1 second per segment, with an average alignment threshold of 0.5 across the dataset.

207   3.2    AUDIO - VISUAL BENCHMARK
208
209   Audio-visual tasks. ACAV-1M supports an extensive range of audio-visual downstream tasks, each
210   designed to leverage the rich, multimodal nature of the ACAV-1M dataset.
211   1. Audio-Visual Classification. The goal is to classify the scenes or objects depicted in the audio-
212      visual clips with accuracy as evaluation. For linear probing and fine-tuning on audio-visual clas-
213      sification, we used VGGSound-Music with 49 classes and VGGSound-All with 221 categories.
214   2. Audio-Visual Source Localization. This task measures the model’s ability to localize sound
215      sources within a visual frame, assessed by the mean Intersection over Union (mIoU). We use
         Flickr-SoundNet (Senocak et al., 2018) with 4,500 pairs for training and testing the model on


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
         250 audio-visual pairs of sounding objects and extended 250 non-sounding objects introduced in
217      SLAVC (Mo & Morgado, 2022b).
218   3. Audio-Visual Retrieval. The focus is the recall of relevant audio-visual content based on query
219      descriptions. We use MSR-VTT (Xu et al., 2016) that includes 10K YouTube videos with 200K
220      description sentences, where 9K is split for training and 1K for testing.
221   4. Audio-Visual Scene-Aware Dialog. This task focuses on generating dialogues that are contextu-
222      ally relevant to given audio-visual scenes, evaluated via BLEU and METEOR scores. We use the
223
         AVSD track of the 10-th Dialog System Technology Challenges (DSTC10) (Shah et al., 2022)
         dataset.
224
      5. Audio-Visual Video Parsing. This involves parsing complex video scenes into simpler seg-
225      ments, evaluated using an F-score at a mIoU threshold of 0.5. The LLP dataset (Tian et al., 2020)
226      contains 11,849 YouTube video clips of 10-seconds long from 25 different event categories, such
227      as car, music, cheering, speech, etc. We follow the official splits (Tian et al., 2020) of validation
228      and test sets to train and test.
229   6. Audio-Visual Question-Answering. This task tests accuracy in answering questions based on
230      the content depicted in the audio-visual clips. we use the MUSIC-AVQA (Li et al., 2022) dataset
231
         that consists of 45,867 question-answer pairs and 9,288 videos.
      7. Audio-Visual Segmentation. This task focuses on the segmentation masks of visual elements,
232
         with performance measured by the F1 Score. AVSBench (Zhou et al., 2022) includes 4,932
233      videos (in total 10,852 frames) from 23 categories, including instruments, humans, animals, etc.
234      We use the official split of 3,452/740/740 videos for train/val/test.
235   8. Audio-Visual Source Separation. The objective is to measure the ability to isolate individual
236      audio sources from a mixed audio track, evaluated using metrics such as Signal to Distortion
237      Ratio (SDR), Signal to Interference Ratio (SIR), and Signal to Artifacts Ratio (SAR). We use
238      VGGSound-Music (Mo & Morgado, 2023) with 40,908 video clips from 49 music categories for
239
         training and 1201 clips for testing. VGGSound-Instruments (Hu et al., 2022) includes 32k video
         clips of 10-second length from 36 musical instrument classes, a subset of VGG-Sound (Chen
240
         et al., 2020b), and each video only has one single instrument class annotation. MUSIC (Zhao
241      et al., 2018) consists of 448 untrimmed YouTube music videos of solos and duets from 11 instru-
242      ment categories, where we use 358 solo videos for training and 90 solo videos for evaluation.
243
      Here, we explain the baselines, task-specific methods, pre-trained models, and multimodal founda-
244
      tion used in our audio-visual benchmark.
245
246   Task-specific methods. ACAV-1M is utilized to establish a variety of task-specific methods tailored
247   to each downstream task. For Audio-Visual Classification, methods are optimized for maximum ac-
248
      curacy. In Audio-Visual Source Localization, algorithms focus on improving the mean Intersection
      over Union (mIoU). For Audio-Visual Retrieval, the emphasis is on enhancing recall rates. Sim-
249
      ilarly, task-specific approaches are devised for Audio-Visual Video Parsing, Scene-Aware Dialog,
250   Question-Answering, Segmentation, and Source Separation, each aiming to excel in metrics such as
251   F-score, BLEU, METEOR, and Signal Decomposition Ratings (SDR, SIR, SAR).
252
      Pre-trained models. We evaluate several models pre-trained on ACAV-1M including audio-
253
      MAE (Huang et al., 2022b), CAV-MAE (Gong et al., 2023), MAViL (Huang et al., 2022a), and
254
      AVMAE (Georgescu et al., 2023). These models leverage masked autoencoding techniques tailored
255   for either audio alone or audio-visual data on audio-visual classification to have a comprehensive
256   understanding on these models.

295   4.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
296
      Datasets. We use audio-visual pairs from our ACAV-1M dataset for pre-training. We finetune the
297
      model on datasets specific to the downstream tasks, as described in Section 3.2.
298
299   Evaluation metrics. Following the prior work (Hu et al., 2022; Mo & Morgado, 2022a;b), we
300
      use the Precision and F1 scores defined in (Mo & Morgado, 2022b) for visual source localization.
      For source separation, following (Zhao et al., 2018), we use Signal-to-Distortion Ratio (SDR) and
301
      Signal-to-Artifact Ratio (SAR). For audio-visual segmentation, we apply mIoU and F1 scores as
302   evaluation metrics, following the previous work (Zhou et al., 2022). Linear probing and fine-tuning
303   classification evaluations are based on top-1 accuracy, which measures the class difference from the
304   ground-truth labels. For video parsing, we use F-scores to evaluate segment-level predictions for
305   audio-visual events and Type@AV & Event@AV for the overall evaluation performance.
306
      Implementation. The input images are resized to 224 × 224. The audio is represented by log
307   spectrograms extracted from 10s of audio at a sample rate of 8000Hz. We follow the prior work (Mo
308   & Morgado, 2022a) and apply STFT to generate an input tensor of size 128 × 128 (128 frequency
309   bands over 128 timesteps) using 50ms windows with a hop size of 25ms. For the audio and visual
310   encoder, we use single-modality MAEs (He et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2022b). The models were
311   trained on four A100 GPUs for 100 epochs using the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014) with a
312   learning rate of 1e − 4 and a batch size of 128.

      probing and 56.05% in fine-tuning, reflecting strong generalization capabilities.

      Audio-Visual Source Localization. To validate the effectiveness of the proposed ACAV-1M dataset
325   for sound source localization, we compare to the following prior work: 1) Attention 10k (Seno-

      visual correspondence maps; 7) EZ-VSL (Mo & Morgado, 2022a) (ECCV 2022): a recent weakly
333   supervised localization framework based on multiple-instance contrastive learning; 8) Mix-and-

335   on a graph of images and separated sound sources. 9) SLAVC (Mo & Morgado, 2022b) (NeurIPS
336   2022): a strong baseline with momentum encoders and extreme visual dropout to identify nega-

      a high accuracy in predicting the correct localization of sound sources. We also achieved 60.75%

      olds, outperforming other methods in handling diverse scenarios. Our model achieves a 65.02% F1
341   score, which reflects the balance between precision and recall, demonstrating the robustness of our
342   approach to effectively localize sound sources.

523   5     C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this work, we introduce ACAV-1M , a novel large-scale dataset with one million samples that are
526   curated to bridge the gap between audio and visual data. Furthermore we propose a comprehensive
527   audio-visual benchmark that supports a wide array of audio-visual tasks, from classification and
528   segmentation to retrieval and scene-aware dialog, each benefiting from the dataset’s rich annotations
529   and precise audio-visual alignment. We demonstrate the superior performance of models trained
530
      on ACAV-1M compared to existing methods. Our experiments also explore the scaling behavior of
      the dataset, showing significant improvements in model performance as data volume increases, thus
531
      confirming the dataset’s scalability.
532
533   Limitations and broader impact. While our dataset covers a broad range of audio and visual
534   contexts, there are still rare scenarios that are underrepresented or absent, which could affect the
535
      generalizability of the trained models to all real-world applications.
536   ACAV-1M has the potential to make a profound impact for audio-visual learning. The insights gained
537   from models trained on ACAV-1M can enhance multimedia applications, improve accessibility fea-
538   tures, and foster the development of intuitive and interactive systems. However, it is essential to be
539
      aware of ethical considerations and potential biases in training data, which could amplify disparities
      if not carefully managed.

880
881   The ACAV-1M dataset is designed to facilitate research in audio-visual representation learning. It
882
      includes synchronized audio and visual data curated from various sources to ensure a diverse and
      comprehensive collection for training and evaluating machine learning models. The dataset doc-
883
      umentation follows the datasheets for datasets framework, providing detailed information on the
884   dataset’s composition, collection process, and intended uses.
885
886
      Composition: The dataset consists of 1 million audio-visual pairs, including video clips with corre-
      sponding audio tracks from various domains such as user-generated content.
887
888   Collection Process: Data was collected using automated scripts and manual curation to ensure qual-
889   ity and relevance. Metadata includes source URLs, timestamps, and content descriptions.
890   Intended Uses: The dataset is intended for developing and benchmarking models in audio-visual
891   representation learning, including tasks like video classification, audio-visual synchronization, and
892   cross-modal retrieval.
893
      Ethical Considerations: We ensured that the dataset adheres to ethical guidelines, including the
894   exclusion of sensitive or inappropriate content and respect for copyright and privacy concerns.

1262
1263   In this section, we provide more implementation and dataset details.
1264
       Audio-visual classification. For linear probing, we follow the prior work (He et al., 2021; Huang
1265   et al., 2022b) and extract frozen audio-visual representations from our ACAV-1M pre-trained audio-
1266   visual masked autoencoder. Then we attach a linear layer as a head to the frozen features for training
1267   with the audio-visual classes. During training, we only fine-tune the linear head to evaluate the qual-
1268   ity of pre-trained features. The models are trained for 50 epochs using the Adam optimizer (Kingma
1269   & Ba, 2014) with a learning rate of 1e − 4 and a batch size of 128. For fine-tuning, we use the same
1270   optimizer and batch size settings, but all parameters are learnable.
1271   Audio-Visual Source Localization. For sound source localization, we train all baselines (Mo &
1272   Morgado, 2022a;b; Hu et al., 2022) using the same backbone (i.e., ViT-Base) for audio/visual en-
1273   coder with different proposed objectives in their original papers. The final localization map is gen-
1274   erated through bilinear interpolation of the similarity map between audio/visual features from the
1275   last self-attention layer. The models are trained for 30 epochs using the Adam optimizer (Kingma
1276
       & Ba, 2014) with a learning rate of 1e − 4 and a batch size of 128.
1277   Audio-Visual Retrieval. The retrieval task processes video frames sampled at 8 fps and utilizes
1278   combined low-level visual features from ResNet-152 (He et al., 2016) and 3D ResNet models (Tran
1279   et al., 2018), both pre-trained on respective large-scale datasets. Audio features are extracted using
1280
       VGGish (Hershey et al., 2017), pre-trained on AudioSet (Gemmeke et al., 2017). The complete
       model, integrating these features, is trained using Adam to optimize retrieval effectiveness across 40
1281
       epochs.
1282
1283   Audio-Visual Video Parsing. Following the data pre-processing in previous work (Tian et al.,
1284   2020), we sample video frames at 8 fps from the 10-second videos with 10 non-overlapping snippets
1285
       of 1 second. For low-level visual features, we concatenate 2D and 3D visual features extracted by
       ResNet-152 (He et al., 2016) pre-trained on ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) and 3D ResNet (Tran et al.,
1286
       2018) pre-trained on Kinetics-400 (Carreira & Zisserman, 2017). We utilize VGGish (Hershey et al.,
1287   2017) pre-trained on AudioSet (Gemmeke et al., 2017) to extract the audio features. The model
1288   is trained with Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) optimizer with β1 =0.9, β2 =0.999 and with an initial
1289   learning rate of 3e-4. We train the model with a batch size of 16 for 40 epochs. Note that each video
1290   includes at least 1s audio or visual event, and 7202 video clips are annotated with more than one
1291   event category. We use 10,000 video clips with only video-level event labels for training. Following
1292   the official splits (Tian et al., 2020) of validation and test sets, we develop and test the model on the
1293
       remaining 1879 videos with the segment-level annotations, i.e., the speech event for audio starts at
       1s and ends at 5s.
1294
1295   Audio-Visual Scene-Aware Dialog. In the audio-visual scene-aware dialog task, our model em-
       ploys an advanced dialog generation framework that integrates audio and visual information to


                                                          24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       produce contextually relevant conversations. The dialog system utilizes a Transformer-based ar-
1297   chitecture, which processes inputs from both modalities through separate encoders before merging
1298   them in a fusion layer. This approach allows the model to understand the context provided by both
1299   the audio and visual data streams effectively. The model is optimized using the Adam optimizer
1300   with a learning rate of 1e − 4 and a batch size of 64. Training is conducted for up to 30 epochs, with
1301   early stopping based on performance on a validation set to prevent overfitting.
1302   Audio-Visual Question-Answering. For the AVQA task, our implementation focuses on integrating
1303   spatial and temporal grounding techniques to accurately answer questions based on the video and
1304   audio content. The system employs a dual-stream encoder that separately processes visual and audio
1305   inputs. The encoded features are then combined using a co-attention mechanism that aligns audio
1306   and visual elements relevant to the question context. This integration allows the model to focus on
1307   specific segments of audio and video that are crucial for answering the given question. The model
       is trained using the Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) optimizer with an initial learning rate of 3e − 4,
1308
       reduced by a factor of 0.1 upon plateauing of validation loss. The system is trained for 40 epochs
1309
       with a batch size of 32.
1310
1311   Audio-Visual Segmentation. For segmentation, we follow the prior work (Zhou et al., 2022), and
1312
       apply an upsampling decoder on features from the last self-attention layer to generate the final
       segmentation mask. We use the binary cross entropy (BCE) loss between the prediction and ground-
1313
       truth masks for training. The models are trained for 20 epochs using the Adam optimizer (Kingma
1314   & Ba, 2014) with a learning rate of 1e − 4 and a batch size of 128.
1315
1316   Audio-Visual Source Separation. For sound source separation, we follow the previous
       method (Zhao et al., 2018; Mo & Morgado, 2023) and attach an audio U-Net decoder to our pre-
1317
       trained audio-visual encoders for separating sounds from the mixture. The decoder depth for self-
1318
       attention layers is 8, and the decoder receives the representations of the audio mixture and the visual
1319   embeddings. We also apply multiple transposed convolutions and an output head to predict a time-
1320   frequency separation mask. This separation mask is then used to multiply the input mixture STFT
1321   to separate the audio. Similarly to (Zhao et al., 2018), the target masks refer to the time-frequency
1322   bins where the source is the most dominant component in the mixture. The sound source separation
1323   is achieved by optimizing a binary cross-entropy loss over these binary targets. The model is trained
1324   for 20 epochs using the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014) with a learning rate of 1e − 4 and a
       batch size of 128.

             • Video Captions: For visual inputs, such as video frames, we use VideoLLaVA (Lin

1387
               oLLaVA processes sampled frames from the video and generates multiple sentence-level
1388           captions that describe the visual content, including objects, actions, and scene attributes.
1389           The model’s ability to capture fine-grained visual details provides a strong foundation for
1390           multimodal representation.
1391         • Audio Captions: For audio inputs, we utilize WavCaps (Mei et al., 2024), a model op-
1392           timized for generating descriptive captions from audio signals. WavCaps processes the
1393           10-second audio segments from each video, capturing key audio characteristics such as en-
1394           vironmental sounds, speech, music, or other auditory cues. This step ensures that the audio
1395           component is accurately represented, complementing the visual descriptions.
1396         • Final Aggregation: To produce a coherent and unified text description, we employ GPT-
1397           4 (OpenAI, 2023), a multimodal large language model. GPT-4 takes the outputs from
1398           VideoLLaVA and WavCaps as input and aggregates them into a single, semantically con-
1399
               sistent caption. This final description integrates visual and auditory details into a cohesive
               narrative, ensuring cross-modal alignment and reducing redundancy. Additionally, GPT-
1400
               4 is prompted to enhance the captions by resolving ambiguities and providing contextual
1401           information where necessary.
