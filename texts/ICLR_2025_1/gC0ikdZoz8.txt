009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Adaptive optimization algorithms, particularly Adam and its variant AdamW, are
012            fundamental components of modern deep learning. However, their training dynam-
013            ics lack comprehensive theoretical understanding, with limited insight into why
014            common practices—such as specific hyperparameter choices and normalization
015            layers—contribute to successful generalization. This work presents a continuous-
016            time formulation of Adam and AdamW, facilitating a tractable analysis of training
017            dynamics that can shed light on such practical questions. We theoretically derive
018            a stable region for Adam’s hyperparameters (β, γ) that ensures bounded updates,
019            empirically verifying these predictions by observing unstable exponential growth
020
               of parameter updates outside this region. Furthermore, we theoretically justify
               the success of normalization layers by uncovering an implicit meta-adaptive ef-
021
               fect of scale-invariant architectural components. This insight leads to an explicit
022
               optimizer, 2-Adam, which we generalize to k-Adam—an optimizer that applies
023            an adaptive normalization procedure k times, encompassing Adam (correspond-
024            ing to k = 1) and Adam with a normalization layer (corresponding to k = 2).
025            Overall, our continuous-time formulation of Adam facilitates a principled analysis,
026            offering deeper understanding of optimal hyperparameter choices and architectural
027            decisions in modern deep learning.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      Adaptive optimization algorithms have become an essential component of modern deep learning,
033   providing significant benefits to the training of neural networks compared to their non-adaptive coun-
034   terparts. Among these algorithms, Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2017) and its variant AdamW (Loshchilov
035   & Hutter, 2019) have become widely used in practice, featuring both an adaptive learning rate (in the
036   sense of RMSprop (Tieleman & Hinton, 2012)) and an adaptive gradient direction (in the sense of
037   momentum (Polyak, 1964)). Despite their wide success, a theoretical understanding of their training
038   dynamics is lacking. Previous theoretical work on adaptive optimization mostly focuses on results
039   regarding asymptotic convergence rates under specific conditions (Chen et al., 2019; Li & Orabona,
040   2019; Zhou et al., 2024; Barakat & Bianchi, 2020; da Silva & Gazeau, 2019), however, there is
041
      limited theoretical insight into why common practices – such as typical hyperparameter choices, and
      the use of normalization layers (e.g. layer-norm) – contribute towards successful generalization.
042
043   In this work, we demonstrate that continuous-time models of optimization can shed light on such
044   questions. A continuous-time approach allows for a mathematically tractable analysis that can
045   leverage the tools of calculus – namely, differential equations. Past work has utilized continuous-time
046   models in contexts of practical interest (Tanaka & Kunin, 2021; Kunin et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2023;
047
      Chen et al., 2024a; Elkabetz & Cohen, 2021), though such work does not consider optimizers with
      adaptive learning rates (e.g. Adam), hence practical insight into modern deep learning is limited.
048
049   Our contributions can be summarized as:
050
051    Sec. 3. Theory of adaptive hyperparameters. We determine a theoretical stability region for
052            Adam’s adaptive hyperparameters (β, γ) that ensures bounded parameter updates and stable
053            training, which we verify empirically. This result implies that instability may occur when
               the hyperparameters move outside the stable region, a phenomenon we empirically verify,


                                                        1
054
               exhibiting predictable exponential growth. We observe a faster rate of generalization when
055            (β, γ) is further from the instability boundary.
056
       Sec. 4. Implicit effect of scale invariance. We perform a theoretical analysis of how scale invariant
057
               architectural components (e.g. layer-norm) influence Adam’s learning dynamics, finding
058
               scale invariance to induce a meta-adaptive normalization effect. We convert this implicit
059            effect to an explicit optimizer, which we name 2-Adam, and consider its extension k-
060            Adam: a generalization of Adam/AdamW (which corresponds to k = 1) that performs a
061            normalization procedure k times successively.
062
063   We discuss related work further in Section 5.

295     4             I MPLICIT ADAPTIVE ROLE OF SCALE INVARIANCE
296
297    The presence of scale-invariant architectural components has become ubiquitous in modern deep
298    learning. One particular instance of such components are normalization layers, such as layer-norm
299    (Ba et al., 2016), batch-norm (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015), and qk-norm (Dehghani et al., 2023; Gilmer
300    et al., 2023). It has been observed that normalization layers provide an implicit beneficial effect to
301    training, in comparison to explicitly fixing the norm of weights after each training step (Tanaka &
302    Kunin, 2021; Lubana et al., 2021; Santurkar et al., 2019). Such benefits include smoothing of the
303    loss landscape (Santurkar et al., 2019; Kohler et al., 2018; Karakida et al., 2019; Lyu et al., 2023)
304
       and prevention of rank collapse (Daneshmand et al., 2020; Dong et al., 2023; Noci et al., 2022).
       In this section, we will apply the continuous-time framework of Section 2 to better understand the
305
       implicit role of scale invariance, allowing us to gain an understanding as to how normalization layers
306
       contribute towards successful generalization.
307
308    In this section, we will first briefly discuss scale-invariant maps (Section 4.1) and then make use of
309    the second-order differential equation for AdamW (Proposition 2) to solve for the dynamics of a scale
310
       invariant weight, uncovering an implicit meta-adaptive effect of scale invariance (Section 4.2). We
       then convert this implicit effect into an explicit optimizer, 2-Adam, and find preliminary evidence of
311
       2-Adam outperforming Adam in image classification and language modeling settings (Section 4.3).
312
313
314     4.1               S CALE - INVARIANT MAPS
315

316

317
       qk-norm (Dehghani et al., 2023; Gilmer et al., 2023) applies a layer-norm to the query and key
318    projections in a transformer, with the attention logits zij of a particular attention head taking the
319    form,
320                                     zij := LN(WQ xi )T LN(WK xj )

        the loss function L is equivalently scale-invariant. We have the following result:

479   5    R ELATED WORK
480
481   Theoretical analysis of adaptive optimization. Previous work has studied adaptive optimization
482   theoretically (Chen et al., 2019; Li & Orabona, 2019; Zhou et al., 2024; Barakat & Bianchi, 2020;
483   da Silva & Gazeau, 2019), focusing on asymptotic convergence rates and hence not covering the
484   results presented in this paper. These works perform a discrete-time analysis, except for (da Silva
485   & Gazeau, 2019; Barakat & Bianchi, 2020) which utilize the same first-order differential equation
      representation for m(t) and v(t) that we describe in Lemma 1 (though they do not explicitly solve


                                                          9
486
      these equations), applied towards understanding the convergence rate of Adam. Additional works that
487   take a continuous-time approach to interpreting adaptive optimization include (Wang et al., 2021),
488   which studies adaptive optimization (with no momentum) in the specific case of homogeneous neural
489   networks with a logistic loss function, using a continuous-time expression for v(t) equivalent to that
490   of Lemma 1 to show that adaptive methods based on exponential moving-averages provably converge
491   to the max-margin solution. There is also (Chen et al., 2024b), which studies the Lion optimizer from
492   the perspective of Lyapunov functions and finds Lion to implicitly perform optimization under the
493   bound constraint ||x||∞ ≤ 1/λ. We also highlight the work (Malladi et al., 2024) which provides an
494   SDE formulation of Adam that accounts for gradient stochasticity, which we neglect here.
495   Various works have derived bounds on Adam’s update using a discrete-time approach. (Zou et al.,
496   2021; Zhang et al., 2024) show that, under certain conditions, Adam’s update can be bounded by
497   a constant (see Lemma A.2 and Lemma 6.5 respectively), and (Xie & Li, 2024) applies a similar
498   approach to bound Adam’s cumulative update (see Lemma 4.2). Such works do not describe the
499   exponential growth outside of the stability region that we derive in this paper, nor do they perform an
500   empirical analysis of this phenomena and its relation to generalization.
501   Implicit role of scale invariance. There has been previous work on interpreting the effect of scale
502   invariance on training dynamics from a theoretical perspective (Tanaka & Kunin, 2021; Kunin et al.,
503   2021; Zhao et al., 2023; Lyu et al., 2023; Lubana et al., 2021). Most relevant to our work is (Tanaka
504   & Kunin, 2021) which, using a Lagrangian-based approach, uncovers an adaptive influence of scale
505   invariance. However this work does not consider optimizers with an adaptive learning rate, such as
506   RMSprop or Adam, which are more relevant to practical deep learning. Our analysis in Section 4
507
      extends this argument to the case of an adaptive learning rate, where we observe a meta-adaptive
      effect which we convert into an explicit optimizer, 2-Adam.
508
509   Extensions to Adam. Various extensions to Adam have been proposed.      √ AMSGrad (Reddi et al.,
510   2019) extends Adam by scaling the learning rate by an adaptive factor 1/ v̂n where v̂n is a maximum
511   of all past moving-averages vn . LAMB (You et al., 2020) makes use of layer-wise adaptive learning
512   rates and a trust ratio mechanism. Lion (Chen et al., 2023) was discovered by an evolutionary
513
      algorithm and uses the sign of a moving-average of the gradient in order to update the parameters.
      k-Adam extends Adam uniquely, applying an adaptive normalization procedure k times in succession,
514
      motivated by a theoretical analysis of how scale invariance influences Adam’s training dynamics.
515
516
517   6    D ISCUSSION
518
519   In this paper, we have aimed to highlight the utility of continuous-time frameworks for understanding
520   practical aspects of training dynamics. Our analysis motivated a natural quantity C(β, γ) relating to
521   training stability which we found to correlate well with generalization – observing that choices of
522
      (β, γ) with larger C(β, γ) along a given normal curve achieve better generalization performance –
      providing justification to practical hyperparameter selection. Furthermore, our analysis of training
523
      dynamics motivated the 2-Adam optimizer that allows us to exploit the implicit benefits of scale
524
      invariance, finding preliminary evidence of 2-Adam outperforming Adam. Our findings are indepen-
525   dent of any specific loss function, architecture, or data distribution, allowing for a potentially wide
526   applicability of our approach.
527
528
      Limitations and future work. We have seen that the derived bound on the max-update (Equation (5))
      is surprisingly accurate to empirical training dynamics, however our bound does not rigorously justify
529
      (only suggests) why we observe a faster rate of generalization for larger C(β, γ) along a given normal
530
      curve. Justifying this observation is an interesting future direction towards a complete understanding
531   of adaptive hyperparameter choice, as well as understanding how stochasticity influences bounding
532   ||un ||∞ and our findings on the implicit effect of scale invariance. We note that the meta-adaptive
533   effect we describe in this paper is not a complete account of the benefits of normalization layers.
534   Normalization layers possess additional benefits, such as avoiding rank collapse (Daneshmand et al.,
535   2020; Dong et al., 2023; Noci et al., 2022) and contributing towards the smoothness of the loss
536   landscape (Santurkar et al., 2019; Kohler et al., 2018; Karakida et al., 2019; Lyu et al., 2023); it is
537   unclear whether such phenomena are related to the meta-adaptive effect we describe in this paper.
