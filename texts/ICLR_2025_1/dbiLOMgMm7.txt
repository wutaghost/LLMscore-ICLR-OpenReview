009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep neural networks learn increasingly complex functions over the course of
012            training. Here, we show both empirically and theoretically that learning of the
013            target function is preceded by an early phase in which networks learn the optimal
014            constant solution (OCS) – that is, initial model responses mirror the distribution of
015            target labels, while entirely ignoring information provided in the input. Using a
016
               hierarchical category learning task, we derive exact solutions for learning dynamics
               in deep linear networks trained with bias terms. Even when initialized to zero,
017
               this simple architectural feature induces substantial changes in early dynamics.
018
               We identify hallmarks of this early OCS phase and illustrate how these signatures
019            are observed in deep linear networks and larger, more complex (and nonlinear)
020            convolutional neural networks solving a hierarchical learning task based on MNIST
021            and CIFAR10. We explain these observations by proving that deep linear networks
022            necessarily learn the OCS during early learning. To further probe the generality of
023            our results, we train human learners over the course of three days on a structurally
024            equivalent learning task. We then identify qualitative signatures of this early OCS
025            phase in terms of true negative rates. Surprisingly, we find the same early reliance
026            on the OCS in the behaviour of human learners. Finally, we show that learning of
027
               the OCS can emerge even in the absence of bias terms and is equivalently driven
               by generic correlations in the input data. Overall, our work suggests the OCS as a
028
               common learning principle in supervised, error-corrective learning, and suggests
029
               possible factors for its prevalence.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Neural networks trained with stochastic gradient descent (SGD) exhibit various simplicity biases,
035   where models tend to learn simple functions before more complex ones (Kalimeris et al., 2019;
036   Rahaman et al., 2019). Simplicity biases hold significant theoretical interest as they provide an
037   explanation for how deep networks generalize or fail to generalize in practice (Bhattamishra et al.,
038
      2023; Valle-Pérez et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2021).
039   The characterisation of simplicity biases is still incomplete. Some explanations appeal to distributional
040   properties of input data, pointing out that SGD progressively learns increasingly higher-order moments
041   (Refinetti et al., 2023; Belrose et al., 2024). Other approaches focus directly on the evolution of
042   the network function, proposing that networks initially learn a classifier highly correlated with a
043   linear model. Importantly, networks continue to perform well on examples correctly classified by this
044
      simple function, even when overfitting in later training (Kalimeris et al., 2019). This implies that
      dynamical simplicity biases help models generalize, by locking in initial knowledge that is not erased
045
      or forgotten during later training (Braun et al., 2022; Kalimeris et al., 2019).
046
047   Deep linear networks have proven to be a valuable tool for studying simplicity biases. A key finding
048   is that directions in the network function are learned in order of importance (Saxe et al., 2014; 2019).
049   This phenomenon, known as progressive differentiation, connects modern deep learning theory to
050
      both to human child development and to the earliest connectionist models of semantic cognition
      (Rogers and McClelland, 2004; Rumelhart et al., 1986).
051
052   Our contribution proposes a connection between these works by characterizing networks in the
053   earliest stages of learning in terms of input, output, and architecture. In the hierarchical setting by
      Saxe et al. (2019), we demonstrate both theoretically and empirically that neural networks initially

      1.1   C ONTRIBUTIONS
094          • We devise exact solutions for learning dynamics to analyse linear networks with bias in the
095            input layer. Even when initialized at zero, this component substantially alters early learning
096            dynamics.
097
098          • We empirically characterise early learning in these linear networks as being dominated by
099
               average output statistics. We explain this result with a theoretical analysis which reveals that
               average output statistics are always learned first when the network contains bias terms.
100
101
             • We further highlight the practical relevance of these theoretical results in a hierarchical
102            learning task for humans as well as complex, non-linear architectures by empirically demon-
103            strating that all learners develop stereotypical response biases during early stages of training.
104
105          • On the basis of the developed theory we show that, in linear networks, early OCS learning

107            empirically demonstrate that learning of the OCS can indeed be purely driven by generic

      1.2   R ELATED WORK
109
110   Deep linear networks. In deep linear networks analytical solutions have been obtained for certain
111   initial conditions and datasets (Saxe et al., 2014; 2019; Braun et al., 2022; Fukumizu, 1998). Progress
112   has also been made in understanding linear network loss landscapes (Baldi and Hornik, 1989) and
113   generalisation ability (Lampinen and Ganguli, 2019). Despite their linearity these models display
114   complex non-linear learning dynamics which reflect behaviours seen in non-linear models (Saxe
115   et al., 2019). Moreover, learning dynamics in such simple models have been argued to qualitatively
116   resemble phenomena observed in the cognitive development of humans (Saxe et al., 2019; Rogers
117
      and McClelland, 2004).
118   Biological response biases. Humans and animals routinely display response biases during perceptual
119   learning and decision making tasks (Gold et al., 2008; Jones et al., 2015; Liebana Garcia et al., 2023;
120   Amitay et al., 2014; Urai et al., 2019). In these tasks decisions are frequently made in sequences
121   where responses and feedback steer decisions beyond the provided perceptual evidence (Jones et al.,
122   2015; Fan et al., 2024; Gold et al., 2008; Verplanck et al., 1952; Sugrue et al., 2004). Non-stationary
123
      response biases can be driven by feedback on previous trials (Dutilh et al., 2012; Rabbitt and Rodgers,
      1977) or might reflect global beliefs about the statistics of a task (Fan et al., 2024; Jones et al., 2015).
124
      Importantly, response biases are particularly pronounced in early learning (Jones et al., 2015; Gold
125
      et al., 2008; Liebana Garcia et al., 2023) and their influence appears to be strongest when uncertainty
126   about the correct response is highest (Gold et al., 2008; Fan et al., 2024).
127
128   Simplicity biases in machine learning. Simplicity biases in neural networks have been studied
129
      extensively both theoretically (Bordelon et al., 2020; Mei et al., 2022) and empirically (Bhattamishra
      et al., 2023; Mingard et al., 2023). Work on the distributional simplicity bias emphasises the
130
      importance of input data and proposes that models learn via progressive exploitation of dataset
131
      moments (Refinetti et al., 2023; Belrose et al., 2024). On the other hand, neural networks have been
132   found to express simpler functions during early training (Kalimeris et al., 2019; Refinetti et al., 2023;
133   Belrose et al., 2024; Rahaman et al., 2019). Our work draws a connection between these findings and
134   highlights how input statistics bias early learning towards output statistics.

328   4.2   T HEORETICAL EXPLANATION
329
330   In this section, we extend the linear network formalism to understand the mechanism behind early
331   learning of the OCS. We first show how bias terms in the input layer are directly related to the OCS.
332   Afterwards, we prove that the OCS is necessarily learned first in these settings.
333
334   The OCS is linked to shared properties. Having established the applicability of the linear network
335   theory in Section 3 we now seek to understand how the early bias towards the OCS emerges. To this
336
      end, notice how bias terms can be written in terms of the constant eigenmode 1N :
337   Proposition 2 (The OCS is linked to shared properties). If 1N is an eigenvector to the similarity
                                                                  PN
338   matrix XT X ∈ RN ×N , then the sample-average x̄ = N1 i=1 xi will be an eigenvector to the
339   correlation matrix XXT ∈ RNin ×Nin with identical eigenvalue λ. An analogous statement applies
340   for YT Y and YYT . The converse does not hold true in general.
341
      We prove this statement in Appendix A.5.5. Importantly, it establishes a connection between the
342
      feature and sample dimensions of X and Y. If 1N is an eigenvector to XT X and YT Y already, it
343

360   Early learning is biased by the OCS mode. We established that network responses are driven by

364   Theorem 1 (Early learning is biased by the OCS mode). If 1N is a joint non-degenerate eigenvector
365
      to positive input and output similarity matrices XT X and YT Y, the OCS mode socs ȳx̄T will have
      leading spectral weight s0 ≡ socs in the SVD of the input-output correlation matrix Σyx .
366
367   We prove this statement with help of the Perron-Frobenius theorem (Perron, 1907) in Appendix A.5.7.
368   Consequently, the optimal constant mode is learned at a faster rate than remaining SVD components
369   and transiently dominates the early network function. Notably, this applies to our task data YT Y
370   (see Appendix A.5.6) and leads to characteristic learning signatures observed in Fig. 3.
371   Theorem 1 hinges on the constant eigenvector 1N being present in the data. We later provide
372   empirical (Fig. 6 and Appendix A.9) and theoretical (Appendix A.5.6) arguments that this assumption
373   is approximately fulfilled in a variety of cases.

377   In Appendix A.5.3, we summarize these results through the neural tangent kernel. Overall, these

410   discretized network responses to enable comparison. We treat network responses in ŷ as logits from
411   which we then sampled responses in {0, 1}Nout . Full procedure details are given in Appendix A.3.
412   Results. The key results of our experiments are presented in Fig. 4. Intriguingly, we find that
413   human learners, linear networks, and CNNs all display characteristic early response biases. Note
414   that chance true negative rate is equal between all three levels of the hierarchy. Biological as well as
415   artificial learners display an initial "drop" in true negative rate at the top level of the output hierarchy.
416   The result indicates a general lack of specificity and an overly liberal response criterion for output
417   labels on the top level of the hierarchy. To appreciate the significance of this result it is important
418   to understand that the task can be learned without the development of these early response biases:
419   In particular, linear networks without bias terms do not show this behaviour (see Appendix A.7).
420
      Surprisingly, the human response signature demonstrates that these learners, just as artifical networks,
      display an early bias towards the OCS. We conjecture that early learning of the OCS might be a
421
      general phenomenon that emerges during error-corrective training. We replicate the human result
422
      with a second cohort of learners in Appendix A.2. Notable is also the difference between shallow and
423   deep linear networks. Response biases seem more transient in shallow networks and appear to more
424   closely mirror human learners. However, quantitative comparisons are challenging due to inherently
425   differing learning timescales.

      7    D ISCUSSION
506
507
      In this work, we found that the inclusion of bias terms in linear networks shifts early learning towards
508
      the OCS, even when initialized at zero. We also highlight how OCS learning can equivalently be
509   driven by input correlations. We demonstrated that early, input-independent simplicity biases occur in
510   practice, affecting both non-linear networks and human learners. Our contribution complements prior
511   work on simplicity biases by highlighting factors that drive networks in the earliest stages of learning;
512   connecting input, output, and architecture. Overall, our findings highlight how simple linear networks
513   can serve as useful tools to investigate simplicity biases in significantly more complex systems.
514
      Relevance. We see promising applications for early OCS learning in the cognitive and behavioural
515   sciences. OCS-like response biases have been noted previously (Herrnstein, 1961; Estes, 1964).
516   However, we believe that a normative theory for these effects is still incomplete. Our theory identifies
517   possible properties of the biological wetware or natural stimuli that may give rise to such biases.
518
      While we do not study generalisation ourselves, we believe that OCS learning is practically important
519
      to understand how neural networks generalize or fail to generalize. Kang et al. (2024) has highlighted
520
      that networks will revert to OCS in a variety of generalisation settings. We demonstrate that the OCS
521   component in the network function is acquired early, and is retained throughout training (effective
522   singular values in Fig. 2 stay constant in late training). We believe that this retention of the OCS
523   mode enables reversion.
524
      OCS learning is also relevant when learning under class imbalance, a common problem in machine
525
      learning where datasets are frequently naturally imbalanced (Feldman, 2020; Van Horn and Per-
526
      ona, 2017), leading to a failure to learn information about minority classes (Ye et al., 2021). In
527   Appendix A.10 we show an exactly solvable case of OCS learning in such settings and highlight how
528   OCS learning can negatively impact performance for minority classes.
529
530
      Limitations and future work. Our work is restricted to qualitative comparisons between linear
      networks and non-linear systems and our work only gives suggestive evidence of factors which drive
531
      early OCS learning in non-linear systems. We chose linear networks to allow for a rigorous description
532
      of the dynamics of learning. Methods from mean-field theory may provide a precise tool to analyze a
533   wider range of systems directly. Second, the ambiguity between architecture and data in driving
534   the OCS does not allow us to determine the underlying mechanism in human learners. Future stud-
535   ies might address this limitation by manipulating correlations in stimuli or by recording of neural data.
536

538   supplementary material to this submission.

1533   Given the hierarchical structure of labels used in the majority of our experiments we also see some
1534   general connections of our work to problems in the domain of imbalanced multi-label learning.

1536

1537

1538

1540   et al., 2015) or via both methods (Pham et al., 2021). Our results on the hierarchical learning task
1541   and on the problem of class imbalanced learning in Appendix A.10 might hint at OCS learning as a
1542   potential contributor to problems observed in multi-label learning as the imbalanced distribution of
1543   output labels might drive learning to undesirable solutions.
