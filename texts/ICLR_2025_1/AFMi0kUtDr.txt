009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Efficient data selection is crucial for enhancing the training efficiency of deep
012            neural networks and minimizing annotation requirements. Traditional methods
013            often face high computational costs, limiting their scalability and practical use.
014            We introduce PruneFuse, a novel strategy that leverages pruned networks for
015            data selection and later fuses them with the original network to optimize training.
016            PruneFuse operates in two stages: First, it applies structured pruning to create
017            a smaller pruned network that, due to its structural coherence with the original
018
               network, is well-suited for the data selection task. This small network is then trained
               and selects the most informative samples from the dataset. Second, the trained
019
               pruned network is seamlessly fused with the original network. This integration
020
               leverages the insights gained during the training of the pruned network to facilitate
021            the learning process of the fused network while leaving room for the network to
022            discover more robust solutions. Extensive experimentation on various datasets
023            demonstrates that PruneFuse significantly reduces computational costs for data
024            selection, achieves better performance than baselines, and accelerates the overall
025            training process.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Deep learning models have achieved remarkable success across various domains, ranging from image
031   recognition to natural language processing (Ren et al., 2015; Long et al., 2015; He et al., 2016).
032   However, the performance of models heavily relies on the access of large amounts of labeled data for
033
      training (Sun et al., 2017). In practical real-world applications, the process of manually annotating
      massive datasets can be prohibitively expensive and time-consuming. Data selection techniques
034
      such as Active Learning (Gal et al., 2017) offer a promising solution to address this challenge by
035
      iteratively selecting the most informative samples from the unlabeled dataset for annotation. The
036   goal of active learning is to reduce the labeling costs while maintaining or even improving model
037   performance. Nowadays, due to tremendous increase in data and model complexity, traditional active
038   learning techniques requiring large models to be trained iteratively to perform data selection, can
039   result in significant computational costs. This computational burden restricts the scalability of active
040   learning methods, particularly in scenarios where training large models is impractical due to resource
041   constraints.
042   In this paper, we propose a novel strategy for efficient data selection in active learning setting that
043   overcomes the limitations of traditional approaches. Our approach builds up on the concept of
044   model pruning, which selectively reduces the complexity of neural networks while preserving their
045   accuracy. By utilizing small pruned networks as reusable data selectors, we eliminate the need to train
046   large models, specifically during the data selection phase, thus significantly reducing computational
047   demands. By enabling swift identification of the most informative samples, our method not only
048   enhances the efficiency of active learning but also ensures its scalability and cost-effectiveness in
049   resource-limited settings. Additionally, we employ these pruned networks to train the final model
050
      through a fusion process, effectively harnessing the insights from the trained networks to accelerate
      convergence and improve the generalization of the final model.
051

053   rapid data selection technique that leverages pruned networks. This approach mitigates the need for
      continuous large model training prior to data selection, which is inherent in conventional active learn-

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
086
087   Data Selection. Recent studies have explored techniques to improve the efficiency of data selection
088
      in deep learning. Approaches such as Core-Set selection (Sener and Savarese, 2017), BatchBALD
      (Kirsch et al., 2019), and Deep Bayesian Active Learning (Gal et al., 2017) aim to select informative
089
      samples using techniques like diversity maximization and Bayesian uncertainty estimation. Parallelly,
090
      the domain of active learning has unveiled strategies, such as uncertainty sampling (Shen et al., 2017;
091   Sener and Savarese, 2018; Kirsch et al., 2019), expected model change-based approach (Freytag
092   et al., 2014; Käding et al., 2016), and query-by-density (Sener and Savarese, 2017). These techniques
093   prioritize samples that can maximize information gain, thereby enhancing model performance with
094   minimal labeling effort. While these methods achieve efficient data selection, they still require
095   training large models for the selection process, resulting in significant computational overhead. Other
096   strategies such as (Killamsetty et al., 2021a) optimize this selection process by matching the gradients
097   of subset with training or validation set based on orthogonal matching algorithm and (Killamsetty
098   et al., 2021b) performs meta-learning based approach for online data selection. SubSelNet (Jain
099
      et al., 2024) proposes to approximate a model that can be used to select the subset for various
      architectures without retraining the target model, hence reducing the overall overhead. However, it
100
      involves pre-training routine which is very costly and needed again for any change in data or model
101
      distribution. SVP (Coleman et al., 2019) introduces to use small proxy models for data selection
102   but discards these proxies before training the target model. Additionally, structural discrepancies
103   between the proxy and target models may result in sub-optimal data selections. Our approach also
104   builds on this foundation of using small model (which in our case is a pruned model) but it enables
105   direct integration with the target model through the fusion process. This ensures that the knowledge
106   acquired during data selection is retained and actively contributes to the training of the original model.
107   Also, the architectural coherence between the pruned and the target model provides a more seamless
      and effective mechanism for data selection, enhancing overall model performance and efficiency.

      Efficient Deep Learning. Efficient deep learning has gained significant attention in recent years.
109   Methods such as Neural Architecture Search (NAS) (Zoph and Le, 2016; Wan et al., 2020), network
110   pruning (Han et al., 2015), quantization (Dong et al., 2020; Jacob et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2016), and
111   knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015; Yin et al., 2020) have been proposed to reduce model
112   size and computational requirements. Neural Network pruning has been extensively investigated as a
113   technique to reduce the complexity of deep neural networks (Han et al., 2015). Pruning strategies can
114   be broadly divided into Unstructured Pruning (Dong et al., 2017; Guo et al., 2016; Park et al., 2020)
115   and Structured Pruning (Li et al., 2016; He et al., 2017; You et al., 2019; Ding et al., 2019) based on
116   the granularity and regularity of the pruning scheme. Unstructured pruning often yields a superior
117
      accuracy-size trade-off whereas structured pruning offers practical speedup and compression without
      necessitating specialized hardware. While pruning literature suggests pruning after training (Renda
118
      et al., 2020) or during training (Zhu and Gupta, 2017; Gale et al., 2019), recent research explore
119
      the viability of pruning at initialization (Lee et al., 2018; Frankle et al., 2020; Tanaka et al., 2020;
120   Frankle et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020). In our work, we leverage the benefits of model pruning at
121   initialization to create a small representative model for efficient data selection, allowing for the rapid
122   identification of informative samples while minimizing computational requirements.

136   3.1   S UBSET S ELECTION F RAMEWORK
137
138   Active Learning is widely utilized iterative approach tailored for situations with abundant unlabeled
139   data. Given a classification task with C classes and a large pool of unlabeled samples U , AL
140   revolves around selectively querying the most informative samples from U for labeling. The process
141   commences with an initial set of randomly sampled data s0 from U , which is subsequently labeled.
142   In subsequent rounds, AL augments the labeled set L by adding newly identified informative samples.
143
      This cycle repeats until a predefined number of labeled samples b are selected.

145   3.2   N ETWORK P RUNING AND I TS R ELEVANCE
146
147
      Network pruning emerges as a potent tool to reduce the complexity of neural networks. By elimi-
      nating redundant parameters, pruning preserves vital network functionalities while streamlining its
148
      architecture. Pruning strategies can be broadly categorized into Unstructured Pruning and Structured
149
      Pruning. Unstructured Pruning targets individual weight removal independent of their location. While
150   it trims down the overall number of parameters, tangible performance gains on conventional hardware
151   often demand extensive pruning (Park et al., 2016). On the other hand, Structured Pruning emphasizes
152   the removal of larger constructs like kernels, channels, or layers. Its strength lies in preserving dense
153   computations, which not only yields a leaner network but also bestows immediate performance im-
154   provements (Liu et al., 2017). Given its computational benefits, particularly in expediting evaluations
155   and aligning with hardware optimizations, we opted for Structured Pruning over its counterpart.
156   Importantly, pruned networks maintain the architectural coherence of the original model. This
157   coherence makes them inherently more suitable for tasks such as data selection. Unlike heavily
158   modified or entirely different models that can be used for data selection Coleman et al. (2019); Jain
159   et al. (2024), the pruned model echoes the original structure, particularly advantageous in recognizing
160   and prioritizing data samples that resonate with the patterns of the original network. The goal is
161   clear to develop a data selection strategy that conserves computational resources, minimizes memory
      overhead, and potentially improves model generalization.

276   4.4   F USION WITH THE O RIGINAL M ODEL
277
278   After achieving the predetermined budget, the next phase is to integrate the insights from the trained
279   pruned model θp∗ into the untrained original model θ. This step is crucial, as it amalgamates the
280
      learned knowledge from the pruned model with the expansive architecture of the original model,
      aiming to harness the best of both worlds.
281
282   Rationale for Fusion. Traditional pruning and fine-tuning methods often involve training a large
283   model, pruning it down, and then fine-tuning the smaller model. While this is effective, it does not
284   fully exploit the potential benefits of the larger, untrained model. The primary reason is that the
285   pruning process might discard useful structures and connections within the original model that were
286
      not yet leveraged during initial training. By fusing the trained pruned model with the untrained
      original model, we aim to create a model that combines the learned knowledge by θp∗ with the broader,
287
      unexplored model θ.
288

      discussed strategy above outperforms baseline active learning, to further optimize and enhance this
325   amalgamated knowledge, we engage in a fine-tuning phase making use of Knowledge Distillation
326   (KD). KD traditionally facilitates a student model to learn and emulate the behavior of a large
327   complex teacher model. While this technique has been employed in various scenarios, its application
328   in our context is unique and particularly advantageous. Given the seamless integration capability
329   of our pruned model, KD stands as a robust tool to complement the learning process. In essence,
330   it’s not merely about transferring knowledge; it’s about leveraging the insights from θp∗ to enrich the
331   training of fused model θF . During the fine-tuning phase, we can make use of two losses. The first
332   is the Cross-Entropy Loss, which quantifies the divergence between the predictions of θF and the
333
      actual labels in dataset L. The second is the Distillation Loss, which measures the difference in the
      softened logits of θF and θp∗ . These softened logits are derived by tempering logits of θp∗ , which in our
334
      case is the teacher model, with a temperature parameter before applying the softmax function. The
335
      composite loss for the fine-tuning phase is formulated as a weighted average of the Cross-Entropy
336   and Distillation losses. The iterative enhancement of θF is governed by:

407   Implementation Details. We used ResNet-50, ResNet-56, ResNet-110, and ResNet-164 architecture
408   in our experiments. We pruned these architectures using the Torch-Prunnig library (Fang et al., 2023)
409
      for different pruning ratios p = 0.5, 0.6, 0.7, and 0.8 to get the pruned architectures. We trained the
      model for 181 epochs following the setup in Coleman et al. (2019) for CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100
410
      and for 100 epochs for TinyImageNet-200 and ImageNet-1K. We use the mini-batch of 128 for
411
      CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 and 256 for TinyImageNet-200 and ImageNet-1K. For all the experiments
412   SGD is used as an optimizer (further details are provided in Suplementary Materials A.3). We took
413   Active Learning (AL) as a baseline for the proposed technique and initially, we started by randomly
414   selecting 2% of the data. For the first round, we added 8% from the unlabeled set, then 10% in each
415   subsequent round, until reaching the label budget, b. After each round, we retrained the models from
416   scratch, as described in the methodology. All experiments are carried out independently 3 times and
417   then the average is reported.

533
      In this work, we present PruneFuse, a novel strategy that integrates pruning with network fusion
534   to optimize the data selection pipeline for deep learning. PruneFuse leverages a small pruned
535   model for data selection, which then seamlessly fuses with the original model, providing fast and
536   better generalization while significantly reducing computational costs. Our extensive evaluations
537   across CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, Tiny-ImageNet-200, and ImageNet-1K demonstrate that PruneFuse
538   consistently outperforms existing baselines, establishing its efficiency and efficacy. PruneFuse offers
539   a scalable, practical, and flexible solution to enhance the training efficiency of neural networks,
      particularly in resource-constrained settings.

799   from training the pruned model on a subset sp of the full dataset D, and pruning error, resulting from
800   the reduced capacity of the pruned model θp . We demonstrate how the synchronization frequency
801   Fsync controls both errors and present a convergence result under reasonable assumptions.
802   The optimization problem is formulated as:
803
804                              min E(x,y)∈sp [l(x, y; θp )] − E(x,y)∈D [l(x, y; θ)]                     (3)
                                  sp
805
806   where sp ⊂ D is the selected subset, θp is the pruned model, and θ is the full model. Our goal is
807
      to minimize the difference in expected loss between the pruned model on the subset sp and the full
      model on the full dataset D.
