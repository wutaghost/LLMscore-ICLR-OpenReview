009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce an open dataset for video event spotting focused on fast-paced events
012            in shadowboxing videos captured at high frame rates. The dataset features ac-
013            curate frame-level annotations for diverse punch types alongside pose keypoint
014            annotations, enabling the development of robust event recognition models. This
015            work presents a novel benchmark exploring two distinct approaches to event spot-
016            ting: direct prediction from image data and a staged approach involving interme-
017            diate pose estimation followed by event detection based on the detected keypoints.
018
               We provide baseline neural network solutions incorporating temporal information
               for both tracks, facilitating comparative analysis of these methodologies. This
019
               shadowboxing dataset advances the field of automatic sports analysis and con-
020
               tributes to the broader understanding of video events recognition.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Understanding and interpreting video content is a fundamental challenge in computer vision with
026   vast real-world implications. From autonomous driving and surveillance systems to sports analytics
027   and human-computer interaction, accurate video analysis enables us to extract meaningful infor-
028   mation about events and actions occurring within visual data streams. One critical task of video
029   understanding is event spotting: the task of precisely identifying specific event of interest occur-
030   rences within a video sequence. Specifically, this involves highlighting the exact frame in which a
031   fast-paced event occurs.
032   However, accurately spotting fast-paced events remains a significant challenge because it often re-
033   quires detecting minute features within vast amounts of data from two modalities: spatial and tem-
034   poral. Existing approaches often struggle with the high temporal resolution required to capture
035   subtle movements and nuances characteristic of rapid actions. Moreover, many datasets used for
036   benchmarking event detection either focus on slower-paced activities or lack detailed frame-level
037   annotations essential for training robust models.
038   To address these critical needs and challenges, we introduce ShadowPunch, a novel open dataset
039   specifically designed for video event spotting in the sports domain of shadow boxing. Shadow
040   boxing is a high-intensity activity characterized by rapid punches and intricate movements, making
041   it an ideal testbed for developing robust event recognition models.
042   Our dataset features accurate frame-level annotations for a diverse range of punch types, includ-
043   ing jabs, hooks and uppercuts. Furthermore, ShadowPunch includes pose keypoint annotations,
044   capturing the positions of key body joints in different moments of the video sequences. This addi-
045   tional layer of information allows researchers to explore sophisticated event recognition pipelines
046   that leverage intermediate spatial cues. By providing this rich and comprehensive dataset, we aim
047   to empower the computer vision community with a valuable resource for developing and evaluating
048   advanced models capable of accurately spotting fast-paced events in video data.
049   This work makes the following key contributions to the field of event recognition:
050
051         • Introduction of ShadowPunch dataset: We present ShadowPunch, a novel open dataset
052           for video event spotting in the domain of shadowboxing. This high-intensity activity
053           presents unique challenges due to rapid movements. ShadowPunch provides accurate
              frame-level annotations for various punches and incorporates pose keypoint annotations.

055            comparing two distinct methodologies for event spotting: direct prediction from image data

065   2   R ELATED W ORK
066
067   Video understanding tasks. Advances in deep learning and available computing power have driven
068
      remarkable progress in video understanding in recent years. The video understanding sphere in-
      cludes several related tasks ordered by their temporal resolution (coarsest to finest): Action classifi-
069
      cation involves assigning a label from a predefined list to an entire video clip based on its content.
070
      Temporal Action Segmentation (TAS) involves identifying the start and end points in time of an ac-
071   tion within an untrimmed video sequence and assigning a class label to the action. Action spotting
072   is a task of detecting the presence and precise temporal location of an event within a video, typically
073   pinpointing it to a single frame and providing corresponding class label. These events are usually
074   brief.
075
      Hong et al. (2022) further differentiates these tasks by introducing precise temporal event spotting.
076
      This defines correct prediction as follows: The predicted event frame timestamp must fall within δ
077   frames (a small window of just a few video frames) of the labeled ground-truth event and also have
078   the correct class label. We will further focus on this task formulation.
079
080
      Public datasets. Several public datasets are available for action detection or spotting in general
      and particularly within the sports domain; these datasets usually come with extra annotations to
081
      provide more context or in-depth information on actions. Historically, many sport-related datasets
082
      have focused on temporal segmentation of prolonged events. For example, FineDiving (Xu et al.,
083   2022) features 3000 video samples from various diving competitions. Each annotation includes not
084   only the type and difficulty level of the diving action but also the judges’ scores.
085
086
      Some datasets provide action annotations with both class and temporal boundaries along with spa-
      tial boundaries of the actor performing an action on a playground, allowing for both action analysis
087
      and multi-object tracking (MoT). For example, FineSports (Xu et al., 2024) consists of 16,000 an-
088
      notated events in basketball videos, accompanied by approximately 123,000 spatial bounding boxes
089   of players.
090
091   There are also datasets focusing primarily on event spotting tasks. For instance, SoccerNet-v2 con-
092
      tains a large collection of football broadcast videos (764 hours) with 110,458 annotated action times-
      tamps over 17 in-game event types. It provides other annotations as well, which enable comprehen-
093
      sive football analysis, but contains no human pose data. However, it is worth noting that the videos
094
      are recorded at a resolution of 1280×720 pixels and a frame rate of 25 frames per second, making it
095   challenging to pinpoint the exact frame of occurrence for some fast events in difficult cases such as
096   very short ball touches.
097
098
      On the other end of the spectrum, there is a high-frame-rate video dataset OpenTTGames (Voeikov
      et al., 2020), which features table tennis videos sampled at 1080×1920 pixels and 120 frames per
099
      second. This dataset offers annotations for 4,271 fast-paced events of three classes and also includes
100
      semantic segmentation maps of the players.
101
102   There are multiple publicly available pose estimation datasets in the sports domain, such as Hu-
103   man3.6M (Ionescu et al., 2014) and SportsPose (Ingwersen et al., 2023), which do not provide
104
      precise action timestamps for event spotting tasks.
105   However, there is a very limited set of datasets that feature both action labels and pose data. A
106   notable recent example is FS-Jump3D (Tanaka et al., 2024), which includes 86 3D pose keypoints
107   obtained through optical markerless motion capture and temporal action segmentation labels for fig-
      ure skating jumps. Notably, all these datasets mainly contain video data collected within laboratory

                                                         2

108
      environments, which limits the diversity of backgrounds and lighting conditions. The FS-Jump3D
109   dataset, in particular, contains data from only four subjects in a single ice rink laboratory environ-
110   ment.
111
112
      Video understanding models. Early approaches were based on 2D CNNs to extract spatial features
      from each frame independently. These frame-level features are then aggregated using temporal
113
      pooling or recurrent networks such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) or Gated Recurrent Units
114
      (GRUs) to capture temporal dependencies across frames (Donahue et al., 2015). To directly model
115   the spatiotemporal dynamics of a video, 3D CNNs extend traditional 2D convolution operations by
116   adding a temporal dimension to the convolutional kernels. Architectures such as C3D (Tran et al.,
117   2015) and I3D (Carreira & Zisserman, 2017) learn to represent both spatial and temporal patterns
118   jointly, making them well-suited for action recognition and temporal action localisation.
119
      Another approach employed a two-stream architecture introduced by Simonyan & Zisserman
120
      (2014). This involves a separate network to process RGB frames, capturing spatial appearance cues,
121   while another stream processes optical flow inputs to focus on temporal features. Enhancements
122   to this approach, introduced by Wang et al. (2016), integrated short-term and long-term temporal
123   dependencies to improve robustness against variations in video content, as well as utilised extra
124   modalities such as RGB difference and warped optical flow fields. The latter was meant to compen-
125   sate for camera motion so that the optical flow field highlights features of human action rather than
126   background.
127   More recent approaches adopt transformer-based architectures to model both short- and long-term
128   temporal relations within a video. For example, TallFormer (Cheng & Bertasius, 2022) utilises a
129   transformer-based encoder to extract short-term features from a video clip. ActionFormer (Zhang
130   et al., 2022) uses a convolutional-based video frames encoder with the embedding features being
131   further encoded into a feature pyramid using a multi-scale Transformer, while employing a convo-
132   lutional decoder to solve the action localisation task. The ASTRA architecture combines several
133   temporal embeddings for visual and audio modalities using a transformer encoder-decoder archi-
134   tecture (Xarles et al., 2023) to address the SoccerNet-v2 action spotting challenge (Deliege et al.,
135
      2021).
136   Meanwhile, there has been growing popularity in approaches that are not directly based on video
137   frames but rather on features from intermediate representations such as pose skeletal data to extract
138   temporal information from videos (Yeung et al., 2024; Ibh et al., 2024; Deyzel & Theart, 2023). For
139   example, TemPose (Ibh et al., 2023) uses skeleton-based temporal self-attention for action prediction
140   by utilising a transformer-based model to process pose-estimation skeleton sequences as well as
141
      encoded auxiliary data on players’ positions on the court and the shuttlecock position for action
      recognition in badminton video. This approach minimises reliance on non-human-related visual
142
      context by decreasing the effect of background actions, as the model consumes manually crafted
143
      intermediate representations that are focused on action-specific features. These models often offer
144   lighter computational loads due to reduced amounts of raw data to be processed.
145
146   Despite significant advances in event spotting within video understanding tasks, there remains a
147
      notable lack of fast-paced action spotting datasets, particularly for shadowboxing. Existing datasets
      often lack precise action timestamp annotations and human pose data. Additionally, current datasets
148
      are predominantly laboratory-based, limiting their applicability to real-world scenarios with varying
149
      lighting conditions and background complexity. Our work aims to address these limitations by
150   introducing a new dataset with detailed shadow boxing action labels and skeletal pose information.
151   We also propose a benchmark for evaluating event spotting models, suggesting comparison of direct
152   prediction versus intermediate representation approaches.

245   4   B ENCHMARK
246
247   We propose the dataset and benchmark for comparison between two families of approaches: a direct
248   approach that predicts punch events directly from the imagery data, utilising its temporal modality;
249   or, alternatively, a multistage approach with an intermediate representation of skeletal keypoints,
250   where final action spotting predictions are based on a sequential stream of pose keypoints.
251
      By directly predicting punch events from raw imagery data, the model can learn a comprehensive
252   representation of temporal dynamics without relying on artificially predefined forms of intermediate
253   representations. This approach may be more robust to noise in the input data since it does not depend
254   on the accuracy of an intermediate step like pose keypoint detection. Additionally, such models
255   may have lower inference latency and could potentially be more efficient for real-time applications.
256   However, the direct approach may be more difficult to extend with auxiliary tasks and may lack
257   interpretability because they do not provide an intermediate representation that could offer insights
258   into the reasoning behind the predictions.
259   On the other hand, the multistage approach allows for greater modularity, where each stage can be
260   optimised independently, potentially improving the pipeline prediction quality and offering some
261   insights via the intermediate representation. This can be particularly useful in scenarios where de-
262   tailed analysis of individual stages is necessary. For instance, evaluating the accuracy of keypoint
263   detection separately from action spotting could help identify specific areas that need improvement.
264   Nevertheless, cumulative error propagation can occur, with errors from earlier stages (e.g., keypoint
265   detection) propagating through subsequent stages and amplifying, leading to poor final predictions.
266   Given the arguments for and against both options, it seems to us a non-trivial to predict without
267   further experimentation which approach will be optimal. Although the answer in actual application
268   may depend on various specific aspects of particular implementations and business requirements, the
269   general question of whether a direct approach or a staged approach with artificially defined interme-
      diate representation may achieve better results seems an interesting research direction. Therefore,

311   5.1   D IRECT METHOD
312
313   The end-to-end baseline architecture employs a ResNet34-3D backbone (Tran et al., 2018), which
314
      has been pretrained on the Kinetics-400 dataset (Zisserman et al., 2017). The input to the model
      comprises rescaled stacks of seven consecutive video frames at a resolution of 224x224 pixels,
315
      normalised to the range [0..1]. These stacks are sampled from the dataset such that the annotated
316
      events coincide with the last frame in each stack. To maintain simplicity and reproducibility of the
317   baseline, no data augmentation techniques were applied.
318
319   The output tensor from the backbone is processed through two linear layers to generate two predic-
320
      tion vectors: one consisting of four elements for punch type classification and another containing
      two values for determining the punch side.
321
322   A Softmax layer serves as the final activation function. The model was trained using Focal loss (Lin
323   et al., 2017) with parameters α = 1.0 and γ = 2.0. Loss values were computed independently for
      both branches (punch type and side classification) and then summed with equal weights. Training


                                                       6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      was conducted using the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) over 50 epochs with a
325   learning rate of 0.0001, selecting the best epoch checkpoint based on the highest accuracy achieved
326   during validation.

329   5.2   S TAGED APPROACH
330
      The staged approach involves a two-step pipeline for punch type and side classification using human
331
      pose keypoint extraction followed by event classification.
332
333   In the first step, 17 keypoints (e.g., nose, shoulders, elbows, wrists) are extracted from each frame of
334   the video using the DEKR-HRNet architecture (Geng et al., 2021), pretrained on the COCO dataset
335   (Lin et al., 2014). The DEKR-HRNet architecture was utilised through the MMPose framework
336
      (OpenMMLab, 2020). Keypoints are detected for seven consecutive frames, sampled similarly to
      the direct method. Their x and y coordinates are normalised by dividing by the width and height
337
      of the video frames, respectively. After normalisation, the input tensor becomes B×34×7, where
338
      B is the batch size (16 in our case), 34 represents the 17 keypoints with their respective x and y
339   coordinates, and 7 is the number of frames in the sequence.
340
341   In the second step, the normalised keypoints are passed into an architecture designed to capture
342
      temporal dynamics. A linear projection layer first transforms the keypoints into a higher-dimensional
      space (512 dimensions). The sequence is then processed by a Transformer encoder (Vaswani et al.,
343
      2017), which models temporal dependencies across the frames. Our model uses a Transformer with
344
      16 attention heads and two layers. Then, attention pooling, inspired by the self-attentive pooling
345   mechanism from Chen et al. (2023), is applied to weigh the most relevant frames, aggregating them
346   into a single vector representation. The pooling layer is followed by Dropout with p = 0.3. This
347   same Dropout probability is also applied in the Transformer backbone. Finally, the resulting feature
348   map is processed by two linear layers to produce predicted probabilities for punch type and side
349   predictions, similar to the direct method described above. Layer normalisation was applied after the
350   linear projection and transformer layers to stabilise training and improve convergence.
351   The loss function, optimizer, and training strategy were the same as those used in the direct method,
352   with the only difference being the total number of epochs. For this approach, the model was trained
353   for 250 epochs.

467   number matches the one annotated as correct in the ground truth data) compared to 74.3% for the
468   two-stage approach.
469
470   6.3   R ESULTS D ISCUSSION
471
472   When comparing the direct and staged approaches, we observe that the direct method outperforms
473   the staged approach in both punch type and side classification, achieving higher overall accuracy
474   (94% vs. 91% for punch type classification and 99% vs. 94% for side classification). This could
475   be attributed to the fact that the direct approach allows the model to learn directly from the raw
476
      video frames without relying on imperfect intermediate representations (human pose keypoints in
      this case).
477
478   Interestingly enough, side classification is less accurate for the staged approach, despite the task
479   seeming straightforward. One possible explanation is that in some cases during a punch action,
480   from the viewpoint prevalent in the dataset, the left hand wrist may appear closer to the right side
481   of the video frame than the right hand wrist, and vice versa. This could confuse the temporal model
482
      used in the second step of the process.
483   Despite its lower accuracy demonstrated in our baseline, the staged approach offers practical advan-
484   tages. By leveraging pose estimation, it provides a modular pipeline that enables not only punch
485   recognition but also detailed analysis of biomechanics and shadowboxing technique. The quality
      of pose estimation plays a critical role in subsequent classification performance. Errors in keypoint


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      detection may directly influence the classification results. Nonetheless, the staged approach offers
487   valuable insights into the biomechanics of punches, potentially allowing for technique improve-
488   ment suggestions in computer-vision-based virtual trainer applications and more granular analysis
489   of movement. It is worth noting that fine-tuning of the pose estimation model on the dataset annota-
490   tion will likely improve the accuracy of the staged method.
491
      In practice, the staged approach can also be advantageous in other scenarios where detailed pose
492
      analysis is essential, such as injury prevention methods, where understanding the specifics of body
493   movement is just as important as the classification accuracy of the punch itself (Stenum et al., 2021).
494
495
      6.4   L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
496
497   The dataset features only front-facing videos of shadowboxing, which is sufficient for punch type
498   and side recognition, as well as for detecting some types of boxing mistakes. However, the data
499   cannot be viewed as a general dataset for shadowboxing processing since other angles of view are
500   not included; thus, some mistakes and intricate movements may be challenging to recognise. This
501   makes it an open area for the next version of the dataset to include such videos.
502   The work also raises the question of the optimal form of data representation: using imagery data di-
503   rectly for end-to-end event spotting pipelines or using a staged approach with intermediate keypoints
504   representations. Currently, we do not offer a ready answer and leave further research to the commu-
505   nity. It is likely that the answer will depend on particular details of one’s application priorities, such
506   as flexibility for auxiliary tasks addition, inference latency, and edge device performance.
507   Additionally, future work could explore the impact of fine-tuning the pose-estimation model specif-
508   ically on this dataset. This could provide insights into how much accuracy can be improved through
509   tailored optimisation. Another promising avenue is exploring temporal pose prediction to better cap-
510   ture the dynamic nature of boxing movements over time. Furthermore, investigating the use of 3D
511   pose predictions instead of the currently included 2D pose results may offer more comprehensive
512   and accurate analysis of movement in a three-dimensional space.
513
514   7     C ONCLUSION
515
516   In this work, we introduced ShadowPunch, a novel dataset designed to support advanced pose esti-
517   mation, action classification, and spotting tasks within the context of boxing. The dataset comprises
518   high-resolution videos with precise frame-level annotations for various punch types, providing a
519   valuable resource for research into fast-paced action recognition.
520
      We evaluated two key approaches: a direct method where models classify punch types and sides
521
      directly from video frames, and a staged approach that leverages pose estimation as an intermediate
522   step. Our results indicate that the direct method outperformed the staged approach in terms of overall
523   accuracy. However, the staged approach offers critical insights into body mechanics and technique,
524   making it particularly useful for detailed sports analysis.
525
      Our findings also highlighted the importance of reducing errors in pose estimation, as deviations
526
      in pose keypoints can significantly affect subsequent classifications. Future work could explore in-
527
      tegrating more sophisticated pose estimation models and action recognition techniques, potentially
528   leveraging temporal pose prediction and 3D pose predictions to enhance accuracy and comprehen-
529   siveness.
530
531
      The dataset and benchmarks set a foundation for further exploration in both sports science and video-
      based action spotting. By addressing the limitations identified in this study, researchers can develop
532
      more robust and versatile systems both sports science and video-based fast-paced action spotting.

      In our method of calculating frame displacement, we infer the punch type at the target frame and
651   compare it against the ground truth label. If the predicted punch type matches the target punch type,
652   we proceed to compute displacement for that event. Displacement is calculated by examining a
653   sequence of seven frames before and seven frames after the target frame (the target frame is labelled
654   as frame 0). If the predicted punch type in any of these surrounding frames matches the target
655   punch type and has a significantly higher probability than at frame 0 (i.e., greater than a predefined
656   threshold (θ = 0.15), the displacement is recorded based on that frame.
657   The displacement formula can be expressed as:
