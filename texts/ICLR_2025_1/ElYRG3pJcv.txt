009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Empowering LLMs to improve their performance through increased inference-
012            time computation is a crucial step in developing self-improving agents capable of
013            operating in open-ended natural language contexts. In this paper, we explore how
014            iteratively revising a chain of thoughts guided by information retrieval significantly
015            improves large language models’ reasoning ability in challenging tasks, while
016
               hugely mitigating hallucination. In particular, the proposed method — retrieval-
               augmented reflection (RaR) — revises the generation tokens step by step, leveraging
017
               multiple pieces of retrieved information relevant to the intermediate reasoning
018
               steps and the instruction. Applying RaR during inference-time to a various set of
019            language models substantially improves their performances on various reasoning
020            tasks; on relatively increasing scores by up to +16.4% on code generation, +11.6%
021            on mathematical reasoning, and 29.1% on embodied task planning. Moreover, we
022            find that with more inference-time computation given to the LLM for multi-times
023            retrieval-augmented reflection, the LLM can continuously improve on various
024            reasoning benchmarks. A small LM can surpass the performance of the LM with
025            more than 10 times parameters, when giving more computation cost.

054
      sophisticated long-horizon reasoning. Our intuition is that the hallucination within the intermediate
055   reasoning process could be alleviated through the help of outside knowledge. The resulting prompting
056   strategy, retrieval-augmented reflection (RaR), comprises two key ideas. Firstly, the initial zero-shot
057   CoT produced by LLMs along with the original task prompt will be used as queries to retrieve
058   relevant information that could help revise the possibly flawed CoT. Secondly, instead of retrieving
059   and revising with the full CoT and producing the final response at once, we devise a progressive
060   approach, where LLMs produce the response step-by-step following the CoT (a series of subtasks),
061   and only the current thought step will be revised based on the information retrieved with task prompt,
062   the current and the past CoTs. This strategy can be an analogy to the human reasoning process:
063
      we utilize outside knowledge to adjust our step-by-step thinking during complex long-horizon
      problem-solving (Holyoak & Morrison, 2012).
064
065   We observe that hallucinations in model outputs often originate from errors in earlier tokens, which
066   can propagate and lead to incorrect final results. To tackle this issue, we extend retrieval-augmented
067   generation to retrieval-augmented reflection. In RaR, the language model retrieves external infor-
068   mation to verify and revise the original generation. This modification allows the model to correct
069
      errors in previously generated tokens—something traditional generation pipelines cannot achieve.
      By adopting retrieval-augmented reflection, we enable the model to iteratively improve its reasoning
070
      process in real-time, making it highly scalable with increased inference-time computation. This
071
      approach resolves the limitation of not being able to revise erroneous tokens generated earlier in the
072   process, thus providing more reliable outputs without requiring model parameter adjustments (Ope-
073   nAI, 2024). Different from methods required reinforcement learning to perform better reasoning
074   during inference-time computation (OpenAI, 2024), our method does not require any modifications
075   to the original model parameters; it only needs to provide more inference-time computation tokens,
076   allowing the LM to automatically verify and revise the generation. As shown in Figure 1, RaR
077   demonstrates better scalability performance.
078   We evaluate Retrieval-augmented Reflection (RaR) on a wide collection of challenging long-horizon
079   tasks, including code generation, mathematical reasoning, embodied task planning, and creative
080   writing. We employ several LLMs of varied scales: GPT-3.5 (Brown et al., 2020), GPT-4 (OpenAI,
081   2023), Deepseek-Coder (Zhu et al., 2024), Llama-3 (AI@Meta, 2024) and Gemma (Team et al.,
082   2024). The results indicate that combing RaR with these LLMs elicits strong advantages over
083   vanilla CoT prompting and RAG approaches. In particular, we observe new state-of-the-art level
084   of performances across our selection of tasks when given the same maximum inference-time token
085   limitation: 1) code generation: ClassEval (+16.4%), HumanEval (+4.7%), HumanEval+ (+2.4%),
086
      MBPP (+2.5%), MBPP+ (+4.2%); 2) mathematical reasoning problems: GSM8K (+11.6%), and
      GSMHard (+3.0%); 3) Minecraft task planning (+2.2% on accuracy); 4) QA (+16.44% on accuracy).
087
      Our scaling experiments show that RaR can be scalable with more inference-time computation and
088
      model parameters. By allowing for more inference-time tokens, RaR can achieve up to +33.3%,
089   +12.2%, and +16.2% relative improvements in code generation, math reasoning, and task planning
090   benchmarks, respectively. Our additional ablation studies further confirm the crucial roles played
091   by the two key ingredients of RaR: revising CoT using RAG and progressive revision & generation.
092   This work reveals how can LLMs revise their reasoning process in a zero-shot fashion with the help
093   of outside knowledge, just as what humans do.

096   2   R ELATED W ORKS
097
098   Retrieval-augmented Generation (RAG). Recently, RAG has gained popularity for boosting the
099   performance of LLMs by guiding their generation process using the retrieved knowledge (Zhao
100   et al., 2023). Without updating model parameters that may be expensive (Lewis et al., 2020a) or
101   unstable (Ke et al., 2022b;a), RAG is a cost-effective way for LLMs to interact with the external
102   world (Gu et al., 2018; Lewis et al., 2020a). RAG is widely applied to downstream tasks, such as
103   code generation (Zhou et al., 2022b; Lu et al., 2022; Nashid et al., 2023), question answering (Baek
104
      et al., 2023; Siriwardhana et al., 2023), and creative writing (Wen et al., 2023; Asai et al., 2023).
105   Reasoning-enhanced RAG. Some recent works also leverage reasoning to enhance the performance
106   of RAG (Li et al., 2023b). For example, IRCoT (Trivedi et al., 2022) exploits CoT to generate better
107   queries for retrieval, IRGR (Ribeiro et al., 2022) performs iteratively retrieval to search for suitable
      premises for multi-hop QA, GEEK (Liu et al., 2023a) can choose to query external knowledge or


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      perform a single logical reasoning step in long-horizon generation tasks, and ITRG (Feng et al.,
109   2023a) performs retrieval based on the last-step generation. Active RAG (Jiang et al., 2023) also
110   utilizes reasoning to enhance the quality of language model retrieval for better completion of QA
111   tasks. These previous RAG methods simply adopt a single query to retrieve the knowledge for
112   question-answering tasks (Gao et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023b; Jiang et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023).
113   Our proposed RaR focuses on retrieval to refine LLM reasoning outputs and ensure consistent results.
114   The reasoning and retrieval processes are interlinked to improve both aspects, as shown in Figure
115   2. Our approach is evaluated across long-horizon content generation tasks such as code generation,
116   math reasoning, embodied planning, and creative writing.
117   Language Model for Reasoning. The advancement of reasoning in language models has seen
118   notable methodologies emerge since CoT was proposed by Wei et al. (2022), which showcased LMs’
119   ability to generate self-derived problem-solving strategies. This foundational work spurred further
120   innovations such as the least-to-most prompting (Zhou et al., 2022a), zero-shot CoT (Kojima et al.,
121   2022), self-consistency (Wang et al., 2022), zero-shot CoT without prompting (Wang & Zhou, 2024).
122   Moving beyond basic prompting, Creswell et al. (2022) introduced the Selection-Inference framework,
123
      while Zelikman et al. (2022) developed STaR to refine reasoning through model finetuning. Creswell
      & Shanahan (2022) proposed a faithful reasoning model, segmenting reasoning into dedicated steps,
124
      similar to Scratchpad’s approach by Nye et al. (2021) for enhancing multi-step computation. Tree-
125
      of-Thought (Yao et al., 2023) and Graph-of-Thought (Besta et al., 2023) also expand the reasoning
126   paths into a complex structure instead of linear CoT. These methods usually aim to improve the
127   reasoning ability of LLM by designing prompts or providing feedback from the environment to assist
128   in better planning and decision-making (Wang et al., 2023c; Yao et al., 2022; Shinn et al., 2023; Li
129   et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2023). However, RaR takes a different approach by using RAG to access
130   external knowledge that can help LLM with its reasoning process.

133   3     R ETRIEVAL AUGMENTED R EFLECTION
134
135   Our goal is to support reasoning and generation while mitigating hallucination when using LLMs.
136   To have satisfying performance on long-horizon tasks, two ingredients are indispensable. Firstly,
137   access to factual information can be facilitated by retrieval. Secondly, appropriate intermediate steps
138
      that outline a scratchpad to finish complex tasks, can be facilitated by CoT. Yet, a naive combination
      of the two would not necessarily yield improvements. Three questions still persist: (1) what is
139
      relevant information to retrieve; (2) how to effectively correct reasoning steps with relevant factual
140
      information; (3) can this combination be scalable with more inference-time computation. To better
141   appreciate our method and why our method can address these questions, we first provide a brief
142   introduction of basic RaR and interactive RaR.

239   Our hypothesis why our method can address the two problems mentioned at the beginning of this
240   section is as follows. Firstly, the most straightforward way to know what information will be used
241   in complex reasoning is to “see” the reasoning steps. Our approach leverages all the generated
242   thoughts along with the task prompt to provide more clues for more effective retrieval. Secondly,
243   some information cannot be directly retrieved, especially information related to the final answer to a
244   hard complex question. Instead, retrieval of information relevant to intermediate questions, which are
245   assumed to be easier, is more accessible. Thanks to the compositional nature of many reasoning tasks,
246   an iterative retrieval process could also be more effective. Thirdly, correcting potential hallucinations
247
      needs to be targeted. Revising a complete CoT with RAG could introduce errors at otherwise already-
      correct steps. Revising every step one by one could be more reliable. The first two points address
248
      question (1) and the last point addresses question (2). Quantitative evidence can be found in our
249
      ablation studies in Section 4.4.

      3.3   O PTIMIZING R A R BY S CALING I NFERENCE - TIME C OMPUTATION
252
253   In this section, we explore how to improve the reasoning performance of LLMs by scaling inference-
254   time computation. While the native approach to scaling inference-time performance typically
255   involves increasing the parameters of the LLM itself, we present an alternative method using Iterative
256   Retrieval-augmented Reflection (RaR) that scales computation by augmenting input and output tokens
257   iteratively.
258   Native Scaling via Model Size. The conventional approach to improving LLM performance, referred
259   to as DIRECT Scaling, relies on increasing the number of model parameters, as suggested by the
260   LLM scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020). Larger models generally exhibit better performance with
261   higher computational costs during inference. However, this method comes with significant drawbacks,
262   such as the increased burden of training large-scale models and the accompanying demand for vast
263   amounts of data (Kaplan et al., 2020).
264   Inference-time Scaling without Increasing Model Parameters. Recent research shows that
265   inference-time performance can be improved for LLMs with fixed parameters by increasing the
266   computational cost through additional input tokens or output tokens during inference (Snell et al.,
267   2024). Scaling input tokens involves extending the user prompt with more tokens. Few-shot CoT
268   adds more demonstrations (instruction-response pair) to the prompt, e.g., increasing from 1-shot
269   to 5-shot CoT, enables better contextual reasoning for complex tasks (Wei et al., 2022). RAG
      retrieves and adds more related documents to the user prompt. For instance, increasing the top-k


                                                             5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      documents from 1-shot RAG to 5-shot RAG can provide richer contextual information for reasoning.
271   Scaling output tokens involves generating more detailed outputs or sampling more generations. For
272   example, Self-Consistency will generate multiple outputs in parallel and merge them into a final
273   response (Wang et al., 2023a).
274
      Iterative RaR simultaneously scales both input and output tokens by performing multiple rounds of
275
      retrieval and reflective reasoning. Specifically, for n iterations, RaR adds n · top-k related documents
276
      to the input tokens and generates more than n-times the output tokens during reasoning. This iterative
277   process enables a gradual refinement of the reasoning process and response quality. As shown in our
278   experiments, the performance of RaR improves consistently with increasing computational cost.
279
280
      Scaling input and output tokens at inference time often encounters the long-context problem, wherein
      the fixed context length of LLMs restricts the number of tokens that can be processed (Brown et al.,
281
      2020). When the token count approaches the model’s maximum limit, performance degradation can
282
      occur (Li et al., 2024). RaR mitigates this issue by iteratively performing retrieval and reflection,
283   thereby avoiding a single, overly lengthy context and maintaining high performance.
284
285   It is worth noting that some approaches employ agent systems that utilize multiple prompts within a
286
      single LLM to create complex pipelines for answering questions (Yao et al., 2022; Gravitas, 2024).
      However, such methods are compositions of foundational techniques and are not directly comparable
287
      to standalone reasoning and generation methods like RaR. For this reason, we exclude agent-based
288
      approaches from our discussions and experiments.

526   5    D ISCUSSION ON L IMITATIONS AND C ONCLUSION
527
528   One limitation of this work is that the performance of RaR relies on the chain-of-thought reasoning
529   and in-context learning (or RAG) capability of the base LLM. Since this work does not involve
530   any model training, the capability of base LLM will not change when applying RaR. Despite RaR
531   achieves significant improvement on powerful LLMs such as GPT-3.5 and GPT-4, the effect on
532   smaller and weaker LLMs is questionable. Another limitation of this work is that the performance of
533   RaR also relies on the quality of the retrieved knowledge. Another limitation of this work is that the
534   performance of RaR also relies on the quality of the retrieved knowledge.
535   We have presented Retrieval Augmented Reflection (RAR), a simple yet effective prompting strategy
536   that synergies chain of thought (CoT) prompting and retrieval augmented generation (RAG) to
537   address the challenging long-horizon reasoning and generation tasks. Our key ideas involve revising
538   the zero-shot chain of thoughts produced by LLMs through RAG with the thoughts as queries, and
539   causally revising the thoughts & generating the response progressively. RaR, a zero-shot prompting
      approach, has demonstrated significant advantages over vanilla CoT prompting, RAG, and other

      A.1   C ODE G ENERATION
813
814   Benchmarks. We select HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021), HumanEval+ (Liu et al., 2023b),
815   MBPP (Austin et al., 2021), and MBPP+ (Liu et al., 2023b) as the code generation evaluation
816   benchmark. These benchmarks are commonly used to test the performance of code generation
817   models, which are briefly introduced below:
818
819
             • HumanEval consists of 164 Python programming problems, each with a function signature,
               docstring, body, and multiple unit tests (Chen et al., 2021).
820
821          • HumanEval+ includes the same programming problems as HumanEval, but with an addi-
822            tional 80 times more unit tests for each of the 164 problems (Liu et al., 2023b).
823          • MBPP is a collection of approximately 1,000 Python programming problems that are
824            intended to be solvable by beginner programmers. Each problem includes an English task
825            description, a code solution, and three automated test cases. We assess the sample test set
826            from index 11 to 175 (Austin et al., 2021).
827          • MBPP+ consists of 399 tasks (Liu et al., 2023b), which are a subset of the original MBPP
828            dataset. Additionally, MBPP+ includes extra unit tests for each of the 399 problems (35
829            times more than the original MBPP). We utilized the first 164 questions as our test set.
830
831
      These benchmarks encompass a wide range of programming problems, from simple function im-
      plementations to more complex algorithmic challenges, providing a robust testbed for assessing the
832
      generative capabilities of various models.
833
834   Metrics. We adopt the pass@k metric for evaluating the efficacy of various code generation
835   algorithms, following the methodology proposed by Chen et al. (2021) and extended by Liu et al.
836   (2023b). This metric quantifies the rate at which generated code snippets successfully execute and
837
      pass all test cases, where k represents the number of attempts or samples generated by the model
      for each problem. This approach allows us to rigorously assess the precision and reliability of
838
      code generation models in producing functionally correct code across a diverse set of programming
839
      challenges.
840
841   Baselines. To establish a comprehensive and equitable comparison landscape, we incorporate a suite
842   of baseline methods and diverse code generation models. Our baselines include the original code
843
      generation language models, referred to as DIRECT, and the Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)
      methodology with n retrieved examples, instantiated in both single-shot (1 shot) and multi-shot (5
844
      shots) configurations, as documented by Lewis et al. (2020b). Additionally, we examine the zero-shot
845
      CoT (CoT) approach, as conceptualized by Kojima et al. (2022), which simulates a step-by-step
846   reasoning process to facilitate complex problem-solving tasks under zero demonstration. To ensure
847   a fair comparison, none of the methods used examples from the benchmark as demonstrations for
848   in-context learning.
849
      The diversity of our evaluation is further enriched by testing across various language mod-
850
      els with differing capacities, including CodeLlama-7b (Rozière et al., 2023), along with Chat-
851
      GPT(gpt-3.5-turbo) (Ouyang et al., 2022), and the more advanced GPT-4(gpt-4) model (Ope-
852   nAI, 2023). Recognizing the potential format discrepancies in code outputs, especially considering
853   that models like gpt-3.5-turbo and gpt-4 may produce code in markdown format which is not
854   immediately executable, we implement post-processing steps to convert the original language model
855   outputs into a form that can be executed within a sandbox environment. This normalization ensures
856   that all models are evaluated under uniform execution conditions, thereby facilitating a fair and direct
857   comparison of their code generation capabilities. Through this methodological framework, we aim to
858   provide a detailed and nuanced understanding of the performance landscape across a spectrum of
859   LLM-driven code generation approaches.
860   RAG Settings. RaR leverages the capabilities of Retrieval-Augmented Generation methods, which
861   enhance the performance of language models by integrating external knowledge sources. Specifically,
862   we employed the codeparrot/github-jupyter dataset as our primary search vector library.
863   This dataset is a comprehensive compilation of 452k markdown and code pairs, meticulously extracted
      from Jupyter notebooks hosted on GitHub BigQuery, representing a rich repository of programming

      A.2     M ATHEMATICAL R EASONING
868
869   Benchmarks. Our evaluation framework for assessing mathematical reasoning capabilities leverages
870   two primary benchmarks: the GSM8K dataset, which comprises over 8,000 multi-step mathematical
871   problems (Cobbe et al., 2021), and the GSM-HARD dataset, an adaptation of GSM8K where
872   numbers in the questions are replaced with larger values to increase problem complexity (Gao et al.,
873   2022). This study employs the PAL methodology to scrutinize the mathematical reasoning results,
874   involving the utilization of Large Language Models (LLMs) to parse natural language problems,
875   generate intermediary programmatic solutions, and subsequently execute these solutions via a Python
876   interpreter. The test set for each benchmark consists of samples ranging from index 1 to 200. Uniquely,
877
      our approach does not use any examples for in-context learning, differing from the original PAL
      methods.
878
879   Metrics and Baselines. Accuracy serves as our principal metric for evaluation, aligning with the
880   established metric for the GSM8K benchmark. Each question undergoes three execution attempts,
881   with the average score recorded as the final result. The baselines, including DIRECT, CoT, RAG
882   (1 shot), and RAG (5 shots), are consistent with those outlined in code generation, facilitating a
883
      comprehensive and comparative analysis across different code generation benchmarks. The RAG
      settings are consistent with the code generation tasks.

889
890   Benchmarks. The complexity and vast item interconnectivity within the open-world Minecraft
891   present an ideal testbed for evaluating the LLM’s capability to generate long-horizon plans (Yuan
892   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023c;b). With thousands of items and intricate relationships between them,
893
      obtaining a specific item in survival mode from scratch may involve dozens of intermediate items and
      their quantitative relationships, such as crafting 1 crafting table from 4 planks. This setting rigorously
894
      tests the planning abilities of LLMs instead of low-level control policies (Cai et al., 2023b; Baker
895
      et al., 2022; Cai et al., 2023a; Lifshitz et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024). Moreover, Wang et al. (2023b)
896   have identified instances of hallucinations about Minecraft knowledge in OpenAI’s ChatGPT and a
897   general scarcity of Minecraft-related knowledge in open-source language models, making this task a
898   suitable benchmark for assessing the RAG algorithm’s effectiveness.
899
      The planning prompts are aligned with those used in DEPS (Wang et al., 2023c), structured as Python
900
      templates and evaluated using MC-TextWorld as detailed by Lin et al. (2023). A set of 100 tasks
901
      were randomly selected for the test set, ranging from simple objectives like obtaining a crafting table
902   to more complex goals such as crafting an iron helmet and even challenging making an enchanting
903   table. The task instruction is formulated as:

908            • Give you nothing in the inventory, generate a step-by-step plan for the task of obtaining a
909              {placeholder:diamond_pickaxe} boat in Minecraft survival mode, and describe the object
910              Minecraft item and its number at every step. For every step, start with ’STEP’ as start.
911
912   There are over 100 tasks involving different Minecraft items.

915   was cleaned and formatted into markdown text, then segmented into trunks not exceeding 2000 tokens

1227   In our experiment, we used RAG-5 as the baseline, which retrieves 5 content sources (commonly
1228   the default choice for most current RAG algorithms). We also set an upper limit on RaR retrieval
1229   times, ensuring that RaR does not retrieve more than 5 contents. Therefore, RaR does not exceed the
1230   standard RAG algorithm in terms of content retrieval.
1231   Compared to including all 5 retrieved contents at once in context for generation in RAG-5, RaR
1232   incorporates one external content at a time to reduce prompt length. This approach prevents exceeding
1233   the language model’s context length limitation that may occur when inputting all knowledge into the
1234   prompt with RAG-5. Using only one external content per revision in RaR avoids issues related to
1235   excessive content length and allows more token space for generating longer content.
1236   Additionally, we utilized the Google search engine directly for RaR searches and compared time
1237   consumption between RaR and RAG-5 when creating creative writing prompts. Our findings indicate
1238   that while increased search time plays a role, more time is consumed during crawling and generation
1239   processes. And RaR does not have a significant efficiency gap compared to RAG-5. The detailed time
1240   consumption of every method is listed in Table 7. However, since RaR must be processed causally, it
