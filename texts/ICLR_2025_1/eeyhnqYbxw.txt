009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Transformer models have revolutionized machine learning, and the theoretical
012            underpinnings behind their success are only now starting to be explored. In this
013            work, we analyze the performance and robustness of transformers by considering
014            the attention mechanism as a graph operator, focusing on the geometry of attention
015            maps viewed as weighted graphs. Specifically, we investigate the role of Ricci
016
               curvature, a metric closely tied to graph spectral properties and system robustness,
               in shaping the training dynamics and robustness of transformers. Our theoretical
017
               analysis establishes a link between Ricci curvature and the convergence of gradient
018
               descent on transformers, and consequently, their training and fine tuning. We
019            also show that a higher frequency of more positive values in the Ricci curvature
020            distribution of attention graphs, therefore more system robustness, leads to more
021            robust transformers, highlighting the impact of curvature on the robustness of
022            transformers. Leveraging these insights, we propose an efficient regularization
023            method to train curvature-adjusted transformers. Supporting our theoretical find-
024            ings, experiments show that our proposed attention curvature manipulation can
025            improve the learning speed, performance, or generalizability of vision and lan-
026            guage transformers. Additionally, our observations point to a trade-off between
027
               their performance and robustness. This work demonstrates that the geometry of
               the attention map provides a theoretically elegant and computationally versatile
028
               framework for analyzing and manipulating transformers training, generalization,
029
               performance, and robustness, opening new avenues for designing models using
030            geometric concepts.

033
034   Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) have become the cornerstone of modern machine learning,
035   excelling in tasks across natural language processing (Brown et al., 2020; Devlin et al., 2018) and
036   computer vision (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021b; Touvron et al., 2021). Recent studies
037   have begun to explain the inner workings of transformers, analyzing their expressive power (Yun
038   et al., 2020; Zaheer et al., 2020), their generalization properties (Li et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024),
039
      in-context learning (Von Oswald et al., 2023), learning dynamics (Abbe et al., 2024; Dovonon et al.,
      2024), and robustness (Bhojanapalli et al., 2021; Shao et al., 2021). A rigorous understanding of
040
      how transformers process their input remains elusive, especially through mathematical tools offering
041
      practical solutions. To address this gap, we analyze transformers by treating attention mechanisms as
042   graph operators and exploring for the first time the geometry of attention maps as weighted graphs.
043   Focusing on graph Ricci curvature (Bauer et al., 2017; Ollivier, 2009), a well-established metric
044   closely tied to graph spectral properties and system robustness (Bauer et al., 2011; Pouryahya et al.,
045   2017), our findings reveal its connection to the training dynamics and robustness of transformers.
046

      3    G RAPH GEOMETRY OF LEARNING REPRESENTATIONS IN TRANSFORMERS
148
149
      Attention mechanisms in transformer models can be viewed as graph operators, where the tokens
150   serve as nodes and the attention scores as edge weights in a complete graph. The geometric properties
151   of this attention graph inherently shape the representation space, with the eigenvalues and eigenvectors
152   dictating the direction and scale of transformations applied to each input. This, in turn, determines
153   the geometry of the representations that the transformer learns. Our analysis demonstrates that Ricci
154   curvature values in the attention graph are linked to the convergence rate during training as well as
155   the robustness of transformers to input perturbations.
156   Informally stating our main results, in this section, we explain how viewing the attention mechanism
157   as a graph allows us to reach these findings. We adopt two complementary perspectives, combining a
158   mathematical one that connects our graph-based analysis to the training dynamics of transformers,
159   and a physics-based one that interprets the transformer as a system of interacting particles. The
160   mathematical perspective links the ORC of the attention graph to the convergence of gradient descent-
161   based optimization, while the physics perspective explores the statistical mechanics of transformers,
      drawing insights from the interplay between graph geometry, dynamics, and robustness in networked


                                                         3

162
      systems. Together, these viewpoints offer a comprehensive understanding of how the geometry of
163   attention influences the transformer behavior, as we elaborate in sections 4.1 and 5 with further
164   technical details and empirical observations.
165
166
167   Geometry of attention and training transfromers. Transformer models are typically over-
168   parameterized (Fan et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2021a; Wang & Tu, 2020), which leads to a non-convex
169   optimization landscape (Bassily et al., 2018; Choromanska et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2022). In such cases,
170   fast convergence of the loss minimization relies on the Polyak-Łojasiewicz (PL) condition (Bassily
171
      et al., 2018; Polyak, 1963; Liu et al., 2022). We demonstrate that the probability of satisfying this
      condition is connected to the attention graph’s eigenvalues, which are in turn linked to its ORC. This
172
      relationship establishes ORC as a key proxy for understanding convergence properties in transformer
173
      optimization. This is implied from Lemma 4.2, which states that a larger smallest eigenvalue in the
174   attention map corresponds to faster convergence of gradient descent on the transformer. Meanwhile,
175   a monotonic function of the Ricci curvature controls the minimum eigenvalue of the attention map.
176   It follows that the ORC is linked to the convergence rate in transformers training –a link that we
177   empirically explore in Section 5. In Section 4.1 we elaborate on the theoretical steps leading to this
178   finding, and the full proof and assumptions are included in Appendix A. This result introduces a
179   novel geometric perspective on loss optimization in transformers, offering new avenues for improving
180   their training processes.

      Graph geometry, statistical mechanics, and robustness of transformers. Extending our geomet-
      ric analysis beyond training dynamics and adapting a physics perspective, we interpret the attention
184
      graph as a system of interacting particles, where tokens represent particles and attention scores
185
      quantify inter-particle interaction strengths. In particular, the output of a deep transformer could be
186   formulated as the solution to the continuous-time approximation of attention dynamics through a
187   forward pass, where each attention layer is modeled as a time snapshot of particle interactions. The
188   dynamics of this particle system, detailed in Appendix B, reveal how token representations evolve
189   through attention layers. This analogy allows us to apply principles from statistical mechanics and
190   dynamical systems to transformers, inheriting properties of networked systems that propagate signals
191   across interconnected units. A crucial property of such networked systems is their robustness to
192   signal perturbation, which for transformers translates to maintaining performance on noisy inputs.
193   This robustness is linked to the system entropy and, consequently, to Ricci curvature (Demetrius &
194
      Manke, 2005; Pouryahya et al., 2017), providing a theoretical basis for using curvature to assess and
      enhance transformer robustness. As the model processes inputs through a forward pass, we observe
195
      shifts in the ORC distribution, trending toward more positive values or reducing the frequency of
196
      smaller curvature values. This observation is in line with previous findings on emergent clustering
197   in attention layers through a forward pass (Geshkovski et al., 2023) (see Appendix B), due to the
198   connection between ORC and weight clustering, which we explain in Appendix C. We discuss the
199   details of how the attention dynamics leads to the shifts in the ORC distribution in Appendix B, where
200   we also show this phenomenon on additional transformer models. These shifts, visualized in Figure
201   3, suggest a strong connection between attention mechanism dynamics and the geometric properties
202   that influence transformer robustness. Thus, Ricci curvature emerges as a multifaceted concept in
203   transformer analysis: it informs training dynamics, serves as a geometric signature of robustness and
204   performance, and offers a promising tool for model improvement. Our empirical results in Section 5
205
      further elucidate these diverse roles and their practical implications.

283   4.2   T RAINING CURVATURE - ADJUSTED TRANSFORMERS
284
      Section 3 discusses the novel connections we establish between attention maps curvature and various
285   aspects of transformer behavior. This raises an intriguing possibility: manipulating the curvature
286   distribution of the attention graph could allow us to adjust their behavior and improve their capabilities.
287   A well-established tool to diffuse the curvature over a manifold is the Ricci flow (Hamilton, 1988),
288   and its discrete variation could be applied to flatten the graph and reduce the frequency of edges
289   with highly negative curvature (representing unstable networks) (Jin et al., 2008; Ni et al., 2019).
290   However, each iteration of the discrete Ricci flow requires computing the curvature distribution, and
291   hence it is computationally taxing to incorporate in the training. To overcome this challenge via a
292   computationally efficient proxy, we introduce a simple, efficient, and effective method to manipulate
293
      the ORC distribution of attention maps through regularization by a function of the variance of
      attention scores. Aiming to either promote or reduce the frequency of low-curvature edges, this
294
      regularization strategy not only provides a means to empirically validate our theory, but also offers a
295
      practical mechanism to enhance the training and robustness of transformers by adjusting its curvature.
296   The experiments in this section are performed using L-ViT and BERT-Tiny, described in Appendix D.
297

      Building on the insights from interpreting attention as a particle system as inroduced in Section 3
350
      and using the well-established connection between ORC and system robustness (Bauer et al., 2017;
351   Ollivier, 2009), we now leverage the geometry of the attention graph, specifically through the ORC
352   perspective, to explain the robustness properties of transformers. We conduct a series of experiments
353   exploring the relationship between ORC and robustness in transformers. The discussions in sections
354   2 and 3 suggest that more positive ORC values, should enhance the robustness of transformers. We
355   empirically investigate this link through two focused studies: first, by examining the curvature and
356   robustness of pre-trained transformers to input perturbation; and second, by assessing the impact of
357   the ORC distribution on model forgetfulness. In line with the theoretical insight, our experiments
358   confirm that more robust transformers feature a higher frequency of larger ORC values in their
359
      attention graphs. Additional experiments in Appendix E further support these findings.

468   7      C ONCLUSIONS , LIMITATIONS , AND ETHICAL CONSIDERATIONS
469   We presented an analysis of transformers through the lens of graph geometry, showing that the Ricci
470   curvature of attention maps is a key factor in training dynamics and robustness of transformers. Our
471   results establish a connection between the Ricci curvature of the attention graph and the convergence
472   behavior of gradient descent in transformers. Meanwhile, we show that more positive attention
473   curvature values correspond to more robust transformers. Our findings also indicate a trade-off
474   between performance and robustness. Informed by these findings, we introduced an efficient curvature-
475
      adjusted regularization method that allows us to improve training or generalizability in transformers.
      Furthermore, we examine transformers’ reliance on attention edges associated with different curvature
476
      values.
477
478   Limitations. A potential limitation of this work lies in the inherent difficulty of theoretical analysis
479   of transformer models employed in practice and direct empirical validation of theoretical results
480   derived in simplified settings. While our theory employs idealized transformers with simplifying
481
      assumptions, such simplifications are well-established traditions, due to the near intractability of fully
      characterizing practical transformer models. Furthermore, our empirical results are well-aligned with
482
      our theoretical discussion, confirming that our theory can extend to practical settings.
483
484   Ethical considerations. Our paper is theoretical and foundational in nature, but addresses the
485   success of transformers, a family of machine learning models that has significant ethical considerations
      which are well-documented and are part of active research in the community.

      Discussion on the assumptions. The self-attention mechanism is in fact a common setup for
780
      transformers, hence the only constraint assumption A0 imposes beyond common practice is sharing
781
      the projection matrix between keys and queries. This yields a symmetric attention, while restricting
782   the keys and queries projections to the column space of a shared weight matrix. Assumptions A3
783   and A5 are purely technical assumptions for our proof. Regarding Assumption A4, first note that the
784   order in which the weight matrices are combined to parameterize F with θ can be completely flexible.
785   Therefore, it suffices if assumption A4 holds for any arrangement of the weights in θ. Despite this
786   flexibility, being simultaneously diagonalizable restricts the degrees of freedom in M and ∇θ M . This
787   assumption implies that the two matrices share an eigenstructure, though they could have different
788   eigenvalues. From a geometric perspective, this assumption limits the degrees of freedom that the
789   changes in M have at each gradient step to the directions of its eigenvectors. While this is a restrictive
790
      assumption, we should keep in mind that transformers are often over-parameterized with a high
      degree of freedom, and hence the assumption is still plausible. Furthermore, we assume A4 for
791
      technical reasons in order to simplify the proof of the theorem, and otherwise, the assumption is not
792
      a conceptual necessity. In other words, while assuming A4 facilitates our proof, it is only a tool to
793   simplify a step of the proof, not the only tool to show the main conclusion, and the implications may
794   still hold without it.

       F    R ELATED WORK
1189
1190
       In this work, we investigate the connections between the geometry of attention maps in transformers,
1191   their training, performance, and robustness properties. While related work in transformers, geometric
1192   deep learning, and robustness is referenced throughout the paper where relevant, we now provide
1193   additional discussion of the most related works in each area here.
1194
1195   Transformers and attention mechanism. Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) have revolutionized
1196   deep learning, achieving state-of-the-art performance across various domains (Brown et al., 2020;
1197   Devlin et al., 2018; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020). Recent works have begun to shed light on their
1198   inner workings, providing insights into their expressive power (Zaheer et al., 2020), generaliza-
1199   tion properties (Li et al., 2023), in-context learning (Von Oswald et al., 2023), learning dynamics
1200   (Abbe et al., 2024; Dovonon et al., 2024), and robustness characteristics (Bhojanapalli et al., 2021;
1201
       Shao et al., 2021). However, many questions regarding the fundamental principles governing the
       behavior of transformers remain unanswered. Our work contributes to this growing body of re-
1202
       search by investigating the connection between the geometry of attention maps and the properties of
1203
       transformers.
1204
1205   Graph Ricci curvature. Ricci curvature captures the deviation of the local geometry from Eu-
1206   clidean space (Bauer et al., 2017). When extended to discrete structures like graphs, it has proven to
1207   be a powerful tool for various deep learning tasks (Di Giovanni et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Southern
1208   et al., 2023; Topping et al., 2021). We employ the Ollivier-Ricci curvature (ORC) (Ollivier, 2009), an
1209   optimal transport formulation of Ricci curvature on graphs, to study the geometry of attention maps
1210   in transformers. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to explore this connection.
1211
1212   Curvature and robustness. Ricci curvature has been linked to the robustness of systems
1213   (Pouryahya et al., 2017), owing to its relationship with entropy. This connection is highlighted
1214   by a fundamental result from optimal transport theory (Lott & Villani, 2009), which provides bounds
1215
       on entropy based on a lower bound of the Ricci curvature. The Fluctuation Theorem (Evans et al.,
       1993) further establishes a positive correlation between the robustness and entropy of a system,
1216
       implying that robustness and curvature are positively correlated (Pouryahya et al., 2017). Leveraging
1217
       this connection, we explore the implications of the ORC distribution for the robustness of vision
1218   and language transformers. In summary, our key contribution is uncovering insightful patterns in
1219   the curvature distribution of attention maps that evolve during training and through the layers of
1220   transformers, establishing a connection between the geometry of attention and the properties of these
1221   models. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to explore this connection, opening up
1222   new avenues for developing more robust and interpretable transformers.
