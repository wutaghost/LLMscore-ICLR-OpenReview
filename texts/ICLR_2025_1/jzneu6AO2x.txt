009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper addresses the utilization of hyperbolic geometry within a Prototype
012            Learning framework. Specifically, we introduce Riemannian optimization for Hy-
013            perbolic Prototypical Networks (RHPN), a novel approach that leverages Prototype
014            Learning on Riemannian manifolds applied to the Poincaré ball. RHPN capitalizes
015            on the efficiency and effectiveness of updating prototypes during training, cou-
016            pled with a regularization term crucial to boost the performances. We setup an
017
               extensive experimentation that shows that RHPN is able to outperform the state-
               of-the-art in Prototype Learning, both in low and high dimensions, extending the
018
               impact of hyperbolic spaces to a wider range of scenarios.

021   1   I NTRODUCTION
022
023   In recent years, there has been a surge of interest among researchers in exploring the embeddings of
024   data onto specific manifolds. This choice can enable the use of metric elements (such as angles and
025   distances) that are particularly suitable to shape the underlying similarity between data, directly in
026   the representation space.
027
      In this context, Prototype Learning (PL) has shown promising results and proved to be a viable
028
      alternative to more conventional approaches in various domains, such as image classification Yang
029   et al. (2018); Chen et al. (2019); Mettes et al. (2019), few-shot learning Snell et al. (2017); Dong
030   & Xing (2018), and zero-shot learning Snell et al. (2017); Dong & Xing (2018). The core idea of
031   PL is to build a representation of the target classes within the embedding space (i.e. the prototypes),
032   enabling the utilization of metric information to compare new examples with the prototypes and
033   infer the probability of predicting a given class.
034
      Identifying the optimal prototype for a class poses a non-trivial challenge and leveraging prior in-
035   formation on the data can significantly improve the performances. For instance, when the labels are
036   organized hierarchically, changing the geometry used for the embeddings may substantially improve
037   results Landrieu & Garnot (2021); Fonio et al. (2023); Ghadimi Atigh et al. (2021).
038
      When the data exhibit a latent hierarchical structure, it is reasonable to suggest the usage of geome-
039
      tries capable of representing more effectively inter-example distances. Indeed, it is well known that
040
      the standard Euclidean geometry does not help in adequately represent trees Linial et al. (1995),
041   while hyperbolic geometries are provably better equipped for dealing with this kind of data Sala
042   et al. (2018); Nickel & Kiela (2018).
043
044
      In recent years, there has been a surge of interest in the neural networks community about leveraging
      the unique properties of non-Euclidean geometries. This exploration began with the seminal work
045
      by Ganea et al. (2018), and further studied by Ryohei et al. (2021), Van Spengler van Spengler et al.
046
      (2023), and Gulcehre Gulcehre et al. (2018).
047
048   For what it concerns PL, there have been explorations into non-Euclidean PL within specific data
049   domains Fonio et al. (2023); Hamzaoui et al. (2024); Khrulkov et al. (2020), while tasks such as
050
      image classification remain relatively unexplored. The main work tackling the image classification
      task with an hyperbolic prototypical approach is by Ghadimi Atigh et al. (2021), where the authors
051
      prove the effectiveness of hyperbolic manifolds when the embedding space is low-dimensional.
052
053   A key characteristic of prototype learning (PL) methods based on neural networks is how prototypes
      are positioned within the embedding space. Some studies Snell et al. (2017) initialize the prototypes

073   In this study, we extend this line of research by proposing Riemannian optimization on Hyperbolic

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
090
      Prototype Learning Prototypical networks are the deep generalization of learning Vector Quan-
091
      tization machines Somervuo & Kohonen (1999) and nearest centroid classifiers Tibshirani et al.
092
      (2002).
093
094   In most of the approaches, the prototypes are defined as centroids of the representations Snell et al.
095   (2017), positioned a priori Mettes et al. (2019); Fonio et al. (2023); Long et al. (2020) or learnt
096
      alongside the training Yang et al. (2018); Landrieu & Garnot (2021). In particular the work from
      Landrieu & Garnot (2021) introduces the importance of updating the prototypes alongside the train-
097
      ing phase and the relevance of adding hierarchical information. On the other hand, Mettes et al.
098
      (2019) introduces a non-Euclidean geometry in PL, i.e. the hyperspherical prototypical networks,
099   keeping the prototypes fixed on the hypersphere, maximizing the cosine separation among them.
100   They also highlight the importance of adding hierarchical information in this non-Euclidean con-
101   text, as investigated also by Fonio et al. (2023). Our approach extends the effort of updating the
102   prototypes on hyperbolic manifolds, defining them as parameters of the network. This approach
103   overcomes the computational issue in calculating the hyperbolic centroid, which is particularly de-
104   manding Mettes et al. (2024), and provides better performances than using fixed prototypes as the
105   existing methods do.
106
107   Hyperbolic Representation Learning Hyperbolic manifolds are claimed to represent hierarchi-
      cal data with arbitrarily low distortion Sala et al. (2018). For what concerns the hyperbolic mod-

      els, five different ones have been defined: Poincaré ball, Lorentz model (Hyperboloid), Poincarè
109   half-plane model, hemisphere model, Beltrami-Klein disk. In the following, we will focus on the
110   Poincaré ball as done by Guo et al. (2022), Khrulkov et al. (2020), van Spengler et al. (2023);
111   Long et al. (2020). While not central to our endeavor, it is worth noting that several works have been
112   based on the Lorentz model Law et al. (2019); Nickel & Kiela (2018), with a comparison of the
113   numerical stability of the Poincaré ball and of the Lorentz model provided in Mishne et al. (2023).
114
      Hyperbolic representations have been also studied in the context of few-shot learning, where some
115   works highlighted the ability of models built on hyperbolic spaces to outperform the state-of-the-art
116   Khrulkov et al. (2020); Hamzaoui et al. (2024). Among these, Khrulkov et al. (2020) gained par-
117   ticular relevance in the recent literature, as it presented the first hyperbolic prototypical framework.
118   However, it is worth noting that this framework is meant for few-shot learning. The adaptation of
119   the proposed method to the image classification task is not considered, nor is it trivial. The use of
120   centroids as prototypes in PL has been proposed by Guerriero et al. (2018), but there is no trace of
121   adaptation to a hyperbolic setting. A few works have tackled the problem of exploiting hyperbolic
122   geometries for image classification. Ghadimi Atigh et al. (2021) pioneered this approach, while Yue
123
      et al. (2024) explored the impact of changing the temperature parameter in a contrastive loss when
      exploring a hyperbolic space. Ghadimi Atigh et al. (2021) introduces a method with fixed ideal pro-
124
      totypes positioned on the boundary of the Poincaré ball, which is conceptually at an infinite distance
125
      from the center of the hyperbole. To overcome the problems derived from placing the prototypes in
126   this way, the authors introduce the usage of the Busemann distance to make it possible to compare a
127   point on the manifold and the prototypes.
128
129
      In our work we place the prototypes within the Poincaré ball (i.e., not on the boundary), but allow
      them to be updated to better capture the structure of the data distribution.

193   Yang et al. (2018) and Landrieu & Garnot (2021) introduced the idea of extending deep networks
194   to learn prototypes by embedding the prototype representations as network parameters. In this sec-
195   tion we introduce RHPN, which extends this idea to work with non-Euclidean geometries and, in
196
      Section 5.2, we show how crucial aspects of hyperbolic embeddings impact on the performances.
197   Our methodology involves extracting the output from a backbone network, such as a ResNet18, and
198   projecting it onto the Poincaré ball. Subsequently, distances from class prototypes are computed.
199   These distances are interpreted as a probability distribution using softmax activation, which is then
200   employed in a cross-entropy loss function for learning purposes.

242
243   We tested RHPN over 4 datasets and compared its results against 4 methods that use PL: HPS ex-
244   ploits an hyperspherical geometry Mettes et al. (2019) with fixed prototypes; CHPS exploits an
245   hyperspherical geometry and optimizes a similarity-based cross-entropy loss Fonio et al. (2023),
246   with fixed prototypes; ECL exploits a Euclidean geometry and optimizes a distance-based cross-
247   entropy loss, updating the prototypes Landrieu & Garnot (2021). HBL exploits an hyperbolic ge-
248   ometry Ghadimi Atigh et al. (2021) with fixed prototypes. As a further baseline, XE exploits a
249
      traditional training minimizing a cross-entropy loss. To the best of our knowledge, RHPN is the first
      non-Euclidean method where prototypes are not fixed. We refer to RHPN for our proposed method-
250
      ology without regularization (λ = 0) and RHPN* for the best λ value. In our experiments, for RHPN
251
      we used clip value equal to 1, curvature of the space fixed to 1, temperature in Eq.5 γ = 10. For
252   the slope parameter, after a hyperparameter optimization we have kept 0.1 for the first dimension of
253   each dataset and 0.01 from the second dimension on. For what concerns the backbone, each method
254   trained a ResNet18 from scratch. Further details about the experiments are available in appendix A.
255
      For what concerns the datasets, we have chosen benchmark datasets in Computer Vision for Fine-

      6           C ONCLUSIONS
518
      In conclusion, this study has introduced a methodology for dynamically updating the prototypes
519
      during the training process within the context of hyperbolic representation learning. RHPN extends
520
      the effectiveness of hyperbolic manifolds to any embedding dimension, leveraging the importance
521   of controlling the norm of the embeddings. We validate our findings through a wide range of exper-
522   iments.
523
524
      For future work, we plan to leverage background knowledge to improve the initialization and posi-
      tioning of prototypes during training. Additionally, we intend to explore other geometries, as RHPN
525
      is flexible and not restricted to the Poincaré model.

635   A.1   E XPERIMENTAL DETAILS
636
637   RHPN was trained for 200 epochs with SGD, using a learning rate of 0.1, weight decay 0.001,
638   momentum 0.9, and linear learning rate scheduler at epochs 60, 120 and 160. The backbone used
639
      was a ResNet18. As discussed in section4, to update the prototypes we used RSGD Bonnabel
      (2013) with learning rate 0.001 and momentum 0.9. The batch size was set to 128 for Aircraft
640
      and Cifar100Krizhevsky et al. (2009) and to 64 for the other datasets. The embedding dimension
641
      was set to 8 for Cifar10, 64 for Cifar100 and AircraftMaji et al. (2013), and 128 for CubWah et al.
642   (2011) and CarsKrause et al. (2015). The choice of the datasets favored the ones that sported an
643   accompanying hierarchy on the label set, as these kind of datasets has been suggested Khrulkov
644   et al. (2020) to better match the inductive bias imposed by learning in hyperbolic geometries. We
645   used the Geoopt library Kochurov et al. (2020) to implement the hyperbolic operations. Following
646   the insights provided by Guo et al. (2022), Hamzaoui et al. (2024) and Yue et al. (2024), we apply a
647   clipping of the features with clipping value equal to 1, before projecting them onto the Poincaré ball
      and we use a temperature γ = 10.

      The experiments were run on a cluster with 4 ARM machine, which consists of Ampere Altra Q80-
649   30 CPU (80-core Arm Neoverse N1), 512GB of memory, 2 x NVIDIA A100 GPU (40GB vram).
650   We used geoopt [34] to implement the hyperbolic operations.

652   A.2   H YPERPARAMETERS SELECTION
653
654   In our experiments, we tried to replicate the settings in competitor papers when possible. Specifi-
655   cally, the basic configuration of RHPN has been selected to match the training settings from Landrieu
656   & Garnot (2021). We did run a few exploratory experiment to evaluate better settings, but we rapidly
657   found out that the given setting was already a very good one.
658   The most challenging method to reproduce was HBLGhadimi Atigh et al. (2021). To reproduce it,
659   we used the official repository of the paper. For CUB and Cifar100 datasets, we adopted the settings
660   provided in the original paper as these datasets were already part of the experimentation therein.
661   The only change with respect to this setting, was the adoption of a ResNet18 instead of a ResNet32,
662   which was necessary to ensure a fair comparison with other methods. This change explains why
663   some of the results in our experiments do not match the ones in the original paper. The comparison
664   remain fair since all methods have been tested using the same backbone networks. Also, the main
665
      results from the Ghadimi Atigh et al. (2021) paper remain valid: the HBL method continues to
      outpeform HPS in low dimensional spaces.
666
667   Cars and Aircraft were not discussed in the papers that introduced competitor methods. In these
668   cases, for each dataset, we started with the same hyper-parameters we adopted for the dataset that
669   was most similar in terms of number of classes: for Cars we adopted a setting similar to CUB, for
670   Aircraft we adopted a setting similar to Cifar100. We then finetuned the slope parameter for HBL
671
      over a separate validation set, resulting in a slope of 0.001 for Cars and of 0.01 for Aircraft.
672   The slope parameter of RHPN was set testing a few values over a separate validation set. Specifically,
673   we tried values of λ in {0, 0.01, 0.1, 0.3}. Since the similarity in terms of number of classes between
674   CUB and Cars and between Aircrafts and Cifar100, we finetuned λ on CUB and Aircrafts and
675   adopted the results over Cars and Cifar100 respectively.
