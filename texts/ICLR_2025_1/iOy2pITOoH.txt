009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This work introduces Spark Transformer, an architectural variant of the Trans-
012            former model that drastically reduces the FLOPs count while maintaining com-
013            parable quality and an identical parameter count. This reduction is achieved by
014            introducing sparse activations in both the feedforward network (FFN) and the At-
015            tention mechanism. In the FFN, this sparsity engages only a subset of parame-
016
               ters for each input. In the Attention mechanism, it limits the number of tokens
               that each token attends to. We achieve this sparsity through statistical top-k, a
017
               lightweight approximate algorithm that is well-suited for accelerator hardware and
018
               minimizes training slowdown. Furthermore, Spark Transformer incorporates ded-
019            icated predictors to identify the activated entries. These predictors are formed
020            by allocating a portion of the model’s parameters and are trained jointly with the
021            rest of the model. This approach distinguishes Spark Transformer from existing
022            methods that introduce sparsity and predictors post-training, which often leads to
023            increased training costs, additional model parameters, and complex modifications
024            to the model architecture. Our Spark Transformer, pretrained using the Gemma
025            2 recipe, achieves competitive performance on standard benchmarks while ex-
026            hibiting significant sparsity. Specifically, it utilizes only 8% nonzeros in the FFN
027
               activation and attends to a maximum of 256 tokens. This results in a 3.1× re-
               duction in FLOPs, yielding a 1.70× speedup for prefill and a 1.79× speedup for
028
               decoding on a 16-core CPU VM.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The machine learning landscape has witnessed a surge in large-scale Transformer models (Anil
      et al., 2023; Almazrouei et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024; Adler et al., 2024), pushing the boundaries
034
      of language understanding and generation. However, the pursuit of scale is often constrained not by
035
      limitations in model quality, but by the escalating computational costs (Sharir et al., 2020; Patterson
036   et al., 2021) associated with increasing parameter counts (Kaplan et al., 2020). This challenge
037   is further exacerbated by the development of models that handle increasingly long context inputs
038   (Reid et al., 2024), where computational demands grow proportionally with context length.
039
      Sparse activation is a popular approach for addressing the computational challenges posed by both
040
      large model size and long context length. To handle large models, sparse activation reduces costs
041
      by engaging only a small subset of model parameters for each input. This approach has gained sig-
042   nificant interest following the discovery of an intriguing phenomenon: the feed-forward networks
043   (FFNs) in classic Transformers like T5 (Raffel et al., 2020) and ViT (Dosovitskiy, 2020) exhibit
044   natural activation sparsity (Zhang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022). In other words, these models demon-
045   strate sparsity without explicit enforcement. This inherent sparsity, reaching remarkable levels like
046   3% nonzeros in T5, presents an opportunity for substantial efficiency gains with minimal modifica-
047   tions to the model architecture.
048   Unfortunately, this natural sparsity is absent in the latest generation of models (Jiang et al., 2023;
049   Gemma Team, 2024; Dubey et al., 2024), which have adopted gated non-ReLU activation func-
050   tions (Dauphin et al., 2017). To re-introduce sparsity, recent work has explored extra pretraining
051   steps, either by switching back to ReLU activations (Mirzadeh et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) or
052   by adding top-k thresholding (Yerram et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024a;b). This is often combined
053   with training a low-cost predictor to identify activated parameters, a crucial step for maximizing ef-
      ficiency gains (Liu et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023; Yerram et al., 2024). However,

072   Sparse activation for attention, often called sparse attention, faces a similar challenge. Sparsity is
073   used to efficiently handle long context input by limiting the number of tokens each token attends to.
074   A straightforward approach is top-k attention (Gupta et al., 2021), which applies a top-k mask to
075   the attention coefficients. This can be combined with a low-cost predictor to maximize efficiency
076
      (Ribar et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024c). However, achieving high sparsity and a
      predictor without complicated procedure and sacrificing quality remains a challenge.
077
078   Contributions. This work introduces Spark Transformer, an architectural variant of Transformer
079   that achieves both high activation sparsity and low-cost prediction in both the FFN and Attention
080   mechanisms. Notably, Spark Transformer can be trained in a single stage without requiring sepa-
081   rate post-processing and maintains quality without introducing additional parameters. This makes
082
      it a suitable drop-in replacement for standard Transformer models. Because the FFN and atten-
      tion components dominate the computational cost in large Transformers with long contexts, Spark
083
      Transformer drastically reduces the overall FLOP count for decoding a token (see Table 1).
084
085   We pretrain a Spark Transformer using the Gemma-2 recipe (Gemma Team, 2024), resulting in
086   a model we call Spark Gemma-2. Evaluation on standard benchmarks demonstrates that Spark
087   Gemma-2 closely matches the quality of Gemma-2, even with a high degree of sparsity: only 8%
088
      activated entries in the FFN and a maximum of 256 attended tokens in attention (see Table 2). This
      sparsity leads to a 3.1× reduction in overall FLOPs compared to Gemma-2. Using this sparsity, we
089
      evaluate model efficiency with gemma.cpp (Google Gemma.cpp, 2024), a C++ inference engine
090
      optimized for serving Gemma models on CPUs, and observe a speedup of up to 1.79× (see Figure 3).
091   Notably, on a readily available 4-core cloud VM, Spark Gemma-2 achieves a decoding speed of 86
092   ms per token, surpassing typical human reading speed (Brysbaert, 2019). This increased efficiency
093   enables wider access to high-quality models for users with limited access to high-FLOP devices,
094   such as GPUs and TPUs.
095
      Spark Transformer leverages the interpretation of both FFN and attention as key-value lookup tables
096
      (Geva et al., 2021) to provide a unified solution for achieving sparsity and prediction. Specifically,
097   the predictor is obtained by repurposing a subset of the dimensions of the query and key vectors to
098   produce an importance score for each key-value pair (see Section 3). Top-k thresholding is applied
099   to these scores to identify the activated keys. In particular, standard top-k thresholding requires
100   performing a sorting, which is inefficient particularly on training accelerators. To address this, we
101   introduce statistical top-k, a linear complexity algorithm for approximate nearest neighbor search
102   (see Section 2) based on fitting a Gaussian distribution to the activation entries and estimating a
103   threshold that yields the top entries. While ideas similar to statistical top-k have been used (Shi
104   et al., 2019; M Abdelmoniem et al., 2021) for the problem of distributed training (Lin et al., 2018),
105
      we are the first to introduce, adapt, and verify its effectiveness for activation sparsity.

159   Given the threshold θ(x, k), a straightforward approach to obtain a sparse vector is to set all entries

161   ferred to as hard thresholding (Blumensath & Davies, 2008), suffers from discontinuity, potentially
      hindering its suitability for gradient-descent-based training.

297   4     E XPERIMENTS
298
      In this section, we present an experimental evaluation of Spark Transformer using the Gemma-2
299
      2B model. Gemma-2 2B is a decoder-only Transformer with 2 billion parameters, pretrained on
300   2 trillion tokens of primarily English text data (see Gemma Team (2024) for details). To evaluate
301   Spark Transformer, we train a Spark Gemma-2 2B model by substituting the standard FFN and At-
302   tention in Gemma-2 2B with their Spark Transformer counterparts (Spark FFN and Spark Attention,
303   respectively). This Spark Gemma-2 2B model is trained using the same procedure and data as the
304   original Gemma-2 2B model.
305
      Implementation details. Gemma-2 uses a model dimension of dmodel = 2304. For FFN, Gemma-2
306   uses the Gated FFN in Eq. (12) with d0ff = 9216. We replace it with Spark FFN in Eq. (9) with
307   dff = 13824 so that the parameter count keeps the same. In addition, we take k = 1106, which gives
308   a sparsity level of 8%, and r = 1024 (due to sharding constraints, r can only be a multiple of 256).
309   For Attention, Gemma-2 alternates between a global attention that have a span of 8192 tokens,
310   and a local attention with a 4096 window size, both with dattn = 256. We replace both with Spark
311   Attention in Eq. (13) where for the latter we use the same 4096 window size. For hyper-parameters,
312   we use k = 256, i.e. each token attends to at most 256 tokens, and r = 128. Extra care need to be
313   taken for handling position embedding. Gemma-2 uses Rotary Position Embedding (Su et al., 2024)
314
      which is applied to q and the columns of K in Eq. (13). For Spark Attention in Eq. (14), we apply
      this position encoding to P q, (I − P )q, the columns of P K, and the columns of (I − P )K.

479   5     D ISCUSSION AND R ELATED WORK
480   Returning to the question posed at the outset: How many FLOPs is a token worth? This paper offers
481   an answer through the Spark Transformer architecture, demonstrating at least a 3× overall FLOPs
482   reduction without sacrificing model quality. This reduction is realized by selectively activating only
483   part of the model parameters and limiting the attended context for each input. This principle of
484   sparse activation finds a compelling parallel in neuroscience, where studies reveal sparse activity
485   patterns in the brain as a key factor in its remarkable efficiency (Attwell & Laughlin, 2001; Barth &
      Poulet, 2012; Lee et al., 2024a). While hardware limitations currently hinder the full exploitation of


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      sparse activation in Transformers, particularly on GPUs and TPUs designed for dense computations,
487   our work with Spark Transformer on CPUs highlights its potential. We believe this opens avenues
488   for research into alternative hardware and platforms better suited for sparse computations, hence
489   circumvents the hardware lottery (Hooker, 2021) and potentially lead to greater efficiency gains in
490   the future.
491
      In the following, we review a few lines of work closely related to ours.
492
493   Mixture of Experts (MoEs) may be considered as a particular case of sparsely activated mod-
494   els which group the neurons in FFN and activate all neutrons in selected groups (Shazeer et al.,
495   2017; Lepikhin et al., 2020). Neuron grouping has the benefit of being better suited for training
496
      accelerators compared to unstructured activation sparsity. However, training of MoEs incurs extra
      complexities in algorithmic design and requires special hardware support (Fedus et al., 2022). More-
497
      over, the structured nature of sparsity limits the model’s flexibility and expressiveness, and recent
498
      work advocates the use of a larger number of smaller experts (Dai et al., 2024; He, 2024). On the
499   other hand, the discovery of the naturally emerging unstructured activation sparsity has motivated
500   the new perspective of naturally emerging experts (Zhang et al., 2022; Dong et al., 2023; Csordás
501   et al., 2023; Qiu et al., 2023; Szatkowski et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024).
502
      Sparse activation is common approach to improve the efficiency of large models and many tech-
503
      niques for a low-cost activation prediction have been developed over the years, such as low-rank
504
      factorization (Davis & Arel, 2013), quantization (Cao et al., 2019), product keys (Lample et al.,
505   2019), hashing (Chen et al., 2020), etc. With the popularity of modern large Transformer models,
506   these techniques become natural choices (Jaszczur et al., 2021; Zeng et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023;
507   Song et al., 2023) for reducing their high computation costs. In particular, a lot of the excitement
508   comes from the discovery that the activations in FFNs are naturally sparse (Zhang et al., 2022; Li
509   et al., 2022) and hence efficiency with activation sparsity are obtained without a quality toll.
510
      Our work falls into the category of the latest work in this direction that aim to bring the benefits to the
511   latest generation large language models that do not have natural sparsity. Early attempts (Mirzadeh
512   et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) seek to bring back sparsity by switching back to
513   ReLU variants, but it usually incurs a quality loss. The quality gap may be largely bridged by more
514   careful tuning, but the activation becomes less sparse (e.g. 25% nonzeros in LLAMA 7B (Song
515   et al., 2024a)). Top-k becomes a more popular choice for obtaining sparsity recently (Song et al.,
516   2024b) and is able to maintain neutral quality while offering strong sparsity, but only in selected
517   layers (Yerram et al., 2024). Moreover, such methods require finetuning to bring sparsity and also
518   obtain a predictor. Without doing finetuning, Lee et al. (2024b); Liu et al. (2024a) obtained at most
519
      50% nonzeros under neutral quality. In contrast to these works, our work not only obtains 8%
      nonzeros in activation of all FFN layers, but also a predictor, all with a single-stage training. We
520
      provide a summary of comparison to these methods in Table 4 in the Appendix.
521
522   Finally, the usefulness of activation sparsity goes beyond efficiency. For example, theoretical studies
523   show its benefits for model generalizability and learnability (Muthukumar & Sulam, 2023; Awasthi
524   et al., 2024). Moreover, activated neurons may be associated with semantic concepts, which of-
525
      fers understanding of the working mechanism and enables manipulating the output of Transformer
      models (Cuadros et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2024).
526
527   Sparse attention broadly refers to the approach of attending to a selected subset of tokens in the con-
528   text as a means of reducing computation cost (Deng et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024). Work on sparse
529   attention include those that use handcrafted attention patterns (Child et al., 2019; Beltagy et al.,
530   2020; Ainslie et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2023), which feature simplicity, and learned attention patterns
531
      (Kitaev et al., 2020; Roy et al., 2021) which feature better modeling capacity. However, learning
      attention patterns often involve learning, e.g., a hash table or k-means centers, which significantly
532
      complicates modeling. Closely related to our Spark Attention is the top-k attention (Gupta et al.,
533
      2021), which obtains data-adaptive attention simply from top-k thresholding. Our work improves
534   upon top-k attention by introducing a low cost predictor which enables an increased computational
535   benefits from sparsity. Finally, KV pruning approaches drop selected tokens permanently as decod-
536   ing proceeds (Zhang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b), and cannot achieve as high compression ratio as
537   Top-k based approaches.

962   C    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS ON S PARSE M ATRIX M ULTIPLICATIONS
963
964   We describe how we implement sparse matrix multiplications for Spark FFN and Attention in
965   gemma.cpp. We start by focusing on a batch size of one for decoding before expanding our
966   discussion to larger batch sizes and prefill.
967   With batch size of 1, both Spark FFN and Spark Attention utilize two types of sparse vector-matrix
968   multiplication: vector-masked matrix multiplication and sparse vector-matrix multiplication (Fig-
969   ure 2). Given a vector q and a matrix w, vector-masked matrix multiplication multiplies q with the
970   non-masked columns of w based on a masking vector m. Masked columns yield a zero output.
971   Sparse vector-matrix multiplication, on the other hand, involves a vector that contains many zeroes
      being multiplied by a dense matrix.

       of the masked rows. Prefetching and SIMD operations are applied similarly in this context.

1011   computations for each head.

1013   vidual masks to skip computations while using a union of masks from each vector within the batch
1014   to create unified masks for memory loading. With larger batches, Spark transformer is expected to
1015   save less memory loading (vs. original Gemma), unless there is significant overlap in top-k positions
1016   within the same batch. Nonetheless, the Spark transformer consistently reduces FLOP by skipping
1017   computations based on individual masks within the batch.

1236   F.2     H ANDLING CASES WHEN THE ACTIVATION IS SHARDED
1237
1238   The training of modern large Transformer models usually requires sharding certain model weights
1239   and activations across multiple computation devices, due to physical limitations on each device’s
1240   memory. In particular, if sharding is used for the activations upon which the statistical top-k is
1241   applied to, i.e., the pre-GELU activation in FFN and the pre-softmax activation in attention, extra
       care needs to be taken so that statistical top-k is applied correctly. While this has not been the case


                                                                 23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       for our experiment on Gemma-2 2B, here we discuss possible solutions if this case arises, e.g., when
1243   training a larger Spark Transformer for which sharding relevant activations may become necessary.
1244
1245   Assume that an activation vector of length N is sharded over m devices, and we are interested in
1246
       finding approximately the top-k entries of N with the largest value. There are two ways of applying
       statistical top-k for this purpose.
1247
1248
       • Global statistical top-k. Here we require each device to compute the mean and variance for entries
1249     on itself, then communicate them to all other devices. In this case, each device receives m − 1
1250     mean and variance values, which can be combined with mean and variance on its own to obtain
1251     global mean and global variance. Then, the rest of the steps in statistical top-k can be carried out
1252     on individual devices. In this method, the output is exactly the same as if applying statistical top-k
1253     without activation sharding. In terms of cost, there is extra computation coming from aggregating
1254     mean and variance from all devices, but the cost is very low as it requires only O(k) FLOPs.
1255     The method also introduces a communication cost, but the cost is again small as each device only
1256     needs to send / receive 2k − 2 floating point numbers.
1257   • Local statistical top-k. Here we simply apply statistical top-k 0 to entries on its own device with
1258     k 0 = k/m. The method is sub-optimal in the sense that it does not necessarily produce the same
1259     output as applying the global statistical top-k. However, it has the benefit of not adding any
1260     computation and communication cost.
1261
1262
       In cases where k   N , the global statistical top-k above is cheap enough and hence could be a
       natural choice.
