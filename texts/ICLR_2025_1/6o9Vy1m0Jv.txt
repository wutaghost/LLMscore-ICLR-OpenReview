009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Robotic manipulation, owing to its multi-modal nature, often faces significant
012            training ambiguity, necessitating explicit instructions to clearly delineate the ma-
013            nipulation details in tasks. In this work, we highlight that vision instruction is nat-
014            urally more comprehensible to recent robotic policies than the commonly adopted
015            text instruction, as these policies are born with some vision understanding abil-
016            ity like human infants. Building on this premise and drawing inspiration from
017            cognitive science, we introduce the robotic imagery paradigm, which realizes
018
               large-scale robotic data pre-training without text annotations. Additionally, we
               propose the robotic gaze strategy that emulates the human eye gaze mechanism,
019
               thereby guiding subsequent actions and focusing the attention of the policy on
020
               the manipulated object. Leveraging these innovations, we develop VIRT, a fully
021            Transformer-based policy. We design comprehensive tasks using both a physical
022            robot and simulated environments to assess the efficacy of VIRT. The results in-
023            dicate that VIRT can complete very competitive tasks like “opening the lid of a
024            tightly sealed bottle”, and the proposed techniques boost the success rates of the
025            baseline policy on diverse challenging tasks from nearly 0% to more than 65%.

                                  “Seeing comes before words.” – John Berger
030
031   In robotic manipulation, a policy is trained to manipulate objects according to environment obser-
032   vations and task requirements Billard & Kragic (2019). The key insight that supports this work
033
      is existing robotic policies are akin to human infants, who are born with visual perception and
      reasoning abilities but do not comprehend natural language according to previous cognitive science
034
      literature Colombo & Mitchell (2009). Specifically, visual signal serves as the primary information
035
      source of recent robotic policies, and the backbones of these policies are pre-trained with large-scale
036   image datasets before the robotic data based training He et al. (2016); Oquab et al. (2024). There-
037   fore, the policies begin with a basic visual understanding capability like human infants. By contrast,
038   natural language inputs are rarely incorporated into the process of pre-training these backbones,
039   suggesting the lack of natural language knowledge in these policies. Moreover, bridging this knowl-
040   edge gap demands extensive image-text alignment training Radford et al. (2021), which is typically
041   impractical for developing real-time robotic policies.
042   Given these thoughts, we can deduce that the currently common practice of utilizing natural lan-
043   guage to instruct policies about task requirements (e.g., what task to do or which object to manipu-
044   late) is unsuitable Zitkovich et al. (2023); Kim et al. (2024b). To address this problem, we introduce
045   the concept of vision instruction, which involves using images to guide policies. In this work, we
046   concern the two main training settings in robotic manipulation learning, i.e., task-unspecified pre-
047   training Mees et al. (2024) and task-specified training Kim et al. (2024a). The optimization objective
048   of the former one is to derive a policy with general manipulation knowledge, which can then be used
049   as the initial model for downstream tasks. Differently, the latter setting focuses on creating a policy
050
      model that can effectively control a robot to complete specific tasks. This work focuses on exploring
      how vision instruction can be leveraged to enhance these two training settings through replacing the
051
      original inappropriate text instructions.

      In task-specified training, a key challenge is the ambiguity in manipulation trajectories. For in-
085   stance, in a table-clearing task with multiple objects, the policy must determine which object to
086   grasp first. To handle this problem, vision instruction should assist in scheduling manipulation
087   procedures. Drawing inspiration from the gaze anchoring hypothesis Land & Hayhoe (2001) in
088   cognitive science, we propose a robotic gaze (RG) strategy, as illustrated in Fig. 1 (b). Specifically,
089   the gaze anchoring hypothesis believes that eye gaze is seeking out objects for future use and set-
090   ting up the operations to perform. To replicate this mechanism, we employ a lightweight detector
091   Wang et al. (2024) to recognize the object to manipulate at each action step. Afterwards, we crop
092   out the recognized object region from the image and enlarge it. The enlarged image region is fed to
093
      the policy, guiding it in object manipulation. Notably, the enlarging image operation is non-trivial,
      because robotic manipulation requires clear perception of object details. However, enhancing the
094
      resolution of the entire image incurs a high computational cost. By contrast, resizing only the target
095
      object region is much more efficient. This design of enlarging the concerned region in an image
096   mirrors human vision, where the eye perceives a small, focused area with sharp clarity while the
097   surrounding regions remain blurred Stewart et al. (2020).

      2.1   D EMONSTRATION L EARNING BASED ROBOTIC M ANIPULATION
115   Robotic manipulation, defined as the capacity for robots to interact with and modify their surround-
116   ings, has advanced markedly due to the integration of machine learning techniques Fang et al.
117   (2019). Among existing methodologies, demonstration learning (also known as imitation learn-
118   ing or behavior cloning) has garnered significant attention for its training efficiency Zhao et al.
119   (2023). Demonstration learning enables robots to acquire complex manipulation skills by observing
120   human demonstrations, thereby bypassing the need for explicit programming of every action. The
121
      fundamental premise of demonstration learning is that a human teacher performs a task while the
      robot records the relevant data, such as sensory inputs, actions, and corresponding outcomes. This
122
      recorded data is subsequently used to train models that allow the robot to replicate the demonstrated
123
      behavior in similar situations Florence et al. (2022).
124
125   After continuous efforts paid by the research community, many advanced demonstration learning
126   based policies have been developed, and they can be broadly categorized into two groups, i.e., ex-
127
      plicit policies Fu et al. (2024b) and implicit policies Chi et al. (2023). Among them, explicit policies
      directly map environment observations to actions, and the policy output is supervised with human
128
      demonstration trajectories by computing regression losses Fu et al. (2024a). In contrast, implicit
129
      policies define the distributions of actions with energy-based models, where predicting the next ac-
130   tion is framed as identifying the manipulation trajectory with minimal energy Chi et al. (2024). This
131   modeling approach allows for the natural representation of multi-modal distributions of manipula-
132   tion trajectories, as multiple actions can simultaneously be assigned low energies. Consequently,
133   some studies suggest implicit policies are more advantageous for robotic manipulation learning Flo-
134   rence et al. (2022). Nevertheless, we contend that explicit policies offer faster response speeds due
135   to their simplicity, which is crucial for robotic manipulation. In addition, the iterative decoding
136   mechanism inherent in Transformer models is similar to the denoising process in implicit policies,
137   and thus can also handle the multi-modal ambiguity in robotic manipulation to some extent. Hence,
138
      in this work, we develop a fully Transformer-based policy adopting the explicit prediction paradigm.
139

      2.2   ROBOTIC P RE - TRAINING
141   Recent advancements in natural language processing and computer vision demonstrate the efficacy
142   of first pre-training models on large-scale data and then fine-tuning them for specific downstream
143   applications Achiam et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023b). Drawing inspirations from these successes,
144   the robotic learning community begins to explore pre-training paradigms to enhance robotic ma-
145   nipulation capabilities. The principal idea behind robotic pre-training is to first expose the robotic
146   policy to a wide range of tasks and environments, allowing it to learn generalizable representations
147
      for diverse robotic tasks Brohan et al. (2022). Subsequently, a fine-tuning phase on specific manip-
      ulation tasks is conducted, utilizing the previously gained prior knowledge to enhance performance
148
      and efficiency Zitkovich et al. (2023).
149
150   Pre-training a policy requires a substantial amount of data Fang et al. (2020). However, robotic ma-
151   nipulation data is expensive to collect. To mitigate this issue, some methods generate data through
152   simulated environments based on the traditional force closure estimation algorithms Wang et al.
153
      (2023a). However, significant discrepancies in appearance and motion dynamics between simulated
      and real-robot data limit the effectiveness of pre-trained policies. Some efforts employ large lan-
154
      guage models to generate grasp positions for objects in 2D images Vuong et al. (2023), but this
155
      approach is constrained by its two-dimensional output, whereas robotic manipulation occurs in a
156   three-dimensional space. Recently, collaborative efforts among various institutions have led to the
157   creation of large-scale datasets by merging existing data sources O’Neill et al. (2024) or collecting
158   new data across diverse scenarios Khazatsky et al. (2024). Thanks to these datasets, a handful of
159   promising pre-trained policies are derived Kim et al. (2024b). Nevertheless, robotic manipulation
160   trajectories remain highly ambiguous if without appropriate task instructions as prompt. Existing
161   pre-training algorithms predominantly use text instructions to inform the policy, which restricts the
      pre-training effectiveness, as previously noted.

      2.3   ROBOTIC I NSTRUCTION
163
164   Robotic manipulation learning is intrinsically a long-sequence autoregressive problem, often involv-
165   ing thousands of action steps within a short duration Chen et al. (2024). Therefore, a basic challenge
166   in manipulation lies in the ability of a policy to determine the appropriate actions based on the cur-
167   rent observation. This problem is especially serious if the task to perform involves multi-object
168
      manipulation or there are many potential operation steps Shi et al. (2023). To alleviate this problem,
      instructions are demanded to guide policies with task-specific information. In previous works, the
169
      instructions are mostly represented as natural language Brohan et al. (2022); Zitkovich et al. (2023),
170
      which is difficult to understand for policies as previously discussed. Alternatively, some researchers
171   have also explored voice instructions Shi et al. (2024), but voice information similarly poses learning
172   difficulties. By contrast, images are more readily comprehensible for policy networks, as the com-
173   monly adopted backbones of these networks are already pre-trained on extensive image datasets He
174   et al. (2016). This pre-existing visual understanding in robotic policies is akin to the innate vision
175   comprehension in human infants. Despite the potential of visual instructions, their applications re-
176   main unexplored in the context of task-unspecified pre-training and task-specified training of robotic
177   manipulation. Existing studies on visual instructions have primarily focused on goal images within
178   game-based reinforcement learning Yuan et al. (2024) and navigation Majumdar et al. (2022). This
179
      work aims to bridge the gap in exploring visual instructions for robotic manipulation.

183   3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
184
185   Robotic manipulation learning is inherently a long-sequence auto-regressive prediction problem and
186   can be formalized as a Markov Decision Process defined by M = (S, A, P). Here, S denotes the
187   set of all possible environmental states, A represents the set of possible actions by the policy, and
188   P indicates the transition probability distribution for transitioning to the next state given the current
189   state and action. At a given timestamp t, with the environment in state st ∈ S, the corresponding
190
      observation ot of a policy π is typically a function of st , denoted as ot = f (st ). This observation ot
      is then used to determine the next action with respect to at ∼ π(ot ), where at ∈ A. Upon executing
191
      the action at , the environment state transitions according to st+1 ∼ P(st+1 | st , at ).
192
193   To train π, we manually collect a set of demonstration data D = {(ŝit , ôit , âit ) | i ∈ {1, . . . , N }, t ∈
194   {0, . . . , Ti }}, where N is the number of demonstrations and Ti denotes the length of the ith trajec-
195   tory. Ideally, we expect the actions predicted by the policy π to change environment states following
196
      the dynamics observed in D, which means that st+1 should be akin to ŝt+1 given similar initial
      states st and ŝt . Nevertheless, directly computing the loss between st+1 and ŝt+1 is infeasible due
197
      to the non-differentiable nature of environment states. Consequently, existing methods approximate
198
      this process through computing the loss between the predicted action at and ground truth action ât .

203
      which primarily consists of 12 encoders and 3 decoders. As depicted in Fig. 2, the training of VIRT
204   includes two phases, task-unspecified pre-training and task-specified training. In the pre-training
205   phase, a vast collection of robotic manipulation videos, along with corresponding proprioception

254   3.3   ROBOTIC I MAGERY P RE - TRAINING
255
      The RIP paradigm enables large-scale robotic manipulation pre-training without the demand for task
256
      description annotations. In this paradigm, we provide the first and last timestamps of observations
257
      in a manipulation segment to π and train π to imagine the actions within this segment. Formally, as
258   defined before, the observations and demonstration actions are denoted as {ot }Tt=1 (ot is the same
259   as ôt during training) and {ât }Tt=1 over a data sequence with T timestamps. The observations o1
260   and oT serve as the input to π, where o1 is the current observation and oT represents the vision
261   instruction. Each ot consists of two parts, the image component It and prioperception information
262   pt . As shown in Fig. 2, the images I0 and IT are split into uniform patches and then transformed
263   into vision tokens by Transformer encoders. Similarly, po and pt are encoded as tokens by a linear
264   projection layer. These tokens, along with n action queries, are input to the Transformer decoders to
265   produce n actions {at }Tt=1 . The policy weights are updated by computing the L1 loss between the
266
      predicted actions {at }Tt=1 and demonstration actions {ât }Tt=1 .
267   As RIP eliminates the need for task-specific priors or manual annotations, it is widely applicable
268   to diverse robotic manipulation data. In this work, we pre-train π with Droid Khazatsky et al.
269   (2024), which is a large-scale in-the-wild robotic manipulation dataset with 76k trajectories. Droid
      is selected due to its extensive diversity and inherent complexity, as many tasks within Droid are


                                                           5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      difficult to describe linguistically and lack task description annotations. Although previous works
271   have attempted to pre-train policies using this dataset, they find that incorporating Droid into their
272   pre-training paradigms harms the downstream performances Kim et al. (2024b), which implies the
273   great challenge behind this dataset posed by its huge task diversity and ambiguity. By contrast, RIP
274   only utilize vision observations and manipulation trajectories to pre-train policis, avoiding relying
275   on any task-specific prior. This characteristic makes RIP easier to handle the challenges posed by
276   ambiguous task content, thereby yielding highly effective pre-trained policy weights.

278   3.4   ROBOTIC G AZE
279
280   Due to the need of considering interations between multiple objects and avoiding collisions, a huge
281   receptive field is critical for policy design in robotic manipulation. Therefore, Transformers are ad-
282   vantageous due to their global receptive field. However, this comes at the cost of reduced sensitivity
283   to image details, which is also crucial for robotic manipulation Carion et al. (2020). In addition, this
284   problem is uneasy to address by simply enlarging the whole image, because doubling the width and
285
      height of an image results in 4× of tokens, which means 16× increase in computational complex-
      ity. To address this problem, we draw inspiration from the human visual system. It is found that
286
      the fovea centralis in a human eye contains cells that differ from those in other regions Willmer &
287
      Wright (1945). This difference makes eyes can only perceive a small region very clearly, but it helps
288   maintain a good balance between the processing burden of brain cortex and observation resolution.
289   Interestingly, this biological trait closely resembles the functionality needed in Transformers. Thus,
290   we design the RG algorithm to emulate this gaze mechanism,
291
      Specifically, we employ a lightweight detector like YOLOv10 Wang et al. (2024) to recognize ob-
292
      jects of interest. The mature state of 2D object detection allows for the easy acquisition of detectors
293
      that meet various application demands. Detectors like YOLOv10 can run at speeds exceeding 100
294   frames per second on an RTX4090 GPU, resulting in minimal inference latency. However, a detec-
295   tor locates all concerned objects, our aim is for the policy π to concentrate on a single crucial region
296   for manipulation at any given moment, akin to the human eye gaze mechanism. To bridge this gap,
297   we divide a manipulation task into multiple stages, each corresponding to a specific object of focus.
298   As described in Section 3.2, the policy π is trained to predict a one-hot vector s that classifies the
299   current manipulation stage. With this information, the corresponding region in It to concentrate on
300   at the timestamp t is obtained, and this region is the vision instruction. Then, we zoom in this vision
301   instruction to the same resolution as It . In this way, the vision instruction is concatenated with It
302
      into a single batch for input into the encoders, enhancing inference efficiency and enabling π to
      perceive object details more clearly. Importantly, directing π to focus on a single object at a time
303
      does not preclude the robot from manipulating multiple objects simultaneously. Similar to human
304
      behavior, where the eyes can only gaze at one object while handling multiple, the robot can perform
305   concurrent operations on several objects.
306
307   In addition, as discussed in Sec. 1, this RG strategy also imitates the gaze anchoring hypothesis in
308
      cognitive science, as the vision instruction produced by RG prompts π about which object to manip-
      ulate before the actions start. It helps scheduling the operation procedures in a task. Benefiting from
309
      this effect, RG significantly mitigates the optimization challenges inherent in long-term manipula-
310
      tion tasks by offering π continuous feedback on task progress. Our experimental results suggest that
311   a naive baseline policy without RG struggles to learn to perform challenging long-term tasks, while
312   the policy with RG achieves high success rates.

      different perspectives. As depicted in Fig. 5, the three tasks are named as Pour Blueberries, Open
      the Lid, and Clean the Table, respectively. We collect 100 demonstrations of data for every task.
448
449   In the Pour Blueberries task, the robot needs to remove the juicer cup from the juicer and place it
450   on the table. Then, the robot picks up the plate containing blueberries and pours all blueberries into
451   the juicer cup. Finally, the plate is returned back to the table. This task is to measure the long-term
452   multi-object manipulation ability of policies, and diverse kinds of actions are required in this task.
453   For the Open the Lid task, the robot uses a robotic hand to hold a bottle with a tightly screwed lid.
454   The another hand first needs to grasp the lid. After a series of twists, the robot gradually unscrews
455   and removes the lid from the bottle. This task tests the dexterous manipulation capability of policies.
456
      Within the Clean the Table task, three plates of different colors and a small cabinet are positioned
457   on a table. The robot is required to move the plates onto the cabinet in a color order that is ran-
458   domly specified during test. The orders are different among various trials. This task is to verify the
459   instruction following performance of policies.
460
      Simulation tasks. The three real-robot tasks are quite challenging and some policies could get
461

462

463   Specified Boxes. The three tasks are visualized in Fig. 6. We collect 50 demonstrations for the first
464   task due to its lower difficulty and 100 demonstrations for each of the other two tasks.
465
466
      In Move a Single Box, the robot needs to transports the sole box on a table in a container. For Trans-
      port the Specified Box, five different colors of boxes are randomly located on a table, and the robot
467
      should move the box described by a random instruction to the container. Different from Transport
468
      the Specified Box, two boxes are specified in Stack the Specified Boxes, and the robot is expected
469   to move the first box in the container and then stack the second box on the first box. Therefore, both
470   Transport the Specified Box and Stack the Specified Boxes test the instruction following ability, and
471   Stack the Specified Boxes demands better long-term operation and precise manipulation capabilities.
472
      Evaluation metrics. The success rate of whether completing a task is the primary metric adopted by
473
      previous works Chi et al. (2023). Nevertheless, for the tasks requiring multiple steps of operations,
474
      this metric does not reflect the completion ratio when a policy does not fullfill a task. To bridge
475   this gap, we design a new metric named the completion score. For a given task comprising k steps,
476   the policy earns a score of i/k if it completes up to the ith step. Four of the designed tasks contain
477   multi-step manipulation, and they are Pour Blueberries, Open the Lid, Clean the Table, and Stack
478   the Specified Boxes. Refer to Appendix A.1 for details of how the steps are defined. In the following
479   experiments, we test each policy on every task for 100 times and report the average results.

508   our proposed techniques, we further investigate potential performance constraints in the compared
509   policies. For ConvMLP and Diffusion Policy, a critical problem is that their network heads for
510   predicting actions are implemented based on fully connected layers, the computation burdens of
511   which are quite heavy. To alleviate this problem, ConvMLP and Diffusion Policy have to compress
512   the image feature into smaller embedding. For example, Diffusion Policy compresses image feature
513   as keypoint embedding using the SpatialSoftmax module Finn et al. (2016), and this compression
514   causes the policy cannot receive sufficient observation information for manipulation. Differently, the
515   problem of ACT is its weakness in handling the ambiguity in manipulation. For example, according
516
      to the results in Table 2, ACT achieves similar performance with VIRT in the Move a Single Box
      task, where there is only a sole box in the environment. However, when we test ACT in the Transport
517
      the Specified Box task, its results become much poorer due to the increasing ambiguity.

531
532
      In this work, we have demonstrated vision observations are more suitable for serving as manipulation
533
      instructions than text descriptions. Afterwards, inspired from the human biological mechanisms, we
534   have proposed the RIP and RG strategies for applying vision instruction to the task-unspecified pre-
535   training and task-specified training in robotic manupulation learing. Based on these two strategies, a
536   fully Transformer-based policy VIRT has been developed. To evaluate the effectiveness of VIRT, we
537   have designed three real-robot tasks using the Cobot Magic robot and three simulated tasks based on
538   Isaac Gym. These six tasks test the manipulation capabilities of policies from different perspectives.
539   Our sufficient experiments suggest that VIRT have surpassed the performances of compared popular
      policies by large margins and all the developed techniques are effective.

674
      In this work, we define a new metric, the completion score, to reflect the task completion ratio when
675
      a policy fails to fulfill the whole task. In our six devised tasks, four of them include multiple steps

678   are defined in these tasks as follows:

      table successfully. The second step is picking up the plate containing blueberries. The third step is

      pouring blueberries into the cup successfully.
682
683   Open the Lid: The first step is that the robotic hand for screwing the bottle lid grasps the lid
684   correctly. The second step is the robotic hand screws the lid in the correct motion. The third step is
685
      the lid takes the lid from the bottle successfully.
686   Clean the Table: As there are three plates in various colors on the table, the ith step is moving the
687   ith specified plate on the cabinet successfully.
