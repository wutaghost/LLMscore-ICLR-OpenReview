009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The Genomic Foundation Model (GFM) paradigm is expected to facilitate the ex-
012            traction of generalizable representations from massive genomic data, thereby en-
013            abling their application across a spectrum of downstream applications. Despite ad-
014            vancements, a lack of evaluation framework makes it difficult to ensure equitable
015            assessment due to experimental settings, model intricacy, benchmark datasets, and
016            reproducibility challenges. In the absence of standardization, comparative analy-
017            ses risk becoming biased and unreliable. To surmount this impasse, we introduce
018
               GeneBench, a comprehensive benchmarking suite specifically tailored for evalu-
               ating the efficacy of Genomic Foundation Models. GeneBench offers a modular
019
               and expandable framework that encapsulates a variety of state-of-the-art method-
020
               ologies. Through systematic evaluations of datasets spanning diverse biological
021            domains with a particular emphasis on both short-range and long-range genomic
022            tasks, firstly including the three most important DNA tasks covering Coding Re-
023            gion, Non-Coding Region, Genome Structure, etc. Our results on GeneBench
024            have led to an interesting discovery: regardless of the number of parameters, the
025            noticeable variation in preference between attention-based and convolution-based
026            models for short—and long-range tasks could offer valuable insights for the future
027            development of GFM. As a result, we propose a straightforward modified model
028            called Genhybrid, which is an effective and efficient SSM-attention hybrid model
029
               suitable for all tasks we covered.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Recently, significant advancements have been made in genomic research by utilizing foundation
034
      models (FMs) to analyze unstructured whole genome data. These genomic foundation models play
035
      a crucial role in various tasks such as predicting gene locations and functions, identifying regulatory
036   elements, and studying species evolution (Ji et al., 2021; Fishman et al., 2023; Zvyagin et al., 2023).
037
038   Despite the importance of modeling Genomics foundations and the advancement of different training
039
      methods, there is still a noticeable absence of a thorough benchmark in this area that encompasses
      a range of practical application scenarios and different foundational model structures. The current
040
      benchmark either restricts its scope to short distances or oversimplifies the challenge by focusing
041
      solely on the classification task (Ji et al., 2021; Marin et al., 2023; Fishman et al., 2023). Moreover,
042   with the influx of long sequence models called state space models (SSMs) (Gu & Dao, 2023; Liu
043   et al., 2024b; Nguyen et al., 2024; Schiff et al., 2024), a systematic approach to evaluating up-to-
044   date GFMs and inspiring subsequent development is also sorely lacking. Based on the current state
045   of research, we thus summarise the three key problems in current GFMs: (1) Incomplete evalua-
046   tion: Long sequence processing is crucial for modeling genetic data. Current tests of these models
047   for long-sequence gene tasks are incomplete. (2) Chaotic training strategies: The variety of tok-
048   enizations and pre-training methods lack a fair platform to compare and select the most appropriate
049   training method for DNA data. (3) Snowy model design: Do the various attention-based and re-
050
      cent state-space/convolution models have unique strengths in analyzing DNA? We need an inspiring
      experience that will influence future designs.
051
052   To address these issues, we propose GeneBench, a comprehensive benchmarking suite covering the
053   three main genomic directions from short to long, as shown in Figure 1: the coding regions, the non-
      coding regions, and the genome architecture. GeneBench aims to establish a standardized platform

      2.2   G ENOMIC F OUNDATION M ODELS
135
136   Attention-based Foundation models in deep learning are trained on extensive data sets using self-
137   supervised learning. The importance of these models has grown due to their capacity to leverage
138   large amounts of unlabeled data. For instance, DNABERT by Ji et al. (2021) was developed based
139   on the BERT model (Devlin et al., 2018) with a k-mer genomic tokenizer. Additionally, Benegas
140   et al. (2023) introduced the Genomic Pre-trained Network (GPN) for predicting non-coding variant
141   effects, surpassing supervised learning methods. Researchers have explored different approaches
142   to enhance performance. For example, Dalla-Torre et al. (2023) introduced NT (Nucleotide Trans-
143   former), a genomic model with billions of parameters. On the other hand, researchers focus on
144
      optimizing model components and efficiency. DNABERT-2 (Zhou et al., 2023) replaces k-mer tok-
      enization with Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) (Sennrich et al., 2015).
145
146
      SSM/Convolution-based Despite the computational cost associated with scaling up in sequence
147
      length due to the quadratic complexity of attention mechanisms, there is room for more efficient
148   alternatives. HyenaDNA (Nguyen et al., 2024) and Caduceus utilize the hyena operator (Poli et al.,
149   2023) and state space model (Gu & Dao, 2023; Liu et al., 2024a) with a complexity of O(L log2 L)
150   and O(L), significantly lower than the O(L2 ) of attention-based models. The mathematical form of
151   the above basic modules is listed in Appendix B.2.

240   3.3            G ENHYBRID M ODEL
241
242   Empirical Findings As shown in Figure 2, through comprehensive benchmarking of current ge-
243   netic foundation models, two key observations emerge that critically inform model design choices.
244   First, models leveraging attention mechanisms, such as NT, DNABERT-2, and GENA-LM, con-
245   sistently demonstrate superior performance on short-range sequence modeling tasks, particularly
246
      when input lengths are within the range of 500 to 3,000 tokens. This is evident across both species
      classification and promoter prediction tasks, where attention-based architectures excel at capturing
247
      intricate local dependencies. However, as sequence lengths increase beyond this range, the computa-
248
      tional complexity of these models becomes a bottleneck, limiting their scalability. Second, models
249   with linear time complexity, such as HyenaDNA and Caduceus, although initially underperform-
250   ing in short-range tasks, exhibit robust accuracy when handling long sequences, with HyenaDNA
251   achieving notable performance at lengths up to 30,000 tokens. Furthermore, attention-based models
252   struggle to converge, while long-sequence models perform much superior in macro-level tasks, such
253   as genome structure prediction. These findings underscore the trade-offs between modeling short-
254   range versus long-range dependencies and reveal a need for hybrid architectures that can harness the
255   strengths of both attention-based and SSMs. The detailed analysis refers to Sec. A.2.

281   We thoroughly assess the performance of the models supported for the tasks mentioned above by

285

472   5     C ONCLUSION AND D ICUSSION
473
474   This paper presents GeneBench, a comprehensive benchmark for GFMs featuring ten representa-
475   tive models covering a broad spectrum of challenging tasks from local to global view of genomics.
476   GeneBench classifies existing approaches into attention-based and convolution-based GFMs. Ex-
477
      tensive experiments are carried out to systematically assess the performance of the models sup-
      ported across various tasks. In short-range tasks, attention-based models excel at capturing intrin-
478
      sic information, while attention-free models achieve comparable yet less efficient performance. In
479
      long-range tasks, the performance difference between attention-based and convolution-based mod-
480   els becomes narrower. Furthermore, increasing input length can significantly enhance performance
481   on conv/SSM models. Based on our experimental results, we, therefore, propose GenHybrid, a sim-
482   ple yet efficient model co-designed by SSM and attention to performing better on all genetic tasks
483   we covered. Limitations. Despite the multifaceted comparisons of GFMs, GeneBench is basically
484   stuck on downstream task prediction, and comparisons on pre-training are lacking. For example, the
485   impact of pre-training data under different model structures, etc. In addition, we have not verified the
      performance of GFM on the whole chromosome due to the limitation of computational resources.

686   A.2   E FFECT OF S EQUENCE L ENGTH
687
688   Recall the results in Figure 2. We conducted an analysis to evaluate the impact of sequence length
689   on model performance in long-range genomic tasks, that is also the motivation why we propose
690   GenHybrid. Specifically, we used input sequences of varying lengths—512, 1000, 2000, and 3000
691   base pairs (bp)—to assess the performance of four models: Hyena-DNA, Nucleotide Transformer,
692   DNABERT-2, and GENA-LM in both species and promoter prediction tasks. Additionally, to ex-
693
      plore the potential of convolution-based models, we tested a significantly longer input sequence of
      30,000 bp with Hyena-DNA, focusing exclusively on the species prediction task. From the data, it is
694
      evident that increasing the sequence length consistently enhances performance across all the mod-
695
      els tested. This trend is particularly pronounced with Hyena-DNA, which, despite trailing behind
696   attention-based models at shorter context lengths, exhibits superior performance with longer con-
697   texts. This improvement underscores the advantages of using extended context lengths in genomic
698   sequence analysis. However, this benefit is not without its challenges. In tasks like promoter predic-
699   tion, where input length is inherently capped at 3000 bp, Hyena-DNA’s reliance on longer sequences
700   becomes a limiting factor. This limitation presents a significant area for future research, aiming to
701   optimize model performance within these constraints and potentially develop novel approaches to
      leverage longer sequences more effectively within the confines of specific genomic datasets.

713
      the concept of Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers (BERT) model (Devlin
      et al., 2018) within the context of genomic DNA. Similar to BERT, DNABERT follows a pre-
714
      training—fine-tuning framework. In the pretraining phase, a portion of k contiguous tokens, cov-
715
      ering 15% of the sequence, is randomly masked, prompting DNABERT to forecast the masked
716   sequences based on the remaining context. The training dataset is derived from the human genome
717   using a direct non-overlapping splitting and random sampling approach, with sequence lengths rang-
718   ing from 5 to 510.
719
      Nucleotide Transformer utilizes an encoder-only transformer architecture. The models are trained
720
      using the BERT methodology. The Nucleotide Transformer employs three distinct datasets for pre-
721
      training the model: The Human reference genome dataset, The 1000G dataset, and The Multispecies
722   dataset (Dalla-Torre et al., 2023).
723
724   DNABERT-2 utilizes the Transformer Encoder architecture, providing flexibility in input length
725
      and enhanced computational and memory efficiency. It replaces learned positional embeddings with
      Attention with Linear Biases (ALiBi) (Press et al., 2021) and incorporates FlashAttention (Dao et al.,
726
      2022) and Low Precision Layer Normalization. The model is pretrained on The Human Genome
727
      dataset and The Multi-Species Genome dataset (Zhou et al., 2023).
728
729   GENA-LM model utilizes the Transformer Encoder architecture and has been trained on the Human
730   T2T v2 genome assembly dataset.
731   Hyena-DNA utilizes a decoder-only design, composed of a series of blocks containing a Hyena
732   operator. It is pretrained using the human reference genome.
733
      Caduceus is a group of bidirectional long-range DNA sequence models that are the pioneers in sup-
734
      porting RC equivariant language modeling. Caduces employ pre-training and fine-tuning techniques
735   with MambaDNA as their foundation.
736
737   GenHybrid is a hybrid model that strategically incorporates two attention layers within an SSM-
738
      based model. In our case, we employed Caduceus as the baseline and replaced the second layer and
      the middle layer with full attention. The training procedure is the same as Caduceus.
739
740   The convolution-based deep learning models such as CNN, SpliceAI, DeepSTARR, and Orca are
741   specifically developed to predict distinct genomic features. These models are trained from scratch
742   using specialized datasets instead of being pretrained on general genomic sequences.

814   information, where each entry contains the mutation type and its related attributes. Next, convert
815   the data into input strings that include a mask for easier processing by the model. Then, load the
816   pre-trained genomic model and set it to evaluation mode. Following this, extract the scores at the
817   masked positions using the logits output by the model, and return the highest-scoring predicted
818   words to identify the mutation sites. Additionally, calculate the overall likelihood of the input text
819
      by applying softmax to the logits and summing the log likelihoods. Finally, for each mutation text,

837          • Genome Structure Prediction: We utilized micro-C datasets from H1-ESCs and HFF
838            cells, obtained from the 4D Nucleome portal, along with genomic sequences from
839            GRCh38/hg38. Using the Selene library, we implemented on-the-fly training data gen-
840            eration, sampling uniformly from training chromosomes. For each micro-C dataset, we
841            trained a separate model. Training samples consisted of input sequences paired with their
842            corresponding genome interaction matrices, which were derived from normalized contact
843
               matrices. To prepare the genome interaction matrices, we applied two key procedures
               to the micro-C datasets using cooler and cool tools packages: iterative correction matrix
844
               balancing and adaptive coarse-graining. The latter smooths sparse areas of the contact
845
               map using variable window sizes to eliminate zeros. We maintained spatial resolution by
846            avoiding additional smoothing. The final processed matrix was normalized using a back-
847            ground matrix derived from distance encoding, with adjustments for numerical stability.
848            We computed distance-based expectations per chromosome using cool tools, aggregated
849            them across chromosomes, and applied lowess smoothing for distances beyond 1.6 Mb.
850            For model evaluation, we split the chromosomes into three sets: training (all except chr8,
851            9, and 10), validation (chr8), and testing (chr9, 10).

883   Discussion We found that current DNA language models primarily fall into two categories: CNN-
884   based and attention-based pre-trained models. While the incorporation of attention mechanisms can
885   help us better understand deoxyribonucleotide sequences, convolutional models outperform atten-
886
      tion models in certain specific tasks. Therefore, it is particularly important to combine attention and
      convolutional modules, as this not only aids in a more comprehensive understanding of both long
887
      and short genomic tasks but also effectively alleviates the high computational costs associated with
888
      the pre-training phase. To this end, we propose GenHybrid, a simple yet efficient model co-designed
889   with SSM and attention, which achieves state-of-the-art performance across multiple tasks.
890
891   In short-range tasks, attention-based models excel at capturing intrinsic information, while attention-
892
      free models perform comparably but less efficiently. In long-range tasks, the performance difference
      between attention-based and convolutional models becomes narrower. Moreover, increasing input
893
      length can significantly enhance performance, especially in extending gene context. Based on our
894
      experimental results, we have developed GenHybrid, a model that integrates SSM and attention to
895   perform better on all genetic tasks we covered.
896
897   It is noteworthy that we introduced central rules and DNA structural prediction data to evaluate the
898
      performance of existing models. The inclusion of these two evaluation datasets allows us to assess
      genomic models not only on the basis of single genomic sequence information but also incorporating
899
      DNA structural information and DNA-protein interactions. Furthermore, we found that existing
900
      models are not effective in handling genomic mutation data, indicating that future research should
901   focus on further optimizing model design for this task.
902
903   Limitations and Outlook Considering that current genomic pre-training models are primarily de-
904   veloped using human data, we mainly evaluated the performance of genomic pre-training models
905   on 45 human downstream tasks in GenBench. However, the genetic sequence differences among
906   biologically distinct species are significant, and designing a universal pre-training model would bet-
907   ter serve the diverse downstream tasks. Additionally, we are unclear about how these models learn
908   biological sequence information during the pre-training phase; assessing the interpretability of the
909   models will help us better design and understand them, thereby supporting ”precision medicine.”
910   To further model the structural information of genomes, we should also consider fine-grained de-
911
      tails such as DNA bond angles, bond energies, and bond lengths, as well as the physicochemical
      properties of deoxyribonucleotides and their impact on the models.
