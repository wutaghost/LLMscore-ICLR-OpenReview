009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               The traditional viewpoint on Sparse Mixture of Experts (MoE) models is that
012
               instead of training a single large expert, which is computationally expensive,
013            we can train many small experts. The hope is that if the total parameter count
014            of the small experts equals that of the singular large expert, then we retain the
015            representation power of the large expert while gaining computational tractability
016            and promoting expert specialization. The recently introduced Soft MoE replaces the
017            Sparse MoE’s discrete routing mechanism with a differentiable gating function that
018            smoothly mixes tokens. While this smooth gating function successfully mitigates
019            the various training instabilities associated with Sparse MoE, it is unclear whether
020            it induces implicit biases that affect Soft MoE’s representation power or potential
021
               for expert specialization. We prove that Soft MoE with a single arbitrarily powerful
               expert cannot represent simple convex functions. This justifies that Soft MoE’s
022
               success cannot be explained by the traditional viewpoint of many small experts
023
               collectively mimicking the representation power of a single large expert, and that
024            multiple experts are actually necessary to achieve good representation power (even
025            for a fixed total parameter count). Continuing along this line of investigation, we
026            introduce a notion of expert specialization for Soft MoE, and while varying the
027            number of experts yet fixing the total parameter count, we consider the following
028            (computationally intractable) task. Given any input, how can we discover the expert
029            subset that is specialized to predict this input’s label? We empirically show that
030            when there are many small experts, the architecture is implicitly biased in a fashion
031            that allows us to efficiently approximate the specialized expert subset. Our method
032
               can be easily implemented to potentially reduce computation during inference. For
               example, using our method on ImageNet, one can perform inference using only
033
               1/8 of the experts and still retain 99% of the test accuracy of using all experts.

037   1    I NTRODUCTION
038
039   It has been well established that scaling the size (i.e., parameter count) of models is necessary for
040   state of the art prediction power (Kaplan et al., 2020; Bahri et al., 2021), but naively scaling the
041   model size is infeasible due to hardware constraints and computational costs. Mixture of Experts
042   (MoE) layers in a model allow one to achieve this goal, while mitigating the increased computational
043   costs for training and inference that accompany a naively larger model. These have been successfully
044   deployed in practice, in a variety of contexts such as language (Fedus et al., 2022) and vision (Ruiz
045   et al., 2021), and MoE layers are considered critical to achieve state of the art performance in today’s
046   models (Jiang et al., 2024).
047   The archetypical MoE layer is the Sparse MoE (Shazeer et al., 2017). Here, each token is only given
048   to a subset of experts, and a router is trained to discretely match a token to its expert subset. Since a
049   single (large) expert can represent complex functions, the traditional viewpoint is that one should
050   partition the large expert into multiple (small) experts, so that the total parameter count of all experts
051   is unchanged. The hope is that the model’s representation power is similar since the total parameter
052   count is the same and since experts can specialize to the tokens they see (rather than all tokens) (Chen
053
      et al., 2022). Yet, training and inference are faster, because any single token activates only a subset of
      experts rather than all of them.

054
      While the Sparse MoE allows scaling of model size, its discrete routing causes optimization issues and
055   load balancing difficulties during training. To tackle these issues, many variants of Sparse MoE have
056   been introduced, such as routing a token to only a single expert (Fedus et al., 2022), incorporating
057   linear programs to ensure load balancing (Lewis et al., 2021) or having the experts select tokens
058   (instead of tokens selecting experts) (Zhou et al., 2022). However, all these approaches remain
059   discrete in nature, and thus suffer from at least some degree of training instability.
060
      To alleviate these issues, the recently introduced Soft
061   MoE (Puigcerver et al., 2024) eschews discrete match-
062   ing in favor of a smoother approach. It computes for
063   each expert a convex combination of the input tokens,
064   and the expert only sees this convex combination. The
065   final output of the model is then a convex combina-
066   tion of each expert’s output. This approach is fully
067   differentiable, and hence is more stable than the Sparse
068   MoE. This novel Soft MoE architecture has been shown
069
      to outperform all other baselines on challenging large
      scale vision tasks, and can scale to thousands of ex-
070
      perts (Puigcerver et al., 2024). Moreover, recent results
071
      show that the Soft MoE is a promising avenue towards
072   providing empirical scaling laws for deep reinforce-
073   ment learning (Obando-Ceron et al., 2024).

      The majority of prior work on MoE focuses on computational issues, such as efficient and stable
      training. In our paper, we adopt an orthogonal perspective. In particular, it remains unclear whether
082
      Soft MoE’s specific manner of combining tokens and experts creates any unexpected implicit
083
      architectural biases. Indeed, it is not even clear that its soft gating mechanism preserves the traditional
084   MoE dogma that many small experts have similar representation power to a single large expert with
085   the same total parameter count. It is also unclear whether combining tokens and experts completely
086   destroys the possibility (or discoverability) of expert specialization, which is what one traditionally
087   desires from an MoE (especially in the regime of many experts) (Krishnamurthy et al., 2023; Dai
088   et al., 2024). Thus, we investigate for the existence of such biases, through the lens of varying the
089   number of experts. In this paper, we make progress along this line of investigation by making the
090   following contributions:
091
092          • We prove that the Soft MoE with a single neural network expert, even with arbitrarily many
093            parameters, cannot represent simple convex functions (while empirically we show that
094            multiple experts can). Thus, in contrast to the traditional viewpoint, having multiple experts
095            is actually necessary to have non-trivial representation power in Soft MoE.
096
097
098          • We introduce a notion of specialization for Soft MoE. While discovering specialized experts
099            generally seems intractable, we empirically demonstrate that as we increase the number of
100            experts, even while fixing the total parameter count, the architecture is implicitly biased in a
101
               manner that allows us to efficiently approximate the specialized expert set (see Figure 1).
102
103
             • Our method for discovering specialized experts can be easily implemented for reducing
104            computation at inference.
105
106
107   These contributions thus show there are benefits to using a large number of small experts relative to a
      small number of large experts, and notably, these benefits are often non-computational.

141   2.2   O UR I NVESTIGATION
142
      At a high level, the Sparse MoE is designed with the following principle. Say we desire a model
143
      with b total parameters, because we believe that b allows for sufficiently large representation power.
144   Instead of using a single large network (n = 1) with b parameters, we use n > 1 smaller experts
145   each with b/n parameters. The hope is that we have similar representation power to the n = 1 case
146   since the total parameter count is the same, but we have faster computation because each token only
147   activates a small subset of experts (Shazeer et al., 2017; Fedus et al., 2022). Moreover, one also hopes
148   that each expert can specialize to the specific type of tokens it sees (Chen et al., 2022; Krishnamurthy
149   et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2024).
150   The Soft MoE is motivated in a similar fashion. It also splits a single large model with b parameters
151   into n ≥ 1 smaller experts each with b/n parameters, hoping that representation power is unchanged.
152   However, Soft MoE differs significantly from Sparse MoE in how it uses these experts. While Sparse
153   MoE discretely assigns tokens to experts, Soft MoE computes convex combinations of tokens and
154   expert outputs. Due to this significant difference, it is unclear how the original motivations for Sparse
155   MoE apply to Soft MoE.
156   As one example of how Soft MoE might deviate from the original motivations for Sparse MoE,
157   consider the extreme case when n = 1. Here, Sparse MoE’s router trivially routes all tokens
158   to the expert, and so classical results show that Sparse MoE can represent arbitrary continuous
159   functions (Cybenko, 1989; Hornik, 1991). But in Soft MoE, the gating function is non-trivial even in
160   n = 1, and so it is possible that Soft MoE has poor representation power even when the expert is
161   very powerful. If this were true, then it would challenge the conventional wisdom that Soft MoE’s
      empirical success with n > 1 smaller experts (each with b/n parameters) is simply because they


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      mimic (albeit computationally efficiently) the representation power of a single large expert (with b
163   parameters). Instead, it would suggest that Soft MoE has some implicit bias that enables its practical
164   success. To this end, we ask the following question.
165
166                  Q1: Can a large single expert in Soft MoE represent simple functions?
167
168   Our motivation for this question has thus far primarily been scientific. Nevertheless, we believe
169
      this question is also practically relevant, since there are empirical results in reinforcement learning
      (RL) which show that Soft MoE with a single expert can outperform traditional neural network
170
      architectures (Obando-Ceron et al., 2024). Indeed, a negative answer to Q1 would imply that there
171
      are implicit biases in Soft MoE that assist in its improved empirical performance.
172
173   As a second example of how Soft MoE might deviate from the original motivations for Sparse MoE,
174   consider the following. Since Soft MoE combines all tokens before feeding them to an expert, and
175
      since it combines all experts to produce its final output, it is natural to wonder whether this prohibits
      expert specialization. Indeed, this limitation is acknowledged in the seminal work (Puigcerver et al.,
176
      2024). We thus ask the following question.
177
178   Q2: Is there a notion of expert specialization in Soft MoE, and if so, can it be efficiently discovered?
179
180   The remainder of this paper is devoted to answering Q1 (in Section 3) and Q2 (in Section 4).

254   4.1   W HAT D OES S PECIALIZATION M EAN ?
255
256   We begin by noting that since the Soft MoE combines tokens before feeding them to experts, it seems
257
      a priori unlikely that experts specialize, as acknowledged in the seminal work (Puigcerver et al.,
      2024). Indeed, it is unclear what it even means for an expert to specialize; for instance it seems
258
      difficult to consider specialization of an expert to a subset of tokens.
259
260   Instead, our notion of specialization is whether there exists, for any input X ∈ Rm×d , an X-dependent
261   (relatively small) subset of experts that are sufficient to accurately predict the label for that input X.
262   Recall from Definition 1 that the output of the Soft MoE layer is C(X)Ye (X), where Ye (X) ∈ Rn×d
263   stores the output of expert i in row i. Hence, zeroing out a row i of Ye (X) means that expert i does not
264   contribute anything to the final prediction. If we can zero out many rows of Ye (X) without affecting
265   the final prediction, then the remaining experts can be understood to have specialized to the input
266   X, since this means that only these remaining experts were actually required to make an accurate
267   prediction, and these experts did not require contributions from the other zeroed out experts. By
268   contrast, if zeroing out rows of Ye (X) changes the prediction, then this indicates a lack of expert
269   specialization, since the knowledge required to predict correctly on X was non-trivially spread out
      across each of the n experts.

350   4.4.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP

352
      We experiment on 4 datasets: MNIST, CIFAR10, CIFAR100 (Krizhevsky, 2009) and ImageNet-
      1k (Deng et al., 2009). For MNIST, we use the same experimental setup as in Section 4.2. Recall
353
      that the network used is very simple, and consists entirely of a Soft MoE layer and a prediction
354
      head. This small network has merely 318K total parameters, of which 307K are expert parameters.
355   As a more practical setting, we use the Astroformer-1 architecture (Dagli, 2023) for CIFAR10 and
356   CIFAR100. This hybrid transformer-convolutional architecture achieves excellent performance on
357   these datasets without leveraging extra training data. We modify Astroformer-1 by replacing the
358   MLPs in the transformer blocks with Soft MoE layers, analogous to the standard practice that is
359   followed in ViTs (Puigcerver et al., 2024). This model is larger, and has 180M total parameters,
360   of which 150M are expert parameters. Finally, we adopt the same Soft MoE variant of the ViT
361   architecture (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) used by the seminal work (Puigcerver et al., 2024) on the
362   ImageNet-1k dataset. Specifically, it replaces the MLPs in the latter half of the encoder blocks of
363
      the ViT Base model with Soft MoE layers. This is the largest architecture we consider, and it has
      513M total parameters, of which 454M are expert parameters. Due to compute constraints, scaling
364
      up our experimental protocol further (either with external pretraining data or larger model sizes) is
365
      infeasible. Nevertheless, our setup spans a range of model architecture types and scales, as well as
366   several canonical datasets. We defer further details of the various architectures and their modifications
367   to Appendix D.2.

369
      For each dataset and associated network architecture, we do the following. We train a suite of models
      where each model has a different n (total number of experts) in each Soft MoE layer. Each model
370
      is trained from scratch without utilizing any external data. As discussed in Section 4.2, when we
371
      increase n we correspondingly decrease the number of parameters in each expert, to ensure the
372   total expert expressivity is (roughly) constant. After training each network, we select for each n a
373   model that has the same test accuracy (as discussed in Section 4.2, this is important because we are
374   investigating the impact of varying n on Algorithm 1’s test performance, and so we must ensure the
375   original networks have similar test performance). For each of these trained networks and various
376   values of k, we then evaluate Algorithm 1’s performance on the test set. Concretely, we compute
377   the test accuracy of using the k experts found by Algorithm 1 for each datapoint in the test set, and
      compare this to the test accuracy of randomly selecting k experts for each datapoint in the test set.

399
400   In Figures 2 and 3 we depict, for each dataset and various choices of k, the performance of Algorithm 1
401   relative to the random selection scheme. We make two main observations.
402
      The first observation is that Algorithm 1’s accuracy may deteriorate (relative to using all experts)
403   when k < n and n is small. But if n is big, then Algorithm 1’s accuracy is often very close to
404   that of using all the experts, even when k ≪ n. For instance, on MNIST, using the k = n/2
405   experts selected by Algorithm 1 gives nearly the same performance as using all experts when
406   n ≥ 64, but has poor performance when n ≤ 8. This result consistently holds across the various
407   datasets/architectures/model scales considered: e.g. Table 13b shows that on ImageNet-1k, when n is
408   high enough, Algorithm 1 can use just 1/8 of the experts while retaining over 99% of the original
409   accuracy of using all the experts. This shows that as n increases, Algorithm 1 is better poised to
410   discover specialized experts, even though the number of expert parameters is invariant to n.
411   We believe this is an instance of the implicit bias that exists in Soft MoE. Concretely, the experts
412   identified by Algorithm 1 for an input X are those highest weighted by Csum , which is derived from
413   C(X). Our result thus shows that as n increases, the Soft MoE trains in a manner such that the C(X)
414   matrix is more informative for which experts are useful in correctly predicting the label of X, even
415   though such a property is not explicitly enforced during training! In Appendix D.4, we show that the
416   skewness of Csum does not change significantly as n increases, which suggests that the specialization
417
      identified by Algorithm 1 is not trivial by simply dropping marginally weighted experts. While we
      do not have a formal explanation for why this phenomenon occurs, we conjecture that this is an
418
      implicit bias that allows the specialized subsets to be more easily identified by Csum as n increases. A
419
      different view of the same results in Figure 2a is provided in Figure 1.
420
421   The second observation is that Algorithm 1’s test performance dominates that of random selection.
422   And often, Algorithm 1 is far better than random selection, as is particularly evident in CIFAR10 and
423
      ImageNet-1k, where random selection is very poor (see Figure 2). There are instances where random
      selection has decent accuracy, such as in CIFAR100 (see Figure 3). So to further validate our result,
424
      we use the following statistic to measure how much better Algorithm 1 is relative to random selection.
425
      We first compute the standard deviation of the random selection test accuracies (which were obtained
426   by averaging over 10 random seeds). Our statistic is then the number of standard deviations by which
427   Algorithm 1 exceeds the mean random selection accuracy. When this statistic is large, it means that
428   Algorithm 1 found an expert subset whose performance is statistically significantly larger than that
429   of the typical random expert subset. The results are shown in Table 2 where we observe very large
430   values for this statistic. This shows that our Algorithm 1 significantly outperforms random selection
431   for CIFAR100, even though random selection has decent performance on this dataset. Analogous
      tables for the other datasets are provided in Appendix D.3.

      In practice, one typically trains a very large model (with many Soft MoE layers, each with many
447
      experts) a single time, then the model is deployed and repeatedly utilized for inference. It is thus of
448   great interest to speed up computation through Soft MoE modules, since this leads to faster inference.
449   Algorithm 1 is well suited for this purpose. The preceding sections show that when the number of
450   experts n is large, then Algorithm 1 can rapidly and cheaply find a small subset of experts that can
451   predict any datapoint with minimal expected accuracy loss. So it can potentially be used for faster
452   inference through the Soft MoE.
453
      A full examination of the usefulness of Algorithm 1 for faster inference is beyond the scope of
454   our paper. A proper analysis would require massive industry-scale models, and would also be very
455   application specific because it would depend on the type and number of devices used for inference,
456   as well as the extent of distributed computing and per-device parallelization. We lack the compute
457   capacity to do such a thorough study. Nevertheless, it is immediate that in the regime of many
458   large experts, where a forward pass through an expert has non-trivial cost, Algorithm 1 should save
459   computation since its overhead is relatively negligible. As a simple example to show the potential of

      5     R ELATED W ORK
476
477
      Mixture of Experts. Our work falls squarely in the literature on MoEs, which originated several
      decades ago (Jacobs et al., 1991; Jordan & Jacobs, 1993) and has been revived as the Sparse
478
      MoE (Shazeer et al., 2017). Nevertheless, there is a significant difference between our investigation
479
      and the majority of past work. The majority of past work in MoE focuses primarily on computational
480   considerations, such as (in the context of Sparse MoE) routing a token to only a single expert for
481   efficiency (Fedus et al., 2022), and ensuring load balancing by incorporating linear programs (Lewis
482   et al., 2021) or having the experts select tokens (Zhou et al., 2022) or re-routing dropped tokens (Zeng
483   et al., 2024). Indeed, the Soft MoE (Puigcerver et al., 2024) was recently introduced to address the
484   various training instabilities suffered by Sparse MoE, and performs very well in applications like
485   vision (Puigcerver et al., 2024), RL (Obando-Ceron et al., 2024) and audio processing (Cappellazzo
      et al., 2024). By contrast, our investigation begins with a more fundamental question – what are the


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      implicit biases that occur as a result of Soft MoE’s particular manner of combining tokens and expert
487   outputs? While analogous fundamental investigations of the gating function do exist in the context of
488   Sparse MoEs (Chen et al., 2022; Dikkala et al., 2023) and some of its variants (Nguyen et al., 2023;
489   2024), to the best of our knowledge this line of investigation is novel in Soft MoE.
490
      Expert Specialization. The use of MoE layers in a network is often motivated by the desire for
491
      expert specialization, since this implies a more efficient use of the network parameters. To our
492
      knowledge, all prior work on expert specialization has been conducted in the context of Sparse MoE.
493   While some studies demonstrate that expert specialization may occur for Sparse MoE (Chen et al.,
494   2022; Dikkala et al., 2023), others show that specialization is often limited and requires architectural
495   innovations (Krishnamurthy et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2024; Oldfield et al., 2024). These innovations are
496   different than Soft MoE, where it is not even a priori clear what expert specialization means. Indeed,
497   the seminal work acknowledges this limitation (Puigcerver et al., 2024). In our paper, we offer a
498   notion of expert specialization in Soft MoE, and show not only that it occurs but can be efficiently
499   detected.
500   Sparse Activation & Pruning. Our work is also related to the literature on sparsely activating or
501   pruning a network, since our Algorithm 1 can be viewed as a form of pruning at inference. For instance,
502   there is work on using RL to conditionally activate only certain units in a generic network for faster
503   training and inference (Bengio et al., 2016), pruning CNN kernels for efficient inference (Molchanov
504   et al., 2017), and developing adaptive computation modules for transformers (Wójcik et al., 2023).
505   While a complete survey of this vast area is beyond the current scope (Blalock et al., 2020), we
506   emphasize that our form of pruning is entirely specific to Soft MoE, since it relies on the C(X)
507
      matrix. It can be used with or without many other types of pruning.
508
509   6    D ISCUSSION
510
511   Limitations. Our work has a number of limitations. While our Theorem 1 is a negative result for
512   a single expert’s representation power, we are unable to prove a corresponding positive result for
513   multiple experts (although in Appendix B we provide empirical evidence for increased representation
514   power as the number of experts increases). A different limitation is that we are unable to provide a
515
      thorough analysis of the extent to which Algorithm 1 can reduce computation at inference (due to a
      lack of compute capacity). We believe both limitations provide exciting directions for future work.
516
517   Conclusion. In this paper, we studied the Soft Mixture of Experts architecture. We eschewed the
518   traditional viewpoint of scaling expert parameter count in a manner that allows efficient training and
519   inference. Instead, we adopted an orthogonal perspective, and studied implicit biases that arise from
520   Soft MoE’s particular manner of combining token and expert outputs. We showed that even with an
521
      arbitrarily powerful single expert, Soft MoE cannot represent simple convex functions, thus showing
      that good representation power is predicated on having multiple experts. We also introduced a notion
522
      of expert specialization for Soft MoE, and provided an algorithm that efficiently discovers specialized
523
      experts when the number of experts is large. Overall, our analysis (both theoretical and empirical)
524   highlights non-traditional reasons for why using many smaller experts is preferable to using fewer
525   larger experts, even when the total expert parameter count remains fixed.

788   B    C AN S OFT M O E WITH M ULTIPLE E XPERTS R EPRESENT ∥ · ∥2 ?
789
790   Our Theorem 1 shows that Soft MoE with a single expert cannot represent the function t defined as
791   t(X) = ∥X∥2 , even when the expert is arbitrarily powerful. However, it leaves open the possibility
792   that Soft MoE with multiple experts could represent this function t. In this section, we provide a
793   simple experiment to empirically check this. We consider the same problem of learning the function
794
      t, and we train a suite of Soft MoE models, each with a different number of experts, n, where
      n ∈ {1, 2, 5, 10}. As always (and as subsequently discussed), we fix the total expert parameter count.
795
796   We consider the input X ∈ R10 , where X ∼ N (0, 5I). For an input vector X, the label y was set to
797   ∥X∥2 . Each batch of data is newly generated from this data distribution. We do two experiments with
798   two different tokenization strategies. Concretely, the input was tokenized into either X ∈ R5×2 or
799   X ∈ R2×5 , i.e. 5 tokens each with dimension 2, or 2 tokens each with dimension 5, by partitioning
800
      the 10 elements of an input vector in order of their dimension index.
801   In the spirit of Theorem 1, the Soft MoE models considered consisted only of a Soft MoE layer, and
802   a non-trainable summation prediction head. Therefore, given an input matrix X ∈ Rm×d (i.e., m
803   tokens each with dimension d), it is passed directly to the Soft MoE layer. Each expert was a two
804   layer MLP that had input and output of dimension d and a single hidden layer with dimension 10d/n.
805   Thus, the number of expert parameters in each model was always constant at 20d2 , regardless of the
806
      number of experts n in the model. The output from the Soft MoE layer was then summed to produce
      the final scalar prediction from the model. Each model was trained with a batch size of 10, 000, with
807
      the Adam optimizer using default hyperparameters and learning rate of 1e-3 over 500 epochs.
808
