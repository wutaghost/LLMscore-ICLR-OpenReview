009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-agent systems are prevalent across various domains, characterized by mis-
012            aligned objectives and information asymmetry, which facilitate the study of in-
013            centive design and information design. Existing research often assumes known
014            models and static environments. Motivated by this, we propose a Dynamic Incen-
015            tive and Information Design (DIID) framework for finite-horizon Markov games,
016            involving a principal and multiple agents. Our focus is on how the principal learns
017
               her optimal policy based on data generated through interactions with agents. The
               main challenge lies in balancing the principalвҖҷs regret and violations of agentsвҖҷ in-
018
               centive compatibility constraints during interactions. We establish a lower bound
019
               characterizing the trade-off between the two objectives and propose an algorithm
020
               attaining the optimal trade-off, i.e. OМғ(T 2/3 ) regret and constraint violation. Ad-
021
               ditionally, with access to additional unilateral deviation information of the agents,
022
               we propose an algorithm attaining improved guarantees that achieve OМғ(T 1/2 ) for
023            both regret and constraint violation simultaneously.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Multi-agent systems, where multiple decision-makers or вҖңagentsвҖқ interact, have become ubiqui-
029   tous. These range from drivers selecting routes in traffic networks to financial entities reacting to
030   market signals. Misaligned objectives and information asymmetry in multi-agent systems facili-
031   tate characterizing rational behavior and designing incentives and information. These issues have
032   been extensively studied in economic literature. For instance, traffic congestion can be alleviated
033
      by steering driversвҖҷ route choices. Measures like congestion pricing impose higher fees during peak
      hours (Barrera & Garcia, 2014), while real-time traffic updates empower drivers to make informed
034
      route decisions (Arnott et al., 1991). Similarly, in financial markets, regulators utilize incentives
035
      like tax benefits and penalties to guide market behavior (Goodhart et al., 2012), while transparency
036   mechanisms shape investor perceptions, fostering stability (Goldstein & Yang, 2017). Many studies
037   in economic literature have examined the existence and definition of equilibrium (Bergemann &
038   Morris, 2013; 2016) and the properties of the solution.
039
      However, studies in the economic literature often assume that the model is known, with few focusing
040
      on how to learn equilibrium notions from data, e.g. learn the model through multiple interactions.
041
      Moreover, these studies mainly address static cases and do not account for dynamics in the system,
042   such as how decisions made now affect the evolution of the systemвҖҷs state and future utility. Moti-
043   vated by this, we propose a general Dynamic Incentive and Information Design (DIID) framework
044   encompassing joint incentive and information design in a finite-horizon Markov game, involving
045   a single principal and multiple agents. The principal manipulates agents through (i) misaligned
046   objectives, by taking actions to directly influence agentsвҖҷ rewards (i.e., incentive design), and (ii)
047   information asymmetry, by having access to a random variable that agents cannot see (an external
048   parameter) to send signals and change the agentsвҖҷ beliefs about that variable, which indirectly influ-
049   ence the followersвҖҷ actions (i.e., information design). In other words, in our framework, the principal
050
      can perform two moves simultaneously in each iteration. It can directly change the agentsвҖҷ reward
      functions by taking an action, and change the posterior by sending a signal.
051
052   Given principalвҖҷs actions and signaling schemes, in each step, the agentsвҖҷ decision making problem
053   is reduced to a Bayesian game. In this case, the policies of rational agents converge to a Bayesian
      Correlated Equilibrium (BCE) (Bergemann & Morris, 2013; 2016). Thus, the principalвҖҷs problem

054
      is to design an incentive and information structure such that the resulting BCE maximizes the prin-
055   cipalвҖҷs cumulative reward, i.e., to find a Stackelberg equilibrium (von Stackelberg, 1952) of the
056   leader-follower game. That is, the principalвҖҷs optimal policy characterizes the optimal way she
057   steers the agents in her favor, by changing their reward functions and beliefs.
058
      Our focus is on how to learn the principalвҖҷs optimal policy in the online learning setting, through
059
      the data generated by the interaction between the principal and agents. Unlike prior works on online
060
      information design (Gan et al., 2022c; Wu et al., 2022; Bernasconi et al., 2022), we do not assume
061   that the principal has knowledge of the agentsвҖҷ reward function. The principalвҖҷs optimal policy
062   is defined by constrained optimization, where the constraint ensures that the agents adopt a BCE
063   against the principalвҖҷs policy. As a result, to find the principalвҖҷs optimal policy efficiently from data,
064   we need to estimate the agentsвҖҷ reward functions and predict how they respond to the principal.
065
      To accurately estimate the reward functions, the principal needs to incentivize the agents to try all
066
      actions, which might not be feasible in general. For example, if some actions are strictly dominated
067   and thus never be executed by the agents. In this case, it is impossible for the principal to estimate
068   the rewards on these actions. To bypass this challenge, we assume that the principal has the power
069   to take actions on behalf of the agents, and she additionally suffers from the violation of the BCE
070   constraint. Under this setting, our goal is to design efficient online learning with both sublinear
071   regret and constraint violation. To this end, we need to (a) explore efficiently so as to obtain an
072   accurate estimate of the BCE constraint, and (b) quantify how the estimation error of the BCE
073   constraint affects the regret. Additionally, our algorithm should be able to handle large state spaces
074   by incorporating function approximations.

095
096   The principal-agent problems have been a persistent focus in economic research, which encompasses
097   problems such as incentive design (Sandholm, 2003), information design (Kamenica & Gentzkow,
098   2011), as well as coordination mechanism design for the generalized principal-agent problems that
099   combine both Myerson (1982); Gan et al. (2022a). These works focus on characterizing outcomes
100   in static environments and addressing computational aspects. In recent years, there has also been
101   growing interest in the computation and learning aspects of sequential principal-agent problems. For
102   example, Zhang & Conitzer (2021); Cacciamani et al. (2023a) studied sequential incentive design,
103   and Celli et al. (2020); Gan et al. (2022b); Wu et al. (2022); Bernasconi et al. (2022); Iyer et al.
104
      (2023) studied sequential information design.
105   The closest works to ours are the line that studies Bayesian persuasion (Kamenica & Gentzkow,
106   2011) under Markov games (Wu et al., 2022; Gan et al., 2022b; Iyer et al., 2023), where the payoff-
107   relevant state evolves according to a Markov chain. Specifically, Wu et al. (2022) considered a
      finite-horizon Markov persuasion process, where a single principal seeks to persuade a stream of

      myopic agents to maximize his cumulative rewards. However, in the learning aspect, it assumes
109   the absence of knowledge about the transition kernel and presumes that the principal knows the
110   reward function. Gan et al. (2022b); Bernasconi et al. (2023) studied a similar setup with a far-
111   sighted receiver, but their focus is on the computational aspect. In contrast, our focus is on learning
112   the optimal persuasion policy from data. Even in the straightforward scenario where the receiver
113   is myopic, we emphasize that the lack of knowledge regarding the receiverвҖҷs reward presents a
114   significant challenge for learning. A recent work by Iyer et al. (2023) further considered the case

117
      unknown, in which case, agentвҖҷs belief now depends on the realized history of the Markov chain.
      In summary, two key differences between our work and these aforementioned related works are that
118
      (i) we do not make the assumption that the principal knows the agentвҖҷs reward function, and (ii) we
119
      allow multiple agents that play a noncooperative game.
