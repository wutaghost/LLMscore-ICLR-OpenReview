010

               Current regulations on powerful AI capabilities are narrowly focused on “foun-
012
               dation” or “frontier” models. However, these terms are vague and inconsistently
013            defined, leading to an unstable foundation for governance efforts. Critically, pol-
014            icy debates often fail to consider the data used with these models, despite the clear
015            link between data and model performance. Even (relatively) “small” models that
016            fall outside the typical definitions of foundation and frontier models can achieve
017            equivalent outcomes when exposed to sufficiently specific datasets. In this work,
018            we illustrate the importance of considering dataset size and content as essential
019            factors in assessing the risks posed by models both today and in the future. More
020            broadly, we emphasize the risk posed by over-regulating reactively and provide
021
               a path towards careful, quantitative evaluation of capabilities that can lead to a
               simplified regulatory environment.

025   1   T HE S HORTCOMINGS OF T ODAY ’ S AI G OVERNANCE
026
027
      As AI has made its way to wider audiences, it has continued its rapid pace of development, giving
028
      everyday users highly specialized computing tools and capabilities. This raises questions for gov-
029
      ernments, academics, and commercial labs about whether certain AI capabilities or behaviors should
030   be deemed as too “risky” for public access (Dragan et al., 2024).
031
032   Today’s AI governance efforts have coalesced around the terms “frontier”, “foundation”, “dual-use”,
033
      and “general purpose” to describe the largest, most capable of these models. In governance docu-
      ments, models described by these terms are subject to additional scrutiny and regulatory interest.
034
      Despite general agreement for the types of AI-accelerated risks that regulations aim to curtail, there
035
      is less clarity and consensus on concrete definitions for such models. In an effort to define the char-
036   acteristics of these large, capable models, a number of policy documents have focused on parameter
037   counts and/or FLOPs, measures of model size and compute requirement (European Union, 2024).
038
039
      We argue that this approach is short-sighted for three reasons. First, there is no consistent definition
      of “frontier”, “foundation”, “dual-use”, and “general purpose” models. This lack of definitional
040
      clarity has led to a governance landscape with misguided quantities, such as FLOPs and parameters
041
      counts, and ceilings for what constitutes a covered capability; we elaborate on this in Section 2.1.
042   Second, advances in efficient machine learning means that models require fewer parameters and
043   FLOPs to achieve the same outcomes, resulting in capable models that fall below regulatory ceilings.
044   Finally, the focus on the largest and most compute-intensive models ignores the fact existing, smaller
045   models can be just as capable as their larger counterparts. These factors culminate in inadvertent
046   loopholes that powerful capabilities can slip through, rendering expensive regulatory efforts not only
047   useless but potentially detractory from beneficial uses of AI technologies.
048   The broader field of machine learning has recognized the role of data as a direct indicator of model
049   performance (Hoffmann et al., 2022b; Ng et al., 2021), suggesting that dataset quality and size
050   should also be included as factors in conversations surrounding model capabilities. In this paper, we
051   first discuss the limitations of the current model-focused governance ecosystem. Then, we demon-
052   strate the value of a data-focused approach to AI governance. In particular, we present experiments
053   corroborating the role that dataset size plays in model capability. Finally, we propose legal and
      technical approaches to AI governance rooted in our understanding of the data-model relationship.

      2     D EFINITIONAL C HALLENGES AND F LAWED L IMITS IN AI G OVERNANCE
055
056
      Much of the conversation around AI regulation has centered itself around the prevention of behaviors
057   that are deemed to be “harmful” or otherwise detrimental to society (Dafoe, 2018; Hoffman & Frase,
058   2023). The mention of “harm” is too often unqualified and does not address the capabilities of
059   existing technologies that may already be capable of much of the malicious behavior discussed in
060   AI policy circles today. For example, AI for biological agent design is widely cited as a potential
061   harm (Callaway, 2024), yet computational drug discovery has been the norm since the 1980s and
062   has enabled the discovery of drugs such as ritonavir, a medication critical in treating both HIV and
063   COVID-19 (Van Drie, 2007). The conversation surrounding the use of AI to further societal harms
064   must contextualize the additional marginal risk posed by these methods when compared to existing
065
      technologies such as search engines or statistical inference algorithms.
066   The AI governance ecosystem’s difficulty in defining and identifying harm extends into fragmented
067   efforts to define modern machine learning capabilities and the factors that make them powerful.
068   In the following sections, we demonstrate the shortcomings and inconsistencies of these model-
069   focused AI governance efforts, while identifying key drivers of AI risk that are currently overlooked
070   in modern AI policy.
071
072   2.1   A N U NSTABLE D EFINITION F OUNDATION
073
      The use of the terms “foundation”, “frontier”, “dual-use”, and “general purpose” to describe ma-
074
      chine learning models has arisen in the past few years in an effort to isolate classes of models seen
075   as posing the greatest risk of harm to public safety. In 2021, Stanford University researchers in-
076   troduced “foundation models” as a term of art in “On the Opportunities and Risks of Foundation
077   Models” (Bommasani et al., 2022). The paper uses the term to describe machine learning models
078   trained using self-supervised learning methods on large sets of data to the point that they demonstrate
079   emergent behaviors during inference.
080
      The term “foundation model” has spread swiftly throughout the AI research community to a point of
081   saturation where any model trained on a subjectively large set of data can be termed “foundational.”
082   More recently, the terms “frontier model,” introduced in “Frontier AI Regulation: Managing Emerg-
083   ing Risks to Public Safety” (Anderljung et al., 2023) and “dual-use model,” found in the “Executive
084   Order on Safe, Secure, and Trustworthy Development and Use of Artificial Intelligence” (The White
085   House, 2023) and the EU AI Act (European Union, 2024), have arisen as similar descriptions of
086   large, cutting-edge models with an increased potential for harm. The cross-cutting motivation of
087   regulatory efforts has been that these types of models can pose serious risks to the general public
088   and should be governed as such.
089   Different regulatory bodies have similar motivations for controlling AI models that they perceive
090   as enabling risky behaviors. Despite shared goals, these efforts are not aligned with respect to
091   the definitions they utilize to bound powerful AI capabilities. In Table 1, we highlight impactful
092   papers and policies that have shaped international AI governance. In particular, we highlight the
093   inconsistencies between how influential works which first introduced various terms and thresholds
094   disagree from their actualization in policy proposals.
095   Terminology such as “foundation” and “frontier” are terms of art that have non-static and con-
096   tentious definitions, suggesting that utility-based terminology such as “general purpose” may be
097   better regulatory terms instead. Furthermore, a leading approach is to to bound “risky” AI models in
098   terms of the amount of computation required to train them. As we demonstrate in Section 2.2, these
099   thresholds do not appropriately bound “risky” AI models—a driving goal for regulatory efforts. Ad-
100   ditionally, the documents that discuss training on “large” amounts of data do not define how many
101
      data points meet the bar, leaving leeway for bound parties to argue exemptions.1

262   3     DATA IS M ISSING FROM THE C ONVERSATION
263
264   Machine learning capabilities are not singularly determined by their model architecture. Rather, ma-
265   chine learning capabilities are defined by both the model and the data provided. We define “data” as
266   any information a model is exposed to, whether it is during training or deployment. This paper aims
267   to center data in AI governance conversations. We suggest that models alone are not harmful; rather,
268   the unique combination of models exposed to specific datasets (whether during training or inference)
269   and subsequently being used for specific purposes may pose a risk to public safety (Baldridge et al.,
      2024).


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Traditionally, training data (both pre-training and fine-tuning) was the only source of information
271   that a model would have access to before making a prediction. However, models can now incorporate
272   new, unseen data during inference through frameworks such as prompting and Retrieval-Augmented
273   Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020). Therefore, both the training and deployment data are relevant
274   when considering how a model incorporates information in its outputs.
275
276   3.1       B IG DATA TO U SABLE I NFORMATION
277
278   The rapid rise of AI since approximately 2010 can largely be attributed to (1) advancements in
279   computational hardware in accordance with Moore’s Law, and (2) a focus on large quantities of
280   data. Models are useless without data, and the availability of “foundational” datasets, such as Im-
281
      ageNet (Deng et al., 2009) and Common Crawl,7 brought modern machine learning capabilities to
      bear. AI datasets today, created through Internet scraping, are often orders of magnitude larger.
282
283   Dataset size is a key component in “scaling laws,” or predictions of performance within a family of
284   models as a function of variables in a training recipe. Research in this area finds strong relationships
285   between model performance and amount of training data, amount of computation, and model param-
286   eters (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022a; Zhai et al., 2022; Google, 2023). Additionally,
287
      both Hoffmann et al. (2022a) and Google (2023) find that model and optimal dataset size scale at
      equal proportions as training compute increases.
288
289   However, even an optimal training recipe with an appropriate amount of data, parameters, and com-
290   pute does not necessarily produce a useful model. The dataset content is a crucial factor. A model
291   “trained on the internet” can unsurprisingly exhibit the same bias (Fleisig et al., 2024) and toxic-
292   ity (Liang et al., 2023) present in the data and also fall short in other areas: it may fail at logical
293
      reasoning (Berglund et al., 2023), algebraic computation, or following a user’s instructions, to name
      a few examples. In a limiting argument, a multi-trillion parameter model trained only on Shake-
294
      speare novels may never be able to reason about chemical weapon design.
295
296   To address this, models are fine-tuned on higher-quality, curated data. Popular techniques that rely
297   on high-quality data include instruction tuning (e.g., reinforcement learning from human feedback,
298   or RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022)), training models to use tools or act as agents (Schick et al., 2023),
299
      or supervised fine-tuning for a specific task, such as generating images in a particular artistic style.
300   There is evidence that with the right data and training regime, models in the millions or single digit
301   billions of parameters can perform comparably, if not better, than counterparts orders of magnitude
302   larger in many domains (Yu et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024; Eldan & Li, 2023). In fact, once a model
303   is sufficiently large, focusing on improving the quality and utilization of data can yield greater gains
304   in performance for a task over simply increasing the size of the model. For example, the Retrieval
305
      Augmented Fine-Tuning (Zhang et al., 2024) framework has been shown to improve the question-
      answering performance of a 7B parameter Llama2 language model over that of GPT-3.5, which
306
      otherwise significantly outperforms it out-of-the-box.

309   4       DATA -C ENTRISM O PENS NEW A NALYTIC F RONTIERS
310
311   Modern machine learning methods are useful beyond traditional data querying and correlation tools
312   such as search engines in part due to their ability to retrieve, compile, and organize data even when
313   given unspecific queries. Below we outline two distinct features that are uniquely enabled by the
314   combination of ML models and data: (1) retrieval, where a model outputs information retrieved
315
      directly from its data, and (2) derivation, where a model compiles or synthesizes items from provided
      data to generate new information. These features enable new ways to interact with complex data that
316
      would otherwise be difficult to manage, offering potential benefits as well as risks, which we explore
317
      further below.
318
319
      4.1       R ETRIEVAL
320
321   As our ability to collect and maintain digital information has soared over the last few decades,
322   retrieving the right results for a certain query has become a core technological focus. Billions of
323
          7
              https://commoncrawl.org/the-data/


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      dollars have been spent towards developing efficient data representations for search engines (Brin &
325   Page, 1998; Dean & Ghemawat, 2008) and databases (Corbett et al., 2012; Shvachko et al., 2010),
326   and towards creating the algorithms to find and retrieve these results. Now, AI models trained on
327   large amounts of data have become both capable encoders and retrievers of data (in addition to
328   generators, as we describe in the section on derivation). This becomes a problem when a model has
329   been exposed to specific data points that would be considered sensitive if directly retrieved, such
330   as credit card numbers or classified information. The retrieval itself could occur either through (1)
331   a model memorizing and then reproducing points in training data, or (2) retrieving from a large
332   amount of data provided at test time, such as a company’s internal database. Below, we describe
333
      these two cases in more detail.
334
335
      Retrieval from training data. Datasets contaminated with outliers have historically relied on
      dataset volume to dilute outlier effects. This leads to the misconception that a small quantity of
336
      “harmful” data points can be negated by massive amounts of otherwise commonplace data. Unfor-
337
      tunately, this intuition does not translate to modern machine learning methods. Large models are
338   known to memorize some parts of training data and reproduce them if queried correctly (Carlini
339   et al., 2022). Therefore, large models can retrieve, and therefore utilize, harmful data even if it is
340   present in a negligible quantity.
341
      However, memorization does not occur across data equally: prior work shows that “average” training
342
      samples are less likely to be memorized, whereas outlierse and duplicated data points are more likely
343
      to be memorized (Feldman & Zhang, 2020; Feldman, 2020; Carlini et al., 2022). As certain types of
344   data, such as child sexual abuse material, are outliers on the Internet (Thiel, 2023), memorization of
345   such data poses an inherent risk in the downstream usage of affected models, especially combined
346   with the powerful retrieval abilities of current models. Nasr et al. (2023) is another example of
347   work where ChatGPT was used to retrieve training data which comprised personally identifiable
348   information of dozens of individuals.
349
350   Retrieval from previously unseen data. An AI system may also be exposed to entire new do-
351   mains of data during inference that were not present during training. Models can utilize new, unseen
352   data through prompting or integration with external databases. Models’ ability to effectively inter-
353   pret new domains without prior training marks a significant shift in how we store and use informa-
354
      tion. Instead of creating expensive, specialized systems to process data like financial documents or
      hospital records, modern general-purpose models can understand and work with novel data formats
355
      they have never encountered before while requiring minimal engineering effort.
356
357   Many of today’s large models are being specifically designed to respond flexibly to new tasks and
358   prompt formats. In-context learning (Brown et al., 2020) allows users to provide example input-
359   output pairs of a task to a large model which can equip it to solve novel instances of that task. Further,
360
      modern AI systems may be used to efficiently sift through large amounts of data at inference time—
      even if they have not seen it before—using frameworks such RAG (Gao et al., 2024). In RAG, given
361
      a user query, an answer can be generated by efficiently searching a database for relevant concepts
362
      and making sense of this new information to return a relevant response (Yasunaga et al., 2023;
363   Kong et al., 2024; Blattmann et al., 2022). As these systems can now return sensitive examples
364   not seen before by dynamically augmenting their knowledge or understanding new tasks from test-
365   time examples, they can therefore be used in the furtherance of actions that pose risks to society
366   by drastically lowering the boundary to both finding and exploiting risk-posing information (Barrett
367   et al., 2023).
368
369   4.2   D ERIVATION
370
371   As AI capabilities increase, a growing concern is the generation of original or derivative information
372   that is more revealing than the data provided to the model. For example, if a system is given two
373   entry-level textbooks in physics and chemistry, respectively, and uses independent concepts from
374
      either to build a toy rocket, we term the process of arriving at the toy rocket instructions “derivation.”
375   This feature is especially present in modern machine learning methods when compared to technolo-
376   gies such as databases due to their ability to synthesize unrelated pieces of information on the fly.
377   While retrieved content is often straightforward to recognize and check—i.e., it may be quickly
      obvious that a generated phone number is real, and possible to check if a particular image was con-


                                                          7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      tained within training or deployment-time data—derived content is more nuanced and difficult to
379   measure, and thus may present a greater concern.
380
381   Under this category, multiple pieces of otherwise mundane information could be compiled to form
382
      information that is now sensitive. For instance, a language model trained for code generation could
      be provided a description of a vulnerability and be used to generate code for exploiting it. Models
383
      have already begun to present synthesis capabilities in different arenas, such as for code generation
384
      of programming languages with low data availability (Mora et al., 2024) and the generation of
385   Mathematics Olympiad-level geometric proofs as part of larger pipelines (Trinh et al., 2024).
386
387   The maximal extent to which current models are capable of derivation is not yet clear as methodolo-
388
      gies for inducing such capabilities are constantly evolving. For example, although modern language
      models have shown nascent indicators of capability to generate novel research ideas in fields such
389
      as natural language processing, the ideas they generate lack diversity and may not be tractable (Si
390
      et al., 2024). Modern image generation models struggle to synthesize images precisely adhering
391   to descriptions of unique combinations of objects and their attributes previously unseen in training
392   data (Huang et al., 2023). Our intent in this section is not to establish a measure for models’ deriva-
393   tion capability but rather to bring attention to derivation as a unique capability offered by modern
394   ML models.

      5     AVENUES FOR DATA -F ORWARD R EGULATION
398
399   Given our analysis above, the inclusion of data in nascent AI governance conversations can sim-
400   plify the regulatory overhead by enabling the use of existing legal frameworks and the creation and
401
      execution of novel, data-backed evaluation schemes. Specifically, there are numerous policies and
      laws surrounding the appropriate use of data in contexts that are deemed to be of risk to the public.
402
      Instead of reinventing these policies using a new set of definitions that are model-specific, expand-
403
      ing and modifying them to account for the use of data by powerful models might offer a simpler
404   path towards effective evaluation frameworks in areas where definitions alone are vague, leading to
405   simpler regulations.
406
407
      5.1   A PPLYING E XISTING DATA -F OCUSED L EGAL AND R EGULATORY A PPROACHES
408
409   Significant work has been and continues to be done to mitigate malicious model outputs or behav-
410   iors. Thus far, model creators have relied on identifying malicious outputs or behaviors through red
411   teaming and safety training (Ganguli et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2023).
412
      However, some classes of outputs or behaviors that are deemed risky could more easily be stemmed
413
      by careful curation of datasets. Unique information such as the relationship between a person and
414
      their social security number, or specific instances of child sexual abuse material, is extremely un-
415   likely to be generated if that data is never provided to a model.
416
417   There exists a range of legal and regulatory frameworks that cover many categories of model outputs
418
      that are of greatest concern, including personal identifiable information, child sexual abuse mate-
      rial, and classified content. Data-centrism prevents models from acquiring the capacity for harmful
419
      behaviors prior to the expenditure of computation. Since existing regulations can be applied, AI
420
      governance can be achieved without the need for new regulatory frameworks.
421
422
423
      5.2   T ECHNOLOGICAL L EVERS FOR DATA -F ORWARD R EGULATION
424
      Although research has shown that certain model capabilities emerge once sufficient model size and
425
      compute are attained (Wei et al., 2022), establishing regulatory thresholds is ill-defined given just
426   these two metrics. As discussed in Section 3, models provided with the right data can perform
427   comparably to, if not better than, larger and more compute intensive alternatives. Further, a model
428   must first be paired with sensitive information for it to make use of it. That is, the model does not
429   exist in a vacuum, and a data-forward approach that prioritizes data content and quality filtration
430   over model size and computation could yield greater benefits in mitigating risks posed by the use of
431   models. Here, we briefly outline examples of existing techniques and argue for the development of
      new methods.


                                                        8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      Existing data filtration. Modern web-scale datasets are extremely large, numbering in the billions
433   to trillions of data points. As such, human review of every data point is not possible from either a
434   labor or monetary perspective. However, the volume of data does not permit the abdication of
435   responsibility or duty to curate datasets responsibly. In response, methods have been proposed to
436   partially or fully automate the filtration process (Albalak et al., 2024). Content can be filtered based
437   on fixed patterns such as blacklisted source URLs or key words, however, these methods can be
438   rigid and insensitive to the nuance of usage context. Large vision and language models such as
439   CLIP (Schramowski et al., 2022) and Meta’s Llama Guard (Inan et al., 2023) have been used to
440   classify whether data points are risky under human-defined criteria and can be more sensitive to
441
      context than blacklist-based methods. However, these methods are far from perfect—offering an
      important avenue for future research.
442
443
444   Quantifying risk for workloads. In addition to data filtration schemes, a rigorous evaluation
445   framework for powerful AI models that is inclusive of both models and data is needed. Many
446   approaches are feasible, and we detail an evaluation framework under development that attempts to
447   solidify this discussion into a quantifiable benchmark.
448
      For example, imagine asking a model a question in a setting where accuracy of the answer matters,
449   say “what materials make up Saturn’s rings?” Short, broken answers such as “rock, water” would be
450   regarded as unreliable or incorrect as opposed to an answer that demonstrates mastery of grammar
451   and facts such as “The rings of Saturn are primarily composed of countless small particles of ice
452   and rock. These particles range in size from tiny grains of dust to larger chunks that can be several
453   meters across.”
454
      For a specific type of output, there is likely a minimum size threshold for a model to be capable of
455   learning the syntax of that output domain (Chen et al., 2024). The initial stage of model training
456   is focused on acquiring fluency—object detection models learn what the shape and proportion of a
457   valid detection looks like, and language models learn the underlying structure and grammar of the
458   languages over which they operate. In this stage, models are parameter-bound—the largest gains
459   in fluency are likely to come from making models bigger. However, once a model has passed this
460   hypothesized stage to learn the syntax “well enough,” we posit that the model is now data-bound
461   and improvements to performance, or correctness, are more likely to come from improvements to
462   the content and utilization of data rather than just from arbitrary scaling (Wei et al., 2022; Yu et al.,
463
      2023; Eldan & Li, 2023).
464   This inherent relationship between fluency and correctness can be used as a powerful tool to regulate
465   AI capabilities in a data-parameter inclusive fashion. For any arbitrary task, the performance of a
466   model on that task can be plotted on a fluency-correctness curve. Once all workloads are plotted,
467   the resulting risk profile can be adjudicated and a resulting judgment—whether a reduction in the
468   parameter count of the model or a specific pruning of the training dataset is necessary—can be made
469
      by the model developers.
470   Ultimately, such an evaluation framework can aid in the development of regulatory system through
471   which the government and model developers can safely, privately, and precisely iterate on removing
472   the ability of models to aid in risky tasks prior to model release.
473
474
475
      5.3   I NCENTIVIZING DATA G OVERNANCE T OOLS AND P RACTICES
476
477
      Just as existing policies and regulations advocate for the standardization of model documentation,
      such as model or system cards (Mitchell et al., 2019), data-centrism motivates the standardization
478
      of dataset documentation. Comprehensive approaches for doing so have been proposed already,
479
      such as Datasheets for Datasets (Gebru et al., 2021) or Data Cards (Pushkarna et al., 2022). These
480   documentation formalisms currently detail dataset properties regarding content, structure, prepro-
481   cessing, distribution, and intended or potential use cases. Given the common practice of aggregating
482   datasets from multiple sources, mechanisms for documenting and tracking the provenance of dataset
483   contents, such as Data Provenance Cards (Longpre et al., 2023), would greatly ease verification of
484   information available to a model. Further, standardized ontologies (Zeng et al., 2024) that categorize
485   and rank “risky information” can be applied to each dataset in a provenance card, which can then be
      used as a first approximation of potential retrieval and derivation capabilities.


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      In practice, red teaming (Perez et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022) has become the standard to evaluate
487   whether models intended for release pose a risk to public safety. However, red teaming is, as of
488   yet, not standardized. Further, with the rapid increase in the amount of models that need to be
489   assessed, there exists no mechanism through which the potential of models to perform specific tasks
490   can be estimated before training them. The development of a technical framework for measuring the
491   dynamics of the performance of a model family for a given task as a function of both model scale
492   and quality of training data, particularly one that can identify inflection points at which a model’s
493   performance becomes bound by its data rather than its size, would be an important tool for more
494   precisely identifying when models could feasibly be used in the furtherance of behaviors that harm
495
      society.
496
497   6   C ONCLUSION : E VOLVING AI G OVERNANCE A LONGSIDE AI
498       T ECHNOLOGY
499
500   Despite rapid growths in both model and dataset sizes in recent years, AI policies have hinged on
501   thresholds, definitional concepts, and qualifiers that limit their medium-to-long term liability. For a
502   technology that will be with us for the foreseeable future, we can, and should, approach governance
503   in a more deliberate manner, with a clear understanding of what enables these capabilities to be
504   powerful in the first place.
505   Similar to how an arbitrarily large engine, no matter how specifically quantified, would be useless
506   without defining the kind of fuel used with it, the AI policy landscape mistakenly focuses on a small
507   set of model-based thresholds, particularly FLOP and parameter counts. Neither fully define how
508   powerful a machine learning model may be without an understanding of the data that accompanies
509   them. Furthermore, the lack of definitional clarity with what constitutes a “frontier”, “foundation”,
510   “dual-use”, or “general purpose” model complicates governance efforts. More generally, these two
511   trends in governance further propagate the outdated idea that the largest, most compute intensive
512   models are those which drive AI risk. As we reach a point where smaller models, when paired with
513
      large, foundational datasets or small, high-quality datasets, can perform as well as larger models,
      this narrow approach creates loopholes and unfairly penalizes otherwise beneficial technologies.
514
515   Centering data offers a more durable approach to AI governance, particularly as trends in quantifi-
516   able measures of model capability are difficult to predict. A focus on data also provides an opportu-
517   nity to better research, define, and respond to benefits and risks posed by AI, a debate that remains
518   nebulous in both policy and technical circles. Centering data also provides avenues for existing reg-
519
      ulations surrounding sensitive types of data to apply while also clearing the way for new evaluation
      methods to quantify the use of data and models together. Expanding model-based regulations to
520
      focus additionally on their paired data builds a stronger foundation that is less prone to collapse.
521
522   While a pivot in the governance landscape may be daunting, a focus on data provides the opportu-
523   nities and incentives for government, academic researchers, civil society, and the private sector to
524   develop new tools and approaches that lead to meaningful policies.

      A    A SSUMPTIONS AND L IMITATIONS
919
920
      Given the rapid pace of AI development, we acknowledge the limits of our core analytic assump-
921   tions, grounded in the current state-of-art in the field, that drive the analysis and recommendations
922   in this work. If these building blocks are outpaced by future developments, then this work should be
923   revisited.
924
925   Assumption 1: Powerful models are unable to reason without memorizing information. Large
926   models can perform well by both learning generalizable semantics over their training data, but also
927   through the rote memorization of data or concepts. Currently, there exists no class of powerful
928   machine learning models which are able to “reason” about the world without having memorized any
929   data during its training period. Put another way, there are no reasoning agents that are derived in
930   a manner that is completely detached from data. One can argue that such a model, should it exist,
931
      would fit the definition of “artificial general intelligence” as it could generalize to any new set of
      data without inherent data priors.
932
933
      Assumption 2: Dataset distillation methods are still over the horizon. The field’s understand-
934
      ing of the amount of data points needed for a model to achieve proficiency on specific tasks is still
935   evolving. This area of research is termed “dataset distillation” and aims to reduce the number of data
936   points necessary to achieve target metrics (Wang et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2021). Further, it remains
937   unclear what exactly constitutes a “data point,” especially with modern methods like transformers,
938   which rely on tokens, the amount of which varies with different tokenization methods (Sennrich
939   et al., 2016; Schuster & Nakajima, 2012). We aim to establish one rigorous definition of “data
940   point” in future work, as well as analysis of how many data points define emergent capability.
941   In a limiting argument, should data distillation methods improve to the point where models can learn
942   generalizable knowledge without any data at all, this work would need to be revisited.
