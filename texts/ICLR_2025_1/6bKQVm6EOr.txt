011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In this work, we propose a new functorial graph coarsening approach that
014            preserves inner products between node features. Existing graph coarsening
015            methods often overlook the mutual relationships between node features, focusing
016
               primarily on the graph structure. By treating node features as functions on
               the graph and preserving their inner products, our method ensures that the
017
               coarsened graph retains both structural and feature relationships, facilitating
018
               substantial benefits for downstream tasks. To this end, we present the Inner
019            Product Error (IPE) that quantifies how well inner products between node features
020            are preserved. By leveraging the underlying geometry of the problem on the
021            Grassmann manifold, we formulate an optimization objective that minimizes the
022            IPE, even for unseen smooth functions. We show that minimizing the IPE also
023            promotes improvements in other standard coarsening metrics. We demonstrate the
024            effectiveness of our method through visual examples that highlight its clustering
025            ability. Additionally, empirical results on benchmarks for graph coarsening
026            and node classification show superior performance compared to state-of-the-art
027
               methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Graph-structured data has become ubiquitous in a wide range of domains, including social
033
      networks (Wasserman & Faust, 1994), biological systems (Pavlopoulos et al., 2011), and
      recommendation systems (Van Steen, 2010), due to its ability to model complex relationships
034
      and interactions. With the exponential increase in data availability, the size of graphs in many
035
      applications has also grown significantly. This surge in graph size presents major challenges, as
036   traditional and even advanced graph processing techniques often become computationally infeasible
037   or excessively time-consuming when applied to large-scale graphs. To address these issues, graph
038   reduction techniques aim to simplify large graphs while retaining key structural features, thereby
039   enhancing computational efficiency. There are three main strategies for graph reduction (Hashemi
040   et al., 2024): graph sparsification, graph condensation, and graph coarsening.
041
      Graph sparsification (Batson et al., 2009; Wickman et al., 2022) reduces graph size by selectively
042   removing edges and nodes while maintaining overall structural properties. However, there is a
043   limit to how much a graph can be sparsified without compromising its integrity. Graph condensation
044   methods (Jin et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023) aim to reduce graph size by generating a smaller, synthetic
045   graph that replicates the performance of the original graph on specific tasks, such as training a Graph
046   Neural Network (GNN). While condensation significantly lowers computational costs, it may not
047   retain a clear structural interpretation, making it challenging to understand how or why certain nodes
048   or edges are represented in the reduced version.
049   In contrast, graph coarsening (Chen et al., 2022) is a more traditional approach that reduces the
050   size of a graph by grouping similar nodes into super-nodes, aiming to approximate the structure
051   of the original graph. Coarsening methods aim to preserve essential structural properties such
052   as the graph’s spectral characteristics (Loukas & Vandergheynst, 2018), connectivity (LeFevre &
053   Terzi, 2010), and community structure (Tian et al., 2008; Amiri et al., 2018). Most existing graph
      coarsening methods primarily focus on the structural properties of the graph and often overlook the

054
      node features, which play a critical role in many graph learning tasks. These methods typically
055   operate solely on the graph topology, neglecting the rich information encoded in the node attributes.
056   Recently, the Featured Graph Coarsening (FGC) method was proposed to address this limitation by
057   incorporating node features into the coarsening process (Kumar et al., 2023). FGC emphasizes the
058   reconstruction of the node features after coarsening and promotes smoothness in the coarsened graph
059   as part of its optimization objective. However, FGC does not fully exploit the relationships between
060   different node attributes, which can encode valuable information about the graph’s structure.
061   In this work, we propose a new approach to graph coarsening from a functorial perspective. We
062   treat node features as functions, or signals, defined on the graph, focusing on preserving the
063   relationships between these functions by maintaining their inner products during coarsening. Our
064   method introduces a new coarsening metric, the Inner Product Error (IPE), which measures how well
065   the inner products between graph signals are preserved. We postulate that minimizing IPE ensures
066   the coarsened graph retains structural consistency and node feature relationships crucial for graph
067   learning tasks. We exploit the geometry of the problem by recognizing that both the coarsening
068   operator and the matrix spanning the node features (under a smoothness assumption) can be viewed
069
      as points on the Grassmann manifold. Leveraging the properties of the Grassmann manifold,
      we extend IPE minimization beyond observed node features, enabling our method to generalize
070
      to unseen features that satisfy the smoothness assumption. This approach is formulated as an
071
      optimization problem, and we compute the coarsening operator using gradient descent. Additionally,
072   we theoretically show that minimizing our proposed approach leads to improvements in common
073   graph coarsening metrics.
074
075
      To demonstrate the effectiveness of our method, we present visual and empirical results showing
      that, while our method focuses on the functional relationships between node features, it also captures
076
      the global structure of the graph. We further validate its performance through extensive experiments
077
      on multiple graph coarsening and node classification benchmarks, where our method consistently
078   outperforms state-of-the-art coarsening methods in terms of various established coarsening metrics,
079   demonstrating its practical utility.

296

      4.2   G RAPH C OARSENING M ETRICS
456
457   Here, we evaluate the performance of our methods, INGC and SINGC, on several benchmark
458   datasets and compare them with other existing graph SOTA coarsening methods: LVN and LVE
459   for preserving graph structural properties (e.g., REE, RE) and FGC for metrics incorporating node
460   features. The evaluation is based on the coarsening metrics presented in Sec. 2.2 that assess different
461   complementary aspects. We conduct experiments on four datasets: The Karate Club(Zachary, 1977),
462   Les Miserables(Knuth, 1993), Cora(McCallum et al., 2000), and (Giles et al., 1998). Note that the
463   Cora and Citeseer datasets include node features, whereas the Karate Club and Les Miserables
464   datasets do not. For the latter two, we generated node features using the signal generation
465
      mechanism presented in Section 3.3.
466   Table 1 summarizes the performance of our methods (INGC and SINGC) and other baselines (FGC,
467   LVN and LVE) across different datasets and coarsening ratios (r = nk = 0.7, 0.5, and 0.3). The best
468   performance for each metric is highlighted in bold, and the second-best is underlined. The last two
469   columns summarize the number of settings in which each method achieved the lowest or second-
470   lowest score compared to the other methods. We observe that our INGC method achieves the best
471
      overall performance across all graph metrics. SINGC, the simplified version of our method, also
      performs competitively, often demonstrating the second-best results in these metrics and, in some
472
      scenarios, even achieving the best performance. Our method’s superior RE score demonstrates its
473
      broader applicability in preserving graph structure during coarsening, extending beyond Theorem
474   1 theoretical scenario, as all datasets used are connected or have far fewer than n − k connected
475   components. The baseline methods, LVN, LVE, and FGC, show varying performance on different
476   metrics. The LVN and LVE show better performance on REE in certain datasets, indicating good
477   preservation of spectral and global properties in these cases. However, it generally falls short in
478   other metrics, particularly those involving node features. The FGC is the only baseline method that
479   considers the node features as part of the coarsening process, and indeed it shows better performance
480   than other baselines considering coarsening metrics that involve node features. We observe that both
481   our methods outperform the FGC which is considered the SOTA method across many settings.
482   An important note is that the best performance w.r.t. each metric is achieved using different
483   hyperparameters, which are reported in Appendix F.2. This variability underscores the importance of
484   selecting hyperparameters based on a specific application and the coarsening metric most relevant
485   to it. For example, minimizing a REE might be crucial for clustering applications. Conversely,
      applications such as graph pooling and node classification, which depend heavily on the node

      4.3   N ODE C LASSIFICATION
491
492   We evaluate the graph coarsening by applying the coarsening methods to the task of node
493   classification using several benchmark datasets. Node classification is a widely used benchmark
494   for evaluating the efficacy of graph coarsening algorithms, as it tests a coarsened graph’s ability to
495   preserve essential structural and feature information necessary for accurate label prediction. In this
496   experiment, a Graph Neural Network (GNN) is trained on the coarsened graph and employed to
497   predict node labels for the original, full-sized graph. This approach speeds up GNN training time,
498   as the coarsened graph has significantly fewer nodes and edges.
499   We follow the evaluation procedure described in Kumar et al. (2023), performing the following
500   steps. First, we learn a coarsened graph from the original graph using the selected coarsening
501   algorithm. Second, we compute labels for the super-nodes in the coarsened graph based on the
502   learned coarsening operator P = C † , using yc = P y, where y represents the labels of the original
503   graph. Third, we train a node classification GCN on the coarsened graph using these super-node
504   labels. Finally, we evaluate the classification performance on the original graph by comparing the
505   labels given by ŷ = GCN(L, X) to the full graph node labels (y). It is important to note that this
506
      node classification task is performed solely for evaluation purposes, as we have access to all the
      labels y during the process.
507
508   In our experiments, we employ a Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) (Kipf & Welling, 2016)
509   and compare the performance of our methods (INGC and SINGC) against current SOTA graph
510   coarsening methods for node classification, including SCAL (Huang et al., 2021), Featured Graph
511   Coarsening (FGC) (Kumar et al., 2023), and Graph Condensation (GCOND) (Jin et al., 2021). The
512
      datasets used in this experiment include two medium-sized graphs—Cora and Citeseer—and three
      large-scale graphs—Co-Physics, Pubmed, and Co-CS. The effectiveness of each method is assessed
513
      using a 10-fold cross-validation procedure. A detailed description of the experimental setting, along
514
      with a brief discussion of the chosen hyperparameters, is provided in Appendix F.3.
515
516   The results are reported in Table 2, which shows the mean accuracy and standard deviation across
517   the folds for each method at different coarsening ratios r. We observe that INGC consistently
518
      outperforms the SOTA methods on large datasets (Co-Physics, Pubmed, and Co-CS), and matches
      their performance on medium-sized datasets. SINGC also outperforms baseline methods in most
519
      settings, despite having a simpler optimization objective. A key takeaway from the results is that the
520
      integration of node features into the coarsening process gives FGC,INGC and SINGC a competitive
521   advantage over methods that primarily focus on structural properties, such as SCAL and GCOND.
522   This relationship is intuitive, as node classification relies not only on structural relationships but also
523   on the meaningful preservation of node features.

526   5     C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we introduced a novel graph coarsening method that focuses on preserving the
529   inner products of graph signals during the coarsening process. We demonstrated that, although
530
      primarily considering node features, our approach also maintains the global structure of the graph.
      Our methods, INGC and SINGC, outperform SOTA techniques across various graph coarsening
531
      metrics and tasks like node classification, showcasing their versatility and effectiveness in preserving
532
      essential graph properties. These results highlight the potential of our approach for diverse graph-
533   based learning applications. Future work includes implementing our coarsening method in graph
534   pooling and evaluating its impact on improving GNN performance.
