011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In recent years, Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated high rea-
014            soning capabilities, drawing attention for their applications as agents in various
015
               decision-making processes. One notably promising application of LLM agents
               is robotic manipulation. Recent research has shown that LLMs can generate text
016
               planning or control code for robots, providing substantial flexibility and interac-
017
               tion capabilities. However, these methods still face challenges in terms of flexibil-
018            ity and applicability across different environments, limiting their ability to adapt
019            autonomously. Current approaches typically fall into two categories: those relying
020            on environment-specific policy training, which restricts their transferability, and
021            those generating code actions based on fixed prompts, which leads to diminished
022            performance when confronted with new environments. These limitations signifi-
023            cantly constrain the generalizability of agents in robotic manipulation. To address
024            these limitations, we propose a novel method called EnvBridge. This approach
025            involves the retention and transfer of successful robot control codes from source
026
               environments to target environments. EnvBridge enhances the agent’s adaptabil-
               ity and performance across diverse settings by leveraging insights from multi-
027
               ple environments. Notably, our approach alleviates environmental constraints,
028
               offering a more flexible and generalizable solution for robotic manipulation tasks.
029            We validated the effectiveness of our method using robotic manipulation bench-
030            marks: RLBench, MetaWorld, and CALVIN. Our experiments demonstrate that
031            LLM agents can successfully leverage diverse knowledge sources to solve com-
032            plex tasks. Consequently, our approach significantly enhances the adaptability
033            and robustness of robotic manipulation agents in planning across diverse environ-
034            ments.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      The development of Large Language Models (LLMs) has remarkably advanced various fields,
      demonstrating impressive capabilities in understanding and generating human-like text. Methods
040
      such as Chain of Thought (CoT) (Wei et al. (2023)) and ReAct (Yao et al. (2023)) have showcased
041
      the high reasoning capabilities of LLMs, while models like OpenAI-o1 have further enhanced these
042   abilities. Building on these strengths, researchers have explored the integration of LLMs into intel-
043   ligent agents capable of complex decision-making tasks. These LLM agents leverage sophisticated
044   language understanding and reasoning abilities to perform a wide range of functions, from customer
045   service chatbots to complex problem-solving systems, demonstrating their versatility and potential.
046
      Among the various applications of LLM Agents, robotic manipulation stands out as a particularly
047
      promising area. Recent studies such as EmbodiedGPT (Mu et al. (2023)) combine LLM with vi-
048   sual components and policy net, building agents based on LLM’s planning ability. Methods such
049   as Code as Policies (CaP) (Liang et al. (2023)) and VoxPoser (Huang et al. (2023)) utilize LLMs to
050   create robotic control code, achieving high accuracy and stability in responding to instructions and
051   removing the need for extensive pre-training specific to robot actions. However, despite these ad-
052   vancements, LLM embodied agents still face significant limitations. LLM agents which use natural
053   language for planning often require policy training to align text action steps with the environment’s
      numerical action space. This need for environment-specific policy training restricts their flexibility


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      to adapt to diverse and new environments. On the other hand, LLM agents that generate control
055   code for robots, while eliminating the need for pre-training, still exhibit poor performance when
056   applied to unseen environments due to the use of fixed, human-created prompts. This shortcoming
057   reveals their limited transferability and lack of robustness across diverse settings. Essentially, cur-
058   rent methods struggle to support continuous learning and adaptation, which are crucial for sustained
059   high performance in changing environments.
060
      Our research addresses these critical limitations by introducing a novel agent, EnvBridge. EnvBridge
061   employs a key process called Cross-Environment Knowledge Transfer to integrate insights from
062   different environments. This is based on traditional human intelligence: gaining insights from suc-
063   cessful experiences in previous familiar environments and transferring knowledge to similar tasks
064   in unseen scenarios. In a similar approach, we leverage successful robotic control code from one
065   benchmark environment and apply it to another, thereby enhancing the agent’s adaptability and per-
066   formance in diverse environments. This approach alleviates the environmental constraints, providing
067   a more flexible and generalizable solution.
068   EnvBridge is comprised of three components: Code Generation, Memory-Retrieval, and Re-
069   Planning with Transferred Knowledge. When encountering the tasks in the target environment,
070   EnvBridge will first use LLMs to generate robotic control code attempting the task. If the task is not
071   accomplished, EnvBridge will retrieve successfully executed codes solving similar tasks in source
072   environment stored in our memory. Subsequently, EnvBridge transfers the codes into knowledge
073   appropriate for the target environment and applies them as in-context learning for the agent. By
074   leveraging on appropriate transfer of knowledge across environments, EnvBridge can operate across
075
      diverse environments with high performance.
076   We evaluate EnvBridge across three representative embodied environments: RLBench, MetaWorld,
077   and Calvin. These benchmarks encompass common actions like pick and place, while each presents
078   unique environments and a diverse range of tasks. EnvBridge achieves an average success rate of
079   69% on RLBench tasks, significantly outperforming both code generation and Re-Planning base-
080   lines. Furthermore, EnvBridge demonstrates robust performance across various knowledge sources
081
      and task instructions, highlighting its adaptability to diverse environments and tasks.
082   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
083
084          • We propose a novel agent named EnvBridge, which tackles the key challenges in LLM em-
085            bodied agents that struggle with transferability using a process called Cross-Environment
086
               Knowledge Transfer. Inspired by human intelligence—where successful experiences in fa-
               miliar environments are applied to similar tasks in new scenarios—EnvBridge leverages
087
               successful examples from one benchmark environment and applies them to another, en-
088
               hancing adaptability and performance.
089
             • EnvBridge enhances adaptability across various environments without relying on pre-
090
               training or human-initiated prompt adjustments. It uses Cross-Environment Knowledge
091
               Transfer to retrieve successful control codes from memory and adapt them for the target
092            environment, allowing the agent to flexibly handle new environments and tasks.
093
             • We evaluate EnvBridge with three distinct robot manipulation benchmarks: RLBench,
094
               MetaWorld, and CALVIN. Each benchmark has different settings and task complexities,
095            making them ideal for evaluating EnvBridge’s performance. Our results demonstrate that
096            EnvBridge consistently achieves high task success rates across varied scenarios, confirming
097            its effectiveness and robustness.

      2.1   A DVANCEMENTS IN R EASONING WITH LLM S
102
103   LLMs have made significant strides in natural language processing tasks, including text generation,
104   summarization, translation, and tasks that require elements of reasoning such as reading, comprehen-
105   sion, and question answering. Though their capacity for reasoning and complex problem-solving is
106   impressive, there is still much room for improvement. To enhance their reasoning skills, researchers
107   are exploring techniques like hierarchical reasoning, where complex problems are broken down
      into smaller sub-problems. Wei et al. (2023) developed chain-of-thought prompting, a strategy that


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      prompts LLMs to reason by articulating their thought process step-by-step. Multi-agent discussion,
109   as demonstrated in Debate (Du et al. (2023)), involves multiple LLMs working together to reason
110   collaboratively. To further improve LLM reasoning, incorporating external knowledge is crucial.
111   Approaches like knowledge graph integration (Pan et al. (2024)) and Retrieval-Augmented Gener-
112   ation (Lewis et al. (2021)) allow LLMs to access and leverage structured information, leading to
113   more accurate and informed responses.
114
115   2.2   LLM AGENTS WITH M EMORY
116
117   LLM-based agents can interact with humans using natural language, offering a more flexible and
118   transparent interface. The memory module is crucial for enabling an agent to acquire, accumulate,
119   and use knowledge through interactions with its environment, achieved via three key operations:
120   memory reading, writing, and reflection. Memory reading extracts useful information from past
121   experiences to guide future actions, while memory writing stores environmental data for later use.
122   Memory reflection allows agents to evaluate their cognitive processes. Unlike traditional LLMs,
123
      agents must learn and act in dynamic environments, necessitating a module that aids in planning
      future actions. RAP (Kagaya et al. (2024)) dynamically leverages past experiences corresponding
124
      to the current situation and context, thereby enhancing agents’ planning capabilities. SayPlan (Ahn
125
      et al. (2022)) is an embodied agent tailored for task planning. In this system, scene graphs and
126   environmental feedback function as the agent’s short-term memory, directing its actions. The Gen-
127   erative Agent (Park et al. (2023)) utilizes a hybrid memory structure to support the agent’s behavior.
128   Its short-term memory holds contextual information about the agent’s present situation, while its
129   long-term memory stores past behaviors and thoughts, which can be accessed based on current
130   events.
131
132
      2.3   E MBODIED AGENTS
133
134   Recent works have developed more efficient reinforcement learning agents for robotics and embod-
135   ied artificial intelligence to enhance agents’ abilities for planning, reasoning, and collaboration in
136   embodied environments. Hierarchical planning models (e.g., Sharma et al. (2022)) represent a type
137   of approach where a high-level planner leverages LLMs to generate subgoals, and a low-level plan-
138   ner carries out their execution. The LLMs outline a sequence of subgoals aimed at reaching the final
139   objective specified by the language instruction, while the low-level planner translates these subgoals
140   into appropriate actions for the given environment. Dasgupta et al. (2023) proposes a similar idea of
141
      a unified system for embodied reasoning and task planning, combining high-level commands with
      low-level controllers for effective planning and action execution. Furthermore, various approaches
142
      that utilize the high code-generation capabilities of LLMs for robotic manipulation are being re-
143
      searched. These include methods for grounding actions available within the environment (Singh
144   et al. (2022)), attempting error recovery in case of failures (Duan et al. (2024)), and learning skills
145   from a lifelong learning perspective (Tziafas & Kasaei (2024)). These methods do not require pre-
146   training and can take advantage of the flexible reasoning abilities of LLMs, making them highly
147   notable. However, their applications across various environments are still limited.

313
314   Using the code adapted by Knowledge Transfer, EnvBridge proceeds with Re-Planning. Figure 2
315   shows this process progressing from the middle to the right. If the initially generated code fails, the

338
339   RLBench(James et al. (2019)) is a robot learning benchmark for tabletop environments with various
340   tasks. We sampled 10 tasks from it and conduct evaluations covering various tasks, instructions, and
341   objects. Each task comprises 20 trials, with instructions randomly selected from those supported by
342   RLBench. GPT-4o-mini was used for this evalution.
343
      We compare EnvBridge with Voxposer(Code Generation baseline) and two Re-Planning baselines:
344   Retry and Self-Reflection. Retry indicates re-executing without making any changes if the previous
345   trial fails. As the method for Self-Reflection, we used a novel conventional approach (Shinn et al.
346   (2023)) to generate a plan for the next trial. Additionally, to extend Self-Reflection to RLBench, we
347   create the prompts ourselves and use the images obtained from RLBench as Observations to generate
348   the next plan. The prompts used for generating Self-Reflection are described in the Appendix A.2.4.
349   In the proposed method, we conduct the evaluation using memory constructed from successful codes
350   in the CALVIN benchmark (Mees et al. (2022)). While detailed settings for the CALVIN benchmark
351   are described in Section 4.3, we used the successful codes from 200 tasks in CALVIN, executed with
352
      the Retry Re-Planning method, to build the memory. Moreover, for methods excluding the baseline,
      the success rate is calculated based on up to three trials.
353
354   The evaluation results are shown in the Figure 3. From these results, our proposed method performs
355   better than Self-Reflection. Furthermore, while our method requires memory from other environ-
356   ments, Self-Reflection necessitates manual creation of prompts for reflection for each environment.
357   Therefore, our method is more generalized for expanding the application range of robotic operations.
358   When analyzing individual task performance, we observe distinct trends between Self-Reflection
359   and EnvBridge. Self-Reflection shows improved performance on tasks partially addressed by the
360   baseline, such as BeatTheBuzz and CloseDrawer. In contrast, EnvBridge demonstrates significant
361   improvements on tasks with low baseline performance, like PushButton and TakeLidOffSaucepan.
362   This difference arises because Self-Reflection enhances the generation of better code for tasks that
363   are already partially solvable, whereas EnvBridge can solve previously unsolvable tasks by incorpo-

518   6     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
519
520   We propose EnvBridge, a novel method for transferring knowledge from various environments to
521
      target environment in robotic manipulation agents. EnvBridge stores successful control codes from
      one environment in memory and transfers this knowledge to other environments to apply it, thereby
522
      enhancing the agent’s adaptability and performance. Our approach demonstrates superior perfor-
523
      mance in robotic manipulation benchmarks.
524
525   While EnvBridge shows promising results, several important challenges remain. Our current im-
526   plementation uses open-loop trajectories with task decomposition; integrating reactive control could
527
      enhance robustness in dynamic environments. While we use affordance and avoidance maps for
      trajectory planning, more sophisticated collision avoidance mechanisms could ensure safer robot-
528
      environment interactions. Additional challenges include efficient memory organization for lifelong
529
      learning and handling diverse sensory inputs like images and audio.
530
531   In conclusion, EnvBridge demonstrates the value of utilizing information from various environments
532   to improve adaptability to new settings and flexible planning capabilities. Addressing these limita-
533
      tions while building upon the current strengths of cross-environment knowledge transfer will be
      crucial for advancing the practical use of AI agents in diverse real-world scenarios.
