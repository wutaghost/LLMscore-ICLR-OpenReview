009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Test-Time Adaptation (TTA) problems involve adapting pre-trained models to new
012
               data distributions in testing time, with access to only model weights and a stream
013
               of unlabeled data. In this work, we present Intra-model Ensemble Learning (IEL),
014            a method of actively adapting a set of pre-trained classifiers to process distribution-
015            shifted data one sample at a time without access to labels. IEL produces higher
016            Ensemble classification accuracy and better individual classifiers that continue
017            to improve and learn from each other during inference, which is why we call
018            our method Intra-model Ensemble Learning. Specifically we minimize the cross-
019            entropy between the classifier output that has the highest predicted probability for
020            the majority-voted class (a high confidence softmax) and all other classifiers. The
021            majority-voted model that all others learn from may change from sample to sample,
022
               allowing the group to collectively and continuously learn from each other during
               testing time. Our method uniquely optimizes all trainable parameters in each model
023
               and needs only a single sample (instead of a batch) for adaptation, which is a
024
               more practical setting of inference for real-world applications. In our extensive
025            experiments, using sets of independently pre-trained base classifiers with distinct
026            architectures, we show that IEL can reduce generalization error significantly better
027            than base Ensemble model for classification tasks on corrupted CIFAR-10, CIFAR-
028            100, and ImageNet datasets while minimizing the entropy of model outputs.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Modern classifiers can reliably produce accurate predictions when training and testing data are drawn
035   from identical distributions. When there is a distribution shift between the training (source) and testing
036   (target) data, i.e. the sets of source and target samples are drawn from distinct distributions, then the
037   recognition performance of many high variance classifiers significantly diminishes (Quionero-Candela
038   et al., 2009; Liang et al., 2023). Distribution shifts are common in many real-world applications such
039
      as when sensors in medical imaging equipment degrade enough to distort readings (Karani et al.,
      2021; Hu et al., 2024). In such cases model deployment may still be necessary, even though available
040
      pre-trained models cannot achieve optimal accuracy on testing data due to distribution shifts. In this
041
      work, we present a method for adapting a set of independently pre-trained classifiers as an ensemble
042   to distribution-shifted data one sample at a time during inference, assuming that the initial training
043   data for all classifiers was similarly distributed, e.g., identical training sets.
044
045
      Given a set of pre-trained models, we propose to use the softmax of the member model which has the
      highest predicted probability for the majority voted class as a soft target (e.g., pseudo-label) to apply
046
      backpropagation to all other member models. Since the model selected by our dynamic majority vote
047
      scheme may change from sample to sample, member classifiers in the ensemble will dynamically
048   learn from each other and continue to adapt during inference until they agree with the predictions
049   of the other models in the set. This marks a significant difference from most methods in Ensemble
050   Learning, such as bagging and stacking, which produce a single strong ensemble classifier from
051   base classifiers in a static way, without changing any of the individual base classifiers. Hence, we
052   title our approach as Intra-model Ensemble Learning (IEL), inspired by mutually beneficial human
053   collaboration that not only yields superior overall outcomes but also improves the ability of the
      participants involved.

      This paper focuses on Intra-model Ensemble Learning in the context of Test-Time Adaptation (TTA)
055   problems, where we seek to adapt pre-trained models to unlabeled and distribution shifted data.
056   Access to training data may be infeasible during the testing stage, such as in confidential medical
057   or financial domains, and adapted predictions may be required on only a single sample. To address
058   these restrictive cases, for IEL we assume that unlabeled inference samples comes in only one at a
059   time (Khurana et al., 2022; Valanarasu et al., 2024), unlike some TTA settings that require batches
060   of multiple samples for adaptation. When only one sample is available for adaptation, estimation
061   of testing data distributions can pose difficulties for many existing Test-Time Adaptation methods.
062   For example, methods like TENT (Wang et al., 2021), whose performance in part relies on accurate
063
      estimations of batch statistics on incoming unlabeled data, become ineffective with access to only a
      single sample per batch. Besides filling this gap in Ensemble learning research, our IEL approach is
064
      more versatile and flexible in many critical domains such as medicine, finance, safety, and security
065
      where timely processing is required and reliable batch statistics are hard to acquire.

      In the general Test-Time Adaptation setting where access to multiple samples per batch is allowed,
088
      many lines of work have found success with minimizing the Shannon Entropy of classifier outputs
089
      (Niu et al., 2023; Jing et al., 2022). This approach optimizes model outputs to achieve high confidence
090   as measured by the Shannon Entropy. Since our choice of soft target has maximal probability for
091   some class it is likely to also have a low Shannon Entropy (high confidence). Compared to using
092   one-hot “hard” targets which correspond to the majority voted class, a soft target also provides more
093   information about relative similarities between classes as shown in the field of Knowledge Distilling
094   (Hinton et al., 2015; Arazo et al., 2020). Still, models are known to be confidently incorrect at
095   times, and so choosing a maximum probability soft target does not always correspond to the correct
096   prediction. By defining our chosen soft target to agree with the majority voted prediction we attempt
097   to leverage the robustness of ensembles to ensure that the target is likely correct.
098   If the ensemble prediction is incorrect, then allowing it to supervise member models could lead to
099   erroneously optimizing strong models on incorrect predictions. This phenomena results in cascading
100   error accumulations known as catastrophic forgetting in the literature on Test Time Adaptation (Wang
101   et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2023). Although the Shannon Entropy has been shown to pose issues as an
102   optimization signal when non-stationary distributions are encountered (Lee et al., 2024), we find that
103   IEL can effectively minimize the Shannon Entropy of model softmax outputs (Figure 1) as a side
104
      effect while improving the classification accuracies of member models in stationary TTA settings
      (Figure 2).

107   new knowledge is generated when ground truth information is unavailable. Collaborating learners
      should be skilled so that their contributions to the group are sound, and the requirement that the set

      of learners be diverse is premised on the idea that the individual contributions of a single learner
109   may shine light on things not known by another. For example, cutting-edge scientific research on
110   a challenging problem with no known solution is similar to the testing phase of classification in

112   progress, naturally they will talk, exchange ideas, and collaborate with other researchers(sometimes

114
      In this collaborative ensemble learning setting, individual learners inspire and gain knowledge from
115   each other in a way that is similar to human collaboration. If successful, the outcome is not only
116   a scientific breakthrough, but also better researchers/learners, which can be more critical for the
117   progression of a scientific field than a single solution in the long run. We aim to emulate the benefits
118   that a group of learners may get from collaboratively solving a problem together by learning from
119   their combined output. By minimizing the cross-entropy distances between a majority voted model
120   output and all others on new samples, we minimize the diversity of the ensemble (we force models to
121   agree with each other) in a way that facilitates generalization of the source training domain to the
122   new testing domain.
123   Our Contributions
124
125          • This work proposes diversity as a new optimization signal for classification in the ensemble
126            learning setting, which complements the primary label and loss signal. Since the diversity

      2.1   E NSEMBLE L EARNING
144
145   Ensemble learning combines the outputs of multiple member models to generate more accurate
146   predictions than any of the constituent models alone (Dietterich, 2000). Popular methods include
147   bagging, boosting, stacking, voting, and Bayesian model averaging (Breiman, 1996; Freund &
148   Schapire, 1997; Schapire & Singer, 1999). Since the objective in the standard Ensemble Learning
149   setting is to only achieve better predictions, constituent models remain unchanged.
150   A simple way to combine the predictions of models on an arbitrary input sample is to choose the class
151   that was most frequently predicted by the member models, a process referred to as Majority Voting. It
152   is well known that ensembles with high diversity (made from models which make independent errors)
153   have improved prediction robustness, where the individual model errors cancel each other out during
154   the model combination process (Wang & Ji, 2023). Despite the common use of ensemble learning
155   in modern machine learning methods, the presence of diversity among ensemble members is often
156   thought of as a “black box” for improving classification accuracy. Still, many works on ensembles
157   indicate that to get a strong ensemble, diversity should be present among members.
158   We can view the cross-entropy between member models and the majority voted model as a rudimentary
159   estimate of the diversity in an ensemble, and by minimizing it we force members to agree with the
160   majority voted model. Recent work has shown that the diversity of an Ensemble directly relates to
161   the degree of error reduction that the ensemble provides over its member models (Wood et al., 2023).
      A natural counter-example to this claim is to form an ensemble from member models that are each an

162
      identical copy of one perfect model and its pre-trained weights such that the copied model achieves
163   100% classification accuracy on validation data. Such an ensemble would have no diversity among
164   its identical members, but the overall ensemble would retain perfect accuracy, perhaps contingent
165   upon the choice of model combination method used. As such, we expect that is possible to reduce
166   diversity while also reducing generalization error in an ensemble.

169   2.2   T EST-T IME A DAPTATION (TTA)
170
171
      The foremost aim in TTA is to improve the performance of a source pre-trained model on a target
      domain when the source and target are not identically distributed (Zhao et al., 2023). Many lines
172
      of work in TTA have found success with minimizing the Shannon Entropy of a model’s softmax
173
      output as a signal in the absence of labels (Gui et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2022). Recently several new
174   studies have shown that reducing prediction entropy can result in problematic model behaviors, such
175   as leading models to produce over confident predictions (poor calibration) and inducing catastrophic
176   forgetting when spurious correlation shifts are encountered. (Lee et al., 2024; Yoon et al., 2024).
177
      Methods like TENT are able to mitigate these difficulties by re-estimating batch statistics on new
178
      data, but not all TTA use cases allow access to more than a single sample at a time. In the setting
179
      where only a single sample is available at a time, EATA proposed to use weight-averaged and
180   augmentation-averaged predictions for entropy optimization, while ROID explicitly depends on
181   forming an ensemble from prior versions of an adapted model to facilitate generalization to new
182   domains (Marsden et al., 2023; Niu et al., 2022). All of these methods are able to adapt a single
183   model at a time to new domains, while we choose to focus on the simultaneous adaptation of multiple
184   models to new data through the ensemble combined soft target. Although our method minimizes the
185   Shannon Entropy of model predictions as a side effect (Figure 1), we rely on the inherent diversity in
186   a set of independently pre-trained models to make our models more accurate (Figure 2).
187   COTTA avoids catastrophic by stochastically restoring small subsets of the model weights to their
188   original values during each iteration, whereas we make no attempts to avoid catastrophic forgetting
189   (Wang et al., 2022). We aim to show the viability of true ensemble based approaches in TTA settings
190   using member models that have been independently pre-trained. The introduction of the Shannon
191   Entropy as an optimization signal in TTA problems was motivated by the general idea of confidence
192   optimization. The Shannon Entropy can be interpreted as measuring the uncertainty present in a
193   softmax layer, so entropy minimization approaches effectively minimize the uncertainty in model
194
      predictions. Since we minimize a statistical distance between member models, we expect that IEL
      also minimizes prediction uncertainty.
195
196   This problem can be addressed within the context of Knowledge Distillation if the only available
197   representation of the target distribution comes from the outputs of a pre-trained teacher model, and
198   the student model has also been pre-trained on a source distribution shifted from the target. Such
199   distribution shifts pose significant challenges for production machine learning models when source
200
      and target data distributions do not exactly align.
201
202   2.3   K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
203
204   Knowledge Distillation uses a complex teacher model to supervise a smaller student model. Transfer-
205   ring knowledge to a smaller model reduces computational overheads and allows well-performing
206   models to be deployed to devices with limited resources, like mobile phones and embedded systems.
207   One early line of work found success in training a small student model on test data with hard-targets
208   generated by a pre-trained ensemble (Bucila et al., 2006). However, using one-hot encodings as
209   pseudo-targets effectively forces the student model to never improve beyond the static teacher since it
210   can draw no additional knowledge about the zeroed out classes in the one-hot target.
211   (Hinton et al., 2015) found that using a teacher model’s real-valued softmax output with a temperature
212   parameter T > 1 yielded more information rich pseudo-targets which better facilitated generalization
213   in student models as opposed to using generated “hard-targets", that is, one-hot pseudo-targets
214   predicted by the teacher. This follows the idea that given an input sample x that can belong to one of
215   k classes and a teacher model H : X 7→ Rk , the probabilities of the incorrect classes relative to each
      other in the teacher’s soft-max contains relevant information about similarities between classes. In our


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      work we opt to have ensembles produce softmaxes to take advantage of their higher self-information
217   content compared to one-hot pseudo-labels, but do not employ any temperature control.
218
219   However, in Knowledge Distillation teacher and student models are usually independent, and learning
220
      is one-way and affects only the student model and not the teacher (Gou et al., 2021). Work following
      this line of thought has found its best results when the teacher outputs used in the distillation process
221
      came from the same input data that the teacher was initially trained on. We propose to let the teacher
222
      model be an ensemble dependent on the output of the student, so that the distillation process becomes
223   dynamic for both the student and the teacher, and achieves “teaching others teaches yourself” and
224   mechanically shows how “two heads are better than one”.

274

      4     E XPERIMENTS
303
      We evaluate the capability of IEL to adapt an ensemble of independently pre-trained classifiers
304
      to test set data. Our code for experiments uses the PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2017) framework
305
      for model implementation with training on up to 3 NVIDIA L40S GPUs. We benchmark our
306   performance on three TTA tasks: adapting models pre-trained on CIFAR-10 to CIFAR-10C, on
307   CIFAR-100 to CIFAR-100C, and ImageNet to ImageNet-C (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019). Pre-
308   trained weights for CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 models were sourced from a public GitHub repository
309   (https://github.com/chenyaofo/pytorch-cifar-models). Pre-Trained weights for ImageNet models were
310   sourced from the PyTorch Vision library. All models used had their batch normalization parameters
311   (if applicable) frozen during the IEL process. This, along with using a batch size of 1, was done to
312   ensure that we are not benefiting from updating batch normalization statistics on new data.
313   Datasets The CIFAR10C, CIFAR100C, and ImageNet-C datasets contain test set images from
314   original CIFAR10, CIFAR100, and ImageNet datasets with 15 common image corruptions applied.
315   Pre-trained models were trained on data from the respective uncorrupted training sets. For CIFAR10C
316   and CIFAR100C we used all 10,000 samples per corruption type for adaptation. Due to computational
317   restraints, for ImageNet we use a class-balanced 7000 sample subset of the data from each corruption
318   type. We separate the data used for each corruption type into two sets, with a 90% split of tuning set
319   samples used for IEL and 10% split of evaluation set samples used strictly for evaluating generalization
320
      error on unseen data.
321   Optimization During testing time, we feed a stream of images from a single corruption type to our
322   models and minimize the IEL loss. Each optimization step requires only a single corrupted image for
323   adaptation. After all images from a corruption type have been used, we reset all model weights and
      start the IEL adaptation process from scratch on the new corruption type. Using only one sample per

409   4.1      E XPERIMENT R ESULTS , A NALYSIS AND D ISCUSSION
410
      Experiment results on CIFAR10C, CIFAR100C, and ImageNet-C datasets are shown in Table 1, 2, 3
411
      respectively. Here is our analysis and discussion on these results.
412
413   IEL can improve generalization error of pre-trained models. Table 1 and Table 2 show that IEL
414   successfully increases classification accuracy on both the tuning data used for IEL and the evaluation
415   data used to estimate generalization error. Additionally, for some corruption types we observe that
416
      the increase in classification accuracy is higher on the unseen evaluation set data than on the tuning
      set data, showing that IEL can avoid overfitting in some cases (see resnet152 accuracies on the zoom
417
      blur and fog corruption types in Table 3).
418
419   IEL enables models to learn from each other. Since our optimization objective minimizes the
420   cross-entropy between models in the ensemble, all performance gains observed stem directly from
421   within the ensemble, showing true intra-model ensemble learning. If adaptation is too difficult to
422
      show significant performance gains, we find that some models can degrade while others marginally
      improve (see accuracies on the Impulse Noise corruption type in Table 1). This shows a significant
423
      difference from conventional ensemble learning methods that leave individual models unchanged.
424
425   IEL can adapt to a majority of corruption types. IEL induces catastrophic forgetting on only 3 of
426   the 15 corruption types in CIFAR10C (Gaussian Noise, Shot Noise, and Impulse Blur in Table 1) and
427   4 of the 15 corruption types in CIFAR100C (Gaussian Noise, Shot Noise, Glass Blur, and Impulse
428
      Noise in Table 2). Surprisingly, on ImageNetC no catastrophic forgetting was encountered on any of
      the corruption types (Table 3).
429
430   IEL reduces entropy and error without dependence on batch normalization layers. Figure 1
431   corresponds to the experimental results shown in table 3 for ImageNetC, and shows that the Shannon
      Entropy is reduced along with our loss in testing time. Figure 2 depicts the accuracy improvements


                                                                  8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      of a ResNet50 classifier over all training epochs, showing that improvements can still be made if IEL
433   was allowed to run for more epochs.
434
435   With these extensive experiments showing IEL’s effectiveness on TTA tasks, it is natural to ask if IEL
436
      could work in a standard Ensemble Learning setting not involving distribution shifted data. We have
      performed experiments to evaluate the capabilities of IEL on uncorrupted datasets. We found success
437
      with adapting sets of models pre-trained on CIFAR100 and ImageNet to the uncorrupted test sets
438
      of both datasets. The average model accuracies per epoch of IEL on the uncorrupted ImageNet test
439   set is shown in Figure 3. It is worth noting that the performance gains are much smaller in Figure 3,
440   we suspect that is because higher performing models have little left to learn from others. Since this
441   paper focuses on TTA, and it is not feasible or optimal to include all details (different architectural
442   components and analysis of loss and entropy) about IEL’s performance on uncorrupted datasets, we
443   will report these findings more comprehensively in another paper.
444
      Limitations and Future Work Similar to many standard Ensemble Learning methods, IEL is more
445   computationally heavy than TTA methods that employ only one model and do not use backpropagation.
446   However, we believe that it is a cost worth paying as IEL is able to affect all trainable parameters in
447   models and adapt multiple models simultaneously. It would be interesting to analyze the behavior of
448   IEL as we change the number of models in our set, as many works in Ensemble Learning indicate
449   that having larger ensembles can significantly improve the resulting reduction in generalization error
450   of the ensemble output compared to the outputs of the individual models. Additionally, there are
451   many techniques for avoiding catastrophic forgetting that were not employed in IEL, but could be
452   considered in future work, such as replacing the cross-entropy with a statistical distance function that
453
      outputs 0 when both input distributions are identical, or stochastically restoring subsets of model
      weights after backpropogation.

      5    C ONCLUSION
481
482   This paper presents our Intra-model Ensemble Learning approach using a new diversity-based
483   optimization signal during inference to improve the classification accuracies of multiple models.
484   This is especially useful for testing phases when the primary optimization signal - ground truth
485   labels - is unavailable. With our IEL approach, member models are optimized and improved like in
      knowledge distillation and create an upward spiral effect, which not only further improves the overall


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      prediction performance but also produces better individual models. We validated our method on
487   multiple challenging TTA tasks, which shows significant and consistent improvement over baselines.
