011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Data-centric approaches in Time Series Forecasting (TSF) often involve heuristic-
014            based operations on data. This paper proposes to find a general end-to-end data
015            transformation that serves as a plugin to enhance any arbitrary TSF model’s per-
016            formance. Our idea is to generate transformed data during an approximating
017            process and to co-train a predictor for evaluating data with the transformation.
018            To achieve this, we propose the Proximal Transformation Network (PTN), which
019            learns effective transformations while maintaining proximity to the raw data to en-
020            sure fidelity. When orthogonally integrated with popular TSF models, our method
021
               helps achieve state-of-the-art performance on seven real-world datasets. Addi-
               tionally, we show that the proximal transformation process can be interpreted in
022
               terms of predictability and distribution alignment among channels, highlighting
023
               the potential of data-centric methods for future research. Our code is available at
024            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/PTN-2FC6/.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Time Series Forecasting (TSF) is a significant task in various domains including energy, finance, en-
030   gineering, and industrial applications. Recently, along with the tremendous achievements of neural
031   networks in deep learning, TSF has seen many learning-based methods that consistently outperform
032   traditional methods in terms of precision, robustness, and capacity (Zhou et al., 2021; Wu et al.,
033
      2021; Salinas et al., 2020; Taylor & Letham, 2018).
034   Meanwhile, modeling specific time series data properties such as distribution shift (Kim et al., 2021),
035   non-stationarity (Liu et al., 2022), frequency bias (Piao et al., 2024), and variate covariance (Wang
036   et al., 2024c) has achieved outstanding successes in TSF. The successes can be attributed to sharp
037   observations of commonalities in time series and sound designs from a model-centric perspective.
038   With the angle shifted, data-centric methods for time series including normalizations (Fan et al.,
039
      2023; Han et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024c; September et al., 2024), augmenta-
      tions (Zheng et al., 2024), and simple preprocessing methods (e.g., down-sampling (Yu et al., 2023;
040
      Wang et al., 2024a) and patching (Nie et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b)) can improve the overall
041
      performance of a given model by adopting data-level operations. These methods either explicitly
042   or implicitly entail a specific transformation applied to the raw time series data. This paper further
043   pushes the boundary of data-centric methods by answering the following: Is there a general data
044   transformation that can improve a model’s generalization capability? If so, can we discover it in an
045   end-to-end manner?
046
      We first verify the insufficiency of specific designs to generalize on data with different complex-
047
      ities. The evidence is provided by an example that involves learning on noisy input-target pairs
048   generated by a linear mapping. When smoothing the noised data is the only transformation to con-
049   sider, linear mapping with different complexities has different best-denoised data selected from a
050   range of common denoising/smoothing methods. We then reformulate our problem to identify a
051   general process, which finds an effective transformed data given any TSF model to achieve better
052   forecasting results than if using the raw time series data directly. We refer to this problem as find-
053   ing the optimal transformed data for forecasting, which extends the original optimization space by
      adding another dimension w.r.t. data fidelity. However, finding such a transformation is challeng-

054
      ing. Assuming the proximal existence of our objective, we constrain the problem as a simplified
055   co-optimization on the proximity of the transformed data and forecasting accuracy for each level
056   of proximity. The transformation is provided by a Convolution Encoder, and an approaching pro-
057   cess is learned by an attention-based Decoder. Combining these two components, we propose the
058   Proximal Transformation Network (PTN) that approximates the raw data at different distances to
059   find the transformed data that results in better performance for a given model.
060
      In summary, our contributions lie in the following aspects:
061
062
      • A Reformulated Problem. We consider the conventional TSF problem as a two-step problem:
        finding a data transformation that enables better generalization and performance, followed by
063
        learning to forecast on the transformed data.
064
065
      • A Plug-and-Play Module. We propose the Proximal Transformation Network to find an effective
        data transformation. The model includes a convolution-based Encoder and an attention-based
066
        Decoder that provide transformation on different levels of proximity. The training involves a
067
        co-optimization of the proximity of the transformed data and forecasting accuracy.
068
      • Extensive Experiments on Real-World Datasets. By orthogonally adding our module to several
069
        representative models, we managed to achieve state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on seven real-
070
        world datasets. The Proximal Transformation can be further interpreted by predictability assess-
071     ments and distribution alignments.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
075   We focus on data-centric aspects within TSF that transform data explicitly or implicitly. A discussion
076   of TSF models based on their designs and advances is presented in Appendix A.2.
077
      Normalization Normalization is crucial for neural networks on time series data (Bhanja & Das,
078   2019). Techniques like Reversible Instance Normalization (Kim et al., 2021) address instance-level
079   distribution shifts, influencing models like the non-stationary Transformer (Liu et al., 2022). Exten-
080   sions include local and global normalizations (Fan et al., 2023; Han et al., 2024), and channel-wise
081   normalization that aligns multivariate series via adaptive modules (Deng et al., 2021; Zhao & Shen,
082   2024). Such adaptive normalizations enhance expressiveness with additional parameters (Liu et al.,
083   2024c; September et al., 2024).
084   Data Augmentation Traditional data augmentations (e.g., cropping, jittering, and scaling (Wen
085   et al., 2020)) improve time series representation learning (Yue et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2020). In-
086   foTS (Luo et al., 2023) is a novel proposed meta-learning-based method that learns an end-to-end
087   augmentation from a predefined set of transformations for Contrastive Learning. AutoTCL (Zheng
088   et al., 2024) further offers theoretical insights based on Information Bottleneck Theory (Tishby et al.,
089   2000) and demonstrates effectiveness on CoST (Woo et al., 2022). These adaptive data augmenta-
090   tions still explicitly rely on specific operations, unable to provide a general data transformation.

200   3.3   L OSS S URFACE T RANSFER IN THE R EFORMULATED P ROBLEM
201
      Our study seeks a validated transformation X̃ = G(X; ϕ) that enhances the predictor f (·; θ)’s gen-
202
      eralization and robustness to varying mapping complexity in (X, Y ). Searching for arbitrary trans-
203   formations can be tedious, and we have no clue about what loss to use to guide such transformation.
204   To address this, we constrain the transformation to be “somewhere near” the raw data, leading to a
205   co-optimization process: learn prediction on the transformed data and align it with the raw series.
206
      We use stochastic gradient descent on a combination of proximity and prediction losses. The prox-
207
      imity loss measures the difference between the transformed and raw data, while the prediction loss
208
      assesses prediction accuracy:

256   Given the preliminaries in Section 3.2, we decompose our design into two parts: the Proximal
257   Transformation Network (PTN) that gradually approaches the raw series via a learnable Encoder-
258   Decoder transformation, and an arbitrary predictor model that is orthogonal to PTN. We further
259   introduce a Mixture-of-Experts design within PTN, enabling the cooperative transformation of data
260   patches and providing a new approach to scaling. The full architecture is shown in Figure 3.

329   4.2   M IXTURE OF E XPERTS FOR PATCHED S UBSERIES
330
331   One major concern with Time Series Transformers and other deep-learning-based TSF methods is
332   the scaling law. Nie et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024b) have shown that increasing the number of model
333   parameters does not consistently improve performance. More often than not, these Transformers
334   achieve their best results with as few as two layers.
335
      We thus shift our approach to scaling by incorporating Mixture of Experts (MoE) into TSF. Specif-
336   ically, we combine MoE with the patching operation, a common practice adopted by (Wang et al.,
337   2024b; Zhou et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023). In our design, we route patches to
338   different expert models, each being a separate PTN with independent parameters, to enhance both
339   performance and efficiency. Patches are routed to each expert based on their sequential order, as
340   depicted in Figure 3(a). Detailed performance boost and a decomposition view of MoE are provided
341   in Appendix A.5.

471   5.3       T RANSFERABILITY OF THE T RANSFORMED DATA
472
473   Generalization capability is a commonly considered issue in machine learning. This topic is par-
474
      ticularly important in TSF, as the diversity of datasets across various domains presents significant
      challenges in model design. Transfer learning adapts models to new datasets to enhance performance
475
      or reduce training costs. Our reformulating of finding a transformation of data offers a novel per-
476
      spective on transfer learning. Specifically, our PTN design facilitates the combination of transformed
477   data with various forecasting models. A simple, lightweight linear model can initially determine the
478   transformation, which is then fixed and transferred to other forecasting models. Table 4 demonstrates
479   the transfer learning results, showcasing the feasibility of our method. The data transfer protocol
480   broadens conventional transfer learning by moving from generalizing a model for different datasets
481   to its dual form of generalizing the data for different models.

530
531   In this study, we approach time series forecasting (TSF) from a data-centric perspective by designing
532   a co-optimization process that balances data transformation proximity and forecasting accuracy. Our
533   design boosts the performance of a variety of TSF models and demonstrates the potential to make
534   TSF more interpretable. For future work, it is relevant to align datasets from diverse sources using
535   our data transformation method. Moreover, it remains open if PTNcan enhance performance in other
536   time series tasks like anomaly detection, classification, and imputation. Further exploration into
537
      different backbone architectures is also interesting. We anticipate that a data-centric approach to
      generalizing datasets, tasks, and architectures could ideally pave the way for enhanced Time Series
538
      Foundation Models (Liang et al., 2024).

830
831
      In this part, we will introduce some popularly adopted methods for TSF, in which our baseline
832   models are included.
833
834
835   Channel-independent Transformers Time Series Transformers (TST) (Zhou et al., 2021; Wu
836   et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022) have recently led to significant progress in the TSF problem, demon-
837   strating convincing superiority over traditional methods and convolution-based models.
838   Inspired by Zeng et al. (2023), Nie et al. (2023) incorporates the Channel Independent (CI) design
839   (sharing weights across all channels) to introduce PATCH TST, a new state-of-the-art (SOTA) model
840   that significantly benefits from CI and the patching operation. More works follow this practice and
841   achieve excellent performance in TSF (Das et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024).
842
843
844
      Channel-wise Transformers Building on this, more recent research has focused on designing
      Transformers capable of capturing channel dependencies inherent in multivariate time series data.
845
      Notable examples include C ROSSFORMER (Zhang & Yan, 2023), I T RANSFORMER (Liu et al.,
846
      2024b), D SFORMER (Yu et al., 2023), and CARD (Wang et al., 2024b). These models shed light
847   on exploiting inter-series relationships to improve forecasting accuracy. Furthermore, Ilbert et al.
848   (2024) propose that Transformers are inherently unstable when trained on time series datasets due to
849   sharp gradient curvature, and suggest a sharpness-aware optimizer to mitigate such issues. Nonethe-
850   less, channel-wise Transformers still suffer from overfitting on small datasets.
851
852
853   Linear Models In contrast to the quadratic complexity of Transformers, lightweight linear mod-
854   els have emerged as competitive alternatives offering simplicity and efficiency. RL INEAR (Li et al.,
855
      2023) verifies that a vanilla single-linear model, when combined with a widely-adopted normaliza-
      tion method (Kim et al., 2021), can achieve near SOTA performance in TSF. Further research (Zhao
856
      & Shen, 2024) on channel dependencies within linear and MLP-based models has yielded perfor-
857
      mance improvements over previous CI approaches. Moreover, models (Xu et al., 2024; Yi et al.,
858   2024) that directly learn linear regression or MLP-based models on complex frequency features have
859   achieved remarkable performances. Through theoretical analysis of these linear models, Toner &
860   Darlow (2024) conclude that normalization-enhanced (Li et al., 2023), decomposition-based (Zeng
861   et al., 2023) and frequency-domain linear (Xu et al., 2024) models are essentially equivalent to
862   vanilla linear regression. Most recently, S PARSE TSF (Lin et al., 2024), a SOTA lightweight model,
863   incorporates 1D convolutions as down-sampling modules and learns linear parameters on the down-
      sampled values of the original series.

      A.4.3   C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS OF PTN
948
949   The proposed PTN serves as a plugin that can enhance a backbone’s performance at the cost of addi-
950   tional computation and memory usage. Hence, we proceed to analyze the computational complexity
951   of PTN, bridging some of the gaps between theoretical complexity from past studies and our empir-
952   ical findings. For a brief conclusion, our complexity analysis offers guidance on implementing PTN
953   efficiently for practical usage. By limiting the size of parallel computations within a batch, we can
954   reduce the relative multiplier of time and memory costs from a number of times to a factor of less
955   than one during training and inference.
956   To be specific, we first identify the actual batch size as a crucial factor in complexity analysis and
957   extend the analysis from previous studies to better align with the empirical results (see P1 below).
958   Second, by reducing actual batch size, we propose a more efficient implementation for the combi-
959   national uses of two orthogonal attentions (core components in PTN). Under certain conditions, this
960   method can achieve reasonable speed-up at the cost of memory usage (see P2). Third, we verify the
961   effectiveness of the variate sampling strategy, which is also supported by our analysis (see P3). Both
962   methods here do not incur performance drop with improvement in efficiency.
963
      P1. Complexities of different Attentions. A previous study by Liu et al. (2024b) reveals that
964
      the channel-wise and inter-patch attentions have different complexities based on dataset features.
965
      Compared to inter-patch attention, channel-wise attention uses more resources on datasets with more
966   variables and less on those with fewer variables. This difference is due to the dimension along which
967   attention is calculated. The complexity of attention is O(N 2 ), where N represents the number of
968   tokens. For channel-wise attention, N is the number of variables, while for inter-patch attention, N
969   is the number of patches. These complexities are represented as O(C 2 ) for channel-wise and O(n2 )
970   for inter-patch, where C is the number of variables and n is the number of patches (calculated as
971   L/lp , with lp being the patch length). Intra-patch attention has a constant complexity of O(lp2 ),
      meaning it doesn’t scale with the series length or horizon.


                                                         18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       However, despite not computing along the variable dimension, the times of inter-patch and
973    intra-patch attention computation increase nearly linearly with more variables. Due to channel-
974    independence strategy Zeng et al. (2023), the number of variates will multiply the batch size, re-
975    sulting in a larger number of parallel computations. Since GPU parallel capacity is limited, a larger
976    batch size does not necessarily speed up computation. What is more, the increased batch size does
977    not lower the number of batches to be computed in a dataset. Therefore we should take actual batch
978    size into consideration for complexity. For large C, inter-patch attention complexity is O(C × n2 ).
979    As shown in Figure 10 and Figure 11, datasets with many variables (like the Traffic dataset with 862
980    variables) result in faster training and inference speeds for models like PATCH TST and DL INEAR
981
       compared to datasets with fewer variables (like the Weather dataset with 21 variables). This leads us
       to adjust the empirical computation complexity of intra-patch attention. We compute intra-patch at-
982
       tention for n×C = L/lp ×C times, resulting in a complexity of O(lp ·LC). Similarly, channel-wise
983
       attention complexity is O(L · C 2 ). By adding the outputs of both attentions, the total complexity
984
       becomes O((lp + C) · LC).

992
       cases by having a smaller number of B.

1112   A.4.4    E FFICIENT STUDENT MODEL BY TRAIN - TIME DISTILLATION
1113
       The training cost for non-linear attention-based models can be reduced by a data transfer protocol
1114   as described in Section 5.3. The transfer utilizes the transformation learned on a linear backbone to
1115   train a non-linear model from scratch, which improves training efficiency at a minor compromise
1116   of performance. We have a more direct and burden-free version for inference as well, ss shown in
1117   Figure 7 and briefly explained in the previous section, The core idea is to train a student model that
1118   directly uses the raw input X to predict the transformed output Ỹ . This practice is an intermediate
1119   version between a standard PTN and the data transfer version. Unlike the data transfer protocol in
1120   Section 5.3, the student model learns the mapping from X to Ỹ during training, which means it
1121   requires more samples than just the converged Ỹ after training PTN. When training a standard PTN,
1122   the backbone predictor uses the transformed series as inputs and labels, and updates computed by
1123   the lp red will also back-propagate the gradients of the PTN parameters. Whereas when training
1124   with a student predictor, we use straight-through method (Van Den Oord et al., 2017) to omit the
1125   calculation of PTN gradients (i.e. use ”tensor.detach()” in pytorch). Thereafter, we only added the
1126
       cost of another backbone model and can achieve similar improvements without any additional cost
       increase during the inference phase.

       As mentioned in Section 4.1, we employ a point-wise linear head as a parameter-sharing module.
1204   We demonstrate the three different parameterizations for the linear head in common TSF models
1205   in Figure 14. PATCH TST(Nie et al., 2023) adopts a patch-dependent linear head (shown in Fig-
1206   ure 14(1)) that first flattens all features in patches and uses an extra large weight matrix with a
1207   shape of Ld × d, where L is sequence length and d the dimension for latent embedding. Lee et al.
1208   (2023) proposes an intriguing idea that some tasks involve patch-dependent design as a necessity
1209   such as forecasting, while other tasks like contrastive learning might prefer patch-independent de-
1210   sign (shown in Figure 14(b)). Our proximity approach process resembles a reconstruction process,
1211   therefore we do not exploit the patch correlations in our task and can even extend this independence
1212
       to point-wise scope (shown in Figure 14(c)). This approach is feasible only when each point-wise
       embedding is rich in information, as supported by our Convolution Encoder with a near-global ERF
1213
       for each point.
