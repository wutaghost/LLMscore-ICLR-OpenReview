009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             Safety is a paramount concern of large language models (LLMs) in their open de-
012             ployment. To this end, safeguard methods aim to enforce the ethical and responsi-
013             ble use of LLMs through safety alignment or guardrail mechanisms. However, we
014             found that the malicious attackers could exploit false positives of safeguards, i.e.,
015             fooling the safeguard model to block safe content mistakenly, leading to a new
016             denial-of-service (DoS) attack affecting LLM users. Specifically, through soft-
017             ware or phishing attacks on user client software, attackers insert a short, seem-
018
                ingly innocuous adversarial prompt into user prompt templates in configuration
                files. This prompt triggers safeguard rejections of nearly all user requests from
019
                the client while remaining hidden in the user interface and non-trivial to detect.
020
                By designing an optimization process that utilizes gradient and attention informa-
021             tion, our attack can automatically generate seemingly safe adversarial prompts,
022             approximately only 30 characters long, that universally block over 97% of user
023             requests on Llama Guard 3. The attack presents a new dimension of evaluating
024             LLM safeguards focusing on false positives, different from the classic jailbreak.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   As large language models (LLMs) have been widely adopted across different domains, their sig-
030   nificant social impact has prompted extensive research into methods of monitoring the interaction
031   between users and LLMs and suppressing bias and harmful content that could be produced by LLMs.
032   To this end, human feedback aligns LLMs to safety standards during training or fine-tuning stages,
033   practised by ChatGPT (Achiam et al., 2023) for instance. Also, guardrail mechanisms are deployed
034   at inference time, involving a flexible combination of safety checks for content moderation. For in-
035
      stance, Llama Guard (Inan et al., 2023) utilizes a separate LLM to classify conversations as safe or
      unsafe. While above LLM safeguards 1 are now standard in deployment, they remain vulnerable to
036
      malicious attacks. Through black-box searches or white-box adversarial optimization, attackers can
037
      find inputs that jailbreak the system, bypassing safeguards and causing the LLM to generate harmful
038   content. These vulnerabilities and their mitigation are a growing focus in LLM security research (Yu
039   et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024). The jailbreak attack exploits the false negatives of LLM safeguards
040   (i.e., incorrectly classifying unsafe content as safe).
041
      Inspired by jailbreak, we raise the research question — can malicious attackers also exploit false
042
      positives of LLM safeguards? By triggering a false positive, the safeguard classifies a proper user
043
      request as unsafe content thus the request is rejected. When a malicious attacker consistently trig-
044   gers the rejection targeting a specific user, it forms a denial-of-service (DoS) attack. The attack can
045   significantly degrade the user experience and cause economic losses or even harm public health, es-
046   pecially in systems related to finance and healthcare. While jailbreak gains the majority of attention
047   for LLM safety, the DoS threat is overlooked by existing studies.
048
      In this paper, we design the LLM DoS attack. The attacker is assumed to be able to inject an adver-
049
      sarial prompt to user prompt templates, stored in a configuration file of the user client. The injection
050   can be achieved by exploiting software vulnerabilities, inducing users to download malicious prompt
051   templates from a phishing website, or controlling a component in an LLM agent system, as detailed

056   service with safeguards. Because of the adversarial prompt, the safeguard consistently recognizes
057   the user requests as unsafe and denies the requests.
058
      The generation of the adversarial prompt is the key to the attack. Briefly speaking, our attack al-
059
      gorithm first selects an unsafe prompt as the initial candidate of the adversarial prompt and then
060
      optimizes it iteratively by replacing or dropping tokens based on gradient and attention information.
061   The first challenge is that stealth of the adversarial prompts. The user easily detects the attack by in-
062   specting the corrupted prompt template if the adversarial prompt is obviously unsafe, e.g., containing
063   harmful instructions in plain text. For instance, when the attacker leverages phishing attacks to inject
064   adversarial prompts, the user will not adopt a corrupted prompt template from the phishing website if
065   it looks abnormal. To this end, we design the optimization process to prioritize adversarial prompts
066   with minimized length, no toxic words, and high semantic difference to harmful content. Jailbreak
067   attacks do not aim for stealth because the attacker fully controls the LLM request in their threat
068   model. Second, the adversarial prompt must be universally effective under diverse user prompts.
069
      For instance, the attacker may not fully control the position of the adversarial prompt in the final
      request, unlike the previous jailbreak attacks always use the adversarial prompt as a suffix.
070
071   We evaluate the LLM DoS attack on a collection of LLM prompt datasets covering various task cat-
072   egories including math, programming, logical reasoning, etc. The tested safeguards include Llama
073   Guard series (Inan et al., 2023) and Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023), which are state-of-the-art open-
074   source safeguard models to our best knowledge. On Llama Guard 3, for instance, our attack can
075
      generate seemingly innocuous adversarial prompts as short as 30 English characters to successfully
      deny 97% of user prompts. More importantly, we conduct comprehensive experiments to analyze
076
      the impacting factors of the attack effectiveness, and prove that our attack is universally effective in
077
      diverse scenarios, involving various length of user prompts from 100 to 3,000 characters, different
078   positions to insert the adversarial prompts, and different levels of stealth.
079
080   As for mitigation, existing defenses against adversarial examples are effective to a limited degree and
081
      often come with significant damage to the performance of safeguarding normal data. For instance,
      the approach of random perturbation (Robey et al., 2023) and resilient optimization (Yuan et al.,
082
      2024) can reduce the attack success rate to around 40% but significantly decrease true positive rates
083
      to below 50% on data without attacks. We discuss potential mitigation methods in Section 5.4.
084
085   We summarize our contributions as follows:
086   • We propose a new adversarial denial-of-service attack on LLMs. In this attack, an optimized
087     adversarial prompt is embedded within user prompt templates, effectively causing user prompts
088     to be misclassified as unsafe by LLM safeguards.
089   • We use extensive experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of the attack under diverse scenarios.
090     We also analyze its impact in real applications and potential mitigation methods.

094
095   Large Language Models (LLMs). Large language models (LLMs) are advanced AI models de-
096   signed to understand and generate human-like text by training on vast text data. These models
097   generally use the Transformer architecture (Vaswani, 2017), which uses self-attention mechanisms
098
      to weigh the importance of different words in a sentence. These LLMs scale up to billions of model
      parameters and present promising performance on various tasks. Representative examples include
099
      GPT (Achiam et al., 2023), BERT (Kenton & Toutanova, 2019), Llama (Touvron et al., 2023), etc.
100
101   Safety alignment or guardrails of LLMs. As LLMs become increasingly integrated into real-life
102   applications, ensuring their safe and ethical operation has become a critical concern. In general, there
103   are two main categories of safeguards, train-time safety alignment and inference-time guardrails.
104   Safety alignment refers to the process of guiding LLMs to produce outputs that adhere to human
105   values and ethical norms. Stiennon et al. (2020) introduced Reinforcement Learning from Human
106   Feedback (RLHF), which utilizes human feedback and preferences to enhance the capabilities of
107   LLMs, becoming a standard approach to LLM training. Supervised fine-tuning (Achiam et al.,
      2023) or instruction-tuning (Touvron et al., 2023) can further improve LLMs on specialized tasks

      On the other hand, guardrails are external protective mechanisms that prevent unsafe or harmful
      content in LLM services at the inference time. The rule-based filter is a conventional and straight-
124
      forward approach to directly match and remove certain patterns in the language, such as toxic words
125
      and hate speech. Model-based filters leverage pre-trained, fine-tuned, or prompted models to deter-
126   mine the safety of a conversation. Llama Guard (Inan et al., 2023), for instance, is a model fine-tuned
127   from LLAMA to identify unsafe content and the category of safety violations. OpenAI (2024) also
128   released moderation API for a similar purpose. Advanced guardrails (Yuan et al., 2024; Rebedea
129   et al., 2023) may integrate multiple protective mechanisms along with defenses against adversarial
130   attacks, or aim to be a flexible framework allowing customization.
131
      Together, safety alignment and guardrails form a comprehensive approach to mitigating safety risks
132   in LLMs. We involve the two approaches as the subject of DoS attacks.
133
134   Attacks and countermeasures on LLM safety. As safeguards have become common in LLM
135
      service, straightforward harmful requests are usually denied. However, malicious attackers may
      launch jailbreak attacks that optimize the malicious prompt eliciting harmful outputs from LLMs
136
      bypassing safeguards. Extensive research has studied the jailbreak attack and countermeasures.
137
138   Initially, people found that certain paraphrases of the harmful prompts could be ignored by safe-
139   guards, e.g., setting up a role-playing scenario, few-shot in-context learning with unsafe examples,
140   leveraging domain shift, etc., as discussed in related surveys (Yu et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024). To
141
      make the discovery of jailbreak prompts efficient, research studies proposed to leverage black-box
      optimization (Shin et al., 2020) or another LLM (Ge et al., 2023) to generate jailbreak prompts au-
142
      tomatically. Zou et al. (2023) introduced a white-box attack optimizing jailbreak prompts which are
143
      effective across different user prompts and transferable across different LLMs.
144
145   Countermeasures against adversarial attacks are proposed. Hu et al. (2023) identifies adversarial
146   prompts based on the degree of the model’s perplexity, assuming the adversarial prompts are differ-
147
      ent from normal generation in the wording style. Others (Robey et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2024) apply
      random perturbation on the content, as the adversarial prompts may be sensitive to it and become
148
      ineffective. Resilient optimization, e.g., optimizing another prompt to play against the jailbreak
149
      prompts, is also considered as an effective mitigation, as discussed in Yuan et al. (2024).
150
151   In general, existing attacks or defenses mostly focus on the jailbreak problem. In this paper, we
152   study false positive triggering or denial of service which is fundamentally different from jailbreak.

161   We assume the existence of three parties, server, user, and attacker. We discuss the assumption of
      these roles separately. An overview of the attack is depicted in Figure 1.

      • Server. The server, which hosts an LLM service, processes requests from users and delivers the
163     LLM-generated responses back to users. The server deploys LLM safeguards to screen incoming
164     requests and refuses to process or respond to those deemed unsafe. The server is trusted.
165   • User. The user interacts with the server through client software with built-in or customizable
166     prompt templates. The templates are stored in configuration files and not showing up in the user
167     interface. Upon receiving a user-composed prompt, this client software utilizes these prompt tem-
168     plates to format the prompt into a complete request before transmitting it to the server. However,
169     the client software is not trusted, as it is susceptible to compromise through cyber attacks. The
170     user may manually inspect prompt templates in configuration files to detect potential attacks.
171
      • Attacker. The attacker, a malicious entity, seeks to create a denial of service for specific users by
        increasing the likelihood that their requests are denied by the server. The attacker can inject ma-
172
        licious prompts into the targeted user’s requests to the LLM server. While unable to compromise
173
        the trusted server, the injection can be achieved by either proactively compromising the client
174     software or passively inducing users to configure it incorrectly, whose practicality is discussed in
175     Section 3.3. Additionally, the attacker has white-box access to the safeguard parameters; e.g., the
176     server may use safeguard models that are open-sourced or could be reverse-engineered.

180
      Effectiveness. We first define the objective of the attack using formal language. The safeguard
181
      deployed on the server is a binary classifier, denoted by f . It evaluates a user request, denoted by m,
182
      and outputs binary values where f (m) = 1 indicates the detection of unsafe content. Each request
183   m is constructed by concatenating a user-provided prompt, denoted by mu , and a prompt template,
184   denoted by mt , i.e., m = mu ⊕ mt where ⊕ is the concatenation operation. The attacker employs
185   an attack function g to manipulate the prompt template mt , e.g., injecting an adversarial prompt.
186   Given a set of safe user prompts M , a safeguard function f , and the attack function g, the (ideal)
187   objective of the attack is to make the safeguard classify all user prompts as unsafe content:

189   Stealthiness. The attacker aims to make the adversarial prompt non-trivial to identify within the
190   prompt template by manual inspection. To this end, we define three metrics assessing the stealth
191   of adversarial prompts. (1) Length. The adversarial prompt should be as short as possible. (2)
192   Semantics. The adversarial prompt is semantically not unsafe, or has low semantic similarity with
193   known unsafe content. (3) Wording. The adversarial prompt should avoid obviously toxic or harmful
194   words. As an example, the attacker would pick the adversarial prompt buffs yayJet bombkup deton

196   and ... (75 characters) which contains obvious harmful instructions. While this does not ensure the
197
      attacks are undetectable, it does reduce the likelihood of detection.

213   inducing users to adopt malicious versions (Alabdan, 2020). Given the critical role of high-quality
214   prompt templates in enhancing LLM performance and the common practice among LLM clients

254        In this section, we detail the algorithm used to generate adversarial prompts for executing the LLM
255        DoS attack. We begin by outlining the overall workflow of the attack and then highlight our distinc-
256
           tive contributions to the DoS strategy, which include: (1) a stealth-oriented optimization method,
           and (2) mechanisms achieving multi-dimensional universality.

259        4.1     OVERVIEW
260
261        Algorithm 1 presents the algorithm generating an adversarial prompt triggering a denial of service.
262        The attack requires the following materials:
263
           • A set of safe prompts. These prompts are recognized as safe by the safeguard mechanisms. The
264          attacker uses these prompts as examples of legitimate user prompts.
265        • A set of unsafe prompts. These prompts include explicit harmful content that should be flagged as
266          unsafe by the safeguards. The initial adversarial prompt is derived from this set.
267        • A safeguard model. This is the attack’s target, accessible to the attacker in a white-box setting. It
268          may be a safety-aligned LLM or an external safeguard system. We also choose a target response
269          as the text that will be generated on detection of unsafe content, e.g., “unsafe” for Llama Guard
             models and “I’m sorry” for safety-aligned models.

      • A loss function. This function evaluates the quality of adversarial prompts based on a weighted
271     sum of their effectiveness (i.e., the likelihood of eliciting the targeted response) and their stealth
272     (i.e., prompt length and semantic appropriateness).
273
274
      The attack can be summarized as the following process.
275   Initialization. At the beginning, the algorithm initializes a set of test cases and a candidate adver-
276   sarial prompt. Each test case is constructed by picking a safe prompt and determining an insertion
277   point for the adversarial prompt. The candidate for the adversarial prompt is chosen as the most
278   effective unsafe prompt from the set, evaluated based on its loss score across these test cases. This
279   initialization strategy ensures the attack begins in a position close to the potential success.
280   After the initialization, the attack iteratively mutates the candidate gradually towards lower loss.
281
      Candidate mutation. We employ token substitutions and deletion for mutation. Token substitution
282
      utilizes the GCG algorithm from Zou et al. (2023), which leverages gradient information to identify
283
      a number of tokens to place on each position in the prompt that would increase the likelihood of
284   eliciting the target response. Additionally, to enhance stealth, we implement token deletion by
285   removing less important tokens from the candidates. The importance of each token is determined
286   based on their attention values from the last layer of the transformer, as discussed in Section 4.2.
287
      Candidate selection. The algorithm chooses the most promising candidate from the mutated set to
288
      initiate a new iteration. It first filters candidates based on their success rate across the designated test
289
      cases. Then, the algorithm selects the candidate with the lowest loss score as the final choice.
290
291   After iterations of candidate mutation and selection, the candidate with the lowest loss score ever
292   appeared is selected as the adversarial prompt. We elaborate more details in the following sections.
293
294   4.2   S TEALTH - ORIENTED O PTIMIZATION
295
      We enforce the stealth of the attack using the following design blocks.
296
297   Token substitution with token filter. We implement a customizable filter to identify and eliminate
298   unwanted tokens, such as toxic words or special characters, from the adversarial prompt. If an
299   unwanted token is detected within an adversarial prompt candidate, its substitution probability is
300
      increased. The replacement token, selected via GCG algorithm (Zou et al., 2023), is also subjected
      to this filtering process to ensure it is not an unwanted token. This approach purges undesirable
301
      tokens from the initial adversarial prompt.
302
303   Token deletion guided by attention. The attention mechanism in transformer architecture deter-
304   mines how each token influences others during tasks. The attention values in the last transformer
305   layer are particularly significant as they directly present each token’s contribution to the final output.
306
      Therefore, we use the last layer of attention values to determine which tokens in the adversarial
      prompt are not important for the target response, thus they have a higher priority to be deleted.

321
322   Unlike jailbreak attacks, the attacker in our LLM DoS attack does not control user-provided prompts,
323   resulting in uncertainties regarding the final request sent to the LLM service. It is therefore essential
      to design mechanisms that ensure the attack is universally effective across diverse scenarios.

      Task categories. The safe prompt set used in Algorithm 1 may encompass various task categories,
325   such as mathematics, coding, and logical reasoning. Employing prompts from multiple categories
326   enhances universality, making the approach well-suited for general LLM chat services. Conversely,
327   targeting a specific task category is practical for specialized LLM services, such as an AI coding
328   assistant. We consider both multi-task and single-task settings.

333   available knowledge about user clients, or opt for random insertion to maximize universality.

342   5.1   E XPERIMENT S ETUP
343
344   Datasets. We collect the safe prompts of general short questions from Alpaca (Wang, 2023), pro-
345   gramming questions from HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021), and math problems, logical reasoning,
346   and reading comprehension tasks from Agieval (Zhong et al., 2023). We collect unsafe prompts
347   from HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024). We divide the safe prompts into a training set comprising
348
      80% of the data, which is used to input the attack algorithm, and a test set comprising 20% of the
      data, designated solely for evaluation. We ensure a balanced distribution of prompt lengths and task
349
      categories in each set. For the unsafe prompts, we selectively retain 100 examples that are relatively
350
      shorter than others, as inputs to the attack algorithm.
351
352   Models. We use public pre-trained models from huggingface, Vicuna-1.5-7b, LlamaGuard-7b,
353   Meta-Llama-Guard-2-8B, and Llama-Guard-3-8B. The Vicuna model is safety-aligned (using Chat-
354
      GPT conversations) while Llama Guard models are standalone guardrails. We use default configu-
      rations as provided by the model authors.
355
356   Implementation details. We heavily use Python packages of PyTorch and transformers to imple-
357   ment the attack. By default, the attack algorithm consumes 12 safe prompts from the training set
358   each time. Each candidate mutation step executes 24 token substitutions and 8 token deletion, i.e.,
359   k1 = 24, k2 = 8 in Algorithm 1. The loss function uses w1 = 10−4 , w2 = 0.1. The success rate
360
      threshold σ is 0.6. We manually tuned the above parameters. The semantic similarity is assessed
      using SentenceBERT (Reimers, 2019).
361
362   Experiment parameters. We consider various settings of the experiments.
363
      • Task scope: Single-task and Multi-task. A single-task attack is optimized and tested on one spe-
364     cific task category while a multi-task attack is supposed to be effective on all task categories.
365   • Token filter: None, Moderate, and Strict. This parameter controls the vocabulary that can be used
366     in adversarial prompts. A moderate filter allows only English words (no special characters). A
367     strict filter additionally disallows a list of toxic words, collected from the unsafe prompt dataset.
368   • Insertion: Prefix, Suffix, and Random. The adversarial prompts could be inserted as a prefix, as a
369     suffix, or at a random location in the user prompts.
370   Evaluation metrics. We assess success rate and length of adversarial prompts. The success rate
371   is the ratio of safeguard warnings triggered on the set of safe prompts with adversarial prompts
372   inserted. The length is the number of English characters. For each experiment setting, we repeat the
373   attack 20 times, 50 iterations for each, and report the averaged metrics.

      Next, we comprehensively analyze the impact of the various factors on the success of the attack and
412
      draw several findings as below.
413
414   Safeguard development lacks attention to false positive triggering. The Llama Guard series, as
415   state-of-the-art open-source guardrails, becomes increasingly vulnerable to the DoS attack with its
416
      development. The attack success rate on the latest Llama Guard 3 is 20.4% higher than that on
      Llama Guard (the initial version). Vicuna is in general a weaker model against adversarial attacks.
417
418   The attack is not task-specific. The success rate of single-task attacks is marginally higher than
419   multi-task attacks in Table 1, with adversarial prompts of comparable lengths. This pattern is con-
420   sistent across all tested models, indicating the task-wise universality of adversarial prompts.
421   Some keywords increase attack success rate. Table 1 involves multi-task suffix attacks with dif-
422   ferent token filters (Experiments x.2, x.5, and x.6), and we show examples of filtered adversarial
423   prompts in Figure 3. Attacks using a moderate filter, which excludes special characters, achieve
424   performance comparable to those without any token filtering. However, the strict filter, which bans
425   specific toxic words, makes adversarial prompts longer. Despite this, the moderate filter maintains
426   significant prompt stealth by embedding toxic words in semantically obscure sentences. Our find-
427   ings suggest that specific keywords significantly influence safeguard responses.
428   Fixed-position insertion is more successful. We examine attacks with different insertion locations
429   (e.g., Experiments x.2, x.3, and x.4 in Table 1). Random insertion poses greater challenges for
430   attackers, as evidenced by its lower success rate and slightly longer prompt lengths compared to
431   fixed-location insertions. In practical scenarios, where prompt templates are typically static, attack-
      ers might opt for fixed-location insertions.

458
459   We implement GCG (Zou et al., 2023) as a baseline and demonstrate the benefits of our design. We
460   use Experiments 3.2 and 4.2 in Table 1 as examples and depict results in Figure 5. Briefly speak-
461   ing, the main contribution of our design is the enforcement of stealth, e.g., dramatically reducing
462   the length of adversarial prompts and making the content semantically obscure. The effect is max-
463   imized by combining the token substitution mechanism and the loss function design, shrinking the
464   adversarial prompt length to 20%, from 179.2 to 40.3.

467   5.4         M ITIGATION M ETHODS
468
469   We evaluate the attack’s resilience to two existing mitigation methods, random perturbation pro-
470   posed by Robey et al. (2023) and resilient optimization proposed by Yuan et al. (2024). The random
471   perturbation method randomly inserts characters, replaces characters, or applies patches in the orig-
472   inal user request, assuming the adversarial prompt is sensitive to the perturbation. We implement all
473   three perturbation types and set the probability of perturbation to 0.1 and the number of perturbations
474   to 31. The resilient optimization method optimizes another prompt to suppress potential adversarial
475   prompts, attached at the end of the user request. We apply two mitigation methods to the multi-task
476
      suffix attack without token filtering (Experiments x.2 in Table 1) and summarize results in Figure 6.
477   While mitigation strategies can lower the attack success rate to at least 40%, they significantly
478   impair the effectiveness of safeguards on normal data without attack. Notably, random patching and
479   resilient optimization severely reduce the true positive rate (TPR) to below 30% and increase the
480   false positive rate (FPR) above 10%, rendering the safeguard system largely ineffective. In contrast,
481   the mitigation of random insertion and replacement brings minor side effects but they reduce the
482
      attack success rate to at least around 60%, not effectively countering the DoS attacks.
483   In conclusion, the results show the imperfection of tested mitigation methods. We recommend that
484   users implement standard protections against software and phishing attacks to prevent early-stage
485   adversarial prompt injections. Additionally, should users notice a high volume of request failures,
      manual validation of prompt templates is advised to identify the attack.

521
      This paper presents a novel LLM Denial-of-Service (DoS) attack that leverages false positives in
      LLM safeguards to block legitimate user requests. We design a method of white-box adversarial
522
      prompt generation, emphasizing the stealth of the generated prompts. Our evaluations demonstrate
523
      the high effectiveness and stealth of the proposed DoS attack across diverse scenarios. The findings
524   urge the necessity for evaluation of safeguard methods on false positive cases.
525
526
527   E THICAL S TATEMENT
528
529   Unlike jailbreak attacks, our approach cannot not assist the generation of malicious content. The
530   additional threat of the attack is a stealthy trigger of denial of service on LLM systems. The threat
531   can be removed by manual inspection on user’s local client and configuration files. More discussion
532   of mitigation methods is presented in Section 5.4. All experiments in this paper is conducted in local
533   computers on public datasets, not harming any online service.
