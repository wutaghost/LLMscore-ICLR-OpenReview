008                                               A BSTRACT
009
010            We propose a comprehensive evaluation framework for concept erasure in lan-
011            guage models, addressing the need for a holistic assessment of effective un-
012            learning. Our framework centers on three critical criteria: innocence (complete
013
               knowledge removal), seamlessness (maintaining conditional fluent generation),
               and specificity (preserving unrelated task performance). These evaluation met-
014
               rics naturally motivate the development of Erasure of Language Memory (ELM),
015
               a new method designed to address all three dimensions. ELM employs targeted
016            low-rank updates to alter output distributions for erased concepts while preserving
017            overall model capabilities including fluency when prompted for an erased concept.
018            We demonstrate ELM’s efficacy on biosecurity, cybersecurity, and literary domain
019            erasure tasks. Comparative analysis shows that ELM achieves superior perfor-
020            mance across our proposed metrics, including near-random scores on erased topic
021            assessments, generation fluency, maintained accuracy on unrelated benchmarks,
022            and robustness under adversarial attacks.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   What does it mean for a language model to “unlearn” a concept? For example, let’s say that we want
027   a model to behave as if it has never seen information about biological weapons in its training data.
028   Should we consider unlearning a success if the model forgets general information about biology, or
029   if it loses the ability to produce fluent text whenever viruses or bacteria are mentioned? What if the
030   model reveals harmful information when prompted with a new kind of question, or if the information
031   can still be found somewhere within the model’s hidden states?
032   In this work, we take a step back to define three desiderata for concept erasure in language models:
033
034         1. Innocence: Erasure should wipe the undesired knowledge completely: specifically, the
035            model should be innocent of the knowledge in response to any prompting method, or even
036            when probed directly in its internal states. This criterion ensures the erased knowledge is
037            fully inaccessible, with no form of indirect retrieval or influence on the model’s processing.
038         2. Seamlessness: Editing should not draw attention to the concept that was erased by dam-
039            aging the model. For example, when prompted to generate the erased concept, the edited
040            model should produce fluent text that gracefully handles the absence of the target knowl-
041            edge rather than producing gibberish. This criterion maintains the model’s overall utility
042
               and prevents obvious indicators of concept erasure.
043         3. Specificity: The erasure process should not impact the model’s performance on unrelated
044            concepts. This ensures the erasure process is precise and targeted, maintaining the model’s
045
               overall functionality.
046
      We argue that robust concept erasure should simultaneously satisfy all three criteria. While prior
047
      works have successfully unlearned undesired concepts, existing approaches all suffer from limita-
048   tions in one or more of these goals. Representation Misdirection for Unlearning (RMU) (Li et al.,
049   2024) fine-tunes the earlier layers of model to unlearn a concept by randomizing and amplifying
050   the internal activations when prompted with text related to the concepts being erased, but it suffers
051   from a lack of seamlessness, since the method creates a model that generates obvious gibberish in
052   response to a dangerous prompt. Other methods, such as WhoIsHarryPotter (Eldan & Russinovich,
053   2023), employ a two-stage approach, training a reinforced model for the concept being erased and
      then training an unlearned model that behaves differently on the reinforced logits. Our analysis

077   To address these triad of objectives, we propose a new method, Erasure of Language Memory
078   (ELM), which enables precise knowledge erasing while maintaining contextual text generation flu-
079   ency for seamless editing. Our core idea is to fine-tune a model using an objective to match the
080
      original model but with reduced likelihood for text belonging to the concept to be erased. When
      applied using low-rank adaptation to specific layers, this procedure can be shown to effectively
081
      eliminate internal representations of the knowledge. We also employ the same objective to synthe-
082
      size fine-tuning training data that can be used to ensure seamlessness: this synthesized data enables
083   the model to maintain fluency in the neighborhood of the erased concept without introducing any
084   information about that concept.
085
086
      Through extensive experiments on various benchmarks spanning WMDP biosecurity and cyberse-
      curity, as well as literary concepts like Harry Potter, we evaluate ELM on each of the three goals
087
      compared to prior techniques. We measure specificity and innocence using multiple-choice ques-
088
      tions. Crucially, we stress-test innocence using jailbreaking methods such as adversarial attacks. We
089   also measure seamlessness by examining text coherence when prompted about erased concepts, and
090   we compare previous methods on all these metrics.

093   2          R ELATED WORK
094
095   Machine Unlearning The idea of removing specific data from machine learning models, known
096
      as machine unlearning, has gained attention in recent years, initially motivated by privacy concerns
      (Cao & Yang, 2015; Harding et al., 2019). Early methods focused on efficiently removing individual
097
      training examples or facts from models (Golatkar et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2022; Jang et al., 2022a).
098
      However, most existing benchmarks evaluate unlearning on artificially created deletion sets (Choi &
099   Na, 2023; Goel et al., 2022; Maini et al., 2024), in contrast to our focus on real-world distributions
100   of broad conceptual knowledge.
101
102   Erasing broad conceptual knowledge from LLMs New approaches to machine unlearning have
103   recently gained traction on the problem of removing dangerous capabilities from LLMs (Lynch
104   et al., 2024; Ilharco et al., 2023; Jang et al., 2022b; Lu et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023; Casper et al.,
105   2024; Eldan & Russinovich, 2023). Our work is most directly comparable to three state-of-the-art
106   techniques: Representation Misdirection for Unlearning (RMU) (Li et al., 2024), WhoIsHarryPotter
107   (WHP) (Eldan & Russinovich, 2023), and Representation Noising (RepNoise) (Rosati et al., 2024).
      RMU fine-tunes models to align internal activations with random scaled vectors when processing

108
      targeted concepts. WHP (Eldan & Russinovich, 2023) employs a two-stage approach, first training
109   a reinforced model for the concept being erased and then training an unlearned model to behave
110   differently on the reinforced logits. RepNoise (Rosati et al., 2024) focuses on removing informa-
111   tion about harmful representations across all layers of the LLM, by doing gradient ascent along
112   with some representation noising (training internal representations to match Gaussian noise). While
113   all these methods successfully reduce model performance on erased knowledge, our measurements
114   reveal that these previous approaches fall short in one or more of the three erasing goals. In con-
115   trast, our current work erases concepts by fine-tuning towards a principled target distribution that is
116   designed to balance all three desiderata of innocence, specificity, and seamlessness.
117   Other methods such as LLMU (Yao et al., 2023), SSD (Foster et al., 2024), and SCRUB (Kurmanji
118   et al., 2024) have also been proposed but face significant limitations. LLMU uses a differentiable
119   membership classifier to identify unlearning set examples but struggles with imprecisely defined
120   target distributions (see Li et al., 2024). SSD offers a retraining-free method for removing specific
121   samples but is limited to individual examples rather than broader knowledge domains. SCRUB aims
122   to remove sensitive data while preserving model performance on other tasks but requires access to
123
      the full training dataset, which is often impractical. Importantly, these methods were compared by
      RMU (Li et al., 2024) and found to be less effective for erasing broad conceptual knowledge.
124

126
      Distilling generative model outputs. Some techniques for controlling generative model outputs
      involve using an auxiliary generative model to specify the desired behavior over some training data,
127
      and then distilling these outputs into a target model by training the target model to mimic the demon-
128
      strated behavior. For example Askell et al. (2021) and Bai et al. (2022) prompt an unsafe model into
129   behaving more safely, and then distil the prompted model. Similarly, Gandikota et al. (2023) train a
130   diffusion image model to mimic the outputs of an edited copy of the model whose generations have
131   been guided to not produce images with certain attributes. Rosati et al. (2024) can also be classified
132   under distillation methods as it mimics a Gaussian distribution when processing harmful tokens.
133   Similarly, ELM matches the harmful logits to a modified output distribution, but unlike the distil-
134   lation methods that usually adhere to the standard matching objective, we employ a multi-objective
135   framework to address the seamlessness and specificity concerns with standard distillation methods.
136   Prior works like Emulator and DeRa leverage probability ratios for behavioral modification - Emu-
137
      lator (Mitchell et al., 2023) scales fine-tuning changes between model sizes, while DeRa (Liu et al.,
      2024) controls alignment strength during inference. In contrast, ELM introduces a simpler, princi-
138
      pled approach focused specifically on reducing the likelihood of knowledge concept generation.
139
140
      Erasing in generative image models Gandikota et al. (2023) train a diffusion image model to
141
      mimic the outputs of an edited copy of the model whose generations have been guided to not pro-
142   duce images with certain attributes. Gandikota et al. (2024) erase concepts by modifying the key
143   value mapping of cross attention layers in a low rank closed form update. Other works remove the
144   knowledge of unwanted concepts from the model weights; proposing attention re-steering through
145   fine-tuning (Zhang et al., 2023), fine-tuning the attention weights (Kumari et al., 2023) and contin-
146   ual learning (Heng & Soh, 2023). We take inspiration from Gandikota et al. (2023) to reduce the
147   likelihood of a concept being generated.

      3.3   PARAMETER -E FFICIENT F INE - TUNING VIA L OW-R ANK A DAPTERS
203
204   Fine-tuning large pre-trained language models involves large sets of parameters, which can result
205   in overfitting. To address this issue, we employ Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021), a
206   method that introduces a factorization of weights to significantly reduce the number of parameters.
207   Consider a pre-trained model layer with weights W0 ∈ Rd×k , where d and k denote input and output
208   dimensions, respectively. LoRA decomposes the weight update ∆W as follows:
209                                    ∆W = BA, B ∈ Rd×r , A ∈ Rr×k                                       (6)
210
      Here, r represents a small rank (r ≪ min(d, k)), constraining the update to a low-dimensional
211
      subspace. By optimizing only A and B while keeping W0 fixed, LoRA significantly reduces the
212
      trainable parameters. During inference, the update can be efficiently incorporated into the weights:
213
214
                                                  W = W0 + ∆W                                             (7)
215   The LoRA approach offers a balance between adaptation capability, reduced overfitting, and param-
      eter efficiency, making it particularly suitable for our setting of fine-tuning large-scale models.

      We introduce Erasure of Language Memory (ELM), an approach for fine-tuning LoRA adaptors on
219   Large Language Models (LLMs) to selectively erase concept-targeted knowledge while preserving
220   the model’s conditional fluency and general capabilities (Figure 1). ELM uses two user-provided
221   datasets: Derase , containing text related to the target concept for erasure, and Dretain , comprising
222   innocent text unrelated to the target concept. Our method learns low-rank parameter changes that
223   modulate the likelihood of specific concepts being generated, effectively enabling targeted knowl-
224   edge erasure while maintaining desired information.
225   The erasing dataset Derase contains text related to the concept being erased. For example, to erase
226   “bioweapons expertise” from the model, this dataset can include any pretraining or custom dataset
227   that has information about bioweapons. The preservation dataset Dretain contains any other informa-
228   tion that we do not wish to erase. This can be as open as general text datasets like WikiText (Merity
229   et al., 2016) or as specific as concepts that are close to the concept being erased, but that need to be
230   preserved, e.g. the bio-retain corpus released by the WMDP team (Li et al., 2024).
231   ELM’s objective for erasing a concept c− from the model θ comprises three components:
232
233       1. Erasing Objective: When processing input logits from Derase , the model’s predicted probabil-
234          ities should diverge from the original distribution, reducing the likelihood of tokens associated
235          with the concept being erased. This encourages the model to predict tokens less likely to follow
236          inputs related to the target concept. Building on our intuition from Section 3.2, we construct
237          our erasing loss as follows:
238

282   5.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
283
284   We design our experiments to evaluate ELM across the three desiderata that we have set out for
285   thorough concepual knowledge erasure.
286
287   Benchmarks. Our primary evaluation focuses on the Weapons of Mass Destruction Proxy
288   (WMDP) dataset (Li et al., 2024), specifically utilizing the biosecurity (WMDP-bio) and cyber-
289   security (WMDP-cyber) multiple-choice questions (MCQs). To demonstrate ELM’s versatility, we
290
      also employ a modified version of the Harry Potter MCQ dataset (Lynch et al., 2024), expanded
      from binary to quaternary choices for consistency with other benchmarks. This diverse set of tasks
291
      allows us to assess ELM’s erasure effectiveness across different domains and knowledge types.
292
293
      Models. We apply ELM to a range of state-of-the-art language models, including Zephyr-
294   7B Beta (Tunstall et al., 2023b), Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023), Llama3-8B, and Llama3-8B-
295   instruct (Dubey et al., 2024) for the WMDP erasure tasks. For the Harry Potter knowledge era-
296   sure, we use the Llama-2-7B Chat model (Touvron et al., 2023) to maintain consistency with prior
297   work from Eldan & Russinovich (2023). This selection of models enables us to evaluate ELM’s
298   performance across various model architectures and training paradigms.
299
300   Baselines. For the WMDP tasks, we benchmark against Representation Misdirection for Unlearn-
301   ing (RMU) (Li et al., 2024) and RepNoise (Rosati et al., 2024). In the Harry Potter erasure task, we
302   compare with RMU and WhoIsHarryPotter (WHP) (Eldan & Russinovich, 2023).
303
304   Data. From WMDP Bio forget corpus, we utilize 5,000 text samples, each with a maximum length
305
      of 700 characters. From Cyber forget corpus we use 1,000 texts of similar length. The Harry
      Potter erasure task employs 3,000 text samples extracted from the novel series, also limited to 700
306
      characters each. To facilitate conditional erasure (Eq. 8), we prepend contexts such as “You are
307
      an expert in” followed by concept-specific keywords. Additionally, we incorporate text completion
308   examples for consistency, following the approach used by Qi et al. (2024).
309
310   Evaluation Metrics. We assess our method, Erasure of Language Memory (ELM), along four key
311   dimensions; in addition to our three key desiderata, we conduct a test of adversarial robustness.
312
313       1. Innocence: We employ multiple-choice questions (MCQs) related to the target erased class to
314          evaluate contextual knowledge extraction. Additionally, we analyze probing accuracies across
315
             internal model layers to detect any traces of latent knowledge.
316       2. Seamlessness: To measure the model’s ability to generate fluent text when prompted with
317          erased concepts, we assess the reverse perplexity of generated samples on forget set prompts
318          using an independent language model. We generate text from edited models and run it through
319
             a different base model, measuring the perplexity of the text as per the second model (R-PPL).
             This approach quantifies fluency without relying on potentially biased self-perplexity scores.
320
321       3. Specificity: We evaluate the modified model on standard benchmarks unrelated to the erased
322
             content to ensure that the erasure process does not degrade overall model performance.
323       4. Robustness: We test against adversarial attacks like GCG (Zou et al., 2023) to understand the
             model’s tendency to display concept knowledge post-erasure.

513   6    LIMITATIONS
514
515   While ELM demonstrates strong performance in targeted concept removal, several limitations merit
516   investigation. First, we observe degradation in MMLU scores for nearby safer concepts, suggesting
517   the need for more fine-grained erasure techniques with optimization constraints to minimize unin-
518   tended knowledge loss. Second, although ELM generates fluent text when prompted with erased
519   concepts, some outputs lack meaningful content or contextual relevance, highlighting the challenge
520   of maintaining semantic coherence during knowledge removal. The effectiveness of ELM in eras-
521
      ing complex, interconnected concepts remains an open question, as language models encode in-
      formation with subtle dependencies between semantic domains. This necessitates more nuanced
522
      erasure techniques capable of handling concept interdependencies. Additionally, while our evalua-
523
      tion framework comprehensively assesses erasure quality, it may not capture all affected aspects of
524   model behavior, warranting further development of rigorous evaluation metrics and exploration of
525   broader effects on model performance and generalization capabilities.

      7    C ONCLUSION
529
530   This work introduces a framework for evaluating concept erasure in language models, centered on
531
      three critical criteria: innocence, seamlessness, and specificity. Our proposed Erasure of Lan-
      guage Memory (ELM) method emerges as a direct consequence of this framework. ELM uses
532
      low-rank model updates to selectively alter output distributions for erased concepts while preserv-
533
      ing overall model capabilities and fluency. The erasing objective effectively eliminates targeted con-
534   cept knowledge, as evidenced by near-random performance on multiple-choice questions related to
535   erased topics. Simultaneously, the retention and fluency objectives ensure coherent text generation
536   and preserved performance on unrelated tasks. Furthermore, ELM demonstrates robust resistance
537   to adversarial attacks which underscores the effectiveness of our approach in creating models that
538   are not only “innocent” of erased knowledge but also maintain their functionality and coherence. In
539   addition to providing a practical solution for concept erasure, we have established a foundation for
      more comprehensive evaluation of knowledge erasure in language models.

      8   E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542   In this work, we develop a framework for thinking about concept erasure in language models, as
543   well as a new approach to erasing conceptual knowledge. Although we focus on removal of poten-
544   tially harmful knowledge, this technology could be misused to remove legitimate knowledge from
545   a language model without users’ awareness. Additionally, if our method is used to remove harm-
546   ful knowledge, it may create a false sense of security, as models could retain harmful knowledge
547   that is undetected by our metrics. Unlearning has an important place in safety considerations for
548   language models, but should not be the only approach. Finally, we also acknowledge that our eval-
549   uations are focused on harmful knowledge encoded in English; we have not evaluated this approach
550
      cross-linguistically. We release our code publicly to enable open and safe research.

878   D    H YPERPARAMETER A NALYSIS
879
880   To optimize the performance of ELM, we conduct an extensive hyperparameter study, focusing on
881   three key parameters: LoRA rank, erasure strength η, and the range of layers to which ELM is
882   applied. Our findings corroborate and extend previous observations in the literature (Meng et al.,
883   2022; Geva et al., 2023). Figure 4a illustrates the impact of layer selection on erasure efficacy.
884   Consistent with prior work, we observe that applying ELM to earlier layers yields more effective
885   knowledge erasure compared to later layers. Specifically, we identified layers 4-7 of the Zephyr
886   model as the optimal range for achieving a balance between thorough knowledge erasure and preser-
887   vation of general capabilities.
888
      The interplay between LoRA rank and erasure strength η is depicted in Figure 4b. Our analysis
889
      reveals that lower values of η result in diminished effects on both erasure performance and general
890   benchmark scores. Interestingly, we found no clear trend with respect to LoRA rank, with lower-rank
891   updates performing comparably to higher-rank alternatives. This suggests that ELM can achieve
892   effective erasure with minimal parametric overhead.
893
      Based on these empirical results, we adopted a configuration of rank 4, η = 500, and application
894
      to layers 4-7 for all subsequent experiments. This configuration strikes a balance between erasure
895
      efficacy, computational efficiency, and preservation of general language capabilities.

      F   C ONDITIONAL F LUENCY T RAINING
939
940   To achieve seamless editing, ELM must generate fluent text even when prompted about erased con-
941
      cepts. The ideal behavior mimics a model that never encountered the concept during pretraining.
      We implement an additional step to make ELM models acknowledge the concept while suggesting
942
      a topic change, although this behavior remains configurable through prompt engineering.
943
944   Our training procedure extends the erasure objective from Equation 9. For each prompt from the
945   harmful dataset, we generate new tokens using the erasure objective. Importantly, we do not consider
946   these newly generated tokens as harmful context for subsequent generations, but rather use them
947
      for positive conditioning. This approach allows the model to continue generating fluently while
      reducing the likelihood of discussing the erased concept. Through this process, the model learns to
948
      maintain fluency while decreasing the probability of elaborating on the queried concept. Inspired
949
      by Qi et al. (2024), we incorporate an additional consistency mechanism. We append a standard
950   response to the initial prompt, such as a paraphrased version of: “This is a harmful concept. Let’s
951   change the topic to something more fun and interesting:” We then initiate the generation process
952   from this augmented prompt. This technique ensures consistent model behavior when encountering
953   erased concepts. The final training step involves generating the complete response, including the
954   initial prompt, consistency prompt, and letting the model generate new tokens. We then pass this
955   entire sequence through the ELM model. Crucially, we fine-tune only the parameters responsible
956   for generating the new tokens. This targeted approach ensures that we preserve the model’s general
957   knowledge while specifically adapting its behavior for erased concepts.
