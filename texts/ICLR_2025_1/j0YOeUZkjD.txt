009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The task of estimating camera poses can be enhanced through novel view syn-
012            thesis techniques such as NeRF and Gaussian Splatting to increase the diversity
013            and extension of training data. However, these techniques often produce rendered
014            images with issues like blurring and ghosting, which compromise their reliability.
015            These issues become particularly pronounced for Scene Coordinate Regression
016
               (SCR) methods, which estimate 3D coordinates at the pixel level. To mitigate the
               problems associated with unreliable rendered images, we introduce a novel filter-
017
               ing approach, which selectively extracts well-rendered pixels while discarding the
018
               inferior ones. The threshold of this filter is adaptively determined by the real-time
019            reprojection loss recorded by the SCR models during training. Building on this
020            filtering technique, we also develop a new strategy to improve scene coordinate
021            regression using sparse inputs, drawing on successful applications of sparse input
022            techniques in novel view synthesis. Our experimental results validate the effec-
023            tiveness of our method, demonstrating the state-of-the-art performance on both
024            indoor and outdoor datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Visual localization, also known as camera relocalization, is a fundamental task in computer vi-
029
      sion that involves estimating the 6-degree-of-freedom (6DOF) camera poses within a known scene
030   based on input images. This task plays a crucial role in Simultaneous Localization and Mapping
031   (SLAM) (Izadi et al., 2011; Mur-Artal et al., 2015; Dai et al., 2017; Tang & Tan, 2018) and has
032   significant applications in areas such as autonomous driving, robotics, and virtual reality.
033
      Traditional methods for camera relocalization can be categorized into two main types: Camera Pose
034
      Regression (CPR) methods (Chen et al., 2021; Ng et al., 2021; Purkait et al., 2018; Taira et al., 2018;
035
      Moreau et al., 2022a;b; Chen et al., 2022) and Scene Coordinate Regression (SCR) methods (Brach-
036   mann & Rother, 2021; Brachmann et al., 2017; Brachmann & Rother, 2019; Shotton et al., 2013;
037   Valentin et al., 2015; Brachmann et al., 2023). Between these, SCR frameworks are particularly
038   favored due to their higher accuracy. However, both approaches require stringent sampling density
039   of training data to ensure reliable pose estimations for arbitrary images captured within a specific
040   scene. Manually collecting a sufficient number of training images is a time-consuming process, and
041   obtaining the corresponding camera pose labels presents further difficulties.
042   In light of this, the CPR-based methods try to enrich the training set with synthetic data rendered by
043   novel view synthesis (NVS) techniques. For example, LENS (Moreau et al., 2022b) employs NeRF
044   to render average sampled novel views, thereby augmenting the training dataset and treating these
045   synthetic images similarly to real data without additional processing. Similarly, DFNet (Chen et al.,
046   2022) utilizes NeRF-W (Martin-Brualla et al., 2021) for NVS and features a cross-domain design
047   that helps to minimize the discrepancies between synthetic and query images, effectively bridging
048   the gap between the two domains.
049   Currently, there is no similar research within the SCR framework, and we raise the question of
050   whether SCR-based pipelines can also benefit from synthetic images. To this end, we attempt to
051   apply NVS for data augmentation within the SCR framework. Nevertheless, we found that SCR
052   methods, which rely on precise pixel-to-pixel (N2N) predictions, are particularly vulnerable to the
053   quality of rendered images. This contrasts with CPR methods, which involve pixel-to-pose pre-
      dictions and are less affected by image quality. As shown in the right section of Figure 1, after

097   available synthetic data as inputs, progressively training the coarse model with valid rendered pixels.
098   Following this, we fine-tune the coarse model using sparse real pixels. This method enables our PoI
099   variant to efficiently leverage sparse inputs while ensuring strong pose estimation performance, even
100   in difficult conditions.

102

111   Camera Pose Regression The CPR methods, i.e., to regress the camera pose from the given image
112   directly, are the most naive ideas and most widely used in learning-based methods (Kendall et al.,
113   2015; Brachmann et al., 2016; Brahmbhatt et al., 2018; Melekhov et al., 2017; Radwan et al., 2018;
114   Wang et al., 2020; Hu et al., 2020; Arnold et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022; Shavit & Keller, 2022).
115   The most straightforward method implicitly uses CNN layers or MLP to represent the image-to-pose
116   correspondence. PoseNet (Kendall et al., 2015) first proposes this using pre-trained GoogLeNet as
117   the feature extractor. Then, several works focus on improving CPR through additional modules.
118
      Geomapnet (Brahmbhatt et al., 2018) estimates the absolute camera poses and the relative poses
      between adjacent frames. AtLoc (Wang et al., 2020) uses a self-attention module to extract salient
119
      features from the image. Vlocnet++ (Radwan et al., 2018) adds a semantic module to solve the
120
      dynamic scene and improve the robustness for blockings and blurs. Marepo (Chen et al., 2024a)
121   first regresses the scene-specific geometry from the input images and then estimates the camera
122   pose using a scene-agnostic transformer. The CPR method has achieved excellent efficiency and
123   simplification of the framework, but there is still room for improvement in accuracy.
124
      Scene Coordinate Regression Recently, the SCR methods (Shotton et al., 2013; Brachmann et al.,
125
      2017; Brachmann & Rother, 2018; 2019; Massiceti et al., 2017; Li et al., 2018; Brachmann &
126
      Rother, 2021) achieve better performance in terms of the accuracy compared with the CPR methods.
127   The SCR method aims to estimate the coordinates of the points in 3D scenes instead of relying on the
128   feature extractor to find salient descriptors, as in CPR methods. SCR was initially proposed using
129   the random forest for RGB-D images (Shotton et al., 2013). Recently, estimating scene coordinates
130   through RGB input has been widely studied. ForestNet(Massiceti et al., 2017) compares the benefits
131   of Random Forest (RF) and Neural Networks in evaluating the scene coordinate and camera poses.
132   ForestNet also proposes a novel method to initiate the neural network from an RF. DSAC (Brach-
133   mann et al., 2017), DSAC++ (Brachmann & Rother, 2018) devise a differentiable RANSAC, and
134   thus the SCR method can be trained end-to-end. ESAC (Brachmann & Rother, 2019) uses a mix-
135
      ture of expert models (i.e., a gating network) to decide which domain the query belongs to, and
      then the complex SCR task can be split into simpler ones. DSAC* (Brachmann & Rother, 2021)
136
      extends the previous works to applications using RGB-D or RGB images, with/without the 3D mod-
137
      els. This means that in the minimal case, only RGB images will be used as the input to DSAC*,
138   just like most CPR methods. More information about the 3D structure will be utilized for most SCR
139   methods than CPR ones. However, approaches like DSAC* can achieve more accurate estimations
140   even if the input is the same as the CPR method. ACE (Brachmann et al., 2023) and GLACE (Wang
141   et al., 2024) abandon the time-consuming end-to-end supervision module and shuffle all pixels of the
142   scene to improve training efficiency. ACE and GLACE use only RGBs without extra 3D geometry
143   information and achieve comparable accuracy compared with former methods.
144   Despite the progress of CPR and SCR methods, both methods still have great problems in data
145   collection and labeling. Therefore, efficient data collection and labeling methods or alternatives
146   with similar effects are needed.
147
      Novel view synthesis (NVS) for pose estimation A major challenge for visual localization methods
148
      is collecting appropriate photos to cover the entire scene. Essentially, the number and distribution
149
      of image sets for training are difficult to decide. For example, most outdoor scenes collect data
150   along roads, such as Cambridge landmarks (Kendall et al., 2015). For indoor datasets (such as
151   the 7Scenes (Shotton et al., 2013) dataset), all translations and orientations within the scene are
152   considered.
153
      To fulfill the diverse requirements of data collecting, some works try to use more flexible NVS to
154
      render synthetic views instead of collecting extra data (Chen et al., 2021; Ng et al., 2021; Purkait
155
      et al., 2018; Taira et al., 2018; Moreau et al., 2022b; Chen et al., 2022), where NVS is the method to
156   render synthetic images from the camera poses, which can verify the accuracy of 3D reconstruction,
157   especially for implicit reconstruction methods like NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2021), and Gaussian
158   Splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023). INeRF (Yen-Chen et al., 2021) applies an inverted NeRF to optimize
159   the estimated pose through color residual between rendered and observed images. However, the
160   initially estimated poses are significant in guaranteeing the convergence of outputs. LENS (Moreau
161   et al., 2022b) samples the poses uniformly all over the area and trains a NeRF-W (Martin-Brualla
      et al., 2021) to render the synthetic images. Then, rendered images and poses work as the additional

252   4.1   P IXEL OF INTEREST (P O I)
253
254   To use rendered images as an auxiliary input for camera pose estimation, most existing methods
255
      estimate the scene coordinates of all pixels (or downsampled pixels) of the rendered image without
      considering the difference in rendering quality of these pixels, which greatly increases the time
256
      and resource cost of training and reduces the effectiveness of auxiliary data. To improve training
257
      efficiency and effectiveness, we are thinking of reducing the number of rendered pixels compared
258   with query images for training. Considering that the Nerf-based reconstruction method predicts the
259   target RGB pixel-wise without cross-pixel guidance, the rendering quality of different pixels from
260   the same image would be independent. So, if we reduce the rendered images frame-wise, some
261   well-rendered pixels of the discarded images would also be removed. To address this problem, we
262   propose a method that finds the well-rendered pixels of the frame: pixels of interest.
263
      The architecture of PoI is illustrated in Figure 2.(b). In order to filter out poorly rendered pixels,
264   we need a method to obtain pixel-level feature supervision instead of frame-level feature map super-
265   vision. We use the pre-trained scene-agnostic convolutional network from Ace (Brachmann et al.,
266   2023) as our backbone to obtain frame-level feature maps, and we would fix the parameters of this
267   backbone network during our entire training process. We input the query images Iq and the syn-
268   thesized images In into the backbone and get query features and novel features. We keep all of the
269   query features, while we use a filtering algorithm to extract features of interest (FOI) from the novel
      features. The filtering algorithm can be divided into two parts: First, we randomly sample the novel

      4.2   P O I AS A PLUG - AND - PLAY MODULE IN NON - END - TO - END SCR METHODS
325
326   For the end-to-end SCR approach, PoI is difficult to use as a plug-and-play module. First of all,
327   end-to-end SCR methods use image-level loss as supervision and have no pixel-level performance,
328   which makes them unusable for PoI. Furthermore, even for two-stage SCR methods (init+e2e) like
329   DSAC*, all pixels of the same image should be supervised within one iteration in the e2e stage.
330   If we filter out some pixels of the rendered images, aligning the rendered features and designing
331   a differentiable RANSAC algorithm is difficult. Finally, pixel-wise shuffling (which is difficult to
332
      achieve in e2e) is also an important factor, without shuffling, poorly rendered pixels are more likely
      to appear in a batch of data. As a result, the network is more likely to get stuck in a local minimum.
333
334   For non-end-to-end training methods like ACE and GLACE, the difference is that we can easily
335   shuffle pixels from all rendered images and query images because the supervision relies only on the
336   camera intrinsics and the planar coordinates of each pixel without further requirements of per-frame
337   joint supervision.
338   Take the GLACE as an example; our PoI could also be used as in Figure 2.(b); the difference is
339   that the backbone should be replaced. We use the global encoder from GLACE to extract the same
340   dimension of global features as the ACE features. We add the global feature and the ACE feature
341   together and get the target feature maps. The following procedure remains unchanged.

343   4.3   S PARSE INPUT
344
345   Sparse input visual localization is a challenging task since both CPR and SCR are not good at
346   estimating unseen parts of the scene because the regression models are trained only from RGB with
347
      weak geometric constraints. However, with the help of sparse-view-NVS, we would obtain enough
      novel views. The challenge is that rendered frames from sparse-view-NVS are generally of lower
348
      quality compared with those from dense-view-NVS. Since our PoI method can make good use of
349
      rendered images, it could be used to solve sparse input visual localization problems.
350
351   In this case, the size of rendered data will be far larger than real data. If we still use the PoI
352   method, the implicit neural map will be mainly contributed by rendered pixels. The accuracy will
353
      be influenced in this case. To address this problem, we propose a coarse-to-fine training approach.
      In the coarse stage, we use the same setting as in PoI; the only difference is that all training data
354
      is rendered images. So, the filter is applied to all rendered pixels which we call the Self-pruning
355
      step. In this step, in order to leave adequate pixels for training, we raise the filter’s threshold (for
356   reprojection errors). We get a coarse model after self-pruning training. In the refinement stage, we
357   fine-tuned the mapping model using real data and the remaining rendered data; we set the learning
358   rate to lower than that of the coarse stage throughout the fine-tuning process. In this step, all pixels
359   are put into the model without filtering.
360
      We finally get the finetuned model and experiment on both indoor and outdoor datasets. The results
361
      and implementation details can be found in chapter 5.3.

473   6     C ONCLUSION
474
475   In this paper, we propose a pixel-of-interest filter for scene coordinate regression. The filter is
476   designed for non-end-to-end methods which enjoy good converging speed. With the filter, we also
477   design a coarse-to-fine pipeline for sparse input scenarios. We conduct experiments on both indoor
478   and outdoor datasets and achieve state-of-the-art camera pose estimation with comparable training
479   time.
