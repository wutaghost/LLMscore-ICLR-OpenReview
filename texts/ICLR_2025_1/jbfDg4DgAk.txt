                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               With the widespread adoption of Large Language Models (LLMs), concerns about
012
               potential misuse have emerged. To this end, watermarking has been adapted to
013            LLM, enabling a simple and effective way to detect and monitor generated text.
014            However, while the existing methods can differentiate between watermarked and
015            unwatermarked text with high accuracy, they often face a trade-off between the
016            quality of the generated text and the effectiveness of the watermarking process.
017            In this work, we present a novel type of LLM watermark, Sparse Watermark,
018            which aims to mitigate this trade-off by applying watermarks to a small subset of
019            generated tokens distributed across the text. To demonstrate this type of watermark,
020            we introduce SpARK, a Sparse WatermARK method that achieves sparsity by
021
               anchoring watermarked tokens to words that have specific Part-of-Speech (POS)
               tags. Our experimental results demonstrate that the proposed watermarking scheme
022
               achieves high detectability while generating text that outperforms previous LLM
023
               watermarking methods in quality across various tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLM) have shown exceptional performance in a
029
      multitude of tasks. From generating documents to answering questions on different topics, LLMs
030   such as Meta’s Llama (Touvron et al., 2023) and OpenAI’s GPT (OpenAI, 2023) have become the
031   foundation upon which many AI applications are built (Luo et al., 2023; Brohan et al., 2023; Luo
032   et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023). However, as these applications increase in their capabilities and
033   accessibility, a growing risk of them being used for malicious purposes, such as generating fake news
034   and being used for cheating assignments, becomes increasingly apparent.
035
      With the ever-increasing problem of LLMs being misused, monitoring the generated text and its usage
036   has become an increasingly crucial direction for research. One effective way for tracking the usage of
037   generated text is by watermarking (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; 2024; Zhao et al., 2024) - embedding
038   imperceptible information into the generated text, thereby making it easier to detect and track for
039   potential misuse. Recent studies have demonstrated the effectiveness and versatility of watermarks in
040   embedding ownership information into generated text and distinguishing it from non-watermarked
041   and human-written text (Krishna et al., 2023).
042   In addition to distinguishing between watermarked and non-watermarked texts, watermarking meth-
043   ods must also preserve the original text quality after embedding the secret information. However,
044   prior works generally agree that there is a trade-off between the quality of the watermarked text and
045   the strength of its watermark. For instance, Kirchenbauer et al. (2023) illustrates this trade-off by
046   introducing a parameter that adjusts the extent to which their method affects the model’s logits. By
047   tuning this parameter, they demonstrate the balance between the quality of the generated text and the
048   robustness of the watermark.
049   In this paper, we aim to circumvent the trade-off between watermark strength and text quality by
050   proposing a watermarking method that augments only a portion of the generated text and checks for
051   that portion of the text for watermark information. The main concept is illustrated in Figure 1. We
052   show that by watermarking only a subset of the generated text, we can still maintain high detectability
053   while minimizing the watermark’s impact on the text quality. Our hypothesis is that while prior
      methods verify a watermark by checking every token within a text, the same effect can be achieved

      2   R ELATED WORKS
092
093
      AI-generated text detection. The methods for monitoring the usage of AI-generated text can
094
      be generally classified into two main categories: AI text detection and watermarking. Of these,
095
      watermarking has proven to be more reliable and effective for distinguishing between generated and
096   human-written text, as well as between watermarked and unwatermarked generated text (Krishna
097   et al., 2023). In addition, as companies and research communities strive to close the gap between
098   LLM-generated and human-written texts, relying solely on AI text detection of the original text
099   will become increasingly challenging. The main objective of LLM watermarking is to inject secret
100   information imperceptible to humans into the generated text, which can later be verified by using
101   watermark detection mechanisms (Kirchenbauer et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024; Kirchenbauer et al.,
102   2024; Liu et al., 2024a; Gu et al., 2024).
103   Text watermarking for LLM. One common approach of text watermarking for LLMs focused on
104   distorting the next token probability distribution of the language model. This is achieved by randomly
105   dividing the vocabulary into two disjoint sets named Green list and Red List, and then promoting
106   the generation of only tokens in the Green list with a bias parameter δ (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023).
107   During detection, a detector with the secret key could recover the watermarked distribution and use a
      statistical test to verify the presence of the watermark.

108
      Recent works have attempted to improve LLM watermarking from the perspective of advancing
109   robustness and security. For instance, Zhao et al. (2024); Kirchenbauer et al. (2024) explored various
110   schemes to enhance the robustness of watermarks. Zhao et al. (2024) illustrated that leveraging a
111   fixed Green list enabled watermarking to be resilient against various types of attacks. Kirchenbauer
112   et al. (2024) explored several hashing schemes for improved robustness. Training-based watermarks
113   are also designed, where one study improved the robustness of the watermark using the semantics
114   of previously generated tokens (Liu et al., 2024b). Liu et al. (2024a) proposed to train two neural
115   networks for text generation and watermark detection to create an unforgeable watermark. Lee et al.
116   (2023) introduced entropy thresholding for code generation as watermarking low-entropy tokens
117
      could compromise the correctness of the generated sequences.
118   Effects of watermark on text quality. However, while these recent works have considerably
119   enhanced the robustness, detectability, and unforgeability of LLM watermarking, it is generally
120   agreed that there is a trade-off between the quality of the watermarked text and the strength of its
121   watermark. The distribution shift introduced in Kirchenbauer et al. (2023) enhances the detectability
122   of the watermark, but it simultaneously allows less likely tokens to be generated, thus affecting the
123
      intrinsic quality of the generated text. Recently, Tu et al. (2023) introduced a benchmark method of
      several LLM watermarking algorithms and verified this deterioration of text quality. To minimize the
124
      impact on the generation quality, Christ et al. (2023); Kuditipudi et al. (2023) proposed to embed
125
      the watermark during the token sampling process, thus inducing zero distortion to the probability
126   distribution of the LLM. However, in practice, the sampling-based schemes struggled to produce a
127   detectable watermark for low-temperature settings (Piet et al., 2023). Huo et al. (2024) introduced a
128   multi-objective optimization method to dynamically generate bias parameters and Green list ratio to
129   achieve both detectability and semantic coherence. In contrast, our approach, SpARK, emphasizes
130   preserving the strength and semantic integrity of generated text by leveraging the innate structure of
131   natural language, eliminating the need for training.

135   3.1   N OTATIONS AND PRELIMINARIES
136
137   We first introduce the notations used in this paper. Let M be an autoregressive language model
138   that takes a tokenized prompt xprompt = {x−N , ..., x−2 , x−1 } and output a sequence of tokens
139   that simulate natural responses. At generation step t, the input for the language model M is
140   combined sequences of tokens xprompt and the tokens x = {x0 , ..., xt−1 } previously generated by
141
      M in the previous steps. The language model M then takes the input and outputs a probability
      distribution of the next token over the vocabulary V of the language model: PM (x−N , ..., xt−1 ) =
142
      (PM (υ|x−N , ..., xt−1 )|υ ∈ V).
143
144   According to Kirchenbauer et al. (2024), watermark algorithms are defined using four parameters.
145   The hash function H generates a pseudo-random hash using the context of the generated text with
146   context width h, the fraction of green list token γ, and the magnitude of the logit bias δ. After the
147
      watermarked text is generated, one can use the same parameters to calculate and retrieve a set of
      green tokens s in the generated text. We then use this set to calculate the statistical significance of
148
      |s| number of green tokens that appeared in the generated text with token length T . We can use a
149
      one-proportion z-test assuming the null hypothesis H0 which states: “The text sequence is generated
150   without a watermark”. The z-score is then calculated as
151

154   If a text sequence’s z-score surpasses a defined threshold, we can confidently determine that the text

190   3.3   S PARSE WATERMARKING USING POS TAGS
191
192   In previous works, most watermarking techniques attempt to encode watermark information into
193   each token in the generated text. As the strength of the watermarking method increases, more tokens
194   are adversely affected, which decreases the quality of the generated text (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023).
195
      We aim to improve the text quality by watermarking the generated text sparsely, which however is
      non-trivial. Attempting to watermark sparsely without knowing the location of the watermarked
196
      elements would be akin to using the previous watermark methods with low strength. This is due to
197
      the statistical test also including the non-watermarked portions of the generated text. To this end, by
198   isolating and conducting the statistical test specifically on the watermarked portions of the generated
199   text, we can significantly enhance detectability while maintaining higher text quality compared to
200   using previous methods with stronger watermarking.
201
      We utilize the Universal Part-of-Speech (POS) tags (Petrov et al., 2012) that exist in the generated
202
      text to mark the positions of the watermarked tokens in the text sequence. Specifically, during the
203
      generation process, we select the positions to watermark based on the POS of tokens that have been
204   generated, allowing the watermark positions to be tied to the sentence structure. This makes the
205   watermark more resilient to insertions/deletions of tokens in the generated text and also makes it
206   easier to extract the watermarked portion of the text using the POS tags.
207
      Before using SpARK, we first select a list of POS tags I to be used for watermarking. When the text
208
      generation process starts, as described in Algorithm 1, we verify when the model has generated a full
209
      word by determining if the next token with the highest probability is the start of a new word. While
210   LLMs sample the next token differently with different sampling schemes, using this strategy could
211   consistently inform us when a full word has been generated, without the need to backtrack during the
212   generation process. Once a full word is produced by the language model, we obtain its POS tag Ptag ,
213   and watermark the next token only if Ptag ∈ I. We choose to watermark the token next to the word
214   with chosen POS tags, as watermarking those words directly would not guarantee it to have the same
215   POS tag after being watermarked, leading to inconsistencies. By using words that have a selected
      POS as an anchor, we can limit the number of watermarked tokens in the generated text and position

242   To watermark the next token, we used a similar process and hashing scheme as described in Kirchen-

      3.4    S PARK WATERMARK DETECTION
252
      Since SpARK watermark is sparse, we identify the specific positions we have selected for the
253   watermark to ensure that the unwatermarked portions of the text are not considered in the z-score
254   calculation. This process would preserve the strength of the sparse watermark. We first identify the
255   words whose POS tags are in the list I and select the next token. These selected tokens are the ones
256   we would watermark during the encoding process and thus would be in the Green list G. At each of
257   the selected token positions, we recover the G using the hashing scheme mentioned in the encoding
258   process and check if the token in that position is in G. We then calculate the statistical significance of
259   the number of green tokens that appeared in the generated text, as shown in Equation 1. However, as
260   we only apply the watermark to tokens after the words with a specific POS, T (the total number of
261
      tokens) would be replaced by the number of tokens in the watermarked positions. The watermark

412   4.3                  R ESULTS OF ROBUSTNESS AGAINST ATTACKS
413
414   As malicious players have the capability of modifying a sequence of watermarked text to evade the
415   detector, watermarking methods need to ensure that the watermark is resilient against changes to the
416
      text. In order to illustrate the robustness of our proposed approach, we consider two realistic types of
      attack: substitution attack and paraphrasing attack.
417
418   Substitution Attack. For the substitution attack, a specified proportion of the text (equal to some r
419   tokens) is replaced with its corresponding synonyms. However, it is worth noting that a simplistic
420   replacement can compromise the semantic coherence of the sentence. Following the settings described
421   in (Wang et al., 2024), we iteratively masked a random token that has yet to be modified and then
422
      utilized RoBERTa-Large to generate candidates for replacement. To ensure the semantic integrity
      of the perturbed text, we only select to substitute a new token if the difference in logits of the new
423
      token and the original is higher than our pre-defined threshold, which we set to be -1. If there is no
424
      token that satisfies the preceding requirement, we proceed to mask a different token. The process is
425   terminated when we have replaced r tokens or we have attempted to replace 3r tokens.
426
427   Table 3 demonstrates the resilience of our method against substitution attack, with SpARK achieving
428
      good performance for the 10% scenario. For higher rates such as 30%, the robustness of our proposed
      method lessens, but they remain competitive with other watermarking algorithms. Detailed results of
429
      each dataset for the substitution attack can be found in Table 10 and Table 11 of the Appendix.

513   5   C ONCLUSION
514
515   In this work, we propose SpARK, a novel watermark method for LLM, that encodes watermark
516   information into the generated text, without degrading its quality. Different from other methods,
517   this approach focuses on encoding a subset of tokens distributed sparsely throughout the generated
518   text. By encoding a small subset of tokens in the generated text and focusing on those subsets for
519   watermark detection, SpARK can minimize the impact of the watermark on the text quality while
520
      maintaining high detectability. Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of our SpARK in
      preserving the text quality, as evidenced by the ROUGE-L score and semantic similarity for four
521
      datasets compared to other methods. Despite watermarking significantly fewer tokens, our approach
522
      maintains competitive robustness against both substitution and paraphrasing attacks.

672   Universal POS tags, are composed of smaller POS tags defined by Penn Treebank. For example, the
673   Universal tag DET when converted to Penn Treebank POS tags would look like so:
674
675                                        I = {DT, EX, PDT, WDT}
676
677   Token Generation Process: For each generation step t, the language model M generates a
678   probability distribution over the vocabulary V:
679                       PM (x−N , . . . , xt−1 ) = (PM (υ | x−N , . . . , xt−1 ) | υ ∈ V)

682   parser:
683                                   Ptag (W ) = POS(W, T )

685   Here, POS(W, T ) represents the function that returns the POS tag of the word W based on its position
686
      in text T .

688

689

692   The modified probability distribution P̃M will be used to generate the next token.

       H    L IMITATIONS
1007
1008
       Although SpARK and sparse watermarking provide a way to encode watermark information with
1009   high detectability while preserving the generated text quality, there are some limitations that can
1010   be improved. As shown in Section 4.3, SpARK can achieve high robustness with only determiners,
1011   while nouns and verbs achieve slightly higher robustness than Hard Watermark. This indicates
1012   that while the SpARK can be robust to attacks, its robustness is usually low compared to other
1013   watermark methods and only gets higher when certain POS tags are used. SpARK is also limited to
1014   watermarking Universal Tags currently (Petrov et al., 2012), which reduced the possible configuration
1015   of this watermark. This would make the process of removing the watermark easier through trial
1016   and error. However, since a Universal POS tag can be broken down into a different set of POS
1017
       tags defined by The Penn Treebank (Taylor et al., 2003), formulating different tag sets from Penn
       Treebank tags would make the watermarking removal process of trial and error harder, negating this
1018
       problem. Furthermore, since the concept of sparse watermark is to watermark only a small set of
1019
       tokens in the generated text, it would be easier to remove the watermarks given the locations of each
1020   watermarked token compared to other watermark methods. While this can be a problem, locating
