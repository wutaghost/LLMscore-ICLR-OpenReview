011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Adaptive optimization algorithms, such as Adam Kingma & Ba (2015) and RM-
014            SProp Tieleman & Hinton (2012), have become integral to training deep neu-
015            ral networks, yet their stability properties and impact on generalization remain
016            poorly understood Wilson et al. (2017). This paper extends linear stability anal-
017
               ysis to adaptive optimizers, providing a theoretical framework that explains their
               behavior in relation to loss surface geometry Wu et al. (2022); Jastrz˛ebski et al.
018
               (2019). We introduce a novel generalized coherence measure that quantifies the
019
               interaction between the adaptive preconditioner and the Hessian of the loss func-
020            tion. This measure yields necessary and sufficient conditions for linear stability
021            near stationary points, offering insights into why adaptive methods may converge
022            to sharper minima with poorer generalization.
023            Our analysis leads to practical guidelines for hyperparameter tuning, demon-
024            strating how to improve the generalization performance of adaptive optimizers.
025            Through extensive experiments on benchmark datasets and architectures, includ-
026            ing ResNet He et al. (2016) and Vision Transformers Dosovitskiy et al. (2020),
027            we validate our theoretical predictions, showing that aligning the adaptive precon-
028            ditioner with the loss surface geometry through careful parameter selection can
029            narrow the generalization gap between adaptive methods and SGD Loshchilov &
030            Hutter (2018).

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Adaptive optimization algorithms, such as Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015), RMSProp (Tieleman &
036   Hinton, 2012), and AdaGrad (Duchi et al., 2011), have become integral to training deep neural net-
037   works due to their ability to adjust learning rates on a per-parameter basis. These methods offer
038
      rapid convergence and alleviate the need for meticulous hyperparameter tuning, making them pop-
      ular choices in various deep learning applications. Despite their empirical success in minimizing
039
      training loss, models optimized with these adaptive methods often exhibit inferior generalization
040
      performance compared to those trained with stochastic gradient descent (SGD) (Wilson et al., 2017;
041   Keskar & Socher, 2017).
042
043   Understanding this generalization gap remains a fundamental challenge in the field of deep learning
044
      optimization. Recent research has begun to shed light on the implicit regularization effects of SGD
      by examining its stability properties in relation to the geometry of the loss landscape (Wu et al.,
045
      2022; Jastrz˛ebski et al., 2019; Cohen et al., 2021). Specifically, the linear stability of SGD near
046
      stationary points has been linked to the sharpness of the minima it converges to, which in turn
047   affects the model’s ability to generalize to unseen data.
048
049   In this paper, we aim to extend the stability analysis framework to adaptive optimization algorithms
050
      to gain a deeper understanding of their dynamics and generalization behavior. We hypothesize that
      the interaction between the adaptive preconditioner inherent in these algorithms and the loss surface
051
      geometry significantly influences their stability properties and the sharpness of the solutions they
052
      find.
053
      Our contributions include:


                                                       1
054
             • Theoretical Advancement: We derive necessary and sufficient conditions for the linear
055            stability of adaptive optimization algorithms near stationary points, contingent on their
056            hyperparameters and the sharpness of the loss landscape.
057
             • Generalized Coherence Measure: We introduce a novel coherence measure that captures
058
               the interaction between the adaptive preconditioner and the Hessian of the loss function,
059            providing deeper insights into how these algorithms navigate the loss surface.

087   1.2   N OTATIONS AND D EFINITIONS
088
      For clarity, we define the notations used throughout the paper. Let θ ∈ Rd denote the parameters
089
      of the neural network, and let L(θ) represent the loss function. The gradient of the loss is denoted
090   by g(θ) = ∇L(θ), and the Hessian is H(θ) = ∇2 L(θ). We use E[·] to denote the expectation with
091   respect to the data distribution.
092
093   Adaptive Preconditioner. Adaptive optimization algorithms adjust the learning rate for each pa-
094   rameter based on past gradients. This adjustment can be represented by a preconditioner matrix Pt ,
095   which is typically diagonal and positive definite. For example, in Adam, Pt is constructed using the
096   exponential moving average of squared gradients.
097
098   Sharpness. We quantify the sharpness of a minimum at θ∗ using the maximum eigenvalue of the
099   Hessian, λmax (H(θ∗ )). A larger λmax indicates a sharper minimum, which is often associated with
100   poorer generalization (Keskar et al., 2017).

      3     T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS OF S TABILITY IN A DAPTIVE O PTIMIZERS
183
      In this section, we extend the linear stability analysis traditionally applied to SGD to adaptive opti-
184
      mization algorithms. We focus on understanding how the adaptive preconditioners inherent in these
185
      methods interact with the geometry of the loss surface, particularly the Hessian, to influence the
186   optimization dynamics and stability near stationary points. Our analysis leads to the derivation of
187   necessary and sufficient conditions for linear stability and the introduction of a generalized coher-
188   ence measure that quantifies this interaction.
189
190   3.1   L INEARIZATION OF A DAPTIVE O PTIMIZER U PDATES N EAR S TATIONARY P OINTS
191
192   Consider an adaptive optimization algorithm characterized by the update rule:
193

195

197   adaptive preconditioner (typically diagonal and positive definite), gt = ∇LBt (θt ) is the stochastic
198
      gradient computed over mini-batch Bt , and ⊙ denotes element-wise multiplication.
199   Let θ∗ be a stationary point of the loss function L(θ) such that ∇L(θ∗ ) = 0. To analyze the stability
200   of the optimizer near θ∗ , we consider a small perturbation δt = θt − θ∗ and linearize the update rule
201   around θ∗ . Expanding gt using a first-order Taylor series approximation:
202
203                                  gt = ∇LBt (θ∗ ) + HBt δt + O(∥δt ∥2 ),                              (8)

214   3.2   A SSUMPTIONS AND S IMPLIFICATIONS
215
      To facilitate the analysis, we make the following mild assumptions:

217              schitz continuous in a neighborhood around θ∗ .
218
219

222
223   These assumptions are reasonable in practice, as the adaptive preconditioners in methods like Adam

225
      stages of training.

263
264   In adaptive optimizers, pi adapts based on gradient information. For instance, in Adam, pi ap-

527   4.4    C ONCLUSION
528
529
      In this study, we have presented a comprehensive theoretical and empirical investigation into the
      stability properties of adaptive optimization algorithms in deep learning. By extending linear stabil-
530
      ity analysis to include the effects of adaptive preconditioners, we have unveiled the mechanisms by
531
      which these optimizers interact with the loss surface geometry, introducing a generalized coherence
532   measure as a pivotal concept in understanding this interaction. Our empirical results validate the
533   theoretical predictions, demonstrating that stability considerations are essential for achieving good
534   generalization performance with adaptive methods. This work provides practical guidelines for hy-
535   perparameter tuning and optimizer selection, with immediate implications for practitioners training
536   deep neural networks. We believe that this study opens new avenues for research in optimization
537   for deep learning, emphasizing the importance of understanding the interplay between optimizer dy-
538   namics and loss landscape geometry as models continue to grow in complexity and scale. Ultimately,
539   our goal is to bridge the gap between theoretical insights and practical performance, advancing the
      field of machine learning.

677   C    P ROOF OF T HEOREM 1
678
679   Theorem 1. The adaptive preconditioner P ∗ modifies the effective curvature experienced by the
680   optimizer, and the stability of the optimizer is governed by the generalized coherence measure γ.
681   Minimizing γ promotes stability and convergence to flatter minima.
682
683   Proof. From the definition of the coherence measure γ = maxi λpii , the maximum effective cur-
684   vature is directly influenced by both the Hessian eigenvalues λi and the preconditioner elements
685   pi .
686
      The stability condition simplifies to η < γ2 , highlighting that reducing γ allows for larger learning
687
      rates while maintaining stability. Since γ depends on the ratio of λi to pi , adjusting pi appropriately
688   can mitigate the impact of large λi , effectively flattening the perceived curvature.
689
690   Therefore, by designing or tuning the adaptive preconditioner to minimize γ, the optimizer experi-
691
      ences a flatter effective loss landscape, promoting stability and potentially leading to better general-
      ization.
