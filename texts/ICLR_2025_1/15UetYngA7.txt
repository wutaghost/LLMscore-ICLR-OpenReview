                                                While training large language models (LLMs) from scratch can indeed lead to
011                                             models with distinct capabilities and strengths, it incurs substantial costs and
012                                             may lead to redundancy in competencies. Knowledge fusion aims to integrate
013                                             existing LLMs of diverse architectures and capabilities into a more potent LLM
014                                             through lightweight continual training, thereby reducing the need for costly LLM
015                                             development. In this work, we propose a new framework for the knowledge fusion
016                                             of chat LLMs through two main stages, resulting in F USE C HAT. Firstly, we
017                                             conduct pairwise knowledge fusion on source chat LLMs of varying structures
018                                             and scales to create multiple target LLMs with identical structure and size via
019
                                                lightweight fine-tuning. During this process, a statistics-based token alignment
                                                approach is introduced as the cornerstone for fusing LLMs with different structures.
020
                                                Secondly, we merge these target LLMs within the parameter space, where we
021
                                                propose a novel method for determining the merging coefficients based on the
022                                             magnitude of parameter updates before and after fine-tuning. We implement and
023                                             validate F USE C HAT using six prominent chat LLMs with diverse architectures
024                                             and scales, including OpenChat-3.5-7B, Starling-LM-7B-alpha, NH2-SOLAR-
025                                             10.7B, InternLM2-Chat-20B, Mixtral-8x7B-Instruct, and Qwen-1.5-Chat-72B.
026                                             Experimental results on two instruction-following benchmarks, AlpacaEval 2.0
027                                             and MT-Bench, demonstrate the superiority of F USE C HAT-7B over baselines of
028                                             various sizes. Our model is even comparable to the larger Mixtral-8x7B-Instruct
029                                             and approaches GPT-3.5-Turbo-1106 on MT-Bench as Figure 1(b).

      1       I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      Large language models (LLMs) such as GPT (Brown et al., 2020) and LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023)
057
      series have demonstrated remarkable success across a wide range of natural language processing
058
      (NLP) tasks. Currently, it has become prevalent and imperative for individuals and corporations to
059   build their own LLMs. However, the computational resources and time costs associated with LLM
060   development remain prohibitively high. Furthermore, despite the structural and functional differences
061   among LLMs, they often exhibit similar capabilities across various tasks. Therefore, besides training
062   an LLM from scratch, another option is to combine the distinct advantages of existing LLMs into
063   a more potent LLM, which is termed knowledge fusion of LLMs (Wan et al., 2024). Figure 1(a)
064   illustrates the results of our preliminary experiment conducted on AlpacaEval 2.0 and MT-Bench,
065   where we plot the percentage of questions each LLM answers best (measured by PairRM (Jiang
066   et al., 2023)) among six prominent chat LLMs. These established LLMs, regardless of their potency,
067
      exhibit distinct strengths. Therefore, knowledge fusion not only reduces the developmental costs of
      creating a new LLM but also has the potential to integrate the diverse strengths of existing models.
068
069   The endeavor to integrate the capabilities of multiple models has been a long-standing pursuit. For
070   example, ensemble methods (Littlestone & Warmuth, 1994; Jiang et al., 2023) directly aggregate
071   the outputs of multiple models to enhance prediction performance and robustness. However, this
072   approach requires maintaining multiple trained models during inference, which is inefficient for LLMs
073
      due to their substantial memory and inference time requirements. Another approach is to directly
      merge several neural networks into a single network through arithmetic operations in the parameter
074
      space (Gupta et al., 2020), whereas this approach typically assumes uniform network architectures and
075
      requires manually-tuned (Wortsman et al., 2022; Yadav et al., 2024) or automatically-learned (Matena
076   & Raffel, 2022; Jin et al., 2023) coefficients to merge the parameters of different neural networks. In
077   contrast, knowledge fusion (Wan et al., 2024) seeks to integrate the capabilities of multiple LLMs,
078   irrespective of their architectures, into a single LLM through lightweight continual training. This
079   process essentially embodies a traditional multi-teacher knowledge distillation procedure (You et al.,
080   2017), but faces new challenges such as token alignment and fusion strategies across different LLMs.
081
      In this study, we introduce a fuse-and-merge framework to extend the fusion of LLMs to chat-based
082   LLMs1 with diverse architectures and scales through two stages, resulting in F USE C HAT. Firstly,
083   we conduct pairwise knowledge fusion for source chat LLMs to generate multiple target LLMs of
084   identical structure and size. To achieve this, we first select a pivot LLM and perform token alignment,
085   followed by knowledge fusion between the pivot and each of the remaining LLMs. These target
086   LLMs are expected to inherit the strengths of source chat LLMs through knowledge transfer during
087   lightweight fine-tuning. Secondly, these target LLMs are merged within the parameter space, where
088   we introduce a novel method called SCE to determine the merging coefficients based on the magnitude
089   of parameter updates before and after fine-tuning. Moreover, SCE allocates parameter matrix-level
090
      coefficients that enable the merging at a fine-grained granularity without additional training efforts.
091   F USE C HAT offers superior potential compared to F USE LLM (Wan et al., 2024). Firstly, while
092   F USE LLM limits its exploration to source LLMs of the same size as the target LLM, F USE C HAT
093   broadens the scope by incorporating six source LLMs with varying scales. This allows for greater
094   adaptability to the fusion of diverse chat LLMs. Secondly, the framework of F USE LLM does not
095   seamlessly support the inclusion of new source LLMs as it requires the combination of distribution
096
      matrices from all source LLMs during continual training. In contrast, integrating a new source LLM
      in F USE C HAT is plug-and-play, requiring only obtaining a target LLM from the new source LLM
097
      and merging it with the existing F USE C HAT. Thirdly, compared to many-to-one knowledge fusion,
098
      pairwise fusion empirically mitigates the challenges of knowledge distillation from source LLMs.
099
100   To verify the effectiveness of F USE C HAT, we implemented F USE C HAT-7B using six prominent
101   open-source chat LLMs: OpenChat-3.5-7B (Wang et al., 2024a), Starling-LM-7B-alpha (Zhu et al.,
102
      2023), NH2-SOLAR-10.7B (Kim et al., 2023), InternLM2-Chat-20B (Cai et al., 2024), Mixtral-
      8x7B-Instruct (Jiang et al., 2024), and Qwen-1.5-Chat-72B (Bai et al., 2023). Experimental results
103
      on two representative instruction-following benchmarks, AlpacaEval 2.0 (Dubois et al., 2024b)
104
      and MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2024), demonstrate the superiority of F USE C HAT-7B across a broad
105   spectrum of chat LLMs at 7B, 10B, and 20B scales. Moreover, we validated the proposed token
106   alignment method and the SCE merging method through a series of analytical experiments.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Model Fusion Combining the capabilities of diverse models has been a long-standing objective.
111   Existing approaches to model fusion mainly fall into three categories. Firstly, traditional model
112   ensemble techniques combine the outputs of multiple models by weighted averaging (Littlestone &
113   Warmuth, 1994) or majority voting (Monteith et al., 2011) to enhance overall system performance.
114
      Recently, Jiang et al. (2023) introduced a sequence-level ensemble framework for LLMs, which
      first conducts pairwise comparisons to rank the outputs of LLMs and then employs another LLM
115
      to consolidate the top-ranked candidates into an improved output. In addition to the sequence-level
116
      ensemble, Ding et al. (2024) blended multiple LLMs using a token-level gating mechanism on the
117   output logits. To avoid additional training during ensemble, Mavromatis et al. (2024) leveraged the
118   perplexity of different LLMs over input prompts to determine the importance of each model.
119
120
      Secondly, model merging facilitates the fusion of models of identical structure and scale within the
      parameter space. Wortsman et al. (2022) combined multiple models, obtained by fine-tuning a model
121
      on the same dataset but with distinct strategies, through linear averaging. Matena & Raffel (2022)
122
      enhanced simple weighted average by incorporating Fisher Information Matrix (Fisher, 1922) to
123   determine the significance of individual model parameter. Jin et al. (2023) performed merging by
124   addressing an optimization problem that minimizes the L2 distance between merged and individual
125   models, and conducting a closed-form solution. Although these methods can automatically compute
126   merging coefficients, they necessitate either forward or backward propagation using additional data,
127   making model merging compute-inefficient and memory-intensive. Ilharco et al. (2023) and Zhang
128   et al. (2023) conducted simple arithmetic operations on the task vectors or LoRA (Hu et al., 2022)
129   modules of different models, thereby enhancing multi-task ability and domain generalization. To
130   mitigate parameter interference, Yu et al. (2023) and Yadav et al. (2024) introduced sparsification
131
      techniques that trim redundant values from task vectors prior to model merging. Moreover, Kim et al.
      (2023) and Akiba et al. (2024) advanced the field by merging multiple LLMs across both parameter
132
      and data flow spaces, yielding a merged LLM with up-scaled depth and superior performance.
133
134   Thirdly, mixture of experts (MoEs) combines specialized expert modules with a sparsely activated
135   mechanism (Fedus et al., 2022), presenting another venue for model fusion. Komatsuzaki et al. (2023)
136   first proposed initializing a sparse MoEs module using multiple copies from a dense checkpoint. To
137
      integrate multiple domain experts, Sukhbaatar et al. (2024) trained multiple domain-specific LLMs
      from a seed LLM separately and then used feed-forward networks on top of these dense experts
138
      to instantiate a sparse MoEs module, followed by further fine-tuning to learn token-level routing.
139
      Similarly, Feng et al. (2024) trained multiple domain-specific LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) modules as
140   experts and combined these domain experts using an explicit sequence-level routing strategy.
141
142   Lastly, F USE LLM (Wan et al., 2024) introduces another paradigm for the fusion of LLMs with struc-
143
      tural differences. This approach builds upon knowledge distillation and leverages the probabilistic
      distribution matrices generated by source LLMs to transfer collective knowledge into a target LLM.
144
      Unlike model ensembles and MoEs, knowledge fusion does not require the parallel deployment of
145
      multiple models (experts). Furthermore, compared to model merging, which only applies to models
146   with identical architectures, F USE LLM allows for the fusion of LLMs with different architectures.
147
      Knowledge Distillation Knowledge fusion essentially performs knowledge distillation to transfer
148
      knowledge from source LLMs to a target LLM. Knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) aims to
149
      train a small student model guided by one or more larger teacher models. Previous studies primarily
150   focus on training a student model to mimic the teacher’s behavior in text classification tasks, by
151   replicating the teacher’s output logits (Sanh et al., 2019; Turc et al., 2019), as well as hidden states
152   (Sun et al., 2019; Jiao et al., 2020) and relations (Wang et al., 2020). In the realm of generative models,
153   prevailing approaches maximize the log-likelihood of the student on the distributions (Khanuja et al.,
154   2021; Gu et al., 2024; Agarwal et al., 2024) or sequences (Kim & Rush, 2016; Peng et al., 2023)
155   generated by the teacher model. This paradigm can be extended to accommodate multiple teachers by
156   either averaging the distributions (You et al., 2017) or blending the sequences (Wang et al., 2024a).
157   Compared to vanilla knowledge distillation, knowledge fusion of LLMs faces new challenges.
158   Firstly, due to the differences in tokenization among various LLMs, token alignment is essential
159   for transferring knowledge from source to target LLMs. Secondly, when dealing with distributions
160   generated from multiple source LLMs, the fusion function becomes crucial for optimally integrating
161   their distributions. Thirdly, to leverage the unique advantages of different LLMs, it is necessary and
      challenging to create a compact knowledge fusion dataset that is diverse in capabilities and domains.

      3.1       OVERVIEW
179
      Figure 2 presents an overview of our F USE C HAT in comparison with F USE LLM (Wan et al., 2024).
180   The F USE C HAT framework consists of two main stages: fuse and merge. In the fuse stage, pairwise
181   knowledge fusion is conducted on source chat LLMs2 to derive multiple target LLMs with identical
182   structure and size. This process begins by selecting a pivot LLM, followed by performing knowledge
183   fusion between the pivot and each remaining LLM. In the merge stage, these target LLMs are
184   combined within the parameter space, where we introduce a novel method to determine the merging
185   coefficients based on the magnitude of parameter updates before and after fine-tuning.
186   Specifically, considering K source LLMs {Msi }K   i=1 with varying architectures and scales, F USE C HAT
187   first specifies one of the source LLMs, Msv , as the pivot and then applies pairwise knowledge fusion
188   to obtain (K − 1) target LLMs, {Mtj }K−1
                                             j=1 , which share the same architecture and initialized weights
189   as the pivot LLM. The selection of the pivot depends on the desired structure and scale for the target
190   LLMs, while also considering the capabilities and performance of a candidate LLM.
191
      To perform pairwise knowledge fusion, F USE C HAT prompts these source LLMs using a supervised
192
      fine-tuning dataset D = {Ii , Ri }Mi=1 to showcase their inherent knowledge by responding to each
193
      instruction in D. Token alignment (Fu et al., 2023; Wan et al., 2024) between the source LLMs and
194   the pivot is then conducted to properly map the resulting probabilistic distribution matrices. These
195   distribution matrices are subsequently used for pairwise knowledge fusion (Wan et al., 2024) through
196   lightweight fine-tuning to obtain (K − 1) target LLMs. Following this, the target LLMs are merged
197   in the parameter space to yield the final fused LLM Mf . To incorporate fine-grained advantages of
198   target LLMs, we introduce a new merging method named SCE to obtain the merging coefficients
199   based on selection, calculation, and erasure on the task vectors (Ilharco et al., 2023) which represent
200   variation of model weights before and after fine-tuning. SCE enables the automatic allocation of
201   parameter matrix-level merging coefficients, facilitating the merging of LLMs at a finer granularity.

      3.3   PAIRWISE K NOWLEDGE F USION
217
218   To facilitate the description of pairwise knowledge fusion, we reframe the above token-level view into
219   a matrix format. Specifically, for each instruction Ii , we transform the token-level predictions into a
220   probabilistic distribution matrix, Pθi ∈ RN ×V , where V denotes the vocabulary size. The distribution
221   matrix is assumed to reflect certain inherent knowledge of the language model in responding to the
222   instruction (Wan et al., 2024). Consequently, different probabilistic distribution matrices obtained
223   from different chat LLMs can be used to represent the diverse knowledge embedded within these
224
      models. Based on this assumption, F USE C HAT performs pairwise knowledge fusion by fine-tuning
      the target LLMs, initialized from the pivot, using the probabilistic distribution matrices.

      Token Alignment Token alignment aims to address the mappings of probabilistic distribution ma-
253             θ
254   trices {Pi j ∈ RN ×V }Kj=1 generated by different source LLMs for a given instruction Ii . Therefore,
255
      the alignment involves two dimensions of the matrices: the sequence dimension for the tokenized
      response and the distribution dimension for the probabilistic distributions. In the sequence dimension,
256
      we follow previous works (Fu et al., 2023; Wan et al., 2024) and adopt dynamic programming to
257
      recursively minimize the total cost of editing the tokens from a source LLM to align them with the
258   pivot LLM. This process may result in 1-1, 1-n, and n-1 mappings, as shown in Figure 7. In the
259   distribution dimension, Fu et al. (2023) focused on aligning distributions based on the exact match
260   (EM) between tokens in source and target distributions, which restricts the alignment to only 1-1
261   mappings and may result in too many unmatched tokens. Wan et al. (2024) relaxed the EM constraint
262   by aligning the distributions based on the minimum edit distance (MinED) between tokens in the
263   vocabularies of source and target LLMs. While this approach improves the mapping success rate and
264   expands to 1-n mappings, it ignores n-1 mappings and may introduce many misalignments.
265   In this work, we propose an enhanced token alignment strategy that utilizes mapping statistics (MS)
266   from the sequence dimension as the criteria for alignment in the distribution dimension. We construct
267   a global statistical matrix, where each column represents the frequency of mappings from a pivot
268   token to all potential source tokens, derived from sequence-dimensional token alignments. In the case
269   of 1-1 and 1-n mappings, we align the distributions based on the maximum mapping frequency in the
      respective columns of the statistical matrix for each pivot token in the distribution. For n-1 mappings,

      3.5 D ISCUSSIONS
316
317
      The reasons why F USE C HAT adopts pairwise rather than many-to-one knowledge fusion as
318   F USE LLM (Wan et al., 2024) are twofold. Firstly, directly fusing all the source LLMs proves
319   to be difficult, as evidenced by the results of OpenChat-3.5-7B Multi in Table 1. Instead, F USE C HAT
320   adopts a fuse-and-merge strategy, wherein the fusing stage employs pairwise knowledge fusion be-
321   tween the pivot LLM and other source LLMs, which reduces the difficulty of model fusion. Secondly,
322   F USE C HAT offers superior scalability compared to F USE LLM. The framework of F USE LLM re-
323   quires the combination of distribution matrices from all source LLMs during continual training, which
      does not easily support the inclusion of new LLMs. In contrast, F USE C HAT supports plug-and-play

328
329   In our experiments, we explore the fusion of chat LLMs with diverse architectures and scales. Specif-
330   ically, we conduct experiments using six representative chat LLMs as the source LLMs, including
331   OpenChat-3.5-7B (Wang et al., 2024a), Starling-LM-7B-alpha (Zhu et al., 2023), NH2-SOLAR-
332
      10.7B (Kim et al., 2023), InternLM2-Chat-20B (Cai et al., 2024), Mixtral-8x7B-Instruct (Jiang et al.,
      2024), and Qwen-1.5-Chat-72B (Bai et al., 2023). As for the pivot LLM, which also serves as
333
      the starting point for the target LLMs, we opt for OpenChat-3.5-7B due to its balanced scale and
334
      performance. To begin, we first apply pairwise knowledge fusion (Section 3.3) to create five distinct
335   target LLMs with the same structure. These target LLMs are then merged using the SCE method
336   (Section 3.4), resulting in the final F USE C HAT-7B.
337
338   4.1      E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
339
      Training Dataset To leverage the strengths of source LLMs during knowledge fusion while
340
      alleviating catastrophic forgetting, we curate a high-quality dataset named F USE C HAT-M IXTURE
341
      from two different sources. First, 50% of the training instances are sampled from the dataset used by
342   the pivot LLM, OpenChat-3.5-7B. Second, we gather the remaining training instances, which have
343   not been encountered by the pivot LLM, from open-source communities. These two sources result in
344   a corpus comprising approximately 95,000 dialogues across spanning various domains. For further
345   details on F USE C HAT-M IXTURE, please refer to Appendix C.
346
347
      Training Details In all experiments, we train the target LLMs using a batch size of 128 and a
      maximum length of 2048 on a single node with 8x80GB NVIDIA A800 GPUs for three epochs,
348
      which takes approximately 9 hours. The models are optimized using the AdamW (Loshchilov &
349
      Hutter, 2019) optimizer with β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.999. We use a weight decay of 0.0 and gradient
350   clipping of 1.0. A cosine learning rate schedule is employed, with a maximum learning rate of 5e-6
351   and a warmup ratio of 0.03. We empirically set the combination weight λ in Eq. 4 to 0.9 and the
352   rationale behind the value of λ is detailed in Appendix H. Our training framework is implemented
353   based on the HuggingFace Transformers (Wolf et al., 2020).
354
355
      Evaluation We assess the performance of F USE C HAT-7B on two representative benchmarks to
      evaluate its ability to follow instructions and engage in conversations effectively. The first benchmark,
356
      AlpacaEval 2.0 (Dubois et al., 2024b), comprises 805 instructions across five test subsets. It compares
357
      the Win Rate and Length-Controlled Win Rate (LC Win Rate) (Dubois et al., 2024a) of a model
358   against GPT-4. We employ the default settings and utilize GPT-4 (GPT-4-1106-Preview) to evaluate
359   the quality of generated responses. The second benchmark, MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2024), consists
360   of 80 multi-turn dialogues spanning various domains including writing, roleplay, reasoning, math,
361   coding, extraction, STEM, and humanities. Originally, GPT-4 (GPT-4-0613) was used as the evaluator,
362   providing a scalar score ranging from 1 (lowest) to 10 (highest) for each generated response. However,
363   due to inaccuracies in the reference responses, we adopt an updated version, GPT-4-0125-Preview, as
364   per the latest works3 , to correct the errors and evaluate the generated responses.
365
      Baselines In our experiments, we compare our F USE C HAT-7B with four categories of baseline
366   LLMs, including (i) Proprietary LLMs, (ii) Source LLMs, (iii) Ensemble LLMs, and (iv) Fused
367   LLMs. The details of these baselines are shown in Appendix D.

515   5     C ONCLUSION
516
517   In this work, we propose a fuse-and-merge framework for knowledge fusion of structurally and
518   scale-varied chat LLMs to integrate their collective knowledge and individual strengths into a more
519
      potent chat LLM, resulting in F USE C HAT. F USE C HAT first undertakes pairwise knowledge fusion for
      source chat LLMs to derive multiple target LLMs of identical structure and size via lightweight fine-
520
      tuning. Then, these target LLMs are merged within the parameter space using a novel method SCE to
521
      calculate the merging coefficients based on the magnitude of parameter updates before and after fine-
522   tuning. Experimental results on two representative instruction-following benchmarks demonstrate the
523   superiority of F USE C HAT across different model scales, even comparable to Mixtral-8x7B-Instruct
524   and approaching GPT-3.5-Turbo-1106 on MT-Bench.
525
      The concept of knowledge fusion shares similarities with related approaches, such as the recently
526
      popular mixture of experts (MoEs). Both methods aim to leverage the strengths of multiple models
527
      (experts). However, while MoEs require loading multiple experts during inference, leading to higher
528   time and memory requirements, knowledge fusion allows the integration of multiple LLMs with
529   diverse architectures and scales into a single LLM without additional time or memory overhead. This
530   makes knowledge fusion more efficient, especially when model size is a critical consideration.
531
532
533
      6     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
534   Our work relies on constructing a knowledge fusion dataset that spans diverse domains and leverages
535   the strengths of source LLMs. This process demands substantial data engineering efforts, which may
536   limit the scalability of our methodology. Future research should focus on developing more efficient
537   data synthesis techniques to expand the scope of the knowledge fusion dataset. Additionally, while
538   our study shows improvements in chat model capabilities, it does not address other critical aspects of
539   LLMs, such as knowledge comprehension and the mitigation of hallucinations. Further investigation
      is necessary to evaluate the applicability and effectiveness of our approach in these areas.

794    Figure 7: Illustration of EM, MinED, and our MS token alignment strategies in 1-1, 1-n, and n-1 mappings.

796
      In Figure 7, we present the token pair mappings employed in three distinct token alignment strategies,
797
      including EM (Fu et al., 2023), MinED (Wan et al., 2024), and our MS. For clarity, these mapping
798   strategies are depicted in a matrix format, where each column represents the probability of a source
799   token being aligned with a corresponding pivot token. The values within these matrices derive from
800   the respective alignment strategies employed. For instance, the matrix WEM relies on exact matches
801   between source and pivot token pairs, while WMinED inversely relates to the edit distance between
802   these pairs. WMS is based on the statistical mapping frequency between the source and pivot tokens.
803
      In scenarios involving 1-1 or 1-n mappings, the EM and MinED methods utilize WEM or WMinED
804   respectively, which may lead to inaccurate mappings. For example, in EM, the token “flow_” might
805   be incorrectly aligned with “<unk>”, and in MinED, “flow_” could map to “flown”, or “belo_” to
806   “below”. In contrast, our MS method achieves more accurate alignments such as mapping “flow_”
807   to “flowers” and “belo_” to “belongs”, using WMS from sequence-dimensional token alignments.
808   For n-1 mapping, where only MS is applicable, multiple source distributions are combined using a
809   weighted average determined by WMS to derive a merged distribution. This unified distribution is
      then processed similarly to the 1-1 mappings.
