009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we propose a novel system for automatically generating multi-shot
012            speech videos with natural camera transitions, using input text lines and reference
013            images from various camera angles. Existing human video generation datasets
014            and methods are largely centered on faces or half-body single-shot videos, thus
015            lack the capacity to produce multi-shot full-body dynamic movements from dif-
016            ferent camera angles. Recognizing the lack of suitable datasets, we first intro-
017            duce TalkCuts, a large-scale dataset containing over 500 hours of human speech
018
               videos with diverse camera shots, rich 3D SMPL-X motion annotations, and cam-
               era trajectories, covering a wide range of identities. Based on this dataset, we fur-
019
               ther propose an LLM-guided multi-modal generation framework, named Orator,
020
               where the LLM serves as a multi-role director, generating detailed instructions for
021            camera transitions, speaker gestures, and vocal delivery. This enables the system
022            to generate coherent long-form videos through a multi-modal video generation
023            module. Extensive experiments show that our framework successfully generates
024            coherent and engaging multi-shot speech videos. Both the dataset and the model
025            will be made publicly available. We encourage the readers to view the illustration
026            of the dataset and generated results at https://oratordemo.github.io/.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Creating multi-shot human speech videos is of significant importance across various industries, in-
032   cluding entertainment, education, the film industry, corporate communications, and content creation.
033
      The production of such videos involves an intricate interplay of several interacting systems. These
      systems encompass the way a speaker articulates a given speech, the manner in which the speaker
034
      gesticulates and moves within a scene to emphasize certain aspects of the speech and the dynamic
035
      camera work that decides between multi-angle shots to emphasize emotions and follow the human
036   subject. However, at its core, a video production begins with a script, and the complex interplay-
037   ing systems are interpretations of the script, performed and executed by experts such as speakers,
038   educators, comedians, and camera operators, and tied together by a director.
039
      In this work, we pose a novel question: can this intricate process be automated by a system of
040
      foundation models? Specifically, can we design foundation models that, given a script and reference
041
      images of a person, collaborate to generate a multi-shot human speech video while accounting for
042   all aspects of the production, including vocal delivery, human motion, and dynamic camera work?
043   A high-level overview of this concept is illustrated in Fig. 1.
044
045

      one (Hu, 2024) and MimicMotion (Zhang et al., 2024a) leverage diffusion models to synthesize
046
      videos of dancing humans based on driving human pose sequences. Audio-driven methods like
047
      EMAGE (Liu et al., 2023) generate 3D proxy geometry from speech inputs. Despite these ad-
048   vancements, current human video generation approaches still fall short in handling the complexity
049   required for multi-shot speech video generation. Firstly, most pose-guided approaches rely on key-
050   points and images, focusing on domains like dancing (Islam et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2021; Wang
051   et al., 2024a; Xue et al., 2024). These methods often depend on pre-defined keypoints, limiting
052   their ability to function in fully automated systems. While some works attempt to generate speech
053   or talk show scenarios, they are typically constrained to single, static half-body shots(Corona et al.,
      2024; Zhou et al., 2020), lacking dynamic camera transitions and failing to maintain visual consis-

074   The question remains: how can we generate long-form human speech videos with dynamic camera
075   shots in a holistic end-to-end system? To address this, we propose Orator, a pipeline automatically
076
      orchestrates the entire process. The system is structured around two key components: a multi-modal
      video generation module that synthesizes the final video, and a DirectorLLM that guides the gener-
077
      ation process. In the multi-modal video generation module, the SpeechGen module first processes
078
      the input text and LLM-generated audio instructions to produce synchronized speech audio. Next,
079   the MotionGen module synthesizes 3D motion sequences based on the audio and motion instruc-
080   tions from the DirectorLLM. These 3D motions are projected onto the reference images, following
081   camera shot instructions from LLM to generate 2D keypoint sequences. Finally, the VideoGen
082   module integrates these keypoints with the reference images via a video diffusion model, producing
083   long-form speech videos with natural camera transitions, synchronized gestures, and dynamic vocal
084   delivery. To naturally tie these modules together, a DirectorLLM serves as a multi-role director,
085   guiding the entire system. By providing instructions for camera shot transitions, speaker gestures,
086   and vocal delivery, ensuring that camera angles and actions are synchronized with the speech con-
087
      tent and emotional flow. The DirectorLLM determines when to switch between camera angles (e.g.,
      close-up, medium, or wide shots) based on the speech content and emotional flow. Beyond camera
088
      control, the DirectorLLM generates natural gesture sequences that align with the speaker’s actions
089
      and offer vocal delivery guidance to modulate tone, emotion, and pacing. By automating the coor-
090   dination between these components, our pipeline effectively overcomes the limitations of existing
091   methods, advancing the generation of long-form human speech videos in dynamic settings.

093
      Another key reason why no existing method has holistically addressed this problem is the lack of
      suitable datasets. Popular public human video generation datasets like TikTok (Jafarian & Park,
094
      2021) and UBC-Fashion (Zablotskaia et al., 2019) focus on dancing and fashion, while datasets like
095
      TED Talks (Siarohin et al., 2021) target speech scenarios but are limited in scale and quality. In
096   summary, as shown in Table 1, current human speech video generation benchmarks are restricted by
097   their limited scale, diversity of identities, and lack of comprehensive 2D, 3D, and camera annota-
098   tions. Moreover, they are constrained to static single-shot settings. To address this gap, we introduce
099   TalkCuts, a large-scale dataset specifically curated for human speech video generation with dynamic
100   camera shots. TalkCuts features a diverse collection of videos from talk shows, TED talks, stand-up
101   comedy, and other speech scenarios, comprising over 10,000 unique speaker identities. Each video
102   contains multiple camera shots and is annotated with 2D whole-body keypoints, 3D SMPL-X esti-
103   mations, and camera trajectories. With 1080p resolution and over 500 hours of footage, TalkCuts
104
      is the largest public dataset of its kind. All videos have been meticulously filtered and annotated to
      ensure high quality, providing a comprehensive resource for training and evaluating models capable
105
      of generating realistic, multi-shot videos in dynamic speech settings.
106

      speech videos with realistic camera shot transitions. The LLM-directed camera shot planning pro-

      Pose-guided Human Video Generation. Current research on pose-driven human video generation
126
      typically follows a standardized pipeline, with a growing emphasis on efficient pose representations
127   such as skeletons, dense poses, depth maps, mesh models, and optical flow. Early works (Yoon et al.,
128   2021; Chan et al., 2019) predominantly based on GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2020). However, with
129   the development of diffusion model like stable diffusion (SD) and Stable Video Diffusion (SVD),
130   more recent approaches (Tu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b) utilize the UNet structure for video
131   generation. For example, MagicPose (Chang et al., 2023) injects pose features into SD by Control-
132   Net (Zhang et al.) meanwhile MimicMotion (Zhang et al., 2024a) and AnimateAnyone (Hu et al.,
133   2023) extract skeleton poses from targvideo frames using DwPose or OpenPose. Unlike skeleton-
134   based methods, DreamPose (Karras et al., 2023) and MagicAnimate (Xu et al., 2023) employ dense
135
      poses, which are directly integrated into the denoising UNet. Furthermore, methods such as Hu-
      man4DiT (Shao et al., 2024) and Champ (Zhu et al., 2024b) extracts 3D mesh maps using SMPLX.
136
137   Audio-Driven Human Video and Motion Generation Holistic body motion generation from
138   speech involves synthesizing whole-body motions (Li et al., 2021; Qi et al., 2024). Recognizing that
139   audio signals convey more than just semantic content, (Yi et al., 2023a) propose a method to gener-
140   ate holistic body movements by segmenting the audio signal into different components, each guiding
141
      a separate motion generation process. Similarly, learning from masked gesture data, EMAGE (Liu
      et al., 2023) utilizes four compositional VQ-VAEs for generation. Witnessing the success of diffu-
142
      sion models, more and more works (Chen et al., 2024) began to utilize a diffusion-based structure.
143
      MotionCraft (Bian et al., 2024b) exemplifies this trend, using a unified DiT structure to incorpo-
144   rate multimodal controls and achieving state-of-the-art results in audio-to-motion generation. In the
145   domain of audio-driven video generation, preliminary works (Sun et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2024; Ji
146   et al., 2024b) have primarily concentrated on facial regions, ensuring a high degree of consistency
147   between lip movements and the semantic content of the corresponding audio. To expand the gener-
148   ated region, (Corona et al., 2024) synthesizes half-body human videos, while Make-Your-Anchor
149   (Huang et al., 2024c) generates anchor-style full-body videos by translating audio into detailed torso
150   and hand movements using a two-stage diffusion model. ANGIE (Liu et al., 2022) employs an unsu-
151   pervised feature to model body motion while DiffTED (Hogue et al., 2024) decouples motion from
152
      gesture videos while preserving additional appearance information.

210   3.2    DATA A NNOTATION
211
212   Camera Shots Definition. In our paper, we classify camera shots into six types: Close-Up (CU),
213   Medium Close-Up (MCU), Medium Shot (MS), Medium Full Shot (MFS), Full Shot (FS), and Wide
214   Shot (WS) based on established cinematographic principles (as is shown in Fig. 2), as outlined by
215   (Brown, 2016). This classification allows for capturing a wide range of visual details and character
      interactions, from intimate facial expressions to contextualizing the subject within their environment.

216
      3D SMPL-X Annotation. We adopt the SMPL-X (Pavlakos et al., 2019) model to represent 3D
217   human motion. For a given T-frame video clip, the corresponding pose states P are represented
218   as: P = {Pf , Pb , Ph , ζ, ϵ}, where Pf ∈ RT ×3 , Pb ∈ RT ×63 , and Ph ∈ RT ×90 represent the
219   jaw poses, body poses, and hand poses, respectively. ζ ∈ RT ×10 and ϵ ∈ RT ×3 denote the facial
220   expressions and global translation. We initially use the state-of-the-art method SMPLerx (Cai et al.,
221   2024) to estimate the whole-body motion sequence P, but observed limitations in the accuracy of
222   face and hand parameters, specifically Pf , ζ, and Ph . To address this, we refine the hand poses Ph′
223   using HaMeR (Pavlakos et al., 2024), and improve the jaw poses Pf′ and facial expressions ζ ′ using
224   EMOCA (Danecek et al., 2022; Feng et al., 2021). We then combine the refined Pf′ , ζ ′ , and Ph′ into
225   the original pose prediction P to obtain the final high-quality motion estimation P ′ .
226
      Camera Trajectory Annotation. To reconstruct global camera trajectories from the monocular
227
      videos in our dataset, we employ TRAM (Wang et al., 2024c), which builds upon DROID-SLAM
228   (Teed & Deng, 2021) for recovering camera trajectories. To achieve metric-scale accuracy, we refine
229   the estimations by leveraging depth predictions (Bhat et al., 2023) and incorporating semantic cues
230   from the background. This process enables us to recover precise, metric-scale camera motion.

313   To achieve this, we leverage the pre-trained capabilities of Stable Video Diffusion (SVD) (Blattmann
314   et al., 2023). SVD is known for its performance in generating high-quality, diverse videos from sin-
315
      gle images, making it an effective model for image-based video generation in our task. By utilizing
      a pre-trained model, we can significantly reduce the data requirements and computational costs. To
316
      enable pose-guide video generation, we integrate ControlNeXt (Peng et al., 2024), a lightweight
317
      convolution module for efficient controllable video generation. ControlNeXt efficiently extracts hu-
318   man pose control features using multiple ResNet blocks, which are then integrated into the denoising
319   process of the pre-trained SVD model. Specifically, the conditional control features derived from the
320   pose sequences are added to the denoising branch of SVD at the middle block, allowing the system
321   to directly utilize the pose sequence Ki during video generation. While combining the pre-trained
322   SVD and ControlNeXt models results in smooth video generation aligned with the pose sequences,
323   we observed an issue: the generated faces often lacked fidelity to the reference images. To address
      this, we fine-tune the ControlNeXt branch on our dataset, specifically adapting the model to the

327   video clips Vi by combining the corresponding 2D keypoint sequence Ki and the reference image
328   Iki . The final long-form speech video V with different camera shots is obtained by concatenating
329   all the generated video clips {Vi }N
                                         i=1 .

332   4.3   D IRETCOR LLM AS A M ULTI -ROLE D IRECTOR FOR H UMAN V IDEO G ENERATION
333
334
      In this section, we describe the DirectorLLM’s role in orchestrating the key elements of video gen-
335
      eration: camera shot planning, speaker gesture control, and vocal delivery guidance. Below, we first
336   elaborate how the DirectorLLM handles camera shot planning based on the input speech script.
337
338   LLM as Camera Shots Planner. The DirectorLLM analyzes the speech script S and generates cam-
339
      era shot instructions {Tic }N                                                                           N
                                   i=1 , which are then utilized to segment the script into N segments {Si }i=1
      corresponding to different camera shots. These shot instructions determine the optimal camera an-
340
      gle transitions based on the narrative structure, emotional flow, and key emphasis points within the
341
      speech. The LLM selects camera shots based on narrative structure, emotional intensity, and key
342   moments in the script, recommending shot transitions like “close-up” (close up shot) during
343   emotional highlights and “wide shots” (wide shot) for contextual emphasis. In our approach to
344   automatic shot division, we employ a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)-based method (Guu
345   et al., 2020; Lewis et al., 2020), leveraging GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023) to produce shot transi-
346   tions {Tic }N
                  i=1 for video content based on speech. The process begins by extracting text embeddings
347   E(S) from the input speech S using a text-embedding model. We then compute the cosine similar-
348   ity between the input embeddings E(S) and a pre-computed set of embeddings {E(Sj )}M             j=1 from
349   our training dataset, the Shot Division Corpus (SDC), which contains speech segments paired with
350
      ground-truth shot transitions {Tjc }M  j=1 . Using this, we retrieve the top-5 most similar speech seg-
351   ments based on the cosine similarity. These retrieved examples {Sj }5j=1 and their corresponding
352   shot transitions {Tjc }5j=1 , are used as few-shot prompts for GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023). Given
353   these contextually relevant examples, GPT-4o generates a shot transition plan {Tic }N     i=1 for the input
354   speech S. This approach enables the model to adapt its predictions by learning from past similar
355   examples, effectively capturing the nuanced relationship between speech content and shot division.
356   LLM as Motion Instructor. The DirectorLLM also acts as a motion planner, guiding the speaker’s
357   body language, gestures, and movement on stage to enhance the delivery of the speech. For
358   each speech segment Si , the LLM motion instructions {Tim }N
                                                                 i=1 , tailored to the content and emo-
359   tional tone of the speech. For gestures, the LLM analyzes key points of emphasis and emotion
360   to suggest actions like “raise right hand” (gesture raise right hand) or “open arms”
361   (gesture open arms) during moments of intensity. For more reflective segments, it might rec-
362   ommend subtler movements like “fold hands” (gesture fold hands). In addition to gestures,
363
      the LLM provides instructions for stage movement. Based on the flow of the speech, the LLM
      suggests where and when the speaker should move on stage, suggesting instructions such as “move
364
      left” (move left) or “step forward” (step forward) to maintain a dynamic presence.
365
366   LLM as Voice Delivery Instructor. The DirectorLLM provides fine-grained vocal instructions
367   for intonation, pitch, pace, and emotion, guiding the speaker’s delivery to enhance engagement.
368   For each speech segment Si , the LLM generates vocal instructions {Tia }N    i=1 tailored to the emo-
369
      tional tone and context. The LLM could conduct prompt-based control for sentence-level adjust-
      ments, controlling overall pitch, emotion, and pacing of a sentence. For example, for introductory
370
      remarks or transitions, the LLM might instruct: “calm tone and lower pitch” (tone calm and
371
      pitch low). During critical moments, the LLM can adjust the pace or suggest pauses for em-
372   phasis: “slow down for emphasis” (slow pace). The LLM can also leverage token-based control
373   for fine-grained adjustments by inserting word-level emphasis, breathing, or laughter tokens. For
374   instance, it can emphasize key terms: “The <strong>only</strong> medication they have
375   for gout kills your liver” or add realism with [breath] or [laughter] tokens: “I’m like, I ain’t taking
376   this... [breath] My foot said, you better try it.”. By combining these sentence-level and word-level
377   controls, the LLM dynamically adjusts the vocal performance to match the speech’s emotional flow,
      providing a more engaging and natural delivery for speech-driven videos.

531
      In this paper, we introduced a novel framework, Orator, for generating human speech videos with
532
      dynamic camera shot transitions. Our system integrates an LLM-guided multi-modal generation
533
      pipeline, effectively orchestrating the generation of expressive speech audio, natural 3D motion
534   sequences, and coherent video outputs. To address the lack of suitable datasets for this task, we
535   presented TalkCuts, a large-scale dataset specifically curated for multi-shot speech-driven video
536   generation, featuring diverse identities, camera shots, and rich annotations. Extensive experiments
537   demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach, advancing the state-of-the-art in speech-driven video
538   generation and opening new avenues for future research in dynamic human video synthesis.

      A.2.1    H UMAN V IDEO DATASETS .
896
897   Recently, various datasets derived from public platforms such as TikTok and YouTube have been
898   introduced to advance human video generation research. For example, the TikTok dataset (Jafar-
899   ian & Park, 2021) includes 340 short video clips, each lasting 10-15 seconds, primarily featuring
900   dancing humans, while UBC-Fashion (Zablotskaia et al., 2019) consists of 500 fashion-related clips.
901   However, these datasets are limited in both scale and quality. To overcome these limitations, several
902   synthetic datasets (Varol et al., 2017; Patel et al., 2021; Cai et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023b) have
903
      been developed, significantly enhancing the diversity of backgrounds and the scale of training data.
      For instance, Bedlam (Black et al., 2023) includes thousands of clips with over 1.5 million frames,
904
      featuring high-resolution rendered humans in realistic environments.
905
906   Recognizing the growing importance of multi-modal data for training, recent datasets Li et al.
907   (2021); Siarohin et al. (2021); Luo et al. (2020) have incorporated various modalities. Further-
908   more, advancements in annotation tools have facilitated the creation of large-scale, highly realistic
909
      datasets.HumanVid (Wang et al., 2024d) consist of more than 50M frames and these frames are well
      annotated and BEAT2 has more than 32M frames with a high resolution of 1080P. However, these
910
      datasets are still limited to identity numbers, which may constraint the ability of generalizations.
911
      While datasets like MENTOR (Corona et al., 2024) exists that have over 80k identities dynamic
912   gestures, the dataset remains private. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first public human
913   video datasets that contains thousands of identities.
914
915   A.2.2    M OVIE & C ARTOON U NDERSTANDING AND G ENERATION
916
917   Recent advancements in generative video models have integrated autoregressive frameworks, dif-
      fusion models, and large language models (LLMs) to address challenges in long-form, multimodal


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      video generation and animation. Early methods such as StoryGAN (Li et al., 2019) and Pororo-
919   GAN(Zeng et al., 2019) used GAN-based models for visual storytelling but were limited by con-
920   textual inconsistencies in generated frames. To address these limitations, Anim-Director (Li et al.,
921   2024) uses LLMs to autonomously manage the entire animation creation process, refining narra-
922   tives, generating scripts, and producing contextually coherent animations from brief inputs. Simi-
923   larly, MovieDreamer (Zhao et al., 2024) combines autoregressive models with diffusion rendering
924   to maintain narrative and character consistency in long-form videos, decomposing complex stories
925   into manageable segments for high-quality visual synthesis. Recent research has also explored using
926   LLMs as ”directors” in video generation, where they coordinate various elements similar to a human
927
      director managing a film production. (Zhu et al., 2024a) use LLMs to decompose complex prompts
      into sub-tasks, enabling precise control over 3D scene generation.(Argaw et al., 2022) introduce a
928
      benchmark for AI-assisted video editing, focusing on decomposing movie scenes into individual
929
      shots based on attributes like camera angles and shot types. This structured representation of shots
930   is conducive to LLM-based systems, which can then manage and edit sequences in a manner similar
931   to a human editor, enhancing the automation of video editing tasks. Additionally, (Rao et al., 2020)
932   propose a subject-centric model to classify shot types, which can enhance LLM-guided video gen-
933   eration by providing structured visual cues. This research suggests that LLMs are well-suited for
934   directing complex video creation processes.

955
956   For subjective evaluation of camera shot changes generated by the LLM, we conduct an experiment
957   with 20 participants, each rating several criteria on a 1-5 scale (1 = poor, 5 = excellent). We compare
958   our model, ground truth (GT), a rule-based system (shots based on speech length, punctuation, and
959   keywords), zero-shot LLM, and a random baseline (shots randomly assigned). Evaluators will assess
960   from the following aspects:
961
962
              • Shot Coherence: measures the logical flow between camera shots and evaluates how well
                the transitions follow the speech content. Evaluators will assess whether the changes in
963
                shots are smooth and whether the cuts feel appropriate based on the context. For instance,
964
                sharp and abrupt cuts during calm moments would detract from coherence, while fluid
965             transitions during significant speech segments should enhance it.
966
967           • Visual Engagement: aimed at evaluating whether the video remains visually captivating
968
                and holds the viewer’s attention throughout.
969           • Shot-Type Appropriateness: refers to how suitable the selected shot types (e.g., close-up,
970             medium shot, wide shot) are in relation to the content being delivered. Evaluators will
971             consider whether emotional intensity or important speech moments are reflected with close-
                up shots and whether wider shots are used to contextualize broader topics or transitions.

972
               • Overall Quality: provides a holistic evaluation of the video, capturing the combined effec-
973              tiveness of shot selection, transitions, and flow.

1136   A.4.1    M ANUAL S CREENING P ROCESS
1137
1138   We outline the detailed steps for ensuring consistency and accuracy in the manual screening process:
1139   1. Scene Segmentation Validation: After performing automated scene segmentation using
1140   PySceneDetect, human reviewers verify the correctness of the detected shot boundaries. Reviewers
1141   ensure that transitions occur at logical points, such as changes in subject focus or significant shifts
1142   in speech content. Incorrectly segmented scenes are manually adjusted to improve coherence.
1143   2. Subject Quality Evaluation: Each video clip is manually inspected to evaluate the clarity and
1144   quality of the human subject within the frame:
1145
1146           • Clarity: The subject must be clearly visible without blurring or obstructions.
1147           • Lighting: The subject’s features must be well-lit and distinguishable.
1148
               • Framing: The subject must be proportionally centered in the frame.
1149
1150   Clips failing to meet these criteria are discarded.
1151
1152
       3. Consistency and Annotation Accuracy: Reviewers ensure: 1) Identity Consistency: The same
       individual is consistent across clips for each speaker. 2) Annotation Validation: Automated an-
1153
       notations (2D keypoints, 3D SMPL-X, camera trajectories) are verified for a subset of samples.
1154
       Anomalies are flagged for correction.
1155
1156   4. General Quality Assessment: Reviewers ensure: 1) Speech Alignment: The subject’s lip move-
1157   ments align with the speech audio; 2) Noise Filtering: Clips with significant environmental noise or
1158
       distractions are removed.
1159   5. Reviewer Training and Quality Audits: To maintain consistency: 1) Reviewers are trained with
1160   examples of acceptable and unacceptable clips. 2) Periodic audits are conducted on random samples
1161   to ensure adherence to standards.
1162   This multi-step process ensures that the dataset maintains high visual and audio quality, providing a
1163   robust foundation for research.

1239   We provide a detailed illustration of the RAG process in Fig. 12. We aim to enhance the shot
1240   transition performance of LLMs using RAG. To achieve this, we use scripts with annotated shot

       scripts, is converted into an embedding dataset using the OpenAI text-embedding-small model.

       A.6     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS .
1287
1288
       Despite the effectiveness of our framework, several challenges remain unsolved. First, interaction
1289
       with props and the environment (e.g., microphones or walking across a stage) is not yet seamlessly
1290   integrated into the generated videos, limiting the naturalness of the speaker’s interaction with ob-
1291   jects. Second, audience engagement elements such as eye contact, gaze shifts, and facial expressions
1292   are critical in talk shows and speeches but are difficult to capture and simulate without audience
1293   cues. Additionally, while our system handles multi-shot transitions effectively, it does not yet incor-
1294   porate moving camera dynamics, which would further enhance the realism of the generated videos.
1295   As future work, we aim to explore moving camera integration leveraging advanced camera control
       modules.

                dynamic, multi-shot speech videos for films, TV shows, and online content. By automating
                camera transitions, gesture synthesis, and vocal delivery, our system reduces the need for
1304
                extensive manual editing and enhances the storytelling quality.
1305

1314            like YouTube, TikTok, or Instagram without requiring advanced editing skills or significant

                production resources.

       By addressing the complex challenge of generating long-form speech videos with dynamic camera
