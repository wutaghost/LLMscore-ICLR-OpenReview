011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advances in zero-shot low-light image enhancement have largely bene-
014            fited from the deep image priors encoded in network architectures. However,
015
               these models require optimization from scratch for each image and cannot pro-
               vide personalized results based on user preferences. In this paper, we propose a
016
               training-free zero-shot personalized low-light image enhancement model that in-
017
               tegrates Retinex domain knowledge into a pre-trained diffusion model, enabling
018            style personalization based on user preferences specified through text instructions.
019            Our contributions are as follows: First, we incorporate the total variation opti-
020            mization into a single Gaussian convolutional layer, enabling zero-shot Retinex
021            decomposition. Second, we introduce the Contrastive Language-Image Pretrain-
022            ing (CLIP) model into the reflectance-conditioned sampling process of Denoising
023            Diffusion Implicit Models (DDIM), guiding the enhancement according to user-
024            provided text instructions. Third, to ensure consistency in content and structure,
025            we employ patch-wise DDIM inversion to find the initial noise vector and use
026
               the reflectance as a condition during the reverse sampling process. Our proposed
               model, RetinexGDP, supports any image size and produces noise-suppressed re-
027
               sults without imposing extra noise constraints. Extensive experiments across nine
028
               low-light image datasets show that RetinexGDP achieves performance compara-
029            ble to state-of-the-art models.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Low-light image enhancement (LLIE) algorithms aim to restore details hidden in dark areas while
035   preserving color accuracy and naturalness. These algorithms not only enhance the human visual
036   experience but also improve the performance of high-level computer vision tasks Liu et al. (2021a).
037   Retinex-based LLIE models Ren et al. (2018); Xu et al. (2020); Yang et al. (2021); Zhang et al.
038   (2019) have gained attention by incorporating Retinex theory into deep neural networks. Accord-
039
      ing to Retinex theory, an image can be modeled as the product of reflectance and illumination:
      S = R I, where denotes the Hadamard product. Thus, Retinex decomposition, which involves
040
      estimating these components, is a central problem for these methods. With the derived reflectance
041
      and illumination, models such as those proposed by Hao et al. (2020); Liang et al. (2022), en-
042   hance images by adjusting the illumination and denoising the reflectance before recombining the
043   two components. Alternatively, some methods separate illumination from reflectance to achieve en-
044   hancement Guo et al. (2017); Zhao et al. (2021). These methods focus on adjusting illumination but
045   pay less attention to the style of the reflectance, which limits their ability to offer personalized image
046   enhancement based on user preferences.
047
      Personalized low-light enhancement (PLIE) is able to enhance low-light image according to diverse
048   user preference, however, it is less explored compared to general LLIE. Existing PLIE models either
049   require users to select preferred images to represent desired styles Kim et al. (2020) or inject user
050   profiles into the network for personalization Bianco et al. (2020). Additionally, these models apply
051   a single style preference vector to all images, limiting the diversity of styles. Recently, masked
052   style modeling is applied to image enhancement, achieving content-aware personalization Kosugi
053   & Yamasaki (2024). Despite the success of these models, a common problem is the inability to
      enhance unseen images in a preferred style that does not exist in the database. Although researchers


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      can enrich the database by collecting more diverse preference images, this would require retraining
055   or fine-tuning the network.
056
057   In this work, we propose a zero-shot text-based personalized low-light image enhancement model,
058
      RetinexGDP, that requires no training or parameter fine-tuning. This model allows users to specify
      enhancement style preferences via text instructions. The core idea behind RetinexGDP is to inte-
059
      grate Retinex domain knowledge into a pre-trained diffusion model while leveraging Contrastive
060
      Language-Image Pretraining (CLIP) Radford et al. (2021) to guide the Generative Diffusion Prior
061   (GDP) Fei et al. (2023). To achieve zero-shot Retinex decomposition, we take the total variation
062   optimization as a layer and incorporate it into a single Gaussian convolutional layer to estimate the
063   illumination map. Instead of designing deep network, our RetinexGDP requires only a single convo-
064   lutional layer for illumination map estimation. We then compute the corrected reflectance in spatial
065   domain and consider it as the initial enhanced result. At the stage of personalized enhancement, we
066   first find the initial noise vector of the corrected reflectance map by patch-wise DDIM inversion. To
067   maintain the content and structure consistency of diffusion model, the corrected reflectance is used
068   as a conditional input during the reverse sampling process of the diffusion model, where the sam-
069
      pling takes the initial noise vector as starting point and is guided by directional CLIP loss, steering
      the enhancement toward the style specified by the user through text. Guided by content and style
070
      loss, RetinexGDP enhances the low-light image according to user-specified preferences without the
071
      need for retraining or fine-tuning from scratch. RetinexGDP is adaptable to images of any input size.
072   Experiments have shown that, despite the absence of additional noise constraints, the enhancement
073   results generated by our model exhibit good performance in noise suppression.
074
075
      In summary, our main contributions are summarized as follows. (I) We propose a zero-shot text-
      based PLIE model named RetinexGDP, enabling flexible enhancement guided by user preferences
076
      specified via text instructions, without the need for additional training or external images. (II) We
077
      incorporate the edge-aware property of total variation optimization into a single Gaussian convolu-
078   tional layer, aiming to perform zero-shot Retinex decomposition. (III) We employ patch-wise DDIM
079   inversion to generate the initial noise vector of corrected refletance and take the corrected refletance
080   as condition of DDIM reverse process, maintaining the image content and structures.

085   Zero-shot Low-light Image Enhancement. While deep learning-based methods Zhang et al.
086   (2022); Huang et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2023), particularly those combined
087   with the Retinex model Yang et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2022); Xu et al. (2022); Fu et al. (2023),
088   have demonstrated superior performance compared to traditional optimization-based techniques
089   Fu et al. (2016); Guo et al. (2017); Li et al. (2018); Xu et al. (2020), zero-shot low-light im-
090   age enhancement (LLIE) remains relatively underexplored. Zero DCE Guo et al. (2020) and its
091   extension Zero DCE++ Li et al. (2021) propose predicting higher-order curves through iterative
092   self-application, independent of paired and unpaired external data. The Retinex decomposition is
093
      transformed into a generative problem in Zhao et al. (2021); Liang et al. (2022), where combined
      deep image priors (DIP) Ulyanov et al. (2020); Gandelsman et al. (2019) are applied to generate
094
      latent Retinex components without the need for any external training dataset. Those methods per-
095
      form zero-shot Retinex decomposition. However, they rely on deep priors encoded in the network
096   structure, with network parameters randomly initialized and no data priors utilized. Consequently,
097   their networks require optimization from scratch for each entry. In this work, we incorporate the
098   edge-preserving property of total variation optimization into a single Gaussian convolutional layer,
099   without requirement of very deep network.

      sidering the content of the preferred images. More recently, masked style modeling is adopted for

      Diffusion Model Based Low-light Image Enhancement. In recent years, diffusion probabilistic
138
      models have achieved significant success in image generation and manipulation. However, chal-
139
      lenges remain in effectively integrating low-light images as conditions and designing appropriate
140   diffusion models. To address this, CLE-Diffusion Yin et al. (2023) concatenates the color map
141   with the low-light input to preserve color information. Diff-Retinex Yi et al. (2023) introduces a
142   three-stage framework where two diffusion models adjust reflectance and illumination components.
143   ReCo-Diff Wu et al. (2023) combines the low-light input with the generated image and performs
144   Retinex decomposition at each time step. Other approaches modify the diffusion process itself for
145   low-light enhancement Wang et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2023). A recent work Reti-diff He et al.
146   (2023) introduces the Retinex priors to latent diffusion, however, it requires additional networks or
147   retraining on external datasets. In contrast, recent works Fei et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2024); Lv
148
      et al. (2024) leverage a pretrained Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM) as a generative
      prior to optimize the reverse sampling process, enabling training-free image enhancement. Build-
149
      ing on these advancements, we incorporate the Retinex domain knowledge into generative diffusion
150
      prior and propose a zero-shot text-based personalized low-light enhancement model.

159   3.1     Z ERO - SHOT R ETINEX DECOMPOSITION

161   Previous Retinex-based LLIE works Zhao et al. (2021); Liang et al. (2022) have leveraged DIP to
      perform zero-shot Retinex decomposition, exploiting DIP’s inductive bias for Retinex component

162
      generation. In practice, these models require additional hand-crafted priors to ensure piecewise
163   smoothness, which is important to illumination estimation. To eliminate the need for such priors,
164   the edge-aware smoothness properties in a bilateral grid and an encoder-like DIP model is combined
165   to estimate the illumination Zhao et al. (2024). Despite its success in illumination estimation, this
166   approach still necessitates a DIP network comprising many convolutional blocks.
167
      Is it possible that illumination estimation be performed with a single convolutional layer? The
168
      main challenge is how to embed inductive bias into a convolutional layer to guide optimization
169   towards a piecewise smooth solution. While convolutional blocks possess inductive biases such
170   as locality and translation equivariance and can be used to smooth the details in an image, they
171   are insufficient for decomposing an image into piecewise smooth illumination. In contrast, the TV
172   optimization can be applied to preserve the image edges, as illustrated in Fig.11 in Appendix A.2.
173   Hence, if the edge-preserving smoothness properties of TV optimization can be incorporated into a
174   single convolutional layer, we do not need a deep network for illumination estimation.
175   How to incorporate the edge-preserving properties into a single convolutional layer? Total
176   variation (TV) is an effective regularizer that has been widely used as a smoothness regularization
177   term in denoising. Considering an input image X and output Y ∈ RM ×N , the TV optimization can
178   be defined as:

      5    C ONCLUSION
526
527
      In this paper, we have introduced RetinexGDP, a zero-shot PLIE model that combines a generative
528   pretrained diffusion model with domain knowledge from Retinex theory. RetinexGDP enables flex-
529   ible enhancement guided by user preferences specified via text instructions, obviating the necessity
530   for additional training data or external images. We incorporate TV optimization into a single Gaus-
531   sian covolutional layer for zero-shot illumination estimation, streamlining the pipeline of Retinex
532   decomposition. To maintain the content and structure consistency, we employ patch-wise DDIM
533   inversion to find the initial noise vector of corrected reflectance and perform sampling conditioned
534   on the correced reflectance. While our zero-shot, training-free PLIE method may not outperform
535   state-of-the-art models across all datasets, it delivers competitive results. Its key strength lies in the
536
      ability to flexibly customize enhancement styles through text prompts, offering a unique and adapt-
      able solution. This novel feature brings valuable flexibility to low-light enhancement, deserving
537
      further attention from the research community.
538
539   Limitations: RetinexGDP has limitation in real-time enhancement due to the inversion process.
      This limitation may be solved by inversion-free method Xu et al. (2024).

      A.3     PATCH - WISE DDIM I NVERSION
831
832
      The patch-wise DDIM inversion process is outlined in Alg.1. The DDIM inversion takes correct
833
      reflectance R as input and produces the corresponding initial noise vector R̂. The corrected re-
834   flectance R is divided into M overlapping patches. These patches are cropped with a stride of p,
835   ensuring that some areas of the image are covered multiple times.
836
837
      For each patch, a noised intermediate result is obtained. At each time step t, the diffusion model
      calculates the mean µm and variance Σm of the Gaussian noise for each patch.
838
839   The mean and variance for each patch are iteratively updated to reflect the overall values for the
840   entire image.
841   Since patches overlap, the areas covered multiple times must be tracked. A binary patch mask Pm
842   identifies overlapping areas, and a weight vector G records how many times each pixel in these
843   areas is included in a patch. The update follows: G = G + Pm , where m indicates the m-th patch.
844
      The final mean Ωt and variance φt for the entire image at each time step are computed by taking the
845
      weighted average, adjusting for overlapping areas:
846
847                                      Ωt = Ωt     G,     φt = φt   G                                (15)
848
849   where     denotes element-wise division.
850   This weighted averaging ensures that the contributions from overlapping patches are aggregated,
851   maintaining the consistency of the image’s structure and texture throughout the diffusion process.

856
857   Initial Noise Vector. The initial noise vector R̂, generated via DDIM inversion, is used as the

859   Gaussian noise, this approach integrates the corrected reflectance R as a condition to ensure that the
