                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010              This paper introduces Domain Shift Tuning (DST), a novel framework designed
011
                 to guide pre-trained language models (PLMs), including Large Language Models
                 (LLMs), in overcoming domain discrepancies (i.e., source-target). PLMs, pre-
012
                 trained on extensive and diverse corpora, the source domain, often encounter do-
013
                 main gaps after fine-tuning over the target domain. Unlike conventional adapters
014              or Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods, DST conceptualizes domain
015              gaps as differences in knowledge encapsulated within multiple subnetworks of
016              PLMs. To bridge this gap, our challenge is to find a subnetwork set that corre-
017              sponds to these pieces of knowledge and their weight. This direction leads DST
018              to employ a lightweight subnetwork, the Knowledge Steering Layer (KSL), and a
019              training objective, Knowledge Distribution Modeling (KDM). These components
020              enable DST to fine-tune PLMs by aligning the knowledge weights of the source
021              domain with those of the target domain. Experimental results on diverse datasets
022
                 demonstrate that DST effectively mitigates the domain gap, allowing PLMs to
                 generate text that closely aligns with even a small target corpus, thereby signifi-
023
                 cantly enhancing domain adaptation for PLMs at lower computational cost.

      1       INTRODUCTION
028
029   PLMs, including LLMs, have demonstrated a powerful capability to generate high-quality text.
030   However, their effectiveness is often limited by the size of the target corpus, which is typically
031
      much smaller than the source corpora used for training PLMs. For instance, the popular pre-training
      datasets of Giga5en Parker et al. (2011), and ClueWeb 2012-B1 occupy 16G, and 25TB, respec-
032
      tively. This size discrepancy can lead to catastrophic forgetting and poor generalization Lin et al.
033
      (2023), especially when all weights of the PLMs are fine-tuned. Given the swift diversification of
034   PLM applications, techniques are needed that can effectively achieve domain adaptation Malik et al.
035   (2023); Diao et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024) or PEFTs Hu et al. (2022); Dettmers et al. (2023); Xu
036   et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2024).
037
      In response to this need, we propose a model-agnostic adaptation framework, Domain Shift Tuning
038
      (DST), to tune PLMs toward the target domain. DST is based on the idea that PLMs encapsulate
039
      multiple pieces of knowledge as subnetworks, with each domain represented as weights over these
040   subnetworks. The domain gap is thus represented as the difference in weights over these subnet-
041   works, specifically those unique to the source and target domains. To find knowledge-equivalent sub-
042   networks in the PLM and infer their weights, DST introduces a lightweight subnetwork, the Knowl-
043   edge Steering Layer (KSL), and a training objective, Knowledge Distribution Modeling (KDM).
044   Unlike other adapters Wang et al. (2022) and PEFTs, DST’s novelty lies in associating domains
045   and PLMs using knowledge and tuning the PLM by finding knowledge-equivalent networks and
046   adjusting their weights.
047   Our experiments confirm the effectiveness of DST, demonstrating its theoretical and practical con-
048   tributions;
049   Theoretical: KSL provides a differentiable access mechanism to represent domain knowledge as
050   weights over multiple subnetworks of the PLM and fine-tune the PLM by adjusting this weight.
051   Practical: The framework’s model-agnostic nature allows it to be applied to various PLMs at lower
052   computational cost, enhancing its versatility and compatibility with other adapters and PEFTs.

      2     PREVIOUS WORK
055
056
057   Transformer Vaswani et al. (2017) based PLMs Devlin et al. (2019); Radford et al. (2019); Yang
058   et al. (2019); Liu et al. (2019); Lan et al. (2020) have made significant strides in Natural Language
059   Processing (NLP) tasks, excelling at exploring local token relationships over global semantics Wang
060
      et al. (2020). However, they face challenges in adapting to tasks that require domain shift using
      topics. This paper introduces DST, a novel approach to address these challenges. PLMs, including
061
      BertSUM Wang et al. (2020) and UNIfied pre-trained Language Model Dong et al. (2019), have
062
      shown promise in various NLP tasks. Despite their success, these models struggle to capture explicit
063   document semantics as effectively as topic models Wang et al. (2020). DST aims to bridge this gap
064   by adapting PLMs to tasks requiring domain shift using topics.
065
066
      Continual pretraining Gururangan & et al (2020) has demonstrated the benefits of optimizing a PLM
      to a target domain before further fine-tuning. UDALM Karouzos et al. (2021) and AdaPrompt Chen
067
      et al. (2022) follow a similar approach, training PLMs on the target domain and then training a target
068
      classifier with source domain labeled data. Ke et al. (2022) decouple global and domain-specific
069   knowledge through continual pre-training on a domain-specific corpus. DST separes PLMs into
070   sub-networks using latent discrete variables, each representing global or local knowledge.
071
072
      To mitigate catastrophic forgetting, PEFT methods have been introduced to keep most of the PLM
      weights frozen. While AdaMix Wang et al. (2022) can leverage a mixture of adapters like Houlsby
073
      et al. Houlsby et al. (2019), a mixture of low rank decomposition matrices like LoRA Hu et al.
074
      (2022) and a minimal additional parameters like (IA)3 Liu et al. (2022) to improve downstream
075   task performance while keeping most of the PLM weights frozen. While PEFT methods focus on
076   reducing the number of fine-tuning parameters and memory usage, DST focuses on bridging the
077   domain gap by adjusting subnetwork weights within PLMs.
078
      Topic models Blei et al. (2003); Wang et al. (2020) and their extensions Dieng et al. (2016); Jo
079
      et al. (2017) take a global statistical view and look at the word distributions of topics across a given
080
      corpus. Although these models organize a given corpus into small sets of prominent topics and have
081   been proven to be powerful tools for uncovering latent structure, they and their application Chang
082   et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2018; 2020) are not, in the strict sense, sequence models. Xu et al. (2024)
083   introduces Energy-Based Concept Bottleneck Models as a unified framework for concept-based
084   prediction, concept correction, and fine-grained interpretations based on conditional probabilities.
085
      Like other PEFT, DST updates only the weights over subnetworks on the top of PLM rather than
086
      overwriting entire parameters. As our introduced lightweight subnetwork is placed in a different
087   location than the other adapters, DST can be compatible with training strategies, adaptation modules,
088   prompt tuning Lester et al. (2021), or continuous prompt models Li & Liang (2021); Liu et al.
089   (2021); Zhang et al. (2022).

096
097   DST aims to bridge the domain gap by leveraging disparities between global (e.g., domain-agnostic
098   linguistic elements) and local (e.g., domain-specific semantic elements) knowledge, as depicted in
099
      Figure 1(left), automatically encapsulate these disparities about a given target dataset. The under-
      lying hypothesis is that each piece of knowledge is embedded within a subnetwork of PLMs, akin
100
      to the lottery ticket hypothesis Frankle & Carbin (2019). Here, knowledge is considered a latent
101
      and relative concept, not as concretely defined as topics in topic models. PLMs encode knowledge
102   as token-level embeddings and subnetworks, referred to as latent knowledge. As these knowledge
103   disparities are relative and corpus-dependent, it is difficult to show a clear definition. Subnetwork
104   weights include both the original PLM network weights and newly added network weights. While
105   the PLM parameters remain frozen, the newly introduced parameters are updated to adjust these
106   weights. PLMs, which are neural language models tailored for specific tasks such as text gener-
107   ation Bengio et al. (2003), encapsulate both global and local knowledge across source and target
      domains. Given a text sequence xd = {xd,1 , · · ·, xd,|xd | } and dataset D = {x1 , · · ·, xD }, PLMs are

159   3.2   A RCHITECTURE DESIGN
160
161   Motivated by previous works Li et al. (2018); Houlsby et al. (2019); Aghajanyan et al. (2021),
      LoRA Hu et al. (2022) injects trainable rank decomposition matrices into each layer of the Trans-


                                                              3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      former architecture. Unlike LoRA, DST focuses on both the risks of overfitting and the global-local
163   knowledge differences rather than exploring the lower intrinsic dimension of the source domain
164   knowledge. Ramasesh et al. (2021) pointed out that catastrophic forgetting occurs mainly in the
165   higher layers. It is often observed that the learned attentive patterns from many heads are not as
166   reasonable as we expect Michel et al. (2019), and we might obtain this global information from the
167   upper blocks by increasing the number of transformer blocks Dosovitskiy et al. (2021); unfortu-
168   nately, as the transformer architecture requires a large number of parameters, its computational cost
169   is very high. As shown in Figure 1(center), DST places a Knowledge Steering Layer (KSL) on the
170   top of the Transformer layer and updates only its related parameters to avoid catastrophic forgetting.
171
      This does not break any PLM structure and allows the reuse of PLMs and their parameters. As 1)
      knowledge describes a co-occurrence pattern of tokens with similar semantics, and 2) the differences
172
      between the pre-training and the fine-tuning datasets are not only in the knowledge itself but also
173
      in the ratio of knowledge, we develop a training task, Knowledge Distribution Modeling (KDM), to
174   align knowledge to each text. Since global distributions do not require additional learning, DST is
175   designed to find target-specific distributions through knowledge, and update them, Pθ (zt |xd,1:t−1 )
176   and Pθ (xd,t |zt , xd,1:t−1 ), in fine-tuning. As shown in Eq (2) and Figure 1(right), This design enables
177   PLMs to interpret Pθ (zt |xd,1:t−1 ) as the distribution over knowledge and Pθ (xd,t |zt , xd,1:t−1 ) as the
178   distribution over next tokens, and emphasize local knowledge that might otherwise be buried, thus
179   preventing catastrophic forgetting.

      4.1   K NOWLEDGE D ISTRIBUTION M ODELING (KDM)
245
246   Inspired by the principles of contrastive learning Khosla et al. (2020) and triplet loss, the KDM is
247   designed to minimize discrepancies between texts at both the knowledge and hidden representation
248   levels, and use the distance between text pairs within the same batch. Models based on the Trans-
249   former encoder, such as BERT, employ a special token, [CLS], to encode an entire input and derive
250   its representation. However, models based on the Transformer decoder do not have an equivalent to-
251   ken. To address this, we append [CLS] to the end of each input text for Transformer-decoder-based
252
      PLMs, as illustrated in Figure 1(center). This modification allows these PLMs to learn representa-
      tions directly through z at the input-level representation, thereby obtaining a knowledge distribution
253
      zd ∈ RK specific to the d-th text on the KSL. Given that texts with similar content are likely to share
254
      similar knowledge distributions, we can define the similarities between texts SIMz ∈ RB×B using
255   z∗ , where B represents each batch. As depicted in Figure (1)(center), T IDd is the final output of
256   the last token of the d-th input text sequence and serves to represent each text, much like [CLS] in
257   BERT. We define another text similarity using T ID∗ as SIMT ID ∈ RB×B . Mathematically, this
258   objective minimizes the following loss function:

292   5.1     DATASETS AND E XPERIMENT DESIGN
293
294   Datasets We conducted evaluations using The New York Times annotated corpus (NYT)2 , Amazon
295   review3 , and arXiv Dataset4 , as they are large publicly available datasets and can be manually evalu-
296   ated by screened colleagues. The experiments focus on review texts (Amazon), news articles (NYT),
297   and scientific articles (arXiv). Although including a broader range of domains, such as social me-
298
      dia and legal texts, would better demonstrate DST’s generalizability, we select these datasets since
      the resulting data size is computationally feasible on a general-purpose server, includes a variety of
299
      topics that are different from the pre-training corpus, meets the public reproducibility requirement,
300
      and can validate our insight that a small corpus can provide significant benefits Gururangan & et al
301   (2020), and ease of evaluation by a consistent set of reviewers. Each record in the reviews contains
302   a review text, review title, star rating, anonymized ID, and coarse-grained product category, we use
303   only review texts. All reviews were truncated after 2,000 characters, and all reviews were at least
304   50 characters long. Among the languages present, we used only English for ease of interpreting the
305   results. We used 90%, 5%, and 5% of each data set as training, validation, and test sets, respectively.
306
      Experiment Setup We implemented DST by using Pytorch 2.35 and will release this code soon.
307   We set ϵ in Eq (6) to 0.2, and λKDM in Eq (7) to 0.5. As the average length of each text used in
308   fine-tuning the data set is around 60, we set the maximum input sequence length to 64. Note that the
309   ground truth texts were excluded from the training/validation data to prevent information leakage.
310   DST uses GPT-2 medium and large (GPT-2) as the PLM, and, BLOOM6 and Meta-Llama-3-8B
311   (Llama-3) AI@Meta (2024)7 as the LLM. Following the training setup, we used Adam with β1 =
312   0.9, and β2 = 0.999 was used for optimization, over mini-batches to update parameters; the dropout
313   strategy Srivastava et al. (2014) was adopted for network optimization. The learning rate was 3e-5,
314   with linear warm up over the first 500 steps and linear decay, where we set the dropout rate, the
315
      weight decay, and the batch size to 0.1, 0.01, and 256, respectively. We conducted all models on 8
      Nvidia Tesla V100 GPUs with 256G memory.

491   6   DISCUSSION AND LIMITATION
492
493   DST introduces z as an indicator for knowledge equivalent subnetworks through KSL and KDM.
494   DST performs domain shift by recalculating Pθ (xd,t |zt , xd,1:t−1 ) and aligning Pθ (zt |xd,1:t−1 ) with
495   the target domain, as shown in Eq (2) and Figure 1. This mechanism ensures that DST differs from
496   the existing topic model and its extensions Wang et al. (2020); Dieng et al.; Chang et al. (2021);
497   Kawamae (2021); Grootendorst (2022); Meng et al. (2022) in understanding the difference between
498   the source (e.g., training corpus) and the target, and in supporting additional learning. Our approach
499   can be applied to both encoder-only (BERTopic) and decoder-only (GPT-2, BLOOM, Llama-3)
500   architectures, demonstrating its versatility and effectiveness, as shown in Tables 2, 3, and 4.
501   While PEFT methods are known for their data efficiency, DST aims to further improve this by
502   incorporating knowledge and providing additional context and structure to the data. By using this
503   concept to identify and overlay relevant knowledge, DST can enhance the capabilities of PLMs
504   without requiring extensive retraining. Section 6 and Table 3 indicate that DST, like other PEFT
505   methods, allows fine-tuning while keeping the PLM frozen. The additional parameters introduced
506   by DST (WZ ∈ Rdh ×K , K × Waz ∈ Rdh ×dh , and K × bz ∈ Rdh ) result in a total of 5,906,688
507
      parameters for dh = 768 and K = 10. This is significantly fewer than the 345 million parameters
      of GPT-2 medium and comparable to LoRA and other PEFT methods. However, as K increases,
508
      the computation time also increases due to the need for calculations for each knowledge, z. This
509
      overhead is managed by parallel processing for different z, as shown in Table 3.
510
511   The knowledge in DST is designed to capture subnetworks, similar to how dropout in deep learning
512   helps prevent overfitting by randomly omitting units during training. This approach ensures that
513
      the model does not rely too heavily on any single feature, thereby enhancing its generalization
      capabilities. By treating z as quantized samples of token distributions, DST can dynamically adjust
514
      to different contexts, improving the model’s adaptability and performance.
515
516   DST excels when there is a significant difference between the source and target domains. When
517   the target data is similar to the source data, the benefits of DST are less pronounced. By aligning
518   knowledge distributions, DST can effectively adapt the model to new domains without catastrophic
519
      forgetting. This is particularly useful in low-resource settings where fine-tuning large PLMs on
      limited data can lead to overfitting. However, in cases where the target domain closely resembles
520
      the source domain, the knowledge distributions may not differ significantly, reducing the impact of
521
      the Knowledge Steering Layer. Additionally, if the target data is already well-represented in the
522   source data, the benefits of DST’s dynamic adjustment may be minimal.
523
524   The latent nature of knowledge in DST, while beneficial for capturing complex patterns, poses chal-
525
      lenges for interpretability. Future work could explore methods to enhance the interpretability of
      these latent variables. DST mitigates the domain gap by highlighting the target-specific token distri-
526
      butions through knowledge and updating only these distributions, even if K is small. Methods like
527
      variational Bayes and Dirichlet processes can determine the optimal number of z but are computa-
528   tionally intensive. Therefore, the current study focuses on demonstrating the effectiveness of DST,
529   with automatic determination of K as a future research direction.

      7   CONCLUSION
534
535   The paper proposes a PLM tuning framework, DST, that reflects the target domain knowledge in
536   NLP tasks. Unlike other adapters or PEFT, DST places a lightweight network, KSL, on just the
537   top of PLM and fine-tunes it via KDM. The novelty of DST lies in 1) focusing on the domain gap,
538   2) representing this gap with subnetwork weights over domains, and 3) guiding PLMs towards the
539   target domain. Experiments showed that both KSL and KDM enable DST to allow PLMs to generate
      valid texts that well reflect even small target data sets.
