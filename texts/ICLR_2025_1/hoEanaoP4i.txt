011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Many studies have shown that evaluating the linear separability of hidden-layer
014            outputs plays a key role in understanding the working mechanism of deep networks.
015            However, it is still challenging to develop the linear separability measure (LSM)
016
               that satisfies all of the following requirements: 1) it should be an absolute measure;
               2) it should be insensitive to the outliers; and 3) its computational cost should
017
               be low for real-time monitoring the behavior of each hidden layer. In this paper,
018
               we propose the Minkowski difference-based linear separability measures (MD-
019            LSMs) that just meet the first two requirements. Moreover, we also introduce an
020            approximate calculation method to significantly decrease their computation costs
021            with only a slight precision sacrifice. As an application example, we conduct the
022            experiments on the real-time monitoring for the hidden-layer behaviors of several
023            popular deep networks, and show that the outputs of the hidden layers adjacent
024            to the output layer have higher linear separability degrees. We also observe that
025            the change of linear separability degree of hidden layers (especially the ones are
026            adjacent to the output layers) are in sync with the change of the training accuracy
027
               of the entire network. It implies that the linear separability of some important
               hidden layers can be treated as a performance criterion to characterize the network’s
028
               training behavior. The relevant theoretical discussion also validates this finding.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Two point sets are said to be linearly separable if they can be correctly separated by a hyperplane.
034   The concept of linear separability plays an important role in measuring the capability of neural
035
      networks (Tajine & Elizondo, 2002; Elizondo, 2004; Elizondo et al., 2010). In the literature, there
      are two main research issues on the linear separability of a neural network, which trended to be
036
      considered as an entire function: 1) whether the current network can achieve all dichotomies, i.e., the
037
      mapping capability (Hornik et al., 1989); and 2) how many dichotomies can be recorded by a network
038   with the specific structure, i.e., the memory capability (Cover, 1965). Since neural networks are of
039   multiple-hidden layer stacking structures, the network outputs are produced by the composition of
040   multiple pseudo-linear maps, each of which corresponds to one hidden layer (Vershynin, 2020). It
041   could be hard to infer the working mechanism of a deep network by treating it as an entire function
042   rather than by analyzing the behavior of each hidden layer.
043   Consider a feed-forward network net(·) : RN → {0, 1} with N input node and L hidden layers.
044   Denote the l-th hidden layer as hidl (·) and let hidl (X ) be the set of hidden-layer outputs w.r.t. the
045   input set X := {xm }M  m=1 . Set Vl as the weights of the l-th hidden layer (1 ≤ l ≤ L), and let w be
046
      the weights of the output layer. Denote Vl′ (1 ≤ l ≤ L) and w′ as the updated weights provided by a
047
      training algorithm implemented on the training set S = {(xm , ym )}M      m=1 . The updated network is
      denoted as net′ (·) with updated weights V1′ , · · · , VL

      This proposition demonstrates that there exists the synchronicity between the linear separability
055   degree of the L-th hidden-layer outputs and the training performance during the process of training a
056   network, i.e., the change of linear separability degree of the L-th hidden-layer outputs is in sync with
057   that of the training accuracy. Although this result cannot explicitly exhibit the relationship between
058   the linear separability degree of the l-th hidden-layer outputs (l < L) and the training accuracy,
059   the L-th hidden-layer outputs actually are determined by the weights V1′ , · · · , VL−1′
                                                                                                 , which also
060   influence the linear separability degree of the relevant hidden-layer outputs. Therefore, the linear
061   separability can be applied to analyze the mapping behaviors of hidden layers and then to understand
062   the working mechanism of deep networks.
063
      In recent years, some pioneering works have been aware of the importance of analyzing deep networks
      via the layer-wise changes of class separability when they pass through the networks (Schilling et al.,
064
      2021; Apicella et al., 2024; Pezzotti et al., 2017; Rauber et al., 2016; Alain & Bengio, 2016; Ben-
065
      Shaul & Dekel, 2022; He & Su, 2023; Rangamani et al., 2023). One common opinion of these works
066   is that the linear separability degree of hidden-layer outputs should become layer-wisely stronger if a
067   deep network has been (or is being) trained suitably. Some empirical evidences were also provided to
068   demonstrate this fact. Therefore, the linear separability provides a feasible manner to analyze the
069   mapping behaviors of hidden layers and then to understand the working mechanism of deep networks.
070   Accordingly, a desired linear separability measure (LSM) should meet the following requirements:
071
072   (1) (Efficiency) It should have a low computational cost, because we would like to layer-wisely
073
          examine the linear separability degree of the hidden-layer outputs after each weight-update epoch
          during the entire training process;
074
075   (2) (Robustness) It should be insensitive to the outliers, because the stochastic gradient descent
076       methods sometimes cause abnormal hidden-layer outputs;
077   (3) (Absoluteness) It should be an absolute measure, which objectively evaluates the degree of
078       linear separability between two sets. If its value is known, one can directly judge whether two
079       sets are linearly separable or how heavy they are overlapped. In contrast, the relative measure
080       only indicates whether the linear separability between the two current sets becomes stronger
081
          (or weaker) than that of the two sets before being transformed. Therefore, it is hard to describe
          the linear separability of two sets based only on its value. Please refer to Remark 2.8 for an
082
          illustration.

      Some mathematical terms appearing in the existing works actually can be treated as LSMs of two
      point sets, e.g., the generalized Rayleigh quotient (GRQ) of linear discriminant analysis (LDA),
087
      which is a relative measure:

091   and the sum of slack variables (SSV), which is an absolute measure, in linear support vector machine
092   (L-SVM) with soft margin. Moreover, since Jω is based on the mean of the point set, it is sensitive
093
      to the outliers in the set. Because of the eigenvalue decomposition, the calculation of Jω could
      be time-consuming especially when the dimension is high. He & Su (2023) introduced the term
094
095
      tr(Sw S†b ), a variant of GRQ, to measure the linear separability degree of hidden-layer outputs, but it
096   is a relative measure and the calculation of the Moore-Penrose inverse S†b is time-consuming as well.
097   Similarly, since L-SVM is expressed as a quadratic programming problem, the calculation of SSV
098
      sometimes brings a high computational burden when the sample size is large.
099   Additionally, Ben-Israel & Levin (2006) introduced the linear divisible angle to measure the linear
100   separability degree of two point sets, where the labels of the data are treated as a new attribute to
101   convert the dimension of points from N to N + 1, and then LDA is used to compute the GRQ of the
102   converted points. Gabidullina (2013) adopted the smallest thickness of the classified hyperplane as
103   the LSM for the linearly inseparable sets. Since this measure is computed via a minimax optimization
104
      problem, its computational cost is high.
105   By incorporating the intra-class and the inter-class distances, Schilling et al. (2021) introduced the
106   generalized discrimination value (GDV) to measure the class separability among the hidden-layer
107   outputs associated with different labels during the training process. By tracking the behavior of MLP’s
      hidden layers in each training epoch, they detected the synchronicity between the class separability,

108
      measured by GDV, of hidden-layer outputs and the training performance. Since the computation of
109   GDV is time-consuming, for the relatively complicated networks (such as CNN, ResNet, VGG and
110   Inception), they only computed the GDVs of hidden-layer outputs of the trained networks, and then
111   made some statistical analysis between the resultant GDVs and the layer number in order to explore
112   the alteration rule of the class separability of the outputs from different hidden layers.
113
      Apicella et al. (2024) introduced a structural manner to detect the behavior of hidden-layer outputs
114
      during the training phase. Specifically, they imposed an auxiliary output layer, called hidden
115   classification layer, into each hidden layer and then combined the loss function of each auxiliary
116   output layer with the loss of the main network to form an entire training objective function. Their
117   experiments have shown some interesting phenomena: 1) the introduction of hidden classification
118   layer can enhance the class separability, measured by GDV, of the corresponding hidden-layer outputs;
119   and 2) when the class separability of each hidden layer increases, the main network gains a higher
120   testing performance. However, it is still challenging to explain them, which also motivates this paper.
121   Alain & Bengio (2016) used hidden-layer outputs to train a linear classifier, called "probe", and its
122   classification performance is regarded as a measure of the linear separability degree of the hidden
123   layer. Some state-of-the-art classifiers (such as logistic regression or naive Bayes) have the potential
124   to act as feasible "probes" because of their low desired computational complexities. However, if there
125   is no priori knowledge on the data distribution, the efficiency and the performance of these classifiers
126   could be heavily influenced by some unavoidable factors such as the choice of hyperparameters
127   and the setting of termination conditions, and thus their desired complexities are usually hard to be
128   achieved in practice. Consequently, the "probe" method is unsuitable (at least cannot be directly
129
      applied) to detecting the mapping behavior of each hidden layer after each training epoch. How to
      develop an efficient tool for real-time monitoring the status of each hidden layer during the entire
130
      training process becomes the main research concern of this paper.
131
132   In addition, there are also other works applying the concept of linear separability to study the
133   properties of deep networks, such as the fold of the data manifold in the high-dimensional space via
134   hidden layers of deep networks (Keup & Helias, 2022), and the trade-offs between the representation
135
      ability and the depth-size of deep neural networks with rectified linear units (ReLUs) (Arora et al.,
      2016).

      1.2   OVERVIEW OF M AIN R ESULTS
152   In this paper, we mainly concern with two issues: one is how to develop the LSM that satisfies
153   the aforementioned requirements of efficiency, robustness and absoluteness; and the other is what
154   behaviors can be captured via the real-time monitoring for the linear separability of hidden-layer
155   outputs.
156   First, we introduce Minkowski difference-based LSM (MD-LSM) for evaluating the linear separability
157   degree of hidden-layer outputs. They are absolute measures and insensitive to the outliers. Since
158   their original forms are hard to calculate, we then design an efficient approximation manner whose
159   computational cost is low. The comparative experiments are conducted to demonstrate that the values
160   of the original MD-LSMs slightly differ from those provided by the approximation manner. In
161   Table 1, we make the comparison between the existing LSMs and the proposed MD-LSMs from the
      viewpoint of whether they meet the requirements of efficiency, robustness and absoluteness.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      As an application example, we use the proposed MD-LSMs for real-time monitoring the hidden-
163   layer behaviors of some popular deep networks during their entire training processes, including
164   multilayer perceptron (MLP) (Bishop, 1994), graph neural network (GNN) (Kipf & Welling, 2016),
165   convolutional neural network (CNN) (LeCun et al., 1998), ResNet (He et al., 2016), VGGNet
166   (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014), AlexNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2017), vision transformer (ViT) (Doso-
167   vitskiy et al., 2020) and GoogLeNet (Szegedy et al., 2015). We verify the effectiveness of MD-LSMs
168   on several real-world datasets including the UCI datasets and the text datasets. We calculate the linear
169   separability of each hidden layer after each training epoch, and observe that when the training sample
170   set passes through a deep network, the outputs of the hidden layers that are closer to the output layer
171
      have higher linear separability degrees. This finding not only accords with the common opinion of
      recent works but also empirically answers the question of why deep networks need a large number of
172
      hidden layers. Since one finite-width hidden layer equipped with the usual activation functions (e.g.,
173
      sigmoid, tanh and ReLU) has limited nonlinear mapping capability, the linear separability degree of
174   its outputs could be slightly higher than that of its inputs. For complicated classification tasks, the
175   composition of multiple hidden layers gradually increases the linear separability degree of outputs of
176   each hidden layer. In this manner, the desired classification accuracy can be achieved.
177
      Moreover, we also find that the changes of linear separability of hidden layers (especially the ones
178
      adjacent to the output layer) are in sync with the changes of the training accuracy. This finding
179
      implies that detecting linear separability of some important hidden layers potentially becomes a
180   reasonable manner of characterizing the real-time training behavior of the entire network. A relevant
181   theoretical discussion is given to validate this finding as well.
182

      2.1 M INKOWSKI D IFFERENCE AND M AXIMUM L INEARLY S EPARABLE S UBSET
194
195   The concept of Minkowski difference (MD) has been widely used in many fields such as data
196
      classification (Mampaey et al., 2012; Takeda et al., 2013) and collision detection (Ericson, 2004).
197   Definition 2.1 (Minkowski Difference). Let A = {a1 , · · · , aI } ⊂ RN and B = {b1 , · · · , bJ } ⊂
198   RN be two point sets. Then, the Minkowski difference between them is defined as
199                     MD(A, B) := mij := ai − bj ∈ RN | ai ∈ A, bj ∈ B .

200
201   Based on Minkowski difference, we convert the linear separability of two point sets into the relative
202   position relationship between a point set and a hyperplane that passes the origin (cf. Fig. 1).
203   Theorem 2.2. Two points sets A, B ⊂ RN are linearly separable if and only if there exists a vector
204   ω ∈ RN such that all points of MD(A, B) locate in one side of the hyperplane ω T m = 0, m ∈ RN .
205
206
      As shown in the proof of this theorem (cf. Appendix D.1), given two linearly separable sets A and B,
      the normal vector ω of any hyperplane that separates the two sets is the one mentioned in the theorem.
207
      Additionally, if the two sets A and B are linearly inseparable, some points of MD(A, B) will lie in
208
      one side of the hyperplane and the rest lie in the other side (cf. Fig. 1):
209
210
      Definition 2.3 (Minor and Major Sides). Given a hyperplane ω T m = 0, if more than half points of
      MD(A, B) lie in one side of ω T m = 0, then this side is said to be the major side of the hyperplane;
211
      and accordingly, the other side of ω T m = 0 is said to be the minor side of the hyperplane.
212
213   Furthermore, we denote majorω (MD(A, B)) (resp. minorω (MD(A, B))) as the subset of
214   MD(A, B) that lies in the major (resp. minor) side of ω T m = 0 (cf. Fig. 1). According to
215   Theorem 2.2, the points mij ∈ minorω (MD(A, B)) can be eliminated by removing the relevant
      points ai from A or bj from B, and the rests turn out to be linearly separable.

      In this section, we conduct the numerical experiments to illustrate the application of the proposed
      MD-LSMs for real-time monitoring hidden-layer behaviors of several popular deep networks. All
405
      experiments are processed in the DELL® PowerEdge® T640 Tower Server with two Intel® Xeon®
406
      20-core processors, 128 GB RAM and a NVIDIA® Tesla® V100 32GB GPU.
407
408   3.1    E XPERIMENT S ETTING
409   Two classes (airplane and automobile) in CIFAR-10 dataset (Krizhevsky, 2012) are selected to form
410   the binary classification task. The SGD method with minibatch is used to update the network weights
411   within 100 training epochs. Since the structures of MLP and CNN are not powerful enough to obtain
412   a good training performance by using all samples of the two classes within the limited epochs, we
413   randomly select 2000 (resp. 1000) samples from the training (resp. testing) data of the two classes
414
      for training (resp. testing) them. Moreover, since their network sizes are not large, we directly use the
      selected 2000 training samples to compute the MD-LSMs of their hidden-layer outputs. In contrast,
415
      we use all training (resp. testing) data of the two classes to train (resp. test) ResNet, VGGNet,
416
      AlexNet, ViT and GoogLeNet. Since the dimension of hidden-layer outputs of these deep networks
417   is high, in view of the computational burden, we randomly select 500 samples from the two classes
418   to compute MD-LSMs for these networks after each training epoch. In addition, we verify the
419   effectiveness of MD-LSMs on the MLP for solving the binary-classification tasks of the UCI datasets
420   (including Diagnostic, Ionosphere, Maintenance and Marketing) (cf. Appendix E.1). We also explore
421   the hidden-layer behaviors of the networks for solving text classification tasks, for example, the MLP
422   and the CNN for the IMDB dataset (cf. Appendix E.2), and the graph neural network (GNN) for the
423   Cora dataset (Kipf & Welling, 2016) (cf. Appendix E.4).

452   each hidden layer during the entire training process rather than the post analysis of the hidden-layer

454   hidden layers closer to the output layer can provide higher linear separability, but also demonstrate
455   that the linear separability of the hidden layers adjacent to the input layers will remain unchanged (or
456   even decrease) in the middle and late stages of the training processes. The latter finding also suggests
457   that the early stopping of training these hidden layers should be beneficial to improving the training
458   performance. In addition, there also arises another interesting phenomenon that the accuracy change

      adjacent to the output layer). This finding implies that the real-time monitoring for linear separability
460
      of individual hidden layers potentially becomes a reasonable manner of characterizing the entire

      network’s dynamical behavior instead of only focusing on the training performance evaluated by
462   using the network’s outputs during the training process.

      4    C ONCLUSION
465
466   Because of multi-layer composite structures, it could be hard to directly analyze the properties of
467   deep networks via the backward inference from the behavior of their outputs. Instead, analyzing
468   the linear separability of hidden-layer outputs becomes a feasible way of understanding the deep
469   networks. However, it is still challenge to develop the LSMs that meet the requirements of robustness,
470
      absoluteness, and efficiency. In this paper, we propose the MD-LSMs LSi (i = ∗, 0, 1), which meet
      the first two requirements, and then derive their approximations LSc i (i = ∗, 0, 1), which meets all of
471
472
      the three requirements. The comparative experiments demonstrate that there is only a slight difference
      between LSi and LS c i (i = ∗, 0, 1).
473
474   Benefited from the low cost of calculating LS  c i (i = ∗, 0, 1), MD-LSMs actually provide a hidden-
475   layer based manner of real-time monitoring the network performance instead of the traditional
476   backward inference from the errors caused by the network outputs. As an application example, we
477
      conduct the experiments on the real-time monitoring for the linear separability of each hidden layer
      after each training epoch of some popular deep networks on different kinds of datasets including the
478
      synthetic datasets, the image dataset (i.e., CIFAR-10), the UCI datasets (i.e., Diagnostic, Ionosphere,
479
      Maintenance and Marketing), and the text datasets (i.e., IMDB and Cora). First, we demonstrate
480   that when a training sample set passes through a training or trained network, its linear separability
481   degree gradually increases layer-by-layer and the hidden layers that are closer to the output layer will
482   bring higher linear separability degrees. This facts explains why deep networks need a large number
483   of hidden layers. Since one finite-width hidden layer equipped with the usual activation functions
484   (such as sigmoid, tanh or ReLU) only has a limited nonlinear mapping capability, and thus slightly
485   increases the linear separability degree of its inputs. Alternatively, the composition of multiple
      hidden layers is a feasible way of layer-wisely increasing network nonlinear mapping capability with


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      acceptable training difficulty. In addition, we find that there exists the synchronicity between the
487   linear separability of hidden layers and the training accuracy in the classification tasks. There is also
488   a theoretical discussion on such a synchronicity phenomenon. This finding implies that the linear
489   separability potentially becomes an applicable tool of layer-wisely exploring the characteristics of
490   deep networks.
491   The main limitations of this paper lie in the following aspects: 1) There still exists a gap between the
492   setting of Proposition 1.1 and the gradient descent training. 2) When the class number S is large, it is
493   time-consuming to calculate MultiLSi (i = ∗, 0, 1, 2) in the OvR way. 3) This paper only focuses
494   on the classification tasks. Our future works will overcome these limitations. Since the quadratic
495   version LS2 is of a well-defined mathematical form, it is potentially used to theoretically analyze
496   the relationship between the network generalization capability and the network structural parameters
497   such as activation functions and network sizes. In addition MD-LSMs can be treated as the criteria
498
      for evaluating the mapping capability of each hidden layer, and thus potentially contribute to achieve
      the explainable network architecture design or pruning. Since the high-dimensional vectors appearing
499
      in the expressions of MD-LSMs are of the inner-product form, we will introduce the kernel trick into
500
      them and then develop the tools of evaluating the degree of non-linear separability between two sets.

      The maximum linearly-separable subset MaxLS(A, B) of two sets A and B can be obtained in an
651   iterative way:
652
653   (1) Build an undirected bipartite graph G(V0 , E0 ) with
654
655
                          V0 = {All points in A and B associated with minorω (MD(A, B))},
656          and
657                       E0 = {The connected relation of each point in minorω (MD(A, B))}.
658
             For example, the connected relation of mij is denoted as (ai , bj ).
659
660   (2) Remove one vertex v1 with the largest degree from V0 and update V1 = V0 \ {v1 }.
661   (3) Eliminate the edges associated with the vertex v1 and update E1 ← E0 .
662
      (4) Repeat the steps (2)-(3) until Et = ∅.
663
664   (5) Remove the points in V0 \ Vt from the original sets A and B, and the rest form the desired
665       MaxLS(A, B).

1190   Moreover, we examine the linear separability of CNNs with two convolution layers and two pooling
1191   layers in binary classification task. All hidden nodes of CNNs are activated by using ReLU (cf.
1192   Fig. 9). Moreover, the linear separability of AlexNet and DBN is also considered in the same task
1193   (cf. Figs. 10 - 12). It is noteworthy that we consider two kinds of AlexNets that have different
1194   output activation functions: one is Softmax, denoted as AlexNet (Softmax), and the other is Sigmoid,
1195   denoted as AlexNet (Sigmoid). We also simplify the process of training AlexNet (Sigmoid), where
1196
       the tricks of learning rate decay and data augmentation are not used. Since the binary classification is
       much simpler than the ImageNet classification task for which AlexNet was originally designed, the
1197
       simplified training process is enough to provide a good performance. Thus, the curves of AlexNet
1198
       (Sigmoid) are smoother than those of AlexNet (Softmax), especially for the LS  c 2.
1199
1200   In addition, we also conduct the experiment on the IMDB dataset, which is a text classification task.
1201   When utilizing neural networks to process text data, the embedding layer is employed to map textual
1202   information into vector spaces. For MLPs, the outputs of the embedding layer are typically flattened
1203   to be compatible with the subsequent dense layer, and this manner could results in the loss of spatial
1204
       information. In contrast, benefited from the specific convolutional structure, CNNs are able to capture
       the spatial information encoded in the outputs of the embedding space, and thus to improve the
1205
       representation capability of the embedding layer. As illustrated in Tab. 9, the linear separability of
1206
       CNN’s embedding layer gradually increases during its training process, and the experiment results
1207   demonstrate the embedding layer of CNN plays a more important role in processing text data than
1208   that of MLP.

       E.4      G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
2107
2108   Graph neural networks (GNNs) have been successfully employed to deal with the graph-structured
2109   data, such as the Cora dataset (Kipf & Welling, 2016). However, there could arise the "over-
2110   smoothing" issue in the application of the classical GNN framework, where the discrepancy among
2111   the node features tends to become less significant as the network depth increases and thus cause the
2112   indistinguishable representations of nodes (Chen et al., 2020; Keriven, 2022). As illustrated in Fig.
2113   11, we find that 1) the discrepancy among the MD-LSM curves of different graph convolutional layers
2114
       becomes smaller when the number of graph convolutional layers increases; and 2) more interestingly,
       the linear separability degrees of the layers close to the input layer are higher than those of the layers
2115
       close to the output layer, i.e., the graph convolutional layers close to the input layer have better node
2116
       representations. The latter finding is in accordance with the aforementioned "over-smoothing" issue.
