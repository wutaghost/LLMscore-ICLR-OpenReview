009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Finding synergistic formulaic alphas is very important but challenging for re-
012            searchers and practitioners in finance. In this paper, we reconsider the discov-
013            ery of formulaic alphas from the viewpoint of sequential decision-making, and
014            conceptualize the entire alpha discover process as a non-stationary and reward-
015            sparse Markov decision process. To overcome the challenges of non-stationarity
016
               and reward-sparsity, we propose the AlphaQCM method, a novel distributional re-
               inforcement learning method designed to search for synergistic formulaic alphas
017
               efficiently. The AlphaQCM method first learns the Q function and quantiles via
018
               a Q network and a quantile network, respectively. Then, the AlphaQCM method
019            applies the quantiled conditional moment method to learn unbiased variance from
020            the potentially biased quantiles. Guided by the learned Q function and variance,
021            the AlphaQCM method navigates the non-stationarity and reward-sparsity to ex-
022            plore the vast search space of formulaic alphas with high efficacy. Empirical ap-
023            plications to real-world datasets demonstrate that our AlphaQCM method signif-
024            icantly outperforms its competitors, particularly when dealing with large datasets
025            comprising numerous stocks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Over the past decades, extensive research has investigated the predictive power of historical stock in-
030   formation for forecasting future returns, resulting in the development of several well-known alphas.
031   Here, each alpha is a function that transforms noisy historical stock data into signals for predict-
032   ing future stock returns. However, recent studies examining the influence of investor behavior and
033   psychology on price dynamics have uncovered the existence of subtle and intricate alphas that are
034   difficult to formalize using standard financial methods (Barberis, 2018).
035
      Most existing AI approaches surpass traditional methods in this field by designing alphas through
036   the use of sophisticated machine learning (ML) models in an end-to-end manner (Feng et al., 2019;
037   Ding et al., 2020; Koa et al., 2023). Intuitively, the well-trained ML models are effective alphas,
038   since they can transform stock data into predictive signals. However, they are inherently complex
039   and lack simple mathematical representations, leading to the so-called non-formulaic alphas. Yet,
040   the non-formulaic alphas have trust issues due to their black-box nature, so they are not widely
041   adopted in the industry.
042   For dealing with the above trust issue, emerging literature focuses on how to automatically discover
043   a set of synergistic formulaic alphas. The formulaic nature of these alphas usually makes them
044   compact, present explicit interpretations, and generalize well; meanwhile, their synergistic nature
045   allows them to be combined into a meta-alpha via some interpretable models (e.g., linear models).
046   Finding these formulaic alphas typically is based on the genetic programming (GP) method (Lin
047   et al., 2019a;b; Zhang et al., 2020; Cui et al., 2021). However, the GP method has a vast search
048   space, which scales exponentially with the number of input features and operators.
049   To overcome the challenge of vast search space in the GP method, the AlphaGen method (Yu et al.,
050   2023) reformulates the alpha discovery problem into the task of finding an optimal policy for a
051   specialized Markov decision process (MDP), and then achieves this task via a reinforcement learning
052   (RL) algorithm. Although the AlphaGen method shows state-of-the-art performance, it has two
053   substantial theory-practice gaps, resulting in the inefficient and unstable alpha discovery procedure
      in practice. The first gap is an unaddressed non-stationary issue (Lecarpentier & Rachelson, 2019),

059
      This paper contributes to the literature by introducing a novel method, AlphaQCM, which addresses
060
      these theory-practice gaps and presents a new RL solution to the alpha discovery problem. Specifi-
061   cally, the AlphaQCM method first leverages the IQN algorithm (Dabney et al., 2018a), to learn the
062   quantiles of cumulative discounted rewards, whereas it studies the mean of cumulative discounted
063   rewards via the DQN algorithm (Mnih et al., 2015). Then, based on the learned quantiles, the Al-
064   phaQCM method adopts the quantiled conditional moments (QCM) method (Zhang & Zhu, 2023)
065   to estimate variance of cumulative discounted rewards, which serves as a natural exploration bonus
066   for the mining agent’s action selection to relieve the issue of reward-sparsity. Remarkably, the esti-
067   mated variance from the QCM method remains unbiased even if the estimated quantiles are biased
068   due to non-stationarity. Hence, by employing the AlphaQCM method, we can alleviate the negative
069
      impacts of non-stationarity and reward-sparsity, thereby achieving a significantly better empirical
      performance in discovering formulaic alphas. Our work clearly generalizes and extends the Alpha-
070
      Gen method and can be applied to other non-stationary and/or reward-sparse environments.
071
072   We apply our AlphaQCM method to three real-world market datasets to assess its empirical per-
073   formance, together with baseline methods such as the AlphaGen method and GP-based methods.
074   Extensive experimental results demonstrate that the AlphaQCM method consistently achieves the
075
      best performance, with Information Coefficient (IC) values of 8.49%, 9.55%, and 9.16% across the
      three datasets. Its superior performance is particularly evident when the dataset originates from a
076
      complex financial system. Finally, we conduct several ablation studies to investigate the contribution
077
      of each component in the AlphaQCM method.

080   2     BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
081
082
      2.1   F ORMULAIC A LPHA
083
      The formulaic alpha has an extensive search space of potential expressions due to the enormous
084   operators and features that are available for selection. Generally speaking, most existing methods for
085   discovering formulaic alphas can be categorized into two classes: GP-based methods and RL-based
086   methods. In the past decade, the GP-based methods have predominantly served as the mainstream to
087   generate formulaic alphas (Lin et al., 2019a;b; Zhang et al., 2020; Cui et al., 2021). For example, the
088   AlphaEvolve method (Cui et al., 2021) evolves new alphas from existing ones using the AutoML-
089   Zero framework (Real et al., 2020), with the IC employed as the fitness measure. However, the recent
090   literature highlights the suboptimal performance of GP-based methods in scenarios involving large
091   populations (Petersen et al., 2021), which are essential for alpha discovery due to the complexity of
092
      considered financial market.
093   Conversely, as a RL-based method, the AlphaGen method (Yu et al., 2023) conceptualizes the alpha
094   discovery process as a Markov decision process (MDP) and employs an RL algorithm, specifically
095   the proximal policy optimization (PPO) algorithm (Schulman et al., 2017), to discover a set of syn-
096   ergistic alphas with high returns. Although the AlphaGen method has significantly outperformed the
097   previous GP-based methods, it has three notable shortcomings. First, due to the reward-sparse nature
098
      of the alpha discovery MDP, the AlphaGen method struggles to explore the search space efficiently.
      Second, the AlphaGen method suffers from the issues related to sample efficiency and convergence
099
      performance, as the alpha discovery MDP is clearly non-stationary. Third, the AlphaGen method
100
      completely ignores the intricate distributional information within the observed expressions and sub-
101   sequent alpha construction, resulting in an inefficient and unstable alpha discovery process.
102

184   3.1       R EPRESENTATION OF F ORMULAIC A LPHA
185
      Before going further, we begin by introducing how to reformulate the alpha discovery problem into
186   a sequential decision-making task. Recall that each formulaic alpha has a mathematical expression
187   comprising operators and features. These operators are broadly categorized into time-series opera-
188   tors and cross-sectional operators (Kakushadze, 2016; Lin et al., 2019a;b). The time-series operators
189   necessitate data spanning multiple days, such as TsRank(Close, d), which calculates the sequential
190   ranking of the most recent d closing prices for each stock. In contrast, the cross-sectional operators
191   manipulate single-day data, exemplified by Rank(Low), which computes the cross-sectional ranking
192   of low prices among N stocks. By combining these two types of operators, the formulaic alphas
193   exhibit high nonlinearity, but are still interpretable for humans.
194   After specifying the operators used in the mathematical expression, we further employ the Reverse
195   Polish Notation (RPN) method to represent the expression (i.e., the form of fp ). Figure 1 gives a
196   specific illustrating example on the RPN method. In this figure, the formulaic alpha “Alpha#4” is
197   encoded into a token sequence, where BEG and SEP tokens indicate the beginning and ending of
198   the expression, respectively, and each feature or operator is denoted as a token. The details of the
199   available features and operators are listed in Table B.3.

      3.2   S PECIFICATION OF MDP
220
221   After introducing the RPN representation, we specify the alpha discovery MDP, as illustrated in
222   Figure 1(d). Below, we show each component in this MDP.
223
      State and action. In accordance with the GP-based methods, the agent manipulates token se-
224
      quences, prompting us to consider a token-based state set X . Specifically, each state observation
225   xt ∈ X corresponds to a token sequence representing the currently generated expression, with ini-
226   tial state X0 ≡ x0 being the BEG token. To maintain the interpretability of discovered alphas, we
227   restrict any state to have fewer than 20 tokens. Aligned with the design of state, each action at ∈ A
228   is a token. However, only a subset of A is allowed to be taken for a specific xt 3 , since not all token
229   sequence are guaranteed to be the RPNs of valid formulaic alphas.
230
      Transition kernel. In this MDP, the transition kernel is deterministic. Given xt and at , the envi-
231   ronment feedbacks the next state xt+1 by appending at to the end of xt , unless at is the SEP token
232   or xt has reached its maximum length. In such cases, this episode of agent-environment interaction
233   terminates, and the environment presents x0 to the agent to initiate a new episode.
234
      Reward. The key component of the alpha discovery process is the design of reward. Intuitively,
235
      the reward is expected to quantify the contribution of the newly discovered formulaic alpha on the
236
      current alpha pool F, which consists of at most P different formulaic alphas collected in previous
237   episodes. Following the idea outlined in Yu et al. (2023), we set rt = 0 for any incomplete token
238   sequence, as the formulaic alpha is only partially formed. Once the token sequence is completed, xt
239   is parsed into a formulaic alpha. If the parsed alpha is invalid, rt = −1; otherwise, the formulaic
240   alpha is evaluated by the environment through the following steps: (1) The new formulaic alpha
241   is added to alpha pool to create an extended alpha pool; (2) A linear model is fitted based on the
242   extended alpha pool to select up to P principal formulaic alphas with the most significant contribu-
243   tion, and the alpha pool is updated accordingly; (3) The meta-alpha is obtained based on the updated
244   alpha pool and the fitted linear model; (4) The reward rt is calculated by the increase in IC of the
245
      meta-alpha based on the updated alpha pool compared to that based on previous alpha pool. See
      Algorithm C.1 for more details.
246
247   Discount factor. Although long alphas tend to lack generalizability and interpretability, they often
248   are more sophisticated and predictive for stock trends. While the max length of episode is restricted,
249   we hence set γ = 1.
250   Non-stationarity. Since the reward function varies across epochs (with different formulaic alphas
251   collected by the alpha pool), the alpha discovery MDP is clearly non-stationary. Specifically, when
252   the agent discovers a new formulaic alpha that proves to be effective (as indicated by a high reward
253   from the environment), the environment incorporates this formulaic alpha into the alpha pool. In
254   the subsequent episode, the similar formulaic alphas to the recently collected one tend to earn little
255   reward, since the alpha pool has been updated to capture the predictive information related to this
256   type of alpha. As a result, once the alpha pool is updated, the reward function of the MDP changes,
257
      necessitating numerous agent-environment interactions and extensive training to re-learn it.
258   Reward-sparsity. In addition to the non-stationarity, the alpha discovery MDP exhibits reward-
259   sparsity from two perspectives. First, the reward can only be non-zero when the episode ends (i.e.,
260   when a new formulaic alpha is generated); otherwise, it must be zero. Second, due to the low
261   signal-to-noise ratio of market datasets, most discovered alphas are meaningless and not beneficial
262   to the alpha pool, leading to zero rewards. Consequently, there are numerous zero rewards in the
263
      transitions, resulting in an inefficient and unstable training process for the agent.

416   Given that Yu et al. (2023) is the most closely related work, our experiments are also conducted
417   on Chinese A-share stock market datasets to capture the 20-day future stock returns. To evaluate
418   the impact of the complexity of the considered financial system on performance, we consider three
419   different stock pools: (1) the largest 300 stocks (CSI300), (2) the largest 500 stocks (CSI500), and
420   (3) all stocks (Market). As one might expect, the more stocks involved in the dataset, the more
421   challenging it becomes to discover synergistic formulaic alphas, as the system becomes more com-
422   plex and chaotic. Moreover, each dataset is split chronologically into a training set (2010/01/01 to
423
      2019/12/31), a validation set (2020/01/01 to 2020/12/31), and a test set (2021/01/01 to 2022/12/31).

               provided by (Kakushadze, 2016), and fit a linear model to form a mega-alpha.

429            Boost model, or LightGBM model to form a mega-alpha.
430         3. GP w/o filter, GP w/ filter (GP-based formulaic alphas): Use the GP method to generate

524   5        C ONCLUSION
525
526   This paper proposes a novel DRL method, AlphaQCM, for alpha discovery in the realm of big mar-
527   ket data. Unlike the existing methods in the literature, the key idea of the AlphaQCM method relies
528   on the unbiased estimation of variance derived from potentially biased quantiles. This approach
529   enables the efficient alpha discovery in the non-stationary and reward-sparse MDP. To implement
530   the AlphaQCM method, we employ the IQN algorithm as the backbone to obtain quantiles, while
531   approximating the Q function using the DQN algorithm. Through extensive experiments on three
532   real-world datasets, we demonstrate the superior advantage of our AlphaQCM method over the
533   previous state-of-the-art alpha discovery approaches. The ablation studies further highlight the con-
534
      tribution of the QCM method, the robustness of DRL backbone, the independence from human
      domain knowledge and the robustness of parameter size7 .
535
536   Overall, the AlphaQCM method serves as a powerful tool for discovering synergistic formulaic
537   alphas, with its superior capability allowing for non-stationarity and reward-sparsity in the alpha
538   discovery process.

      G       BASELINE M ETHODS
811
812
      To evaluate the performance of our AlphaQCM algorithm, we consider four types of baseline meth-
813
      ods in Section 4.1. The first type of baseline methods aims to measure human-level performance in
814   alpha discovery, serving as a benchmark for the remaining machine-based methods. In this category,
815   we apply the well-known Alpha101 method as a representative, which employs the 101 formulaic
816   alphas from Kakushadze (2016) to construct mega-alphas. To ensure model interpretable, these al-
817   phas are combined linearly based on the samples in the training and validation sets8 using a squared
818   loss function. The comparison between the AlphaQCM and Alpha101 methods aims to evaluate
819   whether the RL-based alpha generator can outperform human experts.
820   Next, the second type of baseline methods directly applies some end-to-end machine learning mod-
821   els to capture the linkage between stock features and their future returns. Clearly, these machine-
822   learning-based alphas lack interpretability but are straightforward to generate. Following Yu et al.
823   (2023), we implement the MLP, XGBoost, and LightGBM methods using the open-source library
824   Qlib (Yang et al., 2020), with pre-specified hyperparameters. The comparison between the Al-
825   phaQCM method and the second type of baseline methods is to verify whether the combination of
826   interpretable alphas can outperform complex and non-interpretable alphas.
827   Moreover, the third type of baseline methods depends on the genetic programming algorithm. This
828   type of methods employs the GP method to generate alphas in a one-by-one manner, with the IC
829   being the fitness measure. Specifically, for the GP w/o filter method, the top-P generated alphas
830   with the highest ICs within the training set are used to form a mega-alpha via a linear model, which
831   is fitted on the validation set. In contrast, the GP w/ filter method selects the top-P performing
832   alphas with an additional mutual-IC filter, ensuring that any pair of alphas in the set does not have
833   a mutual IC higher than 0.7. The comparison between the AlphaQCM and the GP-based methods
834
      aims to probe the limitations of the GP method when involving large populations.
835   Lastly, the AlphaGen method (Yu et al., 2023) is the most closely related competitor to our Al-
836   phaQCM method, which also belongs to the category of RL-based methods. The primary difference
837   between these two methods is the algorithm used for discovering alphas. The comparison between
838   the AlphaQCM and AlphaGen methods aims to check whether the simple PPO algorithm is adequate
839   for such a non-stationary and reward-sparse MDP.

      Until now, the AlphaQCM method has discovered formulaic alphas in a completely data-driven
844   manner, neglecting the valuable insights offered by economic experts in the field of alpha discov-
845   ery. One potential approach to incorporate the domain knowledge of experts into our AlphaQCM
846   algorithm is to encode the formulaic alphas proposed in Kakushadze (2016) into token sequences
847   and initialize the replay memory with these corresponding trajectories9 . In other words, we restrict
848   the agent to first learn from the alphas discovered by human experts and then find new alphas in a
849   data-driven way.
850   To check whether the domain knowledge enhances the efficiency of the AlphaQCM method, we
851   report the performance of AlphaQCM method with and without domain knowledge in Table H.5.
852   From this table, we observe that there is some initial gain when the AlphaQCM method leverages
853   domain knowledge, as the AlphaQCM method with domain knowledge outperforms the one without
854   domain knowledge in Panels A and B. However, with more agent-environment interactions and
855   training as in Panels C and D, the AlphaQCM method in a completely data-driven manner achieves
856   higher IC values. This is perhaps because the agent with domain knowledge is prone to fall into local
857   optima by imitating the experts. Hence, to achieve better final performance, we suggest applying
858
      the AlphaQCM method without domain knowledge.

885   I   A DDITIONAL E XPERIMENT: I MPACT OF PARAMETER S IZE
886
887   As mentioned in Appendix F.1, the network-related hyperparameters for both the AlphaGen and
888   AlphaQCM methods are kept consistent to ensure a fair comparison. However, due to differences
889   in their network architectures, the total parameter size of these two methods varies. Specifically, the
890   AlphaGen method employs two types of networks: an actor network and a critic network, with a
891
      total of 298, 609 trainable parameters. In contrast, the AlphaQCM method utilizes four networks:
      an online Q-network, an online quantile network, and their respective target networks (with frozen
892
      parameters), resulting in 572, 000 trainable parameters. Thus, the AlphaQCM method has nearly
893
      twice the number of trainable parameters compared to AlphaGen, with the same network-related
894   hyperparameters.
895
896   To examine whether the superior performance of the AlphaQCM method over the AlphaGen method
897
      stems from its ability to handle non-stationary and reward-sparse alpha discovery MDP rather than
      the increased model complexity, we designed two additional variants: a larger AlphaGen method
898
      and a smaller AlphaQCM method. The larger AlphaGen method has 564, 953 trainable parame-
899
      ters, achieved by setting the hidden dimensions of the LSTM feature extractor to 180, with fully
900   connected heads comprising two hidden layers of 90 dimensions each. Conversely, the smaller Al-
901   phaQCM method has 297, 070 trainable parameters, achieved by setting the hidden dimensions of
902   the LSTM network to 90, with fully connected heads comprising two hidden layers of 48 dimensions
903   each. These configurations ensure that the larger AlphaGen method aligns with the parameter size
904   of the proposed AlphaQCM method, while the smaller AlphaQCM method matches the complexity
905   of the original AlphaGen method.
