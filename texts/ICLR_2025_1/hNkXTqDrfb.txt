011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Transformers have emerged as a cornerstone across various fields with extensive
014            applications. However, the training dynamics of transformers remain relatively
015
               underexplored. In this work, we present a novel perspective on how transformers
               acquire knowledge during the training dynamics, inspired by the feature learn-
016
               ing theory. To this end, we conceptualize each token as embodying two types
017
               of knowledge: elementary knowledge represented by syntactic information, and
018            specialized knowledge represented by semantic information. Building on this data
019            structure, we rigorously prove that transformers follow a syntax-then-semantics
020            learning paradigm, i.e., first mastering syntax in the Elementary Stage and then
021            unlocking semantics in the subsequent Specialized Stage. The results are derived
022            from the training dynamics analysis and finite-time convergence within the in-
023            context learning framework for supervised classification. To our best knowledge,
024            this is the first rigorous result of a two-stage optimization process in transformers
025            from a feature learning perspective. Empirical findings on real-world language
026
               datasets support the theoretical results of the two-stage learning process. More-
               over, the spectral properties of attention weights, derived from our theoretical
027
               framework, align with the experimental observations, providing further validation.

066          How do transformers learn the syntactic and semantic information stage wisely?
067   To demystify the training dynamics of transformers, the first line of work is beyond ICL regimes (De-
068   ora et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Tian et al., 2023a;b). Seminal works by Tian et al. (2023a) and
069   Tian et al. (2023b) analyze how the self-attention mechanism combines input tokens by studying
070   attention maps. However, this line provides few insights into the training dynamics and convergence
071   behaviors. Another closely related line is modelled under the ICL regime like ours (Zhang et al.,
072
      2023; Huang et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024). For example, Huang et al. (2023)
      consider stage wisely learning on the switch of dominant and target features. As a comparison, this
073
      paper derives the stage transitions using (equally dominant) syntactic and semantic features. In sum-
074
      mary, finite-time training dynamics of transformers remain relatively unexplored, especially when
075   attempting to disentangle the learning process of syntactic and semantic information.
076
077   In this paper, we derive a rigorous two-stage learning process where transformers first master syntax
078
      and then unlock semantics. Simultaneously, we investigate how transformer weights evolve over
      time and explore the convergence theory. Our main contributions are summarized as follows.
079
080   (a) Data Modelling with Feature Learning. Inspired by feature learning theory, we categorize
081   token information into two key feature types: elementary knowledge represented by syntactic infor-
082   mation, and specialized knowledge represented by semantic information. Furthermore, we proceed
083   with theoretical abstraction in Section 3, aligning the learning difficulty of foundational knowledge
084
      with linearly separable, easy-to-fit data distributions, while associating specialized knowledge with
      linearly non-separable, hard-to-fit data distributions.
085
086   (b) Mathematical Proof in Two-Stage Learning. Based on the underlying data structure, to our
087   best knowledge, this is the first paper presenting rigorous proofs for the two-stage learning process
088   in transformers, distinguishing between the initial stage of mastering elementary knowledge and the
089   subsequent stage of acquiring specialized knowledge (Detailed proof in Section F.1 ∼ G.2).
090   (c) Optimization Trajectory and Convergence Analysis. We present optimization trajectory and
091   convergence analysis in Section 4, providing deeper insights into the two-stage learning process.
092   Specifically, by adopting feature learning and signal-noise decomposition techniques, we give key
093   propositions and lemmas in Appendix E, carefully discussing the different ReLU activation patterns
094   and the impact of signal or noise weights on network output computations.
095   (d) Alignment of Theory and Experiments. Our theoretical findings are consistent with experi-
096   mental observations of spectral properties, and experiments on real-world language datasets validate
097   the two-stage learning theory (Experiments are provided in Section 5).

      2   R ELATED WORK
100
101
      Optimization Analysis for In-context Learning. Numerous studies have explored the theoret-
102   ical properties of transformers for in-context learning (ICL). In theoretical regimes of ICL, a line
103   of work (Zhang et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024) focuses on
104   optimizing transformers using training prompts structured with input-label pairs, which is similar to
105   this paper. This line of work shows that the global minimum of ICL loss can be reached through
106   gradient flow across different models and tasks (such as models with linear or softmax modules,
107   tasks on linear regression or nonlinear function learning). However, this line usually does not inves-
      tigate how the model weights are optimized and updated or how the loss evolves throughout training.


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Additionally, this line does not address finite-time convergence or the distinct stages of learning var-
109   ious types of information. Among them, Huang et al. (2023) also derive stage-wise learning under
110   linear regression regimes with unbalanced features. Our work differs from them in two aspects: (a)
111   the stage-wise comes from the different types of data in this paper; and (b) this paper focuses on
112   nonlinear classification tasks. This leads to totally different techniques where Huang et al. (2023)
113   emphasize attention maps while we adopt feature learning that accounts for specific data structures
114   to mathematically demonstrate the stages of learning syntactic and semantic information.
115   Training Dynamics of Transformers. Beyond ICL regimes, many studies focus on attention-
116   based models for general learning problems. For example, Deora et al. (2023) investigate opti-
117   mization and generalization of multi-head attention layer in a binary classification setting, using
118   natural sequences rather than synthetic input-label pairs. Li et al. (2023a) offer a theoretical analy-
119   sis of training a shallow ViT for classification tasks, characterizing the sample complexity required
120   to achieve zero generalization error. Additionally, Tian et al. (2023a) and Tian et al. (2023b) ana-
121   lyze the SGD training dynamics for one-layer transformers, focusing on how the self-attention layer
122   combines input tokens by studying attention maps.
123   Optimization Theory of Neural Networks - Feature Learning. A line of work studying the
124   convergence of neural networks relies on Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) technique (Jacot et al., 2018;
125   Li & Liang, 2018; Allen-Zhu et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2019b; Du et al., 2019). It relates the training
126   of over-parameterized (or infinite-width) neural networks to learning over a kernel defined by the
127   network’s randomly initialized weights. However, the parameters of practical networks usually do
128   not remain in the lazy training regime and instead move a large distance. Following NTK, a new
129
      theoretical branch called feature learning theory in deep learning has emerged (Allen-Zhu & Li,
      2020; 2022; Wen & Li, 2021; Li et al., 2023b). Feature learning theory typically assumes specific
130
      data generation models, such as Gaussian mixtures. This paper follows this line and utilizes a
131
      different syntax-semantics data structure, motivated by empirical observations. This data structure
132   allows us to capture the intrinsic interaction between different features and neural network dynamics.

502   5         E XPERIMENTS
503
504   We conduct motivating experiments on Counterfact dataset containing 65,757 question-answer ex-
505   amples dataset (Meng et al., 2022). All experiments utilize the GPT-2 architecture. Our goal is to
506   verify the two-stage learning of syntax and semantics with this dataset, as well as the spectral charac-
507   teristics of model attention weights. Additional dataset descriptions and experiments on HotpotQA
508   (Yang et al., 2020) are detailed in Appendix C.
509   Verify Two-stage Learning of Syntax and Semantics. In Figure 5, we present the training loss
510   over 200 epochs, highlighting three key moments with representative samples, including questions,
511   gold answers, and the model’s predictions. At the initial time T = 1, many predictions are both syn-
512   tactically and semantically incorrect. By T = 5, we observe a significant decrease in training loss;
513   all predictions meet syntactic requirements, but most remain semantically incorrect and inconsistent
514   with the true answers. Thus, the period from T = 1 to T = 5 corresponds to our theoretical Ele-
515   mentary Stage. By T = 100, all predictions are syntactically correct, with most being semantically
516   correct and achieving small training loss. Therefore, the period from T = 6 to T = 100 represents
517
      our theoretical Specialized Stage. Overall, this experiment supports our theory of two-stage learning
      for syntax and semantics.
518
519   Verify Spectral Characteristics. There is a direct Corollary 1 (in Appendix B) from Theorems 2
520   ∼ 4, demonstrating that relatively small eigenvalues of attention weights store syntax information
521   and large ones store semantics. We verify this insight empirically in Figure 6 by preserving different
522   eigenvalues and observing the model performances. Concretely, at time T = 5 (fully syntactically
523
      correct) and T = 100 (fully syntactically correct, nearly fully semantically correct), we set the
      rank preservation ρ ranging from 0.1 to 1.0, to Obtain edited matrices with different eigenvalues
524
      using SVD for comparing predictions. For the left figure, we find that the model’s predictions
525
      become more semantically similar and accurate, as rank preservation ρ increases (maintaining more
526   large eigenvalues). For the right figure, we find that the model gradually grasps correct syntax and
527   semantic information as ρ increases (maintaining more small eigenvalues). In addition, in the middle
528   figure, the number of correct predictions increases with larger rank preservation, which accords with
529   intuition. We defer the detailed discussion to Appendix C.1.

532   6         C ONCLUSION
533
534   This paper provides rigorous proof for the two-stage learning process of transformers in ICL tasks.
535   We categorize token information into two feature types: elementary knowledge represented by syn-
536   tactic information, and specialized knowledge represented by semantic information. By employing
537   feature learning and signal-noise decomposition techniques, we analyze the optimization trajectory,
538   finite-time convergence, and spectral characteristics under the ICL regime, offering deeper insights
539   into the optimization process. Ultimately, our work aims to provide a new perspective and a theo-
      retical framework for understanding the optimization dynamics of transformers.

      Verify Two-stage Learning of Syntax and Semantics. In Figure 5, we present the training loss
790
      over 200 epochs, highlighting three key moments with representative samples, including questions,
791
      gold answers and the model’s predictions. At the initial time T = 1, many predictions are both
792   syntactically and semantically incorrect. By T = 5, we observe a significant decrease in training
793   loss; all predictions meet syntactic requirements, but most are remain semantically incorrect and
794   inconsistent with the true answers. Thus, the period from T = 1 to T = 5 corresponds to our
795   theoretical Elementary Stage. By T = 100, all predictions are syntactically correct, with most being
796   semantically correct and achieving a very low loss value. Therefore, the period from T = 6 to
797   T = 100 represents our theoretical Specialized Stage. Overall, this experiment supports our theory
798   of two-stage learning for syntax and semantics.
799   Verify Spectral Characteristics. From Theorems 2 ∼ 4, based on the relationship of F-norm and
800   trace, it’s straightforward to get T r(Wt1 +t2 ) < T r(Vt1 +t2 ) at convergence time t1 + t2 (Detailed

802   tary syntax and weight V of network g operates on the specialized semantics. Then the corollary
803   of T r(Wt1 +t2 ) < T r(Vt1 +t2 ) hints that, relatively small eigenvalues of attention weights store
804   syntax information and large ones store semantics.
