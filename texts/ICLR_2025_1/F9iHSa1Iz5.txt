               Logical reasoning is a crucial task for Large Language Models (LLMs), enabling
012            them to tackle complex problems. Among reasoning tasks, multi-step reasoning
013            poses a particular challenge. Grounded in the theory of formal logic, we have
014            developed an automated method, Multi-step Deduction (MuseD), for deductive
015            reasoning data. MuseD has allowed us to create training and testing datasets for
016            multi-step reasoning. Our generation method enables control over the complex-
017            ity of the generated instructions, facilitating training and evaluation of models
018            across different difficulty levels. Through RLHF training, our training data has
019            demonstrated significant improvements in logical capabilities for both in-domain
020
               of out-of-domain reasoning tasks. Additionally, we have conducted tests to assess
               the multi-step reasoning abilities of various models.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022) have
027   yielded remarkable outcomes. Among the various capabilities of LLMs, reasoning stands out as
028   one of crucial skills, serving as a foundational ability required for solving complex tasks. Numerous
029   efforts (Sun et al., 2023) have been made to explore and enhance the reasoning capabilities of LLMs.
030
      In this work, we focus on multi-step deductive reasoning tasks (Sun et al., 2023) within the realm of
031   reasoning. Many previous works (Han et al., 2024; Saparov & He, 2023; Saparov et al., 2024) have
032   been done of deductive reasoning data generation. However, most works concentrate on supervised
033   fine-tuning (Sanh et al., 2022) or evaluation. Our work mainly concentrates on generation data for
034   Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022). Deductive reasoning
035   tasks concentrate on deriving correct conclusions from given premises through rigorous and effective
036   reasoning. Our attention is directed towards constructing high-quality data to improve the deductive
037   reasoning abilities of models during the alignment phase. The inherent rigor of deductive reasoning
038   tasks dictates that the corresponding prompts should not contain contradictory information or be
039
      incapable of leading to the correct answer. This presents our first challenge: how to obtain prompts
      with correct answers and no contradictions. Based on the proper conditions in prompts, we expect
040
      LLMs to employ multi-step reasoning to deduce the correct answer. Assessing the correctness of
041
      such a multi-step reasoning process constitutes our second issue, since we need accurate scores of
042   responses to construct training data. Lastly, how to efficiently acquire a substantial amount of data
043   for training is the third problem we need to consider.
044
045
      To address these issues, we propose a generation scheme for multi-step deductive reasoning data,
      named Multi-step Deduction (MuseD). MuseD is a scalable approach from prompt creation to fi-
046
      nal evaluation. To sum up, we base our method MuseD on the syllogistic reasoning of deductive
047
      inference (Copi et al., 2016), employing a backward generation approach to obtain the conditions
048   required for the prompt. This ensures that the conditions in the prompt can lead to the correct con-
049   clusion without any contradictory conditions. Moreover, by controlling the number of generated
050   conditions, we can regulate the number of inference steps required for the prompt. Based on the
051   prompts generated by this method, we can score the responses of LLMs step by step. That is, we
052   provide an evaluation method that can assess whether an answer was correctly obtained through
053   multi-step reasoning rather than merely being the correct answer. The data generation process is
      shown in Fig. 1.

      Using MuseD, we synthesize partially ordered data for multi-step deductive reasoning and use this
055   data for Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) training. We achieve significant
056   performance enhancements on both in-domain and out-of-domain reasoning datasets, validating the
057   effectiveness of our synthesized data. Further experiments demonstrate that step scoring is crucial
058   for improving the model’s performance in RLHF and that positive rewards for correct answers are
059   the primary motivator for model learning.
060
      Additionally, we utilize our method to create a multi-step deductive reasoning evaluation set, also
061   named MuseD. Compared to previous evaluation sets, it can provide insights into the model’s per-
062   formance changes under tasks with different numbers of reasoning steps and offer more granular
063   process evaluations. In summary, our main contributions include: (1) proposing a data synthesis
064   method MuseD based on multi-step deductive reasoning, (2) achieving improvements in deductive
065   reasoning performance on RLHF with this method, and (3) presenting a multi-step deductive rea-
066   soning evaluation set that allows for multi-dimensional automatic scoring.
067   We will briefly introduce the logical concepts involved in our method in Sec. 3 and describe our data
068   generation approach in Sec. 4. In Sec. 5, we will present the experimental results, and in Sec. 6, we
069   will show the performances of some LLMs on our evaluation set.

114   3.1   P ROPOSITION
115
      The proposition provides an object that we can judge to be true or false. Logical reasoning focuses
116
      on the process of deriving a conclusion from one or more premises. These premises and conclusions
117
      often appear in the form of propositions. In natural language, the expression of a proposition may be
118   complex and diverse and it is hard to deal with such flexible data. In this article, we mainly focus on
119   the syllogistic deduction in formal logic, which has relatively simple structure. Therefore, we mainly
120   pay attention to the categorical propositions that judge the relationship between the subject and
121   predicate. In categorical propositions, we judge the relationship between members of one category
122   (the subject of the proposition) and another category (the object of the proposition). Aristotle has
123   established four standard forms of categorical propositions, which we generally write as A, E, I,
124   O (Aristotle, 350 BCE; Copi et al., 2016). Below we use S as the subject and P as the predicate, and
125   we give the formats of the four forms of propositions: (1) form A: All S are P; (2) form E: No S are
126
      P; (3) Form I: There is one S that is P; (4) form O: There is one S that is not P.
127   The four propositional structures presented here provide fundamental statements for judging the
128   relationship between two entity categories. Such structures are independent of the actual semantics
129   of the entities, allowing us to focus more easily on the formal relationships. For large-scale model
130   data production, based on such formal structures, we can effectively carry out data production.
131
132   3.2   S YLLOGISM
133
134   Herein, we provide a succinct introduction to the categorical syllogism in formal logic (Copi et al.,
135
      2016). The data generation process in our subsequent work is predicated on these effective deductive
      reasoning rules.
136
137   The categorical syllogism is an ancient method of logical argumentation that employs deductive rea-
138   soning to study the inferential process of deriving a conclusion from two premises. In the syllogism,
139   we refer to the subject of the conclusion as the minor term, and the predicate as the major term.
140   The two premises of the syllogism each contain the major and minor terms, along with a term that
141
      connects the two premises, which we call the middle term. The premise containing the major term
      is referred to as the major premise, while the premise containing the minor term is called the minor
142
      premise. For instance, we may cite a classic example of a syllogism:
143
144            “All men are mortal. Socrates is a man. Therefore, Socrates is mortal.”
145
146   In this example, the major term is ’mortal’, the minor term is ’Socrates’, and the middle term is
147   ’men’. Hence, ”All men are mortal” is the major premise, and ”Socrates is a man” is the minor
148   premise.
149   The inference process of a categorical syllogism involves establishing a relationship between the
150   major and minor terms using the middle term. In a valid categorical syllogism, if both the major and
151   minor premises are true, then the conclusion must be true. Formal logic has provided several valid
152   syllogistic forms. In our work, we utilize the 15 valid syllogistic forms1 found in modern formal
153   logic (Copi et al., 2016) to generate our logical data.

170   4.1   P ROMPT G ENERATION
171
      The prompt for logical problems should be rigorous and reasonable. We need to ensure that the
172
      prompts we generate can lead to correct conclusions through multi-step reasoning. Therefore, we
173
      must guarantee correctness during the prompt generation process. Our approach to constructing
174   prompts is backward-generated, starting from the conclusion to be proven and progressively in-
175   creasing the complexity of the prompt based on valid formal rules of inference.
176
177
      Our generation process consists of three steps. The first step is to create a deductive reasoning logic
      tree where each node on this tree is a proposition. The root node is the conclusion and the leaf nodes
178
      are the conditions in prompts. The generated tree only gives a logical structure where all terms are
179
      not filled. In the second step, we fill in the content for the missing entities in this tree. Finally, we
180   combine conditions and conclusions to get prompts.
181
182   Specifically, we initially employ the syllogistic method of formal logic to generate a multi-step logic
183
      tree from the ultimate target conclusion. At the beginning of the tree generation process, we create a
      root node, which takes the form of one of the four propositions randomly sampled from the previous
184
      context. This root node is the final conclusion of this prompt. Throughout the process, we maintain
185
      a set of leaf nodes, which initially contains only the root node. As we progress, we iteratively pop a
186   node from the set of leaf nodes, use it as a conclusion, sample a valid categorical syllogism pattern,
187   and transform this conclusion node into two premise propositions by introducing a middle term.
188   These two premise propositions are then added to the set of leaf nodes. Each such sampling process
189   increases the number of leaf nodes by 1 and also necessitates an additional step in the multi-step
190   reasoning from the leaf nodes back to the root node. Thus, by setting the number of times we
191   expand the leaf nodes, we can control the complexity of the generated prompt. When we ultimately
192   have N + 1 leaf nodes, we turn them to N + 1 propositions to construct the prompt. From these
193   propositions, the final conclusion can be inferred through N steps of reasoning. Note that during the
194
      generation process, we use placeholders rather than specific entities as terms to generate a logical
      framework.
195
196   Based on the aforementioned logical conditions, we can also randomly introduce some interfering
197   conditions. We introduce extra conditions that are related to the existing terms but do not conflict
198   with current propositions, serving as noisy conditions in the reasoning process.
199   After establishing the logical trees, we then filling placeholders with entities. The most straight-
200   forward yet challenging approach is to populate it with noun concepts that conform to the logical
201   conditions. However, this method can easily lead models to take shortcuts (Zhang et al., 2023;
202   Saparov & He, 2023) in concluding judgments rather than genuinely engaging in logical reasoning.
203   For instance, given the premises that cats are mammals and mammals are animals, the model can
204   easily arrive at this conclusion that cats are animals with common sense judgment rather than using
205   deductive reasoning. This is what we aim to avoid.
206   In our work, we focus on the formal structure of logical reasoning itself. Therefore, we have chosen
207   two forms of virtual entities: Greek letter names and virtual nouns. Greek letter names are directly
208   sampled, such as “ALPHA” or “BETA”, to substitute for entities. Virtual nouns consist of 4-14
209   English letters and are added to our virtual noun database after confirming they are not actual words.
210   We then sample the required quantity from this database to fill the prompts.
211   Finally, we transform the conditions and conclusion with added entities into a prompt. For the
212   conditions, we concatenate them directly as the given premises. Regarding the conclusion, we have
213   two questioning formats: proof and judgment. For proof questions, we directly ask the model
214   to prove the conclusion. For judgment questions, we reverse the conclusion to its negation with a
215   probability of 0.5 and ask the model to judge whether the given proposition is correct. The templates
      for proof and judgement are given in Appendix F.

      4.2     R ESPONSE G ENERATION
217
218   Based on the generated prompts, we can directly access the model to obtain responses. We refer to
219   this method as “natural” response generation. This approach yields the model’s reasoning process
220   expressed in natural language; however, such responses are not easily evaluated. We also incorpo-
221   rate instructions to generate responses in a specified JSON format within the prompt, allowing for
222   structured answers that can be conveniently scored. We term this approach as “formatted” response
223   generation.
224   The natural method applies solely to the prompt itself, without additional requirements for the format
225   and style of the response. We believe this is more consistent with the model’s output during user
226   interaction and aligns with the distribution of natural language. However, such natural language is
227   relatively challenging to handle during scoring, necessitating additional operations in the evaluation
228   phase to achieve better scoring outcomes.
229   The formatted generation method, on the other hand, introduces a fixed format for responses, re-
230   sulting in outputs that are easier to score. Nevertheless, the significant difference between such
231   structured expressions and natural language may impact the learning process during model align-
232   ment. The added format is provided in a few-shot manner, with details outlined in Appendix G.
233
      We sample responses to the generated prompts using both methods. In our experiments, we utilize
234   the Llama3 8b model and evaluate the model’s responses under these two approaches.

      4.3     R ESPONSE E VALUATION
237
238   The construction of formal logic problems are generated from the root node to the leaf node with
239   the syllogism approach. Consequently, the procedure of proof or judgement constitutes the process
240   of initiating from the leaf nodse and to arrive at the proposition of the root node.
241
      We assess the responses of Large Language Models (LLM) on formal logic reasoning problems in
242   multiple aspects:
243
244   • Step score: Calculate the how much correct step the response reaches. We mainly calculate this
245     score by counting the eliminated middle terms, details given in Sec. 4.3.1.
246
      • Result score: Evaluate whether the response reaches the correct conclusion. A score of 0/1 is
247     assigned. For proof-type questions, if the proposition to be proved emerges in the reasoning
248     process, a score of 1 point is awarded. For judgment-type questions, the score is directly allocated
249     based on the correctness of the judgment.
250
      • Intent score: Evaluate whether the generated formatted response is valid JSON string. If valid, a
251     score of 1 is given; otherwise, a score of 0 is assigned.
252
      • Wrong step: Count the number of wrong reason steps. The scores equals the number of wrong
253
        steps in the propositions within the responses.
254
255   • Noise step: There might be propositions in the responses that we cannot check its correctness or
256     irrelevant to the reasoning process. We count the numbers of these steps as the noise step score.
257   • Extra step: For correct steps in the responses, we count the number of repeated steps.
258
259   Step score, Result score, and Intent score are positive indicators. The higher the value, the better the
260   effect of the LLM. Wrong step score, Noise step score, and Extra step score are negative indicators.
261
      The lower the value, the better the effect of the model.
262
263
      4.3.1    S TEP S CORE C ALCULATION
264   For multi-step reasoning processes, we aim to obtain accurate process scores to evaluate the quality
265   of different answers. It should be noted that the reason process from premises to the final conclusion
266   is not unique. For example, consider conditions: (1) A is B, (2) B is C, and (3) C is D, and we want
267   to conclude that (6) A is D. Using syllogistic reasoning, we can first derive (4) A is C from (1) and
268   (2), and then combine condition (3) to reach (6). Alternatively, we can derive (5) B is D from (2)
269   and (3), and then combine condition (1) to reach the conclusion (6). Both methods are correct and
      should receive equally high step scores.


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Recall that the process of expanding logic tree is to add middle terms to get conditions. Hence
271   reasoning process towards the final conclusion is essentially the process of eliminating these middle
272   terms. In the above example, there are two paths to conduct inference. The first path eliminating B
273   first and then C, while the second path eliminating C first and then B.
274
      Based on observation above, we can enumerate valid middle terms in the logical tree, i.e. term B and
275
      C in the above example. We can check the reasoning process of each response and count the number
276
      of middle terms that are eliminated. Repeated elimination would not be counted. We calculate the
277   rate of the eliminated middle terms and the number of all middle terms to get the step score.

      4.4   P REFERENCE - PAIR C OMPOSITION
280
281   We need to construct preference data to train the reward model (RM), based on the scored data
282   above. Different from Outcome Reward Model (ORM) (Lightman et al.), we have multiple scoring
283   dimensions to construct pairs. Therefore our RM can learn to provide dense reward signals to
284   help PPO training. On the other hand, we don’t train RM in the Process Reward Model (PRM)
285   pattern Lightman et al.; Wang et al. (2023), where rewards are assigned to each step, thus we can
286   train RM with other datasets to ensure the general ability of RM.
287   Since each data point has multiple scoring dimensions, we can construct dataset with different com-
288   position methods, ultimately affecting the learning outcome of the model. Here, we experiment with
289   three methods for generating data pairs:
290
291   • P: uses only positive scores (i.e., the step score and the result score) to construct pairs. For each
292
        pair, the chosen response must have a higher step score than the rejected one, while the result
        score of the chosen cannot be lower than that of the rejected one.
293
294   • PN: uses both positive and negative scores to construct pairs. We use all the scores above. For
295     wrong, noise and extra scores, we use the opposite number to turn the negative signal into positive.
296
        For each pair, each score of the chosen response cannot be lower that that of the rejected one and
        the chosen should has at least one score that is higher.
297
298   • R: uses only result score as the pair standard. That is, the chosen response should has result score
299     1 while the rejected one has result score 0.
300
      One important concern is that whether negative signals should be used. Intuitively, since it should
301
      be easier not to do wrong than to do right, negative signals may be easier to learn than positive ones.
302   We compare method P and PN to see whether the choice of signals matters much. We also conduct
303   method R to show the importance of step scores, since R only provides sparse final result signal, as
304   usually used on ORM training.

      5.3.3   P REFERENCE PAIR C OMPOSITION
433
434   We compare the performance of models trained with PPO across different preference pair gener-
435   ation methods which are presented in Section 4.4. We compare the performances of PPONa P ,
436   PPONa PN and PPONa R .
437   Process signals can significantly improve the effect. RM-Na-P uses preference data incorporating
438   process signals while RM-Na-R uses data constructed with result score only. The step score exhibits
439   significant growth on the MuseD evaluation set. On the public datasets, PPONa P is notably supe-
440   rior to PPONa R . There are about 10-point improvement LogicalDeduction, 7-point imporvement
441   on ProntoQA , and 5-point improvements on both ProofWriter and FOLIO .
442   Negative signals will cause degradation in effectiveness. Introducing negative signals for the con-
443   struction of preference data in the reward model will notably diminish the effectiveness of the PPO
444   model. PPONa PN further incorporates negative indicators on the foundation of PPONa P . There
445   is indeed a reduction in the three dimensions of negative indicators (wrong step count, noise step
446   count, and extra step count) in MuseD. However, it significantly deteriorates in the positive indicator
447   step score. Concurrently, on the public logical verification set, ProntoQA, LogicalDeduction, and
448   FOLIO exhibit a significant decline.
449
450   5.3.4   NATURAL RESPONSE VS. F ORMATTED RESPONSE
451
      We also investigate the influence of data format of prompts. We choose Na (natural responses)
452   and Fo (formatted responses). We train three PPO models with distinct data formats: PPONa P
453   (utilizing Na), PPOFo P (employing Fo), and PPOMix P (mixing Na and Fo in a 1:1 ratio).
454
455
      Natural format performs better. PPONa P is notably superior to PPOFo P and PPOMix P .
      On MuseD dataset, PPONa P exhibits significant superiority over PPOFo P , boasting a 6-7 point
456
      advantage in step score and result score. In comparison to PPOMix P version, PPONa P version
457
      is generally on an equal footing in positive indicators. However, regarding negative indicators such
458   as incorrect step count, noise step count, and extra step count, PPONa P version is markedly better
459   than PPOMix P version. On the public test logic test collection, PPONa P version surpasses
460   PPOFo P and PPOMix P version in the ProntoQA, ProofWriter, LogicalDeduction, and FOLIO
461   datasets. The result shows that natural format, which is more closely to the natural language, is more
462   effective for RLHF training.
463
464   5.3.5   VARIANTS OF LOGICAL PROMPTS IN PPO TRAINING
465
466
      We also experiment on the impact of using additional prompts on top of the prompts covered by RM
      training. We compare the performances among PPONa P , PPONa P All and PPONa P Replace .
467
468   Compare PPONa P All with PPONa P . It can be observed that by adding in-domain prompts,
469   the effect on the MuseD set is significantly increased, while there is a significant drop on the out-
470   of-domain data set. It seems that RM-Na-P has the ability to get relatively correct scores for these
471   in-domain prompts, which helps PPO model to improve. However, a too high data proportion will
472
      cause overfittin on MuseD data and reduce the effect on non-identically distributed datasets.
473   Compare the two experiments of PPONa P Replace and PPONa P . In the MuseD set, PPONa P
474   exhibits superiority in all indicators to PPONa P Replace . On the publicly available logic test set,
475   PPONa P Replace shows worse performance on ProntoQA and LogicalDeduction but better perfor-
476   mance on FOLIO and ProofWriter. On average, it decreased by approximately 1 point. Generally,
477   using prompts trained with the reward model to train PPO yields better effects both on in-distribution
478
      prompts and on open-source data outside the distribution. The possible reason is that on the trained
      prompts, the reward model scores more accurately, thereby better guiding the training process of
479
      PPO. As for the better performance on FOLIO and ProofWriter, it might be caused by a bit over-
480
      fitting on the MuseD deductive format, which hurts the reasoning ability on tasks like FOLIO or
481   ProofWriter a bit, since ProntoQA and LogicalDeduction have more similar formats to MuseD.

519
520   In this work, we propose a multi-step deductive data generation pipline, MuseD, including prompt
521   generation, response scoring and pair composition. MuseD can construct prompts with controllable
522   complexity and check the step scores of responses. We validate the effect of our generated logical
523   data on Lamma3 8B instruct model with RLHF method. The result shows that our data can lead
524
      to significant improvement on in-domain of out-of-domain deductive reasoning tasks. Further, we
      show that natural format and positive step signals are important for RLHF. Finally, we use our pipline
525
      to generate an evaluation dataset, also named MuseD, to evaluate the performance of current LLMs.
