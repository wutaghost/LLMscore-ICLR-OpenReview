                                                   A BSTRACT
013
014
               Modeling the timing of critical events and controlling associated risks through
015
               treatment options are crucial aspects of healthcare. However, current methods
016
               fall short in optimizing dynamic treatment plans to improve clinical outcomes. A
017            key challenge lies in modeling the intensity functions of critical events through-
018            out disease progression and capturing the dynamic interactions between patient
019            conditions and treatments. To address this, we propose integrating reinforcement
020            learning with a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) and a dual-Hawkes pro-
021            cess model to develop intelligent agents capable of delivering personalized and
022            adaptive treatment strategies. The dual-Hawkes process allows us to model the in-
023            tensity of both disease progression and recovery, while accounting for long-term
024            dependencies. The GAN simulates real-world clinical environments using raw
025
               time-to-event data, without requiring detailed treatment annotations. By interact-
               ing with GAN, our model-based reinforcement learning agent learns an optimal
026
               dynamic policy that leverages long-term historical dependencies. When applied
027
               to the MIMIC-III dataset, our approach significantly increased the duration that
028            patients remained in a healthy state, outperforming established machine learning
029            policies.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The patient’s physical condition and illness can change rapidly during clinical treatment, primarily
035   when doctors perform rescues in intensive care units (ICUs) (Goh et al., 2020). In such situations,
036   providing accurate and timely targeted treatment to the patient can significantly alleviate the pro-
037   gression of the illness and may even save lives (Fleming & Harrington, 2013). Therefore, it is
038   crucial to provide prompt and personalized treatment plans based on the patient’s current physical
039
      state and the specific nature of their illness. Given reinforcement learning’s excellent performance
      in various decision-making problems (Silver et al., 2016), training agents based on reinforcement
040
      learning (RL) to develop effective treatment strategies has been widely studied. However, recent
041
      works on RL-based dynamic treatment have some significant drawbacks.
042
043   Current methods rely solely on the final survival outcome to evaluate treatment plans and define a
044   reward function accordingly (Coronato et al., 2020). This limitation prevents the agent from ac-
045
      curately responding to unexpected events during treatment and providing timely interventions (Yu
      et al., 2021). To address this issue, we propose a novel reward function based on the intensity func-
046
      tion, which can promptly reflect the patient’s disease onset tendency in real-time. The intensity
047
      function is the same as the hazard function in survival analysis (Fleming & Harrington, 2013), char-
048   acterizes the likelihood of disease onset under specific conditions; it naturally indicates the patient’s
049   physical state. This enables our method to respond in real-time to changes in the patient’s physi-
050   cal condition. In recent survival analysis research, the intensity is modeled as a function of current
051   covariates (Fleming & Harrington, 2013). If we follow this paradigm, the reinforcement learning
052   method with a reward based on intensity essentially relies on a Markov decision process (Natarajan
053   & Kolobov, 2022). The assumption of a Markov decision process does not align with our real-world
      scenario. In actual medical settings, the future illness intensity should depend on the patient’s history


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      of past occurrences and treatments. Therefore, one might consider building a higher-order Markov
055   decision process (Zhou et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2024).
056
057   Considering the Hawkes process’s demonstrated success in capturing long-term dependencies and
058
      modeling intensity functions (Meng et al., 2024b), we propose a Dual-Hawkes Process that com-
      bines the structures of both Hawkes (Lima, 2023) and Cox processes (Mei et al., 2024) to model
059
      the progression of disease, including both illness and recovery phases. This Dual-Hawkes model
060
      leverages the strengths of both processes, capturing the influence of historical events and current
061   covariates on intensity simultaneously (Meng et al., 2024a).
062
063   To account for the recurring nature of disease events, we define two types of events: illness and
064
      recovery, and examine their respective intensity functions. The illness and recovery events are mod-
      eled using a multidimensional Hawkes process (Meng et al., 2024b), allowing us to simultaneously
065
      capture the effects of both historical events and covariates on the disease progression. Given the
066
      role of the intensity function in reflecting a patient’s physical state, we define the difference between
067   the recovery and illness event intensities over time as the reward function to train the agent. Since
068   only recorded treatment data is available due to the high cost and scarcity of real medical data, the
069   agent is trained using offline reinforcement learning. To enable the agent to interact with the envi-
070   ronment and continuously propose treatment plans, we employ a Generative Adversarial Network
071   (GAN) (Arjovsky et al., 2017) to iteratively generate synthetic clinical data (Kuo et al., 2022). In
072   each iteration, the Dual-Hawkes process is used to compute the reward function, which is then used
073   to train the agent effectively.
074   In summary, the main contribution of our work can be summarized as follows:
075
076          • We introduce a Dual-Hwakes Process model that integrates the Cox model and Hawkes
077            process to capture the impact of a patient’s physical condition and historical treatment on
078            both the illness and recovering progressions, providing a realistic environment for agent
079            interaction.
080          • We establish a model-based reinforcement learning framework embedded within a GAN
081            and utilize the dual-Hwakes process so that agents can learn optimal treatment strategies
082            with higher history dependencies.
083          • We apply the model to the MIMIC-III data, and the experiment result shows that our ap-
084            proach significantly increased the duration of patients remaining in a healthy state, outper-
085            forming current established machine learning policies.

      2   DISEASE HAZARD MODELING WITH DUAL-HAWKES PROCESS
088
089
      In many medical decision-making problems, the goal is to derive a dynamic treatment regime (DTR)
090   (Chakraborty & Murphy, 2014) that provides a sequence of treatment decisions based on a patient’s
091   current health status and prior treatment history, aiming to improve long-term outcomes (Gottesman
092   et al., 2019). Reinforcement learning (RL), due to its ability to optimize sequential decisions, has
093   become increasingly popular in healthcare, particularly in DTR settings (Yu et al., 2021; Abdellatif
094   et al., 2023; Coronato et al., 2020). For instance, several studies have applied RL to derive optimal
095   treatment policies for conditions like sepsis, aiming to prevent critical events such as organ failure
096   or death (Yu et al., 2021).
097   However, in many medical problems, the reward (outcome) is only observed or defined at the termi-
098   nal state, for example, the patient survival (Komorowski et al., 2018; Tamboli et al., 2024). While
099   this binary reward structure may seem straightforward, it oversimplifies the complexities inherent
100   in clinical care. Such methods fail to account for important factors like early signs of deterioration,
101   abnormal vital signs, and the progression of disease, all of which play critical roles in determining
102   patient outcomes. Additionally, the sparse nature of terminal reward signals can exacerbate typi-
103   cal RL challenges, such as credit assignment and sampling inefficiency, leading to high variance in
104
      learning, as highlighted in Luo et al. (2024).
105   To address these limitations, some researchers have turned to continuous clinical risk scores, such
106   as the Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) score and the National Early Warning Score
107   2 (NEWS2) (Inada-Kim & Nsutebu, 2018). These scores have been integrated into RL models to
      provide more consistent and immediate rewards across time steps (Raghu et al., 2018; Wang et al.,


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2022). However, these risk score-based methods still do not fully capture long-term dependencies
109   and the cumulative effects of past clinical events, which are critical in effective healthcare decision-
110   making. Hence, a more advanced approach would be to model the risk of adverse events, rather than
111   relying solely on intermediate scores or terminal outcomes. With this motivation, we introduce a
112   novel reward function based on a Dual-Hawkes Process, designed to model the intensities of both
113   disease and recovering progressions over time.
114
      The reward function is based on the difference between the intensities of recovering and disease
115   illness events. By optimizing the reward function, the agent is trained to provide treatment plans to
116   reduce the patient’s disease illness intensity and increase the recovering intensity. Intensity function,
117   also known as hazard function in survival analysis, is a function of current covariates in recent
118   survival analysis work (Haarnoja et al., 2018). To capture long-term dependency from historical
119   events, actions, and current covariates, we propose the Dual-Hawkes process, a combination of
120   the Hawkes Process (Meng et al., 2024b) and the Cox model (Cox, 1972). Using our proposed
121   Dual-Hawkes process to model the intensity function, the reward function can capture long-term
122   dependencies by incorporating historical information (Wan et al., 2024).

202
      We employ a Reinforcement Learning (RL) framework (Sutton, 2018) to train the agent responsi-
203
      ble for generating treatment actions. In healthcare domains, RL is typically built upon the framework
204   of Markov Decision Process (MDP, (Puterman, 1990)), denoted as M = {S, A, P, r, γ}, where S
205   is the state space, A is the action space, γ ∈ [0, 1) is the discount factor, r : S × A → R is
206   the reward function, and P : S × A → S represents the transition dynamics. The value function
207   v π (s) is defined as the expectation of future P

209   pectation under the policy π and the transition probability. The corresponding action-value function
210   is given by: q π (s, a) = r(s, a) + γEs′ ∼P (·|s,a) [v π (s′ )]. The goal of RL is to find an optimal policy
211   π ∗ that maximizes the value for all s ∈ S.
212   Offline RL is proposed to train RL agents using pre-collected data instead of real-time interactions
213   with the environment. This contrasts with online RL, where the agent learns through direct interac-
214   tion with a real environment or a simulator (Zhou et al., 2024; Schulman et al., 2017). In an offline
215   RL setting, the focus shifts to learning an optimal policy from a pre-gathered dataset. The dataset is

      in offline RL is that πb may not adequately explore all possible actions, leading to potential over-
217   optimistic estimation of out-of-distribution actions. Acting greedily concerning such actions could

219   RL models can be unrealistic in medical problems. To address this issue, we construct a modeling
220   framework that would allow higher order dependencies on the historical state information (Zhou
221   et al., 2023).

      4   R ELATED W ORK
309
310
      Temporal point processes can be used to model discrete event sequences over continuous time
311
      (Shchur et al., 2021). The Hawkes process is a widely used model for capturing event sequences
312   (Hawkes, 1971b), where historical events influence subsequent occurrences (Hawkes, 1971a). With
313   the development of deep learning, methods based on deep neural networks have been introduced for
314   modeling event sequences (Meng et al., 2024b; Zuo et al., 2020). Furthermore, models that capture
315   the impact of covariates on event occurrence have also been proposed, expanding the application
316   scenarios of temporal point processes (Meng et al., 2024a). Interestingly, this scenario of consid-
317   ering the influence of covariates on event occurrence is quite similar to the Cox model in survival
318   analysis (Fleming & Harrington, 2013) and can be viewed as two methods within a unified frame-
319   work. In our proposed framework, the recurrence of diseases (Ren et al., 2019) is depicted by event
320
      sequences (Shchur et al., 2021) and modeled by a temporal point processes-based framework.
321   RL has emerged as a transformative approach in healthcare, particularly in the context of DTRs,
322   where patient responses to treatments are used to adapt interventions over time (Coronato et al.,
323   2020; Yu et al., 2021). Currently, methods mainly focus on Q-learning and value-based approaches
      (Komorowski et al., 2018; Luckett et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2023). And Luo et al. (2024) emphasizes


                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      how results from RL algorithms, such as Conservative Q-Learning CQL, (Kumar et al., 2020) and
325   Deep Q Networks DQN, (Mnih et al., 2015), can be inconsistent when different evaluation metrics
326   or MDP formulations are applied. Furthermore, as Luo et al. (2024) argues, robust policy evaluation
327   methods are lacking. Without reliable ways to assess how well the learned policies generalize to new
328   patients, the effectiveness of these RL models remains uncertain. Moreover, current MDP-based
329   methods are based on 1-order Markov assumptions (Bellman, 1957), contradicting the long-term
330   dependency over time. Consequently, we must consider higher-order MDP (Ye et al., 2024), which
331   is captured by our proposed Dual-Hawkes process in this framework.

      5.2   DATA D ESCRIPTION
367
368   We utilize the MIMIC-III database (Johnson et al., 2016), a comprehensive collection of de-
369   identified clinical data from patients admitted to the Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center in
370   Boston. This publicly accessible database includes information from 53,423 adults and 7,870
371   neonate admissions, spanning over a decade, with a focus on critical care research. The dataset
372   encompasses detailed demographic data, vital signs, laboratory test results, treatment protocols, and
373   outcomes, along with free-text clinical notes.
374   Our analysis specifically targets the sepsis subset of the MIMIC-III database, which includes pa-
375   tients diagnosed with sepsis during their ICU stay. This dataset has been widely used for developing
376   models aimed at sepsis detection and treatment, facilitating research into the management and out-
377   comes of this critical condition. In this work, we use the IV fluid dosages and vasopressor dosages
      regime task for sepsis treatment, and categorize them into five classes, resulting in a 5 × 5 discrete

396   morowski et al. (2018) utilizes a binary reward scheme: r = 0 for non-terminal steps, r = +100
397   for patient survival, and r = −100 for death at the final step. While straightforward, this ap-
398   proach oversimplifies the complexity of medical treatment scenarios, ignoring critical factors like
399   the patient’s risk of deterioration, abnormalities in vital signs, and disease progression rates, all of
400   which significantly impact mortality. From a reinforcement learning perspective, such sparse and
401   binary rewards can exacerbate challenges related to credit assignment, sampling inefficiency, and
402
      high learning variance.
403   In contrast, incorporating intermediate rewards
404   has proven effective in goal-reaching RL tasks
405   (Zhai et al., 2022). In the context of DTRs, in-
406   termediate rewards often take the form of clin-
407   ical risk scores. For example, the SOFA score
408
      has been widely used in reward design for med-
      ical RL applications (Raghu et al., 2018; Wang
409
      et al., 2022). Additionally, lactate levels, which
410
      serve as biomarkers for tissue hypoxia and
411   metabolic dysfunction (Nguyen et al., 2004),
412   have been integrated into reward structures.
413
414
      However, these risk-based rewards fail to fully
      account for the effects of historical events and
415
      the long-term dependencies critical in medical
416
      decision-making. Our proposed reward design,
417   based on the intensity of the dual Hawkes pro- Figure 3: ROC Curves of Dual Hawkes Models
418   cess, addresses these limitations by capturing
419   both immediate and historical factors, offering a more nuanced and dynamic reward framework.

      By integrating these covariates into an adapted likelihood function for discrete data, we can predict

444   5.4   P OLICY E VALUATION
445
446   This section outlines our selection of baseline models and policy evaluation methods. In reviewing
447
      the existing literature, particularly the work of Luo et al. (2024), we identified several gaps in the
      current methodology: varying baseline sets across studies, a lack of state-of-the-art (SOTA) offline
448
      RL algorithms, and the absence of naive baselines for essential evaluations. This reference served as
449
      a guiding framework for our baseline selection, emphasizing the importance of rigorous comparisons
450   in assessing the effectiveness of RL approaches.
451
452   To address these identified issues, we incorporated a comprehensive set of baselines. We employed
453
      naive baselines, including zero-drug, random, and max-drug policies, to provide a foundation for
      comparison. In particular, GAN models, which were used as supervised learning baselines, feature
454
      a generator that leverages a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network. This allows the GAN to
455
      capture behavior policies from the training data and assess the performance of RL against learned
456   behavioral strategies, further enriching our analysis.
457
458   Furthermore, we included advanced RL baselines, specifically CQL (Conservative Q-Learning) and
459
      DQN (Deep Q-Network). These models were trained using clinical risk scores, as discussed earlier,
      with the SOFA score being a prominent example. However, as noted in Luo et al. (2024), existing
460
      RL methods like CQL and DQN may exhibit performance inferior to even naive baselines, such
461
      as the max-drug policy, when rewards are altered. This phenomenon was also observed in our
462   experimental results, where both CQL and DQN underperformed in certain cases when the reward
463   function was modified. By comparing our results against this diverse set of baselines, we aim to
464   demonstrate the advantages of our approach in optimizing dynamic treatment regimes, particularly
465   in the context of sepsis management.
466
      Policy evaluation in RL for DTRs presents significant challenges due to the nature of the data (Luo
467
      et al., 2024). Since the dataset is fixed and observational, RL models cannot be evaluated through
468   direct interaction with the environment, as is common in traditional RL settings. This limitation is
469   compounded by the complexity of medical decision-making, where treatment effects may not be
470   immediately apparent and are subject to a range of confounding variables. The variability in patient
471   responses to treatments further complicates efforts to assess the effectiveness of proposed policies.
472
      Several methods have been developed to address these challenges in offline RL, including Inverse
473
      Probability Weighting (IPW) (Liu et al., 2017), Weighted Importance Sampling (WIS) (Kidambi
474   et al., 2020; Nambiar et al., 2023), the Direct Method (DM) (Huang et al., 2022; Kondrup et al.,
475   2023), and Doubly Robust (DR) (Raghu et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2018), estimators.
476   These approaches attempt to tackle the issue of confounding variables by creating counterfactual
477   estimations based on historical data. However, no single method has proven universally effective,
478   and each has its own limitations, particularly in accounting for the complexities of medical treatment
479   data.
480   Our framework addresses these limitations by providing a virtual environment to simulate policy in-
481   teractions with patients, thus offering a more dynamic evaluation platform. Specifically, we leverage
482   a combination of GAN and Dual-Hawkes Process models to simulate patient responses under dif-
483   ferent treatment policies. By simulating the average intensity of recovering and illness progression
484   (i.e., the integral of recovering intensity minus illness intensity), we can compare the performance
485   of different policies in a more nuanced way. Additionally, the simulation estimates the total time
      patients spend in a healthy state, also offering a measure of policy effectiveness (shown in Figure 5

      in Appendix). During the agent training process, we use the difference between the recovering and
487   illness intensity integrals as the reward to actions.

528
529
      In this study, we introduced the Dual-Hawkes Process, which integrates Cox models and Hawkes
530   processes to simultaneously model disease illness and recovering events while accounting for his-
531   torical events and covariates. Coupled with a novel model-based reinforcement learning framework
532   that employs a GAN for offline training, our approach utilizes the difference in intensity functions
533   as a reward signal to train agents, which provides timely and preventive treatment. Applied to
534   the MIMIC-III dataset, our method significantly extended the duration for which patients remained
535   healthy compared to existing reinforcement learning policies. This work overcomes the limitations
536   of previous approaches by providing more accurate real-time state modeling and multi-outcome
537   optimization, paving the way for more effective and personalized treatment strategies in critical
538
      healthcare settings.
