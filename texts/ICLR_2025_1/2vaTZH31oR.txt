011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Generating high-quality 3D content from text, single images, or sparse view
014            images remains a challenging task with broad applications. Existing methods
015            typically employ multi-view diffusion models to synthesize multi-view images,
016            followed by a feed-forward process for 3D reconstruction. However, these ap-
017            proaches are often constrained by a small and fixed number of input views, limit-
018
               ing their ability to capture diverse viewpoints and, even worse, leading to subop-
               timal generation results if the synthesized views are of poor quality. To address
019
               these limitations, we propose Flex3D, a novel two-stage framework capable of
020
               leveraging an arbitrary number of high-quality input views. The first stage con-
021            sists of a candidate view generation and curation pipeline. We employ a fine-
022            tuned multi-view image diffusion model and a video diffusion model to generate
023            a pool of candidate views, enabling a rich representation of the target 3D ob-
024            ject. Subsequently, a view selection pipeline filters these views based on quality
025            and consistency, ensuring that only the high-quality and reliable views are used
026            for reconstruction. In the second stage, the curated views are fed into a Flexible
027            Reconstruction Model (FlexRM), built upon a transformer architecture that can
028            effectively process an arbitrary number of inputs. FlexRM directly outputs 3D
029
               Gaussian points leveraging a tri-plane representation, enabling efficient and de-
               tailed 3D generation. Through extensive exploration of design and training strate-
030
               gies, we optimize FlexRM to achieve superior performance in both reconstruction
031
               and generation tasks. Our results demonstrate that Flex3D achieves state-of-the-
032            art performance, with a user study winning rate of over 92% in 3D generation
033            tasks when compared to several of the latest feed-forward 3D generative models.
034
               See anonymous project page for more immersive 3D results.

      1   I NTRODUCTION

      Fast generation of high-quality 3D contents is becoming increasingly important for video games
      development (Hao et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2023), augmented, virtual, and mixed reality (Li et al.,
040
      2022), robotics (Nasiriany et al., 2024) and many other applications. Recent advances in computer
041
      vision and graphics (Mildenhall et al., 2021; Kerbl et al., 2023) and deep learning (Dosovitskiy,
042   2020; Caron et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2023), combined with the availability of large datasets of
043   3D objects (Deitke et al., 2023; 2024; Yu et al., 2023; Chang et al., 2015), have made it possible to
044   learn neural networks that can generate 3D objects from text, single images or a sparse set of views,
045   and to do so in an feed-forward manner, achieving significantly faster speeds than distillation-based
046   methods (Poole et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Qiu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b).
047
      A particularly successful family of 3D generators are the ones based on sparse-views reconstruc-
048   tion (Xu et al., 2024c; Siddiqui et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024c; Tang et al., 2024a;
049   Xu et al., 2024b; Xie et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024c). Compared to single-image reconstructors,
050   multi-view reconstruction models generally produce better 3D assets. This advantage arises because
051   the multi-view images implicitly capture the object geometry much better, substantially simplifying
052   the reconstruction problem. However, to generate a 3D object from text or a single image, one must
053   first synthesize several views of the objects, for example by means of a multi-view diffusion model.
      These multi-view diffusion models often generate inaccurate and inconsistent views, which are dif-

086
      This paper thus focuses on the problem of generating a high-quality set of different views of an
      object, with the goal of improving the quality of the final 3D object reconstruction. We build on a
087
      simple observation: the quality of the 3D reconstruction improves as the quality and quantity of the
088
      input views increases (Han et al., 2024b). Hence, instead of relying on a fixed, limited set of views
089   generated by potentially unreliable multi-view diffusion models, we suggest to generate a pool of
090   candidate views and then automatically select the best ones to use for reconstruction.
091
092
      Based on this idea, we introduce a new framework, Flex3D, comprising a new multi-view generation
      strategy as well as a new flexible feed-forward reconstruction model.
093
094   First, we propose a mechanism to generate a large and diverse set of views. We do this by training
095   two diffusion models, one that generates novel views at different azimuth angles and the other at
096   different elevation angles. The models are designed to make the views as consistent as possible.
097   Second, we propose a view selection process that uses a generation quality classifier and a feature
098
      matching network to measure the consistency of the different views. The result of this selection is a
      good number of high-quality views, which help to improve the quality of the final 3D reconstruction.
099
100   Differently from many prior works, then, we need to reconstruct the 3D object from a variable
101   number of views which depends on what the selection process returns. Hence, we require a recon-
102   struction model that (1) can ingest a varying numbers of input views and different viewing angles;
103   (2) is memory and speed efficiency to handle a large number of input views; and (3) can output a
104
      full, high-quality 3D reconstruction of the object, regardless of the number and pose of input views.
105   To this end, we introduce Flexible Reconstruction Model (FlexRM). FlexRM starts from the estab-
106   lished Instant3D architecture (Li et al., 2024b) and adds a stronger camera conditioning mechanism
107   to address the first requirement (1). It also introduces a simple but effective way of combining the
      Instant3D tri-plane representation with 3D Gaussian Splatting, meeting requirements (2) and (3).

108
      Specifically, FlexRM learns a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) to decode the tri-plane features into
109   the parameters of 3D Gaussians used to represent the object. We also simplify the process of learn-
110   ing this MLP, thus leading to notable performance improvements, by pre-training parts of it, where
111   we initialize the color and opacity parts using an off-the-shelf NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2021) MLP.
112   For the remaining Gaussian parameters, we learn rotation and scale in a conventional manner while
113   learning position offsets which are combined with the tri-plane feature sampling locations.
114
      While our view selection pipeline identifies the best views for reconstruction, it still does not elim-
115   inate all multi-view inconsistencies. To mitigate the impact of the minor inaccuracies that remain,
116   FlexRM employs a novel training strategy. Although our training dataset consists of perfectly ren-
117   dered images, we simulate imperfections in the input views by leveraging the output of FlexRM
118   itself. Specifically, we take FlexRM’s reconstructed 3D Gaussians, add noise to them, and generate
119   new noisy views of the object based on these noisy Gaussians. Compared to directly manipulating
120   the views, this approach allows us to inject more expressive and representative types of noise, as
121   shown in fig. 3. The noisy views are then combined with clean rendered views and fed as input to
122   the 3D reconstructor, with the goal of producing clean, noise-free representations of the 3D object.
123
      This approach enables the model to learn how to handle imperfect inputs.
124   We benchmark our method against state-of-the-art feed-forward models in 3D generation and 3D
125   reconstruction tasks, evaluating performance through a user study and various automated metrics.
126   We achieve the best results in reconstruction tasks across all settings (single-view, four-view, and
127   more-view), as well as in generation tasks. We also conduct a thorough ablation study to assess the
128   impact of our design choices.
129   In summary, this paper makes the following key contributions to address the limitations of cur-
130   rent two-stage 3D generation pipelines: (1) We introduce a novel pipeline that generates a pool of
131   2D views of an object and only selects the optimal subset for 3D reconstruction. (2) We propose
132   FlexRM, a 3D reconstruction network that efficiently processes an arbitrary number of input views
133   with varying viewpoints, enabling high-quality feed-forward reconstruction from the selected views.
134   (3) We introduce a novel training strategy to enhance the robustness of the 3D reconstructor by sim-
135
      ulating imperfect input views. This improves FlexRM’s resilience to small noise in the input data
      that may remain.

139
140   2.1   M ULTI - VIEW G ENERATION
141
142   Generating novel views from a single image or text without learning a 3D representation is a highly
143   ill-posed and challenging task. With the development of image and video diffusion models, this task
144   has become easier to address, as solutions can be built upon these pre-trained models.
145   Zero123 (Liu et al., 2023a) first proposed using multi-view data to fine-tune an image diffusion
146   model for generating novel views from a single view, conditioned on camera parameters. Following
147   this approach, subsequent works (Li et al., 2024b; Shi et al., 2023b; Tang et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
148   2023b; Long et al., 2024; Wang & Shi, 2023; Woo et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024b; Ye et al., 2024;
149   Zhao et al., 2024; Zheng & Vedaldi, 2024; Tang et al., 2024b) largely focused on generating multiple
150   views simultaneously to ensure 3D consistency.
151   With the availability of powerful video diffusion models, recent works (Kwak et al., 2023; Voleti
152   et al., 2024; Melas-Kyriazi et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a; Han et al., 2024a; Gao et al., 2024; Zuo et al.,
153   2024; Yang et al., 2024a) have adopted them to improve multi-view generation. However, none of
154   these models can reliably generate a large number of perfectly consistent views. Furthermore, even
155   with camera conditioning, models like SV3D (Voleti et al., 2024) perform poorly when the specified
156   elevation angle deviates from zero. This justify our approach of selecting the best views from a pool
157   of generated views.

213   3.1         C ANDIDATE V IEW G ENERATION AND S ELECTION
214
215   Here we describe how a pool of candidate views is generated from a single image or text and then
      filtered for quality and consistency before performing 3D reconstruction.


                                                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Multi-view generation at varying elevations. Our image/text-to-multi-view-images generator
217   module generates four views of the 3D object from four elevation degrees (-18°, 6°, 18°, and 30°)
218   We utilize the Emu model (Dai et al., 2023), which is pre-trained on a massive dataset of billions
219   of text-image pairs, as our base model. Following prior works (Shi et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2024b;
220   Siddiqui et al., 2024), we fine-tune this model on approximately 130,000 rendered multi-view im-
221   ages. This fine-tuning process enables the model to predict a 2×2 grid of four consistent images,
222   each corresponding to one of the specified elevation angles.
223   Multi-view generation at varying azimuths. After generating four views at varying elevations,
224   we employ a fine-tuned Emu video model (Girdhar et al., 2023; Melas-Kyriazi et al., 2024; Han
225   et al., 2024a) to generate a video with 16 views spanning a full 360° azimuth range. This model is
226   fine-tuned on a dataset encompassing a wide spectrum of elevation angles, enabling it to generate
227   consistent, high-quality views from diverse inputs with varying elevations. We generate the multi-
228   view videos starting from the input view at 6° elevation, which usually results in representative
229   views for the subsequent reconstruction process.
230   View selection. As the two multi-view diffusion models are focused on different aspects (elevation
231   and azimuth) of novel view generation, there are minimal conflicts in the generated views between
232   them. Even so, and despite efforts to improve the quality of the outputs, not all generated views
233   are entirely consistent. Certain views, particularly those from challenging angles like the back, may
234   exhibit suboptimal generation quality, and there can be inconsistencies between different generated
235   views. Including such flawed views as input for 3D reconstruction can significantly degrade the
236   quality of the final 3D asset. Therefore, we introduce a mechanism to filtering poor-quality views.
237
      This is done via a novel view selection pipeline which consists of two steps:
238   (1) Back View Quality Assessment: We employ a multi-view video quality classifier trained to assess
239   the overall quality of generated videos, with particular emphasis on the quality of the back view. This
240   classifier utilized DINO (Oquab et al., 2023) to extract features from the front view and back view of
241   the multi-view video, and subsequently trained a Support Vector Machine (SVM) to classify video
242   quality based on the combined DINO features. The training data consisted of 2000 manually labeled
243
      “good” and “bad” Emu-generated video samples.
244   We apply the quality classifier to the multi-view video to determine whether the back view exhibits
245   reasonable generation quality.
246
      (2) Multi-View Consistency Verification: If the back view quality is deemed acceptable, we designate
247   both the back and front views as initial query views. The front view typically possesses the highest
248   visual quality, as it is directly based on the input provided to the fine-tuned EMU video diffusion
249   model. Conversely, if the back view quality is inadequate, only the front view serves as the initial
250   query view. We utilize the Efficient LoFTR (Wang et al., 2024b) to match features between all 20
251   generated views and the selected query views. Views with matching point counts exceeding the
252   mean minus 60% of the standard deviation are added to the selected results. This step effectively
253   gathers high-quality side views and views at different poses that demonstrate strong consistency
254   with the initial query views.
255   Candidate view generation typically takes about a minute on a single H100 GPU, and the whole
256   process of view selection can be done in less than a second with a single A100 GPU.

258   3.2   F LEXIBLE R ECONSTRUCTION M ODEL (F LEX RM)
259
260   As outlined in the introduction, FlexRM aims to fulfill the following requirements: (1) adaptability
261   to varying numbers of input views and their corresponding viewing angles, (2) memory and speed
262   efficiency, and (3) the ability to infer a full 3D reconstruction of the object independently of how
263   many and which views are available. We follow a minimalist design philosophy and strive to mini-
264   mize modifications to Instant3D, enabling easy reuse of weights from architectures like Instant3D,
265   and simplifying implementation.
266   Stronger camera conditioning. Handling varying numbers of input views with viewing angles
267   necessitates providing camera information to the network. In Instant3D, each view’s camera infor-
268   mation, including its extrinsic and intrinsic parameters, is represented as a 20-dimensional vector.
269   This vector is then passed through a camera embedder to produce usually a 1024-dimensional cam-
      era feature, which is subsequently injected into the DINO (Oquab et al., 2023) image encoder net-


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      work (responsible for extracting image features for each view) using an AdaIN (Huang & Belongie,
271   2017) block. The image encoder’s final output comprises a set of pose-aware image tokens, which
272   has 1024 768-dimensional tokens for every 512×512 resolution input view. These per-view tokens
273   are concatenated to form the feature descriptors for input views.
274
      Our aim is to ensure that the DINO-extracted tokens are not only camera-aware, but also explicitly
275
      incorporate learnable camera information into the final feature descriptors. To achieve this, we
276
      set the output dimension of the camera embedder to 768, enabling it to match the dimension of
277   the pose-aware image tokens and be appended to them, resulting in 1025 (1024 image tokens +
278   1 camera token) 768-dimensional tokens overall. This simple way of attaching explicit camera
279   information enhances the network’s camera awareness, strengthening its performance in complex
280   scenarios where a large number of input views is provided.
281
      Bridging tri-planes and 3D Gaussian Splatting. For rendering speed and memory efficiency, we
282
      opt to use 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023) to represent the 3D object. However,
283   Instant3D uses a tri-plane NeRF representation instead. To bridge these two, we predict a set of 3D
284   Gaussian from the tri-plane features via an MLP. Because 3DGS is notoriously sensitive to the initial
285   Gaussian parameters, we carefully initialize both the MLP predictor and the tri-plane transformer
286   network with an off-the-shelf tri-plane NeRF network.
287
      A tri-plane is a compact representation of a volumetric function [−1, 1]3 → Rd mapping points
288
      p ∈ [−1, 1]3 to feature vectors f (p) ∈ Rd . Starting from an initial position p0 , the model first
289   reads off the corresponding tri-plane feature f (p0 ), and then feeds the latter into an MLP to predict
290   the parameters of a corresponding 3D Gaussian, namely, its position, color, opacity, rotation, and
291   scaling. To obtain a mixture of such Gaussians, we simply start from a set of initial positions p0
292   and apply the MLP at each location. We use a 100 × 100 × 100 grid to sample the initial positions,
293   resulting in the prediction of 1 million Gaussians.
294
      The position of the 3D Gaussian is expressed as p = αp0 +(1−α)δp = αp0 +(1−α) MLP(f (p0 ))
295   where δp is a a positional offset output by the MLP and α = 3/4. These positional offsets δp are
296   constrained to the range of [−1, 1] through the application of the tanh activation function. This
297   approach, akin to residual learning, facilitates the optimization process. The multipliers ensure that
298   p remains within the same range as p0 , which prevents Gaussian points from moving beyond the
299   visible boundaries, which would result in their projection falling outside the 2D image plane and
300   consequently providing no gradients for optimization. Furthermore, since α < 1, this expression
301   biases Gaussians to shift towards the center of the tri-plane grid, where the object is usually located.
302   The color and opacity of the Gaussian are output by the same MLP that, in Instant3D, outputs the
303   color and opacity of their NeRF representation. These two parameters need no conversion as color
304   and opacity in NeRF and 3DGS are similar in functionality. Finally, the part of the MLP that predicts
305   the Gaussian rotation and scaling parameters are learned from scratch.
306
      Data. Our training dataset comprises multi-view dense renders from an internal dataset analogous to
307   Objaverse. Specifically, for each object, we render 512×512 resolution images from 256 viewpoints,
308   uniformly distributed across 16 azimuth and 16 elevation angles. This process yields approximately
309   700,000 rendered objects, with 140,000 classified as high-quality. Furthermore, we leverage the
310   Emu-video synthetic dataset (Han et al., 2024a), which consists of 2.7 million synthetic multi-view
311   videos. Each video comprises 16 frames capturing a 360-degree azimuth at a fixed elevation angle.
312
      Two-stage training. Initially, we pre-train FlexRM using a NeRF MLP architecture. This stage
313   employs the Emu-video synthetic data, where we randomly select 1 to 16 input images (256 × 256
314   resolution) and render 4 views with fixed rendering resolution of 256 × 256 and patch resolution of
315   128 × 128 for supervision (L2, LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018), and opacity). The pre-training phase
316   aims to provide a good initialization for the subsequent GS MLP training and is conducted for 10
317   epochs, requiring 2 days on 64 A100 GPUs.
318
      For the second GS training stage, we utilize the 700,000 dense renders. A random number (between
319   1 and 32) of input images (512×512 resolution) are fed into FlexRM, and we render 4 novel views
320   (512×512 resolution) to compute losses. Given that our dense renders encompass images with
321   diverse elevation angles, we implement weighted sampling for both input and rendered images. This
322   assigns higher selection probabilities to images with elevation angles closer to zero. The training
323   process spans 20 epochs and takes 4 days on 128 A100 GPUs.

345   3.3     I MPERFECT INPUT VIEW SIMULATION
346
347   Even after input view selection, these views may still contain minor imperfections. To enhance
348   FlexRM’s suitability for generation tasks, we require it to be robust to such imperfections while
349   maintaining high-quality 3D outputs. We achieve this robustness by simulating imperfect inputs
350   during a fine-tuning stage.
351
      This necessitates simulating a wide variety of imperfections efficiently. Simply adding noise in im-
352   age space makes it difficult to simulate imperfections arising from geometric distortions. Instead,
353   we propose a three-step process: (1) First, we perform inference using FlexRM with a small ran-
354   dom number of rendered images (between 1 and 8) as inputs to generate another random number
355   of images (between 1 and 32) for subsequent use as inputs. (2) Next, we use these generated im-
356   ages to replace the rendered images at the same viewing angles with a 50% probability, forming a
357   new input set. This replacement probability is based on the observation that multi-view diffusion-
358   generated images typically exhibit inconsistencies and imperfections non-uniformly. Additionally,
359   this approach encourages the reconstructor to focus more on high-quality input views. (3) Finally,
360
      we re-run FlexRM with gradients enabled, using the new input set as inputs and supervising it with
      novel view rendering losses, while utilizing perfectly rendered views as ground truth. This fine-
361
      tuning process enables FlexRM to learn to tolerate minor imperfections in input views and still
362
      produce high-quality 3D reconstructions.
363
364   While FlexRM’s outputs naturally contain small imperfections, we also introduce random pertur-
365   bations to FlexRM’s generated 3D Gaussian points during step (1) to simulate a wider range of
366
      imperfect inputs and promote greater diversity. We sample a randomly sized small cube from the
      large tri-plane cube and add noise with varying intensities to the 3D Gaussian parameters, excluding
367
      rotation. For example, adding noise to positions can simulate part-level 3D inconsistencies, while
368
      adding noise to color parameters can simulate color distortions. Adding noise to opacity results in
369   a speckled or streaky appearance, while adding noise to scale leads to a blurring effect. Figure 3
370   illustrates these effects. During training, each effect is randomly used with a probability of 0.2. This
371   combines them for greater diversity. Please check Appendix A for more details.

      4.3     A BLATION STUDY AND ANALYSIS
490
491   FlexRM in 3D Reconstruction. We first ablate various design choices of FlexRM using 3D recon-
492   struction metrics, including: (1) not using the stronger camera conditioning, (2) directly predicting
493   positions (α = 1), (3) not using positional offsets (α = 0), and (4) not using two-stage training. All
494   experiments here are conducted on a weaker version of FlexRM, trained with 140,000 data points
495   and for 20 epochs only in stage 2. We use the same evaluation setting as in section 4.2.
496   Results are shown in table 3, where we report the averaged results across four different settings: 1,
497   4, 8, and 16 input views. Overall, removing each component leads to a performance drop. Notably,
498   directly predicting positions and removing positional offsets result in significant performance de-
499   creases. This highlights the importance of accurately modeling Gaussian positions for high-quality
500   reconstruction. Interestingly, removing stronger camera conditioning has a relatively smaller im-
501   pact on performance. This is because the advantages of stronger camera conditioning become more
502   pronounced when a larger number of input views with varying camera poses are used. To validate
503
      this, we also test a 32-view input experiment, where incorporating stronger camera conditioning
      improved PSNR by over 0.3 dB.

532   5      C ONCLUSION
533
534   This paper introduces Flex3D, a novel feed-forward 3D generation pipeline that produces high-
535   quality 3D Gaussian representations from text or single-image inputs. We propose a series of ap-
536   proaches to overcome the limitations of previous two-stage methods, significantly improving final
537   3D quality. Extensive evaluations on benchmark tasks demonstrate that Flex3D achieves state-of-
538   the-art performance in both 3D reconstruction and generation. These results highlight the effective-
539   ness of our approach in addressing the challenges of feed-forward 3D generation, paving the way
      for more robust and versatile 3D content creation.

      A    I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
836
837
      Training details. Our FlexRM is trained in a two-stage manner. In stage 1, we train it with 64
838
      NVIDIA A100 (80GB) GPUs and use a total batch size of 512, where each batch consists of 4 multi-
839   view images at a patch resolution of 128 × 128 for supervision. The input images have a resolution
840   of 256 × 256, and the number of input images varies from 1 to 16. The model is trained for 10
841   epochs with an initial learning rate of 2 × 10−4 , following a cosine annealing schedule. Training
842   begins with a warm-up phase of 3000 iterations, and we use the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov &
843   Hutter, 2017). We apply gradient clipping at 1.0 and a weight decay of 0.05, applied only to weights
844   that are not biases or part of normalization layers. Both training and inference are performed using
845   Bfloat16 precision. The optimization target is a combination of three different losses: L2, LPIPS,
846   and opacity, with corresponding coefficients of 1, 2, and 1, respectively.
847   Stage 2 utilizes 128 NVIDIA A100 (80GB) GPUs. We increase the input image resolution to 512
848   × 512 and the maximum number of input images to 32. We maintain a total batch size of 512, with
849   each batch consisting of 4 multi-view images at a resolution of 512 × 512 for supervision. The
850   model is trained for 25 epochs. All other training settings including total batch size are identical to
851   Stage 1.
852   For further fine-tuning using simulated imperfect input views as input, we follow the setting in Stage
853   2 but only train it with 32 NVIDIA A100 (80GB) GPUs for 3 epochs. We use a total batch size of
854   128 and an initial learning rate of 2 × 10−5 .
855
      3D Gaussian parameterization. For predicted 3DGS parameters with 14 dimensions, we provide
856
      implementation details on converting them into position offset, color, opacity, scale, and rotation.
857   We follow the setting used in GS-LRM (Zhang et al., 2024c) for opacity, scale, and rotation.
858
859   Position offset: We activate the predicted offset using a tanh function and apply a scaling factor of
860
      0.25. This scaled offset is then added to the initial positions to obtain the final 3DGS positions.
861   Color: We utilize the same activation function as in Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF). The predicted
862   color values are first passed through a sigmoid function, then multiplied by 1.002, and finally, 0.001
863   is subtracted. These processed values serve as zero-order Spherical Harmonics coefficients for the
      3DGS.

1007   E     L IMITATIONS
1008
1009   While our method can generate high-quality 3D Gaussians, the inherent problems associated with
1010   3DGS are also present. For example, extracting clean meshes is not straightforward and usually
1011   requires multi-step post-processing. This issue can likely be mitigated in the near future given the
1012   fast development of Gaussians, either through new representations of Gaussians (Huang et al., 2024;
1013   Dai et al., 2024) or better ways to convert them to meshes (Wolf et al., 2024). Though our paper
1014   focuses on 3D object generation, another potential limitation is that the tri-plane representation is
1015   usually limited by resolution size and cannot be easily used for large scene generation.
1016

1018   F     FAILURE CASES
1019
1020   We present some failure cases of our candidate view generation and selection pipeline in fig. 8.
1021   A notable failure occurs when the input image contains floaters or small transparent objects. This
1022   leads to incorrect generation results, especially at different elevation angles. The view selection
1023   pipeline only partially removes these incorrect results. Additionally, our view selection pipeline can
1024   sometimes produce incorrect results, particularly with objects containing thin geometries. These
1025   thin components can contribute less to feature matching, making them more susceptible to incorrect
       selection.

       G     I MPLICATIONS FOR FUTURE RESEARCH
1081
1082
       Feed-forward 3D generation. The key insight is that we introduced a series of methods to handle
1083   imperfect multi-view synthesis results in the common two-stage 3D generation pipeline. Our whole
1084   Flex3D pipeline introduces little computational cost but yields significant performance and robust-
1085   ness gains, and it could serve as a common design pipeline for future research in 3D generation.
1086   Additionally, all individual components proposed in this work can be easily adopted by future re-
1087   search in 3D generation to improve performance. Similarly, design ideas analogous to the Flex3D
1088   pipeline could be readily adopted for large 3D scene generation.
1089   Feed-forward 4D generation. Moreover, our work could be beneficial for 4D generation, which is
1090   an even more challenging task that faces similar limitations to two-stage 3D generation pipelines.
1091   Our pipeline could be directly extended to handle 4D object generation tasks. One could first gen-
1092   erate 64 views (16 time dimensions × 4 multi-views) by fine-tuning video-based diffusion models,
1093   then slightly modify the view selection pipeline to keep only those views consistent across multiple
1094   views and time dimensions. Then, extend FlexRM from a tri-plane to a hex-plane or additionally
1095   learn time offsets to enable 4D representation. This should yield a strong method for 4D asset
1096   generation.
1097   Leveraging 3D understanding for generation : Keypoint matching techniques are used in this
1098   work to effectively mitigate multi-view inconsistencies. We hope this will also inspire the 3D
1099   generation community to incorporate advanced techniques from the rapidly evolving field of 3D
1100   understanding. Recent advances in deep learning have led to significant developments in match-
1101   ing, tracking, deep structure from motion, and scene reconstruction. These advancements offer the
1102   3D generation community useful tools (such as pose estimation and keypoint matching), pseudo-
1103
       supervision signals (e.g.,pseudo-depth supervision), and new model design ideas.

1105   H     A DDITIONAL RELATED WORK
1106
1107   H.1   M ITIGATING MULTI - VIEW INCONSISTENCY WITH FEEDBACK .
1108
1109   Methods such as Ouroboros3D (Wen et al., 2024), Carve3D (Xie et al., 2024b), Cycle3D (Tang
1110
       et al., 2024c), and IM-3D (Melas-Kyriazi et al., 2024) stem from similar motivations to our work,
       and they share a key idea: the feedback mechanism. While useful, these methods often require
1111
       new supervision signals and learnable parameters to implement this feedback, potentially creating
1112
       complex, monolithic pipelines that are difficult to decompose into reusable components for future
1113   designs. In contrast, Flex3D’s components are more easily generalized. Another key difference is
1114   Flex3D’s focus on the input to the reconstructor. This adds negligible computational cost and avoids
1115   the significant additional time required for multi-step refinement, preserving the speed advantage
1116   often associated with feed-forward models. Additionally, the feedback mechanism is orthogonal to
1117   our work and could be further combined with it if needed.
