009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across various
012            tasks, primarily attributed to the utilization of diversely sourced data. However,
013            the impact of pretraining data composition on model performance remains poorly
014            understood. This paper introduces B I M IX, a novel bivariate data mixing law that
015            models the joint scaling behavior of domain proportions and data volume in LLM
016            pretraining. B I M IX provides a systematic framework for understanding and opti-
017            mizing data mixtures across diverse domains. Through extensive experiments on
018
               two large-scale datasets, we demonstrate B I M IX’s high accuracy in loss extrapo-
               lation (mean relative error <0.2%) and its generalization to unseen mixtures (R2
019
               >0.97). Optimization of domain proportions yields superior model performance
020
               compared to existing methods. Furthermore, we establish entropy-based measures
021            as efficient proxies for data mixing, offering a computationally lightweight strat-
022            egy. Our work contributes both insights into data mixing dynamics and practical
023            tools for enhancing LLM training efficiency, paving the way for more effective
024            scaling strategies in language model development.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable success, revolutionizing capabilities for
030   comprehending and generating human-like text across diverse applications, from question answering
031
      to code generation (Bubeck et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2024). As these models scale up, the composition
      of pretraining data becomes increasingly crucial for performance and generalization (Longpre et al.,
032
      2023). The emergence of multi-source datasets has presented both opportunities and challenges in
033
      LLM development (Gao et al., 2020; Shen et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024), necessitating a deeper
034   understanding of data mixing strategies.
035
036   Current approaches to data mixing often rely on heuristics (Touvron et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023)
037
      or computationally expensive optimization techniques (Du et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2023; Fan et al.,
      2023). While these methods have shown promise, they require significant computational resources
038
      and lack a general framework for understanding the scaling behavior of mixed-domain training. The
039
      absence of a systematic approach to data mixing hinders efficient resource allocation and limits the
040   ability to predict model performance across varied data compositions. Recent efforts have explored
041   related techniques (Xia et al., 2024; Albalak et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023), yet comprehensive and
042   efficient solutions remain elusive.
043
      The fundamental challenge lies in the complex interplay between different data domains in multi-
044
      source datasets. Existing research (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022) primarily focuses
045
      on scaling laws for individual metrics, overlooking this crucial aspect. This oversight hampers the
046   progress towards more versatile models capable of excelling across multiple domains (Dong et al.,
047   2024). A systematic mixing law remains to be developed to efficiently assess the importance of
048   diverse data sources and understand their impact on model performance.
049
      To address these challenges and fill the gap in current research, we introduce B I M IX, a novel bi-
050
      variate data mixing law that provides a systematic framework for understanding and optimizing data
051
      mixtures in LLM pretraining. Our approach is rooted in the observation that the scaling behavior
052   of LLMs can be disentangled into two key components: domain mixing proportions and training
053   data quantity (embodied by model training steps). By mathematically formulating the relationship


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      between these components and model performance, B I M IX offers a powerful tool for predicting
055   and optimizing training outcomes.
056
057   We validate the proposed mixing law on two large-scale datasets, demonstrating its applicability
058
      across various scaling scenarios. Our experiments show that B I M IX not only provides accurate
      predictions of model performance across different data mixtures but also enables optimization of
059
      domain proportions, outperforming existing high-cost methods in terms of convergence speed and
060
      downstream task performance.
061
062   The key contributions are summarized as follows:
063   • A mathematically formulated mixing law that jointly models the scaling behavior of domain pro-
064     portions and total training volume, providing good interpretability and functional extensibility.
065
      • Comprehensive experiments demonstrating the effectiveness of B I M IX in predicting and optimiz-
066     ing model performance across diverse datasets and training scenarios.
067
      • Empirical evidence supporting the use of entropy-based measures as lightweight mixing proxies,
068
        offering new insights into efficient data mixture optimization.

071   2   R ELATED W ORK
072
073   Pretraining Data Mixtures The coverage and diversity of pretraining data play significant roles
074
      in shaping the generalization capabilities of language models (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al.,
      2020; Touvron et al., 2023). Data mixtures from multiple sources, such as the Pile (Gao et al., 2020)
075
      and ROOTS (Laurençon et al., 2022), are typically curated based on manually devised rules. How-
076
      ever, the heuristics lack universal standards and portability. The GLaM dataset (Du et al., 2022) de-
077   termined domain weights based on the component performance in a small model; however, specific
078   details are not disclosed. SlimPajama-DC (Shen et al., 2023) investigated the effects of data mix-
079   tures using a set of predefined configurations and delivered several insights. Recently, DoReMi (Xie
080   et al., 2023) and DoGE (Fan et al., 2023) proposed learning-based methods to optimize domain pro-
081   portions by iterating between training reference and proxy models. These methods provide viable
082   pathways but require considerable computational costs. In contrast, our study demonstrates that
083   entropy proxies can produce data mixtures of comparable or even superior quality, while providing
084   a more practical training-free solution. Besides, Chen et al. (2023) explored the effects of data se-
085
      quencing from a curriculum learning perspective, whereas our research focuses on the concurrent
      integration of diverse data domains.
086
087   Neural Scaling Laws Investigations into the scaling behavior of neural models have spanned
088   across domains such as computer vision (Klug & Heckel, 2023; Zhai et al., 2022; Jain et al., 2023;
089   Sorscher et al., 2022) and natural language processing (Ivgi et al., 2022; Gordon et al., 2021; Ghor-
090   bani et al., 2022; Bansal et al., 2022). Kaplan et al. (2020) thoroughly evaluated the scalability of
091
      Transformer architectures across a wide range of model sizes and data volumes. Chinchilla (Hoff-
      mann et al., 2022) identified similar scaling patterns through rigorous experimentation and sug-
092
      gested a slightly different configuration for compute-optimal pretraining. The impactful GPT-4
093
      model (OpenAI, 2024) validated the predictive accuracy of scaling laws and underscored their im-
094   portant role in the development of large language models. Concurrently, additional research efforts
095   seek to elucidate the principles governing scaling laws (Sharma & Kaplan, 2022; Michaud et al.,
096   2023) and to investigate scaling effects on downstream tasks (Tay et al., 2022; Isik et al., 2024; Ca-
097   ballero et al., 2023; Cherti et al., 2023). In the context of data mixtures, Ye et al. (2024) proposed
098   a composite exponential law to capture the interactions among domains; yet, its scalability is chal-
099   lenged by increased complexity for expanding domain numbers, as compared in Appendix C. Our
100   study is distinguished by two key aspects: First, we introduce a scalable mixing law that accurately
101   captures the scaling behavior associated with the composition of training datasets, demonstrating
102
      the modeling ability to up to 22 domains. Second, the proposed bivariate mixing law jointly models
      two input variables, domain proportion and data volume, thereby offering broader applicability.

105   3   T HE P ROPOSED B I M IX
106
107   Existing scaling law research primarily investigates the variation of a single scalar metric related to
      trained models concerning certain scaling factors. A prominent example is the relationship between


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      a model’s validation loss and the amount of parameters or training tokens. However, in practice,
109   the training datasets for large language models encompass diverse data domains, and these scaling
110   laws only capture the predictability of the averaged validation loss across multiple domains. Since
111   each domain corresponds to vastly different corpora, knowledge, and formats, modeling solely the
112   average provides a coarse estimate of the model’s performance and fails to reflect the predictability
113   of individual domains.
114
      In the context of data-centric language modeling, this study examines the scaling behavior of pre-
115   trained models across finer-grained data domains. Notably, simply applying existing scaling laws
116   to each domain is inappropriate, as the amount of training data for one single domain is not an in-
117   dependent variable; rather, it is determined by the total amount of training data and the proportion
118   allocated to that domain, calculated as |Di | = |D| × ri . The training data for a specific domain can
119   be adjusted either by changing the total training data or by modifying the allocated proportion. Con-
120   sequently, the data mixing modeling inherently involves a bivariate joint effect. Moreover, when the
121   total amount of training data is fixed, any change in the proportion allocated to one domain will also
122   affect the proportions (and thus the training amounts) of all other domains. This interdependence
123
      among domains highlights the need for dedicated research on the scaling laws of data mixing.

      4   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
271
272
      Datasets We employed two well-recognized datasets spanning diverse domains to conduct com-
273   prehensive experiments. The Pile (Gao et al., 2020) is a diverse language modeling dataset com-
274   prising 22 subsets with a total of 825 GiB of textual data. SlimPajama (Shen et al., 2023) is a
275   high-quality, seven-domain dataset that has been rigorously deduplicated and refined to 627B tokens
276   from the extensive 1.2T RedPajama dataset (Together Computer, 2023). Following the preprocess-
277   ing procedures in Xie et al. (2023), we packed all samples within each domain and chunked them
278   into sequences of 1024 tokens for improved training efficiency.
279   Model Architecture We employed decoder-only transformers based on the DoReMi architec-
280   ture (Xie et al., 2023). The base model comprises 12 decoder blocks, each with 768-dimensional
281   embeddings, 12 attention heads, and 4× MLP hidden size, matching the DoReMi 280M specifi-
282   cations. For scaled-up experiments on optimized mixtures, we expanded to 16 blocks with 2048-
283   dimensional embeddings and 32 attention heads, aligning with the DoReMi 1B model. All models
284   use the GPT-NeoX tokenizer (Black et al., 2022) with a vocabulary size of 50,277.
285   Training Details Experiments were conducted under controlled hyperparameters. Each training
286   run consisted of up to 200,000 update steps with a global batch size of 512. The optimizer used
287   was AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.99, ϵ = 1 × 10−8 , and a weight
288   decay of 0.01. The learning rate, initialized at 1 × 10−3 , decayed exponentially by a factor of 10×
289   over the course of training. We leveraged data parallelism across eight NVIDIA A100 80GB GPUs
290   and bfloat16 mixed precision to improve training throughput. As a reference for the training cost, a
291   single round of DoReMi training took 670 GPU hours on this infrastructure.
292   Fitting Details As noted previously, we trained models for up to 200,000 update steps on each
293   data mixture, which corresponds to approximately 100 billion tokens. Model evaluation results were
294   collected every 5 billion tokens, allowing for a maximum of 20 assessments, denoted by symbol n.
295   During the training of the model on a given data mixture, validation losses for all domains can
296   be obtained simultaneously, with m representing the number of domains. When experiments are
297   conducted on k different data mixtures, we can potentially gather up to m × n × k data points in the
298   form of tuples ⟨ri , s, Li ⟩ for fitting B I M IX. For the Pile dataset, m = 22, while for SlimPajama,
299
      m = 7. Upon collecting the observational data points, we employ the Trust Region Reflective
      algorithm (Branch et al., 1999; Virtanen et al., 2020) to fit the coefficients in Eqs. (2) and (3).
300
301   Evaluation Metrics The investigated mixing law is fundamentally a specific form of scaling law,
302   widely recognized for its ability to describe the predictability of model loss. Following the prevalent
303   consensus in relevant literature, we primarily report the model’s validation loss on each domain,
304   which is sometimes referred to as log-perplexity. To provide a more intuitive understanding of the
305
      model’s actual performance, we also evaluate model performance on downstream NLP tasks when
      necessary. The benchmarks from DoReMi are utilized for generative question-answering, specifi-
306
      cally WebQuestions (Berant et al., 2013), LAMBADA (Paperno et al., 2016), and TriviaQA (Joshi
307
      et al., 2017). We employ the same one-shot prompting and Exact Match metric as used in DoReMi.
308   A response is considered correct if and only if the characters of the model’s prediction exactly match
309   those of the True Answer.
310

312
      number of iterations. We consider the following three types of mixtures:
313
314   (a) Baseline: Represents the original proportions of the datasets, reflecting the intentions of the
315       dataset creators or the inherent distribution of the data collection process.
316   (b) DoReMi (Xie et al., 2023): This approach tunes domain weights by iteratively training reference

321       non entropy (SE), conditional entropy (CE), joint entropy (JE), and von Neumann entropy

      5.2                    E STIMATING DATA M IXTURES WITHOUT T RAINING
379
380   When training language models on multi-source data, determining the appropriate mixing propor-
381   tions for different domains remains a persistent challenge for practitioners. The naive approach is
382   trial and error, where a few random data mixtures are generated to train models and select the best-

387

510   6        D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
511
512   Scaling laws model the empirical behavior of model outcomes with respect to certain variables, typ-
513   ically effective within a limited observational range. The applicability of our proposed mixing law
514   under extreme conditions is not guaranteed. In our experiments, domain proportions ranged from
515   0.0007 to 0.7256, with maximum training data capped at approximately 100B tokens. However,
516
      existing research suggests that loss predictability may extend to larger scales (Kaplan et al., 2020;
      Hoffmann et al., 2022). Our study adheres to settings aligned with relevant work, ensuring consis-
517
      tent domain inclusion during both model training and evaluation (Xie et al., 2023). When evaluating
518
      the trained model on new domains, the model’s generalization capability and the correlation be-
519   tween new and training domains become primary considerations. This aspect, extending beyond the
520   basic mixing laws studied in this work, warrants dedicated exploration. Our work contributes to the
521   limited research on mixing laws, aiming to establish a foundation for more comprehensive studies.
522
      Reflecting on broader implications, our findings on mixing laws can assist practitioners in optimiz-
523
      ing computational resource allocation, promoting advancements in economical and environmentally
524
      friendly AI development. This vision is also relevant to the rapidly evolving field of multimodal
525   large models (McKinzie et al., 2024), where processing images, videos, or audio may consume
526   significant computational power. Consequently, exploring the mixing of multimodal training data
527   presents vast opportunities for enhancing model efficiency and performance. Future work could
528   focus on extending our mixing law framework to multimodal contexts, potentially leading to more
529   efficient and effective training paradigms for next-generation AI models.

      7        C ONCLUSION
532
533
      This paper introduces B I M IX, a bivariate data mixing law for language model pretraining. B I M IX
534   accurately models the joint scaling behavior of domain proportions and training volume, enabling
535   precise loss extrapolation and generalization to different mixtures. Our experiments demonstrate its
536   effectiveness in optimizing domain proportions, outperforming existing methods. Additionally, we
537   show that entropy-based measures serve as efficient proxies for lightweight data mixing. By offering
538   a nuanced understanding of data mixing dynamics, this research contributes to the development of
539   more efficient large-scale language models and opens avenues for further exploration in data-centric
      machine learning.

      domain language modeling. The exponential law, Li (r1...m ), operates on all domain proportions
865   (ri , r2 , . . . , rm ) without considering training steps, while our mixing law incorporates both domain
866   proportion r and training steps s. We analyze complexity across three scenarios, progressing from
867   simple to complex.
868
869   Base Case: Fitting an Individual Domain. The exponential law requires m + 2 fitting coeffi-
870   cients, aggregating proportions across m domains with m weighting coefficients tij , plus scaling
871   coefficient ki and translation coefficient ci . Our bivariate mixing law simplifies to Eq. (8) for fixed
872   training steps, needing only two coefficients: a scaling factor Ãi and an exponent α̃i .
873
874   General Case: Fitting All Domains. For m domains, both mixing law’s coefficient requirement
875   scales by m, while our mixing law maintains an order of magnitude fewer coefficients.
876
877   Extensive Case: Fitting Across Multiple Targets. The exponential law, not accounting for vari-
878   able s, requires (m2 + 2m)n coefficients for n training step targets. Our bivariate mixing law,
879
      incorporating both domain proportions and training steps, shares fitting coefficients across various
      training steps. Specifically, only five coefficients are required to fit an individual domain across
880
      different training steps; this number scales linearly to 5m when generalized to all m domains.
881
882   Overall, Our bivariate scaling law consistently requires significantly fewer coefficients compared
883   to the composite exponential law in Ye et al. (2024). This reduction translates to fewer required
884   observations for effective fitting, enabling our law to be fit with just a few (potentially as few as
885
      two) candidate mixtures, whereas the exponential law needs tens of mixtures. The computational
      efficiency of our method offers economic and environmental benefits through reduced resource uti-
886
      lization, while simultaneously achieving better data mixtures and enhanced model performance, as
887
      demonstrated in Section 5.3.
