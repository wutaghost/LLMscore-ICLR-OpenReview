009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural networks are frequently deployed on multiple unseen target domains, which
012            are distributionally different from the source domain on which the model is trained.
013            Data augmentation is the most popular tool for single source domain generaliza-
014            tion, which expands the source domain by generating simulated ones, commonly
015            adopted by existing approaches. In this work, we observe that the performance
016
               of such augmentation-based methods in the target domains frequently fluctuates
               during training, posing challenges in model selection under realistic scenarios. We
017
               argue that the fluctuation stems from the inability of the model to accumulate the
018
               knowledge learned from diverse augmentations, exacerbating feature distortion
019            during training. Based on this observation, we propose a novel generalization
020            method, coined Parameter-Space Ensemble with Entropy Regularization (PEER),
021            that uses a proxy model to learn the augmented data on behalf of the main model.
022            The main model is updated by averaging its parameters with the proxy model,
023            progressively accumulating knowledge over the training steps. Maximizing the mu-
024            tual information between the output representations of the two models guides the
025            learning process of the proxy model, mitigating feature distortion during training.
026            Extensive experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of PEER in reducing
027
               the OOD performance fluctuation and enhancing generalization across various
               datasets, including PACS, Digits, Office-Home, and VLCS. Notably, our method
028
               with simple random augmentation achieves state-of-the-art performance, surpassing
029
               prior approaches on sDG that utilize complex data augmentation strategies.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
098
099
      Domain generalization. In the multi-source domain generalization (DG) literature, learning domain-
100
      invariant features has shown success in training robust models (Arjovsky et al., 2019). Specifically,
101
      these algorithms aim to disentangle the knowledge shared across domains (Klindt et al., 2021; Ren
102   et al., 2021). A recent line of work highlighted the use of pre-trained models for model-to-model
103   regularization, e.g., Cha et al. (2022) used an external pre-trained model to encourage the learning of
104   domain-invariant features, and Li et al. (2023) expanded this approach by using multiple pre-trained
105   models. In contrast, we refrain from using an external model and show that a training model can
106   effectively perform regularization. On a different note, Arpit et al. (2022) studied the instability of the
107   model’s OOD performance and suggested an ensemble algorithm to alleviate the stochastic nature of
      the learning process. In contrast, we relieve the computational burden of ensembles by using a single


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      parameter-averaged model (Ainsworth et al., 2023; Rame et al., 2022; Jolicoeur-Martineau et al.,
109   2023) and incorporate an alignment strategy (Choshen et al., 2022; Frankle et al., 2020) to assist this.
110
111   Single source domain generalization. In the sDG setting, only one domain is available for training,
112
      which makes it hard to apply conventional approaches developed for DG. To tackle this, a line of
      work focused on generating diverse domains using sophisticated data augmentation strategies, e.g.,
113
      adversarial augmentation (Volpi et al., 2018b) or learnable augmentation modules (Fan et al., 2021;
114
      Qiao et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021b; Xu et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024). On the other
115   hand, we reveal a universal phenomenon (i.e., mid-train OOD fluctuation) associated with utilizing
116   data augmentation for generalization, and present a simple strategy to alleviate it.
117
118
      Mode connectivity and parameter-space ensembles. Our work draws inspiration from the mode
      connectivity (Frankle et al., 2020) property of neural networks, which refers to the presence of
119
      a continuous manifold of non-increasing error that connects the minima identified by two global
120
      minimizers (i.e., trained models) (Garipov et al., 2018; Lubana et al., 2023). The concept is com-
121   monly used to justify how individual models can be merged to produce parameter-space ensembles
122   (Wortsman et al., 2022; Rame et al., 2022) and also form the basis for designing model alignment
123   methods to encourage mode connectivity between models (Entezari et al., 2021; Choshen et al., 2022;
124   Ainsworth et al., 2023; Ramé et al., 2023). To analyze mode connectivity between models, a common
125   practice is to measure the loss barrier (Frankle et al., 2020), quantified as the rise in loss values when
126   the parameters of two models are averaged. Extending this, we suggest an effective alignment method
127   to encourage mode connectivity between models trained with varying augmented data.

137   3.1   AUGMENT- AND -A LIGN : AUGMENTATION - BASED A PPROACHES TO S DG
138
139   Let DS = {(xi , yi )}N  i=1 be a source domain where xi ∈ X is an input image and yi ∈ Y is its
140   corresponding label. The goal of sDG is to build a model F from DS that is capable of generalizing
                                       (1)       (t)
141   to unknown target domains {DT , · · · , DT } distributionally different from the source domain. The
142   model F = C ◦ H consists of a feature extractor H : X → H and the classifier C : H → Y. Clearly,
143   the classifier relying on the domain-specific features would not generalize to unseen target domains,
144   and thus it is crucial to learn domain-invariant features from the source domain.
145   Existing approaches utilize data augmentation to simulate domain shift and aim to extract domain-
146   invariant features by aligning the feature distribution between the original sample x and its augmented
147   view x̄ = G(x), where G is the augmentation function. The objective of such augmentation-based
148   sDG approaches, omitting some arguments for simplicity, can be written as:

      strategies (Wang et al., 2021b; Li et al., 2021) to simulate diverse shifts in distribution.

179   3.2         M ID - TRAIN OOD FLUCTUATION OF AUGMENTATION -BASED S DG M ETHODS
180
181   Recall Fig. 1, we find that augmentation-based sDG methods commonly exhibit large fluctuation
182   of OOD performance throughout training, dubbed mid-train OOD fluctuation. Then, the following
183
      questions naturally arise: “How does the fluctuation relate to the generalization performance? Where
      does the fluctuation stem from?” Here, we investigate the relationships between the fluctuation and
184
      target domain accuracy through the lens of source-target dataset distance and examine the impact of
185
      data augmentation on the fluctuation.
186
187   We begin by observing that the target domain accuracy is closely related to the mid-train OOD
188   fluctuation by comparing two augmentation-based sDG methods: random augmentation (RandAug,
189
      Cubuk et al. (2020)) and adversarial augmentation (AdvAug, Li et al. (2021)). As shown in the last
      column (Avg.) of Table 1-(a) and (b), the models with better generalization performance also display
190
      larger fluctuation. Clearly, the complexity of the data augmentation the models employed aligns with
191
      the target domain accuracy and fluctuation.
192
193   To further investigate their relationships, we adopt a similarity metric that measures the geometric
194   distance between datasets (i.e., OTDD (Alvarez-Melis & Fusi, 2020)). By comparing different target
195
      domains (e.g., SVHN and USPS), we observe that the source-target discrepancy shown in Table 1-(c)
      is closely associated with the target domain accuracy and fluctuation. In other words, the models
196
      exhibit relatively small fluctuation on the target domain that is similar to the source domain (i.e.,
197
      USPS) and vice versa (i.e., SVHN). Similarly, the models tend to show higher accuracy on target
198   domains with smaller discrepancies (i.e., USPS) and vice versa (i.e., SVHN).
199
200   To better understand our observations above, we examine the discrepancy between the original
201
      dataset (MNIST) and its augmented view across varying degrees of random augmentation (Cubuk
      et al., 2020). As shown in Fig. 3, we observe that the discrepancy becomes more significant as the
202
      augmentation becomes diverse and its magnitude becomes stronger. Notably, such discrepancies
203
      often even exceed the source-target distance (i.e., 0.92 in Table 1-(c)).
204
205   Our observations suggest that data augmentation improves generalization capacity by simulating
206   diverse domain shifts, but at the same time, it leads to the distortion of the learned representations and
207
      triggers mid-train OOD fluctuation, as depicted in Fig. 2. Based on our findings, we now proceed to
      present our method that effectively retains knowledge accumulated throughout the training, thereby
208
      alleviating fluctuations while achieving better generalization performance.

315
           PEER as mutual information (MI) maximization. The idea of PEER is that we can leverage the
316
           frozen task model to regularize the proxy model by maximizing the shared information between
317
           the two models. PEER aims to maximize the MI between the intermediate output features of the
318        two encoders Hf and Hp . The entropy regularization aligns the proxy model to the task model,
319        preventing the proxy model from deviating too far from the task model. From this perspective, an
320        intended objective for PEER could be formulated as maxH I(Hf (x̄); Hp (x)) where I(X; Y ) =
321        Ep(x,y) [log p(x | y)/p(x)] indicates the mutual information (MI). PEER uses a feature decorrelation
322        loss Eq. (3) (Zbontar et al., 2021) to maximize the lower bound of MI as a surrogate objective for MI
323        optimization under a Gaussian assumption (Tsai et al., 2021). We further elaborate on the adequacy
           of feature decorrelation loss for MI optimization in Appendix A and report experimental results

338   Datasets. Following prior works (Li et al., 2021; Wan et al., 2022), we evaluate our method on two

343

      remaining domains.
345
346   We also include Office-Home (Venkateswara et al., 2017) and VLCS (Fang et al., 2013), challenging
347   benchmarks for sDG methods. Office-Home is a common multi-DG benchmark consisting of 4
348   datasets (Real-world, Art, Clipart, Product) with differing styles with 65 classes. We train on the
349
      Real-world domain and evaluate with the remaining domains. VLCS is also a benchmark for multi-
      DG, comprised of 4 datasets, PASCAL-VOC (V), LabelMe (L), Caltech-101 (C), and SUN09 (S)

      2022), which learns generalized meta-features.

357   Specifically, we used AlexNet and multi-layer CNN for PACS and Digits, respectively, following

530    6                          C ONCLUSION
531
532   This paper presents PEER, a novel generalization method to address the issues of augmentation-based
533   approaches to single source domain generalization. We highlight the feature distortion induced by
534   augmentation, which triggers fluctuations in the target domain performance during training. Based on
535   our observations, we propose a parameter-averaged task model that accumulates the generalization
536   effect of the training proxy model. Entropy regularization on their learned feature representation
537
      aligns the two models, addressing feature distortion. Experiments on various datasets (PACS, Digits,
      Office-Home, VLCS) demonstrate the effectiveness of our method in stabilizing the learning process
538
      and enhancing the generalization performance.

      A    S TUDY ON M ODEL - TO -M ODEL R EGULARIZATION
811
812
813   In this section, we further study the topic of model-to-model regularization. We first begin by
814
      revisiting previous works on model-to-model regularization, highlighting the differences from our
      approach. Next, we provide experimental results on using a pre-trained teacher for regularization.
815
      Using this, we show the strength of our approach against previous model-to-model regularization
816
      methods.
817
818
819   Previous Methods: Using a teacher for regularization. Model-to-model regularization is fre-
820   quently used to boost a model’s performance in tasks such as knowledge distillation (Hinton et al.,
821   2015; Beyer et al., 2022) or generalization (Cha et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023). Here, an underlying
822
      idea is that the supervisor (i.e. teacher) be a model displaying strong performance, namely OOD
      robustness. A common approach is to use a pre-trained model that is trained on a large dataset, or
823
      with a larger model architecture. However, there exist issues in deploying strong teacher models for
824
      the sDG task. First, using pre-trained teacher models contradicts the grounding idea of single source
825   domain generalization (sDG). To our understanding, the goal of sDG is to devise a generalization
826   method that can function well in a realistic environment where the source data is limited. Reflecting
827   this, the sDG setting strictly forbids the use of additional source domains for training. In this sense,
828   using a model that is already trained on a much larger dataset seems to go against this. Furthermore,
829   if the teacher model is available for use, a more efficient method would be to directly utilize the
830   teacher for inference, while its operating cost would be much larger.
831
832
      Our Method: Using a group of PEER for regularization. Our approach to model-to-model
833   regularization alleviates the irony of using a pre-trained teacher model by replacing it with a parameter-
834   space ensemble (task model F ). Unlike previous approaches (Cha et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023), the
835   PEER does not violate the constraints of the sDG setting. Specifically, the task model in PEER does not
836   use additional training data as it is the training model itself. Second, it is of an identical architecture to
837   the training proxy model, hence we need not worry about excessive computation costs. Furthermore,
838   using a task model regulator of the identical architecture allows the proxy model to directly update
839   the task model via parameter-averaging, without additional cost. On the other hand, when using a
840   pre-trained teacher model, updating the teacher would require excessive gradient computation (e.g.,
841
      online distillation (Gou et al., 2021)).
842   More importantly, our approach to model-to-model regularization is more easily applicable to real-
843   world problems than using a pre-trained teacher, owing to the adaptive nature of the task model. In
844   PEER, the task model is created during the training process. Hence, the task model effortlessly adapts
845   to the new dataset. This adaptivity makes PEER applicable to any given task or dataset. On the other
846   hand, a teacher is a fixed model that is supposedly pre-trained on large datasets. The fixed nature of
847
      the teacher limits its applicability, as the teacher would only work if the teacher’s pre-trained data is
      similar to the new training data. For instance, a strong digit classification (Deng, 2012) model will
848
      not function well as a teacher for the image classification task (Li et al., 2023).
849
850
851   Experiment: PEER vs. Teacher In this section, provide detailed information on our experimental
852   results reported in Sec. 5.3.1, and emphasize the competitiveness of PEER against using a strong
853   teacher model for regularization. Specifically, we demonstrate that a task model in PEER serves
854   as a more robust regulator compared to a pre-trained teacher model. Specifically, we empirically
855
      show that a suitable teacher model is not always available. For analysis, we use the PACS and Digits
      datasets and compare three model-to-model regularization methods (1) None: The baseline without
856
      model-to-model regularization (2) Teacher: Following the practice of Cha et al. (2022), we selected
857
      the pre-trained RegNetY-16GF (Radosavovic et al., 2020) as a teacher for PACS. In contrast, in Digits,
858   we could not obtain a pre-trained model fit for use as the teacher. Hence, we follow the practice of
859   Cha et al. (2022) and use a model pre-trained on both the source and target domains of Digits. We
860   will later elaborate on why the RegNetY-16GF does not apply to the Digits experiment. (3) PEER:
861   The task model in PEER has the same architecture as the proxy model. At the beginning of training,
862   it is identical to the proxy model and then updated during the training process by averaging the
863   parameters of the proxy model and the task model. The model is trained with random augmentation
      and follows the setup stated in Sec. 5.

875
      model, and hence less likely to share the issues exhibited by the teacher. This pattern is repeated in
876   the Digits experiment at Table 5, where the teacher was slightly better in reducing the fluctuation,

884   We find that for certain tasks, a teacher model is hard to obtain. In other words, there is no universal
885
      model for use as the teacher. For instance, in the PACS experiment, the RegNetY-16GF displayed
      sufficient capabilities as a model-to-model regularize. However, using the RegNetY-16GF as the
886
      teacher for the Digits experiment was not available. Notably, RegNetY-16GF marked low validation
887
      accuracy in the target domain, nor was it able to guide the proxy model. We believe that this difference
888   is derived from the discrepancy between the two datasets. For instance, PACS is a collection of
889   images without any distortion, while Digits is a dataset solely comprised of digit images. Hence,
890   we view that the large gap between the pre-trained dataset of the RegNetY-16GF and the Digit
891   classification datasets is responsible for this behavior. This issue can be explained with the work of
892   Wolpert & Macready (1997), where the authors demonstrate that there exists a trade-off between a
893   model’s performance on a certain task and the performance on all remaining tasks. In contrast, the
894   PEER is applicable to any task, as it gradually adapts to the dataset using the proxy model.

899   B.1   D ISCUSSION ON THE FLUCTUATION
900
901   We illustrate the mid-train OOD fluctuation in Fig. 1. Here, the worst-case performance of the
902   fluctuating model (red) consistently falls below that of the stable model (blue). This describes the
903   issues of deploying a fluctuating model, as the fluctuation poses challenges in early stopping and
904
      model selection.
905   Arpit et al. (2022) has studied a similar phenomenon within the multi-DG literature, attributing
906   the fluctuation to the stochastic nature of the learning process (e.g., random seed, order of data).
907   While we acknowledge the role of other contributing factors, we hypothesize that the mid-train
908   OOD fluctuation primarily stems from the model’s inability to accumulate the knowledge learned
909   from varying augmentations. In specific, we view that the model’s trained features are distorted, or
910
      forgotten during training (Kumar et al., 2022; Shi & Wang, 2024).

1047   Generally, for a parameter-averaged model to display ensemble effects, some conditions should be
1048   simultaneously met (Ramé et al., 2023). (1) Share an identical initialization: models that share an
1049   initialization backbone tend to display very low loss barriers, showing mode connectivity. (2) Trained
1050   on same data: Models trained on identical source data (Choshen et al., 2022) tend to display mode
1051   connectivity, while models trained on varying data commonly do not (Ainsworth et al., 2023). In
1052   our case, the first condition is already met, while the second condition may have been broken due
1053
       to the varying effects of data augmentation. Drawing from this, we hypothesize that the failure
       case above potentially derives from violating the second condition. In specific, we believe that
1054
       the discrepancy between two very different augmented domains breaks the alignment between the
1055
       model snapshots. In this sense, the PEER may help parameter-space ensembling by encouraging the
1056   regularized proxy model to align the newly augmented domain to the task model’s source domain
1057   Sec. 4.1. Unfortunately, the alignment of models in its loss landscape is a topic that has not yet been
1058   thoroughly analyzed from a theoretical perspective, especially for models with deep architectures.
1059   While our empirical analysis may provide some insight, we believe further research is required on
1060   this topic.

1164

1170

1171   training without data augmentation, followed by several augmentation-based sDG methods that use
1172   complex adversarial schemes to generate challenging augmentations (Qiao et al., 2020; Wang et al.,

1258   E.7   H YPERPARAMETERS
1259
       In this part, we state the hyperparameters used in our experiments.
1260
1261   λ is a balancing coefficient for LPEER , an objective adopting the feature-decorrelation loss introduced
1262   in Zbontar et al. (2021). We tuned λ using experimental results of the original paper and Tsai et al.
1263   (2021). In the original paper, the author reported the optimal value of the balancing term as 0.005,
1264
       which remains consistent under varying projection dimensions. We set this as a starting point for
       hyperparameter tuning. We find that if λ balances the off-diagonal term (i.e. redundancy reduction
1265
       term) and the diagonal term (i.e. alignment term) to a similar degree, no significant differences are
1266
       observed. Furthermore, switching λ to d1 ≈ 0.0001 showed no significant changes to the learning
1267   process. Here, d denotes the projection dimension of the regularization head R (regularization
1268   head output space). While we cannot guarantee an optimal value for λ, we set λ = 0.005 for our
1269   experiments using PEER.
1270
       k is an augmentation reinitialization criterion that performs two roles. (1) Augmentation reinitial-
1271
       ization: For every k epoch, the augmentation function is initialized. Here, reinitialization refers to
1272
       the change in augmentation policy. For instance, for random augmentation, reinitialization refers
1273   to the change in augmentation strength. Alternatively, for augmentation techniques that utilize a
1274   learnable module (Li et al., 2021), the reinitialization would refer to reinitializing the parameters of
1275   the augmentation module. The motive behind the reinitialization is to expose the proxy model with
1276   diverse augmentations, (2) PEER update: For every k epoch, the parameters of the proxy model P are
1277   used to update the task model by averaging their parameters.
1278
       Lastly, w is a hyperparameter used in Eq. (4), which balances the ERM objective and the regularization
1279   objective Eq. (2). As studied in Appendix D.1, w does not affect the performance of our method. We
1280   have set w as 2.0 based upon experimental results in Table 8.
