009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Watermarking of large language models (LLMs) generation embeds an imper-
012                ceptible statistical pattern within texts, making it algorithmically detectable. Wa-
013                termarking is a promising method for addressing potential harm and biases from
014                LLMs, as it enables traceability, accountability, and detection of manipulated con-
015                tent, helping to mitigate unintended consequences. However, for open-source
016                models, watermarking faces two major challenges: (i) incompatibility with fine-
017                tuned models (ii) vulnerability to fine-tuning attacks. In this work, we propose
018                WAPITI, a new method that transfers watermarking from base models to fine-
019
                   tuned models through parameter integration. To the best of our knowledge, we
                   propose the first watermark for fine-tuned open-source LLMs that preserves their
020
                   fine-tuned capabilities. Furthermore, our approach offers an effective defense
021
                   against fine-tuning attacks. We test our method on various model architectures and
022                watermarking strategies. Results demonstrate that our method can successfully in-
023                ject watermarks and is highly compatible with fine-tuned models. Additionally,
024                we offer an in-depth analysis of how parameter editing influences the watermark
025                strength and overall capabilities of the resulting models. 1

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   As large language models (LLMs; Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI et al., 2024) have been integrated
031   into numerous workflows and play an increasingly significant role in everyday life, controlling these
032   LLMs to prevent potential harm has become even more urgent. Watermarking offers a viable so-
033   lution by embedding traceable information in model outputs. It enables the identification of LLM-
034   generated content and can be used to trace back to the source model, serving as a methodological
035   foundation for regulatory oversight of language models.
036   The vast majority of the prior work on watermarks has focused on closed-source models (Kirchen-
037   bauer et al., 2024a; Aaronson, 2023; Kuditipudi et al., 2024), which are black boxes for users.
038   However, with the growing capabilities of open-source models (Touvron et al., 2023; Biderman
039   et al., 2023), the need for oversight of open-source models has become equally important. In other
040   words, effective watermarking regulation must take both closed-source and open-source models into
041   account to ensure comprehensive oversight and accountability.
042   Open-source models release their full parameters to users, and users can fully customize the gener-
043   ation process. Therefore, users can simply choose an unwatermarked decoding algorithm to evade
044   watermarking, thereby invalidating existing decoding-based watermarking methods. Gu et al. (2024)
045   proposed a parameter-based method that distills the model using watermarked generations. This
046   process, referred to as watermark distillation, ensures that the watermarks are retained within the
047   model parameters, preventing users from easily removing them.
048   However, we observe that this method (Gu et al., 2024) would impair the fine-tuned capabilities of
049   models, revealing it is not compatible with fine-tuned models. Additionally, watermark distillation
050   incurs significantly higher computational costs compared to typical fine-tuning. Furthermore, a
051   severe weakness of parameter-based watermarks is their vulnerability to fine-tuning attacks, where
052   malicious users fine-tune the watermarked models with unwatermarked datasets to eliminate their

      To address these limitations, we propose a new train-free2 watermarking strategy that transfers wa-
      termarks from base models to fine-tuned models (WAPITI, WAtermark Parameter InTegratIon) as
075
      shown in Figure 1. We discover that watermarking bears a similar effect on the output distribution of
076
      both base models and fine-tuned models. The core of our method involves embedding watermarks
077   into models through direct parameter editing, ensuring compatibility with fine-tuned models. Most
078   importantly, WAPITI effectively defends against fine-tuning attacks by binding watermarking with
079   the fine-tuning capabilities of the model.
080
      Our main contributions are as follows:
081
082                  • Problem. We identify the incompatibility between current parameter-based watermarking
083                    methods and fine-tuned models. Distillation leads to a rapid degradation of fine-tuning
084                    capabilities and fails to effectively apply watermarking to models.
085                  • Method. To the best of our knowledge, we propose the first watermarking for fine-tuned
086                    models (WAPITI) based on the fact that watermarking causes aligned distribution shift in
087                    both base models and fine-tuned models.
088                  • Analysis. We analyze the relationship between watermarking parameters and model per-
089                    formance, revealing how parameter-editing strength affects final outcomes. Furthermore,
090                    we establish the relationship between watermarks and the utility of WAPITI from a learn-
091                    ability perspective.
092                  • Evaluation. WAPITI achieves high detectability with an AUROC of 0.92 while maintain-
093                    ing near-identical performance on fine-tuning benchmarks for both the Llama-2-7B and
094                    Pythia-1.4B families, demonstrating its strong effectiveness and generality.

      2.1       D ECODING - BASED WATERMARKING
100
      Large Language Models are generally neural networks based on the transformer architecture, de-
101   noted as fθ : V ∗ → ∆(V), which maps a given prefix string x ∈ V ∗ to a probability distribution
102   over the vocabulary ∆(V) for predicting the next token, denoted as fθ ( · | x). The generation pro-
103   cess involves two main steps: logit generation followed by token sampling (Vaswani et al., 2023).
104
      Decoding-based watermarks are embedded in either stages of generation with the aim of guiding the
105
      output distribution toward a targeted direction, incorporating traceable information for detection.
106

      training.

      For instance, KGW (Kirchenbauer et al., 2024a) increases the frequency of specific tokens during
120
      the generation process, and the detector identifies the origin of a text based on the occurrence rate
121
      of these tokens. More specifically, a watermarking algorithm W employs a watermark key ϕ to
122   modify the original next-token distribution fθ ( · | x) into a watermarked version. The watermark
123   detector D, using the same watermark key ϕ, can then retrieve the embedded watermark information.
124   In general, given a text x and a watermark key ϕ, the detector D calculates a p-value for the null
125   hypothesis that the text x is unrelated to W and ϕ. A text is classified as model-generated if its
126   p-value falls below a predefined threshold.
127
      The key evaluation metrics of watermarking are: (i) Detectability: The watermark must ensure
128   that all content generated by the model can be reliably detected by the detector. (ii) Utility: The
129   integration of the watermark should not significantly interfere with the original capabilities of the
130   model. (iii) Security: The watermark should ensure that its hidden pattern within the text is difficult
131   to remove unless a substantial portion of the model output is significantly altered. And for open-
132   source models, the watermark cannot be removed without impairing their capabilities.
133
      Logit-based: KGW is a watermarking strategy applied directly to output logits of the model (Al-
134   gorithm 2 in Kirchenbauer et al. (2024a)). During the next token generation, the vocabulary is
135   pseudorandomly split into green and red lists based on the previous k tokens. When k = 0 (Zhao
136   et al., 2023), the green and red lists are fixed, and when k ≥ 1, the lists are determined by the previ-
137   ous context. The green list contains γ ∈ (0, 1) proportion of the entire vocabulary, and an additional
138   watermark shift δ is added to the logits of the tokens in the green list. This increases the probability
139   of the green tokens being selected in the final generation. During detection, the p-value is calculated
140   by checking whether the proportion of green list tokens exceeds the predefined γ.
141   Sampling-based: AAR is the Gumbel softmax scheme from Aaronson (2023), which is a special
142   sampling strategy. When generating xi , it hashes the previous k tokens using the key ϕ to generate
143   a pseudorandom score sequence ri for the entire vocabulary V where ri ∈ R|V| whose entries are
144   uniformly distributed in [0, 1]. Given the probability distribution pi ∈ ∆(V) of the next token xi ,
145   AAR uses Gumbel-Max sampling strategy: xi = arg maxj∈|V| (log pi,j − log(− log ri,j )) (Cane &
146   Luce, 1960), which introduces some randomness into the sampling stage by adding Gumbel noise
147   ri . This sampling strategy would result in watermarked texts having comparative higher score sums.
148   During detection, a larger score sum corresponds to a lower p-value against the null hypothesis.
149
150   2.2   W EIGHT- BASED WATERMARKING
151
152   Since the weights of open-source models are fully released, users can modify the decoding method
153   or apply any post-processing to the logits, making decoding-based watermarks easy to remove. The
154   most feasible approach3 for watermarking is to embed the watermark into the model parameters, en-
155   abling LLMs to generate watermarked text under natural sampling distribution. Current research (Gu
156   et al., 2024) has shown that LLMs can learn watermarks via distillation and generate detectable wa-
157   termarked texts. By using decoding-based watermark strategies to generate watermarked texts as
158   distillation data, Gu et al. (2024) has verified the learnability of multiple watermarks on Llama-2-7B
159
      and Pythia-1.4B models. However, we found that this parameter-based method is specifically de-

      Limitation of current weight-based watermarking. The current weight-based method enables
172
      the base model to generate watermarked texts via distillation. In this paper, we explore whether
173   the distillation-based approach is compatible with fine-tuned models. Specifically, we ask: can
174   watermark distillation retain the fine-tuned capabilities of the model while embedding the watermark
175   into the fine-tuned model? To address this question, we conduct a preliminary experiment.
176
      To obtain a watermarked fine-tuned model using watermark distillation, there are three possible
177
      approaches: (i) Distilling a fine-tuned model with watermarked content, (ii) Fine-tuning a distilled
178
      model that already contains a watermark, or (iii) Fine-tuning a base model using a watermarked fine-
179   tuning dataset. We use math-fine-tuned Llama-2-7B and decoding-based watermarking strategies to
180   obtain a watermarked math model. Detailed experimental setups can be found in Appendix A.
181
182
      Figure 2 compares the watermark detectability (measured by p-value) and fine-tuning utility of the
      resulting model from all three different approaches. The utility of the models on GSM8K drops
183
      sharply to nearly zero, and the output text shows poor detectability, with a p-value close to the
184
      baseline of 0.5.
185
186   To better understand this phenomenon, we further analyze the three approaches. The first two meth-
187   ods both involve two-phase fine-tuning, which, as studied in previous research, can lead to capability
188
      degradation or catastrophic forgetting (Wang et al., 2023).
189   For the third method, we believe it holds the most potential to enable the base model to learn math-
190   ematical capabilities while embedding the watermark content. Therefore, we focus on analyzing
191   the distillation data generated by the math-fine-tuned model and identify two main reasons: (i) The
192   quality of the watermarked math data is inferior to that of the original fine-tuning dataset. Table 4
193   presents several samples from the original benchmark dataset alongside the answers generated by
194
      the math model. Although the generated answers might still be correct, they often contain flawed
      procedures or random repetitive sequences. Such data can confuse the model and result in a perfor-
195
      mance decline. (ii) The quantity of watermarked math data is insufficient for the student model to
196
      learn the watermark effectively. As noted in Gu et al. (2024), approximately 1.3 million samples are
197   required for a distilled model to internalize the watermark. With only 7.3k samples in the GSM8K
198   training split and further filtering due to the 40% accuracy of model, our final dataset was just 0.6%
199   of the required size. The insufficient distillation data makes the generated outputs lack detectability.
200
      In a nutshell, our experiments demonstrate that current distillation-based watermarking is incom-
201
      patible with fine-tuned models. This is primarily due to the small size of most fine-tuning datasets,
202
      which are insufficient for distillation. Additionally, the quality of watermarked samples deteriorates
203   compared to the original ones, leading to a decline in the fine-tuning capabilities of model.
204
205   Universal distribution shift from watermarking.        The primary issue with the current weight-
206
      based method is the distillation phase, which underscores the need for a train-free approach to wa-
      termark fine-tuned models. To this end, we aim to investigate whether there are any similarities
207
      between the base models and fine-tuned models when watermarked.
208
209   To be specific, we analyze the n-gram distribution in the watermarked outputs of both the base and
210   fine-tuned models. According to watermarking schemes, n-gram could be the smallest meaningful
211   unit, making them a natural starting point. Check Appendix C for detailed justification.
212   Our experiment compares the n-gram distribution similarities between unwatermarked and water-
213   marked texts generated by the base model and fine-tuned model, respectively. We used Llama-2-7B
214   and the math fine-tuned Llama-2-7B (Agarwalla et al., 2024) to generate 640k samples with and
215   without watermarking. The watermark used were kgw-k1-gamma0.25-delta2 (Kirchenbauer et al.,
      2024a) and aar-k2 (Aaronson, 2023).

245

246

248   ing the frequency of watermarked n-grams in the final generation.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
327
328   In this section, we design experiments to evaluate the utility of WAPITI in two key aspects: water-
329   mark strength and fine-tuning ability, tested across various models and watermarking strategies.
330
      Watermark and hyperparameters. We experiment with two representative decoding-based wa-
331
      termarks, KGW and AAR, with different hyperparameters. To ensure a fair and consistent compari-
332
      son, we adopt the same watermarking hyperparameters as used by Gu et al. (2024). Specifically, for
333   KGW, we set k = {0, 1, 2}, γ = 0.25, and δ = {1, 2}; and for AAR, we use k = {2, 3, 4}. The
334   coefficient λFT for watermark parameter integration ranges from [0, 4].
335
336
      Dataset and model choices. To ensure the generalizability of WAPITI, we conduct experiments
      on two widely used LLM families: Llama-2-7B and Pythia-1.4B, which differ in both architecture
337
      and parameter Their popularity in the community further ensures that our experiments reflect real-
338
      world utility. We utilize the watermark-distilled base models from Gu et al. (2024).
339
340   Evaluation Procedure We evaluate model generation using samples from the RealNewsLike
341   subset of the C4 dataset (Raffel et al., 2023). Specifically, the evaluation sample size is 5,000, with
342
      a 50-token prompt and a sequence length of 200. We use temperature sampling with t = 1.
343   Building on the approach of Gu et al. (2024), we apply deduplication during post-processing to
344   remove repetitive generations, ensuring the validity of the final detectability results.
345
      Evaluation Metrics To test the compatibility of WAPITI with fine-tuned models, we focus on
346   three key fine-tuning capabilities: instruction-following, question answering, and math. We will
347   refer to corresponding fine-tuned models as Llama-chat, Llama-QA, Llama-gsm8k, and Pythia-
348   chat in the experiment results. Detailed information on the fine-tuned model selection can be
349   found in Appendix D. The benchmark datasets used are OpenWebText (Gokaslan & Cohen, 2019),
350   MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021), and GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), respectively.

353
354   Following the evaluation methods used in Kirchenbauer et al. (2024a), Kuditipudi et al. (2024), and
355   Gu et al. (2024), we evaluate the models on 5,000 samples drawn from the RealNewsLike subset of
356   the C4 dataset (Raffel et al., 2023). The evaluation includes the following metrics:
357   Watermark detectability. To assess watermark detectability, we compute the median p-value and
358   AUROC (Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve), which evaluates the ability to
359   distinguish between watermarked and unwatermarked content. The p-value is computed using the z-
360   score method. A lower p-value indicates stronger watermark detectability. The AUROC is calculated
361   using an equal number of human-generated texts and model-generated watermarked content, both
362   truncated to the same length for consistency.
363   Generation quality. Generation quality is evaluated using two metrics: perplexity and seq-rep-3
364   (Sequence Repetition for 3-grams). Perplexity provides an overall assessment of the generated text
365   and is calculated using Llama-2-13B. Seq-rep-3 measures repetition by calculating the proportion
366   of repeated trigrams (Welleck et al., 2019).
367
      Fine-tuning abilities. To assess whether WAPITI preserves the fine-tuned capabilities of models,
368   we evaluate the performance of WAPITI fine-tuned models on the following benchmarks: i) Ques-
369   tion Answering: We use the full MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021) dataset to assess the QA ability of
370   models. This dataset contains approximately 14,000 questions from 57 domains. ii) Math: We eval-
371   uate the model on the test split of GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), which consists of 1,319 grade-school
372   math word problems designed to assess multi-step reasoning and arithmetic skills.

      4.4   A NALYSIS
446
447   In this section, we conduct additional experiments to examine how watermark parameters impact the
448   overall WAPITI models’ watermark detectability and capabilities, providing insights into WAPITI
449   for better utilization and future works.
450
      First, we examine how the norm of watermark parameter integration and the hyperparameters of
451
      the watermarking schemes impact detectability and generation quality. We vary the coefficient λFT
452
      within the range [0, 4] to test WAPITI models’ median p-values and perplexity. The results show
453   that λFT regulates the interference between watermarked and model parameters, and detectability
454   strongly correlates with watermark learnability. Full results and analysis are in Appendix F.1.
455
456
      Second, we evaluate whether WAPITI can defend against fine-tuning attacks by binding fine-tuned
      capabilities with watermarking. The results show that malicious users would significantly degrade
457
      the fine-tuned capabilities of models when attempting to remove the watermark through fine-tuning
458
      attacks. Full results can be found in Appendix F.2.
459
460   Finally, we analyze the relationship between the fine-tuned and watermarked models at the parame-
461   ter level using cosine similarity (Ilharco et al., 2023), illustrating how WAPITI remains compatible
462
      with fine-tuned models. This analysis also provides strong evidence that watermarked parameters in-
      deed encode knowledge about the watermarking schemes. Full results are detailed in Appendix F.3.

466
467
      Text steganography.       Steganography involves embedding information within texts for the pur-
468   poses of detection or secret communication. Steganography methods can be categorized into edit-
469   based and generative approaches. Edit-based methods include rule-based transformations (Wil-
470   son et al., 2014; Wilson & Ker, 2016), synonym-based substitution (Shirali-Shahreza & Shirali-
471   Shahreza, 2008), and neural network-based transformations (Fang et al., 2017; Abdelnabi & Fritz,
472   2021; Ueoka et al., 2021). On the other hand, generative methods embed information directly during
473   the text generation process (Ziegler et al., 2019; Dai & Cai, 2019).
474   Text watermarking. Earlier works in text watermarking typically embedded information through
475   post-processing of texts, closely resembling steganography (Venugopal et al., 2011; Yang et al.,
476   2021). More recent studies have shifted towards decoding-based watermarking, hiding information
477   by perturbing the text during the decoding phase (Kirchenbauer et al., 2024b; Aaronson, 2023; Zhu
478   et al., 2024; Krishna et al., 2023; Kuditipudi et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023; Christ et al., 2023; Wu
479   et al., 2024; Liu & Bu, 2024; Giboulot & Teddy, 2024; Lu et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2024; Wang
480   et al., 2024). Different watermarking strategies bring various improvements: Takezawa et al. (2023)
481   enhance logit-perturbation, while Hu et al. (2023); Zhao et al. (2024) optimize sampling strategies.
482
      Additionally, Lee et al. (2024); Li et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2021) explore code watermarking.
483   Recent advancements have introduced parameter-based watermarking, which embeds watermarks
484   through distillation (Gu et al., 2024). Other studies focus on investigating typical watermarking
485   behaviors (Luo et al., 2024; Singh & Zou, 2023), and some establish robust statistical frameworks
      for watermarking (Huang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). Surveys provide detailed definitions and


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      classifications of text watermarking techniques (Jawahar et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2024; Cai et al.,
487   2024), while benchmarks offer comprehensive evaluations of watermarks (Tu et al., 2024).
488
489   Model interventions.        Beyond fine-tuning, researchers have explored parameter-level interven-
490
      tions to modify model behaviors. Key approaches include model patching (Goel et al., 2020; Ilharco
      et al., 2022; Murty et al., 2022; Sung et al., 2021), parameter editing (Mitchell et al., 2022a;b; San-
491
      turkar et al., 2021; Ilharco et al., 2023), and model alignment (Askell et al., 2021; Glaese et al.,
492
      2022; Kasirzadeh & Gabriel, 2022). Compared to retraining or fine-tuning, model intervention of-
493   fers a more efficient way to introduce new capabilities into models.

      6   C ONCLUSION
497
      In this paper, we propose WAPITI, a training-free, parameter-based watermarking scheme designed
498
      for fine-tuned open-source models. We evaluate its effectiveness on various model architectures
499
      and watermarking strategies. Our method resolves the key technical challenges of applying water-
500   marks to fine-tuned models while retaining the fine-tuned model abilities. Furthermore, we analyze
501   the relationship between parameter integration and the model performance, using cosine similarity
502   analysis to demonstrate that the watermarking parameters encode n-gram related knowledge.
503
      Future work could further enhance WAPITI by developing watermarking strategies better suited to
504
      watermark transfer or optimizing the watermark distillation process to produce better watermark-
505
      distilled base models. Additionally, refining the extraction procedure for watermark parameters
506   could improve the efficiency of watermark transfer. This would also minimize interference with
507   other model parameters, helping to preserve the overall model performance.

896   correct samples, which were used as training data for distilling the Llama-2-7B-gsm8k model.

1000   E    WAPITI’ S ROBUSTNESS TO POST- PROCESSING
1001
1002
1003   Text Edit The text editing experiment focuses on whether watermarked text remains detectable
1004
       after randomly corrupting a certain portion of tokens. We test all hyperparameter sets of KGW and
       AAR watermarking. Specifically, k = {0, 1, 2}, γ = 0.25, and δ = {1, 2} for KGW; and for AAR,
1005
       we use k = {2, 3, 4}. The generations are taken from the WAPITI watermarked model described in
1006
       §4.2.
1007
1008   First, we set the edit proportion ϵ = {0, 0.16, 0.32, 0.48, 0.64, 0.8}. Then, for each generated sam-
1009   ple, we randomly select a proportion ϵ of tokens and replace each with a random token drawn
1010
       uniformly from the tokenizer’s vocabulary. Finally, we compute the median p-value of the edited
       sequences to assess their detectability.
1011
1012   As shown in Figure 6, detectability of text remains robust to text edits when ϵ up to 20%. A higher
1013   corruption rate would lead to substantial decay of watermark detectability. Interestingly, the robust-
1014   ness to text editing appears to be closely related to the window size of the watermarking method.
1015   Specifically, smaller window sizes (k in both KGW and AAR) demonstrate greater robustness to
1016
       token corruption. This observation aligns with the results presented in Appendix F.1.
1017   Changes in decoding parameters Previous watermark’s detectability may rely on specific de-
1018   coding parameter configuration, thus we test how weight-based watermark’s detectability would
1019   change with different decoding parameters.
1020   We use KGW k = 0, γ = 0.25, δ = 2 and AAR k = 2 as examples, varying the temperature
1021   t = {0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75} in temperature sampling. The generation settings follow those described in
1022   §4.2. We evaluate the median p-value under different temperatures to assess watermark detectability.
1023   The results, shown in Table 3, demonstrate that WAPITI is robust to changes in temperature, as
1024   generations across all temperature settings consistently produce small p-values. Additionally, as the
1025   temperature decreases, the randomness in the sampling process is reduced, resulting in increased
       watermark strength.

1081   efficients λFT for watermark parameter integration. Figure 7 illustrates the watermark detectability
1082   (measured by p-value) and perplexity of the WAPITI Llama-math model at different values of λ, for
1083   both the KGW and AAR watermarks. Complete plots for other models are available in Appendix G.
1084
       From the results, we observe that when the coefficient is within the range [0,1], the watermark
1085
       strength increases steadily, while perplexity remains below 5.0, indicating that watermark parameter
1086
       integration does not interfere with the generation capability of the model. Furthermore, the gradient
1087   of watermark strength in (a) and (d) varies based on the watermarking hyperparameters. For KGW,
1088   smaller k means the next token is influenced by fewer preceding tokens and a larger δ corresponds
1089   to better detectability. Similarly, for AAR, a smaller k also implies less influence from previous
1090   contexts on the next token. Thus, a smaller k and a larger δ make the watermark easier for the
1091   model to learn, consistent with the findings from Gu et al. (2024). The results in Figure 7 (a) and
1092   (d) strongly corroborate this, as the gradient of watermark strength aligns with the learnability of
1093   different watermarks. These findings also indicate that the watermark parameter is representative of
1094   the watermarking knowledge the model acquires during distillation.
1095   However, as the coefficient exceeds 1.0, two watermarks exhibit distinct patterns. For KGW, both
1096   watermark detectability and perplexity increase with the coefficient. AAR exhibits a parabolic be-
1097   havior in both watermark strength and perplexity, with their extrema occurring at different parameter
1098   values. This divergence suggests that although the watermark parameter has general applicability
1099   across fine-tuned models, it’s not independent of other parts of models and may cause substantial
1100   interference when the coefficient λ becomes large.
1101   The optimal λF T requires an exhaustive search on both fine-tuned capabilities and watermark de-
1102   tectability. According to current experimental results, simply using F T = 1 achieves a satisfactory
1103   trade-off between watermark detectability and fine-tuned capabilities.
1104
       Figure 7 (c),(f) present scatter plots of perplexity versus p-value, highlighting the key trade-off in
1105   watermarking: watermark detectability versus impact on output quality. To improve clarity, per-
1106   plexity is constrained to the range [0, 20], ensuring the generation quality is preserved. As shown
1107   in Figure 7, KGW displays a linear relationship between watermark strength and perplexity, reflect-
1108   ing the expected trade-off. In contrast, the AAR scatter plot exhibits a more chaotic pattern, with
1109   no clear correlation between perplexity and p-value. This disparity arises from the differing wa-
1110   termarking mechanism of KGW and AAR since KGW can be explicitly decomposed to n-grams,
1111   while AAR relies on both logits and pseudorandom scores, which means it’s comparatively harder
