                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Recently, multimodal large language models (multimodal LLMs) have been ap-
012
               plied to a wide range of video understanding tasks, particularly for Video Ques-
013
               tion Answering (VideoQA). However, existing multimodal LLMs suffer from
014            the following challenge: the classic end-to-end training strategies of multimodal
015            LLMs for VideoQA tasks are black-box, thus lacking interpretability as they can
016            neither present a reasoning path nor indicate where the answer is derived from
017            the video. To tackle this challenge, we propose MSR-ViR (Modularized Self-
018            Reflected Video Reasoner), a self-reflected framework that introduces a Mod-
019            ularized Spatial-Temporal Grounding (MoST-Grounding) module to multimodal
020            LLMs for VideoQA tasks. MoST-Grounding utilizes a question parser LLM to
021            generate execution policies, which serve as a reasoning path from questions to
022
               answers providing interpretability for our VideoQA framework. Based on the
               execution policies, MoST-Grounding invokes various small modules to localize
023
               temporal segments and spatial regions in videos which provide multimodal LLMs
024
               with most relevant visual information, while presenting visual evidence of our fi-
025            nal answers. To avoid the question parser LLM generating unreasonable policies,
026            we further propose a reinforcement learning-based Alternate Self-reflection train-
027            ing strategy to optimize the Multimodal LLM and the question parser LLM. Ex-
028            periments on VideoQA datasets (NExT-QA and STAR) and grounded VideoQA
029            dataset (NExT-GQA) demonstrate that our method significantly improves video
030            understanding capabilities of multimodal LLMs, while providing interpretable
031            reasoning paths together with temporal and spatial localization evidence within
032            the video.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Video Question Answering (VideoQA) is a representative task in video understanding, aiming to
038   answer questions based on the content of a given video. Leveraging their rich external knowledge
039
      and strong generalization capabilities, multimodal large language models (multimodal LLMs) have
      emerged as powerful tools for tackling video understanding tasks such as VideoQA, video caption-
040
      ing and so on. Most multimodal LLMs encode frames from videos with visual encoders and utilize
041
      adapters to align the visual information with the textual query, allowing models to understand in-
042   formation in the video. However, these models face the following challenge in VideoQA tasks: the
043   conventional end-to-end training approaches for multimodal LLMs operate as black-box systems,
044   which inherently suffer from a lack of interpretability. Falling short in terms of transparency, they
045   are unable to unveil the reasoning process or pinpoint the specific segments of the video from which
046   the answers are derived.
047
      To tackle this challenge, we propose the Modularized Self-Reflected Video Reasoner (MSR-ViR)
048   framework, including a Modularized Spatial-Temporal Grounding (MoST-Grounding) module,
049   together with a reinforcement learning-based Alternate Self-reflection Training strategy to train
050   a multimodal LLM on VideoQA datasets, as shown in Figure 1(c). MoST-Grounding module first
051   localizes the most relevant temporal segments and spatial regions in a video by utilizing various
052   small modules according to the execution policy, which is generated by a question parser LLM. Then
053   the spatial-temporal grounding results are encoded by a visual encoder and aligned with the textual
      inputs through a visual adapter, after which a Multimodal LLM utilizes the aligned information to

101   2   R ELATED W ORKS
102
103   Video Understanding with multimodal LLMs. With the development of multimodal LLMs, they
104   have been utilized for video understanding tasks(Zhang et al., 2023b; Lin et al., 2023a; Maaz et al.,
105   2023; Li et al., 2023b; 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2024; Li et al.,
106   2024a). Most multimodal LLMs for videos are built on open-source LLMs such as LLaMA(Touvron
107   et al., 2023) and Vicuna(Chiang et al., 2023), and adapters are utilized to align encoded visual
      information with the textual space. Representative works like Video-LLaMA(Zhang et al., 2023b)


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      utilizes the Vision Transformer (ViT) from EVA-CLIP(Sun et al., 2023) and an Image Q-Former(Li
109   et al., 2023a) as the video frame encoder, after which a Video Q-Former is employed to encode
110   temporal information in the video, with a linear layer projecting this visual information into the
111   textual space. Some other works have explored the temporal perception capabilities of multimodal
112   LLMs(Huang et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024c; Qian et al.; Li et al., 2024c) to
113   enhance their understanding of temporal information in videos. For instance, VTimeLLM(Huang
114   et al., 2024) introduces a boundary perception training process on multi-event datasets, improving
115   the performance of multimodal LLMs on tasks such as video grounding and dense video captioning.
116   However, classic end-to-end training methods of multimodal LLMs remain black boxes, resulting
117
      in a lack of interpretability as they are unable to provide inference process as well as grounded
      evidence of the answer in the video.
118
119   Grounded VideoQA with LLMs. Grounded VideoQA aims to answer the question and at the same
120   time indicates where in the video the answer originates. Existing grounding-based(retrieval-based)
121   VideoQA methods(Wang et al., 2024d; Xiao et al., 2024; Qian et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024; Wang
122   et al., 2024a) attempt to localize time segments relevant to the question within the video in the
123
      first place and then sample frames from the identified segments to serve as inputs to multimodal
      LLMs, as is shown in Figure 1(a). For example, LSTP(Wang et al., 2024d) leverages optical flow
124
      of videos to efficiently extract relevant video frames as visual input of multimodal LLM to achieve
125
      long-form video-text understanding. Grounded-based methods address the issue of providing visual
126   evidence in videos to some extent, but still they lack interpretability as they typically rely on black-
127   box models to perform temporal localization without a clear reasoning path, especially for questions
128   with complicated structures.
129
      Modular VideoQA with LLMs. Modular methods(Min et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023a; Surı́s
130
      et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b;e) utilize various smaller models according to execution policies
131
      generated by certain LLM to handle sub-tasks derived from the original complex question, and an-
132   other LLM integrates the outputs of these smaller models to produce the final answer, as shown in
133   Figure 1(b). MoReVQA(Min et al., 2024) utilizes multiple PaLM-2(Anil et al., 2023) LLMs in a
134   multi-stage modular reasoning process, where the reasoning results and video frame captions ob-
135   tained from an image captioner are provided to another PaLM-2 model to derive the final answer.
136   ViperGPT(Surı́s et al., 2023) uses a code-finetuned LLM as a python program generator to generate
137   programs that invokes various tools and apis provided in prompts. While this approach enhances
138   interpretability, the unimodal LLMs used can only receive video information in the form of captions
139   generated from original video frames, potentially overlooking details in individual frames and miss-
140
      ing important temporal context between frames. Additionally, the lack of self-reflection may cause
      the modular network to execute according to unreasonable policies generated by LLMs, thereby
141
      affecting the accuracy of question answering.

145
146   In this section, we describe our proposed framework MSR-ViR(Modularized Self- Reflected Video
147   Reasoner), a modularized videoQA framework with alternate self-reflection training strategy. Fig-
148   ure 2 demonstrates the overall framework, together with an example for illustration. We will first
149   introduce the question parser which generates modularization policies in Section 3.1, and then in-
150
      troduce our MoST-Grounding module designed for temporally and spatially grounding visual clues
      according to the generated policy in Section 3.2. In Section 3.3, we will present how to processes
151
      various information from both MoST-Grounding module and naive inputs(the video and the ques-
152
      tion) based on a multimodal LLM. Finally, our proposed Alternate Self-reflection Training Strategy
153   is introduced in Section 3.4.

203   3.2   M ODULARIZED S PATIAL -T EMPORAL G ROUNDING M ODULE
204
205   MoST-Grounding module is the core component of our framework, recursively invoking various
206   small modules according to the modular policy generated by the question parser to achieve temporal
207   and spatial localization for complex questions. Our MoST-Grounding module consists of two parts:
208   temporal localizer Ft and spatial localizer Fs , each containing several small modules for temporal
209
      and spatial localization, respectively.

212   video v, where 1 ≤ i ≤ j ≤ T . Later the output of the temporal localizer, namely video segments
213   vs , along with concepts cs are processed to the spatial localizer, generating most relevant visual
214   bounding box bvs within video segments vs . Formally, MoST-Grounding module M is written as

      follows:

      3.4   A LTERNATE S ELF - REFLECTION T RAINING S TRATEGY
288
289   As discussed in Section 3.1, we can teach the question parser to generate modularization policies
290   from complex questions by providing examples in the prompt. However, relying solely on in-context
291   learning does not ensure the quality of the policies. To address this issue, we propose the Alternate
292   Self-Reflection Training Strategy, which enables the question parser to improve the quality of its
293   policies through reinforcement learning.
294
      We assume that for a given question, provided that all our modules remain unchanged, a reason-
295   able modular policy is more likely to accurately localize the correct temporal segments and spatial
296   regions. Consequently, the loss computed by the multimodal LLM is likely to be smaller. In con-
297   trast, an unreasonable policy that leads to incorrect localization will result in an increase in this
298   loss. Therefore, we provide feedback to the question parser LLM using the loss noticed during the
299   training process of the multimodal LLM, thereby guiding it through reinforcement learning training.
300   We utilize Direct Preference Optimization(DPO)(Rafailov et al., 2024), a method for Reinforce-
301   ment Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), to train the question parser LLM to generate more
302   reasonable policies. Different from previous RLHF methods, DPO directly optimizes a language
303
      model without explicit rewarding models, making the training process straight-forward and stable.
      Specifically in DPO, the policy objective is formulated as:
304
305                                                            πθ (pw | q)           πθ (pl | q)
               LDPO (πθ ; πref ) = −E(q,pw ,pl )∼D log σ β log               − β log                    (6)
306                                                            πref (pw | q)         πref (pl | q)
307   where in our case, pw denotes the positive policy, pl denotes the negative policy and q is the input
308   question. πθ is our question parser LLM to be trained, while πref is a reference model initialized
309   with our question parser LLM but remain frozen. σ is the sigmoid function, and β is a controlling
310   parameter. Through DPO training, the probability of generating positive policies increases, while
311   the probability of generating negative policies decreases. In other words, the question parser LLM
312   learns to generate more reasonable policies.
313   We prompt the question parser LLM to view the same question from multiple perspectives, gen-
314   erating different modular policies. The MoST-Grounding module executes each policy, producing
315   their respective grounding results. The multimodal LLM then computes the corresponding losses.
316   We classify the policy with the smaller loss as positive and the one with the larger loss as negative,
317   training the question parser LLM according to Eq. 6.
318   Our training strategy alternates between SFT of the multimodal LLM and reinforcement learning
319   for the question parser LLM, optimizing with the loss functions in Eq. 5 and Eq. 6, respectively.
320   While training one large model, the other model’s parameters remain frozen. During this process,
321   the multimodal LLM periodically pauses to adapt based on the modular policies from the question
322   parser LLM. After a set training period, the question parser LLM utilizes these refined policies to
323   further train the multimodal LLM, allowing both models to optimize continuously. See appendix
      A.4 for detailed training process.

335   4.1   E XPERIMENTS S ETUPS
336
337   Datasets. We conducted our experiments on two VideoQA datasets: NExT-QA(Xiao et al., 2021)
338   and STAR(Wu et al., 2021), together with a grounded VideoQA datasets NExT-GQA(Xiao et al.,
339   2024). NExT-QA is a multi-choice VideoQA dataset focused on temporal actions including three
340   types of questions: Temporal, Causal and Descriptive. STAR is a multi-choice VideoQA dataset
341
      for situated reasoning in real-world videos that contains four types of questions: Interaction, Se-
      quence, Prediction and Feasibility. For STAR, we created a subset (STAR-sub) with Interaction
342
      and Sequence questions (82.5% of STAR), excluding Prediction and Feasibility types as they lack
343
      temporal and spatial grounding in videos, making them unsuitable for our framework. NExT-GQA is
344   derived from NExT-QA, retaining Temporal and Causal questions but removes Descriptive ones,
345   providing ground-truth temporal clips for validation and test sets to evaluate temporal grounding
346   accuracy.
347
      Baselines. On NExT-QA and STAR, our baselines include vision-language models AIO(Wang et al.,
348
      2023), ATP(Buch et al., 2022), VGT(Xiao et al., 2022), MIST(Gao et al., 2023), VFC(Momeni et al.,
349
      2023), CoVGT(Xiao et al., 2023), HiTeA(Ye et al., 2023), InternVideo(Wang et al., 2022), multi-
350   modal LLMs BLIP2(Li et al., 2023a), InstructBLIP(Dai et al., 2023) and grounding-based multi-
351   modal LLMs LSTP(Wang et al., 2024d), SeViLa(Yu et al., 2024), GCG(Wang et al., 2024a). As
352   the direct baseline of our framework, we train Qwen-VL(Bai et al., 2023) and Llava-Next(Zhang
353   et al., 2024) by uniformly sampling frames from videos. On NExT-GQA dataset, our baselines in-
354   clude vision-language models VGT, VIOLETv2(Fu et al., 2023), Temp[CLIP](Radford et al., 2021),
355   FrozenBiLM(Yang et al., 2022)(which achieve grounded VideoQA with the method in Xiao et al.
356   (2024)), grounding-based method LSTP, SeViLa, LangRepo(Kahatapitiya et al., 2024) and modular
357   method LLoVi(Zhang et al., 2023a), MoReVQA(Min et al., 2024). We present zero-shot results for
358
      LangRepo, LLoVi and MoReVQA, while conducting weakly-supervised finetuning on NExT-GQA
      training set for other baselines.
359
360   Implementations. We implement our method based on SWIFT framework(Zhao et al., 2024). We
361   utilize a large language model, Qwen2-7B(Yang et al., 2024) as the backbone for our question
362   parser, and we utilize Qwen-VL(Bai et al., 2023) and Llava-Next(Zhang et al., 2024) as the back-
363   bone for our multimodal LLM, denoted as MSR-ViRQ and MSR-ViRL respectively. Following the
364
      classic training strategy, we uniformly sample 4 frames from videos for Qwen-VL and 8 frames for
      Llava-Next to implement our direct baseline in Table 1. For MSR-ViRQ , we sample 2 frames from
365
      temporal grounding results, and 2 corresponding spatial-grounded frames, while for MSR-ViRL we
366
      sample 8 frames from temporal grounding results, 8 spatial-grounded frames accordingly. We uti-
367   lize LoRA(Hu et al.) during supervised finetuning of our Multimodal LLM. As for our Alternate
368   Self-reflection Training Strategy, the period for alternating training between two LLMs is 200 steps,
369   with the gradient accumulation step set to 16. We conduct 5 epochs of SFT on every training set
370   for Qwen-VL, Llava-Next and our MSR-ViR framework, selecting the best model according to the
371   results on validation set.

461   4.4   A BLATION S TUDY
462
463   As demonstrated in Table 3, to further validate the effectiveness of modules and designs in our MSR-
464
      ViR framework, we conduct ablation study on NExT-QA and NExT-GQA dataset for MSR-ViRQ
      concerning the following questions:
465
466   Is Alternate Self-reflection Training Strategy necessary? We remove the self-reflection training
467   process, only finetuning our Multimodal LLM without training the question parser LLM, and the
468   results are shown by w/o self-reflection in Table 3. The average accuracy on NExT-QA declines by
469   1.5, and accuracy on each subsets decreases to varying degrees. The grounded accuracy as well as
470
      IoU of temporal grounding also decline as shown in experiments on NExT-GQA. This demonstrates
      the necessity of our Alternate Self-reflection Training Strategy.
471
472   Is spatial localizer necessary in MoST-Grounding module? Most existing grounding-based
473   methods only consider temporal grounding, so we remove the spatial localizer including all small
474   modules in it, only providing our Multimodal LLM with temporal grounding results, denoted by w/o
475   spatial modules. The average accuracy on NExT-QA drops by 1.4, proving that spatial grounding
476
      results provided by spatial localizer contain useful information for Multimodal LLM to answer the
      question correctly.
477
478   Are our designs in training Multimodal LLM necessary? In 3.3, we introduce two designs for our
479   Multimodal LLM training: global representation and instruction prompts. We removed these two
480   designs separately and conducted tests on NExT-QA. The results show that the average accuracy on
481   NExT-QA has decreased to varying degrees for both, indicating that the two designs we proposed
482
      for training multimodal LLMs are effective.
483   Furthermore, we visualize the reasoning process of MSR-ViR through an example, as demonstrated
484   in Figure 4. After self-reflection training, question parser generates a more reasonable policy accu-
485   rately grounding the question in the video, leading to a correct answer. More examples can be found
      in appendix A.5.

      5    C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In summary, we propose Modularized Self-Reflected Video Reasoner(MSR-ViR), a self-reflected
533
      framework that integrates a Modularized Spatial-Temporal Grounding(MoST-Grounding) module
534   into a Multimodal LLM for interpretable VideoQA. Modularization policies generated by a question
535   parser LLM demonstrates clear paths from questions to answers, enhancing interpretability of our
536   framework, while spatial-temporal grounding results present visual evidence for answers. Through
537   the proposed alternate self-refection training process, policies are gradually refined, becoming more
538   reasonable. Extensive experiments demonstrate that MSR-ViR significantly improves VideoQA
539   capabilities of multimodal LLMs while grounding answers in videos more accurately. Future work
      could explore further enhancements to the design of modular network and its execution efficiency.
