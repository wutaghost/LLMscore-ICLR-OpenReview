009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Humans have the ability to learn new tasks by inferring high-level concepts
012            from existing solutions, then manipulating these concepts in lieu of the raw
013            data. Can we automate this process by deriving latent semantic structures
014            in a document collection using foundation models? We introduce fPLSA, a
015            foundation-model-based Probabilistic Latent Semantic Analysis (PLSA) method
016
               that iteratively clusters and tags document segments based on document-level
               contexts. These tags can be used to model the structure of given documents and
017
               for hierarchical sampling of new texts. Our experiments on story writing, math,
018
               and multi-step reasoning datasets demonstrate that fPLSA tags help reconstruct
019            the original texts better than existing tagging methods. Moreover, when used
020            for hierarchical sampling, fPLSA produces more diverse outputs with a higher
021            likelihood of hitting the correct answer than direct sampling and hierarchical
022            sampling with existing tagging methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Large language models (LLMs) have shown impressive performance on a wide range of tasks, such
      as reasoning (Suzgun et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023), math problem solving (Wu et al., 2023), and
028
      open-ended text generation tasks (Katz et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024; OpenAI et al., 2024). Given
029
      natural language instructions or in-context examples with chain-of-thought steps, LLMs can adapt
030   quickly to a new task and achieve outstanding performance on challenging tasks that require multi-
031   step reasoning or planning (Wei et al., 2022). However, such methods typically rely on humans
032   to provide the LLM with instructions or chain-of-thought recipes for solving a task. By contrast,
033   humans can directly derive effective methodologies for solving a task by analyzing a separate set of
034   problems and their solutions.
035
      Can we automate the process of discovering latent semantic structures in a document collection using
036   LLMs? Such algorithms would have a wide range of applications, including producing effective
037   guidelines for new tasks, hierarchical sampling for diverse outputs, and document analysis. For
038   example, they can help determine how two document collections differ in text structure and identify
039   the most common plot elements in a story collection.
040
      We frame this problem as an unsupervised clustering and tagging problem, where we discover the
041
      text segments that share common characteristics and assign them to the same tag. Based on these
042   segment tags, we can model the latent structure of a collection of documents by learning a dy-
043   namic model over the latent tags and their transitions in the documents. Traditional document
044   labeling and topic modeling approaches focus primarily on lexical features such as word or term
045   co-occurrence (Hearst, 1997; Blei et al., 2003; Hofmann et al., 1999), which provide minimal infor-
046   mation on the semantics of short text spans. Recent LLM-based approaches discover topics based
047   on higher-level semantic contexts, but rely on one-shot topic generation and merging (Pham et al.,
048   2024; Wang et al., 2023; Mu et al., 2024), which limits the model’s ability to uncover shared char-
049   acteristics among seemingly unrelated text spans.
050   In this paper, we introduce fPLSA, an iterative algorithm that alternatively clusters and tags doc-
051   ument segments using LLMs based on segment- and document-level contexts. fPLSA combines
052   the merits of traditional topic modeling approaches such as Probabilistic Latent Semantic Analy-
053   sis (PLSA) (Hofmann et al., 1999) and LLM-based approaches, and captures shared semantic fea-
      tures among text segments more effectively.

      We evaluate the informativeness of fPLSA tags by measuring 1) how well they help reconstruct
055   the original text spans, and 2) how useful they are in hierarchical sampling to produce structurally
056   diverse outputs that cover more solution paths. Experiments on story writing, math and multi-step
057   reasoning datasets show that fPLSA leads to higher reconstruction likelihood than existing tagging
058   approaches. Furthermore, on math and reasoning tasks, hierarchical sampling using fPLSA tags
059   produces more diverse outputs, which increase the probability of hitting the correct answer over
060   hierarchical sampling with other tagging approaches.

066   2.1   D OCUMENT S EGMENTATION AND L ABELING
067
068   To model the structure and topic shifts in a document, prior work has introduced unsupervised
069   document segmentation and labeling approaches that leverage term co-occurrence features (Hearst,
070   1997), co-occurrence shifts in topic vectors (Riedl & Biemann, 2012), lexical features and word
071   embeddings (Glavaš et al., 2016). These approaches focus mostly on lexical features which are
072   limited in modeling the high-level semantic structure of documents. On the other hand, Neural-
073   based approaches have the potential of modeling sentence-level semantics and document-level topic
074   flows more effective, but rely heavily on supervised training samples in the target domain (Koshorek
075   et al., 2018; Arnold et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2019). Our algorithm infers the structure of documents
076
      based on segment- and document-level contexts using LLMs in an unsupervised fashion.
077
078
079   2.2   T OPIC M ODELING
080
081   Topic modeling is a widely used technique in natural language processing for uncovering hidden
082   thematic structures in large text corpora. The most foundational methods in this domain include
083   Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) (Blei et al., 2003) and Probabilistic Latent Semantic Analy-
084   sis (PLSA) (Hofmann et al., 1999; Hofmann, 1999; 2001). Both methods represent each document
085   as a bag of words and models word-document relationships using a mixture of latent topics, where
086   each topic is represented by a list of top words. These algorithms are mathematically grounded,
087
      but typically rely on manual topic interpretation, which often leads to incorrect or incomplete la-
      bels (Gillings & Hardie, 2022). More recent work introduces neural topic models (Miao et al.,
088
      2016; Dieng et al., 2020; Srivastava & Sutton, 2017), which combine traditional topic models with
089
      word embeddings. These models have shown improved performance in handling large and com-
090   plex vocabularies. However, they sill model each document as a bag of words, disregarding the
091   sentence- and document-level semantics. Additionally, the resulting topics are represented either by
092   semantic vectors or lists of closest words, which still rely on manual interpretation. Furthermore,
093   studies have shown that incorporating expert knowledge in topic modeling improves over traditional
094   unsupervised methods (Lee et al., 2017).
095
      Moreover, the advent of large language models (LLMs) has led to LLM-based topic modeling ap-
096   proaches. Li et al. (2023) propose to use LLMs for topic labeling based their top terms produced
097   by traditional topic models. For short text spans, however, the bag-of-words representation of texts
098   provides limited information for topic modeling. Akash et al. (2023) address the issue by extend-
099   ing each text span into longer sequences using LLMs and extracting topics from the extended texts
100   using neural topic models. Futhermore, Pham et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2023); Mu et al. (2024) pro-
101   pose prompt-based techniques to generate, merge, and assign topics using LLMs. These approaches
102   leverage the domain knowledge embedded in LLMs and produce more interpretable topics based on
103   sentence or document-level contexts beyond bag of words.
104   However, the generate-and-merge approach limits the model’s potential for discovering shared fea-
105   tures among various text spans across documents of different themes and often leads to overly ab-
106   stract, thematical topics, especially on a large-scale document collection. We propose fPLSA, which
107   combines the merits of traditional PLSA, which uses an iterative EM algorithm to model topic and
      text distributions, and LLM-based approaches.

      3     A PPROACH
109
110
      We propose fPLSA, a foundation-model-based EM algorithm that learns the latent tags on a set
111   of segmented documents. We draw inspiration from the traditional Probabilistic Latent Semantic
112   Analysis and use iterative EM steps to learn the latent topics that maximize the estimated likelihood
113   of segmented documents.
114

      3.2   F OUNDATION -M ODEL -BASED PLSA ( F PLSA)
141
142   We introduce fPLSA, which learns the latent tags (similar to topics in LSA)1 on a set of segmented
143   documents d = (x1 , x2 , ..., xL ), where the document d is segmented into L segments xk . A
144   core difference between fPLSA and PLSA is that fPLSA models the probability of the sequence
145   of words (w1 , w2 , ..., wn ) in each text segment xk jointly as pΘ (w1 , w2 , ..., wn |t). Moreover, fPLSA
146   models the distribution over tags t conditioned not only on current segment xk but also on the doc-
147
      ument d. Formally, in fPLSA, the generative model of a segment xk = w1...n in a document d can
      be written as:
148                                                            X
                           pΘ (w1...n , xk , d) = p(d)p(xk |d)     pΘ (t|xk , d)pΘ (w1...n |t)                 (4)
149
                                                                 t
150
151   Another core difference between fPLSA and PLSA is that we model the parametric distribu-
152   tions pΘ (t|xk , d) and pΘ (w1...n |t) using an LLM. Specifically, the parameters Θ in fPLSA include
153   the LLM parameters, which is frozen, and the textual description θt for each tag t.
154   Inspired by PLSA, we also maximize the likelihood in Eq 4 using iterative EM steps.
155
156   At the E-step in iteration i, we approximate the posterior distribution pΘi−1 (t|w1...n , xk , d) of tags t
157
      conditioned on each document d and segment xk = w1...n in it by prompting the LLM to greedily
      assign a tag given the tag descriptions θi−1 from the previous iteration, the current segment xk =
158
      w1...n and neighbouring segments (xk−W/2 , xk+1−W/2 , ..., xk−1+W/2 , xk+W/2 ) as document-level
159
      context, where W is the context window size.

      4.1   E VALUATION DATASETS
170
      We evaluate fPLSA against various baselines on story writing, math problem solving and multi-step
171   reasoning benchmarks. We use WritingPrompts (Fan et al., 2018), a story writing dataset that con-
172   tains 300K human-written stories paired with writing prompts from an online forum. We randomly
173   sample 100 stories from the training set for clustering and tagging. We set the number of tags to 100
174   for all tagging approaches. For math problem solving, we use MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), a
175   popular math benchmark that contains high school math competition problems on seven subjects
176   including Prealgebra, Algebra, Number Theory, Counting and Probability, Geometry, Intermediate
177   Algebra and Precalculus. We learn 100 tags on 1K randomly sampled problems and the step-by-step
178   solutions from the training set. We also experiment on the Big-Bench Hard (BBH) benchmark (Suz-
179
      gun et al., 2022). The original benchmark includes 23 challenging multi-step reasoning tasks, but
      each task only includes three step-by-step solution examples. Instead, we pick the 12 tasks used
180
      in Xu et al. (2024) and use the step-by-step solutions produced by their automatic Chain-of-Thought
181
      prompt inference algorithm for clustering and tagging. We set the number of tags to 50 on BBH.

      4.4   BASELINES
242
243   TradLDA We compare our approach with the traditional Latent Dirichlet Allocation (TradLDA)
244   algorithm designed to discover latent topics in a collection of text spans (Blei et al., 2003).
245
246
      TradLDA+LLM As Li et al. (2023) showed that the topic labels generated by LLMs based on
247   the key terms learned through TradLDA are preferred more often than the original labels, we also
248   include TradLDA+LLM as a baseline. Specifically, we first learn the topics with the key terms for
249   each topic using TradLDA, and then use GPT-4 to generate a description for each topic based on the
250   key terms.
251
252   Prompting Recent work showed that, with appropriate prompts, LLMs are capable of directly
253   generating topic labels given a set of text documents and condensing overarching topics (Mu et al.,
254   2024). As a baseline, we adapt the approach (along with the prompts) to generate topic descriptions
255   for each text segment.
256
257   GenOutline For Hits@K accuracy, we also include a two-step sampling baseline, where we first
258   prompt the LLM to generate a multi-step outline for solving this type of problem and then prompt
259   the LLM to generate the actual solution based on the problem description and the outline.

320   5.1   R ECONSTRUCTION L IKELIHOOD
321
322   First, we compare the reconstruction log-likelihood of fPLSA with the No Tag baseline (without
323   conditioning on any tags). As shown in Table 1, conditioning on fPLSA tags helps predict the
      original texts: fPLSA brings 0.6–1.4 higher log-likelihood than the No Tag baseline.

324
      TradLDA also brings higher reconstruction log-likelihood over the No Tag baseline. However, since
325   TradLDA only captures word or term co-occurrences, it still underperforms fPLSA consistently on
326   all three datasets. Moreover, TradLDA+LLM fails to improve over TradLDA. As shown by the
327   examples in Table 2, it is extremely challenging for LLMs and even humans to extract meaningful
328   semantic information from the keywords learned on short text segments through TradLDA, and the
329   resulting tag descriptions are overly generic, making it challenging to reconstruct the original text
330   segments accurately.
331   Compared with the Prompting baseline, fPLSA achieves 0.2–0.4 higher log-likelihood on all three
332   datasets. We further compared the tags learned using Prompting versus fPLSA. As shown by the
333   examples in Table 3, Prompting tends to merge unrelated topics into a mixed topic (e.g. Tag 1
334   and 2), and the resulting topics become overly broad. Even for tags sharing a common theme, the
335   descriptions often lack specificity and detail (e.g. Tag 3). By contrast, fPLSA identifies segments
336   with similar themes, groups them into a single cluster and produces more detailed tag descriptions
337   with example plots.

477
478
      We introduced fPLSA, a foundation-model-based Probabilistic Latent Semantic Analysis method
479
      that aims to uncover the latent semantic structures in document collections by iteratively clustering
480   and tagging document segments based on document-level contexts. Our experiments on story writ-
481   ing, math and multi-step reasoning tasks show that fPLSA tags are more informative in reconstruct-
482   ing the original texts than tags generated by existing tagging methods. fPLSA tags are also useful
483   in generating more diverse solutions via hierarchical sampling and lead to higher Hits@K Accuracy
484   than existing methods. These results suggest the potential of fPLSA for generating effective task
485   guidelines given some worked-out examples, along with hierarchical sampling and searching for
      problem solutions based on a verification or reward model.
