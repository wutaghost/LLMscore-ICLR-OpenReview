011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Text watermarking is designed to embed hidden, imperceptible, markers within
014            content generated by large language models (LLMs), with the goal of tracing and
015            verifying the content’s origin to prevent misuse. The robustness of watermarking
016
               algorithms has become a key factor in evaluating their effectiveness, but remains
               an open problem. In this work, we introduce a novel watermark removal attack,
017
               the Self-Information Rewrite Attack (SIRA), which poses a new challenge to the
018
               robustness of existing watermarking techniques. Since embedding watermarks
019            requires both concealment and semantic coherence, current methods prefered to
020            embed them in high-entropy tokens. However, this reveals an inherent vulnera-
021            bility, allowing us to exploit this feature to identify potential green tokens. Our
022            approach leverages the self-information of each token to filter potential pattern to-
023            kens that embed watermarks and performs the attack through masking and rewrit-
024            ing in a black-box setting. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our attack by
025            implementing it against seven recent watermarking algorithms. The experimental
026            results show that our lightweight algorithm achieves state-of-the-art attack success
027
               rate while maintaining shorter execution times and lower computational resource
               consumption compared to existing methods. This attack points to an important
028
               vulnerability of existing watermarking techniques and paves way towards future
029
               watermarking improvements.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Large language models (LLMs), exemplified by ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2024) and Claude (Anthropic,
035
      2024), have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in generating coherent, human-like text. However,
036   while these advances significantly expand AI’s potential in content creation, they have concurrently
037   heightened concerns regarding their misuse (Deshpande et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024), includ-
038   ing the spread of misinformation (Monteith et al., 2024) and threats to academic integrity (Stokel-
039   Walker, 2022).
040
      To mitigate risks associated with LLM-generated content, text watermarking has emerged as a
041
      promising countermeasure (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; Aaronson & Kirchner, 2022). This tech-
042   nique subtly alters the LLM’s generation process to embed imperceptible patterns in the output
043   text, which, while invisible to human readers, can be reliably detected using specialized algorithms.
044   This generate-detect framework enables differentiation between AI-generated and human-authored
045   content and allows tracing the text back to the specific LLM that created it (Li et al., 2024). Con-
046   sequently, this mechanism promotes accountability and helps mitigate LLM misuse, providing a
047   reliable means to ensure transparency and integrity in AI-generated content.
048   Recent studies have demonstrated that watermarking techniques exhibit significant robustness
049   against simple manipulations, including word deletions (Welbl et al., 2020) and emoji at-
050   tacks (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023). However, traditional NLP attack strategies, such as token deletion
051   and insertion, are increasingly insufficient for thoroughly evaluating the robustness of advanced
052   watermarking algorithms. As LLMs continue to advance, there is a growing need for more sophisti-
053   cated testing methodologies that account for complex manipulation tactics, ensuring that watermark-
      ing techniques remain resilient against emerging threats. To provide a more rigorous evaluation of


                                                        1

054
      watermarking robustness, model-based attacks have been proposed. These methods typically rewrite
055   watermarked texts using either closed-source commercial LLMs (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023) or fine-
056   tuned open-source models trained on specially designed phrase datasets (Krishna et al., 2024). De-
057   spite their potential, these approaches face several limitations. First, they lack transparency, offering
058   limited control over the output text via a small set of hyperparameters. Second, they are often costly,
059   requiring token-based payments or significant computational resources. Furthermore, for newer
060   and more robust watermarking algorithms like SIR (Liu et al., 2024), these methods fail to deliver
061   effective attacks.
062   To resolve the aforementioned problem, we propose a lightweight and effective watermark removal
063   attack named SIRA(self-information rewrite attack). This method is inspired by a simple intuition:
064   watermarking algorithms aim to be imperceptible to users while preserving text quality, often favor-
065   ing high-entropy tokens to embed watermark patterns (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023).
066   The high-entropy tokens, however, will usually have high self-information. Leveraging this insight,
067   we can identify potential ”green list” token candidates within watermarked text under a black-box
068   setting. By masking these potential tokens and allowing the LLM to complete the masked segments,
069
      we can effectively carry out a more effective paraphrasing attack. SIRA requires minimal resources
      and works effectively even with a small model like LLaMA3-8B. Our experiments in seven wa-
070
      termark algorithms show that SIRA outperforms all other black-box watermark removal attacks.
071
      Specifically, our method achieves over a 90% attack success rate on most current watermarking
072   techniques (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024; Wu et al.,
073   2024; Aaronson & Kirchner, 2022). We will release our code to further promote the development of
074   responsible AI (Gu, 2024).

      2    R ELATED W ORK
078
      Self-information. Self-information, also known as surprisal, is a fundamental concept in Informa-
079
      tion Theory, first introduced by Claude Shannon in his seminal work (Shannon, 1948). Shannon
080
      employed self-information as the principal metric to quantify the information content associated
081   with the occurrence of specific events, effectively linking the rarity of an event to the amount of
082   information it communicates. This measure forms a cornerstone in the understanding of data en-
083   coding and compression, as articulated in Shannon’s source coding theorem, where events that are
084   more probable are represented with fewer bits, while less probable events require more bits, thereby
085   facilitating optimal compression (Shannon, 1948).
086
      In the realm of Natural Language Processing (NLP), self-information plays a crucial role in the
087   analysis and modeling of language. It aids in deciphering language patterns, particularly in evalu-
088   ating the entropy and predictability of tokens within sequences. The concept is particularly useful
089   for quantifying the informativeness or surprise of a token, determined by its probability in a given
090   linguistic context. Language models, for instance, predict the probability of a subsequent token in a
091   sequence using the preceeding context:
092
093                                               P (tk | t1 , t2 , . . . , tk−1 )
094
095   The self-information of the token in this context is computed as follows:
096
097                         I(tk | t1 , t2 , . . . , tk−1 ) = − logb (P (tk | t1 , t2 , . . . , tk−1 ))
098
099   where I(tk | t1 , t2 , . . . , tk−1 ) denotes the self-information of token tk given the context of pre-
100   vious tokens, P (tk | t1 , t2 , . . . , tk−1 ) is the probability of token tk occurring after the preceding
101   sequence of tokens, and b represents the base of the logarithm, typically set to 2. Tokens that are
102   less predictable within a sequence, hence more informative, exhibit higher self-information values.
103   LLM Watermark. Watermarking techniques for large language models are designed to embed
104   identifiable patterns in model outputs, allowing for the traceability of generated text back to its
105   originating source. These watermarks serve as an essential tool for ensuring accountability and
106   ownership, particularly in scenarios where identifying the specific model or version that produced
107   the content is crucial. LLM watermark methods can be broadly classified into two primary cate-
      gories: the KGW Family and the Christ Family. Each family employs distinct mechanisms that are


                                                                 2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      integral to the internal workings of LLMs. The KGW Family (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
109   2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024) focuses on modifying the logits—the raw
110   output probabilities produced by the model—before they are transformed into text. This approach
111   involves selectively adding bias to certain tokens, referred to as “green list” tokens, which influ-
112   ences the model to favor these tokens, thus embedding a statistical signature in the output. Post
113   text generation, a statistical metric based on the proportion of these “green” tokens is computed. A
114   predetermined threshold enables differentiation between watermarked and non-watermarked text.
115   Conversely, the Christ Family (Aaronson & Kirchner, 2022; Christ et al., 2024; Kuditipudi et al.,
116   2023) modifies the sampling process during the generation phase itself. Rather than altering the
117   logits, this family intervenes directly in the token selection during decoding. Techniques such as top-
118   k sampling, temperature adjustment, or nucleus sampling are adapted to ensure preferential selection
119   of watermarked tokens. This method provides more direct control over the generation process,
120   embedding watermarks that are resilient against post-processing attacks, such as paraphrasing.
121   Watermark Removal Attacks. The robustness of a watermarking algorithm is crucial, as it de-
122   termines the effectiveness of the watermark under various real-world conditions, particularly in ad-
123   versarial settings. Attacks against watermarking algorithms, commonly referred to as watermark
124   tampering attacks, can be broadly categorized into two types:
125
      Text manipulations: These attacks involve traditional NLP techniques to straightforward text ma-
126
      nipulations, such as word deletion (Welbl et al., 2020), substitution (Yu et al., 2010), or inser-
127   tion (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023). By altering the surface-level structure of the text, these meth-
128   ods attempt to distort or eliminate the watermark without drastically changing the content’s mean-
129   ing. These techniques exploit the fact that many watermarking algorithms embed patterns with
130   a pre-designed ”green token” and ”red token”, making them vulnerable to such basic modifica-
131   tions. Kirchenbauer et al. (2023) propose emoji attack and copy-paste attack which insert emoji/hu-
132   man writtern text in the generated text to avoid detection.These methods are considered variants of
133   text manipulations, however, they are easily thwarted by detectors equipped with content filters and
134   often alter the semantics of the generated text which makes them inappropriate for real-world use.
135   Model-based paraphrasing: A more advanced form of attack involves using another LLM to para-
136   phrase the watermarked content. This approach takes advantage of the LLM’s ability to generate di-
137   verse rephrasings while maintaining the core meaning of the text. Krishna et al. (2024) propose DIP-
138   PER, a paraphrase generation model developed by fine-tuning T5-XXL (Raffel et al., 2020b) on an
139   aligned paragraph dataset. This model has been widely adopted in recent watermark research (Zhao
140   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Kuditipudi et al., 2023) to evaluate the robustness of watermarking al-
141
      gorithms. Zhang et al. (2023) propose the random walk attack, which utilizes Llama2-7B (Touvron
      et al., 2023) as the generation model, with T5-XXL (Raffel et al., 2020b) serving as a perturbation
142
      oracle to iteratively modify the watermarked text. In each iteration, RoBERTa-v3 large (Liu, 2019)
143
      is employed as a quality oracle to provide rewards, while GPT-3.5 OpenAI (2023) performs a final
144   quality check on the generated text. However, since this method does not ensure that the paraphrased
145   text retains the same semantics as the original watermarked text, it diverges significantly from the
146   goals of typical paraphrase attacks.
147
      Our proposed SIRA falls under the category of model-based paraphrasing attacks. However, un-
148
      like conventional paraphrasing techniques that generate entirely new sentences or passages based on
149
      instructions, SIRA selectively replaces potential green words in the watermarked text to provide a
150   ”neutral” template for rewriting. This targeted replacement approach enables more precise control
151   over the consistency and semantics of the watermark-removed text. Additionally, SIRA can be ef-
152   fectively implemented using a lightweight model, enhancing its efficiency for practical applications.

248   3.2   S ELF - INFORMATION REWRITE ATTACK
249
      A primary challenge in watermark removal attacks is identifying the “green token” defined by the
250
      watermarking algorithm. Some methods, such as Random Walk (Zhang et al., 2023), use grammati-
251
      cal group matching to explicitly replace verbs. In contrast, approaches like DIPPER (Krishna et al.,
252   2024) and GPT Paraphraser (Liu et al., 2024) delegate the task of rewriting and removing green
253   token to large language models through high-level instructions. However, methods of this type lack
254   transparency and control; relying on LLM for consistency with original watermarked text.
255
      Our attack is based on a common principle of watermarking algorithms, as discussed in the KGW
256
      (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) work: since the watermark must remain imperceptible
257
      to the user, high-entropy tokens are ideal candidates for embedding. High-entropy tokens exhibit
258   a more uniform distribution of probabilities, this uniformity means that when logits are adjusted
259   to increase the likelihood of green tokens, it is easier to embed watermarks effectively without
260   significantly compromising the quality of the output. Meanwhile this also implied high-entropy
261   token has lower probability thus higher self-information.
262
      In our approach, we propose a straightforward and easily implementable solution by leveraging
263
      self-information to identify potential green-list tokens and subsequently rewrite them. High-entropy
264   tokens are typically associated with high self-information due to their unpredictability and low prob-
265   ability of occurrence. Meanwhile, small probability changes caused by the watermark algorithm
266   can reduce self-information, as briefly explained in Appendix F. By considering both the change in
267   self-information and high-entropy token inherent nature, we classify tokens with high or moderate
268   self-information as potential green-list tokens and filter them out to obtain a more neutral template
269   for LLM rewriting. Empirically, our preliminary experiments show that utilizing self-information,
      rather than directly filtering based on high entropy, results in higher attack success rates.

289
      language model Mattack ; Mattack is distinct from the generative model M used to produce the
290   watermarked text. We use Mattack to calculate the self-information for each token yt as follows:
291

                                  I(yt ) = − log P (yt |y0 , y1 , . . . , yt−1 ; Mattack ),
294   where P (yt |y0 , y1 , . . . , yt−1 ; Mattack ) denotes the probability of token yt given its preceding tokens
295   in the sequence, as estimated by the language model Mattack . To mask the potential green list
296   tokens, we set a threshold ϵ, and get the overall paragraph threshold by percentile:
297

302   outperformed directly masking specific tokens. The placeholders serve as cues, maintain the text’s
303   structure, indicating where tokens have been masked whihc providing the LLM with hints about the
304   original text’s length and the likely number of words, allowing for more high quality reconstructions.

309   step. The reason we do not use the original watermarked text is that we find this leads LLM to take
310   shortcuts: LLM tend to directly take the content from the watermark text, due to the high similarity
311   between masked and watermark text.

451   In this section, We conducted the attack experiments using 50 distinct watermark texts, each con-
452   taining approximately 230 ± 20 tokens. For each method, we measured both execution time and
453   VRAM usage. The reported execution time reflects the average for a single attack instance. The

455   methods requiring multiple GPUs. The configuration for the GPT Paraphraser follows the setup
456   described in Section 4.1. The results are shown in Table 2.
457   One of the main limitations of current model-based watermark removal attacks is their substantial
458   resource consumption. For instance, DIPPER built on the T5-XXL model, necessitates two 40GB
459   A100 GPUs for effective operation. Similarly, the GPT parser introduces considerable costs due to
460   its dependence on a proprietary model that employs token-based billing.
461
      In contrast, our proposed pipeline operates using a minimal configuration of the LLaMA3-8b model,
462   requiring only 18GB of VRAM. This enables compatibility with many consumer-grade GPUs, sig-
463   nificantly reducing hardware requirements. Our approach is efficient enough to run on GPUs with
464   as little as 20GB of memory, and each attack is completed in just two model inferences which makes
465   our attack faster than other methods. We also experimented with larger models in Appendix E, such
466   as Llama3-80b, and found that using a larger model can further improve semantic preservation and
467   the quality of generated text .

      B ROADER I MPACT
703
704
      In this work, we aim to provide an approach to test the robustness of Large Language Models wa-
705   termark. We propose a method that can remove different watermark in LLM generated text. We are
706   aware of the potential risks that our work entails for the security and safety of LLMs, as they are
707   increasingly adopted in various domains and applications. Nevertheless, we also believe that our
708   work advances the open and transparent research on the challenges and limitations of LLM water-
709   mark, which is crucial for devising effective solutions and protections. Similarly, the last few years
710   the exploration of adversarial attacks (Wei et al., 2023; Madry et al., 2017; Krishna et al., 2024) has
711   led to the improvement of responible AI and led to techniques to safeguard against such vulnerabil-
712   ities,e further coordinated with them before publicly releasing our results. We also emphasize that,
713
      our ultimate goal in this paper is to identify of weaknesses of existing methods.
714
715   L IMITATIONS
716
717   Evaluation:We have observed that existing watermarking techniques tend to embed in high-entropy
718   text, which may represent a potential vulnerability. This characteristic could be exploited by attack-
719   ers to weaken the security guarantees of LLMs. The work most similar to ours is DIPPER, but our
720   motivations differ slightly: while DIPPER aims to evade detectors of AI-generated text, our focus is
721   specifically on LLM watermarking algorithms. Due to this difference in objectives, we did not in-
722
      clude methods for detecting AI-generated text, such as GPT-zero, in our evaluation. We will explore
      in future work whether our method can be extended to detectors for adversarial AI-generated text.
723
724   Empirical: One limitation of our work is that it is specifically tailored to natural language. Some
725   watermarking methods for LLMs, such as SWEET (Lee et al., 2023), are designed for code gen-
726   eration. Due to the inherently lower entropy nature in code generation tasks, our method does not
727   design for such scenario. Additionally, this work primarily focuses on the attack. In our future work,
728
      we will further investigate how to modify watermarking algorithms to effectively defend against our
      proposed attack.
729
730   Theoretical: This work primarily focuses on empirical research. Our attack method targets phrase-
731   level attacks across different watermarking techniques for LLMs and relies on an empirical results,
732   similar to DIPPER. Due to the varying assumptions underlying different watermarking techniques,
733   we cannot provide a theoretical guarantee proof for the final attack’s success on each watermark
734
      type. Meanwhile establish the upper or lower bound on its performance is not in the scope of this
      work.

1317   H    E XTENDED DISCUSSION IN RELATED WORKS
1318
1319   In this section, we discuss the watermark-stealing attack (Jovanović et al., 2024; Wu & Chan-
1320   drasekaran, 2024; Zhang et al., 2024), which is related to our work but operates under different
1321   assumptions. We give a brief introduction to this kind of attack and analyze its advantages and
1322   disadvantages compared to our methods.
1323   In a typical watermark-stealing attack, an adversary interacts with a watermarked LLM model
1324   through its API or queries, attempting to reverse-engineer or approximate the embedded water-
1325   mark. Through repeated queries, attackers may identify patterns in token choices or subtle markers
1326   in output text that are indicative of a watermark. Two main attack methods emerge from this process:
1327
1328
             • Spoofing Attacks: Here, attackers use the approximated watermark pattern to make non-AI-
               generated text appear as though it was produced by the LLM, thereby deceiving detection
1329
               systems.
1330
1331
             • Scrubbing Attacks: These attacks aim to remove the watermark from AI-generated text. By
               understanding how the watermark is embedded, attackers substitute or adjust specific to-
1332
               kens or structures to evade detection, making AI-generated content appear as if it is human-
1333
               authored.
1334
1335   For watermark-stealing attacks, these works assume that the attacker has:
1336
1337        1. Unlimited access to the watermark-generated model API, through which the attacker can
1338           issue input prompts and observe the generated responses.
1339        2. Access to the detector API (with or without different assumptions).
1340        3. Knowledge of the context size.
1341        4. An aligned watermarked model that can follow provided instructions.
1342
1343   In a black-box paraphrasing attack, we assume that the attacker’s knowledge is limited to only
1344   the watermarked text and nothing else. Because this assumption is much weaker than that of a
1345   watermark-stealing attack, we do not include such methods in our main comparisons.
1346   Compared to the paraphrasing attack, these methods have the following advantages:
1347
1348        1. Minimal Semantic Drift: By focusing on substituting only the watermarked tokens, such
1349           methods tend to retain the original semantic structure of the text more effectively than
               black-box rewriting, which can sometimes introduce unintended semantic changes.


                                                       25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
            2. Fine-Grained Control: Watermark-stealing attacks operate at a token level, allowing precise
1351           control over watermark removal without needing to rephrase entire sentences or sections,
1352           which may be challenging in highly sensitive content.
1353
            3. Better piror knowledge: Since such attacks assume the attacker has unlimited access to the
1354
               watermarked model, they can continuously probe the watermark pattern through specific
1355           input queries, thereby gaining stronger prior knowledge.
1356
1357   These methods also have disadvantages:
1358
1359        1. Possible Efficiency Issue: As mentioned in SCTS (Wu & Chandrasekaran, 2024), repeated
1360           probing introduces additional time overhead, resulting in higher resource consumption
1361
               compared to paraphrasing methods.
1362        2. Limited Scope of Application: Black-box rewrite attacks do not require knowledge of the
1363           watermarking mechanism, making them suitable for use against a wide variety of water-
1364           marking techniques.
1365        3. Possible lower robustness: Because black-box rewrite attacks focus on altering the content
1366           through paraphrasing, they can reduce the chance of detection by watermarking systems
1367           that rely on token-level patterns.
1368
       Notably, we need to mention SCTS (Wu & Chandrasekaran, 2024). This method also involves
1369
       modifying color tokens, but it is entirely different from our proposed approach. In SCTS, The
1370
       attacker prompts the watermarked LLM multiple times with various inputs, collecting a significant
1371   amount of output data to capture the frequency distribution of tokens. By examining this collection
1372   of outputs, the attacker can calculate the frequency of each token appearing in the generated text.
1373   Watermarked LLMs favor certain tokens, so these ”green” tokens appear more frequently than others
1374   due to the watermarking bias.The attacker observes these frequencies for anomalies or patterns that
1375   deviate from what would be expected in unwatermarked text. Tokens that consistently appear at
1376   higher frequencies or in specific contexts are likely candidates for ”green” tokens.
1377   Therefore, this method identifies green tokens using a frequency-based approach and has two main
1378   limitations. First, it shares a common limitation of such methods, requiring access to the LLM
1379   API and permission to adjust its parameters. Second, it is only applicable to biased watermarking
1380   methods, such as UMD and its variants.
1381
       In contrast, the key insight of our method is that current watermarking techniques require embedding
1382
       on high-entropy or uniformly distributed tokens to maintain text quality, as explained in Section F.
1383   By leveraging self-information, we can exploit this characteristic to identify potential green tokens
1384   and rewrite them accordingly. Our method offers greater flexibility as it does not require access to
1385   the watermarking model and not limited to biased watermark algorithm.
