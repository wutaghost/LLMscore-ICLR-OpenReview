009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we propose to use Sinc interpolation in the context of Kolmogorov-
012            Arnold Networks, neural networks with learnable activation functions, which re-
013            cently gained attention as alternatives to multilayer perceptron. Many different
014            function representations have already been tried, but we show that Sinc interpo-
015            lation proposes a viable alternative, since it is known in numerical analysis to
016
               represent well both smooth functions and functions with singularities. This is im-
               portant not only for function approximation but also for the solutions of partial
017
               differential equations with physics-informed neural networks. Through a series of
018
               experiments, we show that SincKANs provide better results in almost all of the
019            examples we have considered.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Multilayer perceptron (MLP) is a classical neural network consisting of fully connected layers with
025   a chosen nonlinear activation function, which is a superposition of simple functions. The classical
026   Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem Kolmogorov (1961); Arnol’d (1959) states that every
027
      function can be represented as a superposition of function of at most 2 variables, motivating the
      research for learnable activation functions.
028
029   Kolmogorov’s Spline Network (KSN) Igelnik & Parikh (2003) is a two-layer framework using
030   splines as the learnable activation functions. Recently, Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KANs) Liu
031   et al. (2024b) sparkled a new wave of attention to those approaches, by proposing a multilayer
032   variant of KSN. Basically, any successful basis to represent univariate functions can provide a new
033
      variant of KAN. Many well-known methods have already been investigated including wavelet Bo-
      zorgasl & Chen (2024); Seydi (2024b), Fourier series Xu et al. (2024), finite basis Howard et al.
034
      (2024), Jacobi basis functions Aghaei (2024a), polynomial basis functions Seydi (2024a), rational
035
      functions Aghaei (2024b) and Chebyshev polynomials SS (2024); Shukla et al. (2024).
036
037   We propose to use Sinc interpolation (the Sinc function is defined in Eq. (4)) which is a very ef-
038   ficient and well-studied method for function interpolation, especially 1D problems Stenger (2016).
039
      To our knowledge, it has not been studied in the context of KANs. We argue that the the cubic spline
      interpolation used in KANs should be replaced by the Sinc interpolation, because splines are par-
040
      ticularly good for the approximation of analytic functions without singularities which MLP is also
041
      good at, while Sinc methods excel for problems with singularities, for boundary-layer problems, and
042   for problems over infinite or semi-infinite range Stenger (2012). Herein, utilizing Sinc functions can
043   improve the accuracy and generalization of KANs, and make KANs distinguishing and competitive,
044   especially in solving aforementioned mathematical problems in machine learning. We will confirm
045   our hypothesis by numerical experiments.
046
      Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) Lagaris et al. (1998); Raissi et al. (2019) are a method
047
      used to solve partial differential equations (PDEs) by integrating physical laws with neural networks
048   in machine learning. The use of Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KANs) in PINNs has been ex-
049   plored and is referred to as Physics-Informed Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (PIKANs) Rigas et al.
050   (2024); Wang et al. (2024). Due to the high similarity between KAN and MLP, PIKANs inherit sev-
051   eral advantages of PINNs, such as overcoming the curse of dimensionality (CoD) Wojtowytsch &
052   Weinan (2020); Han et al. (2018), handling imperfect data Karniadakis et al. (2021), and performing
053   interpolation Sliwinski & Rigas (2023). PINNs have diverse applications, including fluid dynamics
      Raissi et al. (2020); Jin et al. (2021); Kashefi & Mukerji (2022), quantum mechanical systems Jin


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al. (2022), surface physics Fang & Zhan (2019), electric power systems Nellikkath & Chatzi-
055   vasileiadis (2022), and biological systems Yazdani et al. (2020). However, they also face challenges
056   such as spectral bias Xu et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2022), error estimation Fanaskov et al. (2024),
057   and scalability issues Yao et al. (2023).
058
      In this paper, we introduce a novel network architecture called Sinc Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks
059
      (SincKANs). This approach leverages Sinc interpolation, which is particularly adept at approximat-
060
      ing functions with singularities, to replace cubic interpolation in the learnable activation functions of
061   KANs. The ability to handle singularities enables SincKAN to mitigate the spectral bias observed in
062   PIKANs, thereby making PIKANs more robust and capable of solving PDEs that traditional PINNs
063   may struggle with. Additionally, we conducted a series of experiments to validate SincKAN’s inter-
064   polation capabilities and assess their performance as a replacement for MLP and KANs in PINNs.
065   Our specific contributions can be summarized as follows:
066
067         1. We propose the Sinc Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks, a novel network that excels in han-
068
               dling singularities.
069         2. We propose several approaches based on classical techniques of Sinc methods that can
070            enhance the robustness and performance of SincKAN.
071         3. We conducted a series of experiments to demonstrate the performance of SincKAN in ap-
072            proximating a function and PIKANs.
073
      The paper is structured as follows: In Section 2, we briefly introduce the PINNs, discuss Sinc
074
      numerical methods, and provide a detailed explanation of SincKAN. In Section 3 we compare
075
      our SincKAN with several networks including MLP, modified MLP Wang et al. (2021), KAN,
076   ChebyKAN in several diverse benchmarks including smooth functions, discontinuous functions, and
077   boundary layer problems. In Section 4, we conclude the paper and discuss the remaining limitations
078   and directions for future research.

503   4   C ONCLUSION
504
505   In this paper, we propose a novel network called Sinc Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (SincKANs).
506   Inspired by KANs, SincKANs leverage the Sinc functions to interpolate the activation function and
507   successfully inherit the capability of handling singularities. To set the optimal h, we propose the
508   multi-h interpolation, and the corresponding experiments indicate that this novel approach is the
509   main reason for SincKANs’ superior ability in approximating complex smooth functions; to choose
510   a proper coordinate transformation for machine learning, we propose the normalized transformation
511   which prevents slow convergence.; to satisfy the decay condition, we introduce the skip-connection
512   with learnable linear functions. After tackling the aforementioned challenges, SincKANs become a
513
      competitive network that can replace the current networks used for PINNs.
514   We begin with training on approximation problems to demonstrate the capability of SincKANs. The
515   results reveal that SincKANs excel in most experiments compared with other networks. However,
516   directly approximating the target function is an impractical objective for almost all machine learning
517   tasks. After verifying the capability, we turn to solving PDEs in the PINNs framework. Although
518   the SincKANs achieve impressive performance in approximation tasks for solving all chosen PDEs,
519
      SincKANs merely have the best accuracy on boundary layer problems, due to the oscillations caused
      by the inaccuracy of derivatives.
520
521   Limitations: Approximating derivative by Sinc numerical methods is always inaccurate in the
522   neighborhood of the Sinc end-points. To address this problem, Stenger (2009) suggested using La-
523   grange polynomial to approximate the derivative instead of straightforwardly calculating the deriva-
524   tive of Sinc polynomials, Wu et al. (2006) used several discrete functions to replace the derivative
525
      of Sinc polynomials, etc. Unfortunately, to the best of our knowledge, there isn’t an approach that
      can be implemented in our SincKAN when we demand the derivatives of SincKAN in PIKANs.
526
      Herein, to alleviate the inaccuracy, we choose small h0 , small M , and small N so that SincKAN can
527
      solve PDEs, otherwise, the solution will have oscillations (see Appendix L). Such kind of setting
528   limits the capability of SincKAN and we argue that this is the main reason that SincKAN can obtain
529   good results but not the best results for some cases. Furthermore, the inaccuracy limits SincKAN
530   in solving high-order problems such as Korteweg–De Vries equations, and Kuramoto–Sivashinsky
531   equations.
532
      Futures: However, as the accuracy of approximating the derivative decreases with the order of
533
      derivative increases if the PDE merely requires the first derivatives, then the SincKANs will release
534   the limitation to have larger enough h0 , M , and N and improve the performance. In literature, to
535   avoid calculating the high-order derivatives, MIM Lyu et al. (2022); Li et al. (2024) is proposed
536   to use the mixed residual method which transforms a high-order PDE into a first-order PDE sys-
537   tem. SincKANs can implement this approach to calculate several first-order derivatives instead of
538   the high-order derivatives so that SincKANs can have accurate estimations for the residual loss.
539   Furthermore, replacing the automatic differentiation Cen & Zou (2024); Yu et al. (2024) by other
      operators is also expected.

1171   degree requires a corresponding value f (jh) at the point jh i.e. Ndegree = Npoints . However,

1173
       methods can be regarded as a single-layer representation while our SincKAN is a multi-layer repre-

1174
       Yarotsky (2017). To explore the relationship between degree and size of data, we train our SincKAN
1175
       with different Ndegree and Npoints . In this experiment, we train our SincKAN on spectral-bias func-

1177   decay {hi }Mi=1 in M = 6 and h0 = 7.0. Moreover, we set the batch size Nbatch = Npoints /4 to
1178   adapt to the changing of Npoints . The results are shown in Table 9 and Fig. 9. Additionally, Fig. 9(a)
1179   shows that our neural scaling law is RMSE ∝ G−4 compared to the best scaling law RMSE ∝ G−3
