011                                                                    A BSTRACT
012
013              Adversarial examples pose a significant challenge to the robustness, reliability and
014              alignment of deep neural networks. We propose a novel, easy-to-use approach to
015              achieving high-quality representations that lead to adversarial robustness through
016              the use of multi-resolution input representations and dynamic self-ensembling of
017
                 intermediate layer predictions. We demonstrate that intermediate layer predictions
                 exhibit inherent robustness to adversarial attacks crafted to fool the full classifier,
018
                 and propose a robust aggregation mechanism based on Vickrey auction that we
019
                 call CrossMax to dynamically ensemble them. By combining multi-resolution
020              inputs and robust ensembling, we achieve significant adversarial robustness on
021              CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets without any adversarial training or extra data,
022              reaching an adversarial accuracy of ≈72% (CIFAR-10) and ≈48% (CIFAR-100)
023              on the RobustBench AutoAttack suite (L∞ = 8/255) with a finetuned ImageNet-
024              pretrained ResNet152. This represents a result comparable with the top three
025              models on CIFAR-10 and a +5 % gain compared to the best current dedicated
026              approach on CIFAR-100. Adding simple adversarial training on top, we get
027              ≈78% on CIFAR-10 and ≈51% on CIFAR-100, improving SOTA by 5 % and
028
                 9 % respectively and seeing greater gains on the harder dataset. We validate our
                 approach through extensive experiments and provide insights into the interplay
029
                 between adversarial robustness, and the hierarchical nature of deep representations.
030
                 We show that simple gradient-based attacks against our model lead to human-
031              interpretable images of the target classes as well as interpretable image changes.
032              As a byproduct, using our multi-resolution prior, we turn pre-trained classifiers and
033              CLIP models into controllable image generators and develop successful transferable
034              attacks on large vision language models.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   Our objective is to take a step towards aligning the way machines perceive visual information – as
058
      expressed by the learned computer vision classification function – and the way people perceive visual
      information – as represented by the inaccessible, implicit human vision classification function. The
059
      significant present-day mismatch between the two is best highlighted by the existence of adversarial
060
      attacks that affect machine models but do not transfer to humans. Our aim is to develop a vision
061   model with high-quality, natural representations that agree with human judgment not only under static
062   perturbations, such as noise or dataset shift, but also when exposed to active, motivated attackers
063   trying to dynamically undermine their accuracy. While adversarial robustness serves as our primary
064   case study, the broader implications of this alignment extend to aspects such as interpretability, image
065   generation, and the security of closed-source models, underscoring its importance.
066
      Adversarial examples in the domain of image classification are small, typically human-imperceptible
067   perturbations P to an image X that nonetheless cause a classifier, f : X → y, to misclassify the
068   perturbed image X + P as a target class t chosen by the attacker, rather than its correct, ground
069   truth class. This is despite the perturbed image X + P still looking clearly like the ground truth
070   class to a human, highlighting a striking and consistent difference between machine and human
071   vision (first described by Szegedy et al. (2013)). Adversarial vulnerability is ubiquitous in image
072   classification, from small models and datasets (Szegedy et al., 2013) to modern large models such
073   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), and successful attacks transfer between models and architectures to
074   a surprising degree (Goodfellow et al., 2015) without comparable transfer to humans. In addition,
075
      adversarial examples exist beyond image classification, for example in out-of-distribution detection,
      where otherwise very robust systems fall prey to such targeted attacks (Chen et al., 2021; Fort, 2022),
076
      and language modeling (Guo et al., 2021; Zou et al., 2023).
077
078   We hypothesize that the existence of adversarial attacks is due to the significant yet subtle mismatch
079   between what humans do when they classify objects and how they learn such a classification in
080   the first place (the implicit classification function in their brains), and what is conveyed to a neural
081
      network classifier explicitly during training by associating fixed pixel arrays with discrete labels (the
      learned machine classification function). It is often believed that by performing such a training we are
082
      communicating to the machine the implicit human visual classification function, which seems to be
083
      borne by their agreement on the training set, test set, behaviour under noise, and recently even their
084   robustness to out-of-distribution inputs at scale (Fort et al., 2021b). We argue that while these two
085   functions largely agree, the implicit human and learned machine functions are not exactly the same,
086   which means that their mismatch can be actively exploited by a motivated, active attacker, purposefully
087   looking for such points where the disagreement is large (for similar exploits in reinforcement learning
088   see (Leike et al., 2017)). This highlights the difference between agreement on most cases, usually
089   probed by static evaluations, and an agreement in all cases, for which active probing is needed.
090   In this paper, we take a step towards aligning the implicit human and explicit machine classification
091   functions, and consequently observe very significant gains in adversarial robustness against standard
092   attacks as a result of a few, simple, well-motivated changes, and without any explicit adversarial
093   training. While, historically, the bulk of improvement on robustness metrics came from adversarial
094   training (Chakraborty et al., 2018), comparably little attention has been dedicated to improving the
095   model backbone, and even less to rethinking the training paradigm itself. Our method can also be
096   easily combined with adversarial training, further increasing the model’s robustness cheaply. Beyond
097   benchmark measures of robustness, we show that if we optimize an image against our models directly,
098
      the resulting changes are human interpretable.
099   We operate under what what we call the Interpretability-Robustness Hypothesis: A model whose
100   adversarial attacks typically look human-interpretable will also be adversarially robust. The aim
101   of this paper is to support this hypothesis and to construct first versions of such robust classifiers,
102   without necessarily reaching their peak performance via extensive hyperparameter tuning.
103   Firstly, inspired by biology, we design an active adversarial defense by constructing and training a
104   classifier whose input, a standard H × W × 3 image, is stochastically turned into a H × W × (3N )
105   channel-wise stack of multiple downsampled and noisy versions of the same image. The classifier
106   itself learns to make a decision about these N versions at once, mimicking the effect of microsaccades
107   in the human (and mammal) vision systems. Secondly, we show experimentally that hidden layer
      features of a neural classifier show significant de-correlation between their representations under

134
135   In this section we will describe the three key methods that we use in this paper. In Section 2.1
136   we introduce the idea of multi-resolution inputs, in Section 2.2 we introduce our robust CrossMax
137   ensembling method, and in Section 2.3 we showcase the de-correlation between adversarially induced
138
      mistakes at different layers of the network and how to use it as an active defense.

140   2.1   T HE MULTI - RESOLUTION PRIOR
141
142
143
144
145

149
150   Drawing inspiration from biology, we use multiple versions of the same image at once, down-sampled
151   to lower resolutions and augmented with stochastic jitter and noise. We train a model to classify this
152
      channel-wise stack of images simultaneously. We show that this by default yields gains in adversarial
      robustness without any explicit adversarial training.
153
154   Classifying many versions of the same object at once. The human visual system has to recognize
155   an object, e.g. a cat, from all angles, distances, under various blurs, rotations, illuminations, contrasts
156   and similar such transformations that preserve the semantic content of whatever a person is looking
157   at while widely changing the ”pixel” values of the image projected on the retina.
158   A classification decision is not performed on a single frame but rather on a long stream of such
159   frames that come about due to changing physical conditions under which an object is viewed as well
160   as the motion of the eyes and changing properties of the retina (resolution, color sensitivity) at a
161   place where the object is projected. We hypothesize that this is a key difference between the human
      visual system and a standard approach to image classification, where still, high-resolution frames


                                                                   3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      are associated with discrete labels. We believe that bridging this gap will lead to better alignment
163   between the implicit human classification function, and the explicit machine classification function.
164
165   Augmentations that preserve the semantic content of images while increasing their diversity have
166
      historically been used in machine learning, for an early example see (LeCun et al., 1998). However,
      typically, a particular image X appears in a single pass through the training set (an epoch) a single
167
      time, in its augmented form X 0 . The next occurrence takes place in the following epoch, with a
168
      different augmentation X 00 . In (Havasi et al., 2021), multiple images are fed into the network at
169   once through independent subnetworks. In (Fort et al., 2021a), the same image X is augmented N
170   times within the same batch, leading to faster training and higher final performance, likely due to the
171   network having to learn a more transformation-invariant notion of the object at once. In this paper,
172   we take this process one step further, presenting different augmentations as additional image channels
173   at the same time. This can be viewed as a very direct form of ensembling.
174
      Biological eye saccades. Human eyes (as well as the eyes of other animals with foveal vision)
175   perform small, rapid, and involuntary jitter-like motion called microsaccades (cf. (Martinez-Conde
176   et al., 2004) for details). The amplitude of such motion ranges from approximately 2 arcminutes to
177   100 arcminutes. In the center of the visual field where the human eye has the highest resolution, it is
178   able to resolve up to approximately 1 arcminute. That means that even the smallest microsaccade
179   motion moves the image projected on the retina by at least one pixel in amplitude. The resolution
180   gradually drops towards the edges of the visual field to about 100 arcminutes (Wandell, 1995). Even
181   there the largest amplitude macrosaccades are sufficient to move the image by at least a pixel. The
182   standard explanation is that these motions are needed to refresh the photosensitive cells on the retina
183
      and prevent the image from fading (Martinez-Conde et al., 2004). However, we hypothesize that an
      additional benefit is an increase in the robustness of the visual system. We draw inspiration from
184
      this aspect of human vision and add deterministically random jitter to different variants of the image
185
      passed to our classifier. Apart from the very rapid and small amplitude microsaccades, the human eye
186   moves around the visual scene in large motions called macrosaccades or just saccades. Due to the
187   decreasing resolution of the human eye from the center of the visual field, a particular object being
188   observed will be shown with different amounts of blur.
189
      Multi-resolution input to a classifier. We turn an input image X of full resolution R × R and
190
      3 channels (RGB) into its N variations of different resolutions r × r for r ∈ ρ. For CIFAR-10
191
      and CIFAR-100, we are (arbitrarily) choosing resolutions ρ = {32, 16, 8, 4} and concatenating the
192   resulting image variations rescaleR (rescaler (X)) channel-wise to a R × R × (3|ρ|) augmented
193   image X̄. This is shown in Figure 3. Similar approaches have historically been used to represent
194   images, such as Gaussian pyramids introduced in (Burt & Adelson, 1983). To each variant we add
195   1) random noise both when downsampled and at the full resolution R × R (in our experiments of
196   strength 0.1 out of 1.0), 2) a random jitter in the x − y plane (±3 in our experiments), 3) a small,
197   random change in contrast, and 4) a small, random color-grayscale shift. This can also be seen as an
198   effective reduction of the input space dimension available to the attacker, as discussed in (Fort, 2023).

201   2.2   CrossMax ROBUST ENSEMBLING
202
203
      Robust aggregation methods, Vickrey auctions and load balancing. The standard way of en-
204   sembling predictions of multiple networks is to either take the mean of their logits, or the mean of
205   their probabilities. This increases both the accuracy as well as predictive uncertainty estimates of
206   the ensemble (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Ovadia et al., 2019). Such aggregation methods are,
207   however, susceptible to being swayed by an outlier prediction by a single member of the ensemble or
208   its small subset. This produces a single point of failure. The pitfalls of uncertainty estimation and
209   ensembling have been highlighted in (Ashukha et al., 2021), while the effect of ensembling on the
210   learned classification function was studied by Fort et al. (2022).
211   With the logit mean in particular, an attacker can focus all their effort on fooling a single network’s
212   prediction strongly enough towards a target class t. Its high logit can therefore dominate the full
213   ensemble, in effect confusing the aggregate prediction. An equivalent and even more pronounced
214   version of the effect would appear were we to aggregate by taking a max over classifiers per class.
215   The calibration of individual members vs their ensemble is theoretically discussed in (Wu & Gales,
      2021).


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Our goal is to produce an aggregation method that is robust against an active attacker trying to
217   exploit it, which is a distinct setup from being robust against e.g. untargeted perturbations. In fact,
218   methods very robust against out-of-distribution inputs (Fort et al., 2021b) are still extremely brittle
219   against targeted attacks (Fort, 2022). Generally, this observation, originally stated as ”Any observed
220   statistical regularity will tend to collapse once pressure is placed upon it for control purposes” in
221   Goodhart (1981), is called Goodhart’s law, and our goal is to produce an anti-Goodhart ensemble.
222
      We draw our intuition from Vickrey auctions (Wilson, 1977) which are designed to incentivize truthful
223   bidding. Viewing members of ensembles as individual bidders, we can limit the effect of wrong,
224   yet overconfident predictions by using the 2nd highest, or generally k th highest prediction per class.
225   This also produces a cat-and-mouse-like setup for the attacker, since which classifier produces the
226   k th highest prediction for a particular class changes dynamically as the attacker tries to increase that
227   prediction. A similar mechanism is used in balanced allocation (Azar et al., 1999) and specifically in
228   the k random choices algorithm for load balancing (Mitzenmacher et al., 2001).
229   Our CrossMax aggregation works a follows: For logits Z of the shape [B, N, C], where B is the
230   batch size, N the number of predictors, and C the number of classes, we first subtract the max
231   per-predictor max(Z, axis = 1) to prevent Goodhart-like attacks by shifting the otherwise-arbitrary
232   overall constant offset of a predictor’s logits. This prevents a single predictor from dominating. The
233   second, less intuitive step, is subtracting the per-class max to encourage the winning class to win via
234   a consistent performance over many predictors rather than an outlier. This is to prevent any class
235   from spuriously dominating. We aggregate such normalized logits via a per-class topk function for
236   our self-ensembles and median for ensembles of equivalent models, as shown in Algorithm 1.
237

      similar, transferable gains. We call this setup a self-ensemble.

306   We find that attacks designed to confuse early layers of a network do not confuse its middle and
307   late layers. Attacks designed to fool middle layers do not fool early nor late layers, and attacks
308   designed to fool late layers do not confuse early or middle layers. In short, there seems to be roughly
309   a 3-way split: early layers, middle layers, and late layers. Attacks designed to affect one of these do
310   not generically generalize to others. We call this effect the adversarial layer de-correlation. This
311
      de-correlation allows us to create a self-ensemble from a single model, aggregating the predictions
      resulting from intermediate layer activations.

315
316   In this section we present in detail how we combine the previously described methods and techniques
317   into a robust classifier on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. We start both with a pretrained model and
318   finetune it, as well as with a freshly initialized model.
319   Model and training details. The pretrained models we use are the ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009)
320   trained ResNet18 and ResNet152 (He et al., 2016). Our hyperparameter search was very minimal
321   and we believe that additional gains are to be had with a more involved search easily. The only
322   architectural modification we make is to change the number of input channels in the very first
323   convolutional layer from 3 to 3N , where N is the number of channel-wise stacked down-sampled
      images we use as input. We also replaced the final linear layer to map to the correct number of classes


                                                           6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      (10 for CIFAR-10 and 100 for CIFAR-100). Both the new convolutional layer as well as the final
325   linear layer are initialized at random. The batch norm (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015) is on for finetuning a
326   pretrained model (although we did not find a significant effect beyond the speed of training).
327
328
      We focused on the CIFAR-* datasets (Krizhevsky, 2009; Krizhevsky et al.) that comprise 50,000
      32 × 32 × 3 images. We arbitrarily chose N = 4 and the resolutions we used are 32 × 32, 16 × 16,
329
      8 × 8, 4 × 4 (see Figure 3). We believe it is possible to choose better combinations, however, we
330
      did not run an exhaustive hyperparameter search there. The ResNets we used expect 224 × 224
331   inputs. We therefore used a bicubic interpolation to upsample the input resolution for each of
332   the 12 channels independently. To each image (the 32 × 32 × 3 block of RGB channels) we add a
333   random jitter in the x − y plane in the ±3 range. We also add a random noise of standard deviation
334   0.2 (out of 1.0). All training is done using the Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) optimizer at a flat learning
335   rate η that we always specify. Optimization is applied to all trainable parameters and the batch norm
336   is turned on in case of finetuning, but turned off for training from scratch. Linear probes producing
337   predictions at each layer are just single linear layers that are trained on top of the pre-trained and
338   frozen backbone network, mapping from the number of hidden neurons in that layer (flattened to a
339
      single dimension) to the number of classes (10 for CIFAR-10 and 100 for CIFAR-100). We trained
      them using the same learning rate as the full network for 1 epoch each.
340
341   Adversarial vulnerability evaluation. To make sure we are using as strong an attack suite as
342   possible to measure our networks’ robustness and to be able to compare our results to other approaches,
343   we use the RobustBench (Croce et al., 2020) library and its AutoAttack method, which runs
344   a suite of four strong, consecutive adversarial attacks on a model in a sequence and estimates its
345
      adversarial accuracy (e.g. if the attacked images were fed back to the network, what would be
      the classification accuracy with respect to their ground truth classes). For faster evaluation during
346
      development, we used the first two attacks of the suite (APGD-CE and APGD-T) that are particularly
347
      strong and experimentally we see that they are responsible for the majority of the accuracy loss under
348   attack. For full development evaluation (but still without the rand flag) we use the full set of four
349   tests: APGD-CE, APGD-T, FAB-T and SQUARE. Finally, to evaluate our models using the hardest
350   method possible, we ran the AutoAttack with the rand flag that is tailored against models using
351   randomness. The results without adversarial training are shown in Table 1 and with adversarial
352   training at Table 2. The visual representation of the results is presented in Figure 6.

397    Multi-resolution finetuning of a pretrained model. In this section we discuss finetuning a standard
398    pretrained model using our multi-resolution inputs. We demonstrate that this quickly leads to very
399    significant adversarial robustness that matches and in some cases (CIFAR-100) significantly improves
400    upon current best, dedicated approaches, without using any extra data or adversarial training. We
401    see stronger gains on CIFAR-100 rather than CIFAR-10, suggesting that its edge might lie at harder
402    datasets, which is a very favourable scaling compared to brute force adversarial training.
403   We show that we can easily convert a pre-trained model into a robust classifier without any data
404   augmentation or adversarial training in a few epochs of standard training on the target downstream
405   dataset. The steps we take are as follows: 1) Take a pretrained model (in our case ResNet18 and
406   ResNet152 pretrained on ImageNet). 2) Replace the first layer with a fresh initialization that can
407   take in 3N instead of 3 channels. 3) Replace the final layer with a fresh initialization to project to 10
408   (for CIFAR-10) or 100 (for CIFAR-100) classes. 4) Train the full network with a small (this is key)
409   learning rate for a few epochs
410   We find that using a small learning rate is key, which could be connected to the effects described for
411   example in Thilak et al. (2022) and Fort et al. (2020). While the network might reach a good clean
412   test accuracy for high learning rates as well, only for small learning rates will it also get significantly
413   robust against adversarial attacks, as shown in Figure 20.
414    In Table 1 we present our results of finetuning an ImageNet pretrained ResNet152 on CIFAR-10
415    and CIFAR-100 for 10 epochs at the constant learning rate of 3.3 × 10−5 with Adam followed by 3
416    epochs at 3.3 × 10−6 . We find that even a simple 10 epoch finetuning of a pretrained model using our
417    multi-resolution input results in a significant adversarial robustness. When using the strongest rand
418    flag for models using randomized components in the RobustBench AutoAttack without any tuning
419    against, we show significant adversarial robustness, as shown in Tab 1. On CIFAR-10, our results
420    are comparable to the top three models on the leaderboard, despite never using any extra data or
421    adversarial training. On CIFAR-100, our models actually lead by +5% over the current best model.
422    In Figure 6 we can see the gradual increase in adversarial accuracy as we add layers of robustness.
423    First, we get to ≈ 40% by using multi-resolution inputs. An additional ≈ 10% is gained by combining
424    intermediate layer predictions into a self-ensemble. An additional ≈ 20% on top is then gained by
425    using CrossMax ensembling to combining 3 different self-ensembling models together. Therefore, by
426    using three different ensembling methods at once, we reach approximately 70% adversarial accuracy
427    on CIFAR-10. The gains on CIFAR-100 are roughly equally split between the multi-resolution input
428
       and self-ensemble, each contributing approximately half of the robust accuracy.

515   4    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
516
517   In this paper, we introduced a novel approach to bridging the gap between machine and human
518   vision systems. Our techniques lead to higher-quality, natural representations that improve the
519   adversarial robustness of neural networks by leveraging multi-resolution inputs and a robust (self-
520
      )ensemble aggregation method we call CrossMax. Our method approximately matches state-of-the-art
      adversarial accuracy on CIFAR-10 and exceeds it on CIFAR-100 without relying on any adversarial
521
      training or extra data at all. When light adversarial training is added, it sets a new best performance
522
      on CIFAR-10 by ≈ 5% and by a significant ≈ 9% on CIFAR-100, taking it from ≈ 40% to ≈ 50%.
523   Key contributions of our work include: 1) Demonstrating the effectiveness of multi-resolution inputs
524   as an active defense mechanism against adversarial attacks and a design principle for higher-quality,
525   robust classifiers. 2) Introducing the CrossMax ensemble aggregation method for robust prediction
526   aggregation. 3) Providing insights into the partial robustness of intermediate layer features to
527   adversarial attacks. 4) Supporting the Interpretability-Robustness Hypothesis through empirical
528   evidence. 5) Discovering a method to turn pre-trained classifiers and CLIP models into controllable
529   image generators. 6) Generating the first transferable image attacks on closed-source large vision
530   language models which can be viewed as early, simple versions of jailbreaks.
531   We believe that our findings not only advance the field of adversarial robustness but also provide
532   valuable insights into the nature of neural network representations and their vulnerability to adversarial
533   perturbations. The connection between interpretability and robustness highlighted in this work also
534   opens up new research directions for developing more reliable and explainable AI systems.

      To demonstrate experimentally different characteristics of prediction aggregation among several
906   classifiers, we trained 10 ResNet18 models, starting from an ImageNet pretrained model, changing
907   their final linear layer to output 10 classes of CIFAR-10. We then used the first 2 attacks of the
908   RobustBench AutoAttack suite (APGD-T and APGD-CE; introduced by Croce & Hein (2020) as
909   particularly strong attack methods) and evaluated the robustness of our ensemble of 10 models under
910   adversarial attacks of different L∞ strength. The results are shown in Figure 19.
911
      The aggregation methods we show are 1) our CrossMax (Algorithm 1) (using median since the 10
912   models are expected to be equally good), 2) a standard logit mean over models, 3) median over
913   models, and 4) the performance of the individual models themselves. While an ensemble of 10
914   models, either aggregated with a mean or median, is more robust than individual models at all
915   attack strengths, it nonetheless loses robust accuracy very fast with the attack strength L∞ and at the
916   standard level of L∞ = 8/255 it drops to ≈0%. Our CrossMax in Algorithm 1 provides > 0 robust
917   accuracy even to 10/255 attack strengths, and for 8/255 gives a 17-fold higher robust accuracy than
      just plain mean or median.
