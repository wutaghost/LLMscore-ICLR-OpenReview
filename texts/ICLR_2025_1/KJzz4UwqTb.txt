009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Due to the high memory and computational costs associated with large language
012            models (LLMs), model compression techniques such as quantization, which re-
013            duces inference costs, and parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods like
014            Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), which reduce training costs, have gained signif-
015            icant popularity. This trend has spurred active research into quantization-aware
016
               PEFT techniques, aimed at maintaining model accuracy while minimizing mem-
               ory overhead during both inference and training. Previous quantization-aware
017
               PEFT methods typically follow a two-step approach: first, applying post-training
018
               quantization (PTQ) to model weights, followed by PEFT on the quantized model.
019            However, recovering from the quantization error introduced by PTQ through fine-
020            tuning has proven challenging. Additionally, most PTQ-based PEFT methods
021            result in a mixture of low-precision quantized weights and high-precision adapter
022            weights, limiting the efficiency of full quantization during inference. While a pre-
023            vious method attempted to address these issues, it still suffers from limited adapt-
024            ability due to the constrained LoRA parameter structure required to produce fully-
025            quantized models. To overcome these challenges, we propose L4Q, a method that
026            integrates Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) with LoRA to effectively reduce
027
               quantization error. By employing a memory-optimized layer design, L4Q signifi-
               cantly reduces QAT’s memory overhead while producing fully-quantized weights,
028
               enabling effective adaptation to downstream tasks. Our experiments demonstrate
029
               that this combined approach to quantization and fine-tuning achieves superior ac-
030            curacy compared to decoupled fine-tuning schemes, particularly in sub-4-bit quan-
031            tization, positioning L4Q as an efficient QAT solution. Using the LLaMA model
032            families and instructional datasets, we showcase L4Q’s capabilities in language
033            tasks and few-shot learning.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Given their impressive scalability, Large Language Models (LLMs) such as GPT, OPT, PaLM, and
039
      LLaMA (Brown et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022; Chowdhery et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023a;b)
      are popular in natural language processing. However, their substantial memory and computational
040
      demands pose challenges for practical deployment, making model compression (Han et al., 2015)
041
      crucial for LLM deployment. Quantization is a prominent method that reduces model size by low-
042   ering the bit precision of model parameters (Hubara et al., 2017), so LLM quantization has been
043   actively studied (Liu et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2023; Frantar et al., 2023; Dettmers & Zettlemoyer,
044   2023). These quantization methods are generally divided into two categories: post-training quan-
045   tization (PTQ) and quantization-aware training (QAT). QAT effectively reduces quantization error
046   by integrating quantization into the training process, where both model weights and quantization
047   parameters, are trained together (Esser et al., 2020; Bhalgat et al., 2020). However, applying QAT
048   to LLM quantization is challenging due to the significant memory overhead it incurs during train-
049   ing. As a result, PTQ, which applies quantization without retraining the pre-trained model weights
050
      and with minimal calibration of the quantization parameters, is widely adopted for LLM quantiza-
      tion (Xiao et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024; Heo et al., 2024).
051
052   Concurrently, to enhance the problem-solving abilities of LLMs for specific applications, fine-tuning
053   pre-trained LLMs on downstream tasks is crucial as it improves accuracy on target tasks and related
      tasks (Wei et al., 2022; Scialom et al., 2022). However, fine-tuning is a resource-intensive pro-

199   2.4   Q UANTIZATION -AWARE PEFT
200
201
      To improve the accuracy of quantized LLMs, recent research has introduced quantization-aware
      PEFT approaches (Dettmers et al., 2024b; Kim et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024). Among
202
      these, QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2024b), QA-LoRA (Xu et al., 2023), and LoftQ (Li et al., 2024)
203
      stand out as notable methods. As illustrated in Figure 2, QLoRA begins by applying PTQ to a
204   pre-trained model. After this initial quantization, LoRA fine-tuning is performed, with the quan-
205   tized weight parameters kept frozen. The LoRA parameters are fine-tuned using higher precision
206   formats such as bfloat16 or float16, allowing the method to correct quantization errors during the
207   fine-tuning. However, QLoRA introduces computational inefficiencies during inference due to the
208   additional forward path on LoRA parameters. This inefficiency arises because the high-precision
209   LoRA parameters and low-precision quantized weights cannot be merged into low-precision values.
210   We further examine the impact of this unmerged LoRA path on inference efficiency by comparing
211   the speed of fully-quantized models with mixed-precision models in Section 4.
212   QA-LoRA (Xu et al., 2023) addresses the issue of high-precision LoRA parameters by modifying
213   the structure of the LoRA matrix, allowing these parameters to be integrated with the quantized
214   weights after training (Figure 2). The input dimension of the LoRA matrix A is set to the number of
215   weight quantization groups. This adjustment ensures that each element of BA corresponds directly
      with individual quantization groups, enabling the LoRA parameters to be seamlessly integrated into


                                                       4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      the quantization bias as b′ = b − αBA at the end of training. However, this solution presents a new
217   challenge: the constrained LoRA structure in this setup limits the model’s ability to achieve optimal
218   accuracy during the PEFT stage.
219
220
      A broader issue with previous quantization-aware PEFT approaches is that quantization and fine-
      tuning are performed sequentially rather than simultaneously. Starting the fine-tuning process from a
221
      pre-quantized model with inherent quantization errors is less optimal than starting from a pre-trained
222
      model. While LoftQ attempts to address these quantization errors by approximating them using
223   LoRA with iterative singular-value decomposition (SVD), the limitations on subsequent adaptation
224   persist, making it challenging to fully recover and optimize model performance during fine-tuning.
225   These challenges highlight the need for continued research to improve quantization-aware PEFT
226   techniques, aiming to enhance both the quantization and PEFT processes for better accuracy and
227   efficiency in LLMs.

232   3.1   S TRAIGHTFORWARD I NTEGRATION OF QAT AND L O RA
233
234   QAT-LoRA One of the key principles in our proposed L4Q scheme is the integration of the QAT
235   and LoRA to facilitate the simultaneous calibration for quantization and fine-tuning on downstream
236   tasks. To achieve this, we begin with a straightforward integration of QAT and LoRA, referred to as
237
      QAT-LoRA, which serves as our baseline approach for combining QAT and PEFT. In QAT-LoRA,
      pre-trained weights are frozen, while LoRA parameters are added to the linear layers (Figure 2).
238
      Additionally, quantization scales and bias parameters are introduced, similar to advanced QAT tech-
239
      niques like LSQ, which are crucial for calibrating the quantization function. Freezing the weights
240   reduces the need for optimizer states, while a small number of LoRA and quantization parameters
241   are introduced to approximate updates to the weight matrix and to update the quantization func-
242   tion, respectively. This results in more efficient memory usage compared to standard QAT. Detailed
243   analysis results of the memory efficiency of QAT-LoRA is further discussed in Section 4.
244
      Limitations of QAT-LoRA However, several issues arise with this straightforward integration of
245
      QAT and LoRA, where quantized weights and LoRA parameters remain distinct. First, although
246                                                                                                ∂L
      freezing the pre-trained weights eliminates the need for optimizer states, weight gradients ∂W   must
                                                                                                     q
247
      still be stored to update the quantization parameters, as shown in Equations 4- 5. As a result, QAT-
248
      LoRA still incurs memory overhead from weight gradients, undermining the memory efficiency
249   benefits of LoRA fine-tuning. Secondly, QAT-LoRA produces a mixed-precision model with both
250   quantized weights and high-precision LoRA parameters. This mixed-precision approach negates the
251   advantages of LLM quantization, similar to previous methods such as QLoRA and LoftQ discussed
252   in Section 2.4. Lastly, the gradient updates for quantization and LoRA parameters are decoupled,
253   limiting the potential for comprehensive optimization across the model. As outlined in Equations
254   4- 5, updates to the quantization parameters rely on the quantized weight matrix Wq , while updates
255   to the LoRA parameters depend on weights A and B. This limits the effectiveness of model train-
256   ing, as it prevents holistic adjustments where changes in LoRA parameters could directly influence
257
      quantization adjustments and vice versa.
258   To address these challenges, we introduce L4Q, an enhanced integration of QAT and LoRA. L4Q
259   features an advanced layer design that seamlessly integrates QAT and LoRA. By applying quantiza-
260   tion after merging the weights and LoRA parameters, along with a custom backpropagation path that
261   reduces the memory overhead from the complex quantization and LoRA processes, L4Q effectively
262   overcomes the primary challenges encountered with QAT-LoRA.

      In L4Q, we address the outlier-sensitive nature of LLMs by improving the quantization parameter
342
      initialization at the start of fine-tuning. While the initialization scheme in LSQ+ (LSQ+init ) has
343
      been shown to be effective by using multiples of standard deviation of weights as the quantiza-
344   tion range and setting the quantization scale accordingly, this approach was originally designed for
345   CNNs, not transformer-based LLMs. Studies on LLM quantization have shown that outliers in acti-
346   vation and their corresponding salient weights significantly impact model performance (Xiao et al.,
347   2023; Dettmers et al., 2024a; Lin et al., 2024). In LLM, the standard deviation of the small number
348   of weight groups is often much smaller than the absolute maximum value, causing large weights to
349   be more prone to clipping, resulting in significant clipping errors. To address this, we introduce a
350   quantization initialization scheme, L4Qinit , which minimizes clipping errors by adopting a conser-
351   vative quantization scale that captures both minimum and maximum outliers during training. The
352
      detailed equation is as follows:

398   termine the quantization range, using these combined weights. While previous QAT schemes use
399   the standard deviation of weights to set the initial quantization range, and prior LLM quantization
400   approaches rely on the min/max of the weights, L4Q employs a relaxed min/max-based initialization
401   as discussed in Section 3.3. Then, the proposed L4Q conducts both QAT and LoRA, with gradient
402   computation as described in Section 3.2, to achieve high-accuracy fully-quantized models.

454
      Memory Cost for Fine-Tuning We measure the peak memory usage during fine-tuning of 4-bit
455
      LLMs using both the baseline methods and L4Q, as reported in Table 1. Since PTQ-based PEFT
456
      methods apply PTQ before fine-tuning, the size of linear layers in the model is reduced by a factor
457   of 1/4, resulting in lower memory usage compared to naı̈ve LoRA, which fine-tunes full-precision
458   models. While both QAT and QAT-LoRA result in 2–3 times higher memory costs compared to
459   LoRA, the memory usage of L4Q is similar to that of LoRA. Although L4Q cannot reduce memory
460   costs as much as PTQ-based PEFT methods due to the use of 16-bit full-precision models during
461   fine-tuning, this result highlights that L4Q is well-designed to leverage the memory-efficient nature
462   of LoRA in training. Further analysis on the training efficiency of L4Q and baseline methods can be
463   found in Appendix F.
464   Inference Speedup We measure the inference speed of 16-bit pre-trained models and quantized
465   models using LLaMA-1 models. The quantized models include fully-quantized 4-bit models (L4Q
466   and QA-LoRA), which contain only quantized parameters, and mixed-precision 4&16-bit models
467   (LoftQ*, QLoRA*, and QAT-LoRA), which use additional 16-bit LoRA parameters. The inference
468   speed was measured with input batch sizes ranging from 1 to 64. The average speedup of quantized
469   models compared to full-precision 16-bit models is reported in Figure 4. The 4-bit models achieve
470   a speedup of 1.8× to 2.3× over the 16-bit models. More importantly, these 4-bit models achieve
471
      a 1.4× to 1.6× speedup compared to mixed-precision 4&16-bit models, which are also quantized
      versions of LLMs. This demonstrates that the full integration of QAT and LoRA in L4Q plays
472
      a crucial role in achieving the best inference speed. More analysis on speedup can be found in
473
      Appendix G.

      5      C ONCLUSION
525
526
      In this work, we introduce L4Q, a parameter-efficient quantization-aware fine-tuning method for
527   large language models. L4Q enables element-wise adaptation of model weights for downstream
528   tasks while simultaneously optimizing quantization parameters. This concurrent optimization en-
529   sures that the adaptation parameters effectively account for quantization errors. We demonstrate
530   the efficiency of L4Q, which significantly reduces training resource requirements compared to tra-
531   ditional QAT. Moreover, since the L4Q layer is designed to produce fully quantized low-bit model
532   weights, it maintains inference efficiency, unlike QLoRA, LoftQ, or QAT-LoRA, which result in
533   mixed-precision models. The effectiveness of L4Q as a QAT framework is further supported by
534   experimental results across various task evaluations. L4Q consistently achieves superior quality
535
      maintenance on language tasks, demonstrating its enhanced adaptability compared to QAT-LoRA
      and PTQ-based PEFT methods.
