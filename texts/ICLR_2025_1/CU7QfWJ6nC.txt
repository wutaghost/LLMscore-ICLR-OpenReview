009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion model has demonstrated remarkable capability in video generation, which
012            further sparks interest in introducing trajectory control into the generation process.
013            While existing works mainly focus on training-based methods (e.g., conditional
014            adapter), we argue that diffusion model itself allows decent control over the gener-
015            ated content without requiring any training. In this study, we introduce a tuning-free
016
               framework to achieve trajectory-controllable video generation, by imposing guid-
               ance on both noise construction and attention computation. Specifically, 1) we first
017
               show several instructive phenomena and analyze how initial noises influence the
018
               motion trajectory of generated content. 2) Subsequently, we propose FreeTraj, a
019            tuning-free approach that enables trajectory control by modifying noise sampling
020            and attention mechanisms. 3) Furthermore, we extend FreeTraj to facilitate longer
021            and larger video generation with controllable trajectories. Equipped with these
022            designs, users have the flexibility to provide trajectories manually or opt for trajecto-
023            ries automatically generated by the LLM trajectory planner. Extensive experiments
024            validate the efficacy of our approach in enhancing the trajectory controllability of
025            video diffusion models. Generated video samples are available at the anonymous
026            website: https://FreeTraj.github.io.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Thanks to the powerful modeling capabilities of diffusion models, significant progress has been
031   made in open-world visual content generation, as evidenced by numerous foundational text-to-video
032   models (Wang et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024b). These models can generate vivid dynamic content
033   based on arbitrary text prompts. However, while text prompts offer flexibility, they fall short of
034   concretely expressing users’ intentions, particularly regarding geometric control. Although existing
035   trajectory control works primarily rely on training ControlNet-like structures (Wang et al., 2023c;
036   Chen et al., 2023d), we contend that diffusion model itself contains the potential of substantial
037   control over the generated content without necessitating additional training. In this paper, we aim to
038   investigate the dynamics modeling mechanisms of video diffusion models and explore the possibility
039
      of explicitly controlling object trajectories by leveraging their internal properties. While most of the
      existing efforts are made by modifying text embeddings or adjusting attention mechanisms to enable
040
      control or editing (Ren et al., 2024; Geyer et al., 2023), the influence of initial noises on video motion
041
      remains under-explored.
042
043   For text-to-video diffusion models, there is considerable diversity in the generated content (e.g.,
044   motion trajectories) from the same text prompt, depending on the choice of initial noises. This
045
      phenomenon motivates us to raise a question: Is it possible to regulate the motion trajectories with
      some designs over initial noises? FreeInit (Wu et al., 2023c) has observed that low-frequency signals
046
      are more resistant to additive noises, which makes the diffusion model biased to inherit layout or
047
      shape information from the initial noises. Consequently, by arranging the low-frequency components
048   of noises across frames, we can manipulate the inter-frame content correlation, i.e., the temporal
049   movements of the generated video. However, this constraint is not that reliable because the inter-frame
050   region correlation is not directly aligned with object semantics. Prior works (Jain et al., 2023a; Ma
051   et al., 2023a) have demonstrated that trajectories can also be influenced by adjusting the attention
052   weights assigned to different objects in some specific areas. Thus, to achieve object-level-based
053   trajectory control, we propose to utilize text-based attention to locate the target objects in cooperation
      with noise space manipulation.


                                                          1
054
      However, introducing alterations to the noise or attention mechanism carries the risk of causing
055   artifacts in the generated videos. For example, applying a local mask to the self-attention operation
056   can cause partially abnormal values because this diverges from the case encountered by the models
057   during training. Furthermore, these minor anomalies can propagate through subsequent layers and
058   become amplified in the following denoising steps, ultimately filling the target region with artifacts.
059   We call such a phenomenon as attention isolation. Previous work (Jain et al., 2023a) suffers from
060   this problem and is easy to generate artifacts in the areas with masks. In our proposed FreeTraj
061   system, we are fully aware of this issue and mitigate these risks by applying our operations to the
062   noise and attention mechanisms with a tailor-made scheme. Instead of hard attention masks used in
063
      Peekaboo (Jain et al., 2023a), our designed soft attention masks relieve the phenomenon of attention
      isolation. This approach strikes a balance between staying close to the training distribution and
064
      maintaining the ability to control trajectories.
065
066   In addition, FreeTraj can be seamlessly integrated into the long video generation framework, enriching
067   the motion trajectories within the generated long videos. Current video generation models are typically
068   trained on a restricted number of frames, leading to limitations in generating high-fidelity long videos
069
      during inference. FreeNoise (Qiu et al., 2023) proposes a tuning-free and time-efficient paradigm for
      longer video generation based on pre-trained video diffusion models. Although FreeNoise brings
070
      satisfactory video quality and visual consistency, it has no guarantee for the various trajectories of
071
      generated objects, which are supposed to appear in long videos. With the help of some technical
072   points proposed by FreeNoise, our FreeTraj successfully generates trajectory-controllable long
073   videos. FreeTraj is also valuable in larger video generation. When we directly generate videos
074   with resolutions larger than those in the model training process, we will easily get results with
075   duplicated main objects (He et al., 2024). However, FreeTraj will constrain the information of the
076   main objects to the target areas. Signals of main objects are suppressed in other areas thus the
077   duplication phenomenon will be reduced.
078   Our contributions are summarized as follows: 1) We investigate the mechanism of how initial noises
079   influence the trajectory of generated objects through several instructive phenomenons. 2) We propose
080   FreeTraj, an effective paradigm for tuning-free trajectory control with both noise guidance and
081   attention guidance. 3) We extend the control mechanism to achieve longer and larger video generation
082   with a controllable trajectory.

086   2    R ELATED W ORK
087
088
      Diffusion Models for Visual Generation. Diffusion models have revolutionized image and video
089
      generation, showcasing their ability to produce high-quality samples. DDPM (Ho et al., 2020) and
090
      Guided Diffusion (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) are groundbreaking works that show diffusion models
091   can generate high-quality samples. To improve efficiency, LDM (Rombach et al., 2022) introduces
092   latent space diffusion models that operate in a lower-dimensional space, reducing computational costs
093   and training time, which serves as the foundation of Stable Diffusion. SDXL (Podell et al., 2023)
094   builds upon Stable Diffusion, achieving high-resolution image generation. Pixart-alpha (Chen et al.,
095   2023b) replaces the backbone with a pure transformer, resulting in high-quality and cost-effective
096   image generation. In terms of video generation, VDM (Ho et al., 2022b) is the first video generation
097   model that utilizes diffusion. LVDM (He et al., 2022) takes it a step further by proposing a latent
098   video diffusion model and hierarchical LVDM framework and achieves very long video generation.
099
      Align-Your-Latents (Blattmann et al., 2023b) and AnimateDiff (Guo et al., 2023) propose to insert
      temporal transformers into pre-trained text-to-image generation models to achieve text-to-video (T2V)
100
      generation. VideoComposer (Wang et al., 2023c) presents a controllable text-to-video generation
101
      framework that is capable of controlling both spatial and temporal signals. VideoCrafter (Chen et al.,
102   2023a; 2024b) and SVD (Blattmann et al., 2023a) scale up the latent video diffusion model to large
103   datasets. Lumiere (Bar-Tal et al., 2024) introduces temporal downsampling to the space-time U-Net.
104   Sora (OpenAI, 2024) is a closed-source video generator that has impressive results announced most
105   recently and has garnered much attention. In this work, we choose VideoCrafter 2.0 (referred to as
106   VideoCrafter in the rest of the paper) as our pre-trained base model, as it is a current state-of-the-art
107   open-sourcing model based on the comprehensive evaluations from Vbench (Huang et al., 2023b)
      and EvalCrafter (Liu et al., 2023b).


                                                         2
108
      Trajectory Control in Video Generation. Given the critical role of motion in video generation,
109   research on motion control in generated videos has garnered increasing attention. One intuitive
110   method involves utilizing motion extracted from reference videos (Liu et al., 2023a; Wei et al., 2023;
111   Zhao et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024a; Yatim et al., 2024). For instance, approaches
112   such as Tune-A-Video (Wu et al., 2023a), MotionDirector (Zhao et al., 2023b), and LAMP (Wu
113   et al., 2023b) use specific videos as references to generalize their motions to various generated videos.
114   Although these methods achieve significant motion control in video generation, they require training
115   for each reference motion. To circumvent the need for specific motion training, ControlNet-like
116   structures, such as VideoComposer (Wang et al., 2023c) and Control-A-Video (Chen et al., 2023d),
117
      employ depths, sketches, or moving vectors extracted from reference videos as conditions to control
      the motion of generated videos. However, these methods are limited to generating videos with
118
      pre-existing motions, constraining their creativity and customization. In contrast, controlling the
119
      motion of generated videos using trajectories or bounding boxes offers more flexibility and user-
120   friendliness (Chen et al., 2023c; Deng et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Huang
121   et al., 2023a). While training-based methods (Chen et al., 2023c; Yin et al., 2023a; Deng et al.,
122   2023; Wang et al., 2023d; 2024) have demonstrated significant motion controllability, they demand
123   substantial computing resources and are labor-intensive during data collection. Consequently, inspired
124   by previous work applying attention mask for image editing (Hertz et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023),
125   several training-free trajectory control approaches (Yang et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023a) have
126   emerged. These methods, such as Peekaboo (Jain et al., 2023b) and TrailBlazer (Ma et al., 2023b),
127   employ explicit attention control to direct the movement of generated objects according to specified
128
      trajectories. Our work also adopts a training-free approach. We enhance motion controllability
      in generated videos by imposing guidance on both noise construction and attention computation,
129
      resulting in improved performance in both motion control and video quality.

189   3.2   N OISE I NFLUENCE ON T RAJECTORY C ONTROL
190
191
      During the training process of the video diffusion model, it cannot fully corrupt the semantics when
      adding noise, leaving substantial spatio-temporal correlations in the low-frequency components (Wu
192
      et al., 2023c). Those low-frequency correlations may still contain information about trajectory.
193
      Therefore, if we simulate the noises of the training process and manually add some spatio-temporal
194   correlations in the low-frequency components, we have a chance to control the trajectory of the
195   generated video.
196
197

      initial noises for all frames, we only sample the noise for the first frame. Then we move the noise
198
      from the top-left to the bottom-right with stride 2 and repeat this operation until we get initial noises
199
      zTf low for all frames. Specially, initial noise ϵ for each frame f in position [i, j] is:

289   3.3       T HE F RAMEWORK OF F REE T RAJ
290
      Given a target bounding box for a foreground object in the video, we suppose the pre-trained video
291
      model to generate results whose trajectory is aligned with the given box. To achieve that, we propose
292   FreeTraj, which designs guidance in both noise and attention as shown in Figure 3.
293
294
      3.3.1        G UIDANCE IN N OISE
295
296   As analyzed in Section 3.2, frame-wise shared low-frequency noise can guide the trajectory of
297   generated objects. Therefore, we inject trajectory in the initial noises through Equation 8. To reduce
298   the phenomena of attention isolation, we still need to remove some of the injected noises through
299   High-Frequency Noise Resampling (Equation 7).
300
301   3.3.2        G UIDANCE IN ATTENTION
302
303
      Object trajectories in generated videos with only noise guidance still tend to follow the prior infor-
      mation of the video model. In addition, the controlled object will be automatically selected by the
304
      model according to the training data distribution and cannot be manually specified. To make the
305
      control more precise and assignable, we also add trajectory guidance in attention. There are three
306   kinds of attention layers in the UNet of VideoCrafter (Chen et al., 2024b): spatial cross-attention,
307   spatial self-attention, and temporal self-attention. Unlike previous work Peekaboo (Jain et al., 2023a)
308   directly masks the foreground and background respectively for all attention layers, we design different
309   strategies according to the supposed behaviors in different attention layers. All attention editing
310   is performed in the early steps t ∈ {T, . . . , T − N } of the denoising process, where T is the total
311   number of denoising timesteps, and N is the number of timesteps for attention editing.
312   Attention Isolation. We find the previous designs in attention may cause attention isolation. It is a
313   phenomenon that some regions become isolated either spatially or temporally and rarely pay attention
314   to information outside their own region. This is often caused by the values in this area deviating
315   too much from the training distribution. Unlucky, it is difficult for this region to restore itself to
316   normal levels through valuable information from the other regions due to the isolation. Therefore, it
317   is necessary to avoid attention isolation when we modify the attention mechanism without re-training.
318   We will discuss more in the ablation study and appendix.
319   Cross Attention Guidance. Spatial cross-attention is the only place for prompts to inject the
320   information from text embedding. Originally, the model would assign the object according to the
321   prompts and initial noises. It is a random and unpredictable behavior. To force the model to only
322   generate the target object in the given bounding box, we first add guidance to the cross-attention.
323   Given query Q, key K, value V of cross-attention, and the re-scaled binary 2D attention masks Ma
      and Ma′ , which indicate the foreground and background areas of the generated video respectively.

367
368   FreeTraj can also be seamlessly integrated into the longer video generation framework FreeNoise (Qiu
369   et al., 2023) to generate rich motion trajectories in long videos. FreeNoise mainly applies Local
370   Window Fusion to the temporal attention to guarantee visual quality and utilize Noise Rescheduling
371   in the noise initialization to reserve video consistency.
372   Local Window Fusion divides the temporal attention into several overlapped local windows along
373   the temporal dimension and then fuses them together. In order to cooperate with Local Window
374   Fusion, our guidance in temporal attention is only applied within each Local Window Fusion. Noise
375   Rescheduling reuses and shuffles the sub-fragment of initial noises. To avoid our guidance in noise
376   being destroyed, Equation 8 and Equation 7 are applied after Noise Rescheduling. Through the
377   combined new framework, our method can achieve trajectory control over a long video sequence
      without any fine-tuning (Figure 7 in the appendix).

      4                 E XPERIMENTS
412
413
      Based on performance and accessibility considerations, we choose the recently published open-source
      video diffusion model, VideoCrafter (Chen et al., 2024b), as our pre-trained video model in this paper.
414
      All experiments are conducted based on this model. The inference resolution is fixed at 320 × 512
415
      pixels and the video length is 16 frames unless stated otherwise.
416
417   Evaluation Metrics. To evaluate video quality, we report Fréchet Video Distance (FVD) (Unterthiner
418   et al., 2018), Kernel Video Distance (KVD) (Unterthiner et al., 2019). Since the tuning-free methods
419
      are supposed to keep the quality of the original pre-trained inference, we calculate the FVD and
      KVD between the original generated videos and videos generated by trajectory control methods. We
420
      use CLIP Similarity (CLIP-SIM) (Radford et al., 2021) to measure the semantic similarity among
421
      frames. In addition, we utilize the off-the-shelf detection model, OWL-ViT-large (Minderer et al.,
422   2022), to obtain the bounding box of the synthesized objects. Then Mean Intersection of Union
423   (mIoU) and Centroid Distance (CD) are calculated to evaluate the trajectory alignment. CD is the
424   distance between the centroid of the generated object and the input mask, normalized to 1. When
425   OWL-ViT-large fails to detect the target object in the generated videos, the farthest point will be
426   assigned as the penalty in CD.

      4.2                A BLATION S TUDIES
511
      Ablation of Noise Guidance. To show the effectiveness of noise guidance, we run our designed
512   attention guidance solely. Figure 6 (a) shows that pure attention guidance can also control the
513   trajectory but may lose some accuracy.
514
515
      Ablation of Attention Isolation. We also study two settings that may cause attention isolation. The
      first one uses no high-frequency noise resampling when applying trajectory injection in initial noises
516
      (Equation 7). The second one employs the hard attention mask in Equation 11. Usually, diffusion
517
      models have some robustness to deal with the input with small deviation and recover it to generate
518   qualified results. However, both of these two strategies will easily cause the value of the attention
519   layer to deviate far from the data distribution in the training stage. It will lead to attention isolation
520   where isolated regions almost pay no attention to other regions, losing the chance to recover back
521   to the normal distribution. As shown in Figure 6 (b) and (c), blocky artifacts appear and follow the
522   given trajectory in the generated videos. In addition, those artifacts happen to fall at the position of
523   the attention mask or inject local noise.

525   5             C ONCLUSION
526
527   In conclusion, our study has revealed several instructive phenomenons about how initial noises
528   influence the generated results of video diffusion models. Leveraging the noise guidance and
529   combining it with careful modifications to the attention mechanism, we introduce a tuning-free
530   framework, FreeTraj, for trajectory-controllable video generation using diffusion models. We
531   demonstrate that diffusion models inherently possess the capability to control generated content
532   without additional training. By guiding noise construction and attention computation, we enable
533   trajectory control and extend it to longer and larger video generation. Although not shown in this
534   paper, our approach offers flexibility for users to provide trajectories manually or automatically
535
      generated by the LLM trajectory planner. Extensive experiments validate the effectiveness of our
      approach in enhancing the trajectory controllability of video diffusion models, providing a practical
536
      and efficient solution for generating videos with desired motion trajectories. However, this tuning-free
537
      paradigm is still limited by the underlying model, such as the consistency of object appearance that
538   easily changes during large movements. We hope the study of initial noises can also inspire the
539   training strategy of basic video models.

942   C.1     M ORE A BOUT N OISE F LOW
943
      Here we show another direction of noise flow. Instead of randomly sampling initial noises for all
944   frames, we only sample the noise for the first frame. Then we move the noise from the bottom-left to
945
      the top-right with stride 2 and repeat this operation until we get initial noises zTf low for all frames.
946   Specially, initial noise ϵ for each frame f in position [i, j] is:
947
948                                 ϵ[i, j]f = ϵ[(i + 2) (mod H), (j − 2) (mod W )]f −1 .                                      (14)
949
      After denoising zTf low , results in Figure 9 show that objects and textures in the video also flow
950   in the same direction (bottom-left to top-right). This phenomenon verifies that the trajectory of
951   the initial noises can have an impact on the motion trajectory of the generated result. When the
952   proportion of high-frequency noise resampled increases, the visual quality is significantly improved.
953   Correspondingly, the flow phenomenon is weakened.
954
      The initial noise guidance also works for some other similar base video models. As shown in
955
      Figure 10, the Noise Flow phenomenon still holds on AnimateDiff (Guo et al., 2023).
