009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) for alignment with human preferences
012            have become a key factor in the success of models like ChatGPT and Gemini,
013            which are now integral to mainstream use. Many effective techniques are based on
014            Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), yet are challenging and
015            expensive to implement. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) offers an accessi-
016
               ble alternative by simplifying the objective, but can exhibit random ranking accu-
               racy and requires a frozen reference model. In this paper, we develop a fast and an
017
               even more lightweight DPO based algorithm — CVX-DPO —- that operates on a
018
               single GPU. The key to achieving this is leveraging the convex optimization re-
019            formulation of neural networks, which eliminates the dependence on copying the
020            reference model and is robust against hyperparameter tuning. CVX-DPO can be
021            trained to global optimality in polynomial time. We use the Alternating Direction
022            Method of Multipliers (ADMM) to solve this optimization problem in order to
023            increase parallelization efficiency, and implement our methods in JAX to lift the
024            memory constraints across experiments. We experiment on three datasets, includ-
025            ing one synthetically generated educational dataset, to demonstrate the efficacy of
026            our novel algorithm in a real world setting. CVX-DPO outperforms traditional
027
               DPO in user preference generation when tested on human subjects, despite being
               trained on one single RTX-4090 GPU.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Language models have been trained on increasingly large amounts of data to capture semantic lan-
033   guage patterns. The current paradigm is a combination of pre-training and fine-tuning these LMs to
034   achieve aligned user preferable responses. Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF)
035   (Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Christiano et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2023) has demon-
036   strated impressive results to achieve alignment, and utilizes a three step approach of: supervised
037   fine-tuning, reward model training, and policy optimization. However this complexity presents sev-
038   eral optimization and resource challenges in its multi-stage approach. Recently, the DPO (Rafailov
039
      et al., 2024) algorithm proposes a simpler and more computationally lightweight alternative to align-
      ing LMs for user preferred responses. DPO reparametrizes the reward function instead of learning
040
      an explicit reward model and incorporates this into the Bradley-Terry ranking objective (Bradley
041
      & Terry, 1952). Although simpler, this also yields the following drawbacks: requiring a reference
042   model to stabilize training incurs additional memory and computational costs, there exists a mis-
043   match between the reward optimized in training and the log-likihood optimized during inference.
044   Recent work (Chen et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024) have shown that models trained with DPO exhibit
045   random ranking accuracy even after extensive training. The SimPO (Meng et al., 2024) algorithm
046   employs an implicit reward formulation that directly aligns with the generation metric, eliminating
047   the need for a reference model yielding a more effective yet memory efficient approach. However
048   this also introduces additional hyperparameters, and the authors note that the strategy is crucially
049   dependent on extensive hyperparameter tuning to achieve optimal performance.
050   In this paper, we introduce CVX-DPO, a novel fast and lightweight framework for preference fine-
051   tuning small language models. Our approach is based on stacking a convex two-layer neural network
052   classifier on top of a pre-trained model. Moreover, we propose a new objective based on fine-
053   tuning the convex network’s last layer. Unlike the original DPO objective, this new objective is a
      much smaller convex optimization problem that can be trained to global optimality. As the convex

      network and the fine-tuning objective are both convex, they can be solved using algorithms for
055   convex optimization in a scalable and near-hyperparameter-free manner. In particular, to train the
056   convex network, we combine the ADMM algorithm (Boyd et al., 2011) with GPU acceleration to
057   facilitate fast training. In addition, we leverage JAX (Bradbury et al., 2018) and its Just-In-Time-
058   Compilation (JIT) feature to lower our CVX-DPO code to fast machine code. As a result, using
059   only one RTX-4090, CVX-DPO can fine-tune GPT-2 medium within a few minutes. Indeed, all
060   of the experiments in this work were performed on a single RTX-4090 GPU. Despite the hardware
061   limitation, CVX-DPO enjoys fast train times — less than one hour and, in some instances, only
062   several minutes.
063   Thanks to its lightweight computational and memory footprint, CVX-DPO provides an important
064   step forward in developing efficient ways of aligning LLMs to human preferences that are more
065   accessible in academic settings. This helps to further democratize AI systems for wider audiences
066   and improve optimization techniques in this area. In order to assess the efficacy of our method, we
067   create a synthetic Educational-Tutor conversational dataset, and evaluate on 25 human volunteers
068   via survey to understand ”preference”.

086   2   R ELATED W ORK
087
088   Fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) that better align to human preferences can be approached
089   through three distinct strategies. Initial algorithms of zero-shot and few-shot in-context learning
090   (Xian et al., 2017) relies on prompt engineering. Although this method is able to improve the
091   performance of LLMs to produce desired outputs and does not require fine-tuning, it is not able
092   to tackle complex tasks. More sophisticated learning methods use reinforcement learning to align
093
      model outputs with user preferences. The most successful classes (such as RLHF and RLAIF (Lee
      et al., 2023)) have been able to create conversational LLMs such as ChatGPT. However despite
094
      their impressive performance, these methods are extremely complex, requires humans in the loop,
095
      and requires significant computational resources. Therefore the authors of DPO developed a simple
096   yet performant learning algorithm to directly optimize to human preferences, without explicit reward
097   modeling. The official implementation of DPO references four 80GB A100s, which reduces the bar-
098   rier to training LLMs. Nevertheless, multi-GPU systems are still out of reach for many researchers
099   in academia. In addition, DPO introduces certain hyperparameters that need careful tuning.
100
      Bengio et al. (2005) have previously shown that it is possible to characterize the optimization prob-
101
      lem for neural networks as a convex program. Pilanci & Ergen (2020) further developed exact
102   convex reformulations of training a two-layer ReLU neural network. The core of this representation
103   lies in semi-infinite duality theory, and was derived in ? to show that two-layer neural networks
104   with ReLU activations and weight decay regularization may be re-expressed as a linear model with
105   a group one penalty and polyhedral cone constraints. This is a step towards achieving globally
106   optimal networks and interpretable results. This yields both practical benefits in implementation,
107   and theoretical advantages in analyzing the optimization of the non-convex landscape of NNs. This
      framework is most efficient on two-layer NNs, and on small scale datasets such as CIFAR-10 or


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      MNIST (Mu & Gilmer (2019)). In order to apply this method to the area of LLMs where large data
109   is paramount, we seek better solutions for scalability.
110
111   To practically solve this convex optimization problem, Bai et al. (2018) have proposed an approach
112
      based on the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) (Boyd et al., 2011). ADMM
      offers several attractive advantages, such as its robustness against hyperparameter selection, linear
113
      decomposability for distributed optimization, and immunity to vanishing/exploding gradients. The
114
      successful application of ADMM in solving optimization problems across a wide range of domains
115   has been well studied. This includes diverse fields such as control theory (Li et al. (2017)), maximum
116   a posteriori (MAP) inference problems (Lu & Lü (2019)), computational biology and finance (Costa
117   & Kwon (2020)). The natural parallelization aspects of ADMM seem to make it particularly suitable
118   to deep learning problems. Therefore we aim to integrate the convex reformulations of NNs with
119   DPO. Our goal is to have the convex model provide clear signals to the DPO loss, thus leveraging
120   the faster convergence to obtain LLMs of better quality.

365   Medium architecture, and FLAX-GPT2 as the policy model. CVX-DPO does not require a reference
366   model for stability, and instead use the convex neural network (NN) to signal the model parameters
367
      towards ”chosen” log probabilities. Our selection of GPT based policy models is due to its versatility
      to run in both JAX and Pytorch frameworks, while utilizing a small number of 82 million parameters
368
      in the form of DistilGPT2. This architecture in particular retains approximately 97% of GPT-2’s
369
      language understanding skills despite its reduced size. All experiments are run singularly on Ubuntu
370   22.04 with one RTX-4090, CUDA 12.6 and Jax 0.4.33. Maximum training time reached 2.15 hours
371   on the Stanford-SHP with the DPO loss, while minimum training time occurred with the custom
372   Educational-Tutor dataset in supervised fine-tuning mode of approximately 2min. We keep the
373   same learning rate and configurations as the official DPO implementation.

376
377   In this section we describe the model implementations and benchmarked methods of this work.
      For each model, we train and evaluate on the Educational Tutor Dataset, then the IMDb Sentiment

378
      dataset as described in section 5.1. The Stanford-SHP dataset is only trained and evaluated on the
379   JAX-DPO re-implementation of the official source code by (Rafailov et al., 2024). All other source
380   code, including all JAX code, is custom coded by the authors of this project. During evaluation,
381   metrics and training loss are monitored on Weights and Biases (Jocher et al. (2021)), validated with
382   custom implemented functions for measuring TFLOPs and VRAM, then during human evaluation
383   we sample from our frozen and custom trained models.
384
      Baseline The baseline model is DistilGPT2 with supervised fine-tuning loss. In subsequent DPO
385   settings, we initiate from the saved model of the SFT baseline for each model.
386
387   DPO Method Next we train and evaluate on the traditional DPO model. The reference model is
388
      essentially frozen, and we optimize the policy model with the DPO loss. Rafailov et al. (2024)
      provide in-depth analysis on the mechanics of DPO. This step is significantly more memory and
389
      time intensive than the baseline model. Naive implementations of the DPO model in Pytorch are
390
      not able to complete training on our dataset due to compute limitations of the experiment setting in
391   section 5.2. Therefore we re-implement this train loop in JAX, remove Fully-sharded-data-parallel
392   (FSDP), and rewrite utilities to load our custom features. The addition of the reference model to
393   stabilize training incurs both memory and compute costs which are significant.
394
      SimPO Method This is a recent reference-free method that has shown to outperform DPO on several
395
      metrics. However the introduction of the additional hyperparameter is crucial to its success, and thus
396
      results in longer training time.
397
398   CVX-DPO Algorithm Our novel algorithm builds on prior work, where we have seen that the
399   combination of the convex ReLU NN implemented with ADMM in JAX is able to handle datasizes
400
      such as ImageNet (Recht et al. (2019)) and IMDb and yields faster convergence with solutions of
      better quality. We are motivated by the DPO objective, which treats the policy optimization task
401
      as a binary classification with cross-entropy problem. Therefore, what if we can speed up the
402
      optimization of the DPO loss by giving it auxiliary signal with the convex-NN model?
403
404   In observing the DPO loss, we note that the main component is the inner log ratio between the policy
405   model and the reference model, and then difference in log ratios between ”chosen” and ”rejected”.
406
      We conjecture that by extracting the hidden features as the policy model optimizes, we should be
      able to leverage the convex-admm method to solve the binary classification problem. The output
407
      of the convex model provides optimal weights and classification metrics, therefore we label all
408
      ”chosen” = 1 and all ”rejected” = 0 in training to optimize for user preferences. This convex block
409   is then added into the training loop of the DPO training pipeline, and used to optimize DPO’s BCE
410   style loss by giving strong reward signal feedback.
411
412
      The official implementation of DPO uses RMSProp (Shi & Li (2021)), which is seen to be as per-
      formant as Adam Kingma & Ba (2014) but more memory efficient since it requires less storage
413
      variables. However we note that the integration of the convex-DPO algorithm can provide advan-
414
      tages such as robustness against hyperparameter tuning and faster convergence. This aims to push
415   the DPO loss towards a more globally optimal solution even faster. Please see Appendix C for
416   performance plots.

524   7   C ONCLUSION
525
526
      In this work we introduce CVX-DPO, a novel algorithm for preference learning using convex opti-
527
      mization. This significantly improves the robustness and speed of convergence of the convex auxil-
528   iary signal with the DPO objective. The resulting algorithm is more robust to hyperparameter tuning
529   (such as learning rate), and allows fast iteration with preferable output on minimal VRAM consump-
530   tion. The ADMM solve method provides further speed, efficiency, and parallelism. We implement
531   our methods in JAX and run experiments run on one GPU for speed and better memory efficiency.
532   Our custom generated synthetic dataset of the Educational Tutor setting simulates real world con-
533   versational data with turns of phrase, as opposed to many preference learning datasets which sample
534   acute ”chosen” and ”rejected” responses to a single prompt. We validate the efficacy of our fast
535   lightweight pipeline against 25 human volunteers, with promising results. Thus we hope this work
536
      can reduce the barrier for entry even more for individual researchers and for various educational
      purposes, and take a step towards democratizing the large language model regime.
537
538   Limitations and Future Work Future work will involve running our JAX experiments on TPUs
539   or GPU clusters. Since JAX and ADMM were developed with easy parallelization in mind, more
      performant scaling results should be explored where we can handle even more data.
