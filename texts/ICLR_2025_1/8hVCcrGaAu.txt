011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Seeking good designs is a central goal of many important domains, such as robotics,
014            integrated circuits (IC), medicine, and materials science. These design problems
015            are expensive, time-consuming, and traditionally performed by human experts.
016            Moreover, the barriers to domain knowledge make it challenging to propose a
017            universal solution that generalizes to different design problems. In this paper, we
018
               propose a new method called Efficient Design and Stable Control (EDiSon) for
               automatic design and control in different design problems. The key ideas of our
019
               method are (1) interactive sequential modeling of the design and control process
020
               and (2) adaptive exploration and design replay. To decompose the difficulty of
021            learning design and control as a whole, we leverage sequential modeling for both
022            the design process and control process, with a design policy to generate step-by-
023            step design proposals and a control policy to optimize the objective by operating
024            the design. With deep reinforcement learning (RL), the policies learn to find
025            good designs by maximizing a reward signal that evaluates the quality of designs.
026            Furthermore, we propose an adaptive exploration and replay strategy based on a
027            design memory that maintains high-quality designs generated so far. By regulating
028            between constructing a design from scratch or replaying a design from memory to
029
               refine it, EDiSon balances the trade-off between exploration and exploitation in the
               design space and stabilizes the learning of the control policy. In the experiments,
030
               we evaluate our method in robotic morphology design and Tetris-based design
031
               tasks. Our results show that our method effectively learns to explore high-quality
032            designs and outperforms previous results in terms of design score and efficiency.

038   Design optimization presents a key challenge across various domains such as robotics (Gupta et al.,
039

041

052   the design, which is only part of the problem. This occurs when a given design also requires a control
053   policy to achieve its task and evaluate the quality of each design (Gupta et al., 2021). For instance,
      constructing a robot optimized for locomotion requires both a suitable morphology design and a

      In the multi-level optimization problem, we have to address two distinct challenges: (1) Constructing
077
      the design as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) with unique transition dynamics and (2) Learning a
078   control policy for that MDP. These problems, while both tractable with reinforcement learning (RL),
079   have different priorities. The first problem focuses on exploring the search space for optimal designs,
080   while the second often suffers from sample inefficiency as each new design may need a newly trained
081   control policy. The interaction between these creates a non-stationary optimization problem requiring
082   additional regularization for better convergence.
083
      To address these challenges, we formulate design optimization as a multi-step MDP and propose a
084   general framework with three key components: the design MDP for design optimization, the control
085   MDP for control optimization, and the design buffer. The design buffer maintains a prioritized queue
086   of high-performing designs, reducing non-stationarity and encouraging exploration-exploitation
087   balance. We employ a bandit-based meta-controller to adjust the exploration probability dynamically,
088   ensuring efficient and adaptive learning. This approach effectively integrates design and control
089   optimization, leveraging past successes while continually seeking new possibilities.
090   Based on our general framework, we present a practical method for efficient design-and-control
091   automation called Efficient Design and Stable Control (EDiSon), which is illustrated in Figure 1. The
092   design policy iteratively generates designs, maximizing the reward signal from the control policy,
093   thereby guiding optimization toward promising designs. We implement design memory through a
094   buffer that collects high-performing and diverse designs. Our adaptive exploration and replay strategy
095   dynamically balances between creating new designs and refining existing ones, encouraging the
096   emergence of diverse, high-quality designs by effectively leveraging past successes while continually
097   seeking new possibilities. The main contributions of our work are summarized as follows:
098
099           • A General and Efficient RL Framework for Design Optimization: We introduce an
100
                efficient and general framework that integrates design and control optimization into a multi-
                step MDP. This framework effectively addresses the dual challenges of optimizing both
101
                design and control policies, offering a more efficient and comprehensive approach to design
102
                automation.
103
104           • Adaptive Exploration-Exploitation Trade-off in Design Optimization: We introduce a
105             practical method, EDiSon, based on adaptive exploration and design replay. Our method
106             leverages a bandit-based meta-controller to dynamically balance exploration and exploitation,
107             enhancing the efficiency of design-and-control automation. By reusing successful designs

      Machine Learning for Design Autonomous design research in robotics has advanced through
      various approaches that have broadly focused on optimizing morphology and control. Early works
117
      proposed evolutionary algorithms to adapt the morphology of rigid body and soft body robots to solve
118
      pushing or locomotion tasks (Lipson & Pollack, 2000; Hiller & Lipson, 2012). Subsequent work
119   extended these ideas to learning neural controllers in parallel to the morphology (Bongard & Pfeifer,
120   2003). Compositional Pattern-producing networks have been shown to be good for discovering new
121   morphologies as they could adapt to the changing number of joints in a robot (Auerbach & Bongard,
122   2012; Jelisavcic et al., 2019). These works illustrate the progression and integration of morphology
123   and control in autonomous design. In addition to robotics, machine learning (ML) has also been
124   applied to many other design problems, including building design (Sun et al., 2021), as well as
125   materials, molecular and protein design (Govindarajan et al., 2024; Ghugare et al., 2023; Watson
126   et al., 2023) and algorithm design (Co-Reyes et al., 2021). The difference between our problem
127
      space and the above prior work is explicitly focusing on problems that include two stages of policy
      learning: a design stage and a synthesis/policy learning stage.
128
129
130
      Design Optimization with RL RL has been increasingly applied to design optimization, offering
      efficient methods for exploring complex design spaces. Sims (1994) pioneered the use of evolutionary
131
      algorithms with RL principles to design virtual creatures with adaptable behaviors. Gupta et al. (2021)
132
      demonstrated the significant impact of optimized morphologies on learning efficiency for targeted
133   tasks. Yuan et al. (2022) introduced an RL framework integrating transformation and control policies
134   to streamline robot design and operation. Ha (2019) jointly optimized agent embodiment using a
135   population-based REINFORCE algorithm. Schaff et al. (2019) applied RL to update distributions
136   over design parameters. These advancements highlight RL’s potential to automate and enhance design
137   optimization. RL has also been applied to many other design problems, including concrete structures
138   (Jeong & Jo, 2021), and electronic placement on microchips (Budak et al., 2022). These prior
139   methods make inroads in using RL for design, but they lack tools to cope with the non-stationarity of
140   the optimization to induce higher-performing solutions. In this work, we include a design buffer for
141
      adaptively managing non-stationarity and evaluating over a larger set of tasks than prior methods.

183   4     A G ENERAL F RAMEWORK FOR L EARNING D ESIGN AND C ONTROL
184
185   The design problems we address involve two interconnected challenges: discovering an optimal
186   design (the design problem) and controlling that design to optimize a specific objective (the control
187   problem). This dual challenge is prevalent in scenarios like designing a robotic morphology with
188   a corresponding locomotion policy or creating building blocks for a geometric task. Solving these
189
      problems is complex due to the vast combinatorial design space and the intricate landscape of the
      design objective function. Additionally, control learning must generalize across various designs,
190
      further complicating the process. The interplay between design and control exacerbates the difficulty,
191
      as design evaluation signals are often noisy and dependent on the ongoing control learning process,
192   while the control problem must handle a non-stationary distribution of designs generated in real time.
193
194   To handle these challenges, in this section, we propose a general framework for learning design and
195
      control. As illustrated in Figure 2, the framework consists of three components as introduced below.
196
197   Design As A Multi-Step MDP In this paper, we assume that the Markov assumption holds (see
198
      Apendix C Assumption 1) allowing us to formulate the design as a multi-step MDP. The design policy
      explores the design space and optimizes the design d ∈ D regarding the design evaluation signal
199
      F (d). We use sequential modeling for the design process, i.e., the design policy starts from an initial
200

228   Design Memory The design memory maintains a design buffer B = {di }. The designs generated
229   by the design policy are kept in B selectively according to their evaluation (i.e., the maintenance

233   the co-optimization of an MDP choice and a solution to the chosen MDP is non-stationary, our
234   framework introduced a buffer to store recent high-value designs which also induces control of the
235   non-stationarity of the designs. Specifically, the design memory keeps useful knowledge of diverse
236   sets of best-performing designs to accelerate the learning process. In learning the design policy, the
237   design memory enables the realization of an exploitation-exploration balance in the design space
238
      that also helps find good designs efficiently. In the learning of the control policy, the design memory
      stabilizes the distribution change of design MDPs and reduces the difficulty of learning over multiple
239
      designs, thus leading to better design evaluation.

      5.2   E XPLORATION AND E XPLOITATION IN D ESIGN S PACE
271
272   In this paper, we propose two general design methods. The first method involves designing from
273   scratch, allowing for greater freedom to explore the entire design space. However, solely exploring
274   the design space without exploiting current designs is often less effective. Therefore, the second
275   method involves designing from good examples dgood , enabling the agent to leverage useful and
276   informative designs. This approach closely mirrors human design processes, where we often base
277   our designs on prior work and masterpieces with exemplary performance. In practice, these good
278
      examples can be sourced from a design history or provided by humans prior to training.
279   For fairness, we propose not to rely on artificially given good examples. Instead, we let the agents
280   exploit good examples they found throughout the entire learning process. To facilitate this, we
281   implement a design buffer B to store good designs encountered during training. Whenever the agent
282   needs to design based on an example, it samples a good design dgood ∼ PB from this buffer, wherein
283   PB = softmax(Gd ). More implementation details of our design buffer can be found in App. G.
284   However, solely relying on existing good examples can lead to sub-optimal solutions by failing to
285   explore the design space adequately. Ideally, the agent should first explore the entire design space
286   and, once good designs have been identified, actively exploit these examples to inform further design
287   efforts. To balance exploration and exploitation, we propose a hybrid approach combining two
288   methods: (1) Exploration: designing from scratch and (2) Exploitation: designing from good
289   examples. During each design stage in training, the agent decides to design from scratch with
290
      probability p and to design from good examples with probability 1 − p. We call this probability p the
      design exploration rate which allows us to control exploration throughout the training process:

      C     D ESIGN O PTIMIZATION AS M ULTI -S TEP MDP
757
758
      In this section, we describe the Markov Decision Processes (MDP) used to formalize the design
759   and control stages of our framework. Using the robotic morphology design with the Transform2Act
760   approach (Yuan et al., 2022) as an example, we demonstrate how our formalizations can be applied
761   to analyze an existing design problem and an RL method for design optimization.
762   Assumption 1 (Markov Assumption of Design Optimization). We assume that the design optimization
763   problems we study are all Markovian, meaning that the future state depends only on the current state
764   and action and not on the sequence of events that preceded it. Formally, this is expressed as:
765
766
                          P (st+1 | st , at ) = P (st+1 | st , at , st−1 , at−1 , . . . , s0 , a0 ) .        (6)
767
768   C.1    D ESIGN A S M ARKOV D ECISION P ROCESS
769
      We model the design optimization process as a multi-step Markov Decision Process (MDP), enabling
770   a structured approach to the design stage within our reinforcement learning framework. The elements
771   of this MDP are defined as follows:
772
773   State st : The state at time t is represented by st ≜ (dt , ot ), where dt denotes the design at the
774   time step t, and ot represents the state information of the task/environment. It’s worth noting that,
775   when the design is fully represented by dt and no more other observation can be obtained from the
776   environment, ot can be ignored.
777
778   Action at : The action at time t is given by at ≜ xt+1 , where xt+1 indicates the next/target design

1214   reach, allowing it to span wider gaps with each step. The increased stride length means the robot
1215

1216

1219   the robot to take more steps to cross the same distance, increasing the number of times it must
1220   navigate the gap edges. The shorter reach means that the robot has to exert more effort to span the
1221
       gaps, which can slow down its progress and increase the likelihood of falling.
1222
1223   Joint Flexibility and Movement Efficiency Our design incorporates strategically placed joints
1224
       that enhance flexibility and movement efficiency. The joints are positioned to allow smooth, natural
       movements that mimic a walking gait, which is highly efficient for crossing gaps. This flexibility
1225
       helps the robot adjust its stride dynamically based on the size and distance of the gaps, providing
1226
       adaptability that is crucial for success in this task.
1227
1228   The Transform2Act design’s joint configuration does not optimize movement efficiency to the same
1229   extent. The joint angles and placements may restrict fluid motion, making it harder for the robot to
1230
       adjust its stride effectively. This rigidity can lead to jerky movements and less efficient navigation,
       reducing the overall performance in the Gap Crosser task.

1238   and move each limb, especially when navigating gaps. The increased energy expenditure can slow
1239   down the robot over time, making it less effective in completing the task within a given time frame.

1405
1406   A fixed probability p can help agents balance the trade-off between exploration and exploitation.
1407   However, it does not allow the agent to adaptively select the most appropriate design method according
1408   to different learning stages. For instance, during the early stages of training, agents should actively
1409   explore the entire design space by selecting a large exploration rate p, rather than spending time
1410   exploiting suboptimal designs. Conversely, in the latter stages of training, when sufficient good
1411   designs have been discovered and the design space has been thoroughly explored, agents should focus
1412   on exploiting these good designs by using a smaller exploration rate p.
1413   To address this limitation, we propose a meta-controller that dynamically adjusts the design explo-
1414   ration rate p, balancing exploration and exploitation throughout the design optimization process.
1415   Specifically, we employ a multi-armed bandit (MAB) approach to help the agent decide whether to
1416   design from scratch or use good examples. Each bandit has two arms: arm 0 represents designing
1417   from scratch, and arm 1 represents designing from good examples.
1418   In this section, we introduce the adaptive exploration mechanism used in our method, leveraging
1419   MAB to dynamically adjust the exploration-exploitation trade-off during the design optimization
1420   process.

1514
       The Design Buffer is a crucial component of our framework, enhancing the efficiency and effectiveness
1515   of the design optimization process. This section provides a detailed description of the Design Buffer
1516   algorithm, along with its pseudocode.
1517
1518   G.1     D ESIGN B UFFER I MPLEMENTATION
1519
1520   The Design Buffer is initialized with a predefined capacity N and begins as an empty set. As training
1521   progresses, it is populated with high-performing designs. Each design di is evaluated based on its
1522   performance score F (di ). Designs that meet or exceed a quality threshold are stored in the buffer to
1523   ensure only the most effective designs are retained.
1524   During the design stage, the agent decides whether to generate a new design dnew from scratch or to
1525   sample an existing design dsampled from the buffer. This decision is governed by the meta-controller,
1526   which dynamically adjusts the exploration probability p. The buffer is continuously updated: when
1527   a new high-quality design is identified, it is added to the buffer. If the buffer is at full capacity, the
1528   design with the lowest performance score is replaced by the new design, provided F (dnew ) > F (dmin ),
1529   where dmin is the design with the lowest score in the buffer.
1530   The designs stored in the buffer are periodically refined and re-evaluated, allowing the agent to
1531   iteratively improve upon successful designs.
