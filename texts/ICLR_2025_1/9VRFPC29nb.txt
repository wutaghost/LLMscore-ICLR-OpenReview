011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advances in deep learning have led to the development of numerous models
014            for Long-term Time Series Forecasting (LTSF). However, most approaches still
015
               struggle to comprehensively capture reliable and informative dependencies inherent
               in time series data. In this paper, we identify and formally define three critical
016
               dependencies essential for improving forecasting accuracy: the order dependency
017
               and semantic dependency in the time dimension as well as cross-variate dependency
018            in the variate dimension. Despite their significance, these dependencies are rarely
019            considered holistically in existing models. Moreover, improper handling of these
020            dependencies can introduce harmful noise that significantly impairs forecasting
021            performance. To address these challenges, we explore the potential of Mamba
022            for LTSF, highlighting its three key advantages to capture three dependencies, re-
023            spectively. We further empirically observe that nonlinear activation functions used
024            in vanilla Mamba are redundant for semantically sparse time series data. There-
025            fore, we propose SAMBA, a Simplified Mamba with disentangled dependency
026
               encoding. Specifically, we first eliminate the nonlinearity of vanilla Mamba to
               make it more suitable for LTSF. Along this line, we propose a disentangled depen-
027
               dency encoding strategy to endow Mamba with efficient cross-variate dependency
028
               modeling capability while minimizing the interference between time and variate
029            dimensions. We also provide rigorous theory as a justification for our design.
030            Extensive experiments on nine real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of
031            SAMBA over state-of-the-art forecasting models.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Long-term Time Series Forecasting (LTSF) plays a critical role in various real-world applications,
037   such as early disaster warning and long-term energy scheduling (Zhou et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2021)).
038   Accurate LTSF often relies on extracting reliable dependencies from longer historical time horizons.
039
      For example, periodic and trend patterns are frequently leveraged in existing literature to guide
      model design and interpret results (Wu et al. (2021)). However, the success of simple linear model
040
      (Zeng et al. (2023)) and patching strategy in Transformer (Nie et al. (2022)) suggests that only
041
      capturing periodic and trend patterns may be insufficient for accurate forecasting. In this paper,
042   we explore the predictability of time series by decomposing dependencies into three key aspects:
043   the order dependency and semantic dependency in time dimension, as well as the cross-variate
044   dependency. Specifically, order dependency represents the sequential nature among time points,
045   semantic dependency captures the high-level temporal variation beyond surface numerical values,
046   and cross-variate dependency refers to the inter-variate relationships in multi-variate time series data.
047   This decomposition offers a new perspective for model design in LTSF.
048   In fact, most existing LTSF models fail to comprehensively consider both order and semantic
049   dependencies. For example, linear models (e.g., DLinear (Zeng et al. (2023))) rely on point-wise
050   mappings to preserve order dependency but are too simplistic to capture complex semantic dependency.
051   In contrast, Transformer-based models are effective in learning semantic dependency through the
052   self-attention mechanism (Vaswani et al. (2017)) and show improved performance when local
053   temporal semantics is enriched using patching strategies(Nie et al. (2022)). However, they struggle
      with perceiving temporal order due to the permutation-invariant nature of self-attention, even with


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      positional encodings (Zeng et al. (2023)). Therefore, capturing both order and semantic dependencies
055   simultaneously remains an underexplored problem in LTSF.
056
057   Besides the order and semantic dependencies, cross-variate dependency is also crucial in many
058
      real-world scenarios, such as traffic flow, where significant correlations among variates have been
      observed (Han et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2020)). However, existing approaches that utilize cross-variate
059
      dependency (Channel Dependent, CD) frequently underperform compared to methods that treat
060
      each variate independently (Channel-Independent, CI) (Nie et al. (2022)). This phenomenon arises
061   because previous CD approaches often encounter issues with over-smoothing and difficulty in fitting
062   individual variate series (Chen et al. (2024)). Although a recent method proposes to perform CI
063   modeling first before CD modeling to alleviate the issues (Liu et al. (2023)), our findings, from both
064   empirical and theoretical, indicate that the less relevant temporal dependencies extracted from other
065   variates can still harm the informativeness of the embedding produced for the target variate sequence.
066   Consequently, effectively leveraging cross-variate dependency while maintaining the unique temporal
067   dynamics and characteristics of each variate continues to be a challenging problem.
068   Recently, Mamba (Gu & Dao (2023)) has emerged as a strong contender to the transformer architec-
069   ture in the field of natural language processing (NLP) (Park et al. (2024)). Given the similar sequential
070   structure of time series and text data, Mamba also demonstrates great potential in solving LTSF
071   task. Through extensive experiments, we identify that Mamba presents three major advantages over
072   Transformer for LTSF: (i) Mamba uses State Space Models (SSMs) to process sequences recursively,
073   inherently capturing order dependency(Gu et al. (2021b)). (ii) Its selection mechanism, similar to
074   self-attention, enables it to focus on or ignore particular inputs, making it well-suited for learning
075
      semantic dependency. (iii) Mamba’s near-linear complexity provides an efficiency advantage in
      encoding cross-variate dependency, which are typically computationally expensive when dealing
076
      with large amounts of variates.
077
078   Despite these advantages, directly adapting Mamba for LTSF is still non-trivial due to two key
079   challenges: (i) Mamba was initially designed for NLP tasks, where semantic dependency is of
080   primary importance. In contrast, LTSF typically emphasizes order dependency due to the sparsity of
081
      information in time series as opposed to natural language (Zeng et al. (2023)). (ii) Although Mamba
      can effectively capture both order and semantic dependencies, it lacks mechanisms for modeling cross-
082
      variate dependency, which is crucial for multivariate forecasting. Simply incorporating cross-variate
083
      dependency into Mamba using a CD strategy may exacerbate the issue by introducing irrelevant
084   correlations, undermining the model’s effectiveness to handle individual variate series properly.
085
086   To overcome these challenges, we propose SAMBA, a Simplified Mamba with disentangled depen-
087
      dency encoding specifically tailored for LTSF. First, we remove the nonlinearities in vanilla Mamba,
      which we find to be redundant and prone to overfitting in the context of semantically sparse time series
088
      data, thereby improving its generalization ability. Furthermore, we introduce a theoretically sound
089
      disentangled encoding strategy that explicitly separates cross-time and cross-variate dependencies to
090   avoid mutual interference between the two dimensions. We conduct extensive experiments on real-
091   world forecasting benchmarks, demonstrating the superiority of SAMBA over the state-of-the-art
092   methods. In particular, SAMBA proves universally effective across datasets with varying degrees of
093   cross-variate dependency. Our contributions are summarized as follows.
094
095
             • We identify and formally define three critical dependencies in time series data to guide the
096
               design of the LTSF models.
097
098
             • Our comprehensive analysis reveals two insights: (i) Compared to Linear model and Trans-
099            formers, Mamba can effectively capture both order and semantic dependencies. (ii) Directly
100            applying MLP, Transformer, and Mamba to LTSF can lead to overfitting issues due to the
101            presence of nonlinearities. To mitigate this, we propose a simplified Mamba by removing
102            nonlinearities and incorporating a theoretically sound disentangled encoding strategy that
103            appropriately integrates cross-variate dependency to the model, enhancing the model’s
104            global representation and predictive capabilities.
105
106          • We empirically demonstrate that SAMBA achieves state-of-the-art performance across
107            mainstream benchmarks. Our analysis also highlights the general utility of disentangled
               encoding, offering a universal strategy for developing future LTSF models.

      2    R ELATED WORKS
109
110   Deep learning has made significant advances in the field of natural language processing (Devlin et al.
111   (2018)) and speech recognition (Dong et al. (2018)), inspiring researchers to repurpose these models
112   for time series forecasting (Zhou et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2021)). The mainstream methods for LTSF
113   currently include linear models and Transformer-based models. In particular, because of the strong
114   modeling capability of the transformer, numerous works have utilized transformers to achieve superior
115   performance. However, applying Transformers to long sequence forecasting presents two major
116   challenges: 1) computational overhead due to quadratic space-time complexity and 2) the difficulty
117   of capturing long-term dependencies. Early approaches concentrated on modifying the architectural
118
      design to reduce complexity. For example, LogTrans (Li et al. (2019)) introduced convolutional
      self-attention to capture local information while utilizing LogSparse techniques to reduce complexity.
119
      Informer (Zhou et al. (2021)) leveraged a self-attention distillation mechanism to extend the input
120
      length and proposed ProbSparse self-attention to optimize computation efficiency. Furthermore,
121   Autoformer (Wu et al. (2021)) selected a decomposed structural framework to disintegrate time and
122   replaced traditional self-attention mechanisms with autocorrelation mechanisms to reduce complexity.
123
124
      Recently, linear models have demonstrated superior performance with fewer parameters and higher
      efficiency, prompting researchers to question whether transformers are suitable for long sequence
125
      forecasting (Zeng et al. (2023)). Researchers have begun exploring how to let transformers exhibit
126
      their powerful performance in scenarios like natural language processing and speech based on the
127   inherent properties of time series. For example, considering the distribution shift problem inherent in
128   time series, methods like Non-stationary transformers (Liu et al. (2022b)) have applied techniques to
129   stabilize and reduce the inherent distribution shift of time series. Given the insufficiency of semantic
130   information at individual time points, the PatchTST (Nie et al. (2022)) model, by patching to extract
131   local information, addresses the issue of insufficient short-time series semantic content, thus enriching
132   the semantic content of each token.
133   Recent research shows that utilizing the CI strategy to achieve promising results (Nie et al. (2022)),
134   inspiring researchers to explore a new encoding method that can consider both intra-variate and
135   inter-variate interactions (Chen et al. (2023)). Crossformer (Zhang & Yan (2022)) adopted a cross-
136   variate encoding method and a two-stage attention mechanism that considers both time and variate
137   relationships. iTransformer (Liu et al. (2023)) applied a data inversion method, considering the time
138   series corresponding to each variate as a token and then capturing the relationships between variates
139   using self-attention mechanisms. CARD (Wang et al. (2024)) explicitly models both cross-time
140   dependency and cross-variate dependency, where the latter directly mixes these dependencies at each
141
      model layer. As a result, CARD still suffers from the issue mentioned in the introduction, which is
      the inappropriate mixing of cross-time and cross-variate dependencies.
142
143   Unlike previous work, we explore a new architecture more suitable for LTSF—Mamba (Gu & Dao
144   (2023)), with modifications to better fit the LTSF scenarios. Moreover, different from previous
145   approaches to serially encoding time and variate relationships, we propose a parallel encoding method
146   that decouples cross-time and cross-variate dependencies. This disentanglement ensures that the
147
      encoding process of each dependency does not adversely affect each other, leading to state-of-the-art
      (SOTA) performance on datasets with varying degrees of variate relationships.

158

159   data points of a variate. Formally, given observations ct and cs where t < s, we omit the superscript
160   i of ct for brevity. The order dependency is significant for prediction if

243   4.2   T HE P ITFALLS OF N ONLINEARITY: OVERFITTING IN D EEP LTSF M ODELS
244
245   The success of Linear models has prompted researchers to reconsider the utility of more complex
246   architectures, such as Transformers (Zeng et al. (2023)). Initially designed for NLP tasks, these
247   models emphasize semantic dependency over order dependency and incorporate multiple nonlinear
248
      activation functions. Consequently, we hypothesize that these nonlinear activation functions may
      increase the risk of overfitting in time series data, which possess lower information density compared
249
      to natural language.
250
251   To test this hypothesis, we carry out ablation studies using nonlinear activation functions across
252   multiple backbone models: MLP, Transformer, and Mamba, along with their respective variants
253   that incorporate a patching strategy. As shown in Table 3, including nonlinear activation functions
254
      negatively impacts the model’s performance. Interestingly, removing these functions results in notable
      performance improvements. Figure 1 further illustrates this trend, showing that MLP, Mamba, and
255
      Transformer exhibit varying levels of overfitting, with the nonlinear versions overfitting more than
256
      their linear counterparts. This observation implies that while nonlinear activation functions enhance a
257   model’s capacity for semantic dependency learning, they may simultaneously impair its ability to
258   generalize from temporal patterns in time series data.
259
260

      pronounced in Transformer architectures, with Mamba and MLP following. Notably, this order is
261
      inversely related to the model’s ability to capture order dependency. This suggests that a model’s

354
      Previous CD approaches encode cross-time dependency (order and
355

362   unified space, our strategy ensures that these dependencies are fully
363   leveraged while minimizing the negative interference between them.

368   icities and value ranges but show evident regularity in local changes.

385

386
      et al. (2023)). This can negatively impact model performance. To overcome this limitation, we
387   design an adjustable bidirectional SAMBA module to capture complex cross-variate dependency in
388   a disentangled way without blurring the distinction between the time and variate dimensions. The
389   input sequence is Eivar ∈ RN ×D . Each input sequence through the module will generate a forward
390   result Eif o and a backward result Eiba , maintaining the disorder of time series in variate dimensions.
391   Thereafter, we employ a flexible aggregation operation to integrate the forward and backward
392   information from the variate dimensions of time series sequences and use layer normalization to
393   increase the convergence: Eivar = LN(αEif o + βEiba + Eivar ) where α and β are hyperparameters.
394   This strategy produces patch representations Evar ∈ RJ×N ×D that encompass complex cross-variate
395   dependency.
396   Unified Representation for Forecasting. After disentangling and separately capturing cross-time
397   and cross-variate dependencies, we concatenate the corresponding representations and utilize a Feed-
398   Forward Network (FFN) to aggregate and map them into a unified space: Eo = FFN(Etime ||Evar ) ∈
399   RN ×J×D . In this way, we obtain patch representations that capture three key dependencies without
400   introducing harmful interference. Finally, we use a flatten layer with a linear head to map these patch
401   representations Eo to the final prediction result Z ∈ RN ×S .
402   Theoretical Discussions. Finally, we theoretically analyze the sufficient expressiveness of our
403   disentanglement strategy compared to LSTF models that alternatively encode cross-time and cross-
404   variate dependencies. To begin with, let Φ : RJ×D → RJ×D and Ψ : RN ×D → RN ×D denote
405   a cross-time encoder and a cross-variate encoder, respectively. With a little abuse of notation, a
406   broadcasting mechanism will be applied when encoding 3D tensor inputs. Then our disentangled
407   model can be denoted as Fd (·) = FFN(Φ(·)||Ψ(·)), which encodes the two types of dependencies in
408   parallel. Based on Theorem 3.5 in Gao & Ribeiro (2022) and the evidence from Liu et al. (2023),
409   we focus on comparing our model with time-then-variate LSTF models, which can be defined as
410
      Fttv (·) = FFN(Ψ(Φ(·))). Without loss of generality, we only consider the single-layer setting. We
      further assume that the cross-time encoder Φ(·) can be decomposed into Φ(·) = ϕ(O(·), S(·))+Z(·),
411
      where O(·) and S(·) extract order and semantic dependency respectively, Z(·) is the noisy component,
412
      and ϕ(·) is a mapping. Let ci := ciT +1:T +S for simplicity. Our main theorem is as follows.
413

      ϵ ≥ 0, where O(E)−i (S(E)−i ) denotes the order(semantic) dependencies of variates except from
416
      the i-th one, then the informativeness of the representations output by the disentangled model and the
417
      time-then-variate model satisfy H(ci |Fd (E)i ) ≤ H(ci |Fttv (E)i ) + ϵ.
418

      encoding cannot extract more informative semantic dependencies from the embeddings of other
420
      variates for forecasting the future of the target variate, our disentanglement strategy can extract
421
      sufficient dependencies while reducing interference between cross-time and cross-variate encoding
422   processes. More discussions on the rationality of the assumption can be found in Appendix A.2

526   7    C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
527
528   In this paper, we identify and rigorously define three crucial dependencies in time series: order
529   dependency, semantic dependency, and cross-variate dependency. Based on these dependencies, we
530   examine the three limitations of existing time series forecasting models: (1) The inability to capture
531   order and semantic dependencies simultaneously. (2) The presence of overfitting issues. (3) The
532   improper introduction of cross-variate dependency. We further empirically verify the superiority of
533   Mamba as an alternative to linear and Transformer models, despite its redundant nonlinear activation
534
      functions. Building on these insights, we introduce SAMBA, a model derived from a simplified
      Mamba with a theoretically guaranteed disentangled encoding strategy. Experimentally, SAMBA
535
      achieves SOTA performance, validating the rationality and efficacy of our method design grounded
536
      in rich empirical findings. Our efforts further demonstrate the great potential of Mamba in time series
537   modeling. In the future, we aim to delve deeper into diverse time series tasks, pushing the boundaries
538   of analysis with Mamba.

      A.2   R ATIONALITY OF THE A SSUMPTION (2) IN T HEOREM 1
703
704   We interpret the rationality of the assumption from two perspectives: (1) When the lookback window
705   is short, like the often used 1-hour window in traffic prediction task Wu et al. (2021), there exist
706   low-density semantic dependencies within each variate’s observations, while the order dependencies
707   from other variates could lead to a direct impact on the future of the target variate due to continuity
708   in physical laws, e.g., the increasing traffic volume spreads to neighboring road segments; (2) Even
709   when the lookback window becomes longer, the widely adopted patching strategy compresses the
710
      most useful local semantics into a D-dim feature vector, which is attached to each time step of
      embedding E. Consequently, the semantic dependencies extracted along the temporal dimension of
711
      E usually become less informative.

      D     C OMPUTATIONAL C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
894
895
      First, the time complexity of each Mamba block is O(D2 T + DT log T ) when adopting the
896
      convolution-based implementation ( Fu et al. (2022); Gu & Dao (2023)), where D is the dimension
897
      of token embeddings and T is the length of tokens. As a SAMBA block only removes the nonlinear
898   activation before the selection mechanism, it possesses the same time complexity.
899
900   Next, we consider the time complexity of the disentangled modeling. (i) The linear projection in the
901
      tokenization process takes time O(N JP D). (ii) In the CI learning, the token length is the variate
      number N, thereby taking time O(N (D2 J + DJ log J)). (iii) In the CD learning, the dimension
902
      of token embeddings becomes the patch number J, while the token length becomes the hidden
903
      dimension D. Considering the bidirectional design, it takes time O(2J(D2 N + DN log N )). (iv)
904   The FFN-based aggregation operation and the final prediction head take time O(N JD2 + N JDS).
905
906   In general, the time complexity of SAMBA is O(N JP D + N (D2 J + DJ log J) + 2J(D2 N +
907
      DN log N ) + (N JD2 + N JDS)) = O(D2 · N J + D · N J log(N J)). This validates that SAMBA
      maintains the near linear time complexity w.r.t. of the patch number and the variate number, a key
908
      merit of Mamba, while also inheriting the efficiency of the patching strategy.

      In Figures 4 and 5, SAMBA consistently shows better performance compared to other variants,
919   mainly due to the removal of the nonlinear activation function, which mitigates overfitting, and
920   the disentangled encoding strategy, which effectively captures both cross-time and cross-variate

930   worse than -w/o time, suggesting that cross-variate dependency has a more significant impact in

933   SAMBA shows only marginal improvements over the -w/o variate variant, indicating weaker cross-

936   the -w/o variate variant, highlighting the crucial role of cross-variate dependency in multivariate time
937   series forecasting. Previous studies(Liu et al. (2023); Zhang & Yan (2022)), which jointly encoded
938   cross-time and cross-variate dependencies, mistakenly mixed information from the time and variate
939
      dimensions, compromising the unique physical meaning of each channel. Our proposed SAMBA, by
      adopting a disentangled encoding strategy, effectively captures both types of dependencies, leading
940
      to enhanced performance on complex multivariate datasets.

1570   In this section, we provide a detailed discussion on the differences and connections between order
1571   and semantic dependencies. As previously mentioned, order dependency is tied to the direct input
1572   sequence and emphasizes local temporal ordering relationships, while semantic dependency abstracts
1573   over these sequences to uncover more global or stable latent patterns in time series data. Below is a
1574   tangible example illustrating order and semantic dependencies.
1575   Example 1.              Suppose we have a sequence of electricity consumption values,
1576   [2, 8, 10, 5, 2, 2, 7, 12, 8, 2], exhibiting a clear daily periodic pattern: electricity consumption
1577   increases in the morning, peaks during midday, and decreases at night. When considering order
1578   dependency, the model observing [2, 8] might predict 10 as the next value, assuming the upward
1579   trend continues. However, if we examine another part of the sequence, [2, 8, 10, 5, 2, 2, 7, 12], order
1580   dependency might incorrectly predict the trend will continue upward, while semantic dependency
1581   captures the cyclic nature of the data and recognizes this as part of the descending phase of the cycle,
1582
       predicting that the sequence will likely decrease to 8 or a lower value.
1583   Beyond periodicity, semantic dependency can also be explained through shape patterns or structural
1584   motifs in a time series. Specifically, semantic dependency identifies higher-level, invariant patterns
1585   in the data that enable the inference of the sequence’s full structure from partial observations,
1586   transcending the explicit order of individual values. The following example provides an intuitive
1587   illustration.
1588   Example 2. Consider a time series, [0.1, 0.3, 1.0, 0.5, 0.2, 0.1], representing the electrical activity of
1589   a heartbeat (ECG signal). A typical heartbeat exhibits a distinctive shape: it begins with a small
1590   upward bump (P wave), followed by a sharp spike (QRS complex), and ends with a smaller bump (T
1591   wave). Semantic dependency enables the model to interpret the observed segment [0.1, 0.3, 1.0] as
1592   part of this larger heartbeat structure. The model recognizes the segment as an incomplete heartbeat
1593   signal and predicts that it will likely be followed by [0.5, 0.2, 0.1] to complete the full pattern.
1594   Finally, in our formulation, semantic dependency is designed to focus on intra-variable patterns, i.e.,
1595   patterns within a single variable’s time series. Interactions between different variables are captured
1596   through cross-variate dependency. Such a definition considers the intrinsic properties of each variable
1597   independently, enabling a more effective analysis of their distinct contributions.

1608   State Space Model (SSM) originates from control theory and is a mathematical tool used to describe
1609   the dynamics of systems. It has been widely applied in computational neuroscience, signal processing,
1610
       and engineering control. The core idea is to use the state vector to represent the internal dynamics
       of a system and model the relationship between input and output through input vectors and output
1611
       matrices.
1612
1613   The continuous-time dynamic system of SSM is represented as:
1614
                                           ḣ (t) = A(t)h(t) + B(t)x(t),
1615
1616
                                           y(t) = C(t)h(t) + D(t)U(t),
1617
1618   where h(t) is the state vector, representing the internal state of the system at time t, ḣ (t) = dt
                                                                                                         d
                                                                                                            h(t),
1619   x(t) is the input signal, y(t) is the output signal, A(t), B(t), C(t), D(t) are the state matrix, input
       matrix, output matrix, and direct feedthrough matrix, respectively.


                                                          30
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1620
       In many practical systems, the direct feedthrough matrix D(t) is often zero, meaning the input signal
1621   does not directly influence the output but instead affects the output indirectly through the state vector.
1622   This assumption simplifies the equations to:
1623
1624                                       ḣ (t) = A(t)h(t) + B(t)x(t),
1625                                              y(t) = C(t)h(t).
1626
1627   This simplification makes the equations more concise and easier to work with, providing a strong
1628   foundation for further mathematical analysis and engineering applications.
1629
1630
1631   Modern deep learning and computational frameworks are built around discrete-time data, while the
1632   original SSM is a continuous-time model. To make it compatible with these frameworks, discretization
1633   is required to convert continuous-time equations into discrete-time ones while preserving their
1634   dynamic properties.

       R    D ISCUSSION ON THE D IFFERENCE B ETWEEN O RDER AND S EMANTIC
1769        D EPENDENCY WITH S EASONAL AND T REND PATTERNS
1770
1771   Order dependency describes the sequential link between consecutive observations in a time series,
1772
       where each data point is affected by its preceding value(s). On the other hand, semantic dependency
       refers to the underlying factors that influence time series behavior over extended periods, like
1773
       high-level semantic in the field of CV and NLP.
1774
1775   In past time series forecasting, researchers consider time seires data from the perspective of seasonal
1776   and trend components. However, this view can be considered as a specific example of our proposed
1777   dependency view. Trend information in particular represents a type of order dependency, illustrating
1778
       the sequential progression and causal relationships inherent in time series data. For example, a gradual
       increase in temperature over time shows how current values are influenced by past observations,
1779
       establishing a long-term directional trend in the data.
1780
1781   Seasonal patterns can be regards as a type of semantic dependency, encapsulating more abstract
       and hidden relationships that extend across non-consecutive intervals. For example, repeated cycles


                                                         33
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1782
       in electricity consumption during the summer are influenced by external latent factors such as
1783   temperature and user behavior. These factors are not directly correlated with adjacent time points, but
1784   indicate deeper contextual impacts. Although models like RLinear that capture order dependency
1785   effectively have demonstrated that straightforward linear models can competently capture cyclical
1786   signals (Li et al. (2023)), this is mainly because these strict cyclical patterns are a kind of linear
1787   semantic. But the core of model to forecast is to utilize the periodic semantics.
1788

1791   S    D ISCUSSION ON L INEAR M ODEL O RDER D EPENDENCY
1792
1793   Regarding the statement "linear models can effectively model order dependency," it is because they
1794
       inherently preserve the temporal order by linearly regressing historical data points to forecast future
       values. If the temporal order of the input data points is altered, the trained linear model will perform
1795
       poorly, which we refer to as’sensitivity.’ This argument has been empirically demonstrated in Zeng
1796
       et al. (2023).
