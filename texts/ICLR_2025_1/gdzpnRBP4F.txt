009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive capabilities in
012            generating coherent and contextually relevant text. These models arguably lack
013            the ability to logically reason, an essential skill required to solving mathemati-
014            cal problems and programming tasks. While step-by-step prompting approaches
015            show some promise, they often depend on finding a suitable prompt tailored to
016            the specific model and task. In this work, we propose a simple, yet an effective
017            approach to enhance reasoning capabilities by leveraging reinforcement learning
018            (RL) and the confidence scores of a well-calibrated LLM. It involves optimising
019
               an implicit reward derived from the model’s confidence levels in the answer to
               the reasoning task at hand. We generate preference data and fine-tune the LLM
020
               in a similar spirit to reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), but
021
               without needing any human provided labels or preferences. Our results show that
022            resulting reasoning abilities of an LLM improve and are transferable to other rea-
023            soning tasks. This warrants further investigation of RL as a facilitator for solving
024            complex language tasks.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) have led to impressive capabilities in text gen-
030   eration and comprehension (Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022). However, these models often
031   struggle with tasks requiring deep logical reasoning, which is a critical limitation when deploying
032   them in real-world applications such as legal analysis, scientific computation, and decision support
033   systems (Kambhampati, 2024). While LLMs excel at generating contextually appropriate text, they
034   frequently falter when asked to perform tasks that require maintaining consistency and accuracy
035   across multiple reasoning steps.
036   To address this challenge, various techniques have been explored to enhance the logical reasoning
037   capabilities of LLMs, the most promising being Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning (Wei et al.,
038   2022). In CoT, prompting encourages models to generate and articulate intermediate reasoning
039   steps before arriving at a final conclusion. For example, prompts such as “Let us think step by step”
040   or “Let us break it down” are used (Kojima et al., 2022). By making the reasoning process explicit,
041   CoT reasoning improves the model’s ability to handle complex tasks that require logical consistency
042   and deep understanding. This approach, however, heavily relies on the prompt design, leading to
043   inconsistent performance and user experience.
044   To address these limitations, approaches such as STaR (Zelikman et al., 2022) have demonstrated
045   that models can be fine-tuned in a supervised manner to improve their logical reasoning capabili-
046   ties. While this method can enhance the model’s reasoning skills, it necessitates a large corpus of
047   question-reasoning-answer triples, which is costly to obtain. Further, the fine-tuning process can be
048   time-consuming and computationally expensive, limiting the scalability of the approach.
049   In contrast, Wang & Zhou (2024) found that while LLMs are often not able to provide a correct
050   answer for a reasoning task using vanilla greedy decoding, the correct answer often appears within
051   K beams. Moreover, they found that confidence of answer tokens is correlated with the presence of
052   reasoning in the decoding. Therefore, they propose generating a number of beams and selecting the
053   one which has the highest confidence answer tokens. The results show that performance of LLMs
      on mathematical word problems improve, without the need for additional supervision or fine-tuning.

093   While these challenges have led to innovations within the domain of LLMs, it is important to note
094   that reinforcement learning (RL) has long been a foundational framework for solving complex tasks
095   that can be formulated as sequential decision making problems. The ability for RL algorithms to
096   solve such tasks is evidenced by success in mastering games like Go, Chess, etc. (Silver et al., 2017).
097   In the domain of LLMs, RL has been deployed to reward the generation of the output which reflect
098   human preferences, referred to as reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF; Christiano
099   et al., 2017). This step of optimisation is often seen as critical to allow the models to achieve their
100   impressive performance (Ouyang et al., 2022). Since RLHF is very sensitive towards the quality of
101   the human provided preferences, Lee et al. (2023) propose that a superior LLM provides feedback
102   for the training of smaller models. However this superior LLM still relies on the (accurate) human
103   feedback.
104   Building upon the strengths of RL and addressing some limitations observed in CoT decoding, we
105   propose a novel approach to reasoning tasks, which we name Reinforcement Learning from Self
106   Feedback (RLSF), illustrated in Figure 1. This approach is based on a simple observation: In a
107   well-calibrated model, answer confidence is correlated with the presence of reasoning, which in
      turn leads to better quality answers. The generated beams can be ranked by answer confidence to

119   2.1   C ONFIDENCE E STIMATION IN LLM S
120
121   The reliability and calibration of confidence estimates in large language models (LLMs) are impor-
122   tant in real-world applications. Overconfident models, which assign high confidence to incorrect
123   answers, can undermine trust in these model. Well-calibrated confidence estimates are not only
124
      important for the model’s trustworthiness, but also for improving its performance (Wang & Zhou,
      2024).
125
126   Recent studies have analysed the calibration of confidence estimates across various LLM config-
127   urations. Supervisedly fine-tuned LLMs, trained on extensive datasets, have demonstrated well-
128   calibrated token-level confidence (Kuhn et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2022). However, aligned LLMs
129   fine-tuned using RLHF often exhibit poorly calibrated token-level confidence (Tian et al., 2023;
130
      OpenAI et al., 2024). This discrepancy likely arises because RLHF optimizes for human prefer-
      ences, which may not always correlate with the correctness of answers.
131
132   Traditional token-level confidence estimation methods primarily focus on the confidence of the fi-
133   nal token in a response. This can lead to high confidence in the final token even if the answer is
134   incorrect, as the model might generate a seemingly plausible continuation that does not reflect the
135   correct answer. Recent advancements have shifted towards evaluating the confidence of the answer
136
      span, which has been shown to offer better calibration than the final or average token-level confi-
      dence (Kojima et al., 2022; Wang & Zhou, 2024).
137
138
139   2.2   C HAIN - OF -T HOUGHT D ECODING
140
141
      The performance of large language models (LLMs) on reasoning tasks improves when the model
      generates a chain of thought (CoT). To elicit CoT reasoning, Wei et al. (2022) include examples
142
      of multistep reasoning in the prompt, while Kojima et al. (2022) prompt the model in a zero-shot
143
      manner to “think step by step”. Reasoning capabilities can be further enhanced through specific
144   training on CoT data (Chung et al., 2024), or by teaching the model how to reason (Zelikman et al.,
145   2022).
146
147
      CoT-decoding (Wang & Zhou, 2024) is proposed as a method which does not necessitate specific
      prompting or supervised fine-tuning. Instead, it elicits reasoning by exploiting the correlation be-
148
      tween answer token confidence and the presence of CoTs. Multiple completions are sampled and the
149
      answer with the highest confidence is selected, as a confident answer is more likely to contain a CoT
150   and, consequently, be correct. While this method effectively generates more accurate answers, it in-
151   creases inference time by an order of magnitude due to the need to generate multiple beams for each
152   input. CoT-decoding is mainly used for logical reasoning tasks, however in a recent study, Vukovic
153   et al. (2024) employ it to improve generalisation of ontology relation extractors for task-oriented
154   dialogue.
155
156   2.3   P REFERENCE L EARNING
157
158   In the field of large language models, RLHF is proposed for aligning models with human values and
159   preferences (Christiano et al., 2017). In preference learning, LLMs are typically first fine-tuned on
160   human-written question-answer pairs, followed by RLHF to further refine the model (Ouyang et al.,
161   2022). During the RLHF phase, human annotators evaluate and rank sampled model outputs based
      on various criteria, such as helpfulness and safety. This ranking generates preference data that is


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      used to train a reward model, which in turn guides the model’s further refinement to better adhere to
163   the specified evaluation criteria and enhance answer quality.
164
165   Building on this foundational RLHF framework, Glaese et al. (2022) have introduced additional
166
      layers of control by instructing human workers to focus on specific rules, such as the avoidance of
      stereotypes. This refinement aims to improve the quality of the preference data, thereby enhancing
167
      the alignment process and mitigating the risk of adversarial attacks. Similarly, Bai et al. (2022) have
168
      employed a trained preference model to rank generated utterances, utilising this ranked data to train a
169   reward model, thereby advancing the alignment and safety of the LLM. Despite these advancements,
170   all existing RLHF approaches are heavily dependent on human labellers for generating preference
171   and safety data.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
175
      Our method is based on the observation that if the model is well calibrated the confidence of the
176
      answer correlates with the presence of reasoning and hence with the accuracy of this answer. There-
177
      fore, a sequential-decision making process is needed that chooses the tokens of the generated text in
178   such a way that the confidence of the answer tokens is maximal. This can be achieved via reinforce-
179   ment learning.
180
181   3.1   C HAIN - OF -T HOUGHT D ECODING P RELIMINARIES
182
183   The goal of this method is to extract the inherent reasoning ability of a large language model by
184   generating multiple hypotheses for a given input q. In contrast to traditional beam-search methods,
185   in CoT decoding one samples the K highest-probability tokens ti , i = 1, · · · , K at the very first
186   decoding step. From here, each hypothesis hi , i = 1, · · · , K is expanded using standard auto-
187   regressive decoding, i.e. hi [0] = ti . Then the tokens contained within the answer ai , i = 1, · · · , K
188
      to the question, q, are identified in each hypothesis hi . This is done by appending the text “So
      the answer is” to each hypothesis hi , subsequently continuing the decoding process to generate
189
      a′i , i = 1, · · · , K. Next, the repeated answer span is located in the hypothesis hi using string
190
      matching to obtain ai .
191
192   The final beam a is the one with the highest answer confidence of the model, a = arg maxi d (hi ).
193   The confidence in the answer is calculated as the average token level probability disparity of the
194
      answer tokens (Wang & Zhou, 2024). That is, given an answer a, consisting of M tokens tm , m =
      1, · · · , M , the confidence of the model in the answer is calculated as:

203   Probability disparity tends to be a more reliable indicator of the model’s confidence than the proba-
204   bility of the token itself. This is because it also considers the probability of the second most likely
205   token, which helps capture the spread of probability mass across the vocabulary. Higher dispar-
206
      ity corresponds to the model having a high certainty in sampling of the answer tokens, since an
      alternative continuation is significantly less likely to be sampled.
207
208   To further improve the reliability of the confidence scores, we consider the frequency of unique
209   answers in the generated hypotheses. It has been shown empirically that the answer is more likely
210   to be correct if it is the most frequent answer in the set of hypotheses (Wang & Zhou, 2024). To
211   combine confidence and frequency, we calculate the final confidence score for an answer as the sum
212
      of the confidence scores of the beams that contain that answer.
213   It is important to note that CoT decoding does not guarantee reasoning (Wang & Zhou, 2024), but
214   rather builds on the observed correlation of answer confidence and reasoning to improve answers.
215   The ability to generate rationales is likely learned during pre-training, since logical reasoning data
      often contains reasoning. However, LLMs tend not to reason when prompted directly and output

      the answer directly instead. Our approach aims at bringing the inherent LLM reasoning abilities
217   forward to be predicted via greedy decoding.

      3.2   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING FROM S ELF F EEDBACK
220
221   Reinforcement learning (RL) relies on a reward signal to guide the learning process. Thus, defining
222   the reward appropriately is an important aspect of any RL-based approach.
223
      In our approach, we utilise a reward model, inspired by Reinforcement Learning from Human Feed-
224
      back (RLHF; Christiano et al., 2017). In RLHF, the reward model is a large language models which
225   is trained using a combination of pre-training, supervised fine-tuning, and fine-tuning on human
226   preference data. The human preference data is used to train a reward model, which assigns a score
227   to generated sequences based on human judgements. The objective function for training the reward
228   model is given as follows (Bradley & Terry, 1952):
229
230                         L h(1) , h(2) ; θ rew = − log σ R h(1) − R h(2)     ,
231
232
      where hypothesis h(1) is preferred over hypothesis h(2) , σ(·) is the sigmoid activation function, and
      R(·) is the reward assigned to a hypothesis by the reward model parameterised by parameters θ rew .
233
234   Once the reward model is trained, it can be used to assign reward values to the generated sequences.
235   In the context of reinforcement learning, this implies that each token in a sequence is assigned a
236   reward of zero, except for the final token, which receives the reward predicted by the model for the
237   entire sequence.
238   In contrast to approaches that rely on human preferences for ranking, our method ranks hypotheses
239   based solely on the confidence scores of their predicted answers. These confidence-based rankings
240   are then used to train the reward model, and the correctness of the answers is not explicitly con-
241   sidered when ranking the hypotheses. Given that the model relies entirely on its own confidence
242   scores rather than external feedback, we refer to this approach as Reinforcement Learning from
243   Self-Feedback (RLSF).

257   4.2   C ALIBRATION
258
259
      As outlined in Section 3, our approach relies on the calibration of the language model. For evaluating
      the calibration, we use the estimated calibration error (ECE; Naeini et al., 2015) that measures the
260
      alignment between model confidence and prediction accuracy. In other words, ECE measures how
261
      much model confidence tells us about answer quality; the lower ECE the better. Consequently, we
262   decided to use the Phi-2 model (Hughes, 2023) in all our experiments, as it demonstrated superior
263   confidence calibration compared to alternative models, including Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023) and
264   Gemma (Team et al., 2024) (see Table 1). Furthermore, all fine-tuning, including the training of the
265   reward model, was performed using Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021).

307   5.1    C HAIN OF T HOUGHT D ECODING
308
309   In this section, we evaluate the original CoT decoding strategy (Section 3.1) on the multi-arithmetic
310   mathematical reasoning task (Figure 2). Our results confirm that it outperforms both greedy de-
311
      coding and beam search. We observe that greedy decoding achieves the lowest performance, as
      anticipated. Beam search performs better than greedy decoding, however, interestingly, its perfor-
312
      mance declines as the number of beams increases. We hypothesise that this decline is due to beam
313
      search’s reliance on token-level probabilities to prune beams. In contrast, CoT decoding, which
314   utilises answer-level confidence to rank beams, significantly outperforms both greedy decoding and
315   beam search. This performance is further enhanced when answer confidences are accumulated.

      We hypothesise that the reduction in performance observed with CoT decoding is due to the diffi-
385   culty in correctly locating the answer span in these more complex tasks. This issue arises because
386   CoT decoding relies heavily on re-ranking hypotheses based on the answer-span confidence. When
387   the correct answer span is challenging to identify, this can lead to undesirable behaviour during the
388   re-ranking process, with incorrect spans being ranked more favourably. In the case of coin flip and
389   last letter the problem arises from the answer tokens being mentioned several times in the generated
390   reasoning, decreasing the impact of the final answer mention when choosing the best beam.
391   In contrast, the RLSF model avoids this problem, as it does not depend on identifying the answer
392   span during inference; only the final answer needs to be parsed for evaluation. This allows the
393   RLSF model to maintain its high performance, even in tasks where span identification is inherently
394   difficult. As a result, the RLSF model remains robust across different types of tasks, showcasing its
395   versatility and ability to adapt to varying reasoning challenges without the limitations imposed by
396   answer span-dependent methods.

      enhanced reasoning capabilities compared to the base model, as evidenced by its ability to produce
      more detailed and accurate responses. The increased length and quality of answers suggest that the
421
      model engages in deeper reasoning. However, the reasoning process is not without flaws. In some
422
      cases, the model generates a final answer prematurely and then attempts to justify it retroactively,
423   which indicates that the answer is not always the result of a coherent reasoning process. Addi-
424   tionally, there are instances where the model digresses, offering irrelevant or repetitive explanations
425   without contributing meaningful logical reasoning. Thus, while the RLSF model demonstrates im-
426   proved reasoning, there remain areas where its reasoning could be further refined to achieve greater
427   precision and coherence.

      An important limitation of our approach is the fact that it only works when the calibration of the

      it only gives us an idea of the calibration for the specific task.

468   responses does not ensure reasoning and answer quality in all cases. It is also important to mention
469   that the initial model has to be capable of generating reasoning in some considered beams for the
470   method to improve reasoning capabilities.

      6     C ONCLUSION
474
475
      In this work, we proposed a novel approach, Reinforcement Learning with Self-Feedback (RLSF),
      to improve reasoning capabilities in language models. Our method leverages self-reflection during
476
      training, coupled with a reward model that guides the language model to generate more accurate
477
      and logically consistent responses. Through evaluation on mathematical reasoning tasks, such as
478   Multi-Arith and GSM8K, as well as common-sense reasoning and synthetic tasks like StrategyQA
479   and coin-state tracking, we demonstrated that the RLSF approach significantly outperforms baseline
480   models and decoding techniques, including CoT decoding.
481
      Our contributions include:
482
483
            1. the introduction of the RLSF framework, which improves a model’s reasoning ability by
484            utilising its own feedback in a reinforcement learning loop,
485
            2. the development of a reward model trained from model-generated preferences, and


                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
            3. empirical evidence showing that RLSF outperforms established methods across multiple
487            reasoning domains.
488
489   Despite these advancements, there are still areas for future improvement. In particular, we observed
490   that the model’s reasoning can be imperfect in certain cases, with tendencies to justify answers post
491   hoc or produce irrelevant explanations. A more critical limitation of the current RLSF framework is
492   that it performs single-step reasoning, which restricts its ability to handle tasks requiring long-term
493
      planning and more complex decision-making. While reinforcement learning is leveraged to enhance
      reasoning, RLSF, like many RLHF-based approaches, remains focused on single-turn interactions.
494
      The incorporation of long-term planning into the reinforcement learning process for language mod-
495
      els is still in its early stages (Zhou et al., 2024).
496
497   In future work, we envision addressing these limitations by integrating more advanced reasoning
498   mechanisms, particularly multistep reasoning and long-term planning, into the RLSF framework.
499
      By extending beyond single-turn optimisation, models could potentially reason over extended se-
      quences of actions, making them more suitable for tasks that require complex reasoning or decision-
500
      making.
501

503   7   E THICS S TATEMENT
504
505   Although the presented approach improves performance and encourages reasoning in LLMs, it does
506   not include measures to prevent harmful output. Depending on the calibration of the LLM and the
507
      underlying data used for comparison data collection, it might be possible that harmful behaviour of
      the LLM is reinforced.
508
      However, by reducing the need for human intervention for reasoning training of LLMs, there are po-
509
      tentially more resources available to mitigate harmful LLM behaviour. Apart from that, the learned
510   reasoning could possibly reduce the risk of harmful behaviour, since the LLMs learn not to rely on
511   the highest probability response from the pre-training data distribution.
