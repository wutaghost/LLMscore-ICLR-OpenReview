009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We present Tensor-GaLore, a novel method for efficient training of neural net-
012            works with higher-order tensor weights. Many models, particularly those used
013            in scientific computing, employ tensor-parameterized layers to capture complex,
014            multidimensional relationships. When scaling these methods to high-resolution
015            problems makes memory usage grow intractably, and matrix based optimization
016            methods lead to suboptimal performance and compression. We propose to work
017            directly in the high-order space of the complex tensor parameter space using a
018
               tensor factorization of the gradients during optimization. We showcase its ef-
               fectiveness on Fourier Neural Operators (FNOs), a class of models crucial for
019
               solving partial differential equations (PDE) and prove the theory of it. Across var-
020
               ious PDE tasks like the Navier Stokes and Darcy Flow equations, Tensor-GaLore
021            achieves substantial memory savings, reducing optimizer memory usage by up
022            to 75%. These substantial memory savings across AI for science demonstrate
023            Tensor-GaLore’s potential.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The advent of foundation models has revolutionized AI, demonstrating unprecedented performance
029   across diverse domains such as natural language processing, computer vision, and scientific com-
030   puting Brown et al. (2020); Kirillov et al. (2023). However, as these models grow in scale and
031   complexity, they present significant computational challenges. With parameters often numbering
032   in the billions, these models demand enormous memory resources for storage and optimization,
033
      making their training and deployment prohibitively expensive for many researchers and organiza-
      tions. Recent work (See Section 6) has focused on parameter-efficient fine-tuning and pre-training
034
      methods to address these issues.
035
036   Gradients in deep neural networks often exhibit low-rank structures during training, implying that
037   the most important gradient information can be stored at a fraction of the memory cost. GaLore
038   (Gradient Low-Rank Projection) Zhao et al. (2024) leveraged this insight to reduce memory usage
039
      in large language model training by projecting large gradients onto low-rank subspaces and opti-
      mizing on the low-rank gradients. Specifically, GaLore operates on weight matrices W ∈ Rm×n
040
      and their corresponding gradient matrices G ∈ Rm×n . For a given rank r, GaLore computes the
041
      Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the gradient matrix, forms projection matrices using the
042   first r singular vectors, then projects the gradient onto this low-rank subspace to perform optimiza-
043   tion. After computing the optimizer update, the gradients are projected back to their full rank for
044   use in the model. This approach allows GaLore to maintain a low memory footprint by storing and
045   updating only the low-rank representations of gradients.
046
      However, GaLore’s approach is limited to matrix operations and relies on Singular Value Decompo-
047
      sition (SVD), which may not be optimal for all neural network layers or data structures. In partic-
048   ular, GaLore faces significant challenges when applied to ”tensor” operations, which are prevalent
049   in many modern deep learning architectures, especially those used in scientific computing and com-
050   puter vision. Tensors are multidimensional arrays that offer a natural framework for representing
051   and manipulating complex, high-dimensional data structures, and the limitations of matrix-based
052   approaches like GaLore when applied to tensor operations is the fact that many models involve
053   inherently tensor-structured gradients, where preserving the multidimensional relationships is cru-
      cial for capturing complex physical phenomena. Simply flattening or ”matricizing” these tensors

077   The field of scientific modeling has seen a significant paradigm shift towards applying AI to it. Neu-
078   ral operators (NOs) Li et al. (2020) is one of the most promising new architectures in this domain.
079   The neural operator is a framework for modeling multi-scale processes on continuous domains. Hav-
080   ing the discretization invariance property, the operator learns a continuum mapping, allowing NOs
081
      to model systems that traditional neural networks cannot accurately capture. The ability to handle

082
      neural network approaches in scientific modeling. FNOs are a class of neural operator architecture
083
      designed to learn mappings between function spaces to solve parametric PDEs, a cornerstone of
084   modern scientific computing.

088
      and its inverse, R is a learnable transformation parameterized by the weight tensor introduced above.

149   2     BACKGROUND : G A L ORE AND N EURAL O PERATORS
150
151   2.1   N EURAL O PERATOR
152
153   A neural operator Gθ : A × θ → U combines linear integral operators K with pointwise non-linear
154   activations σ to approximate non-linear operators, mapping initial conditions a ∈ A to solutions
155   u ∈ U. Its operation is defined as Gθ := Q ◦ (WL + KL ) ◦ · · · ◦ σ(W1 + K1 ) ◦ P, where P and Q
156   are pointwise neural networks for encoding and decoding, Wl are linear operators, Kl are integral
157   kernel operators, and σ are activation functions.
158   The Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) proposes a specific convolution operator for K, defined as
159   (Kvl )(x) = F −1 (R · TK Fvl )(x), where F and F −1 are the Fourier transform and its inverse,
160   R is a learnable transformation, and TK truncates to the lowest K Fourier modes. This formulation
161   allows FNO to be discretization-invariant, producing high-quality solutions for query points not in
      the training grid and enabling transfer between different grid resolutions and discretizations.

      2.2    C HALLENGES OF APPLYING G A L ORE TO NEURAL OPERATORS
163
164   In order to apply standard GaLore to tensor weights, the weights must first be reshaped into a matrix
165   to compute the SVD for projection into a low-rank space. GaLore takes one rank parameter, r, and
166   projects high-rank gradients onto the first r basis vectors of the corresponding SVD rotation matrix.
167   When the weight matrix corresponds to an operator that maps between vectors, a single rank cutoff
168   can be applied while preserving most information.
169   However, in the tensor case, weights correspond to higher-order maps between function spaces.
170   Depending on the chosen strategy for reshaping tensor weights into a matrix, applying a single-
171   dimension rank cutoff to the matrix may discard key information - for instance, for a tensor W ∈
172   CA×B×m×m , where A is the number of input channels, B is the number of output channels, and
173   m is the number of truncated Fourier basis modes along each dimension, reshaping W into W ′ ∈
174   CABm×m and cutting off the first dimension at rank r may remove all information about Fourier
175   modes along the first dimension, making function learning impossible. We call this method GaLore
176
      and provide several comparisons to demonstrate its flaws.
177   One flaw is the Loss of mode-specific information: by collapsing multiple tensor dimensions into
178   one matrix dimension, we lose the ability to preserve different amounts of information along each
179   tensor mode. The other is that we have an imbalanced projection: Projecting only on one side of
180   the reshaped matrix (e.g., only U or only V from the SVD) can severely limit the operator’s capacity.
181   However, projecting on both sides often leads to training instability and failure to converge. This
182
      method also encounters rank selection issues: Choosing a single rank cutoff for the reshaped matrix
      makes it difficult to balance information preservation across all the original tensor dimensions. A
183
      rank that preserves enough information for one dimension may be too restrictive for another.

188   3.1    T ENSOR D ECOMPOSITION
189
190   Tensors are multidimensional arrays that generalize the concepts of vectors (first-order tensors) and
191   matrices (second-order tensors) to higher orders. An N th-order tensor X ∈ CI1 ×I2 ×···×IN is an
192
      N -way array where each mode n has dimension In . Like matrices, in tensors, we can decompose
      the tensors into low-rank factors using the Tucker decomposition, also known as the higher-order
193
      SVD (HOSVD), which decomposes a tensor into a core tensor multiplied by a matrix along each
194
      mode:
195
196
197                   X ≈ G ×1 U (1) ×2 U (2) · · · ×N U (N ) = JG; U (1) , U (2) , . . . , U (N ) K     (1)
198
199   where G ∈ CR1 ×R2 ×···×RN is the core tensor, U (n) ∈ CIn ×Rn are factor matrices, and ×n denotes
200   the n-mode product. Two critical aspects of the Tucker decomposition make it particularly suitable
201   for our Tensor-GaLore method:
202
203
      1. Equivalence to SVD in 2D: In the special case of 2D tensors (matrices), the Tucker decompo-
      sition reduces to the familiar SVD. The core tensor G becomes equivalent to the diagonal matrix Σ
204
      in SVD, while the factor matrices correspond to the orthogonal matrices U and V Kolda & Bader
205
      (2009). This property ensures that our method seamlessly extends the principles of matrix-based
206   techniques to higher-order tensors.
207
208   2. Orthogonality of factor matrices: The factor matrices U (n) in Tucker decomposition are or-
209
      thogonal, mirroring the properties of U and V in SVD. This orthogonality is crucial for the efficiency
      and stability of the GaLore method. Specifically:
210
211   (a) Projection efficiency: The orthogonality allows us to project tensors onto the subspace spanned
212   by these matrices through simple matrix multiplication, without the need for costly inverse compu-
213   tations.
214   (b) Easy inversion: When we need to reverse the projection, we can simply use the transpose of
215   these orthogonal matrices instead of computing their inverses. This property is expressed mathe-
      matically as (U (n) )T U (n) = I, where I is the identity matrix.


                                                            4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      (c) Numerical stability: Orthogonal matrices have a condition number of 1, ensuring that the pro-
217   jection and its inverse are numerically stable operations, even for high-dimensional tensors.
218
219   We use TensorLy’s Kossaifi et al. (2019) implementation of Tucker decomposition, which is based
220   on Higher-Order Orthogonal Iteration (HOI). For an input tensor X, HOI computes approximate
221   values for the Tucker factor matrices {U (i) }i by approximating the SVD of the unfolding of X
222   along each mode. HOI updates these factors iteratively to minimize the Frobenius norm between
223   X and the resulting learned decomposition. These learned factors can be initialized with nonzero
224   values, meaning that once full HOI is computed once, the decomposition can be ”warm-restarted”
225
      to reduce the number of iterations required for convergence.
226   In addition to these steps, like in GaLore, we incorporate per-layer weight updates Lv et al. (2024)
227   and activation checkpointing Chen et al. (2016) to reduce memory usage further. Per-layer weight
228   updates allow the optimizer to update weights immediately after computing gradients for each layer
229   rather than storing gradients for all layers before updating. This method reduces the peak mem-
230   ory requirement during training. Activation checkpointing involves selectively recomputing certain
231
      activations during the backward pass instead of storing them, trading some additional computation
      for reduced memory usage. Combined with low-rank gradient projection, these techniques enable
232
      Tensor-GaLore to achieve significant memory savings while maintaining training efficiency and per-
233
      formance. We denote this method as Tensor-GaLore/GaLore +.
234
235
      Extension: To extend GaLore to methods with learned tensor weights, we replace the matrix-
236   based SVD with tensor decomposition methods. This extension, called Tensor-GaLore, allows us to
237   handle multi-dimensional data and complex network architectures more efficiently.

302   3.3   I MPLICIT R EGULARIZATION
303
      Tucker decomposition is defined with a separate rank along each mode of the decomposed tensor,
304   preserving all key information explicitly. Additionally, the resulting decomposition’s factors can be
305   initialized to non-random values in Tucker decomposition. As learning progresses, results from a
306   previous decomposition can be used to ’warm-restart’ the process, leading to convergence in fewer
307   iterations.
308
      The low-rank tensor approximation acts as an implicit regularizer, helping to prevent overfitting
309
      and promoting smoother optimization trajectories. Hence, we observe much better convergence and
310   generalization in our experiments. In particular, we consistently observed that a rank of around
311   25% - 50% of the total rank provided optimal performance across various tasks. This observation
312   suggests that Tensor-GaLore acts as an implicit regularizer, preventing overfitting by constraining
313   the model to learn more robust, low-rank representations of the underlying physics. These results
314   align with findings from Razin et al. (2022), demonstrating that tensor factorization naturally tends
315   towards low-rank solutions. In our experiments, we saw dramatic improvements in convergence
316   even with a fixed number of epochs, sometimes achieving over 50% improvement in test loss. This
317   result implies that the regularization effect might be even more significant in higher-order tensors
318
      due to the increased structure and redundancy in these higher-dimensional spaces.

473   6   R ELATED W ORK
474
475   Our work, Tensor-GaLore, introduces a novel approach to efficiently training neural operators by
476   decomposing gradients. While significant work has been done in related areas, the specific approach
477   of gradient decomposition in tensors has not been explored. Tensor Methods in Deep Learning:
478   Tensor decomposition has been widely used to compress and improve deep networks, particularly in
479   vision tasks Novikov et al. (2015); Lebedev et al. (2015); Kim et al. (2016). These methods typically
480   focus on decomposing the weight tensors of the network to reduce parameters and computational
481   complexity. However, they do not address the decomposition of gradients during training.
482   Neural Operators: Recent advancements in learning-based approaches for solving PDEs have led
483   to the development of neural operators Li et al. (2020); Kovachki et al. (2021). In particular, FNOs
484   have shown remarkable success in various scientific computing tasks Li et al. (2021). While these
485   methods have made significant strides in learning solution operators for PDEs, they have not ex-
      plored gradient decomposition to improve memory efficiency.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Efficient Training Techniques: Various approaches have been proposed to reduce the memory
487   footprint of large-scale models. In the classical case, several techniques have demonstrated success
488   when model weights are stored as matrices. LoRA Hu et al. (2022) adds a fine-tuning weight matrix
489   created via a low-rank decomposition to an original pre-trained, frozen weight matrix. In the higher-
490   order case, FLoRA Si et al. (2024) extends the idea of low-rank adaptation to higher-dimensional
491   parameter spaces using a Tucker tensor decomposition, which has the demonstrated benefit of ap-
492   plying a low-rank decomposition to each dimension of a higher-order space. In the context of
493   neural operators, which include higher-order tensorized weights, previous works have demonstrated
494   the possibility of model compression via tensor factorization and low-rank weight approximations.
495
      Kossaifi et al. (2024) introduced the Multi-Grid Tensorized Fourier Neural Operator (MG-TFNO),
      which combines tensor decomposition with a multi-grid domain decomposition approach. In order
496
      to balance low-rank memory optimization with model performance at higher ranks, the Incremental
497
      Fourier Neural Operator (iFNO) George et al. (2024) incrementally scales both the size and rank of
498   FNO weights during training in order to boost performance.
499
500   Mixed Precision Training Mixed precision training Tu et al. (2024) utilizes lower precision for-
501
      mats (e.g., FP16) for certain operations in NO, reducing memory usage and potentially accelerating
      training on compatible hardware.
502
503   Tensor-GaLore introduces a novel approach that can complement and enhance many existing tech-
504   niques, potentially leading to even greater memory benefits. It can be combined with mixed preci-
505   sion training, integrated with methods like FLoRA or MG-TFNO to provide an additional layer of
506   optimization for gradient tensors, and incorporated into frameworks like iFNO.
507
508   7   A PPLICATIONS
509
510   Tensor-GaLore has potential applications across various domains where tensor-based models are
511   prevalent. Large language models (LLMs) could enable the training of tensor-based architectures
512   that capture higher-order relationships in language data, offering improved memory efficiency and
513   implicit regularization while preserving the natural tensor structure. Convolutional Neural Networks
514   (CNNs) also heavily utilize higher-order tensor weights in vision. CNN convolution layers include
515   4-dimensional tensor weights. As discussed previously, these weight gradients and optimizer states
516
      have high memory requirements, making memory consumption a significant bottleneck in training
      deep CNNs Yaqub et al. (2020). Future applications of Tensor-GaLore could scale these methods
517
      and improve their performance in constrained environments.

520   8   C ONCLUSION
521
522   The results of our experiments with Tensor-GaLore reveal several key insights into its performance
523   and potential applications. First, the consistent improvement in convergence across various datasets
524   is noteworthy. By projecting gradients onto a low-rank subspace, Tensor-GaLore appears to create
525
      a more stable optimization landscape, potentially smoothing out local minima and facilitating faster
      convergence to better solutions. These results are particularly evident in the Darcy flow and Navier-
526
      Stokes experiments, where we observed improved test loss even at lower ranks. Additionally, the
527
      ability to warm-start each decomposition using factors from the previous iteration likely contributes
528   to maintaining stable convergence despite frequent subspace changes. However, Tensor-GaLore
529   has limitations. The overhead of performing tensor decomposition, while amortized, may still be
530   significant for some applications, and the optimal rank selection remains a challenge that requires
531   further investigation. Future work should focus on exploring the application of Tensor-GaLore to an
532   even broader range of scientific computing tasks.
533
      Lastly, Tensor-GaLore represents a significant advancement in memory-efficient training for large-
534   scale tensor-based models, particularly in AI for Science. Tensor-GaLore opens up new avenues
535   for building and scaling foundational models in scientific computing by enabling the training of
536   more complex neural operators with dramatically reduced memory footprints. Our results demon-
537   strate that this approach not only preserves performance but often enhances it, suggesting that the
538   implicit regularization induced by low-rank projections may be particularly beneficial for capturing
539   the underlying physics of complex systems. This could lead to more accurate and computationally
      efficient models for climate prediction, fluid dynamics, and other critical scientific applications.
