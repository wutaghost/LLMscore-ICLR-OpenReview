009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            As large language models (LLMs) are overwhelmingly more and more integrated
012            into various applications, ensuring they generate safe and aligned responses is a
013            pressing need. Previous research on alignment has largely focused on general
014            instruction-following but has often overlooked the unique properties and chal-
015            lenges of safety alignment, such as the brittleness of safety mechanisms. To bridge
016            the gap, we propose the Superficial Safety Alignment Hypothesis (SSAH), which
017            posits that safety alignment should teach an otherwise unsafe model to choose the
018            correct reasoning direction - interpreted as a specialized binary classification task
019            - and incorporate a refusal mechanism with multiple reserved fallback options.
020            Furthermore, through SSAH, we hypothesize that safety guardrails in LLMs can
021            be established by just a small number of essential components. To verify this,
022            we conduct an ablation study and successfully identify four types of attribute-
023            critical components in safety-aligned LLMs: Exclusive Safety Unit (ESU), Ex-
024            clusive Utility Unit (EUU), Complex Unit (CU), and Redundant Unit (RU). Our
025            findings show that freezing certain safety-critical components (7.5%) during fine-
026            tuning allows the model to retain its safety attributes while adapting to new tasks.
027            Additionally, we show that leveraging redundant units (20%) in the pre-trained
028            model as an “alignment budget” can effectively minimize the alignment tax while
029            achieving the alignment goal. All considered, this paper concludes that the atomic
030            functional unit for safety in LLMs is at the neuron level and underscores that
031            safety alignment should not be complicated. We believe this work contributes to
032            the foundation of efficient and scalable safety alignment for future LLMs.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Large language models (LLMs) are demonstrating remarkable capabilities across a broad spectrum
038   of natural language tasks, ranging from text generation to answering complex questions (Achiam
039   et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Dubey et al., 2024). However, as these models are increasingly
040   integrated into real-world applications, concerns about the risk of generating harmful, unsafe, or
041   unethical content have grown Askell et al. (2021); Bai et al. (2022); Zeng et al. (2024). This has led
042   to a pressing need for safety alignment, which ensures that LLM outputs are not only coherent and
043   informative but also aligned with human values, ethical standards, and safety considerations.
044
045   Previous research on alignment has primarily focused on enhancing LLMs’ ability to follow general
046   instructions without enough attention to model safety. This trend of treating safety alignment as a
047   subset of general alignment has obscured its distinct challenges (Ouyang et al., 2022; Rafailov et al.,
048   2024; Zhou et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023a; Yuan et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b). One major issue is
049   the brittleness of current safety mechanisms. Despite using benign data during model fine-tuning,
050   (Qi et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023) have shown that safety mechanisms can fall apart when models
051   are adapted to new tasks. This failure mode amplifies the brittle nature of current safety alignment
052   techniques. Furthermore, safety alignment also comes with an ‘alignment tax’ - a trade-off where
053   improving safety may reduce the model’s overall utility or downstream task performances Ouyang
      et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2024); Lin et al. (2024). Additionally, current safety alignment approaches

104   Alignment and Safety Alignment: Alignment research in LLMs aims to ensure that models fol-
105   low human instructions and align with human preferences across various tasks. Early work, such
106   as Askell et al. (2021); Bai et al. (2022), focused on enabling LLMs to “Follow instructions and
107   be helpful, truthful, and harmless” throughout the alignment process. Various alignment strategies
      have since been explored (Wang et al., 2024), including Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) (Taori et al.,

      2023; Zhou et al., 2024), Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF) or AI Feedback
109
      (RLAIF) (Ouyang et al., 2022; Lee et al.), Instruction Tuning (Wei et al., 2021), Contrastive Learn-
110
      ing (Rafailov et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024), and Conditional Learning (Korbak et al., 2023). How-
111
      ever, researchers have realized that achieving helpfulness, truthfulness, and harmlessness presents
112
      distinct challenges. More recent work has, therefore, shifted focus specifically toward the challenge

114

115
116   Alignment Tax and Fine-tuning Attack: The process of aligning LLMs with human preferences
117   often incurs an “alignment tax”, where models experience degraded performance on downstream
118   tasks due to the trade-offs required to maintain alignment (Bai et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022; Lin
119   et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). Additionally, fine-tuning attacks present another challenge: Yang
120   et al. (2023); Qi et al. (2023)have shown that fine-tuning LLMs, even with benign data, can weaken
121   safety measures. These findings highlight the inherent tension between safety alignment and utility,
122   where improvements in one area often come at the expense of the other.

134   3    S UPERFICIAL S AFETY A LIGNMENT H YPOTHESIS (SSAH)
135
136   Previous research proposed Superficial Alignment Hypothesis (SAH): A model’s knowledge and
137   capabilities are learned almost entirely during pretraining, while alignment teaches the model which
138   subdistribution of formats should be used when interacting with users (Zhou et al., 2024).
139
140
      However, this claim is centered on general alignment, and directly validating the hypothesis is chal-
141
      lenging due to the complex interplays between pretraining and alignment. When a model fails to
142
      fulfill a user’s request, it can be difficult to determine whether the issue stems from the pretraining
143
      stage (due to lack of sufficient knowledge) or from the alignment process (due to misalignment in the
144
      output format). For example, when a model struggles with solving a math problem, it could either
145
      be a lack of relevant mathematical knowledge or the inability to structure its reasoning effectively.
146
      In such cases, good instruction techniques like the Chain-of-Thought approach can significantly
147   enhance the quality of the model’s responses (Wei et al., 2022).
148   Superficial Safety Alignment Hypothesis (SSAH). Since our focus is specifically on safety align-
149   ment, which has distinct properties compared to general alignment, we carefully define the scope of
150   our hypothesis. A key observation here is that, for a model to be able to fulfill a malicious request, it
151   must already possess the necessary knowledge and reasoning ability to carry out that harmful action.
152   Based on this observation, we propose Superficial Safety Alignment Hypothesis (SSAH):
153
154            SSAH: Given an unsafe model that is capable of fulfilling users’ malicious re-
155            quests, safety alignment teaches the model the correct reasoning direction and a
156            simple refusal mechanisms with reserved options.
157
158   Reasoning direction here refers to the model’s internal decision-making process when confronted
159   with a malicious query. That is, it represents the path the model is inclined to take in such a binary
160   classification task, whether to fulfill the harmful request or to issue a refusal. As illustrated in Fig. 1,
161   compared with SAH which targets the general alignment, our SSAH focuses on the safety alignment
      and has the following key differences :


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
       (1) Knowledge and reasoning ability: Safety alignment simplifies the problem by focusing
163
           specifically on models that already possess sufficient knowledge and reasoning abilities, as
164
           these models are capable of fulfilling malicious requests. This approach allows us to disregard
165
           other influencing factors and concentrate solely on the safety alignment process.
166
       (2) Refusal mechanisms with reserved fallback options: Safety alignment generally requires
167
168
           the model to respond with a relatively standardized refusal format, which is simpler compared
169
           to general alignment, where a wider range of human preferences must be handled. The model
170
           can further simplify this purpose by effectively embedding multiple options of refusal response
171
           for all unsafe queries, such as “I cannot fulfill your request as it violates safety guidelines.” or
172
           “I am unable to assist with that as I am an AI programmed to follow ethical standards.”
173    (3) Correction of reasoning direction: Safety alignment also distinguishes itself by its specific
174        goal of teaching the model to choose the correct reasoning direction, which involves either
175        fulfilling or refusing a user’s request based on whether it is safe. This process can be interpreted
176        as a simple binary classification task.
177
178
      Our SSAH can even provide insight into jailbreak attacks, where models often struggle to defend
179
      against adversarial inputs. In such cases, attackers typically use manipulative tokens to bypass the
180
      model’s safety mechanisms, indicating that the current alignment method can only hold the correct
181
      reasoning direction in limited generated tokens. However, a potential solution inspired by SSAH
182
      suggests that if safety alignment equips the model with the ability to consistently re-select the correct
183
      reasoning direction at each step (by re-evaluating the context of previously generated tokens before
184
      producing the next one), the model can continue to generate outputs that are both safe and helpful,
185   even in the face of adversarial attempts.
186   Challenges in Proving. While SSAH provides a more specific focus than SAH, empirically proving
187   it still presents significant challenges. A key issue is the infeasibility of sampling sufficient outputs
188   to fully capture the model’s distribution of responses across both safety-aligned and non-safety-
189   aligned models. This challenge makes it hard to draw comprehensive and profound conclusions or
190   interpret certain model behaviors solely from benchmark outputs.
191
192   However, we approach the problem from an alternative perspective: if SSAH holds, we should
193   observe distinct and consistent differences in the reasoning direction at each step of generation
194   between safety-aligned and non-safety-aligned models. In a safety-aligned model, the reasoning
195   direction should consistently guide the model in rejecting harmful queries at every token generation
196   step. In contrast, a non-safety-aligned model might exhibit reasoning patterns that lean toward
197   fulfilling malicious requests. Rather than relying solely on surface-level benchmark evaluations, we
198   can probe the model’s reasoning direction to gain deeper insights into its internal decision-making
199   process at each step regardless of the specific outputs produced.

276   Based on the Superficial Safety Alignment Hypothesis (SSAH), we posit that safety alignment only
277   needs to teach the model the correct reasoning direction - either fulfilling or refusing a request - and
278   to equip it with a standard refusal mechanism. This leads to the insight that safety alignment can
279   be achieved using only a small subset of critical computing units, as the task can be interpreted
280   as a binary classification combined with a multi-selection task.

289   Definition of Attribute Groups. This paper categorizes the attributes of LLMs into two main
290   properties: utility and safety. Following the above hypothesis, we first exclusively link safety or util-

296   Units (CU), which concurrently contribute to both safety and utility attributes; and Redundant Units

      of computing units are classified as complex units. This suggests that safety and utility attributes are
386
      deeply intertwined within the aligned model. Second, during the fine-tuning process, a significant
387
      number of exclusive safety units and complex units are converted into exclusive utility units. This
388
      transformation indicates that fine-tuning for utility tends to shift the function of computing units
389
      away from safety, compromising the model’s ability to maintain its safety guardrails.
390
391   Based on these observations, we draw the following insight: During the task adaption of LLMs,
392   the model often obtains the expected attribute (e.g., utility) by converting computing units
393   that originally contributed to the other attribute (e.g., safety). This means that enhancing utility
394   performance in a different task often comes at the expense of the safety performance.

463   4.3      F REE L UNCH : R EPURPOSING RU S AS A LIGNMENT B UDGET E VEN H ELP R EDUCE TAX
464
465
      Up to this point, we have successfully identified safety-critical components and preserved the
466
      model’s safety properties while adapting LLMs to new tasks. From another perspective, an in-
467
      teresting question arises: Can we assign safety or utility attributes directly to certain computing
468
      units, respectively? If we could, this would mean that we can manage the attributes of LLMs in a
469
      fine-grained, controlled way. Building on this, the next straightforward question is whether we can
470
      convert previously redundant units (units that do not contribute to the utility performance of a
471
      pre-trained model) into safety-critical units that strengthen the safety guardrails of a safety-aligned
472
      model.
473   Importantly, we do not need to convert too many computing units to achieve safety alignment. Previ-
474   ous research has found that at least 20% of the parameters in pretrained LLMs are redundant (Li
475   et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2023; An et al., 2024). This special observation motivates us to consider the
476   following: with such a large percentage of parameters in pre-trained LLMs available as an align-
477   ment budget, can we design an alignment method that reduces the alignment tax?
478
479   Attribute Transfer Analysis in the Alignment Process. Before testing the above hypothesis,
480   we conducted an attribute transfer analysis during the alignment process to explore the underlying
481   reasons for the alignment tax. For the pre-trained LLM, we simply categorized the attribute groups
482   into Utility Units and Redundant Units, as the model has not yet undergone safety alignment. This is
483   different from the categorization we used for aligned LLMs. The transfer statistics are summarized
484   in Fig. 6, and they reveal the following key pattern: a large percentage of units that originally
485   contributed to utility in the pre-trained model are transferred to CU and ESU in the aligned LLM. In
      contrast, the originally redundant units remain largely unused after the alignment process.

521   5               D ISCUSSION , L IMITATION , AND C ONCLUSION
522
523   Discussion. While our SSAH offers valuable insights into adversarial scenarios, such as jailbreak
524   attacks, we do not propose a specific solution to address these issues in this work. If these issues
525   could be resolved within the framework of our theory, the term “Superficial” in “Superficial Safety
526   Alignment Hypothesis” may no longer be necessary. Interestingly, recent research provides some
527   supporting evidence in this direction (Qi et al., 2024). However, it is also highly likely that ad-
528   vanced attacks may not be fully mitigated by relying solely on the model’s internal mechanisms. A
529   systematic, multi-layered approach, extending beyond the model itself, may be required to effec-
530   tively defend against sophisticated adversarial threats.
531
      Limitation. When reallocating redundant units for safety purposes, we only explored the impact
532
      of the alignment method SFT. Due to resource limitations, we have not yet tested this approach on
533
      other alignment methods like PPO or DPO.
534
535   Conclusion. This paper distinguishes safety alignment from the general alignment in LLMs and
536   addresses the three key questions: How does safety alignment affect model behavior? Why are
537   safety mechanisms brittle? and How to mitigate the safety alignment tax? By answering these
538   questions, we were able to demonstrate that safety alignment can be a straightforward process,
539   rather than a myth.

876   A.1    S UPERFICIAL A LIGNMENT H YPOTHESIS IN LIMA.
877
878   The Superficial Alignment Hypothesis (SAH), as proposed to Zhou et al. (2024), fundamentally
879   challenges the traditional assumption that a language model requires extensive fine-tuning on
880   instruction-following on preference data to align its responses with human expectation. Instead,
881   SAH posits that the majority of a model’s knowledge and capabilities are acquired during the pre-
882   training phase, while the subsequent alignment phase primarily functions to guide the model’s output
883   format when interacting with users. This hypothesis implies that, for many tasks, fine-tuning on a
884   small, carefully selected set of aligned data is sufficient to achieve strong performance as long as the
885   pretraining stage has effectively captured the necessary underlying knowledge. The key assertion of
886   SAH is that alignment is superficial, in the sense that:
887
888         (1) Capabilities are Learned in Pretraining: During pretraining, the model acquires a vast
889             amount of general-purpose knowledge from diverse datasets. These datasets contain im-
890             plicit structures and information about language, reasoning, factual knowledge, and even
891             ethical guidelines.
892
            (2) Alignment Guides Output Behavior: The alignment process is not responsible for teaching
893
                the model new knowledge or capabilities. Rather, it acts as a filter that directs the model
894
                to produce acceptable formats or styles of responses based on user queries, reflecting the
895
                correct subset of its vast pretrained knowledge.
896
897         (3) For instance, when tasked with generating an informative response, the model must select
898             a format that aligns with user expectations, such as providing clear instructions or expla-
899             nations. However, the actual content of the response, e.g., factual knowledge, reasoning,
900             and domain-specific expertise, stems from pretraining. The alignment stage merely teaches
901             the model how to express that knowledge or when to refrain from providing information in
902             inappropriate contexts.
903
904   Challenges and Motivations Behind SAH. One of the primary motivations for introducing SAH
905   was the observation that models tend to be capable of performing certain tasks after alignment fine-
906   tuning on a minimal dataset. This observation challenges the need for extensive fine-tuning using
907   reinforcement learning (e.g., RLHF) or large-scale human feedback, which can be computationally
908   too expensive and time-consuming. The authors of LiMA argue that most of the functional capa-
909   bilities of a language model are already present after pretraining, and that alignment is more about
910   conditioning the model to apply these capabilities in a user-friendly way.
911
912   The Superficial Alignment Hypothesis can also help explain phenomena where models exhibit brit-
913   tleness - for example, where an LLM generates inappropriate or harmful responses in new domains
914   or under adversarial conditions. This brittleness is attributed to the fact that alignment does not
915   deeply alter the underlying decision-making processes of the model, but only skims the surface to
916   adjust output behavior in specific contexts. Therefore, if an adversary finds a way to bypass these
917   superficial alignments (e.g., via jailbreaking), the model’s underlying pretrained knowledge and ca-
      pabilities may still enable it to produce harmful or misaligned responses.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      Relevance to Superficial Safety Alignment Hypothesis. While SAH deals with general align-
919
      ment (i.e., ensuring that a model follows general user instructions), SSAH is specifically focused on
920
      ensuring that a model safely interacts with users, especially when faced with harmful or malicious
921
      queries. The key parallels between SAH and SSAH include:
922
923
924
            (1) Pretrained Knowledge and Safety Concerns: Just as SAH assumes that knowledge and
925
                capabilities are largely acquired during pretraining, SSAH assumes that a model’s ability
926
                to execute harmful actions (e.g., generating unsafe or unethical content) also stems from
927
                pretraining. Safety alignment, like general alignment, does not aim to teach the model new
928
                facts or capabilities, but rather to guide its reasoning pathways in a safe direction.
929
            (2) Binary Classification in SSAH: While SAH suggests that general alignment helps models
930
                choose the correct output subdistribution, SSAH posits that safety alignment simplifies this
931
                further by focusing on a binary classification task: either fulfill a request (if safe) or refuse it
932
                (if unsafe). This simplified framing of safety alignment is consistent with the “superficial”
933
                nature of SAH, where the alignment process fine-tunes how the model behaves in response
934
                to queries, rather than altering its deep internal structures.
935
936         (3) Refusal Mechanisms and Format Control: Just as general alignment teaches models to
937             structure their outputs in a user-friendly way, safety alignment in SSAH teaches models
938             to issue consistent refusal mechanisms. These refusals take the form of standardized re-
939             sponses that indicate the model’s compliance with safety guidelines, much like how gen-
940             eral alignment might guide a model to give well-structured, polite answers to other types
941             of questions. Importantly, this refusal mechanism makes it easier to choose the appropriate
942             subdistribution of the output format.
943
944
      The Superficial Alignment Hypothesis (SAH) as outlined in the Zhou et al. (2024) provides a theo-
945
      retical framework for understanding how alignment processes operate in large language models. It
946
      suggests that alignment is largely superficial, conditioning the model on how to use its pretrained
947
      knowledge effectively. The Superficial Safety Alignment Hypothesis (SSAH) builds on this by ap-
948
      plying similar principles to the realm of safety, simplifying the task of safety alignment to binary
949
      decisions regarding the fulfillment or refusal of unsafe requests. Both hypotheses underscore that
950
      alignment does not deeply alter the core abilities of the model, but rather adjusts the way those
951
      abilities are applied in specific contexts.

       B.1   D EFINITION OF ATTRIBUTE G ROUPS AND C ATEGORIZATION P ROCESS
1095
1096
       As detailed in Section 4.1, we categorize the computational units (neurons and channels) of LLMs
1097
       into four distinct groups: Exclusive Safety Units (ESU), Exclusive Utility Units (EUU), Complex
1098
       Units (CU), and Redundant Units (RU). Exclusive Safety Units are primarily responsible for safety-
1099
       related behavior, such as refusal mechanisms and detecting unsafe requests. Exclusive Utility Units
1100
       are dedicated to general task performance, including natural language understanding, reasoning, and
1101
       task-specific knowledge retrieval. Complex Units contribute to both safety and utility, as these at-
1102
       tributes are intertwined at a higher level of abstraction. Finally, Redundant Units are not significantly
1103
       involved in either safety or utility and are often characterized by low activation variance across tasks.
1104
1105   To systematically assign computing units to these groups, we employ a structured pruning strategy
1106   based on the variance of activation values. Specifically, we calculate the variance of activations
1107   across a target dataset for each neuron or channel. Neurons with higher variance contribute more
1108   significantly to the model’s performance on a given task, while neurons with low activation vari-
1109   ance are considered redundant and can be pruned. We define two separate importance scores for
1110   each unit—IU for utility-related tasks and IS for safety-related tasks. Units with extreme values in
1111   either dimension are considered Exclusive Units (either ESU or EUU), while units with significant
1112   contributions to both dimensions are classified as Complex Units (CU).
1113
       Datasets Used for Computing IU and IS . To identify safety-critical regions in the model, we fol-

       behaviors, and utility dataset, for attributing utility-related behaviors. Each dataset is structured in
1116
       a (prompt, response) format. Specifically, the safety dataset is compiled using harmful instructions
1117
       from AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023a). We also divide AdvBench into AdvBencheval (100 instructions

1126   attribution of units is aligned with their actual contribution to model behavior.

1201   In our fine-tuning experiments described in Sec. 4.2, we track the attribute transfer of individual
1202   units during the adaptation of safety-aligned models to new tasks. The process involves categorizing
1203   the computing units into ESU, EUU, CU, and RU based on their behavior in the original, safety-
1204   aligned model before and after fine-tuning. As fine-tuning progresses, we measure how many units

1206   importance scores IS and IU for each unit after every few epochs of training.
1207
       The key insight is that when units critical to safety (ESU or CU) are re-purposed for utility tasks
1208
       (becoming EUU), the model’s safety performance degrades. This transformation is tracked in the
1209

       analysis highlights the brittleness of current safety mechanisms: when safety-aligned models are
1211
       fine-tuned on new tasks, many safety-critical components lose their original function, compromising

       the safety guardrails of the model.

1215   B.5   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP FOR F REEZING S AFETY-C RITICAL C OMPONENTS
1216
1217
       To mitigate the safety performance degradation caused by fine-tuning, we experiment with freezing
1218
       the safety-critical components identified through pruning. After categorizing the units into ESU,
1219
       EUU, CU, and RU, we freeze the Exclusive Safety Units (ESU) and the top 6% of Complex Units
1220
       (CU) during the fine-tuning process. This ensures that these units retain a large part of their original
1221
       function and are not re-purposed for utility tasks. The rest of the model is fine-tuned as usual on
1222
       new tasks, allowing the non-safety-critical components to adapt to the task while keeping the safety-
1223   critical components unchanged.
1224   Fine-Tuning Attack Datasets. For fine-tuning attack experiments, we use two popular instruction-
1225   following datasets: Alpaca and Dolly. The Alpaca dataset (Taori et al., 2023) is a publicly available
1226   dataset created using GPT-3.5, and it contains 52,000 instruction-following samples across a variety
1227   of tasks. It has been widely used for instruction tuning due to its diversity in queries. The Dolly
1228   dataset (Conover et al., 2023) is another widely adopted dataset for instruction-tuning, created by
1229   Databricks, which contains high-quality examples designed to improve the model’s capability to
1230   follow instructions, based on their open-source Dolly model. Both datasets allow us to effectively
1231   assess how fine-tuning for general instruction-following can impact the model’s safety guardrails
1232   when safety-critical components are or are not frozen.

1270
       In Section 4.3, we explore the possibility of repurposing redundant units (RU) as part of an align-
1271
       ment budget to minimize the alignment tax. The core idea is that pre-trained LLMs contain a large
1272
       percentage of parameters that do not contribute significantly to task performance, as noted by Sun
1273
1274
       et al. (2023) and Ma et al. (2023). These redundant units can be re-purposed to improve safety
1275
       alignment without sacrificing utility performance.
1276   We identify redundant units using the same variance-based pruning method described in Section 4.1.
1277   Specifically, we compute an importance score for each neuron and channel based on the variance of
1278   activations across the Alpaca dataset (We remove safety-related samples from the original version).
1279   Once the redundant units are identified, we freeze the remaining parts of the model and fine-tune
1280   only these redundant units during the alignment process. By carefully adjusting the proportion of
1281   redundant units re-purposed, we aim to achieve alignment without incurring the alignment tax—i.e.,
1282   without sacrificing utility performance. This selective fine-tuning approach significantly reduces the
1283   computational burden compared to full model fine-tuning while maintaining high task performance.
1284

1402       individual layers to identify safety-critical components. In contrast, our approach performs a

1410       By freezing only 7.5% of the identified computational units, we are able to preserve safety
1411       performance even after fine-tuning on the entire Alpaca dataset. This significant improvement
1412       in maintaining safety mechanisms while adapting to new tasks underscores the efficacy of our
1413       approach.
