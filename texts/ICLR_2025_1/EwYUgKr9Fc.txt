009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Membership Inference Attacks (MIAs) determine whether a specific data point
012            was included in the training set of a target model. In this paper, we introduce
013            the Semantic Membership Inference Attack (SMIA), a novel approach that en-
014            hances MIA performance by leveraging the semantic content of inputs and their
015            perturbations. SMIA trains a neural network to analyze the target model’s be-
016            havior on perturbed inputs, effectively capturing variations in output probability
017            distributions between members and non-members. We conduct comprehensive
018            evaluations on the Pythia and GPT-Neo model families using the Wikipedia and
019
               MIMIR datasets. Our results show that SMIA significantly outperforms existing
               MIAs; for instance, for Wikipedia, SMIA achieves an AUC-ROC of 67.39% on
020
               Pythia-12B, compared to 58.90% by the second-best attack.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Large Language Models (LLMs) appear to be effective learners of natural language structure and
027
      patterns of its usage. However, a key contributing factor to their success is their ability to memorize
      their training data, often in a verbatim fashion. This memorized data can be reproduced verbatim
028
      at inference time, which effectively serves the purpose of information retrieval. However, this re-
029
      gurgitation of training data is also at the heart of privacy concerns in LLMs. Previous works have
030   shown that LLMs leak some of their training data at inference time (Carlini et al., 2022b;a; Jagan-
031   natha et al., 2021; Lehman et al., 2021; Mattern et al., 2023; Mireshghallah et al., 2022; Nasr et al.,
032   2023) Membership Inference Attacks (MIAs) (Shokri et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2022b;a; Zhang
033   et al., 2023; Ippolito et al., 2022) aim to determine whether a specific data sample (e.g. sentence,
034   paragraph, document) was part of the training set of a target machine learning model. MIAs serve
035   as efficient tools to measure memorization in LLMs.
036   Existing approaches to measure memorization in LLMs have predominantly focused on verbatim
037   memorization, which involves identifying exact sequences reproduced from the training data. How-
038   ever, given the complexity and richness of natural language, we believe this method falls short. Nat-
039   ural language can represent the same ideas or sensitive data in numerous forms, through different
040   levels of indirection and associations. This power of natural language makes verbatim memoriza-
041   tion metrics inadequate to address the more nuanced problem of measuring semantic memorization,
042   where LLMs internalize and reproduce the essence or meaning of training data sequences, not just
043   their exact wording.
044   Previous MIAs against LLMs has predominantly focused on classifying members and non-members
045   by analyzing the probabilities assigned to input texts or their perturbations (Carlini et al., 2021; Mat-
046   tern et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024). In contrast, we introduce the Semantic Mem-
047   bership Inference Attack (SMIA), the first MIA to leverage the semantic content of input texts to
048   enhance performance. SMIA involves training a neural network to understand the distinct behaviors
049   exhibited by the target model when processing members versus non-members.
050   Our central hypothesis is that perturbing the input of a target model will result in differential changes
051   in its output probability distribution for members and non-members, contingent on the extent of
052   semantic change distance. Crucially, this behavior is presumed to be learnable. To implement this,
053   we train the SMIA model to discern how the target model’s behavior varies with different degrees of
      semantic changes for members and non-members. Post-training, the model can classify a given text

      sequence as a member or non-member by evaluating the semantic distance and the corresponding
055   changes in the target model’s behavior for the original input and its perturbations.

100   2    R ELATED W ORK
101
102   Membership inference attacks (MIAs) against large language models (LLMs) aim to determine
103   whether a given data point was part of the training dataset used to train the target model or not.
104   Given a data point x and a trained autoregressive model T (.), which predicts P (xt |x1 , x2 , ..., xt−1 )
105   reflecting the likelihood of the sequence under the training data distribution, these attacks compute
106   a membership score A(x, T ). By applying a threshold ϵ to this score, we can classify x as a member
107   (part of the training data) or a non-member. In Appendix A, we provide MIA use cases and details
      about how existing MIA work against LLMs.

      Difference between our SMIA and Neighborhood Attack (Mattern et al., 2023; Mitchell et al.,
109   2023): The Nei attack (Appendix A) perturbs a given text, crafting neighbors by masking words
110   and replacing them with alternatives generated by a model such as BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) or
111   T5 (Raffel et al., 2020). In this approach, if a model has been trained on a text, it typically exhibits
112   a lower loss value compared to average of its neighbors. In contrast, our SMIA adopts a more
113   complex strategy where it consider the semantic distances of neighbors to train a classifier model.
114   Unlike the straightforward comparison of loss values in the Nei attack, SMIA employs a neural
115   network trained to detect subtle semantic variations between inputs and their perturbations. This
116   not only leverages the semantic content but also the behavioral changes in the model’s response,
117
      enhancing both the robustness and sensitivity of the attack. In Section 5, we show that SMIA
      achieves higher performance compared to Nei attack. For example, our results on the Pythia-12B
118
      and Wikipedia dataset show that SMIA achieves a significant improvement, recording 67.93% AUC-
119
      ROC compared to 55.83% by the Nei attack.
120
121
      Difference between our SMIA and Nasr et al. (2019): Nasr et al. (2019) introduced a white-box
122   MIA against traditional deep neural network where it involves training a classifier using detailed
123   model data such as weights, activations, and gradients of members and non-members. This method
124   requires comprehensive visibility into the model’s internals, making them less adaptable to scenarios
125   where only limited information is available. Our SMIA stands out by functioning effectively under a
126   gray-box scenario, where it requires only access to the loss values of the target model without need-
127   ing to probe into its internal states. This capability is further enhanced by the novel use of semantic
128   embeddings to discern changes in the model responses caused by semantic variations in inputs. By
129   capturing these subtleties, SMIA not only sets itself apart from traditional white-box attacks but
130
      also highlights its applicability in practical environments where attackers have constrained access to
      model internals.

133   3     O UR P ROPOSED SMIA
134
135   MIAs seek to determine whether a specific data sample was part of the training set of a machine
136   learning model, highlighting potential privacy risks associated with model training. Traditional
137   MIAs typically verify if a text segment, ranging from a sentence to a full document, was used ex-
138   actly as is in the training data. Such attacks tend to falter when minor modifications are made to
139
      the text, such as punctuation adjustments or word substitutions, while the overall meaning remains
      intact. We hypothesize that a LLM, having encountered specific content during training, will ex-
140
      hibit similar behaviors towards semantically similar text snippets during inference. Consequently, a
141
      LLM’s response to semantically related inputs should display notable consistency.
142
      In this paper, we introduce Semantic Member-
143   ship Inference Attack (SMIA) against LLMs.
144   This novel attack method enables an attacker
145   to discern whether a concept, defined as a set
146   of semantically akin token sequences, was part
147   of the training data. Examples of such se-
148   mantically linked concepts include “John Doe
149   has leukemia” and “John Doe is undergoing
150   chemotherapy.” The proposed SMIA aims to
151
      capture a broader spectrum of data memoriza-
      tion incidents compared to traditional MIA, by
152
      determining whether the LLM was trained on
153
      any data encompassing the targeted concept.

449   5.2   E VALUATION IN M ODIFIED S ETTINGS
450
451   Existing MIAs against LLMs typically assess the membership status of texts that exist verbatim in
452   the training data. However, in practical scenarios, member data might undergo slight modifications.
453
      An important application of MIAs is identifying the use of copyrighted content in training datasets.
      For instance, in the legal case involving the New York Times and OpenAI, the outputs of ChatGPT
454
      were found to be very similar to NYTimes articles, with only minor changes such as the addition or
455
      deletion of a single word (Grynbaum & Mac, 2023). This section explores the capability of SMIA
456   to detect memberships even after such slight modifications.
457
458   To evaluate SMIA and other MIAs under these conditions, we generated three new test member
459
      datasets from our existing Wikipedia test member dataset (Dte-m ) as follows (Figure 5 provides
      examples for each modification): Duplication: A random word in each member data point is du-
460
      plicated. Deletion: A random word in each member data point is deleted. Addition: A mask
461
      placement is randomly added in each member data point, and the T5 model is used to fill the mask
462   position, with only the first word of the T5 replacement being used. We just consider one word
463   modification beacuse more than one word modification reuslts in a drastic drop of performance for
464   all attacks.
465
      Table 3 presents the AUC-ROC performance results of different MIAs and our SMIA under these
466
      slightly modified test member datasets. The table includes the best AUC-ROC values for Min-K and
467
      Min-K++ across different values of K. The results indicate that for the W T non-member dataset,
468   when a word is duplicated or added from the T5 output, the Ref attack outperforms SMIA. For
469   instance, with Pythia-12B, the Ref attack achieves AUC-ROC scores of 57.88% and 57.95% after
470   word duplication and addition from the T5 output, respectively, whereas SMIA achieves scores of
471   55.13% and 54.19% for the same settings. It is important to note that the Reference model is Pythia-
472   1.4B, which shares the same architecture and training dataset (Pile) but with fewer parameters, a
473   scenario that is less feasible in real-world applications. However, when a word is deleted, SMIA
474   retains much of its efficacy, achieving an AUC-ROC of 62.47% compared to 58.25% for the Ref
475   attack on the W T non-member dataset. This indicates that SMIA is more sensitive to additions than
476
      deletions.
477   In scenarios involving the W C non-member dataset, where non-members exhibit lower n-gram
478   overlap with members, SMIA consistently outperforms other MIAs. For example, SMIA achieves
479   AUC-ROC scores of 89.36% and 92.67% for word addition and deletion, respectively, while the Ref

515   6    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORKS
516
517   In this paper, we introduced the Semantic Membership Inference Attack (SMIA), which leverages
518
      the semantics of input texts and their perturbations to train a neural network for distinguishing mem-
      bers from non-members. We evaluated SMIA in two primary settings: (1) where the test member
519
      dataset exists verbatim in the training dataset of the target model, and (2) where the test member
520
      dataset is slightly modified through the addition, duplication, or deletion of a single word.
521
522   For future work, we plan to evaluate SMIA in settings where the test member dataset consists of
523   paraphrases of the original member data points, with minimal semantic distance between them.
524
      This will help us demonstrate that more advanced models tend to memorize the semantics of their
      training data rather than their exact wording.
525
526   Additionally, we aim to apply SMIA to measure unintended multi-hop reasoning. In multi-hop
527   reasoning (Yang et al., 2024), a model could connect two parts of the training data through indirect
528   inferences, potentially disclosing private information. We intend to assess how much the target
529   model reveals about its training data through multi-hop reasoning using our SMIA approach.
530   Another direction is to utilize SMIA to show that anonymization is insufficient. SMIA can reveal
531   the limitations of traditional data redaction techniques, illustrating how anonymization falls short
532   when an adversary can cross-reference (i.e., use supplementary information from another source) to
533   deduce sensitive information, such as a person’s medical condition.
534   Finally, we plan to use SMIA to measure hallucination (Ji et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023; Agrawal
535   et al., 2023) in LLMs. We hypothesize that the issues of hallucination and memorization may be
536   interconnected in interesting ways. Intuitively, the more an LLM memorizes its training data, the
537   less likely it is to hallucinate text that contradicts the memorized data. SMIA can provide a metric
538   for assessing the likelihood of text output being a result of the model’s accurate memorization (direct
539   or multi-hop) versus hallucination. This metric is particularly valuable as it measures the extent to
      which an output is derived from the model’s intrinsic semantic beliefs, shaped by its training data.

      As mentioned in Section 2, MIAs assign a membership score A(x, T ) to a given text input x and
678   a trained model T (.). This score represents the likelihood that the text was part of the dataset on
679   which T (.) was trained. A threshold ϵ is then applied to this score to classify the text as a member
680   if it is higher than ϵ, and a non-member if it is lower. In this section, we provide the description of
681   existing MIAs against LLMS.
682
      LOSS (Yeom et al., 2018): The LOSS method utilizes the loss value of model T (.) for the given
683
      text x as the membership score; a lower loss suggests that the text was seen during training, so
684   A(x, T ) = ℓ(T, x).
685
686   Ref (Carlini et al., 2021): Calculating membership scores based solely on loss values often results
687
      in high false negative rates. To improve this, a difficulty calibration method can be employed to
      account for the intrinsic complexity of x. For example, repetitive or common phrases typically
688
      yield low loss values. One method of calibrating this input complexity is by using another LLM,
689
      Ref (.), assumed to be trained on a similar data distribution. The membership score is then defined
690   as the difference in loss values between the target and reference models, A(x, T ) = ℓ(x, T ) −
691   ℓ(x, Ref ). Follwoing recent works (Shi et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024), we use smaller reference
692   models, Pythia 1.4B and Pythia 70M, which are trained on the same dataset (Pile) and share a similar
693   architecture with the Pythia target models.
694
      Zlib (Carlini et al., 2021): Another method to calibrate the difficulty of a sample is by using its zlib
695
      compression size, where more complex sentences have higher compression sizes. The membership
696   score is then calculated by normalizing the loss value by the zlib compression size, A(x, T ) =
697   ℓ(x,T )

720   B     SMIA C OST E STIMATION
721
722    The cost estimation for deploying the SMIA involves several computational and resource consid-
723   erations. Primarily, the cost is associated with generating neighbours, calculating embeddings, and
724   evaluating loss values for the target model T (.).
725
      For each of the datasets, Dtr-m (members) and Dtr-n (non-members), consisting of β data samples
726
      each, we generate n neighbours per data item. Consequently, this results in a total of 2 × n × β
727
      neighbour generations. Assuming each operation has a fixed cost, with cN for generating a neigh-
728   bour, cT for computing a loss value, and cE for calculating an embedding, the total cost for the
729   feature collection phase can be approximated as: 2 × (n × β + 1) × (cN + cE + cT ). In this esti-
730   mation, the training of the neural network model A(.) is considered negligible due to its relatively
731   small size (few million parameters) and its architecture, which primarily consists of fully connected
732   layers. Additionally, the costs associated with cT and cN are not significant in this context as they
733   are incurred only during the inference phase. Thus, the predominant cost factor is cE , the cost of
734   embedding calculations.
735   In practical terms, for our experimental setup (Section 4.2) using the Wikipedia dataset as an exam-
736   ple, we prepared a training set comprising 6,000 members and 6,000 non-members. With each data
737   item generating n = 25 neighbours, the total number of data items requiring embedding calculations
738   becomes: 6, 000 + 6, 000 + 150, 000 + 150, 000 = 312, 000 Each of these data items, on average,
739   consists of 1052 characters (variable due to replacements made by the neighbour generation model),
740   leading to a total of 312, 000 × 1052 = 328, 224, 000 characters processed. These transactions are
741   sent to the Cohere Embedding V3 model (Cohere, 2024) for embedding generation. The cost of
742
      processing these embeddings is measured in thousands of units. Hence, the total estimated cost for
      embedding processing is approximately: 32, 822 × $0.001 = $32.82.

829   C.6   E VALUATING DATASET C OMPLEXITY FOR MIA S : WT VS . WC
830
831   Evaluating the effectiveness of MIAs requires careful consideration of the underlying dataset proper-
832
      ties. As highlighted by prior work Das et al. (2024), high success rates of MIAs are often attributed to
      distributional shifts between members and non-members rather than genuine privacy vulnerabilities.
833
      The authors demonstrated this through blind attacks that distinguish members from non-members
834
      without access to the target model, outperforming state-of-the-art MIAs. Their findings underscore
835   the critical need to disentangle dataset characteristics from model behaviors when assessing the
836   privacy risks posed by MIAs.
837
838
      In this section, we evaluate the complexity of our main dataset, WT, in comparison to WC and the
      widely-used WikiMIA dataset Shi et al. (2023). WT consists of members and non-members drawn
839
      from the Pile training and test splits, respectively, while WC draws non-members from Wikipedia
840
      pages published after the training cutoff. This difference makes WC inherently easier for MIAs,
841   as the non-members exhibit a clear distributional shift from members. To demonstrate this, we
842   follow the methodology of Das et al. (2024) and apply two blind attack strategies. Similar to their
843   setting, we first train the blind attack models on 80% of data and then report the MIAs success rate
844   using the rest of 20%. We applied two blind attacks: a) Greedy Rare Word Selection: This attack
845   extracts all n-grams (n ∈ [1, 5]) and ranks them by their TPR to FPR ratio on a training part of
846   the data. The n-gram with the highest ratio is selected iteratively. On the test set, membership is
847   predicted for samples containing any of the selected n-grams. b) Bag-of-Words Classifier: We train a
848   simple bag-of-words classifier on 80% of the datasets and evaluate its performance on the remaining
849
      20%. Additionally, we evaluate these methods on the WikiMIA dataset Shi et al. (2023), which was
      constructed similarly to WC and has been widely used in prior MIA studies. We include WikiMIA
850
      to validate the accuracy of our implementation.
851
852   Table 8 summarizes the results of our experiments, reporting AUC-ROC scores and TPRs at low
853   FPR thresholds (1%, 2%, 5%, and 10%). Key insights include: 1) Implementation Validation:
854   Our implementation of greedy rare word selection and the bag-of-words classifier produces results
855
      consistent with those reported in [1] on the WikiMIA dataset. Specifically, these methods achieve
      AUC-ROC scores of 87.8% and 98.53%, respectively, validating our approach (similar to origianl
856
      results in Das et al. (2024)). 2) Ease of Distinguishing Members in WC: On WC, blind attacks
857
      achieve AUC-ROC scores of 58.30% and 83.6% for the two methods. This demonstrates that WC,
858   like WikiMIA, is relatively easy for MIAs due to the distinct distributional shift between members
859   and non-members (refre to Section 5.1 for more infomration). 3) Challenge of Distinguishing
860   Members in WT: In stark contrast, WT proves significantly more challenging for blind attacks.
861   The AUC-ROC scores for greedy rare word selection and the bag-of-words classifier are 52.21%
862   and 51.8%, respectively —equivalent to random guessing. TPRs at low FPRs using the bag-of-
863   words classifier further highlight this difficulty, with values of 0.0%, 1.0%, and 2.0% for FPRs of
      1%, 2%, and 5%, respectively. While the same attack on WC dataset achieved TPRs of 53.85%,


                                                        16

864
      87.18% and 88.46% and it also got TPRs of 13.0%, 23.0% and 32.0% for the same set of FPRs
865   respecitvely.
866
867   These results emphasize the limitations of datasets like WC and WikiMIA for evaluating MIAs, as
868
      their success is largely driven by the distributional differences between members and non-members
      rather than true privacy leakage. In contrast, the WT dataset presents a realistic and challenging

904   D.2   M ETRICS
905
906   In our experiments, we employ following privacy metrics to evaluate the performance of our attacks:
907   (1) Attack ROC curves: The Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve illustrates the trade-
908   off between the True Positive Rate (TPR) and the False Positive Rate (FPR) for the attacks. The FPR
909   measures the proportion of non-member samples that are incorrectly classified as members, while
910   the TPR represents the proportion of member samples that are correctly identified as members. We
911   report the Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC-ROC) as an aggregate metric to assess the overall
912   success of the attacks. AU-ROC is a threshold-independent metric, and it shows the probability that
913   a positive instance (member) has higher score than a negative instance (non-member).
914   (2) Attack TPR at low FPR: This metric is crucial for determining the effectiveness of an attack at
915   confidently identifying members of the training dataset without falsely classifying non-members as
916   members. We focus on low FPR thresholds, specifically 2%, 5%, and 10%. For instance, the TPR
917   at an FPR of 2% is calculated by setting the detection threshold so that only 2% of non-member
      samples are predicted as members.
