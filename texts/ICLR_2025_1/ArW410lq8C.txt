009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recommender systems often manifest biases toward a small user group, result-
012            ing in pronounced disparities in recommendation performance, i.e., the User-
013            Oriented Fairness (UOF) issue. Existing research on UOF faces three major
014            limitations, and no single approach effectively addresses all of them. Limita-
015            tion 1: Post-processing methods fail to address the root cause of the UOF issue.
016            Limitation 2: Some in-processing methods rely heavily on unstable user sim-
017            ilarity calculations under severe data sparsity problems. Limitation 3: Other
018
               in-processing methods overlook the disparate treatment of individual users within
               user groups. In this paper, we propose a novel Individual Reweighting for User-
019
               Oriented Fairness framework, namely IR-UOF, to address all the aforementioned
020
               limitations. IR-UOF serves as a versatile solution applicable across various back-
021            bone recommendation models to achieve UOF. The motivation behind IR-UOF is
022            to introduce an in-processing strategy that addresses the UOF issue at the in-
023            dividual level without the need to explore user similarities. We conduct exten-
024            sive experiments on three real-world datasets using four backbone recommen-
025            dation models to demonstrate the effectiveness of IR-UOF in mitigating UOF
026            and improving recommendation fairness. The code of this paper is available at
027            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/IR-UOF-D53B/

088   In this paper, we propose a novel Individual Reweighting for User-Oriented Fairness framework,
089   named IR-UOF, to address all the aforementioned limitations. IR-UOF serves as a versatile solution
090   applicable across various backbone recommendation models to achieve UOF. The motivation behind
091   IR-UOF is to introduce an in-processing strategy that addresses UOF issues at the individual level
092
      without the need to explore user similarities based on user-item interactions. In detail, to tackle
      Limitation 1, IR-UOF addresses the root cause of the UOF issue by introducing an in-processing
093
      strategy to focus on mitigating the unfair training processes of recommendation models. To tackle
094
      Limitation 2, IR-UOF avoids relying on user similarity calculations based on limited user-item
095   interactions. Instead, IR-UOF focuses on the loss value of each user, as it directly reflects the quality
096   of the user’s training. By adjusting the weights of losses for different users, the model’s emphasis on
097   various users can be modulated. To address Limitation 3, IR-UOF introduces an individual-level
098   reweighting method, transcending the constraints of group-level optimization. As illustrated in
099   Figure2, it is not only disadvantaged users but also individual users among the advantaged group who
100   may be overlooked. IR-UOF adaptively adjusts the weight of the loss value for all users, providing
101   precise strategies tailored to each user’s training situation. This approach allows IR-UOF to enhance
102   the training quality of every individual user who is dominated by recommendation models, achieving
103
      overall fairness through individual optimization, i.e., One to All.
104   We conduct extensive experiments on three publicly available real-world datasets using four backbone
105   recommendation models. The effectiveness of IR-UOF is comprehensively assessed using evaluation
106   metrics from various perspectives. Experimental results demonstrate that IR-UOF outperforms all
107   State-Of-The-Art (SOTA) methods across all datasets and backbone models.

116   2.1   FAIRNESS IN M ACHINE L EARNING
117
118   As outlined in Mehrabi et al. (2021), fairness in decision-making processes is broadly defined as
119   the absence of bias or favoritism toward an individual or group based on their inherent or acquired
120   characteristics. Research on machine learning fairness can be categorized from various perspectives
121   into distinct domains.
122
      Groups Affected by Fairness Issues. Fairness research primarily addresses two aspects: group
123
      fairness and individual fairness Mehrabi et al. (2021); Dai et al. (2022). Group fairness aims to ensure
124
      equal treatment for users from different demographic groups. Key approaches in this domain include
125   Demographic Parity Kusner et al. (2017), Equalized Odds Hardt et al. (2016), Equal Opportunity Hardt
126   et al. (2016), Conditional Statistical Parity Corbett-Davies et al. (2017), and Treatment Equality Berk
127   et al. (2021). Individual fairness focuses on providing similar recommendations to similar individuals.
128   Research areas include Fairness Through Unawareness Grgic-Hlaca et al. (2016), Fairness Through
129   Awareness Dwork et al. (2012), and Counterfactual Fairness Kusner et al. (2017).
130
      Stages of the ML Process. Research on fairness can also be categorized according to different stages
131
      of the machine learning process: pre-processing methods, in-processing methods, and post-processing
132
      methods Mehrabi et al. (2021); Dai et al. (2022). Pre-processing methods aim to transform training
133   data to eliminate underlying biases before model training d’Alessandro et al. (2017); Kang et al.
134   (2020). In-processing methods incorporate fairness considerations into the training process to mitigate
135   bias during model development Dai & Wang (2020); Bose & Hamilton (2019). Post-processing
136   methods adjust the predictions of a trained model to ensure fairness Li et al. (2021).
137
      In this paper, we introduce an in-processing framework specifically designed to ensure user-oriented
138
      fairness, a kind of group fairness in RSs.
139
140
141   2.2   FAIRNESS IN R ECOMMENDER S YSTEMS
142
143   Fairness in RSs can be examined from three primary perspectives: user fairness, item fairness, and
144   provider fairness Deldjoo et al. (2023).
145   User Fairness: Studies in this area aim to ensure that similar users receive comparable recommen-
146   dation outcomes. Key considerations include ranking accuracy Deldjoo et al. (2021c), diversity
147   coverage Melchiorre et al. (2021), under-ranking Gorantla et al. (2021), and selection rate Sühr et al.
148   (2021). Item Fairness: The goal is to ensure that similar items receive equal exposure, regardless of
149   sensitive attributes Rastegarpanah et al. (2019); Deldjoo et al. (2021a); Dash et al. (2021) or previous
150   exposure history Biega et al. (2018), such as in cold-start scenarios. Provider Fairness: There is a
151
      tendency for providers with a more extensive interaction history to be recommended more frequently,
      creating a "superstar effect" Ferraro (2019); Gharahighehi et al. (2021). Efforts to mitigate exposure
152
      disparities arising from the relationship between providers and items Sühr et al. (2021) and private
153
      characteristics Shakespeare et al. (2020) are crucial for fostering an equitable market.
154
155   In this paper, we focus on the underexplored issue of user-oriented fairness (UOF), specifically
156   addressing fairness among users with varying levels of activity.

      3.1   U SER -O RIENTED FAIRNESS
163
164   In RSs, let U and I represent the user set and the item set, respectively. NU and NI represent the
165   number of users and items. Following prior research Li et al. (2021); Rahmani et al. (2022); Han et al.
166   (2023a), users are divided into two groups: disadvantaged users (D) and advantaged users (A). The
167   disadvantaged group D = {D1 , D2 , . . . , DND } consists of users with fewer interactions, while the
168   advantaged group A = {A1 , A2 , . . . , ANA } includes users with more frequent interactions. Here,
169   ND and NA represent the number of users in each group. Users with more interactions are more
170
      likely to be advantaged. The goal is to minimize the performance disparity between D and A, thereby
      achieving UOF while maintaining overall recommendation quality.
171
172   UOF is a type of group fairness Hardt et al. (2016); Dwork et al. (2012), which ensures that groups of
173   users with different protected attributes are treated comparably. Specifically, UOF aims to provide
174   users with different activity levels with the same recommendation performance. The definition of
175   UOF is as follows Li et al. (2021); Rahmani et al. (2022); Han et al. (2023a):
176   Definition 1 (User-Oriented Fairness (UOF)).
177
                                              E[M(A)] = E[M(D)].                                            (1)
178
179
      Here, M is a metric (e.g., NDCG and Hit Ratio) evaluating recommendation performance. M(u)
180
      represents the recommendation performance for user u.
181
182   UOF aims to offer users with different activity levels the same recommendation performance, which
183   is always impossible in real-world RSs. Therefore, researchers Li et al. (2021); Rahmani et al. (2022);
184   Han et al. (2023a) calculate the difference in average recommendation performance for different user
185
      groups to evaluate the fairness of a model:
186   Definition 2 (The UOF metric (MU OF )).
187

                                               |A|           |D|

191   The MU OF value is used to evaluate the fairness of a recommendation model. A lower MU OF indi-
192   cates a fairer algorithm, aiming for equal treatment across different user activity groups. Note that
193   some researchers Han et al. (2024a) have proposed alternative metrics for assessing UOF. However,
194
      these metrics often replicate the characteristics of standard metrics like NDCG or Hit Ratio, and thus,
      they fail to specifically address the fairness aspect. Therefore, we utilize the more widely accepted
195
      MU OF metric, as adopted by Li et al. (2021); Rahmani et al. (2022); Han et al. (2023a), which is
196
      better suited to evaluate fairness within RSs.

      3.2   I NDIVIDUAL R EWEIGHTING
199
200   Individual reweighting aims to allocate different weights to each user’s loss values, thereby giving
201   more weight to users
                        P who are likely to be disadvantaged by the recommendation models. Consider the
202   utility loss L = Ui ∈U L(Ui ) of a recommendation model, where L(Ui ) represents the loss value
203   for user Ui . For instance, this could be the cross-entropy loss. The individual reweighting strategy
204   involves calculating
                   P        a set of weights β = {β1 , β2 , . . . , βNU } that maximize the total weighted loss:
205   maxβ L = Ui ∈U βi L(Ui ). By amplifying the loss values of poorly trained users, this approach
206   ensures that the model pays more attention to these users, thereby enhancing their training process
207   and improving overall fairness.

209   4     M ETHODOLOGY
210
211   In this paper, we propose a novel Individual Reweighting for User-Oriented Fairness framework,
212   named IR-UOF, to address the UOF issue in RSs. Designed as a versatile framework, IR-UOF can
213   be integrated with any existing backbone recommendation model to enhance fairness. The key
214   motivation behind IR-UOF is to introduce an in-processing framework that employs an individual-
215   level optimization strategy, thereby avoiding the need to calculate user similarities in sparse datasets
      and overcoming the three key limitations. Firstly, IR-UOF assigns reweighting ratios to advantaged

219   to ensure the smooth optimization of the algorithm.

350   extensive experiments on three publicly available real-world datasets using four backbone models.
351   The results are detailed in Table 1. Across all datasets, IR-UOF consistently outperforms all SOTA
352   methods, highlighting the importance of addressing all three critical limitations (introduced in Section
353   1) in solving UOF.
354   (1) Tackling Limitation 1 (Compared with UFR). IR-UOF outperforms UFR across all datasets.
355   Unlike the post-processing method UFR, IR-UOF effectively mitigates the training gap between
356   advantaged and disadvantaged users, thereby addressing Limitation 1. UFR’s failure to tackle the
357   root cause of the UOF issue results in its generally poor performance compared with all baselines.
358
359
      (2) Tackling Limitation 2 (Compared with In-UCDS, HyperUOF, and II-GOOT). IR-UOF consis-
      tently outperforms In-UCDS, HyperUOF, and II-GOOT across all datasets, especially on the sparser
360
      Gowalla dataset. These methods rely heavily on identifying essential similarities among users based
361
      on user-item interactions, a process that is unstable under severe data sparsity. As a result, their
362   performance is limited, particularly in sparser datasets. IR-UOF focuses on reweighting loss values
363   for each user, directly reflecting their training quality, making it a more stable and direct solution to
364   the UOF issue.
365
366   (3) Tackling Limitation 3 (Compared with S-DRO). Compared to S-DRO, IR-UOF achieves
367
      fairer models with better recommendation performance. S-DRO sorely assign more weight to the
      entire disadvantaged user group, overlooking the different treatments individual users within this
368
      group receive. This inability to target users individually limits its performance. IR-UOF adaptively
369
      reweights each user’s loss value, providing unique treatment to each user and accurately improving
370   the training of users dominated by recommendation models. Thus, IR-UOF can narrow the training
371   gap across the entire user group, naturally improving both fairness and recommendation performance.

         5.4                 M ODEL E FFICIENCY (RQ3)
445
446
        This section aims to analyze the efficiency of IR-UOF. As an in-processing framework, it is crucial to
447     demonstrate that it does not impose excessive additional time costs on the original models’ training
448     process. We compare the training time of IR-UOF with all in-processing methods. The experimental
449     results are presented in Figure 3. Our analysis reveals that IR-UOF maintains a time cost comparable
450     to the original backbone model, incurring only a slight additional time cost. The additional time
451     expenditure associated with IR-UOF is primarily due to sorting the loss values of users, which has
452     a time complexity of O(NU log NU ). This sorting process is time-efficient and depends solely on
453     the size of the datasets. Consequently, when applied to more complex and time-intensive backbone
454     models (e.g., LightGCN), the relative increase in training time due to IR-UOF becomes less significant.
455
        This observation underscores IR-UOF’s applicability and practicality, especially in scenarios where
        backbone models are inherently resource-intensive.

      7     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      This paper addresses the User-Oriented Fairness (UOF) issue in Recommender Systems (RSs),
533
      specifically focusing on narrowing the recommendation gap between advantaged and disadvantaged
534   user groups. We introduce a novel framework, Individual Reweighting for User-Oriented Fairness,
535   referred to as IR-UOF, designed to overcome three significant limitations that existing research
536   has not adequately addressed. IR-UOF adaptively reweights the loss values of each user, ensuring
537   that no users are dominated by the recommendation models. As a result, IR-UOF enhances the
538   training quality for each individual user, achieving overall fairness and improving recommendation
539   performance through individualized optimization, i.e., One to All. We conduct extensive experiments
      on three real-world datasets to demonstrate the efficacy of IR-UOF.

773
774   Baselines and Backbone Models. We compare IR-UOF with the SOTA UOF methods UFR (failing
775   to tackle Limitation 1) Li et al. (2021), In-UCDS Han et al. (2023a), HyperUOF Han et al. (2024b),
776   and II-GOOT Han et al. (2024a) (failing to tackle Limitation 2 and Limitation 3), S-DRO Wen
777   et al. (2022a) (failing to tackle Limitation 3). (1) UFR: A post-processing re-ranking method that
778   modifies recommendation results of a given backbone model. (2) In-UCDS: An in-processing
779
      method that allows disadvantaged users to learn from advantaged users based on dominant sets. (3)
      HyperUOF: An in-processing strategy that utilizes hypergraph to explore high-order correlations
780
      among advantaged and disadvantaged users. (4) II-GOOT: An in-processing framework that narrows
781
      the training gap between advantaged and disadvantaged users through intra- and inter-group stages.
782   (5) S-DRO An in-processing method that minimizes the loss function value for disadvantaged users
783   during training.
784
785
      To fully evaluate the performance of IR-UOF and SOTA methods, we choose four different backbone
      recommendation models, including a traditional matrix factorization method (MF), two deep-learning-
786
      based methods (NeuMF, VAECF), and a graph neural network-based method (LightGCN). (1)
787
      MF Koren et al. (2009): Matrix Factorization maps both users and items to a joint latent factor
788   space and calculates the similarities among users and items. (2) NeuMF He et al. (2017): Neural
789   Collaborative Filtering introduces a deep neural network with non-linear activation functions to train
790   a user and item matching function. (3) VAECF Liang et al. (2018): Variational Autoencoders for
791   Collaborative Filtering proposes a generative model with multinomial likelihood and uses Bayesian
792   inference for parameter estimation. (4) LightGCN He et al. (2020): Light Graph Convolution
793   Network simplifies the design of GCN by including only the most essential component in GCN
794   neighborhood aggregation — for collaborative filtering.
795
      Evaluation Protocols. (1) User Grouping: Users are ranked by interaction counts, with the top 5%
796   as advantaged and the rest as disadvantaged Li et al. (2021); Dai et al. (2022); Han et al. (2023a). (2)
797   Dataset Spilting: Using the Leave-One-Out (LOO) strategy Chen et al. (2023b); He et al. (2017),
798   we split data into training, validation, and testing sets. (3) Performance Metrics: We adopt the
799   widely-used Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) Wang et al. (2013) and Hit Ratio
800   (HR) Waters (1976) to evaluate the recommendation performance of each model. A higher value
801   indicates superior recommendation performance, with a predicted cut-off of topK = 10 Li et al.
802   (2021); Dai et al. (2022); Han et al. (2023a). Besides, we utilize MU OF to evaluate the UOF level of
803   a recommendation model, with a lower value of MU OF means a fairer performance. (4) Statistical
804
      Robustness: Each evaluation is repeated 10 times, reporting average performance with significance
      testing (p-value < 0.05).
805

808   the parameters as their default values. (3) For S-DRO: Implemented as recommended in Wen et al.
809   (2022b), with hidden layer dimensions (128, 64) and temperature τ set to 0.07. (4) For backbone
      models: We set the dimension of user and item embeddings to 64 for all of them, and adopt their
