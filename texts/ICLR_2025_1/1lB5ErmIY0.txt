009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            We examine diverging preferences in human-labeled preference datasets. We de-
012            velop a taxonomy of disagreement sources spanning 10 categories across four
013            high-level classes—task underspecification, response style, refusals, and annota-
014            tion errors. We find that the majority of disagreements are in opposition with stan-
015            dard reward modeling approaches, which are designed with the assumption that
016
               annotator disagreement is noise. We then explore how these findings impact two
               areas of LLM development: reward modeling and evaluation. In our experiments,
017
               we demonstrate how standard reward modeling methods, like the Bradley-Terry
018
               model, fail to differentiate whether a given preference judgment is the result of
019            unanimous agreement among annotators or the majority opinion among diverging
020            user preferences. We also find that these tendencies are also echoed by popular
021            LM-as-Judge evaluation methods, which consistently identify a winning response
022            in cases of diverging preferences. These findings highlight remaining challenges
023            in LLM evaluations, which are greatly influenced by divisive features like re-
024            sponse style, and in developing pluralistically aligned LLMs. To address these
025            issues, we develop methods for identifying diverging preferences to mitigate their
026            influence in evaluations and during LLM training.

      As large language models (LLMs) continue to rise in prominence and to serve millions of people on
031   a daily basis, there is an increasing need to ensure that systems are pluralistically aligned (Sorensen
032   et al., 2024). Learning from human preferences has emerged as the standard method for adapting
033   LLMs to facilitate user-assistant interactions with much success. Despite these advances, however,
034   the field continues to struggle with the challenge of handing diverging preferences, where users dis-
035   agree on the ideal response to a prompt. Prior works on developing pluralistically aligned LLMs
036   have focused on the development of synthetic preference datasets, where disagreements are simu-
037   lated based on author-defined features and frequencies Poddar et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2024). In
038   this work, we take a step back to ask the foundational question when and why do human annotators
039
      disagree in their preferences?
040   To make this research possible, we to introduce MultiPref-Disagreements and HelpSteer2-
041   Disagreements.1 With these datasets, we also include a novel taxonomy of disagreement sources
042   spanning 10 categories and 4 high-level classes (Table 1). Based on our analysis of these datasets,
043   we offer two significant findings. First, we find that diverging preferences are hardly rare, with over
044   30% of examples across both datasets showing diverging preferences across annotators. Second,
045
      our analysis shows that most disagreements in preference annotations are the result of individual
      predilections rather than annotator errors. We find that over 75% of disagreements are influenced by
046
      factors such as response complexity, verbosity, or interpretations of underspecified prompts.
047
048   Our findings, that most disagreements in preference annotations are the result of individual predilec-
049   tions rather than annotation errors, run counter to how standard preference learning pipelines and
050   reward models are designed, where dissenting opinions are treated as undesirable noise. We demon-

086   We introduce alternative methods for training reward models that make the two following changes:

093   Next, we move onto studying the impact of diverging preferences of popular LLM-as-Judge methods
094   for evaluating LLMs. In cases where diverging preference may occur, practitioners concerned with
095   pluralistic alignment often opt to enforce consistent policies in their LLMs (e.g., refuse if any users
096   believe the model should, or ask for clarification in cases of task ambiguity). We find that these
097
      evaluations, which are used to measure general model capabilities, unduly punish models that exhibit

098

099

100   LLM-as-Judge benchmark (Yuchen Lin et al., 2024), and find that we are able to use our problematic
101   examples where LLM-as-Judge evaluation methods unduly punish systems for refusing on unsafe
102   prompts or for prompting the user for further clarification on an underspecified prompt.

168   mining quality of a given response. Below, we describe each disagreement cause and class.
169   Task Underspecification Disagreements often arise from underspecification in the prompt, where
170   both responses consider and address distinct, valid interpretations of the task.
171   Response Style We identify several disagreements causes that arise due to differences in response
172   style, where preferences are primarily influenced by an individual’s tastes rather than content.
173   • Verbosity Disagreements arise over the preferred levels of detail, explanation, or examples in each
174      response. While prior works have noted that RLHF annotations are often biased toward lengthy
175      responses in aggregate (Prasann Singhal & Durrett, 2023), we find that individuals frequently
176      disagree on the preferred level of detail or explanation in a response.
177   • Format We find that another common source of diverging preferences is disagreement over how
178      responses should be organized. LLMs frequently present responses as paragraphs, lists or under
179      headings. We find frequent disagreements over when such formatting is appropriate and how
180      headings and lists should be semantically structured.
181   • Complexity Responses often differ in the level of assumed domain expertise of the user and the
182      level of technical depth with which to consider the user’s request. As such, diverging preferences
183      arise over responses that are catered toward individuals with different backgrounds and goals.
184   • Aesthetic tastes Prior work has noted that creative writing or writing assistance comprise a sig-
185      nificant portion of user requests Zhao et al. (2024). We find that preferences often diverge for such
186      requests, where a preference often comes down to a matter of personal taste.
187   Refusals We find that refusals based on safety concerns or model capabilities are often the subject
188   of disagreement among annotators. This finding is consistent with prior work, which has demon-
189   strated that judgments of social acceptability or offensive language can vary based on their personal
190   background and identity (Forbes et al., 2020; Sap et al., 2022). We, furthermore, find that diverg-
191   ing preferences often occur when comparing refusals versus refusals. Recent work has studied
192   establishing different types of refusals (e.g., soft versus hard refusals) and rules for when each are
193
      appropriate (Mu et al., 2024b). Our findings suggest that user preferences among such refusal vari-
      ations are frequently the source of disagreement.
194
195   Errors Prior work has noted that an individual’s judgment of a response’s correctness has almost
196
      perfect agreement with their judgment of a response’s overall quality (Wang et al., 2024b). During
      annotation, however, errors can be difficult for annotators to detect or their impact may be perceived
197
      differently across annotators, leading to variation among preferences.

204   Our analysis above demonstrates that disagreements in preference annotations are often the result
205   of differences in individual user perspectives rather than simple noise. In this section, we study the
206   behaviors of standard reward modeling methods in cases of diverging and non-diverging preferences.
207
      Aligning LLMs via RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022) involves training a reward model on human prefer-
208
      ence data to assign a reward rA for a given prompt x and response A that is indicative of its quality
209
      ((x, A) → rA ). LLMs are then adapted to generate responses that receive high rewards from the
210   trained reward model. As such, reward models that heavily favor a single response in cases of diverg-
211   ing preference result in LLMs that learn to only predict responses tailored to a single perspective.
212   Ideally, when comparing two responses (A, B) where there is high-agreement in user preferences,
213   reward models should assign significantly higher rewards to the preferred response, rA >> rB .
214   Likewise, in instances of diverging preferences across users, reward models should recognize this
215   disagreement either identifying such examples as ties, rA = rB , or by only identifying a lesser
      advantage in the model’s preferred response rA > rB .

236   3.1   E XPERIMENTS
237
238   Below, we describe the two standard reward modeling methods explored in this work. When training
239   such models, it is standard to aggregate labels across multiple annotators by taking the majority
240   vote (Wang et al., 2023; Köpf et al., 2024). We experiment with two such reward modeling methods,
241   training each on both the aggregated labels as well as over all annotations in the dataset, treating each
242
      annotator label as its own training instance.
243   Bradley-Terry is a widely used approach for training reward models in the RLHF paradigm (Bai
244   et al., 2022a; Dubey et al., 2024a). It defines the likelihood of a user preferring response A over
245
      response B as P (A > B) = logistic(rA − rB ) and is trained via minimizing the negative log
      likelihood on annotated preferences. In our experiments, we track how heavily reward models favor
246
      a single response by computing P (C > R) where C and R are the reward model’s chosen and
247
      rejected responses, respectively.
248
      MSE-Regression is an alternative method that utilizes the individual Likert-5 scores for each re-
249
      sponse found in Regression-style datasets such as HelpSteer2 dataset (Wang et al., 2024b). Here,
250
      reward models predict the scalar reward of each response, and training is done by minimizing mean
251   squared error against the 1-5 score assigned by annotators. To track how heavily reward models
252   favor a single response, we track the distance in predicted rewards given by |ra − rb |.
253
      Large-Scale, SOTA Reward Models We also inlcude two large-scale, state-of-the-art reward mod-
254   els in our analysis. Skywork-Reward-Gemma-2-27B-v0.2 (Liu et al., 2024) is a bradley-terry
255   reward model trained from Gemma-2-27B-Instruct (Team et al., 2024). Llama-3.1-Nemotron-
256   70B-Reward is a reward model based on Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct that utilizes a novel approach
257   that combines standard Bradely-Terry and MSE-regression training methods aggregated labels. Be-
258   cause both systems are trained on different splits of HelpSteer2, we avoid test-train overlap by only
259   evaluating these systems on MultiPref.

      4   M ODELING D IVERGING P REFERENCES WITH D ISTRIBUTIONAL R EWARDS
277
278
      As we demonstrated above, standard Bradley-Terry and MSE-Regression based approaches to re-
279   ward modeling fail to distinguish diverging and high-agreement preferences, predicting similar re-
280   ward distributions in either case. Performing RLHF training on such reward models, therefore, can
281   lead to breakdowns in pluralistic alignment. In this section, we explore methods for training distri-
282   butional reward models which can fulfill the dual objectives of both (1) identifying which responses
283   annotators prefer and (2) identifying responses where preferences may diverge. By identifying such
284   instances, they can be removed or specially handled during RLHF training to prevent systems from
285   learning to only respond to a single-user viewpoint. Learning such a reward model is cheaper and
286   more efficient than having to obtain multiple annotations for every data point one wants to evaluate.
287   Evaluation Metrics To evaluate reward models on these dual objectives of both identifying pre-
288   ferred responses and their ability to distinguish between diverging and high-agreement preferences,
289   we use the following two metrics.
290   • Preference Accuracy: Following existing work on evaluating reward models (Lambert et al.,
291     2024), we evaluate reward models on binary classification accuracy. Here, we test a reward
292     model’s ability to assign greater reward to responses that were chosen by human annotators, eval-
293     uating systems against all annotator labels.
294   • Diverging ID AUROC: We evaluate systems using area-under the receiver operating characteris-
295     tic curve (AUROC) on the binary task of identifying preference pairs with significantly diverging
296     preferences. We select this metric, commonly used in evaluating binary classification calibration,
297     as it directly correlates with the use-case of detecting divisive responses during RLHF training.
298
        Here, systems are directly evaluated on their ability to successfully identify examples with diverg-
        ing preferences (true positive rate), while minimizing the number of high-agreement preferences
299
        that are erroneously identified as diverging (false discovery rate).

341
      tator labels using KL-Divergence loss. For training, we experiment using the Pytorch Paszke et al.
342   (2019) approximation of the normal distribution CDF Φ(x), as well as using the (1 + tanh(x))/2
343   and logisitic(x). We find that training with the logisitic function approximation yielded better
344   training stability than the base Φ(x) implementation, and use this in all our experiments.
345
      To evaluate our Mean-Variance reward models for preference accuracy, we compare the expected
346
      rewards of each response (µA , µB ). To identify disagreements when evaluating Diverging ID AU-
347
      ROC, we weigh the standard deviation in each response’ reward against the difference of their means
348   by computing |µA − µB | − λ(σA + σB ), where the λ is tuned on a development set of 500 examples.
349
350   Classification-based Reward Models (KL) Similar to the single-value MSE-regression reward
351   model above, we train classification-based reward models utilizing the individual Likert-5 scores for
352   each response found in the HelpSteer2 dataset. This 5-way classifier model predicts the distribution
353   of Likert-5 assigned by annotators, and is trained using KL-divergence loss. To identify preferred
354   responses when evaluating Preference Accuracy, we predict the distribution over the Likert-5 scores
355
      for each response and compare the expected scores. To identify disagreements when evaluating
      Diverging ID AUROC, we use the predicted joint probability of annotators labeling the response as
356
      a 1 or 5 , which is computed as the product of the probabilities assigned to the 1 and 5 labels.

      5.3   R EMOVING D IVISIVE E XAMPLES FROM LLM- AS -J UDGE B ENCHMARKS
474
475   Our experiments above demonstrate that LLM-as-Judge systems exhibit bias when evaluating LLM
476   completions where preferences diverge. We argue that general model capability evaluations should
477   therefore focus on evaluating over only high-agreement instances. To accomplish this, we need
478   ways of identifying divisive examples from LLM-as-Judge benchmarks so they can be removed.
479   Below, we propose a method for using our trained distributional reward models to identify divisive
480   examples and experiment with identifying such problematic examples in an existing benchmark.
481   Identifying Divisive Examples in Wildbench In our experiments in Section 4, we demonstrated
482   that our distributional reward models are effective at detecting diverging preferences between two re-
483   sponses. We, therefore, propose to use such models to identify and remove divisive prompts, prompts
484   that consistently yield divisive responses, from these benchmarks. We use our trained distributional
485   reward models to identify such instances in the WildBench benchmark (Yuchen Lin et al., 2024), an
      LLM-as-Judge benchmark that sources prompts from real user-LLM interactions (Yuchen Lin et al.,


                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      2024). To identify divisive prompts in this benchmark, we run our Classification (KL) distributional
487   reward model over the responses from the five LLMs with the highest WildBench-ELO scores. Fol-
488   lowing suit with our methods for identifying diverging preferences, we compute the divisiveness of
489   each response as the joint probability of an annotator labeling the instances as a one or a five on the
490   likert-5 scale. We then average these values across all five LLM completions to predict a measure
491   of the divisiveness of each prompt.
492   Results and Recommendations We use the above method to rank each example in the Wild-
493   Bench Benchmark by the divisiveness of the prompt. We then manually annotate the top 5% (50
494   total) examples with the most divisive prompts to identify instances of Comply vs. Refuse and Task
495   Underspecification. We find that 42% (21 total) of examples contain Comply vs. Refuse disagree-
496   ments and 16% (8 total) of examples Task Underspecification disagreements. Furthermore, we find
497
      that WildBench’s LLM-as-Judge method for scoring completions consistently prefers the complying
      response 100% of the time in these cases of Comply vs. Refuse disagreements. We also find that in
498
      Task Underspecification examples where one of the models prompted users for further clarification
499
      rather than directly predicting an answer (6 total), this response lost 83% (5 total) of the time. In
500   Appendix E, we provide examples of identified prompts.
501
502   In summation, our results analyzing biases in LLM-as-Judge evaluation methods demonstrate that
503
      LLMs make decisive and biased decisions over examples where user preferences diverge. These
      findings highlight that using LLM-as-Judge methods to evaluate LLM capabilities on examples with
504
      diverging preferences may unduly punish pluralistically aligned systems, like those trained to enact
505
      a consistent policy in cases where preferences may diverge (e.g., refuse if anyone thinks complying
506   is unsafe). We, therefore, propose that general LLM-as-Judge evaluations should only evaluate over
507   instances where there is high-agreement between annotators. We further demonstrate that reward
508   models can effectively be used to achieve this, by identifying divisive prompts in LLM-as-Judge
509   benchmarks so they can be further examined by benchmark authors and removed. Future work
510   might also explore methods for training pluralistically aligned models using distributional rewards.

512   6   R ELATED W ORK
513   Annotator Disagreement in NLP To the best of our knowledge, this is the first study on diverging
514   preferences on general human preferences. Annotator disagreement has been studied in prior works
515   in specific domains. Santy et al. (2023) and Forbes et al. (2020), explore annotator disagreement
516   in safety, looking specifically at how morality and toxicity judgments vary across users of different
517   backgrounds. Prior works have analyzed disagreements in NLI (Pavlick & Kwiatkowski, 2019;
518   Liu et al., 2023), and Jiang & Marneffe (2022) develop an NLI-specific taxonomy of disagreement
519
      causes. Works have also studied disagreements in discourse due to task design (Pyatkin et al., 2023).
      Frenda et al. (2024) presents a survey of works studying different user perspectives across NLP tasks.
520
      Prior works have advocated for the importance of considering disagreements (Basile et al., 2021) and
521
      have proposed shared tasks for modeling with annotator disagreements (Uma et al., 2021). Earlier
522   works have also studied annotator disagreements due to ambiguity (Poesio & Artstein, 2005) and
523   veridicality (de Marneffe et al., 2012) and collect datasets for studying such disagreements.
524
      Pluralistically Aligned Reward Models Several recent works have also developed pluralistically
525   aligned reward models via personalization (Chen et al., 2024; Poddar et al., 2024), distributional
526   reward modeling (Siththaranjan et al., 2023), or alternative RLHF objectives (Ramesh et al., 2024;
527   Chakraborty et al., 2024). These works, however, have relied on simulating user disagreements
528   based on author-defined features and frequencies. Pitis et al. (2024) explores developing context-
529   aware reward models, which may resolve predictions over diverging preferences by providing addi-
530   tional context to the prompt, specifying different user perspectives during reward modeling. In this
531   work, the authors introduce methods of synthesizing different contexts from an LLM. Our work, in
532   contrast, investigates reasons for variation and disagreements in real human-preferences, and high-
533
      lights such datasets as more realistic, complex test beds for such modeling efforts.
534

      7   C ONCLUSION
536   We analyze causes of diverging preferences in human-annotated preference datasets and demonstrate
537   that standard reward models and LLM-as-Judge evaluation methods and methods make decisive
538   decisions over diverging preference, causing issues for training and evaluating plualistically aligned
539   LLMs. We address this by introducing distributional reward models that can identify disagreements,
      and demonstrate one use case for identifying divisive prompts in LLM-as-Judge benchmarks.

844   Mean-Variance Modeling Details To predict values of standard deviation σ, we use the absolute
845   value as our activation function for predicting non-negative values. We then square this value to get
846   our predicted variance σ 2 . For training stability, we further add 0.1 to all σ predictions. Likewise,
847   when training such models with our proposed KL-Loss, we add 0.05 to the predicted probability
848   over each label and renormalize, ensuring that no class receives a predicted probability of zero
849   and accounting for floating-point errors. When computing the CDF when training Mean-Variance
850   models with KL-loss, we experiment using the Pytorch Paszke et al. (2019) approximation of the
851
      normal distribution CDF Φ(x), as well as using the (1 + tanh(x))/2 and logisitic(x) functions
      as approximations. We find that training with the logisitic function approximation yielded better
852
      training stability than the base Φ(x) implementation, and use this in all our experiments. For tuning
853
      values of η, experiment with values of η ∈ {0.00, 0.50, 1.00} and select the best performing value
