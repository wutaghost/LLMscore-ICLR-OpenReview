011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Designing and optimizing neural network architectures typically require extensive
014            expertise, starting from handcrafted designs followed by manual or automated
015            refinement, which significantly hinders rapid innovation. To address these chal-
016
               lenges, Younger is introduced as a comprehensive dataset derived from over 174K
               real-world models across more than 30 tasks from various public model hubs. After
017
               extensive processing and filtering, Younger includes 7,629 unique architectures,
018
               each represented as a directed acyclic graph with detailed operator-level informa-
019            tion based on ONNX operator definitions, enabling compatibility across different
020            deep learning frameworks. The dataset is designed to support the emerging research
021            area of Artificial Intelligence-Generated Neural Network Architecture (AIGNNA),
022            which aims to automate their generation and refinement. Comprehensive statistical
023            analysis, including architecture component analyses, highlights the diversity and
024            complexity of architectures in Younger, revealing the potential for future research
025            in this domain. Initial experiments, including operator and dataflow predictions,
026            demonstrate the dataset’s utility for architecture exploration and evaluation, and
027
               highlight its potential as a benchmark for graph neural networks. Furthermore,
               an online platform ensures continuous maintenance and expansion of the dataset,
028
               supporting global researchers in their endeavors. The dataset and source code are
029
               publicly available to encourage further research and lower entry barriers in this
030            challenging domain.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      The proliferation of large language models like ChatGPT (OpenAI et al., 2023) has decisively
036   demonstrated the critical importance of large-scale data collection and innovative neural network
037   architecture design in advancing Artificial Intelligence (AI) (Schuhmann et al., 2022; Tang et al.,
038   2024), which has further highlighted the need for more efficient and effective architecture design
039   processes. As a result, in recent years, neural architecture search (NAS) has garnered significant
040   attention and research focus, aiming to address the limitations of manual architecture design in
041   handling increasingly complex AI tasks.
042   Despite the significant research attention NAS has received, it has not produced revolutionary results.
043   Major architectural shifts, such as the transition from RNNs and CNNs to Transformers, were
044   primarily driven by human designers (Vaswani et al., 2017). NAS frameworks such as DARTS (Liu
045   et al., 2018b) and datasets like NAS-Bench-* (e.g., NAS-Bench-101 (Ying et al., 2019), NAS-
046   Bench-NLP (Klyuchnikov et al., 2022), DeepNets-1M (Knyazev et al., 2021)) have automated the
047   architecture search process. Yet, they remain constrained by predefined search spaces, which refer to
048   specific macro-architecture topologies and a limited set of operator types. This limitation restricts the
049   diversity and novelty of architectures that can be explored, leaving significant room for improvement
050
      in neural network architecture design innovation. Instead of creating fundamentally new designs,
      NAS has primarily focused on pushing model performance within predefined architectural patterns,
051
      limiting its ability to generate groundbreaking architectures.
052
053   To overcome the limitations of existing NAS frameworks and datasets, and to explore more diverse
      and innovative architectures, the Younger dataset was developed. Unlike the predefined, constrained


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      architecture search spaces in NAS datasets such as NAS-Bench-* and NAS frameworks like DARTS,
055   Younger offers a more flexible and diverse exploration space, enabling researchers to break free from
056   rigid macro-architecture topologies and a limited set of operator types.
057
058
      The Younger dataset is constructed based on the Open Neural Network Exchange (ONNX) operator
      definitions (Bai et al., 2019), ensuring compatibility across various deep learning frameworks.
059
      Derived from approximately 174K real-world models across more than 30 tasks from multiple public
060
      repositories (as listed in Table 3), these models are first converted into the ONNX format, then
061   transformed into directed acyclic graphs (DAGs), where nodes represent ONNX operators with
062   detailed configurations and hyperparameters, and edges represent data flows between operators. To
063   ensure uniqueness, isomorphic architectures are filtered out. Additionally, model parameter values
064   are excluded due to concerns about privacy and security. Finally, Younger includes 7,629 distinct
065   neural network architectures and supports all operator types defined by ONNX (about 200 types),
066   striking a balance between the limited operator types in traditional NAS spaces, which typically
067   define only a few operators, and the extensive operator sets in deep learning frameworks like PyTorch,
068   which contains over 2,000 operators, as shown in Table 1. This balance provides a wider range of
069
      operator types and data flow configurations than existing NAS spaces while maintaining a manageable
      exploration space. This flexibility enables researchers to explore novel architectures better suited to
070
      the rapidly evolving demands of AI tasks, overcoming the constraints of traditional search spaces.
071
072   Extensive statistical analyses were conducted at the operator, component, and architecture levels,
073   validating Younger’s capacity to support various design patterns and configurations. These analyses
074   confirmed the dataset’s potential for advancing neural architecture research, demonstrating its rich
075
      prior knowledge and feasibility in real-world applications. The diversity and complexity of the
      architectures in Younger provide a robust foundation for exploring new paradigms in neural network
076
      design and optimization.
077
078   Based on these findings, the Younger dataset has provided a strong foundation for introducing the
079   concept of Artificial Intelligence-Generated Neural Network Architecture (AIGNNA), a powerful
080   approach to automate the generation of neural architectures. This process is characterized by two
081
      paradigms: 1) Local, which focuses on fine-tuning and optimizing components of pre-existing
      architectures by selecting the most suitable operator types and data flows; and 2) Global, which
082
      represents the more challenging task of generating entire neural network architectures from scratch,
083
      fully automating the design process.
084
085   Initial experiments have demonstrated the success of the Local paradigm in optimizing pre-existing
086   architectures, particularly in selecting the most suitable operator types and data flows. However,
087
      as the Global paradigm involves generating entire architectures from scratch, it remains an open
      challenge without readily available methods to automate this process fully. Consequently, while
088
      the potential of Younger to support such a paradigm is clear, further research and development are
089
      required before comprehensive experiments can be conducted in this area.
090
091   To support global collaboration and continuous dataset expansion, Younger is publicly available
092   along with a platform that allows researchers worldwide to upload their models. These models are
093
      automatically converted into DAG format and integrated into future releases of Younger, ensuring
      the dataset remains up-to-date. This open platform lowers the barriers to entry for research in neural
094
      architecture generation, empowering researchers worldwide to contribute to and benefit from this
095
      evolving field.

101   2.1   A RTIFICIAL I NTELLIGENCE -G ENERATED N EURAL N ETWORK A RCHITECTURE
102
103   The design and optimization of neural network architectures have historically been labor-intensive
104   tasks, relying heavily on the intuition and expertise of human researchers. This process has evolved
105   from manual designs, exemplified by early architectures such as AlexNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012),
106   ResNet (He et al., 2016), LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997), and Transformer (Vaswani et al.,
107   2017), to more automated methods employing neural architecture search (NAS) like NASNet (Zoph
      et al., 2018) and DARTS (Liu et al., 2018b).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Although these manually designed architectures were groundbreaking at the time, they were con-
109   strained by the reliance on expert knowledge and required significant time and effort to design.
110   This inefficiency became a growing challenge as the scale and diversity of AI tasks expanded. The
111   emergence of NAS frameworks marked a significant advancement in automating the design process,
112   aiming to improve the efficiency and adaptability of architecture generation.
113
      NAS frameworks such as DARTS (Liu et al., 2018b) and benchmarks like NAS-Bench-101 (Ying
114
      et al., 2019) and NATS-Bench Dong et al. (2021) introduced methods that automate the exploration
115   of predefined search spaces, typically consisting of fixed macro-architectures and limited operator
116   types. While these frameworks automate the search for optimal cells (building blocks or micro-
117   architectures) (Zoph et al., 2018; Real et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2018a;b; Pham et al., 2018; Tan & Le,
118   2019; Klein & Hutter, 2019), they are inherently restricted by these predefined search spaces. As a
119   result, the innovation potential is constrained, limiting the diversity and novelty of the architectures
120   that can be explored.
121   Addressing these challenges, the Younger dataset and the AIGNNA methodology offer a revolutionary
122   departure from these constraints. By eliminating the need for predefined macro-architectures, Younger
123   allows for a more explorative approach to architecture design, supporting various operator types and
124   data flow configurations, as seen in Table 1. This flexibility facilitates the generation of innovative,
125   customized architectures better suited to specific applications and more adaptable to emerging
126   challenges in neural network design.

      3.2    S TEP 2: C ONVERTING M ODELS TO ONNX F ORMAT
217
218   Different deep learning frameworks define distinct operators, which can lead to increased dataset
219   usage costs and inefficiencies in architecture design when models lack a unified representation. To
220   address this issue, the Open Neural Network Exchange (ONNX) format was adopted as the standard
221   representation for models in the Younger dataset. ONNX provides a standardized set of operators,
222   enabling model exchange and deployment across various deep learning frameworks (such as PyTorch
223   and TensorFlow).
224   In addition to standardizing operator definitions, ONNX serves as a unified representation, sig-
225   nificantly reducing the complexity of neural architecture representation by consolidating operator
226   definitions across frameworks. For instance, ONNX reduces the 2,000+ PyTorch operators to approx-
227   imately 200 standard operators. Several open-source tools, including Optimum5 and tf2onnx6 , were
228   utilized to convert models into ONNX format.
229
230   3.3    S TEP 3: E XTRACTING DAG S F ROM ONNX MODELS
231
      To address security and privacy concerns, and because neural network architecture design does
232
      not require specific parameter values, all parameter data were removed from the ONNX models.
233
      Removing parameter data not only addresses security and privacy concerns but also reflects the focus
234   on architecture design, independent of parameterization. Additionally, ONNX models defined in
235   Protocol Buffers7 format are less suited for direct analysis by standard graph processing tools (e.g.,
236   NetworkX (Hagberg et al., 2008)) or deep learning frameworks (e.g., PyTorch Geometric (Fey &
237   Lenssen, 2019)) compared to directed acyclic graphs (DAGs). A tool was developed to convert ONNX
238   models into DAGs to improve compatibility and streamline analysis. This transformation ensures
239   architecture designs can be shared while maintaining parameter privacy and avoiding unnecessary
240   parameter information.
241   In these DAGs, each operator within a neural network architecture is represented as a node, with
242   detailed information such as the operator type and its attribute definitions recorded. Directed edges
243   represent the data flows between operators, and each node’s inflow and outflow order is meticulously
244   documented. The DAGs are represented using the open-source graph library NetworkX, enabling
245   seamless integration with various analysis tools.

256   3.4    S TEP 4: F ILTERING O UT I SOMORPHIC DAG S
257
258
      Public model hubs often contain many isomorphic neural network architectures, making it necessary
      to filter these architectures to ensure the uniqueness of each architecture in the dataset. The Weisfeiler-
259
      Lehman (WL) graph hash algorithm (Shervashidze et al., 2011) was employed to compute the hash of
260
      extracted DAG and identify heterogeneous architectures. The WL algorithm ensures that isomorphic
261   graphs receive identical hash values while heterogeneous graphs are assigned distinct hashes, firmly
262   guaranteeing architectural diversity.
263
264
      Operator types and their attributes, represented in the nodes, were used as iteration objects within the
      WL hash algorithm. This process ensures that all architectures in the dataset are heterogeneous, both
265
      in terms of hyperparameters and operator types. After applying this filtering method, 7,629 unique
266
      neural network architectures were retained from an initial pool of about 174K real-world models.

272   After the whole process, the resulting dataset serves as a foundation for global collaboration and open-
273   source contribution. Overall, the open-source nature of Younger alleviates the need for researchers
274   to invest significant resources in constructing similar datasets. The creation of Younger required
275   substantial computational resources, approximately 8,000 CPU hours, and considerable human effort,
276   including the development of around 24K lines of specialized code. By making both the dataset and
277   its construction methodology open-source, along with accessible interfaces and websites, researchers
278
      worldwide can easily contribute to the maintenance and expansion of Younger or build similar
      datasets. This global collaboration ensures that Younger can continuously evolve to meet the needs
279
      of the research community.
280
281   When the first version of Younger began construction, there were 743.5K publicly available models,
282   of which 341K could be converted into ONNX format. As of its first release, 174K models were
283   extracted for processing, resulting in 7,629 unique heterogeneous neural network architectures.
284
      Despite the vast number of available deep learning models and their rapid growth, less than 1% of
      these models represent heterogeneous and effective architectures. This notably low proportion of
285
      heterogeneous architectures highlights the limitations of current neural network design methods,
286
      both manual and NAS-based, in fostering architectural innovation. Younger breaks through these
287   limitations by offering a foundational platform for more flexible and expansive neural architecture
288   design research. It also lays the groundwork for the development of Artificial Intelligence-Generated
289   Neural Network Architecture (AIGNNA), an initiative aimed at exploring new design methods
290   beyond traditional frameworks.

      4.1.1    H OMOGENEOUS OR H ETEROGENEOUS ?
301
302   Neural network operators vary significantly in their attributes. For example, a Convolution (Conv)
303   operator may include attributes such as dilations, kernel shape, and strides, whereas a Batch Nor-
304   malization operator contains attributes like epsilon and momentum. This diversity poses a critical
305   question in graph-based neural network architecture design: should these architectures be treated as
306   homogeneous or heterogeneous graphs?
307   In the homogeneous graph approach, all nodes represent the same type (i.e., "operator"), ignoring
308   the specific operator type or its attributes. In contrast, a heterogeneous graph treats the nodes as
309   distinct operator types, capturing the full diversity of operator behaviors and configurations. Although
310   heterogeneous graphs more accurately reflect the complexity of neural network architectures, they
311   introduce additional challenges in analysis and design.
312   For this study, all architectures in the Younger dataset are treated as homogeneous graphs. This sim-
313   plification allows the focus to remain on the structural and topological properties of the architectures
314   without introducing excessive variables into the analysis. Future work may explore the treatment of
315   Younger as a heterogeneous graph dataset.
316
317   4.1.2    O PERATOR C ONFIGURATIONS IN THE DAG S
318
319   Given that the DAG nodes contain discrete information such as operator types and integer attributes,
320
      processing node features using conventional approaches can be challenging. To address this, two
      configurations are explored for handling operator attributes:
321
322   Operator w/o Attributes: This configuration treats all nodes based solely on their operator types
323   without considering the detailed attribute configurations. This reduces the number of node features to
      match the size of the ONNX operator set, streamlining the analysis.


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Operator w/ Attributes: In this approach, operators of the same type but with different attribute
325   configurations are treated as distinct node features. This significantly increases the number of
326   node features, adding complexity to the learning process but more accurately reflecting the detailed
327   structure of the operators. The subsequent experiments will evaluate these two configurations to
328   determine their impact on the learning process.

464
      Local: Architecture Refinement In Detail.The local paradigm addresses the need to fine-tune
465
      specific aspects of existing neural network architectures. This approach is divided into operator and
466
      data flow designs, as shown in Figure 7. Operator Design involves determining the most suitable type
467   of operator for a given node based on local or global architectural information, as illustrated in the
468   leftward of Figure 7. This design assesses potential replacements for current operators and suggests
469   appropriate operators for new nodes based on neighboring structural information.
470
      The second type, data flow design, evaluates the existence of data flows between operators. This
471
      fine-tuning method determines whether a directed edge representing data flow should connect any
472
      two nodes, utilizing insights from local and global architectural contexts.
473
474   Challenges within the local paradigm arise from the vast diversity of operators and the binary nature
475   of data flow decisions (existing or not). The efficacy of this paradigm is assessed by employing five
476
      different graph neural networks as baselines, focusing on operator and data flow design. Operator
      design presents a greater challenge than data flow design.
477
478   Global: Architecture Design From Scratch. Designing neural network architectures from scratch
479   is an open and complex challenge. Unlike neural architecture search, which limits the search
480   space to a predefined macro-architecture while optimizing micro-architectural elements for specific
481   performances, the global paradigm seeks to generate comprehensive neural network architectures
482
      incorporating detailed operator-level elements from the ground up.
483   As shown in the rightward flowchart of Figure 7, this generative process is conditioned on specific
484   properties, denoted by z in Figure 7, such as a noise that represents the architecture’s intended
485   application or required characteristics. Moreover, the architecture’s design objectives are defined
      by the goals it needs to achieve. Importantly, global paradigms can also iteratively leverage local

486
      paradigms to progressively achieve their comprehensive design objectives. To assess the potential
487   and feasibility of the global paradigm, a robust baseline is implemented for validation.

525
      In the global paradigm, the graph generation model DiGress, which employs a diffusion model
526   for graph generation, was adopted. Due to computing resource constraints and the fact that some
527   architectures in Younger have node counts reaching hundreds of thousands, only architectures with
528   node counts in the range of [1, 300] were selected for training. The DiGress model achieved a
529   negative log-likelihood of at least 345.4988 on the test set. As the global paradigm presents a highly
530   challenging task, further research in this area is planned for the future.

      5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
533
534   This article introduces Younger, a dataset of neural network architectures extracted from real-world
535   models across various public model repositories. This dataset proposes a new challenging field:
536   Artificial Intelligence-Generated Neural Network Architecture (AIGNNA). Two critical challenges
537   regarding neural network architecture design are introduced within this field: the Global Design
538   Paradigm and the Local Design Paradigm. Preliminary experiments have demonstrated the potential
539   and effectiveness of Younger’s neural architecture design in this emerging field, encouraging more
      researchers to explore this promising.

      A.2     L OCAL O PERATOR D ESIGN
881
      A.2.1     DATASET S PLITS
882
883    Due to the lack of relevant research on extracting building blocks for neural network architecture.
884   Therefore, we performed community detection on all DAGs (Neural Network Architecture) in the
885   ‘Filter’ dataset to extract the building blocks of the neural network architecture. Through community
886    detection, we can identify the closely connected node sets in the graph to help identify subsets of
887    nodes with specific correlations or functional associations. Although there is no evidence to suggest
888
       that the subgraphs extracted by community detection are effective building blocks for neural network
       architecture, in this paper, it is reasonable to use this method to extract subgraphs for preliminary
889
      validation to test the feasibility of Local Operator Design.
890
891   We adopt the Clauset Newman Moore Grey modularity maximization method (Clauset et al., 2004)
892   as the community detection algorithm and set it to detect at least one community, the DAG itself. For
893   each community, we simultaneously query its node boundary and label it as the node to be predicted.
894
      The community and node boundary form a new subgraph, and the definition of node boundary is
      shown in Formula A.2.1.
895
896                                  B = {v|v ∈ D − C, u ∈ C, (u, v) ∈ E},                                 (5)
897
      where D, C, and E represent the node set of DAG and the node set of community and edge set of
898
      DAG, respectively, and (u, v) indicates two directed edges < u, v > and < v, u >.
899
900   Finally, we will deduplicate the subgraphs formed by all community and node boundary pairs, i.e.,
901   remove isomorphic subgraphs. Finally, 38,803 and 29,581 non-isomorphic subgraphs were obtained
902   under the configurations of ‘Operator w/ Attributes’ and ‘Operator w/o Attributes’, respectively.
903
      To obtain the final training, validation, and test sets, we split all non-isomorphic subgraphs in an
      8:1:1 ratio. Specifically, under the ‘Operator w/ Attribute’ configuration, the training, validation, and
904
      testing sets contain 31,282, 3,769, and 3,752 subgraphs, respectively, while under the ‘Operator w/o
905
      Attribute’ configuration, they include 23,775, 2,907 and 2,899 subgraphs, respectively.

1014   A.2.6     R ESULTS AND A NALYSIS
1015
1016   We set configuration as stated in Section A.2.3. Baseline models, including GCN, GAT, GAE, VGAE,
1017
       and SAGE, were used under the operator design paradigm. As shown in Table 11, all baseline
       models achieve high accuracy but perform poorly in other metrics. The reason can be attributed
1018
       to the complexity of Younger and further to the complexity of the neural network architectures
1019
       in the real world. Another reason is that some typical types of operators appear more frequently
1020   while others appear less frequently, causing the model to be biased toward predicting the majority of
1021   operators. It can be seen that all baseline models in experiments w/o attributes achieve higher F1,
1022   Precision, and Recall compared to those in experiments w/ attributes. This indicates that reducing the
1023   variety of operators and making their distribution more uniform can improve the multi-classification
1024   performance. In addition, among these baseline models, SAGE performs excellently on almost all
1025   metrics. Notice that GAT lacks experiments with Operator w/ Attributes due to excessively large
       parameter counts as shown in Table 9, resulting in out-of-memory issues during execution.
