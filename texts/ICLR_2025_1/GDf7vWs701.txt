011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            With the proliferation of social media, influencer marketing has emerged as a pop-
014            ular strategy for brands to promote their products. Recent studies have increas-
015            ingly explored the use of machine learning to recommend suitable influencers for
016
               brands. This typically involves analyzing the compatibility of influencer profiles
               with brand attributes. However, for brands entering new markets or promoting
017
               products in unfamiliar categories, existing solutions may be limited due to insuf-
018
               ficient information for accurate compatibility matching.
019
               In this paper, we propose ProdInfluencerNet (PIN), a product-centric framework
020
               designed for influencer recommendation. PIN effectively models the complex re-
021            lationships between brands, products, and influencers using Heterogeneous Infor-
022            mation Networks (HINs). We categorize sponsored post images using the Google
023            Taxonomy through image classification techniques. By leveraging the taxonomy’s
024            hierarchical structure and adopting an inductive learning approach, PIN can accu-
025            rately recommend influencers for brands, even in new markets or with innovative
026            products. We validate PIN’s effectiveness and superiority over existing methods
027            using two Instagram datasets. Furthermore, our analysis reveals that text features
028            in profiles are more critical than images for identifying cooperative relationships
029
               between product categories and influencers.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Influencer marketing has emerged as a dominant force in modern marketing strategies Campbell &
034
      Farrell (2020). It leverages the trust and authenticity that influencers have built with their audiences
035
      to promote products or services, often through social media platforms like Instagram.
036
037   Existing research on influencer recommendation has predominantly focused on analyzing influencer
038   and brand profiles to identify potential matches (Gan et al., 2019; Elwood et al., 2021; Kim et al.,
039
      2023). For example, if a brand’s profile expresses the brand’s market and the product information
      they sell, and some influencer has post a number of articles related to the brand’s products, then
040
      a recommendation system would likely recommend the influencer for marketing the brand’s prod-
041
      ucts. This profile-based matching approach may face a cold-start challenge if there is not enough
042   information in the profiles: for example, when a brand is lunching a new product, or is entering
043   a new market. We observe that a typical collaboration post often features the target product being
044   promoted. By extracting the product information, we can recommend influencers for brand without
045   resorting much to the brand’s profile. In addition, by classing products into a hierarchical taxonomy,
046   every product can be attributed to some features such that, the lower category the product can be
047   classified into the hierarchy, the more detailed information we have about the product. As such, even
048   when a brand is to put some new product into the market, as long as the product can be classified
049   into the taxonomy (perhaps in a high level), we can still use the somewhat general information of
050
      the product category to find a suitable influencer to promote the product.
051   In this paper, we introduce ProdInfluencerNet (PIN), a product-centric framework designed for influ-
052   encer recommendation. PIN effectively models the complex relationships between brands, products,
053   and influencers using Heterogeneous Information Networks (HINs). We leverage image classifica-
      tion techniques to categorize sponsored post images based on the Google Taxonomy (Google, 2024).


                                                         1
054
      By adopting an inductive learning approach, PIN can effectively recommend influencers for brands,
055   even in new markets or with innovative products. We validate our framework’s performance and
056   compare it to existing benchmarks using real-world datasets collected from Instagram.

060
061   2.1   OVERVIEW OF I NFLUENCER M ARKETING
062
063   An increasing amount of research has emerged to explore various techniques to enhance the effec-
064   tiveness of influencer campaigns. These studies can be categorized into several subdomains:
065
066   Detecting undisclosed sponsorships Detecting sponsorships in influencer marketing is crucial
067   for maintaining transparency and trust (Villegas et al., 2023). Prior work has explored detecting
068   undisclosed sponsorships using multimodal approaches on both Instagram (Kim et al., 2021) and X
069   (formerly Twitter) (Villegas et al., 2023). Our experiments build upon the Instagram dataset of Kim
070
      et al. (2021), focusing on posts with confirmed sponsorships.
071
072   Predicting post popularity Forecasting post popularity is crucial for effective influencer market-
073   ing. Prior research has explored predicting popularity using features like image, text, and video on
074
      Instagram (Gayberi & Oguducu, 2019). Additionally, modeling user interactions on platforms like
      Sina Weibo has also been used to predict content popularity (Cao et al., 2020).
075
076
077   Account and content classification Analyzing influencer content and style helps brands align
078   with suitable influencers. Previous research has classified influencers based on textual content
079
      (Nebot et al., 2018) or a combination of text and image features (Kim et al., 2020). Building on
      this, we leverage product-focused categorization in our work, using the cover image of commercial
080
      posts to identify the specific product being promoted.
081
082
083
      Influencer Recommendation Finding the right influencers for a brand is a complex topic that
      goes beyond just profiling them. Some research delves into specific tiers for more in-depth analysis
084
      (Gan et al., 2019; Elwood et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022), while others identify influencers whose
085
      target audience aligns with the brand’s desired audience to maximize the impact of their campaigns
086   (Farseev et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022). Since influencer recommendation is central to this research,
087   we will next provide an overview of the existing literature on this topic.

      2.2   M ACHINE L EARNING IN I NFLUENCER R ECOMMENDATIONS
090
091
      Most research in influencer recommendation follows the general workflow illustrated in Figure 1.
092   It begins with collecting commercial posts and account profiles from both influencers and brands.
093   Next, features are extracted from both the post content and account profiles. These features are
094   then fused into a multimodal representation, which is used to train a ranking model. The ultimate
095   goal is to generate an accurate influencer ranking list for brands. While the overall process is sim-
096   ilar, systems differ primarily in how they analyze influencer/brand features and design the model
097   architecture.

      Researchers have explored various approaches to influencer recommendation, utilizing data from
109   platforms like Instagram and X. For example, Farseev et al. (2018) employed psychographic user
110   profiling, including demographics, MBTI (Schweiger, 1985), and emotion detection. However, their
111   closed-source nature limits reproducibility. Gan et al. (2019) focused on micro-influencer ranking
112   using multimodal embeddings and ListNet(Cao et al., 2007). Their dataset, however, is limited to
113   micro-influencers. Elwood et al. (2021) built upon Gan et al.’ work Gan et al. (2019), incorporating
114   Tok2Vec (Honnibal et al., 2020) and VGG-16 (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014) for feature extraction.
115   Wang et al. (2022) proposed MORNING, a micro-influencer ranking framework incorporating target
116   audience and cooperation preferences.
117   Graph-based approaches have also been employed due to their ability to capture complex relation-
118   ships. Kim et al. (2023) integrated various entities into a heterogeneous network, utilizing GCN
119   encoder (Kipf & Welling, 2016) for node embeddings. However, GCN’s reliance on a fixed graph
120   limits its applicability to dynamic social media environments. Park et al. (2024) introduced GNN-
121   IR, employing pre-trained models for feature extraction and GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017) for
122   link prediction. While GraphSAGE can theoretically handle unseen nodes, its real-world application
123
      remains untested. Our PIN experiment aims to address this gap and use GNN-IR as benchmark.

142   Heterogeneous Information Network (HIN) is an abstraction of the real world, connecting different
143   types of nodes through a network and emphasizing interactions between various entities (Sun &
144   Han, 2013). Figure 2(a) illustrates a heterogeneous information network schema of movie recom-
145   mendation (Yu et al., 2014), which links movies with actors, genres, and directors, as well as users
146   that have given feedback before. Inspired by this, we propose a network structure for influencer
147   recommendation in Figure 2(b). According to the schema, if two brands offer the same product
148
      category, they can leverage the network structure to share information about previously collaborated
      influencers. This expands the pool of potential influencer candidates, providing brands with a wider
149
      range of options to consider.

153
154
      A heterogeneous information network G = (V, E) consists of a set of nodes V and a set of edges
      E between nodes. Nodes can be further categorized into three types: influencer, product category,
155
      and brand. Given a set of brands’ social media accounts B = {B1 , B2 , ..., Bm } and influencers’
156
      accounts K = {K1 , K2 , ..., Km }, we extract the set of products P = {P1 , P2 , ..., Pm } launched by
157   the brands and promoted by the influencers.
158
159   Each type of node in the network has distinct features, represented as XK , XB and XP for influ-
160
      encers, brands, and products, respectively. The combined feature representation can be expressed
      as:

205   Before delving into our framework, we first outline our methodology for defining products within
206   collaboration posts. We adopt Google Taxonomy, a hierarchical classification system, where prod-
207   ucts are typically categorized across 4-7 levels. Figure 4 illustrates examples of Google’s taxonomy
208   for some eye makeup products. Google Taxonomy’s fine-grained detail enables us to identify spe-
209   cific products rather than just broader categories. For instance, within the Eye Makeup category,
210   the last-level product categories are further classified into Eye shadow, Eye Primer, and Eyebrow
211   Enhancers.
212   All products are assigned a corresponding product category from Google’s product taxonomy. As
213   previously mentioned, we also aim to address scenarios where manufacturers introduce new product
214   types to the market. In such cases, lower-level taxonomy classes can be combined with product
215   descriptions to form new product category nodes. By simply adding an edge linking the brand to
      this new node, the graph can aggregate its neighborhood and proceed with subsequent predictions.

      3.2.3    DATA P IPELINE
217
218   Our data pipeline can further be described in three part, with two involved in using the images and
219   captions of posts.
220
221   Zero-shot classification of product categories We first classified the cover image of each post
222   into Google Taxonomy. To avoid manually labeling product categories for each post, we opted for
223   zero-shot classification. Specifically, we employed Sigmoid Loss for Language Image Pre-Training
224
      (SigLIP) (Zhai et al., 2023), a model that has demonstrated superior performance compared to state-
      of-the-art models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and OpenCLIP (Ilharco et al., 2021) in both
225
      zero-shot classification and zero-shot retrieval tasks. SigLIP’s advantage lies in its use of a sigmoid
226
      loss function, which operates directly on image-text pairs without requiring global normalization
227   of pairwise similarities. This characteristic makes SigLIP a well-suited tool for our classification
228   needs. By simply inputting the complete list of classes from Google Taxonomy, we can leverage
229   SigLIP to obtain the most relevant taxonomy class for each post image.
230
231   Image embedding generation for influencer features We utilize Vision Transformers (ViTs)
232   (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) as the tool of our image feature extraction task. ViTs have emerged as a
233   powerful alternative to traditional convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for image feature extrac-
234   tion. Images in social media datasets are often difficult to collect due to their large size. Therefore,
235   we aim to investigate whether incorporating image features, in addition to existing influencer meta-
236
      data (followers, categories, biography, etc.), can improve prediction accuracy.
237
      Word2vec generation based on post caption We now shift our focus to converting post captions
238
      into text features. After SigLIP classification, each post is associated with a product category based
239
      on its cover image. We then aggregate all posts related to each influencer, brand, and product
240   category, respectively, into a single document. This means that each document is composed of all
241   the post captions associated with that entity. Utilizing BertTopic (Grootendorst, 2022), we obtain
242   the TF-IDF representation of the document, and further convert them into text embeddings, serving
243   as features for the respective entity. BertTopic is suited for our scenario due to its ability to discover
244   latent topics in a corpus of documents without requiring prior knowledge or labeling. This allows
245   us to quickly extract key points about each entity.
246   As illustrated in the lower right corner of Figure 3 (a), each type of nodes comprises distinct feature
247   sets. Influencer nodes consist of text and image embeddings, as well as attributes extracted from
248   their account profiles. Product category nodes consist of text embeddings and their corresponding
249   class encoding. Finally, due to the absence of images in the brands’ data, brand nodes contain only
250   text embeddings and information from their account profiles. These node data was utilized in the
251   subsequent graph construction process.

      3.3   G RAPH -BASED E MBEDDING FOR L INK P REDICTION AND R ECOMMENDATION
295
296   As illustrated in Figure 3 (b), the workflow of PIN is divided into the following two main stages:
297
298
299   Graph Construction and Embedding Aggregation The data used for graph construction can
300
      be split into two categories: data for inductive learning and data for transductive learning. In the
      transductive learning, all the edges connecting product categories and influencers are divided into
301
      training, validation, and testing sets. Consequently, all nodes are included in the training phase,
302
      making it unfeasible for scenarios with unseen nodes.
303
304   In contrast, in the inductive learning paradigm, we first divide the product category nodes into train-
305   ing, validation, and test sets based on predetermined proportions. Then, we obtain edges and their
306
      connected influencer/brand nodes linked to the product nodes. The inductive learning ensures that
      the model doesn’t access validation or test data during training, forcing it to learn general patterns
307
      from the available features and graph structure. This approach enables the model to accommodate
308
      unseen product category nodes, thereby addressing the cold-start problem we aim to solve.
309

311   GraphSAGE Convolutional Layer, employing the Mean Aggregation Function in Eq. (2) to generate

345
346   Influencer and Brand (I&B) Dataset Kim et al. (2021)’s dataset includes details of 38,113 in-
347   fluencers, 26,910 brands, and over 1.6 million posts where influencers tagged brands on Instagram.
348   Each entry contains an influencer’s account, the name of the JSON file storing the post content, a
349   list of image file names associated with the post, and a sponsorship label. The sponsorship label is
350   used to identify whether a post is a commercial collaboration.
351   Since our study focuses on commercial collaborations, we neglect posts without sponsorship rela-
352   tionships. This initial filtering leaves over 180,000 posts for further analysis. To enhance the quality
353   of model training and mitigate noise, we exclude influencers with insufficient data (fewer than ten
354   collaborative posts) from the dataset. After data cleaning, our study utilizes a total of 3,281 influ-
355   encers with 14,801 brands and their corresponding 70,417 collaborative posts for the experiment.
356   After applying Google Taxonomy classification, the dataset encompasses 2,356 product categories,
357
      resulting in the generation of 45,792 Brand-launch-ProductCategory edges and 47,944 Influencer-
      promote-ProductCategory edges.

376
377   The experiment was set up in Python 3.9.19 and utillized PyTorch Geometric 2.5.3. Regarding
      hardware, a RTX 3090 GPU with CUDA version 12.2 was employed. The model configuration


                                                         7
378
      was based on GraphSAGE as the backbone architecture, with 3 convolutional layers and a hidden
379   channel size of 128. The optimization process used the Adam optimizer and a binary cross-entropy
380   loss function, with a batch size of 1024.
381
382
      The data was split into three parts for training, validation, and testing, with proportions of 80%, 10%,
      and 10%, respectively. Additionally, negative link sampling was performed at a 1:1 ratio relative to
383
      positive links.
384
385   Our experiments and evaluation were conducted in two parts as shown below. To facilitate a direct
386   comparison with GNN-IR (Park et al., 2024), we aligned our metrics with theirs.
387
             1. Link Prediction: The goal is to predict the existence of each link within the graph struc-
388
                ture, along with the associated probability of the link’s existence. We employed ROC AUC,
389
                Precision, Recall and F1-Score to assess the performance of our link prediction tasks.
390
391          2. Recommendation: We leveraged the link probabilities obtained from the previous part to
392
                generate recommendations. We evaluated the recommendation performance using rank-
                based metrics like Precision@K, Recall@K and F1-score@K.
393
394   Each experiment explored the following two settings, resulting in a total of six (2 * 3) experimental
395   configurations:
396
397          1. Learning Paradigm
398
                      • Transductive Learning: The model learns from the entire graph structure, including
399                     both labeled and unlabeled nodes.
400
                      • Inductive Learning: The model learns from a subset of labeled nodes and generalizes
401
                        to unseen nodes.
402
403
             2. Influencer Feature Set
404                   • Text: Use only textual features extracted from influencer profiles and posts.
405                   • Image: Use only visual features derived from influencer images.
406                   • Multimodal: Combine both textual and visual features for a comprehensive represen-
407                     tation of influencers.

      Next, we used the iKala dataset to further verify the robustness of PIN. In both the iKala Dataset and
487   the I&B Dataset, the overall performance of PIN was similar. PINtext with inductive learning con-
488   sistently yielded better results, achieving a recall rate close to 1. Additionally, in our experimental
489   setup using the inductive learning paradigm, the product category nodes in the testing phase include
490   nodes that were not seen during training. This indicates that the model maintains high accuracy even
491   when processing unseen product category nodes.
492
      We used inductive learning to address the cold-start scenario, as it is well-suited for launching new
493   product categories. Given its effective performance, we applied the inductive learning settings to
494   further analyze the effectiveness of different features. As can be seen from Table 1 & 3, in both
495   datasets, PINtext consistently outperforms PINmulti , which in turn outperforms PINimage . We attribute
496   this to the fact that images lack the context to fully convey an influencer’s expertise in a particular
497   domain. Text, on the other hand, provides a more comprehensive understanding of an influencer’s
498   proficiency with a specific product category. Although previous research on brand-influencer pair-
499   ings often prioritized visual style and emphasized image features (Arifianto et al., 2018; Gan et al.,
500   2019; Elwood et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2023), our product-centric research shows that text features
501
      are more effective in revealing the connection between influencers and product categories, thereby
      diminishing the importance of images in this context.
502
503   It’s important to note that these findings do not imply that influencers should avoid using images in
504   their posts. Images can enhance user experience and increase engagement, but text appears to be
505   more effective for matching product categories with influencers in our PIN framework.
506   In conclusion, the appealing performance of PIN, with roc auc consistently exceeding 0.95 across
507   diverse datasets, validates the effectiveness of a product-centric approach in influencer marketing.
508   Moreover, the framework’s overall F1 score averages around 0.7 for influencer recommendation,
509   not only exhibits a significant improvement over previous work, but also demonstrates the efficacy
510   of using heterogeneous network structures and text features for modeling influencer marketing rela-
511   tionships and identifying product-influencer connections.
512
513   5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
514
515   We proposed PIN, a product-centric framework for influencer recommendation that utilizes het-
516   erogeneous information networks (HIN) to expand the pool of potential influencers and uncover
517   non-obvious collaborations for new products. Our experimental results using two different datasets
518   confirm that the network structures can effectively model the complex relationships inherent in be-
519   tween influencers, product categories, and brands. In addition, we showed that text features are more
520   crucial than images for identifying cooperation relationship between product categories and influ-
521   encers. This helps reduce resources that are need to build the framework in real work applications.
522   In short, this research contributes a novel and effective solution for influencer marketing, enabling
523
      businesses to discover new partnership opportunities and maximize the impact of their campaigns.
524   Future research may enhance product category prediction using supervised models trained on man-
525   ually labeled data or leveraging unlabeled data from Instagram’s shopping feature. Linking product
526   categories to e-commerce descriptions could further improve text embeddings. Additionally, as
527   brand profiles become richer, incorporating brand influence analysis could offer a more compre-
528   hensive understanding of influencer marketing dynamics. Further exploration into diverse brand
529
      types and their interaction with different influencer tiers could pave the way for more sophisticated
      machine learning applications in influencer marketing.
