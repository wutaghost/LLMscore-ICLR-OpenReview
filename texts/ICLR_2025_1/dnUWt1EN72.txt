009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Estimating treatment effects can assist decision-making in various areas, such as
012            commerce and medicine. One application of the treatment effect estimation is to
013            predict the effect of an advertisement on the purchase result of a customer, known as
014            individual treatment effect (ITE). In online websites, the outcome of an individual
015            can be affected by treatments of other individuals, as people often propagate
016
               information with their friends, a phenomenon referred to as interference. Prior
               studies have attempted to model interference for accurate ITE estimation under
017
               a static network among individuals. However, the network usually changes over
018
               time in real-world applications due to complex social activities among individuals.
019            For instance, an individual can follow another individual on one day and unfollow
020            this individual afterward on an online social website. In this case, the outcomes of
021            individuals can be interfered with not only by treatments for current neighbors but
022            also by past information and treatments for past neighbors, which we refer to as
023            dynamic interference. In this work, we model dynamic interference for the first time
024            by developing an architecture to aggregate both the past information of individuals
025            and their neighbors. Specifically, our proposed method contains a mechanism
026            that summarizes historical information of individuals from previous time stamps,
027
               graph neural networks that propagate information about individuals within every
               time stamp, and a weighting mechanism that estimates the importance of different
028
               time stamps. Moreover, the model parameters should gradually change rather than
029
               drastically because information of every individual gradually changes over time.
030            To take it into account, we also propose a variant of our method to evolve the model
031            parameters over time with long short-term memory. In our experiments on multiple
032            datasets with dynamic interference, our methods outperform existing methods for
033            ITE estimation because they are unable to capture dynamic interference. This result
034            corroborates the importance of dynamic interference modeling.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Treatment effect estimation has been applied to decision-making in various areas, such as
      medicine (Ma et al., 2022a; Schnitzer, 2022; Chang et al., 2023) and commerce (Nabi et al., 2022;
040
      Ellickson et al., 2023; Waisman et al., 2024). For instance, estimating treatment effects can help busi-
041
      ness owners understand whether an advertisement encourages customers to purchase the advertised
042   item. Then, owners can make informed decisions about whether to proceed with the promotion based
043   on the treatment effect estimation results. The individual treatment effect (ITE) quantifies the relative
044   change of an individual outcome with/without treatment.
045
      Our goal is to estimate treatment effects from observational graph data under a dynamic environment.
046
      In this scenario, data typically contains covariates of individuals, a network among individuals,
047
      treatments, and outcomes, all of which change over time. This gives rise to dynamic ITE. Estimating
048   dynamic ITE enables us to deeply understand how the effects of a treatment change over time for
049   individuals, which can result in more reasonable decisions. In a graph, an individual outcome can
050   be influenced by treatments assigned to its neighboring individuals, a phenomenon referred to as
051   interference (Ma & Tresp, 2021). A dynamic network may result in more complex interference among
052   individuals than a static network. For instance, a user can follow another user and unfollow this user
053   afterward in an online social application. In this case, an individual possibly receives interference
      from his/her own past treatment, neighbors in the past time stamps, and even from individuals never

078   To capture dynamic interference, we need to overcome the following challenges: (I) Historical
079   information aggregation. Each time stamp often contributes interference differently. This introduces
080   challenges for aggregating historical information. (II) Within-time interference modeling under a
081   dynamic environment. At every time stamp, individuals can have different covariates (e.g., age),
082   receive new treatments, and dynamic networks among individuals, all of which result in different
083
      within-time interference at different time stamps. (III) Cross-time interference modeling. As shown
      in Figure 1, cross-time interference can occur among Users 1 and 3 who are not neighboring at any
084
      time stamp, which introduces a significant challenge in capturing cross-time interference. Previous
085
      approaches fail to model dynamic interference, as they cannot overcome challenges (I), (II), and (III)
086   jointly. Existing studies to ITE estimation capture within-time interference only (Ma & Tresp, 2021;
087   Jiang et al., 2023), or model interference in panel data that have dynamic treatments only and thus
088   are incapable of handling dynamic graphs (Wang, 2021), or estimate ITE from dynamic graphs but
089   do not model interference among individuals (Ma et al., 2021).
090
      To overcome these challenges, we propose a novel approach: Dynamic Interference modeling
091
      for estimating Treatment Effects from dynamic graph (DITE). The key idea of our approach is
092   to model the propagation of dynamic interference across time and individuals. To this end, we
093   design a dynamic interference modeling (DIM) layer. Specifically, every DIM layer contains a
094   mechanism that aggregates historical information. To overcome the challenge (I), DIM first uses
095   a weighting mechanism to estimate the importance of different time stamps, inspired by attention
096   mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017). Subsequently, the DIM layer summarizes historical information
097   weighted by the estimated importance of different time stamps. Next, DIM applies single-layered
098   GNNs (Welling & Kipf, 2016) to propagate information among individuals within every time stamp,
099   which captures within-time interference at every time stamp and overcome the challenge (II). By
100
      stacking DIM layers, we can overcome the challenge (III) and capture dynamic interference received
      by every individual. Moreover, as the covariates and treatments usually change gradually over time,
101
      model parameters should also change gradually over time. To take it into account, we propose a
102
      variant of DITE, which uses long short-term memory (LSTM) (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997)
103   to evolve the model parameters of DITE. Results on extensive experiments in Section 5 reveal the
104   powerful ability of the proposed methods in estimating ITE with dynamic interference.
105
106

107    • We formalize the problem of ITE estimation with dynamic interference, which is a novel and
         challenging issue.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
116
      ITE estimation without interference. Many existing methods for ITE estimation, such as balancing
117
      neural network (BNN) (Johansson et al., 2016), deep treatment-adaptive architecture (DTANet) (Li
118
      et al., 2022), and counterfactual regression (CFR) (Shalit et al., 2017) assume that there is no
119   interference among individuals. This assumption hardly holds for real-world data, as individuals
120   in real-world data are usually connected, such as Flicker (Wang et al., 2013) and BlogCatalog
121   datasets (Li et al., 2015), and often propagate information with neighboring individuals. Some studies
122   consider estimating ITE from graph data (Guo et al., 2020; Chu et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2021). However,
123   these methods make use of graphs to discover latent information but still do not take interference into
124   account. For a more thorough literature review on this line, confer the survey paper (Yao et al., 2021).
125   ITE estimation with interference under static environments. The earliest studies introduced group-
126   level interference (Hudgens & Halloran, 2008; Tchetgen & VanderWeele, 2012; Liu & Hudgens,
127   2014). They split individuals into several subgroups and consider interference only within subgroups.
128   Group-level interference may not apply to real-world situations, as interference can occur among
129   individuals in different subgroups. The follow-up studies model interference, by assuming that
130   interference exists among some close neighbors (Aronow & Samii, 2017; Rakesh et al., 2018; Viviano,
131   2019; Forastiere et al., 2021; Jiang & Sun, 2022). In real-world data, interference can propagate
132   widely over a graph not only close neighbors. To address this issue, networked interference (Ma
133
      & Tresp, 2021; Forastiere et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2023; Sui et al., 2024) is assumed to consider
      interference propagation over an entire graph. GNN-based estimators (Ma & Tresp, 2021) were
134
      proposed to capture networked interference through the propagation mechanism of GNNs (Welling
135
      & Kipf, 2016). As the structure of a graph may be hidden due to privacy preservation (Sävje et al.,
136   2021; Cortez et al., 2022), a subsequent study (Bhattacharya et al., 2020) construct graph structures
137   for interference modeling and treatment effect estimation. To estimate ITE from more convoluted
138   graphs, HyperSci (Ma et al., 2022b) is proposed to capture joint interference of multiple individuals
139   to an individual on hypergraphs using a hypergraph-GCN method (Bai et al., 2021), and HINITE (Lin
140   et al., 2023) is proposed to model interference propagation on heterogeneous graphs. However, these
141   methods model within-time interference under a static environment only.
142   ITE estimation under dynamic environments. Wang (2021) considers interference from past
143   time in panel data that have dynamic treatments only, whereas dynamic graph data has dynamic
144   covariates of individuals and a dynamic topological structure among individuals. (Zhu et al., 2024)
145   considers dynamic networks only, but static covariates and treatments, whereas we focus on dynamic
146   graphs where both covariates and treatments are dynamic. DNDC (Ma et al., 2021) and NEAT (Ma
147   et al., 2023) estimate ITE from a dynamic graph but does not model interference. CF-GODE (Jiang
148   et al., 2023) considers interference under a dynamic environment and assumes that the treatment and
149   covariates of individuals can change, whereas the graph structure of individuals remains unchanged
150
      over time. Moreover, CF-GODE assumes that interference exists among immediate neighbors at
      the same time stamp, i.e., one-hop within-time interference only. However, real-world data can
151
      often change its graph structure as seen in PeerRead dataset (Kang et al., 2018), and there often
152
      exists cross-time and dynamic interference due to complex social activities, as shown in Figure 1.
153   To overcome this issue, our work relaxes the restriction on graph structures and models networked
154   interference under a dynamic environment that contains dynamic covariates, treatments, and networks.

258   4.1     DYNAMIC I NTERFERENCE M ODELING

260   To capture dynamic interference, it is essential to properly model the propagation of within-time
261
      and cross-time interference. To this end, we propose a dynamic interference modeling (DIM)
      layer, as illustrated in Figure 2b. A DIM layer contains a historical aggregation (HA) mechanism
262
      that aggregates all historical interference representations for every individual and generates new
263
      representations of individuals. As each time stamp contributes interference and outcome differently,
264   the HA mechanism first applies a mechanism that automatically estimates the importance. This
265   mechanism is inspired by the attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2019), so we
266   refer to it as time-attention (abbreviated as T-Attention) mechanism. Subsequently, HA mechanism
267   aggregates historical information by summarizing historical interference representations weighted
268   by the learned importance. Next, the DIM applies a within-time aggregation (WA) mechanism
269   that aggregates information for every individual within every time stamp using t single-layered
      GNNs (Welling & Kipf, 2016), which take the outputs of the HA mechanism as inputs and generate

328   4.2    A DDRESSING C ONFOUNDERS
329
330   To mitigate the confounding bias, we use an existing method (Shalit et al., 2017) that maps the
331   covariates into a representation space where the distributions of representation of covariates in
332   different groups are aligned well (Johansson et al., 2016; Shalit et al., 2017). To this end, we also
333   minimize MMD between representation distributions of covariates in control and treated groups at
334
      every time stamp.
335   To be specific, ϕt is a feed-forward network at a time stamp t, which takes covariates Xt as input
336   and outputs representations of covariates Ut = ϕt (Xt ). Let Mϕ be the number of layers of ϕt for
337   every time stamp, and uti be the representation of the covariates of the individual i at the time stamp t.
338   Similar to representation balancing for interference representation, we minimize MMD between the
339   representation distributions of different treatment groups to mitigate confounding bias (Shalit et al.,
340
      2017). Let MMDtϕ be estimated MMD of covariate representations in different treatment groups at t.
341

505   5.2   E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
506
      Performance of treatment effect estimation. To investigate the answer to RQ 1, we conducted
507
      experiments and compared the proposed methods with the baseline methods on all datasets. Table 1
508   lists the results of all methods for ITE and ATE estimation on the test sets of all datasets. It can be
509   seen that the proposed DITE and DITEev outperform all baseline methods in ITE estimation with
510   significant performance gaps. In ATE estimation, the proposed methods also outperform all baseline
511   methods on the Blog and PeerRead datasets and achieve a close performance as BNN, which gets the
512   best performance in ATE estimation on the Flickr dataset. These results reveal the powerful ability of
513   the proposed methods to capture dynamic interference.
514   Ablation experiments. To investigate the answer to RQ 2, we conducted ablation experiments.
515   There are two variants of the proposed methods: DITEP and DITEPR . DITEP applies DITE to the
516   projection graph APro , and DITEPR replaces the DIM layers with general GCN layers and applies it
517   to the projection graph APro (as detailed in the description for the baseline method GCN-TE+APro ).
518   Results of ablation experiments are shown in Figure 3 and Appendix F for the PeerRead dataset.
519   These results present significant performance gaps between the DITE/DITEev and DITEP /DITEPR ,
520   which indicate the importance of the historical information and DIM layers.
521   Sensitivity analysis. To investigate the answer to RQ 3, we conducted experiments with different
522   values of α with the range {0.01, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.5, 1.0} on the Flickr and PeerRead datasets. Results
523   are presented in Figure 4. The results show that no significant changes in performance were observed.
524   This indicates that the proposed methods are not particularly sensitive to the value of α.
525
      A closer look at dynamic interference. To investigate the answer to RQ 4, we conducted experiments
526   with different tmax on the Flickr dataset, which provides a closer look at dynamic interference. A
527   larger tmax results in more severe dynamic interference among individuals than a small tmax . The
528   results are shown in Figure 5. Results show that the performance gap between these baseline methods
529   and the proposed methods with tmax = 1 is small, as there is only within-time interference, no
530   dynamic interference. With tmax increase, the performance gap in ITE estimation becomes significant,
531   and the performance of proposed methods achieves better than that of baseline methods in ATE
532   estimation. This indicates the efficacy of capturing dynamic interference of the proposed methods.
533   Results of additional experiments with another outcome simulation are shown in Appendix H.

782   A    I DENTIFIABILITY OF ITE
783
784   To identify ITE under interference, a positivity assumption is introduced for neighbor interference in
785   the literature (Jiang & Sun, 2022). However, it is not enough for dynamic interference. We extend
786   the positivity assumption (Jiang & Sun, 2022) to dynamic interference, as follows:
787
      Assumption 4 The probability of an individual with dynamic interference to receive treatment or
788   not is always positive, 0 < P (τ t | xti , sti ) < 1, ∀xti , ∀ sti .
789
790   This tells the treatment is probabilistic regardless of covariates and dynamic interference received by
791   an individual. Under the set of assumptions, the expected ITE is identifiable, which can be derived as
792   follows:
793                    E[Yit (T t = 1, S = sti ) − Yit (T t = 0, S = sti ) | Xit = xti ]
794                = E[Yit (T t = 1, S = sti ) | Xit = xti ] − E[Yit (T t = 0, S = sti ) | Xit = xti ]
795
                   (a)
796                = E[Yit (τ t = 1, S = sti ) | Xit = xti , Tit = 1, Sit = sti ]−
797                      E[Yit (τ t = 0, S = sti ) | Xit = xti , Tit = 0, Sit = sti ]
798                (b)
799                = E[Yit | Xit = xti , Tit = 1, Sit = sti ] − E[Yit | Xit = xti , Tit = 0, Sit = sti ],
800   where (a) is based on the assumption 3, (b) is based on the assumptions 1, 2, and 4. This suggests
801   that once we properly capture sti , we can recover the ITE.

1038   J      D ISCUSSION
1039
1040   In our experimental setting, we consider a transductive setting for properly modeling dynamic
1041   interference, i.e., information on all networks and individuals is given during training, which follows
1042   the existing study (Ma & Tresp, 2021). We now discuss the scenario when complete information on
1043   the dynamic networks and covariates of all individuals is not given. As historical information ( < t
1044   ) is typically available at a time stamp t, this complex scenario can be simplified to one when the
1045   information on the network and individuals is partially accessible at the time stamp t. In this case, we
1046   can mask the covariates of unobserved individuals and hide the edges connecting to these unobserved
1047
       individuals in the graphs. Here, the performance of the model usually decreases and depends on how
       much information about individuals and the network is provided.
1048
1049   To properly model dynamic interference, the WA mechanism of the proposed methods is applied
1050   to aggregate information from all neighbors of individuals. This might be time-consuming when
1051   the input graphs are large. To take this issue into account, we also provide two strategies that can
1052   accelerate the WA mechanism, as follows:
1053       • Splitting the graph into some subgraphs. Then, training our methods on each subgraph.
1054       • Applying some sampling mechanisms, such as the neighbor sampling mechanism of Graph-
1055         SAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017), to the WA mechanism.
1056   However, there is usually a trade-off between performance and efficiency. Performance of the model
1057   may decrease when applying such accelerating technologies.

1060   K       L IMITATION
1061
1062   For simplicity and focus on capturing dynamic interference, we use a strong unconfoundedness
1063   assumption, i.e., Assumption 3 for confounders. This assumption holds when there are no hidden
1064   confounders. In other words, all confounders need to be observed from covariates. However, there
1065
       might exist hidden confounders, which is another popular topic for ITE estimation (Guo et al., 2020;
       Ma et al., 2021). Thus, if there exist hidden confounders, the component of addressing confounders
1066
       of DITE/DITEev can only capture the observed confounders, and omit hidden confounders, which
1067
       may result in bias in ITE/ATE estimation. Many existing works (Chu et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2020;
