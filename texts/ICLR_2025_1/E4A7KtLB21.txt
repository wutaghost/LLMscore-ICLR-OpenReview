009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The importance of attribution algorithms in the AI field lies in enhancing model
012            transparency, diagnosing and improving models, ensuring fairness, and increasing
013            user understanding. Gradient-based attribution methods have become the most
014            critical because of their high computational efficiency, continuity, wide applica-
015            bility, and flexibility. However, current gradient-based attribution algorithms re-
016            quire the introduction of additional class information to interpret model decisions,
017            which can lead to issues of information ignorance and extra information. Infor-
018
               mation ignorance can obscure important features relevant to the current model
               decision, while extra information introduces irrelevant data that can cause fea-
019
               ture leakage in the attribution process. To address these issues, we propose the
020
               Attribution with Intrinsic Information (AII) algorithm, which analyzes model de-
021            cisions without the need for specified class information. Additionally, to better
022            evaluate the potential of current attribution algorithms, we introduce the metrics
023            of insertion confusion and deletion confusion alongside existing mainstream met-
024            rics. To continuously advance research in the field of explainable AI (XAI), our
025            algorithm is open-sourced at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/
026            AII-787D/.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   As deep learning continues to advance rapidly, its performance in tasks such as image recogni-
032   tion (Xu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) has reached unprecedented heights. These technological
033
      breakthroughs have brought revolutionary changes to various fields, including healthcare (Sug-
      anyadevi et al., 2022), autonomous driving (Grigorescu et al., 2020), and management decision-
034
      making (Shrestha et al., 2021). However, as these fields increasingly rely on deep learning technolo-
035
      gies, the need for decision transparency has become more critical. If a model’s decision-making
036   process is not explainable, users may find it difficult to fully trust the results and to assign responsi-
037   bility in the event of an incident.
038
039
      Therefore, the research and development of Explainable AI (XAI) are of paramount importance.
      There has been extensive research in the XAI domain, with early interpretability methods such as
040
      Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017) and LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016) using heatmaps and local lin-
041
      ear models to explain the decisions of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs). However, these methods
042   have limitations in providing fine-grained and one-to-one explanations for each input feature. Con-
043   sequently, researchers have proposed more detailed attribution methods, with Integrated Gradients
044   (IG)Sundararajan et al. (2017) being one of the most significant. IG addresses the shortcomings
045   of earlier methods and introduces axioms for attribution, providing a consistent and fair framework
046   for feature importance. As research progressed, new adversarial example-based attribution methods
047   were proposed, such as Adversarial Gradient Integration (AGI)Pan et al. (2021), MFABA (Zhu et al.,
048   2024), and AttExplore (Zhu et al., 2023).
049   We note that existing attribution methods typically select specific class outputs or cross-entropy as
050   the loss function and use backpropagation to obtain gradients concerning input samples to guide the
051   attribution algorithm. We have identified two phenomena that will cause attribution bias due to such
052   gradient selection: information ignorance and extra information. Information ignorance refers
053   to the omission of important features from classes not directly related to the model’s final
      decision, while extra information involves the incorrect identification of irrelevant features

054
      as significant. Information ignorance leads to interpretability methods overlooking many features
055   crucial to the model’s current decision and failing to explain low-confidence situations (applicable
056   under any non-100% confidence conditions). Extra information results in feature leakage (Shah
057   et al., 2021), where features not contributing to the model’s decision are incorrectly identified as
058   important. To address these phenomena, we propose the Attribution with Intrinsic Information (AII)
059   algorithm. In AII, we redefine the form of accumulated gradients and eliminate the need to introduce
060   class information into the gradients. Additionally, we conduct rigorous mathematical derivations to
061   ensure the validity of the AII algorithm and its adherence to attribution axioms (Sundararajan et al.,
062   2017).
063   Beyond the extra information phenomenon introduced by attribution algorithms, it can also occur
064   during the evaluation of attribution algorithms. This happens because neural networks cannot dis-
065   tinguish between feature removal behavior and the feature representation of black information. For
066   image tasks, removing features and replacing their values with zero might be interpreted by the neu-
067   ral network as introducing black information, which is unfair for tasks where black is a key feature
068   (e.g., black-and-white cat classification). Thus, we propose the fair insertion and fair deletion met-
069
      rics to avoid bias during the evaluation process. Additionally, to assess the impact of confusion, we
      introduce the KL insertion and KL deletion metrics. We summarize our contributions as follows:
070
071
             • We systematically pinpoint two phenomena that cause bias in current gradient-based attri-
072
               bution algorithms: information ignorance and extra information, which severely undermine
073            the reliability of interpretability algorithms.
074
             • To address the extra information phenomenon in the evaluation of attribution algorithms,
075
               we propose two fairer evaluation metrics.
076
077          • Based on the first contribution listed above, we design a novel gradient accumulation
078
               method and propose the AII algorithm, supported by rigorous mathematical derivations
               to ensure its stability.
079
080          • We open-source our experimental code to facilitate subsequent research and replication of
081            experiments.

083   2   R ELATED W ORK
084
085   Methods for explaining deep neural networks (DNNs) have evolved through three distinct phases:
086   local approximation and early layer-wise relevance propagation (LRP) methods (Bach et al., 2015),
087   gradient-based attribution methods, and adversarial example-based attribution methods. Local ap-
088   proximation methods, such as LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016), construct an approximate, more inter-
089   pretable model in the vicinity of specific inputs to understand the behavior of the original model.
090   LIME was the first to provide local interpretability using multiple interpretable structures near the
091   sample, although it is time-consuming and the reliance on assumptions may be inaccurate. LRP,
092   an early layer-wise relevance propagation method, analyzes sample features using the relevance of
093
      inputs, while DeepLIFT (Shrikumar et al., 2017), a general form of LRP, quantifies feature impor-
      tance by comparing input features to predefined reference points, though it is highly sensitive to the
094
      choice of reference points. DeepLIFT does not satisfy the Implementation Invariance axiom pro-
095
      posed in Integrated Gradients (IG) (Sundararajan et al., 2017), leading to different attribution results
096   for models with the same functionality. The limitations of these early methods have been discussed
097   in (Zhu et al., 2024; 2023).
098
099
      Gradient-based attribution methods leverage the gradient information during the training of neural
      networks to explain model decisions. These methods include Saliency Map (SM) (Simonyan et al.,
100
      2013), Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017), Score-CAM (Wang et al., 2020), IG (Sundararajan et al.,
101
      2017), Fast IG (FIG) (Hesse et al., 2021), Expected Gradients (EG) (Erion et al., 2021), Smooth-
102   Grad (SG) (Smilkov et al., 2017), and Guided IG (GIG) (Kapishnikov et al., 2021). SM computes
103   the gradient of input features concerning the model output to identify the most important features,
104   but it is prone to gradient saturation issues. Grad-CAM and Score-CAM use gradient information
105   from intermediate layers for explanations, though they cannot provide high-resolution, fine-grained
106   explanations. IG addresses the limitations of SM by integrating gradients along a path from the
107   baseline to the input, but it has a high computational cost, which Fast IG mitigates by improving
      numerical integration techniques to accelerate IG. EG enhances stability and consistency by averag-


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      ing gradients over multiple baselines, while SG improves the smoothness and stability of attribution
109   results by adding random noise to the input, though it may obscure important subtle features. GIG
110   combines the principles of IG and guided backpropagation to selectively backpropagate gradients,
111   enhancing interpretability but potentially overemphasizing directly related features while neglecting
112   equally important indirect features.
113
      Adversarial example-based attribution methods represent a highly effective branch of gradient-based
114
      attribution methods. These methods provide deeper explanations by generating adversarial exam-
115   ples and exploring model decision boundaries, thus avoiding the need to manually specify base-
116   line points. However, this class of methods introduces numerous intermediate states from out-of-
117   distribution (OOD) space during adversarial attacks, leading to the introduction of extra information
118   into the attribution process. Representative methods in this category include Adversarial Gradi-
119   ent Integration (AGI) (Pan et al., 2021), Boundary-based Integrated Gradients (BIG) (Wang et al.,
120   2021), AttEXplore (Zhu et al., 2023), and More Faithful and Accelerated Boundary-based Attribu-
121   tion (MFABA) (Zhu et al., 2024). AGI uses targeted adversarial attacks to explore decision bound-
122   aries and improves attribution performance through non-linear path-integrated gradients. BIG intro-
123
      duces boundary search mechanisms to optimize baseline selection, achieving more accurate feature
      attribution, but the linear integration path limits its ability to capture the non-linearity and complexity
124
      of model decisions. MFABA improves explanation accuracy and computational efficiency through
125
      second-order Taylor expansion and decision boundary exploration. AttEXplore (Zhu et al., 2023)
126   combines adversarial attacks with model parameter exploration, emphasizing transition capabilities
127   across different decision boundaries, thus enhancing the generalizability of its interpretability.
128
129
      It is noteworthy that these gradient-based attribution methods typically use the output of the max-
      imum class or cross-entropy as the loss function during gradient accumulation. This leads to the
130
      information ignorance and extra information phenomena discussed in the following sections, which
131
      significantly exacerbate the bias of attribution algorithms. We will analyze these issues in detail in
132   Section 3.2.

      3.3   E XTRA I NFORMATION IN ATTRIBUTION E VALUATION
283
284   Besides the Extra Information phenomenon in the attribution process, it also appears in the eval-
285   uation of attribution algorithms. The core idea of evaluating attribution algorithms is to insert in-
286   formation from the original image into an all-black pixel image based on the attribution score from
287   high to low, assessing how quickly the current model decision can be recovered, corresponding to
288   the Insertion score. Conversely, the Deletion score replaces the original image pixels with black
289   pixels from high to low based on the attribution score, evaluating how quickly the current model
290
      decision can be destroyed. However, this process inherently assumes that a black image represents
      ”no information.” In reality, the model cannot distinguish between feature removal and features
291
      represented by black pixels. For instance, in the task of distinguishing between black and white
292
      cats, the color of the cat’s fur is an important feature. Zeroing out the features during this process
293   makes a white cat more likely to be seen as a black cat, introducing black cat features rather than
294   removing features.
295
296

      Confusion Feature Algorithm (CFA).

368   4.4   E VALUATION M ETRICS
369
370   We used four groups of evaluation metrics to assess the performance of the attribution methods.
371
      The Insertion Score (INS) evaluates the area under the curve (AUC) as information from the original
372   image is incrementally inserted into an all-black pixel image based on the attribution score, from
373   high to low (Samek et al., 2016). This metric measures how quickly the current model decision
374   can be recovered. The Deletion (DEL) Score evaluates the AUC as pixels from the original image
375   are progressively replaced with black pixels based on the attribution score, from high to low. This
376   metric assesses how quickly the current model decision can be disrupted. Compared to the DEL,
377   the INS is generally considered more important (Pan et al., 2021). Therefore, to eliminate the
      inconsistency between these two parameters in this paper, we use the GAP metric, which is the


                                                            7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      difference between INS and DEL, for unified comparison. The results of INS and DEL are provided
379   in Appendix E.1.
380
381   We found discrepancies in the implementation of Insertion Score and Deletion Score in the open-
382
      source code of RISE (Petsiuk et al., 2018), MFABA (Zhu et al., 2024), BIG (Wang et al., 2021),
      and AGI (Pan et al., 2021). Previous work sorted the importance of each image channel separately,
383
      whereas the evaluation process should treat each input dimension equally. Therefore, we improved
384
      this by not calculating separately for each channel. We refer to these improved metrics as the Unified
385   Insertion Score (U-INS) and Unified Deletion Score (U-DEL).
386
387   To address the issue of extra information in the evaluation process, we propose the Fair Insertion
388
      and Fair Deletion metrics. The Fair Insertion (F-INS) metric replaces the initial all-black image in
      the Insertion score with the learned x∗ . Similarly, the Fair Deletion (F-DEL) metric replaces the
389
      zero-out operation in the Deletion score with the x∗ pixel operation. These modifications aim to
390
      provide a more accurate assessment by avoiding the introduction of extra information.
391
392   Furthermore, we introduce the KL Insertion (KL-INS) and KL Deletion (KL-DEL) metrics to eval-
393   uate the change in model decision uncertainty during the attribution process. KL Insertion replaces
394
      the current class output probability with the KL divergence KL(Q, P (x)) and calculates the AUC
      of the KL curve. A smaller area indicates that the important features identified by the attribution
395
      method quickly reduce the model’s decision uncertainty. Conversely, KL Deletion calculates the
396
      AUC of the KL curve as important features are progressively removed, with a larger value indicat-
397   ing a rapid increase in model decision uncertainty. To illustrate this, we use the case where entropy
398   is maximized. For example, in a scenario with 1000 classes, if the model’s output probability for

532   5   C ONCLUSION
533
534   This paper identifies the phenomena of Information Ignorance and Extra Information, which can
535   cause attribution bias in current gradient-based attribution algorithms. We propose a novel AII attri-
536   bution algorithm that can avoid these issues and achieve accurate attributions. Additionally, we have
537   designed a more comprehensive multi-dimensional attribution evaluation method. Looking towards
538   future directions, our algorithm, like current gradient-based attribution algorithms, is primarily lim-
539   ited to visual models due to the continuity of pixels in image tasks. In future work, we will attempt
      to address the challenges of continuity in NLP tasks and apply our algorithm to NLP tasks.
