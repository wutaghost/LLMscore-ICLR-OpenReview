009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement Learning (RL) has emerged as a powerful tool for neural combina-
012            torial optimization, enabling models to learn heuristics that solve complex prob-
013            lems without requiring optimal solutions. Despite significant progress, existing
014            RL approaches face challenges such as diminishing reward signals and inefficient
015            exploration in vast combinatorial action spaces, leading to inefficient learning. In
016            this paper, we propose Preference Optimization (PO), a novel framework that
017            transforms quantitative reward signals into qualitative preference signals via sta-
018
               tistical comparison modeling, emphasizing the superiority among generated so-
               lutions. Methodologically, by reparameterizing the reward function in terms of
019
               policy probabilities and utilizing preference models like Bradley-Terry and Thur-
020
               stone, we formulate an entropy-regularized optimization objective that aligns the
021            policy directly with preferences while avoiding intractable computations. Further-
022            more, we integrate heuristic local search techniques into the fine-tuning process to
023            generate high-quality preference pairs, helping the policy escape local optima.
024            Empirical results on standard combinatorial optimization benchmarks, such as
025            the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP), the Capacitated Vehicle Routing Prob-
026            lem (CVRP) and the Flexible Flow Shop Problem (FFSP), demonstrate that our
027            method outperforms traditional RL algorithms, achieving superior sample effi-
028            ciency and solution quality. Our work offers a simple yet efficient algorithmic
029
               advancement in neural combinatorial optimization.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Combinatorial Optimization Problems (COPs) are fundamental in numerous practical applications,
034
      including route planning, circuit design, scheduling, and bioinformatics Papadimitriou & Steiglitz
035
      (1998); Cook et al. (1994); Korte et al. (2011). These problems require finding an optimal solution
036   from a finite but exponentially large set of possibilities and have been extensively studied in the
037   operations research community. While computing the exact solution is impeded by their NP-hard
038   complexity Garey & Johnson (1979), efficiently obtaining near-optimal solutions is essential from a
039   practical standpoint.
040
      Deep learning, encompassing supervised learning and reinforcement learning (RL), has shown great
041
      potential in tackling COPs by learning heuristics directly from data Bengio et al. (2021); Vinyals
042   et al. (2015). However, supervised learning approaches heavily rely on high-quality solutions, and
043   due to the NP-hardness of COPs, such training data may not guarantee optimality, which can lead
044   models to fit suboptimal policies. In contrast, RL has emerged as a promising alternative, achieving
045   success in areas involving COPs such as mathematical reasoning Silver et al. (2018), chip design
046   Mirhoseini et al. (2021), and discovering efficient algorithms Fawzi et al. (2022). RL leverages neu-
047   ral networks to approximate policies and interactively obtains rewards from environment, allowing
048   models to improve without requiring high-quality solutions Bello et al. (2016); Kool et al. (2019).
049   Despite its potential, applying RL to COPs presents significant challenges. Diminishing reward
050   signals: Current methods often frame the training process at the trajectory level due to the interde-
051   pendence of actions at different timesteps. As the policy improves, the differences in trajectory-level
052   reward signals between solutions diminish, leading to negligible gradients and slow convergence
053   during later training phases. Unconstrained action spaces: The vast combinatorial action spaces
      complicate efficient exploration, rendering traditional exploration techniques like entropy regular-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      ization of trajectory computationally infeasible. Additional inference time: While neural solvers
055   are efficient in inference, they often suffer from finding the near-optimal solutions. Many works
056   adopt techniques like local search as a post-processing step to further improve solutions, but this
057   incurs additional computational time during inference.
058
      To address the issue of diminishing reward signals and inefficient exploration, we propose trans-
059
      forming quantitative reward signals into qualitative preference signals, focusing on the superiority
060
      among generated solutions rather than their absolute reward values. This approach stabilizes the
061   learning process and theoretically emphasizes optimality, as preference signals are insensitive to
062   the scale of rewards. By deriving our method from an entropy-regularized objective, we inherently
063   promote efficient exploration within the vast combinatorial action spaces of COPs, overcoming the
064   computational intractability associated with traditional entropy regularization techniques. Addition-
065   ally, to mitigate the extra inference time induced by local search, we integrate such techniques into
066   the fine-tuning process rather than using them as post-processing steps, which enables the policy to
067   learn from improved solutions without incurring additional inference time.
068   Furthermore, preference-based optimization has recently gained prominence through its application
069   in Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) for large language models Christiano
070   et al. (2017); Rafailov et al. (2024); Meng et al. (2024). Inspired by these advancements, we in-
071   troduce a novel update scheme that bridges preference optimization with COPs, leading to a more
072   effective and consistent learning process. In this work, we propose a novel algorithm named Pref-
073   erence Optimization (PO), which can seamlessly substitute conventional policy gradient methods in
074   many contexts. In summary, our contributions are:
075
076         1. A Novel Preference-Based Framework: We introduce a new framework that transforms
077
               quantitative reward signals into qualitative preference signals, stabilizing the learning pro-
               cess and theoretically emphasizing optimality independently of reward scaling.
078
079         2. An Efficient Optimization Objective: By reparameterizing the reward function in terms
080            of the policy and utilizing preference models such as Bradley-Terry and Thurstone, we
081            formulate an entropy-regularized optimization objective that aligns the policy directly with
082            preferences, avoiding intractable computations of traversing whole action space.
083         3. Integration with Appealing Solutions: We demonstrate the compatibility of our approach
084            with heuristic local search methods by incorporating them into the fine-tuning process. This
085            integration generates high-quality preference pairs and helps the policy escape local optima
086            without incurring additional inference time.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
090
091   RL-based Neural Solvers. The pioneering application of Reinforcement Learning for Combinato-
092   rial Optimization problems (RL4CO) by Bello et al. (2016); Nazari et al. (2018); Kool et al. (2019)
093
      has prompted subsequent researchers to explore various frameworks and paradigms. We classify the
      majority of RL4CO research from the following perspectives:
094
095   End-to-End Neural Solvers. Several works have focused on designing end-to-end neural solvers that
096   directly map problem instances to solutions. Techniques exploiting the inherent equivalence and
097   invariance properties of COPs have been proposed to ease the difficulty in approaching near-optimal
098   solutions Kwon et al. (2020); Kim et al. (2022); Ouyang et al. (2021); Kim et al. (2023). For exam-
099
      ple, POMO Kwon et al. (2020) utilizes multiple diverse starting points to improve training efficiency,
      while Sym-NCO Kim et al. (2022) leverages problem symmetries to enhance performance. Other
100
      studies have incorporated entropy regularization at the step level to foster exploratory behaviors,
101
      thereby improving solution diversity and quality Xin et al. (2021a); Sultana et al. (2020). Addition-
102   ally, efforts have been made to diversify the training dataset to develop more generalized solvers
103   capable of handling a wider range of problem instances Bi et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2024); Zhang
104   et al. (2022); Zhou et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2024). While most of these works aim to boost per-
105   formance through architectural innovations or learning paradigms, less attention has been given to
106   algorithmic advancements in the optimization objectives themselves. For instance, Jin et al. (2023)
107   propose a normalized reward for updating the policy, but this approach still struggles to effectively
      emphasize optimality in the solutions.


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Hybrid Solvers. Blending neural methodologies with conventional optimization techniques presents
109   a promising research direction. Such integration incorporates established heuristics like k-opt, Ant
110   Colony Optimization, Monte Carlo Tree Search, and the Lin-Kernighan algorithm, enhancing solu-
111   tion quality as demonstrated in d O Costa et al. (2020); Wu et al. (2021); Ye et al. (2023); Xin et al.
112   (2021b). For example, NeuRewriter d O Costa et al. (2020) combines neural networks with local
113   search heuristics for graph rewriting, while NeuroLKH Xin et al. (2021b) integrates deep learn-
114   ing with the LKH algorithm. While such techniques often serve as post-processing steps to refine
115   near-optimal solutions, as in Fu et al. (2021); Ma et al. (2021); Ouyang et al. (2021), the additional
116   inference time interferes with efficiency and may not be suitable for time-critical scenarios.
117   Preference-based Reinforcement Learning. Preference-based reinforcement learning (PbRL) is
118   another area related to our work, which has been widely studied in offline RL settings. PbRL in-
119   volves approximate the ground truth reward function from preference information rather than relying
120   on explicit reward signals Wirth et al. (2017). This approach is particularly useful when reward sig-
121   nals are sparse or difficult to specify. Recently, works such as Hejna & Sadigh (2024); Rafailov et al.
122   (2024); Meng et al. (2024) have proposed novel paradigms to directly improve the KL-regularized
123
      policy without the need for learning an approximate reward function, leading to more stable and
      efficient training. This has led to the development of a series of works Azar et al. (2024); Park et al.
124
      (2024); Hong et al. (2024) in the RLHF phase within language-based models, where preference
125
      information is leveraged to fine-tune large language models effectively.
126
127   Our work bridges the gap between these domains by introducing a preference-based optimization
128   framework specifically tailored for COPs. By transforming quantitative reward signals into qualita-
129
      tive preferences, we address key challenges in RL4CO, such as diminishing reward differences and
      exploration inefficiency, while avoiding the need for explicit reward function approximation as in
130
      traditional PbRL.

      3.2   P REFERENCE -BASED R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
181
      In PbRL Wirth et al. (2017), the agent optimizes a learned reward function based on an offline
182   dataset of preferences, rather than directly receiving reward signals through interaction with the
183   environment. We assume access to a preference dataset Dp = {(τ1 , τ2 , y)}, where each triplet
184   consists of two trajectories τ1 and τ2 , and a preference label y ∈ {0, 1}. Here, y = 1 if τ1 is
185   preferred over τ2 (i.e. τ1 ≻ τ2 ), and y = 0 otherwise.
186
      Preferences are considered to be generated by an underlying (latent) reward function r̂(x, τ ). Vari-
187
      ous models can be used to relate reward differences to preferences, such as the Bradley-Terry (BT)
188   model, the Thurstone model David (1963), and the Plackett-Luce (PL) model Plackett (1975). These
189   models bridge the gap between the reward function and observed preferences, allowing us to derive
190   an optimization objective to learn the reward function.
191
      In paired preference models like BT and Thurstone, a function f (·) is used to map the difference
192
      between rewards into preference probabilities. The preference probability distribution will be:

312   3.4    C OMPATIBILITY WITH L OCAL S EARCH (O PTIONAL )
313
314
      To further enhance the quality of generated solutions, we investigate the compatibility of PO with
      heuristic Local Search (LS) techniques, which are widely used to iteratively improve existing solu-
315
      tions generated by traditional or neural solvers. Local search methods have the property of mono-
316
      tonic improvement for fine-tuning existing solutions, which means that for any τ , the improved
317   solution LS(τ ) satisfies r(x, LS(τ )) ≥ r(x, τ ) through small adjustments to τ .
318
319   Typically, during evaluation, LS is applied as a post-processing step, which can introduce additional
320
      inference time due to the multiple iterations required for convergence. To maintain time efficiency
      during inference while still benefiting from the improvements provided by LS, we propose integrat-
321
      ing LS into the solvers’ training process rather than serving it as post-processing techniques.
322
323   Our proposed Preference Optimization (PO) algorithm relies on the comparison of superiority be-
      tween trajectories τ . By incorporating LS into fine-tuning, high-quality preference pairs close to


                                                                  6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      optimality can be generated. Specifically, for each solution τ generated by the neural solver, we
325   apply a small number of LS iterations to obtain an improved solution LS(τ ). In most cases, LS(τ )
326   is preferred over τ , i.e., r(x, LS(τ )) > r(x, τ ), except when LS fails to find an improved solution.
327
328
      We then form preference pairs (τ, LS(τ ), y), where y = 1[r(x,LS(τ ))>r(x,τ )] . Our policy optimization
      objective becomes:

                            θ

333   By incorporating these preference pairs into the policy optimization, higher probabilities are encour-
334   aged to assign to solutions that are improved by LS. This serves the purpose that incorporating LS
335   during training helps the neural solver escape from local optima, especially during later stages when
336   gradient updates may become less effective due to diminishing differences in reward signals.
337   It is worth noting that integrating LS introduces additional computational overhead due to the extra
338   LS iterations applied to each sampled trajectory. However, by controlling the number of LS itera-
339   tions and limiting them to a small number, the additional computational cost can be managed. This
340   trade-off is justified by the significant benefits in learning efficiency and solution quality obtained
341   through this integration. The algorithm is summarized in Algorithm 1.
342   Combining LS with the proposed PO method, we leverage the strengths of both neural solvers and
343   local search techniques. The neural solver benefits from the fine-tuning capabilities of LS, while
344   maintaining time efficiency during inference by avoiding the need for LS as a post-processing step.
345   This synergy leads to more effective learning and improved final solutions.

347   4   E XPERIMENTS
348
349   In this section, we present the main results of our experiments, demonstrating the superior perfor-
350   mance of the proposed Preference Optimization (PO) algorithm for COPs. We aim to answer the
351   following questions: 1. How does PO compare to prior RL algorithms on standard benchmarks
352   such as the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP), the Capacitated Vehicle Routing Problem (CVRP)
353   and the Flexible Flow Shop Problem (FFSP)? 2. How efficiently does PO balance exploitation and
354   exploration by considering entropy, in comparison to traditional RL methods?
355   Benchmark Setup. We implement the PO algorithm across various models, emphasizing that it is
356   a strategy optimization method not tied to a specific model structure, but rather reliant on sampling
357   multiple solutions from identical instances for qualitative comparisons. The fundamental COPs,
358   such as TSP and CVRP, serve as our testbed. In these problems, the reward model r(x, τ ) is defined
359   as the Euclidean length (Len.) of the trajectory τ . The TSP aims to find a Hamiltonian cycle on a
360   graph, minimizing the total trajectory length, while the CVRP incorporates capacity constraints for
361   vehicles and points, along with a depot as the starting point. Our main experiments utilize problems
362   with uniform distribution and 100 nodes, as prescribed in Kool et al. (2019); Kwon et al. (2020). The
363
      experiments on the FFSP are conducted to schedule tasks across multiple stages of machines with
      the objective of minimizing the makespan (MS), which refers to the total time required for complet-
364
      ing all tasks. These experiments build upon the model structure proposed by Kwon et al. (2021).
365
      Most settings in the model follow the original work, with the exception of the training objective
366   for PO. Further hyper-parameters settings can be found in the Appendix E.2. Most of experiments
367   are conducted on an NVIDIA 24G-RTX 3090 GPU and an Intel Xeon Gold 6133 CPU. Additional
368   experiments on large scale TSP with DIMES Qiu et al. (2022) are included in Appendix F.2.

      5    C ONCLUSION
529
      In this paper, we introduced Preference Optimization, a novel framework for solving COPs. By
530
      transforming quantitative reward signals into qualitative preference signals, PO addresses the chal-
531
      lenges of diminishing reward differences and inefficient exploration inherent in traditional RL ap-
532   proaches. We enhanced PO by integrating heuristic local search techniques into the fine-tuning pro-
533   cess, enabling neural solvers to generate near-optimal solutions without additional inference time.
534   Extensive experimental results demonstrate the practical viability and effectiveness of our approach,
535   achieving superior sample efficiency and solution quality compared to traditional RL algorithms.
536
      While PO shows significant promise, the stability of the reparameterized reward function across
537
      different COPs requires further investigation. Looking ahead, applying PO to optimization problems
538   where reward signals are difficult to design but preference information is readily available, such as
539   multi-objective optimization, remains a valuable direction.
