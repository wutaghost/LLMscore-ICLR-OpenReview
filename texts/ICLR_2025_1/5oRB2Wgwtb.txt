011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013             This paper is concerned with the online bandit nonlinear control, which aims to
014             learn the best stabilizing controller from a pool of stabilizing and destabilizing
015             controllers of unknown types for a given nonlinear dynamical system. We develop
016             an algorithm, named Dynamic Batch length and Adaptive learning Rate (DBAR),
017             and study its stability and regret. Unlike the existing Exp3 algorithm requiring an
018             exponentially stabilizing controller, DBAR only needs a significantly weaker notion
019
                of controller stability, in which case substantial time may be required to certify the
                system stability. Dynamic batch length in DBAR effectively addresses this issue
020
                and enables the system to attain asymptotic stability, where the algorithm behaves
021
                as if there were no destabilizing controllers. Moreover, adaptive learning rate in
022             DBAR only uses the state norm information to achieve a tight regret bound even
023             when none of the stabilizing controllers in the pool are exponentially stabilizing.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem aims to minimize the total cost of pulling a series of arms
030   while receiving immediate cost feedback for each arm pulled. Given a finite number of arms, the
031   problem balances between exploration and exploitation of arms without knowing the exact cost
032   structure of each arm. On the other hand, the online optimal control problem considers a transition
033
      dynamic xt+1 = f (xt , ut , wt ) and a set of cost functions ct (xt , ut ), t = 0, . . . , T , where the goal is
      to minimize the sum of costs over time, while both f and ct are fully or partially unknown. Basically,
034
      MAB is a special type of the online optimal control problem in the sense that MAB is stateless
035
      and simply selects an action each time, while the online control problem has a countable or an
036   uncountable number of states and selects a controller, acting as a function from states into actions,
037   each time without knowing the cost functions. Bandit algorithms can thus be leveraged for online
038   control, wherein the average cost incurred with a controller can be interpreted as the bandit feedback
039   of pulling the controller-arm (Lin et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023).
040
      In this paper, we address the online nonstochastic control problem where both a transition dynamic
041
      f and cost functions ct can be unbounded, nonlinear, and adversarially chosen. We only have
042   knowledge about xt and the bandit feedback ct (xt , ut ) at time t, with adversarial disturbances wt
043   injected at each time step as in Gradu et al. (2020) and Cassel & Koren (2020). We operate the system
044   with a single trajectory where the system state cannot be reset. To overcome the difficulties of an
045   unknown nonlinear system, we are given a finite set of N controllers in advance, where we are not
046   aware of whether each controller can stabilize the system but we are allowed to alternate between
047   these controllers within a single trajectory according to a specific logic. We refer to this problem as
048   the online bandit nonlinear control problem.
049   To deal with this online bandit nonlinear control, Li et al. (2023) adopted their Exp3-ISS algorithm,
050   which uses the well-known Exp3 algorithm (Auer et al., 2002) with a mini-batch approach (Arora
051   et al., 2012), while successively removing destabilizing controllers when detected in terms of input-
052   to-state stability (ISS). In this paper, we aim to significantly relax the requirement on the controllers
053   and yet guarantee asymptotic stability of the closed-loop system and sharpen the regret bound by
      designing our algorithm DBAR (Dynamic Batch length and Adaptive learning Rate).

064   Motivation and contribution. Our main contribution is to allow a broader class of controllers to
065   qualify as a stabilizing controller within a priori controller pool. For the motivation, consider a
066   continuous-time gradient flow in the vector space:
067                                            ẋ(t) = −∇F (x(t)),                                           (1)
068
      where F : Rn → R is a smooth function. A merely convex F can be extremely flat around its
069   minimum, leading to a slowly (asymptotically) converging trajectory unlike exponentially converging
070   behavior achieved for strongly convex F (Khalil, 2015). In fact, assuming that a minimizer x∗ of
071   F exists, the decay rate F (x(t)) − F (x∗ ) is O(1/(t log2 t)) if F is convex1 (Siegel & Wojtowytsch,
072   2023), and O(e−t ) if F is strongly convex. In the machine learning literature, a loss function
073   l(g(x), y) of a gradient-based method is often given as a convex function in g (e.g., mean-squared
074   error or cross-entropy loss), but not necessarily strongly convex since g is often over-parameterized
075   and there could be a continuum of parameters corresponding to the value of g. Analogous to this
076   concept, one can consider F as f (xt , π(xt ), wt ), a dynamic governed by a given controller π and its
077   converging behavior as a (asymptotic or exponential) controller stability. Our work merely requires
078   the existence of at least one asymptotically stabilizing controller in the pool, which is far weaker than
079
      exponentially stabilizing notions and represents a more realistic environment one may encounter.
080   The existing literature on online bandit control of linear dynamics with adversarial disturbance has
081   intrinsically assumed the existence of strongly stable controllers, which are exponentially stabilizing
082   controllers in our context, and achieves Õ(T 2/3 ) regret under general convex cost functions (Cassel
083   & Koren, 2020; Chen & Hazan, 2021; Ghai et al., 2023). In this paper, we will achieve the same
084   Õ(T 2/3 ) regret bound even when none of the stabilizing controllers are exponentially stabilizing.
085   Algorithm Design. The idea of our algorithm is two-fold:
086
087
         1. We adopt a dynamic batch length instead of a fixed length to certify the stability of the system
      without requiring exponentially stabilizing controllers and achieve both asymptotic system stability
088
      and a sublinear regret bound. The batch length is scheduled to be non-decreasing and growing
089
      unboundedly over time, but its growth amount eventually saturates. However, the strategy suffers
090   from a resulting multiplicative exponential regret in return.
091
         2. To alleviate the multiplicative exponential regret without requiring the conservative notion of
092
      exponentially stabilizing controllers, we adopt a novel adaptive learning rate scheme that relies on
093
      the system state norm, instead of a fixed learning rate. While the conventional way to apply the Exp3
094   Algorithm is to use a non-increasing learning rate, we decrease the learning rate if the state is unstable
095   and subsequently increase the learning rate if the state returns to a stable region. By implementing
096   this approach, we can alleviate the multiplicative exponential term in all cases. In particular, for a
097   specific class of stabilizing controllers beyond exponential notions, we attain a regret bound order
098   [Õ(T 2/3 ) + Õ(T −1/3 ) · exp(O(|U|))] · (|U| + 1)α , where α = 1/3 if |U| is known and α = 1/2 if
099   |U| is unknown.

      Online nonstochastic control considers a dynamical system with adversarial disturbances, which is
115   more challenging than having statistical noise. Early papers assumed full access to cost functions,
116   enabling us to leverage optimal policy structure with cost function gradients (Agarwal et al., 2019;
117   Foster & Simchowitz, 2020; Hazan et al., 2020; Hazan & Singh, 2022). Later, studies were generalized
118   to address the problem without cost gradients information (Gradu et al., 2020; Cassel & Koren, 2020;
119   Ghai et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023); instead, they estimated the cost gradients, using the history of
120   scalar cost (bandit feedback) along the trajectory. However, the above research restricts the system
121   to linear transition dynamics. Instead, our work considers the candidate controller pool to handle
122   unknown nonlinear systems.
123   Multi-armed bandits with adversarial disturbances were first addressed in the pioneering work by
124   Auer et al. (2002) under bounded costs in their notable Exp3 algorithm. Arora et al. (2012) later
125   improved the algorithm using the same controller within a mini-batch, attaining a regret bound
126   equivalent to the lower bound presented in Dekel et al. (2014). As we have access to the candidate
127   controller pool in our problem setting, we adopt a bandit-related approach.
128   Dynamic batching gained considerable attention for training deep neural networks by increasing the
129   batch size over time and adaptively increasing the learning rate to maintain the ratio between the two
130   (Devarakonda et al., 2017; Bollapragada et al., 2018; Shallue et al., 2019; Ma et al., 2023). Although
131   this has been widely used in the machine learning literature, we adopt this idea to online control,
132   progressively increasing the batch length within a single trajectory to achieve asymptotic stability.
133   Adaptive learning rate in machine learning is generally determined by a set of gradients observed
134   so far (Ruder, 2016). As we do not have access to the gradients in our problem, we focus on the
135   learning rate for bandit algorithms. Several works (van Erven et al., 2011; de Rooij et al., 2014) in
136   hedge setting, an instance of multi-armed bandit problem, suggested using decreasing learning rate
137   as the batch length increases. Building on this idea, Li et al. (2023) proposed to use a non-increasing
138   learning rate over time, while no theoretical guarantee was presented. To the best of our knowledge,
139   this paper is the first work to provide theoretical guarantees for the adaptive learning rate scheme
140   based on the stability of state norm, where the rate is not necessarily non-increasing.
141   Outline. The paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we formulate the problem and provide
142   necessary definitions and assumptions. In Section 3, we propose our DBAR algorithm. In Section 4,
143   we study the stability of the algorithm, the regret bound, and its applications in switched systems. In
144   Section 5, we present numerical experiments on the DBAR algorithm with an ablation study on batch
145   length and learning rate. Finally, concluding remarks are provided in Section 6.

      Input-to-state (asymptotic) stability (ISS) is a classic notion of stability implying that the controller
191
      successfully stabilizes the system under any bounded noises (Sontag, 2008; Khalil, 2015). Incremental
192
      (asymptotic) stability extends the input-to-state stability to describe the asymptotic behavior of some
193   trajectory towards a different trajectory (Tran et al., 2016). It is worth noting that Li et al. (2023) also
194   adopted these concepts under an exponential stability assumption; i.e., they require some controllers
195   to satisfy exponential ISS and exponential incremental stability. However, in practice, general
196   asymptotic concepts need to be considered for stabilizing controllers. We will address this controller
197   stability issue below.
198   Definition 2.3 (Input-to-state stable controller). A controller π is (asymptotically) input-to-state
199   stable (ISS) if there exists a non-increasing function β(·) : Z+ → R that satisfies β(0) = 12 with
200   limt→∞ β(t) = 0 and γ > 0 such that for any x0 ∈ Rn and ∥wt ∥ ≤ wmax for all t ≥ 0, the sequence
201   {xt }t≥0 determined by xt+1 = f (xt , π(xt ), wt ) satisfies ∥xt ∥ ≤ β(t)∥x0 ∥ + γwmax .
202   Definition 2.4 (Incrementally stable controller). A controller π is (asymptotically) incrementally
203   stable if there exists a non-increasing function β(·) : Z+ → R that satisfies β(0) = 1 with
204   limt→∞ β(t) = 0 such that for any x0 , x̃0 ∈ Rn and ∥wt ∥ ≤ wmax for all t ≥ 0, it holds that
205   ∥xt − x̃t ∥ ≤ β(t)∥x0 − x̃0 ∥ for any two sequences determined by xt+1 = f (xt , π(xt ), wt ) and
206   x̃t+1 = f (x̃t , π(x̃t ), wt ).
207   Assumption 2.5 (Controller pool). Consider the candidate controller index set P0 = {1, . . . , N },
208   in which there exists a controller satisfying Definitions 2.3 and 2.4. There exists π0,max ≥ 0 such
209   that ∥πi (0)∥ ≤ π0,max for all i ∈ P0 . All candidate controllers are Lπ -Lipschitz continuous; i.e.,
210   ∥πi (x) − πi (x̃)∥ ≤ Lπ ∥x − x̃∥ for all x, x̃ ∈ Rn and i ∈ P0 .

308   sb+1 < sb with (αb+1 )2 > τb+1   τb . Apart from this scenario, the effective learning rate experiences
309   a polynomial decay with polynomial batches defined in Assumption 3.1, which does not cause any
310   contradiction with the polynomially decreasing learning rate concept proposed in Aubert et al. (2023).
311   Our adaptive learning rate stabilizes the cost of current batch, alleviating the multiplicative exponential
312   term in the regret bound (see Table 1). Moreover, since we run the algorithm along a single trajectory
313   with the selection of the policy only relying on the state norm as a context, we obtain a linear-time
314   algorithm by harnessing a form of contextual bandit without requiring strict assumptions.

400

401

403   bound, the exponentially increasing term can be tackled by reducing it by the inverse power term

406   initial learning rate η0 = O(T −2/3 ), which inherently serves as a mitigating factor. Note that instead
407   of dramatically reducing the regret bound, our main contribution is on significantly relaxing the

409   Remark 4.9 (Nonlinear control). Our approach is useful to extend the stability and regret analysis
410   beyond linear dynamics, but if |U| is too large, it would be difficult to reach good enough performance
411   as the regret bound depends on exp(O(|U|)). This occurs because we have focused on a discrete set
412   of controllers instead of a connected set as in linear dynamics. Note that in the linear dynamics case,
413   it is guaranteed that the set of stabilizing controllers is connected. However, adopting a discrete set
414   was inevitable to handle unknown nonlinear systems since the set of stabilizing controllers may not be
415   connected. To address this limitation, we believe that this issue can be mitigated by the formulation
416   where the problem of interest is |U| number of connected sets, where |U| is not too large and each
417
      set is disjoint from the others. The agent can apply techniques of continuous parameterization (e.g.
      gradient descent) within a set and also transition between separate sets by leveraging our technique.
418
      This mixture of algorithms for discrete and connected sets will be an interesting future work.

530   6     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In an online bandit nonlinear control problem, an agent makes decisions with the bandit feedback
533
      information, while suffering from nonlinear dynamics and adversarial disturbances. To address
534   such challenges, this paper develops a novel Exp3-type algorithm with theoretical guarantees. The
535   proposed algorithm uses a dynamic batch length to achieve asymptotic stability of the system without
536   requiring an exponential assumption on stabilizing controllers in the pool. Our adaptive learning rate
537   scheme observes the stability of state norm to overcome the inherent multiplicative exponential term
538   in the regret, thereby improving the overall regret. Future directions include extending these results
539   to problems with explicit safety constraints while selecting the best stabilizing controller among a
      continuum of candidate controllers.

      A    N ECESSITY OF DBAR UNDER WEAKER STABILITY NOTION OF REQUIRED
703
           CONTROLLERS
704
705
706   To illustrate how significant the weaker controller stability notion is compared to the exponential
707   notions, let us further present a one-dimensional system, where the current system state is 1. The goal
708   is to achieve a state near 0, and we would like to detect this stability by observing whether one arrives
709   at a state less than 1 − ϵ, where ϵ is an arbitrarily small positive number. Exponentially stabilizing
710
      controllers guarantee to detect the stability in O(log(1/ϵ)) time. However, with an asymptotically
      stabilizing controller, if the controller is designed to keep the system state unchanged for an arbitrarily
711
      long time T and then collapse the state towards 0 afterward, one cannot detect the stability before
712
      time T regardless of how small ϵ is. In such a case, even though the controller ultimately achieves the
713   goal, it may take a lot of time to learn whether a closed-loop system would be stable or not.
714
715   Note that dynamic batch length is an important part of our work. If an exponentially stabilizing
716
      controller is applied to a system, one can quickly certify the stability. However, if we only have the
      asymptotically stabilizing controllers as in our problem setting, it may take a long time to observe
717
      any abnormal behavior in the closed-loop system. Such an issue cannot be handled by a fixed batch
718
      length and in that sense dynamic batch length is a necessary part of our work. In Table 1, we have
719   stated the intermediate step "Dynamic Batching" to achieve closed-loop system asymptotic stability,
720   which was not achievable by the previous works.
721
722
      Figure 4 also demonstrates the necessity of a dynamic batch length regardless of the noise assump-
      tion. The blue and orange lines represent the state norms generated by a fixed batch length and a
723
      dynamic batch length, respectively. With both relatively easier statistical noise and more challenging
724
      adversarial noise, the blue line shows a larger state norm than the orange line. Moreover, the blue line
725   occasionally has higher values than the red line, which is our asymptotic stability bound γwmax = 1.5,
726   while the orange line remains below the red line after a certain time.
